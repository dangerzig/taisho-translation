# 佛說象頭精舍經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha at the Elephant-Head Monastery

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0466

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, translated by Viniścaya (毘尼多流支)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling at the Elephant-Head Monastery in the city of Gayā.

At that time, having attained buddhahood not long before, the Tathāgata was together with a full assembly of one thousand great bhikṣus, all of whom were former matted-hair ascetics of past lives. They had accomplished what was to be done, cast off all heavy burdens, long ago departed from birth and death, exhausted all afflictions, and attained the equal wisdom of emptiness. With right reception, wise minds, and full awakening to all things, they had reached the other shore. They were all arhats. He was also accompanied by an immeasurable assembly of great bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

At that time, the World-Honored One sat alone in contemplation, entered into various samādhis, and universally observed the dharma-realm. Having himself awakened and attained the path, possessing all-knowledge, with everything to be done completed, having cast off all heavy burdens, having crossed the stream of birth and death, having abandoned miserliness and greed, having extracted the thorns of the three poisons, having exhausted all craving, having assembled the great Dharma-ship, having struck the great Dharma-drum, having blown the great Dharma-conch, having raised the great Dharma-banner, having severed birth and death, he proclaimed the true Dharma:

"I have closed the gates of evil destinies and opened the doors of wholesome paths, forever departed from evil lands and traveled through all pure lands. I observe this Dharma: who cultivates bodhi? Who attains bodhi? Who desires to attain it? In past, present, and future, who has realized it? Is it the body that attains it, or the mind?

"If bodhi is attained by the body, the body is without awareness, like grass, wood, sand, gravel, walls, and fences, knowing nothing. It is a combination of the four great elements, born of father and mother, constantly requiring food, drink, clothing, bathing, and rubbing, and in the end it will perish and decay: it is a dharma of wearing away. As for bodhi, it has only an empty name without real characteristics. It is without sound, without form, without accomplishment, without being seen, without entry, without awareness, without going, without coming. Dharmas such as these are also without binding. It surpasses all dharmas, transcends the three realms. It is without being seen or heard, without self or what belongs to self, without an agent, without a location, without a dwelling, without grasping, without attachment, without going out, without entering, without vow, without abiding, without marks, without appearance, without that or this, without demonstration, like an illusion or transformation. It arises through the twelve links of dependent origination, without location, invisible, free from marks, appearing like empty space: tranquil and still, without sound, without echo, without written characters, without words, and also without verbal expression. Knowing in this way: this is called bodhi.

"If bodhi is attained by the mind, the mind is without stability, like an illusion or transformation. It is entirely produced by the deluded karma of past false thinking. It is without form, without substance, like empty space. As for bodhi, it has no location. It is neither past, nor future, nor present. All dharmas are empty. Although there are verbal expressions, they have names but no substance. This is the unconditioned dharma: empty, signless, without intention, neither existent nor non-existent, neither demonstrable nor manifest, without speech, without hearing. Bodhi is neither attained in the past, nor in the future, nor in the present, and yet it is not attained apart from the three times. It is without marks, neither made nor not made. If one can thus awaken to and clearly comprehend the dharmas of the three times, this itself is bodhi."

At that time, the youth Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha, saying: "World-Honored One! If bodhi is markless, then upon what basis should good men and good women abide in order to accomplish bodhi?"

The Buddha addressed Mañjuśrī: "Those bodhisattvas who study bodhi should abide in this way."

Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How should the bodhisattvas abide?"

The Buddha addressed Mañjuśrī: "Bodhi transcends the three realms, is beyond verbal expression, free from all written characters, and has no dwelling place. Furthermore, Mañjuśrī, when a bodhisattva-mahāsattva abides in non-abiding, this is abiding in bodhi. Abiding without attachment is abiding in bodhi. Abiding in the dharma of emptiness is abiding in bodhi. Abiding in dharma-nature is abiding in bodhi. Abiding in all dharmas as having no substantial characteristics is abiding in bodhi. Abiding in immeasurable faith is abiding in bodhi. Abiding in neither increase nor decrease is abiding in bodhi. Abiding in non-discriminating thought is abiding in bodhi. Abiding as in a mirror-image, as in a valley echo, as in the moon reflected in water, as in a shimmering mirage: Mañjuśrī, abiding in such dharmas is abiding in bodhi."

At that time, the Devaputra Pure-Light-Flame addressed the youth Mañjuśrī: "How does one practice? Through what kind of action does one carry out the bodhisattva practice?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! Giving rise to great compassion toward all sentient beings: this is the bodhisattva practice."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "How do the bodhisattvas practice and give rise to great compassion?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! A mind without deceit or deception: this is the bodhisattva's great compassion."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "How does one practice to be free from deceit and deception?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! Giving rise to an equal mind toward all sentient beings: this is being free from deceit and deception. The bodhisattvas should study in this way."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practice to give rise to an equal mind toward all sentient beings?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Without 'that' or 'this,' without any discriminating views: this is the practice of equality. The bodhisattvas should study in this way."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva further practice without 'that' or 'this,' without any discriminating views, and give rise to the practice of equality toward all sentient beings?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! Wholesome mindfulness and reverence, without 'that' or 'this,' without any discriminating views, and with the dharma of equality: the bodhisattvas should practice in this way."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva's wholesome mindfulness and reverence, up to being without 'that' or 'this' and without any discriminating views: from what do these arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! Wholesome mindfulness and reverence arise from generating the aspiration for bodhi. The bodhisattvas should study in this way."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "From what does the aspiration for bodhi arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! The aspiration for bodhi arises from the six pāramitās."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "From what do the six pāramitās arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! They arise from skillful means and wisdom."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "From what do the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's skillful means and wisdom arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! Skillful means and wisdom arise from non-negligence."

The devaputra further addressed Mañjuśrī: "From what does non-negligence arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! Non-negligence arises from cultivating the three wholesome karmas."

The devaputra further asked: "From what does cultivating the three wholesome karmas arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! It arises from cultivating the ten wholesome courses of action."

The devaputra further asked: "From what does cultivating the ten wholesome courses of action arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! It arises from properly guarding body, speech, and mind."

The devaputra further asked Mañjuśrī: "From what do these three wholesome karmas arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "They arise from wholesome reflection."

The devaputra further asked: "From what does wholesome reflection arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "It arises from wholesome contemplative observation and practice."

The devaputra further asked: "From what does wholesome contemplative observation and practice arise?"

Mañjuśrī said: "It arises from retentive mindfulness that does not forget."

The devaputra further asked: "How many kinds of mind does retentive mindfulness that does not forget have? Through what causes and conditions does one attain the fruit?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! A bodhisattva-mahāsattva has four kinds of aspiration, and through causes one attains the fruit. What are the four? First, initial aspiration. Second, sustained mindfulness and practice. Third, non-retrogression. Fourth, being born together with the good. Through initial aspiration, one attains sustained mindfulness. Through cultivating sustained mindfulness, one attains non-retrogression. Through non-retrogression, one is born together with the good.

"Furthermore, devaputra, initial aspiration is like planting a seed in good soil. Sustained mindfulness and practice are like the sprout growing. Practicing without retrogression is like the stem, trunk, branches, and leaves increasing. Being born together with the good is like the flower and fruit forming and ripening. Aspiration, sustained mindful practice, non-retrogression, and being born together with the good are all likewise.

"Furthermore, devaputra, one who initially aspires is like a cart-builder who knows the various kinds of wood. Sustained mindful practice is like joining the wood together. Practicing without retrogression is like the cart being completed. Being born together with the good is like loading and using the cart. Aspiration, sustained mindful practice, non-retrogression, and being born together with the good are all likewise.

"Furthermore, devaputra, initial aspiration is like the new moon. Sustained mindful practice is like the moon of the fifth to seventh night. Practicing without retrogression is like the moon of the tenth night. Being born together with the good is like the moon of the fourteenth night. The Tathāgata's wisdom, full and without deficiency, is like the moon of the fifteenth night. Aspiration, sustained mindful practice, non-retrogression, and being born together with the good are all likewise.

"Furthermore, devaputra, one who initially aspires surpasses the stage of the śrāvaka. The second aspiration surpasses the stage of the pratyekabuddha. The third aspiration surpasses the indeterminate stage. The fourth aspiration reaches the determined stage.

"Furthermore, devaputra, just as the sounds 'a' and 'ā' are the root of all written characters, so too initial aspiration is the root of all that is wholesome. Just as learning written characters produces partial wisdom, so too sustained mindful practice produces partial wisdom. Just as an accountant who tallies immeasurable quantities knows their proper divisions, so too the non-retrogressing mind knows the mind's non-retrogression. Just as a person who clearly understands the treatises, so too being born together with the good, with a clear and wholesome mind, is likewise.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first aspiration sustains wholesome causes. The second aspiration sustains wisdom. The third aspiration sustains meditative concentration. The fourth aspiration sustains the fruit.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first aspiration receives and upholds wholesome causes. The second aspiration receives and upholds wisdom. The third aspiration receives and upholds meditative concentration. The fourth aspiration receives and upholds the fruit.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first aspiration brings wholesome causes to accomplishment. The second aspiration brings wisdom to accomplishment. The third aspiration brings meditative concentration to accomplishment. The fourth aspiration brings the true fruit to accomplishment.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first aspiration enters the path through wholesome causes. The second aspiration enters the path through wisdom. The third aspiration enters the path through meditative concentration. The fourth aspiration enters the path through the fruit.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first aspiration is like a physician recognizing medicines. The second aspiration is like knowing the properties of each medicine. The third aspiration is like prescribing medicine according to the illness. The fourth aspiration is like causing the patient to take the medicine and recover.

"Furthermore, devaputra, through the first aspiration one is born into the family of the Dharma-King. Through the second aspiration one studies the Dharma-King's Dharma. Through the third aspiration one cultivates the Dharma-King's practice. Through the fourth aspiration one fulfills the royal position. These are called the four kinds of aspiration."

At that time, the Devaputra Pure-Light-Flame addressed Mañjuśrī: "What is the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's swift path? Through this path, the bodhisattva swiftly attains anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi."

Mañjuśrī said: "Devaputra! The swift path has two kinds. The bodhisattva-mahāsattva, traveling these two paths, swiftly attains anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi. What are the two? First, the path of skillful means. Second, the path of prajñā. By receiving and upholding skillful wisdom, one can observe all sentient beings. By receiving and upholding prajñā, one observes that all dharmas are empty and can sever doubtful attachments. Through skillful wisdom, one harmonizes all dharmas. Through prajñā, dharmas do not coalesce. Skillful means is the cause; prajñā is the fruit. Through skillful means, one knows all dharmas. Through prajñā, one knows that all dharmas are empty. Through skillful wisdom, one adorns buddha-lands. Through prajñā, one knows that all buddha-lands are entirely equal. Through skillful means, one knows that the faculties and dispositions of sentient beings differ. Through prajñā, one knows that the faculties and dispositions of sentient beings are all empty. Through skillful means, one attains the taste of ambrosia and realizes bodhi. Through prajñā, one awakens to the dharmas of all buddhas and the equal, true path.

"Furthermore, devaputra, there are two more practices that enable a bodhisattva-mahāsattva to swiftly attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi. What are the two? First, the conditioned. Second, the unconditioned. The conditioned encompasses the five pāramitās. The unconditioned encompasses the Prajñā-pāramitā. There are two more practices that enable a bodhisattva-mahāsattva to swiftly attain bodhi. What are the two? First, contaminated practice. Second, uncontaminated practice. Contaminated practice is the five pāramitās. Uncontaminated practice is the Prajñā-pāramitā. There are two more practices that enable a bodhisattva-mahāsattva to swiftly attain bodhi. What are the two? First, the practice of abiding. Second, the practice of non-abiding. The practice of abiding is the five pāramitās. The practice of non-abiding is the Prajñā-pāramitā. There are two more practices that enable a bodhisattva-mahāsattva to swiftly attain bodhi. What are the two? First, the measurable. Second, the immeasurable. The measurable is the five pāramitās. The immeasurable is the Prajñā-pāramitā. The measurable practice belongs to the dharma of marks. The immeasurable practice belongs to the dharma of marklessness. There are two more practices that enable a bodhisattva-mahāsattva to swiftly attain bodhi. What are the two? First, the wisdom-practice. Second, the concentration-practice. Through the wisdom-practice, one proceeds from the first stage to the seventh stage. Through the concentration-practice, one proceeds from the eighth stage to the tenth stage."

At that time, the Bodhisattva Fearless-Wisdom-Dhāraṇī addressed the youth Mañjuśrī: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva know meaning? How does one know wisdom?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Good man! Meaning is without substance, and wisdom is also without substance."

The Bodhisattva Fearless-Wisdom-Dhāraṇī further asked Mañjuśrī: "How is meaning without substance and wisdom also without substance?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Good man! Meaning being without substance means it is unconditioned, without action, without marks, without appearance, without coming, without going. This is called meaning. Wisdom being without substance means it is neither a fixed dharma nor a non-fixed dharma. This is called wisdom. One should receive and uphold it thus. Meaning is without substance, neither existent nor non-existent. The substance of wisdom is empty, neither existent nor non-existent, without grasping, without abandoning. One should receive and uphold it thus. Furthermore, meaning is neither fixed nor non-fixed. Wisdom is called the mind's path. Mind and wisdom are equal, without differentiation. One should receive and uphold it thus. Furthermore, wisdom takes meditation as its substance. Meditation and wisdom are equal, without differentiation. Through skillful means, one observes the aggregates, sense-bases, elements, and the twelve links of dependent origination, the turning of birth and death, and the characteristics of good and evil, all as like an illusion, neither existent nor non-existent. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva should observe all dharmas in this way.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has ten kinds of wisdom-practice. What are the ten? First, cause-wisdom. Second, fruit-wisdom. Third, meaning-wisdom. Fourth, skillful-means-wisdom. Fifth, prajñā-wisdom. Sixth, receiving-and-upholding-wisdom. Seventh, pāramitā-wisdom. Eighth, great-compassion-wisdom. Ninth, the wisdom of compassionately teaching and transforming sentient beings. Tenth, the wisdom of non-attachment to all dharmas. These are called the bodhisattva's ten kinds of wisdom-practice.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva generates ten kinds of pure practice. What are the ten? First, generating the pure practice of one's own bodily karma. Second, generating the pure practice of all sentient beings' bodily karma. Third, generating the pure practice of one's own verbal karma. Fourth, generating the pure practice of all sentient beings' verbal karma. Fifth, generating the pure practice of one's own mental karma. Sixth, generating the pure practice of all sentient beings' mental karma. Seventh, generating the practice of inner pure equality for all sentient beings. Eighth, generating the practice of outer pure equality for all sentient beings. Ninth, generating the pure wisdom-practice of all buddhas. Tenth, generating the practice of purifying buddha-lands and bringing sentient beings to accomplishment. If there are sentient beings afflicted with various illnesses, one provides medicine and enables them to gain ease. For those beset by afflictions, one teaches and transforms them with unconditioned wisdom, enabling them to leave the three realms. One enables all to fully accomplish the unconditioned path of merit, virtue, and wisdom. These are called the bodhisattva's ten kinds of pure practice.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has ten kinds of skillful means. What are the ten? First, the skillful means of the other shore. Second, the skillful means of receiving and upholding. Third, the skillful means of wisdom. Fourth, the skillful means of expedient methods. Fifth, the skillful means of great compassion. Sixth, the skillful means of fulfilling knowledge. Seventh, the skillful means of fulfilling discernment. Eighth, the skillful means of tranquil mindfulness. Ninth, the skillful means of authentic practice. Tenth, the skillful means of equality toward all sentient beings, without any aversion or fondness. These are called the bodhisattva's ten kinds of skillful means.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has ten kinds of inexhaustible analysis of the body. What are the ten? First, inexhaustible analysis of objects and affairs. Second, inexhaustible analysis of afflictions. Third, inexhaustible analysis of dharmas. Fourth, inexhaustible analysis of craving. Fifth, inexhaustible analysis of views. Sixth, inexhaustible analysis of good and evil. Seventh, inexhaustible analysis of fabricated actions. Eighth, inexhaustible analysis of non-attachment and non-clinging. Ninth, inexhaustible analysis of harmony and union. Tenth, inexhaustible analysis of the complete fulfillment of bodhi-wisdom. These are called the ten kinds of inexhaustible analysis of the body.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has ten kinds of taming practice. What are the ten? First, taming wholesome practice. Second, taming miserliness, giving generously like rain. Third, taming non-diligent practice. Fourth, taming the three karmas. Fifth, taming the poisonous mind and not giving rise to anger. Sixth, taming by generating a compassionate mind. Seventh, taming the lazy mind. Eighth, taming by diligently cultivating the Buddha-Dharma. Ninth, taming the unwholesome mind and not committing evil deeds. Tenth, taming through the sovereign mastery of meditative concentration and liberation. There are ten more kinds of taming practice. What are the ten? First, taming by destroying ignorance and lack of wisdom. Second, taming through skillful means, dhāraṇī, and the Prajñā-pāramitā. Third, taming afflictions. Fourth, taming by giving rise to the path. Fifth, taming through the dhāraṇī of faith and truthfulness. Sixth, taming by not falling into evil destinies. Seventh, taming the unwholesome mind. Eighth, taming through sovereign mastery of what is timely and untimely. Ninth, taming one's own conduct. Tenth, taming through contemplation of emptiness. These are called the ten kinds of taming practice.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has ten kinds of taming others. What are the ten? Namely, taming by distancing oneself from the three evil bodily karmas, the four evil verbal karmas, and the three evil mental karmas. There are also ten kinds of internal and external observation of dharmas without giving rise to attachment. What are the ten? First, observing the internal realm of the body as entirely empty, without giving rise to attachment. Second, observing the external realm of the body as also entirely empty, without giving rise to attachment. Third, observing all internal and external dharmas as entirely empty, without giving rise to attachment. Fourth, toward all-knowledge, not giving rise to attachment. Fifth, toward the path one cultivates, not giving rise to attachment. Sixth, observing the stages of the worthy and noble without giving rise to attachment. Seventh, in long cultivation of purity, not giving rise to attachment. Eighth, abiding in the Prajñā-pāramitā without giving rise to attachment. Ninth, in teaching, discussing the Dharma, and transforming sentient beings, not giving rise to attachment. Tenth, observing all sentient beings with great skillful means, compassion, and pity, without giving rise to attachment. These are called the ten kinds of internal and external observation of dharmas without giving rise to attachment.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should in this way make the mind firm and cultivate the path of bodhi. If one does not thus make bodhi firm, one cannot be called a bodhisattva. What does 'firm' mean? When the three karmas of body, speech, and mind are in accord and do not contradict each other: this is called firm. What is 'not firm'? When the three karmas of body, speech, and mind are not in accord and contradict each other: this is called not firm.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has two more kinds of right practice that make the bodhi-mind firm. What are the two? First, the practice of right mindfulness of bodhi. Second, the practice of cultivating meditative concentration and severing all afflictions. These are called the two kinds of right practice that make the bodhi-mind firm.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has two more kinds of right practice that are firm. What are the two? First, taming one's own conduct. Second, taming the conduct of sentient beings. Good man, these are called the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's two kinds of firm right practice.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has two more kinds of firm right practice. What are the two? First, through diligent cultivation and study, one attains all-knowledge. Second, without cultivation and study, one attains all-knowledge. These are called the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's two kinds of firm right practice.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has two more kinds of firm right practice. What are the two? First, the firm right practice of abiding in the stages through skillful means. Second, the firm right practice of abiding in the stages without moving. These are called the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's two kinds of firm right practice.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has two more kinds of firm right practice. What are the two? First, the firm right practice of distancing oneself from the defiled stages. Second, the firm right practice of completing one's own practice in each stage through skillful means. These are called the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's two kinds of firm right practice.

"Furthermore, good man, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva has two more kinds of firm right practice. What are the two? First, the firm right practice of manifesting in the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Second, the firm right practice of non-retrogression through skillful means in the Buddha's bodhi. These are called the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's two kinds of firm right practice.

"Good man! Through such firm right practice and immeasurable skillful means, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva will attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi."

At that time, the World-Honored One praised the youth Mañjuśrī, saying: "Excellent, excellent! Mañjuśrī! You have spoken these words splendidly."

When this Dharma was taught, Mañjuśrī, immeasurable devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and the entire great assembly, hearing the Buddha teach the Dharma, faithfully received it, prostrated, and joyfully put it into practice.
