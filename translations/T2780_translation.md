# Commentary on the Bathhouse Sūtra

## *Wēnshì Jīng Shū* (溫室經疏)

Taishō 2780

Composed by Huìjìng (*Huìjìng*, 慧淨) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a commentary by the Tang-dynasty monk Huìjìng (578-645? CE) on the short Sūtra on Bathing the Saṃgha in a Bathhouse (Wēnshì Xǐyù Zhòngsēng Jīng, T701), one of the shorter sūtras in the Taishō canon. The sūtra tells how Jīvaka (Qíyù, "the Great Physician," rendered here as Zhīyù), the famous physician of ancient India who served King Ajātaśatru and the Buddha, visits the Buddha and requests permission to bathe the saṃgha. The Buddha teaches that bathing the monks with seven supplies (fire, clean water, bean-powder, sesame oil, purified ash, willow-sticks, and inner garments) cures seven ailments and produces seven kinds of merit, leading through successive rebirths as ministers, kings, sun and moon devas, Indra, wheel-turning kings, Brahmā-devas, bodhisattvas, and finally buddhahood. Huìjìng's commentary follows the standard Chinese exegetical format: he provides a preface (xù), then works through the sūtra phrase by phrase, explaining the terms, identifying the narrative structure (the "attestation preface" and "arising preface"), correlating the seven supplies with the seven cures and seven rewards, and distinguishing three types of karmic fruit (similar-cause fruit, retribution fruit, and dominant fruit). The commentary reflects the Chinese Buddhist emphasis on merit-making through service to the saṃgha and illustrates the genre of detailed phrase-by-phrase commentary applied even to minor sūtras.*

---

## Preface to the Bathhouse [Sūtra]

The Bathhouse Sūtra is the excellent foundation for accumulating pure karma, the nourishing waters that cleanse the dust of accumulated [karmic] burdens. It is a stairway that surpasses the heavenly realms, a vessel that crosses the ocean of suffering. It encompasses both cause and effect, embracing merit and wisdom. Its principle is rich, its words concise; its style is forceful, its purport elegant. If one gives rise to a single thought of pure faith, one is permanently separated from the four floods [of desire, existence, views, and ignorance]. If one undertakes a single branch of wholesome action, one swiftly ascends to the ten bhūmis. The King of Physicians [Jīvaka] thereby established the beginning of his career, and the General of the Dharma thereby opened the way.

### Title

"Sūtra on the Buddha's Teaching on Bathing the Assembly of Monks in a Bathhouse" (*Fó Shuō Wēnshì Xǐyù Zhòngsēng Jīng*): "Buddha" means one who is uniquely outstanding amid the darkness of birth-and-death, who has awakened to the myriad dharmas and realized the four kinds of birth. His awakened practice being perfectly complete, he is therefore called "Buddha." "Teaching" (*shuō*) means he expresses the four kinds of unhindered eloquence from the tip of his tongue, streaming the eight kinds of [vocal] sounds. "Bathhouse" (*wēnshì*): "warm" (*wēn*) is used as a noun, for "spirit," and as a principle, for the divine purpose. "Room" (*shì*) is for sheltering the body, and for principle. Externally it cleanses the eight kinds of dust, hence it is called "washing and bathing." Internally cultivating [purity] is called "assembly of monks" (*zhòngsēng*). "Sūtra" (*jīng*), though having five meanings, may be briefly presented in two: first, a "gushing spring" [of meaning, inexhaustible]; second, a "plumb-line" [for determining the straight from the crooked].

## Commentary on the Attestation Preface

**"Ānanda said: 'Thus have I heard. At one time, the Buddha was in the kingdom of Magadha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa.'"**

The two phrases of the preface establish the structure for the whole. This is the attestation preface (*zhèngxìn xù*). Ānanda was the Buddha's cousin... "Thus have I heard" attests to what was heard through personal reception. "At one time" expresses the occasion of the transformation. Magadha, this is translated as "Victorious Kingdom" (*Shànshèng Guó*). Gṛdhrakūṭa, this is translated as "Vulture Peak" (*Língjiù Shān*).

---

## Commentary on the Arising Preface

**"Within the city of Rājagṛha, there was a great elder, the son of the Āmra-woman, named Jīvaka, who was a great physician-king."**

Below is the arising preface, which has three parts: (1) naming Jīvaka, (2) narrating his deeds, and (3) summarizing. Under naming, there are five aspects: the place is distinguished, the position is honored, the lineage is noble, the name is indicated, and the reason is explained.

"The city of Rājagṛha" shows the distinguished place. "Great elder" shows the honored position. "Son of the Āmra-woman" shows the noble lineage. The Āmra-woman was born from an āmra [mango] fruit; she received her nature differently from ordinary beings. Jīvaka, being the Āmra-woman's son, possesses noble lineage. "Jīvaka" (*Zhīyù*) in Sanskrit means "Able to Bring to Life" (*Nénghuó*). He expertly understood the origins of the four kinds of disease and was thoroughly versed in the essentials of the eightfold medical arts. When he applied his needle, [the patient] was settled; when he administered medicine, [the patient] was invariably cured. Having this ability, he was called "Able to Bring to Life."

**"From youth he loved learning; as he grew, his devotion deepened. His talents and arts were surpassingly comprehensive; his wisdom penetrated the five treatises, astronomy, and geography."**

This praises his accomplishment in learning. The "five treatises" (*wǔ míng lùn*) are: the inner treatise [Buddhist doctrine], the treatise on logic, the treatise on grammar, the treatise on arts and crafts, and the treatise on medicine.

**"All whom he treated were healed without exception. The dead were restored to life; funeral carriages turned back."**

This praises his healing accomplishments. Those previously ill, once they encountered Jīvaka, had their spirits restored. Funeral carriages were turned around and the dead returned to life.

**"His virtues are too many to describe fully. The eight kingdoms looked to him with respect; all who saw him were delighted."**

This is the summary.

## Commentary on the Main Teaching

**"Then Jīvaka, in the night, suddenly gave rise to a thought. At dawn, he went to the Buddha's place to ask about his doubt."**

Below narrates his deeds. In the quiet of a fine evening, Jīvaka stirred his mind. Having awakened to the right cause for entering the path, he formed the intention to bathe the saṃgha. But although the thought was in himself, the Dharma does not arise from oneself alone. Therefore he waited eagerly for dawn to go before the Buddha and request a resolution.

**"At early dawn, he commanded his household, great and small, his retinue, to dress formally and proceed to the Buddha's place."**

He departed. The morning star had barely faded and the rising sun was just beginning to spread its light. Having seen the sign pointing the way, he suitably urged his company to set out.

[The commentary continues phrase by phrase through the sūtra narrative: Jīvaka arriving at the monastery; seeing the Buddha's radiant light; the assembly of thousands seated around the Buddha; the Buddha teaching the Dharma; Jīvaka alighting from his carriage, prostrating before the Buddha, and sitting to one side.]

---

### The Buddha's Encouragement

**"The Buddha comforted him, saying: 'Welcome, physician-king! If you wish to ask something, do not hesitate.'"**

This is the Buddha's encouragement. Since this teaching depends upon the physician-king, the Buddha calls out "Welcome" to comfort his heart, and "Do not hesitate" to advance his questioning.

### Jīvaka's Request

[Jīvaka expresses self-reproach for having been "wild and uncultivated," following worldly currents without having cultivated merit. He then requests permission to bathe the Buddha and the saṃgha, and makes his vow: "May all sentient beings be purified through the long night, their defilements eliminated, and may they not encounter manifold afflictions."]

### The Seven Supplies

**"The Buddha told the physician-king: 'The method of bathing requires seven supplies, which remove seven ailments and produce seven kinds of merit.'"**

This marks the beginning of the answer, with three sections: heading, explanation, and verse.

**"What are the seven supplies? (1) Fire for heating, (2) clean water, (3) bean-powder, (4) sesame oil, (5) purified ash, (6) willow-sticks, (7) inner garments."**

These are the seven supplies. Fire blazes in the furnace... Clean water is clarified like a mirror, its impurities removed... Bean-powder is smooth and lustrous... Sesame oil is rich and moist... Purified ash is fiercely refined from the choicest burned wood... Willow-sticks are supple, broken from the riverside willow... Inner garments are freshly cut fine silk, white as excellent bleached cloth.

### The Seven Cures

**"What are the seven cures? (1) The four great elements are stable and secure; (2) wind-ailments are removed; (3) dampness and numbness are removed; (4) cold and chill are removed; (5) heat is removed; (6) grime and filth are removed; (7) the body becomes light and nimble, and the eyes become clear and bright."**

Stability of the four elements is the function of the inner garment. Removing wind-ailments is the function of purified ash. Removing dampness is the function of sesame oil. Removing cold is the function of fire. Removing heat is the function of willow-sticks. Removing grime is the function of bean-powder. Lightness of body and clarity of eyes is the function of clean water.

### The Seven Kinds of Merit (Similar-Cause Fruit)

**"(1) The four great elements are secure, without illness; in lives to come, one is born brave and robust, and people pay their respects."**

This is the similar-cause fruit of fire. Warm qi flowing through the body brings complete ease; cold wind invading the bones brings trembling. Because fire advances warmth and drives out cold, the reward is a body that is secure and robust.

**"(2) In lives to come, one is pure and clean, with a dignified appearance; dust and water do not cling, and people show respect."**

This is the similar-cause fruit of clean water.

**"(3) The body is always fragrant, clothing is clean; all who see one are delighted and show respect."**

This is the similar-cause fruit of bean-powder.

**"(4) The skin is smooth and lustrous, one's majestic radiance and virtue are great; none fail to show respect, and one is without equal."**

This is the similar-cause fruit of sesame oil.

**"(5) One has many attendants who brush away one's dust; naturally receiving blessings, one always knows one's past lives."**

This is the similar-cause fruit of purified ash.

**"(6) The mouth and teeth are fragrant and fine, aligned and white; whatever one speaks and teaches, all respectfully follow."**

This is the similar-cause fruit of willow-sticks.

**"(7) In the place where one is reborn, one naturally has clothing, adorned with precious ornaments; those who see one stand in awe."**

This is the similar-cause fruit of inner garments.

### The Retribution Fruit

**"From this cause and condition, one may be born as a minister, or as an emperor, or as the sun and moon, one of the four deva-kings, as Indra, as a golden wheel-turning king, in the Brahmā heaven receiving immeasurable blessings, or as a bodhisattva who arouses the aspiration, cultivates the stages, accomplishes the work, and finally attains buddhahood."**

This is the retribution fruit. Being a minister, a king, or the various heavenly rulers all represent successive stages of karmic reward. "Arousing the aspiration" is the stage of joyful resolve. "Cultivating the stages" is the stage of insight. "Accomplishing the work" is the stage of cultivation. "Fulfilling the aspiration" is the ultimate stage. "Finally attaining buddhahood" is the stage of liberation.

### The Dominant Fruit

**"This cause comes from making offerings to the assembly of holy monks, an immeasurable field of merit: drought and flood cannot harm it."**

This is the dominant fruit. Offering to the saṃgha is like planting seeds in a field of merit. "Drought and flood cannot harm it" is the reward.

## The Verse Section

[The commentary notes that the prose section explains the meaning, while the verse section is for retention. There are twenty verses in three sections: the first verse is a general heading calling for attention; the next eighteen verses elaborate the nine grades of reward (common people, ministers, kings, the four heavenly kings, sun and moon, Indra, wheel-turning kings, the transformation-heaven devas, and Brahmā, plus bodhisattvas); the final verse inspires joy through the metaphor of the merit-field.]

---

## Resolving Doubts

**"After the Buddha spoke the verse, he told Jīvaka: 'Observe the beings of the three realms, the various classes of devas and humans, high and low, great and small, more and less in merit. All of this is due to the unequal effort of mind in past lives. Therefore the rewards received by each are different.'"**

This resolves doubts about the practice. In principle, if the cause (bathing the saṃgha) is one, the fruit should also be one. Why then are the rewards so different, ranging from humans to buddhas? The Buddha explains: although the practice is the same, the aspiration differs. Practice can produce existence or non-existence; aspiration can determine superiority or inferiority. If the aspiration is for the supramundane, one obtains supramundane fruit. If the aspiration is for the worldly, one obtains worldly reward.

**"Such blessings and rewards all come from bathing the holy assembly of monks."**

This resolves doubts about the vow. One might ask: if the aspiration alone determines the result, why bother with the practice? The answer is that a vow alone, without the power of practice, would have nothing to draw upon. The practice is precisely the bathing of the saṃgha.

---

## Transmission Section

**"When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, Ānanda addressed the Buddha: 'What should this sūtra be called? How should it be used to exhort and instruct?'"**

Two questions: the first asks for the sūtra's name, the second asks how it should be used for exhortation.

**"The Buddha told Ānanda: 'This sūtra is called the Sūtra on Bathing the Assembly of Monks in a Bathhouse.'"**

Since the sūtra's substance is the bathing of the saṃgha, its name is taken from that.

**"What all buddhas have recorded is not my creation alone. Those who practice attain liberation; it is not bestowed by spirits. Those who seek the blessings of purity should practice this themselves."**

All buddhas have recorded it; therefore it can be used for exhortation. Practitioners attain liberation; therefore it can be used for instruction.

**"When the Buddha had finished speaking, Jīvaka and his retinue, hearing the sūtra, rejoiced, attained the fruit of stream-entry (*srotāpanna*), bowed to the Buddha, withdrew, and began preparations for the bathing."**

Jīvaka, whose roots of the Way had long matured, upon hearing the sūtra attained the fruit. Since his request was not in vain, he withdrew to make preparations.

**"The assembly, great and small, each attained a stage of the path. Together they bowed and departed."**

Since they came together [with Jīvaka], they did not attain the fruit alone. Having heard the Dharma and obtained the Way, they expressed their reverence, rose, and departed.
