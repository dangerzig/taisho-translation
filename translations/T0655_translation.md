# 佛說勝義空經
## Sūtra on Paramārtha-śūnyatā Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 655

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護) et al.

---

The Buddha, the World-Honored One, was on one occasion in the land of the Kurus, together with an assembly of bhikṣus.

The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "There are teachings that I have expounded previously; there is also a correct teaching called paramārtha-śūnyatā—emptiness in the ultimate sense. Listen carefully and attend to it with utmost mindfulness; I will now explain it to you.

"Bhikṣus! What is meant by paramārtha-śūnyatā? It is this: when the eye arises, there is no slightest dharma that comes from anywhere; and when the eye ceases, there is no slightest dharma that disperses and goes anywhere.

"Bhikṣus! The eye has no inherent reality; it is apart from any real dharma. To state it concisely: there is karma and there is its result, yet no agent can be apprehended. When this aggregate has ended, it is subsumed by another aggregate—distinct dharmas converging together, arising through causes and conditions. So too with the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. When mind arises, there is no slightest dharma that comes from anywhere; and when mind ceases, there is no slightest dharma that disperses and goes anywhere.

"Bhikṣus! Mind has no inherent reality; it is apart from any real dharma—as stated above, distinct dharmas converging together. These converging dharmas have no reality that can be apprehended; they arise through causes and conditions. These conditionally arisen phenomena are: avidyā conditions saṃskāras; saṃskāras condition vijñāna; vijñāna conditions nāmarūpa; nāmarūpa conditions the six āyatanas; the six āyatanas condition sparśa; sparśa conditions vedanā; vedanā conditions tṛṣṇā; tṛṣṇā conditions upādāna; upādāna conditions bhava; bhava conditions jāti; jāti conditions aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, and distress—thus the entire great mass of suffering arises. These arisen phenomena have no reality that can be apprehended; having arisen, they cease.

"Through this principle of cessation: the cessation of avidyā leads to the cessation of saṃskāras; the cessation of saṃskāras leads to the cessation of vijñāna; the cessation of vijñāna leads to the cessation of nāmarūpa; the cessation of nāmarūpa leads to the cessation of the six āyatanas; the cessation of the six āyatanas leads to the cessation of sparśa; the cessation of sparśa leads to the cessation of vedanā; the cessation of vedanā leads to the cessation of tṛṣṇā; the cessation of tṛṣṇā leads to the cessation of upādāna; the cessation of upādāna leads to the cessation of bhava; the cessation of bhava leads to the cessation of jāti; the cessation of jāti leads to the cessation of aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, and distress—thus the entire great mass of suffering ceases. All that has been expounded in this way is called paramārtha-śūnyatā.

"Bhikṣus! The teachings that have been expounded above—this correct teaching—you should all study and practice."
