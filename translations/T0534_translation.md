# 佛說月光童子經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Boy Candraprabha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0534

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus it was heard.

At one time the Buddha was traveling in the city of Rājagṛha, at Vulture Peak, together with a great assembly of one thousand bhikṣus, all of whom possessed sacred penetration without obstruction and had all become true adepts. Their bonds of outflows were resolved, birth and death severed, impure darkness dispelled, and the light of wisdom alone remained. Twenty thousand bodhisattvas, possessing sacred penetration and holy attainment, presided over the three realms. They had gathered in all dharmas; their skillful wisdom was without bounds. They had attained the spiritual virtue of a buddha and abided in non-abiding.

At that time the World-Honored One was traveling through the great city of Rājagṛha, broadly expounding the principles of the path and teaching the multitude of beings. Celestial spirits, earth spirits, sea spirits, ghosts and spirits, kings and ministers, the people, and beings of the three realms all came to pay homage and make offerings to his spiritual transformations.

When there is no buddha in the world, the heterodox paths flourish, like torches and candles that serve as light in the darkness of night. But when a buddha appears in the world, all heresies cease, like the way fire has no more light when the sun comes out. The original affair in this kingdom was that the heterodox teachers of wrong views, Pūraṇa Kāśyapa and the other six masters, were all being instructed by the Buddha through the path of true correctness.

At that time the six masters had all been abandoned and no one believed in or served them. The various heterodox practitioners harbored jealousy and plotted to undermine the Buddha so that they might regain the devotion of the people. The six masters then summoned their disciples and gathered their followers in one place to discuss the matter: "Our path and virtue are lofty and far-reaching, our fame renowned throughout the four seas. All the learned look up to us, and the king himself serves us. Men and women of the populace all believe in and admire us. Throughout the sixteen great kingdoms, the entire world offers us sustenance. Our virtue is like the wind and the people respond like grass; none fail to bow down at our feet. But now the Buddha has appeared in the world, displaying supernatural transformations, and we have been discarded by the people and are no longer believed in or served. This has made our food tasteless and our sleep restless. Going about in our daily activities, we are constantly distressed and agitated. The poison in our hearts drives us nearly mad. What scheme can we devise to disgrace him? If we can drive him from the kingdom so that people no longer see him, then our offerings will be restored." After deliberating, they said to one another: "In the great city of Rājagṛha there is a powerful and wealthy elder, a minister of state named Śreṇya, who believes in and serves us with an undivided heart and constantly disparages the Buddha's path as false. Only this man, with his power and might, can assist us in disgracing and removing him."

Then the six masters rose from their seats and, with five hundred disciples, went to the great city of Rājagṛha to the home of Śreṇya. When Śreṇya saw his masters coming, he descended from the main hall to welcome them and bowed in greeting. He set out fine couches and served them food and drink. After they had rinsed their hands, he sat facing them with hands clasped together. The masters said: "We have come deliberately with this great assembly to visit you, the elder, to discuss a certain matter. Please listen."

Śreṇya said: "I humbly receive your instruction and am glad to hear it."

The six masters said in unison: "We are the teachers of this kingdom. Our accomplishments and fame are esteemed, the learned pay us homage, the king shows us respect, the great ministers serve us, and throughout the sixteen great kingdoms we stand alone. As you know, the Buddha has appeared in the world, calling himself a sage of wisdom, but he uses magical arts and cunning deceptions to speak with inverted meanings. He seeks to rival our assembled sages in wisdom, to match us in power and spiritual might, and thus beguiles the people through illusions. The foolish people of the kingdom believe in and serve his path. He confuses the world and undermines wholesome intentions, and further causes people to say that we are without path or virtue, so that none believe in or serve us. You, elder, are powerful and mighty, esteemed by kings, trusted by the people of the kingdom. Your authority resounds to the four seas and the world looks up to you. Considering deeply the enduring bond between teacher and disciple, recording merit and listing achievements to be handed down to future ages, we ask that you disgrace and remove him on our behalf, making him disappear, and restore our offerings. Would that not be splendid!"

The elder replied: "I respectfully receive your instructions. I recall that in times past, my foolish elder brother, because he revered the Buddha, disgraced our masters. Since then I have harbored resentment and have always wanted to act but never had the occasion. Today your sacred instruction accords with my heart's desire. I shall exert all my strength and devote my life to serving my masters."

The brahmins said: "We have discussed and agreed. We wish you to dig, before your gate, a pit five zhang and six chi deep, filled with charcoal fire to the halfway mark, with thin iron bars as the rafters and a thin layer of earth covering the top. Then prepare a variety of foods and put poison in them. If the fire pit does not burn him, the poisoned food will surely kill him. Then go and invite the Buddha. The Buddha always claims to have the wisdom of the three penetrations, supernatural powers without obstruction, and the ability to perceive what has not yet happened, to see clearly the origin of calamity and fortune, the direction of good and bad fortune. In truth he possesses no such ability and merely deceives the multitude. If he truly has this wisdom, he will not accept the invitation. If he lacks this wisdom, he will certainly accept. We wish to use this scheme against the Buddha."

Śreṇya, hearing this, was delighted and exclaimed: "Excellent! Truly as the great masters have instructed, this scheme is deep, secret, lofty, and marvelous; truly extraordinary. With this plan, how can we fail?" "I shall follow the sacred instruction, prepare the fire pit and poisoned food, and make everything ready."

At that time, the five hundred disciples of Pūraṇa Kāśyapa, seeing Śreṇya's agreement and shared plotting, were ecstatic with joy and could not contain themselves. They all rose together, raised their hands in unison, and praised Śreṇya: "Excellent! You are truly the most illustrious first disciple."

Śreṇya further said: "I am already making preparations myself. By tomorrow the masters will see the result." The elder bowed, and the masters all departed.

Then Śreṇya ordered his chariot prepared and, with an escort before and behind, went to the Buddha's dwelling. He dismounted from his chariot, removed his parasol, unbuckled his sword, removed his shoes, advanced with clasped hands, prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and kneeling, addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I have hungered and thirsted for your spiritual transformations and delighted in your pure teaching. Worldly affairs are many, and I have had no occasion to serve you. I have come now to pay homage and receive your instruction."

The Buddha told the worthy one: "Tomorrow set your heart sincerely upon the Tathāgata."

Śreṇya said: "Tomorrow I wish to prepare a simple meal. May the World-Honored One and all the sacred assembly condescend to come."

At that time the World-Honored One, pitying Śreṇya's madness and folly and wishing to save him, silently accepted the invitation. Śreṇya was secretly pleased and thought to himself: "It has gone just as planned." He bowed and took his leave. Passing by the six masters, they asked: "Did he accept the invitation?" Śreṇya reported: "Through the majestic power of the masters, he has accepted my invitation. It has gone just as planned." The masters and their five hundred disciples all rejoiced beyond measure. The brahmins all exclaimed with delight: "Elder, return quickly and prepare the fire pit and poisoned food. Make everything ready."

The elder returned home and secretly ordered that outside the middle gate a fire pit be dug and poisoned food prepared, all made ready.

The elder had a son named Candraprabha, sixteen years old, of extraordinary natural talent and handsome appearance. He was widely versed in all the texts and had comprehensively mastered the patterns of the divine. Astronomy and geography: nothing escaped his insight. His fame stirred the world; the learned looked up to him as a teacher. Compassionate and merciful regarding the suffering of birth and death, he humbly reached out to all, wishing to deliver sentient beings. He admonished his father Śreṇya: "The Buddha is the Great Sage, the Most Honored of the Three Realms. His path and virtue are pure, and his words and teachings are true and correct. A sage of the three penetrations, with loving-kindness, compassion, joy, and protection, his mind surpasses that of a loving mother. You should receive his deliverance. Instead, you raise up adversity and evil while hoping for heavenly blessings. Is this not delusion?

"In the past, when the World-Honored One was seeking the path, he sat at the site of enlightenment beneath the auspicious tree. The Māra of the Sixth Heaven, seeing the Buddha's lofty attainment, that the three poisons had been extinguished, impure darkness dispelled, and the light of wisdom alone remaining, saw that his sacred and spiritual powers were supreme and that all the devas paid him homage. The Lord of Heaven himself descended, and ten thousand spirits attended and guarded him. Inwardly harboring jealousy and resentment, Māra thought: 'If he attains the path, he will surely surpass me. My food has no taste; my entertainments give me no pleasure.' He then ascended to his throne and summoned his wisest minister to devise a plan to ruin the Buddha's path. His ministers replied: 'The bodhisattva's spiritual holiness and the power of his path are supreme. His authority shakes the three realms, and his virtue subdues the ten directions. This is not something your ministers can scheme against.'

"At that time the Māra King, blazing with fury, shook heaven and earth with his terrible radiance. He summoned all his ghost soldiers, raised his army and massed his hosts: armored warriors numbering hundreds of millions, with banners and flags darkening the sun and brilliant lights covering the sky. He commanded his troops to transform themselves into bizarre and alien shapes, hundreds of thousands of kinds: with worm-heads and human bodies, dreadful ghouls and specters, carrying mountains and boulders on their backs, spewing fire from their mouths and eyes. They surrounded the bodhisattva and stamped the earth with one voice, bellowing like oxen, their shouts shaking the eight directions. Mountains crumbled, the earth split, trees snapped and fell, the waters of the sea surged and boiled across the six plains, overflowing in vast torrents with waves towering to the sky. The people were terrified, climbing trees and fleeing to the mountains. Hidden dragons leaped wildly, fierce birds flew in alarm, lions cowered and fled, and the golden-winged Garuḍa King and the great makara fish turned and tumbled in the current. All the spirits were panic-stricken, fleeing and hiding. A thousand changes and ten thousand transformations, hordes of fiends accompanying one another, venomous and vicious creatures spreading in every direction: all their evil converged upon the World-Honored One at once.

"The Buddha simply released the power of his compassionate mind and pointed at them with his hand. All the evil ghost soldiers were at once laid prostrate, and with one mind they joined their palms and prostrated, taking refuge.

"At that time, the Tathāgata immediately subdued the evil that filled the heavens and transformed them into disciples. Though he won this victory, he had no thought of joy or concern.

"By this measure, what are a fire pit and poisoned food worth speaking of? Even if the poison of Mount Sumeru, the fires of the great trichiliocosm, or swords, spears, and blades were brought to bear, they could not move a single hair of the Buddha. To try to destroy the Buddha with a fire pit and poisoned food is like a mosquito trying to topple a great mountain, or a gnat's wings trying to block the sun and moon. You will only destroy yourself. It would be better to repent early and confess your offenses.

"Devas, nāgas, ghosts and spirits, the people of the world, Brahmā, Śakra, and the Māra kings: none fail to bow their heads and receive the Buddha's transformation. The body of the Tathāgata is a vajra body of virtue. All evil has been extinguished and ten thousand kinds of goodness accumulated. His marks and radiance, his spiritual vessel, are beyond comparison. He has forever dispelled the great darkness of the five destinies and obtained the solitary light of unsurpassed, true correctness. Seated above all the sages, he is like the moon among the stars, like the sun first rising and illuminating the morning. He broadly sets forth the teachings of the path to guide the world's violent and foolish, bearing loving-kindness and the four immeasurables, compassionate toward sentient beings, bestowing grace and relieving suffering with a mind surpassing that of a loving mother. Such are the path, virtue, and spiritual transformations of the Buddha. I beg my father to change his mind, repent, and take refuge in the Three Honored Ones."

At that time the elder Śreṇya, blinded by the covering of his transgressions, could not open his mind to understanding. He told his son Candraprabha: "Even granting that the Tathāgata's virtue is as you praise it, and that the Buddha possesses supernatural powers to perceive what has not yet happened, he should have known in advance that I was preparing a fire pit and poisoned food. Why then did he accept my invitation? By this reasoning, I know he lacks such foresight."

Candraprabha said again: "The Buddha truly possesses the wisdom of the three penetrations, the illumination of the six supernatural powers, the great loving-kindness and the four immeasurables, comprehending all things without exception. He always delivers the deluded and darkened and never refuses a person's request."

Śreṇya's mind was deluded and he stubbornly refused to let go of his folly. The next morning he sent a messenger to invite the Buddha. The messenger, having received his instructions, went to the World-Honored One's dwelling, knelt, and said to the Buddha: "The elder Śreṇya sends word: 'The hour has come and the meal is prepared. May the World-Honored One and the entire assembly condescend to come.'"

At that time the World-Honored One thought to himself: "Today, accepting Śreṇya's invitation is not the same as usual. I should release my majestic spiritual power and move the ten directions, so that his son's mind will be subdued and he will certainly attain the great path. The transforming influence will be vast and boundless, and I will also transform the six masters and the ninety-six kinds of heterodox paths."

Then the World-Honored One radiated a great light, illuminating upward to the Thirty-Three Heavens and penetrating downward through the eighteen hells. Throughout the entire scope of the buddha-realm, there was a great radiance of supernatural power. Bodhisattvas, arhats, pratyekabuddhas, Brahmā, Śakra, the devarājas, sea spirits, earth spirits, and ghost soldiers, each with their retinues numbering hundreds of millions beyond count, hearing that the Buddha would deliver Śreṇya, all came to the Buddha, prostrated at his feet, and flanked him on left and right. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Take up your begging bowls. We shall go to the invitation of the elder Śreṇya."

The bhikṣus all said: "We receive the instruction." Solemnly and reverently they took up their bowls, and the ground turned to the color of gold. Above the Buddha's crown, a fragrant cloud of seven steps arose, with lotus flowers of crimson, yellow, blue, indigo, purple, and white spontaneously emerging along both sides of the path.

As the Buddha stepped out the gate, he told the Bodhisattva Akāśa-Maṅgala and six hundred thousand others to lead the procession. Some traveled through the sky; some appeared upon the ground. The Buddha proceeded in the center. The rest of the limitless multitude all followed behind the Buddha. His radiance blazed and shone, eclipsing the sun and surpassing the moon, illuminating everything. Devas spread jeweled canopies as flowers fell like snow. Devas, nāgas, and birds dared not fly above. No being of the three realms could see the crown of his head. Celestial music descended, all playing at once. Venomous creatures hid away; auspicious birds flew and sang. The Buddha did not touch the ground, yet the imprints of the wheel-marks appeared. His radiance blazed as bright as seven suns. Jeweled trees, medicinal trees, and all manner of fruit trees leaned, stretched, bowed, and rose like people kneeling in reverence. Yakṣas, garuḍas, malevolent ghosts, and spectral ghosts, each with their companies, arrayed in splendor and order, in groups of hundreds and thousands, held musical instruments, or bore incense, flowers, garlands of seven jewels, celestial silk, banners, and canopies. Assemblies from the ten directions arrived; devas also descended and their instruments also played. The blind could see, the deaf could hear, the mute could speak, the lame could walk. Enemies and foes became kind and compassionate. The confused awoke; the intoxicated sobered. Women's bracelets and ornaments struck against one another, making sounds. Birds and beasts all took human form. Withered trees a hundred years old spontaneously blossomed. Hungry ghosts were sated and the hells were at peace. Lutes, zithers, flutes, and drums all sounded together.

By the Buddha's spiritual virtue, the fire pit was transformed and became a bathing pool of seven jewels and lapis lazuli, filled with water of eight flavors. Its bottom was golden sand, and lotus flowers lined its edges. Fish, turtles, and tortoises leaped joyfully in the pool. Birds and beasts harmonized their voices beside the pool. Along the banks stood rows of trees bearing silk garments. Between the trees hung gauze, brocade, and embroidered cloth. Trees of coral and jewels, of crystal and lapis lazuli, of gold and silver, bore them as branches and leaves. Ropes of burnished gold connected and interlaced among the trees, with celestial garlands joined to one another. When the wind arose and stirred them, they touched and struck one another, producing a hundred kinds of wondrous sounds that naturally proclaimed the praise of the Buddha's spiritual virtue as something never before experienced. All proclaimed the sounds of impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self, causing those who grieved to rejoice and those who rejoiced to be moved to tears. Upon the jeweled trees were flocks of birds: peacocks, parrots, the golden-winged Garuḍa King, red-beaked divine birds, phoenixes, auspicious birds, kiṃnara auspicious birds, numbering hundreds of thousands of kinds. Their brilliant colors shone as they flew in flocks among the trees. Their mournful cries of a hundred kinds moved people's hearts, and all came to listen. Running beasts stopped eating; flying birds paused to hear. Different in form but one in mind, they took refuge in the World-Honored One.

At that time, the sea spirits, earth spirits, and beings of the three realms who had come as attendants saw the fire pit transformed into a bathing pool and heaven and earth shaking, and marveled at such extraordinary spiritual transformations and surpassing virtue. They all looked at Śreṇya. When Śreṇya saw this, his heart was shaken and his spirit startled. His hair stood on end; he was panic-stricken and terrified, his spirit losing its composure. He thought to himself: "Alas! How deluded! My wickedness knows no bounds." He turned to the six masters and said: "Because of you beasts, I am plunged into boiling water and fire. What am I to do now?" The six masters were frightened and ashamed, unable to speak. Fearing they would receive heavy punishment, they each fled and scattered.

Then Śreṇya, together with his son Candraprabha, his wife, attendant women, relatives both male and female, and his heterodox companions, all came out to welcome the Buddha. From afar they beheld the World-Honored One, his vast flames blazing and radiating like a jeweled mountain. His celestial form of purple gold, his great body of one zhang and six chi, his divine countenance serene, his faculties tranquil: with the thirty-two major marks and eighty minor excellences, the deva among devas, his path and virtue magnificent, the World-Honored One of the Ten Powers, the King among Devas and Humans. The Buddha's spiritual virtue illuminated Śreṇya's darkened mind. Śreṇya's understanding opened, like one who was lost finding the right way, like the cure of a madman's illness. His five emotions stirred from within; grief and joy intermingled. He came forward at once to confess his offenses, prostrating with his entire body upon the ground, bowing in homage: "World-Honored One, forgive my blindness and darkness. I did not recognize true correctness. Trusting in evil words, I harbored poisonous intent to harm the Honored One of Heaven. Fortunately, through your compassionate transformation, I beg pardon for my offenses. Please have mercy, take me in, and save me from this great transgression. I beg you to come forward and take your seat."

At once the Tathāgata ascended to the main hall, and when the assembly was seated, Śreṇya, ashamed and fearful, his mind unable to rest, came forward and said to the Buddha: "Misled by blindness and darkness, I trusted the words of demonic heretics, taking them for truth. Like a madman who takes medicine only to worsen his illness, it is only today, beholding the Buddha, that the disease of heresy has been cured. The food I wickedly prepared was all laced with poison and is unfit to serve the Great Sage. Please wait a moment while I prepare fresh food."

The Buddha told the elder: "Simply bring the food. There is no need to prepare more. Greed, aversion, delusion, and wrong views are the grave poisons of the world. I am without these poisons. My poisons have been fully extinguished. Poison does not harm me."

Śreṇya served the food, and its fragrance reached the eight difficult conditions, bringing peace to the hungry ghosts. After eating, water was brought for washing, and the assembly sat in silence. Śreṇya withdrew to his seat and, weeping, said: "World-Honored One, forgive my delusion. I dared to scheme wickedness against the Tathāgata. I judge my own transgressions to be grave enough for the hells, where I would suffer in boiling water and fire through the long night of pain. How will I ever escape? The Buddha is the Great Compassionate One, the savior of the three realms. Taking pity on sentient beings, you deliver them from burning coals. Why did you not warn me yesterday and prevent my transgression from being consummated? Could it be that the World-Honored One did not foresee it?"

The Buddha told Śreṇya: "Long ago, when the World-Honored One Dīpaṃkara gave me a prediction, he said: 'After innumerable asaṃkhyeya kalpas, in a world of the five turbidities and evil, you will become a buddha and broadly deliver sentient beings as I do today. There will be an elder named Śreṇya who will use a fire pit and poisoned food in a scheme to harm you.' At that time I attained the Bright Wisdom Samādhi and already foreknew your name. How much more so yesterday!"

Śreṇya joyfully said: "The World-Honored One foreknew events of innumerable kalpas. Since I have already awakened, I will surely be pardoned of my offenses. I now devote my whole heart to taking refuge in the Buddha. For the wrongs I have committed, I beg that my grave transgressions may be reduced to light."

The Buddha has always vowed never to abandon the deluded. He then told Śreṇya: "Excellent, elder! You are able to awaken on your own and repent. Your grave transgressions will surely be removed. I shall now expound the principles of the path for you, so that your mind may understand."

Śreṇya replied: "Indeed, Deva among Devas! I am glad to hear it. I shall devote my life to the wholesome path and never forget it with all my heart."

Then the World-Honored One cleared his throat and raised his great voice, producing the eight kinds of sounds and ten million tones. He broadly taught the immeasurable Dharma-words: the eight liberations, the four truths, the three emancipations, the six pāramitās, the essential and subtle practices of probing the path. He explained that the three realms are empty, that all dharmas arise through causes and conditions whose creation produces transgression and merit. Observing the illness and selecting the medicine, he taught the Dharma as it was needed.

Śreṇya's feelings awakened to a broad, open understanding. His mind opened, his doubts were resolved, his bonds were removed. Suddenly, without thought, he entered tranquil concentration. Right there upon his seat, he attained non-retrogression. In joyful exultation he rose into the sky to a height of one hundred and forty zhang. From the sky he came down and prostrated upon the ground. He kissed the Buddha's feet and caressed the Buddha's feet, kneeling to declare: "I have now awakened and been delivered by the Buddha." Exultantly he praised the Buddha: "How powerful is the Buddha's ten powers! The Tathāgata's spiritual might subdues the ten directions; none dare stand against it. The fire pit and poisoned food were destroyed in advance. How serene is the Buddha! All swords and blades cannot cause him harm; sharp arrows and keen edges are all transformed into flowers. How courageous is the Buddha! The Tathāgata can subdue the ninety-six kinds of heterodox paths, and with different mouths they all praise the great path with one voice. How vajra-like is the Buddha! The Buddha is like a golden mountain, radiantly illuminating everything. Darkness and blindness are shattered; the fire of wisdom alone shines. Transcending the assembly of all sages, his virtue surpasses space. His eloquence is inexhaustible; he proclaims the Dharma to the great trichiliocosm. He tames the stubborn and brings them to ripeness. His authority shakes the three realms; he stands alone without a companion. Free from all defilement, pure as celestial gold, he rains down the treasures of Dharma, satisfying all. He gives the inexhaustible seven great treasures. Observing the mind and selecting the medicine, he first vanquished the armies of Māra. Teaching the Dharma as needed, none fail to be delivered. The ninety-six kinds of heterodox paths are hidden and eclipsed."

At the time Śreṇya praised the Buddha-Dharma, the limitless multitude that had come for the great assembly all delighted in the sound of the Dharma. Those who gained merit and those who gained deliverance were beyond reckoning. Heaven and earth shook, and instruments of music sounded. The Seventh Brahmā King proclaimed the words of the Dharma. Lutes, chimes, and drums, and a thousand kinds of instruments, all sounded of themselves. Śakra, Lord of the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, scattered flowers upon the Buddha and praised the Buddha's sacred virtue as something never before experienced.

At that time, all rejoiced, prostrated in homage, and departed.
