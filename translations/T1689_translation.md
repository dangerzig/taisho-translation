# 請賓頭盧法
## Method for Inviting Piṇḍola

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1689

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Huijian (慧簡).

---

In the land of India, there are upāsakas, kings, and elders who, whenever they hold any assembly, always invite the Arhat Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja. "Piṇḍola" is his given name, and "Bhāradvāja" is his clan name. Because he displayed his supernatural powers before the elder Jyotiṣka, the Buddha banished him and did not permit him to enter nirvāṇa, commanding him to serve as a field of merit for the fourfold assembly in the age of the Dharma's decline.

When inviting him, one should burn incense and bow in a quiet place, facing toward Mount Marīci in India. With utmost sincerity, call his name, saying: "Venerable Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja, you received the Buddha's command to serve as a field of merit for people in the age of the Dharma's decline. May you accept my invitation and partake of food at this place." If one has newly built a dwelling, one should also invite him, saying: "May you accept my invitation and rest upon the bed and bedding in this dwelling." When generally inviting the saṅgha for bathing, one should also invite him, saying: "May you accept my invitation and bathe here." Before dawn, one should prepare fragrant water, clean water, washing beans, and willow twigs, and blend fragrant oil to a moderate temperature, just as for a person's bathing. Open the door and invite him in, then close the door. After enough time for a person to finish bathing, the saṅgha may then enter. In general, for assembly meals and bathing, it is essential to invite all monks, and one must have a mind seeking liberation, without doubt or confusion, with pure faith. Only then may one properly invite him.

In recent times, there was an elder who heard that the Arhat Piṇḍola had received the Buddha's command to serve as a field of merit for people in the age of the Dharma's decline. Following the proper method, he established a great assembly and sincerely invited Piṇḍola. Beneath the woolen rugs, he spread flowers everywhere, wishing to verify Piṇḍola's presence by this means. After the assembly had finished eating, he lifted the rugs and found the flowers had all wilted. He was distressed and blamed himself, not knowing whence the fault had come. He again exhausted his efforts questioning sūtra masters and held another great assembly. As before, the flowers again all wilted. He further poured out all his family's wealth and held yet another great assembly, but it was still the same as before. Distressed, he blamed himself. He then invited over a hundred Dharma masters to seek what had gone wrong and to repent his faults. Among them, in the upper seat, there was one elderly person. The elder bowed in all four directions, repenting his transgressions. The senior monk told him:

"You invited me to all three assemblies, and I accepted every invitation. But you yourself sent a slave to bar the gate. Because I am old and my robes are worn and tattered, he took me for a banished, disreputable śramaṇa and refused to let me through. Because of your invitation, I tried to force my way in, but your slave struck my head with a staff, breaking it open, leaving a wound on the right side of my forehead. At the second assembly, I also came, and again I was not permitted through. I again tried to force my way in and was struck again, leaving a wound in the center of my forehead. At the third assembly, I also came and, as before, was struck, leaving a wound on the left side of my forehead. All of this was your own doing. What is there to be distressed about?"

Having spoken, he vanished. The elder then knew it was Piṇḍola. From that time on, whenever people established merit feasts, they dared not obstruct the gate again. If one succeeds in obtaining Piṇḍola's attendance, the flowers at his seat will not wilt.

When one has newly established a dwelling with beds and couches and wishes to invite Piṇḍola, one should sprinkle the ground with fragrant water, light incense and oil lamps, and spread new bedding and new quilts with silk floss padding, covering the padding with white silk. In the first watch of the night, invite him according to the proper method, then close the door of the room. Be careful not to disrespectfully peek inside. All should wholeheartedly believe that he will certainly come. With utmost sincerity and devotion, there is none who will not arrive. When he has come, the bedding will show the impression of where someone has lain. The bathing room will also show evidence of hot water having been used. When accepting the invitation to a great assembly, he may sit in the upper seat, the middle seat, or the lower seat, appearing in the form of an ordinary monk wherever he sits. People who seek anything unusual about him can never find it. After he has departed, one sees that the flowers at his seat have not wilted, and thereby knows it was him.
