# 護國經
## The Huguo Sūtra (Raṭṭhapāla)

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0069

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmabhadra (法賢)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the World-Honored One was in the land of Kuru. Traveling about to teach and benefit beings, he gradually arrived at the village of Thūla, where he stayed with a great assembly of bhikṣus. At that time, the brahmins and great householders of that village discussed among themselves: "The great śramaṇa Gautama has renounced the throne and left home to pursue the Way. His attainment is complete, his brilliance perfect, and his renown is heard everywhere. He is the Worthy of Offerings, the Perfectly Enlightened One, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone One, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Trainer of Those to Be Tamed, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One. Among the realms of gods, Māra, Brahmā, śramaṇas, brahmins, humans, and nonhumans, through his own practices and vows he has attained perfect enlightenment, and with a great compassionate heart he proclaims the true Dharma, which is good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, profound in meaning, pure and unmixed, perfectly complete in the marks of the holy life. Being thus supremely honored and most exalted, if we were to see him, we would together obtain great benefit. Therefore, we should go to the Buddha to pay reverence and offer praise." Having discussed this together, they all went to the Buddha's assembly. When they arrived before the Buddha, some bowed to the Buddha, some merely joined their palms, and some offered various praises. After paying their respects in these ways, each sat to one side. At that time, the World-Honored One taught the Dharma for the assembly, causing their hearts to rejoice and inspiring them to the great aspiration for the Way. When the brahmins and great householders had heard and received the Dharma, they were all delighted and gave rise to the great mind of the Way. Rising from their seats, they joined their palms toward the Buddha, offered various praises, bowed to the Buddha, and departed.

At that time, in the assembly there was a great householder named Huguo (Raṭṭhapāla). Out of deep attachment to the Buddha, he did not leave the Dharma assembly, and he reflected: "The Dharma I have heard is reliable and dependable, and will surely lead to perfect enlightenment. If I remain at home, I will forever be trapped in the cycle of rebirth. A Buddha is rarely encountered. Going forth in faith to seek liberation is the way. Therefore, I shall now abandon heedlessness and arouse great diligence, go forth under the Buddha, and purely cultivate the holy life. I shall resolve to shave off my hair and beard and don the Dharma robes." After Huguo the householder had reflected thus, he rose from his seat, went before the Buddha, prostrated touching his head to the Buddha's feet, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and addressed the Buddha: "Having heard the true Dharma from the World-Honored One, I am weary of the suffering of cyclic existence and have given rise to a mind of faith and joy. Therefore, I now seek to go forth under the Buddha. I humbly pray that the World-Honored One will accept me."

The Buddha said to Huguo: "You wish to go forth in faith. Have your parents given permission?"

Huguo replied: "No, World-Honored One. My parents have not given permission."

The Buddha said to Huguo: "Without your parents' permission, you cannot go forth."

Huguo again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, although my parents have not yet given permission, I shall earnestly request until they firmly consent."

The Buddha told Huguo: "As you wish. Now is the right time."

At that time, Huguo the householder, having received the Buddha's instruction, bowed to the Buddha and departed. He returned to his home and addressed his parents: "Dear father and mother, please hear my words with compassion. At the Buddha's assembly I heard the Buddha teach the Dharma, and I fully understand all that I heard. I have given rise to true faith and wish to go forth. I humbly ask that my parents grant me permission."

At that time, the parents told Huguo: "You wish to go forth. What benefit do you seek? What gain is there in going forth? If you go forth, will it not be merely to beg for your livelihood? You should know that we now possess immeasurable wealth and precious treasures. You need only stay at home, give wealth in charity, and enjoy prosperity. Why must you go forth?" Thus his parents gently tried to persuade him. Then Huguo again addressed them: "Dear father and mother, please consider: I am weary of cyclic existence and wish to abandon worldly glory to seek the renunciant life. I humbly ask that you grant permission." Thus Huguo requested again and again. Then the parents again told him: "You are so resolute in seeking to go forth, but is it not merely to beg for your livelihood? Our family treasury holds immeasurable quantities of gold, silver, and precious treasures. You need only stay at home, give wealth in charity, and enjoy prosperity. Why must you go forth?" Thus his parents tried to persuade him two and three times. Then Huguo again declared: "If my parents will not grant permission, from this day forward I vow not to eat or drink, even until the end of my life." Having made this vow, he immediately ceased eating and drinking.

At that time, the many friends and acquaintances of Huguo, hearing of this matter, together went to the home of Huguo the householder's parents and all addressed them: "Head of the household, we have all heard that your son Huguo loves the renunciant life. Though he earnestly seeks it, you will not grant permission. We know your son: though he enjoys prosperity, his resolve for the Way is firm. If you do not now grant permission, he will instead suffer grief and distress and approach death, which will cause you the suffering of separation from a loved one. Therefore, you should grant permission and allow him to go forth." When the head of the household saw that his son's friends were earnestly requesting on his behalf, he then permitted the going forth. At that time, Huguo's many friends received his father's consent and went to Huguo, telling him: "Huguo, you should know that your parents have now permitted your going forth." Huguo, receiving his parents' consent, was overjoyed. He went to his parents, bowed in farewell, and departed. He returned to the Buddha's presence. Having arrived before the Buddha, he prostrated touching his head to the Buddha's feet, joined his palms to one side, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, my parents have now permitted my going forth. I pray that the Buddha will compassionately accept me and ordain me as a bhikṣu."

At that time, the World-Honored One told Huguo: "Excellent, excellent! Now is the right time. I accept you." Thereupon Huguo's hair and beard fell away of their own accord, and he was clad in the kāṣāya robe, assuming the appearance of a bhikṣu. He cultivated and maintained the holy life, abandoned heedlessness, was free from all grief and affliction. His mind became gentle and subdued, and he realized the purity of the Dharma: "Birth is ended, the holy life has been established, what was to be done has been done, there will be no further existence."

At that time, the Venerable Huguo, having attained the exhaustion of the outflows, relied on the Buddha for ten rains retreats. After completing ten rains retreats, he donned his robes, took up his bowl, went before the Buddha, prostrated touching his head to the Buddha's feet, joined his palms to one side, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I was originally born and raised in the village of Thūla. Having abandoned my relatives and gone forth in faith, I now think of returning to my home village to be near my family. I respectfully await the Buddha's instruction."

At that time, the World-Honored One knew the Venerable Huguo's thoughts and wishes, and observing that Huguo's resolve was firm and he wished to broadly benefit beings, and that moreover when he was formerly a householder he had always been free from desires, the Buddha told him: "Venerable Huguo, now is the right time. You may follow your wish."

At that time, the Venerable Huguo, having received the Buddha's instruction, was overjoyed. He circumambulated the Buddha three times, prostrated touching his head to the Buddha's feet, took leave of the Buddha, and departed. He returned to his birthplace, traveling by stages until he reached his home village. After spending the night there, in the morning he donned his robes, took up his bowl, and went begging for food in order, arriving at his own former home. He saw a woman outside the gate holding a full vessel of food, about to throw it away. The Venerable One, seeing this, told the woman: "Have you no sense of shame? Do not throw away food. Would it not be better to give it to me, placing it in my bowl? You would thereby obtain merit." At that time, the woman heard the Venerable One's words and felt deep reverence, immediately offering the food and placing it in his bowl. The Venerable One, having received it, went to a great tree and spread his seat, preparing to eat. Then the woman thought: "This is my master's son, the Venerable Huguo." Having thought this, she went to the head of the household and told him: "Just now, outside the gate, I saw the young master holding a bowl and begging for food. He has gone to a great tree, where he has spread his seat and is about to eat." When the head of the household heard this, his heart was greatly overjoyed, and he asked the woman: "Is what you say true?" The woman replied: "It is indeed true."

At that time, the head of the household quickly left his home and went to the great tree. Arriving there, he indeed saw Huguo about to eat, and told him: "My son Huguo! You left our home and wandered abroad for so long, and now you have come to the village but do not enter our home. What is the meaning of this?" Huguo then replied to his father: "In the way of a śramaṇa, this is the proper deportment. To enter another's home is not fitting for me." The head of the household then supported Huguo and brought him back to the family home. After arriving, he spread a seat for him and had him sit down. Then Huguo's mother came before her son, and with a heart full of love repeatedly inquired after him, saying to Huguo: "My son, why were you so resolute in going forth? What benefit has your going forth brought you? What have you gained? Is it not merely begging for your livelihood? Therefore, you should now stay home, give wealth in charity, and enjoy prosperity."

At that time, his compassionate mother devised various stratagems, clinging to her son and unwilling to be separated. She further piled up gold, silver, and various precious treasures before the Venerable One, telling her son: "My child, you should know that I possess these vast and abundant treasures, and I now give them to you. How much more your father's wealth, which is likewise immeasurable! For this reason, why go forth? You should stay at home, give wealth in charity, and enjoy all manner of prosperity." At that time, the Venerable One addressed his mother: "Dear mother, please consider: these great quantities of gold, silver, and various treasures are the root of all misfortunes." Having said this, he had them loaded onto carts and carried away to be cast into the river. He again addressed his mother: "Dear mother, please consider: such treasures, because of the craving and attachment they engender, give rise from this cause to various faults and bring about calamities, namely the disasters of flood, fire, kings, thieves, and unworthy heirs. Such various sufferings of destruction arise, and understanding this cause of suffering, I do not follow my mother's wishes."

At that time, Huguo's mother, clinging to her son and unwilling to let go, devised another stratagem, thinking: "The wives my son had when he was a householder, I shall have them adorn themselves with jewels and ornaments and go to Huguo's presence to delight his heart." Having thought this, she went to his wife and told her: "When your husband Huguo was formerly at home, he had beloved precious jewels and ornamental finery. Adorn yourself with these now and go to Huguo's presence to please him." At that time, Huguo's wife, having received this command, immediately adorned herself with the various precious ornaments and went to Huguo's presence. Arriving there, she said: "Son of the householder, what is your intention? Do you maintain the holy life in order to obtain heavenly maidens?" Huguo replied: "No, elder sister. I maintain the holy life in order to attain the fruit of the Way. What you suggest is not the case." When Huguo's wife heard herself called "elder sister," her countenance changed with embarrassment, and she withdrew in shame.

At that time, the Venerable Huguo reflected: "The time for the meal is approaching." He then addressed his father: "Householder, do you have any food and drink to offer me?" At that time, his parents personally brought excellent food and drink of various kinds to offer the Venerable One. When the Venerable One had finished his meal, he washed his bowl, put away his robes, spread his seat, and sat down. He then proclaimed the true Dharma for his parents, causing them to give rise to joyful hearts and to generate the aspiration for the Way. Then, through his spiritual powers, he rose into the air and spoke these verses:

> "Behold this painted image, adorned with many jewels:
> The foolish are deluded and cling to it,
> But the wise always keep their distance.
>
> Desire is like a rope that binds the world.
> Fools are beguiled by it,
> But the wise always keep their distance."

At that time, the Venerable One, having spoken these verses, descended from the sky and returned to rest beneath the great tree.

At that time, there was a king named Kuru, who had gone out for an excursion and was approaching the outskirts of the village of Thūla. An attendant reported: "Great King, you should know that in this village there is a householder's son named Huguo, whose family is extensive and whose treasures are immeasurable, who has abandoned all this and gone forth as a renunciant." When the king heard this, he asked the people of the village: "Is it true that here in your village there is a householder's son named Huguo who has abandoned his family and gone forth?" The village people then reported to the king: "Great King, you should know that it is indeed true. The Venerable Huguo is in our village beneath a great tree, constantly maintaining the holy life, and we, the people, attend and make offerings to him." Having heard this report, the king went to that place in the village and proceeded to the great tree where the Venerable One was staying. The Venerable One, seeing the king of Kuru approaching from afar and coming toward him, rose from his seat, stepped forward before the king, and said: "Great King, welcome! This territory is under your rule. I now invite the Great King to come beneath that tree and take my seat." The king replied: "Huguo, that is indeed my wish, to go to your place and take your seat." Huguo said again: "Since that is the king's wish, therefore I invite you." At that time, the king together with the Venerable One went to the great tree and took their seats.

At that time, the great king told Huguo: "There are four conditions because of which people seek to go forth. What are these four? First, knowing the loss of relatives. Second, knowing the loss of wealth. Third, knowing illness. Fourth, knowing old age. These are the four conditions.

"What is knowing the loss of relatives? Suppose there is a person whose family is extensive, and they are suddenly scattered and lost. This person thinks: 'My relatives, friends, and acquaintances have all been scattered and lost. I am alone and solitary. I should go forth.' This person, understanding the absence of relatives, seeks to go forth. But you, Huguo, have an extensive family and are not solitary. Without understanding this, why have you gone forth?

"Furthermore, what is knowing the loss of wealth? Suppose there is a person who formerly had great treasures and was very wealthy, but later was exhausted and poverty followed. Because of poverty, this person thinks: 'I am now poor and destitute. I should go forth.' This person, understanding poverty, seeks to go forth. But you, Huguo, possess immeasurable wealth and are not poor. Without understanding this, why have you gone forth?

"Furthermore, what is knowing the suffering of illness? Suppose there is a person who has long been bedridden with illness and cannot be cured, and thinks: 'This illness of mine brings deep and painful suffering. Therefore I should now seek to go forth.' This person, understanding the suffering of illness, seeks to go forth. But you, Huguo, have few illnesses and few troubles, and are without sorrow. Without understanding this, why have you gone forth?

"Furthermore, what is knowing decrepitude? Suppose there is a person who is aged and declining, and thinks: 'I am now old and decrepit. I can no longer benefit from any pleasures. Therefore I should now seek to go forth.' This person, understanding that old age makes one unfit, seeks to go forth. But you, Huguo, are in the vigor of youth and have not yet enjoyed pleasures. Without understanding this, why have you gone forth?

"Huguo, you should understand: it is because of these four conditions that one may go forth. I now ask you again, Huguo: what have you seen or heard that caused you to go forth?"

At that time, the Venerable One replied to the king: "Great King, you should know that there are four truths because of which I sought to go forth. What are these four? They are aging, illness, craving, and death. Deterioration that does not last long is called aging. Affliction with no cure is called illness. Never being satisfied is called craving. Abandoning all realms of experience is called death. These four truths, my Buddha the World-Honored One has thoroughly understood and perceived, and I too have personally seen and heard them from the Buddha. Because of this, I gave rise to great faith and went forth."

The king said: "Venerable One, as you have spoken briefly, I do not yet fully understand. I pray that the Venerable One will explain at length so that I may comprehend."

At that time, the Venerable One, hearing the king's words, told the king: "Great King, very well! As you wish, I shall explain."

The king said: "Venerable One, you said that deterioration that does not last long is aging. What does this mean?"

The Venerable One replied: "Great King, what do you think? When a person goes from the age of twenty to thirty and reaches forty, what are their appearance, bodily strength, and deportment like?"

The king said: "Venerable One, when a person goes from twenty to forty, they possess a fine appearance, their bodily strength is robust, and their deportment is vigorous and healthy. In all their undertakings they consider themselves without equal. But when they reach old age, they become decrepit and feeble, their appearance deteriorates, their bodily strength weakens, and their deportment declines."

The Venerable One said: "As the Great King has described, these are the marks of aging that deteriorate the world. Great King, you should know that this is the first truth proclaimed by my Buddha. Moreover, the Buddha the World-Honored One thoroughly understands, knows, and teaches this truth. I too have seen and heard it, and because of this true Dharma, with love and aspiration I went forth in faith."

The king said: "Venerable Huguo, I too rejoice in encountering this true Dharma, and I love and aspire to it."

The king further said: "Venerable Huguo, how is affliction with no cure called the mark of illness?"

The Venerable One replied: "Suppose there is a person with great treasures and numerous relatives, beyond count. What do you think? If this person falls ill and suffers affliction, can their relatives and attendants take their suffering upon themselves?"

The king said: "No, Huguo. If a person falls ill, they suffer alone. No one can take their place, nor is there any cure."

The Venerable One said: "As the Great King has described, having no substitute and no cure is the mark of illness. This is the second truth proclaimed by my Buddha. Moreover, the Buddha the World-Honored One thoroughly understands, knows, and teaches this truth. I too have seen and heard it, and because of this true Dharma, with love and aspiration I went forth in faith."

The king said: "Venerable Huguo, I too rejoice in encountering this true Dharma, and I love and aspire to it."

The king further asked: "Venerable One, how is never being satisfied called craving?"

The Venerable One replied: "Great King, what do you think? You are a wealthy ruler. Your territories and cities, extending to the east, south, west, and north, with all the people therein under your rule, are all greatly prosperous." The king said: "Venerable One, it is so, it is so!"

The Venerable One further said: "Great King, your territories, cities, and settlements are thus greatly prosperous. But suppose someone came from across the sea and addressed the Great King: 'I have seen a certain country with vast cities, a thriving population, immeasurable gold, silver, precious treasures, rare and exotic goods, elephants, horses, and soldiers.' Great King, upon hearing this, what would you think?"

The king said: "Venerable One, upon hearing this, if I did not go myself, I would send an army to conquer that country and bring back its treasures and goods to add to my storehouses."

The Venerable One said: "Great King, this insatiability is what is called craving. This is the third truth proclaimed by the Buddha. Moreover, the Buddha the World-Honored One thoroughly understands, knows, and teaches this truth. I too have seen and heard it, and because of this true Dharma, with love and aspiration I went forth in faith."

The king said: "Venerable Huguo, I too rejoice in encountering this true Dharma, and I love and aspire to it."

The king further asked: "Venerable One, how is abandoning all realms of experience called death?"

The Venerable One replied: "Great King, what do you think? I have seen those who possess great treasures and are very wealthy. When such a person departs from this world and is reborn in another, can they take their treasures with them?"

The king said: "No."

The Venerable One further said: "Great King, in this world, to abandon all beloved realms and be reborn in another world is called death. This is the fourth truth proclaimed by the Buddha. The Buddha thoroughly understands and teaches this truth. I too have seen and heard it, and because of this true Dharma, with love and aspiration I went forth in faith."

The king said: "Venerable One, I too rejoice in encountering this true Dharma, and I love and aspire to it."

At that time, the Venerable One further told the king: "I wish to elaborate on this meaning. You should listen well." The king said: "Very well. I am glad and wish to hear."

At that time, the Venerable One spoke these verses:

> "I see the people of this world, craving and accumulating.
> Because of wealth they encounter calamity, yet their desires only increase.
>
> A king rules over territories and cities, extending as far as the ocean's edge,
> And still he is not satisfied, but goes on to conquer other lands.
>
> All beings in the world are destroyed because of craving and attachment.
> Grief and sorrow give rise to weeping. Alas, how swiftly they perish!
>
> Like a person who amasses treasures only to be consumed by fire,
> So too do beings follow their karma, reaping what they have sown.
>
> Wealth does not prolong life, nor does it prevent old age.
> Rich and poor alike perish; young and old are all impermanent.
>
> Illness does not choose the strong, nor does it choose the noble or the young.
> All because of craving and attachment, none are exempt from impermanence.
>
> Just as a thief who steals brings harm upon himself,
> So it is in this world: beings create their own causes of suffering.
>
> Just as ripe fruit falls of its own accord, so too do the old and young deteriorate.
> With joyful intention they create karma, but in suffering they receive its retribution.
>
> The foolish people of this age create karma without awareness.
> Whether reborn in another world, through aversion and attachment they obtain suffering.
>
> If life ends in the womb, what person can provide protection?
> Even if one has relatives and friends, when life is ended, how can they save you?
>
> Desire can destroy and bind, producing suffering and increasing terror.
> Seeing this world as illusion, therefore I went forth."

At that time, the great king of Kuru, having heard the Venerable One speak these verses, joyfully accepted them with faith and further said: "The Venerable Huguo has excellently accomplished the going forth. Therefore, I now take refuge in the Venerable One."

Huguo said: "Great King, do not take refuge in me. The one in whom I take refuge is the Buddha the World-Honored One, along with the Dharma and the Saṅgha. The Great King should take refuge in them."

The king said: "Just so, just so! I now take refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha, and for the rest of my life I shall uphold the precepts of an upāsaka."

At that time, the great king, having made this vow, reverently paid his respects to the Venerable One and returned to his royal palace.
