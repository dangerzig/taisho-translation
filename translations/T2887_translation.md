# Sūtra on the Weightiness of Parents' Kindness

## *Fùmǔ Ēnzhòng Jīng* (父母恩重經)

Taishō 2887

Translator unknown. An apocryphal text, widely popular in East Asian Buddhism.

Thus have I heard. At one time the Buddha was in the city of Rājagṛha (*Wángshè*, 王舍), on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa (*Qíshéjué Shān*, 耆闍崛山), together with great bodhisattva-mahāsattvas and the assembly of śrāvaka disciples, as well as bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, all the devas, humans, and devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits. They all gathered in assembly, singlemindedly listening to the Buddha teach the dharma, gazing up at his honored countenance, their eyes not leaving him for an instant.

The Buddha said: "For a person born in this world, parents are one's closest kin. Without a father, one is not born. Without a mother, one is not raised. It is thus that one is entrusted to the mother's womb, carried in the body for ten months. When the months are full and the time complete, mother and child are both in danger. The child falls upon the grass. The parents rear and nurture it. When lying down, it is in the cradle-cart. The parents hold it in their embrace. With gentle sounds of cooing, it smiles before it can speak. When hungry, it needs food: without the mother, it is not fed. When thirsty, it needs drink: without the mother, it is not nursed. When the mother is hungry, she swallows the bitter and gives the sweet. She pushes the child to the dry place and moves herself to the wet. Without a sense of duty, one would not be close. Without a mother, one would not be raised.

"The compassionate mother raises the child, and from the time it leaves the cradle, she eats the child's impurities from under her ten fingernails. In total this amounts to eight *hú* and four *dǒu*. When one reckons the mother's kindness, it is vast as the boundless sky."

Alas, the compassionate mother! How can we repay her?

Ānanda (*Ānán*, 阿難) said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how can we repay this kindness? We pray that you will explain."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Listen carefully and reflect well upon it. I shall explain for you in detail. The kindness of father and mother is vast as the boundless sky. How so? If there are sons and daughters of filial piety and compassion who are able to create merit for their parents, produce sūtras, or on the fifteenth day of the seventh month prepare the ullambana (*yúlánpén*, 盂蘭盆) vessel as an offering to the Buddha and the saṃgha, the merit obtained is immeasurable and can repay the kindness of father and mother.

"Furthermore, if there are people who copy this sūtra and distribute it throughout the world, and who receive, uphold, read, and recite it, they should know that such people are repaying the kindness of their parents.

"How can the kindness of father and mother be repaid? Simply consider: when parents go about, east and west, to the neighbors, the well, the stove, the mortar, they sometimes do not return home for a while. Their child cries at home, longing for them. The parent then returns at once. The child, seeing the parent coming from afar, whether in the cradle-cart, shakes its head and moves its body, or crawls along following on its belly, crying out toward its mother. The mother bends down toward her child, stretches out both hands, brushes off the dust, calls to it soothingly, opens her breast, takes out her nipple, and nurses it. The mother, seeing the child, is happy. The child, seeing the mother, is joyful. The two hearts meet in grieving love. Such tender compassion is beyond compare.

"At two or three years of age, the child begins to walk about with intention. At mealtimes, without the mother, it does not recognize its father. When the parents go about and happen upon a gathering, if they obtain cakes or meat, they do not eat, but save the taste, carry it tucked away, and bring it home to give to the child. Nine times out of ten they succeed, and are always happy. If even once they fail, the child wails and pretends to cry.

"A spoiled child who is not filial will surely suffer five kinds of ruin. A filial child without resentment will surely have the reward of compassion and compliance.

"As the child grows, it follows friends and companions, combs its hair, and wishes for fine clothes to cover its body. Old, tattered clothing the parents wear themselves; new, fine silk and cotton they give to the child first. When they go about, whether on public or private urgent business, the parents' hearts incline in every direction, following the child east and west, placing concerns above the child's head. When the child takes a wife and acquires another's daughter, the parents gradually become estranged. In the private chamber, husband and wife talk and enjoy together. The parents grow old, their energy and strength declining. From morning to evening, no one comes to ask after them.

"Sometimes the father is alone, the mother widowed, keeping watch over an empty room. They are like travelers lodging in another's house. There is never love or care, not even a damp blanket in the cold. Through bitterness, hardship, and adversity they suffer this. Old in years and faded in appearance, they are covered in lice. Through the long night they cannot sleep, but sigh and lament: 'What sin, what past fault, has produced such an unfilial child?'

"Sometimes when they call, the child glares with angry eyes. The child curses and reviles them; the parents lower their heads and smile bitterly. If the wife is also unfilial and the child also brings the five kinds of ruin, husband and wife join in the five rebellious acts. When the parents call, the child is harsh and commanding. Called ten times, disobeyed nine. Never compliant. Cursing and raging: 'Better if they just died. They are a burden on the earth!' The parents, hearing this, weep in grief and anguish, tears streaming from both eyes, crying until their eyes are swollen: 'When you were small, without me you would not have grown. I gave you birth; it would have been better if I had not.'

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "If good men and good women are able, for the sake of their parents, to receive, uphold, read, recite, and copy this Sūtra on the Weightiness of Parents' Kindness, the Mahāyāna Mahāprajñāpāramitā sūtra, even a single phrase or verse, or have it pass once through their ears, then all of the five heinous offenses and heavy sins will be extinguished without remainder. They will constantly see the Buddha and hear the dharma, and will swiftly attain liberation."

Ānanda rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, knelt for a long time with palms joined, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what should this sūtra be called? How should we uphold it?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "This sūtra is called the Sūtra on the Weightiness of Parents' Kindness. If there are any sentient beings who are able to create merit for their parents, produce sūtras, burn incense, invite the Buddha, bow in worship, and make offerings to the Three Treasures, or provide food for the sangha, they should know that such people are able to repay the kindness of their parents."

Śakra (*Dìshì*, 帝釋), Brahmā (*Fànwáng*, 梵王), the devas, all the people, and all sentient beings heard the sūtra and rejoiced. They gave rise to the bodhisattva-mind. Weeping and sobbing, shaking the earth, their tears falling like rain, they prostrated with all five limbs on the ground. They faithfully received the sūtra, bowed to the Buddha's feet, and joyfully put it into practice.
