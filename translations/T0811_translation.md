# 佛說決定總持經
## Sūtra on the Definitive Dhāraṇī

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0811

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in Rājagṛha on Vulture Peak Mountain, together with a great assembly of monks, twelve hundred and fifty in number, and eighty thousand bodhisattvas. All these great sages had perfected supernatural penetrations, were irreversible, had fathomed all dharmas, had attained dhāraṇī and subtle eloquence, and had clear understanding of skillful means and expedient methods. They had thoroughly studied the immeasurable bodhisattva precepts and had entered into the profound essentials, comprehending the twelve links of dependent arising in their combination. Their conduct was free of fabrication: empty, signless, and wishless, neither arising nor being produced. They caused all dharmas to enter the one Way. Through all penetrating wisdoms and the ocean of wisdom, they embraced the treasury of the Dharma. Their minds were vast and universal; their sagely awakening knew no limit. They clearly recognized all meanings and distinguished the import of beautiful phrases and passages. When questioned, they answered without obstruction. They understood the minds of sentient beings and dissolved all bonds and fetters. Their minds were as equanimous as space, without aversion or attachment, free from all defilements, tranquil and eternally at peace. They had realized freedom from fault and accomplished the ground of the Mahāyāna. They radiated the light of wisdom and subdued the heterodox schools. It was like the sun dispelling the glow of fireflies, or like the radiance of maṇi jewels, fire, and lightning. They had utterly abandoned heedlessness and cast off all inversions and views of permanence and annihilation. They clearly knew non-self, no soul, no life-force, no person, and no sustainer of being. In tranquility they observed all that arises. For incalculable kalpas they had practiced the bodhisattva path, donning the armor of true virtue, transforming all beings through the six boundless perfections, and cultivating the four kinds of benevolence. Through skillful means they transcended the path of birth and death. With this they guided deluded beings, showing them fortune and misfortune, rescuing them from suffering and distress, causing them no longer to plant the roots of birth and death, and enabling all to advance toward the gate of samādhi. Their names were: the Bodhisattva Carefree Chief, the Bodhisattva Lion's Delight, the Bodhisattva Radiant Flower King, the Bodhisattva Whose Brahma Voice Resounds Like Thunder and Rain, the Bodhisattva Immeasurable Virtue-Jewel, the Bodhisattva Various Flowers, the Bodhisattva Adorned with Diverse Jeweled Necklaces, the Bodhisattva Grinding Stone King, the Bodhisattva Dharma Rain, the Bodhisattva Lotus Treasury King, the Bodhisattva Who Destroys Hollow Wearied Views, the Bodhisattva Great Wisdom-Light, and the Bodhisattva Eloquent Speaker of Various Kinds. Together with eighty thousand bodhisattvas of this type.

At that time the World-Honored One, surrounded by his retinue of immeasurable hundreds of thousands, was teaching a sūtra. This Dharma was called the Gate of the Definitive Dhāraṇī.

At that time in the assembly there were ten young men of good family who together listened and received the teaching. Hearing the Tathāgata proclaim the Gate of the Definitive Dhāraṇī, they were filled with joy and delight, and wholesome minds arose in them. They abandoned sleep, delighted in dwelling in solitude, cultivated the virtue of contentment, devoted themselves to walking meditation, strove diligently day and night without daring to slacken, and abandoned heedlessness and thoughts of desire. With such earnestness they persevered in recitation for seven years. At the end of seven years, their minds had raced about, unable to gain even a moment of settled concentration, much less attain the Gate of the Definitive Dhāraṇī. Not obtaining what they wished for, their minds became weary and disillusioned with the virtues of contentment and solitude. They took pleasure in sleeping, abandoned diligent effort, gave up walking meditation and the renunciation of desire, and no longer studied the Gate of the Definitive Dhāraṇī. They cast off the śramaṇa robes and abandoned the precepts, returning to a low station. Confused in their aspirations, they looked for faults in the sagely teaching and all returned to household life, resuming the five worldly occupations.

At that time, the World-Honored One had resolved King Ajātaśatru's false doubts and removed his uncertainties. For seven days in silence the Buddha then displayed his sacred power, causing the king not to be heedless or harboring contentiousness in his mind, dissolving the king's bonds of playful attachment and all his fetters of wrong views. When the seven days had passed, together with seven hundred million people, the king went to the Buddha's abode, wishing to receive the Dharma. The ten young men who had sought faults in the sagely teaching were also among the king's retinue in the great assembly.

At that time, there was a bodhisattva in the assembly named Fearless Action, who had attained dhāraṇī. For countless kalpas he had accomplished patience with dharmas. He had attained the unborn and was free from inversion. His eloquence was immeasurable, and he had entered omniscience. Through the power of right wisdom, he discerned and accorded with all, practiced the boundless perfections, and comprehended the inconceivable levels of the dhāraṇī gate. He universally perceived the aspirations, faculties, and destinations of all sentient beings, and taught the Dharma to those ready for transformation without missing the mark. He rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms, and said to the Buddha: "O Teacher of Gods and Humans! These ten young men of good family left home to cultivate the Way. They strove diligently day and night for seven years without slackening, seeking the dhāraṇī. Not obtaining what they wished, they lost their original aspiration, cast off the śramaṇa robes, and returned to household life, finding fault with the sagely teaching. I pray the World-Honored One will expound the Dharma's meaning and, through skillful means, give them appropriate guidance, so that these young men may reform their past and cultivate anew, taking refuge in the supreme great Way."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Fearless Action: "These young men, in a past life long, long ago, slandered and reviled the Buddha-Dharma and refused to listen to or accept it. Because they slandered and transgressed the Buddha-Dharma, they have been unable to swiftly attain the supreme great Way."

The Bodhisattva Fearless Action then asked the Buddha in verse:

> "I have heard that the Honored Among Humans, the Dharma King, radiates light.
> Through the practices of the bodhisattvas, the burden of innumerable offenses is removed.
> Merit and wisdom spread far and wide; none can surpass them.
> The ten powers are without equal; sacred wisdom knows no obstruction.
> Already liberated from the three states, never being heedless.
> Please tell of their practices, so that this path may be purified.
> I now ask the immeasurable one, who holds the wisdom of sweet dew.
> Please explain and distinguish the nets of doubt and fettered minds.
> I now inquire of the Tathāgata: may the World-Honored One resolve this.
> What practices do the bodhisattvas cultivate in the Mahāyāna,
> Striving daily with supreme and unwavering effort,
> Subduing the armies of Māra, burning away all defilements,
> Understanding all practices and revealing them to sentient beings?
> Please explain and distinguish the foundational practice of the Way.
> With words subtle and wondrous, with a gentle face, speak for people.
> In the midst of birth and death, delight in liberating beings from suffering.
> Listen carefully to the teaching of praise, resolving the doubts of the multitude.
> Most excellent one, please expound how one should cultivate the Way.
> Well cultivating virtue and purity, with foundational practice extinguishing birth and death,
> With compassion bestowing peace, rescuing the minds of sentient beings.
> Abandoning and renouncing places of attachment, generously giving treasures and carriages,
> Head and eyes given to others, wife and children likewise.
> Revering patience and endurance, delighting in the virtue of precepts,
> Diligently cultivating the vow of tranquility, understanding the steadfast aspiration.
> With this virtue the assembly can endure a hundred thousand afflictions.
> The benevolent honored one's wandering steps: supreme penetration without obstruction.
> Clearly knowing what should be practiced, like the seasons without hesitation.
> Extinguishing the three defilements: what is accomplished is boundless.
> Distinguishing the work of renunciation, transforming beings in the five destinies.
> Abiding peacefully: please speak of how one should practice the Way."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Fearless Action: "These ten young men of good family, in past lives, transgressed against the buddhas and slandered the sūtras. How did they transgress against the buddhas' Dharma? Long, long ago in the distant past, thirty-two kalpas before the present, in the world called Yánqì, there was a buddha named Light-Sound Tathāgata, Worthy One, Fully and Perfectly Awakened, Complete in Clarity and Conduct, Well-Gone One, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Guide of the Dharma, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. These ten young men of good family, in that world, were the sons of a wealthy and powerful elder. After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, during the period of the latter teaching, they established merit in honor of the World-Honored One, building five hundred stūpas, monasteries, and lecture halls, and providing various offerings to supply the community of monks. In each of the stūpas and their monasteries, hundreds of thousands of monks resided.

"At that time in that world, there was a bodhisattva named Eloquence Accumulated, who had attained dhāraṇī and wielded skillful means. Advancing and retreating as appropriate, he guided all beings and taught the Dharma, ascending the high seat. The eloquence of five hundred buddhas' teachings instructed him. Eighty billion heavenly sons, in the sky, purified all defilements, adorned and decorated, creating lecture halls and raising banners as offerings to the Dharma teacher. With compassion for all, he taught the sūtras. Seventy thousand people became irreversibly established on the path to supreme perfect awakening, and ten thousand people attained the fruit of stream-entry.

"At that time in that world, there was a king named Moon-Gift, who loved the Way and thirsted for the meaning of the sūtras, delighting in the Dharma. The king then made offerings to the Dharma teacher, the Bodhisattva Eloquence Accumulated. Together with the five hundred women of the inner palace and court, he performed great music, fashioned jeweled flowers and scattered them above, mixed sandalwood paste to anoint the teacher's body, covered him with five hundred canopies, and offered five hundred fine garments. For seven days and nights, the king stood without daring to sit, attending to the teacher's comfort and needs without missing the proper occasions. His reverence for the Dharma teacher was of the highest order; his majestic honor and merit were unsurpassed.

"At that time, the sons of the wealthy and powerful elder slandered and falsely accused the Dharma teacher, the Bodhisattva Eloquence Accumulated, saying he violated the Dharma precepts and did not follow the monastic discipline. Because of this offense, they fell into hell for ninety thousand years. Born in the human realm, for fifty thousand lives they were born in the borderlands among barbarians, deluded in wrong views, obscured by the veil of offenses. For six hundred lives they were always born blind, mute, and without tongues, unable to speak. Having left home to practice the Way as śramaṇas, for seven hundred lives they strove earnestly in cultivation, not sparing their bodies and lives, yet did not attain the dhāraṇī. Now, reborn in my era, their minds are repeatedly disturbed and unable to settle in concentration. Because of this, the residual calamity of past offenses, the obscuring veil, prevents these young men from attaining the dhāraṇī. Therefore, worthy one, I entrust and urge you all, solemnly admonishing: when you hear this sūtra and personally see a Dharma teacher who proclaims and disseminates it, you should all join in making offerings. You should not harbor worry, enmity, or harmful intent. How much worse would it be to hear from afar and generate evil thoughts!"

The Buddha said: "Young men of good family! Suppose someone were to gouge out the eyes of all sentient beings. This offense, though grave, can still be endured, and the kalpas of retribution will eventually end. But if someone harbors harmful intent toward a Dharma teacher, the kalpas of that offense exceed even that. Suppose someone were to take all the quarrelsome sentient beings and reconcile them, separating those in conflict and establishing them in peace. Even so, if one sees a Dharma teacher and stands before them with a single-pointed mind, this merit surpasses that of reconciling all quarrelsome beings and bringing them peace, by a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a ten-thousandfold, a hundred-million-fold, a trillion-fold. Even these cannot match standing before a bodhisattva with a joyful face. Why? Because one who slanders a Dharma teacher slanders the Tathāgata. One who wishes to make offerings to the Tathāgata should honor the Dharma teacher. One who wishes to revere the Tathāgata should obey the Dharma teacher. One who wishes to prostrate before the Tathāgata should prostrate before the Dharma teacher. Why? Because all bodhisattvas are born from the Dharma. From the bodhisattvas, omniscience is accomplished. From omniscience, a buddha, a world-honored one, is accomplished. What the bodhisattva initiates comes from generating the aspiration for the Way. It is not by abiding in desire or in the defilements that one attains prostration. Through non-arising and non-abiding, purifying the practice of brahmacarya, one sees prostration. One does not worship the formless through the samādhi of formlessness. It is not through residual calamity that one comes into birth, but through self-sovereign aspiration that one takes rebirth. Having already been liberated from all the practices of deluded and foolish ordinary beings, to suppose that a bodhisattva has residual calamity would be like supposing that empty space has form and appearance. It is like, young men of good family, when the Dragon King Anavatapta wishes to see the nāgas sporting, he instructs all the nāga retinue: they all depend on the dragon king for peace and safety, and are always freed from the three kinds of suffering, able to be without affliction. A bodhisattva is the same: relinquishing all pleasures, severing offenses in the three states, extinguishing the veil of defilements and afflictions, destroying manifold suffering, and only then bestowing peace and liberating sentient beings. It is like a great nāga swimming in the water without any fear. A bodhisattva is the same, wandering through the three realms without any dread. It is like a great nāga moving through deep water, seeing all that is within, taking whatever it desires at will, and not being harmed by the water. A bodhisattva is the same, wandering among the blind and dark ordinary beings, abiding in their midst, seeing only wisdom-counsel and not departing from the practice of the Way, not mingling with the foolish in their dust, and not being submerged by the three states. Therefore, a practitioner should always protect and serve the Dharma with care and equanimity. This is the practice that a bodhisattva cultivates."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "One who wishes to take refuge in the Buddha and reverently serve the immeasurable sage
> Should always make offerings to the bodhisattvas and honor the guiding teacher.
> My robes, bowl, bed, bench, and all furnishings:
> The Buddha offers all of these. Let them be given to those who aspire to the Way.
> The lamps offered to the Buddha: let these lamps be given to them.
> In this way, perfect awakening is attained: supreme among all the sages.
> Food, drink, carriages, and vehicles: give to these wise ones.
> As guides for the deluded, they lead them to the region of sweet dew.
> Among all sentient beings, granting peace to gods and humans:
> By according with those who first generate the aspiration, one cannot repay their kindness."

The Bodhisattva Fearless Action again said to the Buddha: "O Teacher of Gods and Humans! Is there some skillful means by which these ten young men of good family may have their offenses removed and attain the Way?"

The Buddha said: "If these ten people single-mindedly receive and study what the Buddha has proclaimed, cultivate this Dharma and vinaya, become śramaṇas again, and diligently recite the verses of the definitive dhāraṇī, they may accomplish the Way. What are the verses of the definitive dhāraṇī?

> "'Cultivate clarity. Pure phrases. Clear and cool. Nothing is conferred.
> Nothing is created. Nothing is attained. Follow swiftly. Receive the newly born.
> Offer diligent effort. Revere walking meditation. Strenuously cultivate. Wisdom understands clearly.
> Mastering contemplation. Nothing arises. Remove affliction. Wander in delight.
> Raise up lightly. Universal purity. Nothing is impure.'"

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Fearless Action: "If these ten people recite these verses of the definitive dhāraṇī, and for seven days cultivate benevolent virtue without descending into the formless, without harboring anger, calm and unhurried, wandering without fabrication, with a mind free of fault, equanimous, relinquishing all possessions, free from the five aggregates, releasing impure mental states, and being mindful of the Buddha, then, young men of good family, if they can thus follow and comply with the Way's teaching, a thousand buddhas from each of the ten directions will appear before them and make themselves visible. The young men will set their minds firmly, reproach themselves, and take refuge in the sagely honored ones. Their offenses may be removed and they will attain the wisdom of the Way. When these young men heard the Buddha's teaching, they complied with the Dharma and vinaya. They immediately abandoned their household occupations and became śramaṇas. They recited the verses of the definitive dhāraṇī as the sage had instructed, striving diligently day and night for seven days. When the seven days were complete, they immediately saw a thousand buddhas from each of the ten directions, who each explained for them the practice of removing offenses. They immediately attained the gate of the practice of universal wisdom and the definitive dhāraṇī, leaping beyond thirty-six kalpas of the difficulties of birth and death. They became irreversible and will attain supreme perfect awakening."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Fearless Action: "The King Moon-Gift of that time is now Amitābha Buddha. Eloquence Accumulated is now the Tathāgata Akṣobhya. The ten elder's sons are now these ten young men of good family. Therefore, Fearless Action! Those who aspire to study the bodhisattva vehicle should earnestly cultivate as the Buddha has taught, abide in the true Dharma, cast off ignorance, and take care never to seek out the faults of others."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Fearless Action: "There are four practices that, if cultivated, purify the Buddha's Way. What are the four? First, practicing the work of emptiness, always maintaining a compassionate mind, not harming sentient beings. Second, the bodhisattva should respect and be compassionate toward fellow practitioners, never treating them with contempt. Third, when teaching the Dharma for others, giving generously without attachment or expectation. Fourth, maintaining a single-pointed aspiration, not harboring thoughts of offerings, robes, and food. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "May one's aspiration always delight in the firm faith of emptiness and nothingness,
> Cultivating and purifying the most supreme Way.
> Just as the sun's light radiates at the proper time,
> Having attained the Buddha's Way, what is illuminated surpasses even that.
> May no one ever know of any fault in them.
> Being a sage of virtue, universally enduring all.
> Such is the pure and gentle ground:
> Abandoning arrogance and conceit, they can bear the immeasurable.
> All the finest garments one possesses
> Should always be given to those who aspire to the Buddha's Way.
> Nor should one expect offerings from others:
> This is the pure stage of the Buddha's Way.
> Through giving the immeasurable Dharma without expectation,
> Always cultivating compassion and mindfulness toward sentient beings,
> With an aspiration always cherishing benevolence and virtue:
> This is the purification of the Way of the buddhas.
> If one delights in serving all the sages
> And wishes to make offerings to the sūtras of desirelessness,
> One should always serve the boundless Buddha's Way:
> These Three Treasures are all born from it."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Fearless Action: "There are four further practices that swiftly attain the Buddha's Way. What are the four? First, loving the sūtras of illumination and delighting in the Mahāyāna. Second, keeping far from desire and lust and not habituating to heedlessness. Third, always practicing aid to the impoverished and destitute, providing what they lack. Fourth, being able to bestow the wealth of the seven-fold Dharma upon others. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Loving the Dharma teacher, aspiring to the Mahāyāna, keeping far from desire and lust,
> Cultivating the practice of purity, having compassion for the poor and those in straits,
> Rescuing all who are in danger, always giving the seven kinds of wealth,
> Giving without wearying on the path."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Fearless Action: "There are four further practices that swiftly attain the Buddha's Way. What are the four? First, always practicing great loving-kindness, having compassion for all living beings. Second, always practicing great compassion, shedding tears for them. Third, always practicing great joy, with a gentle face bringing delight to all, looking upon sentient beings. Fourth, always practicing great protection, rescuing beings from the afflictions of birth and death in the three realms. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always practicing great loving-kindness, having compassion for all sentient beings,
> Thinking of bringing them to fulfillment, like a mother nurturing her child.
> With great compassion, wandering through birth and death, not fearing any affliction.
> The five destinies are like foam and bubbles; with compassion, one sheds tears for them.
> With a gentle face, observing sentient beings, bringing them joy through the Dharma,
> Showing them how to leave all suffering, free from pain and forever at peace.
> Submitting gently and following the five destinies, showing what is fitting through skillful means,
> Enabling them to cross the afflictions of the three realms and attain the unsurpassed Way."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Fearless Action: "If anyone diligently seeks this dhāraṇī, they should practice dwelling in solitude, frequently attend Dharma assemblies, practice with equanimity and purity, wear clean garments, cultivate the four kinds of dignified deportment and proper conduct, with an aspiration that never slackens. With various kinds of offerings, they should serve the Dharma teacher with sincere faith in the Three Treasures. Always maintaining reverence, humility, and modesty, never weary but always striving. Without flattery, deceit, or crooked conduct, their thoughts should never depart from the Buddha. What the mind cultivates should be the understanding that nothing truly exists. Since nothing exists, there is nothing to conceive. One should be able to understand the nature and conduct of all sentient beings, diligently and carefully keeping mind and speech in accord. Delighting in the buddhas, one should ask questions and receive instruction, confessing faults and observing propriety, encouraging and assisting in the roots of virtue. In dignified deportment and conduct, one should not depart from the Way's teaching. Accepting whatever comes, and when hearing non-human sounds, not being frightened. Not fearing snakes, vipers, or venomous insects. Revering one's teacher and cultivating this sūtra without ever growing weary."

When the Buddha taught this sūtra, thirty thousand people who had never before generated the aspiration for the Way all now generated the aspiration for supreme perfect awakening. Five thousand people freed themselves from the dust of defilement, and the Dharma-eye became pure. Thirty thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of the unborn dharma.

The Buddha said: "If any bodhisattva hears this sūtra taught, they will all fully accomplish all meritorious virtues and immeasurable eloquence. If good sons and good daughters who study the bodhisattva path were to fill a great thousandfold world-system with the seven treasures and, for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, present them as offerings to the Three Treasures, and another person heard this sūtra, the merit of the latter would surpass the former. If a bodhisattva were to practice five of the perfections for a hundred thousand kalpas, but without great wisdom and without skillful means, that would not compare to hearing the essentials of this sūtra; the merit of the latter surpasses the former. Therefore, young men of good family, I entrust this sūtra to you all. Earnestly encourage one another. Protect it according to the time so that it is not lost or forgotten. Do not allow it to be increased or diminished, but preserve it in the world forever. Uphold, recite, read, and explain its meaning widely for others. Even if you should encounter calamity and lose your life, you should still take care of this sūtra and not let it be lost. This is the Buddha's teaching."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the Bodhisattva Fearless Action, the ten young men of good family, the great śrāvakas, and the entire assembly of devas, nāgas, spirits, asuras, and worldly people, having heard the sūtra, all rejoiced, paid obeisance, and departed.
