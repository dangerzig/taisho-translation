# 佛說隨勇尊者經
## Sūtra of the Venerable Anuvīrya, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0505

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護) and others

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the city of Rājagṛha, at the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove Monastery, together with an assembly of bhikṣus.

At that time, there was a venerable one named Anuvīrya, who was beside the city of Rājagṛha, in the Śītavana charnel ground, on the peak called Snake Gathering, in a great monastic dwelling, where he was walking alone in meditation. He was suddenly bitten by a venomous snake. The snake's body was thin, slender, and glistening, fierce and terrifying, like an añjana-śaraka. Immediately, the Venerable Anuvīrya called out to the assembly of bhikṣus, saying: "Venerable ones! I have been bitten by a venomous snake. My flesh is breaking down. You should place me outside the monastery, lest the spreading poison contaminate others."

At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra was not far from where Anuvīrya was staying, seated under a tree. He heard Anuvīrya make this declaration, and immediately went to him. Arriving, he said: "Venerable Anuvīrya! Just now I observed your face and all your faculties. Originally there was nothing unusual about them. Why then do you now make such a cry?"

Anuvīrya replied: "Venerable one! I have been bitten by a venomous snake. My flesh is breaking down. You should place me outside the monastery, lest the spreading poison contaminate others.

"Venerable Śāriputra! If I were to grasp and attach, thinking: 'The eye is mine, the eye is my possession; the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind are mine, the mind is my possession; form is mine, form is my possession; sound, scent, taste, touch, and dharmas are mine, dharmas are my possession; the earth element is mine, the earth element is my possession; the water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness elements are mine, the consciousness element is my possession; the form aggregate is mine, the form aggregate is my possession; the feeling, perception, formation, and consciousness aggregates are mine, the consciousness aggregate is my possession': if I were to grasp and attach to all such dharmas, then my face and all my faculties might show some change.

"Venerable Śāriputra! I do not grasp and attach to all these dharmas in that way, thinking the eye is mine, the eye is my possession, and so on up to the consciousness aggregate is mine, the consciousness aggregate is my possession. Why then should my face and all my faculties show any change?"

Śāriputra said: "Venerable Anuvīrya! If the views of self and mine, as well as the conceit of self, are severed and the grasping is ended, and one can further know that the root of what has been severed is permanently severed without remainder, like the heart of a tāla tree that is broken and can no longer grow again, and one has realized the dharma of non-arising: for such a one, what change could there be to perceive?"

Then the Venerable Śāriputra placed the Venerable Anuvīrya outside the monastery, where he lay upon the ground.

At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra spoke these verses:

> The wise one well practices the holy life,
> And constantly cultivates the causes of the right path.
> When the time comes to relinquish life, joy arises in the mind,
> Like one gravely ill who finds relief from suffering.

> The wise one well practices the holy life,
> And constantly cultivates the causes of the right path.
> When the time comes to relinquish life, joy arises in the mind,
> As if casting aside a vessel of deadly poison.

> The wise one well practices the holy life,
> And constantly cultivates the causes of the right path.
> One is able to slip free at the moment of releasing this form,
> Like a person escaping from a burning house.

> The wise one practices the holy life and cultivates the right path,
> And perceives all things in the world as grass and trees,
> Without meaning and without accomplishment.
> Ultimately, there is no attachment to anything at all.

Then the Venerable Śāriputra, having arranged matters for the Venerable Anuvīrya, went to the Buddha. Arriving, he prostrated at the World-Honored One's feet and reported the above events in full.

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra! That good man Anuvīrya, if at that time he had heard me teach the supreme and wondrous verses and the great vidyā-mantra, he surely would not have been bitten by the venomous snake and had his body destroyed."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What are these supreme and wondrous verses? What is this great vidyā-mantra? May you please teach them for me."

Then the World-Honored One spoke these verses:

> Toward King Dhṛtarāṣṭra I practice loving-kindness;
> Toward the nāga Airāvaṇa, likewise.
> Toward Aśvatara, Kṣemara,
> And all the sons of Takṣaka, I practice loving-kindness.

> Toward King Virūpākṣa I practice loving-kindness;
> Toward the nāga Kṛṣṇagautama, likewise.
> Toward the nāgas Nanda and Upananda,
> Those two also, I practice loving-kindness in the same way.

> Toward the footless I practice loving-kindness;
> Toward the two-footed and four-footed, likewise.
> And toward those with many feet, all sentient beings,
> I practice loving-kindness equally, without distinction.

> Toward all the nāgas I practice loving-kindness;
> Those nāgas who dwell in the great ocean.
> Toward all sentient beings, with a universal mind of compassion,
> Whether sentient or inanimate, it is the same.

> Toward all classes of sentient beings,
> Even the flying insects and the crawling creatures,
> May the causes of illness and affliction be removed,
> And may all obtain peace and happiness.

> Toward all classes of sentient beings,
> Even the flying insects and the crawling creatures,
> With the vision of goodness and equality,
> May all wrongdoings and evils be removed.

> The poison of the snake is extremely fierce;
> Its venom can destroy a person's body and life.
> Yet in those snake-dwelling mountain peaks,
> I always dwell and wander above.

> I am the unsurpassed teacher of the world;
> I always declare words of truth.
> By the cause of my truthful speech and karma,
> The poison of snakes cannot enter my body.

> Craving, aversion, and delusion:
> These three are the great poisons of the world.
> The World-Honored One, the Great Awakened One, is not touched by poison;
> The true power of the Buddha can destroy all poison.

> Craving, aversion, and delusion:
> These three are the great poisons of the world.
> The World-Honored One's correct Dharma is not touched by poison;
> The true power of the Dharma can destroy all poison.

> Craving, aversion, and delusion:
> These three are the great poisons of the world.
> The World-Honored One's pure assembly is not touched by poison;
> The true power of the Sangha can destroy all poison.

> Among all the poisons in the world,
> The poison of craving is the greatest.
> Only the Buddha can destroy it and master it;
> Through this, all poisons are extinguished.

Then the World-Honored One spoke the great vidyā:

*Tadyathā oṃ ḍuṃmiri ḍuṃmiri ḍuṃmi pra ḍuṃmi nāti sunāti kevati munāye sasamaye nanti nantiri nirī nirī rākeśi vārī varākeśi urni yaṅguri svāhā.*

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra! That good man Anuvīrya, if at that time he had heard me teach these verses and this great vidyā-mantra, he surely would not have been bitten by snake venom and had his body destroyed."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The supreme and wondrous verses and the great vidyā-mantra that the Buddha has now taught: at the time when the bhikṣu Anuvīrya was bitten by the snake, he was far from the Buddha. How could he have obtained such a wondrous Dharma?"

Then the Venerable Śāriputra, having spoken these words, touched his forehead to the ground, prostrated at the World-Honored One's feet, and departed from the Buddha's assembly.
