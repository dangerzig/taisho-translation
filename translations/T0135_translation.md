# 佛說力士移山經
## Sūtra on the Strongmen Moving the Mountain Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0135

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the country of Kuśinagara, in the grove of great trees in the birthplace of the Malla strongmen, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. It was near the time of his passing into nirvāṇa. The ministers and people of the country all came out to assemble.

The Buddha asked Ānanda: "Why has this great crowd of people gathered?"

The worthy Ānanda said to the World-Honored One: "There is a great boulder not far from here, sixty zhang on each side and one hundred and twenty zhang in height, which blocks the road and obstructs travelers. Five hundred strongmen have discussed the matter together and said: 'We are famed throughout the world for our extraordinary physical strength, yet we idly maintain ourselves without any benefit to the age. We should move this boulder together and establish a feat for posterity.' They then joined their strength and cried out in unison, but exhausted themselves and could not move it even slightly. The sound of their shouting reached far and wide, and so the people have converged here from all directions to watch."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Adjust your Dharma robes properly and let us go observe."

Ānanda received the instruction, rose from his seat, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and attended alone, following behind the Buddha as they proceeded. The great crowd and the five hundred strongmen saw the Buddha approaching from afar: his golden countenance composed, his majestic brilliance towering, his form exceptionally wonderful, his appearance pure, his body adorned with the marks of a great being, having subdued the aggregates, without any decline, his mind tranquil, his faculties still and settled, harmonious and peaceful. He was the foremost of devas and humans. His great radiance blazed dazzlingly, shining like a jeweled mountain, like a great torch illuminating the darkness, like a great mountain ridge capped with accumulated snow, like the light of the sun rising in the morning, like the autumn moon at its fullest with the stars especially bright, like a wheel-turning king accompanied by his precious ministers and the four divisions. Like a tree in flower, resplendent and flourishing, its blossoms immeasurable, his holy person surpassed all others.

The five hundred strongmen and the countless crowd, gazing upon his supernatural transformations, all leapt with joy. Good hearts arose spontaneously, and they all came forward to welcome him. They prostrated with the five points of their body touching the ground and bowed at his feet, then with unified minds withdrew and stood to one side.

Then the World-Honored One asked the strongmen: "Why are your bodies weary and your faces haggard?"

They answered: "This great boulder, sixty zhang on each side and one hundred and twenty zhang in height, we wished to lift and move together. From the first day we have exerted ourselves with all our strength, and after a full month it still cannot be moved. We are ashamed of our failure and ridiculed by the world. Therefore we are exhausted and our complexions are haggard."

"What do you hope to accomplish?"

The strongmen answered: "Indeed, Great Sage! Our blessed strength is without compare. We had hoped to move the boulder to benefit the world, to make our names known and our achievements remembered by future generations, so that the royal road would be straight and the wild regions would submit."

The Buddha told the strongmen: "Your compassionate intention is clear, but your strength is not sufficient. I shall move it for you, fulfilling your original wish, so that you may claim the achievement. Have no shame or fear."

The strongmen were delighted and said: "We respectfully accept."

Then the World-Honored One adjusted his Dharma robes. With the big toe of his right foot he kicked up the boulder, flipping it up to the Brahmā heaven. With his right palm he received it, spun it around three times, placed it in empty space four zhang and nine chi above the ground, then returned it to his palm. With three fingers he crumbled it to powder, and blowing upon it, dissolved it completely. At that moment the great trichiliocosm trembled and shook in six ways.

The strongmen, seeing the Buddha's supernatural transformation and the display of his awesome spiritual power, were struck with awe and their hair stood on end. They said to the World-Honored One: "This lifting with a toe: was this the strength of the Great Sage's parents' nurturing and nursing, or the power of supernatural ability, wisdom, and mental action?"

He answered: "This was the power of nursing. It was no other power. If I were to bring forth the power of supernatural ability, I could move this entire great trichiliocosm of the Buddha's world and place it in an extraordinary land of hundreds of thousands of buddha-fields, without causing any being to have any sense of coming or going, without endangering any being or harming any insect in the earth."

The strongmen asked: "What is the nature of this nursing power?"

The World-Honored One replied: "The strength of one hundred ordinary cattle equals the strength of one water buffalo. The strength of one hundred water buffaloes equals the strength of one blue bull. The strength of one hundred blue bulls equals the strength of one yak. The strength of one hundred yaks equals the strength of one bamboo bull. The strength of one hundred bamboo bulls equals the strength of one grass elephant. The strength of one hundred grass elephants equals the strength of one ordinary elephant. The strength of one hundred ordinary elephants equals the strength of one dark elephant. The strength of one hundred dark elephants equals the strength of one white elephant. The strength of one hundred white elephants equals the strength of one nāga. The strength of one hundred nāgas equals the strength of one fearsome strongman. The strength of one hundred fearsome strongmen equals the strength of one tier-strongman. The strength of one hundred tier-strongmen equals the strength of one crumbling strongman. The strength of one hundred crumbling strongmen equals the strength of one great-destruction strongman. The strength of one hundred great-destruction strongmen equals the strength of one half-vehicle strongman. The strength of one hundred half-vehicle strongmen equals the strength of one vehicle strongman. The strength of one hundred vehicle strongmen equals the strength of one great-vehicle strongman. Incalculable multiples still cannot equal the nursing power of the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Awakened One."

The Buddha told the strongmen: "You should know: this is the nursing power of the Tathāgata."

The strongmen said to the World-Honored One: "The Great Sage has demonstrated his nursing power. What about the power of supernatural ability?"

The Buddha told the strongmen: "I recall that in the past, I was traveling through various countries together with Mahāmaudgalyāyana. At that time there was a famine and the grain was scarce. The bhikṣus could not obtain food through their alms rounds. Maudgalyāyana said to the Buddha: 'Grain is dear and the people are starving. The bhikṣus cannot obtain any food through their alms rounds. Their strength is failing and they cannot recite or study. Day by day they grow weaker, and I fear they will not survive. In ancient times when the world was first formed, the earth produced a natural sweet-dew flavor. Those who ate it were healthy and the four elements were at ease. Later, as people's merit declined, the flavor sank into the earth. Now I wish to turn the earth over and bring forth that ancient flavor, so that the bhikṣus and the people of the country may all be saved, fed until satisfied, and able to recite the sūtras and contemplate the path.'

"The Buddha told Maudgalyāyana: 'Desist! If you were to turn the earth over, there are worms, moths, and creeping creatures in the earth that would be harmed. Moreover, the people's merit is thin, and they should not consume the ancient earth-flavor.'

"Maudgalyāyana said: 'I shall bring the bhikṣus and the starving people to the continent of Uttarakuru, so they may eat the naturally growing rice there.'

"The World-Honored One said: 'Those who have supernatural powers can travel there by themselves, but those who have not attained the power of levitation, how can they go?'

"Maudgalyāyana replied: 'Those without supernatural power I shall support and carry, enabling them to go and be saved.'

"Such was Maudgalyāyana's virtue of awesome transformation. Now the continent of Jambudvīpa is two hundred and eighty thousand li in length and breadth, broad above and narrow below. The continent of Aparagodānīya is three hundred and twenty thousand li in length and breadth, shaped like a half-moon. The continent of Pūrvavideha is three hundred and sixty thousand li in length and breadth, perfectly round. The continent of Uttarakuru is four hundred thousand li square, perfectly square, encircled by mountains on all four sides. If the entire population were to be filled with supernatural power equal to Mahāmaudgalyāyana's, and each and every one were to fill the entire great trichiliocosm, they still could not match the Tathāgata's supernatural power by a hundredfold, a thousandfold, ten-thousandfold, a hundred-millionfold, or a billion-fold. Compared to empty space, there is no comparison and no analogy. This is the power of the Tathāgata's supernatural ability."

The strongmen said further: "Great Sage! You have demonstrated the powers of nursing and supernatural ability. We beseech you to further demonstrate the power of wisdom."

The World-Honored One said: "The great ocean is three million three hundred and sixty thousand li deep, its breadth and length immeasurable. Mount Sumeru, the king of mountains, stands in the great ocean, three million three hundred and sixty thousand li high, its base reaching the ocean floor at the same depth of three million three hundred and sixty thousand li, and its width the same. One could drink the entire ocean dry until nothing remains, yet Śāriputra's wisdom cannot be measured or diminished. If the population of the four continents all attained wisdom equal to Śāriputra's, and each and every one filled the entire great trichiliocosm, they still could not match the Tathāgata's power of wisdom by a hundredfold, a thousandfold, ten-thousandfold, a hundred-millionfold, or a billion-fold. Compared to empty space, there is no comparison and no analogy. This is the power of the Tathāgata's wisdom."

The strongmen said further: "Great Sage! You have demonstrated the powers of nursing, supernatural ability, and wisdom. We beseech you to further demonstrate the power of mental action."

The World-Honored One said: "Suppose clouds were to fill all four continents and the entire great trichiliocosm, and a great rain were to fall universally. Every place through which the rain passed, every stem, node, branch, leaf, flower, and fruit, every vessel of water, mountain, stone, grass, and reed, every crawling and breathing creature, every person and thing, great and small: every single drop would flow into the great ocean. Yet one could distinguish each drop, trace it and name it, and further identify and sort each one, restoring it to its original source without error. The Tathāgata's mental power knows and understands all of this without any hindrance. This is the power of the Tathāgata's mental action."

The strongmen said further: "Great Sage! You have demonstrated the powers of nursing, supernatural ability, wisdom, and mental action. Is there yet another power that surpasses these?"

The World-Honored One said: "The Tathāgata's nursing power, Mahāmaudgalyāyana's power of supernatural ability, Śāriputra's power of wisdom, and the mental-action power of the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas: none of these can match the Tathāgata's ten powers, which are vast and immeasurable."

The strongmen asked: "What are the ten powers?"

The World-Honored One said: "Seeing everything subtle and wonderful, far and near, wrong and right, the possible and the impossible, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the first power.

"Knowing the karmic retributions of past, future, and present, and all the places one has passed through, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the second power.

"Knowing the meditative absorptions, right attainments, and the three gates of liberation, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the third power.

"Seeing the mental faculties and original purity of all beings' minds, understanding all without exception, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the fourth power.

"Understanding the various languages of all beings, whose thoughts differ and whose forms are distinct, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the fifth power.

"Distinguishing the various dispositions of living beings, measureless in number and each different in temperament, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the sixth power.

"Wisdom as vast as the ocean, with immeasurable good speech, tracing back and recognizing all previous existences and past deeds, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the seventh power.

"Understanding the essentials of how craving binds and how to unbind it, following the ailment in every place and prescribing the right medicine. With the divine eye, seeing the good and evil deeds of beings, their beginnings and endings, and where their calamity or fortune leads, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the eighth power.

"With the divine ear, hearing completely the voices of devas and humans, and the sounds of all crawling, breathing, and creeping creatures without exception, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the ninth power.

"The Buddha is without any defilements; the cycle of beginning and end is forever exhausted, with no further bondage or attachment. Through divine, true, and profound wisdom, knowing and seeing for himself as witness, having thoroughly penetrated the practices of the path, what can be done has been done, and there is no further birth or death. Seeing the original nature of all beings in the ten directions without exception, knowing clearly all that exists: this is the tenth power."

The strongmen said to the World-Honored One: "Great Sage! You have demonstrated the powers of nursing, supernatural ability, wisdom, mental action, and the ten powers. Is there yet another extraordinary power that surpasses all these?"

The World-Honored One said: "All these powers, though they be strong and mighty, a hundredfold, thousandfold, ten-thousandfold, or hundred-millionfold, the power of impermanence is to be reckoned as the most supreme, conquering and subduing all others. Why? The body of the Tathāgata is of the nature of vajra, yet impermanence conquers even me, and I shall inevitably be destroyed. Tonight, at midnight, in the birthplace of the Malla strongmen, I shall take my final nirvāṇa. At the crossroads, you should venerate the relics and construct a stūpa shrine. Why? So that people from the four directions may bring various flowers and incense, erect banners and streamers, hang silks, bells, and canopies, and light lamps as offerings. All of this leads to the true and wonderful Dharma."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "What arises must return to cessation; what flourishes must come to decline.
> All things are impermanent; to contemplate this is true peace.
> One may gain a mountain of gold a hundred thousand times over, with merit beyond analogy,
> Yet it does not compare with making offerings at a stūpa of clay, joyfully taking refuge in the supreme shrine.
> One may obtain a hundred thousand treasure-stores, with blessings beyond counting,
> Yet it does not compare with making offerings at a stūpa of clay, leaping with joy and taking refuge in the supreme shrine.
> One may have a hundred thousand jeweled chariots, laden with colors like purple gold,
> Yet it does not compare with making offerings at a shrine of earth, leaping with joy and taking refuge in the Buddha."

When the Buddha taught this sūtra, the assembly of five hundred strongmen, knowing the world to be impermanent and the three realms to be without lasting refuge, with no single truth to rely upon except the path, immediately abandoned their arrogance and no longer reckoned self or ego. They all generated the mind of the unsurpassed, true, and right path, and at that very moment all attained the ground of non-retrogression. Innumerable hundreds of thousands of devas and humans became free from the dust of defilement, and the Dharma-eye of all dharmas arose in them.

The Buddha spoke thus. All rejoiced. They placed their heads upon the ground and bowed in homage to the Buddha.
