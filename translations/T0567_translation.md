# 佛說梵志女首意經
## Sūtra of the Brahmin Girl Prathamacittā, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0567

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Vārāṇasī, at the Deer Park where the sages dwell, where he had turned the Dharma wheel. He was with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus and countless bodhisattvas who had come from various buddha-lands.

At that time the World-Honored One, at dawn, arranged his robes, took his bowl, and entered the city to bring blessings to all. The worthy Ānanda attended him in the customary manner. The Great Sage then entered the great city of Vārāṇasī, proceeding in order from street to street until he came to a brahmin's gate.

The brahmin had a daughter named Prathamacittā. From afar she saw the Buddha's body, dignified, extraordinary, and wonderful, his supernatural majesty towering. His mind was tranquil, his faculties perfectly still. He was established in the highest serenity and foremost calm. There was no decline, and fear had been forever removed. His mind was pure and without defilement, like a great clear pool. His body, the color of burnished gold, gleamed like a jeweled mountain. He was like a great celestial tree in full bloom, its flowers and fruit flourishing. The thirty-two marks of a great person adorned his body, and the eighty refinements were displayed throughout his form. He was radiant like a great palace with jeweled pillars, lofty as Mount Sumeru, towering and prominent. He was like the full moon shining bright among the stars, like the rising sun whose brilliance illuminates all.

The girl, seeing the Buddha come and stand outside the gate, was filled with joy. She went inside to prepare a seat, returned to the Buddha's presence, touched her head to his feet, and knelt: "Welcome! May you be at peace. I beg the Holy One to condescend and turn his gaze upon us." The Buddha, out of compassion, entered her home and took his seat.

Prathamacittā, delighted, bowed again, took a small stool, and sat before the Buddha. She spoke: "God of Gods! I have heard that the Tathāgata is traveling in Vārāṇasī, at the Deer Park where the sages dwell, and that for śramaṇas, brahmins, Brahmā, nāgas, spirits, the māras, worldly people, and all living beings, he has turned the unsurpassed wheel and made the true Dharma manifest."

The Buddha told the brahmin girl: "Just so! Just so! It is truly as you say. I have traveled in this land and turned the unsurpassed wheel. For those who had not yet heard, I have revealed the true Dharma, and not by means of false dharma."

Prathamacittā asked the Buddha: "What is this Dharma wheel that the Tathāgata has turned?"

The World-Honored One told the girl: "Through the twelve links of dependent origination, I have turned the Dharma wheel."

Prathamacittā said to the Buddha: "With your additional great kindness, please explain in detail for me the twelve links of dependent origination, and where the Dharma wheel leads, so that I may understand its meaning and purport."

The Buddha said: "Listen carefully and reflect well upon it. I shall explain its meaning for you." The brahmin girl Prathamacittā received the teaching and listened.

The Buddha told the girl: "If one posits a self, then there is saṃsāra. From arising in what has not arisen: from the condition of ignorance there are formations; from the condition of formations there is consciousness; from the condition of consciousness there is name-and-form; from the condition of name-and-form there are the six sense-bases; from the condition of the six sense-bases there is contact; from the condition of contact there is feeling; from the condition of feeling there is craving; from the condition of craving there is grasping; from the condition of grasping there is becoming; from the condition of becoming there is birth; and from the condition of birth there are old age, sickness, and death, toil, sorrow, and lamentation, and the great mass of suffering.

"Thus, girl, when ignorance ceases, consciousness, formations, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, old age, sickness, and death cease, and toil, sorrow, and lamentation and the great mass of suffering are all removed. You should know that it is by means of this Dharma that in the Deer Park of the sages in Vārāṇasī I turned the Dharma wheel for all the śramaṇas, brahmins, Brahmā, nāgas, spirits, māras, and worldly people, explaining the twelve links of dependent origination. If one receives and practices this, one may thereby attain liberation."

Prathamacittā asked the Buddha: "God of Gods! Does the Dharma have an inside or an outside?"

The Buddha told the girl: "No."

The girl asked again: "Does the Dharma have a fixed state called 'ignorance'?"

He answered: "No."

The girl asked again: "Is there something called 'arising of knowledge' and something called 'cessation'?"

He answered: "No."

The girl asked again: "Does the one who has penetrating knowledge have any form or appearance?"

He answered: "No."

The girl asked again: "Through what causes and conditions does this body come about? If there is no ignorance, from what does it arise? When the mind gives rise to pride and thus produces formations, what are the causes and conditions by which they come together?" She also said: "Great Sage! It is like a tree without roots: what causes it suddenly to produce a trunk, branches, leaves, flowers, and fruit? Likewise, World-Honored One, if ignorance has no form or image, and if causes and conditions arise from the absence of wisdom and produce arrogance, then what is this origin of the absence of wisdom that gives rise to formations? From what does this great mass of suffering come together?"

The Buddha told the girl: "If you wish to know: all characteristics are originally completely pure, and it is likewise so. The dharma of dharmas is that dharmas do not know dharmas. Foolish and unenlightened ordinary people, not hearing of merit and wrongdoing, engage in harmful deeds. From harmful deeds comes saṃsāra. From saṃsāra come suffering and happiness. If one thoroughly understands this, there is neither wrongdoing nor merit-making, there is neither progress nor retreat, neither saṃsāra, nor suffering nor happiness."

The Buddha told Prathamacittā: "If you wish to know, it is like a magician who creates illusions. The illusory person does not think or reflect, 'I am an illusion.' That magician may make the real seem unreal or the unreal seem real. All of this is the dharma of deception, foolishness, and falsity. Consider the magician and the illusory person: there is something illusory, yet there is nothing illusory. All of it is the dharma of emptiness, delusion, bewilderment, and ignorance. What do you think? Does the illusion have an inside or an outside?"

She answered: "No, God of Gods."

"What do you think? Does the illusion have a fixed state with a name of 'self'?"

She answered: "No, God of Gods."

"What do you think? Does the illusory creation go on to a future life and come back?"

She answered: "No, God of Gods."

"What do you think? Does the illusion have arising or ceasing?"

She answered: "No, God of Gods."

"What do you think? Does the illusory creation have any existence or form?"

She answered: "No, God of Gods."

"What do you think? Among the illusory creations, can one see or hear whether there is illusion or no illusion?"

She answered: "No, God of Gods." The girl then said to the Buddha: "I have heard that among illusory creations, there is no seeing or hearing of whether there is illusion or no illusion."

The World-Honored One asked again: "What do you think? If the illusory creation has no body, can one cause the illusion to give rise to various arts?"

The girl answered: "God of Gods! The illusory creation is truly like this: in reality it has nothing whatsoever."

The Buddha said again: "Just so! Just so! Girl! Ignorance has no inside and no outside. If one examines the Dharma, there is truly nothing whatsoever, nor is there any name. Ignorance does not go on to a future life, nor does it come back. Ignorance has no arising and no ceasing. Ignorance has no form or image. Yet from the arising of ignorance, there are formations as conditions. Then name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, old age, sickness, and death arise, along with toil, sorrow, and lamentation and the great mass of suffering."

Then the girl Prathamacittā said to the Buddha: "How extraordinary! Truly unprecedented! What the World-Honored One has set forth is unsurpassable. Why? Buddha, God of Gods! You have turned the Dharma wheel in empty space: an inconceivable Dharma wheel, a Dharma wheel beyond all measure, an ungraspable Dharma wheel, a formless Dharma wheel, an unborn Dharma wheel, a nirvāṇa Dharma wheel."

The World-Honored One said: "Just so! Just so! It is truly as you say. The wheel I have turned is an empty wheel. The Dharma wheel I have turned is inconceivable. The Dharma wheel I have turned is beyond all measure. The wheel I have turned is boundless and ungraspable, formless and unborn. It is the wheel of nirvāṇa."

Then the girl Prathamacittā, joyful and exultant, with wholesome mind arising, took sandalwood powder and various flowers and incense and offered them to the Buddha, scattering them upon him: "World-Honored One! By this root of virtue, I vow to uphold and safeguard this well, and to save and gather in all. I shall subdue the sense-faculties, master desire, and attain the turning of the Dharma wheel: the turning of the empty wheel, the inconceivable wheel, the wheel beyond all measure, the boundless and ungraspable, formless and unborn wheel of nirvāṇa."

The Buddha was immediately pleased. Five-colored light of blue, yellow, red, white, and green issued from his mouth. This great radiance illuminated the ten directions and countless buddha-lands, covering and surpassing the brilliance of the sun and moon. It returned and circled his body three times, then entered through the crown of his head.

The worthy Ānanda, who understood the seven qualities (knowing the meaning, understanding the Dharma, discerning the time, comprehending moderation, knowing the assembly, understanding himself, and recognizing the capacities of others) rose from his seat, straightened his robes, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms, and said to the Buddha: "Now the Great Sage has shown his pleasure. What has caused this response? May you explain its meaning. God of Gods! Out of great compassion, for the sake of the devas, worldly people, and all, please distinguish and explain this."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Did you see the brahmin girl Prathamacittā offer sandalwood, flowers, and powdered incense to the Buddha? And that her mind's vow was to attain the turning of the Dharma wheel?"

He replied: "I have seen it."

The Buddha said: "By this root of virtue, this girl shall protect herself and bring peace to others, saving and gathering in many. After the end of her present life, she shall transform her female body. For eighty-four billion kalpas she shall not fall into the evil destinies. She shall make offerings to sixty thousand Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, leaving the household to become a śramaṇa, aspiring to the śramaṇa life, hearing and receiving the sūtras and the Dharma. Having received the sūtras and the Dharma, she shall recite them at once. She shall uphold the Tathāgata's true Dharma while the Buddha is in the world, and after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa she shall make offerings to the relics, converting immeasurable and countless beings beyond reckoning, establishing them upon the path of unsurpassed true enlightenment. In her very last life, when the kalpa is exhausted, in this great trichiliocosm she shall attain unsurpassed true enlightenment and become a perfectly awakened Buddha. The kalpa shall be named Jewel-Brilliance, and the Buddha shall be called Ratnaprabhā Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. That Buddha's lifespan shall be a full kalpa, and for immeasurable multitudes beyond count, she shall encourage and guide them, and in the end, in the realm of nirvāṇa without remainder, she shall enter cessation."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Under which Buddha did the girl Prathamacittā plant her roots of virtue?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Long ago, in the time of Vipaśyin Buddha, this girl, in a female body, first generated the resolve for the Way. She took precious pearl necklaces and scattered them upon the Buddha.

"In the time of Śikhin Tathāgata, the Perfectly Awakened One, she generated a joyful mind and with deep faith removed her garment and offered it to the Buddha. With household faith she left the household, became a śramaṇa, and fulfilled a thousand years of pure conduct. She repeatedly inquired into the profound and wonderful Dharma.

"In the time of Viśvabhū Tathāgata, the Perfectly Awakened One, for a period of fifteen days she made offerings to the World-Honored One and his disciples, providing for them fully and completely. She also vowed to attain unsurpassed true enlightenment.

"In the time of Krakucchanda Tathāgata, the Perfectly Awakened One, she delighted in learning and gathered the chapters and verses of the Dharma. She scattered real pearls and fine incense upon the Buddha, and her mind also vowed to attain perfect enlightenment.

"In the time of Kanakamuni Buddha, the Perfectly Awakened One, she offered fragrant ointments to the stūpa for her entire life, and offered garments, food and drink, medicine, and bedding. For two days she made offerings to the World-Honored One and the holy assembly.

"She saw Kāśyapa Buddha and made offerings and served him, never abandoning the resolve for the Way. Now she has made offerings to me, and hereafter she shall transform her female body and then attain the buddha-path and liberate all beings."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, all were filled with joy.
