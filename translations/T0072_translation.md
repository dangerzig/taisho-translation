# 三歸五戒慈心厭離功德經
## Sūtra on the Merit of the Three Refuges, Five Precepts, Loving-Kindness, and Disenchantment

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0072

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Śrāvastī, in Jeta's Grove, the park of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha spoke to the elder Anāthapiṇḍada: "Long ago in the distant past, there was a brāhmaṇa named Virāma, who was rich and possessed great treasures. When he made offerings, he used eighty-four thousand golden bowls filled with silver fragments, eighty-four thousand silver bowls filled with gold fragments, eighty-four thousand golden and silver water ewers, eighty-four thousand oxen with gold and silver covering their horns, eighty-four thousand maidens adorned with full ornaments, eighty-four thousand beds covered with multi-colored cloth, eighty-four thousand garments, eighty-four thousand elephants and horses all with gold and silver saddles and bridles, and eighty-four thousand residences for giving. Furthermore, at the four city gates he made gifts, giving to all according to their wishes. He also donated one residence to the community of saṅgha. Yet the merit of all these gifts does not compare to receiving the three refuges. Why is this? One who receives the three refuges gives fearlessness to all living beings. Therefore, taking refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha yields merit that is incalculable.

"The merit of the above gifts combined with receiving the three refuges still does not compare to the merit of receiving the five precepts. One who receives the five precepts has merit that is complete and full; that merit is superior. The merit of the above gifts combined with receiving the three refuges and five precepts still does not compare to the merit of a single finger-snap of loving-kindness toward all beings. The merit of the above gifts combined with receiving the three refuges, the five precepts, and loving-kindness toward all beings still does not compare to the merit of arousing the perception that all things in the world are undesirable. Why is this? The merit of arousing the perception that all things in the world are undesirable can cause the practitioner to extinguish the suffering of birth and death and ultimately accomplish the path of Buddhahood. Therefore, that merit is the most supreme."

At that time, the elder, hearing what the Buddha had spoken, joyfully received and practiced it.
