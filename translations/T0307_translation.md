# 佛說莊嚴菩提心經
## Sūtra on Adorning the Bodhi-Mind Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0307

Translated from the Chinese. Yao Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in the city of Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas. Their names were: Jñānaprabha Bodhisattva, Dharmaprabha Bodhisattva, Candraprabha Bodhisattva, Sūryaprabha Bodhisattva, Anantaprabha Bodhisattva, and the sixteen right-minded ones such as Bhadrapāla, who practiced as Mañjuśrī originally practiced. There were also sixty bodhisattvas who practiced as Maitreya Bodhisattva originally practiced, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas of this Fortunate Kalpa and others. At that time, the World-Honored One, surrounded by an immeasurable great assembly, was teaching the Dharma for them.

At that time, in the assembly there was a bodhisattva named Cintāmṛtārtha, who rose from his seat, straightened his garments, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, and scattered various jeweled flowers upon the Buddha. Having scattered them, he joined his palms and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I wish to ask something. May the World-Honored One compassionately permit me."

The Buddha told Bodhisattva Cintāmṛtārtha: "You may freely ask."

Bodhisattva Cintāmṛtārtha addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How does a bodhisattva cultivate the bodhi-mind? What is the bodhi-mind?"

The Buddha told Bodhisattva Cintāmṛtārtha: "As for the bodhisattva's cultivation of the bodhi-mind, it is not that the bodhi-mind is something that can be found among sentient beings. This mind is neither form nor perception, and in the Dharma too there is no one who obtains it. Why? Because sentient beings are empty."

Bodhisattva Cintāmṛtārtha addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The nature of dharmas is so profoundly deep. How should a bodhisattva cultivate the practice?"

The Buddha told Bodhisattva Cintāmṛtārtha: "Good man! The bodhi-mind is neither existent nor created, and it is apart from language. Bodhi is the mind, and the mind is sentient beings. If one can understand in this way, this is called a bodhisattva cultivating the bodhi-mind. Bodhi is neither past, nor future, nor present. Such a mind, and sentient beings too, are neither past, nor future, nor present. If one can understand in this way, one is called a bodhisattva. Yet in this there is truly nothing obtained; it is through non-obtaining that one obtains. If one does not obtain any dharma at all, this is called attaining bodhi. For the sake of beings who are just beginning, it is said that there is bodhi. Just as an arhat who takes realization obtains nothing in the dharmas; it is by conventional language that it is said there is bodhi, yet bodhi truly cannot be obtained. If one does not obtain any dharma at all, this is called attaining bodhi. Yet in this there is also no mind and no creator of mind; there is no bodhi and no creator of bodhi; there are no sentient beings and no creator of sentient beings; there are no śrāvakas and no one who aspires to the śrāvaka path; there are no pratyekabuddhas and no one who aspires to the pratyekabuddha path; there are no bodhisattvas and no one who aspires to the bodhisattva path; there are no Buddhas and no one who becomes a Buddha; there is no conditioned and no one who creates the conditioned; there is no unconditioned and no one who creates the unconditioned. What has already been obtained, is being obtained, or will be obtained: all these cannot be obtained."

The Buddha said: "Good man! Listen carefully, reflect well upon this. I shall now explain what should be explained.

"A bodhisattva who gives rise to the bodhi-mind has ten dharmas. What are the ten? Giving rise to the first mind is the accomplishment of the foundation of all good, like Mount Sumeru adorned with many treasures. Giving rise to the second mind is the practice of the pāramitā of generosity, like the great earth that nourishes all good dharmas. Giving rise to the third mind is the practice of the pāramitā of moral discipline, like the lion king who can subdue all beasts, eliminating wrong views. Giving rise to the fourth mind is the practice of the pāramitā of patience, like Nārāyaṇa, firm and indestructible, eliminating afflictions. Giving rise to the fifth mind is the practice of the pāramitā of diligence, manifesting all good dharmas, like heavenly flowers that fulfill wishes and teach the Dharma. Giving rise to the sixth mind is the practice of the pāramitā of meditation, like the sun's radiance that eliminates all darkness. Giving rise to the seventh mind is the practice of the pāramitā of wisdom, with all aspirations fulfilled, like a merchant who escapes all difficulties. Giving rise to the eighth mind is the practice of the pāramitā of skillful means, eliminating all hindrances, like the full moon, pure and free from defilement. Giving rise to the ninth mind is the aspiration to fulfill one's original vows, traveling to pure buddha-lands, delighting in hearing the profound and wondrous Dharma, eliminating poverty. Giving rise to the tenth mind is like empty space, with wisdom without limit, like a wheel-turning king who accomplishes the knowledge of all modes. Good man! One who can give rise to these ten kinds of mind is called a bodhisattva, also called a mahāsattva, also called an unconditioned being, also called an unobstructed being, also called a being who has crossed over, also called an inconceivable being. Yet in all of this there is no mind and no bodhi.

"Furthermore, good man! There are ten samādhis that protect the bodhi-mind. What are the ten? Giving rise to the first mind is protected by the Dharma-Jewel Samādhi. Giving rise to the second mind is protected by the Firm Samādhi. Giving rise to the third mind is protected by the Immovable Samādhi. Giving rise to the fourth mind is protected by the Non-Retrogression Samādhi. Giving rise to the fifth mind is protected by the Jeweled-Flower Samādhi. Giving rise to the sixth mind is protected by the Sunlight Samādhi. Giving rise to the seventh mind is protected by the All-Meaning Samādhi. Giving rise to the eighth mind is protected by the Wisdom-Illumination Samādhi. Giving rise to the ninth mind is protected by the All-Buddhas-Present-Before-One Samādhi. Giving rise to the tenth mind is protected by the Śūraṅgama Samādhi.

"Furthermore, good man! The signs of the bodhisattva's first bhūmi are seeing three thousand buddha-lands filled with billions of nayutas of hidden treasuries. At the second bhūmi, one sees three thousand buddha-lands flat, level, and adorned with many treasures, brilliantly resplendent. At the third bhūmi, one sees mighty warriors subjugating enemies. At the fourth bhūmi, one sees wind-wheels coming from the four directions, with various wondrous flowers spread everywhere upon the ground. At the fifth bhūmi, one sees many women adorned with various jeweled garlands, with utpala-flower heavenly crowns, campaka-flower heavenly crowns, vārṣika-flower heavenly crowns, and atimuktaka-flower heavenly crowns beautifying their appearance. At the sixth bhūmi, one sees many jeweled pools filled to the brim with water of eight meritorious qualities, with seven-jeweled stairways on all four sides, golden railings surrounding them, clear springs flowing in, fragrant sandalwood paste for mud, and golden sand strewn among it, and one sees oneself sporting and delighting in these pools. At the seventh bhūmi, one sees on one's left and right the various hells, and passes through them without any hardship. At the eighth bhūmi, one sees upon one's shoulders lion kings, of handsome and dignified appearance, with banners upon their heads, of great awesome power, subjugating all beasts. At the ninth bhūmi, one sees a wheel-turning holy king surrounded by hundreds of thousands of ministers, kṣatriyas, and householders, transforming immeasurable sentient beings with the correct Dharma, and sees in the sky jeweled canopies hanging over and covering him. At the tenth bhūmi, one sees the Buddha's form-body, his body of true gold emitting a great radiance, surrounded by a great assembly, teaching the Dharma for them. Good man! These ten kinds of signs correspond to the proper discernment and accomplishment of the ten bhūmis, through the power of samādhi.

"Furthermore, good man! At the first bhūmi there arises the Surpassing-Progress Dhāraṇī. At the second bhūmi there arises the Indestructible Dhāraṇī. At the third bhūmi there arises the Peace and Security Dhāraṇī. At the fourth bhūmi there arises the Difficult-to-Undermine Dhāraṇī. At the fifth bhūmi there arises the Merit-Flower of Various Adornments Dhāraṇī. At the sixth bhūmi there arises the Wisdom-Circle-of-Illumination Dhāraṇī. At the seventh bhūmi there arises the Increasing Dhāraṇī. At the eighth bhūmi there arises the Non-Discrimination Dhāraṇī as foremost, and eighty-four thousand dhāraṇīs arise together with it. At the ninth bhūmi there arises the Boundless Dhāraṇī as foremost, and sixty-two billion nayuta dhāraṇīs arise together with it. At the tenth bhūmi there arises the Inexhaustible Dhāraṇī as foremost, and billions of thousands of Ganges-sand dhāraṇīs arise together with it.

"Furthermore, good man! At the first bhūmi one practices the pāramitā of generosity. At the second bhūmi, moral discipline. At the third, patience. At the fourth, diligence. At the fifth, meditation. At the sixth, wisdom. At the seventh, skillful means. At the eighth, the pāramitā of knowledge. At the ninth, the pāramitā of maturing sentient beings and fulfilling all. At the tenth, the pāramitā of fulfilling all vows. All these pāramitās are accomplished throughout all the bhūmis.

"Furthermore, good man! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva's practice of the pāramitā of generosity has ten dharmas. What are the ten? First, the root of faith. Second, the root of concentration. Third, great loving-kindness. Fourth, great compassion. Fifth, one's own joy. Sixth, the joy of others. Seventh, making all vows. Eighth, upholding all sentient beings. Ninth, the four means of gathering. Tenth, drawing near to the Dharma of the Buddhas. These are the ten dharmas that accomplish the pāramitā of generosity.

"Furthermore, good man! The practice of the pāramitā of moral discipline has ten dharmas. What are the ten? First, freedom from the eight difficulties. Second, accomplishment of the Buddha's merit. Third, freedom from the śrāvaka stage. Fourth, freedom from the pratyekabuddha stage. Fifth, purity of body. Sixth, purity of speech. Seventh, purity of mind. Eighth, adorning the mind. Ninth, cutting the conditions for the hells. Tenth, the practice of having one's prayers fulfilled. These ten dharmas accomplish the pāramitā of moral discipline.

"Furthermore, good man! The practice of the pāramitā of patience has ten dharmas. What are the ten? First, the power of patience. Second, joyful enthusiasm. Third, maturing sentient beings. Fourth, patience with the profound Dharma. Fifth, freedom from self and other. Sixth, cutting off anger. Seventh, not sparing one's body. Eighth, not sparing one's life. Ninth, abandoning delusion. Tenth, contemplating the equality of the dharma-body. These ten dharmas accomplish the pāramitā of patience.

"Furthermore, good man! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva's practice of the pāramitā of diligence has ten dharmas. What are the ten? First, the root of diligence. Second, the power of diligence. Third, correct effort. Fourth, correct mindfulness. Fifth, assisting sentient beings with one's body. Sixth, following beings with one's mind and speech. Seventh, non-retrogression in one's field of practice. Eighth, eliminating laziness. Ninth, subduing evil companions. Tenth, gathering all wisdom. These ten dharmas accomplish the pāramitā of diligence.

"Furthermore, good man! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva's practice of the pāramitā of meditation has ten dharmas. What are the ten? First, the root of concentration. Second, the power of concentration. Third, equal concentration. Fourth, sporting in the various dhyānas. Fifth, samādhi. Sixth, the fruits of samādhi. Seventh, not destroying all good dharmas. Eighth, eliminating the enemy of afflictions. Ninth, equanimity toward the correct Dharma. Tenth, the aggregate of concentration. These ten dharmas accomplish the pāramitā of meditation.

"Furthermore, good man! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva's practice of the pāramitā of wisdom has ten dharmas. What are the ten? First, the root of wisdom. Second, the power of wisdom. Third, correct views. Fourth, correct mindfulness. Fifth, skillful means regarding the aggregates. Sixth, discerning the elements. Seventh, the noble truths. Eighth, unobstructed knowledge. Ninth, turning wrong views. Tenth, the practice of the patience of non-arising dharmas. These ten dharmas accomplish the pāramitā of wisdom.

"Furthermore, good man! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva's practice of the pāramitā of skillful means has ten dharmas. What are the ten? First, sharing the practices of sentient beings. Second, upholding sentient beings. Third, great compassion. Fourth, tirelessness. Fifth, freedom from the practices of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Sixth, entering the pāramitās. Seventh, accurately discerning the measure of capacity. Eighth, supporting the good mind. Ninth, entering the stage of non-retrogression. Tenth, subduing all māras. These ten dharmas accomplish the pāramitā of skillful means.

"Furthermore, good man! What is the meaning of 'pāramitā'? The practice of surpassing progress and fulfillment is the meaning of pāramitā. The accomplishment of the foremost wisdom is the meaning of pāramitā. Neither abiding in the conditioned nor dwelling in the unconditioned is the meaning of pāramitā. Clearly perceiving the great affliction of birth and death is the meaning of pāramitā. Fully understanding now what was formerly not understood is the meaning of pāramitā. Broadly revealing the inexhaustible Dharma-treasury is the meaning of pāramitā. Skillfully removing obstructions is the meaning of pāramitā. Practicing generosity, moral discipline, patience, diligence, meditation, wisdom, and skillful means without hoping for recompense is the meaning of pāramitā. Understanding the realm of all sentient beings is the meaning of pāramitā. Fulfilling the patience of non-arising dharmas is the meaning of pāramitā. Accomplishing non-retrogression is the meaning of pāramitā. Purifying the buddha-lands is the meaning of pāramitā. Maturing sentient beings is the meaning of pāramitā. Dwelling at the seat of enlightenment and awakening to omniscience is the meaning of pāramitā. Subduing all māras is the meaning of pāramitā. Accomplishing the knowledge of all modes of all the Buddhas is the meaning of pāramitā. Destroying all heterodox views is the meaning of pāramitā. Accomplishing and perfecting the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, and eighteen exclusive dharmas is the meaning of pāramitā. Accomplishing the twelve-fold turning of the Dharma-wheel is the meaning of pāramitā.

"Thus, good man! The meaning of pāramitā is profoundly deep and immeasurable. I have merely explained it briefly for you."

At that time, in the assembly there was a deva prince named Siṃhavikrīḍitaprabha, who rose from his seat, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "Well done, World-Honored One! What you have spoken above is profoundly deep and rare. The merit of the Buddhas is like sweet dew that satisfies all."

The Buddha told the deva prince: "Excellent! Excellent! It is as you say. If there are bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, non-humans, and bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who hear this sūtra, they will certainly not retrogress from anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

"Why? Deva prince! If there are good men and good women who have planted the roots of virtue in past lives, they will be able to hear this sūtra. It is not persons of small merit who can hear it. If there are those who even briefly hear this sūtra, read, recite, and copy it, after they leave this body they will constantly see the Buddhas. Having seen the Buddhas, they will be able to turn the wondrous Dharma-wheel in the presence of the Buddhas, and will attain the Inexhaustible Dhāraṇī Seal, the Dhāraṇī for Understanding the Mind-Activities of All Sentient Beings, the Sunlight Universal Illumination Dhāraṇī, the Pure and Stainless Dhāraṇī, the Immovability of All Dharmas Dhāraṇī, the Indestructible Vajra Dhāraṇī, the Dhāraṇī for Expounding the Treasury of Profound Meaning, the Dhāraṇī for Well Understanding the Languages of All Sentient Beings, the Stainless-Space Sporting Inexhaustible Seal Dhāraṇī, and the Dhāraṇī for the Transformation-Bodies of All Buddhas. How much more so if, having heard it, they practice as taught!

"Good man! If there are bodhisattvas who attain these dharmas, they will be able to manifest the Buddha's body throughout the buddha-lands of the ten directions and teach the wondrous Dharma for sentient beings, yet without moving from the nature of dharmas, also without coming or going. Though they mature sentient beings, there are no sentient beings that can be found. Though they constantly teach the Dharma, there is nothing that is taught. Though they constantly manifest birth, there is no arising or perishing. Though they manifest coming and going, there are no marks of coming and going."

At that time, when the World-Honored One taught this Dharma, three thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of non-arising dharmas, and immeasurable sentient beings all gave rise to the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

Bodhisattva Cintāmṛtārtha, and devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, humans, non-humans, and the rest, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully practiced it.
