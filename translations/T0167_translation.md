# 太子慕魄經
## Sūtra of Prince Mūrdhagata

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0167

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the Anāthapiṇḍada Monastery.

At that time, the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "In a past life, I was a crown prince of the king of Vārāṇasī, named Mūrdhagata. From birth I was extraordinary: my features were handsome and upright, without equal in the world. I could recall my own past lives, the events of innumerable kalpas, all that I had undergone of good and evil, the retribution of sin and merit, long life and short, beauty and ugliness, death here and birth there, and whence I had come to be born. I knew and saw all of it. At the age of thirteen, I closed my mouth and did not speak.

"The king had only this one son. The people of the entire kingdom cherished and loved him, for he was to inherit the succession and take the throne. Yet because I recalled past lives, remembering ages of existence and destruction, fortune and calamity, I was silent and did not speak until the age of thirteen. I abandoned concern for my body and devoted my will to emptiness. Detached and undisturbed, I did not speak. Hunger and cold did not move me. Plain and unadorned, my mind was like dead ashes. Though I had ears and eyes, I did not concern myself with seeing or hearing. Though my wisdom reached far, I seemed to have no will. I did not fear disgrace, nor did I have hatred or love. As if blind, as if deaf, I did not speak of west or east. I appeared dim-witted and deaf, unlike other people.

"The royal father was anxious and distressed, deeply ashamed before the neighboring countries, fearing they would mock and look down on him. He summoned the brāhmaṇas of the country and asked them: 'Why is this child unable to speak?' The brāhmaṇas looked at each other and said: 'This child is an evil person. Though his face and features are exceptionally handsome, inwardly he harbors malice. Observing his silence, he intends to harm his father and mother, endanger the kingdom, and destroy the lineage. This will come to pass before long. He cannot be raised. Since he cannot speak, what benefit will he be to the king? The reason the king has not been able to beget another son is entirely because of this evil child's obstructive influence. This is why the great king can no longer produce a son. The king should abandon and discard him by burying him alive. Only then will the king's person be preserved, the kingdom protected, the lineage secured, and thereafter the king will be able to beget a noble son. Otherwise, the danger is great!'

"The king believed these foolish words and took them to be true. He was immediately filled with anxiety, unable to sit or stand in peace. He could not enjoy music, and fine food had no flavor. He then discussed it with his elders and ministers: 'What shall we do?' Some ministers said: 'Abandon him far away in the deep mountains where no one dwells.' Some ministers said: 'Cast him into deep water.' One minister said: 'We should follow the teacher's words: simply dig a deep pit, with a side entrance like a room. Provide him with provisions and assign five servants to attend him. Place him inside alive. Let fate take its course, and seal off the opening completely.'

"The king then followed this minister's advice. At dawn he dispatched servants, who went out to bury the prince. The prince was inwardly sorrowful, grieving over their foolish delusion, with boundless compassion. His mother was heartbroken, weeping: 'I was unfortunate to have borne a child without good fortune. My wretched fate has led to this calamity. It cuts through my very bowels.' Choking with sobs and tears, she mourned and lamented, unable to bear it. Having no choice, she reluctantly let him go and sent people to carry him out to be buried. All of the prince's clothing, necklaces, pearls, and treasures were sent with him.

"She then ordered that once outside, all his clothing, pearls, and treasures be removed and placed to one side while they dug the pit together. Before the pit was finished, Mūrdhagata was alone on the cart, deeply reflecting. He spoke in his mind: 'Now the king and all the people believe that I am truly deaf, foolish, mute, and unable to speak. The reason I did not speak was simply because I wished to renounce worldly ties, to keep my body safe and avoid trouble, to deliver my spirit and escape from suffering. But now, because of deceptive and fraudulent words, my life is about to be destroyed, and this will bring those people to ruin as well.' He then silently took the clothing, pearls, and treasures and departed. Those digging the pit did not notice Mūrdhagata taking the things and leaving.

"Then Mūrdhagata went to the water's edge, bathed himself clean, anointed his body with incense, put on all his clothing and necklaces, and went to the pit. He asked: 'What is this pit for?' The servants replied: 'The king has a son named Mūrdhagata. He is mute, deaf, and foolish. At the age of thirteen he cannot speak. The king asked the brāhmaṇas, and the brāhmaṇa teachers said: "He should be buried alive. Only then will there be peace, prosperity for the kingdom, glory for the lineage, and benefit for future descendants." For this reason we are digging this pit, to bury Mūrdhagata.' Mūrdhagata then said: 'I am Prince Mūrdhagata.' The people were immediately astonished and terrified, their hair standing on end. They ran to look at the cart, but Mūrdhagata was not there. They returned to the pit and looked carefully, listening to his speech. His voice was extraordinarily marvelous, his radiance like the moon, rarely heard in the world. His words moved all those around him: those walking stopped, those sitting rose. Birds in flight and beasts running all came and gathered, prostrating before the prince, listening to the prince's words.

"Mūrdhagata then said: 'Look at my hands and feet. Examine my form. How can all of you be so deluded by deceptive and fraudulent words, taking falsehood for truth, and abandoning me alive?' His intentions, once expressed, formed eloquent discourse. All around him were awed and respectful. All were alarmed and exposed, those above and those below in agreement, none not compliant. Their demeanor was greatly awed; they trembled and shook. They looked at one another, their faces pale. All said: 'The prince is truly divine, to such a degree!' They all came forward and bowed, knocking their heads and pleading for mercy: 'We beg forgiveness for our offense. Let us return together to the palace, to the royal father's presence.' Mūrdhagata said: 'I have already been cast out. It is not fitting for me to return. Go directly and report to the king so that he may know.' The servants then rushed to report this to the king. His mother, in her grief, sent someone to ask about the situation. The servants said: 'The prince is truly divine. When he opened his mouth to speak a single word, it truly alarmed and awed all people. All who heard were stirred, and those who came to see filled the roads.' The king was astonished, both joyful and sorrowful, deeply puzzled by it all.

"The king and his queen then prepared their carriage and went to welcome the prince. All the people of the country, great and small, rushed to see him, and onlookers filled the roads. All said: 'The prince appears as if one wishes to see the form of a deity.' Before the king arrived, Mūrdhagata thought to himself: 'I should study the path.' The moment this intention arose, the god Śakra transformed and created a garden with bathing pools, various fruit trees, and pleasures beyond compare. Mūrdhagata then took off the fine clothing, pearls, and treasures he was wearing and transformed himself into a practitioner, his robes dignified and composed.

"As the king was about to arrive, he saw Mūrdhagata sitting under a tree. When Mūrdhagata saw the king approaching, he rose to welcome him, and the king bowed to him. Mūrdhagata then said: 'Great king, please be seated.' When the king heard Mūrdhagata's voice and speech, his awesome spiritual radiance shook heaven and earth, absolutely without equal. The king was greatly overjoyed and urged Mūrdhagata: 'Let us return to the kingdom together. Take the throne and govern. I shall step aside and retire.'

"Mūrdhagata said: 'That cannot be! I dread and am weary of the toilsome sufferings of hell, the myriad kinds of anguish and poison. I once served as king of this very country, named Sunian. I governed with the right Dharma and practiced all that is good for twenty-five years. The rod and staff were not employed, weapons were not deployed, and the prisons held no one. I was generous and benevolent. My grace flowed and my virtue spread. I rescued the destitute and withheld nothing. Although I had such conduct, I still committed minor faults. In the end I fell into hell for more than sixty thousand years, where I was boiled, steamed, flayed, and torn apart. The pain was excruciating and unbearable. I sought to die but could not; I sought to live but could not. At that time, though my parents were still living, though they possessed wealth beyond measure, wealthy and noble, with pleasures without end, could they know that I was there, being tortured in hell with such severity? Could they come and take a share of my bodily suffering? No.

"'Why did I incur this punishment? In the past, when I was king of this great country, the petty kings of the vassal territories were all under my rule. The king was of a compassionate nature, his virtue supremely pure. But because the laws were not strict, the petty kings all looked down on him with contempt. Together they plotted: "This great king is gentle, soft, and weak. His authority cannot restrain us. His virtue is not sufficient to govern a great kingdom. We should attack him together and depose him." They then raised their armies and attacked the great kingdom. At that time, King Sunian met them in advance with rare and precious treasures, bestowing gifts upon them all. He further gave them lofty offices and generous stipends, soothed and comforted them with gentle persuasion, enticed and pacified them. They all ceased hostilities and each returned to their own country. But before long, they came attacking again, repeatedly, more than once. The ministers of the great kingdom were all angered and reported to the great king: "The petty vassal states are foolish and without righteousness. They have no regard for their offenses and repeatedly show contempt and disrespect. They have created rebellion and offended against their sovereign, causing the people to be troubled and defenses never to rest. They should be punished and attacked, to eliminate the threat." The king said: "As the parent of the people, I must devote myself to benevolent governance, extend my own forbearance to nurture all beings, and risk my life to save the multitude. They are still like infants. I pity their ignorance and would gradually guide them. I cannot bear to do them harm." The king cherished great compassion, universally pitying all living things, and had no thought of punishing or attacking. But the ministers could not endure being repeatedly humiliated by the vassal states. In their anger, disregarding the danger, they secretly raised armies and attacked the various countries, causing great slaughter among the people.

"'When the great king heard of this, he was deeply grieved. He shed tears for them and wore mourning clothes for all the people who had died in the various countries, as though mourning his own children, with boundless compassion. When the petty kings saw that the great king's compassionate heart cherished the people to such a degree, they all submitted and came to offer their allegiance. For those who came to submit, the great king arranged banquets. The great official banquets required the slaughtering of cattle, sheep, and the six kinds of livestock to prepare the various dishes. Before each slaughter, they would first report to the king. Though the king's heart was compassionate, the matter could not be helped, and he nodded his head in assent. Because of this, I incurred punishment and endured such toil. Whenever I recall it, my heart is filled with dread, my hair stands on end, and my body breaks out in cold sweat. The reason I did not speak was that I recalled the good and ill, safety and danger, success and ruin of past lives, and feared encountering them again. So I tied my tongue and did not speak for thirteen years, hoping that through silence I might be freed from flaw and cleansed of defilement, transcend the dust and toil, bid farewell to the worldly forever, and not again meet with calamity. Yet precisely when I wished to keep my mouth closed and not speak, I was about to be buried alive by the king. I feared that later the king would also incur this punishment, and once entering hell there would be no time of release. I did not wish the king to incur sin, and so I spoke again. I merely wish to practice the path, to guard the mind in non-action. I have no desire to be king. People dwelling in the world live in a haze as if in a dream. The joys of home and family last but a moment. The span of life is brief, yet worry and fear are prolonged. Pleasures are few and sufferings many, with myriad afflictions. Therefore the wise regard kingdom, wealth, treasures, and loved ones as burdens, and all desires as dust. Were I to become king, I would again become arrogant and greedy, seeking pleasure and causing the people anxiety, becoming the world's great affliction. Therefore I wish to remove worry, cast off the bonds of dust, go against the current to seek the source, and rescue those not yet saved. Life in this world is but a sojourn; there is nothing one can rely upon. As years advance and time passes, old age and death hasten. One must not delay, for every day one moves further from the path. I do not covet wealth and honor, do not prize gems and treasures. I cast away worldly glory, set my thoughts on the great path, soar high and depart far, and save myself from the world.'

"The royal father said: 'How can this be? You are a wise one. You should forgive my shortcomings. You cannot simply leave me like this.' The king's heart was both sorrowful and glad, and he deeply regretted what he had done.

"The prince then said: 'Where has it been heard that father and son, having been born, cast each other off? The bond of love has been broken, flesh and bone have been severed. Such conduct is a grave fault that cannot be condoned. To have come in painful hardship to welcome me only adds to the toil and trouble.' When the father heard his son's words and saw that his resolve was firm, he was at a loss and filled with shame, having no words to reply.

"The king said: 'As you say, when in a past life you were king and practiced all that is good, with only the slightest fault, not something you intended or remembered, you still received such punishment and endured such toil. Now I govern the kingdom without following the right Dharma. Not only do I lack any merit, but I pursue what is wrong and reject what is right, arrogant and self-indulgent, my conduct entirely perilous. What punishment must I then face?' He then released the prince and permitted him to go and study the path. The prince thereupon abandoned the kingdom and renounced kingship, with no attachment to people or things. With single-minded concentration, he devoted himself to the path and cultivated virtue. His accumulated merit reached fulfillment, and he ultimately attained Buddhahood. Having attained the path, the Buddha then liberated the devas and people of the ten directions, beyond reckoning, through innumerable kalpas without considering it toil. The bodhisattva's past hardships were such as this."

The Buddha said: "The prince of that time is my present self. The royal father is my present father Śuddhodana. The mother is Māyā. The brāhmaṇa physiognomist of that time is now Devadatta. The five servants of that time are now Ājñāta-Kauṇḍinya and the five. All who wish to practice the path should follow the Buddha's teaching and not violate the sūtras and precepts. Though the path involves suffering, it is better than being in the three evil paths and the eight difficult conditions. Those who violate the precepts and transgress the prohibitions will afterward fall into the evil paths. If they escape and become human, they will be born in poverty and hardship, or become servants and slaves, unable to be free. Uphold the precepts and practice good: the Three Honored Ones can be attained."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The assembly of bhikṣus, and all the devas and people, were without exception joyful and bowed to the Buddha.
