# 佛說檀特羅麻油述經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Dandatara Sesame-Oil Recitation

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1391

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin, translated by Tan Wulan (曇無蘭)

---

The Buddha was residing at Mount Indrasāla in Magadha. At that time the Buddha's son Rāhula was accompanying the Buddha on the mountain. While lying down at night, Rāhula was disturbed by spirits and woke in fright. The following morning he went to where the Buddha was, paid homage to the Buddha, and sat down to one side beneath a tree. Rāhula propped his cheek with his hand, bowed his head, and sat unhappy and silent, saying nothing. The Buddha then asked Rāhula: "Why do you bow your head as though frightened?"

Rāhula said: "Last night while I was lying down, I was disturbed by spirits."

The Buddha said to Rāhula: "In this world, sometimes living humans disturb people; sometimes mountain spirits disturb people; sometimes spirits dwelling along the roadside ditches disturb people; sometimes spirits of those who died peacefully disturb people. They come wishing to test people, to frighten them regarding the path of the teaching, wishing to know whether their minds are firm or soft."

The Buddha said to Rāhula: "Take this Buddha's spirit-repelling incantation. Should spirits ever come to disturb you hereafter, hold their names in mind and recite this with a mind of loving-kindness."

[Dhāraṇī:] *a bo jie zheng zheng jie wu duo sa xi chi bi chi zhan bo zhan bo jia luo zhun wei ling wu yin lun wu zhi lun wu han sha wu yin deng luo song lin luo he lin luo bo ye yue luo tan te luo*

The Buddha said: "This is the Dandatara Sūtra—the Buddha has composed it as a sūtra of binding beneficence especially for all his disciples." The Buddha told all his disciples: "Those who are in urgent need should recite it. Should spirits come to disturb a person, one should hold a mind of loving-kindness, compassion, and purity, and then turn inward to observe and contemplate the five viscera. When the Buddha preaches this sūtra, even if the sun and moon were to fall to the earth, the Buddha's words would still not be otherwise. Now the Buddha preaches this Dandatara Sūtra: living humans who wish to come and disturb people shall not be able to disturb them; mountain spirits shall also not be able to disturb people; spirits of the roadside ditches shall also not be able to disturb people; spirits of those who died an untimely death shall also not be able to disturb people; spirits of those who died peacefully shall also not be able to disturb people. Hearing these words: fire shall not burn; if one eats poisoned food, the poison shall not act; a sword meant to kill shall not strike; if one falls into deep water, one shall not drown or come to harm. Whether the four kinds of people travel through open and deserted places, or through districts and towns or great kingdoms, whether at assemblies or great gatherings, among elders, among companions, while walking, or while sitting and lying down—if one encounters a practitioner of sorcery, recite this sūtra toward them, and the sorcery shall not take effect."

The Buddha then explained this to Rāhula, directing Rāhula to share it with all bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, and all laypeople, causing them all to chant and memorize it.

---

**Translator's notes:**

- *Mount Indrasāla*: 因沙奪山 is a phonetic rendering of uncertain Sanskrit original; reconstructed here as Indrasāla (Skt. *Indraśaila*), a mountain associated with Magadha in the Pali sources.
- *The dhāraṇī* is rendered in standard Mandarin romanization (pinyin without tones), as the Eastern Jin phonetic values used for the original Sanskrit/Prākrit cannot be recovered with certainty. The final term *tan te luo* (檀特羅) corresponds to the dhāraṇī name Dandatara, which also appears in the sūtra title.
- *The four kinds of people* (群有四子人): the four-fold assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, listed explicitly at the sūtra's close.
- *Five viscera* (五藏): the heart, liver, spleen, lungs, and kidneys; contemplation of the internal organs is a recognized Buddhist meditation practice.
- *Spirits of untimely death* (腥死鬼神): literally "foul-smelling death spirits," referring to those who died violently or inauspiciously, as distinguished from 善死鬼神, spirits of those who died peacefully.
