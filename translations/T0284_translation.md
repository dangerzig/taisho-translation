# 佛說菩薩十住經
## Sūtra of the Ten Abodes of the Bodhisattva Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0284

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Gītamitra (祇多蜜)

---

When the Buddha had finished teaching the twelve-period bodhisattva precepts, Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "By what merit does a bodhisattva attain these ten abodes? May the God among Gods explain them in detail."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! Mañjuśrī, bodhisattva-mahāsattva! Your concern and compassion are great, bringing peace and comfort to many. I shall fully explain the essentials for you. Listen carefully and receive them well."

Mañjuśrī said: "I accept the teaching."

The Buddha said: "There are ten abodes of bodhisattva merit, each with degrees of higher and lower, proceeding in proper sequence."

Mañjuśrī said: "What are these ten?"

"The first abode is the Dharma-abode of the Pramuditā Bodhisattva."

The Buddha said: "At the outset, one sees a Buddha of matchless beauty. Gazing at his countenance, one never tires; there is none who equals him. His dignity is unsurpassed; his instruction is beyond compare. Seeing the Buddha's awesome deportment and manner, one gradually enters into the path of the Buddha. Progressively one guides and leads others, teaching each according to their inclinations and liberating them. Seeing those who suffer, one has compassion for them all. Gradually one comes to understand the Buddha's words and to place one's faith in them. Having newly given rise to the aspiration to study the Buddha's path, one wishes to fully comprehend the Buddha's ten difficult attainments and to reach them all.

"What are the ten difficult attainments? They are the Buddha's ten powers. First, one should make offerings to the Buddhas. Second, one should teach others according to their dispositions. Third, wherever one is born, one is always in a position of honor. Fourth, there is none above or below in heaven and earth who can equal one. Fifth, one completely attains the Buddha's wisdom. Sixth, in every life wherever one is born, one sees immeasurable Buddhas. Seventh, one fully attains the Buddha's sūtras. Eighth, one completely crosses over all births and deaths. Ninth, one's own liberation is not far off. Tenth, one liberates all beings of the ten directions.

"The second abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Adhimukti Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "There are ten intentions directed toward the beings of the ten directions. What are these ten intentions? First, a mind that thinks of the good of all in the world. Second, a pure mind. Third, a mind that brings peace and comfort to all. Fourth, a gentle and supple mind. Fifth, equal love for all. Sixth, a mind that thinks only of giving and generosity. Seventh, a mind of equal protection for all. Eighth, a mind that regards all beings as no different from one's own body. Ninth, a mind that regards all beings of the ten directions as one's teacher. Tenth, a mind that regards all beings of the ten directions as the Buddha.

"The Adhimukti Bodhisattva should study the sūtras extensively. Having studied extensively, one should dwell in solitude. Having dwelt in solitude, one should always attend upon and serve a good teacher, and should be obedient at the good teacher's side. One should act according to the proper time; when the proper time comes, one should act with courage. Having acted with courage, one should study the wisdom of others. With the Dharma one has received, one should uphold it all and not forget it. Having not forgotten, one should dwell in a place of peace and comfort. Why? Because it benefits the beings of the ten directions.

"The third abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Yogācāra Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "One enters into all dharmas by means of ten things. What are these ten? First, understanding that all that exists is impermanent. Second, understanding that all that exists involves suffering. Third, understanding that all that exists is empty. Fourth, understanding that all that exists is not-self. Fifth, understanding that all that exists has no abiding place. Sixth, understanding that all that exists yields no ultimate benefit. Seventh, understanding that all that exists has no resting place. Eighth, understanding that all that exists has no fixed location. Ninth, understanding that all that exists is without attachment. Tenth, understanding that all is without inherent existence. All dharmas enter into one dharma; one dharma enters into all dharmas. This is the teaching of the Yogācāra Bodhisattva.

"The fourth abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Janmaja Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "One always aspires to be born in the presence of a Buddha. There are ten things. First, one does not regress. Second, one deeply contemplates the Buddha. Third, one deeply contemplates the Dharma. Fourth, one mindfully regards the saṅgha of bhikṣus and views the beings of the ten directions with a compassionate mind. Fifth, one contemplates that all things are without inherent existence. Sixth, one contemplates that all buddha-lands of the ten directions are empty. Seventh, one contemplates that all deeds of past lives are without inherent existence. Eighth, one contemplates that all phenomena are like illusions and entirely empty. Ninth, one contemplates that all suffering is without inherent existence. Tenth, one contemplates that nirvāṇa is empty and likewise without inherent existence. Because of these things, one is born within the Buddha's Dharma. This is the teaching of the Janmaja Bodhisattva.

"The fifth abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Pūrṇasaṃpad Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "The merit one cultivates universally liberates the beings of the ten directions. There are ten things. First, universally protecting all beings of the ten directions. Second, universally thinking of the good of all beings. Third, universally wishing all beings peace and comfort. Fourth, universally loving all beings. Fifth, universally having compassion for all beings. Sixth, universally wishing that all beings not create evil. Seventh, universally guiding all beings onto the bodhisattva path. Eighth, universally purifying all beings. Ninth, universally liberating all beings. Tenth, universally causing all beings to enter nirvāṇa. This is the teaching of the Pūrṇasaṃpad Bodhisattva.

"The sixth abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Abhimukhi Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "There are ten dharmas of profound compassion. First, if someone speaks well or ill of the Buddha, one's mind remains unchanged. Second, whether the Dharma is praised or criticized, one's mind remains unchanged. Third, whether bodhisattvas are praised or criticized, one's mind remains unchanged. Fourth, whether those seeking the bodhisattva path guide each other well or ill, one's mind remains unchanged. Fifth, whether someone says the beings of the ten directions are many or few, one's mind remains unchanged. Sixth, seeing the beings of the ten directions guide each other well or ill, one's mind remains unchanged. Seventh, whether someone says the beings of the ten directions are easy or difficult to liberate, one's mind remains unchanged. Eighth, whether someone says the Dharma is much or little, one's mind remains unchanged. Ninth, whether someone says the Dharma can be destroyed or cannot be destroyed, one's mind remains unchanged. Tenth, whether there is Dharma or no Dharma, one's mind remains unchanged. This is the teaching of the Abhimukhi Bodhisattva.

"The seventh abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Avaivartika Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "The bodhisattva has ten things by which one stands firm and does not waver. First, whether someone says there are Buddhas or there are no Buddhas, one's mind does not waver or turn back. Second, whether there is Dharma or no Dharma, one does not waver. Third, whether there are bodhisattvas or no bodhisattvas, one does not waver. Fourth, whether there are those who seek the bodhisattva path or there are not, one does not waver. Fifth, whether holding to this Dharma one attains it or does not attain it, one does not waver. Sixth, whether there are past Buddhas or no past Buddhas, one does not waver. Seventh, whether there are future Buddhas or no future Buddhas, one does not waver. Eighth, whether there are present Buddhas or no present Buddhas, one does not waver. Ninth, whether the Buddha's wisdom is exhaustible or inexhaustible, one does not waver. Tenth, whether the affairs of past, future, and present are of many kinds or one kind, one does not waver. This is the teaching of the Avaivartika Bodhisattva.

"The eighth abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Kumārabhūta Youth Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "The bodhisattva abides in ten things. First, the deeds of body, the words of speech, and the thoughts of mind are all pure. Second, there is none who can find fault with them. Third, in a single thought one can be reborn wherever one wishes. Fourth, one knows who among the beings of the ten directions has a compassionate mind. Fifth, one knows all that is believed and practiced in the ten directions. Sixth, one knows all the various kinds of beings in the ten directions. Seventh, one knows all the deeds of beings in the ten directions. Eighth, one knows the formation and destruction of all buddha-lands in the ten directions. Ninth, one attains supernatural powers and can fly to wherever one wishes. Tenth, all dharmas are entirely pure. This is the teaching of the Kumārabhūta Youth Bodhisattva.

"The ninth abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Yauvarājya Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "It is attained by means of ten things. First, one knows all the births and origins of beings in the ten directions. Second, one knows all the bonds of affection and attachment of beings in the ten directions. Third, one knows the thoughts, beginnings and ends, and origins of all beings in the ten directions. Fourth, one knows all the past-life deeds and future destinations of beings in the ten directions. Fifth, one knows all the various kinds of dharmas of beings in the ten directions. Sixth, one knows all the various kinds of thoughts and transformations of beings in the ten directions. Seventh, one knows the good and evil, formation and destruction of all buddha-lands. Eighth, one knows all the affairs of immeasurable past, future, and present worlds. Ninth, one knows the equality and inequality of all beings in the ten directions. Tenth, one knows how to teach all beings of the ten directions, proclaiming the Dharma of emptiness. This is the teaching of the Yauvarājya Bodhisattva.

"The tenth abode: what is the Dharma-abode of the Abhiṣeka Bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "The bodhisattva enters into the ten wisdoms and can discern and know them. There are ten things. First, for what reason should one cause the buddha-lands of all the ten directions to tremble? Second, one should illuminate the affairs of immeasurable buddha-lands. Third, every day I should establish and appoint bodhisattvas in immeasurable buddha-lands. Fourth, every day I should liberate the sentient beings of immeasurable buddha-lands. Fifth, I should bring peace and comfort to the sentient beings of immeasurable buddha-lands. Sixth, may all beings of the ten directions hear my voice, rejoice, and be liberated. Seventh, I should cause all the peoples of the ten directions to attain the path of the Buddha, to leave household life and become śramaṇas. Eighth, I should know the good and evil thoughts of all beings of the ten directions. Ninth, I should place all beings of the ten directions within the path of the Buddha and cause them all to give rise to the bodhisattva aspiration. Tenth, I should liberate all beings of the ten directions.

"The Yauvarājya Bodhisattva is utterly unable to know or reach the bodily deeds, verbal words, mental thoughts, and actions of the Abhiṣeka Bodhisattva; nor can it know or reach the Abhiṣeka Bodhisattva's supernatural powers and flight; nor can it know the affairs of the Abhiṣeka Bodhisattva's past, future, or present. This is the teaching of the Abhiṣeka Bodhisattva."

When the Buddha taught this, the Bodhisattva Harmonious Wheel and seventy-two thousand others all leaped up with great joy, and each manifested radiance that illuminated one another. Each rose, straightened their robes, came forward, bowed their heads to the ground in homage to the Buddha, and departed.
