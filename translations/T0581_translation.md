# 佛說八師經
## Sūtra of the Eight Teachers, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0581

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada.

At that time there was a brahmin named Yajña who came to the Buddha's dwelling.

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "A brahmin of a different school has now come and is outside. He wishes to consult you about his doubts."

The Honored One said: "Admit him."

The brahmin entered, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet.

The Honored One said: "Be seated."

The brahmin took his seat, and after a moment sat back and said: "I have heard that the Buddha's Way is profoundly deep in meaning, vast and boundless as the ocean, that there is nothing it does not accomplish, nothing it does not liberate. Lofty and majestic, it is like the moon among the stars. Its divine wisdom is marvelously penetrating: the king among all sages, unmatched by the devas, unheard of by the common people. I beg you to open the darkness of my blindness and release my ignorance. What teacher did you serve to attain such honor?"

The Honored One sighed and said: "Excellent, this question! It opens up the great practice. The teachers of my past lives are too numerous to name. In my present life, I have attained the Way through my own naturally radiant spiritual power, without any teacher. Yet I have eight teachers, from whom I obtained understanding through perceiving the punishments they represent.

"First is killing. Those who kill living beings, or are punished by the king's law and put to death, may have their entire clan extinguished. After death they enter Naraka, where they are burned, boiled, beaten, and flayed, experiencing every kind of torment, unable to obtain death. When their punishment is finished, they emerge to become hungry ghosts, or animals that are slaughtered, flayed, and butchered. Their spirits transmigrate through one death after another, tormenting one another. I saw that the punishment of killers is thus, and I did not dare to kill again. This is my first teacher."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Killers have merciless hearts;
> The strong and weak harm and wound each other.
> Killing in one life, they must pass through the next,
> Accumulating enmity across many kalpas.
> They receive punishment and die short-lived,
> In terror suffering violent calamity.
> Because I feared this,
> With a compassionate heart I conquered Māra's palace."

The Buddha said: "Second is stealing, seizing others' property by force. Some are struck with swords and rods by the owner and destroyed on the spot. Some are arrested by the king's law, imprisoned, interrogated under torture, flogged with the five punishments, and executed in the public square, their clan reduced to ashes. After death they enter Naraka, where they hold fire in their hands and molten copper is poured into their mouths, unable to obtain death. When their punishment is finished, they emerge to become hungry ghosts. When they wish to drink water, the water turns to pus. When they wish to eat food, the food turns to charcoal. Their bodies constantly bear heavy burdens, and every affliction follows them. Or they become animals, dying only to face the blade again, their flesh serving others, repaying their former debts. I saw that the punishment of thieves is thus, and I did not dare to steal again. This is my second teacher."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Thieves take what is not given,
> Robbing and seizing others' treasures.
> Whether the loss is great or small,
> The victims harbor anger, resentment, and sorrow.
> After death they receive an animal's body,
> Repaying their former debts.
> Because I feared this,
> I abandoned home to practice the Way."

The Buddha said: "Third is sexual misconduct, violating others' wives and daughters. Some are discovered by the husband or those nearby, and disaster strikes at once with swords and rods. Some are arrested by the king's law, imprisoned, subjected to cruel punishment, and executed in the public square. After death they enter Naraka, where they are laid upon an iron bed or made to embrace a copper pillar while the jailer-ghosts set fire to burn their bodies. When the punishment of Naraka is complete, they become animals. If they are again born as humans, their households are licentious and disordered. They go against the Buddha and are far from the Dharma, they do not associate with the worthy, they are always filled with fear, often in danger and seldom at peace. Having seen this, I did not dare engage in sexual misconduct. This is my third teacher."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Sexual misconduct is an impure practice,
> Leading one astray from the right path.
> Spirit and soul are scattered,
> Life is harmed and cut short.
> One receives punishment in dull stupor,
> And after death falls to evil paths.
> Because I feared this,
> I abandoned home to delight in the forest groves."

The Buddha said: "Fourth is harsh speech, forked tongue, false speech, and frivolous speech. Slandering the innocent, defaming the Three Treasures: the tongue brings beatings with rods and also the destruction of one's clan. After death one enters Naraka, where the ghosts of the prison pull out one's tongue and plow it with an ox. Molten copper is poured into one's mouth, and one cannot obtain death. When the punishment is finished, one emerges to become an animal, always eating thorns and brambles. If later born as a human, one's words are not trusted, one's mouth constantly stinks, one frequently encounters slander and abusive speech, one always has bad dreams when sleeping, and though one has a mouth, one cannot taste the supreme flavor of the Buddha's sūtras. Having seen this, I did not dare speak harshly. This is my fourth teacher."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Deceit has four transgressions;
> Flattery and slander wound the good and upright.
> One receives punishment as a fool, deaf and blind,
> Stammering, with a stinking mouth.
> Mad and unable to speak,
> After death one enters the tongue-pulling prison.
> I cultivated the four purities of speech
> And attained the eight kinds of voice."

The Buddha said: "Fifth is indulging in alcohol. Alcohol is a poisonous vapor that produces all manner of evils. The king's way is ruined, benevolence and grace are extinguished. Ministers become disrespectful to superiors, loyalty and reverence decay. Fathers lose propriety, mothers lose compassion. Children become violent and rebellious, filial piety is defeated. Husbands lose trustworthiness, wives become extravagant and licentious. The nine degrees of kinship fall into strife, family property is squandered. The country is endangered, the body is endangered: none of these do not arise from alcohol. The ways alcohol disrupts the path are thirty-six. Having seen this, I renounced alcohol and do not drink. This is my fifth teacher."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The drunkard is unfilial;
> Enmity and disaster arise from within.
> It bewilders the pure and lofty,
> And ruins virtue and propriety.
> Therefore I do not drink alcohol;
> With a compassionate heart I aid all beings.
> With pure wisdom I traverse the eight difficulties
> And attain the accomplishment of the Way of Awakening."

The Buddha said: "Sixth is old age. The suffering of old age is this: the hair turns white, the teeth fall out, the eyes grow dim, the ears lose their sharpness. Vigor departs and decline arrives. The skin sags and the face wrinkles. Every joint aches with pain. Walking is extremely difficult. Sitting down and rising up, one groans and sighs. Worry, sorrow, vexation, and suffering prevail. The spirit of consciousness gradually fades, and what was just done is immediately forgotten. One's remaining days grow fewer. To speak of it brings tears. Having seen that impermanence brings such disaster and change, I therefore practiced and sought the Way, not wishing to experience it again. This is my sixth teacher."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "I reflect that the world is impermanent;
> Those born as humans must grow old.
> Vigor departs, daily growing more frail,
> The body withers and the hair turns white.
> From anxiety and toil a hundred ailments arise;
> Sitting and rising, one is grieved with pain.
> Because I feared this,
> I abandoned home to practice the Way."

The Buddha said: "Seventh is illness. The flesh wastes away until the bones stand bare. Every joint is in pain, as though beaten with rods. The four great elements advance and retreat, and hands and feet are useless. Strength and vitality are utterly exhausted. Sitting and lying require another's help. The mouth is parched, the lips cracked, the sinews torn, the nose split. The eyes cannot see forms, the ears cannot hear sounds. Impurities flow from the body, and one lies in them. The heart is filled with anguish, and every word is spoken in grief. Now I observe the people of the world who in the prime of youth are strong and radiant in their splendor, yet when their blessings are exhausted and their offenses ripen, impermanence brings a hundred changes. Having observed this affliction, I therefore practiced and sought the Way, not wishing to experience it again. This is my seventh teacher."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "I reflect that when a person declines with age,
> A hundred ailments all arise at once.
> Water recedes and fire is extinguished;
> The wind like a blade dismembers the form.
> Bones separate and sinews and veins are severed;
> The great life-force must come to an end.
> Because I feared this,
> I sought the Way, wishing never to be born again."

The Buddha said: "Eighth is death. The four hundred and four ailments all arise at once. The four great elements are about to scatter. The spirit is uneasy. The wind departs and breathing ceases. The fire is extinguished and the body turns cold. Wind goes first and fire follows, and then the soul departs. The body lies stiff, knowing nothing more. Within days the flesh decays and the blood flows. The body swells, rots, and stinks; nothing of it is worth taking. Worms within the body consume the flesh. The sinews and veins rot away, and the bones and joints fall apart. The skull lies in one place; the spine, ribs, shoulders, arms, thighs, shins, feet, and fingers each lie scattered in different places. Flying birds and running beasts compete to eat them. Devas, nāgas, ghosts and spirits, emperors and kings, common people, rich and poor, noble and lowly: none escape this affliction. Having seen this transformation, I therefore practiced and sought the Way, not wishing to experience it again. This is my eighth teacher."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "I reflect on old age, illness, and death,
> The three great afflictions of the three realms.
> When blessings are exhausted and life ends,
> The breath is severed in the Yellow Springs.
> The body rots and returns to earth;
> The soul follows causes and conditions.
> Because I feared this,
> I practiced the Way and ascended to nirvāṇa."

At this the brahmin, hearing the Buddha's teaching, had his mind opened and his understanding freed. He immediately attained the fruit of stream-entry. He came forward, received the five precepts, and became a pure-faith layman: not killing, not stealing, not engaging in sexual misconduct, not deceiving, and being filial. He also vowed not to drink alcohol, and departed with joy.
