# 月明菩薩經
## Sūtra of Bodhisattva Candraprabha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0169

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas.

At that time, in Rājagṛha there was a great and wealthy family named Śreṣṭhin. Śreṣṭhin had a son named Candradharma, who practiced pure and virtuous conduct. The Buddha likened him to a young man of purity, and so called him Candraprabha. The young man went to where the Buddha was, came forward and bowed to the Buddha, then sat to one side. The Buddha told the young man Candraprabha: "Whether a bodhisattva mahāsattva is a householder or a bhikṣu, he should practice the giving of Dharma and the giving of food. He should always, with a good heart, receive and respond to the minds of all people. In upholding the practice of offering food, there are four vows he should make. He should generate the aspiration to seek Buddhahood and swiftly attain the supreme, perfect, true path. What are the four vows? The first vow: may all people swiftly attain skillful means and expedient methods. The second vow: may I, life after life, meet with good spiritual friends. The third vow: may I share my wealth and treasures with all people. The fourth vow: may I practice these two things, the giving of Dharma and the giving of food, and always delight in obtaining this practice. These are the four vows.

"Furthermore, young man Candraprabha! A bodhisattva mahāsattva, whether at home or having gone forth, should always delight in the gift of sūtras and the Dharma. He should always, with skillful means, receive and respond to people, with a mind free from greed, standing firmly within the Dharma, upholding the precepts in accordance with the Dharma. There is yet another thing, young man Candraprabha! If a bhikṣu is gravely ill, destitute, and suffering, one should be concerned to bring him ease and provide him with medicine. And not only medicine: one should not begrudge even one's own flesh, but should make offerings to ensure his recovery.

"There is yet another thing, young man Candraprabha! A bodhisattva mahāsattva, when giving, should never harbor doubt. Why is this? In the past, through incalculable asaṃkhyeya kalpas upon asaṃkhyeya kalpas, immeasurable, innumerable, and exceedingly vast, at that time there was a Buddha in the world named Satyasaṃkalpa-anuttararāja Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Perfectly Enlightened One. He manifested his body in the world, following what beings delighted in, practicing and training them as the supreme honored one.

"There is yet another thing, young man Candraprabha! At that time, on the day when the Satyasaṃkalpa-anuttararāja Tathāgata, the Perfectly Enlightened One, manifested the attainment of perfect enlightenment, he transformed and created innumerable bodies, guiding beings according to their delight. Innumerable people attained the path of a stream-enterer, innumerable people attained the path of a once-returner, innumerable people attained the path of a non-returner, innumerable people attained the path of an arhat, innumerable people attained the path of a pratyekabuddha, innumerable people were born in the Cāturmahārājika heaven, innumerable people were born in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, innumerable people were born in the Yāma heaven, innumerable people were born in the Tuṣita heaven, innumerable people were born in the Nirmāṇarati heaven, innumerable people were born in the Paranirmita-vaśavartin heaven, innumerable people were born in the Brahma heaven, and innumerable people received the prediction of having generated the aspiration for supreme, perfect, true enlightenment. Every one of them was to pass through to the great path of nirvāṇa.

"At that time, the Satyasaṃkalpa-anuttararāja Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Perfectly Enlightened One, having thus taught and guided in a single day, and having completed all those who were to be liberated, entered parinirvāṇa. After his parinirvāṇa, his Dharma remained for two billion years. As this period neared its end, during the final fifty years, the bhikṣus mostly no longer believed in the profound sūtras, but delighted in shallow matters. The sūtras and the Dharma gradually diminished and were not yet completely gone.

"At that time, in Jambudvīpa there was a king named Jñānabala, who always cultivated the Buddha's three practices. What are the three? First, always protecting the Buddha's profound Dharma. Second, receiving and practicing the Buddha's profound Dharma. Third, having true faith in the Buddha's profound Dharma. At that time, there was in the world a bhikṣu named Puṇḍarīka, who always practiced three things. What are the three? First, always upholding this samādhi. Second, always protecting this samādhi. Third, always reciting this samādhi. He additionally practiced eight things: always practicing the mind of loving-kindness, always practicing the mind of compassion, always practicing the mind of pity, always practicing the mind of protection, always practicing the mind of wisdom, always practicing the mind that responds to questions, always practicing the mind of joyful exultation, always practicing the foremost mind. By these practices he completely overcame the ninety-six kinds of non-Buddhist paths and fully comprehended every profound Dharma without any doubt. The bhikṣu of true sincerity and King Jñānabala were closely connected. He was revered by the king, and loved and honored by the people of the country as well. When the king wished to see this bhikṣu, he never had enough. When he listened to this bhikṣu teach the sūtras and the Dharma, he never had enough. When he wished to bow to this bhikṣu, he never had enough.

"At that time, this bhikṣu developed a great, evil sore on his thigh. The medicines of the country could not heal it. The king was deeply grieved and shed tears. At that time, the twenty thousand court ladies all likewise mourned together for this bhikṣu. One night, the king had a dream in which a celestial being came and said to the king: 'If you wish to heal the illness of the bhikṣu of true sincerity, you must obtain the blood and flesh of a living person for him to eat. Then he will be healed.' The king awoke, startled and unhappy, thinking: 'The bhikṣu's illness is severe, requiring such medicine. It is an extremely difficult thing to obtain.' He then inquired of his ministers: 'Where can we obtain living human blood and flesh?'

"At that time, the king's first crown prince, named Jñānaratha, addressed the king: 'Great king, do not grieve! Do not worry! Do not be sorrowful! Human blood and flesh are the most base and lowly things. What people of the world value is the path, which is not violated by this.' The king answered the prince: 'Excellent! Excellent!' The prince was silent. He withdrew into his purification chamber, took a knife, and cut his own thigh. He took the flesh and blood and sent them to the bhikṣu. When the bhikṣu consumed them, his sore was immediately healed and his body was at ease. When the king heard that the bhikṣu was healed, he was overjoyed, his delight beyond measure. His mind was on the bhikṣu, and he did not think of the prince's pain. Holding this joy, each had utmost sincerity. The prince also recovered, and the king then bestowed the wealth and treasures of the kingdom upon the prince. The prince replied to the king with this verse:

> "Having given blood and flesh for the ease of the offering,
> Having cut blood and flesh to give to another,
> Immediate healing, with nothing more to fear:
> This is the offering praised by the Buddha.
> The virtue among virtues, most peaceful and secure.
> He who in the future shall become a Buddha
> Will cut off greed and lust, remove anger and hatred,
> And all people will be healed."

The Buddha told the young man Candraprabha: "The bhikṣu of true sincerity of that time is now the Buddha Dīpaṃkara. The King Jñānabala of that time is now the Bodhisattva Maitreya. The Prince Jñānaratha of that time is my present self. The Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Perfectly Enlightened One, benefits the world beyond reckoning. He accumulated merit, wishing to liberate all beings. The conduct of a bodhisattva mahāsattva is entirely like this. If a good man or good woman wishes to be liberated from the sufferings of the world, they should generate the aspiration for supreme, perfect, true enlightenment, and recite and study this samādhi."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, all were joyful, bowed, and departed.
