# 根本說一切有部毘奈耶羯恥那衣事
## Kaṭhina Robe Matters from the Vinaya of the Mūlasarvāstivāda School

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1449

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨).

---

At that time, the Buddha was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park. At that time, many bhikṣus had completed their three-month rainy-season retreat in the city of Svayam-āgatā. Each carrying his robes and bowl, they made their way to the World-Honored One. Along the road they encountered mud and rain, were oppressed by the heat, wild grasses cut their bodies, and they were covered in sweat. Traveling gradually, they arrived at the city of Śrāvastī. The bhikṣus set down their robes and bowls, washed their feet, and went to the World-Honored One. They prostrated at his feet and sat to one side. It was the World-Honored One's constant practice, upon seeing visiting bhikṣus arrive, to exchange mutual greetings: "From what place did you complete your retreat before coming here?" They replied: "World-Honored One, we completed the three-month retreat in the city of Svayam-āgatā and have come here." He asked: "While you were in retreat there during the three months, did you dwell in comfort? Was it difficult to obtain food and drink by alms?" They answered: "World-Honored One, we dwelt in comfort during the three months, and food was not difficult to obtain. However, carrying our robes and bowls from there, along the roads we encountered mud and rain, extreme hardship, and our bodies became weary and exhausted as we gradually made our way here." Having heard this, the World-Honored One reflected thus: "My disciples, having completed the rainy-season retreat, travel among people carrying their robes and bowls. Along the roads they encounter mud and rain, endure extreme hardship, and their bodies become weary and exhausted. I should now enable the bhikṣus to dwell in comfort. And so that the merit of the donors may increase, I shall permit the bhikṣus to spread the kaṭhina robe. When this robe is spread, there are five superior benefits: first, no offense for keeping cloth beyond ten days; second, no offense for keeping cloth beyond one month; third, no offense for being separated from one's robe overnight; fourth, one may travel among people wearing only the upper and lower two robes; fifth, one may keep as many extra robes as one wishes. Furthermore, there are five kinds of advantage: first, one may eat at a separate assembly; second, one may eat repeatedly; third, one may go to a lay household and accept food without being invited; fourth, one may freely seek as many robes as one wishes; fifth, from the fifteenth of the eighth month to the fifteenth of the first month, a period of five months, all goods received are profits of the kaṭhina robe. By thus opening these permissions, my disciples may dwell in comfort." He then addressed the bhikṣus: "In order that you may dwell in comfort, and that the merit of the donors may increase, the retreat assembly shall spread the kaṭhina robe and obtain the many benefits as described in the ten kinds above." As the World-Honored One said to spread the kaṭhina robe, the bhikṣus did not know how to spread the robe. The Buddha said: "When the three-month retreat assembly obtains robing materials, they should use them to make the robe. First, make a verbal announcement informing the assembly: 'The assembly should know: the summer-retreat bhikṣu sangha has obtained this cloth. If the assembly approves, let this material be made into a kaṭhina robe for the sangha.' The next day, sound the ghaṇṭā, assemble the sangha, and when the announcement has been circulated, let one bhikṣu make the following motion:

"'Let the venerable sangha hear! This cloth has been obtained as a profit by the rainy-season retreat sangha of this place. The sangha will now use this cloth to make a kaṭhina robe. This cloth shall be spread as a kaṭhina robe for the sangha. Once the robe has been spread, even if one goes beyond the boundary, there is no offense of separation from one's three robes, let alone from other robes. If the time has come and the sangha approves, the sangha should consent. The sangha now entrusts this cloth to the bhikṣu so-and-so, who shall make the kaṭhina robe for the sangha. Once the robe has been spread, even if one goes beyond the boundary, there is no offense of separation from one's three robes, let alone from other robes. Thus is the motion.' The karmavācanā follows the same form as the motion. Next, a bhikṣu should be appointed to spread the kaṭhina robe. One who possesses five virtues should be selected. These are: being free from desire, free from favoritism, free from anger, free from delusion, and knowing what constitutes proper and improper spreading. If one differs in these respects, one should not be appointed. The appointment should be made as follows: after the preliminary preparations, when the assembly is present and gathered, one should first ask whether the person is willing, saying: 'You, so-and-so, are you willing to serve as the robe-spreader for the sangha?' If he says he is willing, he answers: 'I am willing.' Next, one bhikṣu performs the motion-and-karmavācanā to appoint him, saying: 'Let the venerable sangha hear! This bhikṣu so-and-so is willing to serve as the kaṭhina robe-spreader, and will now spread the kaṭhina robe for the sangha. If the time has come and the sangha approves, the sangha should consent. The sangha now appoints so-and-so as the kaṭhina robe-spreader. This so-and-so shall spread the kaṭhina robe for the sangha. Thus is the motion.' 'Let the venerable sangha hear! This bhikṣu so-and-so is willing to serve as the kaṭhina robe-spreader, and will now spread the kaṭhina robe for the sangha. The sangha now appoints so-and-so as the kaṭhina robe-spreader. This so-and-so shall spread the kaṭhina robe for the sangha. If the venerables approve of appointing so-and-so as the kaṭhina robe-spreader, and this so-and-so shall spread the kaṭhina robe for the sangha, let them remain silent. If they do not approve, let them speak.' 'The sangha has approved this so-and-so as the kaṭhina robe-spreader. This so-and-so shall serve as the kaṭhina robe-spreader for the sangha. The sangha has given approval. Since there was silence, I now so record it.' Next, the robe-spreading bhikṣu should perform the motion-and-karmavācanā to make the kaṭhina robe.

"'Let the venerable sangha hear! This cloth shall be made into the kaṭhina robe for the sangha. This bhikṣu so-and-so has been appointed by the sangha as the robe-spreader. If the time has come and the sangha approves, the sangha should consent. The sangha now entrusts this cloth to the bhikṣu so-and-so to make the kaṭhina robe. Thus is the motion.' The karmavācanā should follow the same form.

"The bhikṣu who makes the kaṭhina robe must follow the prescribed conduct. At the place where the robe is made, one must first wash, dye, and treat the cloth, cut and sew it, sewing at least two or three stitches together with the assembly. Furthermore, one must repeat three times the following thought: 'This cloth shall be spread as a kaṭhina robe for the sangha; it is now being spread as a kaṭhina robe; it has been spread as a kaṭhina robe.' Of these three mental acts, even if only the latter two are performed, the procedure is valid. If one does not do this, one incurs a minor offense. Next, on the fifteenth day of the bright half of the eighth month, the administrator should announce to the great assembly, making the following declaration: 'Venerables! Tomorrow I shall spread the kaṭhina robe for the assembly. Each of you should relinquish your old held robes and come to assemble at such-and-such a place.' The robe-spreading bhikṣu should arrange flowers upon the robe, perfume it with fine incense, sound the ghaṇṭā, assemble the assembly, and circulate the announcement. Then, bringing the robe before the senior monk and standing with the robe held in both hands, he should make the following declaration:

"'Let the venerable sangha hear! This cloth has been approved by the sangha for spreading as a kaṭhina robe. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, have been appointed by the sangha to spread the kaṭhina robe. I, so-and-so, with this cloth shall now spread the kaṭhina robe for the sangha.' This is said three times. Next, the robe is unfolded and spread. Standing before the senior monk, one says: 'Senior monk, please take note! This cloth has been approved by the sangha for making the kaṭhina robe. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, am the robe-spreader. I now spread this robe for the great assembly.' The senior monk responds: 'Well done, the spreading of the robe! Excellently done, the spreading of the robe! The material benefits herein, I shall receive them.' This is said three times, and so on down to the last in the row, each saying: 'Well done, the spreading of the robe! Excellently done, the spreading of the robe! The material benefits herein, I shall receive them.' Next, the rules for the robe-keeper should be known: one must not take this robe to the latrine, must not enter a kitchen or place of smoke and fire, must not leave it in the open, and must not take the robe beyond the boundary. If one briefly goes out, one should not be away overnight. If the robe-keeper does not follow these rules, he incurs an offense against the regulations. When the robe-keeper reaches the fifteenth day of the first month, he should make the following declaration before the assembly: 'The assembly should know: tomorrow the kaṭhina robe shall be taken up. Each of you should keep and guard your own robes. Any material profits should be divided among the great assembly.'"

---

Upāli asked the World-Honored One: "How many kinds of persons do not validly spread the robe?" The Buddha said: "There are five kinds of persons: those without a summer retreat, those who broke the summer retreat, those of the later retreat, novices, and those not present at the time of spreading. Furthermore, there are five kinds who do not validly spread: those undergoing the pārivāsika probation, those who have completed probation, those undergoing the six-night penance, those who have completed the six-night penance, and those under supervised training." "Venerable One, how many kinds of persons receive only the material profits but not the advantages?" The Buddha said: "There are five kinds of persons: those without a summer retreat, those who broke the retreat, those of the later retreat, novices, and those not present. Furthermore, there are five kinds: those undergoing probation, those who have completed probation, those undergoing the six-night penance, those who have completed the penance, and those under supervised training. These receive the profits but not the advantages." "Venerable One, how many kinds of persons receive neither profits nor advantages?" The Buddha said: "Upāli, there are five kinds of persons: those suspended for not acknowledging an offense, those suspended for repeated serious offenses, those suspended for not relinquishing wrong views, those who sat the retreat elsewhere, and those of the non-Dharma-non-Vinaya faction after a schism."

---

At that time, many bhikṣus were traveling among people and were robbed by bandits. They gradually arrived at the city of Śrāvastī. The bhikṣus there saw them and asked: "Welcome, venerables! Was your journey comfortable?" They answered: "What comfort could there be? All our robes and belongings were seized by bandits." The others said: "Venerables, here we have an abundance of robe-materials. If we take up the kaṭhina robe, we can share them with you." They reported this to the Buddha. The Buddha said: "If bhikṣus face the condition of bandits, the kaṭhina robe should be taken up for their sake. It should be taken up as follows: after the usual preliminary preparations, one bhikṣu performs the motion-and-karmavācanā: 'Let the venerable sangha hear! At this residence, the harmonious sangha has together spread the kaṭhina robe. Now many bhikṣus have been robbed by bandits and arrived here without robes. If the time has come and the sangha approves, the sangha should consent. The sangha now takes up the kaṭhina robe for the sake of these robbed bhikṣus. Thus is the motion.' The karmavācanā follows the same form, down to the conclusion. Once the procedure is performed, all material profits are equally divided among those who received the robe. Each person, from his share, may as he wishes distribute to the robbed bhikṣus who have no robes."

---

Upāli asked the World-Honored One: "Venerable One, may thin and flimsy cloth, poor-quality cloth, cloth with many knots, ramie cloth, cloth with piped edges, worn-out cloth, cloth subject to forfeiture, or cloth from a deceased person be used to make the kaṭhina robe?" The Buddha said: "They may not." "Venerable One, may stone-pressed cloth, or cloth that is one of someone's personal three robes, or cloth that does not meet the measure of three by five cubits, or cloth that is not cut and sewn, or cloth that is not pure, or cloth spread without appointing a robe-spreader, or cloth spread outside the boundary be used to spread the robe?" The Buddha said: "They may not." "May cloth obtained during the three months of the summer retreat be used to make the robe?" The Buddha said: "It may." "May cloth obtained after the three months of the summer retreat are completed be used to make the kaṭhina robe?" The Buddha said: "It may." As for additional cases of proper and improper cloth, they should be understood according to the meaning of the above text.

### The Eight Types of Loss of the Kaṭhina Robe

The characteristics of taking up the kaṭhina robe originally comprise eight types. What are the eight? The summary verse says:

> First, definite departure and indefinite departure; definite loss and loss of the robe;
> Hearing of the taking-up; doubt upon leaving the boundary; severance of hope; and consensual taking-up.

What is loss by definite departure? Suppose a bhikṣu has received the kaṭhina robe together with others at a single place. Having finished making the robe, he has no attachment to this place and, carrying his robes and bowl, intends to go elsewhere. He leaves the boundary with no intention of returning. Having definitively departed, this is called loss by definite departure.

What is loss by indefinite departure? Suppose a bhikṣu has received the kaṭhina robe together with others at a single place, but before making the robe he leaves the boundary to seek cloth, or has not yet made the robe, or has made only half. Regarding the material profits and the residence, he may or may not have attachment, may or may not have expectations. He intends to return to make the robe, or he is uncertain. This is called loss by indefinite departure.

What is definite loss? Suppose a bhikṣu has received the kaṭhina robe together with others at a single place, but before making the robe he leaves the boundary to seek cloth, thinking: "I shall return to make the robe." Then he thinks again: "Having gone out now, I shall not return, nor shall I be able to make the cīvara." This is called definite loss.

What is loss by losing the robe? Suppose a bhikṣu has received the kaṭhina robe together with others at a single place, but before making the robe he leaves the boundary to make the cīvara. He begins making the robe, but then loses the cloth. This is called loss by losing the robe.

What is loss by hearing of the taking-up? Suppose a bhikṣu has received the kaṭhina robe together with others at a single place, but before making the robe he leaves the boundary to seek cloth, thinking: "When I return, I shall make the robe." After he departs, the great assembly takes up the robe. He hears that the kaṭhina robe has been taken up and rejoices in his mind: "Well done, the taking-up of the robe!" This is called loss by hearing of the taking-up.

What is loss by doubt upon leaving the boundary? Suppose a bhikṣu has received the kaṭhina robe together with others at a single place. Before making the robe, the bhikṣu thinks to himself: "I shall leave the boundary to make the cīvara. Whether it is finished or not, I may or may not return." Having given rise to such a thought, upon leaving the boundary it is lost. This is called loss by doubt upon leaving the boundary.

What is loss by severance of hope? Suppose a bhikṣu has received the kaṭhina robe together with others at a single place and begun making the robe, but before completing it, he leaves the boundary to seek cloth, intending to return and finish it. Having arrived at that other place, he cannot obtain cloth, and his hope is cut off. This is called loss by severance of hope.

What is consensual taking-up? Suppose a bhikṣu has received the kaṭhina robe together with others at a single place and made the robe. He leaves the boundary to seek cloth. Afterward, he returns to the residence and, together with the assembly, performs the motion-and-single-karmavācanā to take up the robe. This is called consensual taking-up.
