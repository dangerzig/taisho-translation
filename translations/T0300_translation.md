# 大方廣佛華嚴經不思議佛境界分
## The Great Vaipulya Buddha Avataṃsaka Sūtra: Chapter on the Inconceivable Realm of the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0300

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Devaprajña (提雲般若)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the land of Magadha, beneath the bodhi tree, having attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, the perfect and right awakening. That bodhi tree had deep and firm roots, well planted in the earth, like a sandalwood pillar. Its trunk was tall and straight, without side branches or leaves, firm, solid, and full, called an Aśvattha tree. No bird could fly past it. Its bark was fine and soft, patterned like damask. Its immeasurable branches were beautifully interspersed and adorned. The surfaces of its leaves were a plain green, splendidly arranged; the leaf veins were visible, of an indigo-blue color. Its branches and leaves hung down, full on all sides. Its flowers bloomed forth, extremely delightful, radiating light and fragrant perfume. Its roots, trunk, and other parts displayed various colors, fully endowed with all virtues, like Mount Sumeru. Beneath it was adorned like the Nandana Garden. Its light and fragrance extended one yojana. At night its radiance shone far, like a great heap of fire. The ground on all four sides was entirely level. Within it grew grass, soft and lush, of bright and pure color, like peacock feathers, with a wondrous fragrance that delighted the mind. Immeasurable small trees surrounded it on all sides. That bodhi tree king was supremely adorned and wonderful; apart from the Pārijāta and Vidāra trees, nothing could compare.

The Buddha sat beneath the tree, surrounded by the great assembly, majestic and composed, like the moon amid the stars, abiding in the pure sky. At that time, beings from ten buddha-lands, as numerous as atoms, each came from their own lands to this place in order to adorn Vairocana and for the sake of the assembly, appearing in the forms of bodhisattvas. Their names were: the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Avalokiteśvara, the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, the bodhisattva Kṣitigarbha, the bodhisattva Ākāśagarbha, the bodhisattva Vajragarbha, the bodhisattva Vimalakīrti, the bodhisattva Sutejas, the bodhisattva Sarva-nīvaraṇa-viṣkambhin, the bodhisattva Ratnapāṇi, the bodhisattva Mahāmati, and the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Samantabhadra, who served as leaders. This assembly of innumerable thousands of koṭis of bodhisattvas all came and gathered. Further, innumerable thousands of koṭis of bodhisattvas, appearing in the form of śrāvakas, also came and gathered. They were: Śāriputra, Subhūti, Maudgalyāyana, Rāhula, Kauṇḍinya, Mahākāśyapa, Upāli, Aniruddha, Revata, Ānanda, Devadatta, and Upananda, who served as leaders. All of them had long practiced the six pāramitās, were near to bodhi, and in order to benefit sentient beings, manifested the form of śrāvakas in this defiled buddha-land. Further, there were innumerable thousands of bhikṣuṇīs, with Mahāprajāpatī Gautamī as their leader. In order to tame beings of inferior capacity, although they appeared in female form, they were accomplished in the deeds of great men. Further, there were Śakra, Brahmā, the world-protectors, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans and non-humans. None among them were ordinary; all were bodhisattvas who had come to the assembly. Why is this? Because those Buddhas possess the awesome spiritual power and divine transformations of the secret Dharma and samādhi. How could bodhisattvas come to know this? And as for all Buddhas' awesome spiritual powers, which are of boundless strength, and all dharmas are empty, and the powers are able to turn: how could sentient beings come to know this? It is like a person who in a dream sees all manner of things, but upon awakening sees nothing. Like the thinking in a dream, just so! In the dream, due to delusion, perceptions of dharma-substance arise. The Buddhas, being awakened, see nothing at all. Through the most profound, unobstructed teachings, they benefit sentient beings, for they have accomplished the subtle liberation.

Beneath that bodhi tree, the Bhagavān sat upright and entered a samādhi called the Inconceivable Realm of the Buddha. This samādhi is supremely vast and great. By means of this samādhi, all Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, are constantly in samādhi: when teaching, when eating, when walking, even at the time of parinirvāṇa; thus adorned. The Buddha Bhagavān was like the great Mount Sumeru, like a wish-fulfilling jewel placed beneath a Pārijāta tree. Within his pure thirty-two marks of a great man, all the buddha-lands and all the Bhagavāns appeared, as in a pure, clear mirror, universally manifesting all forms. Within each of his minor marks, the bodhisattva's original practices were fully displayed: from the initial circumstance of King Radiance up to the final encounter with Dīpaṃkara Buddha, every difficult ascetic deed, including giving away heads, eyes, hands, feet, limbs, wife, children, servants, as well as relinquishing high positions, palaces, and the like.

At that time, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Guṇagarbha, whose practices were not yet completed and who had not yet attained right awakening, asked the bodhisattva Samantabhadra: "Child of the Buddha! What samādhi is it that the Tathāgata has entered? Child of the Buddha! How is it that naturally, in the buddha-lands of the ten directions, he delivers sentient beings and manifests the deeds of the Buddha? Child of the Buddha! What is the name of this samādhi? Venerable one! How is this samādhi realized?"

Then the bodhisattva Samantabhadra said: "Good son! Listen carefully! I shall now explain this matter for you."

Thereupon all the bodhisattvas, with minds of reverence, gazed upon his honored countenance, abiding in right mindfulness. Together they said: "Excellent, excellent! Guṇagarbha! Your question is most subtle and wondrous. However, that venerable one can know all." At that time, the great earth quaked in six ways: moving, moving everywhere, moving equally everywhere; shaking, shaking everywhere, shaking equally everywhere; roaring, roaring everywhere, roaring equally everywhere; striking, striking everywhere, striking equally everywhere; spinning, spinning everywhere, spinning equally everywhere; splitting open above and below, splitting open everywhere above and below, splitting open equally everywhere above and below. The east surged and the west sank, and so forth from center to periphery, and likewise. At that moment, all sentient beings' afflictions and sufferings temporarily came to rest, and wondrous celestial flowers rained down.

Then the bodhisattva Samantabhadra told the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha: "Child of the Buddha! This samādhi is the bodhi of all Buddhas. Child of the Buddha! This samādhi is called the Inconceivable Realm of the Buddha. All Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, constantly abide in it. From the time the World-Honored One received his prediction from Dīpaṃkara Buddha, at that very moment he entered this samādhi. From that time onward, he has dwelt without effort, and on the tip of a single hair, there are buddha-lands as numerous as the atoms of all buddha-lands, all universally manifest. He may appear being born in Tuṣita Heaven, descending from there, entering the womb, dwelling in the womb, emerging from the womb, walking seven steps, and proclaiming: 'I shall forever leave birth, aging, illness, and death; what was to be done has been done.' He may appear dwelling in the palace, leaving home, practicing austerities, attaining perfect awakening, conquering the host of Māra, turning the wondrous Dharma-wheel. He may appear abiding in the world for immeasurable kalpas, delivering sentient beings from all their sufferings, and even showing the appearance of entering parinirvāṇa. In a single thought-moment, he universally manifests all the buddha-lands. These deeds, in a single thought-moment, encompass all kalpas, without increase or decrease, all without effort. And so on until all sentient beings have been completely liberated, in each and every kṣaṇa, in all places simultaneously, he constantly performs these various deeds of the Buddha, without ever resting and without effort. Just as on the tip of a single hair in empty space, in immeasurable lands, thought after thought, there is the universal manifestation of all the Buddhas' various deportments and rules, all without effort; and so on to the ends of space, in every hair-tip measure, it is also just like this. Moreover, in every atom of all those lands, there are further lands surpassing in number all the atoms of all buddha-lands; and in a single kṣaṇa, in each and every land, there is the natural and universal manifestation of all the Buddhas' deportment and conduct. They appear being born in the Tuṣita celestial palace and so on through to parinirvāṇa, liberating immeasurable asaṃkhyeya sentient beings. Thus, thought after thought, to the end of the future, they constantly perform the deeds of the Buddha and benefit sentient beings, to the ends of space and the realm of sentient beings, without ever resting. Yet the buddha-lands do not shrink, and the atoms do not expand. Why is this? Because all dharmas are not solid, like an illusion, like a mirage. And so on to the very end of empty space, thus immeasurable deeds of the Buddha arise, kṣaṇa after kṣaṇa, for the sake of sentient beings, constantly benefiting them without a moment's rest. It is like this very great assembly of bodhisattvas, as numerous as the atoms of ten buddha-lands, all dwelling together in the twelve-yojana area of Magadha, not obstructing one another. Just so, within a single atom, there are contained immeasurable asaṃkhyeya buddha-lands. Some lands face each other, some face away, some are side by side, some contain one another, and so on without mutual obstruction. It is like a person in a dream who in a single place sees all manner of things. Because they are not real, there is no obstruction. Just so, all these buddha-lands are nothing but the transformations manifested by the mind's own measure. In some worlds one sees the kalpa-fire burning; in some, the fire has already burned out. In some, one sees wind arising. Some are pure, some defiled. Some appear to have no Buddha. Sentient beings each see differently according to their own karma. Just so, there are immeasurable variations. It is like hungry ghosts, tormented by hunger and thirst, who go to the banks of the Ganges: some see water, some see ashes, some see pus and blood, some see feces and filth. Just so, sentient beings in their own buddha-lands may see defilement or purity, may see the Buddha present or having entered nirvāṇa, or see him in a great assembly teaching the Dharma. Some hear the teaching of the ultimate truth, some hear the expansive teaching of dāna-pāramitā. Some see him walking, some see him standing, some see him sitting, some see him at mealtime. Some see a body twice the size of a man, or seven times, or one yojana, or a hundred yojanas, or a thousand yojanas. Some see radiant light like the rising sun, or like the full moon in a clear sky. According to their karmic deeds, their visions differ. Some see one who long ago entered parinirvāṇa; some do not even hear the name of any Buddha. It is like hungry ghosts at a riverbank: some see raging fire, some see other things, and so on. In this great assembly of bodhisattvas, each in their own buddha-land sees the Buddha Bhagavān in bodhisattva form. In some single lands, sentient beings see only the kalpa-fire burning, or the fire already burned out. In some single lands, the land is filled with sentient beings and the Buddha appears to benefit them. There are Bhagavāns who take all buddha-lands and place them within a single buddha-land, causing similar manifestations. Like a person with cataracts who, due to the cataracts, does not clearly see all colors, just so, due to the conditions of consciousness, one does not clearly see all marks."

Thereupon the bodhisattva Samantabhadra told Guṇagarbha: "Child of the Buddha! I shall now briefly explain for you the dharma of this samādhi. In a single kṣaṇa, in all places, to the very end of empty space and the realm of sentient beings, in every hair-tip's measure, there are immeasurable, boundless buddha-lands. In a single atom, there are buddha-lands as numerous as the atoms of the dharma-realm. In each and every buddha-land, there are exceedingly many deportments and paths. In a single kṣaṇa, for the benefit of sentient beings, there are manifestations as numerous as the atoms of ten buddha-lands. Thus, in every thought-moment, in all places, and for all sentient beings, until all have attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, this benefiting activity never ceases. Such deportment: the second, the third, the fourth, the fifth, and so on through the Buddhas of the ten directions, immeasurable and boundless, their awesome virtue and power, are also just like this."

At that time, the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha, having heard this teaching, clearly penetrated and understood this treasury of samādhi. He entered the Inconceivable Realm of all Buddhas, awakened to the spiritual powers of all Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and right there, through his own awesome power, skillfully tamed immeasurable sentient beings. Hundreds of billions of Ganges-sands of bodhisattvas, in the dharma of the samādhi, each variously awakened; some realized the stage of patient endurance, some realized the various grounds. Among them, Avalokiteśvara and the other bodhisattva-mahāsattvas were like a vase already full of water placed in the rain: it cannot hold a single additional drop. Just so, the bodhisattvas dwelling on the tenth ground, whose wondrous practices were all fully accomplished, had long been abiding in this samādhi, manifesting the deeds of the Buddha. In a single kṣaṇa they can contain immeasurable kalpas; in a single atom they can contain immeasurable lands. In a single thought they pervade all buddha-lands, naturally delivering immeasurable sentient beings without effort.

Then the Bhagavān, abiding in samādhi, emitted from between his brows a white ūrṇā-hair light, named Able to Arouse. That light shone forth with the power of active effort. All the bodhisattvas on the ten grounds had not yet awakened to it, but when the light touched them, they were able to see all the buddha-lands. In every hair-tip's measure and in every atom, there were immeasurable buddha-lands. At that moment, pervading empty space, immeasurable and boundless buddha-lands were manifested, like a lapis lazuli vase filled with mustard seeds. Just so, within the measure of a single atom, one could see the buddha-lands and the Buddhas Bhagavān. In the body of a single Tathāgata, the forms of all Tathāgatas were displayed. Each Tathāgata had immeasurable names. In a single kṣaṇa, in each and every buddha-land, for the sake of each and every sentient being, there was the natural manifestation of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Like a wish-fulfilling jewel placed on a high banner, it naturally rains down all manner of precious jewels, fulfilling the wishes of sentient beings. Just so, all Tathāgatas manifest perfect awakening and naturally deliver immeasurable sentient beings. And in these lands, the sentient beings are each different, yet do not obstruct one another, like one with spiritual powers who traverses empty space, through mountains, rivers, and stone walls without any obstruction. Why is this? Because all destinies are not solid, like an illusion, like a mirage. All the bodhisattvas, having witnessed this, each saw their own bodies pervading all the lands. In a single thought-moment, before each and every Tathāgata, for one kalpa, or two kalpas, or three kalpas, or a hundred kalpas, or a thousand kalpas, or a single kṣaṇa, or a single muhūrta, they made all manner of offerings. From the Tathāgatas, they heard the teaching of the pāramitās, or dhāraṇīs, or the various grounds. They saw the Tathāgatas' divine transformations. Taking all kalpas into a single kṣaṇa, the thought arose of how rare and difficult to encounter this is, thinking: "How can the Tathāgata possess such awesome virtue, manifesting immeasurable kalpas in a single thought, and for our sake, broadly expounding these accumulations of merit? The inconceivable realm of the Buddha, the samādhi practiced over immeasurable kalpas, awakens to awesome power and awesome virtue of this kind."

At that time, the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha asked the bodhisattva Samantabhadra: "Venerable one! What merit must one cultivate to realize this samādhi? How should one practice prajñā? How should one practice dāna? How should one uphold śīla?"

Then the bodhisattva Samantabhadra said: "Child of the Buddha! In the ten directions, in all the pure and perfect buddha-lands, among all sentient beings, manifesting immeasurable deeds of the Buddha, the bodhisattvas accumulate various merits and good roots. They should make offerings to the Buddha, make offerings to the Dharma, make offerings to the Saṅgha, and filially serve their parents, never ceasing. All the poor, suffering, orphaned, and destitute: the bodhisattva should compassionately receive them all, even to the point of giving one's own blood and flesh as needed, without giving rise to stinginess. Why is this? Because making offerings to the Buddha produces exceedingly great merit. Such sentient beings receive supreme happiness and will swiftly realize anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Making offerings to the Dharma produces good roots, increases wisdom, and brings the attainment of the mastery of self-realization over all dharmas, enabling one to correctly know all dharmas. Making offerings to the Saṅgha plants good roots, and that immeasurable accumulation of provisions increases, leading to the attainment of Buddhahood. Therefore the bodhisattva, from the time of practice up to the attainment of Buddhahood, should repay with increased offerings all those who have shown kindness, whether parents, preceptors, or anyone in any lifetime of birth and death from whom one has received benefit. Why is this? Because among sentient beings, those who know and repay kindness, even when they commit offenses in the midst of birth and death, their good roots are never destroyed. Those who are ungrateful: their good roots are cut off, and they create evil karma. Therefore, all Tathāgatas always praise the grateful and censure the ungrateful. How does a bodhisattva firmly accomplish all good roots? By being compassionate toward the poor, orphaned, and destitute; through such broad great good roots, the bodhisattva never regresses. If a person can diligently cultivate merit, always remember to repay kindness, and have compassion for sentient beings, then bodhi is already in their hand. One should know that the Buddha has said: by making offerings to these three fields, one accomplishes immeasurable good roots in each.

"Furthermore, Guṇagarbha! The bodhisattvas should next plant vast seeds, for from these arises the sprout of this samādhi, which becomes the fruit of bodhi. How does one plant the seeds? One should take various exquisite garlands, perfumes, unguents, powdered incense, and various forms of music, and reverently offer them to the Buddhas presently in the world or to Buddha images, thinking: 'As described above, pervading empty space, in every hair-tip's measure and in every atom, in immeasurable lands, one by one I see the awesome power of all the Buddhas and all the bodhisattvas. Before them all, with a pure mind, I evenly recollect and make offerings. The dharma-nature of one Tathāgata is the dharma-nature of all Tathāgatas. If I make offerings to one Tathāgata, I have already made offerings to all Tathāgatas. By the divine power of each and every Buddha, however many kalpas can be contained in a single thought, so too for that many kalpas I make offerings to the Tathāgata.' If sentient beings have faith in and understand this Dharma, and plant the great seed, they will attain this Inconceivable Realm of the Buddha, the great and vast samādhi. Good men and good women should make such offerings daily. By this, even a single joining of palms before all the Buddhas Bhagavān can cause the seed to grow and the sprout of samādhi to arise. One should also constantly irrigate it with generosity, precept-keeping, great vows, and wisdom. Furthermore, the bodhisattva, to irrigate the samādhi, when practicing generosity should not discriminate between meritorious fields, friends or foes, good or bad, those who keep the precepts or break them, the rich or the poor. Furthermore, one should think: 'Even giving to the wealthy, who have no need, I should still cultivate the practice of giving.' The bodhisattva should also keep the precepts purely. Seeing those who break the precepts, one should give rise to great compassion and should not feel aversion or rejection toward them.

"Furthermore, one should profoundly generate the great aspiration for bodhi: 'I shall certainly, thought after thought, universally in every hair-tip's measure throughout all of space, and even in all the atoms of all buddha-lands, in the immeasurable worlds therein, attain perfect awakening, turn the wondrous Dharma-wheel, and deliver sentient beings. Just as the World-Honored One Vairocana does now, exactly the same, without arising any effort, containing immeasurable kalpas in a single thought.' And then, in each and every one of these lands, one manifests deportments as numerous as the atoms of buddha-lands, and in each deportment, delivers sentient beings as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, all causing them to leave suffering, to the very ends of empty space and the realm of sentient beings, without ever resting."

The bodhisattva Samantabhadra told Guṇagarbha: "Child of the Buddha! As you asked, 'How should one practice prajñā?' Listen carefully; I shall explain for you. If good men or good women seek unsurpassed bodhi and wish to realize this samādhi, they should first cultivate wisdom, because this samādhi is attained through wisdom. Those who cultivate wisdom should distance themselves from false and deceptive speech and all forms of scattered and purposeless activity. Although they give rise to great compassion for all sentient beings, they should always collect their minds, not being stained or scattered. They should go to a monastery and behold a Buddha image adorned with gold, or made of pure gold, fully endowed with all marks and limbs, within a circular halo containing immeasurable Buddhas arrayed in fine adornment, seated in cross-legged meditation, in samādhi. Before the image, one should prostrate with reverence and think: 'I have heard that the Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, of the ten directions, presently in the world, are immeasurable and boundless, namely: the Tathāgata Sarvārthasiddha, the Tathāgata Amitāyus, the Tathāgata Ratnaketu, the Tathāgata Akṣobhya, the Tathāgata Vairocana, the Tathāgata Ratnacandra, the Tathāgata Ratna-āloka, and others. Toward them, with broad and great faith, joy, and reverence.' One should further give rise to the thought: 'This Tathāgata is here,' generating deep reverence, until naturally the Buddhas appear before one. In all places, one should carefully observe. Thinking thus, go to a secluded place, with the mind in right recollection, holding the image near, within the reach of an arm, concentrating on the recollection without letting it be lost. If it is lost, one should go back and observe again. While observing thus, give rise to a mind of deep reverence, imagining the Buddha as actually present, and one should not think of it as a mere image. Then naturally one sees the Buddhas face to face. One should then offer perfumes, powdered incense, burning incense, and garlands, circumambulating to the right, and making various offerings. Because one's entire mind is thus concentrated, the Buddha Bhagavān naturally appears before one, able to see all, able to hear all, able to know all minds. Repeatedly and with certainty, seize upon this visualization, then go again to a secluded place and do not let the mind lose its hold. If one is a person of merit, within twenty-one days of diligent cultivation, the Buddhas will swiftly appear before them. If one's past-life karma creates obstacles, preventing one from seeing them, one should persist with vigor, courage, and diligent practice, and observe with firm determination. Why is this? Because when one concentrates the mind on a single object, taming it with exertion and dedicated effort, no provision for bodhi will fail to be accomplished. If one is drowsy, lazy, and heedless, one naturally cannot attain liberation; how much less can one liberate all sentient beings from their suffering? This would be like a heavy burden upon the great earth. If one is like this, one cannot even cultivate the straight path of bodhi diligently. It is like a person who drinks a single handful of water from the great ocean: they have already drunk all the water of the rivers in Jambudvīpa. Just so, if one cultivates the ocean of bodhi, all samādhis, all forms of patient endurance, all grounds, and all dhāraṇīs have already been cultivated. Therefore, one should always be diligent and courageous, far from heedlessness. Thinking in this way, naturally the Buddhas appear before one.

"If one has already seen them, one should then think: 'Is this the Tathāgata, or is it the image?' If one becomes aware that the Tathāgata has appeared before one, then before the Buddha, kneel with joined palms and think: 'The awesome virtue and great compassion of the Buddhas, as described before, have caused them to appear before me now.' If one is able to ask questions, one should request: 'May the World-Honored One please teach me the great samādhi Dharma of the Inconceivable Realm of the Buddha.' And one should not give rise to doubt about it. If the teaching is given, receive it with a determined mind. If one hears it, right in that place one will fully accomplish it. Or, if past karmic obstructions prevent one from making the request, one should think and observe as follows: 'All dharmas are like dew, like cataracts, like illusion, like a mirage, like a mirror-image. Just so, all Buddhas are like empty space, of an equal intrinsic nature, neither illusory nor dreamlike. Yet all dharmas are like illusion, like a dream; one should thoroughly understand this. Through the wisdom and compassion of the Buddhas, the World-Honored One now appears. May he now emit the great light of compassion for my sake, and may all my afflictions be extinguished.' One should further think and observe the subtle nature of emptiness. At that time, the World-Honored One emits from between his brows a great white ūrṇā-hair light, named Blue Flame, which touches the top of one's head. All afflictions are extinguished, and right there one attains a dharma called the Vairocana Patient Endurance, and all manner of samādhis are awakened. On the seventh night, the Buddha Bhagavān appears in a dream and bestows the prediction of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. If one knows in this way that this is an image, then one should think: 'All Tathāgatas and all sentient beings, just like seeing an image, have no reality that can be grasped.' Just so, all Tathāgatas are like illusion, like transformation, like a dream, like a mirage; their nature is just so. Seeing the Tathāgata face to face, as in a dream, it is all without reality. Just so, all Tathāgatas are not born yet born, not extinguished yet extinguished, different from all conditioned things, the dharma-nature is exceedingly profound, cannot be spoken of yet is spoken of, do not go yet go, do not come yet come, are not known by consciousness yet are known, are without the perception of self, without the perception of a life-span, without the perception of a sentient being, without the perception of a nurturer, without the perception of a person, without the perception of an eater, are not aggregates yet not not-aggregates, have the appearance of aggregates. The eighteen elements and the twelve sense-fields are ultimately nonexistent, yet are not different from them. Because all Buddhas and all dharmas are equal and the same, like a mirage. All sentient beings, all Tathāgatas, and all buddha-lands are inseparable from thought. Such thoughts appear: consciousness conditions form, and its intrinsic nature is just so, ultimately without birth. The Tathāgata has left behind all consciousness; therefore it is different from form. One should observe and know: if all dharmas are inseparable from thought, then one recognizes the Tathāgata. Even in a hair-tip's measure, there is no difference. It is like empty space: equal and the same. If I discriminate, the Buddha appears before me. If there is no discrimination, there is nothing to see. Thought can create a Buddha; apart from thought there is no Buddha. Just so, all dharmas of the three realms are inseparable from mind. If one can know that all Buddhas and all dharmas of the dharma-nature are merely the measure of one's own mind, one attains the patient endurance of accordance, or realizes the Ground of Joy. Upon leaving the body in the next life, one is swiftly born in the world of Abhirati, or is born in the pure buddha-land of Sukhāvatī, where one always sees the Tathāgata and personally attends and makes offerings."

Thereupon the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Guṇagarbha addressed the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Samantabhadra: "Child of the Buddha! If sentient beings hear this dharma-gate, and receive, uphold, read, recite, copy, and broadly teach it, how much merit do they gain?"

Then the bodhisattva Samantabhadra told Guṇagarbha: "Good son, listen carefully! The measure of merit produced by this is as follows: suppose a person were to gather all the sentient beings of the three realms and cause them all to be freed from birth and death and attain the fruit of arhatship. Suppose further that toward those beings, with a pure and joyful intention, free of distress, for a hundred kalpas one offered them clothing, food, bedding, and medicine, with various offerings. And after they entered nirvāṇa, one raised seven-jeweled stūpas in their honor and reverently made offerings: that merit would be exceedingly great. Yet if a sentient being hears this dharma-gate and generates faith and reverence, and does not disparage it, and further for a hundred kalpas purely cultivates brahmacarya, for a hundred kalpas cultivates patient endurance, for a hundred kalpas cultivates diligence, for a hundred kalpas cultivates dhyāna, the merit produced would be without limit. And if further a person hears this dharma-gate, generates faith and reverence, and is able to correctly receive and uphold it, they will swiftly attain Buddhahood, and their merit surpasses all of that."

At that time, in all the buddha-lands of the ten directions, all the Buddhas Bhagavān manifested their bodies and praised the bodhisattva Samantabhadra, saying: "Excellent, excellent! Child of the Buddha! It is so, it is so! As you have spoken."

Thereupon the Tathāgata Śākyamuni emitted from his face immeasurable colored light, illuminating the three realms. Various wondrous flowers rained down, and musical instruments sounded of their own accord without being struck, producing subtle and wondrous tones that delighted the mind. At that time, the great earth gently trembled. From within the light, immeasurable wondrous gāthās were proclaimed:

> "If one hears this Dharma with a purified mind,
> One attains the various grounds, samādhis, and dhāraṇīs,
> Together with the patient endurance, mastery, and wondrous spiritual powers,
> And swiftly realizes the fruit of unsurpassed bodhi.
> In the future one will turn the Dharma-wheel,
> Just as the great ṛṣis of the past did.
> In a single kṣaṇa, containing many kalpas,
> In a single atom, manifesting all the lands.
> Immeasurable sentient beings fill the three realms,
> Among them, the heedless undergo all manner of suffering.
> Bound by wrong views, they lose the right path;
> Thought after thought, they are delivered without any being passed over."

When the bodhisattva Samantabhadra taught this dharma-gate, innumerable thousands of koṭis of devas and humans, freed from all their afflictions, attained non-retrogression from anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

The bodhisattva-mahāsattva Samantabhadra, having long since thoroughly penetrated this dharma-gate and personally realized it, taught this sūtra. At that time, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Guṇagarbha, together with all the bodhisattvas, devas, humans, gandharvas, asuras, and all the world, hearing what the Buddha taught, all greatly rejoiced, believed, received, and practiced accordingly.
