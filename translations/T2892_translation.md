# 現報當受經
## Sūtra on Retribution to Be Received in This Life

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2892

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

...a single person walking alone, without any companion. He asked her: "Sister, why is your face both lovely and so haggard? What afflicts you?" The woman answered: "My fortune is thin. I married a husband, hoping we would protect and keep each other. Unexpectedly, one day he fell ill and died. My husband's family seized me and buried me alive in the tomb. After seven days had passed, a band of robbers broke open the tomb seeking valuables. They took me back to their lair and made me a wife. Later, the bandit chief raided a village and was killed by the people there. They gathered his remains and buried him. His kinsmen took me and again buried me alive. After three days, a wild fox, wanting to eat the corpse, came and dug open the tomb. Through this I escaped. My parents and family knew nothing of it."

The young man then said to the woman: "My wife has just died. Since you have no husband, let the two of us become husband and wife. Will you agree?" The woman, having nowhere else to turn, consented. She went with him to his home, and they loved each other dearly. After a year she bore a son. Later she bore another son. Still later she became pregnant again. When her time of delivery came, the young man had gone out to feast and drink with his companions. He returned at nightfall. His wife, after he left, had firmly bolted the door and was on the ground giving birth. Blood and fluids were everywhere. When the husband returned and called for his wife, she was on the ground amidst the blood and fluids of childbirth and could not open the door. He called but she did not answer. He drew his sword and broke down the door, then asked his wife: "I went out this morning. When I returned and called you, the door was bolted and you did not answer. Have I wronged you in some way?" The wife answered: "It was because I was giving birth. Blood and fluids were everywhere." The husband said: "The child you bore, where is it now?" The wife handed the newborn child to her husband. The husband took the child and fried it in fresh ghee, then compelled his wife to eat it. The wife could not bear to eat it. He drew a sharp knife and was about to take her life. Clinging to life, the wife ate the child, consuming it entirely.

The next morning, when the husband had sobered up, he asked his wife: "When I returned last night, I was in a drunken stupor. Surely I committed no offense?" The wife answered: "When you returned last night, you called for me to open the door. Because I was giving birth, I could not open it in time. You hacked through the door and came in, then asked me whether you had wronged me. I answered that it was because of giving birth and the blood and fluids. You then demanded the newborn child from me. I handed it to you. You took the child, fried it in fresh ghee, and compelled me to eat it. I could not bear to eat it. You then drew a knife and were about to kill me. Clinging to life, I ate the child until it was entirely consumed." When the husband heard this, he hung his head in the deepest shame and begged his wife's forgiveness for his drunken offense, asking her not to hold it against him. From then on, he swore never to drink alcohol again.

Later, the wife said to her husband: "Since I parted from my parents, months and years have passed with no news. Let the two of us and our two sons visit my parents to pay our respects and ask after them." The young man then set out with his wife and two sons along the road. The weather was extremely hot. The husband stopped beneath a tree to rest in the shade and fell asleep. Suddenly a black snake bit the husband and he died. The wife wept and wailed. Carrying her two sons, she continued down the road. She came upon a deep stream, the current rushing and difficult to cross. She first carried the older son to the far bank. As she was starting back to fetch the younger son, the child, seeing his mother, plunged into the water and was swallowed and drowned by the waves. She climbed back to the bank to take up the older son, but he had been devoured by tigers and wolves.

At that time, the woman cried out to heaven and earth: "What evil karma have I done to encounter such suffering today?" She continued onward and came near her parents' village. Acquaintances from the village asked after her, and she said: "Are my parents, brothers, and relatives all safe and well?" The acquaintances answered: "Your family's house caught fire. Everyone, great and small, was burned to death in the blaze. No one remains." When the woman heard this, she fainted and collapsed, with nowhere to turn.

At that time, the World-Honored One was teaching the wondrous Dharma to the great assembly. With his divine eye he observed this woman. He told Ānanda: "Take a precious garment and go to welcome this woman." Ānanda received the instruction and, carrying the precious garment, went to welcome the woman. She came into the assembly, bowed to the World-Honored One, and with shame and remorse made confession. The Buddha taught her the Dharma. She severed all fetters and defilements and attained the fruit of arhatship.

The Buddha further told the great assembly: "If you wish to know the good and evil deeds of this woman's past lives, listen carefully, reflect well, and be mindful of them. Do not forget. In a past world, an immeasurable number of kalpas ago, there was a young man of means, whose wealth was immeasurable. He took a wife, but after months and years they had no children. The young man thought to himself: 'Now I have great wealth but no sons. When impermanence suddenly comes, my wealth will have no one to entrust it to.' Later he took a concubine, and after a year she bore a son. The child was handsome, and the young man loved him dearly. The primary wife thought to herself: 'I am the primary wife but have no children. This concubine was brought in not many years ago and has already borne a son. When he grows up, the wealth will belong to him. My husband will certainly cast me aside.' She then devised a scheme to kill the child. When the concubine-mother was away, the primary wife, her plan decided, took two iron needles and pierced the child's cheeks. The infant wailed in unbearable pain. The concubine-mother held the child but could not find the source of pain. She cried out to heaven and earth: 'Whoever with an evil mind, an angry mind, a jealous mind, or an envious mind has killed my child: may they, life after life and age after age, receive the retribution of suffering in return!' The primary wife's conscience was uneasy. Before her husband and the concubine, she made an oath: 'If I, with an evil mind, an angry mind, a jealous mind, or an envious mind, killed your child, may I, life after life and age after age, be reborn as a woman. May I marry a husband who buries me alive in a tomb. May I, life after life and age after age, whenever I bear a child, be made to fry and eat it in fresh ghee. May I, life after life and age after age, have my husband bitten by a snake, my son drowned by water, and my child devoured by tigers. May my entire household, great and small, be burned to death by fire.' When the oath was finished, the child died. The primary wife's conscience was uneasy, yet she had uttered the oath. There is no escaping sin and merit; like a shadow following the body, they never let go. For five hundred lifetimes, this primary wife, shedding one body and receiving another, constantly received the retribution of suffering."

The Buddha further told the great assembly: "I shall now tell you what merit this woman cultivated in her past life that caused her to encounter me, to be covered with a garment, to hear the Dharma taught, to sever all fetters and defilements, and to attain the path of arhatship. In a past life, an immeasurable number of kalpas ago, this woman's household was extremely poor. There was a pratyekabuddha who came to teach and convert her. The woman had no money, so she removed her belt and offered it to the pratyekabuddha. Because of this karmic connection, wherever she was reborn she was beautiful and was always born into a wealthy family. Although she received the suffering described above for five hundred lifetimes, she was still able to encounter me, to be covered with a garment, to hear the Dharma taught, to sever all fetters and defilements, and to attain the path of arhatship. Even after attaining the path, six times each day she still experienced the pain of a burning iron entering from the crown of her head and piercing through to her heart."

The Buddha further told the great assembly: "From this day forward, be careful never to harbor evil minds, angry minds, or envious minds, never to kill or harm one another, and never to curse or swear against each other. There is no escaping the retribution of sin. Like a shadow following the body, it never lets go. Whether one does good or evil, the evil consequence will never be such that 'I do it but another person receives the retribution.' Though the body may decay and perish, good and evil do not decay. As stated above, receive and uphold this carefully."

At that time, the great assembly, together with all the devas, nāgas, the eight classes of beings, and ghosts and spirits, including those who had harbored evil minds, all gave rise to the thought of compassion. Having heard what the Buddha said, they received and upheld it with the deepest reverence.
