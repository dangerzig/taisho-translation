# 佛說甚深大迴向經
## Sūtra of the Profound Great Transfer of Merit, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0825

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of eight thousand bhikṣus. At that time the World-Honored One, surrounded by the great assembly before and behind, was teaching the Dharma. In that assembly there was a bodhisattva named Bright Heaven, who rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms in reverence, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I wish to ask a question. I pray the World-Honored One will explain it in full."

At that time the Buddha told the Bodhisattva Mahāsattva Bright Heaven: "Good man! If you wish to ask, do not hesitate. The Tathāgata will answer according to your question."

The Bodhisattva Bright Heaven then addressed the Buddha: "How can a bodhisattva cultivate a small amount of good roots and obtain great results, or perform many meritorious deeds and gain immeasurable reward?"

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Mahāsattva Bright Heaven: "Excellent, excellent! Bright Heaven! You are able to ask such a question before the Buddha. You have already planted roots of virtue and made offerings at the place of countless Buddhas of the past, drawn near to good teachers, and are able to raise this profound question for the sake of beings who delight in merit. Listen carefully, listen carefully! Reflect well upon it."

The Bodhisattva Bright Heaven addressed the Buddha: "Yes, World-Honored One! I receive the teaching."

The Buddha told Bright Heaven: "Bodhisattva mahāsattvas should cultivate loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind toward the Buddhas of the past, future, and present, and single-mindedly be mindful of the merits of the Buddha's practice. Furthermore, Bright Heaven! Bodhisattva mahāsattvas should go to the honored shrines of the Tathāgata to bow and make offerings, kneeling with the right knee on the ground, joining palms, and circumambulating to the right, scattering flowers and burning incense, hanging silken banners and canopies, performing various kinds of music with reverence and respect, singing in subtle tones the meaning of profound verses, and praising the Buddha's merits with sympathetic joy and admiration."

The Buddha told Bright Heaven: "How do bodhisattva mahāsattvas cultivate loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind toward the Buddhas of the past, future, and present, and remain mindful of the Buddha's merits? Good man! Bodhisattva mahāsattvas should be mindful that the Tathāgata is a firm hero, an unsurpassed hero, a supreme hero, a lion-king, fearless and courageous, who ferries himself and ferries others, who brings peace to himself and peace to others, who extinguishes his own suffering and extinguishes that of others, who speaks the Dharma of true truth and establishes sentient beings therein, whose mind is without deceit or adornment, whose pure precepts are fully complete, whose powers, fearlessnesses, and eloquence have forever removed the habits of obstruction, who is sovereign over the Dharma and without equal. Having thus single-mindedly been mindful of the Buddha's merits, one kneels with the right knee on the ground, scatters flowers and burns incense, and makes offerings with banners, silks, pennants, canopies, and music: this is the bodhisattva's cultivation of loving-kindness in body. Singing in subtle tones the meaning of profound verses and praising the Tathāgata's immeasurable merits: this is the bodhisattva's cultivation of loving-kindness in speech. Through the good roots of body and speech, being mindful of the Buddha's merits with sincerity and reverence: this is the bodhisattva's cultivation of loving-kindness in mind. Bright Heaven! This is how bodhisattva mahāsattvas cultivate the right mindfulness of loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind toward the Buddhas of the past, future, and present."

The Buddha again told Bright Heaven: "Furthermore, bodhisattva mahāsattvas should also cultivate loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind toward the sentient beings of the past, future, and present, regarding all beings with equanimity. Bright Heaven! How do bodhisattva mahāsattvas cultivate loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind toward the sentient beings of the three times, regarding all beings with equanimity? Thus, Bright Heaven! Bodhisattva mahāsattvas do not kill living beings, do not steal others' property, do not engage in sexual misconduct, do not speak falsely, do not speak frivolously, do not speak divisively, do not speak harshly, are not covetous, are not angry, and do not hold wrong views. How does a bodhisattva not kill living beings? Toward all sentient beings one is loving, compassionate, caring, conscientious, and sympathetic, forever abandoning weapons. Not stealing means that in settlements or empty places, any item left behind that is not given one does not take. Not engaging in sexual misconduct means that if a woman belongs to another, or is protected by parents, brothers, or clan, one does not give rise to desire even upon seeing her receive a single flower-stem. Not speaking falsely means that whether in a village or before a king, when called to serve as witness, one speaks truly and sincerely, guarding the truth even unto death. Not speaking divisively means always giving rise to thoughts of harmony toward both sides; what one hears from one side one does not tell to the other. Not speaking harshly means speaking gently and kindly, greeting others first, never using cruel or cutting words toward any sentient being. Not speaking frivolously means speaking at the right time, speaking truthfully, speaking with understanding of the meaning, speaking for the benefit of others, with mind and mouth in accord. Not being covetous means not giving rise to desire for others' wealth; seeing others take things, the mind feels no reluctance. Not being angry means toward all sentient beings one removes all resentment and gives rise to a mind of compassion, benefit, and peace, skillfully embracing all sentient beings with accordance. Not holding wrong views means: there is giving, there is aid, there is teaching; there are parents; there is this world and the next; there are the fruits of suffering, happiness, and conduct; there are in the world arhats who know for themselves and realize: 'My births are exhausted, the holy life has been completed, what was to be done has been done, and I know I will not receive any further existence.'

"Bright Heaven! You should know that not killing, not stealing, and not engaging in sexual misconduct constitute the bodhisattva's cultivation of loving-kindness in body. Not speaking falsely, not speaking divisively, not speaking harshly, and not speaking frivolously constitute the bodhisattva's cultivation of loving-kindness in speech. Not being covetous, not being angry, and not holding wrong views constitute the bodhisattva's cultivation of loving-kindness in mind. Cultivating loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind: this is the bodhisattva's regarding all beings with equanimity."

The Buddha told Bright Heaven: "When bodhisattva mahāsattvas cultivate loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind toward the Buddhas of the past, future, and present, and also cultivate loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind toward all sentient beings of the past, future, and present, all the resulting merit and reward they share with all sentient beings, transferring it to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Bright Heaven! When a bodhisattva makes such a transfer of merit, this is how a bodhisattva cultivates a small amount of good roots and obtains great results, performs many meritorious deeds and gains immeasurable reward."

The Buddha told Bright Heaven: "When this bodhisattva accomplishes immeasurable merit, he takes this merit and transfers it to immeasurable wisdom, and together with all sentient beings, transfers it entirely to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. This merit is of three kinds and has three kinds of transfer. What are the three? The past is empty, the future is empty, and the present is empty. There is no one who transfers, there is no dharma of transferring, and there is no destination of the transfer. Bodhisattva mahāsattvas should make their transfer in this way. When making such a transfer, all three aspects are pure. With this pure merit, together with all sentient beings, one transfers it to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. When making such a transfer, there are no ordinary beings and no dharma of ordinary beings, no practice of faith and no practice of Dharma, no eight persons, no srotāpanna and no one approaching srotāpanna, no sakṛdāgāmin and no one approaching sakṛdāgāmin, no anāgāmin and no one approaching anāgāmin, no arhat and no one approaching arhatship, no pratyekabuddha and no one approaching pratyekabuddhahood, no Buddha and no one approaching Buddhahood. Why? Because the nature of dharmas is without conditions, neither arising nor ceasing, and has no abiding place. Therefore, bodhisattva mahāsattvas should, with these three kinds of transfer and three kinds of pure merit, together with all sentient beings, transfer it to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Having made this transfer, the bodhisattva further makes this vow: 'Wherever I am born, may I always encounter the Buddhas and attain the most profound samādhi. May I behold countless Buddhas, accomplish extensive learning and pure wisdom, and with firm vows never abandon any sentient being.'"

When this Dharma was taught, a hundred thousand devas all vowed to be born in the land of Akṣobhya Buddha.

At that time the Buddha told the Elder Ānanda: "When I just now taught this profound Dharma, a hundred thousand devas all vowed to be born in the land of Akṣobhya Buddha. Ānanda, you should know that when they end their lives here, they shall all be born in the wondrous, joyful land of Akṣobhya Buddha. From one Buddha-land to another they will go, making offerings to the Buddhas and receiving the right Dharma, attaining dhāraṇī and practicing as taught. They will all accomplish inconceivable wisdom. In the defiled lands of the five turbidities they shall become Buddhas, all bearing the same name: King of Sweet Dew Sound Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened One. You should know that when those devas received this prophecy, a hundred thousand sentient beings all gave rise to the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

At that time Śakra, lord of the devas, addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! As I understand the meaning of what the Buddha has said, this should be known as the path of great merit, of immeasurable merit, of boundless merit."

The Buddha said: "Kauśika! It is because this Dharma is ultimately pure."

"World-Honored One! What should this sūtra be called? How should we receive and uphold it?"

The Buddha told Śakra, lord of the devas: "Kauśika! This sūtra is called 'The Great Transfer of Merit.' It is also called 'The Profound Dharma-Nature Transfer.' You should receive and uphold it thus."

The Buddha told Kauśika: "If there are good men or good women who study this transfer of merit, you should know that these persons will certainly attain the patient acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas, and will be able to ferry across those not yet ferried, bringing peace and joy to a hundred thousand immeasurable sentient beings."

When this Dharma was taught, the assembly of bhikṣus, Śakra, Brahmā, devas, humans, and asuras, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully received it and practiced accordingly.
