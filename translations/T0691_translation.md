# 最無比經
## The Most Incomparable Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0691

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling at the Śrāvastī Jetavana, in Anāthapiṇḍada's park, together with an immeasurable assembly of great monks, immeasurable bodhisattvas, devas, humans, and others.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda was sitting alone in a quiet place, thinking and reflecting. He had this thought: "If a good man or good woman, with a mind of pure faith, takes the three refuges and says these words: 'I, [so-and-so], now take refuge in the Buddha, most honored among two-footed beings; take refuge in the Dharma, most honored among those free from desire; take refuge in the Sangha, most honored among assemblies,' how much merit does such a person who takes refuge obtain?"

Having had this thought, at sunrise he rose from his seat at his dwelling place, arranged his robes, and calmly went to where the Tathāgata was. Arriving there, he prostrated at the feet of the World-Honored One, bared one shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms in reverence, and addressed the Buddha, saying: "Just now, while I was sitting alone in a quiet place, thinking and reflecting, I had this thought: 'If a good man or good woman, with a mind of pure faith, takes the three refuges and says these words: "I, [so-and-so], now take refuge in the Buddha, most honored among two-footed beings; take refuge in the Dharma, most honored among those free from desire; take refuge in the Sangha, most honored among assemblies," how much merit does such a person who takes refuge obtain?' I have not been able to comprehend this. I humbly request that the World-Honored One, out of compassion, explain this for us, so that all beings may attain right understanding."

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Ānanda: "Excellent, excellent! You are truly possessed of marvelous wisdom, able to think well and ask the Tathāgata about this matter. Listen carefully now! Reflect upon it with utmost attention. I shall explain it for you in detail."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Just so, World-Honored One! I am glad and eager to hear."

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Ānanda: "You should know that this continent of Jambudvīpa measures seven thousand yojanas in circumference, broad in the north and narrow in the south, shaped like a chariot box, and the faces of its people are likewise. Suppose that within it, all were combined into a single mass of sugarcane groves, or reed groves, or bamboo groves, or rice paddies, or sesame fields, filling it completely without any gaps. Suppose further that stream-enterers filled all of Jambudvīpa, packed tightly without any gaps, like the sugarcane and the rest. Suppose that good men or good women made offerings to each and every one of these stream-enterers for a full one hundred years with the finest worldly pleasures: robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicines, serving them with reverence and prostrations. And after each one of those stream-enterers passed into nirvana, their bodies were properly cremated, their relics collected, and stūpas were erected for them, lofty and grand and adorned. And all manner of anointing incense, powdered incense, fragrant incense, flower garlands, fine banners and canopies, jeweled standards, music, lamps, and candlelight were offered in their praise. What do you think? Would the merit generated from these causes and conditions be great or not?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Extremely great, World-Honored One! Extremely great, Sugata!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, with a mind of pure faith, says these words: 'I, [so-and-so], now take refuge in the Buddha, most honored among two-footed beings; take refuge in the Dharma, most honored among those free from desire; take refuge in the Sangha, most honored among assemblies,' that good man or good woman obtains immeasurable merit. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit from offerings described above, compared to the merit generated by these three refuges, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "Set aside this southern continent of Jambudvīpa. To the east lies the continent of Videha, measuring eight thousand yojanas in circumference, shaped like a half-moon, and the faces of its people are likewise. Suppose that within it, all were combined into a single mass of sugarcane groves, or reed groves, or bamboo groves, or rice paddies, or sesame fields, filling it completely without any gaps. Suppose further that once-returners filled all of eastern Videha, packed tightly without any gaps, like the sugarcane and the rest. Suppose that good men or good women made offerings to each and every one of these once-returners for a full two hundred years with the finest worldly pleasures: robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicines, serving them with reverence and prostrations. And after each one of those once-returners passed into nirvana, their bodies were properly cremated, their relics collected, and stūpas were erected for them, lofty and grand and adorned. And all manner of anointing incense, powdered incense, fragrant incense, flower garlands, fine banners and canopies, jeweled standards, music, lamps, and candlelight were offered in their praise. What do you think? Would the merit generated from these causes and conditions be great or not?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Extremely great, World-Honored One! Extremely great, Sugata!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, with a mind of pure faith, says these words: 'I, [so-and-so], now take refuge in the Buddha, most honored among two-footed beings; take refuge in the Dharma, most honored among those free from desire; take refuge in the Sangha, most honored among assemblies,' that good man or good woman obtains merit even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit from offerings described above, compared to the merit generated by these three refuges, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "Set aside the eastern continent of Videha. To the west lies the continent of Godānīya, measuring nine thousand yojanas in circumference, shaped like the disk of the sun, and the faces of its people are likewise. Suppose that within it, all were combined into a single mass of sugarcane groves, or reed groves, or bamboo groves, or rice paddies, or sesame fields, filling it completely without any gaps. Suppose further that non-returners filled all of western Godānīya, packed tightly without any gaps, like the sugarcane and the rest. Suppose that good men or good women made offerings to each and every one of these non-returners for a full three hundred years with the finest worldly pleasures: robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicines, serving them with reverence and prostrations. And after each one of those non-returners passed into nirvana, their bodies were properly cremated, their relics collected, and stūpas were erected for them, lofty and grand and adorned. And all manner of anointing incense, powdered incense, fragrant incense, flower garlands, fine banners and canopies, jeweled standards, music, lamps, and candlelight were offered in their praise. What do you think? Would the merit generated from these causes and conditions be great or not?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Extremely great, World-Honored One! Extremely great, Sugata!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, with a mind of pure faith, says these words: 'I, [so-and-so], now take refuge in the Buddha, most honored among two-footed beings; take refuge in the Dharma, most honored among those free from desire; take refuge in the Sangha, most honored among assemblies,' that good man or good woman obtains merit even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit from offerings described above, compared to the merit generated by these three refuges, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "Set aside the western continent of Godānīya. To the north lies the great continent of Uttarakuru, measuring ten thousand yojanas in circumference, its land square in shape, and the faces of its people are likewise. Suppose that within it, all were combined into a single mass of sugarcane groves, or reed groves, or bamboo groves, or rice paddies, or sesame fields, filling it completely without any gaps. Suppose further that arhats filled all of northern Uttarakuru: those who have exhausted all defilements, who are free from afflictions, whose minds have attained mastery, who are rightly liberated, who possess right knowledge and liberation, whose minds are well-tamed like great dragon-elephants, who have accomplished what was to be done, who have cast off all heavy burdens, who have attained their own benefit, who are free from further existence, who have completed the holy life, whose bodies and minds are unobstructed, who know the natures of sentient beings, who possess the six supernormal powers and have realized the eight liberations. These were packed tightly without any gaps, like the sugarcane and the rest. Suppose that good men or good women made offerings to each and every one of these arhats for a full four hundred years with the finest worldly pleasures: robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicines, serving them with reverence and prostrations. And after each one of those arhats passed into nirvana, their bodies were properly cremated, their relics collected, and stūpas were erected for them, lofty and grand and adorned. And all manner of anointing incense, powdered incense, fragrant incense, flower garlands, fine banners and canopies, jeweled standards, music, lamps, and candlelight were offered in their praise. What do you think? Would the merit generated from these causes and conditions be great or not?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Extremely great, World-Honored One! Extremely great, Sugata!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, with a mind of pure faith, says these words: 'I, [so-and-so], now take refuge in the Buddha, most honored among two-footed beings; take refuge in the Dharma, most honored among those free from desire; take refuge in the Sangha, most honored among assemblies,' that good man or good woman obtains merit even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit from offerings described above, compared to the merit generated by these three refuges, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "Set aside the northern continent of Uttarakuru. Altogether, the four great continents have an iron ring mountain encircling them, illuminated by a single sun and moon. Suppose that within this entire realm all mountains and great seas were removed and combined into a single mass of sugarcane groves, or reed groves, or bamboo groves, or rice paddies, or sesame fields, filling it completely without any gaps. Suppose further that pratyekabuddhas, each endowed with various virtues surpassing those of the arhat, filled the entire iron-ring world, packed tightly without any gaps, like the sugarcane and the rest. Suppose that good men or good women made offerings to each and every one of these pratyekabuddhas for a full ten thousand years with the finest worldly pleasures: robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicines, serving them with reverence and prostrations. And after each one of those pratyekabuddhas passed into nirvana, stūpas were erected for them, lofty and grand and adorned. And all manner of anointing incense, powdered incense, fragrant incense, flower garlands, fine banners and canopies, jeweled standards, music, lamps, and candlelight were offered in their praise. What do you think? Would the merit generated from these causes and conditions be great or not?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Extremely great, World-Honored One! Extremely great, Sugata!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, with a mind of pure faith, says these words: 'I, [so-and-so], now take refuge in the Buddha, most honored among two-footed beings; take refuge in the Dharma, most honored among those free from desire; take refuge in the Sangha, most honored among assemblies,' that good man or good woman obtains merit even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit from offerings described above, compared to the merit generated by these three refuges, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "Set aside this one world of four continents illuminated by a single sun and moon. Beyond this there is a small chiliocosm, encircled by an iron ring mountain, containing within it a thousand suns, a thousand moons, a thousand great seas, a thousand Mount Sumerus, king of precious mountains, a thousand seven golden mountains and ring mountains, a thousand Jambudvīpas, a thousand Videhas, a thousand Godānīyas, a thousand Uttarakurus, a thousand heavens of the Four Kings, a thousand heavens of the Thirty-Three, a thousand Yāma heavens, a thousand Tuṣita heavens, a thousand Nirmāṇarati heavens, a thousand Paranirmitavaśavartin heavens, a thousand Brahmā-assembly heavens, with one Great Brahmā sovereign over them all. This is called a small chiliocosm. Beyond this there is a medium dichiliocosm, encircled by an iron ring mountain, containing within it a thousand small chiliocosms. This is called a medium dichiliocosm. Beyond this there is a great trichiliocosm, encircled by an iron ring mountain, containing within it a thousand medium dichiliocosms. This is called the great trichiliocosm. Suppose that within it all mountains and great seas were removed and combined into a single mass of sugarcane groves, or reed groves, or bamboo groves, or rice paddies, or sesame fields, filling it completely without any gaps. Suppose further that buddhas, tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly enlightened ones, filled the entire trichiliocosm, packed tightly without any gaps, like the sugarcane and the rest. Suppose that good men or good women made offerings to each and every one of these tathāgatas for a full twenty thousand years with all the finest worldly pleasures: robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicines, serving them with reverence and prostrations. And after each one of those tathāgatas passed into nirvana, their bodies were properly cremated, their buddha-relics collected, and stūpas were erected for them, lofty and grand and adorned. And all manner of anointing incense, powdered incense, fragrant incense, flower garlands, fine banners and canopies, jeweled standards, music, lamps, and candlelight were offered in their praise. What do you think? Would the merit generated from these causes and conditions be great or not?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Extremely great, World-Honored One! Extremely great, Sugata!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman made offerings to even a single buddha for twenty thousand years with all the finest worldly pleasures: robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicines, serving with reverence and prostrations; and after that buddha's nirvana, properly cremated the body, collected the buddha-relics and erected a stūpa, lofty and grand and adorned, also offering all manner of anointing incense, powdered incense, fragrant incense, flower garlands, fine banners and canopies, jeweled standards, music, lamps, and candlelight in praise and offering; such merit would already be immeasurable, boundless, and incalculable. How much more so would the merit of filling the entire great trichiliocosm with buddhas, tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly enlightened ones, and making offerings for twenty thousand years as described above? That merit would be even more immeasurably, boundlessly, and incalculably great."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, with a mind of pure faith, says these words: 'I, [so-and-so], now take refuge in the Buddha, most honored among two-footed beings; take refuge in the Dharma, most honored among those free from desire; take refuge in the Sangha, most honored among assemblies,' that good man or good woman obtains merit even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit from offerings described above, compared to the merit generated by these three refuges, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, having taken refuge in the Buddha, having taken refuge in the Dharma, having taken refuge in the Sangha, can further practice the ten wholesome deeds even for a mere snap of the fingers, the merit obtained from this cause is even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit generated by merely taking the three refuges as described above, compared to the merit of additionally cultivating the ten wholesome deeds for a snap of the fingers, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, having taken refuge in the Buddha, having taken refuge in the Dharma, having taken refuge in the Sangha, can further observe the eight precepts for one day and one night, the merit obtained is even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit of the ten wholesome deeds practiced for a snap of the fingers as described above, compared to the merit of the eight precepts observed for one day and one night, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, having taken refuge in the Buddha, having taken refuge in the Dharma, having taken refuge in the Sangha, can further observe the five precepts for their entire life, the merit obtained is even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit of the eight precepts observed for one day and one night as described above, compared to the merit of observing the five precepts for one's entire life, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman, having taken refuge in the Buddha, having taken refuge in the Dharma, having taken refuge in the Sangha, can further observe the śrāmaṇera or śrāmaṇerī precepts for their entire life, the merit obtained is even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit described above, compared to this merit, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "If one can further observe the śikṣamāṇā precepts for one's entire life, the merit obtained is even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit described above, compared to this merit, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "If one can further observe the bhikṣuṇī precepts for one's entire life, the merit obtained is even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit described above, compared to this merit, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "If one can further observe the great bhikṣu precepts for one's entire life, the merit obtained is even more surpassing. You should know, Ānanda, that the merit described above, compared to this merit, does not amount to even one hundredth, nor one thousandth, nor one hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction calculable by number, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, nor even one upaniṣad part."

At that time, the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman can generate the mind of supreme, perfect enlightenment (anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi) and observe the bodhisattva's threefold collection of pure precepts throughout all future ages, without deficiency or transgression, the merit obtained is immeasurable and boundless, inconceivable and beyond analogy, most surpassing, most honored, most supreme, and most wondrous."

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, hearing the Buddha explain the immeasurable, boundless merit obtained from taking the three refuges up through the bodhisattva's threefold collection of pure precepts, was overjoyed and exclaimed that it was unprecedented. He addressed the Buddha, saying: "How marvelous, World-Honored One! This teaching is the most surpassing, rare, and inconceivable. It illuminates the most profound meaning, and its merit is vast and difficult to measure. By what name should it be known? I shall uphold it."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This teaching is called 'The Most Incomparable,' a comparison of the true and actual merits of various kinds. You should uphold it by this name. Why? Because this sūtra teaches the most incomparable Dharma, which is exceedingly difficult for anyone in all the worlds to hear."

When the Bhagavān had finished speaking this sūtra, the Venerable Ānanda, the great assembly of monks, the immeasurable bodhisattvas, and all the devas, humans, asuras, and others who heard what the Buddha taught were all filled with great joy, exclaiming that it was unprecedented. They accepted it with faith and put it into practice.
