# 佛說內藏百寶經
## Sūtra of the Hundred Treasures of the Inner Treasury, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0807

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by Lokakṣema (支婁迦讖)

---

The Buddha was at Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa. At that time there were twelve thousand bhikṣus and seventy-two thousand bodhisattvas, all seated together.

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva rose from his seat and came forward to address the Buddha: "Now that the great assembly of bodhisattvas has gathered, we wish to hear from the Buddha about the matters pertaining to the practice of upāya-kauśalya. By what means does a bodhisattva come to distinguish and understand inner and outer matters?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Listen to what I shall teach. The Buddha enters the teaching in accordance with the conventions and customs of the world. The Buddha's wisdom is immeasurable, and the sūtras are beyond reckoning. Not even the arhats and pratyekabuddhas can know them; how much less can worldly people hear and understand? What worldly people do is all characterized by attachment, but what the Buddha does is free from all attachment. Only a Buddha can fully know another Buddha. As stated in the Buddha's sūtras, so are the Buddha's inner and outer mind and wisdom.

"By what means does the Buddha appear in the world? How should one come to understand this? Although the Buddha is in the world, he is never attached to anything within it, yet he creates illumination for the entire world. What his body does, what his mouth speaks, and what his mind contemplates: he enters according to the conventions and customs of the world. He practices inner matters, practices the dharmas of all the Buddhas. None can surpass what he practices; none can match the Buddha's conduct. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, yet none can know this.

"Because the Buddha has compassion for the people of the ten directions, he universally manifests illumination. According to what the world delights in, he teaches the sūtras and the Dharma.

"The bodhisattva is not born from the union of father and mother; his body is a transformation. Like a magician who conjures the appearance of parents, he enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's light is immeasurable, illuminating the ten directions. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting a seven-foot aura.

"The Buddha has never placed his feet upon the ground, yet his marks appear upon the earth. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha, through numberless millions of asaṃkhyeya kalpas, has perfected prajñā-pāramitā. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting the form of a small child.

"The Buddha illuminates the people of the ten directions who dwell in the darkness of sensual desire. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, also manifesting a wife and children.

"When the bodhisattva is born and touches the ground, he himself declares: 'In the heavens above and below, none surpasses me. I shall deliver the people of the ten directions.' He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, also appearing as a prince. When he sat beneath the tree in Jambudvīpa and arose from there, he practiced austerities for six years. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting to people such diligent effort.

"When the Buddha's Way was about to be accomplished, he sat alone beneath the tree. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, emitting light so that Māra would know of it.

"The Buddha's wisdom was already complete; all was equal, with none able to surpass it. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, attaining the Buddha's seat of peace, manifesting this to the world.

"Although he had attained Buddhahood, out of compassion for the people of the ten directions, he was to teach the sūtras and liberate them. Brahmā and Śakra besought the Buddha with compassion, and for the sake of the people he caused the Buddha to teach the sūtras. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's wisdom cannot be increased or diminished. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting wisdom as if it were greater or lesser.

"The Buddha has nowhere from which he comes, nor any place to which he goes; he abides as originally without anything. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world; people call the Buddha one who comes and goes, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha has no fixed abode; like space, he has no fixed dwelling. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world; people call the Buddha one who abides, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's feet are like lotuses that do not receive dust or defilement, yet the Buddha washes his feet. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's body is like gold that does not receive dust or defilement, yet the Buddha bathes. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's mouth is fundamentally pure, fragrant as the scent of saffron, yet the Buddha uses a tooth-stick and rinses his mouth. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha is never hungry, yet out of compassion for the people of the ten directions, he manifests hunger. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's body is like vajra, pure and without blemish; he has no excrement. Yet he manifests to people the acts of relieving oneself. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's body never ages; it possesses only accumulated virtues. Yet he manifests the aging of the body. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's body has never been ill, yet he manifests illness, calls for a physician, and takes medicine. Those who give medicine to the Buddha gain immeasurable merit. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's power is irresistible; with a single finger he can move the buddha-lands of the ten directions. Yet he manifests to people a body that is thin, weak, and exhausted. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"In a single thought-moment the Buddha can fly to incalculable buddha-lands, yet he manifests exhaustion. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's body is like an illusion; his body is the sūtras and the Dharma. Yet he manifests to people a body of impure substances. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha fundamentally has nothing. According to what the world delights in, he manifests possessions. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's bodily strength is immeasurable; he never actually sits, rises, walks, lies down, or goes out. Yet he manifests to people sitting, rising, walking, lying down, and going out. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's body is never moved by cold or heat, yet he obtains warmth in cold, coolness in heat, and shade. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha is like space, yet he manifests to people as one who always wears clothing and never removes it, like the people of the Brahma Heaven who always wear garments. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's head never loses hair; his Dharma merely shows this to people. No one has ever seen someone come with a razor to shave the Buddha's head. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha never needs to sit, yet he manifests to people the hardship of sitting upon a stone. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's throat possesses the mark of flavor and has never been hungry. Yet out of compassion for the people of the ten directions, when someone offers coarse and unpleasant food, he accepts it all. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's stores of meritorious blessings are inexhaustible, and none can surpass him. Yet the Buddha enters a city to collect alms and comes out with an empty bowl. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's stores of meritorious blessings are inexhaustible. If he wished to obtain the finest garments in the heavens above and below, he could have them all. Yet he deliberately wears patched robes. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"If the Buddha wished to obtain dwellings, beds, bedding, or the precious jewels and palaces of the heavens above and below, he could have them all. Yet he manifests as a worldly person, sitting exposed to the elements upon a grass mat in deep contemplation. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"With his awesome spiritual power, the Buddha could blow the ocean dry. Yet when he sees rain falling, he takes up an umbrella. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"In a single thought-moment, the Buddha can cause numberless millions of Māras not to know the Buddha's location. Yet he manifests to people as one troubled by Māra. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha perfectly comprehends all the sūtras and Dharma of the incalculable Buddhas of the ten directions. Yet he manifests to people as one who asks questions back and forth. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"Out of compassion for the ten directions, the Buddha appears in the world wishing to teach and liberate. He also manifests to people that those who make offerings gain immeasurable merit. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha has no origin. According to what the world delights in, he manifests forms. Though fundamentally one, he enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"If one gathered together the sounds of thunder and lightning throughout the ten directions into a single sound, it could not stir a single hair of the Buddha. Yet he manifests entering into meditation and samādhi in a place of silence. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"All the sūtras and Dharma are fundamentally without names. The Buddha manifests to people sūtras and teachings beyond number. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's concepts and thoughts are fundamentally all severed; he never departs from samādhi. Yet he manifests to people as one who teaches many kinds of sūtras and Dharma. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's past actions, whether wholesome or unwholesome, cannot be received in a past life but will inevitably be received in a future life. The Buddha manifests to people that one's own actions produce one's own results. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha knows that the world is fundamentally without anything. People and all their possessions are fundamentally without form. Yet the Buddha manifests as one who liberates incalculable people. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha knows that all the sūtras and Dharma are fundamentally empty and originally without any substance. Yet he manifests to people the existence of repeated death and rebirth. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"Fundamentally there are no matters of this life or the next, yet the Buddha manifests to people the existence of this life and the next. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The five aggregates, the six sense-objects, and the four elements combine as one, yet are fundamentally without existence. The Buddha manifests to people the desire-realm, the form-realm, and the formless realm. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"Fundamentally there are no persons of the past, the future, or the present. The Buddha manifests people dying and being reborn within the five destinies. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha manifests all things in order to show the foolish that their delusion can be entirely exhausted. He manifests to people the original practice of generosity. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The sūtras and Dharma of the past, future, and present: the Buddha fully knows their root. The Buddha manifests to people what can be spoken and what cannot be spoken. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha knows that all the sūtras, from their origin to their conclusion, are entirely profound. Yet the Buddha distinguishes them, teaching each according to its own subject. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's speech is without any contradictory teaching. Yet according to what people can understand, he teaches the Four Noble Truths. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Saṃgha of bhikṣus is difficult to destroy. Even if numberless millions of Māras and all manner of evils were to come, they could not break the Saṃgha. Yet the Buddha manifests to people the breaking of the Saṃgha. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The sūtras and Dharma fundamentally have nothing from which to be learned, nor is there anyone who does not learn. Yet the Buddha manifests to people the sūtras and Dharma, showing what is precept-keeping and what is not. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"Empty space is also without bondage and without liberation from the world. Yet the Buddha manifests to people the act of liberation. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's parinirvāṇa leads nowhere. The arhat's parinirvāṇa also leads nowhere. Yet the Buddha teaches the Dharma and manifests this to people. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha teaches that there is no arising and no ceasing; this is the essential point. There is nothing gained and nothing lost. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha teaches that nirvāṇa is like a lamp extinguished: without form, only a name. The sūtras and Dharma are indestructible. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha fully knows that all the sūtras and Dharma are fundamentally without form. Yet the Buddha manifests to people by teaching sūtras and Dharma in great abundance. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The minds of all the Buddhas are without any obstruction; they never depart from samādhi. Yet they manifest to people as ones who generate thoughts. They enter according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"All the Buddha's evils are entirely exhausted; he possesses only complete merit and virtue. Yet the Buddha manifests to people as if his evils were not yet exhausted. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's bodily conduct, verbal speech, and mental contemplation are always accompanied by wisdom; this is fundamental. Yet the Buddha manifests to people by having bhikṣus teach the sūtras while he himself wishes to listen. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's wisdom and understanding are inexhaustible. The past, future, and present are fundamentally empty. Yet the Buddha manifests to people by teaching the sūtras and Dharma according to what each delights in. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"All the Buddhas are united in a single body; their body is the sūtras and the Dharma. Yet the Buddha manifests to people by teaching the sūtras and Dharma. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"Buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, arhats, and those who have not yet attained the Way: he manifests the acts of death, rebirth, and attainment of nirvāṇa. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha manifests to the arhats by teaching the sūtras and Dharma in their complete form. Although he knows their completeness, it does not reach the level of omniscience. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's wisdom is unsurpassed. He fully knows that there is no past, no future, and no present. Yet the Buddha manifests the teaching of sūtras and Dharma according to causes and conditions. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"If anyone wishes to know the Buddha and thoroughly comprehend the Buddha-Dharma, the original ground of the sūtras where all things enter, such a person truly understands the Buddha. The Buddha enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The bodhisattva neither enters the mother's womb nor emerges from the mother's womb. Why? Because the original ground of the sūtras and Dharma is such that there is nothing it does not pervade. Yet the bodhisattva manifests to people as entering the mother's womb. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"In the Dharma of the unborn there is bliss. All the sūtras and Dharma are likewise without any arising. Yet the bodhisattva manifests to people as being born for the first time. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The bodhisattva's mother's womb is empty and settled, containing and receiving the realm of a single Buddha. Each bodhisattva manifests to people the conditions of birth. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's transformed emanations pervade incalculable, innumerable buddha-lands, reaching everywhere. Yet the Buddha's body neither increases nor decreases. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The bodhisattva constantly manifests birth without cease. He constantly dwells in the original state of nonexistence, without any struggle. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's wisdom, merit, and spiritual power are beyond reckoning. Yet the Buddha manifests to people as having a limited lifespan, enabling people to know this. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The original ground of the sūtras and Dharma is unsurpassable, because the past, future, and present are all empty. Yet the Buddha manifests to people as one who honors the sūtras and Dharma. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"Originally there is no arising and no ceasing; the original ground of the sūtras pervades all. Yet the Buddha manifests to people that realms are destroyed and ruined. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The sūtras and Dharma are fundamentally without any arising; they abide without form. Yet the Buddha manifests the sūtras and Dharma. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"Contemplating the original: there is also nothing seen, nothing viewed. Yet the Buddha sees all people, fully sees, fully comprehends, and fully knows. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"All the sūtras and Dharma have no creator and nothing from which they arise. Yet the Buddha manifests to people that the sūtras and Dharma originally have no arising. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"Nirvāṇa and emptiness have no form or sound, nor even a name. Yet the Buddha manifests the four elements, form, and sound. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's power has no equal or parallel; it is beyond reckoning and never diminishes. Yet he manifests to people as one who ages and seeks others' assistance. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's compassion pervades all equally; there is never a time of hardship or extremity. Yet the Buddha manifests that one should not associate with the foolish. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha's merits and virtues are all accomplished and complete. Yet the Buddha manifests as if he had only a small portion of merit. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"There is no origin from which things arise, nor is anything produced from within. Yet the Buddha manifests the three gates of liberation. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha manifests originally and ultimately without any obstruction. His stores of meritorious blessings are unsurpassed. Yet the Buddha manifests to people that when someone gives, he accepts only what is sufficient and no more, showing that he is without greed. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"When a person with sincere and wholesome intentions thinks of the Buddha, the Buddha immediately appears before them. Yet the Buddha has no fixed location. The Buddha manifests as one who practices the bodhisattva path. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Buddha has liberated incalculable asaṃkhyeya people, yet he has not liberated a single person. Why? Because originally there is nothing. He enters according to the conventions and customs of the world, manifesting things in this way.

"The Sūtra of the Hundred Treasures of the Buddha's Inner Treasury: whoever hears it will surely obtain peace. It transcends numberless millions of kalpas and immeasurable time. Such is what the bodhisattva practices in life after life, for the sake of the people of the ten directions. The bodhisattva practices the sūtras and precepts in every life, never once transgressing them. Through this the bodhisattva attains the Buddha's wisdom. Those who accord with this practice attain Buddhahood swiftly. The bodhisattva practices compassion, benefiting the ten directions without end. Those who engage in this practice attain Buddhahood swiftly. What person, hearing this, would not practice accordingly? The Buddha's awesome spiritual power is vast and majestic. All who hear the sūtras and Dharma will without exception be ferried across."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva and all the bodhisattvas were greatly delighted. They came forward, bowed to the Buddha, and departed.
