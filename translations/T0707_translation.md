# 佛說出家功德經
## Sūtra of the Merit of Going Forth, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0707

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Vaiśālī. When the time for eating arrived, he entered the city to beg for food. At that time, within the city of Vaiśālī, there was a Licchavi prince named Vairambhyena. Just as a deva enjoys himself with celestial maidens, so this prince was in his upper pavilion enjoying himself with his attendant women, indulging in sensual pleasures in the same way.

At that time the World-Honored One, with his omniscient knowledge, heard the sounds of their merrymaking and said to Ānanda: "I know that this person who delights in the five sensual pleasures will before long reach the end of his life. After seven days have passed, he will have to leave behind such companionship and happiness, and will certainly die. Ānanda! If this person does not abandon his sensual pleasures and does not go forth from the household life, at the end of his life he may fall into the hells."

At that time Ānanda, reverently upholding the Buddha's instruction and wishing to benefit this prince, went in due order to his residence. When the prince heard that Ānanda was outside, he came out to welcome him with respect and devotion and invited Ānanda inside to sit. After sitting for a short while, the prince rose with a reverent mind and said to Ānanda: "Excellent! My good friend has come; now is the right time. Seeing you today, I am overjoyed with delight. Your name is Ānanda, 'Joy'; you should now teach and instruct me in the Dharma the Buddha has taught, so that I may be joyful." The prince made this request three times. But Ānanda, wishing to create a great benefit, remained silent and said nothing.

The prince spoke again: "The Vaidya Muni, the Great Sage who benefits all sentient beings, what resentment could he have that you remain silent and say nothing? Not even the slightest instruction!"

Then the Third Master, the one who upholds the Buddha's Dharma treasury and benefits the world, sorrowfully said: "Listen well now! After seven days, you will reach the end of your life. If within these five sensual pleasures you cannot awaken and do not go forth from the household life, at the end of your life you may fall into the hells. The Buddha, the Omniscient One, with right speech and right words, has declared it thus concerning you. It is like a fire burning fuel: it never goes forth in vain. Reflect on this carefully!"

When the prince heard these words, he was greatly distressed and fearful, troubled and unhappy. He accepted Ānanda's instruction: "I shall go forth. But please grant me six more days to enjoy my pleasures, and on the seventh day I shall take leave of my family and dependents and will certainly go forth." Ānanda consented.

On the seventh day, fearing birth and death, he sought the Buddha and went forth. The Buddha permitted it. For one day and one night he cultivated and upheld the pure precepts, and then his life came to an end. After the cremation rites were completed, the Venerable Ānanda, together with the prince's family, went to the Buddha and said: "World-Honored One! This bhikṣu Vairambhyena has now reached the end of his life. Where has his spirit been born?"

Then the Buddha, the World-Honored One, Teacher of Devas and Humans, the Omniscient One, with the great Brahmā voice surpassing the sounds of thundering drums, the kalaviṅka bird, and all wondrous sounds, proclaimed to Ānanda with the eight kinds of voice: "This bhikṣu Vairambhyena, fearing the sufferings of birth, death, and the hells, abandoned his desires and went forth. Because he upheld the pure precepts for one day and one night, after leaving this world he was born in the Heaven of the Four Great Kings as a son of the Northern Heavenly King Vaiśravaṇa, where he freely enjoys the five sensual pleasures, indulging in pleasures with celestial maidens, for a lifespan of five hundred years. After five hundred years, at the end of his life he is reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven as a son of Śakra, where he fully enjoys the five sensual pleasures, the supreme delights of that heaven, with wondrous celestial maidens freely for one thousand years. When his life there ends, he is born in the Yāma heaven as a son of the Yāma King, where he freely enjoys the heavenly delights of form, sound, scent, taste, and touch for two thousand years. At the end of his life he is born as a son of the Tuṣita King, where he freely enjoys the five sensual pleasures, merely by gazing at one another his desires are satisfied, and he constantly discusses the Dharma, liberation, and wisdom, for the full heavenly lifespan of four thousand years. After four thousand years, at the end of his life he is born in the Nirmāṇarati heaven as a son of that heavenly king, enjoying the various wondrous delights of the five pleasures, freely transforming to fulfill his wishes among the celestial maidens for eight thousand years. After eight thousand years, at the end of his life he is born in the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven as a son of that heavenly king. The pleasures of this sixth heaven surpass those of the five heavens below. Born there, he enjoys the most wondrous delights, a treasury of all pleasures. When enjoying these pleasures, his mind is deeply intoxicated. He fully enjoys all the wondrous supreme pleasures for sixteen thousand years. Thus enjoying pleasures, he passes back and forth through the six desire heavens seven times. This Vairambhyena, because of going forth for one day and one night, for a full twenty kalpas does not fall into the hells, the realm of hungry ghosts, or the animal realm, but is always born among devas and humans, naturally receiving blessings. Finally, among humans, he is born in a wealthy and happy family, with abundant riches and treasures. When his prime years have passed and his faculties have matured, fearing the afflictions of birth, old age, sickness, and death, he grows weary of the world and goes forth, shaves his head, and dons the Dharma robes. With diligent effort he cultivates the four deportments, constantly practices right mindfulness, and contemplates the five aggregates as suffering, empty, and without self. Understanding the Dharma of causes and conditions, he becomes a pratyekabuddha named Vailuḍi. At that time he emits a great radiance, and many humans and devas plant wholesome roots, enabling all sentient beings to sow the causes and conditions for liberation through the three vehicles."

At that time Ānanda joined his palms and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If someone enables another person to go forth from the household life, or if there is someone who has gone forth and one provides whatever they need, how much merit does one obtain? And if someone destroys the conditions for another's going forth, what retribution of suffering does one receive? I humbly wish the World-Honored One to explain this fully!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Even if for a full hundred years you were to ask me about this matter, and I, with my inexhaustible wisdom, were to explain this person's merit to you broadly for a full hundred years, excluding only mealtimes, I still could not exhaust it. This person is constantly born in the heavens and among humans, always becomes a king of nations, and enjoys the pleasures of devas and humans. If there is someone who in this teaching of the śramaṇa enables another to go forth, or who assists and supports the conditions for going forth, they will always enjoy happiness in the cycle of birth and death. Even if I were to speak of their merit for a full hundred years, it could not be exhausted. Therefore, Ānanda, even if you were to ask me for a full hundred years throughout your entire life, and I were to speak of this merit until my nirvāṇa, it still could not be exhausted."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If there is someone who destroys the conditions for another's going forth, that person robs an inexhaustible treasury of wholesome merit and destroys the thirty-seven factors of the path to awakening and the cause of nirvāṇa. If anyone wishes to destroy the conditions for going forth, they should carefully consider these matters. Why? Because of this wrongful karma, one falls into the hells, always blind and without eyes, suffering the utmost torment. If born as an animal, one is also always born blind. If born among hungry ghosts, one is also always born blind. After long suffering in the three evil paths, one finally gains release. If born as a human, one is blind from within the mother's womb, blind from the time of conception. Even if you were to ask about this matter for a hundred years, and I were to explain this retribution of suffering with my inexhaustible wisdom for a hundred years, it still could not be exhausted. In all four paths of rebirth, one is born always blind. I would never declare that such a person will have a time of liberation. Why? All because of destroying the conditions for going forth.

"One may have accomplished boundless merit, yet because of destroying such a wholesome cause, one receives immeasurable suffering, all because of obstructing going forth. Within this pure mirror of wisdom, for the sake of liberation and all wholesome dharmas, if one sees someone going forth, upholding pure precepts, and heading toward the place of liberation, and one destroys their going forth by creating obstacles, because of this cause and condition, one is born always blind, unable to see nirvāṇa, all because of destroying going forth. One should contemplate ignorance and the other twelve links of dependent arising and should attain liberation; but because of destroying another's eye of wisdom, because of destroying the conditions for going forth and covering the eye of discernment, from birth to birth one is always blind and without eyes, unable to see the three realms, all because of obstructing going forth. Through going forth one should see the five aggregates and the twenty views of self, and enter the right path; but because of destroying the cause of going forth and wrecking right view, one is born always blind and cannot see the right path. Through going forth one should see the assemblage of all dharmas, the abode of wholesome dharmas, and should contemplate the pure Dharma-body of all the Buddhas; but because of destroying the wholesome conditions for going forth, one is born always blind and cannot behold the Dharma-body of the Buddha. Through the cause of going forth one should be endowed with the appearance of a śramaṇa and the observance of precepts, becoming a pure field of merit and planting the seeds of the Buddha Way; but because of destroying going forth, all hope of wholesome dharmas is cut off. Because of this sinful condition, from birth to birth one is always blind, all because of destroying going forth. Through going forth one should skillfully contemplate all body and mind as suffering, impermanent, without self, and impure; but by destroying another's going forth and creating obstacles, one destroys this eye. Because this eye is destroyed, one cannot see the four paths, the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of spiritual power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, and the noble eightfold path leading to the city of nirvāṇa. Because of this sinful condition, one is born always blind, and one cannot even see the pure wholesome dharmas of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness that lead to the city of nirvāṇa.

"Therefore the person of wisdom, knowing the one who goes forth, should bring about such wholesome dharmas and should not destroy the conditions for wholesome dharmas and incur such transgressions. Whoever destroys the conditions for another's going forth, the right view of the śramaṇa, will never be able to see the city of nirvāṇa. From birth to birth one is always blind.

"Furthermore, if there is someone who, for a full hundred kalpas, goes forth in other regions and upholds the pure precepts; and if there is another person who, here in this Jambudvīpa, goes forth and upholds the precepts for even one day and one night, or even just a moment, with pure going forth: the former, who upheld the precepts through going forth for a hundred kalpas, does not equal even one sixteenth part of the latter.

"If there is someone who, in a perverted way, engages in sexual misconduct with a sister or daughter, in a place where it is not allowed, and further generates miserly jealousy, the retribution of suffering for this is beyond calculation. If there is one person who can rightly reflect and has a mind inclined toward going forth, wishing to abandon all evils; and if someone destroys this person's conditions for going forth and prevents their aspiration from being fulfilled, the sin from this cause increases and surpasses the former by a hundredfold through many kalpas."

At that time Ānanda further said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Were the wholesome roots planted by this Vairambhyena, whereby he will be born in noble places and receive merit and happiness, due to past wholesome deeds as well? Or is it solely by the merit of this one day and one night of going forth that he receives such blessings?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "You should not look at past causes and conditions. It is solely because of this one day and one night of pure going forth that, by these wholesome roots, he receives blessings through the six desire heavens, passing back and forth seven times, and for twenty kalpas constantly enjoys the worldly pleasures of birth and death. Finally, among humans, he is born in a wealthy and happy family. When his prime years have passed and his faculties have matured, fearing the suffering of birth, old age, sickness, and death, he goes forth, upholds the precepts, and becomes a pratyekabuddha."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "I shall now explain by an analogy; listen well! Suppose the four continents, Pūrvavideha in the east, Jambudvīpa in the south, Godānīya in the west, and Uttarakuru in the north, were all filled with arhats, as dense as a thicket of rice and hemp. If there were someone who for a full hundred years wholeheartedly made offerings to all these arhats of robes, food and drink, medicine for illness, and dwelling places with bedding, and after their parinirvāṇa built stūpas and temples and offered all kinds of precious treasures, flowers, incense, garlands, banners, canopies, and music, hung jeweled bells, swept and sprinkled fragrant water, and praised and made offerings with verses: the merit obtained thereby, compared to the merit of someone who, for the sake of nirvāṇa, goes forth and receives the precepts for even one day and one night, does not equal even one sixteenth part. For this reason, good men should go forth and uphold the pure precepts. Good men! All those who seek merit, who seek wholesome dharmas, who receive the Dharma for themselves, should not create obstacles to the conditions for going forth, but should diligently and skillfully encourage and assist others to accomplish it."

At that time the entire great assembly, hearing what the Buddha had said, all grew weary of the world and went forth to uphold the precepts. Some attained the fruit of stream-entry, and so on up to arhatship. Some planted the wholesome roots of a pratyekabuddha. Some generated the aspiration for unsurpassed bodhi. All were filled with great joy, bowed their heads, and reverently practiced the teaching.
