# T1919 天台智者大師禪門口訣

# Oral Instructions on the Gate of Meditation by the Great Master Zhiyi of Tiantai

*Note: This text records the oral meditation instructions (kǒujué 口訣) of the Sui-dynasty Tiantai master Zhiyi (Zhìyǐ 智顗, 538-597), the systematizer of Tiantai Buddhism. It is a practical manual of ānāpāna-smṛti (mindfulness of breathing) covering the full spectrum of meditation practice: settling the body (ānshēn 安身), regulating the breath (tiáoxī 調息) with its four modes (wind, breath, panting, and breathing), concentration on the navel, the stages of deepening absorption, and an extensive section on diagnosing and treating meditation-related illnesses through breath therapy. The text classifies five types of meditation sickness (self-caused illness, ghost-caused illness, demon-caused illness, illness from improper breathing, and karmic-obstruction illness) and prescribes specific breath techniques (pervading breath, scorching breath, cold breath, hot breath, knife-breath) for each. The teaching style is direct, clinical, and experience-based, reflecting the master-disciple oral transmission tradition of early Chinese meditation practice.*

---

The Master said: In mindfulness of breathing (ānāpāna-smṛti), one should know that form and mind are interdependent like the breath. Just as fire depends on fuel to produce smoke, by observing whether the smoke is clear or murky one can perceive the form of dryness or dampness. By examining whether the breath is soft or coarse, one mirrors the signs of what is true or deviant. Guarding the breath is like looking at clouds to find the harbor and watching for the shore: controlling the oar and knowing the way, the boat reaches its crossing.

The Master said: When the body is settled and the mind is correct, it is like clean oil and a bright lamp. Therefore, wishing to guard the mind, one must first settle the body. Settling the body means settling the body's winds so that the wind-activities do not clash and conflict. What are the wind-activities? Within the body there are the moving wind, the transverse wind, the joint wind, the hundred-channel wind, the sinew wind, the strength wind, the bone-interval wind, the waist wind, the spine wind, the upward wind, and the downward wind. All these winds each have their proper place and must not be thrown into disorder. If disordered, they become thieves. In the major case, this leads to madness and collapse; in the minor case, deficiency and excess attack each other: deficiency produces itching, excess produces pain. With pain and itching in the body, how can one focus the mind? Those who have gone mad need not be spoken of further.

In all actions, whether in motion or at rest, one should first form a mental image, visualize it clearly before oneself, and only then proceed to the activity. Otherwise, coarse and fine will clash, and old and new will conflict, like trying to use ritual propriety to stop wickedness, which cannot be accomplished.

Let me briefly give one example; the rest may be understood by analogy. Suppose one is about to sit down. First form this visualization: "I am going to sit at that place. From here to there is approximately so many steps." Then, as each foot is set down, gradually extend the breath and slowly relax the body. The resting wind gradually arrives as the moving wind gradually departs. The other deportments are likewise. Such visualization is called "settling the body."

The Master spoke a verse:

> Advancing and stopping have their sequence;
> Coarse and fine do not conflict.
> It is like a well-trained horse:
> Wanting to go is like wanting to stop.
> One who can always be careful in this
> Settles body and mind and enters meditation.

The Master said: In walking, standing, sitting, and lying down, one should always keep the mind focused. However, lying too much leads to sinking and dullness; standing too much leads to fatigue and exhaustion; walking too much leads to distraction and agitation, making one-pointed mind difficult. Sitting has none of these faults, which is why it is most used.

The practitioner should stay in a quiet, secluded place and sit in full lotus posture (women may use half-lotus). Keep the body level and upright. Relax the body and let the four limbs rest naturally. Arrange the bones and joints so that the connections align properly: not leaning, not crooked. Loosen the clothing and belt. If there is any discomfort, make a slight adjustment for ease; the key is to achieve comfort and proper alignment.

Calmly place the left hand on top of the right hand. Let the tip of the left little finger rest against the base of the right thumb. The tips of both thumbs should barely touch. Relax the jaw. Slightly part the teeth and gently raise the tongue. Exhale a long breath through the mouth four or five times. Then level the gaze and slowly, gently close the eyes. Do not let the eyelids press too tightly; they should be slightly hazy.

The Master spoke a verse of admonition:

> Entering, abiding, and exiting: three times,
> Like the formation, abiding, and destruction of a kalpa.
> Those who rush will not arrive;
> Those who arrive know proper measure.

## The Four Kinds of Breath

The Master said: There are four kinds of breath that should be distinguished. First, wind (fēng 風); second, breath (qì 氣); third, panting (chuǎn 喘); fourth, breathing (xī 息). What has sound is called wind. What is blocked and stagnant is called breath. What does not fully go in and out is called panting. What is soundless, unobstructed, and fully goes in and out is called breathing: continuous, as if barely existing.

Guarding panting leads to toil. Guarding breath leads to blockage. Guarding wind leads to distraction. Guarding breathing leads to concentration.

Master Wen's tradition focuses the mind on the center of one's navel, at the bean-shaped spot. Loosen the clothing and carefully observe it, taking note of its appearance. Then close the eyes, close the mouth, part the teeth, raise the tongue to the palate, let the breath become even, and with one-pointed mind, carefully observe. If external thoughts arise, gather them back. If one cannot visualize the navel, loosen the clothing again and look at it, carefully noting its appearance, then resume as before.

Question: Why focus on the navel?

Answer: The breath goes out from the navel and comes in to cease at the navel. The navel is the place from which the breath issues and where it ceases. To know impermanence, therefore one focuses on the navel. Furthermore, the navel is the root-source of the intestines and stomach. Tracing the path from the source, one naturally sees impurity. If one sees impurity, one stops craving. In the foundations of mindfulness, this pertains to the body foundation. In the six wondrous gates, this is the gate of calming. Furthermore, when the consciousness-spirit first enters gestation, it merges with the father's semen and mother's blood, rooted at the navel. This is why at birth, a person is connected only through the navel. When the practitioner sees the navel clearly, disenchantment naturally arises and one is freed from bondage. The sūtra says: "Wherever the mind arrives, focus and carefully examine." This refers to the breath going in and out, rooted at the navel.

## Meditation Sensations

Question: During focused concentration, sometimes there is pain like being pricked by needles, sometimes tightness like being pulled by a rope, sometimes itching like insects biting, sometimes cold like being washed with water, sometimes heat like being roasted by fire. What about these various sensations?

Or a light and spirit that are completely still: this is the "lightning-flash concentration." At such times, there are countless similar experiences. The practitioner at such times must not become attached to any of them; simply exert oneself in one-pointed mind, and one will naturally get through. If one is not diligent, one will regress and fall.

Question: Why does one suddenly forget the count when counting the breath, and only know the breath?

Answer: The mind is gradually becoming subtler and approaching one-pointedness, which is why one loses the count. Previously, the breath was one thing and the count was another; the object and the two together made things coarse.

Question: Why does one then not even know the breath, but only know the object?

Answer: The practitioner's coarse observation has been removed and the breath is gradually becoming subtle, which is why it is not perceived. Some people say the pores have all opened and the breath goes out everywhere, which is why there is no more perception of it. The Master said: It is because the breath is subtle that it is not perceived. Those who speak of the breath scattering out through the pores may be right in terms of the entire body's permeability, but from the standpoint of focused observation, it is because the breath is subtle.

Some people say: When the breath is so subtle that it seems to stop, the mind's eye suddenly brightens, as if in a flash of lightning. They wonder if this is the "lightning-flash concentration." Experienced practitioners say: If one merely no longer perceives the breath, this is because it has become subtle. If the eyes suddenly become bright, many people speak of this. This is silence.

Before this, there may also be minor pleasant sensations in the body. Many people take these for the joy and bliss of meditation. The Master said: This corresponds to the mind of the ten wholesome actions, not to the higher realms. Passing through this stage, one enters the realm of meditation. One must tread as if on ice over an abyss, and only then can one get through.

## Meditation Sensations and Instructions

The Master said: Regarding the meditation sensations, one naturally knows them without needing to be told. The various masters have always used these to test whether a person's practice is genuine or not. If someone claims to know them, there is a danger that those with mere fantasy will be harmed.

The Master said: If one suddenly sees the breath again, start the counting anew from one as before. If one has been sitting a long time and needs to come out, the breath will appear. At that time, follow it: follow for about ten breaths, then count, count for about ten breaths, and then gently move the hands and feet. Next, move the body as in the massage method. Then open the eyes, going from fine to coarse, and only then may one rise.

During this time, through constant breath-counting, one comes to know the breath only approximately. One simply no longer concentrates with full intensity.

The Master, when giving instructions, always recited the verse about entering and exiting having their proper sequence.

When meditators asked about various illnesses, the Master admonished: Simply keep the mind on its object without allowing it to be distracted elsewhere, and the illness will naturally be cured. Those who use this method are cured within three days at most.

Question: What about a distracted mind with random visions?

Answer: Gathering the attention can cure this.

Question: What about imbalances of the four great elements? How can those be cured?

Answer: The mind's movement and turning are like a fan producing wind; when it stops, the principle naturally settles.

The Master's constant teaching instruction was: A sincere mind focused on a real object; real conditions sequentially produce what is real. Reality upon reality, successively pouring into one another, naturally one enters true principle.

Question: Does simply obtaining meditation concentration lead to entering true principle, or are there definite stages?

Answer: For those of sharp faculties, once the mind is settled, they enter directly without fixed stages. For those of dull faculties, they must pass through all nine sequential concentrations; only when all concentrations are completed do they awaken.

Question: Non-Buddhists cultivate meditation up to the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Why do they not awaken to true principle?

Answer: Non-Buddhists cultivate meditation while always clinging to a self. Because the mind is obstructed by this, they do not awaken to true principle.

## Causes of Meditation Sensations

Pain arises from disordered wind, caused by conflict with external forces. What is "conflict with external forces"? It means that when the mind goes outward, it is dragged by external appearances. It is like teaching an infant to walk: just support the child; you must not pull or drag. Can one who wants to reach the destination quickly not be careful?

Tightness arises from the constricting wind, caused by the practitioner being too intense and forceful, so that the momentum of the breath cannot fully disperse. Whether one goes too far or not far enough, one has gone astray.

Itching is caused by the practitioner mistaking the arising of the breath for its cessation, and the cessation of the breath for its arising. The intention and the breath are at cross-purposes. Therefore it is said: "If one is not careful and not clear, the thief arises from within."

Cold is caused by the practitioner not contemplating with proper engagement at the place of mindfulness, resulting in an increase of the water element.

Heat is caused by the practitioner constantly having hopes and expectations, resulting in an increase of the fire element.

## Prerequisites for Practice

The Master said: Those who wish to cultivate practice must have the requisite qualifications. Without them, one cannot accomplish the work. What are the qualifications? They are the four kinds of mind: (1) Firm faith, meaning deep trust in the teacher. (2) Receptivity, meaning accepting the teacher's method. (3) Diligence, meaning constantly encouraging oneself. (4) Skillful means, meaning subtle and flexible adaptation.

It is said that the channels in the body are like hundreds of rivers flowing to the sea. The heart-mind is like a kumuda flower: four petals joined together, each petal having ten connecting channels interwoven, so at the tip of the heart there are forty channels. Below, one channel goes straight down to the navel; it is called the udāna wind. "Udāna" means "center," so this channel is called the central channel.

## Demonic Visions

In the early stages of sitting, all kinds of external transformations may appear: the forms of parents, teachers, kings, beautiful women, lions, tigers, and wolves. These are called demonic ghosts. The practitioner should use visualization to mentally form a vajra-knife and point it at them; they will immediately depart.

## Five Types of Meditation Illness

There are many types of illness: (1) self-caused illness; (2) ghost-caused illness; (3) demon-caused illness; (4) illness from improper breathing; (5) karmic-obstruction illness. One must learn to recognize them.

**First, self-caused illness:** When sitting, one sometimes leans against a wall or lets the garment-front slip; or one lies down before the assembly has dispersed. Due to this mental laziness, demons gain entry, enter the body, and cause illness, making the body and spine ache with pain. This is called "piercing illness" and is difficult to treat. Practitioners should be cautious. The treatment: use the breath to stream energy from the head downward along the spine, passing through each vertebral joint. One must apply the mind well to be cured.

**Second, ghost-caused illness:** Some people, while sitting, think about various things, or wish to gain special knowledge, or want to know others' fortune and misfortune. Then the dūhela-ghost enters their body and creates various manifestations. One may see auspicious and inauspicious events and know about other people, or know about an entire household, village, district, prefecture, or country's fortune and misfortune, good and evil. It may resemble a saint, but this is not sainthood; it is all the work of the aforementioned ghost. Do not seize upon it. If left untreated, over time it can kill the person.

**Third, demon-caused illness:** Some people, while sitting, think about gain and profit. Demons then manifest various garments, food, the seven treasures, and assorted offering-articles. One should recognize this. If one becomes aware, one should treat it urgently. If left untreated and it enters the heart, it becomes an illness that is difficult to cure.

**Fourth, illness from improper breathing:** Some people sit without understanding how to regulate the breath, resulting in illness that makes the body dry, blocked, and emaciated. Such people have no fault; the illness is easy to treat. The treatment: use the "pervading breath" (biànxī 遍息), properly applying the heart-breath. This is called the breath that pervades and fills. The method: let the mind rest on the breath. Stream energy from the head, pervading and filling all four limbs. The cause of this illness is not understanding how to use the breath, which causes the sinews and channels to become dried and the body to lose its moisture. Now use these two breaths, streaming from above to below, letting the breath pervade the whole body; hence the name "pervading breath" and "filling breath." When the breath pervades and fills, the body will be restored. Then one may use the "stopping breath." What is the stopping breath? Leveling the mind, abiding directly, without moving: this is called the stopping breath.

**Fifth, karmic-obstruction illness:** The entire body swells, the complexion is puffy and yellowish. Such a person's karmic obstructions are heavy and the illness is difficult to treat; one must repent. The treatment: use the "scorching breath." What is the scorching breath? These two breaths are not something external brought into the body; one still uses the heart-breath to visualize scorching. Stream the two breaths from head to feet and into the four limbs, pervading throughout, causing the swelling to subside. Then use the heart-breath, level the mind, abide directly. When the breath is restored to balance, one must continue repenting.

Additionally, if the four limbs are weak and powerless, the two arms and two legs seeming about to fall off, or one arm seeming about to fall: the treatment is to use the pervading breath, streaming energy to fill the four limbs for a long time, and it will naturally be cured.

If the mouth tilts to the left, direct the breath to the left. If to the right, direct the breath to the right. If it tilts to the upper-left or upper-right, follow the same method. For chronic cold or heat illnesses, use the breath directed downward to eliminate the illness.

For the body's various minor ailments, all use breath to treat them. For cold illness, use hot breath to stream through it. For hot illness, use cold breath to stream through it. Apply the counter-treatment method using these two breaths in each case.

## Breath During Walking

One must also use the breath when walking. If the feet feel light and the head heavy, this is from the mind being elevated with delusive thoughts. One should calm the delusive thoughts, calm the elevated mind, and direct the gaze downward. If the feet feel heavy and the head light, direct the heart toward compassion. When walking, one should further use the mind, neither too high nor too low, making the breath harmonious, using the breath to pervade both body and mind. Then one enters the penetrating-clarity contemplation (tōngmíng guān 通明觀), and may contemplate the samādhi of mind-nature and the samādhi of bodily characteristics. The mind-characteristic is fundamental. The sūtra says: "Know the mind and reach the original source; nothing is false."

## Treating Specific Ailments

**Treating dysentery:** Settle the mind below the navel. For cold dysentery, visualize warm breath. For hot dysentery, visualize cold breath. Continue until cured.

**Treating cough:** When the cough is about to arise, exhale three times, then settle the mind in inner contemplation. When the cough is about to start, expel the breath. Return to settling the mind as before, and it will naturally be cured.

**Treating swelling:** Visualize the breath gathering and concentrating toward the swelling until it fills. Over the swelling, with the mind settled in concentration, pierce a single hole and let the breath flow in and out through it. This cures it.

**Treating ear fullness:** Settle the mind outside the ear canal and gently, gently draw out the energy. The condition will naturally be cured shortly.

**Treating a sensation of an object in the throat:** If while sitting one suddenly feels something in the throat, one or two inches long, that will not come out: settle the mind on the object, with the mind in settled concentration. Then distribute the mind in the four directions and mentally say: "Inside and outside are both empty; from where does this illness arise?" One must also understand the nature of emptiness and dismiss it, and it will depart on its own.

**Treating sudden shortness of breath during meditation:** First exhale, then breathe in through the nose, letting the breath fill the entire body and then exit. Afterward, settle the mind in the palm-lines of the hand. Before long, it will resolve.

**A single breath method for treating all bodily illnesses:** Take the breath in through the nose, let it fill the entire body completely, then gently with mouth closed, let the breath go out following its own course. Then draw the mind down through the bones, straight out through the lower opening, and let it rest outside. When sleeping, draw the breath into the soles of the feet. In walking, standing, sitting, and lying, always use this mind.

From these four methods, when miscellaneous illnesses arise, one must first use this breath to open the channels. Then, following the location of the illness, guide the breath to flow in and out from the site of the illness. Where it flows empty and unobstructed, the illness is cured. For cold illness use warm breath; for hot illness use cold breath. For bone-marrow illness use the knife-breath (dāoxī 刀息). Pierce through the tips of the ten toes and also use the breath to pierce through the bones on top of the feet, letting the energy emerge; this cures it. For bodily swelling, use the knife-breath to pierce through the odd spaces between the ten toes, letting the energy flow and connect. For full-body swelling, settle the breath below the soles of the feet. For body heaviness, use the light breath; for body lightness, use the heavy breath.

## Treating Spirit-Possession

In the early stages of sitting, when counting the breath long or short, an external spirit may come through the nose, and the mind rises straight to the crown, becoming steaming hot. The method for repelling it: close the mouth, draw the breath upward to the crown, then pull the breath back downward, and it will stop. If left untreated, both eyes become dark. What is this affliction? It is caused by a ghost-spirit named "Bāwéiduō." It stretches out its hand to cover the eyes, causing blindness. Immediately recite the mantra:

[Dhāraṇī]

Recite three times. The eyes will immediately become clear, and one returns to the initial state of sitting.

When one has achieved subtle settled mind, if an outsider creates a disturbance, the breath rises and blocks the chest, the abdomen swells, and one cannot sit comfortably. This is caused by the six spirits in the body all rushing to the chest. The treatment: first loosen clothing and belt, exhale making the breath long and inhale making it short, for ten breaths. Then recite the mantra above, invoking ānāpāna, up to ten repetitions, and the condition is cured.

When one has first achieved subtle settled mind during sitting, if someone comes to disturb, and the breath surges upward to attack the lungs, causing illness, the lungs then swell. After a hundred days, one vomits and retches and cannot eat or drink. This is caused by an external evil spirit that enters the person's body, named the Bāduō spirit, red in color with six eyes and eight openings, sometimes large and sometimes small. There are also three small spirits named Hūsōuxī. They may transform into children or into serpent-bodies, red-black in color. The Dōuluóxī spirit may be large or small, through three transformations.

In the early stages of sitting, if one experiences aching pain in the body, joints, waist, and back, and calls it a "piercing affliction": this is the six root-spirits playing throughout the body. Before long, the entire body will feel as if lifted straight up. Stretch the body three times and invoke ānāpāna; this cures it.

In the early stages of sitting, if the six spirits sink into obscurity because an outsider has created a disturbance, the six spirits may exit the body sideways. From each pore a spirit rushes in, often causing the body to swell and turn blue, preventing one from sitting. The treatment: first close the mouth, draw the breath upward twice, then within the body pull the breath back downward. Then recite the mantra above, invoking ānāpāna. Also call out the names of the evil ghost-spirits: Hūsōuxī and Bāwéiduō. This cures it.

It is also said that the out-breath is called "ān" (settling). The ānāpāna practitioner counts only the out-breath.

In the early stages of sitting, if there is restlessness and agitation, this is not a chronic condition. The treatment: first invoke ānāpāna. When they speak wildly, tell them to stop, and it ceases.

In the early stages of sitting, if fire arises in the body, this is not an illness. The treatment: from outside, in the dark, bring in a fire without letting the person know or hear. Cover the person's head with a basin and have them sit. Someone beside them announces: "This is a great fire!" When they open their eyes and see nothing, repeat three times, and it stops.

In the early stages of sitting, if water issues from the body, this is not an illness. The treatment: fill a basin with water without letting them know. Hold water in the mouth and sprinkle it on their face. When they open their eyes and see nothing, repeat three times, and it stops.

In the early stages of sitting, if one obtains heavy meditation-sensations and the mind is disturbed by someone's interference, the mind feels pressed and confined. Immediately feel and press below: the illness manifests as blocked bowels. This is caused by the six spirits in the body all descending and exiting, producing illness. The treatment: draw the breath downward, then pull it back upward. Recite the mantra invoking ānāpāna until cured.

When going to the toilet, use the mind-method: when urinating or defecating, visualize all illness departing.

## Illness from Excessive Contemplation

Furthermore, if the practitioner's mind begins to open slightly and develop some understanding of the profound meaning, and with this gradual comprehension the mind becomes delighted and one overthinks, one contracts an illness of mental fatigue. The practitioner should measure wisely: when one should calm, calm; when one should contemplate, contemplate. When calming and contemplation are balanced and appropriate, one can sustain practice for a long time.

The symptoms of mental fatigue are many; they may also produce headache and fullness of breath, both treated with the breath.

**Treating headache:** Breathe in through the nose and gently draw the breath out through the mouth. Mentally visualize the pain in the head subtly exiting through the mouth. When the mind becomes subtle, gentle, and settled, draw the breath out three times; one will naturally yawn. Continue drawing breath and yawning, not more than ten times, and the headache will be cured.

**Treating abdominal fullness:** Breathe in through the nose and gently draw the breath out through the mouth. Mentally focus on the stifling energy in the abdomen, causing it to exit through the mouth. When the illness is present, one should belch. If the abdomen is extremely full and distended, lie on one's back, extend the hands and feet, and gently press the skin of the abdomen. Massage in circles ten or five times; this should produce belching. Then sit up again and draw the breath as in the previous treatment.

If the mind becomes harmonious and subtle, even fullness and stagnation of ten or twenty years' duration requires only one day and one night of harmonious mind and breath, neither too slow nor too fast, neither too coarse nor too fine, properly balanced, and the condition is cured. If one cannot properly balance the coarseness, fineness, slowness, and rapidity of the breath, the condition can never be cured.

If the out-breath has sound, it is coarse and fast. If the out-breath seems about to stop, it is fine and slow. Fine and slow is good. Furthermore, when the abdomen is full and stifling, drawing the breath out long will worsen the condition. Make the out-breath neither long nor short, harmonious and comfortable, properly balanced, and the condition will certainly be cured.

## Additional Cautions

Using harmonized breath to treat illness can cure all intestinal energy disorders. Also, when seated meditation is comfortable and properly balanced, all conditions of illness are cured. Also, when entering good, subtle, settled concentration in seated meditation, one must not harbor worry, anger, or vexation, or energy-illness will certainly develop.

Counting the in-breath during breath-counting can also cause energy illness. Using the five organs as meditation objects for cultivating concentration can also cause illness. Even chanting sūtras, connecting phrases, or failing to properly coordinate the timing of inhalation and exhalation can all cause illness. All are treated by the preceding methods.

Also, when treating headache, one must not simultaneously treat abdominal fullness. When treating abdominal fullness, one must not simultaneously treat headache. Treating two conditions at once will worsen both.

If the energy in the head is extreme, when treating it: breathe in through the nose, open the mouth wide, and gently draw the breath out.

Furthermore, those who sit diligently often develop energy conditions. If one has an energy condition, one should always use the mouth to exhale in treatment. Over time, the teeth may become cold. When the mind becomes harmonious and subtle, breathe in through the nose and gently draw the breath out through the nose to treat it. When the mind is harmonious and subtle, all energy conditions can be treated entirely through nasal breathing.

If one also uses the breath-counting method, focusing on the condition and following the breath, this is also good. On the in-breath, do not focus; only focus on the out-breath.

If contemplation is excessive, the mind becomes agitated and scattered. If resting in emptiness is excessive, the mind becomes sinking and dull. The practitioner should always carefully observe and reflect to treat these conditions.

Also, if the meditation practitioner focuses the mind on the five organs within the body to establish meditation objects, or thinks that concentration comes from the five organs' interaction with objects: the diligent sitter will before long develop illness. This can cause the channels in the practitioner's five-organ region to become linked with the meditation object, producing a cramping and spasming illness. The treatment: visualize the body as empty above and below, like a hollow wooden tube, extending all the way to the cushion and the ground, all made empty through visualization. Visualize all the breath in the body flowing downward into the earth and departing. If the mind becomes harmonious, the condition will be cured before long. Once cured, instruct the person to contemplate the meditation object above the ankle bones of the feet.

One should also instruct the patient: "You developed this condition because your mind went astray, thinking the meditation object was inside the body. The dharma of the mind is neither inside the body nor outside the body; it arises wherever conditions lead. You should understand this."

Furthermore, when a sitting practitioner focuses on a meditation object, the intention may create the appearance of seeing with the eyes. Or when the practitioner achieves a settled state, a light illuminates the meditation object. Not knowing whether this is the light of the mind or the light of intention, one follows the light out and dwells on the meditation object. Before long, such a person will suffer pain in the eyeballs, causing the eyes to become dim and painful. The treatment: use the mind to focus on the eyes. When the mind sees the eyes are closed, form this thought: "The method of seeing and contemplating requires the eyes to be open. Now, seeing the eyes closed, I know this is not seeing with the eyes." Furthermore, when focusing on the eyes, one sees the brightness on the meditation object rising toward the eyes. This is the mind; the mind has come out from the brightness. But the dharma of the mind is neither inside nor outside nor in between. The eyes are a dharma of mind; therefore, they arise wherever the mind's conditions lead, also neither inside, outside, nor in between. When one contemplates in this way, the mistaken and false mind is eliminated. The condition is removed and the eye ailment is cured.

Also, when sitting, one must not let clothing and belts be too tight, as this causes fullness and energy-illness. Furthermore, when a sitting practitioner attains higher-realm concentration, one must not engage in wholesome or unwholesome discursive thinking while in the seated posture, as this causes headache. All such conditions should be guarded against and treated by the above methods.

Furthermore, if a sitting practitioner sees dust rising above the meditation object, congealing and spinning like a spinning wheel: this person has a wind-condition and should not practice higher-realm meditation concentration, as it will cause madness. One should only practice the body-foundation of mindfulness, using contemplative methods to treat the wind-condition. Once cured, one may then practice higher-realm concentration.

If one is in shallow concentration and is disturbed by an external sound, or if something touches the body, one simply becomes aware, exits concentration, and no illness results. But if the mind becomes resentful, angry, or vexed, then fullness and energy-illness will develop.

If one is in deep concentration and a loud sound occurs: though the sound is external, it is experienced as being in the body, or on the meditation object. The person tries to exit concentration but cannot. The mind is in the body, frightened and confused, the spirit in turmoil. Only afterward does one manage to come out. This immediately causes abdominal distension, or sudden bleeding. Being touched by something in the body also produces illness in the same way. The treatment also uses harmonized breath as described above. But if one gives rise to anger and fury, the condition cannot be treated, and it may sometimes cause death.

At the beginning of sitting, if urination and defecation are blocked, draw the breath downward and then pull it back upward. Recite the mantra invoking ānāpāna until cured.

At the beginning of sitting, if various strange events arise and people come to disturb, or if they slander the Buddha-Dharma in Chinese or foreign speech that one cannot understand: this is an evil ghost that has entered the body, named Dōuxīluó. Recite the mantra above and the condition is cured. The Dōuxīluó spirit's body is yellow, sometimes large and sometimes small.

Invoke: in-breath "ānā," out-breath "pānnā."

**Treating sudden bodily pain during meditation:**

Question: Why does the ānāpāna practitioner suddenly experience pain throughout the body?

One master says: It is caused by eating too much, with food not yet digested, and then focusing the mind; the breath is coarse while the attention is subtle, causing this. The treatment: for two or three days, eat less. Make the breath long, carefully focus on the appearance of the out-breath. After two or three days of sitting, it will be cured.

Another master says: It is caused by focusing attention too tightly, which causes the breath to become knotted, producing illness. The treatment: visualize the body as rice steaming atop a steamer. Then visualize a wind arising from the navel, exiting through the face-gate (the mouth), becoming increasingly powerful, blowing the body's energy to scatter. After scattering, the bodily pain is immediately cured. For several days afterward, one should eat rich, oily food and be careful not to be exposed to wind.

Another treatment: at a time before eating, in a sealed room, being careful not to hear any sounds, relax the four limbs completely, and at the face-gate harmonize the breath five times. After two or three days of sitting, the condition is cured.

It is also said: At the beginning of sitting, the moving wind is still strong. Without using skillful means, one suddenly seizes the body as in the resting wind. The resting wind and the moving wind contend in force, producing the condition. The treatment: one must walk and move, starting slowly and gradually increasing speed up to about five li distance. Then return, gradually slowing from fast to slow, and sit down. Exhale a long breath four or five times. Relax the four limbs completely and let the mind rest for the duration of a meal. Then draw the breath two or three times, being careful not to let the ears hear sounds. Again rest the mind as before for the duration of one meal. Then gather the attention and sit in one session, and the condition is cured.

This completes the Oral Instructions on the Gate of Meditation by the Great Master Zhiyi of Tiantai.
