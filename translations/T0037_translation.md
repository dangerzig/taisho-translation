## Fascicle 1

### Sūtra on the Root-Causes of Arising, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Garden, in the kingdom of Śrāvastī. The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus:

"Craving, though deeply established, has not yet given rise to its outward flourishing; one is already entangled and submerged in bondage. This too has a root and an implanted seed; it is not without causes and conditions. Craving leads to dissipation and indulgence; when its causal root has fully ripened, one experiences mad intoxication and lacks clear contemplative understanding. This is called the root of delusion.

"What is born from the condition of delusion also has a root. What is this root? Dazzling forms and sounds, and the five hindrances running wild in confusion—these give birth to the root of delusion.

"The obscuring darkness of the five hindrances, bringing shadowy brooding, also has a root. What is this root? Being dragged along by the seeds of the three unwholesome roots—this is called the root.

"The cultivation and habituation of the three unwholesome roots also has a root. What is this root? Failing to guard and restrain the sense faculties—this too is the root.

"Failing to guard and restrain the sense faculties also has a causal basis. What is this basis? Giving sustained and unceasing attention to what should not be attended to—this is the root.

"Improper attention also arises from a causal basis; it is not without a root. What is this root? Not following right faith and being confused about one's direction—this is the root.

"Faithlessness and following error also have causes and conditions; they are not without a root. What is this root? Being led astray by bad teachers and listening to and receiving what is not the Dharma—this is the root.

"Receiving wrongful teachings and losing right hearing also has a causal basis. What is this basis? Departing from and going against the assembly of the wise and noble—this is the root.

"Going against and departing from the wise and noble also has a root. What is this root? Losing the joy of looking up to them with reverent and humble mind—this is the root.

"Thus, O bhikṣus! When one loses association with the noble assembly of the virtuous and lofty, the acts of body, speech, and mind become filled with the confusion of what one sees and hears, and the heart and mind grow blocked and closed. Through wrong views, one's faith and sense of direction are destroyed. Turning one's back on the right path, wrong views increase; when wrong views increase, inner restraint is lost; when inner restraint is lost, the three unwholesome roots rise and run wild; when the three unwholesome roots run wild, the five hindrances become full; when the five hindrances are full, the root of delusion is complete; when the root of delusion is complete, craving and clinging proliferate. In this way the seeds are transmitted and transformed, attachment to existence grows ever greater, and defilement by the dust of afflictions is without limit.

"The luminous wisdom that transcends the world and ascends to the essentials of liberation also has a root. What is the root of world-transcending wisdom and liberation? The wondrous seven factors of awakening—these are called the causal root.

"The mirror-like illumination of the seven factors of awakening also has a root. What is this root? The four foundations of mindfulness—these are the root of awakening.

"The four foundations of mindfulness in still repose also arise from a root; they are not without a root. What is this meaning? It lies in purity, cleanliness, and wondrous virtue, whose practice rests in the three pure conducts.

"The three pure conducts also have a root; they are not without a root. What is this root? Guarding and restraining the sense faculties—this is the root.

"Guarding and restraining the sense faculties also has a root; it is not without a root. What is this root? Profound mirror-like illumination—this is the fundamental mindfulness.

"Fundamental mindfulness illuminating what has arisen also has a root, serving as the condition that leads to entry into what is right. What is this root? Fundamental mindfulness accomplishing excellence—this is called establishing faith.

"Faith also has a root; it is not without a root. What is the root of faith? Extensive learning of the sequential and essential teachings of the sūtra path—this is called the root.

"Listening to the Dharma of the sūtras also has a root; it is not without a root. What is the root of hearing the Dharma? Those whose conduct is virtuous and whose deeds are pure and true.

"The purity of the virtuous also has a root. What is this root? The assembly of those whose conduct is excellent, pure, true, and spotless.

"Thus, O bhikṣus! By drawing close to the excellent among the virtuous and relying upon the wise and learned, through service and extensive learning one is able to establish the root of faith. Once the root of faith is established, one attains right mindfulness. Once right mindfulness is established, one guards and restrains the sense faculties. Once restraint of the faculties is established, one ascends to the three pure conducts. Once the three purities are accomplished, one achieves the four foundations of mindfulness. Once the four foundations of mindfulness are established, the seven factors of awakening arise. Once the seven factors of awakening are fully accomplished—this is unconditioned liberation transcending the world."

Thus spoke the Buddha, and the disciples received and practiced his teaching.
