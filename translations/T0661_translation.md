# 大乘百福相經
## Mahāyāna Sūtra of the Hundred-Merit Marks

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0661

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Divākara (地婆訶羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Universally Wondrous Palace, seated upon a jewel-adorned lion throne, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus and innumerable bodhisattva mahāsattvas, who surrounded him with reverence, gazing upon his honored countenance, body and mind unmoving. At that time the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, from within the great assembly, sustained by the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and spoke thus: "World-Honored One! The dispositions and inclinations of all sentient beings differ. Yet the Tathāgata, with a single voice, teaches according to each kind, in infinite variety, and all receive benefit. The great Dharma-sounds thus spoken are all accomplished through the Tathāgata's merit. What is the Tathāgata's merit? What is the measure of this merit? I humbly wish you to explain this meaning for the benefit of innumerable hundreds of thousands of sentient beings."

At that time the World-Honored One said to the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī: "Good man! You have already transcended the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. With great wisdom and great compassion you are able to ask the Tathāgata about such a matter. Listen carefully and reflect well upon it! I shall explain it for you in detail. Mañjuśrī! Suppose all the sentient beings of Jambudvīpa practiced the ten wholesome courses of action: the total of all their merit, accumulated and multiplied a hundred-thousand times, would equal the measure of a single wheel-turning holy king's merit. Mañjuśrī! A wheel-turning holy king is endowed with the seven treasures and possesses a thousand sons. What are the seven treasures? First, the golden wheel-treasure; second, the elephant-treasure; third, the horse-treasure; fourth, the gem-treasure; fifth, the queen-treasure; sixth, the treasurer; seventh, the general. His thousand sons are all handsome and valiant, able to subdue their enemies. Such is the merit of a wheel-turning holy king. Mañjuśrī! Suppose all the sentient beings in the four continents each possessed the merit of a wheel-turning holy king: the total, accumulated and multiplied a hundred-thousand times, would equal the measure of Śakra's merit. Mañjuśrī! Suppose all the sentient beings in the four continents each possessed Śakra's merit: the total, accumulated and multiplied a hundred-thousand times, would equal the measure of the merit of the king of the sixth heaven, Paranirmitavaśavartin. Mañjuśrī! Suppose all the sentient beings in the four continents each possessed the Māra king's merit: the total, accumulated and multiplied a hundred-thousand times, would equal the measure of the merit of the Brahmā King of the first dhyāna who rules a small thousand-world system. Mañjuśrī! Suppose all the sentient beings in a small thousand-world system each possessed the merit of the first-dhyāna Brahmā King: the total, accumulated and multiplied a hundred-thousand times, would equal the measure of the merit of the Brahmā King of the second dhyāna who rules a middle thousand-world system. Mañjuśrī! Suppose all the sentient beings in a middle thousand-world system each possessed the merit of the second-dhyāna Brahmā King: the total, accumulated and multiplied a hundred-thousand times, would equal the measure of the merit of Maheśvara, the Brahmā King of the fourth dhyāna who rules the three-thousand great-thousand world system. Mañjuśrī! Maheśvara possesses great merit, great wisdom, and great supernatural power. This is not accomplished through minor roots of merit. Why? When, after the kalpa-fire has ended, the world is about to be re-established, great rain descends from the fourth dhyāna for five intermediate kalpas, and the waters accumulate, filling the three-thousand great-thousand world system up to the Brahmā heaven. Every single raindrop is known by Maheśvara. Mañjuśrī! Suppose all the sentient beings in the three-thousand great-thousand world system each possessed Maheśvara's merit: the total, accumulated and multiplied a hundred-thousand times, would equal the measure of the merit of a single solitary-awakening pratyekabuddha. Mañjuśrī! Setting aside such a three-thousand world system, suppose all the sentient beings in all the buddha-lands of the ten directions each possessed the merit of a pratyekabuddha: the total, accumulated up to an immeasurable, boundless hundred-thousand-billion times, would equal the measure of the merit of a single final-body bodhisattva.

"Mañjuśrī! Take such a final-body bodhisattva along with all sentient beings throughout the ten directions and the entirety of space, whether egg-born, womb-born, moisture-born, or transformation-born, with form or without form, with perception, without perception, or with neither perception nor non-perception, all such beings. Suppose they all possessed the merit of the final-body bodhisattva: the total, accumulated up to an immeasurable, boundless hundred-thousand-billion times, would equal the measure of the merit contained in a single pore of the Tathāgata's body. Mañjuśrī! You should know that the Tathāgata's body has precisely nine thousand nine hundred million pores, each one endowed with immeasurable merit as described above. Mañjuśrī! The merit of each of the Tathāgata's pores, accumulated up to an immeasurable, boundless hundred-thousand-billion times, completes the merit of a single minor mark of the Tathāgata's body. Mañjuśrī! The minor marks of the Buddha's body, briefly enumerated, are eighty in kind: first, the uṣṇīṣa is high and prominent, with no one able to see its top; second, the nose is high and straight, with the nostrils not visible from outside; third, the eyebrows are like the new moon and of deep blue color; fourth, the ear-lobes are full and rounded; fifth, the body is as firm as Nārāyaṇa; sixth, the bone-joints are linked like hooks and chains; seventh, when walking, the feet are four inches above the ground, with the print-marks perfectly formed; eighth, the body turns like the elephant-king; ninth, the nails are like red copper, thin and lustrous; tenth, the knee-bones are smooth and round; eleventh, the body is always fresh and clean; twelfth, the skin is soft and smooth; thirteenth, the body is upright and straight; fourteenth, the fingers are slender and long; fifteenth, the finger-lines are beautiful and elegant; sixteenth, the sinews and veins are hidden; seventeenth, the body's color is moist and fine; eighteenth, the ankles are not exposed; nineteenth, the body does not waver; twentieth, the body's form is full and round; twenty-first, the consciousness is pure; twenty-second, the deportment is perfect; twenty-third, one's dwelling is secure, with nothing able to shake it; twenty-fourth, one's authority shakes all; twenty-fifth, sentient beings delight in seeing one; twenty-sixth, the face is neither narrow nor long; twenty-seventh, the countenance is undisturbed; twenty-eighth, the face is gracious and broad; twenty-ninth, the lips are the color of the bimba fruit; thirtieth, the voice is deep and far-reaching; thirty-first, the navel is deep and round; thirty-second, the navel spirals to the right; thirty-third, the hands and feet are full and round; thirty-fourth, the hands and feet act according to the mind's intent; thirty-fifth, the lines on the hands and feet are bright and clear; thirty-sixth, the lines on the hands and feet are unbroken; thirty-seventh, the light of the hands and feet has five colors; thirty-eighth, all sentient beings who see one are gladdened; thirty-ninth, the face is like a full moon; fortieth, one speaks kindly before others speak; forty-first, the pores emit the supreme fragrance; forty-second, the soles are level and full; forty-third, one's bearing is like the lion-king; forty-fourth, one's gait is like the elephant-king; forty-fifth, one walks like the goose-king; forty-sixth, the head is like the madana fruit; forty-seventh, the body is extremely well-proportioned; forty-eighth, all the voice-characteristics are complete; forty-ninth, the teeth are sharp and bright white; fiftieth, the tongue is the color of red copper; fifty-first, the tongue is thin and long; fifty-second, all the sense-faculties are pure; fifty-third, the body's color is bright and clean; fifty-fourth, the hands and feet are moist and smooth; fifty-fifth, the hands and feet bear auspicious marks; fifty-sixth, the facial features are complete; fifty-seventh, the palms of the hands and feet are like red lotus flowers; fifty-eighth, the abdomen is not visible; fifty-ninth, the navel does not protrude; sixtieth, the waist is slender and proportionate; sixty-first, the body-hair curls upward; sixty-second, the body is dignified and weighty; sixty-third, upon the chest is the śrīvatsa emblem; sixty-fourth, the body is great in stature; sixty-fifth, the hands and feet are soft; sixty-sixth, the circular radiance is one arm-span; sixty-seventh, the constant radiance illuminates the body; sixty-eighth, one looks upon all sentient beings equally; sixty-ninth, one does not look down upon sentient beings; seventieth, one's speech responds to the sounds of sentient beings, neither increasing nor decreasing; seventy-first, one teaches the Dharma without attachment; seventy-second, with a single voice one pervades all, speaking in the languages of all sentient beings; seventy-third, one teaches the Dharma with cause and condition; seventy-fourth, no sentient being can fully behold one; seventy-fifth, one moves in a clockwise direction; seventy-sixth, one has no appearance of anger; seventy-seventh, the hair is long and beautiful; seventy-eighth, the hair is not disheveled; seventy-ninth, the hair spirals to the right; eightieth, the hair is deep blue.

"Mañjuśrī! As described above, these are the Tathāgata's minor marks. The accumulated merit, multiplied immeasurably, boundlessly, a hundred-thousand-billion times, completes the merit of a single secondary mark upon the Tathāgata's body. These secondary marks are likewise eighty: first, the image of Brahmā; second, the image of Śakra; third, the image of Dhṛtarāṣṭra; fourth, the image of Virūḍhaka; fifth, the image of Virūpākṣa; sixth, the image of Vaiśravaṇa; seventh, the image of the Water Deva; eighth, the image of the Sun Deva; ninth, the image of the Moon Deva; tenth, the image of the Fire Deva; eleventh, the image of the Wind Deva; twelfth, the image of the Nāga King; thirteenth, the image of a ṛṣi; fourteenth, the image of a youth; fifteenth, the image of a maiden; sixteenth, the image of a sage's seat; seventeenth, the image of a jeweled banner; eighteenth, the image of the bull-king; nineteenth, the image of the goddess Śrī; twentieth, the image of the mountain-king; twenty-first, the image of the makara fish; twenty-second, the image of the garuḍa king; twenty-third, the image of a tiger-king; twenty-fourth, the image of the horse-king; twenty-fifth, the image of the peacock-king; twenty-sixth, the image of the jīvañjīvaka bird; twenty-seventh, the image of the kalaviṅka bird; twenty-eighth, the image of a kingfisher; twenty-ninth, the image of a parrot; thirtieth, the image of a cakravāka bird; thirty-first, the image of the goose-king; thirty-second, the image of a dove; thirty-third, the image of the elephant-king; thirty-fourth, the image of a palace; thirty-fifth, the image of a maṇi gem; thirty-sixth, the image of a necklace; thirty-seventh, the image of the great ocean; thirty-eighth, the image of a lotus flower; thirty-ninth, the image of the nandyāvarta; fortieth, the image of a bathing pool; forty-first, the image of sacred kuśa grass; forty-second, the image of a svastika; forty-third, the image of a flower garland; forty-fourth, the image of a jeweled crown; forty-fifth, the image of the śrīvatsa; forty-sixth, the image of a parasol; forty-seventh, the image of a river; forty-eighth, the image of clouds and sky; forty-ninth, the image of a jeweled sword; fiftieth, the image of a long hook; fifty-first, the image of a bimba fruit tree; fifty-second, the image of a ring; fifty-third, the image of an earring; fifty-fourth, the image of a vajra pestle; fifty-fifth, the image of a halberd; fifty-sixth, the image of a lance; fifty-seventh, the image of a long sword; fifty-eighth, the image of a discus; fifty-ninth, the image of a bow and arrows; sixtieth, the image of an axe; sixty-first, the image of a lasso; sixty-second, the image of a plough; sixty-third, the image of a medicinal herb; sixty-fourth, the image of a milch cow; sixty-fifth, the image of a wild ox; sixty-sixth, the image of a ram; sixty-seventh, the image of a white whisk; sixty-eighth, the image of a celestial drum; sixty-ninth, the image of a golden hammer; seventieth, the image of a conch shell; seventy-first, the image of a jeweled mirror; seventy-second, the image of a great tortoise; seventy-third, the image of a wheel-blade; seventy-fourth, the image of a flower-vase; seventy-fifth, the image of rice grain; seventy-sixth, the image of a flowering tree; seventy-seventh, the image of a fruit tree; seventy-eighth, the image of a wild goose king; seventy-ninth, the image of a lion within a wheel; eightieth, the image of a deer-king.

"Mañjuśrī! As described above, these are the secondary marks and their merit. The accumulated merit, multiplied immeasurably, boundlessly, a hundred-thousand-billion times, completes a single major mark of the Tathāgata's body. The Tathāgata's body has thirty-two marks: first, the soles are level and stable; second, the hands and feet have the thousand-spoked wheel with its rim; third, the fingers and toes are long and slender; fourth, the hands and feet are soft like cotton; fifth, the heels are full and well-formed; sixth, the fingers and toes are webbed; seventh, the insteps are high and level, proportionate to the heels; eighth, the calves are shapely and long like those of the eṇeya deer-king; ninth, when standing upright with arms extended, the hands reach past the knees; tenth, the male organ is concealed; eleventh, the body's height and breadth are equal, like the nyagrodha tree; twelfth, each pore has a single hair; thirteenth, the body-hair curls upward, is blue-colored, soft, and spirals to the right; fourteenth, the body's color is sublime, surpassing Jāmbūnada gold; fifteenth, the body's radiance extends one fathom; sixteenth, the skin is thin, smooth, and does not attract dust; seventeenth, both shoulders are round and well-formed; eighteenth, the body is broad and well-proportioned; nineteenth, the chest is like that of the lion-king; twentieth, the underarms are full; twenty-first, the teeth are white and large; twenty-second, there are forty teeth; twenty-third, the teeth are white, even, close-set, and deep-rooted; twenty-fourth, the seven sites are full; twenty-fifth, the cheeks are like the lion-king's; twenty-sixth, one obtains the supreme taste, and from two places in the throat flows saliva; twenty-seventh, the tongue is soft and thin, able to cover the face up to the hairline; twenty-eighth, the Brahmā voice is deep and resonant, like the kalaviṅka; twenty-ninth, the eyes are like the utpala flower; thirtieth, the eyelashes are like those of the bull-king; thirty-first, the white ūrṇā between the brows is the color of conch shell and snow; thirty-second, the uṣṇīṣa on the crown is formed of bone.

"Mañjuśrī! As described above, these are the Tathāgata's thirty-two marks. The total of their merit, accumulated to the full in immeasurable, boundless asaṃkhyeya, inconceivable, inexpressible multiples, completes the Tathāgata's great voice of the Dharma. Mañjuśrī! The great voice of the Dharma of all the Buddhas can reach the immeasurable, boundless, asaṃkhyeya worlds and all sentient beings, teaching according to the differences in their inclinations, making them understand according to their kind. Mañjuśrī! The speech of all the Tathāgatas is endowed with such immeasurable merit, universally pervading the worlds and benefiting sentient beings, all in this manner. Mañjuśrī! The measure of merit described above is inconceivable and is not shared with the śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas. Why? Because this merit is born from generosity, morality, meditation, great compassion, great wisdom, the power of skillful means, and all such qualities. Therefore it is not shared with the śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas. Mañjuśrī! There are two dharmas that give rise to the Tathāgata's body. What are these two? First, the power of supreme vows; second, the power of skillful means. Through these two dharmas the Tathāgata's body is produced. Even the voice, the marks, the teaching of the Dharma, and all conduct are all accomplished through these two causes. Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata, wishing to pity, benefit, and bring peace and happiness to all sentient beings, appears in the world. Yet sentient beings have manifold natures and differing inclinations. According to their differences, the Tathāgata manifests various forms, teaches the Dharma, shows the way of practice, and enables them to enter the Buddha-Dharma."

At that time, the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, having heard the Buddha explain all these merits, said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I have today obtained a great benefit. I can now know that the Tathāgata is the unequaled among equals, the supreme among all that is supreme, the great refuge for all sentient beings, pure and undefiled like space. That I have encountered this is truly rare."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī and the others, together with all the bhikṣus, joined their palms, faithfully received it with joy, and practiced it.
