# 因緣心論頌因緣心論釋
## Verses and Commentary on the Heart of Dependent Origination

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1654

Composed by Nāgārjuna (龍猛). Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

## Verses on the Heart of Dependent Origination

> The twelve differentiated links:
> The Sage proclaimed dependent origination.
> Within affliction, karma, and suffering,
> All are subsumed in these three.
>
> The first, eighth, and ninth are affliction;
> The second and tenth are karma.
> The remaining seven are all suffering;
> The twelve are subsumed in only three.
>
> From three arise two;
> From two arise seven.
> Again arise three;
> Thus the wheel of existence repeatedly turns.
>
> All destinies are merely cause and effect;
> Herein there are no sentient beings.
> Only from empty dharmas
> Do empty dharmas arise again.
>
> Recitation, lamp, mirror, and seal,
> Fire-crystal, seed, plum, and sound:
> The aggregates' continuous binding does not transfer;
> The wise should investigate.
>
> Regarding extremely subtle matters,
> If there are those who sever views,
> They are unskillful regarding causes and conditions
> And have not seen the meaning of dependent origination.
>
> Herein nothing can be seen,
> Nor is there the slightest establishment.
> By contemplating the true within the true,
> Seeing the true, one is liberated.

## Commentary on the Heart of Dependent Origination

Herein, a śramaṇa who delights in hearing and can listen well, who skillfully remembers and retains, who can awaken and contemplate, and who possesses discernment, comes to the teacher's place and within the Tathāgata's teaching asks the following question: "The Bhagavān differentiated twelve links; the Sage proclaimed dependent origination. In what are they subsumed? I now desire to hear."

Knowing that this person asks the true meaning, the teacher calls out and says: "They are all subsumed within the three: affliction, karma, and suffering. I make this discriminating and precise statement. Herein, ten and two make twelve links; since they are differentiated, they are called 'differentiated.' Like the parts of a chariot, they are called 'links.' Since he stills body and speech, he is called 'the Sage.' 'The Sage proclaimed' means he expounded and explained; 'proclaimed' is another name for explanation. These are not determined by self-nature or a person-agent. Not from illusory characteristics of self-mastery, nor from time, nature, desire, a creator-lord, or chance are they produced; they are produced by causes and conditions. These differentiated twelve links, in affliction, karma, and suffering, pass from hand to hand in mutual dependence, like a bundle of reeds. Within those three, all are subsumed entirely. 'Entirely' means without remainder."

Question: "What are afflictions? What is karma? What is suffering? These differentiated dharmas, in what should they be subsumed?"

Answer: "The first, eighth, and ninth are afflictions among the differentiated twelve links. The first is ignorance, the eighth is craving, and the ninth is grasping; these three are subsumed under affliction. What is karma? The second and tenth are karma: the second is formations and the tenth is becoming; these two dharmas are subsumed under karma. The remaining seven are all suffering. Apart from what is subsumed under affliction and karma, the seven kinds are suffering. What is subsumed should be known: namely, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, feeling, birth, and aging-and-death. 'All' is a word of total inclusion, also subsuming the suffering of separation from the beloved, meeting with the despised, and not obtaining what one seeks. Therefore these twelve links are all subsumed entirely within karma, affliction, and suffering. 'Only' has the meaning of exclusion: the dharmas stated in the sūtras are subsumed herein completely, with nothing remaining."

Question: "This meaning is now understood. How do affliction, karma, and suffering produce one another? Please explain."

Answer: "From three arise two: from three afflictions arise two karmas. From two arise seven: meaning the suffering-dharmas stated above. From seven again arise three: namely the afflictions. Again from three afflictions arise two karmas. Thus the wheel of existence repeatedly turns. 'Existence' has three kinds: desire, form, and formlessness. Within these, without ceasing, there is transmigration. Those ordinary beings in the world themselves ride the waves of transmigration. 'Thus' indicates indeterminate meaning: it is not that transmigration sequentially produces the various existences in order; this is indeterminate."

Question: "What is the autonomous being of the body? What is its function and activity?"

Answer: "All destinies are merely cause and effect. Apart from conventional names, herein there are no sentient beings. This is the true meaning; they are not established through mere designation. What is designated has no substantial reality."

Question: "If that is so, who goes from this world to the next?"

Answer: "No dharma, not even a paramāṇu, transfers from this world to the next. Nevertheless, only from empty dharmas do empty dharmas arise again. From the five kinds of empty causes, selfless and without 'mine,' consisting of afflictions and karma, there again arise the seven kinds of empty results, the dharmas of suffering, selfless and without 'mine.' Those are without self and without 'mine'; both sides are without self and without 'mine.' Nevertheless, from dharmas whose self-nature is without self, there again arise dharmas whose self-nature is without self. One should know thus and speak thus.

"Herein the question arises: what simile illustrates how from dharmas whose self-nature is without self, there again arise dharmas whose self-nature is without self?

"Herein the answer states: recitation, lamp, mirror, seal, fire-crystal, seed, plum, and sound. Through these and similar similes, established through provisional examples, one should know that self-nature is without self and that the next world is accomplished.

"For example, what the teacher recites, if transmitted to the student: the teacher subsequently has no further speech, so it does not go over. The student's recitation also is not obtained from elsewhere, for that would make cause and effect nonexistent. Like the teacher's recitation, the mind-consciousness at death is also like this, for it would become the fault of permanence. It does not go to the next world, and the next world is also not obtained from elsewhere, for that would make cause and effect nonexistent. Like the teacher's recitation and the student's recitation: it is that very thing yet different from that thing, not easy to designate. Thus, dependent on the mind-consciousness at death, the mind-consciousness of the birth-portion arises. One who is born is also like this: it is that very thing yet different from that thing, not easy to designate.

"Thus, as a lamp produces a lamp, as a face appears as a reflection in a mirror, as a seal produces a text, as a fire-crystal produces fire, as a seed produces a sprout, as a plum produces saliva, as a sound produces an echo: it is that very thing yet different from that thing, not easy to designate.

"Thus the aggregates' continuous binding does not transfer; the wise should investigate. 'Aggregates' means the aggregates of form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. 'Continuous binding' means what has arisen from those causes, the subsequent ones. No dharma, not even a paramāṇu, transfers from this world to the next. Therefore transmigration arises from the habitual tendencies of false discrimination.

"The subsequent word 'should' means reverse contemplation; the meaning should be understood in reverse. One should contemplate all dharmas as impermanent, suffering, empty, and without self; then one will not be deluded regarding things. If not deluded, there is no craving. If there is no craving, aversion does not arise. If there is no aversion, there is no karma. If there is no karma, there is no grasping. If there is no grasping, one does not create future existence. If there is no future existence, then there is no birth. If there is no birth, then in body and mind suffering does not arise. Thus, because the five kinds of causes are not accumulated, correspondingly elsewhere the fruit does not arise. This fruit is liberation. Therefore one severs the evil views of annihilationism, eternalism, and the rest."

Herein there are two verses:

> "Regarding extremely subtle matters,
> If there are those who sever views,
> They are unskillful regarding causes and conditions
> And have not seen the meaning of dependent origination.
>
> Herein nothing can be seen,
> Nor is there the slightest establishment.
> By contemplating the true within the true,
> Seeing the true, one is liberated."
