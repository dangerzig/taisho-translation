# 新歲經
## Sūtra on the Pravāraṇā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0062

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin, translated by Zhu Tanwulan

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in Śrāvastī, in Jeta's Grove, the park of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of eighty-four thousand bhikṣus. Śāriputra, Maudgalyāyana, and others surrounded him, listening to the Buddha expound the sūtra. The Buddha presiding over the great assembly was like Mount Sumeru, king among mountains, standing alone in towering eminence. Like the full moon illuminating the host of stars with its majestic radiance. Like the glow of purple gold upon the ground of that assembly place, all turning to golden hue, extraordinary and distinct, lofty and without peer. At that time, the World-Honored One, together with the assembly of bhikṣus, pure and immeasurable, like the sun, like the clouds, having completed three months, arrived at the time of the pravāraṇā. The bhikṣu assembly sat in tranquil serenity, single-mindedly reflecting on the path and samādhi, with no other thoughts.

Then the Worthy One Ānanda rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, knelt with palms joined, and prostrated himself at the Buddha's feet. He praised the Buddha in verse:

> The Honored One has come here,
> Traveling to protect and save.
> Three months he has dwelt in this place,
> In Jeta's Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park.
> All aspirations are now fulfilled,
> And now the proper time has come.
> O Guide without equal,
> May you proclaim the pravāraṇā.

At that time, the World-Honored One, hearing Ānanda's verse of praise, sat in true stillness to one side, and told the Worthy Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "Go throughout the three-thousand great-thousand world system, to secluded mountains and valleys, to cliff-top stone chambers, and with your voice announce to all bhikṣu assemblies, both newly ordained and long-practiced, including all who have not yet attained awakening, that they should all come to assemble here at Jeta's Grove. Why is this? The Tathāgata has arrived at the time for establishing the pravāraṇā." Then Mahāmaudgalyāyana soared into space and, bearing the Buddha's sacred command, issued a great sound announcing throughout the three-thousand great-thousand world system. Within that great resonance, a verse naturally emerged:

> You who dwell among
> Forests, mountains, and rocks:
> The time of the pravāraṇā has arrived;
> May your heart's wishes be fulfilled.

Then the bhikṣus dwelling throughout the three-thousand great-thousand world system, hearing the announcement of this verse, each by supernatural power and various skillful means transformed their bodies and arrived at Jeta's Grove. They proceeded to where the Buddha was, to receive the establishment of the pravāraṇā. Assembled at the Buddha's side, the gathered disciples, each coming from different regions and other lands, all convened at once: eighty-four thousand koṭīs in all, wishing to receive the pravāraṇā.

At that time, the World-Honored One told the Worthy Ānanda: "Go and strike the ganḍī. The time has now arrived." Ānanda received the instruction, rose from his seat, and struck the ganḍī. The sound pervaded the Buddha's land. Throughout the entire Buddha realm, in the hells, among hungry ghosts and animals, those who heard the sound of the ganḍī, by the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, were freed from all illness, suffering, and poison, and all attained peace and security.

Then the World-Honored One, with his pure brahma voice, told the bhikṣus: "You should now rise and distribute the śalākā counting-sticks. Each of you, facing one another, should confess your transgressions, reproach yourselves, apologize to the assembly for faults committed and violations of the Dharma. Forgive and be in harmony with one another, purifying body, speech, and mind, so that no defilement remains." The bhikṣus then received the Buddha's teaching. Each rose from their seats and, before the World-Honored One, apologized to one another and confessed their faults. When this was completed, they returned to sit in their original places.

The Buddha then saw that the assembly had each returned to their seats. The Buddha, extending his compassion, rose from his seat and, joining his palms, addressed the bhikṣus: "Assembly of bhikṣus! With harmonious hearts, turn toward one another. I confess my faults to you. Why is this? If my body, speech, or mind have erred against any of you. Though the supreme honored one, the Tathāgata, the Worthy One of True Perfection, has no transgressions or omissions in his karma, his mind is without negligence, his wisdom is not lost, he has no cravings, he does not violate the precepts, and among all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas his virtue surpasses all devas, people of the world, and beings of the three realms, standing supreme without peer. Nevertheless, bhikṣus, those of good family who have gone forth to practice the way and cultivate the śramaṇa dharma have minds of varying natures and aspirations of different kinds. In this work of the Buddha, they should practice accordingly and follow the admonitions, not transgressing them. Why is this? If a bhikṣu, dwelling in the noble assembly, establishes the pravāraṇā while his bodily conduct is at variance with his mind, harboring flattery and deceit, that bhikṣu does not receive the true and correct dharma and should not possess the precepts. Why is this? Only when body, speech, and mind are pure is one truly correct. To receive the Buddha's complete precepts, one must hold reverence in one's heart, be obedient to those above, in the middle, and below, and not act with arrogance. One should be humble with a sense of shame; only then does one accord with the Dharma precepts. Why is this? One who practices in this way has no enmity or hatred, harbors no grudges, observes the dharmas of past and present without confusion, and establishes the pravāraṇā without anger or pride. Why is this? When the precepts are pure, the establishment of the pravāraṇā is the greatest. If the precepts are impure, one is not a disciple of the Buddha, like a dead person, a corpse lying on the ground, cast away among the graves. Even if devas and humans of the world, each bringing fine medicines, divine incantations, and excellent techniques, take pity and try to heal them, they cannot restore them to life. In the same way, bhikṣus! If one has violated the precepts, even though one enters the assembly and participates in however many pravāraṇā ceremonies, one cannot save oneself or accomplish the pravāraṇā. Why is this? Such a person, having violated the precepts, will inevitably meet the natural consequences and receive retribution in the hells, as hungry ghosts, or as animals, with immeasurable and inexpressible anguish. Be diligent and careful!"

After the Buddha spoke this teaching, he rose from his seat and left the kuśa-grass mat. Immediately the three-thousand great-thousand world system trembled and shook in six ways. Harps and musical instruments sounded of themselves without being played. From the sky, the devas rained down various precious fragrances and scattered diverse flowers. When the Buddha proclaimed this section on the precepts, he addressed the assembly of bhikṣus. At that time, all the bhikṣus, each in their original seats, through the Buddha's sacred command and the instructions of his awesome spiritual power, their merit and radiance pervading everywhere, each stood up. Their hearts no longer delighted in sitting constantly beneath mountain cliffs and trees; this alone was their aspiration. Each rose from their seats, bowed their heads at the Buddha's feet, and personally declared: "All dharmas are received from the Buddha. The Sage is the root of the path, the protector of all, the eye of compassion, most honored and distinguished, of supreme sacred virtue, surpassing all, towering and lofty, proclaiming the teaching of the Way."

At that time, the Tathāgata, moving with dignity from his exalted seat, confessed and paid respects to the noble assembly, with compassion for the world, and returned to his kuśa-grass seat. As the Buddha resumed his seat, so too did the noble assembly, each returning to their former places and sitting properly according to the Dharma.

Then the World-Honored One, seeing that the time for the pravāraṇā had come, with compassion for the assembly, three times before the bhikṣus completed the ceremony. When the establishment was finished, five bhikṣus rose from their seats to establish the pravāraṇā. Just as the pravāraṇā was established, ten thousand bhikṣus attained the path of realization, eight thousand bhikṣus attained arhatship, and eighty-four thousand devas in space all witnessed the teaching and aroused the aspiration for supreme, true awakening, expounding the sūtra dharma. Immeasurable and countless beings established the three vehicles. Now the Buddha, with compassion, condescended in his supreme dignity to sit among the assembly, liberating those in peril, benefiting beings in all ten directions.

At that time, the Nāga King Nandopananda, leaving his own dwelling, brought fragrant sandalwood and various incenses and proceeded to where the Buddha was, to the ground of the pravāraṇā ceremony. He took refuge in the Buddha and the noble assembly, bowed his head at their feet, and with sandalwood and various incenses made offerings to the Buddha and the bhikṣu saṅgha. Then with this verse he joyously proclaimed:

> Those who dwell in mountain caves,
> Sit beneath shaded trees,
> Or wander the great ocean,
> Harboring hunger and hatred:
> Come sit for the pravāraṇā.
> Koṭīs of beings assemble,
> All making offerings to the Buddha,
> Attaining the gate of amṛta.

Then the Sea Nāga King, bearing red true pearls, conjured a supremely wonderful jeweled pavilion, four hundred leagues in breadth and length, composed jointly of deep indigo vaiḍūrya. Holding it aloft with his hands, he traveled through space, rising above the nāga palace. From the jeweled pavilion, pools of water of eight flavors flowed pure and clean upon the ground, making offerings to the Tathāgata and the bhikṣu assembly. He presented the jeweled pavilion to the Great Sage and the bhikṣu saṅgha, and scattered pearl ornaments upon the Buddha and the noble assembly. Then he spoke in verse:

> Pure as empty space,
> Equal and naturally without self;
> Precept purity most supreme
> Surpasses precious bright pearls.
> Countless multitudes
> Sit in the great assembly,
> All making offerings to the Secure One
> And the assembly of śrāvakas.

Then the bodhisattvas, devas, nāgas, and spirit kings from the ten directions each came together from every quarter, conjuring various wondrous offering implements to make offerings to the World-Honored One and the bhikṣu assembly. They bowed their heads in reverence, sought to receive the sūtra teachings, and each likewise, equal and without distinction, all came to bow their heads and aroused the aspiration for supreme, true awakening. Then devas and humans, each arousing their hearts, made offerings to the World-Honored One and the noble assembly, and praised in verse:

> Those whose minds are pure,
> Foremost and inconceivable:
> The noble assembly, most honored elders,
> Sit in the midst of the gathering.
> Having departed from all conceptions,
> Having well removed all defilements,
> Today we make offerings
> To those worthy of boundless reverence.
> Teaching amid constant difficulties,
> All afflictions and defilements;
> Their precept purity
> Is like a precious bright moon-pearl.
> Ever mindful and right in contemplation,
> Severing all fetters and hatred:
> Today the Stainless Honored One
> Assembles to establish the pravāraṇā.
> Training minds difficult to tame,
> Their practice firm as a great mountain;
> Dissolving obstacles, ever practicing the Dharma,
> The Buddha peacefully establishes the pravāraṇā.

When the devas and humans had spoken these verses, they bowed at the Buddha's feet, suddenly vanished, and each returned to their own palaces, joyful, delighting in the Dharma.

Then the World-Honored One, manifesting a great cool shade with the jeweled canopy spread over the noble assembly, spoke this verse:

> The precepts being most pure,
> Such practice most difficult to attain:
> Today with joyful generosity,
> The pravāraṇā is established, covering all.
> Offering to the children of the Secure One,
> Guarding the precepts with utmost purity,
> As the pheasant loves its plumage,
> The Buddha's power protects the pravāraṇā.

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Now the Buddha, the World-Honored One, although establishing the pravāraṇā, once a year assembling, cultivates the Dharma method with pure protection of the Way, strictly purifying the three karmas of body, speech, and mind from defilement, practicing the ten wholesome actions, the four immeasurable minds, the six perfections, eradicating the six sense-faculties, the three poisons, the five hindrances, and the twelve links, pure as the rising sun illuminating the world, bright radiance shining everywhere, all darkness dissolving, entering the light of the Way: supreme, true awakening. All in harmony, suffering and joy without duality; only then does one accord with the Dharma of the Way." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> The arising of all buddhas is joyful,
> Expounding the Dharma is also joyful.
> Harmony of the noble assembly is joyful,
> And harmony always brings peace and happiness.

Thus spoke the Buddha. The assembly of bhikṣus, the devas, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, asuras, and the people of the world, hearing what the Buddha had spoken, all rejoiced, paid their respects, and departed.

> The Most Honored One was dwelling in Śrāvastī,
> Speaking thus to his assembly of disciples.
> All were gathered in the assembly at Jeta's Grove;
> Now the pravāraṇā is established as a constant practice.
> Śāriputra, joining palms, addressed the Buddha:
> "May we hear and witness the pravāraṇā equally.
> In the three realms, at this renowned and truly spoken time,
> How should bhikṣus purify themselves according to the Dharma?"
> "Śāriputra, when I spread the grass
> Beneath the tree to subdue Māra's power,
> You should spread the grass in the same way;
> Now you who are born should purify yourselves.
> Sitting as is proper for worthy ones,
> This is being without fear.
> Already seated in joy, free from desire,
> Secure in the three realms.
> The summer practice is now complete.
> As disciples of the Buddha,
> Each should reflect and discern,
> With compassion for the world.
> Now is the great month of the pravāraṇā,
> Awakening to foremost wisdom,
> Able to relinquish bodily conduct,
> Seeing the severing of manifold suffering.
> Having expounded all afflictions,
> Awakened to immeasurable suffering,
> Those who have attained the path of realization:
> Now is the pravāraṇā.
> Desire and hatred diminished,
> Able also to remove fundamental delusion,
> Able to sever birth and death,
> And no longer afflict the root of perception.
> Having attained birth in heaven,
> And also come to be born among humans:
> Those who have attained these two paths,
> Now is the pravāraṇā.
> Having attained birth in heaven,
> And not descending to lower birth,
> Dwelling in the heavens where they are born,
> All craving already extinguished.
> The foremost dharma of the world
> The Honored One has already attained:
> Those who have attained these two paths,
> Now is the pravāraṇā.
> All evil afflictions
> Entirely exhausted, liberation attained,
> Having attained the four amṛtas:
> This is being without fear.
> All darkness already destroyed,
> All suffering in every direction:
> These are the two sets of four supreme paths,
> Now is the pravāraṇā.

"When one month is completed, one attains one wisdom. When two months have arrived, one attains two wisdoms. When the three months at once are completed, one attains three wisdoms. Delighting in the three wisdoms, now is the pravāraṇā. Having extracted the four wounds with nothing remaining, having crossed to the far shore and attained security, the four months being completed with nothing further, having accomplished merit, now is the pravāraṇā. Those who can subdue the nāga chief Nandopananda, who can shake Mount Sumeru and even the ocean waters, causing all the heavenly realms to tremble: those like Maudgalyāyana, now is the pravāraṇā.

> Self-awakened, most worthy,
> All destinations already exhausted,
> Protectors of the world:
> The Buddha himself speaks these words.
> Ever walking alone among the trees,
> As an example, like a rhinoceros:
> These supreme pratyekabuddhas,
> Now is the pravāraṇā.
> No longer attached to the three,
> The mind of all purified,
> The three supernatural powers already established,
> Having departed from pride.
> In the three realms, without desire,
> Not agitating the still mind,
> Secure as Mount Sumeru:
> Now is the pravāraṇā.
> Having practiced the four right efforts,
> The four paths already established,
> The Buddha declares these worthy ones
> The most meritorious of the assembly.
> Offerings and giving are most secure,
> Never to be defeated.
> The Buddha has attained the assembly's wisdom:
> Now is the pravāraṇā.
> Holding the precepts fully accomplished,
> Immovable by all forces,
> Dharma food and robes,
> Ever delighting in dwelling among mountains.
> This is firm practice,
> Complete and without blemish,
> The ground of those who practice purity:
> Now is the pravāraṇā.
> Holding the precepts, severing all evil,
> The assembly joins palms to receive the Dharma,
> Wherever they direct themselves,
> All māras are subdued.
> The devas, ever virtuous,
> Praise the perfection of the precepts.
> No longer seeing any māra:
> Now is the pravāraṇā."
