# 寺塔記
## Record of Temples and Pagodas

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2093

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, compiled by Duan Chengshi (段成式).

---

In the summer of the third year of Huichang under Emperor Wuzong, the guihai year (843 CE), I, together with Messrs. Zhang Xifu and Shanji, fellow officials at the Secret Archives, and Mr. Zheng Fumeng, who held a consecutive post at the Transcendents' Bureau, took advantage of a day of leisure to visit Daxingshan Temple. Upon inquiry, we found that both the *New Record of the Two Capitals* and the *Record of Sightseeing* had many omissions. We therefore agreed to spend approximately ten days visiting the temples along the two main avenues, beginning with Xingshan Temple on the eastern avenue. What was not covered in the two records we separately noted. When we reached Ci'en Temple, we first learned that the authorities were about to merge the temples. The monks were in a state of agitation, so we could only make casual inquiries of one or two senior monks, and recorded the paintings beneath the pagoda. Our excursions thereupon ceased.

Three years later, I was posted to the capital at Luoyang and then appointed prefect of Ancheng. In the seventh year of Dazhong (853 CE), I returned to the capital. During my six jiazi cycles away, roughly half of the books I had stored had been damaged by insects. Among the old papers, I came upon our visit to the temples with my two late friends. Tears of blood mingled as they fell. The pleasant excursions of that time are remote beyond recall. I then set about editing and restoring the record, barely managing to piece together what the bookworms had perforated. Still, five or six out of ten entries were lost. I arranged what remained into two fascicles and transmitted them to the Buddhist community.

Duan Chengshi, styled Kegu, of Dongmou.

---

**Daxingshan Temple, in Jinggong Ward.** The temple takes the two characters "Daxing" and one character from the ward name to form its name. The *New Record* states that the Udayana image was burned in a fire at the beginning of the Zongzhang era (668 CE). According to the Liang dynasty, the Udayana image from the Western Regions was in Jingzhou. The claim that the Sui dynasty moved it from Taicheng to this temple is incorrect. There is now also a sandalwood image with open eyes, the workmanship of which is rather crude. This too is erroneous.

Before the stūpa of Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra, there are many old pines. In years of drought, officials cut their branches to use as "dragon bones" for rain prayers. This is because of the tripiṭaka master's power over dragons; these trees must surely have spiritual efficacy. On the rear wall of the Incense-Offering Hall, in the Yuanhe era (806-820 CE), the painter Liang Qia painted a pair of pines, somewhat departing from the conventional style. The Mañjuśrī Hall has extremely exquisite and wondrous molded figures. The outer walls have gilt-mud panels that Amoghavajra brought from the Western Regions.

In the Sandalwood Image Hall, there is a copy of the *Sūtra on Timely and Untimely*, written in vermilion within ruled lines, stored in a lacquered shrine. The monks say it is a relic from the Sui dynasty.

Behind the temple there was formerly a winding pool. When Amoghavajra was nearing his end, it suddenly dried up completely. When Chan Master Weikuan came to reside there, a flood opened up a spring. White lotuses and water-plants grew of their own accord. Now it has once again become dry land.

**In the southern part of the eastern corridor is the Cloister of Preceptor Su.** In the courtyard are four green paulownia trees, planted by Su's own hand. During the Yuanhe era, high officials and ministers often visited this cloister. In summer, the paulownias exude sap that stains people's clothing like axle-grease and cannot be washed out.

**The Clam Image on the left.** According to old tradition, Emperor Yang of Sui was fond of clams. Every meal had to include clams, and over time the number consumed exceeded tens of millions. Suddenly there was one clam that resisted all cracking and prying. The emperor, marveling at it, placed it on a stand. That night it emitted light. By dawn, the flesh had fallen away of its own accord, and inside were images of one buddha and two bodhisattvas. The emperor was filled with grief and remorse, and vowed never again to eat clams. This is not the Khotan jade image of Emperor Xuan of Chen. It is one foot seven inches tall, a little over an inch wide, with one buddha, four bodhisattvas, and one flying celestial, all carved from a single piece of jade. Cut from pure fat-white jade, without flaw, its lustrous colors seem almost to drip.

**The Heavenly King Pavilion** was constructed during the Changqing era (821-824 CE). It was originally inside the Chunming Gate, sharing a wall with the Southern Palace. Its form was the largest in all under heaven. In the second year of Taihe (828 CE), by imperial decree it was moved to this temple. When it was dismantled, five hundred bolts of cloth and several dozen containers of lacquer were found inside its belly. Today the forms of the subordinate spirit-generals are damaged and fallen, but the Heavenly King alone is undamaged.

---

**Anguo Temple, in Changle Ward.** The Red Tower was the dance pavilion of Emperor Ruizong when he was a prince.

**The Eastern Chan Cloister,** also called the Wooden Pagoda Cloister. On the five wall-panels of the northern and western corridors at the cloister gate, Shi Sidao, a disciple of Wu Daoxuan, painted Brahmā, Śakra, and the eight classes of beings without applying colors, still possessing canonical form.

**Guangming Temple.** The Hāritī image and the sculptured likeness of Crown Prince Wenhui are so lifelike in their bearing and manner that they seem alive. The craftsman's name was Li Xiu.

**The Mountain-Garden Cloister.** With ancient trees and lofty mounds, it is as secluded as a mountain valley. At the time, earth was carted in to construct it. In the cloister of Senior Monk Lin, there is a single ear-of-grain cypress whose spreading branches arch over and shelter more than ten people seated beneath.

---

**Zhao Jinggong Temple, in Changle Ward.** Established in the third year of Kaihuang under the Sui (583 CE), originally named Hongshan Temple, renamed in the eighteenth year. On the eastern wall inside the southern middle gate, Wu Daoxuan painted a white-line *Transformation of the Hells*. The brush-force is vigorous and fierce; the transformed figures are dark and uncanny. Beholding it, one cannot help but feel one's hair stand on end. This is the most inspired passage in all of Wu's paintings.

In the western corridor of the Three Stages Cloister, Fan Changshou painted a *Western Paradise Transformation* and sixteen paired scenes with jeweled pools. The pools are especially wondrous and sublime; gazing closely, one has the sensation of water deepening. Above the cloister gate is a white-line painting of trees and rocks, rather resembling the style of Yan Lide. I brought a powder-sketch of Yan Lide's *Traveling the Celestial Words* to compare, and found no difference.

On the south side of the gate, inside the western middle gate, Wu Daoxuan painted a dragon and also brushed the beard of a Heavenly King. The brush-strokes are like iron. There is a censer-bearing celestial maiden who steals a sidelong glance as if about to speak.

**In the Huayan Cloister,** there is a standing image of Vairocana in brass, six feet tall, of antique style and exquisite workmanship.

Beneath the pagoda are three dou and four sheng of śarīra. When the pagoda was moved, the monk Shouxing established a practice-site and brought out the śarīra for the officials and commoners to view. Before the chanting and hymns were finished, śarīra appeared all over the ground. The men and women dared not tread upon them and all went outside the temple. Monk Shou then made small clay stūpas and wooden stūpas, numbering close to a hundred thousand, and buried the śarīra within them. Several tens of thousands still survive today.

The temple has over six hundred small silver images and one gold buddha-image, several feet in height. There is a large silver image over six feet tall, of antique style and exquisite workmanship. There is also a small folding screen with the *Heart Sūtra* inlaid in characters of the seven jewels, stored in a jeweled casket. On top are variegated pearls and white pearls, arrayed so densely they dazzle the eyes. During the An Lushan rebellion, officials hid it in this temple. The screen has fifteen leaves, thirty columns. After the sūtra it reads: "Sponsor of the aspiration: Sima Hengcun. Sponsor who fulfilled it: Pillar of State Suo Fubaoxi. Pillar of State Zhende. Made for all sentient beings of the dharmadhātu, on golden tablets." Shanji suspected it was a foreign object.

The needle cypress described in the *Sightseeing Record* was felled during the Taihe era for use as palace timber.

---

**Baoying Temple, in Daozheng Ward.** Han Gan was a native of Lantian. In his youth he often delivered wine for a wine-shop on credit. The Wang brothers, the Right Assistant Director and others, had not yet achieved success and frequently bought wine on credit while wandering idly. Gan often went to the Wang household to collect debts. He would play at drawing figures and horses on the ground. The Right Assistant Director, deeply absorbed in painting, found his artistic sensibility remarkable, and so provided him with twenty thousand cash to study painting for more than ten years. The Brahmā, Śakra, and celestial maidens in this temple are all true portraits of Duke Qi's singing-girl Xiaoxiao and others. The temple has a painting by Han Gan of a *Descent to the World* panel: Maitreya wearing a purple kāṣāya, with a bodhisattva on the right looking upward, and two lions, all truly inspired work.

In the cloister at the northwest corner, there is calligraphy by Huaisu and a preface by Yan, the Duke of Lu, with encomia by Vice Director Zhang Wei and Gentleman Qian Qi.

---

**Pusa Temple, in Pingkang Ward.** On the eastern wall of the refectory, Wu Daoxuan painted a *Transformation of the Verse on Form from the Mahāprajñāpāramitā Śāstra*. The verse was inscribed by Wu himself. The brush-strokes are powerful and vigorous, as if pulling at the hair of ghosts and spirits. On the adjacent wall-section is a painting of a skeleton-ascetic, with celestial garments fluttering and wind seeming to move across the entire wall.

On the rear face of the inner colonnade of the buddha-hall, Wu Daoxuan painted scenes from the *Sūtra for Eliminating Calamities*. The trees and rocks are ancient and precipitous. During the Yuanhe era, the emperor wished to have the painting moved, but feared it would be damaged. He therefore issued a decree selecting master painters to make copies and submit them.

On the eastern wall of the inner colonnade of the buddha-hall is a *Vimalakīrti Transformation*. Śāriputra is shown turning at the corner with knees bent. At the end of the Yuanhe era, the popular-lecture monk Wenshu refurbished it, and the original brush-traces were entirely lost.

---

By temple regulations, the bell-tower should be in the east. Only this temple, because the residence of Li Linfu, the Right Seat, was to the east, built its bell-tower in the west.

Within the temple are the tortoiseshell whip of Commander Guo and the seven-jeweled canopy of Commander Guo's consort, Lady Wang. The temple's abbot, Yuanjing, was well-versed in Buddhist history. He said: "Every birthday, the Right Seat Li would always invite the monks of this temple to his residence for a vegetarian feast. Once, a certain Monk Yi praised the Buddha. He was given a saddle worth seventy thousand cash when sold for its materials. Another monk named Guang, of great reputation, had waited his turn for several years and was next in line to praise the Buddha. He therefore closely observed the Right Seat's meritorious deeds, hoping for a generous gift. After the feast, from beneath a curtain appeared a colorful basket with a fine silk handkerchief, upon which lay a single object resembling a decayed nail, several inches long. The monk returned home disappointed and ashamed. After several days, he reconsidered that a great minister would not deceive him. He took it to the Western Market and showed it to a foreign merchant. The merchant, upon seeing it, exclaimed in astonishment: 'Venerable sir, how did you obtain this? If you must sell it, do not go below the price.' The monk tentatively asked for a hundred thousand cash. The merchant laughed broadly and said: 'Not enough.' He was told to name his highest price. He raised it to five hundred thousand. The merchant said: 'This is worth ten million.' And so it was sold. The monk asked its name. He replied: 'This is a precious bone.'"

Also, the temple previously had a monk who never gave his name. He always carried a bundle of straw and sat and slept beneath the temple's two corridors, refusing to stay in any room. After several years, the temple administrators urged him to move into a room. He said: "Are you tired of me?" That evening he used the bundle of straw to set himself on fire. By dawn, there were only ashes remaining, with no smell of blood or fat. The assembly then realized he was an extraordinary person, and molded the ashes into a likeness. It is now in the buddha-hall. The world calls him "The Straw-Bundle Master."

---

**Guangzhai Temple, in Guangzhai Ward.** The Samantabhadra Hall was originally Empress Wu's toilette hall. When grapes bore fruit, she would visit this hall. Today the hall contains paintings by Yuchi that have many remarkable passages. On the four walls are painted images and peeled-skin skeletons; the artist's conception is extremely striking. There are also transformed forms of the three Māra-maidens, whose bodies seem to emerge from the wall. The buddha's circular halo has evenly blended colors interwoven so as to dazzle the eye. Before the buddha-seat on the east wall, the brocade is like a severed ancient standard. On the west wall, when one approaches closely, it seems to flutter.

---

**Jingyu Temple, in Xuanyang Ward.** Originally the residence of Empress Taimu. The temple monks say that outside the gate of the Three Stages Cloister is the place where Emperor Shenyao shot peacocks. Above, there are coiling serpents and sooty smoke that inspire dread. The trees and rocks of the eastern corridor are precipitous and strange; the eminent monks depicted are equally uncanny.

---

**Chongji Temple, in Zhaoguo Ward.** Behind the temple there are embroidered garments of Empress Wu, including a woven dragon-patterned jacket and six pieces of embroidered clothing. In the second cloister from the south in the eastern corridor, there is a hall for the kāṣāya made under the regulations of Vinaya Master Xuanzhang, and a Mañjuśrī Hall. There are several pines of remarkable appearance.

---

**Zisheng Temple, in Chongsheng Ward.** Outside the gate of the Pure Land Cloister, it is traditionally said that Wu Daoxuan, one evening holding a candle, painted while intoxicated. Among the figures, one with a halberd-hand glaring back at the viewer is terrifyingly fierce. Inside the cloister gate, Lu Lengqia, who had long studied Wu's style, and to whom Wu had also transmitted hand-techniques, painted half the wall of the Dhāraṇī Three-Gate Temple. Wu greatly admired it and told people: "Lengqia has not grasped the mental technique; he labors too hard with his thinking. Can he last long?" After finishing the painting, Lengqia died.

---

**Ci'en Temple.** The temple was built because of an impure awakening at an old monastery, which was then developed and constructed. There are altogether more than ten cloisters, totaling one thousand eight hundred ninety-seven bays. By imperial decree, three hundred monks were ordained. When the Tripiṭaka Master first returned from the Western Regions, Emperor Taizong ordered the Grand Master of Ceremonies, Prince Daozong of Jiangxia, to provide the nine kinds of music to escort the sūtras and images into the temple. The colorful carts numbered over a thousand vehicles. The emperor personally observed from the Anfu Gate. Emperor Taizong once bestowed upon the Tripiṭaka Master a monastic robe worth over a hundred gold pieces; its workmanship showed no trace of needle or thread.

Within the temple, the persimmon tree and white peony were planted by the hand of Dharma Master Fali.
