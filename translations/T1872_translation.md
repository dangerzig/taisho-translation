# 華嚴策林
## Avataṃsaka Inquiry Grove

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1872

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, expounded by Fazang (法藏).

---

The essential purpose of the Avataṃsaka school is not of a single meaning. When its ultimate teaching is investigated, it comprehensively illuminates two approaches: cause and fruit. The cause is the vows and practices of Samantabhadra; the fruit is the functioning of Vairocana's activity. The dharma-realm of all these forms the substance; conditioned arising forms the function. Substance and function are entirely embraced, perfectly penetrating as a single horizon. If one speaks of the reason for this, it does not go beyond conditioned arising. When the meaning is turned and applied, it fuses and penetrates existence and emptiness. Following the discernment of wisdom in its function, one speaks of many approaches. Following the quiescent substance of the teaching, one speaks of a single meaning. If one discusses the profound purport, neither the one nor the many will do. Yet because the "not" brings the one to completion, and the "not many" brings the many to completion, permanence is always preserved in the constant emptiness and constant existence. Such is the general outline. I have roughly presented the main structure. Now, taking up the essential meanings, I briefly set forth questions and answers, stating only the general purport; the rest belongs to the sūtra text. May those who have attained the path explore its spirit therein.

**Contents:** 1. Inquiring into the Universal Eye. 2. Illuminating Principle and Phenomena. 3. Discerning the True Cause. 4. Fusing the Great and the Small. 5. Connecting Formation and Destruction. 6. Penetrating the Two Realms. 7. Illuminating the Hidden and the Manifest. 8. Illuminating Cause and Fruit. 9. Comprehending Form and Emptiness.

---

## 1. Inquiring into the Universal Eye

**Question:** The eye and form are mutually dependent; surely there must be such an affair. The sense-faculty and its object combine; the purport of this cannot be denied. When the convergence of conditions manifests, how can one not have an object? How does one come to be called "the universal eye"?

**Response:** The five conditions serve as cause, and what is called "eye" serves as the fruit. If the conditions manifest, then by the name of the conditions one names the eye. If the cause is submerged within the fruit, the conditions are all called "eye." Thus, taking form entirely as the eye, one constantly sees yet is without conditions. Taking the eye entirely as form, one constantly speaks of seeing yet it is not "I." Since it is "not I," one is free from emotional conceptions; since there are no conditions, one is free from covetous seeking. One gathers the myriad phenomena before one's eyes and completes the ten directions at the horizon of the eye. Therefore the meaning of conditions is inexhaustible: following seeing after seeing, one never reaches the end. The nature of things is difficult to see or think about, and in response to each dharma it is difficult to assess. When the ten dharmas are complete, the universal meaning of the eye penetrates. Then the penetration of seeing and the substance have long been hidden of themselves. The hidden illumination then layers upon layers. Yet when one investigates the ten directions at the horizon of the eye, it mirrors emptiness and existence with brilliant clarity. Gathering the myriad phenomena to form the body, it manifests principle and phenomena in thorough penetration. This is my respectful response.

---

## 2. Illuminating Principle and Phenomena

**Question:** Conditioned arising and phenomenal characteristics are distinctly differentiated. The principle-nature of the dharma-realm is without any fixed locus. If one preserves or destroys either one, substance and function are not skillfully handled. How can one also penetrate both phenomena and principle, gathering them into non-duality? To dispel this defect, may the profound teaching be made clear.

**Response:** The phenomenal characteristics of conditioned arising must embrace the nature in order to be completed. The profound teaching of the dharma-realm also depends on conditions to manifest in emptiness. Existence and existence penetrate each other; substance and function are profoundly interconnected. To speak of phenomena is for the existent to penetrate to the source of emptiness; to speak of existence is for emptiness to penetrate through to the surface of the existent. Sometimes, when both are negated, not the slightest thread of discourse is complete. Sometimes, when both are mutually accomplished, the vast discourse is fully seen. Entirely gathering phenomena, entirely negating phenomena: this constitutes principle. Phenomena are not separate phenomena; things are endowed with principle and this constitutes phenomena. How could emptiness follow existence in manifesting, or principle follow phenomena in becoming apparent? When one contemplates them at a single horizon, the myriad things can be determined. This is my respectful response.

---

## 3. Discerning the True Cause

**Question:** If in the present time one cultivates the path and enters practice, one must surely see the Buddha and seek the Dharma. From cause to fruit, like a box and its lid, they mutually depend. If one takes the thirty-two marks as the cause, then there are no characteristics to be seen. If one explains a single emptiness as the cause, one fears that principle and phenomena will be far apart. Please discern the cause of this buddha, that we may hear of the fruit of buddhahood.

**Response:** The Buddha takes the unborn, empty, and quiescent as his body, and also takes the dharma-realm, without arising, as his substance. If one simply realizes this principle, the Buddha, following principle, manifests his body. If one simply enters the unborn, the Dharma, following wisdom, displays its characteristics. Characteristics are themselves without characteristics; the form-body need not lose the principle. The non-existent is yet not non-existent; the gate of emptiness cannot be taken as the real. Long free from silence, what the three times have not yet examined: one must go beyond form and sound, what the six sense-faculties rarely contact. Afterward, realizing it within the mind, illuminating the principle, one accomplishes buddhahood, and likewise accomplishes the quiescence of external conditions. Wisdom achieves the Dharma, and likewise achieves it. When the cause is fulfilled, it is the fruit. Firmly generating the aspiration, one has already established the foundation of perfect enlightenment. When the fruit is fulfilled, it is the cause accomplished. Attaining the path was originally from the initial aspiration's foundation. Why must one bring the entire cause to the fruit? One fears it would invite the defect of something attained, making principle and phenomena subject to the discourse of having a nature. This is my respectful response.

---

## 4. Fusing the Great and the Small

**Question:** There is great and there is small; their appearances are clearly distinct. The one is broad, the other narrow; things and affairs are differentiated. If one gathers the great into the small, one fears the great loses its original form. If one gathers the small into the great, one fears the small is damaged and loses its substance. Since width and narrowness are not equal, how can entry and exit mutually contain each other? Brush away the covering clouds and make the sun of wisdom shine.

**Response:** The great must embrace the small to truly deserve the name "great." The small must contain the great to truly receive the designation "small." Each lacks inherent nature; this is why great and small mutually contain each other. Neither is ultimately established; this is why broad and narrow equally admit each other. Thus one knows: the great is the small's greatness, and the small is the great's smallness. The small has no fixed nature and ultimately pervades the ten directions. The great has no fixed form and across kalpas is brilliant in a single instant. Then one knows: when small, it is precisely great, and a mustard seed contains Mount Sumeru. When great, it is precisely small, and the ocean's water is contained in a hair-pore. If each does not destroy its nature, how could entry and exit not be complete? Furthermore, since each preserves its original form, expansion and contraction are naturally unobstructed. This is my respectful response.

---

## 5. Connecting Formation and Destruction

**Question:** The meaning of formation and destruction, though its force is clear, when the distinctions are differently presented, it contradicts the purport. Now, when formation is occurring, where does destruction reside? When destruction is occurring, where is formation? If they are different, the sage would not permit it. If they are the same, the ordinary person would not believe it. Please respond with pearl and jade to dispel these shards and rubble.

**Response:** Destruction is destruction at the very locus of formation; destruction is itself formation. The dharma-realm is empty yet constantly existent. To preserve destruction is for the existent to penetrate to emptiness-destruction. What truly exists is for emptiness to be made manifest at the surface of the existent. After this, difference is also non-difference, sameness is also non-sameness. The substance is of itself brilliantly clear; delusion of itself brings obscurity. If through the nature's foundation one ties together the two extremes of emptiness and existence, and if through the illumination of wisdom one penetrates formation and destruction as a single horizon, this is my respectful response.

---

## 6. Penetrating the Two Realms

**Question:** Sentient beings are deluded; the buddhas are awakened. Though the substance is one, in terms of function there is a difference. If one penetrates sentient beings to the Buddha, then the buddhas should be deluded. If one penetrates the Buddha to sentient beings, then sentient beings should be awakened. I hope you will brush the dusty mirror and display the great brightness, and open the profound gate to illuminate the darkened room.

**Response:** One constantly takes what is not a sentient being as a sentient being, and what is not a buddha as a buddha. This does not obstruct the conventional being always preserved while always negated, nor does it prevent the destroyed being always accomplished. Following conditions, one provisionally establishes the name "sentient being"; how could there be a sentient being to be obtained? In terms of the substance, one provisionally bestows the title "Dharma-body"; how could there be a buddha to be sought? Without exception, delusion penetrates to the source of the real, abiding in the one characteristic while constantly existent. The real fully embraces the reaches of delusion, entering the five destinies while constantly empty. If emotion is all-embracing, then the two realms are hard to penetrate. If wisdom speaks, then the one suchness is easily spoken. After this, both negated and both established, mutually accomplished: one sees the buddhas within the body of sentient beings, and contemplates sentient beings within the substance of the Buddha.

---

## 7. Illuminating the Hidden and the Manifest

**Question:** The meaning of hidden and manifest is difficult to trace to its source. One relies on emptiness and form to penetrate the profound, and takes the one and the many to display the dense and the subtle. If when manifest there is something hidden, then contemplating form should allow one to see emptiness. If when hidden there is something manifest, then seeing quiescence should allow one to constantly perceive movement. Please open the Dharma treasury and reveal this jewel of wisdom.

**Response:** When this can embrace, then this is called "that." If that gathers this, then also this is manifest while that is hidden. Hidden being present is precisely the manifest being present. The time of the manifest is precisely the time of the hidden. When the one is destroyed and entirely embraced, the many are accomplished. This is manifest; entirely gathering that, it is hidden. The substance of the Dharma is constantly bright, yet the bright is also not the manifest. The Buddha moves yet is constantly quiescent, yet the quiescent is also not the hidden. The hidden is established through the manifest; the dharma-realm opens within conditioned arising. The manifest is accomplished through the hidden; the myriad things are mirrored upon the one emptiness above. Thus, seeing emptiness is precisely seeing form; comprehending delusion is precisely comprehending the real. How could one need to traverse eighty thousand? The substance of the mind has been primary from the start. This is my respectful response.

---

## 8. Illuminating Cause and Fruit

**Question:** Cause and fruit are mutually dependent, like a box and its lid. If from the cause one obtains the fruit, then when the fruit is accomplished it differs from the time of the cause. If the cause itself is the fruit, then sentient beings are identical to the buddha-ground. If one cannot call this annihilationism, then this falls into eternalism. May you kindly point out the direction for the confused and open the correct path.

**Response:** The two positions of cause and fruit belong to one and the same conditioned arising. Mutually dependent for their establishment, their meaning does not arise in isolation. The cause penetrates to the source of the fruit; the fruit investigates the reaches of the cause. Samantabhadra's vows and practices are accordingly called the perfect cause. Vairocana's ten bodies are accordingly called the fulfilled fruit. However, dharmas have no before and after; wherever comprehension occurs, the cause is complete. Though the Buddha transcends annihilationism and eternalism, at the time of awakening the principle is manifested. The fruit of the fruit is not something attained, and the cause too does not perish. It is still from generating the aspiration that one first verifies it. The fruit investigates the horizon of the cause. The bodhisattva has already accomplished perfect enlightenment. Thus one knows: when the source of the cause and the position of the fruit are set in opposition, one naturally incurs the defect of annihilationism and eternalism. With the same-substance single understanding, one further incurs the awareness of substance and function. Naturally, within the approach of conditioned arising, the cause is permitted to be mutually identical. At the place where the dharma-realm is realized, the fruit is distinct from preliminary discourse.

---

## 9. Comprehending Form and Emptiness

**Question:** The phenomena of conditioned arising are differentiated and distinct. The empty principle of the dharma-realm transcends all limitation. The one penetrates, the other obstructs; the meaning of sovereign freedom is at odds. Sometimes hidden, sometimes manifest, the basis for their fusion cannot be determined. If one wishes to make their power equal in a single flavor and their meaning display the same source, please let us hear your virtuous words and open your lofty proclamation.

**Response:** Form embraces emptiness to be accomplished, like a moving wave that gathers water. Phenomena contain principle to be completed, like golden vessels that follow their mold. Form depends on emptiness to be established; emptiness depends on form to be illuminated. When they mutually negate each other, both meanings must vanish. When they mutually accomplish each other, both approaches are simultaneously manifest. When manifest, they are simultaneously hidden; therefore, contemplating form, it is always empty. When empty, they are simultaneously manifest; therefore, contemplating emptiness, it is always form. Form is precisely non-form; emptiness is likewise non-emptiness. Each has power over the other, and each is without the other. They mutually accomplish each other, and they mutually negate each other. Therefore the meaning of "unborn" is made manifest. Through mutual accomplishment, the approach of conditioned arising is revealed. Form has no inherent nature; raising it up, the entire substance is emptiness. Emptiness has no inherent substance; raising emptiness up, the entirety is form. Form and emptiness are not two; they perfectly penetrate as a single horizon. Furthermore, making the one penetrate and the one obstruct, pervading all characteristics yet not yet fused. When there is hidden understanding at the level of the nature, then and only then does one hit the center. This is my respectful response.
