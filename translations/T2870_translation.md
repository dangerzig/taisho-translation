# Sūtra on Resolving Doubts in the Age of the Semblance Dharma

## *Xiàngfǎ Juéyí Jīng* (像法決疑經)

Taishō 2870

Author unknown. (Classified as apocryphal.)

*Note: This is a sūtra, likely of Chinese composition (classified in the Taishō as apocryphal), set at the time of the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, between the sal trees at the Bhadrika River. It takes the form of a dialogue between the Buddha and a bodhisattva named Chángshī ("Constant Giving") about the proper practice of merit in the coming Age of the Semblance Dharma (xiàngfǎ), when the true Dharma has declined. The Buddha warns of numerous abuses that will arise: monks who seek fame and profit rather than liberation, donors who exclude the poor from charitable feasts, people who build new temples rather than repair old ones, monks who engage in commerce and fortune-telling, and those who distort the Buddha's teaching through literalistic interpretation. The text strongly emphasizes compassionate giving to the poor, orphaned, and sick (the "field of compassion," bēitián) over donations to monastics (the "field of respect," jìngtián), a theme characteristic of Chinese Buddhist moral literature. It also addresses the controversial question of meat-eating, with the Buddha declaring that no true disciple has ever eaten the flesh of sentient beings. The sūtra concludes with Chángshī's contemplation of the Tathāgata and sentient beings as ultimately empty, illusory, and dreamlike, and the Buddha's praise of this understanding as correct contemplation.*

---

Thus have I heard. At one time, the Buddha was at the Bhadrika River, between the twin śāla trees. He had finished converting Subhadra. The great bodhisattvas, śrāvaka disciples, great Brahmā-kings, devas, nāgas, spirits, kings of various lands, and the entire great assembly remained solemnly gathered, not dispersing.

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the great assembly: "The Great Parinirvāṇa [Sūtra] has been extensively taught. I have already described for the Bodhisattva Pǔguǎng the buddha-lands of the ten directions. If any of you in the great assembly have doubts, you may quickly ask. The unsurpassed Dharma-treasure will not long endure before it is destroyed." When the great assembly heard these words, they wept inconsolably. Only those who had attained liberation were unable to feel grief and attachment.

---

## The Question of Chángshī Bodhisattva

At that time, from within the assembly, a bodhisattva named Chángshī ("Constant Giving"), empowered by the Buddha's majestic presence, rose from his seat, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask a question, but I fear it may grieve the holy heart. May the Tathāgata not blame me."

The Buddha told Chángshī: "The Tathāgata has already transcended the eight worldly conditions. Why should you hesitate?"

Chángshī Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! After the Tathāgata departs from the world, all sentient beings will no longer behold the Tathāgata's physical form or hear the true Dharma. In the future age, during the time of the Semblance Dharma, wholesome dharmas will gradually decline and evil will blaze fiercely. At such a time, what kind of merit should you instruct sentient beings to cultivate that would be most supreme?"

---

## The Buddha's Teaching on Merit

The World-Honored One told Chángshī Bodhisattva: "Excellent, excellent! Sentient beings of future ages are truly pitiable. Why? Because all sentient beings will toil painfully in practice without understanding the correct principle. They will pile up merit, yet the reward they receive will be meager.

"Good son! In the future age, bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, kings, ministers, elders, householders, and brahmins will show contempt for my Dharma and treat the Three Treasures as trivial and insignificant. Though they perform various good deeds, they will do so seeking fame, profit, and superiority over others, without a single thought of the mind that transcends the world.

### Errors in Charitable Giving

"Good son! In the future age of the Semblance Dharma, there will be immeasurable disasters and evils. What are they?

"All religious and lay people will not know the rules of the Dharma. When donors hold feasts and invite monks, they will send people to guard the gates, barring certain bhikṣus from entering the feast. If the poor and destitute wish to enter to beg for food, they too will be blocked and refused. Feasts held in this way waste food and produce no share of merit.

"Furthermore, some sentient beings will only wish to cultivate good for themselves alone, without transforming other sentient beings. When they see others doing good, they cannot rejoice or contribute even a small amount. Such people's merit is meager and inferior.

"Furthermore, some sentient beings, seeing that others' old monasteries, stūpas, images, and sūtras are damaged and ruined, will refuse to repair them, saying: 'These were not originally built by me. Why should I repair them? I would rather build new ones myself.' Good son! For all sentient beings, repairing what is old produces far more merit than building something new.

"Furthermore, some sentient beings, seeing others gathered to perform meritorious works, give away their entire family fortunes in charity only for the sake of fame. Yet when they see the poor, orphaned, and destitute, they scold and drive them out, not sparing even a hair's worth. Such sentient beings are called 'inverted practitioners of good,' 'deluded cultivators of merit,' 'improper performers of merit.' Such people are truly pitiable: they spend much wealth yet obtain little merit.

### The Field of Compassion

"Good son! I once told the great assembly: if a person were to make offerings to all the buddhas, bodhisattvas, and śrāvakas of the ten directions for an incalculable number of lifetimes, this would not compare with giving a single mouthful of food to an animal. That merit surpasses the former by hundreds of thousands of millions of times, beyond measure.

"Good son! When I have spoken of giving (*dāna*) in various sūtras, my intention was to cause all monastics and laypeople to cultivate the compassionate mind and give to the poor, orphaned, and aged, and even to hungry dogs. My disciples do not understand my meaning: they give exclusively to the 'field of respect' and do not give to the 'field of compassion.' The field of respect is the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṃgha. The field of compassion is the poor, orphaned, and aged, down to the ants. Of these two fields, the field of compassion is most supreme.

"Good son! If a person of great wealth practices giving alone, from birth to old age, this does not compare with a multitude of people, regardless of wealth or status, whether monastic or lay, who encourage one another, each contributing a small amount, pooling it in one place, and distributing it as appropriate to the poor, orphaned, aged, gravely ill, and distressed. That merit is exceedingly great. Even if they do not give, the merit of [the aspiration to give] arises continuously, moment after moment, without exhaustion. Giving practiced alone produces very little merit.

### Corrupt Monks of the Future

"Good son! In the future age, my disciples will love fine clothing and crave delicious flavors. They will be greedy for profit, miserly in their accumulation, and will not cultivate the compassionate mind. They will practice only anger. When they see others doing good, they will contend with and disparage them, saying: 'This person lives by wrong livelihood, flattery, and deception, seeking fame and profit.' When they see someone giving to the poor and destitute, they will grow angry and think: 'A monastic has no use for giving! One should only cultivate meditation and wisdom. Why stir up these useless affairs?' Those who think thus are Māra's retinue. When such persons die, they will fall into the great hells and experience suffering for ages. From hell they will fall into the hungry ghost realm and suffer great torments. From the hungry ghost realm they will be born five hundred times as dogs. After that, they will be born five hundred times in extreme poverty, constantly plagued by hunger and various sufferings, without a single moment of ease. Why? Because when others were giving, they did not rejoice.

"Good son! I recall that I attained buddhahood entirely because, throughout immeasurable kalpas, I practiced giving (*dāna*) and rescued the poor and distressed. All buddhas of the ten directions also attained buddhahood through giving. Therefore, in sūtra after sūtra, I have said that giving is the first of the six pāramitās.

"Good son! It is like a person whose two feet are both broken: though he wishes to walk far, he cannot go. A bhikṣu is the same: though he practices the other five pāramitās for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, if he does not practice giving, he cannot reach the far shore of nirvāṇa.

"Good son! One who does not practice giving will have impure precepts. One whose precepts are impure will lack compassion. One without compassion cannot practice patience. Without patience, there is no effort. Without effort, there is no concentration. Without concentration, there is no wisdom. Without wisdom, one is constantly overwhelmed by immeasurable adventitious afflictions.

"Good son! This dharma-gate of giving is honored by all buddhas of the three times. Therefore, among the four methods of attraction, material giving is the most supreme.

### Against Literalist Interpretation

"Good son! At one time I praised precept-keeping; at another time, patience; at another, concentration; at another, wisdom; at another, the ascetic practices (*dhūta*); at another, fewness of desires; at another, the śrāvakas; at another, the bodhisattvas. All these were adapted to different occasions and faculties. In future ages, evil monks who do not understand my intent will each cling to their own views, mutually condemn one another as right or wrong, and destroy my Dharma.

"Evil monks will also sit on the teaching-seat and expound the sūtras, yet without comprehending my deep intent, they will follow the letter and grasp the meaning, contradicting the true Dharma of the supreme reality. They will constantly praise themselves, saying: 'My interpretation accords with the Buddha's intent. Other Dharma masters deceive monks and laypeople.' Those who speak thus will sink forever in the ocean of suffering.

"Evil monks who see others practicing meditation will say: 'This person is foolish, sitting like a dead stump. He does not understand the sūtras and treatises. What practice is there?' Those who speak thus will bear the consequences for kalpas.

### On Meat-Eating

"Evil monks, for the sake of fame and profit, will mutually slander one another. Some will cultivate merit without following the sūtras and treatises, pursuing their own views, taking the wrong for the right, unable to distinguish what is correct from what is deviant...

"Evil monks will also uphold the Vinaya, yet without understanding its deep meaning, they will say: 'In the Vinaya, the Buddha permitted eating meat.'

"Good son! If I were to explain the meaning of eating meat, śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and bodhisattvas of the lower grounds would be bewildered, and ordinary monks who heard it would slander [the teaching]. Therefore, what the Vinaya permits regarding eating meat is all inconceivable.

"Good son! From the time I first attained the Way until this day, wherever my disciples have been seen eating meat: ordinary people truly saw them eating meat. Furthermore, some sentient beings saw bhikṣus manifesting the eating of meat. Furthermore, some sentient beings understood that when those bhikṣus ate meat, they were deeply entering immeasurable gates of antidotes, and immeasurable bhikṣus thereby severed the upper, middle, and lower afflictions, and immeasurable bhikṣus liberated sentient beings and guided them into the Buddha's Way. The Tathāgata's methods of transformation are inconceivable.

"Since I attained buddhahood, my disciples have never eaten the flesh of sentient beings. What I permitted in the Vinaya as 'meat' is known with certainty not to arise from the four great elements; not to be born from a womb, egg, moisture, or transformation; not to be conjoined with consciousness or life. You should know that in all the world, there is no such meat [as what ordinary people imagine].

### The Buddha's Vision

"Good son! The immeasurable beings gathered here today each see differently. Some see the Tathāgata entering parinirvāṇa. Some see the Tathāgata abiding in the world for one kalpa. Some see him abiding for immeasurable kalpas. Some see the Tathāgata's sixteen-foot body; some see a small body; some a great body. Some see the reward-body seated in the Lotus Treasury World-Ocean, teaching the mind-ground dharma-gate to billions of Śākyamuni Buddhas. Some see the dharma-body, identical with empty space, without distinction, without marks, without obstruction, pervading the dharma-realm. Some see this place of śāla trees as nothing but earth, sand, grass, wood, and stone walls. Some see this place adorned with gold, silver, the seven treasures, pure and magnificent. Some see this place as the realm where all buddhas of the three times have walked. Some see this place as the inconceivable true dharma-body, the real realm of all buddhas.

"Good son! When buddhas appear in the world, traveling, stopping, and dwelling, all their actions are far removed from worldly characteristics, yet they also do not depart from the world to reveal the Dharma of true reality. What the Tathāgata teaches encompasses all dharmas. In expounding a single word, a single phrase, a single sound, he causes all sentient beings of every kind and every faculty to understand differently, each in their own way. The unshared dharmas of the Tathāgata are inconceivable, beyond the knowledge of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas.

"Evil monks in the age of the Semblance Dharma who do not understand my intent, who cling to their own views in expounding the twelve divisions of scripture, who follow the letter and make definitive pronouncements: know that such persons are enemies of all buddhas of the three times and will swiftly destroy my Dharma.

"Good son! All buddhas teach the Dharma always relying on the two truths. When teaching the conventional truth, they do not contradict the ultimate truth: [they use] what is near to point to what is distant, establishing images to express the profound. Evil monks who cannot understand this, who slander and disbelieve, who grasp the meaning according to appearances, will suffer the consequences for kalpas."

---

## On Corrupt Practices

"These evil monks also call themselves 'Dharma masters,' 'Vinaya masters,' and 'Meditation masters.' These three kinds of practitioners can destroy my Dharma more than any others. These three kinds of people denounce one another's faults. These three kinds of people fall into hell as swiftly as an arrow."

Chángshī Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! When will these monks appear?"

"Good son! After I have passed away, one thousand years later, evil dharmas will gradually increase. Eleven hundred years later, evil monks and nuns will fill Jambudvīpa everywhere, not cultivating virtue but seeking wealth, practicing what is unlawful, keeping the eight kinds of impure possessions, lacking the ten virtues of a teacher, keeping two novices despite not having completed ten rains [of ordination]..."

The Buddha continued to describe the decay of the Dharma: monasteries and stūpas built everywhere but falling into ruin; laypeople who build temples but do not provide monks with food, clothing, bedding, or medicine, instead borrowing from and exploiting them; secular officials who tax monastic property, commandeer the Three Treasures' servants and animals; monks who engage in commerce, fortune-telling, and medical practice instead of cultivating the Way.

---

## The Correct Practice

"Good son! At such a time, all monastics and laypeople should cultivate great compassion and patience, enduring the wrongs of others, thinking: 'All sentient beings, from beginningless time, are my fathers and mothers. All sentient beings, from beginningless time, are my brothers, sisters, spouses, and relatives.' For this reason, one should have compassion and sympathy for all sentient beings, rescuing them according to one's ability.

"Good son! Giving with a compassionate heart to the poor, orphaned, aged, and all who suffer, down to the ants: that merit is most supreme. Good son! If I were to describe at length the merit of giving to the orphaned, poor, and sick, even an entire kalpa would not suffice. As the time of nirvāṇa has come, I speak to you in brief."

---

## Chángshī's Contemplation

At that time, the entire great assembly, hearing the Buddha describe the various disasters of the future age of the Semblance Dharma, felt their hair stand on end and wept inconsolably. The Buddha told the assembly: "Cease your weeping. This is the way of the world: good inevitably has evil, flourishing inevitably has decline."

The Buddha again told Chángshī Bodhisattva: "Set aside these matters. With what marks do you contemplate the Tathāgata? And with what marks do you contemplate sentient beings?"

Chángshī Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I contemplate the Tathāgata as not coming from a prior limit, not reaching a far shore, not abiding in between; as neither existent nor non-existent, neither appearing nor disappearing, neither form nor formlessness, neither conditioned nor unconditioned, neither permanent nor annihilated, neither defiled nor undefiled; as identical with empty space and identical with the dharma-nature. From the time of his initial attainment of the Way to the time of nirvāṇa, in the interval between, I do not see the Tathāgata speaking a single sentence of Dharma. Yet sentient beings see him appearing and disappearing, teaching the Dharma and liberating people. The realm of the Tathāgata is inconceivable: it cannot be known by consciousness, it cannot be understood by wisdom. It transcends the three times yet does not leave the three times. Only the Tathāgata himself awakens to this Dharma. This is how I contemplate the Tathāgata.

"World-Honored One! I now contemplate the marks of sentient beings' four great elements as being like clouds in the sky, like a mirage in the heat, like a gandharva-city, like an illusion, like a transformation, like an empty village, like an image in a mirror, like the moon in water, like an echo in a valley. Feeling, perception, volitions, and consciousness are all likewise.

"World-Honored One! The marks of sentient beings' minds are inconceivable, beyond the knowledge of śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and lower-ground bodhisattvas. The marks of sentient beings neither come nor go, are neither existent nor non-existent, neither internal nor external. They come from nowhere and go nowhere, yet they constantly flow and revolve, falsely undergoing suffering. This is entirely because sentient beings, from beginningless time, have been deeply attached to the view of self. Because of this attachment, craving increases, and through the twelve links of dependent origination, they suffer through the long night without end. The true mark of sentient beings has always been empty and quiescent. For this reason, the bodhisattva arises great compassion in their midst.

"World-Honored One! All the good and evil karma of sentient beings is created by the one mind alone, with no other dharma. This is how I contemplate the marks of sentient beings."

---

## The Buddha's Praise

The Buddha told Chángshī Bodhisattva: "Excellent, excellent! You have spoken the Dharma well. What you have said is acceptable to the buddhas. When bodhisattvas practice the four methods of attraction and the six pāramitās, they should contemplate the marks of sentient beings in this way.

"Good son! When a bodhisattva practices giving, one should not distinguish between the field of merit and the non-field of merit. If one sees sentient beings in poverty and suffering, one should give to all. When practicing giving, one should contemplate thus: there is no receiver, no giver, and the gift is likewise [empty]. All three are equally empty, with no attachment. Why? Because all dharmas are without self or 'mine.' When practicing giving, one should not hope for reward in the present or for human or heavenly pleasures in the future. One should give solely for the sake of sentient beings, seeking great bodhi, for the sake of bringing peace and joy to immeasurable sentient beings, and for the sake of gathering in all evil sentient beings and causing them to abide in wholesome dharmas.

"Furthermore, one should contemplate thus: the realm of bodhi and the realm of sentient beings are both empty and quiescent. Relying on words and letters, sentient beings attain bodhi. In the true Dharma, there is neither attainment nor realization.

"Good son! It is like a person who dreams at night of various things: dreaming of being imprisoned and suffering greatly, giving rise to immense grief, and then being freed; then dreaming again of being a great king, powerful and free, enjoying great happiness; and within the dream thinking: 'Just before, I suffered like that, and now I am free and enjoying great happiness.' Upon this thought, one suddenly awakens: the suffering and happiness are nowhere to be found. Such dream-events are neither existent nor non-existent. All dharmas are likewise. Contemplating in this way is called 'correct contemplation.'"

When this Dharma was taught, immeasurable bodhisattvas attained the stage of buddhahood; immeasurable bodhisattvas entered the stage of one-life-to-go (*ekajāti-pratibaddha*); immeasurable bodhisattvas each advanced in their respective practices; immeasurable humans and devas attained the four fruits of the path; immeasurable śrāvakas entered the bodhisattva stage; and immeasurable beings of various classes aroused the aspiration for bodhi.

"Good son! In the future age, if the four groups of disciples hear this sūtra and give rise to a joyful mind, the merit they obtain will be immeasurable and boundless."

The Buddha told Ānanda and the great assembly: "Receive and uphold this well. Do not neglect or forget it. This sūtra is called 'Resolving Doubts in the Age of the Semblance Dharma.' It is also called 'Rescuing the Orphaned and Destitute.' Uphold it accordingly."

At that time, the great assembly, having heard what the Buddha taught, received it with single-minded reverence, prostrated, and departed. Together they prepared the arrangements for the cremation, their grief shaking heaven and earth.
