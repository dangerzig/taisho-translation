# 佛說阿含正行經
## Sūtra on Right Practice of the Āgama Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0151

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "I shall teach you a sūtra. The beginning is good, the middle is good, and the end is good. Its meaning is profound and it explains the path that transcends the world. With a right mind as the foundation, listen to my words, so that future generations may transmit and practice them." The bhikṣus joined their palms and received the teaching.

The Buddha said: "Within the human body there are five thieves that drag a person into the evil paths. What are the five? First, form. Second, sensation. Third, perception. Fourth, birth and death. Fifth, consciousness. These five are what people constantly think about."

The Buddha said: "People are constantly deceived by their eyes, deceived by their ears, deceived by their nose, deceived by their mouth, and deceived by their body. The eyes can only see but cannot hear. The ears can only hear but cannot see. The nose can only know smells but cannot hear. The mouth can only know flavors but cannot know smells. The body can only know cold and warmth but cannot know flavors. These five all belong to the mind. The mind is the foundation."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Those who seek the path should straighten your minds. From delusion one falls into the twelve links of dependent origination, and thus there is birth and death. What are the twelve? First is fundamental ignorance, second is formations, third is consciousness, fourth is name-and-form, fifth is the six sense bases, sixth is contact, seventh is feeling, eighth is craving, ninth is grasping, tenth is becoming, eleventh is birth, twelfth is death. Those who practice good deeds will again be reborn as humans. Those who practice evil deeds will after death enter hell, the hungry ghost realm, or the animal realm. The Buddha sat reflecting: it is because of people's delusion that there is birth and death. What is delusion? Originally one comes from delusion, and being born as a human in this life, one is again deluded in mind, the eyes are not opened, and one does not know where death will lead. Seeing the Buddha, one does not ask. Seeing the sūtras, one does not read. Seeing śramaṇas, one does not serve them. One does not believe in the path and its virtues. Seeing one's parents, one is not respectful. One does not reflect on the sufferings of the world. One does not know the severity of the punishments in hell. This is called delusion, and because of it there is birth and death. If birth and death do not cease, life passes as quickly as the space between breaths, and nothing is more fragile than a human life. Within the human body there are three things: when the body dies, consciousness departs, the mind departs, and thought departs. These three constantly follow one another. For those who practice evil, after death they enter hell, the hungry ghost realm, the animal realm, or the ghost realm. For those who practice good, there are also three that follow one another: they are reborn either in heaven or among humans. Those who fall into these five paths all do so because their minds are not right."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Straighten your minds, straighten your eyes, straighten your ears, straighten your nose, straighten your mouth, straighten your body, straighten your thoughts. The body will eventually return to dust, but the spirit-soul need not again enter hell, the hungry ghost realm, the animal realm, or the ghost realm. Consider how in a household an unruly son is seized by the authorities: it is all because his mind was not right. The human body has a hundred parts, like a cart with a hundred parts. If a person is greatly greedy and prone to anger, not reflecting on the things within the body, after death one enters hell, and regret will be of no avail."

The Buddha said: "I myself abandoned my kingdom and renounced the status of a wheel-turning king, anxious to sever birth and death, wishing to save the people of the world and enable them to attain the path of nirvāṇa. Those who are most diligent immediately attain the path of an arhat. Those who are second in diligence attain the path of a non-returner. Those who are third in diligence attain the path of a once-returner. Those who are fourth in diligence attain the path of a stream-enterer. For those who cannot be greatly diligent, they should uphold the five precepts: first, do not kill; second, do not steal; third, do not speak with a forked tongue; fourth, do not engage in sexual misconduct; fifth, do not drink alcohol."

The Buddha said: "In sitting and rising, people should constantly reflect on these four things. What are the four? First, observe one's own body and observe the bodies of others. Second, observe one's own sensations and observe the sensations of others. Third, observe one's own mind and observe the minds of others. Fourth, observe one's own mental phenomena and observe the mental phenomena of others. If internally one's desires are again stirred up, one should slightly straighten the mind and examine the body: being full is also an extreme, being hungry is also an extreme, standing is also an extreme, sitting is also an extreme, walking is also an extreme, being cold is also an extreme, being hot is also an extreme, lying down is also an extreme. When drowsiness comes, one should rouse oneself and sit up. If sitting is not upright, one should stand. If standing is not upright, one should walk. If the mind is perhaps not upright, one should correct it. It is like a king leading his troops into battle: the brave are in front. Already in front, the cowardly then retreat, but if they retreat they run into those behind them. A śramaṇa who has already renounced his home, left wife and children, shaved his hair and beard, and become a śramaṇa may suffer for one lifetime, but afterward will gain lasting liberation. One who has attained the path is inwardly joyful. One views one's wife as one would view a sister, and views one's children as acquaintances, without any greedy or loving attachment. One constantly has compassion for the people of all ten directions, for devas and humans, for those in hell, the hungry ghost realm, the animal realm, and for flying and crawling creatures of all kinds, wishing them all to be wealthy, at ease, and liberated, attaining the path of nirvāṇa. Seeing insects on the ground, one should feel compassion for them. Knowing birth, one is no longer deluded. One who can have such a mind constantly recollects and serves the Buddha as a person recollects father and mother. It is like a prisoner condemned to death in a prison: a worthy person comes and intercedes for the prisoner, and the wise prisoner constantly remembers the worthy person's grace. A bhikṣu who has attained the path constantly recollects the Buddha in this way. One recollects the sūtras as a person recollects food and drink."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! You should serve one another as a younger brother serves an elder brother. If among you there are those who are deluded, they should ask those who are wise. Teach one another in turn. Asking the wise is like having a lamp in the darkness. Do not plot or commit evil in secret. Do not quarrel or argue. Seeing gold and silver, regard them as you would dirt. Do not bear false witness to have a person convicted. Do not transmit another's evil words or stir up discord with divisive speech. Do not wound another's feelings. What you have not heard, do not say you heard. What you have not seen, do not say you saw. When walking on the path, always keep your head lowered and look at the ground, so as not to step on and kill insects. Do not covet another's wife. Do not gaze at another's wife. Sit and reflect on your own thoughts, and remove the mind of greedy attachment. Only then can you practice the path."

The Buddha said: "Those who seek the path should sit in a secluded and quiet place. Breathe and observe your own inhalation and exhalation. Know whether the breath is short or long. When the breath does not return, the body is entirely strained. When one holds the breath without breathing, the body is also strained. Distinguish and reflect: who made this body? The mind should observe outwardly and also observe inwardly. Reflecting on oneself, one is joyful and different from ordinary people. At such a time, the mind has no use for all the precious treasures in the world. The mind gradually follows the right path. If the thoughts are about to stir again, one should restrain them and immediately return to one's watchfulness. The mind then returns. It is like a person who has a mirror that is not bright and cannot reflect the face: when one polishes away the grime, one can see one's own reflection. When a person has already removed greed, lust, anger, and delusion, it is like polishing a mirror. Carefully reflect: nothing in the world is solid, and nothing is permanent."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Hold your mind as you would a square stone. A stone stands in the middle of a courtyard: rain falling cannot destroy it, the sun blazing cannot melt it, wind blowing cannot move it. Hold your mind like that stone!"

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The minds of all people in the world are like flowing water with grasses and trees in it, each drifting along without looking back at one another. Those in front do not look back at those behind, and those behind do not look at those in front. The grasses and trees drift along, each as before. People's minds are also like this: one thought comes and one thought goes, like grasses and trees, without front or back looking at one another. In heaven above and earth below, there is nothing more to delight in. One sojourns temporarily between heaven and earth, abandoning the body and not being reborn again. When the path is accomplished, only then does one know the teacher's grace. Seeing the teacher, one serves the teacher. Not seeing the teacher, one reflects on the teacher's instruction, as a person recollects father and mother. When the mind is settled, only then can one have single-pointed concentration, and then one has compassion for the people of the world, for devas and humans, and for all flying and crawling creatures. Sitting, one spontaneously laughs: 'I have already freed myself from the world and the five paths.' The first is the path of devas. The second is the path of humans. The third is the path of hungry ghosts. The fourth is the path of animals. The fifth is the path of hell. One who has attained arhatship and wishes to fly, to transform, can do so. To produce water and fire from within the body, one can do so. To pass through without gaps and enter without openings, one can also do so. To leave behind the sufferings of the world and attain the path of nirvāṇa, one can also do so."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The path must not be left unpracticed. The sūtras must not be left unread."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, five hundred śramaṇas all attained arhatship. All the śramaṇas rose, came forward, and placing their heads on the ground, bowed to the Buddha.
