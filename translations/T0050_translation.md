# 受歲經
## Sūtra on the Pravāraṇā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0050

Translated from Chinese by Dharmarakṣa (Zhu Fahu) of the Western Jin dynasty

---

## Fascicle 1

### Sūtra on the Pravāraṇā, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling at Rājagṛha in the Kalandakanivāpa Bamboo Grove, performing the Pravāraṇā together with a great assembly of bhikṣus. At that time, the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana addressed the bhikṣus: "Friends, with regard to a bhikṣu who performs the Pravāraṇā: you should speak to him, you should instruct him, you should admonish him, you should hold him in affectionate regard, for this is of first importance. Why so? Friends, there may be a person perverse and difficult to teach, joined with unwholesome qualities. Among those who live the brahma-life with him, none will speak to him, none will instruct him, none will admonish him, none will hold him in affectionate regard, and this is of first importance for such a person. How, friends, is one perverse and difficult to teach? Among those who live the brahma-life with him, none will speak to him, none will instruct him, none will admonish him, none will hold him in affectionate regard, and this is of first importance for such a person. Friends, such a person is one with wrong seeking, joined with wrong seeking. Friends, one with wrong seeking joined with wrong seeking, this quality is perverse and difficult to teach. Likewise: attachment and desire, hatred and anger, avarice and envy; not abandoning flattery, obsequiousness, and deceit; without shame, without moral dread; with the fetter of resentment, speaking words of resentment; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he retaliates with words; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he repeats it to many; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he slanders; whatever each one says, he spreads it abroad outside; with the fetter of hatred, he widely associates with evil friends and evil companions; without gratitude, without reciprocity. Friends, one without gratitude and without reciprocity, this is perverse and difficult to teach. These, friends, are the qualities of one who is perverse and difficult to teach.

"Among those who live the brahma-life with him, none will speak to him, none will instruct him, none will admonish him, none will hold him in affectionate regard, and this is of first importance for such a person. Friends, a bhikṣu should reflect on himself: 'Such a person with wrong seeking, joined with wrong seeking, is one I do not hold in mind. If I were to have wrong seeking and be joined with wrong seeking, others likewise would not hold me in mind.' A bhikṣu, observing with equanimity, should not have wrong seeking; one should train in this manner. Likewise: attachment and desire, hatred and anger, avarice and envy; not abandoning flattery, obsequiousness, and deceit; without shame, without moral dread; with the fetter of resentment, speaking words of resentment; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he retaliates with words; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he repeats it to many; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he slanders; whatever each one says, he spreads it abroad outside; with hatred, he widely associates with evil friends and evil companions; without gratitude, without reciprocity. Friends, one without gratitude and without reciprocity is one I do not cherish. If I were without gratitude and without reciprocity, others likewise would not hold me in mind. A bhikṣu, observing with equanimity, should not be without reciprocity; one should train in this, friends.

"With regard to a bhikṣu who has not yet performed the Pravāraṇā: you should speak to him, you should instruct him, you should admonish him, you should hold him in affectionate regard, for this is of first importance. Why so? Friends, there may be a person without perversity, amenable to instruction, who lives the brahma-life: one should speak to him, instruct him, admonish him, and hold him in affectionate regard, for this is of first importance for such a person. How, friends, is one without perversity and amenable to instruction? Among those who live the brahma-life with him, one should speak to him, instruct him, admonish him, and hold him in affectionate regard, for this is of first importance for such a person. Friends, such a person is one without wrong seeking, not joined with wrong seeking. Friends, one without wrong seeking, not joined with wrong seeking, this quality is without perversity. Likewise: free from attachment and desire, free from hatred and anger, free from avarice and envy; not given to flattery, obsequiousness, or deceit; not without shame, not without moral dread; without the fetter of resentment, not speaking words of resentment; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he does not retaliate with words; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he does not repeat it to many; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he does not slander; whatever each one says, he does not spread it abroad; he is not given to hatred and spreading ill will; he does not associate with evil friends or evil companions; not without gratitude, not without reciprocity. Friends, such a person, this is the quality of amenability to instruction; this, friends, is the quality of one without perversity who is amenable to instruction. Among those who live the brahma-life with him, one should speak to him, instruct him, admonish him, and hold him in affectionate regard, for this is of first importance for such a person. Friends, a bhikṣu should reflect on himself: 'Such a person without wrong seeking, not joined with wrong seeking, is one I hold in mind. If I were without wrong seeking and not joined with wrong seeking, others likewise would hold me in mind.' A bhikṣu, observing with equanimity, should not have wrong seeking; one should train in this manner. Likewise: free from attachment and desire, free from hatred and anger, free from avarice and envy; not abandoning freedom from flattery, obsequiousness, and deceit; not without shame, not without moral dread; without the fetter of resentment, not speaking words of resentment; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he does not retaliate with words; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he does not repeat it to many; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, he does not slander; whatever each one says, he does not spread it abroad; he is not given to hatred and spreading ill will; he does not associate with evil friends or evil companions; not without gratitude, not without reciprocity. Friends, one who is not without gratitude and not without reciprocity is one I cherish and hold in mind. If I were not without gratitude and not without reciprocity, others likewise would cherish and hold me in mind. I, a bhikṣu, observing with equanimity, should be not without gratitude and not without reciprocity; one should train in this manner.

"Friends, a bhikṣu who makes these observations will benefit greatly: 'Am I abiding in wrong seeking, joined with wrong seeking, or am I not abiding in wrong seeking, not joined with wrong seeking?' Friends, a bhikṣu who observes and knows 'I am abiding in wrong seeking, joined with wrong seeking' will not be pleased, and will strive to put a stop to it. Friends, a bhikṣu who observes and knows 'I do not abide in wrong seeking, not joined with wrong seeking' will then be pleased, pure and luminous, proceeding in the domain of the Buddha, the World-Honored One, and having seen this, practices joyfully. Friends, it is as if a person with good sight were to take up a perfectly clear mirror to examine his own face. Friends, if that person with good sight sees dust and grime on his face, he will not be pleased, and will strive to remove that grime. Friends, if the person with good sight does not see dust and grime on his face, he will be pleased and pure, and having seen this, practices joyfully. In the same way, friends, a bhikṣu who observes and knows 'I am abiding in wrong seeking, joined with wrong seeking' strives to put a stop to it. Friends, a bhikṣu who observes and knows 'I do not abide in wrong seeking, not joined with wrong seeking' will be pleased, pure and luminous, abiding in the domain of the Buddha, the World-Honored One, and having seen this, practices joyfully. Similarly: abiding in attachment and desire, not abiding, free from attachment and desire; abiding in hatred and anger, not abiding, free from hatred and anger; abiding in avarice and envy without abandoning them, not abiding, free from avarice and envy; abiding in flattery, obsequiousness, and deceit, not abiding, free from flattery, obsequiousness, and deceit; abiding in shamelessness and lack of moral dread, not abiding, free from shamelessness and lack of moral dread; abiding in the fetter of resentment and speaking words of resentment, not abiding, without the fetter of resentment and not speaking words of resentment; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, retaliating with words, not abiding in this, when spoken to by a bhikṣu, not retaliating; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, repeating it to many, not abiding in this, when spoken to by a bhikṣu, not repeating it to many; when spoken to by a bhikṣu, slandering, not abiding in this, when spoken to by a bhikṣu, not slandering; whatever each says, spreading it abroad, not abiding in this, whatever each says, not spreading it abroad; hatred, not abiding in this, not given to hatred; widely associating with evil friends and evil companions, not abiding in this, not associating with evil friends and evil companions; without gratitude and without reciprocity, not abiding in this, not without gratitude and not without reciprocity. Friends, a bhikṣu who observes and knows 'I am abiding in ingratitude and lack of reciprocity' will not be pleased, and will strive to put a stop to it. Friends, a bhikṣu who observes and knows 'I am abiding in gratitude and reciprocity' will be pleased, pure and luminous, proceeding in the domain of the Buddha, the World-Honored One, and having seen this, practices joyfully. Friends, it is as if a person with good sight were to take up a clear mirror to examine his own face. Friends, if that person with good sight sees dust and grime on his face, he will not be pleased, and will strive to put a stop to it. Friends, if that person with good sight does not see dust and grime on his face, he will be pleased and pure, and having seen this, practices joyfully. In the same way, friends, a bhikṣu who observes and knows 'I am abiding in ingratitude and lack of reciprocity' will not be pleased, and will strive to put a stop to it. Friends, a bhikṣu who observes and knows 'I am abiding in gratitude and reciprocity' will be pleased, pure and luminous, proceeding in the domain of the Buddha, the World-Honored One. Having seen this, he practices joyfully; having practiced joyfully, he experiences gladness; having experienced gladness, the body becomes tranquil; with the body tranquil, he knows ease and happiness; with ease and happiness, the mind becomes concentrated; with the mind concentrated, he knows and sees things as they truly are; having known and seen things as they truly are, he is disenchanted; with disenchantment, he becomes dispassionate; with dispassion, he is liberated; having attained liberation, he attains the knowledge of liberation, knowing truly: 'Birth is exhausted, the holy life has been accomplished, what had to be done has been done, there is no further existence.'"

The Venerable Maudgalyāyana spoke thus, and those bhikṣus, having heard what the Venerable Maudgalyāyana said, rejoiced and were glad.

---

In the Dan canon this sūtra is entitled the Sūtra on the Pravāraṇā, which the Dan has but the Song does not. In the Song canon it is entitled the Sūtra on the New Pravāraṇā, which the Song has but the Dan does not. According to both sūtras, they are translated by Dharmarakṣa; though the titles are slightly similar, the contents differ considerably, and it is unclear which is correct, nor why the two canons should each contain what the other lacks. Upon consulting the Kaiyuan Catalogue, in the re-translations section of the Rong fascicle-group, there is a Sūtra on the Pravāraṇā translated by Dharmarakṣa (Zhu Fahu), noted as sharing the same original text as the beginning of the twenty-third fascicle of the Madhyamāgama, being a parallel translation of it. In the unique-translations section of the Jing fascicle-group, there is a New Year Sūtra translated by Zhu Tanwulan of the Jin dynasty, also known as Fazheng; the annotation states: "These sūtras are not found in the great text of the Madhyamāgama, and are therefore compiled here." Upon examination, the Dan canon's Sūtra on the Pravāraṇā is largely identical to the twenty-third fascicle of the Madhyamāgama, and is therefore truly the re-translation of the Sūtra on the Pravāraṇā within the Rong fascicle-group. The Song canon's Sūtra on the New Pravāraṇā is entirely distinct from the Madhyamāgama; compared with the New Year Sūtra of the Jing fascicle-group, however, though the wording differs, the main import is not unlike, it appears to be an alternate translation of that sūtra. Yet as for this Sūtra on the New Pravāraṇā in the Song canon's Rong fascicle-group, relative to the New Year Sūtra in the Jing fascicle-group: if it is considered a unique translation, why are there differences between expanded and abbreviated versions and differences of translator, and why is it compiled among the re-translations? If it is considered a re-translation, why is the other listed among the unique translations? This matter must await further investigation. For now, wishing to gather these texts together for learned scholars to consider, we transfer the Sūtra on the New Pravāraṇā and compile it in the Jing fascicle-group; in the present Rong fascicle-group, we take the Dan canon's sūtra as the authentic text.
