# 佛說處處經
## The Sūtra on Various Topics

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0730

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高).

---

The Buddha said: "When a practitioner of the Way is walking the path and a thought of anger arises, one should immediately restrain oneself. Having restrained it, one should then turn to wholesome thoughts. This is the practice of one who walks the Way. If one receives material gifts through generosity, upon obtaining them one should reflect on their impermanence. One should not follow bodily transgressions, meaning not committing killing, stealing, or sexual misconduct. This is called not following bodily transgressions. Freeing oneself from anger means that the mouth also does not speak of it and the mind also does not dwell on it. This is called freeing oneself from anger. The great deep voice within the mind is like a thief whom someone tells: 'What you have done is utterly outrageous.' This is the great deep voice within the mind."

The Buddha said: "The sequential discernment of flavors means that when one eats food, one externally combines it and internally discriminates each taste without confusion. This is the sequential discernment of flavors. The reason the bodhisattva is able to know this is that in past lives, whenever eating, he always first offered food to his parents and to practitioners of the Way, and only then ate himself. This is why he obtained the thirty-two marks and eighty subsidiary characteristics."

The Buddha said: "The bodhisattva, through four things, attains the four fearlessnesses. First, what he himself knows, he also wishes all others to know. Second, in teaching others he is never weary. Third, he is of equal mind, with nothing hidden. Fourth, when he teaches the sūtras to others, he never regrets it afterward. These are the four things. The Buddha also, through four things, attained the four fearlessnesses. First, he speaks in accordance with the Dharma. Second, he does not accept others' possessions. Third, his mind is impartial. Fourth, his precepts are complete."

The Buddha said: "There are also four fearlessnesses. First, he speaks of things as they are. Second, he has no attachment or reluctance. Third, he possesses supernatural powers. Fourth, he does not engage in disputes with others.

"The Buddha obtained the radiance at the crown of his head through three causes. First, offering lamps at buddha temples. Second, delighting in and loving the sūtras of illumination. Third, resolving people's doubts and perplexities.

"The Buddha raises his right hand for four reasons. First, because evil people are ungrateful. Second, because good people are grateful. Third, to demonstrate his teaching. Fourth, to tell people not to do evil.

"The Buddha raises his right hand for four further reasons. First, he wishes people of the ten directions to all come and study the Way, following his intention in practice. Second, he wishes to show people the merit he has practiced, the results of which now manifest as these marks. Third, he exhorts people to uphold the precepts. Fourth, it is his natural deportment, and also because he does not wish to see people's evil attitudes. This is why he always raises his right hand.

"The Buddha does not wear shoes for three reasons. First, to encourage practitioners to have few desires. Second, to display the wheel-marks on the soles of his feet. Third, so that people who see him will be filled with joy.

"The Buddha walks with his feet four inches above the ground for three reasons. First, because he sees worms and ants on the ground. Second, because there is living grass on the ground. Third, to display his supernatural powers, and also to cause people to fix their minds on the Buddha's walking.

"The ground becomes level wherever the Buddha walks, whether high or low, for three reasons. First, from his fundamental practice of the four immeasurable minds, wishing to bring safety and ease to all. The earth rests upon water, and within the water there are spirits, and worms and ants. All beneath the Buddha's feet experience safety and ease, with their minds unified and their thoughts settled. Therefore what is low becomes high and what is high becomes low. Second, the devas and spirits, practicing meritorious deeds, level the ground for the Buddha. Therefore the high and low become even. Third, when the Buddha was a bodhisattva, he repaired roads and bridges to help people cross. Because of the merit obtained from this, the high and low are made level. He wishes people's minds to be likewise.

"The Buddha does not fly for four reasons. First, to exhort his disciples to be diligent. Second, so that his disciples will listen to the sūtras. Third, to repay the kindness from past lives. Fourth, to manifest his marks. This is why he does not fly.

"When the Buddha was in the world, all the devas, spirits, nāgas, and people came to the Buddha to listen to the sūtras, in hundreds and thousands of rows. Whether walking or sitting, in front or behind, all could see the Buddha's face. Why? Because in his past lives, the Buddha's speech made no distinction between front and back. Therefore there was no one who could not see the Buddha's face. When people lay down, they all placed their heads in the direction of the Buddha, out of reverence.

"When the Buddha first attained the Way, he did not eat for seven days, for four reasons. First, absorbed in contemplation of the Way, he forgot to eat. Second, his mind was unified and felt no hunger. Third, he was filled with joy and felt no thirst. Fourth, he did not think of sensation, perception, mental formations, birth and death, or consciousness. These are the four reasons. He wished people who listen to the sūtras to be likewise.

"The Buddha's robe does not attract dust or water on the inside, nor does dirt adhere to the outside, for two reasons. First, because he never dwells on the faults of others. Second, because he sees that all people have desires, and he wishes to extinguish them. Yet the Buddha also washes his robe, because while external impurities do not cling, internal ones do, since he still has a body. Why? Because his external practices are already complete, therefore impurities do not cling externally. But his internal practices are not yet complete, therefore impurities cling internally. Having a body constitutes internal incompleteness. Having a body is itself a form of transgression, which is why one should not cling to life. And yet one also cherishes life, because through life one attains the Way.

"The Buddha taught eighty billion sūtras dealing with causes and conditions, all to treat three ailments of people. First, greed and lust. Second, anger. Third, delusion. These three are further divided into six, corresponding to the six bases of decline that treat the three ailments. The reason the sūtras are so numerous is like a person taking medicine whose illness is not cured, who must then take another medicine." The Buddha said: "People's minds have many aspects and change rapidly, so there are many sūtras, treating each according to its condition, wishing to bring about swift understanding."

"Even after the Buddha attained the Way, he still had three ailments and six concerns. The six concerns are concern over the six sense-gates. The three ailments are the mind, intention, and consciousness receiving adversity. If the Buddha, having attained the Way, still has these concerns and ailments, how much more so for ordinary people, whose negative karma is not yet exhausted and who cannot cross beyond the world? Until the Buddha's work is complete, he cannot cross beyond the world.

"The Buddha relinquished the remaining twenty years of his lifespan for three reasons. First, because worldly people are attached to the body. Second, because all his teachings had been given. Third, for fear that evil people would slander him and thereby incur heavy offenses. Therefore he entered parinirvana. When the Buddha crossed beyond the world, he did not take his body, nor did he take his mind. It was simply that suffering was extinguished. Earth, water, fire, wind, and space are always present in the world and are never cut off.

"The Buddha's body was cremated after his passing for three reasons. First, because it would decompose. Second, because worms and insects would be born in it. Third, so that people who bring incense and flowers to make offerings can obtain merit.

"The Buddha smiles, and five-colored light issues from his mouth, for five reasons. First, he wishes people to ask questions, and through asking, to gain benefit. Second, he fears people would say the Buddha does not know why he smiles. Third, to display the light within his mouth. Fourth, he smiles at those who lack sincerity. Fifth, he smiles at the arhats who cling to emptiness and cannot attain the bodhisattva path. The light returns and enters through the crown of his head to show future generations the way to great illumination."

When the Buddha was about to pass beyond the world, the monks addressed the Buddha, saying: "All people in the world obtain merit from the Buddha. Now that the Buddha is passing beyond the world, from whom shall the people of the world obtain merit?"

The Buddha said: "Monks! Though I pass beyond the world, the sūtras and Dharma will remain. Furthermore, there are four causes from which merit can be obtained. First, when animals have nothing to eat, feed them and preserve their lives. Second, when people fall ill and have no one to care for them, provide for them and tend them so they may be at ease. Third, protect and look after the poor, orphaned, and alone. Fourth, when a person is alone, practicing meditation and contemplating the Way, without clothing or food, provide for and look after them. These are the four things. If one practices generosity with a wholesome intention, the merit obtained is equal to that of offerings made to the Buddha, without any difference." At that time, the Buddha's entire body was in pain, and he was about to pass beyond the world.

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "The reason I have others teach the sūtras is threefold. First, I fear that after the Buddha's departure, people will not believe the words of other monks. Second, to encourage the minds of my disciples toward understanding: if even the Buddha has monks teach the sūtras, how much more so for others. Third, to display Śāriputra's merits, fearing that other monks might become arrogant, each wanting to teach the sūtras themselves." This is why the Buddha had Śāriputra teach the sūtras.

After the Buddha passed beyond the world, the arhats together reproached Ānanda: "When the Buddha was in the world and wanted water, why did you not give it to him? All beings of the ten directions are to be liberated by the Buddha. Why did you not persuade the Buddha to remain and not enter parinirvana? The Buddha was about to exercise the four bases of supernatural power, and could have remained for one kalpa, or a hundred kalpas, or a thousand kalpas. Why did you not urge the Buddha to exercise the four bases of supernatural power?" Ānanda said: "According to your words, was the Buddha not self-sovereign? Did he need my words? Even supposing he had remained for one kalpa in this world, how then could Maitreya descend and become a buddha? When the Buddha originally practiced the Way, there were eighty billion people who studied with him, all seeking the bodhisattva path. Only two attained buddhahood: one was Śākyamuni and the other was Maitreya. The rest attained the levels of arhat or pratyekabuddha. The Buddha's patience surpasses the earth, his mind is softer than water, his determination is firmer than Mount Sumeru, his merit surpasses the waters of the ocean, and his wisdom surpasses empty space. This is why he attained buddhahood first!"

The Buddha said: "Maitreya has not yet descended for four reasons. First, because the era of merit corresponds to that realm. Second, because the people here are coarse and unable to receive the sūtras. Third, because his merit is not yet complete. Fourth, because there are still those in the world who can teach the sūtras. This is why Maitreya has not descended. When he does descend, there remain five hundred and seventy million six hundred thousand years. In Maitreya's time, people's eyes will be able to see four thousand leagues. The people of Maitreya's time will have eyes that see four thousand leagues because of ten causes cultivated in past lives. First, not obstructing others' eyesight. Second, not gouging out others' eyes. Third, not covering or concealing others' eyes. Fourth, not hiding others' virtues. Fifth, not watching killing. Sixth, not watching stealing. Seventh, not watching sexual misconduct. Eighth, not looking at others' private matters or shortcomings. Ninth, not looking at any evil things. Tenth, offering lamps at buddha temples. These are the ten things.

"The Buddha, the pratyekabuddha, and the arhat: these three share the same Dharma but differ in their practice. The Buddha is one who has an awakened mind. The pratyekabuddha knows through seeing causes and conditions. The arhat knows through sitting in meditation. The pratyekabuddha sees the marks of birth and death through causes and conditions, guards himself and dares not depart from practice, and attains the Way through seeing causes and conditions; therefore he is called a pratyekabuddha. The Buddha is one who, wishing to liberate others through universal practice, therefore seeks beyond the pratyekabuddha. A pratyekabuddha once cut his own body to save five hundred people, and pierced his own neck with a stick. Seeing causes and conditions, he reflected: just as cutting earth reveals water, and as all phenomena are impermanent, he thereby attained the Way. There was a pratyekabuddha who, in past lives, was a bodhisattva for five hundred kalpas. His body already possessed thirty of the marks; he lacked two marks and did not equal the Buddha. The reason he did not equal the Buddha was that he lacked skillful means.

"The Buddha taught the diligent suffering of birth and death and the matters of the three evil paths. A person of keen understanding comprehended this and immediately attained arhatship. Though he became an arhat, he was the most honored among them. Why? Because for five hundred kalpas in past lives he had practiced the bodhisattva path with a compassionate mind, wishing to liberate all beings of the ten directions. Therefore, though he attained arhatship, his wisdom could comprehend regional languages. When he first became an arhat, he could not recall that in past lives he had been a bodhisattva. The Buddha then explained the Buddha's merits to him, and also explained the merits of an arhat. Only then did he understand that the Buddha's merits were greater, and he regretted and wished to change his mind and seek buddhahood."

The Buddha said: "Having already attained the arhat's path, one cannot change course. He then regretted, saying: 'How foolish and deluded I was to take only the arhat's path!' The Buddha then resolved his mind, saying: 'Your wisdom surpasses that of other arhats, but your wisdom does not equal the Buddha's. An arhat cuts off his own suffering but not the suffering of others. The Buddha in his original practice cut off his own suffering and also cut off the suffering of others. This is why they are not equal.'"

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra! There are three kinds of speech by which one can fully understand a person's mind. First, coarse speech. Second, profound speech. Third, leading speech. Possessing these three kinds of speech, one can discern good intentions, evil intentions, and patient intentions. Through these three kinds of speech, one can fully understand a person's mind."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha, saying: "In the time of a finger-snap, does the human mind have sixty births and deaths?"

The Buddha said: "There are nine hundred and sixty births and deaths, which is not even many. Essentially there are three types of mind: wholesome mind, unwholesome mind, and desiring mind. The wholesome mind has three hundred and twenty; the unwholesome mind has three hundred and twenty; the desiring mind has three hundred and twenty. In the time of a finger-snap, the three minds operate simultaneously, combining for nine hundred and sixty births and deaths. Excluding the wholesome mind's three hundred and twenty, there remain six hundred and forty minds of birth and death. The Buddha and Śāriputra both practice the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment, yet their wisdom is not equal. It is like breathing: though breath passes through all the pores, one is only aware of the breath through the nose. The Buddha knows all the pores; Śāriputra's knowledge is like the nostril alone. If you divided Śāriputra's knowledge into sixteen parts, and all the wise people throughout a single buddha-realm of a great trichiliocosm of a thousand suns and moons were combined, they would not equal one part of Śāriputra's knowledge. If you divided the Buddha's wisdom into sixteen parts, all of Śāriputra's knowledge throughout a single buddha-realm would not equal one part of the Buddha's wisdom. The thirty-seven factors of practice have three levels: the great thirty-seven factors, the middle thirty-seven factors, and the small thirty-seven factors. A great mind attains a great mind; a middle mind attains a middle mind; a small mind attains a small mind. When one profoundly and completely practices the thirty-seven factors, one penetrates thoroughly. Why? Because when properly divided into sixteen parts, the human mind originally has sixteen aspects. Therefore the Buddha makes distinctions, which is why they are not the same.

"Śāriputra knew all kinds of Dharma-speech: timely speech, protective speech, and guarding speech. The Buddha then told Śāriputra: 'Set aside untimely speech, because one cannot entrust things to others.' Śāriputra's original name was Upatissa.

"Śāriputra passed beyond the world before the Buddha for three reasons. First, his allotted lifespan was exhausted. Second, he did not wish to see the Buddha pass beyond the world. Third, there was no one left for him to liberate."

After Śāriputra had gone begging and received his food, he returned to the monastery and said he should repay his debt: "Sitting in meditation and contemplating the Way is repaying the debt. Not contemplating the Way while receiving others' offerings is incurring debt. It is like someone who has not finished repaying a debt and then takes on more: the former offenses are not yet finished, and now more offenses are created."

The Buddha said: "Having eaten others' offerings, one must not fail to contemplate the Way. This benefits oneself and also benefits all others. Therefore one should repay the debt."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra is foremost in wisdom. Maudgalyāyana is foremost in supernatural powers. Ānanda is foremost in learning. Rāhula is foremost in precepts. Aniruddha is foremost in divine vision."

The Buddha sent Maudgalyāyana to summon Śāriputra. Śāriputra said: "Try to lift my belt; I fear you will not be able to." Maudgalyāyana then pulled the belt, and the entire great trichiliocosm of a thousand suns, moons, and earth all trembled, yet he could not make the belt budge. Śāriputra then made the intention to arrive first at the Buddha's place. Maudgalyāyana walked behind him but arrived before Śāriputra. The Buddha knew that each of the two was foremost in his own way.

"Among all the monks in the ten directions, none upholds the precepts like Rāhula. Rāhula upholds the precepts for four reasons. First, he thinks: 'I am the Buddha's son.' Second, it is said that he upholds many precepts. Third, he constantly thinks: 'I am a śramaṇa.' Fourth, in upholding precepts, he always wishes to surpass the other monks.

"Śāriputra once took a single bowl of water and poured it into the ocean, stirred the water, and the next morning went back to retrieve the original water. If Śāriputra could do this, how much more so the Buddha! Maudgalyāyana was beaten by people and could not fly away because he could not exercise his supernatural powers. Why could he not exercise his supernatural powers? Because his karmic debts were not yet exhausted."

The Buddha said: "King Bimbisāra went to the twelve gates and returned without knowing that within his own body, six parts were capable of the Way through concentrated intention. He did not know that the body gives rise to the mind or that the mind gives rise to the body; he could not distinguish these. The Buddha, knowing that the king could be saved, went and stayed the night with him. The king did not know it was the Buddha. The Buddha asked: 'King, what dharma do you practice? What path do you follow?' And the king's hair stood on end. The Buddha explained to him the emptiness of all things within the body, and thereupon the king attained the Way. But he did not cut off everything and so did not attain arhatship." The Buddha said: "All practitioners should know that it is the mind that can attain the Way."

The Buddha said: "Long ago, there was one named Mālī who, on a full platform, studied the sūtras for twenty-four years and obtained five words of understanding, cast off defilements, and was no longer anxious. Why? In past lives he had encountered five hundred buddhas and fully understood all the sūtras, but he had locked away the sūtras and the Way, refusing to teach others. Later he fell ill for twenty-four days, and only at the point of death did he repent and call people to teach them. Because of this one merit, he understood five words. How much more so for one who fully teaches others: the merit obtained is incalculable."

The Buddha said: "Long ago, there was a monk named Channa who fell ill. The Buddha sent Śāriputra to bring him alms-food. Śāriputra then asked him: 'Do your eyes still have any awareness?' Channa answered: 'The eyes themselves have no awareness; they only see forms. When consciousness arises, the body arises. When illness arrives as its counterpart, there is illness. But the body itself does not know death. If the body were to die, earth, water, fire, wind, and space would all be extinguished. It is only the consciousness that transfers and is reborn.'

"The Buddha sent Śāriputra begging for alms, and he passed by the house of a courtesan. The woman then closed the door and built a fire, and said to Śāriputra: 'If you will not lie with me, you must enter this great fire.' Śāriputra replied: 'I would rather enter this fire than lie with you. Lust causes people to fall into the evil paths with no time of escape.' A deva then descended and rescued him." The Buddha said: "People simply cannot keep the precepts. But if they can keep them, they need never fear being struck by deviant paths."

The Buddha said: "Long ago, there was a monk sitting in meditation and practicing the Way. The Buddha's younger brother Nanda, drunk on wine, repeatedly came and went near him, singing and playing. The monk addressed the Buddha, saying: 'I wish to go elsewhere to avoid him.' The Buddha said: 'There is no need. This Nanda will presently attain the first fruit of the path.' The Buddha immediately sent Ānanda to go to Nanda's house, with Mahākāśyapa following behind. Śāriputra taught him the sūtras, and Maudgalyāyana displayed supernatural powers. Nanda was then filled with joy and immediately attained the first fruit of the path." The Buddha said: "When monks practice the Way, if they simply keep their minds firm, why worry about not attaining the Way?" When a certain monk heard the Buddha say this, he was filled with joy, and his mind opened in understanding, and he immediately attained arhatship.

The Buddha's cousin was named Sunakṣatra. He attended the Buddha for eight years, then this thought arose in him: "My brother and I walk together, yet he alone is handsome and possesses the thirty-two marks." An evil intention arose. Walking behind the Buddha, he swept away the Buddha's footprints so that people could not see the Buddha's marks. He also told people that the Buddha had no real attainment, but merely used clever words to win people's minds. When Śāriputra and Ānanda heard this, they became anxious and unhappy. The Buddha said: "Sunakṣatra is not speaking evil of me; he is actually praising the Buddha's merits. When he says the Buddha uses words to win people's minds, it is because people's minds have many afflictions." The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "Sunakṣatra does not reflect; he is merely angry. Why does he not reflect? The Buddha has the thirty-two marks, radiance, and supernatural powers, all to subdue deviant paths." The Buddha repeatedly admonished Sunakṣatra, but his truthful words were treated as heretical curses, and therefore Sunakṣatra was merely angry.

The Buddha had an elder aunt who, old in years, with her own hands wove a robe of gold thread and offered it to the Buddha. The Buddha did not accept it. She then said: "The Buddha should have compassion for me and accept it, so that I may obtain merit." Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Could you accept it on my behalf?" The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Have her offer it to all the monks. I too am one of the monks, but it is better to give to the assembly, for it will serve as a cause and condition in future lives. That people in this life meet and feel joy with one another is because they were relatives and good friends in past lives. How do we know this? Because upon meeting, their minds are resolved in understanding." The Buddha said: "People in the world treat each other well only when times are thin, not when times are thick."

The Buddha said: "The first attendant of the Buddha was named Mīxǐ (Meghiya), the next was Sunakṣatra, and the next was Ānanda." The Buddha told the monks: "I am old and wish to have one person attend me." Śāriputra, Mahākāśyapa, Maudgalyāyana, and others each volunteered to attend the Buddha. The Buddha said: "That will not do." Mahākāśyapa and Maudgalyāyana, through meditative contemplation, knew the Buddha wished for Ānanda, and called to Ānanda, saying: "You should attend the Buddha." Ānanda said: "I dare not attend the Buddha. I am unable to attend the Buddha." Maudgalyāyana said: "You must attend the Buddha." Ānanda said: "You must grant me four conditions. First, I do not wish to receive the Buddha's leftover robes. Second, I do not want the Buddha's leftover food. Third, if someone invites the Buddha and the monks do not go, I also will not go. Fourth, when the Buddha is sitting in meditation contemplating the Way, I should be free to come and go." The Buddha said: "Excellent! I grant your wishes." Thereupon Ānanda attended the Buddha. This is why Ānanda alone was able to question the Buddha.

The Buddha's disciples Aniruddha, Nandika, and Kimbila, the three of them sitting together, contemplated seven things. First, having few desires leads to attaining the Way; having many desires does not. Second, knowing contentment leads to attaining the Way; not knowing contentment does not. Third, diligence leads to attaining the Way; lack of diligence does not. Fourth, guarding the six bases of decline and not allowing them to arise leads to attaining the Way; not guarding them and letting them arise does not. Fifth, self-protection leads to attaining the Way; lack of self-protection does not. Sixth, guarding the mind leads to attaining the Way; not guarding the mind does not. Seventh, wisdom leads to attaining the Way; lack of wisdom does not. Together they contemplated these seven things. At that time, they were sixty leagues from the Buddha. The Buddha, then sitting in meditation, immediately knew what the three were thinking. He transported his awareness and appeared before them, saying: "Excellent! Excellent! I will add one more thing for you: not thinking of household life leads to attaining the Way; thinking of household life does not." Thereupon the three were filled with joy and immediately attained the bodhisattva stage.

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "The human eye can see forty-two thousand yojanas. How do we know this? The sun and moon are forty-two thousand yojanas from the earth, and the human eye can see the sun and moon; from this we know what the eye can see. The vastness of the ten directions, billions upon billions of times greater than the sun and moon, is like the sands of the great ocean, billions upon billions of times incalculable. The good and evil, misfortune and fortune that people create, together with the misfortune and fortune of what came before and after, are billions of times incalculable. All good and evil culminate at the time of death. Those who have done evil encounter evil destinations; those who have done good attain good destinations. Misfortune and fortune are all predetermined; all destinations are predetermined. With parents, siblings, spouse, and children: once one attains the Way, one is free; if one does not attain the Way, there is no end."

The Buddha said: "Devadatta only taught people to create merit; he did not teach them to practice the Way. The Buddha teaches people to create merit, uphold the precepts, and guard the mind; only when they attain the Way does he stop. Devadatta himself conceived this thought: 'I shall display supernatural powers before King Ajātaśatru. If the king follows me, everyone else will follow too.' He then transformed into a white elephant, going in and out of the palace alone. He further transformed into a small child of incomparable beauty, who climbed onto the king's lap. The king loved him greatly. Seeing what Devadatta had done, the king thought Devadatta surpassed the Buddha. The king then followed whatever Devadatta said. The king's officials, people, and monks who had not yet attained the Way all followed the king to Devadatta to consult him. Devadatta then went to the Buddha and said: 'The Buddha is old; surely you no longer need to teach the disciples.' Devadatta then called Śāriputra to leave, along with the other monks. Devadatta, foolish and greedy, having this evil intention, thereby lost his supernatural powers."

The Buddha said: "The deeds of a fool are inverted and not in accord with the dharma of the buddhas. Devadatta thought: 'I shall fly away, and people who see me fly will all follow and serve me.' But when he tried to fly, he could no longer fly. Devadatta could not even perceive the moment he lost his supernatural powers. At that time, a brahmin of impartial mind descended from the first heaven and told Śāriputra: 'Devadatta has already lost his supernatural powers.'" The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "Devadatta wishes to call you away, which will cause him to incur still greater offenses." The Buddha said to the deva: "Even before Devadatta obtained supernatural powers, I knew he would lose them. How much more so now?" The Buddha said: "One should not be greedy. Devadatta lost his supernatural powers solely because of greed."

The Buddha said: "There are two kinds of arhats. One is called 'foam-arhat,' who is concerned only for himself, attains the Way, and immediately wishes to pass beyond the world. The second kind brings all his companions to liberation as well."

The Buddha and the arhat both practice the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment. It is like a lamp: the Buddha calculates and knows how many things compose it, understands its origin and end, and guards it as it is extinguished. The arhat does not calculate the origin and end but simply extinguishes it. The Buddha is like following a tree from root to tip, discerning and knowing each part. The arhat's practice is like going from the tip to the root. Going from root to tip means understanding the fundamental emptiness. One who does not understand emptiness does not attain the Way.

The Buddha said: "The arhat has the residual aggregates but not the five aggregates of clinging. All that exists is manifest, but he is simply not attached to it. He has form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness, but no birth and death."

The Buddha said: "The arhat practices noble silence. Having attained arhatship, there are three marks. First, one no longer transgresses in body, speech, or mind. Second, the three poisons are extinguished. Third, regarding clothing and food, one does not seek what is fine but merely extinguishes hunger and cold. With these three conditions, one can transform freely. If the mind becomes attached to the bases of decline, one loses this freedom." The Buddha said: "The mind should only discern the six bases of decline, not become attached to them. Attachment prevents attaining the Way."

The Buddha said: "The arhat flies but for two reasons makes himself invisible to people. First, for fear that people who see him will make excessive offerings. Second, for fear that evil people will be jealous of him." The Buddha said: "Displaying supernatural powers has two benefits. First, people who see the flying become diligent and mindful of the Way. Second, those from whom one receives offerings obtain great merit. This is why supernatural powers are displayed. Because the mind is joyful, one obtains great merit."

The Buddha said: "There was an arhat named Gavāṃpati who, having attained the arhat's path, still behaved like a cowherd." A disciple asked the Buddha: "Why is this?" The Buddha said: "This monk, in past lives, was a cow for seven hundred lifetimes. Though in this life he attained the Way, his residual habits are not yet exhausted."

The Buddha said to the monks: "You should contemplate the body as impermanent." One monk immediately responded to the Buddha: "I contemplate impermanence. A person in the world lives at most fifty years." The Buddha said: "Do not say such a thing." Another monk said: "Perhaps thirty years." The Buddha again said: "Do not say such a thing." Another monk said: "Perhaps ten years." The Buddha said: "Do not say such a thing." Another monk said: "Perhaps one year." The Buddha again said: "Do not say such a thing." Another monk said: "Perhaps one month." The Buddha again said: "Do not say such a thing." Another monk said: "Perhaps one day." The Buddha again said: "Do not say such a thing." Another monk said: "Perhaps a single moment." The Buddha again said: "Do not say such a thing." Another monk said: "Perhaps the space of a single breath." The Buddha said: "That is it." The Buddha said: "If the out-breath does not return, one already belongs to the next life. A person's life hangs on the space of a single breath."

The Buddha said to the monks: "When you gather together, you should all discuss the Dharma. If you cannot, then simply close your eyes, quiet your voices, guard your minds, and listen well. Through this, you can attain the Way." When the monks heard the Buddha say this, they were filled with joy, their minds opened in understanding, and they immediately attained the arhat's path.

The Buddha said: "When a monk enters a person's home, he should be like a hand moving through empty space, with nothing to obstruct it. The mind also should have no attachment. If the ear hears something pleasing, that is attachment. If it hears something displeasing, that is also attachment." A monk addressed the Buddha: "If both the pleasing and the displeasing are attachment, how can one attain the Way?" The Buddha said: "When the monk's mind understands emptiness and has no attachment whatsoever, this accords with the bodhisattva's practice." When the monks heard this, they were all filled with joy and delight, and immediately attained the patience of the unborn dharmas.

The Buddha said: "There was a monk sitting in the mountains who sighed. Someone asked the monk: 'You practice the Way; why do you sigh?' The monk answered: 'When I was in the hells, the five torments were extremely painful. Now I have been born as a human and have even encountered the Buddha's sūtras and precepts, yet I still have not attained the Way. I fear that when I die, I will enter the evil paths again. This is why I sigh!' As a monk, one worries about lay people, worries about the sūtras and precepts, and also worries about oneself. Wholesome thoughts cannot be maintained constantly. When certain conditions arise, one immediately thinks of wife and children and wealth. This leads to falling into the evil paths."

The Buddha said: "A monk went to the home of a laywoman for alms-food and taught the sūtras for her. The laywoman knelt for a long time with tears flowing. The monk taught the sūtras until midnight, and the laywoman knelt without rising. The monk said: 'In all my travels teaching the sūtras, I have never seen anyone so diligent.' The laywoman answered: 'I had a donkey at home that brayed until midnight and then died. That is why my tears flow.'" The Buddha said: "When teaching the sūtras, it is not about speaking at length; what matters is whether understanding and wisdom are attained." A monk, having heard the Buddha tell this story, went and asked the Buddha: "Please teach me one essential dharma so that I may attain the Way." The Buddha said: "What is not yours, do not take. What is yours, then take." Thereupon the monk reflected: "Nothing in all the world is mine; only goodness is my own possession." The monk then addressed the Buddha: "I have understood." The Buddha said: "To understand this is itself the Way." At that time, another monk heard the Buddha say this and went to ask: "Has Śāriputra attained this?" The Buddha said: "Śāriputra has fully attained it." The monk said: "Then why did Śāriputra scold me and brush my face with his robe?" The Buddha said: "Call Śāriputra and ask him." Śāriputra said: "I had no ill intent. The reason I scolded him was to train him in patience. The reason I brushed his face with my robe was to purify his eyes, ears, nose, and mouth." The Buddha said to the monk: "This is Śāriputra's skillful means at work." Thereupon the monk was filled with joy and immediately attained the arhat's path.

The Buddha said: "Once, a person went to challenge a monk, saying: 'When a person dies, consciousness, thought, perception, and knowledge all cease. When a practitioner attains concentration, knowledge also ceases. What is the difference?' The monk replied: 'Both cease alike. When a person dies, their lifespan is exhausted, but the spirit does not perish; it follows one's deeds and is reborn. But it is so subtle that it cannot readily be perceived. What one obtains differs: doing good, one ascends to heaven; doing evil, one sinks into the abyss. This is the difference.'"

The Buddha said: "A monk went to a laywoman's home for alms-food. Seeing that she was very beautiful, the monk gave rise to an improper thought and was about to leave. The laywoman immediately prepared food and offered it to him. The monk then put down his bowl, saying: 'I should not accept others' offerings.' The laywoman replied: 'You have already had a mind of repentance; you may accept the offering.' The laywoman said this again, and the monk accepted." The Buddha said: "It is like a person who, having done evil, immediately becomes aware of it: the offense is dissolved. The monk's acceptance of the offering was without fault." The Buddha told his disciples: "If one can contemplate emptiness and control the mind, even for the time of a finger-snap, one can immediately attain the Way."

The Buddha said: "There was a monk who, while sitting in concentration, was disturbed. A layman saw that his sitting-place was uneven and pulled him up to show him a more comfortable spot. That layman later went five hundred kalpas without being able to find peace. Why? Because he interrupted the monk's contemplation of the Way." The Buddha told his disciples: "When someone is teaching the sūtras, you must never interrupt. The offense of interrupting sūtra-teaching is immeasurably heavy."

The Buddha said: "A monk practicing the Way attained the first dhyāna and said to himself: 'I have attained the first fruit of the path. The merit of the first dhyāna enables rebirth in the seventh heaven, with a lifespan of one kalpa.' Having attained the second dhyāna, he reckoned he had attained the second fruit. 'The merit of the third dhyāna enables rebirth in the fifteenth heaven, with a lifespan of eight kalpas.' Having attained the fourth dhyāna, he reckoned he had attained arhatship. 'Why then am I not reborn in the nineteenth heaven with a lifespan of sixteen kalpas?' Having said this, he declared: 'The Buddha is a liar!' Immediately he fell from heaven into the hells. Without even noticing that he had lost his heavenly lifespan, he was already suffering in hell. It is like a person in the world who obtains precious jewels, and in a single day a fire breaks out and burns them all. When a person gives rise to an evil thought, it is like a fire that burns away wholesome thoughts." The Buddha said: "Good and evil follow a person; there is no separation. Only by attaining the Way can one leave worldly attachment behind."

The Buddha said: "Not eating after noon brings five blessings. First, less lust. Second, less sleep. Third, one-pointed mind. Fourth, no digestive disturbances. Fifth, the body is at ease and does not become ill. Therefore śramaṇas and practitioners of the Way, knowing these blessings, do not eat. As for washing and rinsing, there are three reasons. First, for fear of dirt under the nails. Second, so that scratching an itch is pleasant. Third, to kill fleas and mosquitoes. One also wishes to purify the mind and eliminate desires, and not to soil the sūtras. But this only removes external impurities. If the mind harbors evil thoughts, these cannot be removed. For students, the foremost thing is to purify the mind as the foundation. The mind is the root of the Dharma. When the mind is right, conduct is upright. When conduct is upright, it accords with the Way."

The Buddha said: "Long ago, a novice was walking with his teacher and saw gold on the ground. He silently picked it up and said to his teacher: 'Let us hurry through here; there is no one around and it is frightening.' The teacher said: 'It is only because of the gold that you are afraid.' He threw away the gold and was no longer afraid. The disciple then prostrated before his teacher, saying: 'I was foolish and ignorant. Now that I have discarded it, I am no longer anxious.'" The Buddha told his disciples: "If students craved the Way as that novice craved the gold, why would they worry about not attaining the Way?"

The Buddha said: "There was a monk studying the Way who received sūtras from his teacher but was not diligent. The teacher told him to gather several liters of cow and horse dung and burn it. By evening it had not all burned away. The teacher then took a large stone from the hells, and the fire burned it to ash instantly. The disciple was greatly frightened, knelt, and asked his teacher: 'What kind of fire is that?' The teacher said: 'If you do not study the sūtras and practice the Way, after death you will enter the hells, where the fire that burns you will not take even a moment.' Thereupon the monk was terrified and became diligent. Each day he studied one āgama, and he attained the arhat's path." The Buddha said: "This is how skillful means can save people."

The Buddha said: "The non-returner (anāgāmin) has three fetters. First, the fetter of delusion. Second, the fetter of worldly love. Third, the fetter of not yet having fully realized the truths. The non-returner is like a flame above a fire: smoke rises but cannot burn anything. The stream-enterer (sotāpanna) eliminates eighty-eight fetters. The once-returner (sakṛdāgāmin) eliminates seven fetters. The non-returner eliminates three fetters. The arhat has no fetters. The stream-enterer has attained the first fruit of the path. The once-returner attains the Way through one more coming and going. The non-returner does not return to the world. The arhat is one who is no longer attached. What does 'attaining the first fruit of the path' mean? It means recognizing suffering. Worldly people all fail to recognize suffering; the stream-enterer recognizes suffering. The once-returner abandons the origin of suffering. The non-returner knows cessation. The arhat has completed the path. After attaining stream-entry, it takes a hundred kalpas to attain arhatship. Why? Because for a hundred kalpas, the stream-enterer still has ten remaining fetters that are not severed; without severing them, one cannot become an arhat. The stream-enterer is only concerned with himself, fearing offenses, not daring to involve himself in worldly affairs. With ten remaining doubts and fetters unresolved, he remains stationary. The stream-enterer always abandons the five lower fetters: first, the fetter of sensual desire; second, the fetter of anger; third, the fetter of views about practices; fourth, the fetter of attachment to merit from precepts; fifth, the fetter of doubt. These also refer to the desires and anger from the sixth heaven downward throughout the world, attachment to the body, wishes, and doubts, which are the lower fetters. What the stream-enterer sees through causes and conditions means not violating the five precepts, even at the point of death. Other people, when pressed, all violate the five conditions. The stream-enterer severs the old and receives the new. The arhat severs both old and new completely. The bodhisattva exhausts former offenses; when offenses are exhausted, he attains the Way. Only then does he know impermanence, suffering, emptiness, non-self, and the path. A practitioner of the Way should have no attachments. Practicing stream-entry, one attains stream-entry. Practicing once-returning, one attains once-returning. Practicing non-returning, one attains non-returning. Practicing arhatship, one attains arhatship. Practicing as a pratyekabuddha, one becomes a pratyekabuddha. Practicing as a bodhisattva, one becomes a bodhisattva." The Buddha said: "Students receive their reward according to their intentions and actions, like a shadow and an echo that follow without fail."

The Buddha said: "The bowl has four names. First, 'not for luxurious food.' Second, 'precepts.' Third, 'receiving.' Fourth, 'following.' Obtaining merit without any blemish, one is fit to receive blessings. Holding the bowl establishes the precepts, and within the mind there are no evil thoughts."

The Buddha said: "The arhat does not eat meat because he considers that each part of an animal, from head to foot, has its own name, but none is called 'meat.' The pratyekabuddha considers that all originated from impure essence, and therefore does not eat meat. The Buddha considers that everything under heaven is empty and without anything; what exists will be extinguished, and what is extinguished will arise again, all ultimately returning to emptiness, which is 'nothing whatsoever.'" The disciples, having heard the sūtra, were filled with joy, prostrated before the Buddha, and departed.
