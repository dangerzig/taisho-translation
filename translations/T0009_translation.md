## The Sūtra of the Human Sage

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0009

Translated from the Chinese of Faxian (Song dynasty)

## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra of the Human Sage, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the Kuñjakā monastery in the city of Nādikā, together with a great assembly. At that time, the Venerable Ānanda was staying alone in a certain place and gave rise to the following thought: "My Buddha, the World-Honored One, has previously spoken of all the various regions and countries and all their cities and towns, namely: the country of Aṅga, the country of Magadha, the country of Kāśī, the country of Kosala, the country of Mithilā, the country of Mallā, the country of Puṇḍra, the country of Soma, the country of Aśmaka, the country of Vatī, the country of Kuru, the country of Pañcāla, the country of Vatsa, the country of Śūrasena, the country of Yavana, and the country of Kamboja. As for the śrāvakas of those countries who have already passed into extinction, the Buddha has in each case declared the retribution into which they were reborn. Yet regarding the most eminent upāsakas of the country of Magadha, all of whom have already departed this life, leaving the country of Magadha empty and desolate, my Buddha, the World-Honored One, has not yet declared where they have been reborn."

Having thus reflected, the Venerable Ānanda left his dwelling, went to where the Buddha was, and upon arriving, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, bowed his head to the feet of the World-Honored One, stood before the World-Honored One, and addressed him thus: "As for what was previously taught concerning the various regions and countries, I have heard it from the Buddha and know it thoroughly. As for the Dharma I have heard proclaimed by the Buddha, I know it fully as well. The places of rebirth of the upāsakas of the city of Nādikā—of this the Buddha has likewise already spoken. Furthermore, there were five hundred upāsakas in the city of Nādikā who have likewise passed away. Those upāsakas thoroughly severed the three fetters, realized the fruit of śrotāpatti, flow against the current of birth and death, will return to the human realm seven times and be reborn in the heavenly realm seven times, will bring suffering to its very limit, and will assuredly realize bodhi. Again, there were three hundred upāsakas in the city of Nādikā who subsequently passed away. Those upāsakas also severed the three fetters together with greed, hatred, and delusion, will return to the human realm once, will bring suffering to its very limit, and will realize the fruit of sakṛdāgāmī. Again, there were two hundred and fifty upāsakas in the city of Nādikā who thereafter passed away. Those upāsakas were able to sever the five kinds of afflictions and secondary afflictions, realized the fruit of anāgāmī, will not return to the human realm, and will no longer undergo transmigration. All such matters I know thoroughly. Yet regarding the most eminent upāsakas of the country of Magadha alone—after they departed this life, leaving the country of Magadha empty and desolate—why, World-Honored One, have you not spoken of where those upāsakas were reborn? I pray that the World-Honored One will declare to me: where have the upāsakas of the country of Magadha been reborn? Through what practices and aspirations have they obtained what fruit and retribution? World-Honored One, furthermore, King Bimbisāra of the country of Magadha was single-mindedly devoted to the Buddha, had knowledge of the true Dharma, and upheld the Saṃgha throughout his entire life, constantly mindful and never forgetting. After his death, the people of that country unanimously praised the king's virtue, saying: 'This is a Dharma-king. May this Dharma-king be reborn in a good world and attain the highest happiness.' World-Honored One, why have you not yet spoken of where that king was reborn, of his heart's aspiration, and of his attainment of a fruit? I pray that the World-Honored One will declare each of these matters one by one." He further added: "World-Honored One, the country of Magadha is none other than the ground where my Buddha attained perfect enlightenment—it is supremely excellent and without equal. And it was that king who was lord of this supreme land. I pray that the World-Honored One will declare his place of rebirth."

At that time, having received Ānanda's request, the World-Honored One remained in silence. The Venerable Ānanda, seeing that the Buddha was silent, understood that the request had been accepted, bowed his head to the feet of the World-Honored One, and returned to his own dwelling.

At that time, after the night had passed and the following morning had come—at the hour of eating—the World-Honored One put on his robe, took up his alms bowl, entered the city of Nādikā, and went about begging for food in due order. Having obtained a suitable offering of food, he returned to his dwelling, put away his robe, washed his feet, arranged his seat, and ate. When the meal was finished, he walked in meditation for a time, then returned to his seat and reflected upon Ānanda's question: "The king of Magadha and the upāsakas—having passed away in this place, where will they be reborn? Through what practices and aspirations, what fruit and retribution will they obtain?" While he was thus reflecting, through the Buddha's spiritual power, a voice arose in the empty sky, calling out a name: "World-Honored One, I am the Human Sage! O Well-Gone One, I am the Human Sage!"

At that time, having heard the voice in the sky, the World-Honored One rose from his seat and went to the place where the śrāvaka assembly was gathered, seated around him in a circle. The Venerable Ānanda came to where the Buddha was, bared his right shoulder, bowed to the feet of the World-Honored One, stood before the World-Honored One, and addressed him: "World-Honored One, for what reason and on what account does your countenance appear unusually joyful today?"

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Ānanda: "In accordance with your request—asking where King Bimbisāra of Magadha and the upāsakas, after passing away in this place, would be reborn, and through what practices and aspirations they would obtain what fruit and retribution—the time for me to speak of this matter had not yet come, and so, outwardly appearing as is common in the world, I kept it in mind and remained thus. After that night had passed, at the hour of eating, I entered the city to beg for food, returned to this place, finished eating, walked in meditation, and then returned to my seat. As the proper time for addressing your question had arrived, I called it to mind and reflected upon it—contemplating the king of Magadha and the upāsakas, their passing away here and rebirth elsewhere, their practices, aspirations, and retributions. Thereupon, through my spiritual power, a voice arose in the empty sky saying: 'World-Honored One, I am the Human Sage! O Well-Gone One, I am the Human Sage!'"

The Buddha further said: "Ānanda, have you ever before heard such a name as this?"

Ānanda replied: "World-Honored One, I have never before heard such a name. When I heard this name, the hair on my body stood on end with joy." While the Venerable Ānanda was speaking these words, a voice was again heard from the sky: "World-Honored One, I am King Bimbisāra! O Well-Gone One, I am King Bimbisāra! I now speak before the Buddha, calling out my name and lineage two and three times. World-Honored One, long ago the Human Sage, after his death, was reborn among humans, became a king of men, and realized the fruit of śrotāpatti. Now, in his seventh birth, he has been reborn in the palace of Vaiśravaṇa as a son of the Heavenly King, and is also called the Human Sage. World-Honored One, I, as the son of Vaiśravaṇa, the Heavenly King, am well able to understand the words of the Dharma spoken by the Buddha—subtle, tranquil, and blissful in their import—and will in the future attain the fruit of sakṛdāgāmī."

The Buddha thereupon praised him: "Excellent! Excellent! You, O Human Sage, have done very well indeed. You have thus practiced non-heedlessness. Previously in what place, and by what cause and condition, were you able to attain the fruit of śrotāpatti?"

The Human Sage replied: "I had no other cause and no other condition. I only knew the Buddha-Dharma to be subtle and supremely excellent, and with deep faith and devoted practice I attained the first fruit." He further said: "World-Honored One, I had received a command from Dhṛtarāṣṭra, the Heavenly King, and was journeying to the realm of Virūḍhaka, the Heavenly King of the South. Thereby I came to know that my Buddha, the World-Honored One, was in the Kuñjakā monastery, staying alone in a hall, examining where the king of Magadha and the upāsakas had gone after passing away in that place—through what practices and aspirations they had obtained what fruit and retribution—matters which my Buddha wished to speak of. World-Honored One, I heard this directly from my father, King Vaiśravaṇa, held it in memory without forgetting, and therefore now come before the Buddha, precisely on account of this, wishing to speak of these matters."

The Buddha said: "Human Sage, now is indeed the proper time. You should speak at length."

The Human Sage, receiving the Buddha's holy command, then addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, once I heard my father, Vaiśravaṇa, the Heavenly King, announce to the assembly: 'You sages, listen with single-minded attention. Long ago, I was at a supreme assembly of the Dharma in the Trayastriṃśa Heaven. All the gods were assembled there, and the guardian kings were also present at that assembly, each in his own direction. Dhṛtarāṣṭra, the Heavenly King of the East, sat facing west. Virūḍhaka, the Heavenly King of the South, sat facing north. Virūpākṣa, the Heavenly King of the West, sat facing east. I was in the north, seated facing south. The audience who had come to hear the Dharma sat before the guardian kings. At that time, the gods and guardian kings had all assembled to hear the Dharma. Having heard the Dharma, they wished to return to their own palaces, when suddenly a great light appeared, illuminating the entire assembly, and the radiance of all the gods was obscured and became invisible.

"'At that time, Śakra, lord of the gods, announced to all the gods: "You all should know that this great light now illuminating the entire assembly has caused the radiance and form of all you gods to be obscured and invisible. Therefore you should all know that it will not be long before Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, arrives at this assembly. What do you think? Wherever Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, goes, auspicious signs appear beforehand. You gods should not rise from your seats; wait until you know what cause has given rise to this manifestation of light." The assembly of gods and the guardian kings replied to Śakra: "Accepting your command, we will not rise from our seats until we know the cause of this manifestation of light." Then Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, in the form of a youth, suddenly appeared at that supreme assembly. His head bore five topknots, his appearance was complete with every mark, and he thereupon spoke a verse in the midst of that assembly, declaring to all the gods:

> "Take refuge in the Buddha, the World-Honored One,
> Who serves as the eye of the world,
> Who well teaches the sublime Dharma,
> Enabling attainment of the tranquil state.
> You, the assembled hosts of gods,
> With your great power and magnificent forms,
> Have upheld the brahmacarya of the Buddha;
> By this cause you have been reborn in the heavenly realm.
> Moreover, those who practice the pure life,
> Endowed with form, long life, and renown,
> These great and wise children of the Buddha,
> Will before long be reborn in this realm.
> The heavenly assembly, hearing these words,
> Gave rise in their hearts to great rejoicing,
> Taking refuge in the Buddha, the World-Honored One,
> With faith in the most sublime of Dharmas.
> When the Brahma King spoke this verse,
> His voice was endowed with five wondrous tones,
> Profound as the rumbling of clouds and thunder—
> Those who heard it took delight in what is true."

"'When Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, spoke this verse, his voice was endowed with five wondrous tones, namely: the great brahmā voice, the kalaviṅka voice, the great drum voice, the great thunder voice, and the voice of delightful music. Furthermore, the Brahma King withdrew his youth's form in that supreme assembly and manifested a great body. What was the reason? Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, manifests his body in accordance with what pleases the hearts of the assembly. The great body he manifested possessed two kinds of excellence: great and splendid form, and universally known renown. Just as gold possesses two kinds of excellence—namely color and name—so too does Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, appear in these two kinds of excellence among the assembly of gods. Furthermore, when the Brahma King arrived at the supreme assembly, the gods in attendance did not rise from their seats, nor did they make obeisance. The assembly of gods remained with palms joined, each giving rise to this thought: "Ah! The lord of the Sahā world, Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, has already manifested a body before this assembly, and now manifests a yet greater body." At that time, the Brahma King, knowing the minds of the gods, within his great body manifested a body greater still, and in the midst of that supreme assembly, sat cross-legged in the empty sky above—just as a great strongman sits upon the ground, so too did Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King.

"'At that time, Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, again announced to the assembly of gods: "The great body I have manifested comes through the power of the four bases of spiritual power. Only the Buddha, the World-Honored One, knows and sees all of this fully, and is able to teach it, able to cultivate it, and able to manifest it. Therefore you all should also with sincere minds cultivate those bases of spiritual power, until you can manifest spiritual abilities and bring about great benefit. The four bases of spiritual power are: intention, effort, mind, and wisdom." At that time, the assembly of gods gave rise in their minds to this thought: "Ah! Great Brahma King, may you transform each one of us in the assembly of gods to be like the body of the Brahma King, with one heavenly king seated in the embrace of each Brahma King." At that time, Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, knowing the minds of the gods, immediately by his spiritual power gathered up the bodies of all the gods, transformed them into the bodies of Brahma Kings, and seated one heavenly king in each embrace. All the wishes that had arisen in the minds of those gods were entirely fulfilled, and they obtained great bliss—just as a kṣatriya king, receiving his father's royal consecration and succeeding to the royal throne, has his wishes fulfilled and obtains great bliss, so too was it with those gods.

"'At that time, Mahābrahmā again announced to the assembly of gods: "You gods and guardian kings, listen with single-minded attention. Among all the sages, it is only the Buddha, the Tathāgata, the Samyaksaṃbuddha, who is fully able to expound at length on the four bases of spiritual power, to long cultivate them, and to greatly manifest them. Therefore you all should give rise to sincere minds and diligently cultivate them, manifesting them freely and bringing about great benefit." At that time, the heavenly kings sitting in the embrace of the Brahma King each gave rise to a doubtful thought: "There is only one Great Brahma King, and I am sitting in his embrace—how is it that when he speaks, all the gods speak, and when he is silent, all the gods are likewise silent?" Furthermore, Śakra, lord of the gods, gave rise to this thought: "Ah! Great Brahma King, may you gather up the forms of all of us gods, transform them into one great body, and sit in my embrace." At that time, the Great Brahma King, knowing the thought of Śakra, gathered up the forms of the gods, manifested one great body, and sat cross-legged in Śakra's embrace. Having completed these various transformations through such power of the bases of spiritual power, Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, again announced to the gods and guardian kings: "My Buddha, the World-Honored One, with these four bases of spiritual power, together with the Dharma of the śrāvakas, previously transformed and guided to liberation the eighty thousand upāsakas of the country of Magadha, who thoroughly severed the three fetters, brought suffering to its very limit, and realized the fruit of śrotāpatti. Going and coming seven times between heavens and humans: some have been reborn in the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven, some in the Nirmāṇarati heaven, some in the Trayastriṃśa heaven, some in the Cāturmahārājakāyika heaven; and among humans, some have been reborn in the palaces of great kṣatriya kings, some in the households of the most eminent great brāhmaṇas, and some in the households of the most eminent great householders."

"'Furthermore, among the assembly of gods, some had this thought: "Ah! How might four Buddhas appear in the world?" Others had this thought: "Ah! How might eight Buddhas appear in the world?" Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, knowing what was in the minds of those gods, again announced: "You, the assembly of gods, do not give rise to such thoughts—wishing for four Buddhas to appear in the world, or even eight Buddhas to appear in the world at once. This is not right. You should know that I have heard from the Buddha that no two Buddhas ever appear in the world simultaneously. How much less could four or eight Buddhas appear in the world at the same time! You should only wish that my Buddha, the World-Honored One, whose body is free of all āsravas, may have his lifespan increased and dwell long in the world." At that time, those gods again gave rise to this thought: "How does Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, know the mind of each one of us?" Those gods were then struck with fear, and sorrow and distress arose in their hearts. Then the Great Brahma King announced to the assembly: "You, the assembly of gods and guardian kings, listen one-pointedly with care. The Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Samyaksaṃbuddha, proclaims the one vehicle of the true Dharma, enabling all sentient beings to be far removed from sorrow, grief, suffering, and affliction, to attain complete purification, and to realize the true principle." He further announced: "There are three kinds of Dharma of which the Tathāgata has complete knowledge. What are the three kinds? First, there is a person who previously performed unwholesome acts of body and unwholesome activities of mind, but who afterwards, through drawing near to a good spiritual friend, hearing the sublime Dharma, and applying attentive reflection, abandoned the unwholesome acts of body and performed wholesome acts of body, and abandoned the unwholesome activities of mind and practiced wholesome activities of mind. That person, from happiness, gives rise to further happiness; from contentment, gives rise to further contentment. Just as there is a person who, from joy, gives rise to further joy, and from joy gives rise to yet more joy—so too is it with that person's delight and happiness. This is called the first kind of Dharma. Furthermore, there is a person who previously engaged with the five sensual pleasures and performed unwholesome deeds, but who afterwards, drawing near to a good spiritual friend, hearing the sublime Dharma, and applying attentive reflection, relinquished sensual pleasures and likewise no longer performed any unwholesome deeds. That person, from happiness, gives rise to further happiness; from contentment, gives rise to further contentment. Just as there is a person who, from joy, gives rise to further joy, and from joy gives rise to yet more joy—so too is it with one who delights in the Dharma. This is called the second kind of Dharma. Furthermore, there is a person who truly knows the unwholesome dharmas as they are, and also truly knows the wholesome dharmas as they are—knowing even suffering, arising, cessation, and path as they truly are. Afterwards, drawing near to a good spiritual friend, regarding unwholesome dharmas and wholesome dharmas, regarding suffering, arising, cessation, and path, and regarding all these dharmas, such a person attains yet further and more thorough understanding. That person, from happiness, gives rise to further happiness; from contentment, gives rise to further contentment. Just as there is a person who, from joy, gives rise to further joy, and from joy gives rise to yet more joy—so too is it with one who delights in the Dharma. This is called the third kind of Dharma."

"'Furthermore, Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, again announced to the gods and guardian kings: "You sages, listen with single-minded attention. There are four kinds of Dharma of which the Buddha, the World-Honored One, has complete knowledge and direct perception. What are the four? They are: body, feeling, mind, and phenomena. The Tathāgata, with wisdom, observes these four dharmas—whether inner or outer—knowing them as they truly are. Wisdom comes into activity; cultivation reaches its fulfillment. He well teaches the one vehicle of awakening, the true Dharma, enabling all sentient beings to attain complete purification, to be far removed from sorrow, grief, suffering, and affliction, and to realize the principle of the sublime Dharma."

"'Furthermore, Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, again announced to the gods and guardian kings: "You sages, listen with single-minded attention. There is the Dharma of the Noble Eightfold Path, of which the Buddha, the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Samyaksaṃbuddha, has complete knowledge and direct perception. What are the eight? They are: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration. This Noble Eightfold Path is precisely the Dharma to be received and cultivated through samādhi. If one thus attains right thought, practices the holy life, and completes cultivation, one attains the bliss of the Brahma heaven. Moreover, right speech—speaking rightly in all words, fulfilling every characteristic, rightly teaching the holy life, making distinctions to reveal the teaching, and attaining the true and authentic meaning—proclaiming right speech opens the door of amṛta, demonstrates the one vehicle Dharma, and enables all sentient beings to attain complete purification, to be far removed from sorrow, grief, and suffering, and to realize the principle of the sublime Dharma."'"

At that time, the Human Sage addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the various Dharma-teachings I have spoken of in this way were all proclaimed by Mahābrahmā, the Great Brahma King, in the palace of Śakra, for the gods of the Trayastriṃśa Heaven and the four assemblies of guardian deities. My father, Vaiśravaṇa, the Heavenly King, after returning to his own palace, proclaimed them to me, and I held them all in memory without forgetting. Now, through the great majestic power of the Tathāgata, and because the Venerable Ānanda wished to know the place of rebirth and passing, the practices, aspirations, and retributions of King Bimbisāra, I have now before the Buddha truly and in full declared these matters."

The Buddha thereupon praised him: "Excellent! Excellent! You have spoken well."

At that time, after the Human Sage had expounded this Dharma, the Venerable Ānanda and all those in the assembly, having heard this Dharma, received it with joy and faith, bowed to the Buddha, and withdrew.
