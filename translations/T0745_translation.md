# 佛說雜藏經
## The Miscellaneous Treasury Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0745

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Fǎxiǎn (法顯).

---

Among the Buddha's disciples, the arhats each excelled in a particular practice. Śāriputra was foremost in wisdom, delighting in explaining the subtle and marvelous Dharma. Maudgalyāyana was foremost in supernatural powers, constantly traveling by means of his supernatural abilities through the six destinies, observing sentient beings receiving the fruits of their good and evil actions, and returning to tell people about them.

Once Maudgalyāyana went to the bank of the Ganges River and saw five hundred hungry ghosts coming in a group toward the water. There was a water-guarding ghost who wielded an iron staff and drove them away, not letting them approach. The ghosts then went directly to Maudgalyāyana, bowed at his feet, and each asked about the conditions that caused their offenses.

One ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "Venerable one! Having received this body, I constantly suffer from burning thirst. I had heard that the water of the Ganges was cool, refreshing, and sweet. I joyfully went to it, entered to bathe, but the water became scalding hot, and my entire body was blistered and ruined. If I swallowed a single mouthful, my five organs were scorched and rotted, and the stench was unbearable. What conditions caused me to receive such punishment?"

Maudgalyāyana replied: "In your past life you were a physiognomist who read people's fortunes of good and ill. Your readings were rarely true and mostly false, sometimes slandering and sometimes praising. You claimed to be precise in order to stir people's hearts. Through deceit and fraud, you sought profit and sustenance, leading sentient beings astray and causing them to lose what they desired. Therefore, although you have heard that this water is cool, refreshing, and sweet, when you arrive it is not as desired. This is the flower-retribution of your evil actions; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "I am constantly pursued by a great dog with sharp fangs and red eyes that devours my flesh, leaving only bones. The wind then blows upon them, flesh grows back, and the dog comes again to devour me. I constantly suffer this torment. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were the keeper of a temple to a heavenly deity. You constantly instructed people to slaughter sheep and offer their blood in sacrifice, while you yourself ate the meat. Therefore today you repay with your own flesh. This is the flower-retribution of your evil actions; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come, multiplied billions of times."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "Venerable one! My body is constantly smeared all over with excrement, and moreover I must eat it. What conditions caused me to receive such punishment?"

Maudgalyāyana said: "In your past life you were a brahmin, evil and unbelieving in the workings of offense and merit. A mendicant practitioner came begging for food, and you did not wish him to come again. So you took his bowl, filled it with excrement, and placed food on top, then gave it to the practitioner. When the practitioner received it and took it back to his place, he ate the food with his hands, and the excrement defiled his hands. Therefore today you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution of evil actions; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "Venerable one! My belly is enormously swollen like a great jar, while my throat, hands, and feet are as thin as needles, and I cannot eat or drink. What conditions caused me to suffer this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were a village chief. Relying on your power and wealth, you drank wine and acted lawlessly. You scorned and bullied others, seizing their food and drink, leaving people hungry and destitute. Due to these conditions, you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "I constantly go toward the latrine wishing to eat the excrement. A great horde of ghosts with staffs drive me away, not letting me near the privy. My mouth is foul and rotten, and I am hungry and wretched beyond endurance. What conditions caused me to receive such punishment?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were the keeper of a Buddhist monastery. When lay devotees came to make offerings and provide food for the saṅgha, if a visiting monk arrived, you served coarse and meager provisions. After the visiting monk left, you ate the fine food yourself. Due to these conditions, you cannot even obtain excrement, much less good food. This is merely the flower-retribution; the fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "My body is entirely covered with tongues growing out of it, and axes come to chop the tongues. They are severed but grow back again, and so it continues without end. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were a practitioner. The saṅgha assigned you to prepare honey drinks. The blocks of rock sugar were large and hard to dissolve, so you chopped them with an axe. With a thieving mind, you ate one mouthful. Due to these conditions, the axe now chops your tongues."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "Seven burning iron balls constantly enter my mouth, going down into my belly and scorching my five organs. They emerge and then enter again. What conditions caused me to receive such punishment?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were a novice monk. When distributing fruit to your teacher's quarters, out of partiality and favoritism for your teacher, you gave him more, specifically an extra seven pieces. Therefore you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution; the fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "Two burning iron wheels constantly turn beneath my two armpits, and my body is scorched and ruined. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life, when you were making flatbreads for the saṅgha, with a thieving mind you took two flatbreads and hid them under your armpits. Therefore you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution; the fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another hungry ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "My testicles are enormously swollen like great jars. When walking I carry them on my shoulders, and when sitting I sit upon them. Whether moving or resting, I suffer terribly. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were a market inspector. You always used light scales and small measures when giving to others, and heavy scales and large measures when receiving. You constantly wished to profit yourself greatly at the expense of others. Therefore you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "I constantly have eyes on both shoulders and a mouth and nose on my chest, but I never have a head. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were the apprentice of an executioner. Whenever criminals were put to death, you always felt joy in your heart and pulled them by the topknot with a rope. Due to these conditions, you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution of evil actions; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "Burning iron needles constantly pierce in and out of my body, and the suffering is beyond endurance. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were a horse-trainer, or an elephant-trainer. When the elephants and horses were difficult to control, you pierced their feet with iron needles. At other times, when oxen were slow, you also pricked them with needles. Therefore you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution of evil actions; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "Fire constantly issues from my body, scorching and tormenting me. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were a queen. There was another queen whom the king greatly loved and favored, and you were constantly jealous, watching for a chance to harm her. Once the king had been lying down and then left. The favored queen was still sleeping and had not yet risen to dress. You immediately conceived an evil thought. It happened that flatbreads were being made, and there was hot sesame oil. You poured it on her belly, which burst open, and she died instantly. Due to these conditions, you receive such punishment."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "A whirlwind constantly spins my body around, and I cannot go freely wherever I wish. My mind is constantly vexed and distressed. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were a diviner. Sometimes you spoke truthfully and sometimes falsely, confusing people's minds so that they could not act as they wished. Therefore you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "My body is always like a lump of flesh, without hands, feet, eyes, ears, nose, or other features, and it is constantly eaten by insects and birds. The suffering is unbearable. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you constantly gave others medicine to abort their unborn children. Therefore you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution; the bitter fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "A burning iron cage constantly encloses my body, scorching and tormenting me. What conditions caused me to receive such punishment?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you constantly used nets to trap fish and birds. Therefore you receive such punishment. This is the flower-retribution of evil actions; the bitter fruit is yet to come."

Another ghost said to Maudgalyāyana: "I constantly cover my head with something and am always afraid that someone will come to kill me. My heart is perpetually terrified, and the suffering is beyond endurance. What conditions caused this?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you committed sexual misconduct with others. You were always afraid of being seen, or feared that the husband would catch, bind, beat, and kill you, or feared that the law would execute you in the public square. You constantly lived in fear, one terror following another. Therefore you receive this punishment. This is the flower-retribution of evil actions; the fruit of hell is yet to come."

Another ghost asked: "Having received this body, I constantly have a copper vessel on my shoulders, full of molten copper. With my hand I hold a ladle, scoop it up, and pour it over my own head. My entire body is scorched and ruined. The suffering I endure is without number or measure. What conditions caused this punishment?"

Maudgalyāyana answered: "In your past life you were a renunciant in charge of the saṅgha's food and drink. You took a jar of ghee and privately hid it elsewhere. When visiting practitioners came, you did not share it with them. After they left, you brought out the ghee and distributed it only to the resident monks. This ghee belonged to the saṅgha of all directions, in which everyone has a share. To hide it in this way, even though you distributed some, was not equitable. Due to these conditions, you receive this punishment."

Maudgalyāyana also saw a celestial maiden seated upon a lotus flower, a hundred leagues in both length and width. This flower was uniquely magnificent, surpassing all others. All the necessities of life, halls, palaces, and food and drink that she desired arose from the flower in accordance with her thoughts, and followed her wherever she went. Maudgalyāyana asked: "What good deeds did you perform to receive such a reward?"

The celestial maiden answered: "After the Buddha Kāśyapa had passed into nirvāṇa, his complete relics remained. His disciples built a stūpa of the seven treasures, forty leagues in both height and width. At that time I was a woman. Going out and seeing the beautiful image of the Buddha in the jeweled stūpa, faith and reverence welled up in my heart. I thought of the Buddha's merits, removed the flower from my hair, and reverently offered it to the image. Due to these conditions, I received this uniquely magnificent reward."

During the intense heat of summer, Śāriputra was traveling and came to an āmra garden. A hired laborer was drawing well water to irrigate the trees. This man had no great faith in the Buddha, but upon seeing Śāriputra he felt a small measure of faith. He called out to Śāriputra: "Venerable one! Come, remove your robes and sit beneath the tree. I will pour water over you. I need not stop my irrigating, and at the same time I can benefit you." Thereupon Śāriputra removed his robes and was washed. His body felt cool and refreshed, and he went on his way.

That very night, the hired laborer died and was reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven with great supernatural power, second only to Śakra, Lord of the Gods. He thought to himself: "How did I come to be born here?" Looking back at his past life, he saw that his faith had been slight, and that through his work of irrigating, he had incidentally washed Śāriputra with water. "If my faith had been pure and strong, and I had known there would surely be a reward, I would have deliberately prepared bathing supplies as an offering. Though my merit was small, because I encountered an excellent field of merit, I received an abundant reward!" He then went to Śāriputra's abode and scattered flowers as an offering. Śāriputra, in response to his pure faith, taught him the Dharma, and he attained the fruit of stream-entry.

Maudgalyāyana also saw a spirit of tremendous bodily size with golden hands, from whose five fingers flowed sweet dew. Whenever travelers needed food, drink, or other necessities of life, everything issued from his fingers, and he gave freely. Maudgalyāyana asked: "What kind of heavenly being are you? Your reward and merit are remarkably extraordinary!"

The spirit king answered: "I am not a king of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, nor of any heaven up to the sixth, nor a king of the Brahmā heaven. I am a great yakṣa spirit who dwells in the great city of a certain country, and have come here on a tour of inspection."

Maudgalyāyana asked: "What good deeds did you perform to receive such a reward?"

He answered: "In that country there was a great city called Rālū. In a past life I was a poor woman there, weaving wool yarn into bags and selling them to survive. My household grew ever poorer until my dwelling was completely ruined. I ended up at the roadside, near the house of a wealthy and generous elder, and continued weaving bags to survive. Each day around midday, when renunciants or brahmins came carrying their bowls to beg for food and asked me, 'Where is the house of such-and-such an elder?' I answered with a truthful heart, without any falsehood, joyfully raising my hand and pointing the way, saying: 'Go there, go there! The hour for eating is almost past; do not seek elsewhere.' Due to these conditions, I received such a reward."

If a poor woman, with a mind of sympathetic joy, assists the practice of giving and receives such a reward, how much more so for those who actually practice giving themselves?

When the Buddha was in the world, there were five great kings. In the time of the Buddha Kāśyapa, they had been spiritual friends and had left home to practice the Way. When the Buddha Śākyamuni appeared in the world, they all attained the first fruit of the Way. Here is told the story of how one of them attained the Way. His country was called Pāṇḍi, and the king was named Udayana. His country was prosperous and his people flourishing. The king had twenty thousand consorts. The foremost queen, named Moon-Light, was beautiful in form and bearing, and the king loved and honored her greatly. Once the king held a great assembly with various musical entertainments and commanded Moon-Light to dance. Queen Moon-Light was dressed in the finest garments, adorned with gold, silver, and precious jeweled necklaces, and she danced with extraordinary grace, delighting the hearts of all.

The king was skilled in physiognomy. He saw that his queen bore the signs of approaching death and would not survive more than six months, and that she would suddenly perish. Thinking of the pain of separation from one he loved, he grew sorrowful and could not bear to watch. Moon-Light was puzzled and asked him. Because death was a weighty matter, the king feared she would become distressed and concealed it. She pressed him earnestly again, and the king answered: "Your lifespan is short, and your end will come before long. The feeling of parting from one I love, this is why I grieve!" Moon-Light said: "Where there is birth, there is death. This is the way of the world. Why do you grieve alone? If you truly care for me, just tell me, and allow me to leave home to become a renunciant." The king approved her words and permitted her to enter the path. Wishing to verify the truth of karmic retribution and strengthen his faith, he made a covenant with her, saying: "If, after leaving home, you uphold the precepts and contemplate with concentration, and even if you do not attain the Way you are surely reborn in heaven, then after being reborn in heaven, return to my presence. I permit you to leave home." Moon-Light agreed to this covenant. Thereupon the king summoned the bhikṣuṇīs, and they immediately ordained her and took her away. Because she was of noble rank and had been able to renounce the five desires, many people came to pay their respects and make offerings, interfering with her practice. Therefore she traveled to other countries.

From the day she left home, after a full six months, having upheld the precepts purely and striven in contemplation of the Way, weary of the world, she attained the fruit of the non-returner. In a certain village her life came to an end, and she was immediately reborn in the form realm heavens. Observing her past conditions, she recalled her covenant with the king and went to fulfill it. She observed that the king was sunk in the five desires, obstinate and difficult to transform. If she simply went to him as she was, there would be nothing to stir his heart. She should use fear to compel him, and only then would he be subdued. So she transformed herself into a great rākṣasa, with hair standing on end, wielding a five-foot sword. While the king was sleeping peacefully at night, she appeared not far from him in the empty air. The king awoke in great terror. She said: "Though you have a thousand and ten thousand soldiers, you now belong to me and are not free. The time of death has come. How can you be saved?" The king replied: "I have no other recourse but to rely on the good deeds I have done in the past, cultivating a pure mind, so that at death I may be born in a good place." The celestial being approved his words: "Such conditions are the most reliable. There is no other principle!" The king then asked: "What spirit are you, that you have made me so terrified?" She answered: "I am your Queen Moon-Light. You allowed me to leave home. Through contemplation I was freed from desire and was reborn in the form realm heavens. I have come now to fulfill our covenant." The king said: "Though you say this, I still do not believe you. Resume your original form, and then I will believe." The celestial being then transformed back into Moon-Light as she had been, with her original garments and adornments, standing beside the king.

Desire arose in the king, and he immediately moved to embrace her. Moon-Light thought: "This person's state of desire is impure. How can I approach him?" She immediately withdrew and ascended into the empty air. She taught the Dharma for the king, saying: "This body is impermanent; it cannot be relied upon even for the snap of a finger. It is like the morning dew, which vanishes when the sun rises. Not heeding impermanence, you cling to the body. Does the king not see that youthful beauty and bloom are swallowed up by old age? The faculties decay; the eyes cannot see clearly, the ears cannot hear well. The body deteriorates and rots, of no further use. It is like brewing wine: you extract the pure flavor, and the dregs are worthless. When the body has aged, there is nothing to desire or delight in; only death remains. Once this body is born, death is always its companion. Does the king not see those who die in the womb, those who die at birth, those who die in their prime, and those who die in old age? This body is fragile and precarious; the bandit of death always follows. It cannot be trusted even for a moment. Body and mind are ablaze with fire; there is nothing but manifold suffering. The mind has the three poisons of worry and affliction; the body has the manifold ailments of cold and heat, hunger and thirst. Yet you do not grow weary of it, but cling to this body of mine. Palace women and courtesans, the beauty of the five desires, the kingdom's wealth, wife and children: none of these are yours. When death comes, not a single one follows you. Even the body itself is abandoned; how much more so all other things? In the sorrows and joys of birth and death, nothing is remarkable. The petty, the ignorant, and the benighted are submerged in the five desires, swept in the current of birth and death, not knowing the way out. The king is a wise person. Why do you not grow weary and depart, leaving home to seek the Way?" At that time a wholesome mind arose in the king, and he agreed to leave home. Moon-Light exhorted him further: "If you are to leave home, you should seek a good teacher. You should hear the marvelous Dharma. Having heard the marvelous Dharma, receive it and practice it. Strive diligently day and night without slackening!" Having spoken these words, she suddenly vanished.

At dawn the king abdicated in favor of the crown prince, renounced the five desires, and went to Mahākātyāyana to leave home and practice the Way. Because a king had renounced such great glory and profit to seek the true Way, ministers, officials, and people came in great numbers to make offerings and pay their respects, interfering with his practice. He therefore traveled to the kingdom of Magadha. The Buddha taught him the Dharma, and he attained the fruit of arhatship. His faculties became tranquil and still, without any desire. Carrying a clay bowl, he entered the city of Rājagṛha, begged some leftover rice, and returned to the forest to eat it, seated on grass.

King Bimbisāra, out on an excursion, encountered him and went into the forest to greet him: "Formerly you were a king, going about attended by retinues, with bells struck and drums beaten, with people and settlements, taxes and treasuries, and precious things arising naturally. Now you are a beggar, going about alone begging for food. Can this be pleasant? Come back and abandon the Way, and we shall share and rule half the kingdom together!" The practitioner answered: "I was king of a great country, with many settlements. Why should I now abandon what is great for what is small? That would not be fitting for me!" King Bimbisāra asked further: "Formerly you ate the finest delicacies, served in jeweled vessels. Now you hold a clay bowl and beg for stale leftover food. Is this not hard? Formerly, as a king, brave warriors attended and guarded you. Now you are alone. Are you not afraid? Formerly you dwelt in the inner palace, with queens, consorts, and courtesans for your entertainment, beautiful sounds and fine sights filling your ears and eyes, seated on jeweled beds spread with fine silk cushions. Now you wander alone, sleeping in the forest wild, lying on grass mats. Is this not bitter?" The practitioner replied: "I am content with this and desire nothing more."

King Bimbisāra said: "You are a pitiable person!" The practitioner answered: "It is you who are pitiable, not I! Why? Because you are entangled in the five desires, driven about by love and attachment, not free. My mind and heart are now tranquil and joyful, free from desire and at ease, with all kinds of happiness." Having taught the Dharma for King Bimbisāra, the king departed.

It was asked: "These fourfold assemblies all love the Buddha's Way and wish to practice the bodhisattva's three undertakings. Some wish to practice for one day and one night, some for seven days, and some for an entire lifetime. How much merit do they obtain?"

The answer was: "This question is extremely profound. I cannot answer it. Only the Buddha can know the extent of this merit. Apart from the Tathāgata, no one can fathom it! For example, a king of the Yuezhi country, wishing to seek the Buddha's Way, built thirty-two stūpas to make offerings to the Buddha's image. As he built them one by one, upon reaching the thirty-first, a wicked person offended the king. The king's mind turned back in retreat: 'How can such evil people possibly be saved?' He immediately changed his aspiration, turned away from birth and death toward nirvāṇa, and built the thirty-second stūpa seeking liberation. Due to these conditions, he attained the arhat's Way. Therefore this monastery is called Prātimokṣa. From that time to the present, not yet two hundred years have passed. This monastery still stands, and I myself have seen it. Every monastery has fine images."

After the king had passed from the world, a certain man obtained a flower from an āmra tree, its color like gold. Having obtained this fine flower, he wished to use it as a head ornament. But then he reflected: "This head is impermanent. When it decays, foxes and dogs will devour it, and it will merge with dirt and filth. What use is adorning it?" He then took the flower into a Buddhist stūpa and, seeing the beautiful image of the Buddha, a thought arose in his mind: "This is an image of the Buddha Śākyamuni, with all its fine marks." He then contemplated the Buddha's merits: "The Buddha is an omniscient person, of great compassion and great kindness, with the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, and other merits." Having contemplated thus, his heart burned and his hair stood on end. He then offered the flower to the Buddha.

Having made the offering, he thought: "Although I have heard the Buddha say that offering a single flower will surely bring a great reward, I do not know the extent of it." He went out and met a proselytizing monk, and asked: "How much merit does one obtain by scattering a single flower before the Buddha?" The monk answered: "I grew weary of the world's suffering, renounced the five desires, and left home to receive the precepts. That is all. I do not study the sūtras. Such a profound matter I cannot know. You should ask someone learned who studies the sūtras!" He then went to ask a sūtra-studying monk, who answered: "I am like a painter who merely follows what is heard and seen. I have no divine eye or supernatural power and cannot know or see the fruits of good and evil actions." He then directed him to a meditating monk: "The senior meditating monk is an arhat with the six supernatural powers. He will surely know!" So the man went and asked about the merit of contemplating the Buddha's virtues, his heart burning and hair standing on end, and scattering a single flower before the Buddha: "How much merit does this yield?" The arhat then examined it: after this man relinquished his present body, he would successively receive the blessings of heavenly and human states, from one lifetime to ten million billion lifetimes, from one great kalpa to eighty thousand great kalpas, and still the merit would not be exhausted. Beyond this, the arhat could not see further.

The arhat, being one whom the community esteemed, was troubled that he could not determine the full fruit of a single flower. He said to the man: "Wait a moment." He then sent a transformation body up to the Tuṣita heaven to visit Maitreya. He fully recounted what the worthy man had said and asked Maitreya: "How much reward does this yield?" Maitreya answered: "I cannot know! Even if there were as many one-birth-supplement bodhisattvas as grains of sand in the Ganges, they still could not know, much less I alone! Why? Because the Buddha has immeasurable merit; his field of merit is supremely excellent. The fruits planted within it are without end. Wait until I become a Buddha in the future, and then I will be able to know!"
