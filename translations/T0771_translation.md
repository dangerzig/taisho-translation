# 四品學法經
## Sūtra of the Four Grades of Dharma Study

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0771

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

There is one who studies the three virtues, called "True Study," and is of the upper grade. There is one who upholds the complete precepts, called "Receiving the Dharma," and is of the middle grade. There is one who accepts the lesser precepts, called "Relying on Merit," and is of the lower grade. There is one who practices three things, called a "Scattered Attendant," and is of the outer grade.

The True Study of the three virtues consists of: first, complete and perfect conduct of the precepts; second, extensive knowledge of the sūtras and Dharma; and third, the ability to teach and transform others. These are the three virtues, and one who possesses them is called a True Student. One who receives the Dharma with complete precepts means one who purely upholds the five precepts, who has faith in and carefully examines the workings of merit and transgression, and who reverently follows the Dharma's teaching. One who relies on merit with the lesser precepts means one who upholds only the first four precepts but does not observe the precept against alcohol, who follows worldly customs and does not change the affairs of desire; this is one who Relies on Merit.

The Scattered Attendant's three things are not precepts. What are these three? First, entrusting one's body to the Dharma; second, making offerings to the Dharma; and third, fellowship with co-practitioners. One practices on one's own, without a teacher and without receiving from anyone, following the natural inclination of the heart without any restraint; this is called the Scattered Attendant's Dharma. The merit of the True Student surpasses that of one who Receives the Dharma a hundredfold. The merit of one who Receives the Dharma surpasses that of one who Relies on Merit a hundredfold. The merit of one who Relies on Merit surpasses that of the Scattered Attendant a hundredfold. The merit of the Scattered Attendant surpasses that of an ordinary worldly person a hundredfold.

An ordinary worldly person may sometimes be worse than an animal, and an animal may sometimes be superior to a human. Why is this? When a person commits offenses without ceasing, one falls into the hells. When the hell punishment is finished, one becomes a hungry ghost. When the hungry ghost punishment is finished, one becomes an animal. When the animal punishment is finished, one returns to being a human. All those among the animals are completing their punishments before they can become human again. Therefore a person should do good, reverently practice the teaching of the Three Honored Ones, and study the Dharma of the upper four grades. One will then be forever separated from the three evil destinies, will pass from one heavenly existence to the next, and when born in the human realm will be noble and honored, receiving blessings life after life, with merit ever increasing toward liberation.

---

If one loses proper deportment in a single respect, one should constantly study to correct it according to the Dharma, and upon timely admonition one will promptly understand. If one is lazy and not diligent, one should be demoted to a lower seat, and only when one later demonstrates merit should one be restored. If one does not heed instruction or violates the precepts, one should be expelled from the assembly, lest one corrupt the others. If one is newly ordained and less than three months have passed, any infractions should not initially be questioned, for one has not yet learned the ways.

If all those who study the path follow and employ this discipline, examining one another from above and below, then they may see it through to the end and swiftly fulfill the great aspiration.

---

## The Scattered Attendant's Dharma

The question was asked: "If a good man wishes to enter the right path and follow the great path, but cannot endure the precepts, what practices should he undertake to seek good fortune?"

The teacher said: "There are also three things, called the Scattered Attendant's Dharma, which are good and worthy of practice. What are these three? First, practices that the body is able to maintain; second, the practice of making offerings; and third, the practice of fellowship with co-practitioners. Practicing these three things, one's merit immediately surpasses that of an ordinary worldly person a hundredfold.

"What are the Scattered Attendant's bodily practices? Though one does not uphold the precepts, one should differ somewhat from ordinary worldly people. In the places where one dwells and travels, one should frequently visit places where the sūtras and the path are taught. If one sees that worldly people do something good, follow it; if something evil, do not adopt it. Speak good words and be discerning; ugly speech should not be uttered. This is the Scattered Attendant's first practice.

"What is the Scattered Attendant's practice of making offerings? How should one attend to the Three Treasures? Morning and evening without laxity, the mind should always turn toward them, and one should also study the sūtras and scriptures. If one dwells in poverty and has nothing with which to make offerings, one should be all the more diligent. When one sees others performing meritorious deeds, one should personally assist them and feel joy on their behalf in one's heart. This is the Scattered Attendant's second practice.

"What is the Scattered Attendant's practice of fellowship with co-practitioners? One should respect and cherish one's companions without arrogance. When sitting or rising, think of them; when going out or coming in, inquire after them, as one would look after oneself. For those who travel, if nearby, call on one another; if far away, await them with longing. Be careful never to turn one's back on them or treat them with indifference. This is the Scattered Attendant's third practice."

The teacher said: "Though practicing these three things may not lead to immediate deliverance, it is like stony ground overgrown with weeds: though the seed is not good, one still reaps a small harvest, enough to carry on through the season. This is still better than not planting at all. If one does not abandon the work of planting, one will eventually reach good soil, and then what one plants will succeed and the harvest will be plentiful. Just so it is with this! If one practices without ceasing, merit and virtue will sustain one, and one will eventually receive the true precepts."
