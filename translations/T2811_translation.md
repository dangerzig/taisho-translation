# Preface and Commentary on the Record of Opening the School of the Hundred Dharmas Bright Gate Treatise of the Mahāyāna

## *Dàshèng Bǎifǎ Míngmén Lùn Kāizōng Yìjì Xùshì* (大乘百法明門論開宗義記序釋)

Taishō 2811

Author not attributed.

*Note: This is a detailed scholastic commentary explaining the preface of the "Record of Opening the School" (Kāizōng Yìjì), itself a sub-commentary on Vasubandhu's celebrated "Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas Bright Gate" (Bǎifǎ Míngmén Lùn). The hundred dharmas classification is one of the foundational frameworks of the Yogācāra school, categorizing all phenomena into one hundred types. This text glosses each phrase of the preface with painstaking detail, explaining technical terms, metaphors, and doctrinal references. It includes a colophon dating its completion to the twelfth month of a xīnyǒu (辛酉) year. The style is characteristic of Tang-dynasty Chinese Buddhist scholastic exegesis.*

---

The phrase "the omniscient one (*biànzhī*, 遍知) sheds his illumination in full detail, blending the true and the conventional at the mind-source; his deep compassion moistens all universally, expounding the half and the complete teachings through the streams of his words":

The word "the" (*fū*, 夫) introduces the beginning of the discourse. "Beginning" (*xù*, 緒) means the initial thread drawn from a silkworm's mouth, the collective thread called the "beginning." This "collective" is the source from which things proceed. Moreover, "collective" has the meaning of the initial thread that can produce a bolt of cloth to cover against cold and heat, fulfilling people's wishes.

"Omniscient" (*biànzhī*, 遍知) means: ordinary beings, lost in darkness, are altogether called "unknowing." The two vehicles, although they know, their knowledge has limitations; since their wisdom does not arise universally, they cannot be called omniscient. Bodhisattvas above the grounds surpass the former two; although they possess knowledge of both mundane and supramundane matters, because they still have subtle cognitive obscurations (*suǒzhī zhàng*, 所知障), they cannot be called omniscient like the Buddha. Only all buddhas and tathāgatas are omniscient regarding the mental and mental-factor dharmas of all sentient beings in the three realms and six destinies.

"Sheds illumination in full detail" (*wěi zhào*, 委照): "full detail" means subtle; "illumination" means brightness. The wisdom of the two vehicles can illuminate the emptiness of persons. The deluded sentiments of ordinary beings cling to the existence of all things. All buddhas and bodhisattvas awaken to the emptiness of both persons and dharmas, fully comprehending the two truths of the conventional and the ultimate, and can illuminate both mundane and supramundane matters in full detail.

"Blending the true and the conventional at the mind-source" (*hún zhēnsú yú xīnyuán*, 渾真俗於心源): "blending" means mixing and merging. This means blending the dharmas of the true and the conventional together at the one mind-source. The one mind-source can contain the ten thousand dharmas. It is precisely because all buddhas and bodhisattvas realize this that they are able to exercise deep compassion universally.

"Deep compassion" (*shēn cí*, 深慈): all beings without exception are sunk in the extremes of attachment. The Buddha, through right wisdom, transcends to great compassion, universally conveying beings of the three realms beyond existence and non-existence. Truly there are no sentient beings who attain cessation. This compassion that is free from characteristics is what is called "deep." Compassion that perceives characteristics is of two kinds: that which combines with the view of attachment is the guest-dust affliction; that which arises from meditative concentration belongs to practitioners of the two vehicles. The great compassion without conditions (*wúyuán dàcí*, 無緣大慈) is free from the characteristics of self and other. In the equal dharma-realm, ordinary beings and sages are the same. How could one perceive differences of self and other within the one suchness? Ordinary beings and sages equally realize compassion, which comprises three kinds: ordinary people first give rise to the notion of self, and then exercise compassion and relief, called "compassion conditioned on the self" (*wǒ yuán cí*, 我緣慈), generating compassion toward their own friends, relatives, and kin. Practitioners of the two vehicles see that sentient beings are constantly oppressed by three sufferings, five sufferings, and eight sufferings, and using the dharma to ferry them across, seeing beings as capable of being saved, this is called "compassion conditioned on dharmas" (*fǎ yuán cí*, 法緣慈). All buddhas and bodhisattvas attain the wisdom of equality and awaken to the compassion of the same essence, viewing all sentient beings as their own children, equally and impartially, without bias toward friend or foe.

"Expounding the half and the complete teachings through the streams of his words" (*yǎn bàn mǎn yú yánpài*, 演半滿於言派): as with a child in the world beginning to study, first learning characters like "superior," "great," and "man" constitutes the "half-word" teaching; later, when many characters are assembled to form complete [sentences], enabling thorough recognition and comprehension, this constitutes the "full-word" teaching. Initially, for people of shallow capacity, one teaches the small-vehicle doctrines of the Four Noble Truths, twelve links of dependent arising, and so forth, which follow the gateway of conventional reasoning and speak only of six consciousnesses: this is called the "half-word teaching." Later, for bodhisattvas of deep faith, one teaches the definitive-meaning Mahāyāna, establishing the middle-way teaching of eight consciousnesses: this is called the "full-word teaching." "Streams" (*pài*) means branching tributaries.

---

"Truly, because nature and characteristics mutually converge, the ten thousand dharmas return to one suchness; text and meaning interpenetrate, and the eight piṭakas course through the four kinds of unobstructed eloquence":

"Truly" (*shí*, 寔) means "really." If the deluded treat nature and characteristics as different paths, then for one who converges and harmonizes them, the ten thousand dharmas all return to the same track. If we speak of raising up nature, it does not leave characteristics; raising up characteristics does not leave nature. Likewise, raising up waves does not leave water; raising up water does not leave waves. Water and waves share the same source and are fundamentally of the same wetness. Therefore the ten thousand dharmas all return to one suchness. The essential natures of all beings are fundamentally not different; it is only because of the perception of characteristics that true suchness is obscured. Milk, curd, and ghee are originally of one flavor; failing to understand root and branch, one perceives differences.

"Text and meaning interpenetrate": "text" means names, phrases, and syllables; "meaning" means the principle being conveyed. "The eight piṭakas" refers to the sūtras, vinaya, abhidharma, miscellaneous collections, and so on; the Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna each have four piṭakas. "Course through" means to gallop, like a bird soaring through space. Here it means coursing through the four kinds of unobstructed eloquence in explanation and elucidation. Through the four kinds of eloquence, one courses through the waves; the shallow and deep aspects of text and meaning are proclaimed through the ocean-like mouth. "The four kinds of unobstructed eloquence" are: (1) unobstructed eloquence in words, meaning that when teaching the dharma, following whatever sounds and phrases, there is no obstruction in expression; (2) unobstructed eloquence in meaning, meaning that the principles of the dharma-teaching follow one upon another inexhaustibly across kalpas; (3) unobstructed eloquence in dharma, meaning that all buddhas and tathāgatas teach according to the capacity of beings, speaking of both nature and characteristics without doubt or obstruction; (4) unobstructed eloquence in exposition, meaning that the Tathāgata's words and meanings are manifold, and with a single sound he expounds the teaching so that each type [of being] understands according to its own kind, with skillful means and abundant wisdom, never weary, and his words and reasoning are never halting.

---

"How are the different tracks of emptiness and existence, or the divergent paths of the extensive and the brief? This record illustrates that a thousand [dharmas] are of one track, and ten thousand [dharmas] are one":

"How" (*hé*, 曷) means "what?" "Tracks" (*zhé*, 轍) means paths. The names "emptiness" and "existence" are spoken provisionally through hidden intent. When investigated to the ultimate, existence and emptiness are no different. "Existence" means the real existence of the aggregates, spoken in terms of clinging views. "Emptiness" means that [things] are provisionally constituted, and when one awakens to their nature, they are not existent. Thus we know that delusion and awakening differ of themselves, while the source-essence of emptiness and existence is not different.

The terms "extensive" and "brief" are likewise not two. For the awakened, four phrases suffice without needing to seek more: this is the brief. For the deluded, even reading ten thousand volumes of true words still amounts to falling into delusion: this is the extensive. It is just that sharp and dull capacities differ, while the extensive and the brief are not two.

"Investigating the root, one must share the same flavor with a hundred streams; clinging to the branches, one competes in the confused currents of the five slanders":

"Investigating" (*jiū*, 究) means "to exhaust." "The root" means the essence, the extinction of names and the transcendence of characteristics, the true principle. Speaking comprehensively and in terms of convergence: if one finds the source of the root, then one knows that all streams return to the same single ocean. Tasting a single drop of the great ocean's water, one knows the flavor of all streams. "Clinging to the branches" means: "clinging" is being mired in delusion; "the branches" refers to those who cling to the teachings of emptiness and existence, spoken in terms of biased attachment.

The *Dvīrātra Sūtra* says: "On a certain night I attained true awakening; on a certain night I entered parinirvāṇa. Between these two, I said nothing at all. Not-speaking is the Buddha's speaking." If one says the Tathāgata has spoken any dharma, that is to slander the Buddha; one cannot understand the meaning of what I have spoken. Know clearly that the dharma of the fundamental essence is beyond the reach of verbal expression. The mind wishes to think, but deliberation is extinguished; the mouth wishes to speak, but words fail. It cannot be known through intellect; it cannot be recognized through consciousness. Yet those of perverse views rashly establish the five slanders:

(1) The slander of addition (*zēngyì bàng*, 增益謗): saying that all dharmas exist when they fundamentally do not; calling them "existent" is the slander of addition, for the dharma-nature is fundamentally empty. (2) The slander of diminution (*sǔnjiǎn bàng*, 損減謗): saying that all dharmas do not exist when they fundamentally are not non-existent; calling them "non-existent" is the slander of diminution, for the dependent nature has existence. (3) The slander of contradiction (*xiāngwéi bàng*, 相違謗): saying both existent and non-existent; the fundamental dharma transcends both, and both emptiness and existence contradict the principle. (4) The slander of idle disputation (*xìlùn bàng*, 戲論謗): saying neither existent nor non-existent; the fundamental dharma, not having come, is not existent, yet in the present is not non-existent; saying "not existent" means empty, saying "not non-existent" means existent: since emptiness and existence are not fixed, this is clearly idle disputation. Moreover, in the ultimate truth, not the slightest dharma can be obtained; if one establishes any dharma by giving rise to names and characteristics, this all constitutes idle disputation. (5) The slander of stupidity (*yúfèi bàng*, 愚廢謗): saying neither with existence nor with non-existence; the fundamental dharma is without written words or speech; if one says the dharma has no names, that is the slander of foolish annihilation. Reversing the fourth produces the fifth: the fourth says "not empty" is existent, but that existence is not existence; saying "not existent" is empty, but that non-existence is not non-existence. Existence that is not existence reverts to the meaning of non-existence; non-existence that is not non-existence reverts to the meaning of existence. If it is so, then clearly these are the confused and foolish, not the wise.

---

"Therefore the Hīnayāna drifts upon the two existences, while the views of the schools proliferate; the ultimate meaning drowns in the two emptinesses, while disputations multiply":

"Drifting" means floating. Personal self and dharma-self are both held to be definitely existent; hence "two existences." "Drowning in the two emptinesses" means sinking: both of the above are entirely empty, hence "two emptinesses." The former school clings to existence; the latter is mired in emptiness. Emptiness and existence being mutually opposed, disputations arise in competition.

"Personal self" (*rénwǒ*, 人我) means: at the place where the five aggregates are provisionally combined, one falsely imputes a person. Apart from the self and what belongs to the self, there is no person: this is the selflessness of persons. "Dharma-self" (*fǎwǒ*, 法我) means: clinging to dharmas of causes and conditions as truly arising, all having self-nature, and therefore calling them "self." But dharmas arising from causes and conditions are entirely illusory; they fundamentally do not arise, and their essence is unmoving: this is the selflessness of dharmas.

---

"Therefore our successor, Maitreya (*Mílè*, 彌勒), broadly proclaimed the Yogācāra, blocking the two gates of views and opening the one true path":

"Successor" (*bǔchù*, 補處): as in the world when a crown prince is designated to succeed at the Eastern Palace to carry on the affairs of the kingdom, and when one day the king passes away, the crown prince at the Eastern Palace succeeds as sovereign. Now, the one-lifetime bodhisattva is the one of whom the Buddha gave the prophecy: "Next, hereafter, when the human lifespan is 84,000 years, at the time of the wheel-turning king Śaṅkha, the successor will become a buddha." In Sanskrit he is called Maitreya (*Mídìlì jiā*, 彌帝隸迦), abbreviated to Mílè. Maitreya is his surname, translated as Cí shì (慈氏, "Kindly Clan"). Ajita (*Āyìduō*, 阿逸多) is his name, meaning "Unconquered" (*Wú shèng*, 無勝): no one surpasses him, hence this bodhisattva is called Unconquered. "Kindly Clan" refers to this bodhisattva's possession of deep compassion, his heart of rescue being earnest and kind. The compassion of the two vehicles is small, only observing the desire realm; the Buddha's great compassion universally saves the three realms.

"Broadly proclaimed" (*chǎn*, 闡) means to explain, to spread. "Blocking" (*dù*, 杜) means to obstruct. "The two gates of views" means the two gates of emptiness and existence. "Opening the one true path" means the middle way.

"However, because the principle encompasses events extensively and the purport is dispersed in vast words, those of former cultivation grew weary at transmission, and those of later progress became fatigued in their studies."

Completed on the twelfth day of the twelfth month.

"Thereupon there was a bodhisattva whose name was Vasubandhu (*Shìqīn*, 世親). His rank reached the pinnacle of the three stages of the worthy, his virtue bordered on the ten stages of the sage. He composed a thousand treatises, his fame reaching the five regions of India. His mind marvelously accorded with the Buddha-mind, and he elegantly supported the prophecy given from afar."

"Thereupon" (*yuán*, 爰) means "here." "His" (*jué*, 厥) means "that." "His rank reached the pinnacle of the three stages of the worthy" means: this bodhisattva's relationship with the world's devas was intimate, hence his name Vasubandhu, also called Tiānqīn (*Tiānqīn*, 天親, "Kinsman of the Devas"). He was at the pre-ground three stages of worthiness, in the subsequent preparatory stage. "Pinnacle" means foremost. "Worthy" means that one's conceptual mind is somewhat subtle, and one practices goodness with harmonious compliance; this is called "worthy." Also, the practice of harmonizing with the truth is called "worthy."

"His virtue bordered on the ten stages of the sage": his meritorious deeds approached the ten grounds (*bhūmi*). He had not entered the true grounds. "Grounds" because: (1) they can produce and bring to completion the Buddha's wisdom; (2) one arouses the great compassion of the same essence and bears the burden of all beings. "Sage" means: because one accords with principle, penetrates the spiritual, and transforms without obstruction, one is called a sage.

"The people at the three stages of worthiness, although they awaken to the true teaching in essence, still have habitual obstructions not yet removed, and outflow-afflictions still remain; therefore they are called 'inner ordinary beings.' The initial aspiration of the ten stages of faith, spoken of collectively: all thirty minds practice the contemplation of emptiness and non-self. Speaking of them individually: the initial ten abidings practice the contemplation of the selflessness of persons; the next ten practices practice the contemplation of the selflessness of dharmas of causes and conditions; the next ten dedications practice the contemplation of the selflessness of dharmas of true suchness; the next ten grounds practice the contemplation of the three emptinesses and the two selflessnesses; the next stage of equal awakening practices the contemplation of illuminating quiescence; the stage of true awakening practices only the contemplation of quiescence. When contemplation and illumination are equal, the buddha-ground is the great quiescence."

"This sage composed five hundred treatises in each of the Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna, and in the West he was acclaimed as the 'Master of a Thousand Treatises.' The five Indias means 'heaven.' 'Accorded' means matched and joined. 'Elegantly' means wonderfully. 'The prophecy given from afar' means: the Buddha prophesied that this person would be the twenty-second transmitter of the dharma after his passing. The fruit accords with this prophecy, truly matching the sage-mind."

---

"And so, grieving that people of his time were frivolous, that the age was thin, that capacities were shallow and natures feeble, that they feared the dharma-ocean and would remain long separated from it, that they shrank from the mountain of meaning and would be permanently discouraged":

"He therefore gathered the finest essence from the extensive division, bound up the essential points in a concise text, enticed those of initial capacity, and compiled this small treatise": "gathered" means to pick up and collect; the "extensive division" is the great treatise of the Yogācārabhūmi; "bound up" means to collect; "enticed" means to lead; "capacity" means faculties; "compiled" means to assemble; "the small treatise" is the Hundred Dharmas Treatise.

"Wishing that [students] would linger over the supreme teaching, gradually be dyed by the true vehicle, show the lost wanderer the way home, and guide the weary traveler to a shortcut": "linger" means to remain; by engaging with the great Yogācāra treatise without sinking into what is subtle, one can progress from the small to the great, from the shallow to the deep, causing the great dharma to endure long and the lineage to continue without interruption. "Gradually be dyed by the true vehicle" means: gradual learning is easy to accomplish, while sudden immersion is hard to achieve. Like receiving color in the world, through habitual practice one becomes a sage. As the saying goes: "Those near red become red; those near ink become black; those near the sycophantic become flattering; those near the worthy become virtuous."

"Showing the lost wanderer the way home" means: "lost wanderer" is like a young child who has lost their parents and forgotten their home; they need a wise person to show them the way. The "young child" is a metaphor for deluded and confused sentient beings; "the way home" is a metaphor for the supreme teaching. "Guiding the weary traveler" means: the practitioners of biased views and the two vehicles have lost their way in the great teaching; they have mistakenly entered the wrong path and wearied themselves on tortuous trails. The sages rebuke them, saying that with their backs turned [to the right path], they will fall into the division of those cut off, that the road is distant and hard to ascend. Therefore they show the shortcut, which is this treatise.

---

"The first part illuminates the existence of the hundred dharmas; the latter part reveals the emptiness of the two selves. Beginning and end shine upon each other; start and finish reflect one another":

"The first part illuminates the existence of the hundred dharmas": the dependent (*yītā*, 依他) and the perfected (*yuánchéng*, 圓成) natures, the principle of conditioned arising, are established through mutual dependence. Through provisional conditions they come into being; the nature of the perfected is essentially suchness, constant and unchanging: this reveals the two existences.

"The latter part reveals the two emptinesses": the two attachments to the self of persons and the self of dharmas are the mind of deluded imputation, merely deluded sentiments, false and vain computation. If the deluded conceptualization ceases, persons and dharmas are originally empty: this reveals the fundamental absence of self-attachment.

"Beginning" means the initial part; "end" means the latter part. "Shine upon each other": the earlier text speaks of existence to dispel clinging to emptiness; the latter text speaks of emptiness to remove the view of existence. "Start and finish reflect one another": placing the earlier against the latter, "empty yet not empty"; placing the latter against the earlier, "existent yet not existent." Know clearly: when "not empty" and "not existent" are both removed, biased clinging is dispelled.

"When biased clinging is removed, the middle contemplation first appears; the waves of consciousness thereby subside and become subtle; the true source, thanks to this, becomes clear and vast": "Middle contemplation" means the middle way. "First" means initially. "Appears" means becomes manifest. "Waves of consciousness" means: the mind of consciousness is like waves. "Subside" means to sink down. "Subtle" means feeble. "True source" means the original nature. "Thanks to" means by means of. "Clear" means pure. "Vast" means spacious. When the waves subside, the ocean becomes clear; when delusion is removed, the nature becomes vast. "One" is the beginning of number; "ten" is the end of number. Together they form the character for "man" (*shì*, 士). A man must preserve both head and tail to be called an "excellent man."

"Supreme indeed! The profound purport of this treatise!": "supreme" means great; "profound" means deep; "purport" means intention, direction.

---

"The heading 'Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas Bright Gate of the Mahāyāna' (*Dàshèng Bǎifǎ Míngmén Lùn*) raises the supreme benefit of the teaching's school and establishes the common name of its own division": raising the supreme benefit of the teaching's school means this treatise takes the Mahāyāna as its school; using this vehicle to benefit all beings is called "supreme benefit." "Establishes" means to set up; "the common name of its own division" means that within the Mahāyāna, it is called the Hundred Dharmas Treatise.

"Furthermore, the words 'selected summary of names and numbers from the division on fundamental events' (*běnshì fēn zhōng lüè lù míngshù*, 本事分中略錄名數) marks the distinctive basis from which the treatise arose, and distinguishes it by a different title from other expositions": this treatise definitely comes from the division on fundamental events (*běnshì fēn*, 本事分) of the Yogācārabhūmi, not from the counterpart treatise or other divisions. Although this treatise comes from the division on fundamental events, it establishes a separate name: the Hundred Dharmas Treatise. It is not called the Yogācāra Treatise, nor is it called the Fundamental Events Treatise.

"Mahāyāna" takes "great conveyance" as its exalted title; "hundred dharmas" takes the number of essences to manifest its name. "Great" obtains its name by contrast with "small"; "vehicle" has the meaning of "conveyance." It is also a title established by exclusion: "exclusion" means it excludes the small vehicle, not an affirmative designation. "Hundred" is a number: ten tens mutually supporting makes a hundred. "Dharma" means essence. In terms of essence, it strictly determines one hundred. "Bright" means wisdom. "Gate" means passage. These hundred dharmas are the gate through which wisdom travels, whereby one can open the Buddha's knowledge and vision; therefore it serves as [the method of] accomplishment. "Treatise" means to weigh and assess correct principles, discerning right and wrong.

"Fundamental events" means the fundamental essence, the essence of the hundred dharmas. "Investigating" means to exhaust; "ultimate" means wondrous. "From the division" means from within the division on fundamental events, not from other divisions.

"Selected summary" means to restrain the verbose wording. "Names and numbers" means to raise these essential points. "Selected" means to abridge the verbose and cumbersome wording; "summary" means to record the essential points and wondrous purport. "Restrain" means "to the utmost."

"By employing this to display the elegant purport, one establishes the great name; hence it is called: 'Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas Bright Gate of the Mahāyāna: Selected Summary of Names and Numbers from the Division on Fundamental Events.'" "Display" means to signal, like a banner. Raising a banner, one discerns the military strategy; establishing a marker, one makes the truth manifest. If one wishes to know the military situation, one simply looks at the banners and flags. If one wishes to know that this dharma is profoundly deep, the title and exposition convey it. "Elegant" means correct and wondrous. "Great" means vast.
