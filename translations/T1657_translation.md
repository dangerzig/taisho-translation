# 手杖論
## The Walking-Stick Treatise

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1657

Composed by Śākyaprabha (釋迦稱). Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨).

---

Because a certain class of sentient beings in the world, lacking the discernment of wisdom, give rise to wrong views, and because out of compassion for their submersion and sinking, this treatise is now composed. The verse says:

> Suppose on a level, smooth road,
> At every step one stumbles and falls:
> For such foolish and confused persons,
> We expound this "Walking-Stick" treatise.

The treatise says: Each and every world-honored one appears in the world, and thereupon brings to cessation incalculable numbers of sentient beings. Yet sentient beings are without final limit, like empty space, because their nature is without boundary. This is the principle set forth by the Bodhisattva Vasubandhu. "Boundary" means cutting off at a certain definite number; this is the same meaning. To preclude this, it is said "without boundary." "Final" means the meaning of exhaustion and completion. Therefore, there is no fallacy of the thesis and the reason being inseparable. The thesis states "without end"; the reason states "without boundary." This is a fallacy of partial overlap with the thesis.

A certain disputant makes the following argument: "It is admitted that there are newly arising sentient beings who never existed before, like the Devadāru forest. As he argues: 'Just as a forest or thicket, though subject to much felling and destruction, is seen to regenerate after being destroyed, with its causes being inexhaustible, by this one must definitely admit that there are separate, previously non-existent sentient beings who newly arise.'" This is stated in brief form. As the brief synopsis states, in response to a question, the one named "Medicine-Army" was answered: "There are old sentient beings and new sentient beings," and so forth in detail. "Therefore, one should definitely admit this. If it were otherwise, then, like a heap of sesame seeds with no new seeds added, it would necessarily diminish, and one would definitely see it come to an end."

If so, then if one admits previously non-existent sentient beings newly arising now, this amounts to admitting that saṃsāra has a very beginning. If one admits a beginning, then the fallacy of lacking a cause follows. And if one admits no cause, then everything should exist without cause. This is the original intention of the ācārya. The opponent then responds: "Through the karma of mutual connection performed in relation to children, the seeds of the two dharmas of wholesome and unwholesome, the appropriating consciousness, the kalala and other stages: these constitute the very first arising. Once connected, through proximity to good or evil, one creates one's own karma and enters the cycle of transmigration or departs into nirvāṇa. This accords smoothly with the principle of dependent origination; this is the saṃskāra-pratyaya and the vijñāna-pratyaya." Since such a meaning exists within the categories, why do you forcibly insist on admitting newly arising sentient beings? The proponent would then incur the fallacy that saṃsāra has a first beginning.

Another disputant says: "This is not a proper answer. Why? If it is possible for new beings to arise, then there is an increase. Since increase implies decrease, the initial being has no cause." If one says that these new sentient beings have never known the direction of saṃsāra and are suddenly born solely through the conditioning power of others' karma, then: the sentient beings now receiving the cycle of transmigration, moment by moment generating one another in succession, would constitute an increase. By this reasoning, the theory of new arising should admit an initial single sentient being, since admitting increase implies decrease. At all times, admitting newly arising multitudes of sentient beings means the number of sentient beings definitely increases. Since each preceding moment implies decrease, if this is so, the decrease should gradually diminish until, approaching the end, there is only one being alone. But this very first being, not depending on any other karma, becomes the first, existing without cause. This certainly cannot resolve the fallacy of no cause.

If one says there is definitely an increase: one can incur this fault. Having addition without loss is the cause of increase, like a miser's possessions. Yet sentient beings do have a principle of decrease. How so? Each and every buddha-stūpa causes incalculable multitudes of sentient beings to enter nirvāṇa, and yet it is not like this. The verse says:

> The appearance of a buddha is hard to encounter;
> Faith in the teaching is also hard to meet.
> Since the causes of birth are easily obtained,
> The principle of liberation is different from these.

The treatise says: The appearance of a buddha in the world is truly hard to encounter. As the treatise states: "In the first incalculable kalpa, one has already served five times seventy thousand buddhas; in the second, six times seventy thousand buddhas; in the third, seven times seventy thousand buddhas." The provisions for buddhahood are extremely difficult to obtain. As a verse says:

> Through countless hundreds of austerities,
> Countless roots of merit are born.
> Advancing through countless long periods,
> Severing countless obstructive afflictions,
> One obtains the omniscient wisdom,
> Purifying and removing all obstacles and afflictions,
> Becoming the unsurpassed World-Honored One:
> Like opening a chest to behold the jewels.

Even if one sometimes encounters the appearance of a Tathāgata, those who reverently believe in the pure Dharma and Vinaya he teaches are truly hard to find, because such faith can only be complete in the eight conditions of leisure, and these are extremely hard to obtain. Yet the causes of birth are extremely easy to obtain, namely afflictions and karma, because these causes and conditions are abundantly present at all times. These afflictions have three kinds of conditions: first, latent tendencies not yet severed; second, the binding objects presently manifesting; third, giving rise to improper attention regarding them. Ordinary fools are much attached to these. As for karma, since the causes of connection through the capacity to create are also not hard to obtain, there may sometimes be liberation. Trying to answer the new increase with a small decrease certainly cannot avoid incurring the fault; the reasoning does not correspond. The verse says:

> The vessel-world has no increasing number;
> It may possibly have times of decrease.
> Since saṃsāra has no beginning,
> This should produce a great constriction.

The treatise says: If it is possible for new beings to arise, they should flow in and accumulate. This is because the vessel-world does not have a principle of increase like the sentient-being world. The definite arrangement of worlds, horizontally and vertically, may have times of decrease, as when a kalpa is destroyed and realms become empty. As it is said: "Like torrential heavenly rain, if the rain of Īśāna fell without gap or break from the sky, in the same way, in the eastern direction, without gap or break, world-systems are either forming or dissolving," and so forth. Yet there is no limit or measure for the new arising and growth of sentient beings. If sentient beings increase in number without limit, it would be unreasonable: would they not press upon each other and suffer the anguish of constriction?

Now, a further question is posed to the proponents of new arising: You admit that previously non-existent sentient beings suddenly arise, and that consciousness comes to be born through the conditioning power of others' karma. Is this through habituation or not through habituation? If you say it is through habituation, then from the moment of connection the newly born consciousness is habituated, which incurs the fallacy of one essence being many. In general, when discussing habituation, it must involve co-arising and co-ceasing with defiled and pure dharmas, and depending on this, serving as the cause of their arising. It is not the case that a sentient being first has a cause of arising that enables those two dharmas to co-arise and co-cease, and depending on this, serves as the cause of their arising. However, the consciousness that serves as the connector co-arises and co-ceases with the preceding two dharmas and serves as their cause of arising, without any contradiction. If so, then the first moment of consciousness admitted as habituated, and the connector from the preceding moment, being the cause of this, the next moment should also be the same. If it were otherwise, the principle of habituation would not be established. By this reasoning, if a father exerts effort and attains liberation, or acquires great learning, the son, without exerting effort, should be the same as his father. Or sometimes the father should be the same as the son. Faults of this kind multiply and are all incurred.

If one says it is not through habituation and that the multiple functions arise of themselves: if one further argues that because there are no defilement and purity co-arising and co-ceasing, and depending on them one serves as a cause, the previous faults apply. Now, taking only the connector, because causes and conditions combine, it serves as a dominant condition, and without any root-source, that being arises. This meaning certainly avoids the fault of not producing a result. If so, then the function and the result perish, like the function of karma. The functions associated with craving and the rest, having repaid the result, all cease, like the karma of maturation that, having produced its result, immediately sinks away. If it were otherwise, the fault of endlessness would follow. This case is the same. Even if so, what fault is there? Because one does not admit that liberation comes from planting another seed, no effort is needed. Since the functions of craving and the rest have already perished, apart from the functions associated with craving and the rest, with respect to their own results one further does not admit planting a new seed. Since the seed is severed, even without cultivating the noble path, one achieves nirvāṇa. This then accords with and follows the words of the Sunakkhatta Deva, indulging private views and praising what is not the Dharma. As he said:

> I observe the multitudes of beings
> Tormenting body and tormenting mind,
> Abandoning all pleasures and desires,
> Separately seeking a cause to remove suffering.
> One need only indulge in exquisite pleasure;
> Why labor at other views?
> Simply with a mind of craving and defilement,
> One directly attains the principle of nirvāṇa.

Further faults arise, because karma becomes useless. As the brief synopsis says: "There are three kinds of meritorious activity: giving, morality, and meditation." If one arouses diligent effort to cultivate these, is it truly useless? Why is this so? Because the combined function produces the result of its own accord. If the meritorious activities are about to perish, they place a maturation-habituation in the ālaya consciousness. Later, this habituation, according to its own function, obtains an assisting cause and will produce the future maturation in combination. If so, then the effort expended on those meritorious activities is not in vain. But if, without previously cultivating wholesome karma, merely through the connector serving as a condition, the maturation-function suddenly arises and from this can produce the present life and future maturation-suffering body, then diligent cultivation would be useless as before.

Furthermore, the two kinds of karma, volition and volitional action, wholesome, unwholesome, and indeterminate, and all such classifications would all become empty fabrications. If one says "afterward it is habituated," and excluding the very first moment of the appropriating consciousness, up until the arising of adverse conditions, one habituates craving and the rest within one's own continuum, then the faults charged above would not arise. If that is so, then the previous habituation is also the same: taking one's own habituation, this is all already established. If not, then how can it serve as a cause of arising? Although supramundane wisdom has not yet been obtained, merely by being situated within one's own continuum, the wisdom resembling the twofold selflessness, which is commonly admitted as arising from habituation at this point, serves as a cause of arising. This habituation through hearing is supremely pure, because its essential nature is the equal flow of the dharma-realm. The dharma-realm is the Tathāgata's Dharma-body; the understanding of the twofold selflessness of person and dharma is the effect flowing from the dharma-realm, namely the Dharma-body. "Realm" means "cause": it is the realm of all supramundane dharmas, because it can sustain the nature of that habituation. And the residual tendencies of the two obstructions, afflictive and cognitive, when severed, are called "supremely pure." The habituation through hearing resembles that result; since it arises with that as its dominant condition, it shares the same essential nature. As it is said: "The seeds of right hearing and habituation of bodhisattvas who have first aroused the aspiration for enlightenment, though worldly, should be known as subsumed under the Dharma-body. For śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, they should be known as subsumed under the liberation-body." Therefore, it is definitively known that, though worldly, they can directly serve as the seed of the supramundane mind.

The meaning of the Dharma being taught, like the cognitive aspect, though presently non-existent, yet through its sentences and phrases, in accordance with meaning, reveals the principle of connection therein. Showing one section as a type for all the rest, the verses are all the same. At this point of the section-break, though there are no supporting parts, it is for the purpose of understanding. If so, what fault is there? This is all already established: as with what comes after, so too with what came before, it is the same. It is merely situated within one's own continuum, habituated by craving and the rest. If that is so, then it arises from a seed born previously, and that in turn from a previous one, and that again from one before. Thus, all things entirely arise from established seeds, and the claim of new arising becomes baseless. Based on this, one can establish the existence of former lives.

> Devadatta's initial eye-consciousness
> Definitely comes from a prior consciousness.
> Because of the nature of consciousness, one should know
> It is like subsequent consciousnesses.

> Suppose one emerges from the cessation attainment:
> That mind too is subsumed therein.
> Within the thesis, by the principle of its nature,
> There is equally no fallacy of inconclusiveness.

> All minds and mental factors
> Are admitted to arise from four conditions.
> Then, for the very first mind you posit,
> What serves as its immediately preceding condition?

Minds and mental factors arise from four conditions; this is definitively established in Abhidharma reasoning. Then, for the very first arising of mind that you admit, what is its equal-and-immediately-preceding condition? It is also not the case that a mind-aggregate in another being's continuum can reasonably serve as a condition for one's own mind-aggregate, because they differ in degree.

There is further a definitive principle. All theories hold that the birth-existence is associated with afflictions of one's own ground. But the birth-existence mind you admit would not be an affliction of one's own ground. Even with respect to objects of the three times, the reasoning cannot be established. It is not past, because it has never been experienced. It is not future, because aspiration arises from the thought of having experienced something. It is not present, because the mental consciousness arises of its own accord, which would entail non-existence, deafness, blindness, and so forth.

Furthermore, the five sense-faculties and the life-faculty of all destinies are all maturation. But on your theory, they would not be maturation. "Wonderful, one who speaks the sūtras well!" Yet the sūtras say: "Internally, the five are nourished by maturation." Therefore, we know that the five faculties such as the eye are maturation in nature. The five destinies are also maturation in essence, taking the brief synopsis as authoritative. As it is said: "The beings in the hells, through the presently manifesting power of affliction, thereupon create the karma of birth in the hells, causing it to multiply." This means: "Through crooked, defiled, and turbid karma of body, speech, and mind, at the end of life, in a base and wretched place, the maturation of form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness is severely cooked." Once the maturation has arisen, it is called "a being in the hells." Herein, apart from those dharmas, there is no separate hell-being to be found. "Those dharmas" means the aggregates that are maturation-born. Here, the word "aggregate" is not without distinction. This meaning is to preclude a real self that traverses the destinies; one should understand by implication that it merely gathers the distinct aggregates. "Maturation-born" means: although the name and essence of sentient beings are not different, in order to make clear that there is maturation, within the five aggregates the wholesome, unwholesome, and indeterminate are not the nature of the destinies and are not maturation-retribution. If they too were the essence of the destinies, then the unwholesome karma of birth in the hells being created at present, or its multiplication, should already be called "hell-being." Therefore, the essential nature of the consciousness-destinies is solely unobscured-indeterminate, which is called "sentient being." The *Abhidharma* also says this. It further says: "The life-faculty is maturation." Upon examination, the life-faculty is definitely and solely maturation. Yet the *Yogācāra* treatise necessarily establishes all dharmas as the maturation-consciousness; there has never been a single dharma that transcends the nature of maturation. But for your proponent of newly arising sentient beings, the faculties and the rest would not be maturation. The karma of present-life and future-life retribution of those new sentient beings would not have existed previously, and the karma of present-life retribution at that time could not exist. Because the causes such as superiority and the rest would be absent, it would further follow that karma done by others would cause another to receive retribution. "Wonderful, one who speaks the sūtras well!": this is meant as ridicule. The intention is to say that such a one does not properly understand the sūtras and treatises, presents only his own views, and contradicts the āgamas.
