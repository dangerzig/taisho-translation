# 菩薩戒羯磨文
## Bodhisattva Precept Karmavācanā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1499

Spoken by Bodhisattva Maitreya (彌勒菩薩). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘).

---

### Section One: Karmavācanā for Receiving the Precepts

If bodhisattvas wish to study the three clusters of pure precepts of a bodhisattva, whether lay or monastic, having first aroused the great vow for unsurpassed perfect bodhi, they should carefully seek out a fellow-Dharma bodhisattva who has already aroused the great vow, who possesses wisdom and power, and who is able to confer and explain the meaning expressed in words. Before such a bodhisattva, fully endowed with these meritorious qualities and superior, they should first prostrate at their feet, bare the right shoulder, place the knees on the ground, join the palms in reverence, and make the following request:

"May the great worthy be mindful! I, named so-and-so, before the great worthy, request to receive all the pure precepts of a bodhisattva. I pray that you will bear with the effort for a brief moment, and with compassion, consent to confer them."

Having made this proper request without error, they bare the right shoulder, reverently prostrate, and make offerings to the buddhas and world-honored ones of the ten directions and three times, to the assemblies of bodhisattvas who have entered the great stages and attained great wisdom and great supernatural powers. With their minds focused on the present, they contemplate the meritorious qualities of those beings and give rise to a deeply pure mind.

When bodhisattvas wish to confer the bodhisattva precepts upon a bodhisattva, they should first explain the Mātṛkā of the bodhisattva Dharma-treasury, the rules of training for bodhisattvas, and the marks of transgression, causing the recipient to listen and accept. With wisdom they should examine their own disposition: they should be capable of reflection and able to receive the bodhisattva precepts not merely through the urging of others or for the sake of surpassing others. One who is known to be a steadfast bodhisattva is fit to receive the pure precept-discipline of a bodhisattva. The method of conferring the precepts should then be properly administered.

The bodhisattva receiving the precepts should further approach the wise and powerful superior bodhisattva with humility and reverence, place the knees on the ground, and before a buddha image, join the palms and make this request: "I pray that the great worthy will compassionately confer upon me the pure precepts of a bodhisattva." Having made this request, with mind focused on one object, they nurture the pure mind, thinking: "Before long, I shall obtain the inexhaustible, immeasurable, unsurpassed great treasury of merit." Then, reflecting on this meaning, they remain silent.

At that time, the wise and powerful bodhisattva, toward the bodhisattva who is able to practice the correct practice, with undistracted mind, whether seated or standing, speaks thus: "You, good man named so-and-so, listen! Are you a bodhisattva?" The other should answer: "I am." "Have you aroused the vow for bodhi?" The answer should be: "I have."

From this point on, the following should be said: "You, good man named so-and-so, listen! You now wish to receive from me all the rules of training and all the pure precepts of a bodhisattva, namely the precepts of discipline, the precepts of gathering wholesome dharmas, and the precepts of benefiting sentient beings. These rules of training and these pure precepts: all bodhisattvas of the past have already possessed them, all bodhisattvas of the future will possess them, and all bodhisattvas of the present throughout the ten directions now possess them. In these rules of training and these pure precepts: all bodhisattvas of the past have already trained, all bodhisattvas of the future will train, and all bodhisattvas of the present now train. Can you accept them?" The answer is: "I can accept them." The conferring bodhisattva repeats this a second and third time in the same manner, and the receiving bodhisattva answers likewise a second and third time.

Having thus received, the receiving bodhisattva does not rise from their seat, and the conferring bodhisattva, before the buddha image, reverently makes offerings, prostrates at the feet of all the buddhas and bodhisattvas presently dwelling throughout the ten directions, and makes this declaration: "I respectfully announce to all the buddhas and bodhisattvas in the boundless and limitless world-systems of the ten directions. Here now, a bodhisattva named so-and-so, from me, a certain bodhisattva, has received the bodhisattva precepts, stated up to three times. I serve as witness. I pray that all the buddhas and bodhisattvas in the boundless and limitless world-systems of the ten directions, the foremost true sages, who in all places whether seen or unseen, at all times and in all places, are presently aware of all sentient beings: may they also serve as witnesses for this bodhisattva named so-and-so who has received the precepts." This is repeated a second and third time.

When the karmavācanā for receiving the precepts is thus completed, from that moment onward, before all the buddhas and bodhisattvas presently dwelling throughout the ten directions in the boundless and limitless world-systems, a natural sign appears, indicating that this bodhisattva has received the pure precepts that bodhisattvas receive. At that time, all the buddhas and bodhisattvas of the ten directions, seeing this natural sign of the bodhisattva, give rise to recollection. Through recollection, right knowledge and insight arise. Through right knowledge and insight, they truly know: in a certain world, a bodhisattva of a certain name, from a certain bodhisattva, has duly received the pure precepts that bodhisattvas receive. All of them, toward this bodhisattva who has received the precepts, like a son, like a younger brother, give rise to thoughts of kinship, affection, and compassion. Through the affection and compassion of the buddhas and bodhisattvas, this bodhisattva's aspiration for wholesome dharmas is further increased and enhanced, without any regression. This is called the declaration, request for witnessing, in the karmavācanā for receiving the bodhisattva precepts.

"If bodhisattvas abide in the precept-discipline, there are four kinds of pārājika dharmas. What are the four? If bodhisattvas, out of desire for gain and respect, praise themselves and disparage others: this is the first pārājika dharma. If bodhisattvas who actually possess wealth, being stingy by nature, when suffering, impoverished beings without protector or refuge come before them seeking wealth, do not arise compassion and practice generosity; and when those seeking the Dharma come before them, being stingy regarding the Dharma, though they actually possess the Dharma, do not bestow it: this is the second pārājika dharma. If bodhisattvas nurture this kind of anger, and because of this, not only issue harsh words before ceasing, but further, obscured by anger, strike with hands, feet, clods, stones, knives, or staves, injuring and harming sentient beings, inwardly harboring fierce, intense angry intentions; and when there is transgression and others come to apologize, they do not accept, do not tolerate, and do not relinquish the grudge: this is the third pārājika dharma. If bodhisattvas slander the bodhisattva treasury, and delight in proclaiming, demonstrating, and establishing a semblance of the true Dharma, and regarding that semblance-Dharma, either believe in it themselves or follow others' lead: this is the fourth pārājika dharma.

"These are called the four kinds of pārājika dharmas for bodhisattvas. If a bodhisattva commits even one of these four pārājika dharmas, let alone all of them, they can no longer, in this present life, increase and gather the vast provisions for bodhi of a bodhisattva. They can no longer, in this present life, have pure aspirations. They are then called a semblance-bodhisattva, not a true bodhisattva.

"If bodhisattvas commit the four kinds of pārājika dharmas with mild or moderate entanglement, they do not lose the bodhisattva's pure precept-discipline. If committed with severe entanglement, it constitutes forfeiture. If bodhisattvas commit the four kinds of pārājika dharmas with repeated occurrence, entirely without shame or remorse, deeply delighting in them and seeing them as meritorious: one should know this is called committing with severe entanglement, and such a person is not a bodhisattva. It is not the case that a bodhisattva who merely once commits a pārājika dharma thereby forfeits the bodhisattva's pure precept-discipline, as when bhikṣus who commit a pārājika dharma thereby immediately abandon the prātimokṣa precepts. If bodhisattvas, through such transgression, forfeit the bodhisattva's pure precept-discipline, in this present life they are fit to receive it again; it is not that they are unfit, as bhikṣus who abide in the prātimokṣa precepts and commit a pārājika dharma are unfit to receive them again in this present life.

"The pure precepts thus received by a bodhisattva, compared to all other received precepts, are the most supreme, unsurpassed, immeasurable, and boundless, accompanied by a great treasury of merit. Arising from the foremost, most supreme wholesome intention of mind, they can universally eliminate all kinds of evil conduct toward all sentient beings. All prātimokṣa disciplines, compared to this bodhisattva discipline, do not reach even one hundredth, one thousandth, one countable fraction, one calculable fraction, one computable fraction, one comparable fraction, or even one upaniṣad fraction, because it gathers all great meritorious qualities."

Having thus performed the karmavācanā and other matters for receiving the bodhisattva precepts, both the conferring and receiving bodhisattvas arise together and make offerings, universally prostrating at the feet of all the buddhas and bodhisattvas presently dwelling throughout the ten directions in the boundless and limitless world-systems, and reverently withdraw.

Furthermore, bodhisattvas should not seek to receive the pure precepts that bodhisattvas receive from every wise person. From those without pure faith one should not receive them, meaning those who from the start have no faith in or understanding of these received precepts, who cannot enter into them, who do not reflect well upon them. From those who are stingy, obscured by stinginess, who have great desires, who lack contentment, one should not receive them. From those who violate the pure precepts, who have no respect for the rules of training, who are lax and careless regarding the precept-discipline, one should not receive them. From those who are resentful, who are mostly intolerant, who cannot endure the transgressions of others, one should not receive them. From those who are lazy, who are idle, who for the most part indulge in daytime and nighttime sleep, who delight in reclining, lounging, and gathering in idle company for frivolous talk, one should not receive them. From those whose minds are scattered, who cannot for even the time it takes to milk a cow maintain a single-pointed wholesome mind and cultivate it, one should not receive them. From those who are dull, foolish in kind, of extremely inferior mind, who slander the bodhisattva Sūtra-treasury and Mātṛkā, one should not receive them.

Furthermore, although bodhisattvas have already fully received and upheld the bodhisattva precept-discipline to completion, they should never casually proclaim or reveal it to those beings without faith who slander the bodhisattva treasury. Why? Because upon hearing it, such beings cannot have faith or understanding, being covered by the great obstruction of ignorance, and they give rise to slander. Through this slander, just as one who abides in the bodhisattva's pure precept-discipline accumulates an immeasurable great treasury of merit, so the slanderer is accompanied by an immeasurable great treasury of evil karma. Until they have permanently abandoned all evil speech, evil views, and evil thoughts, they will never escape or be free from it.

If bodhisattvas wish to receive the bodhisattva's pure precept-discipline but do not encounter a person fully endowed with meritorious qualities, at that time they should, before an image of the Tathāgata, receive the bodhisattva's pure precept-discipline by themselves. They should receive it as follows. Baring the right shoulder and placing the right knee on the ground, they should say: "I, named so-and-so, respectfully announce to all the tathāgatas of the ten directions and the assemblies of bodhisattvas who have entered the great stages. I now wish, before the buddhas and bodhisattvas of the worlds in the ten directions, to vow to receive all the rules of training and all the pure precepts of a bodhisattva, namely the precepts of discipline, the precepts of gathering wholesome dharmas, and the precepts of benefiting sentient beings. These rules of training and these pure precepts: all bodhisattvas of the past have already possessed them, all bodhisattvas of the future will possess them, and all bodhisattvas of the present throughout the ten directions now possess them. In these rules of training and these pure precepts: all bodhisattvas of the past have already trained, all bodhisattvas of the future will train, and all bodhisattvas of the present throughout the ten directions now train." This is repeated a second and third time. After speaking, one should rise. All the rest should be known as before.

### Section Two: Karmavācanā for Repentance of Transgressions

If bodhisattvas, having duly received the precept-discipline from another, through the wholesome and pure aspiration to study, the aspiration for bodhi, and the aspiration to benefit all sentient beings, give rise to the utmost respect and reverence, from the beginning they should be dedicated and not transgress. Should there be a transgression, they should promptly repent and remove it in accordance with the Dharma, so as to become pure again.

All transgressions of a bodhisattva should be known as belonging to the category of wrongdoing (duṣkṛta). One should confess and eliminate them before a powerful person who can understand the meaning expressed in words, who can perceive and receive, whether of the Lesser or Greater Vehicle. One should first state the name of the matter transgressed and should say: "Elder, may you be mindful!" or "Great worthy! I, named so-and-so, have violated the bodhisattva vinaya dharma." Then, as with the corresponding matter of the transgression, one confesses the wrongdoing. The rest follows the method for bhikṣus' confession and elimination of wrongdoing, and should be spoken in the same way.

If one has committed one of the above pārājika dharmas with severe entanglement and has lost the precept-discipline, one should receive it again. If one has committed one of the above pārājika dharmas with moderate entanglement, one should confess before three or more persons, following the method of confession for the elimination of wrongdoing. One should first state the name of the matter transgressed, and the rest should be known as before. If one has committed one of the above pārājika dharmas with mild entanglement, or any other transgression, one should confess before one person, following the method of confession. The rest should be known as before.

If there is no appropriate person before whom to confess and eliminate the transgression, then the bodhisattva, with a pure aspiration, should arouse the resolve of self-commitment: "I shall certainly guard against the future and will never transgress again." In this way, one emerges from transgression and becomes pure again.

### Section Three: Distinctions of Obtaining and Forfeiting

Briefly, there are two conditions by which one forfeits the bodhisattva's pure precept-discipline: first, abandoning the great vow for unsurpassed perfect bodhi; second, committing a pārājika dharma with severe entanglement.

If bodhisattvas, even though they pass through successive rebirths throughout the ten directions of the world-systems, in every place of birth they do not forfeit the bodhisattva's pure precept-discipline, because they do not abandon the great vow for unsurpassed bodhi, and also do not commit a pārājika dharma with severe entanglement.

If bodhisattvas take rebirth in another life and forget their original recollection, and then encounter a good teacher who, wishing to awaken their recollection of the bodhisattva precepts, causes them to receive them again multiple times, this is not a new reception, nor is it a new obtaining.

### Preface to the Bodhisattva Precept Karmavācanā

The vast ocean, boundless and deep, gathers all rivers that flow toward it as their destination. The dharma-nature is profoundly mysterious, uniting all things in its convergence. Thus it is known that wordless expression of the Way honors the silence of Vimalakīrti, and transcendent listening to the Dharma-rain honors the closed room at Magadha. Without a foundation of vast and far-reaching virtue, who could fathom its profound meaning?

There was the Tripiṭaka Dharma Master, known as Xuanzang. In his youth he transcended the mundane, condensing the spirit-breath of white clouds. In his prime his aspiration roamed in the truth, illuminating the light of wisdom upon the profound and wondrous. Those who drank at his source followed and welcomed him without knowing it; those who bathed in his current swam and dove without fathoming it.

In the year of the Great Turtle's descent into cessation, he laid aside the thumb-ring of youth and arranged the flower-field. In the year of Sudatta's inquiry into the Way, he polished the jewel of the precepts and delighted in the ground of practice.

Then, in the final years of the turbulent Sui dynasty, he first received the three clusters of precepts. Deeply pondering the essential meaning, he realized there were further teachings still to be expounded. Lamenting the confusion in translation of the sacred texts and grieving the errors in transmission of the divine principles, he was therefore able to pass through the Jade Gate on his distant journey and reach the Golden River in pursuit of the wondrous.

There was one known as the Great Treasury of True Dharma, truly named Śīlabhadra. His Way fulfilled the four reliances; his renown spread through the five Indias. Whenever anyone sought to verify the ocean of the Tripiṭaka, they took this person as their authority, and thus the precepts prominently graced his great reputation. Thereupon, at the kingdom of Magadha, Xuanzang respectfully received his staff of instruction, seeing what he had never seen and hearing what he had never heard. Even Sadāprarudita's encounter with Dharmodgata could scarcely surpass this.

He then earnestly requested to receive the bodhisattva precept-discipline. After a full year and three petitions, his earnest wish was finally granted. The Dharma Master considered the bodhisattva's pure precepts to be the constant norm of the One Vehicle, and the grand procedures of conferring and receiving to be the great standard of the ten stages. He especially savored them and never set them aside, keeping them as close as the throat and collar.

In the twenty-third year of Zhenguan of the Great Tang (649 CE), on the first day of the first month of His Majesty's reign over all under heaven, at Dacien Monastery, by imperial decree he translated the karmavācanā precept-text, opening two scrolls. This is the flowing remnant of the Bodhisattvabhūmi.

Mai, though unworthy, was inadvertently placed among the translation staff. He personally received the grand instructions and witnessed this transmission of the flame. Stirred in his heart and moved to words, he offers this modest tribute to the great enterprise, briefly recording the occasion of translation as a reminder against forgetting. The verifiers of meaning, verifiers of text, correctors, and scribes were the Dharma-practitioner Mingyan and some twenty others, each fulfilling their duties, all sharing in the transmitted teaching.
