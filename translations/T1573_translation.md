# 壹輸盧迦論
## Treatise on a Single Verse

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1573

Composed by Nāgārjuna (龍樹). Translated from the Chinese. Later Wei dynasty, translated by Gautama Prajñāruci (瞿曇般若留支).

---

> Substance and self-substance are impermanent;
> Thus substance is without substance.
> Self-substance by nature is without substance;
> Therefore emptiness and impermanence are proclaimed.

Question: For what reason was this single-verse treatise composed? What meaning does it explain, and what persons does it refute?

Answer: It was composed for those readers who develop weariness of mind when reading extensive works, and also for the intelligent who, having already broadly studied immeasurable treatises, develop weariness when contemplating the ocean of meaning in the Tathāgata's Dharma, and who, regarding the non-different meaning of impermanence and emptiness of self-substance, give rise to doubts about different characteristics. To sever these doubts, this treatise was composed. As for what meaning it explains, I shall now state it. All dharmas are impermanent and empty of self-substance; emptiness of self-substance is not separate from impermanence. The self-nature and self-substance of all dharmas are empty; therefore there is nothing permanent. All buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, and śrāvakas attain liberation within the empty dharmas; it is not within the dharmas of annihilationism and eternalism regarding conditioned phenomena that they attain liberation. The verse says:

> "If one negates emptiness and abides in existent substance,
> Then one falls into the eternalist view.
> If one says it is destroyed at a later time,
> Then one falls into the annihilationist view."

For this reason, all dharmas are said to be empty of self-substance, for all buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, śrāvakas, and arhats receive benefit within this meaning. As for what persons it refutes, I shall now state it. If a person who holds to apprehension speaks of impermanence as separate from conditioned phenomena, this is not right view. If impermanence were separate from the conditioned, then impermanence would be permanent, like space. If that were so, there would be no distinction in substance between conditioned and unconditioned. If the conditioned were conjoined with the unconditioned, then because of conjunction with the unconditioned, a pot could not be broken. If the unconditioned were conjoined with the conditioned, then because of conjunction with the conditioned, nirvāṇa could be destroyed. If they were not different, then all dharmas would be indestructible, permanent like nirvāṇa, since they would not be produced from conditions. If conditioned phenomena were not produced from causes and conditions and were not different from space and nirvāṇa, then conditioned dharmas could not be called impermanent. If conditioned phenomena were not produced from causes and conditions yet were impermanent, then space and nirvāṇa could not be called permanent. If that were so, then among the conditioned and the unconditioned there would be no superior dharma. If impermanence, though separate from the conditioned, were still called impermanent, then the conditioned, though separate from the permanent, should be called permanent; but this is not so. If so, in which sūtra is such a statement made? With what meaning is it stated? What you now state, with what meaning do you state it? What you now state is inconsistent in meaning. It cannot be fathomed by your wrong thinking; therefore your statement is not right view. If a person, holding to apprehension, maintains that the self-substance of past, future, and present dharmas is established, know that this person does not hold right view. Why? Because it is the view of production without a cause. If one says that future substances are not produced from causes and conditions and that their self-substance is established, then present dharmas too would not be produced from causes and conditions, with their self-nature-substance being established, since the self-nature of the future and the present is equal and without difference. If their natures are equal, and present existing dharmas are all produced from conditions, then why should future dharmas not be produced from conditions? Do you state this on the basis of a sūtra, or do you state it based on reasoning? If what you state is inconsistent, there is no logical basis, and without a logical basis, it cannot be believed. If future dharmas are not produced from causes and conditions and their self-nature is established, then future dharmas would be like space, without causes and conditions. Being separate from causes and conditions and not produced from causes and conditions, there would be no real future substance. Since there is no future, there is no present and no past either. Since there is no present and no past, the three times are without substance. If there were substance, it would be the eternalist view, since it would be produced without a cause. If a disciple of the Buddha holds the view of apprehension, there is no difference from the outsiders Kapila and the like. This treatise is not for the outsiders Kapila, Ulūka, and others; it was composed for those like you who share their views. As stated above regarding what persons it refutes: it is to cause you who hold to apprehension to sever wrong views that this treatise was composed.

I shall now explain the meaning of the phrases of the eka-śloka verse. The verse says "self-substance by nature is impermanent." "Self-substance" refers to what has production; because there is a dharma, it is called "substance." A person who holds to apprehension mentally grasps this dharma as substance. These dharmas, within the skandhas, dhātus, and āyatanas, have verbal conventions in which they operate, as when one speaks of one substance, two substances, or many substances; just as this person and that person, one, two, or many, each having their own self-substance, hence the term "self-substance." Just as earth, water, fire, and wind each have their own self-nature of solidity, moisture, heat, and motion, each having its own particular characteristic and self-substance, hence the term "self-substance." A person who holds to apprehension maintains that production, abiding, and destruction share the same characteristic. This is untenable, because self-substance by nature is impermanent. "That substance" refers to what arises through the discriminations of a person who holds to apprehension. Therefore, apart from dharmas there is no substance of impermanence, because impermanence pertains to their own characteristics. As the Buddha told the bhikṣus: "All conditioned phenomena are entirely impermanent." Because of this statement, to say that apart from dharmas there is a separate self-characteristic of impermanence is untenable. If you do not understand how it is impermanent, I shall now explain. The verse says "thus substance is without substance." "Substance without substance" means: what you discriminate as impermanence, that impermanence is without substance; therefore "substance is without substance." Self-nature is without substance, hence the expression "without substance." The verse says "self-substance by nature is without substance": apart from non-substance there is no further separate substance, hence "self-substance is without substance." If you suppose that apart from non-substance there is substance, this is untenable, because your doctrine is not stated in any sūtra. If one says that non-substance is itself self-substance, this too is untenable, because it is not stated in any sūtra. In which sūtra of the World-Honored One is such a doctrine stated? In none of the sūtras of the Buddha, the World-Honored One, is there any such meaning. Since it is not stated in the sūtras, it is not established. What is not stated with the meaning of the great sage's sūtras should not be believed. Therefore it is not by mere verbal assertions that one can obtain realization. The verse says "therefore emptiness and impermanence are proclaimed." As in the *Sūtra of Disciplining Samiddhi*, the Buddha told Samiddhi: "The eye is empty, impermanent, not unmoved, not indestructible, not unchanging. Why? Because its nature is thus. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind are likewise." The World-Honored One in this sūtra spoke of emptiness and spoke of impermanence. By this meaning, it is known that all dharmas are empty and impermanent. Impermanence is without substance; therefore all dharmas by nature are without substance, and the meaning of non-substance is established. If one can thus enter the meaning of the sūtras, the meaning is established; if one does not enter the sūtras, the meaning is undermined. Since what I have stated enters the sūtras, the meaning is established. Therefore "by nature without substance": its meaning is established.

*Treatise on a Single Verse*, in one fascicle. Generally, "substance" (體), "nature" (性), "dharma" (法), "existence" (有), and "thing" (物) are different names with the same meaning. Therefore, whether one says substance, nature, dharma, existence, or thing, all are distinctions of existence. The correct pronunciation is *svabhāva*. It has been variously translated as "self-substance-substance," "without-dharma, having-dharma," or "without self-nature, nature."
