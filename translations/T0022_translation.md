# 寂志果經
## 寂志果經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0022

Translated from the Chinese. 東晉 竺曇無蘭譯

---

The Buddha told the King: "Ask whatever you wish; I shall distinguish each matter for you so that the knots in your mind may be loosened. Let me first put a question to the King, and may the King answer according to his understanding. What do you think, great King? Suppose there were a man finely dressed and in service to the King, entertaining himself—yet not content with his dwelling place and native land—who thinks to himself: 'King Ajātaśatru is a person; I too am a person. The King entertains himself with the five pleasures, adorning himself with fine garments, yet is not content with his native land, nor does he cherish his dwelling. I should establish virtue and be free from transgressions and misfortune. How much better if I were to shave off my hair and beard, don the kaṣāya robes, and become a śramaṇa—leaving the household through faith, abandoning home for the path.' He then receives the Dharma precepts, upholds the moral discipline of the path, refraining from killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct, from false speech, divisive speech, harsh words and abuse, from anger, jealousy, and delusion. What would the King think of such a person who comes before the King and says: 'That man who was finely adorned and in service to the King—not content with his dwelling and not cherishing his native land—through the faith of a householder he has abandoned home for the path, guarding body, speech, and mind, not transgressing the various evils, and cultivating the ten wholesome actions'? What would the King do?"

The King addressed the Buddha: "Upon seeing such a person, I would joyfully greet him with proper courtesy and reverential respect, providing robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine for illness."

The Buddha said to the King: "Yet such a person has not yet practiced the great Dharma, nor has he attained the fruit of the path."

The King addressed the Buddha: "Then please present his practice of the Dharma."

The Buddha said to the King: "In this world I am the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly and Completely Enlightened, Endowed with Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, World-Knower, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Persons to be Tamed, Teacher of Gods and Humans—called Buddha, World-Honored One. I then set forth the Dharma: good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end, endowed with meaning, wisdom, and subtlety, expounding the pure practice. If a noble elder's son hears the teaching expounded by the Buddha, he gains faith in the Tathāgata's Dharma and Vinaya, and within it sees great benefit for himself. Within the Buddha's Dharma there is great benefit; he then attains patient acceptance of the Dharma, coming to stillness thought by thought. The household life is crowded and filled with the dust of defilements; the one who leaves home is without impediments. He unifies his mind and rests, removing pleasurable desires, and throughout his entire lifetime upholds the pure practice. He thinks: 'I wish to abandon household wealth, property, gates, rooms, and family; to shave off hair and beard and don the kaṣāya robes; to leave home through faith, following the path, abandoning household attachments and household aspirations; to uphold the two hundred and fifty precepts of a bhikṣu, not transgressing the path's prohibitions.

> He guards the means of liberation, observing deportment and etiquette,
> not losing composure and discipline—casting off all possessions at once,
> dwelling in stillness, reverence, and heedfulness.
> With mind unified and equanimous, he cultivates right conduct.

"'He is far removed from killing, not grasping knife or staff, his mind filled with shame and remorse. He brings security to all, inflicting no terror; his mind is pure, doing harm to none.

"'He is far removed from stealing, abandoning the taking of what is not given, joyfully delighting in generosity, his mind wishing to release and be at peace in all his thoughts, always protecting himself; his mind is pure, not taking what is not given.

"'He is far removed from sexual misconduct, purely cultivating the holy life, devoted to chastity, extinguishing turbid desire; his mind is pure, not deluded by form.

"'He is far removed from false speech, not prizing empty boasting, never resorting to deception, his will fixed in sincerity, dwelling steadily in truth, not violating worldly pledges; his mind is pure, harboring no deceit.

"'He takes no delight in divisive speech, nor in slandering and corrupting virtue, never transmitting such speech to sow discord between others, reconciling disputes and dissolving enmities; his mind is pure, not harboring divisiveness.

"'He is far removed from harsh speech, not fond of abuse, ever restraining himself, never recklessly uttering unwholesome words; his speech is gentle, without rough or coarse expressions, and when heard it brings joy and inspires reverence; his mind is pure, never given to abuse.

"'He is far removed from frivolous speech, his utterances measured and appropriate, causing no harm, deliberate in the principles of right speech, his words calm and peaceful, still and free of error, distinguishing the truth of things; his mind is pure, not disposed to frivolous speech.

"'He is far removed from foolishness, casting ignorance from his heart, not coveting what belongs to others, not seeking out others' faults, for himself and for others always seeking the great stillness; his mind is pure, harboring no delusion.

"'He is far removed from anger and resentment, without a mind of hatred or harm, always cherishing loving-kindness, devoted to skillful means, compassionately protecting all crawling and moving creatures, comforting and reassuring all classes of beings; his mind is pure, harboring no resentment.

"'He is far removed from drowsiness and torpor, his mind accustomed to practicing the contemplation of emptiness, always walking in stillness, never resting at ease in sleep, his aspiration for thought turning toward luminosity, seeking to arise; his mind is pure, not disposed to drowsiness.

"'He is far removed from frivolity, joking, and incoherent talk, his conduct without attachment and without rashness; his mind is pure, not disposed to frivolity.

"'He is far removed from doubt and indecision, his will firmly settled, established in wholesome states; his mind is pure, not disposed to doubt.

"'He is far removed from wrong views; in this life and the next, he believes that generosity brings merit, in filial piety toward parents, in reverence for the worthy and the holy, in upholding the path of goodness, in faith in others, and that after the end of life one will be reborn and attain the path, the six supernormal powers, and the practice of equality; his mind is pure, free of wrong views.

"'He is far removed from flattery, his mind forthright, not harboring cunning deception, not using measures and weights to cheat and defraud others, not engaging in bondage and imprisonment, without defamation or false expectations, seeking only to attain clarity; his mind is pure, not disposed to flattery.

"'He is far removed from entanglement with men and women, not fond of household life with wife and family amidst love and desire, reducing and eliminating uncertainty; his mind is pure, free of vain aspiration.

"'He does not keep slaves, servants, attendants, or concubines, taking no pleasure in managing the household; his mind is pure, not falsely attached to such affairs.

"'He does not keep elephants, horses, cattle, or sheep, taking no pleasure in keeping animals; his mind is pure, not devoted to riding and conveyance.

"'He does not keep chickens, ducks, dogs, or pigs, seeking no benefit from them; his mind is pure, not preoccupied with fowl and swine.

"'He is far removed from lodges and hostels, not adorning rooms and buildings, not keeping fields, residences, gardens, or orchards; his mind is pure, not preoccupied with fields and farmsteads.

"'He is far removed from high and broad seats, not delighting in ornate mattresses and cushioned furnishings; his mind is pure, not reclining in high beds.

"'He is far removed from the seven precious things, not keeping rare gems, casting aside playthings, without vain hopes; his mind is pure, not coveting wealth or beauty.

"'He is far removed from flowers and incense, not delighting in mixed fragrances, not perfuming his body, seeking nothing; his mind is pure, not devoted to flowers and incense.

"'He is far removed from eating at improper times, regulated and always taking but one meal, holding that as his constant limit; his mind is pure, not eating at wrong times.

"'Tilling the ground and planting seeds; in the middle adorning himself and perceiving what conduct is to be practiced; practicing the affairs of a śramaṇa, he purifies his mind, brightening it and dispelling sorrow, removing all defilements and harm, always walking in truth, measured and content with sufficiency, his mind unified in the path. Such a śramaṇa or brahmin is worthy to receive the faithful offerings of food. Whether in any territory, commandery, district, or village, his practice is always in accord with the Dharma; his root is ever pure, and his trunk, joints, branches, leaves, flowers, and fruits are also pure. He is fully endowed with various kinds of purity; such is the character of his kind. In commanderies, districts, and villages, such is the śramaṇa who is far removed from all this.'

"If there is a śramaṇa or brahmin who receives the faithful offerings of food, yet at his place of dwelling acts contrary to what is appropriate—seeking meals through improper reckoning, seeking flowers, garments, and bedding, hoarding precious treasures, seeking such things—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should be far removed from this. If there is a śramaṇa or brahmin who receives the faithful offerings of food, yet sits on high and broad couches adorned with precious materials and whose conduct seeks what is improper—tables and cushioned seats decorated with paintings, embroidered silks, and fine brocades that cause the hair to stand on end—who holds banners and fly-whisks, rides elephants, chariots, and horses, seeks fine food, and is always preoccupied with fame and appearance—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should be far removed from this. If there is a śramaṇa or brahmin who receives the faithful offerings of food, bathing freely in ways contrary to proper conduct—cutting himself off from proper practice, perfuming himself with flowers and incense, seeking offerings not through the proper way, hand-grasping knife and parasol, adorned with pearl necklaces at arms, wrists, neck, and feet, body clad in white pure garments, pruning and cutting trees—living and acting in such ways—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should be far removed from this.

"If there is a śramaṇa or brahmin who receives the faithful offerings of food, adorning his own body and dwelling in improper places with conduct contrary to what is appropriate—staging fights between elephants, cattle, horses, chickens, sheep, dogs, and pigs; stirring up strife among people, men and women, young and old; going to watch battles and large gatherings—practicing such things and seeking such residences—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should be far removed from this. If there is a śramaṇa or brahmin who receives the faithful offerings of food, adorning his own body and dwelling improperly with conduct contrary to what is appropriate—merely going about listening to the sounds of elephants, horses, chariots, people, cattle, and sheep, to drums, music, singing, dancing, and frivolous talk—practicing such things, following the conduct of heterodox ways—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If there is a śramaṇa or brahmin who receives the faithful offerings of food and engages in gambling and games—dwelling contrary to the Dharma with conduct that is not unified—competing and contending with others in various kinds of games—residing contrary to the Dharma and practicing what is improper—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If there is a śramaṇa or brahmin who receives the faithful offerings of food and constantly engages in disputes and verbal contests—conduct contrary to the Dharma, residence contrary to the Dharma, behavior not in accordance: 'This is the Dharma; this is not the Dharma. This is not so. This is one Dharma. Your words are not proper. My practice is proper; yours is not. You have cause and condition; I have none. You went ahead; later you spoke falsely. I have the better of you; you have planted nothing. You practice all manner of evils and will encounter danger. I have attained liberation; you are cast aside and cannot be at ease'—engaging in such contentious and improper speech—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If there is a śramaṇa or brahmin who receives the faithful offerings of food and engages in idle chatter contrary to what is appropriate: 'What is the king doing? What are the bandits doing? What of the battles? What of food and drink? What of clothing? What of men and women, young and old?'—speaking of worldly affairs, incidental matters, and pleasant things—in their various forms, such improper speech—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and speaks falsely of improper matters contrary to conduct—speaking of the king's affairs, the brahmin's affairs, speaking of trees and persons, speaking of national affairs: 'In this place it should be thus; in that country there will be such-and-such; this person should go there; that person should come here'—such phrases and statements contrary to the Dharma—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this.

"If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and practices flattery—conduct contrary to the Dharma and not in accordance, sitting and speaking: 'This will bring gain; this will bring decline'—dealing in trade and commerce—engaging in such flattery and improper conduct—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and studies and practices magical arts, arousing wrong views—speaking of portents of the sun, divining by physiognomy, speaking falsely to seize benefit, studying techniques of various arts and divinatory methods, studying deceptive spells: the Gandharva spell, the Peacock spell, and miscellaneous minor spells—such heterodox arts of deception and confusion in their various forms—such improper arts—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and studies spells of confusion and deception, examining people's facial features and physiognomy, the disasters and changes in constellations, wind, clouds, thunder, and mist, seeking auspicious days—in summer saying: 'This village will receive rain or not receive rain; this land will be auspicious or not auspicious'—speaking of the king's affairs—in their various forms, such improper arts—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and studies various kinds of improper methods—the work of animals—practicing regional medicine, residing wherever he wishes, causing people shortness of breath, vomiting and purging, shedding of tears, disturbance of the blood and vital energy—his will not loyal or upright—speaking deceptive arts and divining for peace and security—in their various forms, such animal work—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food, with conduct contrary to the Dharma—using his mouth to speak of marriages and weddings wherever he resides—'In such-and-such a lodge, such-and-such a house, such-and-such a hall there is a woman with child; in such-and-such a hall there is a place of pleasure; such-and-such has a palace and practices with vigor; such-and-such a hall has no vigor in practice'—speaking of the king's miscellaneous affairs—in their various forms, such animal work—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this.

"If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and practices various kinds of animal conduct—the work of wrong views—divining the physiognomy of pearls, jewels, cattle, horses, households, and knives; observing the physiognomy of men, women, young and old—in their various forms, such work of wrong views—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food, or there is one who practices the root conduct of deceitful and false improper activities—affairs devoid of wisdom, yet considering oneself wise—divining by inquiry and performing talismanic spells—in their various forms, such work of wrong views—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and predicts either good or evil—prophesying that grain will be scarce and expensive or cheap and plentiful, that there will be terror or peace and security, that there will be great plague or widespread death—in their various forms, such work of wrong views—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and says: 'Such-and-such a king will win his battle; such-and-such a king will fare worse; such-and-such a king will travel to observe another country; the king of that country will not be at ease; this one will win; that one will be defeated and ruined; this king has many elephants, horses, six kinds of livestock, and chariots; that king has few elephants, horses, and chariots'—in their various forms, such work of wrong views—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and discusses the regular motions of the sun and moon, the irregular motions of the sun and moon, the regular motions of constellations, the irregular motions of constellations, the slow and fast movements of the sun and moon deviating from their proper courses—predicting that calamities and unusual transformations will occur, that the sun and moon will be eclipsed, that there will be rain, frost, and hail, or that there will be thunder and lightning—in their various forms, such work of wrong views—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. If a śramaṇa or brahmin receives the faithful offerings of food and says: 'The sun and moon move regularly thus for such-and-such a reason; the constellations move regularly because of such-and-such conditions; their irregularity also has such-and-such conditions; there are obstructions, anomalies, and strange phenomena; the sun and moon move westward, or they move eastward; there will be an eclipse, or alternatively why there will be no eclipse; there will be thunder, lightning, and thunderbolts'—always seeking to cite verification and evidence—all śramaṇas and path-practitioners should already be far removed from this. This is what is called noble.


"My disciples, the śramaṇas, uphold in this way the precepts of the noble ones. They practice knowing contentment and sufficiency, taking clothing only to cover the body, food only to fill the mouth. Wherever they travel, robe and bowl accompany them, with no attachments or longings. Like a bird flying through the air, its two wings going with it wherever it flies, so too the bhikkhu, upholding the noble precepts, the mind knowing contentment, harboring no further hopes. Having fully comprehended moderation, his conduct is peaceful and composed; all his actions are calm and deliberate. In looking ahead or looking to the side, he does not lose his proper bearing. In bending and stretching, advancing and stopping, he follows the Dharma as appropriate; in sitting and rising he is graceful and composed, his conduct causing no harm. Upholding these precepts, knowing supreme contentment, the sense-doors calm and composed, the mind settled in a place of peace, the faculties undisturbed, guarding the mind—protecting it so that no thoughts arise on the path—when the eye sees a pleasing form, he does not give rise to thoughts of desiring it as good, cutting off whatever is received through the senses. Practicing the foundations of virtue, the mind abides inwardly, turning away from internal forms, guarding the eye-faculty. Likewise with ear and sound, nose and smell, tongue and taste, body and touch—he does not give rise to thoughts of seeking them, nor does he cling to any of them. Removing all that is unfitting, abandoning and eradicating delusion, cutting off unwholesome dhammas, the mind abides inwardly, protected from disturbance, causing the mind-faculty to become settled.

"This bhikkhu, upholding the noble precepts, knowing supreme contentment, the mind calm and settled, the faculties composed in proper bearing, nothing arising within, his conduct peaceful—dwelling in seclusion and quietude, in mountain forests, withdrawing from crowded places, in rocky caves and remote wilds—the body rests at ease therein, apart from the world without cunning, the mind's thoughts free from conceptualization. Not coveting what belongs to others, not giving rise to delusion, not intruding upon or disturbing others. Constantly practicing loving-kindness, the mind is pure, free from deluded thoughts. Wherever he wanders, the mind clings to nothing; he is joyful, virtuous, and at ease.

"It is like a person who travels far in search of gain, passing through a dangerous road, successfully crossing the treacherous narrows, obtaining abundant profit with nothing lost, able to provide for wife and household, children and relatives; that person thinks to himself: 'My heart is filled with joy.' So too the bhikkhu, having departed far from delusion, the mind is pure, without turbidity or defilement; anger and harmfulness have been removed, and he abides in joy free from taint.

"It is like a person who falls ill and is bedridden, languishing for years in weakness, who later recovers—peaceful and strong, food and drink properly digested; that person thinks to himself: 'I was formerly afflicted and in misery; now I am healed and restored.' So too the bhikkhu, having removed the mind of anger and ill-will, deeply contemplating within himself, the mind also rejoices.

"It is like a person who serves another as a bond-servant, fettered and put to labor, with no rest or leisure, unable to be free; who later obtains release and becomes a free person; that person thinks to himself: 'I formerly belonged to another; now I have been freed and released. My heart also leaps with joy.' The bhikkhu, having removed doubt from the mind, free from hesitation, stands established in purity, elated and rejoicing.

"It is like a person imprisoned and locked in a dungeon, suffering pain and bitter torment, who later obtains release; that person thinks to himself: 'I was formerly shut away in darkness; now I have obtained freedom. How fortunate and precious is this.' So too the bhikkhu, having removed doubt, the mind pure and flawless, joyful; the bhikkhu removes the unwholesome mind, the wholesome mind being free from tainted thoughts, purifying his resolve.

"It is like a person who encounters a famine, in fear and dread of starvation, who obtains relief and safety, the life being saved and sustained; who then encounters abundance, with grain and rice cheaply available, at leisure without fear; that person thinks to himself: 'I was formerly hungry and destitute, in danger and distress beyond words; now I have obtained fullness and security. The mind is also joyful.' So too the bhikkhu, having removed the unwholesome mind, free from the multitude of conceptual formations, reverting to original purity, the mind free from doubt, having removed the five hindrances, the mind-power that had been bound by the dust of defilements now obtaining wisdom, freed from the many afflictions—imprisonment and hunger have departed, sensual desire and the multitude of unwholesome states have departed. With perception and intention, calm and pure, he practices the first dhyāna.

"It is like a person who enters the water to bathe, clean and free from defilement, arriving on the far bank; the mind is also joyful. So too the bhikkhu, dwelling alone in quietude, peaceful and joyful, contemplates everything—the original arising of the body—perceiving that there is no substantial body; observing universally, he finds no fixed ground. The mind becomes calm, joyful and at ease: the first unified mind.

"With this concentration, the mind is pure, calmly abiding without any disturbance; sensual desire has been exhausted, conceptual formations removed; inwardly fulfilling what is contemplated, peaceful and virtuous in conduct: the second unified mind.

"Furthermore, with this body, attaining samādhi, joyful and at ease, with no impediments, contemplating completely, with no bodily kind of being, accomplishing a state of non-attachment, becoming settled in joy. It is like the blue lotus, the white lotus, and the fragrant lotus, born in muddy earth, nourished within the water—though dwelling in the water, their roots, leaves, flowers, and fruits are unattached to the water and unsullied by it. So too the bhikkhu, in this body together with samādhi, is at ease and joyful.

"With this mind of right reception, reaching firm abiding without any disturbance, the mind is purified, free from the dust of desire: the third unified mind.

"With this mind, the body at ease, the mind-intention settled and at ease, without clinging; even with no body, observing universally throughout—a state of nothing whatsoever—no longer rejoicing, at ease and undisturbed. It is like a mountain, complete and without deficiency, vast and boundless: when wind comes from the east it cannot be moved; winds from south, west, and north are likewise. Why is this? Because its foundations below are firm and cannot be shaken. Within flows water cool and lovely, which nothing can pollute—owing to the mountain, the flow is full and complete, spreading universally in all directions, reaching everywhere—owing to the purity of the water. So too the bhikkhu: in this contemplation of the body, with no delight or attachment, his support is peaceful, his conduct complete; contemplating, he sees no body, and thus perceives universally.

"With this mind of peaceful conduct, pure and flawless, firmly abiding without change, having removed sensual desire, practicing a state free from both pain and pleasure: the unified mind of the fourth dhyāna.

"It is like a person on the seventh or eighth day of the month, wearing new garments, face and head radiant and pleased, observing that the body is not bare or unclothed—owing to the desire to wear fine and excellent clothing. So too the bhikkhu: bodily conduct is pure, the mind without defilement, joyful and having attained the crossing-over; practicing without any fixed abode, not seeing a body, observing universally—no fixed place; owing to the purity of mind, there is no multitude of taints.

"It is like a great assembly hall not far from a district or township, where someone within has lit a fire, the light shining equally throughout, neither too high nor too low; wind cannot extinguish it, birds cannot cover it, nor can other creatures obscure the light—it remains firmly fixed and unmoving. So too the bhikkhu: the mind undisturbed, firmly established and unmoving, having obtained emptiness and purity; the bhikkhu, knowing this thoroughly, has attained right reception, the body in complete stillness.

"This body of the four great elements, born from father and mother, upon which the spirit-mind relies—abandoning bodily attachment and delight, always regarding body-mind as something subject to affliction and worthy of dispassion, no longer receiving such a body again; causing the mind to be without form, removing all varieties of bodily existence and all seeds of becoming; without losing the fundamental establishment of body-mind, manifesting and appearing in various bodies, complete in all excellences, lacking nothing.

"It is like pulling out the root and stump of a plant; one who is wise sees and understands: when the root is pulled out, it will not grow again. So too the bhikkhu, understanding this clearly: regarding all that this body contains, he sees that nāmarūpa, composed of the four great elements, born from father and mother, nourished by food and clothing, is covered in falsity and deception—what in it is solid and firm? It is a thing subject to erosion and destruction. The spirit-mind relies upon it; he causes it to remain undisturbed and unshaken. 'I shall establish body-mind, manifest various bodies—without form or mind, yet complete in appearance, the faculties unimpaired; arising from samādhi, I manifest numerous bodies, each complete in appearance.'

"It is like a person removing a serpent from a box; one who is wise, seeing this, understands it to be a box of four serpents. So too the bhikkhu, understanding this clearly: this formed body, dependent upon the warmth of vital heat, composed of the four great elements, born from father and mother, with the spirit-mind relying upon it; establishing body-mind, manifesting various forms—without nāmarūpa, yet complete in appearance, the faculties without deficiency; arising from samādhi, manifesting innumerable bodies.

"It is like a person drawing a sword from its sheath; one who is wise, seeing this, understands: there is a sharp iron sword within the sheath, which is now being drawn out. So too the bhikkhu, understanding this clearly, observing universally the person within—manifesting innumerable forms, complete in all excellences, with nothing lacking or diminished.

"The bhikkhu, through this samādhi and right reception, the mind pure and without flaw or taint, having removed the weariness of defilements, pliant and without desire, firmly established and unmoving—with the wisdom of the supernormal powers, having now attained and realized the wisdom of supernormal cognition; the mind without increase or decrease, conduct equanimous, greatly revered and supremely free, the mind's contemplation without fear. From one body he manifests innumerable bodies; innumerable bodies he causes to return to one; standing alone he manifests transformations with multifold wisdom; he passes through walls and barriers, leaving no trace. Like a bird traveling through empty space, going out where there is no gap, entering where there is no aperture; entering the earth without obstruction, as if entering and emerging from water; walking upon water as upon the ground; in empty space, sitting in the full lotus posture, like a bird among clouds—and in that state, with the awesome spiritual power of sun and moon being vast and far-reaching, he grasps them with his hand and touches them; transforming his body, he ascends to the ninth Brahma heaven.

"Like a skillful and clever potter who tempers his clay, shaping fine forms and figures, molding and fashioning vessels—there is nothing he cannot make. So too the bhikkhu, having obtained supernormal powers, transforming as he pleases, reaching up to the Brahma heavens.

"Like a trainer of elephants and horses who trains them all to become accomplished. So too the bhikkhu, with supernormal transformations, his body reaches the Brahma heavens.

"Like a goldsmith of great craftsmanship who takes refined gold and fashions armlets, necklaces, signet rings, and radiant ornaments—all fashioned according to his desire. So too the bhikkhu, with supernormal transformations, his body reaches the Brahma heavens.

"The bhikkhu, maintaining samādhi and right reception, the mind pure and flawless, attaining realization-wisdom and obtaining the supernormal powers; the mind clinging to nothing; the eye penetrating in its sight, seeing the good and evil destinations of beings above and below in the heavens; the ear penetrating in its hearing, hearing the speech of all devas and humans, as well as the sounds of all crawling and breathing creatures.

"It is like an accomplished and able person who blows a great horn, standing atop a great platform, blowing with all his strength—the sound heard in all four directions. So too the bhikkhu: with the Dharma-ear penetrating in its hearing, the good and evil destinies of all devas and humans—all are heard and known. Having attained the Dharma-realization of supernormal powers, he penetrates universally to know the thoughts of other minds—good and evil, with desire or without desire, with anger or without anger, with loving-kindness or without loving-kindness, with delusion or without delusion, with cleverness or without cleverness, with the dust of defilements or without, having attained Dharma-realization or not yet, disturbed mind or tranquil mind, the diligent or the lazy, merit and wisdom, limited or limitless—all are known completely.

"It is like a great pavilion or tall building not far from a district or township; a person living above it sees countless people coming and going, entering and leaving—one who is wise, seeing them, observes those coming in and going out. So too the bhikkhu: seeing the good and evil, right and wrong thoughts in the minds of others, extending universally to all forms of beings throughout the entire world."

The Buddha said: "The bhikkhu who has obtained supernormal powers recollects past events of innumerable world-ages—minds of wisdom, minds of delusion—seeing one world-age, ten world-ages, one hundred, one thousand, ten thousand, ten million, innumerable world-ages, going and coming in succession, the formation and dissolution of heaven and earth. His original births in other lives—coming from there to be born here; the places where he was, the names of the lands, the castes, names and forms of his past lives, tall or short, beautiful or ugly, virtuous or wicked, dying there to be born here, dying here to be born there—all of these he knows completely.

"It is like a person who goes from this village to another village, sitting, rising, speaking, lying down in silence; then coming back from that village to this one, sitting, rising, speaking, walking—all of this he recognizes and sees. So too the bhikkhu: his knowledge is like the sea; he sees events of innumerable past world-ages."

The Buddha said: "The bhikkhu who has obtained supernormal powers—the mind clear and lucid, the Dharma-eye penetrating in its sight, surpassing ordinary human vision—sees the deaths and rebirths of beings, good and evil, beautiful and ugly, going toward good paths or bad paths. One whose bodily conduct is good, whose speech is good, whose mental intention is good, whose view is correct and free from wrong view: owing to this as its basis, at the end of life he is born in the heavens. One whose bodily conduct is evil, whose speech is evil, whose mental intention is evil: owing to this as its basis, after the body dies, he falls into the evil paths.

"It is like a person dwelling in a tall building, watching passersby coming and going, entering and leaving, sitting, rejoicing and grieving. So too the bhikkhu: having obtained supernormal powers, the Dharma-eye penetrating in its sight, pure and flawless, he sees events of past and future."

The Buddha said: "The bhikkhu who has obtained supernormal powers—all āsravas exhausted, wisdom realizing the three higher knowledges, free from frivolous doubt—understands completely the noble truths of suffering, its arising, its cessation, and the path; having removed the floods and reached the state without floods, free from any deluded mind, he sees the root of everything, the deepest principles without any variation. Seeing and knowing thus, having crossed beyond the āsrava of sensual desire and all āsravas of delusion, the mind is purely liberated; having crossed over and been liberated, the wisdom of liberation is complete, birth-and-death has been cut off, the brahmacarya has been fulfilled, what needed to be done has been accomplished, and the root of nāmarūpa is known."

The Buddha said: "Great King! Such are the present fruits of the śramaṇa's path."

Thereupon King Ajātaśatru of Magadha rose from his seat, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, and confessed and repented his transgressions: "I pray that the World-Honored One will pardon my offenses and faults. I was like a small child—foolish and without wisdom, deluded and having lost my senses, without any skillful means. The Buddha is the Dharma-king, father and mother to all, constantly establishing right Dharma, rescuing the deluded and establishing the Dharma, without anger, harm, or the veil of obscuration. Now, as if reborn anew, I pray that the World-Honored One will receive my surrender of body and life. Seeing my own transgressions and evil, I now again receive admonition and instruction, reforming and amending the past, cultivating compliance with the Dharma for the future."

The Buddha said: "Great King! As your benevolence has spoken, you were truly like a small child—foolish and without wisdom, deluded and without skillful means, taking the life of your own royal father. Now, taking refuge in the Dharma-king, it is as if you have been reborn anew, seeing your own transgressions and faults. In this Dharma and Vinaya, you have obtained wholesome benefit; there is no loss."

Then King Ajātaśatru, palms joined toward the Buddha, said: "I pray that the World-Honored One will accept my offerings, as well as those for the assembly of bhikkhus." At that time the Buddha, in silence, accepted the invitation. The king, knowing his invitation was accepted, his heart leaping with joy, circumambulated the Buddha three times, bowed his head, and withdrew.

When the king had withdrawn, not yet far from the Buddha, he addressed the young man Jīvaka: "You have greatly benefited me; you caused me to come before the Buddha, to receive the instruction of the Dharma and attend upon the World-Honored One, to be freed from my transgressions, causing my heavy wrongdoing to become light."

The Buddha addressed the bhikkhus: "King Ajātaśatru has attained the patience of birth. Though he harmed the Dharma-king, having thoroughly removed flaw and taint, free from all āsravas, established in the Dharma without wavering—there on that very seat he departed from dust and separated from defilement, and the eye of all dharmas arose."

The king returned to his palace, and that very night prepared a hundred varieties of fine and exquisite food and dishes. The next morning he went to where the Buddha was, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, and said to the Buddha: "The time has arrived; may the Honored One graciously condescend." The Buddha then, together with the assembly of the bhikkhu-saṅgha, surrounded by his following, proceeded to the royal palace. When the Buddha and the assembly were seated and settled, the washing water having been offered and completed, the food was then laid out, and the king personally poured and served. When the meal and ablutions were fully concluded, the king took a small seat and sat before the Buddha to listen to the Buddha expound the sūtra.

The king addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I pray you will accept my invitation for one summer retreat. At the Rājagṛha palace I shall provide for all that is needed, and for the assembly of bhikkhus I shall establish five hundred monasteries, so that the twelve hundred fifty persons may have places of rest and repose. The granaries of grain and rice, inner palace and outer—all shall be offered in support."

The Buddha said: "Great King, your joy is wholesome and the gifts offered are complete and sufficient. However, I have already previously accepted the invitation of the elder Sudatta of the kingdom of Śrāvastī for one summer retreat."

The king addressed the Buddha: "That elder from that country has obtained great wholesome benefit, in that the Buddha—Heaven above all heavens—accepted his invitation first."

At that time the Buddha expounded the Dharma for King Ajātaśatru, causing his mind to open and comprehend. The Buddha spoke in verse:

> Those who perform fire sacrifices consider themselves supreme above all;
> The king is the most honored among men; the ocean is the source of all streams;
> Among the constellations the moon shines bright; the sun sheds its light through the day;
> Up and down, beings come and go, serving what they call their lord—
> Yet in the heavens and in the world, the path of the Buddha is the most supremely revered.

When the Buddha had finished expounding the sūtra, King Ajātaśatru, the assembly of bhikkhus, and all the devas and asuras, hearing the sūtra with joy, performed obeisance and departed.
