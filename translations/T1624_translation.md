# 觀所緣緣論
## Treatise Examining the Object-Condition

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1624

Composed by Dignāga (陳那). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘).

---

Those who wish to assert that external forms serve as the object-condition for the five consciousnesses of the eye and so forth either maintain that paramāṇus, being admitted to have real substance, serve as conditions because they can generate consciousness, or maintain that aggregates serve as the object-condition because consciousness, when it arises, bears their appearance. Both positions are unreasonable. Why?

> Paramāṇus, for the five consciousnesses,
> May serve as conditions but not as objects of cognition,
> Since consciousness has no appearance of them,
> Just as with the eye-faculty and the like.

An "object-condition" is that which enables the perceiving consciousness to arise bearing its appearance, and which possesses real substance that allows the perceiving consciousness to depend on it for arising. Paramāṇus of form and so forth, even granting they have real substance and can generate the five consciousnesses, may serve as conditions; however, they are not the objects of cognition, just as the eye-faculty and the like, because the eye consciousness and such have no appearance of them. Thus paramāṇus have no status as objects of cognition for the eye consciousness and the rest.

> Aggregates, for the five consciousnesses,
> May serve as objects of cognition but not as conditions,
> Since their substance is not truly existent,
> Just as the second moon.

Aggregates of form and so forth, having an appearance for the eye consciousness and the rest, may serve as objects of cognition; however, they have no status as conditions. Just as when one's eyes are disturbed and one sees a second moon: since it has no real substance, it cannot generate consciousness. Likewise, aggregates have no status as conditions for the eye consciousness and the rest. Therefore, both external entities, with respect to the object-condition, each lack one of the two requirements, and neither position is tenable.

Some maintain that forms and so forth each have many characteristics, among which one aspect is the domain of direct perception. Therefore the paramāṇus, through mutual assistance, each have one aggregate-characteristic. This characteristic is truly existent and each can generate a consciousness bearing its own appearance. Hence it serves as the object-condition for the five consciousnesses. This too is unreasonable. Why?

> The aggregate, like solidity and such characteristics,
> For the eye consciousness and the rest,
> Serves as a condition but not as an object of cognition,
> Since it is admitted to be the characteristic of a paramāṇu.

Just as solidity and such characteristics, though truly existent, may serve as conditions for the eye consciousness and the rest, they are not the objects of cognition, since the eye consciousness and such bear no appearance of them. The aggregate-characteristics of paramāṇus of form and so forth should logically be the same, since both positions maintain them to be characteristics of paramāṇus. Maintaining that the eye consciousness and the rest perceive the aggregate-characteristics of paramāṇus leads to a further error:

> The cognitions of a pot, a bowl, and so forth
> Should then be indistinguishable.
> It is not that they differ because of different shapes,
> Since different shapes are not truly existent.

In the case of pots, bowls, and other objects of equal size, since the composing paramāṇus are equal in number, the cognitions of them should be indistinguishable. If one argues that the appearances differ because the shapes of those objects differ, this too is unreasonable. Distinctive shapes such as a top and so forth exist only in the conventionally designated object like a pot, not in the paramāṇus. One should not maintain that paramāṇus also have distinct shapes. Why?

> Since paramāṇus are equal in size,
> Shape distinctions exist only in conventional things.
> When those things are analyzed down to the paramāṇu,
> That cognition is certainly abandoned.

It is not the case that the composing paramāṇus of pots, bowls, and the like have distinct shapes and sizes while abandoning the round form of the paramāṇu. Therefore one knows that distinct shapes exist only in the conventional, not in the real. Furthermore, when objects of distinct shape are analyzed down to paramāṇus, the cognition of their shape is certainly abandoned. This is not the case with blue and other qualities: when analyzed down to paramāṇus, that cognition cannot be abandoned. Therefore shape distinctions exist only conventionally, not, like blue and other qualities, in real objects as well. Hence it is thoroughly established that the substance of the object-condition for the five consciousnesses is not external forms and such. Is that object-condition then entirely nonexistent? It is not entirely nonexistent. If so, what then?

> Internal form, appearing as if external,
> Serves as the object-condition for consciousness,
> Since its appearance is admitted to be within consciousness
> And it is able to generate consciousness.

Though external objects do not exist, there is internal form that appears as if external, serving as the object-condition. The eye consciousness and the rest are admitted to arise bearing its appearance and to arise from it, fulfilling both requirements. Since this internal object-appearance is not separate from consciousness, how can it arise simultaneously and serve as the condition for consciousness?

> Because it invariably accompanies,
> Even what is simultaneous can serve as a condition;
> Or the preceding serves as condition for the succeeding,
> By bringing forth that capacity.

Since the object-appearance invariably accompanies the consciousness, although they arise simultaneously, it can serve as the condition of consciousness. Logicians state: if two things are invariably co-present and co-absent, even though they arise simultaneously, there can still be a causal relationship between them. Alternatively, the preceding consciousness-appearance serves as condition for the succeeding consciousness by bringing forth within the root consciousness the capacity to produce an effect resembling itself, which does not violate reason. If the five consciousnesses arise depending solely on internal form, how is it also said that the eye and the other faculties serve as conditions?

> The capacities for form on consciousness
> Are rightly called the five faculties.
> These capacities and the object-forms
> Are, from beginningless time, mutual causes.

Based on the capacity to generate consciousness, one infers the existence of the faculties. These are only capacities, not externally created things. Therefore, the five capacities for form on the root consciousness are rightly called the eye and the other faculties, without inconsistency, since there is no logical difference whether the capacity that generates consciousness is within consciousness or elsewhere. Since external dharmas logically do not exist, one must accept that these capacities exist within consciousness and not elsewhere. These faculty-capacities and the preceding object-forms have been, from beginningless time, mutual causes. That is, when these capacities reach maturation, they generate the five kinds of internal object-forms on the present consciousness. These internal object-forms in turn bring forth the five faculty-capacities on the ripening consciousness. As to whether the two kinds of form, faculties and objects, are one with or different from consciousness, or neither one nor different, one may explain as one prefers. Thus, all consciousnesses having only internal object-appearances as their object-condition is well established in principle.
