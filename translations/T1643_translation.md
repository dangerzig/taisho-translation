# 尼乾子問無我義經
## Sūtra on the Nirgrantha's Question about the Meaning of Non-Self

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1643

Compiled by Aśvaghoṣa (馬鳴). Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Sūryakīrti (日稱).

---

At that time, the Nirgranthas and others, harboring heterodox views and giving rise to doubt, went seeking to the place of one who understood the Mahāyāna. Having arrived, they paid respects, joined their palms reverently, and asked about the meaning of non-self, wishing to dispel their dullness and ignorance. "As the Buddha has taught, please explain it to us. If you say this body has no self, then the supreme self also has no existence. Why then does the body manifestly exhibit weeping, laughing, play, anger, pride, jealousy, slander, and the like? Regarding existence and nonexistence, we cannot reach a definitive understanding. We pray that you, venerable one, will sever our doubts." The one who understood the Mahāyāna addressed the Nirgranthas:

"What you previously maintained as the 'supreme self' is certainly false and deluded. By what is it existent? By what is it nonexistent? In both of these, nothing whatsoever is obtainable. If one takes the hair, nails, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, fat, marrow, intestines, stomach, hands, feet, and all bodily limbs as the supreme self, searching within and without, what can be seen?"

The Nirgranthas addressed the wise one: "Because we have only the fleshly eye, we cannot see it. Others who have the divine eye can see it."

The wise one said: "It also cannot be seen by the divine eye. It is neither manifest form nor shape. Because it is empty by nature, what is there to be seen?"

The Nirgranthas said: "Is it then definitely nonexistent?"

The wise one said: "If one says it is nonexistent, how does one explain the manifest appearances of weeping, laughing, and the like that arise from causes and conditions? Whether one says it exists or says it does not exist, both are wrong and deluded, and neither is the correct principle."

The Nirgranthas said: "If it should be said to be neither existent nor nonexistent, how does this body manifestly have a place of abiding?"

The wise one said: "Not even the slightest mark of abiding is to be seen."

The Nirgranthas said: "If there is no abiding, then it is the same as empty space."

The wise one said: "As you say, it is like space."

The Nirgranthas said: "If so, how should the appearances of weeping, laughing, and the like be dispelled?"

The wise one said: "In this matter there are two aspects: the first is the conventional, the second is the ultimate. According to the conventional, there exist self and other, life-span, person, pudgala, agent, experiencer, wealth, wife and children, relatives, and friends, with all such distinctions. According to the ultimate, there is no self or other, no life-span, and so forth up to relatives and friends; not even the slightest distinction or mark exists. If spoken of conventionally, there is birth and death, good and evil, and karmic retribution. In the ultimate, there is no birth and death, no good and evil, and no karmic retribution. That dharma of true suchness is inherently pure, without afflictions, without defilement, and also without cognition. It is originally quiescent. This is called the inherent nature of true suchness."

To further clarify this meaning, he spoke in verse:

> "The conventional and the ultimate truths,
> These two I shall now explain.
> The conventional is the worldly dharma;
> The ultimate is unsurpassed.
>
> Sentient beings, relying on the conventional,
> Increase their afflictions.
> Long dwelling in the cycle of rebirth,
> They do not understand the ultimate dharma.
>
> Because of relying on the conventional,
> Self and other arise through conceptual imputation.
> Discrimination, doubt, and confusion arise,
> And one undergoes all manner of suffering.
>
> Those foolish, deluded ordinary beings,
> Long oppressed by suffering,
> Do not cultivate the causes of liberation;
> How can they know the principle of release?
>
> The foolish constantly rely
> On worldly dharmas of arising and ceasing.
> Flowing through the five destinies,
> Their continuity is never broken.
>
> By not understanding the ultimate,
> There is no essential for extinguishing suffering.
> They undergo rebirth again and again,
> Like silkworms binding themselves.
>
> Like the sun and moon
> Revolving without rest,
> So too are sentient beings in the three realms of existence,
> Coming and going in just this way.
>
> All formations are impermanent,
> Changing moment by moment.
> Leave behind conventional dharmas
> And seek the truth of the ultimate.
>
> Even those who dwell among the devas,
> Gandharvas, and the like,
> Are not exempt from impermanence:
> These are all conventional fruits.
>
> Yakṣas, ghosts, spirits, and others
> Who attain the accomplishment of vidyā
> Are not exempt from falling into evil destinies:
> These are all conventional fruits.
>
> Indra and the wheel-turning kings,
> Whose karmic rewards are without equal,
> Are not exempt from falling among animals:
> These are all conventional fruits.
>
> The pleasures of the five desires in human and heavenly realms
> Must definitely be relinquished.
> With respect to the mind of bodhi,
> One should always contemplate with wisdom.
>
> The inherent nature is without attachment;
> All is entirely empty.
> Transcending all conceptual elaboration:
> This is the characteristic of the mind of bodhi.
>
> Neither hard nor soft,
> Neither hot nor cold,
> Neither touch nor sensation:
> This is the characteristic of the mind of bodhi.
>
> Neither long nor short,
> Neither round nor square,
> Neither subtle nor coarse:
> This is the characteristic of the mind of bodhi.
>
> Neither white nor red,
> Neither black nor yellow,
> Neither shape nor manifest form:
> This is the characteristic of the mind of bodhi.
>
> Neither form nor radiance,
> Neither movement nor bondage,
> Non-abiding, like space:
> This is the characteristic of the mind of bodhi.
>
> Beyond thought and investigation,
> Not the domain of non-Buddhist paths,
> In accord with prajñā:
> This is the characteristic of the mind of bodhi.
>
> Without likeness, without counterpart,
> Without equal, ever quiescent,
> Its inherent nature is originally still:
> This is the characteristic of the mind of bodhi.
>
> Like gathered foam and floating bubbles,
> Like illusions, transformations, and mirages:
> Without self and impermanent,
> Nothing whatsoever is solid.
>
> This body is like an earthen vessel,
> Empty and illusory yet appearing full.
> Conjoined with the three poisons,
> It is ultimately empty and without anything.
>
> Like the moon amid clouds,
> Vanishing in an instant,
> With the profound prajñā,
> One comprehends the conditioned as illusion.
>
> Sentient beings and the vessel-world,
> All are entirely like a dream.
> By one's own mind's discrimination:
> That mind too is like a dream.
>
> If a person, relying on correct principle,
> Cultivates with wisdom,
> Turning away from all obstructive defilements,
> One swiftly attains the unsurpassed path.
>
> This most excellent prajñā
> Is praised by all buddhas.
> The wise should carefully reflect
> And diligently seek the unsurpassed Dharma.
>
> Leaving behind the faults of the conditioned,
> Realizing the true, eternal, and supreme virtue:
> Through this liberation,
> All is without defilement."
>
> At that time, the assembly of non-Buddhist practitioners,
> Having heard this, were filled with joy.
> Well contemplating and dispelling their doubts,
> They awakened to the wisdom of the Mahāyāna.
