# 拔陂菩薩經
## Sūtra of the Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0419

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

The Buddha was at Rājagṛha in the Bamboo Grove beneath the tree of many birds, together with five hundred monks who were his disciples. All were arhats whose defilements were exhausted, with no remaining fetters; their tasks were fulfilled, their aspirations achieved. They had all laid down the heavy burden, all reached their goals, severed the bonds of continued existence, and been liberated through right insight. They had all passed through the eight liberations, except for one ordinary monk.

At that time the Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla, together with five hundred bodhisattvas, all of them laypeople who had taken the five precepts, came to receive the true Dharma from the Tathāgata. The Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla rose and approached the Buddha, paid respects, and sat to one side. All the bodhisattvas and monks bowed their heads to the ground before the Buddha and each sat to one side.

The Buddha then employed his supernatural power of concentration, causing all the monks in other districts and lands to come and assemble in the Bamboo Grove. They all paid respects to the Buddha. At that time more than a hundred thousand monks had all gathered in the Bamboo Grove. The Buddha again employed his supernatural power of concentration, causing the great elder bhikṣuṇīs and more than thirty thousand nuns to all come and assemble in the Bamboo Grove. They paid respects to the Buddha and each sat to one side.

The Buddha again employed his supernatural power of concentration, causing the Bodhisattva Ratnākara from the great city of Vaiśālī, the Bodhisattva Kāśyapīya from the great city of Campā, the Bodhisattva Nārada from the great city of Vārāṇasī, the Bodhisattva Sucintana from the great city of Kapilavastu, the Great Teacher of Multitudes Bodhisattva and the householder Anāthapiṇḍada from the great city of Śrāvastī, the Bodhisattva Sundarī from the great city of Kauśāmbī, and the Bodhisattva Mālunārjuna from the great city of Sāketa, each with twenty-eight thousand people, to all assemble in the Bamboo Grove. They all bowed their heads to the ground before the Buddha's feet and each sat to one side.

At that time King Ajātaśatru, together with more than a hundred thousand people, came to the Buddha. They all bowed their heads to the ground before the Buddha and each sat to one side.

At that time the Four Heavenly Kings, the Lord of the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, and the Lord of the Brahmā Heaven, with immeasurable hosts of devas, all came to the Buddha. They paid respects and each sat to one side. The devas of the Śuddhāvāsa heavens, in immeasurable numbers, all came to the Buddha, paid respects, and each stood to one side. The four asura kings, with immeasurable hosts of asuras, all came to the Buddha, paid respects, and each stood to one side. The Nāga King Nandopananda, the Nāga King Sāgara, the Nāga King Manasvin, the Nāga King Anavatapta, and the Nāga King Airāvaṇa, together with immeasurable hosts of nāgas, came to the Buddha, paid respects, and each stood to one side.

At that time, throughout the entire trichiliocosm, there was not an empty space without beings present. All the great and marvelous devas, nāgas, spirits, humans, and non-humans filled the space from below up to the Brahmā Heaven.

The Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla then rose from his seat, arranged his robes, placed his right knee on the ground, and with palms joined addressed the Buddha: "I now wish to ask something. May the Buddha explain and resolve my difficulty."

The Buddha replied: "Bhadrapāla! Ask as you wish. The Tathāgata, I, can fully resolve your questions. I know the arising, ceasing, forms, and characteristics of your thoughts."

The Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla then said: "How does one attain the bodhisattva's settled concentration, so that what one hears is like the ocean's vast treasury; so that hearing, one has no remaining doubts; so that one does not forget, generates mindfulness, and does not retreat, nor become lax on the unsurpassed path of supreme, right awakening? How does one follow the Buddha in rebirth, never falling into places devoid of the Dharma, always recognizing the places from which one has been born, and never abandoning one's original vow to see the Tathāgata, so that even in sleep and dreams one does not depart from what is right? How does one attain a form that is always handsome, lovable, and delightful to behold; be born in a noble and wealthy family; always maintain a firm and strong resolve; swiftly master one's studies; never forget what one learns; have a mind that is broad, reflective, steadfast, and pure; be endowed with understanding that is rare and extraordinary; broadly practice loving-kindness; always possess silent realization; encounter manifold spiritual powers; always be diligent without regret?

"How does one constantly expound meaning, always enter the Dharma, always enter cessation, always enter contemplation, always enter meditation, always enter concentration; enter emptiness without grasping at characteristics, and in the formless realm of the Dharma have no fear or dread? How does one always delight in teaching the Dharma and always delight in receiving the Dharma; fulfill one's aspirations in every birth without violating one's original vow; have great power of past virtue, great power of original intent, and strong original conditions; have the five faculties be strong; always be strong in one's actions, strong in restraining evil, and strong in contemplation; and in understanding one's aspirations always be strong?

"How does one become difficult to conquer and immeasurable like the ocean? Like the moon, self-sufficient in pure fullness. Like the sun, gradually dispelling all darkness. Like fire, able to reveal forms. Like space, difficult to pollute, with the mind free from attachment and already empty. Like a bright pearl, perfectly comprehending the true Dharma. Like a stone, dwelling firmly and unmoved. Like roots, difficult to uproot. Like the gentle breeze, the mind free from disturbance. Like a bird that has alighted, the mind subdued. Like a beggar who does not rise. Delighting in remote districts, delighting in mountain valleys. Like a deer or monkey, not being intimate with worldly people.

"How does one who has studied always lead the multitude and guide the assembly, be free from anger toward others, transform all māras, and sequentially liberate according to their understanding? How does one understand all dharmas without needing instruction, abide firmly in meditation on the Dharma of awakening without inclining toward error, have great compassionate power that is difficult to disturb, always think deeply and never depart from right practice, have immeasurable attainments and thoughts, and upon hearing the Dharma weep with joy?

"How does one always be supremely circumspect; thoroughly fulfill all the practices and vows of a buddha; draw near to the multitude who wish to gather immeasurable wholesome thoughts; take up the ocean of pure faith and increase one's vow of purity; sever jealousy and anger; always aspire to illumination; arrive at the inner joy of omniscient radiance; sever the bonds of disbelief through practice and aspiration; be cleansed by the wise; fulfill the immeasurable heavenly aspirational practices; sever all adornments of the world; be purified through good practice and complete in pure precepts; be able to transcend the grounds of disciples and those awakened through conditions; have the mind's aspirations be firm and never waver; accomplish everything one undertakes, always be without artifice in what one seeks; never weary of the wholesome dharma practices among devas and humans; be fully perfected in all bodhisattva practices without sorrow or joy; never deviate or incline toward the wrong among all other schools; always revere and delight in all buddhas; see the radiance of the Dharma without being corrupted or destroyed; and always encounter all buddhas face to face?

"How does one see all dharmas as not-self, like an illusionist? See future dharmas like a transformation-being? See past, future, and present affairs like a person in a dream? Illuminate all worlds with one's form like a radiance? Not go and not stop in the Dharma of dependent arising, like a double sun? Abide without entering the womb in saṃsāra, like a reflection? Already sever the perception of a womb, transcend without grasping at dharmas, abandon and discard the vessel of sorrow?

"How does the bodhisattva's mind become immeasurable, unshakeable by anything in the world, approaching non-attachment, having crossed through all gateways of buddha-lands, understanding all dharmas of skillful dissolution, rising up like a Dharma-vessel, being in the presence of all buddhas, being venerated by all tathāgatas, standing in the Buddha's spiritual power with not a hair stirring, having power like a lion's solitary roar, always being revered by all, satisfying the multitudes of the world without confusion, being known and acknowledged by all buddhas, awakening to timely practice and transformation, abandoning all doubt, with the Dharma receiving no conditions from the other side, illuminating and knowing all three times, having wisdom and eloquence that are inexhaustible, always practicing loving-kindness and attaining compassion, never ceasing to explain the sūtras with illumination and wisdom, never siding with one or another among all people, with body, speech, and mind all arising in uprightness, serving as the world's eye and the light of the three realms, and being such that no one in the three realms can take lightly?

"How does one practice non-self in all that the world possesses, always seeking to stop the revolving of successive existences, practice without seeking the pleasures of the multitude, walk alone in faith and sincere trust, neither grasping nor rejecting the Dharma in all knowledge and exposition, encouraging others to abide in the great gateway of learning, having arrived at fearlessness, skillfully teaching the Tathāgata's true Dharma, always practicing all the sentences and verses, delighting all who come to see, bringing great joy to all the assemblies who gather, cutting the bonds of defilement at the Tathāgata's feet, delighting in skillfully teaching all the buddha-dharmas, delighting in asking questions without hostile or attached thoughts, severing all fear in what one undergoes among the assembly, rising with power in speech and action to turn all things, always being upright in what one takes up and unmoved, helping all the weak and speaking the Dharma seated on the lion's throne, being fully known by all the buddhas, never falling in the world, always teaching and dwelling in right knowledge and always honoring the Dharma, following all the practices of the buddhas, always aspiring to the Dharma without wearying, understanding and patiently instructing the foolish, being skilled in the Tathāgata's good relations without any fixed abode of practice, always practicing well throughout the worlds of the ten directions, abandoning wrongdoing and accomplishing merit for all people, silently increasing daily entrance into the Dharma-gate?

"Like the heavenly Dharma-body that is formless, how does one always gather people inward; never sever the practices of all the buddhas for all bodhisattvas; not abide in the desire to stop; be fully complete in the bodhisattva-vehicle, having donned the armor for all people? Though many are hostile and jealous, seeking to overcome all, how does one repeatedly aspire to the Tathāgata's ground of ten powers, having already departed from all perceptions and knowing everything thoroughly, being skilled at examining and knowing the gathering and dispersing of the world?

"How does one enter into all the practices of birth-and-death, having abandoned all abiding without confusion, having crossed the sea of the Dharma as a treasury of precious treasure, traveling everywhere through the world having left the world far behind, having practiced great transformation with the Buddha's power, perfectly attained and incomparable, gathering the mind in a single aspiration to see the buddhas, having discarded all that the world does not need, being born in different realms wherever the Tathāgata abides, and being a perfectly awakened one free from attachment?

"How does one see the Buddha and his realm from afar, hear his Dharma, see his saṅgha, not through the five supernatural penetrations, not through the sixth penetration? How does the bodhisattva not travel from this world to that, but rather, while still abiding in this world, see the Perfectly Awakened One from afar, hear the illuminated Dharma, accept and practice it all, just as if receiving it face to face, making it correct and equal, so that I have no doubt, and likewise so that all bodhisattvas are without laxity, always seeing the Buddha face to face, hearing the Dharma well, so that even in dreams the mind does not incline toward what is wrong?"

The Buddha then said to the Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla: "Excellent, excellent! Bhadrapāla! These are words of compassion for all beings, seeking their peace, thinking with pity of the three times, seeing their meaning, seeing their joy, seeing their security, and for the sake of humans and devas being able to ask the Tathāgata with such meaning. You yourself have had virtue from the past, having planted many wholesome merits with past buddhas, having waited upon many buddhas, having sat on the seat of Dharma and meaning, having aspired to the Dharma without aspiration for support, having accepted and practiced the holy life, having had few desires and being easily satisfied, having been free from attachment to food, having been complete in joyful precepts, having given rise to all bodhisattvas, always encouraged the completion of bodhisattvas, always aspired to honor bodhisattvas, always aspired to the bodhisattva's great mind, aspired to the bodhisattva's flourishing, aspired to the bodhisattva's attainment, always having great compassion in what you seek, relying on the mind of universal equality toward all, mastering your mind for the sake of immeasurable beings, always seeing the Buddha and fathoming the Tathāgata's words and seeking the Buddha's intent. Like steel that cannot be weighed, knowing and awakening to the practices before all people's minds: this is your virtue.

"Bhadrapāla! I already know your mind fully. Nevertheless, there is a concentration of the presently abiding buddhas, called 'Constantly Dwelling and Abiding.' This concentration is a settled concentration that is firm, that does not fall, and does not lose the mind."

"May the Honored among the Heavenly descend to explain this settled concentration for us, to serve as a guide for our studies, so that many people may find peace. May you have compassion for this world, and for the sake of humans, devas, and all bodhisattvas of great aspiration, may all be able to see this radiance and illuminate themselves."

The Buddha then told the Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla: "Always practice one dharma, always act without weariness, carry it out as described above, ever increasing and never diminishing. Like a completed chariot that one mounts and rides, one may then go wherever one wishes. Already complete in purity, already firm in mindfulness, already complete in steadfastness, through the power of past action, accomplishing all wholesome dharmas, one naturally transcends and surpasses.

"What is this one dharma, the concentration of the presently abiding buddhas called 'Dwelling in Settled Abiding'? It is as follows: through causes and conditions, the mind is directed toward the Buddha; the mind does not incline toward darkness or confusion. Having silently attained and maintained diligent effort, one does not stumble. In the formless, there is encounter with associates. One always cultivates emptiness. Deep slumber is a fierce enemy: keep your distance and avoid gatherings. The body should always remain secluded. Avoid evil acquaintances and do not be intimate with them. Follow good and correct companions on the wholesome Dharma-path. The straight faculties should not be falsely estimated, being close to weariness. Have few desires in food, without aspiring to greed. Prefer Dharma robes. Do not aspire to a long life; follow one's natural lifespan. Let the wise body have no attachment. Do not be concerned with relatives; quickly abandon the land of one's birth.

"Having been intimate with a compassionate mind, having attained a mind of pity, having abided in a mind of joy, having practiced a mind of equanimity, having abandoned all hindrances, having practiced all meditations, having distinguished the perception of form, not grasping the perception of the aggregates, not accepting the sense-gates, not tolerating the great elements, not allowing the mind to become confused in arising, dwelling in non-impurity in what is received, having attained complete relinquishment toward all, directed toward complete liberation of all beings.

"Regarding all people as oneself, regarding all people as not-self. Not grasping at any dharma. Making no aspiration based on precepts. Always cultivating the concentration of aspiration. Having much learning and delight. Not breaching the aggregate of moral discipline. Not being shaken or falling from the aggregate of concentration. Having no remaining doubt regarding the Dharma. Having no dispute with the Buddha. Abandoning nothing of the Dharma. Not slandering the Saṅgha. Having severed coarse and evil speech. Encountering and associating with those on the path. Always keeping far from transgressors. Not delighting in worldly sounds. Always delighting in the sounds that transcend the world. Keeping far from the sounds of animals.

"Having practiced the six firm dharmas. Always practicing the five paths of liberation. Having abandoned the ten evil actions. Having embraced the ten wholesome actions. Being able to free oneself from the nine disturbances. Having completely departed from the eight states of powerlessness. Having always practiced the eight forms of diligence. Having already practiced the nine contemplations. Having attained the eight contemplations of the great person. Not grasping at attachment to any meditation. Not being conceited about one's learning.

"Being appropriately elegant. Lowering the ear to listen. Always valuing the Dharma. Always desiring the Dharma. Having distinguished the perception of form and contemplating the body as having nothing to grasp. Having completely relinquished the perception of others. Though born, not finding it delightful. Having distinguished the perception of the aggregates. Not abiding in what one possesses. Always seeking nirvāṇa. Not aspiring to the practices of birth-and-death. Generating fear toward birth-and-death. Regarding the aggregates as enemies. Regarding the four great elements as snakes. Having contemplated the sense-gates as empty. Having contemplated the three realms as having no abiding place. Seeing nirvāṇa as the sole delight. Having no further use for worldly deeds. Abandoning the world and following the Buddha. Having no disputes with anyone. Having no intimacy with anyone in the world. Always encountering all buddhas face to face. Regarding this body as a dream seen while facing liberation. Always being pure and acting well. Always seeking to discern all perceptions.

"Distinguishing and examining the three firm concentrations. Always directing mindfulness toward all the buddhas. Relying upon and attaching oneself to the roots of wholesome merit. Always thinking and aspiring to all the buddhas. Being self-mastered in the concentration of aspiration. Not aspiring to equal the physical marks of a buddha. Not discriminating among any dharmas. Knowing worldly meaning without disputing it. Being able to follow in proper sequence from what one has received. Following the Tathāgata's abiding ground of benefit. Having attained patient endurance, having entered the Dharma-body that is empty of body. Having already known the human body: the body of non-arising and non-ceasing, the body of nirvāṇa. Always contemplating with the eye of wisdom, already purified.

"All dharmas are not-self. Aspiring to the Buddha's mind without falling or transgressing. Unifying one's practice with all buddhas. Not being mindful of the desire to seek arrival, for it is the immeasurable consciousness that straightens the mind. Not acting for the sake of the other side, but following the Buddha's wisdom. Encountering good companions as if seeing the honored one. Having the same mind toward all bodhisattvas without distinction. Completely overturning everything that Māra has done. Regarding all that the world possesses as an illusion. Regarding all the buddhas as radiance. Seeing the Tathāgata and always practicing the bodhisattva's aspiring mind. Perfecting the pāramitās in complete equality. With a hundred billion sincere faiths, seeing all the buddhas as equal in all wholesome dharmas.

"Bhadrapāla! This is the settled concentration of the presently abiding buddhas standing before one. It is also through this Dharma that the settled concentration becomes complete. What is this settled concentration that brings this Dharma to completion? It is the present. What is the settled concentration of the presently abiding buddhas standing before one?

"Bhadrapāla! If there is a monk, a nun, a layman, or a laywoman who is always complete in moral discipline and always dwells alone, not among the multitude, they should give rise to a thought and say: 'In what direction is the Buddha Amitābha, the one who always teaches the Dharma?' According to what they have heard, they should generate mindfulness, directed toward the west: 'The buddha-land governed by the Tathāgata Amitābha, the Perfectly Awakened One: beyond a hundred thousand billion buddha-realms from here, there is a land called Sukhāvatī, where bodhisattvas assemble and listen to the Honored One teach the Dharma.' With undistracted mind, one should always be mindful of the ground of that land.

"Bhadrapāla! It is like a person who, while lying asleep, dreams of seeing piles of silver or gold and many treasures, friends, relatives, and the dearest of kin. They constantly delight in seeing them without weariness and play together in joyful intimacy. When they awake, they can still recognize what they saw, and in telling others about it, they weep as they recall those images.

"Bhadrapāla! Likewise, a bodhisattva who is a layperson or a renunciant, hearing of the land where the Buddha Amitābha resides, should always direct mindfulness toward that direction, without breach or leakage of the precepts, and without using the aggregate of precepts to disturb the mind. With a purified mind, directing mindfulness for one day and one night up to seven days and seven nights, when the seven days and nights are complete, one will be able to see the Buddha Amitābha. Whether in a dream one sees the Tathāgata Amitābha appear face to face: it is like the man described above, who in his dream thinks he is abiding in space, having no thought of night or day, whose eye-faculty is not obstructed by walls or darkness.

"Bhadrapāla! The bodhisattva is also like this. When the mind is directed and practices in this way, even though there be Mount Sumeru and the surrounding mountain ranges and all dark places between the buddha-lands, they cannot obstruct the vision, and they cannot obstruct the mind. The bodhisattva has not yet attained the divine eye to see the Buddha Amitābha, has not yet attained the divine ear to hear the Buddha Amitābha teach the sūtras, has not yet attained supernatural powers to travel to the Buddha Amitābha's land. The bodhisattva also does not depart from this world and be reborn there; rather, while still abiding in this world, they see the Buddha Amitābha and also hear him teach the Dharma. They accept and practice just as if hearing and receiving face to face.

"Bhadrapāla! It is like a person who hears in the country of Vaiśālī that there is a beautiful woman named Sumanaḥ, and another man hears of a beautiful woman named Āmrapālī, and a third man hears of a beautiful woman named Lotus-Colored. Hearing of them in succession, they are stained and aroused with desire. These men have never seen these beautiful women but only hear of them from afar, and repeatedly giving rise to thoughts and generating mindfulness, lust arises. While sleeping they dream and go to where the women are. In the city of Rājagṛha, all these men, generating such thoughts and thereby seeing and uniting with them, engage in lustful actions. At dawn, upon waking, they recognize it just as if it had been a question asked and answered."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla: "I explain it for you in this way, through causes and conditions, through such Dharma-teaching, leading to non-retrogression and the receipt of a prediction on the path of supreme, right awakening. I further explain: in the distant future, their name shall be Well-Awakened Tathāgata, Free from Attachment, Perfectly Awakened. But those people only obtained their desire through imagined perception, and such is the correct seeing.

"Likewise, Bhadrapāla, abiding in this world, in the land where the Buddha Amitābha resides: having heard and being repeatedly mindful, one then sees the Tathāgata Amitābha appearing before one in the settled concentration of the presently abiding buddhas standing face to face. Seeing, one then asks the Tathāgata a difficult question: 'Through what Dharma-assembly does a bodhisattva come to be born in this world?' The Buddha Amitābha then tells all the bodhisattvas: 'Always be mindful of the Buddha in your thoughts, cultivate well without abandoning this, and always practice as if it were an illusion, and you will come to be born in this buddha-land.'

"What is 'always being mindful of the Buddha'? Being mindful of the Tathāgata's Dharma without forgetting. Now this Tathāgata, Free from Attachment, Perfectly Awakened, has a body with the thirty-two marks of a great person, a body of purple-gold color, a body like a pure bright moon of crystal, a body adorned as if with many jewels, teaching the Dharma alone among the assembly of disciples. Such is the sincere teaching for them.

"Why? Because nothing is destroyed. And what is not destroyed? Earth, water, fire, wind, spirit, deva, Brahmā King: none of these perish. Form, sensation, perception, formations, consciousness: when there is mindfulness of the Tathāgata, the conditions are like space. When space is attained, this is being mindful of the Buddha's thought. The bodhisattva, having always awakened, relinquishes that concentration.

"Bhadrapāla! You should use this to arrive at that place, and having arrived, teach this matter. From having this, through such Dharma-teaching, one may receive a prediction of non-retrogression and attain supreme, right awakening.

"Bhadrapāla! Also you, and Mahākāśyapa, and the Bodhisattva Indradatta, and the deva-son Sucinta, and others who have attained this settled concentration can attest to it."

"Bhadrapāla! Long ago, in the distant past, there was a man in the wilderness of a vast marsh who was extremely hungry and thirsty. In that marsh he lay down, and in a dream he obtained fine food and ate to full satisfaction, and his hunger and thirst were satisfied. Upon waking, his body and mind felt full and satisfied, and he examined this experience. It was like food in a dream. Contemplating it thus, he accepted the prediction and patiently endured within the Buddha's Dharma.

"The bodhisattva is also like this. Whether a householder or a renunciant, hearing the direction where the Buddha resides, one should always generate mindfulness with full intention directed toward that direction, always aspiring to see the Buddha. Do not grasp at the perception of a womb, and do not conceive of one's own body as existing. Always abide in the perception of emptiness. When there is a perception, one should direct mindfulness toward the Buddha. From the perception of emptiness, one then abides. Through being able to generate mindfulness of the Buddha, pure like a lapis lazuli jewel, with the Honored One: through mindfulness like this, one then sees the Tathāgata.

"Bhadrapāla! It is like a person who leaves the country of their birth and goes to another land. After a long time, they think again of the country where they were born and the pleasures they saw there. Lying down, they dream of returning to their homeland and playing there, and their feelings are just as in the places they once visited, enjoying themselves freely in that country. Upon waking, they tell those close to them, their friends, and attendants: 'I went thus, I saw thus, what I arrived at and did was thus.'

"Bhadrapāla! The bodhisattva is also like this. Whether a householder or a renunciant, hearing the direction where the Buddha resides, one should always direct mindfulness toward that direction, always aspiring to see the Buddha. Mindful in this way, the bodhisattva will see the Tathāgata, pure like the Honored One within a lapis lazuli jewel.

"Bhadrapāla! It is like a monk contemplating impurity, who takes a half-decomposed corpse and places it before him. Looking at it, he sees blue-black, decay, and emptiness dissolving like smoke, until he sees only white bones before him. Where did these bones come from? Who placed them there? Who made them? They were all made by the mind.

"Bhadrapāla! The bodhisattva is also like this. The Buddha is not directed elsewhere. Standing in this settled concentration, whichever direction one faces and aspires to see the Buddha, if there is a buddha in that direction, one then sees the Tathāgata's body. Why? Because one relies upon the concentration. And further, because one upholds the Buddha, standing in this concentration through the Buddha's spiritual power, and further through one's own concentration power, and further through the power of past meritorious deeds: these three together enable one to see the Tathāgata.

"Bhadrapāla! It is like a person, still young, who delights in self-adornment, using a clean vessel of sesame oil, a clean vessel of clear water, a newly polished mirror, or a crystal vessel, and looking therein sees their own form appearing in each."

"Bhadrapāla! Would you say that this person's form enters the oil, water, mirror, or crystal vessel? Or is it already within them?"

He replied: "Tathāgata! No. I would say it cannot be found within. The sesame oil, water, mirror, and crystal all show the reflection appearing before them, and it does not come from the light itself, nor does it emerge from the body."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! Bhadrapāla! It is truly so. Because form has been purified and distinguished, the bodhisattva who wishes to see the Buddha easily sees them, and seeing, is able to ask, and upon asking obtains an answer. Hearing this, one generates inner joy, and then further reflects: 'Where do these buddhas come from? Where do I go? They are all without a place of origin. Know that the Tathāgata is without any place of departure. And my own body: I further generate the reflection that it is merely the practice of mind in the three realms. I wish to contemplate the heavens, and the mind immediately sees the heavens. Through the mind one creates the Buddha, and also through the mind one sees. But this is only my mind. The Buddha-tathāgata is only mind, and my body too is mind. Through the mind one sees the Buddha, yet the mind cannot know the mind, the mind cannot see the mind. When the mind has conception, that is without wisdom. When the mind is without conception, that is nirvāṇa.' These dharmas have no solidity. They all arise from what one finds acceptable. What one finds acceptable is entirely empty. Seeking what is acceptable, there is also nothing.

"Bhadrapāla! The bodhisattva abides thus in this concentration.

"Bhadrapāla! There are four dharmas by which a bodhisattva quickly attains this concentration. What are the four? First, having boundless faith. Second, having diligence that none can match. Third, having wisdom that does not follow others' teachings. Fourth, being close to good companions. Bhadrapāla! These are the four dharmas by which a bodhisattva quickly attains this concentration.

"There are four more dharmas by which a bodhisattva quickly attains this concentration: always seeking to see the Buddha, always aspiring to hear the Dharma, being mindful that there is no abiding, and seeking the Buddha's mind without forgetting. By these four dharmas, a bodhisattva quickly attains this concentration.

"There are four more dharmas. What are the four? Not delighting in worldly talk, not delighting in being known by others, not desiring worldly pleasures, and not submitting to sleep, except for the needs of eating, relieving oneself, and walking in meditation. Not seeking to gather the four assemblies or other groups. Always increasing Dharma-giving. Not delighting in beautiful forms or waiting for excessive gain. These are the four dharmas by which a bodhisattva quickly attains this concentration.

"There are four more dharmas for quickly attaining this concentration: always making Buddha images, and further, painting them beautifully; always upholding this settled concentration, and also delighting in the mind's settled abiding for a long time, and also copying it to receive and practice; when arising from this concentration, abandoning what delights and pleases the mind and abiding in the peerless, supremely honored path, free from what delights and pleases; and always maintaining and protecting the Tathāgata's teaching, keeping it true and not forgetting. These are the four dharmas by which a bodhisattva quickly attains this concentration."

The Buddha at that time, understanding this meaning, spoke in verse:

> "Generate faith in the Honored One's instruction;
> do not fear the emptiness the Dharma teaches.
> Practice diligently; sever and cut off sleep.
> For the full three months, one should sit and walk.
> Always teach the Dharma, the Honored One's words.
> Broadly instruct and expound its merit.
> Do not delight in attachment to encounters and associations.
> Free from attachment, one attains this Dharma.
> The purple-gold marks, a hundred blessings:
> always generate mindfulness of the Buddha before one.
> Joyful radiance illuminating all,
> the form supremely beautiful like a golden pearl.
> Buddhas of the past and of the future:
> always be mindful and stand with palms joined.
> And the presently honored among devas and humans:
> with complete reverence, generate mindfulness of the wholesome Dharma.
> Always encounter and associate with the King of Virtue.
> Scatter flowers and incense upon his form.
> Offer food and drink, always with a pure mind.
> Then what difficulty is there in seeking this concentration?
> Drums and music, harps and lyres:
> always make such immeasurable offerings,
> generating joy and immeasurable gladness.
> Always aspire and seek this boundless concentration.
> Always create incomparable images;
> beautifully distinguish all the marks and excellences.
> The golden body: pay homage with purity.
> Then what difficulty is there in aspiring to this concentration?
> Always remembering and practicing the Dharma of mindfulness,
> pure and flawless precepts with joyful aspiration,
> all that gathers together breaks apart and disperses:
> then one attains this concentration before long.
> Do not disturb those with form,
> but practice loving-kindness and fulfill the protective virtues.
> Moreover, contemplate the suffering of desire for what is pleasant:
> then one attains this concentration before long.
> Generate delight in teaching the Dharma,
> attend upon the Honored One, and always pay respects.
> Do not be ornamental; abandon greed and miserliness.
> In the Dharma-teaching, do not be hasty or playful.
> In this way, being well capable of attending,
> the arising and ceasing that the Tathāgata teaches,
> the innumerable buddhas and all their instruction:
> the concentration you seek, do not consider it difficult."

"Bhadrapāla! A bodhisattva should be respectful toward the monk who teaches the Dharma, should always pay respects and treat them as one treats the Honored One. Through this, the settled concentration is advanced.

"Bhadrapāla! If a bodhisattva has a disturbed mind toward the sūtra-teaching monk, harboring jealousy, resentment, or hatred without a pure mind, then, Bhadrapāla, the multitude carries what is without meaning: it is impossible that such a bodhisattva can attain this settled concentration and fully realize the supreme, honored path. This will never be.

"Bhadrapāla! It is like a man with eyes who on a clear night, with no clouds or fog, gazes upward at midnight and sees innumerable stars.

"Bhadrapāla! The bodhisattva is also like this. One who receives and protects the Buddha's Dharma, contemplating the mind, looks to the east and sees innumerable buddhas, immeasurable hundreds, thousands, tens of thousands, hundreds of millions: what one seeks to see is very easy, seen by the focus of the eye. Bhadrapāla! Like the eye of the Tathāgata, the Perfectly Awakened One: omniscient, all-seeing. The bodhisattva is likewise. In this settled concentration of the presently abiding buddhas, through having heard and practiced in completeness, through the completeness of energetic practice, the completeness of the practice of generosity, the completeness of the practice of moral discipline, the completeness of the practice of patient endurance, the completeness of the practice of meditation and concentration, the completeness of the practice of wise concentration, the completeness of the practice of liberation-wisdom, the completeness of the practice of transcendent supreme wisdom."

The Buddha at that time explained this meaning and spoke in verse:

> "With eyes gazing upward to view the sky,
> on a clear night at midnight arising,
> one sees immeasurable billions beyond a thousand leagues.
> Though at dawn the mind's awareness remains,
> just so the bodhisattva attains this concentration
> and sees immeasurable thousands of buddha-lands.
> Arising from concentration, the mind still recognizes them,
> and among the assembly teaches the beauty of those lands.
> The eye of concentration is pure: upon awakening, one sees them.
> With no obstruction, contemplating the ten directions,
> victorious and lovely: this is the eye of awakening.
> This concentration is pure; through it one sees the Honored One.
> The Honored One at all times contemplates the world.
> Contemplating the honored ones of the ten directions and three times:
> poisons severed, purified, free from the mark of a womb.
> May you listen well and study this supreme virtue.
> Also hearing the Dharma, so refreshing and cool,
> may you swiftly be mindful of emptiness, cessation, and the essential.
> I aspire to be together with this Dharma;
> may it bring peace to beings, and may I become a buddha.
> Like the immeasurable forms of bodhisattvas,
> seeing buddha-lands numbering hundreds of millions:
> the bodhisattva thus, having attained this concentration,
> sees the immeasurable forms of the honored ones.
> With the intention to study, practicing loving-kindness broadly,
> hearing from me and upholding it all in practice:
> the bodhisattva thus, attaining this concentration,
> upholds innumerable hundreds of dharmas.
> With faith and humility, generating the thought of love,
> completely abandoning all that the world finds acceptable:
> why not practice Dharma-giving for the world?
> From this one attains the pure ground of cessation."

"Bhadrapāla! A bodhisattva who attains this settled concentration should arise from the concentration and generate diligent effort.

"It is like a ship full of many treasures that has already crossed the great sea and arrives near this shore, but the ship sinks near the bank. At that time the people of Jambudvīpa would all raise their voices in great lamentation, crying and grieving: 'We are all impoverished; the multitude will never see these fine treasures.'

"Bhadrapāla! If this settled concentration can be heard but then fades far away, and no one copies, reads, chants, practices, or seeks its meaning, then the monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen, and all the devas of that land will all raise their voices in great lamentation, crying and grieving: 'We are all made poor and empty! The multitude will never see this Dharma-gift. The world already has this profound and marvelous settled concentration, personally taught by the Buddha, praised by the Buddha, honored and taught by the Buddha. Having heard it, they do not copy it, do not chant and receive it, do not read it, do not practice it, do not abide in it. They delight in hearing ornamental and harmful talk that fills and destroys wisdom. They do not wish to hear it fully, do not seek broad knowledge, and though they hear it, they do not desire it. They also do not delight in receiving this concentration.'

"Bhadrapāla! It is like a foolish person of little wisdom. If someone comes and gives them sandalwood, the foolish person generates the thought of impurity. A wise person then explains to them: 'This is the finest sandalwood, extremely fragrant. Why do you generate the thought of impurity toward it? Why not smell it and perceive its good fragrance? Why not look at it and see its good color?' But the foolish person plugs their nose, not wishing to smell the fragrance, and closes their eyes, not wishing to see its color. When this settled concentration is taught, it is also like this. Those without precepts do not delight in it and keep far away; they do not delight in receiving it, having already become habituated to its absence. The unwise, having painfully lost their wisdom, arrive at meditation and then rely upon clinging to the womb and returning to the womb. Such people, hearing this settled concentration, neither believe it, nor use it, nor follow it, nor generate inner joy. Moreover, they raise their voices and say, making a vow before the assembly: 'How remarkable! Today's scholars of illumination can even do such things! Now in the world there are eminent monks: it is like Ānanda. This sūtra is like a great fabrication! They go off to secluded places among the crowd and teach what is not true. These sūtras are designed to deceive the ears through clever confusion. This is not what the Buddha taught.'

"Bhadrapāla! It is like a merchant who goes to a foolish and benighted person and brings out a supremely fine maṇi pearl to show to the fool. The fool asks the owner: 'How much is this pearl worth?' The owner says: 'This pearl, in the dark, produces light. Fill the space illuminated by its radiance with treasures: that is the price among merchants.' The fool laughs and mocks the pearl's owner, then appraises the maṇi pearl and says to the merchant: 'This pearl is worth one cow. That should be its price.'

"Bhadrapāla! It is also like this. In the future there will be this bodhisattva concentration. If there is a monk who is able to uphold it with firm faith, greatly wishing to study, upright in practice, circumspect in the face of doubt, wishing to seek liberation, with much learning and wisdom, deeply mindful, practicing loving-kindness and attaining compassion, this concentration will freely abide with them, and teaching such a concentration will cause it to endure long in the world.

"But there are fools who surpass others in their worthlessness, who have never practiced, never cultivated merit, who are conceited and greatly jealous, who act for their own gain, who seek worldly fame, who have little learning and fundamentally no good training. Such people, hearing this concentration, neither uphold it, nor believe it, nor practice it, nor delight in receiving it. Moreover, they raise their voices and say: 'How exceedingly strange! These monks have no shame whatsoever! These monks have no understanding! All they hear is clever rhetoric and elegant diction, which they boldly claim the Buddha taught.'

"Bhadrapāla! I have now deliberately spoken at length so that you may clearly understand, as well as the world of devas and humans.

"Bhadrapāla! If someone were to take the weight of the entire trichiliocosm and fill it with the seven treasures and give them to all the buddhas, the tathāgatas, the perfectly awakened ones, yet had not heard this settled concentration; and if there were a bodhisattva who heard this settled concentration and, having heard, further spoke of it: this latter merit alone would be greater."

The Buddha at that time explained this meaning and spoke in verse:

> "All this weight of the trichiliocosm,
> given entirely, filled with treasures,
> aspiring to become a buddha through such practice,
> always in the world, without a second:
> whoever is able to attain this sūtra,
> the wholesome settled concentration honored by the buddhas,
> hearing and believing, receiving and practicing it,
> and the merit and joy of this alone is supremely honored.
> Those with flattering, frivolous minds, always self-assured,
> whose minds incline to evil without a root of stability,
> always seeking the company of bad acquaintances,
> mutually teaching disbelief in this:
> with defilements, without precepts, fully equipped with evil dharmas,
> self-satisfied and firmly established in foolishness,
> they trip over each other, thinking they will be freed,
> boldly corrupting and defeating the supreme teaching.
> 'This sūtra is not what the Buddha taught.
> The Dharma-king also never said such things.'
> They boldly raise their voices in such evil speech,
> their evil deeds fully accomplished, their minds unchecked.
> Yet there will be those who see the Great Hero and Honored One,
> the light of the three times, boundless in its leading,
> and this meaning arises for such people:
> they are all those who uphold and study the Dharma.
> Having heard this profound and marvelous sūtra,
> hearing it, receiving it, generating inner joy:
> such people should have no doubt,
> and need not worry that they will not become buddhas.
> With precepts complete and conduct pure,
> with faith, all upright, free from error,
> their mouths teaching the Dharma like the ocean's flow:
> what I teach is for these worthy ones."
