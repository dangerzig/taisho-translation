# 六趣輪迴經
## Sūtra of Transmigration through the Six Destinies

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0726

Compiled by Bodhisattva Aśvaghoṣa. Song dynasty, translated by Sūryakīrti (日稱) and others

---

> I pay homage to the Honored Ones of the three times.
> What the Samyaksaṃbuddha has taught,
> Always acting for the benefit of others,
> Is the accumulation of all merit.
> Whatever deeds of body, speech, and mind,
> Whether good or evil in their making:
> The resultant fruit is never in error;
> There is no separate creator.
> The most supreme guide,
> Having directly realized, extends compassion,
> Universally for all sentient beings,
> Teaching that retribution follows karma.
> This corresponding, correct principle:
> Having heard it, one should accept it.
> All karma originates from one's own mind;
> Through it one courses through the six destinies.

> Through the three poisons and the fear they bring,
> Trafficking in and slaughtering living creatures,
> Nurturing oneself by killing others:
> One falls into the Saṃjīva hell.
> There the lifespan is a hundred thousand years,
> With added beatings by swords and clubs.
> After dying one is born again
> To suffer such retribution.
> Toward parents and kinfolk
> One brings harm and injury,
> Deceiving with false speech:
> One falls into the Kālasūtra hell.
> Black threads mark the body
> As lumber is sawn in the world.
> The saw blazes with fierce fire;
> Such is the retribution one suffers.
> By setting fire to mountains, rivers,
> Forests, trees, and fields,
> Burning and harming sentient beings:
> One falls into the Tapana hell.
> Flames burn everywhere;
> Cries of anguish never cease.
> Both eyes lose their sight;
> Such is the retribution one suffers.
> Calling the Dharma non-Dharma,
> Slandering without basis,
> Causing others heated distress:
> One falls into the Pratāpana hell.
> These sinful beings
> Are scorched by a great fire,
> Burning without a moment's rest;
> Such is the retribution one suffers.
> Killing pigs, sheep, foxes, rabbits,
> And other kinds of living creatures,
> Slaughtering them beyond count:
> One falls into the Saṃghāta hell.
> Born in that hell,
> One suffers every kind of torment,
> Beaten to death then revived again;
> Such is the retribution one suffers.
> With evil deeds of body, speech, and mind,
> Through slander and creating discord:
> Such sinful beings
> Fall into the Raurava hell.
> Fierce, blazing flames
> Burn without rest;
> One constantly utters great evil cries;
> Such is the retribution one suffers.
> From the Buddha, the Dharma, the Saṃgha,
> And from the poor and destitute,
> If one plunders their property:
> One falls into the Mahāraurava hell.
> Scorched and burned by fire,
> Suffering the most extreme torment,
> One utters great, fierce, evil cries;
> Such is the retribution one suffers.
> Toward parents, teachers, and elders,
> And those of virtue and worth,
> If one commits the gravest killing:
> One will certainly fall into the Avīci hell.
> Blazing, great masses of fire
> Burn thoroughly to the bone and marrow.
> Long is the time of utmost suffering;
> There is certainly never a moment of happiness.

> Initiating enmity and armed conflict,
> With spears and halberds causing mutual harm:
> At death one falls into the Copper Claw hell,
> Where the claws are exceedingly sharp;
> They tear each other apart.
> Or they transform into swords and clubs
> And fiercely hack and stab each other's bodies;
> Such is the retribution one suffers.
> The violent who violate others' bodies
> Later fall into the Iron Thorn hell.
> The thorns are sixteen finger-widths long,
> Cutting flesh and piercing bone.
> There are also great iron women
> With flaming teeth most terrifying,
> Who devour them from crown to heel;
> Such is the retribution one suffers.
> Those who secretly plot to harm others
> Fall into the Forest of Sword Leaves,
> Where mad dogs and hawks and vultures
> Rush and compete to devour them.
> Those who rob others of their wealth
> Suffer the utmost agony,
> Constantly swallowing hot iron pellets
> And having molten copper poured upon them.
> Those who harm innocent beings
> Fall into the Iron Eagle hell,
> Where sharp talons strike and seize;
> Long is the time of suffering.
> Those who love to kill
> The creatures of the waters
> Later fall into the Molten Copper River,
> Cooked by the fire of karma.
> Those who covet success for themselves
> While suppressing others' goodness
> Fall into the Iron Millstone hell,
> Crying out in grief with no salvation.
> Those who bring no benefit to others
> Receive many kinds of forms;
> Two mountains press upon their bodies,
> Crushing and further squeezing.
> Those who teach non-Dharma
> And destroy bridges and causeways:
> Sharp blades line the road
> And they are driven to walk back and forth.
> Those who crush fleas and lice with their nails
> Fall between two ram-headed mountains
> That clash together; they die and are revived,
> Suffering again and again.
> Those who rely on the path of renunciation
> Yet do not guard the precepts
> Fall into the Hot Ash hell,
> Where their limbs all melt away.
> Those who feign deportment
> And make a living through false pretenses
> Fall into the Corpse-and-Excrement hell,
> Where worms and larvae eat their flesh.
> Those who see insects in the five grains
> Yet grind without sorting them out
> Fall into the Iron Mortar hell,
> Where they are constantly pounded.
> Those who delight in others' suffering,
> Who are deceitful and full of doubt,
> Who constantly harbor anger and evil:
> They become attendants of Yama.
> The seeds of suffering's retribution
> Have been briefly distinguished here.
> Purify body, speech, and mind,
> And ultimately depart far from them forever.

*End of the section on the hell destiny.*

> Those who have no joy in giving
> And who steal food and drink from others
> Fall among the great goiter-ghosts,
> Constantly eating filth and excrement.
> Those who obstruct others' generosity
> And cling to their own possessions
> Fall among the needle-mouth ghosts,
> With large bellies, constantly hungry and thirsty.
> Those without heirs who are stingy with wealth,
> Neither giving nor using it themselves,
> Fall among the destitute ghosts,
> Obtaining only others' discarded remnants.
> Those who hope others will give to them
> Yet regret their own small gifts
> Fall among the inferior ghosts,
> Constantly eating saliva and spittle.
> Those who love to hear others' faults
> And spread them with malicious words
> Fall among the flaming-mouth ghosts,
> Suffering long and bitterly.
> Those who love to stir up quarrels
> Without the least compassion
> Fall among the exhausted ghosts,
> Eating worms and crawling creatures.
> Those who threaten and seize others' wealth,
> Then give a small portion after obtaining it,
> Fall among the extremely ugly ghosts,
> Receiving meager offerings through others' fear.
> Those who hold many feasts
> And broadly slaughter living creatures,
> Eating themselves and giving to others,
> Later fall as rākṣasa ghosts.
> Those who smell or secretly take
> Flowers and incense meant for offerings,
> Through a small arising of greed,
> Later become gandharva ghosts.
> Those whom others pay court to
> Who have demands upon themselves,
> Yet show angry faces while coveting their wealth,
> Later become fierce and evil ghosts.
> Those who in others' marriages
> Always love to serve as matchmaker,
> Then harbor evil and cause separation,
> At death become bhūta ghosts.
> Those who love to drink wine,
> Drinking excessively and becoming disorderly,
> Or who take it and urge it on others,
> Later become yakṣa ghosts.
> Toward parents, teachers, and elders,
> Constantly violating their wishes:
> One is later born in the yakṣa palace,
> Courageous yet impulsive and violent.
> The faults of stinginess and greed
> Mean one is always born among hungry ghosts.
> Suffering and pleasure follow one's own cause;
> Therefore do not create them again.

*End of the section on the hungry ghost destiny.*

> Humans, devas, and the three evil paths:
> Only oneself can rescue oneself.
> Rushing through the six destinies,
> It is like a dream combining and dispersing.
> Observe one's own and others' kin:
> Lament! How can it last long?
> Like an actor on a stage,
> Changing form and color again and again.
> Having finished suffering in the hells,
> One may be born among the heavens.
> When merit is exhausted one sinks again,
> Falling into the animal realm.
> Receiving various forms and colors,
> Later born in the human world:
> Poverty, suffering, and extreme hardship,
> Like a carriage wheel turning round.

*End of the section on the animal destiny.*

> The lifespan of those in the human realm
> Was originally long in measure,
> But much killing creates the cause
> By which it is diminished.
> Entangled in various sufferings and illnesses,
> Leprosy, wasting diseases, and epidemics,
> Possessed by ghosts and demons,
> And beaten by the king's law.
> Those who toil broadly
> In pursuit of wealth and profit,
> Without the least charitable heart,
> Later become wealth-guarding ghosts.
> Those who steal others' wealth
> And use it, sometimes giving some away,
> Later fall among the ghost destinies,
> Losing whatever they obtain.
> Those who from their own wealth
> Give according to their means
> Will obtain abundance,
> Unharmed by others.
> Those who with pure wealth
> Give broadly without miserliness
> Obtain the finest food and drink;
> All their wishes are fulfilled.
> Those who with precious delicacies
> Give with a pure mind:
> The merit they receive
> Is good complexion, strength, long life, and peace.
> If one gives clothing,
> One is born with love and joy,
> Obtaining a beautiful and dignified form,
> Complete with garments of modesty.
> If one builds monasteries
> And joyfully gives them as offerings,
> One obtains splendidly adorned palaces
> With the five pleasures complete.
> If one builds bridges and causeways
> And gives carriages and such,
> One obtains the most supreme comfort:
> Precious jeweled palanquins.
> If in the wilderness
> One gives ponds, wells, and springs,
> In every place of birth
> One is free from thirst and the anguish of heat.
> If one gives concubines
> Beautifully adorned as offerings,
> The retribution one receives
> Is wealth, nobility, and the fullness of desire.
> If one gives sūtras and teachings
> And secular texts and classics
> And can transmit them to others,
> One obtains broad learning and great wisdom.
> If one gives medicine
> And the gift of fearlessness,
> The retribution one receives
> Is happiness and freedom from fear.
> If one gives lamps, one's eyes are always clear.
> If one gives music, one obtains a beautiful voice.
> If one gives bedding, one obtains ease and comfort.
> If one gives servants, one is always surrounded by attendants.
> Giving milk cows and such, one obtains complexion, strength, and long life.
> If one gives fine fields, one obtains overflowing granaries.
> Whatever each seeker desires,
> Whether flowers, fruit, or clear springs:
> Give with kind words and gentle comfort.
> Yet there are those who, though they give,
> Compel others and give but little,
> Or whose generosity arises from fear,
> Or who hope for others' praise,
> Or who seek present wealth and honor
> And heavenly rebirth with its pleasures:
> Such acts of giving
> Yield extremely meager merit.
> If one joyfully benefits others,
> Not sparing even one's own life,
> Always harboring a compassionate mind:
> The holy fruit is not hard to attain.
> To all who come to beg,
> Give according to the time;
> Make them joyful,
> And the fruit one obtains will be as one wishes.
> If giving to others is difficult,
> The retribution received is likewise so.
> Setting aside one's own to benefit others
> Is the most supreme dharma of happiness.
> Giving is the root of all happiness,
> Like a seed producing its fruit.
> Have compassion for those who come in need;
> Give to them and let none go away empty.
> Be careful not to violate others' bodies;
> Regard them as one's own children.
> Even with one's own spouse,
> The moment desire arises, restrain it.
> If a person in the realm of desire
> Binds the mind with attachment and delight,
> In a future birth in this world
> One will certainly receive a female body.
> If a person wearies of the female body,
> Abandons desire, and upholds the pure precepts,
> Generating a firm and resolute mind,
> One transforms into a male body.
> If a person cultivates the holy life,
> There will be no harm or affliction.
> Endowed with merit, virtue, and spiritual power,
> Devas and humans constantly pay respect.
> If a person does not drink wine
> And abides in right mindfulness,
> Always speaking words of truth,
> One presently obtains safety and peace.
> If one sees others in conflict
> And persuades them to harmony and joy,
> One obtains an extensive retinue
> Without the suffering of separation.
> Toward the instructions of teachers and elders,
> Always receiving them with joy,
> Weighing carefully gain and loss:
> One is endowed with skillful wisdom.
> The poor and lowly who toil as servants,
> If one delights in beating them,
> Afflicting the innocent:
> One's own suffering only increases.
> If a person, possessing fine appearance,
> Relies on it and becomes arrogant,
> Mocking and trifling with others:
> One will obtain a dwarfed and hunched body.
> If a person is stingy by nature,
> Feigning deafness to kind entreaty,
> Such extremely heavy delusion
> Will bring the retribution of deafness and muteness.
> Doing good brings lasting blessings;
> Accumulating evil brings suffering.
> Each produces its corresponding cause;
> Following one's karma, one will certainly receive it.

*End of the section on the human destiny.*

> Always engaging in deceit,
> Delighting in anger, fighting, and quarrels:
> Because of past generosity,
> One becomes a lord of the asuras.

*End of the section on the asura destiny.*

> Joyfully cultivating the ten wholesome deeds
> And causing no harm to others:
> The devas constantly protect one,
> And one is born in the Cāturmahārājika heaven.
> Toward parents and the Three Treasures,
> Offering respectfully according to one's ability,
> Complete in patience and gentleness:
> One is born in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven.
> Not delighting in anger or quarrels,
> Encouraging others toward harmony,
> Purely cultivating the cause of goodness:
> One is born in the Yāma heaven.
> Delighting in learning the right Dharma,
> Specially cultivating the wisdom of liberation,
> Rejoicing in praising others' merit:
> One is born in the Tuṣita heaven.
> In the practices of giving and morality,
> Naturally finding joy,
> Arising with vigorous courage:
> One is born in the Nirmāṇarati heaven.
> These are the highest of beings,
> Complete in the conduct of śramaṇas and brahmins,
> Increasing the cause of liberation:
> They are born in the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven.
> Through the cultivation of precepts and concentration,
> Universally aided by the power of vows,
> Born in the heavens or among humans,
> One realizes the true and real.
> Thus the retribution of good and evil
> Has been clearly displayed.
> Doing good leads to human and heavenly birth;
> Doing evil brings the most extreme suffering.
> Before old age, sickness, and death arrive,
> Diligently reflect and seek the right Dharma.
> When one's own retribution comes,
> All that one loves will be parted from.
> The faults of greed and the rest
> Should deeply engender weariness and revulsion.
> The wise should reflect well;
> Therefore depart far from them.
> If one always acts for others' benefit,
> There will be no obstructions or afflictions.
> Sin and merit are never in error.
> This has been briefly spoken; listen carefully.
