# 海八德經
## 海八德經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0035

Translated from the Chinese. 後秦 鳩摩羅什譯

---

## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra on the Eight Virtues of the Ocean, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the Land Without Superior. At a riverside, it was his regular custom on the fifteenth day to expound the precept sūtra to the assembly of śramaṇas. The Buddha sat in stillness, long silent. Ānanda adjusted his robes, knelt upright with reverence, and addressed him: "The śramaṇas are seated in composure, joyfully awaiting the pure Dharma." The World-Honored One remained silent. Three times Ānanda rose and addressed him: "The night is already half-gone; please expound the precept sūtra." The World-Honored One then said: "Among the śramaṇas there are those who are impure and defiled — their minds are deviant, their conduct violates the Dharma, and their words contradict the precepts of the śramaṇa. The solemn power of these precepts is profound; those who are base and vile are incapable of upholding them. Since the pure and the impure are in conflict, I will not expound them."

The venerable Maudgalyāyana entered samādhi with one-pointed mind and, with the pure vision of the dharma-eye, clearly perceived that one among them harbored conduct worthy of expulsion. Maudgalyāyana immediately addressed that person, saying: "Rise! This is not a place where a worldling such as you should sit." The person refused to rise, so Maudgalyāyana took him by the arm and led him out, saying: "You possess no supreme virtue; your mind harbors the six defilements. How dare you, with a body foul and corrupt, sit upon a seat fragrant as heaven? You are a cast-off person, not a śramaṇa." Maudgalyāyana then returned and took his place among the pure assembly.

The Buddha said to Maudgalyāyana: "My son, why this single clumsiness? You could have used a well-chosen analogy, yet instead you took him by the arm and led him away." The Buddha then addressed the śramaṇas: "Listen quietly to my words." The śramaṇas replied: "Yes, we receive your teaching."

"Observe the great ocean: it possesses eight beautiful virtues.

"Its breadth is vast and boundless; its depth is unfathomable. As one enters ever deeper, deeper still it becomes, with no obstruction before one. This is the first virtue.

"The tides of the ocean never exceed their appointed times. This is the second virtue.

"The ocean contains all manner of treasures, encompassing everything without omission. Yet rotting, foul corpses the ocean does not retain; divine winds blow them adrift to the shore's edge. This is the third virtue.

"The ocean holds within it all precious things: gold and silver, lapis lazuli and crystal, coral and dragon rubies, divine moon-pearls — a thousand wonders and ten thousand rarities, whatever one seeks can be found. This is the fourth virtue.

"Under all heaven there are five great rivers that flow and pour into the ocean. The one flowing west is named Gaṅgā; the one flowing south is named Yamunā; the two flowing east are: one named Sarabhū, one named Ajiravatī; the one flowing north is named Mahī. The five rivers, having completed their journeys, all enter the ocean; all shed their former names and together become the ocean. This is the fifth virtue.

"The five rivers and ten thousand streams — through all seasons of rain, since the very formation of heaven and earth, rain falls and rivers pour in — yet the ocean remains as before, without increase or decrease. This is the sixth virtue.

"The ocean contains many fish of magnificent size. The body of the first fish is four thousand li in length; the second, eight thousand li; the third, twelve thousand li; the fourth, sixteen thousand li; the fifth, twenty thousand li; the sixth, twenty-four thousand li; the seventh, twenty-eight thousand li. This is the seventh virtue.

"The waters of the ocean are uniformly salty — at the edges and in the center alike. This is the eighth virtue.

"It is for these reasons that the pure and luminous nāgas rejoice in the ocean with gladness of heart.

"My wondrous sūtras likewise possess resplendent virtues. In reading them there is no exhaustion; their meaning deepens day by day. Brahmā, Māra, and Śakra are none of them able to fathom their depth — just as the ocean is vast and its depths hard to plumb. For this reason, the śramaṇas delight in them. This is the first virtue.

"My disciples mutually hold one another to account: reciting sūtras and practicing seated meditation, observing rites and proper forms, never losing their proper times — just as the ocean's tides never exceed their appointed times. This is the second virtue.

"My Dharma is pure and clean; its aim is tranquility and non-attachment. With robes and food adequately provided, nothing further is hoarded. If there is a śramaṇa whose aims are impure and defiled, he is censured and expelled by the Dharma, not permitted to dwell in the monastery — just as the vast ocean does not retain foul corpses. This is the third virtue.

"In my way and its many sūtras, all meanings are thoroughly encompassed. When a śramaṇa contemplates deeply, he refines away the defilements of the mind — greed, lust, anger, jealousy, delusion, and all manner of impurities — like polishing a mirror until all tarnish is removed, then sweeping away even the faintest cloudiness until all is illuminated and nothing is left unseen.

> In a single sitting of self-reflection, contemplating the source of birth and death throughout all past ages, there is nothing one does not come to know.
> Second, contemplating that the ten thousand things of heaven and earth are like illusions — whatever has come together must in time be parted.
> Third, constantly maintaining a compassionate heart, pitying the world's delusion, its inverted conduct, and its failure to recognize its own error.
> Fourth, through refined self-contemplation, having already known all past ages, illuminating also what is yet to come — the destinations toward which the spirits and souls of sentient beings are directed.

"In my way, a pure mind is the precious jewel. A śramaṇa who removes impurity and attains pure conduct rejoices in his heart — just as those pure and luminous ones delight in the ocean's many treasures. This is the fourth virtue.

"My way is vast and great, uniting the many into one. Those of royal lineage, brāhmaṇa lineage, noble lineage, and base lineage — all who come to become śramaṇas abandon their original surnames. Through the Dharma they are bound to one another; the learned and the ignorant advance together, their hearts as if brothers — just as the many streams converge and are called the ocean. For this reason, śramaṇas delight in it. This is the sixth virtue.

"My way is subtle and wondrous; its scriptures are deep and profound. Superior persons who attain it are designated in four ways: the first is called Stream-Enterer; the second, Once-Returner; the third, Non-Returner; the fourth, Worthy One. The way of the Worthy One: his mind is pure as a heavenly luminous pearl, with virtue concealed within apparent defilement. He can manifest multiple bodies and scatter his form at will; he is free over existence and cessation; his lifespan is without limit; he neither ages nor falls ill — like the great dragon-fish of the vast ocean. For this reason, śramaṇas delight in it.

"My scriptures, their meanings beautiful as amṛta, are unheard even by immortals and sages, rarely beheld by Brahmā and Śakra. From the ancient past to the present, there is nothing they do not record; at the margins and at the center, all is correct — just as the ocean is uniformly salty. For this reason also, śramaṇas delight in them.

"Those who read my sūtras all turn their minds toward the unconditioned. The ocean has eight virtues; my sūtras likewise possess them."

Ānanda rose again, bowed his head to the ground, and addressed him: "Dawn is approaching in the east; we beg that you expound the prātimokṣa."

The World-Honored One said: "From this day forward, I will no longer personally expound the prātimokṣa sūtra. When the precepts are not followed, I fear the wrathful power of divine thunder may descend upon those who transgress. For this reason, I will not expound the precept sūtra. From now on, hold one another mutually to account, and on the fifteenth day, gather together to expound the precept sūtra."

All the bhikṣus rose and made obeisance to the Buddha.

---

Upon examination, this sūtra's language is decidedly not a translation by Kumārajīva; it appears to be a text from the Later Han dynasty. It is suspected to be a translation by Zhu Falan, one of whose originals has been lost, and that it was erroneously attributed to Kumārajīva in the Canon.
