# 德光太子經
## Sūtra of Prince Guṇaprabha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0170

Translated during the Western Jin dynasty by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護).

---

## Fascicle One

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at Rājagṛha on Vulture Peak Mountain, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus and five hundred bodhisattvas.

At that time, the Venerable Rāṣṭrapāla had been residing at Śrāvastī. Having completed the three months of the summer retreat, he renewed his robes and bowl, donned his garments, and together with a hundred newly ordained bhikṣus, having accomplished what was to be done, traveled through the various lands and proceeded to the great city of Rājagṛha and Vulture Peak Mountain.

Thereupon the Venerable Rāṣṭrapāla went to where the Buddha was, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, and stood to one side. Rāṣṭrapāla addressed the World-Honored One, saying: "What practices should a bodhisattva-mahāsattva undertake to attain the dharma of all extraordinary merits, to achieve wisdom that is unshakable and fearless, and knowledge that is transcendent; to dispatch eloquence whose radiance illumines everything, to enter sarvajñā and instruct sentient beings; to enable them to attain liberation and sever all doubts, and through skillful means to demonstrate sarvajñā; to have words and deeds in accord, and when questioning the buddhas, always through skillful means to grasp the buddhas' intent; and to be able to retain all dharmas heard and swiftly attain sarvajñā?"

At that time, the Venerable Rāṣṭrapāla praised with verses and questioned the Buddha, saying in gāthā:

> "How does a bodhisattva fulfill all practices? What constitutes action that is true and certain?
> Complete in wisdom, merit, and vows: may the Honored One among humans explain this.
> With a body of finest purple-gold, the Honored One among humans has accumulated supreme virtue.
> Saving and protecting all sentient beings: may the Buddha explain the unsurpassed practice.
> How does one attain inexhaustible wisdom, immeasurable dhāraṇī, and the supreme path of awakening?
> How does one achieve the practice of equanimity? Resolving the doubts of all people,
> For countless koṭis of kalpas delighting in birth-and-death, one's mind never becoming defiled or weary.
> Having seen innumerable beings in suffering, through skillful means one instructs and enlightens them,
> Purifying one's buddha-land, with retinue complete, with radiance, longevity, and assembly likewise.
> All that is spoken leads to quiescence: may the World-Honored One explain the supreme practice.
> Vanquishing Māra and his retinue, severing all views, liberated from desire, transcending perception and formations.
> How does one expound the meaning of the sūtras? May the Buddha explain all true practices.
> With beauty, distinction, and complete eloquence, speaking to all people with a gentle voice,
> Satisfying the world like timely rain: may the Buddha explain all practices of awakening.
> What is spoken is subtle like the kalaviṅka bird, with Brahmā's voice, free of doubt, a sound of clear wisdom.
> The assembly thirsts for the sūtras and Dharma; with sweet dew one satisfies them all.
> If any wish to study the honored Buddha-path, they should be diligent and devoted to the Dharma-practice.
> What the Tathāgata teaches is entirely equal: may the Dharma King teach at the proper time.
> I wish to hear of the true and correct path; the Buddha, God among Gods, knows my mind.
> Now I dare not disturb the World-Honored One; may you kindly explain the unsurpassed practice."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "Excellent, excellent! That you should ask the Tathāgata about such a matter as this. Out of great compassion and for the peace and security of many, pitying the gods and worldly people, you make this offering for the bodhisattvas of the future, enabling them to guard their practice. Rāṣṭrapāla! Listen carefully and reflect well upon it! I shall explain for you."

Rāṣṭrapāla said: "So be it, World-Honored One! I joyfully wish to hear."

The Buddha said: "Rāṣṭrapāla! A bodhisattva has four dharmas by which one attains pure practice. What are the four? First, practicing with an impartial mind, free from flattery. Second, maintaining equanimity toward all. Third, comprehending the practice of emptiness. Fourth, acting in accord with one's words. These are the four dharmas by which a bodhisattva swiftly attains pure practice."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva further has four dharmas by which one attains peaceful encouragement and progress. What are the four? First, attaining dhāraṇī. Second, finding good spiritual friends. Third, attaining dharma-patience. Fourth, being pure in morality with equal practice. These are the four dharmas."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva further has four dharmas for entering into the afflictions and delighting in the dharma of birth-and-death. What are the four? First, the bodhisattva manifests a buddha-body entering into birth-and-death, encouraging those subject to arising and ceasing, enabling them to attain the dharma of delight. Second, teaching gentle and compliant dharma. Third, being without attachment to any possession. Fourth, attaining the patience of non-arising dharmas. These are the four dharmas."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva further has four dharmas of non-attachment. What are the four? First, a bodhisattva should not be attached to household dwellings. Second, a renunciant bodhisattva should not covet material gain. Third, a bodhisattva should not seek reward for meritorious deeds. Fourth, a bodhisattva should not cherish one's own body and life. These are the four dharmas."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva further has four dharmas of being insatiable in the Dharma. What are the four? First, in morality, having no deficiency. Second, cultivating the practice of dwelling in seclusion and the wilderness. Third, following the four noble practices. Fourth, attaining vast learning. These are the four dharmas."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva has four dharmas by which one attains the state of no-thought with universal access. What are the four? First, being reborn in good destinations, always encountering a buddha's era. Second, receiving the teachings of elders without flattery. Third, joyfully accepting instruction with a mind unattached to material gain. Fourth, attaining eloquence that penetrates the essentials of the profound Dharma. These are the four dharmas."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva has four dharmas by which one attains pure practice. What are the four? First, in practicing the bodhisattva path, having no intention to harm others. Second, abandoning flattery and false conduct, and delighting in seclusion. Third, giving all one's possessions without reluctance and without expecting repayment. Fourth, day and night constantly seeking the Dharma, and when encountering a Dharma teacher, not looking for their faults. These are the four dharmas by which a bodhisattva-mahāsattva attains pure practice."

At that time the Buddha spoke in gāthā:

> "When the mind does not cling to defiling dharmas, then there is no evil blemish.
> When the will is not weary of instructing in the Dharma, then one can attain the unsurpassed path.
> Though encountering the unvirtuous, one remains single-minded, universally entering the roots of wrong paths and evil destinies.
> Leaving home to study the Way without reluctance, dwelling in the mountains seeking liberation.
> In seclusion and stillness, with nothing arising, the mind not clinging to wealth, gain, or form,
> Casting aside the body without cherishing life, acting like a lion, fearless.
> The mind finds delight and knows contentment, like a bird in flight without fear.
> All things in the world are impermanent; one aspires to the Buddha-path and the practice of great wisdom.
> Always delighting in solitude like a rhinoceros, fearless like a lion,
> The mind without terror or coarseness; whether receiving offerings or not, neither increasing nor diminishing.
> Casting away wrong speech and evil views, with wisdom one comprehends the great practice and resolves upon the Way.
> 'I shall be a protector for all the world'; the mind is set on skillful means, without heedlessness.
> The mind upholds morality as the path for all; the heart does not cling to attachments and affections.
> Carefully following right practice as if extinguishing a fire, constantly seeking the World-Honored One's supreme and wondrous practice.
> Already liberated in emptiness, free from perception, complete in all respects, truly quiescent,
> Dwelling in stillness with the radiance of wisdom, tasting the sweet dew, constantly joyful.
> If one attains the mind of the Buddha's awakening, always being pure and free of doubt,
> With dhāraṇī and eloquence, single-minded, enduring all suffering without thought of repayment.
> If any bodhisattva hears of this practice and wishes to seek the Buddha-path, one should rejoice,
> Always aspiring with diligence, abandoning laziness, comprehending defilement and ignorance, the mind doing no harm."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva has four dharmas that cause one's own downfall. What are the four? First, a bodhisattva who is arrogant and disrespectful causes one's own downfall. Second, a bodhisattva who is ungrateful and practices flattery causes one's own downfall. Third, a bodhisattva who seeks offerings and covets gain causes one's own downfall. Fourth, a bodhisattva who engages in deceitful and crooked conduct to seek offerings causes one's own downfall. These are the four dharmas by which a bodhisattva causes one's own downfall."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva has four dharmas that cause one to fall into the wrong path. What are the four? First, laziness is a dharma of falling into the wrong path. Second, lack of pure faith. Third, giving rise to discriminative thinking. Fourth, upon seeing someone receive offerings, harboring jealousy. These are the four dharmas by which a bodhisattva falls into the wrong path."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva should not practice four dharmas. What are the four? First, a bodhisattva should not associate with those holding wrong views. Second, a bodhisattva should not associate with those who slander the correct Dharma. Third, a bodhisattva should not associate with bad spiritual companions. Fourth, a bodhisattva should not associate with those who covet food and clothing. These are the four dharmas."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva has four dharmas that bring suffering and transgression. What are the four? First, taking pride in one's own wisdom while harboring resentment and jealousy. Second, the mind being without delight and lacking pure practice. Third, being unable to endure insult and only coveting others' possessions. Fourth, conceiving a self and a person, and being attached to dharmas. These are the four dharmas by which a bodhisattva incurs suffering and transgression."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "A bodhisattva further has four self-imposed bondages. What are the four? First, a bodhisattva who delights in looking down on others binds oneself. Second, a bodhisattva who engages in worldly cleverness, giving rise to thoughts of trading and making a living, binds oneself. Third, a bodhisattva whose mind does not accept the Dharma-wisdom and engages in heedless conduct binds oneself. Fourth, a bodhisattva who binds the mind to attachment to lineage binds oneself. These are the four things."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "In the future age, those studying the bodhisattva path will have such flawed and wayward people among them. They will make offerings to the wayward and to flatterers. They will make offerings to the unwise: those who covet food and clothing, who are without upright minds, who are jealous of lineage, who harbor flattery and crookedness, who are without sincerity, who deceive their elders and families for the sake of offerings. They will slander one another, with minds coveting material gain, entering various districts, not thinking of teaching the Dharma to enlighten people, and without skillful means. Among the people they have no wisdom, yet they consider themselves wise. Seeing that others possess wisdom and are good teachers, they look down on them. Should there be those without proper conduct, they become instruments of destruction. They seek one another's faults, abandon diligent practice, are without wisdom and lazy, do not often reflect on wisdom, and undermine the Dharma. They disrupt the assembly and form grudges, contend and fight, calling others wayward while claiming 'I uphold the Dharma's teaching.' They do not observe the precepts, do not wish to hear the Dharma, and do not practice diligence. Born into poverty, in destitute households, they become monastics concerned only with seeking material gain. Wherever they are, they cannot find peace, let alone gather a focused mind. Although they practice the Buddha's merit, they continue to covet the benefits of household life, calling themselves monastics."

The Buddha said: "I do not consider such people to be practicing the bodhisattva Dharma. People like these, even in a hundred thousand kalpas, cannot attain gentle dharma-patience, let alone the wisdom and right awakening of the Buddha."

The Buddha said: "Rāṣṭrapāla! I do not say that such people merely fall into the three evil paths; they will also fall into eight misfortunes. What are the eight? First, being born in a borderland. Second, falling into a family of poverty. Third, being born with an ugly appearance. Fourth, being born into a wicked family opposed to goodness. Fifth, being born where one encounters bad companions. Sixth, having many illnesses. Seventh, having a short lifespan in the place of birth. Eighth, untimely death. These are the eight misfortunes of a bodhisattva who falls into the wrong path. Why is this? Rāṣṭrapāla! I do not consider one who merely makes verbal vows to be a bodhisattva; I do not consider one who is disordered and false to have pure practice; I do not consider flattery to be bodhisattva practice; I do not consider one who covets food and clothing to be making offerings to the Buddha; I do not consider one who is arrogant to possess pure wisdom; I do not consider one who relies on one's own perceived wisdom to have severed the defilement of doubt. I do not consider one who is jealous to have a pure mind; I do not consider one who is excessively acquisitive to attain dhāraṇī; I do not consider one who does not perceive the merit of truthfulness, yet has obstructions, to be born in a good destination; I do not consider one who is attached to lineage and form to attain a pure body. I do not consider one who engages in discriminative practice to attain the Buddha's samādhi; I do not consider one who lacks sincere practice to attain purity; I do not consider one who is arrogant to attain a pure mind; I do not consider one who does not know contentment to love the Dharma; I do not consider one who clings to body and life to be seeking the Dharma."

The Buddha said: "Rāṣṭrapāla! I do not blame the six external teachers; I blame these foolish people, who are worse than the six external teachers. Why? Because their words are at odds with their deeds, and they deceive the gods and worldly people."

At this the Buddha spoke in gāthā:

> "Without wisdom, confused, heedless, looking down on others without respect, excessively acquisitive,
> Associating with defilement and giving rise to desire: such people are far from the Way.
> Coveting offerings, laziness increases; without diligence, pure faith is lost.
> They ruin pure practice and destroy right morality; those who violate the precepts lose the good path.
> Born into poor families, becoming monastics, in destitution they seek offerings,
> Like a person who, impoverished and without possessions, borrows from others seeking wealth.
> Coveting offerings, they dwell in seclusion, abiding there wishing to advance themselves,
> Obtaining spiritual powers, wisdom, and eloquence, yet abandoning family matters to receive what is given.
> Not seeing the path, they follow disordered conduct; born into poor, lowly families,
> In ugly circumstances without power or influence, they fall into the ground of arrogance and foolishness.
> Being lowly, without fame or virtue, coveting wealth and gain with heedlessness,
> They are afterward born in great evil destinies; for koṭis of kalpas there is no trace of good.
> Were there no greed for gain on the path, all gods and humans would attain buddhahood.
> The wind following the indigo plant does not move people; because of offerings they cannot accomplish it themselves.
> Without merit, relying on others, without diligent aspiration, they lose good conduct.
> They corrupt the teaching and do not uphold the Dharma; they cannot attain the wisdom-mind of the Way.
> Through sincere benefit one attains the Buddha-dharma and never loses one's practice or the mind of the Way.
> With aspirations most firm, always pure, one's devotion being as it should be, this is the Way.
> I sought buddhahood without reluctance, giving body and life in search of the sūtras and Dharma.
> Such people abandon the Dharma without diligence; they lose the phrases and meaning of the Way.
> There is a great lamp that none can see; I originally sought and found the good teaching.
> Upon hearing the instruction, I immediately practiced it, severing all desires and cravings.
> Having heard the Buddha's various Dharma-teachings, unable to fully comprehend even a single dharma-phrase,
> How can one who practices what is not Dharma attain the Way? It is like showing a path to the blind."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "Long ago in the past, incalculable kalpas ago, a time so distant that it is immeasurable and inconceivable, there was a buddha named Tathāgata Śubhārtha (Auspicious Meaning), the Worthy One, the Perfectly Awakened One, Teacher of the World, Buddha, God among Gods. At that time there was a king named Ajñāna."

The Buddha said: "Rāṣṭrapāla! King Ajñāna ruled over the entire continent of Jambudvīpa, which was six hundred and forty thousand li in length and breadth. At that time Jambudvīpa had twenty thousand great cities and a hundred million households. King Ajñāna had a great city called Jewel Radiance, where the king ruled. The city was four hundred and eighty li long and two hundred and eighty li wide, built of the seven precious substances, with eight avenues running north to south, all perfectly and completely constructed. At that time people lived for ten billion nayutas of years."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "King Ajñāna had a son named Guṇaprabha (Virtue-Light), who was beautiful and extraordinary, of wondrous and supreme dignity. At the time of his birth, a thousand treasure-stores spontaneously appeared, all filled with the seven precious substances; in each treasure-store there spontaneously arose the royal jewels of all kingdoms, rising eight zhang high in the seven precious substances. At the moment of Guṇaprabha's birth, all the people of Jambudvīpa greatly rejoiced, and prisoners in confinement were all set free. Within seven days of Prince Guṇaprabha's birth, there was no knowledge he did not possess; he was completely accomplished in both worldly and spiritual matters."

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, the gods of the pure abodes came in the middle of the night to Prince Guṇaprabha and said: 'Prince! Do not engage in heedless conduct.' From that time onward, for a full ten thousand years, Prince Guṇaprabha never slept, nor did he play; he never sang or danced, nor did he make music; he did not travel about or go sightseeing; he never indulged his body, nor did he think of song, dance, or entertainment; he did not covet material gain, nor did he think of household affairs; he was not attached to districts or kingdoms, nor did he seek anything. Of all his possessions, he was without attachment. Thus with a single-pointed mind he always dwelt in solitude, pacifying all difficulties, achieving a mind of few desires. 'There is none who is born and does not die. Body and life cannot be preserved; there is no mutual respect. All worldly affections must end in separation. There is none who can serve as a true guide. Those who disorder the Dharma and commit transgressions live in worry, fear, and dread. Ordinary people do not know contentment; through the power of ignorance they constantly delight in contention. Now I am fallen among the wayward. I wish to be silent and act without contrivance.' At that time the prince dwelt alone in seclusion, free from heedless thoughts, removed from all desires, practicing with equanimity."

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, King Ajñāna had a great city built for Prince Guṇaprabha in another region, named Joy-in-Giving-Wealth, with eight rows running north to south and eight hundred intersecting avenues, with walls of the seven precious substances. The city had seven layers of walls, with canopies of the seven precious substances, all adorned with strings of white pearls. Between all the balustrades stood eighty thousand jeweled pillars; from each jeweled pillar hung sixty thousand jeweled cords interlaced with one another, each cord bearing fourteen billion ornamental streamers. When the wind blew, they struck against each other, producing the sounds of a hundred thousand kinds of music. Before each balustrade stood five hundred courtesans, skilled in music, all expert in song and dance, possessing the highest accomplishments, complete in their abilities, capable of delighting the kings of all nations under heaven, all provided to attend upon Prince Guṇaprabha. The king commanded the courtesans: 'You shall set aside all other affairs and day and night produce music to delight the prince and please his mind. Do not let him see any unwholesome thing.' At each balustrade, provisions for giving were placed: food for the hungry, drink for the thirsty, carriages and horses for those who desired them, clothing, flowers and incense, seats and dwellings, lamps and fire, all provided according to what was sought. Gold, silver, bright moon pearls, lapis lazuli, crystal, elephants and horses, and all manner of jeweled ornaments were distributed throughout the land. In the center of the city, a palace of the seven precious substances was built for Prince Guṇaprabha, with eight layers of open galleries. Within a single hall there were four hundred million seats provided for the prince. In the city there were garden parks with flowering trees and jeweled trees, always in bloom, covering everything with their canopy."

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "In those garden parks, in the center, was a bathing pool of the seven precious substances, with balustrades of four treasures: gold, silver, crystal, and lapis lazuli. Eight hundred lion-head fountains fed water into the bathing pool. Within the pool there were another eight hundred lion-head outlets through which the water flowed out. In the pool there constantly grew four kinds of lotus flowers: blue, red, white, and yellow. All around were jeweled trees, all bearing flowers and fruit. Beside the bathing pool there were another eight hundred ornamental jeweled trees, and between each pair of jeweled trees stood twelve more jeweled trees, each connected by eighty-eight jeweled cords interlaced together. When the wind arose and blew through the trees, they struck against one another, producing sounds of a hundred thousand varieties. Above all the bathing pools were canopies of the seven precious substances with open lattices, and Prince Guṇaprabha bathed within.

"Upon the hall there were four billion seats of the seven precious substances, each spread with five hundred cushions. In the center was spread one great seat of the seven precious substances, draped with eight billion fine garments as coverings. The seat rose five zhang and six chi high, and Prince Guṇaprabha sat upon it. Beneath each seat was an incense burner; three times each day and night, honey-fragrant incense was burned, fine flowers were spread about, covered with jewels, with golden lotuses hanging down. Above the hall was a canopy of bright moon pearls, with eighty thousand bright moon pearls suspended, emitting their radiance and illuminating everything. Upon all the trees were hung banners and canopies, and in each garden park were ninety thousand bright moon pearls. The light of each pearl illuminated forty li, pervading the entire buddha-land."

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "In those garden parks there were parrots, cormorants, cuckoos, peacocks, geese, mandarin ducks, kinnara birds, pheasants, and jīvaka birds, all calling together in mournful song with various sounds, to delight Prince Guṇaprabha. Five hundred kinds of food were constantly prepared as offerings.

"At that time, within all the chambers were five hundred young men, limited to those aged sixteen and above but not yet twenty-one, all of them young men, selected from across all the kingdoms. These young men were brought into the city, all clever and capable, knowing everything about the affairs of the world. Furthermore, eighty billion young women were brought into the city, beautiful and lovely, aged sixteen and above up to twenty, all skilled in song and dance, able to delight men. Their speech was gentle and artful, their words always fitting, neither too tall nor too short, neither too plump nor too thin, neither too fair nor too dark. From their mouths came the fragrance of utpala flowers; from their bodies came the fragrance of candana. They were like celestial maidens, all of one mind, all surrounding Prince Guṇaprabha with the playing of music and singing. Thereupon Prince Guṇaprabha thought: 'I have now naturally gained a great enemy. This multitude disrupts my pure and noble dharma. I must now practice the way of non-attachment.' The prince was thus sorrowful and without joy. Just as a person who finds themselves imprisoned is without joy, so too was Prince Guṇaprabha. Seeing the courtesans and music, his mind was without heedlessness; he did not consider them remarkable, nor was he attached to the city, nor attached to carriages. For a full thousand years, he never indulged in form, neither perceiving nor not perceiving; sounds, fragrances, tastes, and tactile objects, he removed all perception of them, always concentrating single-mindedly, thinking: 'This is my assembly of enemies. How shall I escape from this assembly of enemies and attain liberation, practicing without heedlessness?' At that time, the courtesans reported to King Ajñāna: 'The prince does not listen to song and dance; he is sorrowful and without joy.'"

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, King Ajñāna, together with eighty thousand lesser kings, went to where Prince Guṇaprabha was. Weeping with tears flowing, sorrowful and without joy, overwhelmed, the king collapsed on the ground. His attendants supported him, and the king was helped to his feet. He spoke in gāthā to the prince, saying:

> "'May my son look upon all my treasures, which appeared spontaneously when you were born.
> Who has wronged you? Tell me now, and I shall punish them severely.
> Behold, this is like heaven; I follow your wishes in all things.
> What does the prince now lack? I can give you whatever you desire.
> Look upon these desires, with fine eyes: courtesans playing music,
> Sharing in pleasure to dispel your sorrow, all skilled in music, always smiling.
> You should listen to these beautiful sounds, the instruments in harmonious melody.
> Now is the time for enjoyment; in the pools there are lotus flowers.
> In the garden parks there are blossoms, leaves, and fruit, of various kinds, wondrous and without blemish.
> Behold this supreme and sovereign wisdom; may you take delight and have pity on me.
> Enter the pools and splash about freely; within are lotuses of blue, yellow, and white,
> Various red flowers whose radiance awakens; why does my son not find joy in seeing this?
> Cormorants, parrots, cuckoos, cranes, kiṃnaras with mournful, graceful calls,
> Fragrant white flowers like snow: who upon hearing these sounds would not rejoice?
> The bright-moon hall of equal splendor, with balustrades of gold and lapis lazuli,
> All manner of precious and supreme jewels, the sounds of the trees producing nayutas of melodies.
> The gifts at the balustrades are for your sake; a thousand courtesans play music.
> Also hear the celestial maidens' song and music; what does my son think of that he finds no delight?
> The prince is beautiful and handsome; may you take pleasure and heed my words.
> Your father and mother stand here with tears in their eyes; does the son have no compassion for us?'

"At that time Prince Guṇaprabha answered the king in gāthā:

> "'He who possesses merit, who has left behind all evil views and speech,
> I have grown weary of suffering and pleasure; I do not covet unprofitable desires.
> I have seen in all five destinies the people of birth-and-death;
> Now I shall speak of liberation; may the royal father hear my words.
> No one has touched or wronged me; what now shall I say?
> I do not covet desires; how could I delight in song and dance?
> All desires I regard as enemies.
> Afflictions and all cravings follow people into the five destinies.
> These courtesans, without awakening, foolishly delight in them;
> These are the works of Māra, binding people with great fetters.
> The noble and wise ones never praise such things.
> Those who indulge in these desires plant the root of causal conditions.
> The bodies of these courtesans are skin and hide wrapped together,
> Sinew and bone propping each other up, like an illusion without true benefit.
> Just as a painted vessel filled with filth within,
> Like being in a charnel ground: how could one delight in this?
> The sounds of music that are played have no substance and no reception;
> All pleasures are without truth; understanding this, one is not deluded.
> If one indulges in thought and perception, one immediately loses single-mindedness.
> Following the sounds of affliction, one is like a foolish old person.
> All trees that exist may have a time of flourishing,
> But they cannot be possessed forever; there are also times without delight.
> Their fruits are not permanent, nor do they always stay upon the tree.
> Having understood this, how could I play with a short-lived existence?
> Father and mother cannot be relied upon, nor brothers and wives;
> Relatives are likewise; at the end, one has no mastery.
> All that exists is like dew upon the grass.
> One should not indulge the mind, giving free rein to heedlessness.
> This mind cannot be satisfied, just like the great ocean;
> Affections are exceedingly vast; having obtained, one seeks again.
> Because of craving, all people fall into laziness;
> None can diminish it; it is like Mount Sumeru.
> The mind is the root of all; body and life pass swiftly,
> Like the flow of a river: coming together, they immediately part.
> Everything is destroyed and does not long endure, like a flash of lightning.
> Those who cling to desires in the three realms are without wisdom.
> The gods came and told me: Do not be heedless.
> For one who practices the bodhisattva path, one does not covet any possession.
> Wishing to attain the Buddha-path, with compassion for all people:
> It is not through the practice of desire that one can attain the Buddha-path.
> Those who accept craving make their minds into slaves,
> Thereby ruining themselves; they cannot establish merit.
> I will never accept desire, nor give rise to anger.
> Like a bird fallen into a net, how can it be free?
> Manifesting evil thoughts, one binds oneself.
> The mind has no mastery; it is an unprofitable, empty assemblage.
> Craving this body of fear is like the flower of a poison tree.
> Who is the honored one among people? It is the one who crosses the swift current.
> Look upon all the people falling into the evil destinies,
> Contending over empty and meaningless phrases, giving rise to all wrong views.
> May the king understand my mind: I wish to liberate these beings.
> Not coveting the dharma of accumulation and pride, swiftly attaining the crossing to the other shore,
> Awakening those who slumber, healing the sick.
> Removing worry and distress, establishing them on the path of delight,
> Wishing to liberate the triple-thousand worlds, those bound and attached to mere echoes.
> Teaching the meaning of the good sūtras, satisfying the long-impoverished,
> Taming those who are unaccomplished, pulling them out of the evil destinies.
> Giving sight to the blind, enabling the deaf to hear,
> Fashioning the lamp of liberation, establishing wisdom and supernatural powers.
> Enabling all people of the three realms to attain the three patiences of equanimity,
> Creating the rain of compassion, crossing the shore of clouds and mist.
> For all people, manifesting the flame of radiance,
> Then taking up the mind of good awakening, enabling them to find cool shade.
> Raining down all medicines, enabling all to find peace.
> Having reflected on this, O royal father, I now sit with single-pointed mind.
> Toward all desires I have no further aspiration;
> I only wish to seek the Buddha-path, out of compassion for all people.
> Toward all that exists in craving, I have no further aspiration.
> What wise person would delight in remaining here?
> How could one violate the precepts? It makes the mind confused and deluded.
> If one clings to desire and form, one falls into the great evil destinies.
> What practitioner of the Buddha-path would still be heedless?
> All people follow the current; I shall now go against it.
> It is not through words alone that one attains the Buddha-path.
> One must radiate the light of compassion, illuminating all people.
> I do not covet desires; I am not bound to material things.
> I now ask my royal father to please return with the assembly.
> I wish to leave the assembly and all the districts and kingdoms.
> People mostly seek what pleases them; from this comes sickness.
> Restraining the mind from heedlessness surpasses gaining a hundred million kingdoms.
> One cannot dwell in desire and attain the Buddha-path.
> If one wishes to attain the unsurpassed, peaceful, and blissful state,
> One should go to the great mountains and sit beneath the trees,
> Cultivating the practice of seclusion, one can attain the honored path of awakening.'"

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, Prince Guṇaprabha, upon the upper hall, surrounded by those given to heedlessness, felt revulsion in his mind. The prince then undertook three forms of practice. What are the three? First, standing. Second, walking meditation. Third, seated meditation. He abandoned sleeping, fully accomplished in the supreme practice, and had already attained the eight stages of meditative abiding.

"At that time, in the middle of the night, the prince heard a voice from the sky: the gods of the pure abodes exclaiming in praise of the Buddha's merit, vast and universally complete, and also praising the Dharma and the Saṃgha. When Prince Guṇaprabha heard this, the hairs of his body stood on end, tears fell, and he was filled with sorrowful longing. With palms joined, he asked the gods in gāthā:

> "'I am in the midst of hardship; may the gods have pity on me.
> Please stay and hear my words; I wish to ask something.
> Traveling through the sky, whose merit do you praise?
> Hearing these sounds, my heart is filled with grief and joy.'

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, the gods spoke in gāthā to Prince Guṇaprabha:

> "'There is now a buddha in the world; has the prince not heard?
> The Buddha is named Śubhārtha (Auspicious Meaning); he saves, rescues, and protects.
> He cultivates all roots of goodness and opens the way to honored merit.
> The Saṃgha, through study, numbers koṭi-nayuta-thousands.'

"Prince Guṇaprabha asked the gods in gāthā:

> "'Should I happen to see the World-Honored One, how shall I know he is the Buddha?
> Please describe his compassionate merits; I wish to know the Perfectly Awakened One.
> If I were to go and see the Buddha, what should I ask about the Way?
> What dharmas does a bodhisattva practice to become a protector of all?'

"Thereupon the gods spoke in gāthā to Prince Guṇaprabha:

> "'His hair is soft, beautiful, and excellent, distinguished and curling to the right.
> His uṣṇīṣa is of majestic power, beautiful like a mountain peak.
> Between his brows is a mark of radiance, shining like the rising sun,
> Born beautifully, curling to the right, its color white as snow.
> His awakened mind is pure; his eyes are deep blue.
> The honored one among humans, a son of heaven, with a countenance of perfect beauty.
> His face is always harmonious and joyful, radiating immeasurable billions of rays of light,
> Pervading the triple-thousand lands, extinguishing all evil destinies.
> The Buddha's teeth within his mouth are all even and pure,
> Fresh and clean like kumuda flowers, bright like a fine tree's radiance.
> Evenly set, twenty on each side, making forty in all.
> His tongue within his mouth is wondrous, able to cover his own face.
> The wonderful words spoken from his mouth cause people's minds to rejoice;
> Always free from all flattery, his Brahmā-voice is supremely pure.
> What the Buddha proclaims surpasses a hundred thousand kinds of music,
> Removing and quelling all doubts, bringing people benefit and delight.
> His various merits are inexhaustible; with skillful means he elucidates the Way's meaning.
> Having understood the flower of the wise Dharma, it becomes a garland of a hundred thousand ornaments.
> The sound of the earth produces heavenly music,
> Like the sound of heavenly echoes: the Buddha's speech is just like this.
> The kinnara, pheasant, cuckoo, and mandarin duck,
> The goose, crane, cormorant, and kalaviṅka bird all inquire.
> His voice is like Brahmā's, gentle and supremely harmonious,
> Without flattery, without fault, comprehending all meaning.
> Brilliant and transcendent, pleasing to the wise,
> Pure, beyond reproach, free from all conceptual aspirations.
> Well bestowing the meaning of virtue, never heard to produce a blemish:
> That one who practices the Dharma, the Perfectly Awakened One, whose speech and merit are thus.
> The World-Honored One's body possesses all manner of colors;
> His arms extend past his knees, seven points are all full.
> His fingers are slender, long, and beautiful, with various and wondrous features.
> A body of finest purple-gold, a heart like a bright moon pearl.
> The hair on his body is soft and beautiful, pointing upward and curling to the right.
> His navel is round and raised; his male organ is concealed like a horse's.
> The soles of his feet are flat and even, with wheel-marks beneath.
> His knees are perfectly formed, of even and various colors.
> He walks like a dragon king, with the stride of a lion.
> When he walks, he silently lowers his gaze; all his faculties are completely pure.
> If someone scatters flowers, they transform into flower-canopies.
> There is increase and never decrease: this is the Buddha's correct Dharma.
> Whether gaining or not gaining, whether in hardship or in comfort,
> Whether praised or reproached, his mind neither increases nor decreases.
> Just like a lotus flower, not clinging to mud or water:
> The true lion is thus; there is none who is his equal.'"

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, Prince Guṇaprabha, hearing the praise of the Buddha's merit and the Dharma and the Bhikṣu-saṃgha, leaped with joy. Just as a person who is poor, hungry, and cold, upon finding a hidden treasure, rejoices; just as a blind person who gains sight; just as a prisoner who is released and rejoices: so too did Prince Guṇaprabha rejoice upon hearing the praise of the Buddha's merit and the Dharma and the Bhikṣu-saṃgha.

"Thereupon Prince Guṇaprabha thought: 'Now I have heard of the Buddha's majestic power, the attested sūtras and Dharma, the Saṃgha complete in honored practice without deficiency. In birth-and-death, people engage in wrong conduct. Ordinary people are mostly ungrateful, clinging to the body and the self; this is not right practice. Household life is full of flaws and impurities; those who indulge in desire will fall into suffering. Heedless conduct is what the wise abandon. Ignorance is darkness; among such people I should create a lamp of equanimity. The human mind is difficult to tame; name-and-form are profoundly deep; the six sense-bases are insatiable; habitual tendencies are not severed. One will encounter bitter, sharp pain and discomfort; affection is like stocks and fetters; all experiences are difficult to relinquish. Being united with what exists leads to long-lasting hostile encounters; birth-and-death is difficult to sever; human life involves much commotion and confusion. Sickness, delusion, and the body are not solid; one must eventually return to death. Pleasures are few and sorrows are many; the Buddha-dharma is the foremost peace. One cannot, through the conduct of affliction, with a mind of craving and heedlessness, establish meritorious practice. Now I am in the midst of ignorance, unable to attain single-pointed concentration. One cannot, with a mind that delights in birth-and-death, associating with evil people, adorn the good path, let alone wish to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi! I would rather throw myself from this high tower facing east than let my family and retinue obstruct me at the gates and prevent me from leaving.'"

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, Prince Guṇaprabha threw himself toward that Tathāgata Śubhārtha, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Awakened One, speaking these words: 'If the World-Honored One possesses sarvajñā and can see all things, may the God among Gods now think of me and save me.' Thereupon Tathāgata Śubhārtha, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Awakened One, extended his right arm and from his hand emitted a radiance that illuminated Prince Guṇaprabha. Within that radiance there spontaneously appeared a thousand-petaled lotus flower as large as a chariot wheel, and from that lotus there radiated a hundred billion thousand rays of light that illuminated everything. Thereupon Prince Guṇaprabha stood upon this lotus flower and, wishing to go to where Tathāgata Śubhārtha, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Awakened One, was, from afar joined his palms in reverence and took refuge three times. At that time, Tathāgata Śubhārtha turned his light back, and the prince followed the light until he reached the Buddha. He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and saw that all the World-Honored One's faculties were in serene concentration. At that time, Prince Guṇaprabha praised Tathāgata Śubhārtha in gāthā, reciting:

> "'Not long have I heard the name of the King of Physicians; now I have been able to see the Buddha.
> How, while standing amid flawed conduct, can one attain all dharmas?
> Earlier tonight at midnight, I heard from the gods that the Buddha is without conceptual thought.
> Upon hearing, I became sorrowful and without joy; who is the one who is without heedlessness?
> He who shows the correct road to those who have lost the way, who gives equal sight to those without eyes:
> May you now reveal to me the great Way, with compassion healing sickness and making faith pure.
> Enabling the impoverished to find wealth and happiness, liberating those imprisoned,
> Severing my doubts and removing all fetters: may you please explain the practices of the Way.
> Reveal to me the correct and separate from the heterodox; in the midst of darkness, be a lamp.
> For all who are harmed, remove defilement: may the Great King of Physicians sever my doubts.
> May you liberate me from the path of birth-and-death, severing and removing all my attachments,
> Enabling me to cross the sea of sorrow, and through the Eightfold Path enter the Great Vehicle.
> Now life is short and the life of the Dharma is ending; there are many obstructions to meritorious practice.
> Those without merit do not fulfill their wishes; now that I have this opening, may you resolve my doubts.
> Now I hear the guide's essential instruction: how can a bodhisattva be heedless?
> May one practice the Buddha's honored and wondrous Way, liberating people from the affliction of birth-and-death.'"

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, Tathāgata Śubhārtha, knowing what was in Prince Guṇaprabha's mind, extensively explained for him the practices of all bodhisattvas. Prince Guṇaprabha, upon hearing what that buddha taught, immediately attained the gate of inexhaustible dhāraṇī and achieved the five supernatural powers. He then rose into the sky and conjured wondrous flowers, which he scattered over Tathāgata Śubhārtha.

"At that time, King Ajñāna, upon hearing at dawn the sound of the courtesans weeping in the prince's palace, his face immediately changed, and he went to the prince's palace and asked: 'Why are you weeping?' The courtesans answered: 'Prince Guṇaprabha has disappeared; we do not know where he has gone.' When King Ajñāna heard that the prince had disappeared, he collapsed on the ground, and together with several thousand attendants he raised his voice in weeping. At that time, the city deity came to his dwelling and told King Ajñāna: 'Great King! Do not weep and grieve. The prince has gone eastward to see Tathāgata Śubhārtha; he has bowed his head in reverence and knelt in attendance.' When King Ajñāna heard the deity's voice, together with his retinue, great ministers, the prince's harem courtesans, and eighty-four billion nayuta-hundreds-of-thousands of people, he went eastward to where Tathāgata Śubhārtha was, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, and stood to one side."

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, Tathāgata Śubhārtha, knowing King Ajñāna's mind, taught the Dharma as appropriate, causing the entire assembly to attain non-retrogression from anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Thereupon Prince Guṇaprabha addressed Tathāgata Śubhārtha: 'May the Buddha accept my offering of a pure meal.' The Buddha silently accepted. Prince Guṇaprabha said to his parents and retinue: 'May you now help adorn the city with ornaments and jewels to offer to the Tathāgata. Do not have a covetous mind or hold anything back; may all unite in encouraging generosity with open hearts.' Thereupon Prince Guṇaprabha and his retinue offered to Tathāgata Śubhārtha ornaments and jewels, palaces and cities, their minds without reservation. Each day five hundred kinds of flavors were prepared as offerings to the Buddha and the bhikṣu-saṃgha. For all the bhikṣus, chambers were built of red candana wood and the seven precious substances. The walking-meditation paths were made of maṇi jewels, with canopies of precious substances above, with rows of flowering trees running north to south, and bathing pools along the sides growing utpala flowers. The margins were clean and pure and without blemish, and the flowers had a hundred thousand petals with a hundred thousand seats set upon them; each bhikṣu had such provisions. At that time, Prince Guṇaprabha ensured that the bhikṣus had no worry about clothing and did not think enviously of other bhikṣus receiving garments.

"For a hundred million years he never slept; he did not think of what he cherished, nor did he cling to his body. He made offerings to the Buddha with unwavering mindfulness. At that time he never had a thought of desire, nor was there contention or disorder; his mind bore no harm and he did not covet the kingdom. He was without attachment to any possession, not clinging to body or life, free from attachment both within and without. Whatever Dharma the Buddha taught, he received and retained it all, never asking the Tathāgata a second time. He never bathed, nor washed his feet, nor anointed his body with fragrance; he never gave rise to a thought of weariness, nor did he ever sit down, except when eating and attending to necessities.

"After Tathāgata Śubhārtha entered parinirvāṇa, the prince erected a stūpa of red candana wood and for a hundred thousand years made offerings. At the place where the Tathāgata was cremated, he offered all the flowers, incense, powdered incense, mixed incense, and music from throughout the land. He erected ninety-four billion stūpas, all made of the seven precious substances and rare treasures, with canopies covering them, each supplied with five hundred jeweled parasols as offerings to the stūpas, and a hundred thousand kinds of music and all the flowers and jeweled trees of Jambudvīpa as offerings to the stūpas. At each he lit a hundred thousand lamps, each lamp's oil worth a hundred thousand in value, and scattered fragrant flowers of every kind. In this manner, he made complete offerings for a hundred million years. Afterward, Prince Guṇaprabha left the household life and became a śramaṇa, wearing the three dharma-robes, always practicing the alms round, never involving himself in worldly affairs, nor sleeping. He was free from any thought of clothing or food. For a full four hundred million years, he constantly gave the gift of Dharma, never conceiving of a self. Nor did he doubt others, let alone seek offerings. Nor did he speak of birth-and-death. When teaching the Dharma to the assembly, he did not urge people to seek rebirth in the heavens. He practiced in this way and instructed all people and the inner palace retinue, causing them to become śramaṇas."

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "At that time, the gods of the pure abodes thought: 'Prince Guṇaprabha instructs all people, causing them all to become śramaṇas. We too should perform acts of offering to the Three Treasures, for it is through the Three Treasures that the teaching is established and does not perish.' After Tathāgata Śubhārtha entered parinirvāṇa, his Dharma endured for sixty-four billion years, all due to the protection of the bhikṣu Guṇaprabha. In this manner, Prince Guṇaprabha made offerings to ninety-four billion nayuta-hundreds-of-thousands of buddhas."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "Do you know who King Ajñāna was at that time?"

He answered: "I do not know."

The Buddha said: "He was Tathāgata Amitāyus. Do you know who Prince Guṇaprabha was at that time?"

He answered: "I do not know."

"He was none other than myself. The city deity at that time was Tathāgata Akrodhajñā (Non-Anger Awakening)."

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "For this reason, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who wishes to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, the most perfect enlightenment, should study the practices of Prince Guṇaprabha and the teaching of quiescence: casting aside affection, practicing without heedlessness. When I sought anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, the diligent effort and suffering I undertook were like this. These wayward people who covet clothing and food, anxious and unrelenting, for the sake of offerings, distance themselves from the Buddha-dharma. What they study brings no benefit; they pollute the monastic community and corrupt the bodhisattva Dharma. Indulging their body, speech, and mind, they make vain wishes and abandon their original practice. They covet robes, bedding, and medicine for illness, without any sense of shame. They do not delight in right practice, they do not study the dharma of impermanence, they do not follow the honored teaching, they distance themselves from the Buddha's practice, they abandon their aspiration for the Way, and they do not delight in the practice of liberation."

The Buddha said to Rāṣṭrapāla: "For this reason, having heard this Dharma, one should awaken to it. Abandon bad companions; do not follow those without proper conduct; abandon all craving and desire."

At that time the Buddha spoke in gāthā:

> "Those who study the Way yet covet gain and food no longer delight in the practice of the Ten Powers.
> They abandon the Buddha's hundred-fold teaching of virtue; for the sake of gain and offerings, they fall into others' homes.
> Stubborn, wicked, and without shame, they indulge themselves and fall into all kinds of covetous company.
> Giving rise to afflictions and falling into wrong conduct, they then claim: 'I am virtuous.'
> Living in seclusion yet wandering to the city, for gain and offerings they act with self-indulgence.
> Far from liberation, they leave the ground empty; therefore one should abandon and leave behind all existence.
> Because of disrespect for the Buddha and the correct Dharma, distant from the Saṃgha's merit,
> Abandoning the good path, they fall into the three evils, and lose eight hundred honored practices.
> If one hears this sūtra taught, one should purify one's mind and always be diligent.
> A buddha is difficult to encounter in countless koṭis of kalpas; therefore one should practice in accord with the Dharma.
> Those who speak of attaining the Buddha's Great Vehicle should constantly reflect upon these meritorious phrases.
> Having reflected, remaining truly single-minded, one will attain the unobstructed path of peace.
> Always establishing the noble and wise, cultivating the contemplation of virtue, with a mind of contentment and self-restraint:
> You must not abandon the good field, lest you fall into the five destinies like the foolish.
> Cultivate seclusion and stillness with constant diligence; remain firm, do not belittle yourself or slight others.
> Admonish and discipline yourself, quiet your mind: I have in the past followed the teaching of a hundred million buddhas.
> Without sparing body or life, with a sincere mind, diligent in the Dharma, practicing with reverence:
> I have always spoken these words of instruction; having practiced thus, the Way is not difficult.
> Those who rejoice upon hearing of the Great Vehicle yet cannot be diligent and do not delight in listening:
> Those who are wise will cherish these words and afterward abandon evil and hostile entanglements."

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "If a bodhisattva practices the five pāramitās but does not study this sūtra and follow and practice its teachings, the merit of that one does not equal even a hundredth part of one who studies this sūtra."

When this sūtra was taught, thirty nayutas of gods and humans generated the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and all attained the stage of non-retrogression. Seven thousand bhikṣus attained the patience of non-arising dharmas and the liberation of the mind through the exhaustion of the outflows.

Thereupon the Venerable Rāṣṭrapāla addressed the Buddha: "What is this sūtra called, and how should it be practiced?"

The Buddha told Rāṣṭrapāla: "This sūtra is called 'Departing from Delusion, Aspiring to Practice in Purity.' One should study it and retain it. It is what the noble ones delight in, determining the bodhisattva's practice, complete in all meaning."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, Rāṣṭrapāla, the gods, worldly people, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, and all were greatly delighted. They rose and came before the Buddha, paid their respects, and departed.
