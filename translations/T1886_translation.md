# Inquiry into the Origin of Humanity

## *Yuán Rén Lùn* (原人論)

Taishō 1886

Composed by Zōngmì (*Zōngmì*, 宗密) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a celebrated treatise by Guīfēng Zōngmì (780-841 CE), the fifth patriarch of the Huáyán school and simultaneously a master in the Hézé lineage of Chan Buddhism. The treatise systematically evaluates the teachings of Confucianism, Daoism, and four levels of Buddhist doctrine regarding the origin and nature of humanity, proceeding from the shallowest to the most profound. Zōngmì critiques each position in turn: Confucian and Daoist naturalism (which derive humans from primordial qi), the Buddhist teachings of karma and rebirth (human-and-deva vehicle), the Hīnayāna analysis of the person into aggregates, the Yogācāra doctrine of the ālayavijñāna, and the Madhyamaka teaching that all is empty. He then presents the "One Vehicle Teaching that Reveals the Nature" (the Huáyán doctrine of original awakening) as the ultimate explanation, and concludes by harmonizing all five levels into a coherent account. The work is remarkable for its irenic spirit, according a legitimate (if limited) role even to Confucian and Daoist teachings within an overarching Buddhist framework.*

---

## Preface

Among all the myriad living spirits, each has its root; among all the myriad things, each returns to its source. There has never been anything with branches and tips that lacked a root and origin. How much less could the most numinous being among the three powers (heaven, earth, and humanity) lack a fundamental source?

To know others is wisdom; to know oneself is clarity. If I have obtained a human body but do not know whence I came, how can I know where I will go in future lives? How can I know the affairs of all people throughout the world, past and present? Therefore, over several decades of study under many teachers, I have broadly investigated both inner (Buddhist) and outer (Confucian and Daoist) teachings to trace the origin of this very self. Tracing without ceasing, I finally found its root.

Yet those who study Confucianism and Daoism only know that, in the near term, grandfather and father transmitted the bodily form in continuous succession, producing this body; and that, in the remote past, from the undifferentiated chaos of primordial qi (*yuánqì*), it split into the two of yin and yang; from the two arose the three of heaven, earth, and humanity; from the three arose the myriad things. The myriad things and humanity all have qi as their root.

Those who study the Buddha-dharma only say: "In the near term, one's previous life created karma, and following karma one received retribution, obtaining this human body. In the remote past, karma in turn arose from delusion, extending back until the ālayavijñāna (*ālàiyē shí*, 阿賴耶識) serves as the fundamental root of body." Both claim to have exhausted the inquiry, but in truth they have not.

Yet Confucius, Laozi, and Śākyamuni were all supreme sages. Responding to their times and adapting to beings, they established teachings by different paths. Inner and outer complement each other, jointly benefiting the multitudes. In spurring the myriad practices and clarifying the beginning and end of cause and effect, and in investigating the myriad dharmas and revealing the root and branches of arising: although all are saintly intentions, some are provisional (*quán*) and some definitive (*shí*). The two [outer] teachings are solely provisional; Buddhism combines both provisional and definitive. In spurring the myriad practices, punishing evil and encouraging good, all three teachings converge on good governance, and all three may be followed. But in investigating the myriad dharmas, exhausting principle and fully realizing the nature, reaching the fundamental source: only the Buddhist teaching provides the definitive resolution.

The text has four chapters and is titled *Inquiry into the Origin of Humanity*.

## Chapter One: Refuting Confused Attachments

The two teachings of Confucianism and Daoism say that humans, animals, and other types of beings are all produced and nourished by the Great Way of Emptiness. They say the Way follows naturalness and gives rise to primordial qi; primordial qi gives rise to heaven and earth; heaven and earth give rise to the myriad things. Therefore, foolishness and wisdom, nobility and baseness, poverty and wealth, suffering and joy are all bestowed by heaven and determined by timing and destiny. After death, one returns to heaven and earth and reverts to emptiness.

However, the essential aim of these outer teachings lies only in establishing conduct based on the body, not in ultimately investigating the body's origin. What they say about the myriad things does not go beyond phenomena. Although they point to the Great Way as the root, they do not fully elucidate the causes and conditions of forward and reverse arising and ceasing, defilement and purity. Therefore, their followers do not know these are provisional teachings and cling to them as definitive. I shall now briefly raise objections.

If all things are produced from the Great Way of Emptiness, then the Great Way is the root of birth-and-death, worthiness and folly, and the foundation of fortune and misfortune. If the foundation permanently exists, then calamity, disorder, misfortune, and folly cannot be eliminated, and blessings, celebration, worthiness, and goodness cannot be increased. What use then are the teachings of Laozi and Zhuangzi?

Furthermore, if the Way nurtures tigers and wolves, gives birth to tyrants like Jié and Zhòu, cuts short the lives of Yán Huí and Rǎn Bóniú, and brings disaster upon Bóyí and Shūqí, how can it be called "venerable"?

Furthermore, if all things arise through natural transformation without causes and conditions, then everything without causes and conditions should also arise: rocks should produce grass, grass might produce humans, humans produce animals. There should be no before or after in birth, no early or late in arising. Immortals would not need elixirs, an age of peace would not need worthy ministers, benevolence and righteousness would not need teaching. What use would Laozi, Zhuangzi, the Duke of Zhou, and Confucius have in establishing teachings as guiding standards?

Furthermore, if all things are produced from primordial qi, then the spirit suddenly born [from qi] has never practiced deliberation. How is it that infants can immediately love, hate, and act with pride and willfulness? If one says that upon sudden existence these capacities naturally arise with thought, then the five virtues and six arts should also be understood naturally with thought. Why would one need causes, conditions, and study to accomplish them?

Furthermore, if birth is the sudden existence of receiving qi, and death is the scattering of qi into sudden non-existence, then who are the ghosts and spirits? There are those in the world who can clearly recall past lives and remember former events; this shows that before birth there was continuity, not sudden existence from receiving qi. And the verified spiritual cognition of ghosts and spirits shows that after death, there is not sudden non-existence from qi scattering.

## Chapter Two: Refuting the Partial and Shallow

Buddhist teaching, from shallow to deep, has roughly five levels: (1) the human-and-deva teaching, (2) the Hīnayāna teaching, (3) the Mahāyāna Dharma-characteristics (Yogācāra) teaching, (4) the Mahāyāna refutation-of-characteristics (Madhyamaka) teaching, (5) the One Vehicle teaching that reveals the nature.

**(1)** For beginners, the Buddha first taught the karmic retribution of the three times and the causes and results of good and evil: committing the ten evils in their upper grade leads to rebirth in hell at death; the middle grade, to hungry ghosts; the lower grade, to animals. Therefore the Buddha, paralleling the worldly teaching of the five constant virtues, enjoined upholding the five precepts to avoid the three evil paths and be reborn in the human realm. Cultivating the ten good deeds in their upper grade, together with giving and precepts, leads to rebirth in the six desire heavens. Cultivating the four dhyānas and eight concentrations leads to rebirth in the form and formless heavens. Hence the name "human-and-deva teaching."

In this teaching, karma serves as the root of the body. I now raise an objection: If one receives the body of the five destinies through creating karma, it is unclear who creates the karma and who receives the retribution. If the eyes, ears, hands, and feet create karma, why do the eyes, ears, hands, and feet of one who has just died, still intact, not see, hear, or act? If the mind creates karma, what is the mind? If it is the fleshy heart, it is material, situated inside the body: how can it swiftly enter the eyes and ears to discern external rights and wrongs? ... Therefore, those who only study this teaching, though they believe in karmic conditions, do not comprehend the root of the body.

**(2)** The Hīnayāna teaching says that the form of the body and the thinking of the mind, from beginningless time, through the power of causes and conditions, arise and perish moment after moment in unbroken succession, like water flowing ceaselessly, like a lamp's flame flickering. Body and mind, provisionally combined, seem unified and permanent. Ordinary fools, not realizing this, cling to them as a self. Cherishing this self, they give rise to greed, anger, and delusion. The three poisons agitate the mind and set body and speech in motion to create all karma...

By analyzing the body into the four great elements (earth, water, fire, wind) and the mind into the four aggregates (sensation, perception, formations, consciousness): if all were the self, there would be eight selves. The body alone contains 360 segments of bone, each separate; skin, hair, sinew, flesh, liver, heart, spleen, and kidneys are each different. The mental factors are also each different: seeing is not hearing, joy is not anger, extending to 84,000 afflictions. With so many things, which one is taken as the self? ... One then realizes this body is merely an assemblage of conditions, provisionally appearing combined, originally without self or person...

However, I now raise an objection: That which serves as the root of the body across lifetimes must itself be uninterrupted. Yet the five sense-consciousnesses do not arise when conditions are lacking, the mental consciousness sometimes does not operate, and the formless-realm heavens lack the four great elements. How can these sustain the body without interruption life after life? Therefore, those who specialize in this teaching also have not traced the origin of the body.

**(3)** The Mahāyāna Dharma-characteristics teaching says all sentient beings have, from beginningless time, naturally possessed eight kinds of consciousness. Among these, the eighth, the ālayavijñāna, is the fundamental root. It suddenly transforms into the seed-body, the sense-faculties, and the vessel-world. It gives rise to the seven [other] consciousnesses, all of which can transform and manifest their own objects. There are no real dharmas at all. ... Consciousness is the root of the body.

**(4)** The Mahāyāna refutation-of-characteristics teaching refutes the attachment to dharma-characteristics of both the preceding Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna, and secretly reveals the principle that the true nature is empty and quiescent. ... If mind and objects are both nonexistent, who is the one who knows they are nonexistent? If there are no real dharmas at all, upon what do the various illusions appear? ... Therefore, this teaching only breaks attachment; it has not yet clearly revealed the true numinous nature. As the *Dharma-Drum Sūtra* says: "All sūtras teaching emptiness are teachings with remainder."

## Chapter Three: Directly Revealing the True Source

**(5)** The One Vehicle teaching that reveals the nature says all sentient beings possess the originally-awakened true mind, which from beginningless time has been permanently abiding, pure, luminously clear, and always aware. It is also called "Buddha-nature" and "tathāgatagarbha." From the beginningless past, delusory characteristics have obscured it so that it does not know itself, recognizing only the ordinary form. Therefore it indulges in attachments, creates karmic bonds, and suffers birth-and-death.

The Great Awakened One, in compassion, taught that all is empty, and further disclosed the numinous, awakened true mind, pure and entirely identical with all buddhas. As the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* says: "Children of the Buddha! There is not a single sentient being that does not fully possess the Tathāgata's wisdom. It is only because of delusory thinking and attachment that they do not realize it. If they depart from delusory thinking, then omniscient wisdom, spontaneous wisdom, and unobstructed wisdom will immediately manifest before them."

**Evaluation**: We have for many kalpas not encountered the true teaching and have not understood how to turn back and trace the origin of this very self. Clinging only to illusory characteristics, we have meekly accepted ourselves as ordinary and lowly, as animals or humans. Now, tracing the origin according to the ultimate teaching, we discover that we have originally been buddhas. Therefore we must conduct ourselves according to the Buddha's conduct and align our minds with the Buddha's mind. Returning to the root and reverting to the source, severing ordinary habits, diminishing again and again until reaching non-action: naturally responding and functioning like the sands of the Ganges, this is called "buddha." One should know that delusion and awakening share the same true mind. How great is this wondrous gate! The inquiry into the origin of humanity reaches its conclusion here.

## Chapter Four: Harmonizing Root and Branches

Although the true nature is the root of the body, its arising has causes and conditions; it cannot suddenly, without reason, become the form of a body. But because the preceding levels were not yet complete, I refuted each in turn. Now I shall harmonize root and branches, extending even to Confucianism and Daoism.

In the beginning there was solely the one true numinous nature: unborn and undying, neither increasing nor decreasing, neither changing nor altering. Because sentient beings from beginningless time have been lost in sleep and do not know themselves, through being concealed it is called "tathāgatagarbha." In dependence on the tathāgatagarbha, the characteristics of the arising-and-ceasing mind appear. The non-arising, non-ceasing true mind united with the arising-and-ceasing delusory thoughts, neither one nor different: this is called the ālayavijñāna.

This consciousness has two aspects: awakening and non-awakening. Based on non-awakening, the first stirring of thought arises, called the "karma-characteristic." Not yet knowing that this thought is originally without substance, it transforms into the perceiving consciousness and the appearance of perceived objects. Not yet knowing that these objects arise from the deluded manifestation of one's own mind, one clings to them as fixed realities: this is called "dharma-attachment." Clinging to these, one then sees the distinction of self and other, producing "self-attachment." Clinging to the self-characteristic, one craves objects that accord with one's feelings, wishing to nourish the self; one resents objects that oppose one's feelings, fearing harm; feelings of delusion expand further. Thus the mind-spirit, riding on this evil karma of killing, stealing, and so forth, is born in the hells, hungry ghost realm, animal realm, and so on.

Others, fearing these sufferings, or being of good disposition, practice giving, precepts, and the like. The mind-spirit, riding on this good karma, travels through the intermediate existence (*antarābhava*) and enters the mother's womb. Receiving qi and taking on physical substance: the qi immediately possesses the four great elements, which gradually form the sense-faculties; the mind immediately possesses the four aggregates, which gradually form the consciousnesses. After ten months of gestation, one is born and called "a human being": this is precisely the body and mind we now have. Therefore, know that body and mind each have their own root; the two types combine to form one person. For devas, asuras, and the rest, the principle is broadly the same.

Although one receives this body through the drawing power of karma, the differing karmic fruitions further determine nobility or baseness, poverty or wealth, longevity or early death, health or illness, prosperity or decline, suffering or joy. ... This is why in this life one may suffer misfortune without having done evil, or enjoy blessings without having done good, or live long without benevolence, or die young without having killed. All these are determined by the fulfilling karma (*mǎnyè*) of past lives. Therefore what happens in this life does not match present actions. The outsider scholars, not knowing past lives and relying only on what they see, cling to the doctrine of natural spontaneity.

Ultimately speaking, the qi that one receives, traced back to its root, is the undifferentiated primordial qi. The mind that arises, traced back to its source, is the true and unitary numinous mind. In the final analysis, apart from mind there is definitely no other dharma. Primordial qi is also a transformation of mind, belonging to the objects manifested by the transforming consciousness; it is subsumed under the objective aspect (*xiàngfēn*) of the ālayavijñāna. From the initial single thought, the karma-characteristic, it divides into mind and objects. Mind evolves from subtle to coarse, through successive deluded projections, all the way to creating karma. Objects evolve from minute to manifest, through successive transformations, all the way to heaven and earth. When karma has ripened, one receives the two kinds of qi from father and mother, and the karmic consciousness combines with them to form the human body.

According to this, the objects transformed by consciousness divide into two parts: one part combines with consciousness to form the person; one part does not combine with consciousness and becomes heaven, earth, mountains, rivers, and kingdoms. The reason that among the three powers, only humans are numinous is because they are united with the mind-spirit. When the Buddha said that the inner four great elements differ from the outer four great elements, this is precisely what he meant.

Alas! Those of little learning hold diverse erroneous views in confusion. I send these words to fellow seekers of the Way: those who wish to attain buddhahood must thoroughly understand the coarse and subtle, root and branches, so that they can abandon the branches and return to the root, reversing the light to illuminate the mind-source. When the coarse is exhausted and the subtle is eliminated, the numinous nature manifests, comprehending all dharmas: this is called the dharma-body and reward-body. Responding and manifesting without limit: this is called the transformation-body buddha.
