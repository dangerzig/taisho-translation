# 佛說法律三昧經
## Sūtra on the Dharma-Vinaya Samādhi

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0631

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was traveling in the land of Magadha, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, as well as bodhisattvas, the four assemblies of disciples, devas, humans, nāgas, spirits, and the whole great congregation.

The Buddha addressed the assembly: "There is a dharma-vinaya samādhi. Bodhisattvas who study it should use harmony and compliance to regulate their temperament, entering deeply into its subtleties without becoming careless. Why is this? Those who have not entered deeply do not recognize the relative weight of merit in the three trainings. If they become self-indulgent, they lose their original purpose. You should know that such people commit twelve kinds of offenses that bring great blame from which there is ultimately no recovery. What are the twelve?

"There are those who study the Way but do not find an illumined teacher. Though their learning and experience are not yet broad, they become self-important, seeking reputation and fame. They slander the bodhisattva dharma, saying it is unnecessary. They recklessly fabricate false claims, saying 'My teacher said this.' Having already brought themselves to ruin, they then bring ruin to others as well. This is like the parable of a monkey poisoning the broth: intending only to harm others, it does not realize it kills itself. This is the first self-burning.

"There are those who study the bodhisattva dharma treasury and hear the profound sūtras, but have never questioned a teacher and do not understand their meaning and purpose. They interpret them according to their own whims, and they slight accomplished bodhisattvas. This is the second self-burning.

"There are those who have already studied the profound sūtras but then fall back midway into the path of the śrāvaka vehicle. They mock and ridicule the great Way, calling it roundabout and far off. This is the third self-burning.

"There are those whose study has not gone deep but who merely rely on the Way to preserve their livelihood. Not understanding its principles, they devote themselves to slandering and disparaging the profound sūtras and the great dharma. This is the fourth self-burning.

"There are those who study but lack genuine intent. They merely wish to find a comfortable place, adorning themselves with false pretense. When they obtain fine passages from the sūtras, they give them to the wrong people, causing those who encounter them to become flattering and insincere, sneering and claiming it is not so. They gather together deceptive appearances, fabricating slanders both great and small, hoping for praise. This is the fifth self-burning.

"There are those who are dull and ignorant, jealous of the worthy and capable. They constantly harbor poisonous thoughts toward dharma teachers, caring nothing for their virtue, but coveting only material offerings. This is the sixth self-burning.

"There are those who first enter study and receive instruction from an illumined teacher, yet do not remember the teacher's kindness. Instead, when others are present, they cleverly claim 'I knew it myself.' Since they neither understand on their own nor are willing to ask the teacher about the sūtras, this ingratitude carries no small blame. This is the seventh self-burning.

"There are those who have aroused the bodhisattva aspiration and wish to study the Buddha's sūtra path, but who have followed a teacher only briefly and are not yet familiar with proper conduct. Having heard the teacher's explanations, which contain skillful means, they have not yet grasped the intent, yet they recklessly embellish with empty rhetoric, interpreting the dharma incorrectly. Furthermore, with subtle criticism they scrutinize the teacher's conduct, considering their own views to be right. Thereupon they lose their skillful wisdom and fall into Māra's net. This is the eighth self-burning.

"There is the matter of abandoning one's commitment in study. What does this mean? It means that after receiving from an illumined teacher an understanding of skillful wisdom, upon meeting those who have entered deeply, one does not frequently inquire of them. Growing lax midway, one comes to harbor poisonous thoughts, dwelling on the teacher's faults. Having already fallen into wrong dharma without being aware of it, one departs from the Way and loses wisdom. This is called abandoning one's commitment. This is the ninth self-burning.

"There are those who have come to understand subtle skillful wisdom, yet become disrespectful. They disregard the sūtras, make light of transgressions, and offer side explanations. In private they say: 'The teacher knows nothing. I have already studied under him, and his teachings are all wrong. From now on I should no longer have anything to do with him.' They cause the ignorant to trust their words. Such people have already drunk poison themselves and then give poison to others. This is the tenth self-burning.

"There are those who study the Way and obtain profound sūtras from an illumined teacher. When faithful bodhisattvas who practice with sincere hearts wish to study under them, they cut them off and refuse, calling them unworthy. Yet when there are mere śrāvakas who have never seen the sūtras and whose faith and discipline are not yet established, and these people ask for sūtras, they give them instead. They then explain them by means of worldly language and analogies, and they pass on these deceptive teachings to one another, causing the true Way to become diluted and faint. They bring great blame upon themselves and mislead others as well. This is the eleventh self-burning.

"There are those who study but lack genuine faith. Unable to discern true from false and without fear of wrongdoing, they flatter the powerful, and not understanding the Way, they follow along in disparaging the buddha-dharma and slandering the bhikṣu sangha. Such people not only cast themselves into the eight difficulties but increase others' wrongdoing as well. This is the twelfth self-burning.

"Those who commit these offenses cannot remove their blame through repentance. Those emerging from the three evil paths still have some hope. But these twelve types are exceedingly difficult to liberate."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "I have therefore expounded this dharma-vinaya samādhi. When you teach it to people, you must make them understand it clearly. They should guard their mental conduct and not commit great transgressions as vast as Mount Sumeru for the sake of minor merit as small as a hair's breadth. To long forfeit the Three Treasures and descend into the evil paths without end, all for the sake of craving a moment's pleasure and thereby bringing upon oneself severe suffering: this is beyond words."

When the Buddha spoke these words, all were moved and felt shame. The students all entered the sixth stage of the path. The entire assembly arose and spoke in unison, praising: "The Buddha has opened and revealed the great light for us and for those who will study in the future, enabling us to gain the eye of wisdom." All came forward and bowed their heads, vowing to receive the teaching upon their foreheads.

The Buddha said: "Listen now! Long ago, in the distant past, there was a bodhisattva named Yǒudàozhì ('Aspiring to the Way') who, together with one hundred and forty thousand people, aroused the bodhisattva aspiration under the Buddha Wéiluótí. Among them was one of the highest ability, named Xiánxíng ('Worthy Conduct'). At that time Yǒudàozhì took him as teacher and followed him through successive kalpas without deviating from his purpose, firmly and diligently persevering. Later this teacher attained buddhahood, with the name Shìtóubāo. Thereupon Yǒudàozhì received his prediction of buddhahood. But the rest all fell back into the śrāvaka path, and to this day they remain in the five destinies, still unable to escape."

Ānanda asked: "After those people saw the Buddha at that time, did they subsequently obtain the profound sūtras?"

The Buddha said: "They all obtained them. But they did not study diligently, did not inquire into the intermediate wisdoms, did not respectfully serve their teacher, and treated others' dharma lightly."

Ānanda asked again: "Having already aroused the great aspiration, how could they fall back?"

The Buddha said: "It was due to four things. First, in their original study they did not understand skillful means, they slighted their teacher and friends, lacked single-mindedness, and their resolve kept shifting. Second, they did not practice diligently, had no power in the Way, but only coveted fame and expected others' respect. Third, in serving their teacher they did not think of the hardships that must be endured to achieve accomplishment. They adorned themselves with empty arrogance and lacked genuine intent. Fourth, they were fond of studying the ways of outsiders and associating with those of wrong views. They took these heterodox techniques and compared them to the Buddha's profound sūtras, claiming the paths were equal. Because those one hundred and forty thousand people all harbored such attitudes, in subsequent lives they gradually fell back, moving far from the great Way. Only Yǒudàozhì, whose aspiration was great and whose mind was firm, followed and served Xiánxíng until his teacher attained buddhahood, and he then received his prediction."

When this was taught, one million, one hundred thousand devas and humans all aroused the bodhisattva aspiration.

The worthy Ānanda said: "As the Buddha has explained, those who study should be respectful, but when they instead become self-indulgent, they lose the root of the great Way. Should we not be careful! From now on, can those who are new to the study and wish to enter the dharma distinguish good from evil and not lose a good teacher and good friends to the very end?"

The Buddha said: "There are such people, but they are few. Most follow their old habits and cannot be transformed."

Ānanda also asked: "When ordinary people speak ill of each other, do they know that it is wrong?"

The Buddha said: "Foolish people in the world only see the faults of others and do not know their own faults. They see only their own goodness and not the goodness of others. Those who call themselves wise are all lacking in wisdom. Those who consider themselves illumined are in fact the most deluded. Those who say 'I understand the sūtras' are also confused. Those who claim to know the great dharma but do not serve a teacher cannot be trusted. The Buddha's wisdom is vast and immeasurable and cannot be fathomed. Those who have seen and heard just a little and consider it sufficient, using it for self-importance: how can they be called wise? Only those of the utmost learning who have entered deeply and who are close to a good teacher can be called truly wise. How can the foolish know that there exist people of illumined wisdom in the world? Only then can one distinguish the worthy from the foolish.

"Those who are ignorant and deluded only see others' arrogance and do not know their own arrogance. Those who can see their own faults, you may speak to them of good things. Those who see only their own goodness, you cannot discuss with them. Why? Because they all consider themselves right. Those who can analyze difficulties, you may discuss the Way with them; if they cannot, you only increase their pride. Those who are self-satisfied, you should not speak to them of patience, for they will never be able to accept it. Those whose understanding of the Way's meaning is humble and modest, you may discuss the essentials of the profound sūtras with them; otherwise they will only become more bound. Those who understand the subtle and have entered deeply, you may discuss with them matters beyond beginning and end; otherwise they will remain in doubt.

"Those who say 'I know the bodhisattva dharma' but whose words betray attachment, who say 'my practice is pure' but do not know their own defilement, who have barely begun to give and already claim they have finished, who can detect Māra's doings but do not know they are themselves entirely within Māra's net: they are like silkworms making cocoons, entangling and wrapping themselves.

"If one wishes to fully perceive and discern the intentions, whether inner or outer, deep or shallow, one should ask a long-practiced, accomplished bodhisattva who is close to a good teacher. Only then can one clearly perceive Māra's doings."

The Buddha said: "Study must be precise, practice must be guarded. Those who forget their original purpose and fall into wrong dharma do so entirely because they follow their own notions and comply with a deviant mind. All the things one should not do and must not do, carelessly committing great transgressions and falling into the evil paths: these all come from obstinate foolishness, from not following the dharma's commands, from being struck by Māra, and the resulting blame is no small matter."

Thereupon the worthy Śāriputra addressed the Buddha, saying: "It is truly fitting that those who have newly aroused the aspiration should guard themselves. To lose the great for the sake of a small satisfaction: this is by no means rare! In the past, in a former life, I had such thoughts and lost the great to obtain the small. Though I wished to regret it, there was no way back."

The Buddha said: "Those whose original mind does not understand all share this experience."

Śāriputra asked the Buddha: "What is the root of a person? What is the root of the five aggregates? The root of the six sense bases? The root of the twelve links of dependent arising and the root of the entry of the ninety-six kinds of paths? What is the root of the four noble truths? The root of the śrāvaka? The root of the pratyekabuddha? The root of the tathāgata? How is each known? I once heard the Buddha say: 'Those who do not understand that the root is emptiness fall into the lesser paths.' Please distinguish and explain this for those who are new to the study!"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Your question is very good! It opens up much understanding. Let those who wish to hear, listen."

At that time everyone in the assembly said: "We accept the teaching."

The Buddha said: "The root of a person: there is nothing from which it arises, nothing that is received, no creator, no master. It has no form and no consciousness. It is neither born nor destroyed. To know the root in this way is correct; to lose this understanding is to depart from the root.

"The root of the five aggregates: they have no abiding place. Whatever one grasps at, that becomes an aggregate. Their formation and decay are like an illusion. Nothing in them is enduring. Those who understand it thus recognize that there are no aggregates.

"The root of the six sense bases: they are like an empty wilderness. Through contact with pleasurable objects, they are called 'entries.' Their entry is into empty space; there is no place where they accumulate. Those who know the root to be pure recognize that there is nothing that enters.

"The root of the twelve links of dependent arising: they have no beginning or end. They come from nowhere and go to nowhere. Ignorance cannot be seen, and what it conditions is without limit. Even through old age and death, it is like a dream, not real. To be as if understanding the patience of the non-arising of dharmas: this is knowing the root.

"The root of the entry of the ninety-six kinds of paths: they all arise from craving and desire, from the sixty-two views that abandon the inner and grasp the outer. What does it mean to abandon the inner and grasp the outer? The body is a transformed seed, empty and not real. Yet they seek existence by myriad means, unable to relinquish craving. Wishing to preserve and protect what exists, yet in time it must decay, and birth and death continue without end.

"The root of the four noble truths: they too have no root or stem. Suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path all arise through contemplative understanding. Those who see emptiness and purity know the root of the truths.

"The root of the śrāvaka: at first one contemplates the world as existent and does not understand that the root is emptiness. Weary of the suffering of birth and death, one gathers the mind and contemplates the dharma, cuts off the five aggregates, guards emptiness, practices purity, and when perception ceases and the outflows are exhausted, one then attains liberation. This is the root of the arhat.

"The root of the entry of the pratyekabuddha: from the time of arousing the aspiration, one does not serve a good teacher. One considers the world as existent, and one's practice is always attached. What does this attachment mean? The merit one cultivates is done in hopes of becoming a buddha and gaining the honored title, but without great compassion and without understanding skillful means. One calls the body existent, holds to perception when regarding the Buddha, delights in purity and adheres to the Way, and does not draw near to good friends. Though one accumulates merit as vast as the sands of the Ganges, it is still of no benefit. Without entering skillful wisdom, without cultivating the benefits, marks, and excellences, with practice only half complete, by understanding causes and conditions one attains buddhahood. This is the root of the pratyekabuddha.

"The root of the entry of the tathāgata: from the time of first arousing the aspiration, one sees that the body is entirely empty and all dharmas are pure. One understands the root of all beings, the entry of their wisdom and virtue, and that the aggregates, sense bases, elements, and supports are all fundamentally empty. One understands the entry of the fulfillment of all merits: established in accordance with the root, possessing wisdom, neither arising nor ceasing, neither going nor coming, not abiding in nirvāṇa yet not departing from fundamental emptiness, equal to space and immovable. This is the root of the entry of the tathāgata."

Śāriputra said: "The Buddha's meaning is truly marvelous, beyond what the assembly of arhats and pratyekabuddhas can know. We who are beginners all started from bondage and attachment. Our aspirations are meager and our learning shallow. Through the Buddha's aid we have crossed over, but our mental faculties are already extinguished. Though we hear the great Way, we no longer have the mind to study it. We are like country folk who hear of the affairs of the emperor: they briefly delight their ears, but can never emulate him. Even a person who has long dwelt in the three evil paths, if they emerge and study the great Way, can become a buddha. But for us arhats, the mind for it is gone. Those who are bodhisattvas are truly blessed beyond measure! Having already aroused the great aspiration and entered deeply into its breadth and profundity, they need only persevere diligently and carefully guard their practice."

Thereupon a bodhisattva named Yǒngshēng ('Courageous Voice') addressed the Buddha, saying: "The great Way is truly marvelous! It is not something that worldly talent and cleverness can know. Only those who have entered deeply can penetrate its subtleties. I fear that future students of the three vehicles, bodhisattvas who have newly aroused the aspiration, and those who practice the paths of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha may be confused and not yet awakened, unable to distinguish deep from shallow, and that having seen and heard just a little, they will consider themselves illumined, arguing and contending for victory, further deceiving one another. May the Buddha, in his compassion, resolve the great doubt. All these students alike practice meditation, yet the paths they attain differ. How can one distinguish the meditation of the śrāvaka, the pratyekabuddha, the bodhisattva, the mahāyāna, the various outside practitioners, and the five-superpower immortals?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! The great being wishes to protect all, and therefore raises this question. Though they all practice meditation, their aims and destinations differ.

"Those who study as śrāvakas have heard that there are four dhyānas. With concise and straightforward minds, they seek to quickly attain the Way. They do not extend their understanding to the other profound and subtle great dharmas. Dreading suffering and loathing the body, they concentrate single-mindedly on contemplation, seeking to cross over by themselves alone, without thought for sentient beings. They only guard their practice of cessation. What does 'cessation' mean? The merit they cultivate, the precepts they keep, and the diligence they practice are all done in hopes of reaching nirvāṇa, without knowing the great compassionate nirvāṇa from which the Buddha enters and exits. They say: 'Nirvāṇa is reached at one point, pervading the four dhyānas. By attaining the three knowledges, birth and death are cut off, and one has crossed beyond the world.' This is the meditation entered by the arhat.

"Those who study as pratyekabuddhas, from the time they first arouse the aspiration, do not serve a good teacher. They consider the world as existent, and their practice is always attached. What does this attachment mean? The merit they cultivate is done in hopes of becoming a buddha, yet they lack the great compassion, do not understand skillful means, and call the body existent. They hold to perception when regarding the Buddha, delight in purity and adhere to the Way, and do not draw near to good friends. Though they are not thoroughly familiar with the tathāgata's meditation, which is without thought, without perception, without seeing, and without attainment, within it they fix their minds and guard purity. Without knowing the dharma-meaning of skillful wisdom and non-action, by achieving meditation and understanding causes and conditions, they attain the Way. Their illumination surpasses the arhat, but does not reach the Buddha. They lack the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, and the eighteen distinctive qualities. This is the meditation entered by the pratyekabuddha.

"The bodhisattva's meditation: from the time of first arousing the aspiration, one does not leave an illumined teacher. One's learning is broad and wisdom deep, thoroughly understanding the root of meditation. What does it mean to thoroughly understand the root? The root of mind and dharmas is emptiness. The Way too is without root, without attachment, without bondage, without liberation, without practice, without exit, without entry, without abandoning, without grasping. One manifests great wisdom and the practice of skillful means, does not cut off the root of merit or the mind of great compassion, cultivates the marks and excellences, adorns the buddha-land, and perfects the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, and the eighteen distinctive qualities. One's seeing is all-encompassing, one's knowing is all-encompassing, and there is nothing of which one is not aware, and therefore one is called 'Buddha.' Because the world is full of craving and confused minds, the Buddha sat beneath the tree and closed his eyes, demonstrating the method of meditation, wishing to help those who can understand it use the Way to bind their minds, and also to give each one according to their delight what they need. This is the root of the meditation entered by the tathāgata.

"The meditation of the various outside practitioners with the five superknowledges: they prize non-action in their study but do not understand the essential point. They avoid the world and seek to secure themselves. Holding to perception and guarding the One, they close their eyes and relax their bodies, contemplate inwardly through the internal organs, preserve the spirit and guide the vital breath, nourish their nature and seek to ascend. When evil is eliminated and merit flourishes, they attain the five superknowledges through concentration. Their lifespan becomes exceedingly long, and they are called immortals. Their practice reaches its limit here. They do not know nirvāṇa, and afterward, when their merit is exhausted, birth and death continue without end. This is the meditation of the five-superpower outsiders."

The Buddha said: "As for the śrāvaka and the pratyekabuddha, though they attain nirvāṇa, they do not know the root. Why is this? Their study originally assumed the world is existent and the Way is non-existent, and therefore they destroyed the five aggregates to achieve cessation. Only the tathāgata truly knows the root of nirvāṇa. Why is this? Because he knows that the mundane and the Way and all dharmas are fundamentally empty, established in accordance with the root, with nothing arising and nothing ceasing. This is nirvāṇa. The Buddha's meaning is thus, and therefore he is called 'Tathāgata.' The śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha are called 'those of complete cessation.' Bodhisattvas should understand the profound and subtle great dharma, study it with clear precision, and even when apart from an illumined teacher, keep their minds pure and never become careless."

Thereupon Yǒngshēng pressed his palms together and addressed the Buddha: "To encounter a buddha is truly rare! The great sage's great compassion delivers beings beyond all measure. Today, through the Buddha's kindness, I have no more doubts. Those who wish to study should accept the Buddha's teaching and not engage in unworthy conduct that gives Māra an opening. As the Buddha has spoken, it will never be otherwise. Neither Māra the celestial nor his retinue can destroy one who practices purity." Having spoken, he came forward and bowed at the Buddha's feet.

At that time, two million, one hundred thousand devas and humans all joyfully established themselves in the patience of the non-arising of dharmas.

The worthy Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "What is the meaning of this dharma-vinaya?"

The Buddha said: "This is the dissection and resolution of the mind of the Way, the elucidation of the root of human wisdom and virtue and their entry, the distinction of the practices and mental conduct of the śrāvaka, the pratyekabuddha, and the bodhisattva, and the knowledge of what is precise and what is imprecise in these essential matters. It is called the dharma-vinaya samādhi."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! Those who have faith in and understand this sūtra have all already heard about skillful means from the buddhas of the ten directions."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, all who had come to the assembly rejoiced, and each came forward to pay respect to the Buddha before departing.
