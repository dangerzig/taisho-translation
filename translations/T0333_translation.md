# 佛說大乘日子王所問經
## Mahāyāna Sūtra on the Questions of King Udayana, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0333

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmadeva (法天)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Kauśāmbī, in the Ghositārāma, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus and bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

At that time, the woman Anupamā Māgandikā gave rise to anger and hatred, harboring jealousy toward the queen Śyāmāvatī. She went to King Udayana and said: "Does the prince know? Queen Śyāmāvatī and her five hundred women have been conducting illicit affairs with the śramaṇas. I now tell you this; the prince must know. It is truly intolerable."

At that time, King Udayana, having heard what the woman Anupamā Māgandikā said, was enraged to the extreme. Unable to restrain himself, he resolved to kill Queen Śyāmāvatī. He took his bow in hand, drew an arrow, and shot at her. At that moment, Queen Śyāmāvatī entered the samādhi of loving-kindness. The arrow the king had shot rose into the sky, burst into flames of great intensity, turned around, and came back to rest beside King Udayana on his left side. The Buddha secretly protected him, so the arrow did not strike his body and caused no harm.

At that time, King Udayana was seized with terror; every hair on his body stood on end. He fell to the ground, then rose again. He addressed Queen Śyāmāvatī in verse:

> "Are you a celestial maiden, a ghost-maiden, a rākṣasī,
> Or a gandharva maiden? I ask you, tell me.
> What practice do you uphold? I have never seen, never heard,
> Nor ever known of this.
> Never has there been a woman
> Whom weapons could not harm.
> I have great skill and strength,
> Well trained in the bow and arrow.
> My arrows never miss their mark;
> Never have I loosed one in vain.
> Whatever I have aimed to shoot,
> Whether monkey or bird in flight,
> A human body or a target,
> I have never failed to hit.
> Yet my arrow today turned back
> And came to rest before my face,
> Without harming my body.
> I now bow to you;
> I pray, save me from this suffering.
> Hear my honest words:
> I intended to kill you.
> Forgive my offense and do not be angry.
> Think of this as a good thing
> And free me from this affliction.
> Never again will I do such a thing."

Then Queen Śyāmāvatī answered King Udayana in verse:

> "I am not a celestial maiden,
> Nor a gandharva maiden,
> Nor a ghost, nor a rākṣasī.
> I am Śyāmāvatī.
> I am a disciple of the Buddha,
> And for the sake of his great compassion,
> My mind practices goodness.
> I generated the aspiration at the World-Honored One's place.
> For koṭis of hundreds of thousands of kalpas,
> I observed his supreme compassion;
> Therefore I practice the practice of loving-kindness.
> A man bound by female beauty,
> Who encounters one who sees things as they are,
> Is like ripened grain
> Destroyed by thunder and hail.
> If one leaves the stain of female beauty,
> Both I and they find joy.
> All the World-Honored Ones
> Are far removed from sensual desire.
> I have also heard the World-Honored One say:
> 'The Buddhas and bodhisattvas,
> Pratyekabuddhas and śrāvakas,
> All leave female beauty behind.
> The foolish cannot understand this,
> And are universally conquered by Māra.
> Leave the stain of female beauty,
> And the body attains peace and happiness,
> Ultimately attaining liberation.'
> Beings without wisdom
> Never depart from the love of desire.
> They create boundless offenses
> And fall into the three evil destinies,
> A bottomless pit of desire's fire,
> With fierce flames that never go out.
> The wise delight in liberation
> And are not stained by female beauty.
> Seeing her, one is immediately bound;
> Her deceitful words bring false pleasure,
> Dragging sentient beings down.
> At death, one enters the perilous evil paths.
> Do not listen to women's words,
> And do not be angry at me.
> May your intention give rise to joy;
> Generate the aspiration at the World-Honored One's place.
> If you wish to see the Buddha,
> I shall go with the great king.
> When you arrive, listen carefully;
> He will surely teach the wondrous Dharma."

Then King Udayana said to Queen Śyāmāvatī: "Now is the right time. Please go swiftly to the World-Honored One's place."

The king, together with his ministers, attendants, and retinue, went and saw the Great Sage, majestic and imposing like a great golden mountain, with radiant flames of light, auspicious and adorned. He also saw the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, the bhikṣus and bhikṣuṇīs, the upāsikās and upāsakas, the devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans and non-humans, all surrounding the World-Honored One.

Then the great king prostrated at the Buddha's feet and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Something unprecedented has happened to me, something never before seen or heard. I have come to the Buddha's place. I pray the World-Honored One will compassionately explain it for me."

The World-Honored One said: "Great king! Tell me of this unprecedented event."

The king further said: "Today in my palace, due to a cause involving sensual desire, the woman Anupamā Māgandikā, with a slanderous mind, said: 'Queen Śyāmāvatī has been conducting illicit affairs with the śramaṇa śrāvakas.' When I heard these words, I was enraged to the extreme and shot an arrow to kill Queen Śyāmāvatī. But from the arrow, flames blazed forth in dazzling intensity, and the arrow turned back to rest beside my body on the left, without harming me."

Queen Śyāmāvatī then prostrated at the World-Honored One's feet and said: "The king asked me: 'Are you a celestial maiden, a nāga maiden, a gandharva maiden, a piśācī, a ghost-maiden, or a rākṣasī? What practice do you uphold, that such a thing could happen?' I answered the king: 'I am the great king's queen. I am not a celestial maiden, nor a nāga maiden, nor a gandharva maiden, nor a piśācī, nor a rākṣasī.' World-Honored One! The disciples of the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, are good and pure of mind, like the celestials."

Then Queen Śyāmāvatī praised the World-Honored One's merit: "Our Buddha, the Tathāgata, possesses such right and perfect enlightenment, such great compassion and great kindness, fully endowed with great merit and wisdom, accomplished in great awesome virtue, and having attained great mastery. Why is this? Because the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, is the teacher of devas and humans. I pray he will witness this sincerity."

Then King Udayana, before the Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus, confessed and revealed his offenses: "We are like foolish children, like ones with confused minds, like ones in darkness, like ones without good roots. I thought of the Tathāgata's śrāvakas and gave rise to slander. Excellent, World-Honored One! I pray you will dispel our doubts and regrets. We shall receive and uphold the teaching."

The World-Honored One said: "Generate the mind of loving-kindness, receive and uphold the precepts, and broadly benefit sentient beings."

King Udayana then rose from his seat and joined his palms in reverence.

The Buddha said: "Great king! Simply sit at ease."

Then King Udayana prostrated at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side. King Udayana addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! My mind was impetuous. I listened to a woman's words and created the karma of sinful suffering. I know that at the end of my life I shall fall into hell. Excellent, World-Honored One! I pray you will explain the faults of women."

The World-Honored One said: "Women's karmic activities are of many kinds. They deceive sentient beings, feigning different appearances. They are flattering, deceitful, and dishonest; their minds are not genuine. They think with inverted and crooked falseness, acting and moving to aggressively seek intimacy, binding sentient beings and always practicing wrong conduct. You must be aware of this."

The king said: "World-Honored One! I pray you will grant your compassion and allow me to say: from now on I shall not go near women, nor shall I create sinful karma because of women. Having not created sin, I shall not fall into hell. World-Honored One! May I, throughout the long night, benefit and bring peace to all sentient beings."

The Buddha said: "It is so, it is so."

Furthermore, King Udayana said: "World-Honored One! From the Buddha I have heard this teaching on desire. It is truly because of women that one receives evil retribution."

The Buddha said: "Great king! Female beauty runs deep. It produces parents who are one's enemies, parents who are violent and cruel. If one gives rise to delight in it, one falls into hell. Therefore, women have such faults."

The Buddha said: "Great king! If we measure a man's faults regarding sensual desire, your future parents and relatives also share in the fault."

King Udayana addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Excellent, excellent! Please explain for me a man's faults, and how it is that parents and relatives also share in the fault."

The Buddha said: "Great king! Listen carefully, listen carefully! Reflect upon it well! I shall explain for you."

The World-Honored One said: "Great king! A man's faults regarding sensual desire are of four kinds."

The great king asked: "What are the four faults?"

The World-Honored One answered: "Great king! If a man is addicted to sensual desire, intoxicated by desire, and because of that intoxication, his thoughts are inverted, and because of that inversion, he develops deep attachment to women, then toward bhikṣus of virtuous conduct among the sangha, toward śramaṇas and brahmins who uphold the precepts, he does not wish to see or hear them. Because he does not wish to see or hear śramaṇas and brahmins of virtuous conduct, he neither draws near to them, nor takes refuge in them, nor makes offerings to them. Toward śramaṇas and brahmins without precepts or conduct, he also keeps his distance. Further, he has no root of faith, does not cultivate virtue, does not practice generosity, is entirely without wisdom, is deficient in learning and thin in virtue, is arrogant and conceited, and practices the ways of ghosts and spirits. He also associates with the unwise, is attached to evil dharmas, delights in foulness, distances himself from good friends. Even if born among the heavens or in the human world, he constantly criticizes his own life and destiny. Toward śramaṇas and brahmins, he does not perform fire-offerings. He distances himself from the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha. He turns his back on the fruit of nirvāṇa and its virtues. He constantly thinks of women, singing girls, dancing, drinking, and idle talk. Thus bound, he reaches the end of his life and falls into the hells of Saṃghāta, Kālasūtra, Saṃjīva, Raurava, Mahāraurava, Tapana, Pratāpana, and Avīci, undergoing all manner of suffering. Emerging from hell, he is born in Yama's realm. In the animal realm he is born as a lion, tiger, wolf, or other evil beast. He is even born as a garuḍa body, dwelling upon an iron-thorned tree. Great king! If a man practices such foolish conduct, he receives this evil retribution. This is the first fault of a man."

Then the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Sensual desire, foul and impure at its root,
> After the deed, karmic suffering deepens.
> The wise Dharma-teacher condemns the stain of desire:
> Even one's parents of the next life gain nothing.
> It is like a vast pit of filth,
> Filled to the brim with dung and stench,
> Or like a bloated corpse in a charnel ground;
> Those addicted to desire are just like this.
> Like flies and gnats sucking at sores,
> Like donkeys and horses rolling in dung,
> Like pigs and dogs eating stinking fish:
> Those who indulge in women are just like this.
> They destroy their good name and virtuous conduct,
> Always breaking precepts and lacking shame.
> They are not born in the celestial realms but fall to Avīci.
> Therefore the Dharma-teacher condemns love and desire.
> Like one who mistakenly drinks a lethal poison,
> Delirious and raving, the whole body in pain,
> Not perceiving impermanence, struck by the poison:
> Those addicted to desire are just like this.
> Delighting in fine flavors and exquisite foods,
> Loving to hear songs and yearning for beauty,
> Forgetting household affairs and losing their memory,
> They only create causes for the wheel of suffering.
> Attached to desire, always praising it,
> Not understanding the deluded bag of filth that is the body.
> Day and night they always practice base conduct;
> With scant merit, they sink and turn in the evil destinies.
> Praising desire and practicing what is not practice,
> Full of anger and increasing in delusion,
> Like walking on a perilous, towering cliff,
> Not realizing that in an instant they will lose their lives.
> Once parted from the Jambudvīpa world,
> They die and sink into the boundless ocean of karma,
> Surrounded by five peaks and iron mountains,
> Where the light of sun, moon, and lamps is never seen.
> Like the wind, wild and confused, without understanding,
> Going back and forth and astray, losing the path.
> Reborn in such a way, always dwelling there,
> Not respected by anyone in the world,
> And all manner of good deeds never arise.
> Even if they have sons or daughters, there is no filial respect.
> Turning their backs on their own parents, committing the five heinous crimes,
> Harmoniously caring for wife and daughters with redoubled devotion,
> They spread nets of sin without any thought,
> Falling into the pit of greed, delusion, and desire for pleasure.
> Parents are left far behind, never to return.
> Not thinking of the hardship of being raised and nourished,
> Heedless and addicted to desire, attached to amusement,
> They increase each other's afflictions all the more.
> Destroying their cultivation, doubting everything,
> Never meeting sages and saints to serve as good causes,
> Delighting in wrong conduct, undergoing extreme suffering,
> Heedless of punishment, shame, and disgrace.
> Quarreling, cheating, robbing, and killing,
> Squandering wealth and losing good friends,
> They are not born in the celestial realms or the human world,
> But at death enter Avīci Hell.
> An iron forest of blue blades with pointed edges,
> A fierce blaze of burning embers, a city of raging fire,
> Sword-trees and knife-mountains spread across the ground,
> Molten copper and hot iron for food and drink:
> Such great suffering comes from sensual desire,
> Which conceals and destroys the root of bodhi and wisdom.
> You should be fearful of women;
> Do not let them draw near or become entangled.
> If you are in accord with the good paths of devas and humans,
> Before long, bodhi will be attained of itself.

"Furthermore, great king! When parents give birth to a child, the matter is extremely difficult; this the world knows well. During pregnancy, they bear the burden for ten months, with all manner of pain and suffering, difficulty in rising and sitting, and moderation in eating and drinking. Even when they finally give birth, it is like slaughtering a pig or sheep. They disregard their own bodies, caring only for the child, nursing and raising it, never letting it leave their embrace. The soiling of urine and excrement they themselves wash away. As the child gradually grows, they instruct with sincere words in every way, having it learn the arts and skills of Jambudvīpa: writing, calculation, commerce, trade, and all manner of occupations. Further, they seek to bring it physical and mental comfort, generously providing wealth and belongings, choosing suitable relatives, and arranging a marriage. They hope the child will be filial toward parents, serve and care for them. But instead the child's mind becomes deranged, wholly infatuated, deeply attached to sensual desire, without any awakening. He even secretly takes wives and concubines from other families, and they become attached to each other. Toward his own parents, he becomes unfilial and disrespectful. When his father later grows old and frail, thin and weak, blind and deaf, having difficulty rising and sitting, needing support, the son feels aversion, disdain, and dislike, and oppresses his father in all manner of ways, driving him out of the house. He takes in the wives from outside families and gathers in the home for all kinds of revelry."

The Buddha told the great king: "If a man practices such wrong conduct, turning his back on his parents, he will certainly fall into Avīci Hell at the end of his life, with no prospect of escape. This is the second fault. If a good man leaves behind female beauty, keeps his mind pure, and serves and makes offerings to his parents, practicing filial conduct, then after death he will not fall into the evil destinies but will be born among the devas, receiving happiness and joy. When his celestial merit is exhausted and he descends to the human world, he will not suffer poverty or low station, but will enjoy prosperity and good fortune."

Then the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Leaving desire, practicing compassion and filial piety,
> At death one is born in the celestial realms.
> As Brahmā or Śakra,
> One always receives happiness.
> Serving and making offerings to aged parents,
> In the next life, born in the human world,
> Going to sea as a merchant,
> One peacefully obtains precious treasures.
> Serving and making offerings to aged parents:
> This is the most supreme of all virtues.
> When planted fields yield ripe fruit,
> Such merit cannot be measured.
> Serving and making offerings to aged parents,
> One never carries a heavy burden,
> Always served by donkeys and horses;
> Blades cannot harm one.
> Serving and making offerings to aged parents,
> One does not cross salt-water rivers;
> Raging fire and weapons
> Also cannot come near.
> Serving and making offerings to aged parents,
> One always gains a good wife and children.
> Grain, wheat, and resources,
> Lapis lazuli and gold treasures.
> Serving and making offerings to aged parents,
> One always dwells in celestial palaces,
> Surrounded on all four sides
> By countless pleasure gardens.
> Serving and making offerings to aged parents,
> One always hears the sound of the Buddha's Dharma,
> Endowed with marks and beautiful in form:
> Who would not revere such a person?

"Furthermore, great king! If that man practices unlawful deeds, if his mind is not genuine, if he is always given to wrong views, if he does not know what is good, if he falsely gives rise to inversions, if he is always praised by fools while the wise are always angered, then his sinful karma deepens and he permanently loses the great benefit. In a buddha-age he will never encounter the Buddha. He is arrogant and conceited, poor and of low station; the multitude does not delight in him. This is the third fault of a man."

Then the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "A man addicted to sensual desire,
> With inverted discrimination of self,
> Deluded, his sinful karma deep,
> Turns in the wheel and falls into the evil destinies.
> Far from the Buddha's merit,
> Without wisdom to discern,
> Falsely seeking happiness,
> Like seeking a reed on a riverbank.
> The fool who is addicted to desire,
> Flattering and full of deceit,
> Hopes for unlawful pleasure
> But instead creates the suffering of hell.
> Attached to desire, with inverted views,
> Base and ignorant of himself,
> Like one in the darkness of night
> Who cannot tell the path from what is not.
> Without shame, faith, or a root of goodness,
> He only indulges in sound, beauty, and taste.
> He never makes offerings to bodhisattvas or śrāvakas.
> Even when meeting one who practices rightly,
> Who broadly proclaims the subtle sound,
> He slights the Dharma and will not listen,
> And sinks into the hells,
> Never again to receive a human body.
> He cuts off the practices of generosity and the rest,
> Lost and deluded, not cultivating them.
> The supreme bodhi is lost.

"Furthermore, great king! If men, for the sake of their own livelihood and because of attachment to desire, are closed off by delusion and ignorance, they engage in all manner of arts and trades: writing, calculation, praising, debating, and eloquent discussion; they seek proximity to kings and ministers, practicing unlawful conduct, punishing sentient beings, deceiving in all manner of ways, broadly seeking wealth, and creating all sorts of evil deeds. Further, for the sake of livelihood, they practice wrong livelihood: trading in oxen, donkeys, camels, horses, pigs, sheep, chickens, and dogs, even to the point of casting spells on dragons, trapping rabbits, and butchering. Or they go on trading voyages without choosing the road, traveling perilous paths, foul paths, and roads of bandits and swords, even crossing the great ocean, enduring cold, heat, hunger, thirst, and all manner of suffering in pursuit of wealth. Toward śramaṇas and brahmins they are stingy and unwilling to give, always attached to desire. Further, they are conquered and driven about by women like servants, always together for long periods, never parting, talking and conversing and gazing at each other, deeply attached. Therefore, by keeping women, at the end of their lives they fall together into hell. This is the fourth fault."

Then the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "The man who pursues and clings to desire,
> Intoxicated: when has he ever been happy?
> Base, falsely pursuing,
> How can he attain peace and happiness?
> Not the deeds of a true man,
> He acts without knowing it is wrong.
> Shameless like a camel or donkey,
> He cannot bear the extreme filth.
> This man has little wisdom,
> Not realizing the depth of the root of sin.
> He rushes toward women
> Like a dog toward excrement.
> Foul and unpleasant,
> Yet cherished by the deluded.
> Not knowing the fault of desire,
> He is like a blind man who cannot see colors.
> The fool attached to desire
> Is like a dog rushing at filth.
> He clings to sound, smell, taste, and touch;
> His attachment is also like this.
> The fool attached to desire
> Turns in the various destinies,
> Like a monkey tied to a post:
> He never escapes the three realms.
> The deluded man attached to desire
> Is like a crow that clings to rotten meat,
> Always dragged by evil Māra,
> Falling into the evil destinies.
> The fool who craves sweet flavors,
> Entangled by beauty,
> Is no different from a worm in a latrine:
> How could it know the impurity?
> The wise one attains liberation;
> Female beauty must not be touched.
> Seeing her, terror arises,
> And one casts her off like a putrid corpse.
> The fool, confused and scattered,
> Attached to desire without letting go,
> Is like one walking the scorching road in hardship,
> Parched, yet drinking salt water.
> Seeing such a drinker:
> The fool, deluded, loses his life.
> One firmly attached to desire
> Has faults also like this.
> Truly, this woman
> Is like a body covered in festering sores
> With worms that feed upon it;
> Lust is also like this.
> A woman adorned with ornaments
> Is like a painted jar filled with dung.
> One only sees the outside;
> Who knows the impurity within?
> Or like an oil-soaked cloth
> Hung upon one's body:
> The adornment of a woman
> Is also just as staining.
> Like a cloth draped over a sword,
> Or fire hidden beneath light ash:
> The adornment of a woman
> Harms in the same way.
> Or like when the kalpa-fire arises
> And the great earth burns through and through,
> Grass and trees are seen no more,
> Rivers and seas dry up.
> The dwelling-places of bhūtas,
> Sumeru and the iron ring mountains,
> The six desire-heavens and the first dhyāna:
> All are destroyed. Who can save them?
> Just so, one addicted to female beauty,
> With the fire of desire blazing fiercely,
> Burns sentient beings:
> The harm cannot be rescued.
> The human body is swiftly impure,
> Made of foul substances:
> Nails and hair,
> Saliva and earwax,
> Filthy sweat and excrement,
> Fat, grease, and membranes,
> Skin, flesh, and bone marrow,
> Pus, blood, sinews, and veins,
> Spleen, kidneys, heart, and lungs,
> Intestines, stomach, gallbladder, and liver,
> The lower and upper digestive tracts,
> Red phlegm and white phlegm.
> Further, eighty thousand
> Subtle worms feed upon it,
> Always dwelling within the body.
> How could the fool be aware?
> He gives rise to lust for this body
> Like a fly craving pus and blood.
> The stench he perceives as fragrance;
> Suffering, he takes for pleasure.
> Such a man addicted to desire,
> Wielding a stick, strikes at others.
> The fire of desire rushes in to burn him;
> Intoxicated, who can awaken him?
> The fool attached to the taste of pleasure
> Is like a dog in an empty house,
> Or like the sound of a drum:
> In the end, it is all just delusion.
> Or like a monkey
> Always clinging to and climbing trees,
> Even up to the moment of impermanence,
> Never leaving the treetop.
> Just so, the man who craves desire,
> Pursuing the realm of form,
> Falls into the evil destinies,
> Never leaving the suffering of birth and death.
> The fool addicted to desire,
> After dying in that place,
> Is cast into an iron cauldron.
> Thus he remains for one kalpa.
> Floating and sinking like beans being boiled.
> The size of that cauldron
> Is sixty-four koṭis.
> The sentient beings dwelling there:
> Each one who falls in
> Is beyond reckoning.
> Boiled in the cauldron,
> They suffer for a hundred kalpas.
> Or two, or three, or four kalpas,
> According to the weight of their karma.
> Skin and flesh all rot away,
> Bones appear white as conch.
> Again, by the force of their own karma,
> Grasping sharp pointed hooks,
> They are cast onto flaming iron troughs;
> After dying, they come back to life.
> Again, the hell-wardens,
> Wielding iron pestles, pound them.
> Bones and marrow are all ground to dust;
> Wind blows, and they come back to life.
> Or they are struck with iron rods,
> Split open as if by an axe.
> Three, four, five iron beasts
> Follow behind, biting and gnawing.
> Further, there are iron crows,
> Iron dogs, and jackals,
> With teeth and beaks sharp as swords,
> That eat the brains and marrow of sinners.
> If a person has created sinful karma,
> They fall into a river of excrement,
> Or fall upon sword-blades,
> Where all is foul.
> If a person has created sinful karma,
> They fall into Extreme Heat,
> Raurava, Mahāraurava,
> Kālasūtra, and the Burning.
> If a person has created sinful karma,
> They fall into the Ash River,
> Layer upon layer, ever increasing;
> The pain and suffering are unendurable.
> If a person has created sinful karma,
> After death they fall into hell,
> Where, starving, they swallow hot iron pellets,
> And when thirsty, drink molten copper.
> If a person has created sinful karma,
> They fall between iron mountains,
> Which all close in at once,
> Crushing the body to powder.
> If a person has created sinful karma,
> They constantly suffer affliction.
> Having received such a result,
> There is no one who can save them.
> This is no place of happiness;
> It is summoned by past-life karma.
> Parents, wife, and children:
> How can they come to the rescue?
> The base practice of sensual desire
> Leads straight to Avīci.
> The suffering endured is unbearable:
> The Buddhas of all three ages have said so.
> Therefore, the base man
> Who dwells with women
> Is like carrying a bag of dung:
> A fool who travels everywhere.
> A man bound by a woman
> Is as if bound by fetters and chains.
> Hell-fire burns his body,
> And he can never find peace.
> In an instant, wisdom arises;
> Hearing the Buddha's Dharma like this,
> One leaves behind all desire
> And, going forth, attains liberation."

The Buddha told the great king: "If a man practices the conduct of desire, he will fall into hell and suffer these great agonies. Therefore, great king! Always be mindful of the Buddha and the Dharma. Observe your body and mind; do not let faults arise."

King Udayana said: "It is so, World-Honored One!" At the Tathāgata's place he generated deep faith, and said: "World-Honored One! How rare! The Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, has well explained the faults of women and of men. I shall receive and uphold this teaching. I take refuge in the Buddha, take refuge in the Dharma, take refuge in the assembly of bhikṣus. From now on I shall abandon the faults of desire, weapons, and the rest, and with compassion I shall benefit all sentient beings."

When these words were spoken, King Udayana, together with all the bhikṣus, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, asuras, garuḍas, gandharvas, mahoragas, humans and non-humans in the assembly, hearing what the Buddha taught, all greatly rejoiced, paid their respects, and departed.
