# 禪要經
## Sūtra of the Essentials of Meditation

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0609

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

## Chapter One: Reproaching Desire

When a practitioner seeks the Way and wishes to cultivate concentration, the Dharma-teacher should, according to the practitioner's faculties and dispositions, practice the four methods of attraction: showing, teaching, benefiting, and gladdening, and broadly purifying faith and the precepts. Having purified faith and the precepts, one should next remove the six desires: desire for form, desire for appearance, desire for bearing, desire for voice, desire for touch, and desire for a person's characteristics. For those attached to the first five desires, one should have them contemplate the repulsive characteristics that can be found in each. For those attached to the desire for personal characteristics, one should have them contemplate the skeleton as segmented and dismembered. Contemplating a complete corpse can sever two desires: desire for bearing and desire for voice. If one contemplates a decomposing corpse, all six desires are severed.

There are two kinds of contemplation of the accessible impurities. The first: the stench and rot of a dead corpse is impure; my own body's impurity is also just like this. Having contemplated thus, aversion arises in the mind. Having grasped this characteristic, go to a quiet place, whether among mountains, marshes, or burial grounds, in an empty dwelling or beneath a tree, and contemplate your own impurity wherever it can be found. Fix the mind upon the body and do not let it scatter. The second is hearing the Dharma and recollecting through imagination. Discriminate and contemplate the thirty-six things within the body: hair of the head, hair of the body, nails, teeth, mucus, tears, saliva, spittle, sweat, grime, fat, grease, skin, membranes, muscle, flesh, sinews, veins, marrow, brain, heart, liver, spleen, kidneys, lungs, stomach, intestines, belly, bladder, gall, phlegm, rheum, living organs, pus, blood, feces, urine, and various worms. Impure and foul, assembled into a body, transmigrating through the five destinies, blazing with every suffering, like a floating corpse drifting east and west on the current: wherever it arrives, all things are loathsome.

Again reflect: my body has bones as pillars, flesh as plaster, sinews binding it together, blood irrigating it. It is like a wound, like a poison. The skin and hair with nine openings serve as doorways. The intestines, stomach, bladder, and membranes serve as storerooms. Jealousy, arrogance, and evil thoughts are taken as the body. Craving and seeking are insatiable, like a ravine. Therefore the practitioner should remove the three desire-conceptions. When receiving the offerings of the faithful, think of them as fire and poison. Think of saving the worms within. Think of tying a dead corpse to oneself. Think that saliva, tooth-grime, and filth taint the flavors. Think that because I lack the wisdom of emptiness, I corrupt the conception of purity. Think that because of the causes and conditions of craving love, foul discharge is produced. Contemplating thus, with shame and remorse fully present, one can cross beyond birth and death and become a field of merit for the world.

If one contemplates the skeleton: the nail-bones of the two feet, the toe-bones, the instep-bones, the ankle-bones, the shin-bones, the knee-bones, the thigh-bones, the hip-bones, the waist-bones, the spine-bones, the neck-bones, the skull-bones, the jaw-bones; the nail-bones of the two hands, the finger-bones, the palm-bones, the wrist-bones, the forearm-bones, the elbow-bones, the upper-arm-bones; the chest-bones, the heart-bone, the tooth-bones, the rib-bones. Contemplate left and right as though seeing them with the eyes. Contemplate the external body that one is attached to in the same way. Three hundred and twenty bones prop one another up within; the skin-sack with nine openings leaks foulness without. Contemplating the body thus, it is like a dead corpse raised by a ghost: in walking, standing, speaking, and silence, it is always a dead corpse. Then upon this very body make the conception of a dead corpse, the conception of lividity, the conception of bloating, the conception of putrid decay, the conception of breaking apart, the conception of being smeared with blood, the conception of what is left after being eaten, the conception of worms crawling out, the conception of a chain of bones, the conception of dispersal, the conception of disintegration, and the conception that among all beings in the world there is nothing delightful.

If the mind becomes fearful, one should make the contemplation of emptiness through conditioned arising and falsity, the contemplation of there being nothing whatsoever as in a magical illusion, and the contemplation of the pure wisdom of emptiness in the ultimate sense. If the mind becomes lazy, one should reproach oneself, saying: "Old age, illness, and death are very close at hand. Life is like a flash of lightning, difficult to preserve even for a moment! A human body is hard to obtain, a good teacher is hard to encounter. The Buddha-Dharma is about to perish; right speech seems inverted, like a lamp at dawn, which although it exists is useless! Evil people who have left the household assist the worldly in destroying the Dharma. Craving, lust, and impure corruption cause the Way to decline and sour. Evil dharmas increase and the great darkness approaches. The causes and conditions that destroy concentration are extremely numerous. Internally there are the afflictions; externally there are Māra and Māra's minions, ghosts spreading epidemics and disasters across the desolate world, hostile adversaries spreading slander, and myriad vexations. The wheel of the eight sufferings revolves day and night without cease. My body is to be pitied, destined for this disaster. Against the thieves of affliction, I have not done the slightest damage. In the Dharma of dhyāna-concentration, I have not gained anything. Though I wear the Dharma-robes, I still think of the taste of desire. Inwardly I am truly empty, no different from a worldly person. The doors to all the evil destinies are wide open. Among all the good dharmas, I have not entered right concentration. Among all the evil dharmas, there is not one I have not committed. How can I cling to this sack of excrement and give rise to arrogance and self-indulgence, unable to be diligent and subdue my own mind? A wretched body like this is reproached by all the noble sages: impure and loathsome, with foulness flowing from the nine openings. If one craves this body, one is no different from an animal. After death, one is cast into the great darkness. Upon what will one then rely? Now that I have obtained a human body, if I cannot find the essential way out, and if I am born into the evil destinies, how will liberation ever come about?"

Having thus whipped the mind, return it to its original place. At other times, encourage and inspire it, causing the mind to feel joyful. "The Dharma-King of Liberation, whose life of wisdom is everlasting, whose supernatural radiance constantly illuminates the five destinies: his teaching of the Way is direct, easy to understand, and easy to practice. Since he is my teacher, I take refuge in him, offer incense and flowers, praise him, and my mind is at ease and joyful. Like relying on the Lord of the Devas and roaming the sky without fear, the great bodhisattvas and arhats are all my companions. Because they can subdue the mind like a cat controlling a mouse, their faculties are tamed and compliant, and the six supernatural powers are at their command. I too should be like this: I should subdue my mind and seek to escape birth and death. Like a prisoner in jail who looks in all four directions and sees only solid walls, with only a latrine-hole and no other way out; like a person who has been poisoned and can only be cured by excrement, with no other medicine." Having contemplated thus, carefully observe the impurities, and further make this reflection: "When first beginning practice, the mind often advances and retreats. The evil winds of the eight worldly dharmas blow and shatter my mind. If I were to attain the Way, my mind would be stable as a mountain; even the most exquisite of the five desires could not destroy it, how much less these wretched desires!" Just as the great Maudgalyāyana, after attaining arhatship, was approached by his wife, who brought entertainers, lavishly adorned herself, and tried to corrupt Maudgalyāyana. At that time Maudgalyāyana spoke a verse for her:

> "Your body stands on a frame of bones,
> Wrapped round with skin and flesh.
> Impurity fills the inside;
> Not a single thing within is fine.
> A skin-sack stuffed with feces and urine,
> With blood flowing always from nine openings:
> Like a ghost, utterly worthless.
> How can you esteem yourself?
> Your body is like a walking privy,
> Covered over with a thin skin.
> The wise cast it far away,
> As a person leaves a latrine.
> If people knew your body
> As I loathe and detest it,
> All would keep far away,
> As one avoids a cesspit.
> You adorn your body
> With fragrant flowers and necklaces.
> The foolish crave and love it;
> The wise are not beguiled.
> You are a heap of impurity,
> An assemblage of every foul thing.
> Though you wear precious, wondrous garments,
> It is like adorning a privy.
> Your ribs cling to the spine
> Like rafters leaning on beams.
> The five organs within the belly
> Are impure, like a chest of feces.
> I observe your impurities
> As though they were five-colored dung,
> Adorned with pearl necklaces:
> Beautiful outside, like a painted vase.
> If someone tried to dye the sky,
> No color could ever stick.
> Your trying to trouble me
> Is like a moth casting itself into fire.
> All the poisons of desire
> I have now completely extinguished.
> The five desires are far removed;
> Māra's net is shattered.
> My mind is like empty space,
> Attached to nothing at all.
> Even if heavenly desires came,
> They could not stain my mind.
> Falling into the worldly, born to suffering,
> Life is swift as a flash of lightning.
> When old age, illness, and death arrive,
> No power can stand against them.
> There are no kin to rely upon,
> No place to hide.
> Even heavenly blessings come to an end;
> How could a human life endure?
> Nothing is more fragile than life,
> Like wind blowing a floating cloud.
> The floating cloud dissolves so swiftly;
> Body and life are not long joined.
> When the body dies and the soul disperses,
> Know that this is not my body.
> Exert yourself and be diligent:
> Nothing is harder to obtain than a human birth.
> Birth and death are never cut off
> Because of craving desire and savoring flavors.
> Nourishing anger only builds the burial mound;
> In vain one endures every hardship.
> The body stinks like a dead corpse,
> With impurity flowing from nine openings.
> Like a privy-worm that delights in feces,
> The fool's craving for the body is no different."

> Though the bright one was within the palace,
> Amid the five desires and sensory pleasures,
> His aspiration found no delight in them;
> He constantly thought of secluded meditation.
> Day and night he gazed through the windows,
> And a deva appeared, palms joined, saying:
> "The time has come; you may now go.
> All the entertainers are asleep.
> The world has nothing worth delighting in;
> It is always accompanied by worry and vexation.
> Those joined in love and affection
> Must again face separation.
> Households weep for one another,
> Not knowing where the dead have gone.
> The wise one sees the truth of suffering
> And therefore goes forth to study the Way.
> The world's days of joy are few;
> Worry and vexation are far too many.
> How could one find this suffering as peace?
> One's own actions bring it, not another.
> Worldly people delight in love and affection;
> In the Way, these are seen as enemies.
> Wealth and honor are the root of suffering,
> Like a bird falling into a net.
> Human life is extremely swift;
> Five horses could not overtake it.
> One's remaining life diminishes daily.
> Let each person reflect upon this:
> Love and affection are together for a moment;
> What flourishes must decline.
> Therefore pull yourself free
> And, having attained the Way, return."
