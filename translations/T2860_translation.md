# Biography of Lord Cunhui

## *Fǔjūn Cúnhuì Zhuàn* (府君存惠傳)

Taishō 2860

Author unknown. A Dunhuang biographical inscription.

*Note: This is a Dunhuang commemorative text for a military official named Cunhui, who died in the fifth year of the Taiping Xingguo era of the Northern Song (980 CE). The text includes both a laudatory preface with verse and a formal biographical inscription tracing his ancestry to the Lǐ clan of Lǒngxī, who claimed descent from Laozi. He served in the Guīyì Army (Return-to-Allegiance Army) administration at Dunhuang.*

### Eulogy

Lord Cunhui (*Cúnhuì*, 存惠), styled Cháyuǎn (*Cháyuǎn*, 察遠), bore the epithet Huáiqì Yì (*Huáiqì Yì*, 懷氣義, "Cherishing Spirit and Righteousness"). From his youth he possessed warm refinement in the civil and martial arts. Uniting severity and leniency in his sincerity, he was skilled at riding and with the bow: when he drew the bow, the apes would cry out first. He was well versed in military formations and freely commanded both advance and capture. Thus among his peers he excelled, maneuvering with grace beyond the common rank. Whenever he served in an office or took up a post, he alone shone among the assembled worthies. With universal approval and public recommendation, those he put forward invariably received the lord's careful selection and were accordingly promoted. In his posts he served with purity, fearing that others might know of his deeds. In yielding treasure he surpassed the worthies of old; in knowing contentment he exceeded those who came after.

It was expected that within the garrison gates he would share the lord's worries, and within the prefectural administration he would serve as a strategist for the state. But unexpectedly, the fires of earth were secretly reversed and his spiritual nature returned to the eternal void. Left behind was the white jade of his physical form, no longer to hear the golden beauty of his words. His wife, dwelling alone in her widowed chamber, shed tears of blood in a streaming flow. In this world it is hard to meet face to face again; after parting, one can only rely on letters carried by birds.

She then requested vermillion brush and assembled his appearance and bearing. She also invited scholars to compose praises of his virtue. The praise says:

> The Lord's appearance was natural in its grace,
> His spiritual principle was rich and manifold.
> Among the crowd, all respected him;
> Before others, none could compare.
> In letters he sought the learning of Confucius;
> In arms he bowed at the altar of the King of Chǔ.
> The lord's grace of rank and salary was heavy;
> His meritorious service numbered in the thousands.
> When he plucked the strings, the five tones were complete;
> When he chanted verse, the four tones were full.
> In the prefecture he governed with precision;
> His divine counsel was famed at the frontier.
> Who could have known the waters of death would come so soon,
> The ghosts of the dark arriving first?
> In the hall, footsteps echo in vain;
> Before the courtyard, all ties are severed.
> His colleagues all wept aloud;
> His wife again grieved before the bier.
> We have captured his living likeness in full,
> So that those who come after may look upon him.
> In future years, beneath the burial mound,
> The waters flow forever toward the Yellow Springs.
> The bird-script letters must be sent afar;
> The worm-script words must yet be transmitted.

### Biographical Inscription

Lord Cunhui, styled Cháyuǎn. His distinguished surname of old derives from the lineage of Wǔdīng (*Wǔdīng*, 武丁), king of Yīn. His seedline traces to Bóyáng (*Bóyáng*, 伯陽) of Lǒngxī (*Lǒngxī*, 隴西). The genealogical records are quite clear; we state them briefly. Their clan took its name by pointing to a tree, and its renown endured through the archery contest. When the white horse came east and the blue ox departed west, they converted the Hu lands and left descendants. To this day they are people of Dunhuang.

His great-grandfather held the position of Mòlí (*Mòlí*, 墨釐) Military Affairs Governor, Prefect of Guāzhōu (*Guāzhōu*, 瓜州), Grand Master of Splendid Happiness with Gold Seal and Purple Ribbon, Vice President of the Board of Personnel by commission, concurrent Censor-in-Chief, and Superior Pillar of State.

His grandfather served as Director of the Circuit Revenue Commission, Grand Master of Splendid Happiness with Silver Seal and Blue Ribbon, Vice Minister of War by commission, concurrent Censor-in-Chief, and Superior Pillar of State. His name was Shàoqiū (*Shàoqiū*, 紹丘).

His father served in the Guīyì Army (*Guīyì Jūn*, 歸義軍) as Military Commander and Acting Garrison Commander of Shíchéng Fort (*Shíchéng Zhèn*, 石城鎮), Grand Master of Splendid Happiness with Silver Seal and Blue Ribbon, Vice Cavalier Attendant-in-Ordinary of the Left by commission, and Superior Cavalry Commandant. He was named Ān (*Ān*, 安), now deceased.

The son: Inner Personal Attendant, Military Commander, Director of Left and Right Frost-Guard, Commander of Cavalry and Infantry, Grand Marshal of Military Training, Vice Minister of War by commission, concurrent Censor-in-Chief, and Superior Pillar of State. Yīn Zhùyán (*Yīn Zhùyán*, 陰住延).

His mother, Lady of the Small Name (*Xiǎo Niángzǐ*, 小娘子), was of the Yīn (*Yīn*, 陰) clan of Wǔwēi (*Wǔwēi*, 武威).

His late uncle: Military Commander for Dispatches and Tax Collection, Grand Master of Splendid Happiness with Blue Ribbon, State Libationer by commission, concurrent Vice Censor-in-Chief. Named Āndìng (*Āndìng*, 安定), now deceased.

His son: Inner Personal Attendant and Commander, Grand Master of Splendid Happiness with Silver Seal and Blue Ribbon, Vice Cavalier Attendant-in-Ordinary of the Right by commission, concurrent Censor-in-Chief. Yīn Zhùqiān (*Yīn Zhùqiān*, 陰住千).

His elder brother: Saṃgha Rector (*Sēngzhèng*, 僧正) of the Buddhist clergy, Preceptor-in-Attendance and Great Worthy of Imperial Offerings, concurrent Dharma Master of Doctrinal Studies, holder of the purple robe. The śramaṇa Fǎdìng (*Fǎdìng*, 法定).

The lord's martial accomplishments were prepared from an early age, and his literary endeavors shone from his youth. He had scarcely applied his methods for governing the state when, unable to fulfill his way of assisting the lord, he was unexpectedly called by the songs of Qí sung in the dark, with the phrases of Lǔ chanted first. His spirit-soul was drawn away on startled waves, his ethereal soul followed the flowing waters.

On the twenty-sixth day, *gēngzǐ*, of the first month, *yǐhài* new moon, of the fifth year of Tàipíng Xīngguó (*Tàipíng Xīngguó*, 太平興國) [980 CE], the *gēngchén* year, he died of illness at his private residence in the Xiūwén Ward (*Xiūwén Fāng*, 修文坊).

On the third day, *bǐngwǔ*, of the second month, *jiǎchén* new moon, of that same year, he was temporarily interred at the sunny side of Gāolǐ Plateau (*Gāolǐ Yáng*, 高里陽), according to the rites of Kāihé Northern Plain (*Kāihé Běiyuán*, 開河北原). A later date was to be divined for burial at the ancestral cemetery.

His younger sister's husband, Military Commander Dèng Qìngshùn (*Dèng Qìngshùn*, 鄧慶順), and his wife's younger brother, Military Commander Cáo Yòuchóng (*Cáo Yòuchóng*, 曹祐崇), were both of connected bone and blood, branches of the kindred. Some had passed away to the springs below, some remained among the living. Leaning on the mourning hut in piercing grief, their entrails torn by thoughts of brothers and sisters; pillowing on departed souls consumed with sorrow, tears flowing from sons and daughters. Worldly affairs change with the passing years; fame and renown fade with the days and months. For the departed worthy, relying on bird-track writing, we inscribe this dragon-head stele. The inscription says:

> Heaven arrayed the stars; a title was surely deserved.
> When worthy talent and wind and cloud meet in accord:
> The bond of lord and minister, well matched, does not falter.
> Pure as ice, clean as jade, decisive in one region.
> Upright and unbending, who could have expected
> The wind to carry away his spirit to the night terrace?
> Pines and peppers stand silent; only foxes and hares are seen.
> A tomb inscription transmitted afar: the stone alone serves as marker.
> For a thousand autumns the record reaches far: all is in bird-script.
> For ten thousand years the fragrance endures: in brush and ink indeed.

Composed on the third day of the second month, the *gēngchén* year, the fifth year of Tàipíng Xīngguó [980 CE].
