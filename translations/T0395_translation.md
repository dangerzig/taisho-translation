# 佛說當來變經
## Sūtra of Future Changes Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0395

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana grove of Anāthapiṇḍada's park, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, five hundred bhikṣus, and various bodhisattvas.

At that time, the World-Honored One told the bhikṣus: "In ages to come, there will be bhikṣus who, due to a single thing, will not follow the Dharma's transformation, causing the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed, unable to grow and flourish. What is this one thing? Not guarding the precepts, not being able to control the mind, not cultivating wisdom, letting the mind be lax and negligent, seeking only a good reputation, not following the teachings of the path, and not willingly striving toward the work of transcending the world. This single thing causes the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "There are furthermore two things that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed. What are these two? First, not guarding the precepts, not restraining the mind, not cultivating wisdom, keeping wives and raising children, letting the mind run wild, engaging in trade and commerce and farming to support one another. Second, forming cliques and factions, hating those who honor the Dharma and wishing to bring about their downfall, and deliberately making arguments that are nothing but flattery and deception: inwardly committing evil deeds while outwardly feigning purity. These are the two things that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "There are furthermore three things that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed. What are these three? First, not guarding the precepts, not being able to restrain the mind, and not cultivating wisdom. Second, reading texts by oneself but not recognizing the punctuation, placing what should be above below and what should be below above, turning head and tail upside down, unable to understand the import of the meaning, yet considering oneself correct. Third, when wise persons reproach them, they do not follow the instruction but instead harbor resentment, saying the reproach is motivated by jealousy. Those who understand the meaning are few; most cannot distinguish principle from error, yet all insist they are right. These are the three things that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "There are furthermore four things that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed. What are these four? First, bhikṣus in the future, having already left behind household affairs, dwelling in secluded places, will not cultivate the practices of the path. Second, they will delight in wandering among bustling crowds, going about in idle conversation, seeking fine kāṣāya robes of five colors. Third, they will affect a manner of gazing into the distance as if sophisticated and elegant, considering their own virtue to be lofty and beyond anyone's reach, comparing their fragmentary cleverness to the brilliance of the sun and moon. Fourth, they will not restrain the three actions or guard the sense-gates, but will frequent the company of women, adorning their words and embellishing their speech with many clever and rhyming phrases to stir people's hearts, turning what is clear to what is turbid. Their personal conduct will be dissolute and disordered, and the true Dharma will be neglected and delayed. These are the four things that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "There are furthermore five things that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed. What are these five? First, there may be bhikṣus who originally left home to cultivate the path for the sake of the Dharma, but who abandon the profound sūtra-teachings: the twelve links of dependent origination, the thirty-seven aids to enlightenment, the profound and wondrous wisdom of the vaipulya teachings, the prajñāpāramitā, and the skillful means of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness that lead to the ultimate transformation. Second, instead they study miscellaneous phrases, shallow and superficial minor sūtras, worldly texts, and the classics of kings, which are the source of confusion in the path. They delight in teaching such matters, which are easy to understand and worldly, aimed only at winning people's approval and making them happy, and thereby gaining fame and recognition. Third, those who have newly heard the Dharma and understand it only superficially find this clever and delightful; those who have deep understanding do not consider it excellent. Fourth, the devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits are not pleased by this; their hearts are filled with sorrow, and they utter these words: 'The great Dharma is about to perish; that is why it has come to this.' Abandoning the wondrous Dharma-transformation and instead proclaiming miscellaneous phrases, the devas shed tears and swiftly depart. Fifth, because of this, the true Dharma is gradually abandoned, and none practice it with diligence. These are the five things that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "After my passing, there will be these fifteen disordered perversities that cause the Dharma to be ruined and destroyed. How painful this is! If there are bhikṣus who wish truly to study the path, they should cast aside ornamentation and not seek fame and recognition. Simple and sincere, guarding the truth, they should proclaim the true sūtras, the Buddha's refined teachings, and the transformation of the profound Dharma. They should not use many words but should teach the sūtras according to the original text without abandoning the correct phrases, speaking sparingly yet hitting the mark, not missing the Buddha's intent. Wearing coarse robes and eating just enough, receiving good food without relishing it and receiving poor food without disdain, accepting whatever good or bad clothing and food the donors offer without reacting with anger or delight. Restraining body, speech, and mind, guarding the sense-gates, not violating the Buddha's teachings, they should reflect that life is exceedingly brief, passing in a flash like a dream: upon waking, one does not know where one is. The hardships of the three evil paths are beyond reckoning. One should diligently cultivate the Buddha's Dharma as urgently as if one's head were on fire. The five precepts, the ten wholesome deeds, the six pāramitās, the four immeasurables, the four kinds of gratitude, wisdom, and skillful means: all should be practiced with diligence. Though one may not live in an age when a Buddha is present, leaving home and cultivating the path is not in vain. Calm your fundamental mind, have compassion for all, and the beings of the ten directions will receive your kindness."

When the Buddha had spoken this, the bhikṣus, with mixed sadness and joy, came forward and took refuge in the Buddha, bowed in reverence, and departed.
