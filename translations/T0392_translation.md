# 佛滅度後棺斂葬送經
## Sūtra on the Coffining and Funeral After the Buddha's Parinirvāṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0392

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was traveling in the country of Pāṭaliputra. At the time of the first watch, Ānanda addressed the Bhagavān: "After the Bhagavān has passed away, what should be the rites for coffining and honoring the sacred body?"

The Bhagavān said: "Concern yourself with your own self; do not worry about the Buddha. After my passing, there will be brahmins and householders who will perform the funeral rites."

Ānanda said: "What are those rites?"

The Bhagavān said: "As the funeral ceremony for a cakravartin emperor."

Again he asked: "May I hear the rites and procedures?"

The Bhagavān said: "When a holy emperor passes away, a thousand sheets of kalpaka cotton are used to wrap the body. Fragrant ointment is poured over it until the moisture penetrates throughout. The body is placed upon fragrant substances, evenly on all sides, above and below. Fire is set for the cremation. The bones are collected, washed in fragrant liquid, and placed in a golden urn. Stones are used to make a chamber, three feet in length and width, one foot thick, with one slab placed on each of the four sides, top, and bottom. The golden urn is placed within. A kṣetra-pole is erected with banners and streamers, and all appropriate offerings are made. An earthen stūpa is raised, and flower and incense offerings are presented.

"The rites for the Buddha should surpass even those. Why? Because I, through immeasurable kalpas, with the four immeasurables and great compassion, practiced the six pāramitās, extending through the ten directions to save and deliver all beings. My merit is illustrious and lofty, having accomplished this attainment as Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Master of the Path of Dharma, Teacher of Devas and Humans, the most honored, whom none can equal. Whoever takes even a handful of earth to make offerings at the stūpa, their merit will be immeasurable.

"In the defiled and turbid final age, people's lives will be unstable, and wealth will be divided among five claimants. For this reason I leave behind relics and also the alms-bowl, to ward off the calamities of this turbulent final age and bring peace and protection to all beings. Images will be established as ancestral shrines so that the people may gaze upon them. Śramaṇas will use the sūtras to guide and transform those who have not yet heard, causing those who live to forever leave behind the cruelty of the prison-house, and those who have died to be freed from the punishment of the three evil paths, and all will certainly ascend to the heavens. When rites are performed for the Buddha, they should surpass all others."

Ānanda said: "What shall become of the bowl?"

The Buddha said: "My bowl was offered by the four great heavenly kings, who combined four bowls into one. It is the vessel from which the Buddha ate; sentient beings should take care never to eat from it. After my passing, the nations will contend for it. The people's minds will become perverse and deranged: they will scorn life and treasure licentiousness, turn their backs on filial piety and honor the deviant. The bowl will transform and manifest five-colored radiance, flying and ascending through the air, to open and transform the people's hearts. When the multitudes see it, they will recall the Buddha's virtue, abandon folly and embrace clarity, and follow the correct teaching. All will build temples and monasteries, and make known the Buddha's virtue.

"The bowl will gradually travel eastward. In every country it passes through, epidemics will be quelled, rulers and ministers will be healthy and at peace, grain and cloth will be abundant, and there will be joy without calamity. In the end, they will be far from the three evil paths, and all will be reborn in the heavens. In the far eastern country, the king will be benevolent and wise, and the bowl will alight there. After that king passes away, his heir will be licentious and deranged, abandoning truth and following deviance. The people's minds will be the same: they will look upon the bowl without reverence or respect. When the devas and nāgas see this, they will weep with mixed grief and joy, welcome the bowl, and return it to the ocean to honor and make offerings to it.

"The king, having lost the honored bowl, will be filled with sorrow and indignation. He will send proclamations to all nations, offering a thousand gold pieces for the bowl. After years of searching, he will demand it be produced. The people, craving the great reward, will search everywhere but not find it. At that time there will be a lowly person named Śilpa, who will fraudulently pose as a bhikṣu, a glutton for wine and food, keeping a wife and raising children. While drunk, he will carry his child to the palace gate and say: 'I know where the bowl is.' The king will hear this with great delight and invite the śramaṇa in, saying: 'Where is the bowl?' He will answer: 'First give me the gold.' The king will bestow a thousand catties of gold. Śilpa will say: 'Only the śramaṇas could have stolen it.' The king will then issue an order to interrogate and torture all the śramaṇas, with extreme cruelty. The ministers and people, seeing this, will all resent the king.

"The king will say to him: 'Whose śramaṇa are you?' He will answer: 'I serve the Buddha as my teacher.' The king will say: 'What precepts does the Buddha have?' Śilpa will say: 'There are two hundred and fifty precepts.'

"The king will say: 'What is the first precept?'

"He will answer: 'The first is to honor compassion. Universal kindness and benevolence should extend to all living beings. One should regard the lives and bodies of all beings under heaven as one's own life and body. With compassionate pity and sympathetic concern, one should put oneself in others' place to bring them peace. With the joy of the path, one opens and transforms others, protecting them as one would oneself. This moistening benefit reaches even to the grasses and trees; nothing should be wantonly destroyed.'

"The king will say: 'Excellent! The Buddha's benevolent transformation embraces heaven and wraps the earth. What living being does not depend upon it?'

"'The second is to honor purity. One should not hoard foul treasures. The glory and territory of a nation are not to be usurped. Not even a blade of grass should be taken unless it is given.'

"The king will say: 'Excellent! This is what may truly be called pure and clean.'

"'The third is to honor chastity. The mind should harbor no thought of lust; the mouth should speak no seductive words. False voices and deviant appearances: one should neither look at nor listen to any of them. Seeing a woman, one should regard her as a mother, as an elder sister, as a younger sister, as a daughter. One would rather burn one's own body than commit lustful acts.'

"The king will say: 'Excellent! Modeling truth and reflecting purity, the Buddha's transformation is foremost indeed.'

"'The fourth is to be careful with speech. No divisive speech, harsh speech, false speech, or frivolous speech; no praising in front and slandering behind; no false testimony against the innocent; no sorcery, ghost-worship, spellbinding, or cursing. One would rather swallow hot coals than let a poisonous voice emerge.'

"The king will say: 'Excellent, the Buddha's transformation! Trembling and cautious, careful with speech to such a degree.'

"'The fifth is to abstain from alcohol. Alcohol causes a ruler to be without benevolence, a minister to be without loyalty, a parent to be without righteousness, a child to be without filial piety, and a woman to become extravagant and lustful. Its faults number thirty-six. The ruin of nations and the destruction of families are invariably due to this. One would rather drink poison and die than live in the disorder of drunkenness.'

"The king will say: 'Excellent! The Buddha's brilliant transformation causes one to swallow virtue and cherish the path, to extinguish all evil and foster all good. Purity is the body; serenity is the aspiration. The sūtras' transformation commands benevolence, yet you teach me to kill. The precepts say to guard purity and not to covet, yet you steal gold. The precepts say no lust, yet you keep a wife. The precepts say to be completely sincere, yet you falsely slander the śramaṇas, saying they stole the bowl, causing me to punish the innocent. The precepts say no indulgence in alcohol, yet you came here drunk. Among the other śramaṇas outside, are there those who possess these five virtues and are of lofty practice?'

"He will answer: 'Their impurity and defilement are worse than mine.'

"The king will ask his officials: 'What are the śramaṇas' occupations?'

"They will answer: 'They go begging without moderation. Their defilements are worse than that Śilpa.'

"The king will say: 'The Buddha's precepts number two hundred and fifty. His benevolence surpasses all benevolence! His purity equals the primal simplicity! His chastity is equal to empty space! His sincerity is like the four seasons! His brilliance surpasses the sun and moon! How is it that such people as these usurp the Dharma-robes, steal the offering-vessels, and fraudulently pose as śramaṇas, disordering the true and correct? They do not observe even a single precept, yet claim two hundred and fifty.' He will order his officials: 'The Buddha's pure monasteries are places revered by sages and worthies, not the nests and dens of animals. Drive out the impure and defiled ones; do not allow them to remain in the Buddha's monasteries.'

"The gentlemen of the nation who wish to prosper and benefit the monasteries will have no wise and worthy persons dwelling therein to proclaim the Buddha's divine transformation; they will wipe their tears and desist. From then on, the great path will decline and deteriorate, and the divine transformation will daily diminish."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Though I pass away, I leave behind the bowl and relics. If there are worthy persons who reverently make offerings, they will all ultimately ascend to the heavens."

Ānanda said: "At the end of a thousand years, the bowl manifests divine virtue and transformation to such a degree. How much more so the spiritual transformation of the unsurpassed, perfectly awakened one?"

When the Buddha taught this sūtra, the devas, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, kings, ministers, and the four assemblies were all choked with tears. They bowed their heads and departed.
