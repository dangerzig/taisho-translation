# 佛說沙彌十戒儀則經
## Sūtra on the Ten Precepts and Ritual Procedures for Novices Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1473

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護).

---

> I bow to the All-Knowing One, the wondrous Dharma, and the holy saṃgha.
> I briefly explain the novice's conduct to inspire the mind to go forth.
> Within the teaching of Śākyamuni, firmly uphold the prohibitive precepts.
> Guard the precepts as one guards one's body; be vigilant and do not let them be broken.
> At night when lying down and rising in the morning, clean the mouth and teeth.
> Chant and recite until dawn, then bow to the Perfectly Awakened One.
> Approach the teacher's quarters and lightly tap the door with one's hand.
> Upon entering, inquire of the teacher: "Is your body well and at ease?"
> Having done this, next prepare a maṇḍala,
> Making offerings with reverence. In the early morning, examine the water within.
> Do so likewise at midday and after. In jars, vessels, bowls, and pots,
> Water insects may be large or small; with sincere mind, constantly observe.
> Filter and strain the water with a cloth; examine carefully before using it.
> When eating and drinking, harbor compassion; do not cause insects to be killed.
> Even upon grass and wood, or in the cow-dung used for plastering altars,
> When making use of such things, protect sentient beings.
> Whether within beds and seats, or in fields and manured soil,
> Examine each one carefully: this is the conduct of one gone forth.
> In all things, serve the teacher. Wash the bowl until it is perfectly clean.
> At the proper times, make three prostrations, and with palms joined stand before the teacher.
> Ask the teacher what he will eat, and prepare food to cure the illness of hunger.
> When thus preparing food, use the previously purified water.
> Rinse the hands clean with water, and eat in silence according to the Dharma.
> After eating, recite two verses, dedicating merit to the faithful donors.
> Practice as taught, and increase the practice of right mindfulness.
> With faith, understand the Dharma of pure precepts; cultivate in accord with the Dharma.
> When right contemplation accords with one's practice, the mind of one gone forth is at peace.
> If a person himself breaks the precepts, others who see it develop contempt.
> Both persons incur offense; the upholder must be single-minded.
> Whether one upholds or breaks the precepts, even for a person who is ill,
> If the Dharma is taught inappropriately, the hearer's mind does not esteem it,
> And at death one suffers greatly. If a person administers poison
> Or employs spells and such methods to cause another's death,
> Or further uses various skillful means to carry out killing,
> Such causes of breaking the precepts lead to permanent sinking into the three evil destinies:
> Hell, hungry ghosts, and animals, receiving punishment in that order.
> Even the hosts of devas, if they too should kill living beings,
> Fall back into the evil destinies and receive those evil retributions.
> If one who has gone forth recklessly employs body, speech, and mind,
> And inadvertently kills ants and such creatures, within these three karmas
> One incurs three kinds of offense. If a person with fists, sticks,
> Clods of earth, stones, or bricks strikes and hurls them at sentient beings,
> Camels, mules, birds, or beasts, one also incurs the offense of breaking the precepts.
> To ride upon camels, mules, elephants, horses, and such,
> Coming and going, oppressing others, also incurs the offense of breaking the precepts.
> One who has gone forth must not commit theft.
> Whether oneself or by instructing others, if one steals property
> Worth one kārṣāpaṇa or a quarter-fraction thereof,
> Such a cause of breaking the precepts constitutes the gravest offense.
> Food, drink, grain, rice, and the like, flowers, fruits, grass, and wood,
> Whether growing on land or in water, to steal any of these,
> Or to steal official taxes, to smuggle past customs posts,
> Or to steal sentient beings, whether two-footed or many-footed:
> Even if a renunciant has sufficient provisions,
> If out of greed one still steals property, both incur the grave offense.
> If one's own robes, bowls, and such are robbed and stolen by thieves,
> One must not forcibly take them back. Teach the Dharma as skillful means to convert them,
> Or buy them back; if they will not agree, let them go as they wish.
> This is the mind of one gone forth: not disturbing the three karmas' wounds,
> The nature of the precepts is self-perfected. One who has gone forth
> Must not engage in sexual conduct. Women, men, and paṇḍakas,
> If they come of their own accord with mutual desire, one who foolishly and blindly becomes attached
> Incurs the root offense. If one deliberately engages in sexual conduct,
> It is like a snake, like poison, damaging one's own body.
> One suffers the great hell, and the path to nirvāṇa is obstructed;
> One does not escape birth and death. The Perfectly Awakened One was not like this;
> He never physically violated the precepts. Upholding them brings great benefit.
> One who has gone forth must not utter false speech.
> If one says "I have seen devas and they spoke with me,
> The dwelling place of great persons, I myself have been there,"
> Or even mentions gandharvas, nāgas, yakṣas together,
> Mahoragas, kiṃnaras,
> Preta ghosts and the like, saying "they always converse with me" ...
> All this is false speech. Or to say "I have attained the five supernatural powers,
> The true path and the four fruits, I understand the most profound Dharma" ...
> To claim attainment of what one has not attained: such false speech
> Causes permanent sinking into the evil destinies. One must not engage in divisive speech,
> Separating others' good friends, or in coarse and abusive words,
> Or frivolous talk confusing right and wrong. Even if reviled by others,
> One must not respond in kind. All manner of contentious words
> Must each be patiently endured. If one does not practice accordingly,
> One incurs the offense of breaking the precepts. The wise single-mindedly uphold them
> And are freed from the transgressions of verbal karma. If one uses grain and rice and the like
> To brew alcohol that intoxicates people, one must not deliberately drink it.
> Whether from sugarcane, flowers, grapes, or fruits and the like,
> Brewed until it can intoxicate: one must not deliberately drink it.
> Drinking oneself, teaching others to drink, becoming confused and losing mindfulness,
> Increasing the mind of heedlessness: the drinker incurs a grave offense.
> Therefore the World-Honored One said: if a person with a blade of grass
> Drops a single drop of alcohol into the mouth, it increases transgressions.
> Singing, dancing, and theatrical performances: to deliberately go and watch them
> Incurs the offense of breaking the precepts. To anoint and adorn the body with fragrant oil,
> Sandalwood, saffron, and the like, or with fine flower garlands,
> Gold, silver, jewels, and such to adorn the body in various ways
> Incurs the offense of breaking the precepts. To apply decorations upon the eyes
> To make oneself appear more attractive incurs the offense of breaking the precepts.
> Sitting-beds and sleeping-beds should be no higher than one cubit.
> One should not adorn them, nor make them overly large.
> Heedlessly failing to practice accordingly incurs the offense of breaking the precepts.
> The Buddha says one gone forth should keep far from transgressions.
> When receiving the midday meal, one must not eat past noon.
> From sunrise until noon, one may receive the fasting meal.
> Eating at an improper time, the Buddha says, incurs a grave offense.
> If a bhikṣu is ill, to treat the illness and save the body,
> Eating after noon is permitted. Without illness, not following the proper time
> Incurs the offense of breaking the precepts. Gold, silver, precious jewels, and the like:
> One gone forth must not touch them. To accept them gives rise to greed,
> And incurs the offense of breaking the precepts. If faithful donors make offerings
> To the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṃgha, for the sake of generating merit,
> One may accept and use them. All objects of comfort and pleasure:
> One must not become attached to them. If one develops attachment,
> That person must be expelled. All manner of play and jest:
> A novice must not engage in them; doing so incurs an offense.
> When yawning, cover the mouth with one's hand;
> Not doing so incurs an offense. When a senior monk sneezes,
> One should respectfully prostrate. When a junior novice sneezes,
> The senior should offer the blessing "long life and freedom from illness."
> A novice before the teacher must not blow the nose or spit,
> And must not brush the teeth. One must not, before the teacher,
> Pace back and forth or sit facing him. Elders of long standing and great virtue
> Should be respected in accordance with the Dharma. When entering the latrine,
> If someone comes behind, announce one's presence in a low voice.
> Filter insect-free water and use it to fill the pure pitcher.
> With clean hands, walk composedly, not losing the conduct of one gone forth.
> The teacher's own bed and sitting-mat should always be covered with a clean cloth.
> The various beds and chairs must not be sat upon by the novice.
> If the teacher himself is absent, rely on a pure member of the saṃgha
> Who understands the Vinaya rules; serve and attend upon him.
> Do not further rely on a different monk; to violate this incurs offense.
> If a bhikṣu goes out on a journey to seek provisions,
> If he returns within five days, he shares in the community's gain.
> What the community gains is likewise shared with visiting monks.
> If more than five days pass, neither party receives a share.
> When a novice needs to urinate or defecate, one must ask about the Buddha's and saṃgha's ground.
> One must not wander about haphazardly; walk within one's own precinct.
> In all that one does, join palms and first ask the teacher.
> By performing actions in this way, one does not lose the right conduct.
> At sunset, bow to the Buddha's stūpa. After bowing, again visit the teacher.
> Wash the teacher's feet, and when the task is done, listen single-mindedly,
> Attentive to whatever is needed. During the first and last watches of the night,
> Chant and recite without ceasing. During the middle watch, when sleeping,
> Lie like a lion. All the Dharma that has been taught:
> Practice it with a sincere mind. Plant the seeds of virtue in the consciousness.
> Afflictions will be severed of themselves, and one will swiftly accomplish unsurpassed enlightenment.
