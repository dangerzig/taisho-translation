# Ritual of Repentance and Vows for Rebirth in the Pure Land

## *Wǎngshēng Jìngtǔ Chànyuàn Yí* (往生淨土懺願儀)

Taishō 1984

Compiled by Zūnshì (*Zūnshì*, 遵式) of the Sòng dynasty.

*Note: This is a comprehensive liturgical manual for Pure Land repentance practice, compiled by the Tiāntái master Zūnshì (964-1032 CE), one of the most influential liturgists of the Northern Sòng dynasty. The text organizes the entire Pure Land devotional retreat into ten systematic sections: (1) preparing the practice-hall, (2) preliminary methods, (3) the correct intention for practice, (4) offering incense and scattering flowers, (5) invocations and requests, (6) praise, (7) prostrations to the buddhas, (8) repentance and vows (the heart of the liturgy), (9) circumambulation and sūtra-recitation, and (10) seated meditation. The practice is designed for a retreat of seven to forty-nine days, during which practitioners maintain six daily sessions of prostration, recitation, circumambulation, and meditation, all directed toward rebirth in Amitābha's Pure Land. Zūnshì draws on Vasubandhu's Five Gates of Mindfulness (from the Jìngtǔ Lùn), the Contemplation Sūtra's sixteen visualizations, and the three truths of Tiāntái contemplation. The repentance section includes confession of the gravest offenses (the four pārājikas, five heinous crimes, and icchantika transgressions), followed by formal exhortation to turn the dharma-wheel, rejoicing in merit, dedication, and the vow for rebirth. The meditation section offers two visualization methods: a general Pure Land visualization and a specific contemplation of Amitābha's white ūrṇā (curl of hair between the eyebrows). The text remains in active liturgical use in Chinese Buddhist monasteries.*

## Introduction

The buddhas, in their compassion for beings lost in delusion, have established many skillful means to draw them in. Among these, the pure karma leading to the Land of Peace and Nourishment [Sukhāvatī] is the most direct and accessible path of practice. All the great Mahāyāna sūtras open this essential gate. All buddhas of the ten directions praise it without exception.

If bhikṣus, the four assemblies, or good men and women whose faculties are incomplete wish to swiftly shatter the darkness of ignorance; to permanently eradicate the five heinous crimes, the ten evils, violations of the precepts, and all other offenses; to restore the purity of the great and small precepts in their present form; to attain the samādhi of mindfulness of the Buddha and fully perfect all the gates of the bodhisattva's pāramitās: they should study this method.

If they wish, at the moment of death, to be free from all terror, with body and mind at ease and joyful, as if returning home, with light illuminating their dwelling, extraordinary fragrance and music, with Amitābha Buddha, Avalokiteśvara, and Mahāsthāmaprāpta appearing before them, bestowing a purple-gold platform and extending their hands in welcome; to cut horizontally across the five destinies, to advance swiftly through the nine grades, departing from feverish afflictions and resting in coolness; to immediately upon leaving the dust of affliction reach the stage of non-retrogression, and without traversing long kalpas to swiftly attain the patience of non-arising: they should study this method.

If they wish to cultivate a small practice yet experience a wondrous reward, with all buddhas of the ten directions simultaneously praising and giving prophecy of awakening before them, and in a single thought to make offerings to immeasurable buddhas and immediately return to their own land; to sit with Amitābha and eat, to discuss the Dharma with Avalokiteśvara, to walk with Mahāsthāmaprāpta; with eyes and ears that see and hear everything, bodies of limitless size that fly freely through space, recollection of all past lives, and a vision of the five destinies as clear as a mirror's reflection; moment after moment entering the inexhaustible samādhis: such blessings are beyond full description. One should cultivate and practice this supreme Dharma.

All that has been described is real and not false. The buddhas of the ten directions extend their broad, long tongues to praise these things and demonstrate their truth. How could we dare not believe the Buddha?

Now, drawing on the various Pure Land sūtras, I establish this practice-method. Those who wish for a more detailed account may consult the sūtras to supplement it. For now, I shall briefly explain it in ten sections:

1. Preparing the practice-hall
2. Preliminary methods
3. The correct intention for practice
4. Offering incense and scattering flowers
5. Invocations and requests
6. Praise
7. Prostrations to the buddhas
8. Repentance and vows
9. Circumambulation and sūtra-recitation
10. Seated meditation

## Section 1: Preparing the Practice-Hall

One should select a quiet, secluded room. First remove the old earth, then bring fresh, clean earth from a pure place, free of broken tiles and stones and any prior defilement, and fill the ground. Mix fragrant substances into the plaster and apply it to make [the floor] supremely clean. Above, hang a new jeweled canopy. From the center of the canopy, suspend five-colored pennants, and throughout the room hang various silk pennants and flowers for fine adornment. Install the buddha-image facing east, seated in the western position. Avalokiteśvara attends on the left, Mahāsthāmaprāpta on the right. Before the image, arrange various fine flowers and lotus flowers. If one can install the Nine Grades of Rebirth images, this is best; if not, it does no harm. Other adornments should be arranged according to one's means.

Spread mats around [the room] to guard against damp ground. Practitioners must wear fresh, clean clothing. If absolutely no new clean clothing is available, wash and dye one's best garments. After bathing, put them on and then enter the practice-hall. One should enter and exit from the left or right of the door-frame. Shoes and sandals should be lined up neatly, not in disorder. When leaving [the hall], one must change into ordinary clothing. After bathing, put the clean clothing on again. Do this every day.

In the practice-hall, one should pour out everything one has in offerings to the Three Treasures. If one does not exhaust one's possessions in making offerings, the practice will not be focused and will surely lack spiritual response. Only if one truly has nothing of one's own may one seek [offerings] from others.

The practitioners should number ten or fewer; more than this is unmanageable. It is fitting to begin on one of the six monthly fast-days.

## Section 2: Preliminary Methods

Before entering the practice-hall, the practitioner's body and mind will be scattered and disordered. One must practice preliminaries in advance. For seven days, arrange a separate room (not the same as the practice-hall; if there is no other room, the same room is permissible). Day and night, adjust and practice, rehearse and test [the liturgy]. Pre-memorize the Five Repentances and other texts below until perfectly fluent. At the same time, attend to washing, mending, and sewing, and settle all practical matters. All other worldly affairs should be immediately set aside.

One should think: "Before long, I will certainly be reborn in the Pure Land." With single-minded aspiration for repentance and freedom from obstacles, each person should set a firm deadline, not sparing body or life, and resolve that the pure karma will be accomplished at once. Not a single thought should recollect the five sense-objects. Desire should be rebuked; anger and delusion diligently quelled. Each practitioner has beginningless evil habits; quickly seek to abandon them. Lest they become obstacles to the main repentance practice, one should examine which habits are strongest, rebuke and dismiss them, and adjust until equilibrium is restored. Do not let the practice-method waste its power. This can be understood intuitively.

## Section 3: The Correct Intention for Practice

The *Mahāsaṃnipāta Sūtra* specifies forty-nine days. The *Gǔyīnwáng Jīng* and the *Larger Amitāyus Sūtra* specify ten days and nights. The *Contemplation Sūtra* and the *Smaller Amitāyus Sūtra* specify seven days and nights. Take any of these three as the period; under no circumstances should it be shortened.

The "correct intention for practice": Vasubandhu's *Jìngtǔ Lùn* (Treatise on the Pure Land) says: "What is the meaning of contemplating the world of Peace and Bliss, seeing Amitābha Buddha, and vowing to be reborn in that land? How does one contemplate? How does one generate faith? By cultivating the five gates of mindfulness and bringing them to completion, one will certainly be reborn in the Land of Peace and Bliss and see Amitābha Buddha. What are the five? (1) The gate of prostration, (2) the gate of praise, (3) the gate of vow-making, (4) the gate of contemplation, and (5) the gate of dedication." And further: "The bodhisattva's skillful dedication means that all the merit and roots of merit gathered through the five practices of prostration and the rest are not directed toward one's own abiding happiness, but, wishing to uproot the suffering of all sentient beings, one makes the vow to gather all sentient beings and be reborn together in the Land of the Buddha of Peace and Bliss. This is called the bodhisattva's skillful dedication."

Such skillful dedication yields three kinds of accordance with bodhi: (1) the undefiled, pure mind, because one does not seek pleasures for oneself; (2) the peaceful, pure mind, because one uproots the suffering of all sentient beings; (3) the joyful, pure mind, because one enables all sentient beings to attain great bodhi, gathering sentient beings for rebirth in that land.

Therefore, I entirely adopt the text of the treatise as the correct intention of the present [liturgy]. But I add repentance, because it eliminates the obstacles to rebirth and accords with the Buddha's compassion for swift liberation.

One should maintain single-minded, single-pointed [concentration] for the full forty-nine days (or at least seven days), throughout the six daily sessions, prostrating to the buddhas of the ten directions and to Amitābha, the World-Honored One. Whether sitting or walking, one must never become scattered. Not even for an instant should one think of the five worldly desires, converse with outsiders, engage in jesting or laughter, or use pretexts to delay, become lax, or sleep. In every blink and bow, one's mindfulness should be continuous and unbroken, striving for rebirth with a single-pointed, diligent mind.

Question: "Since the practices are many, how can one maintain single-mindedness?"

Answer: There is both principle and practice. (1) "Single-mindedness in principle": from the moment of first entering the practice-hall through to the end, although one engages in many activities, all are without self-nature, unborn and undying, of one characteristic like the dharma-realm, conditioned by the dharma-realm. This is called single-mindedness in principle. (2) "Single-mindedness in practice": when prostrating, one does not think of anything else but only prostrates; when reciting sūtras or circumambulating, likewise. This is called single-mindedness in practice.

## Section 4: Offering Incense and Scattering Flowers

With all reverence, single-mindedly I pay homage to the permanently abiding Buddha of the dharma-realm of the ten directions.

Single-mindedly I pay homage to the permanently abiding Dharma of the dharma-realm of the ten directions.

Single-mindedly I pay homage to the permanently abiding Saṃgha of the dharma-realm of the ten directions.

All in this assembly, each kneeling solemnly, hold incense and flowers and make offerings according to the Dharma, offering to the Three Treasures of the dharma-realm of the ten directions.

> May this cloud of incense and flowers
> Fill the ten directions of space,
> Making offerings to all buddhas,
> The honored Dharma, all bodhisattvas,
> And the immeasurable assembly of śrāvakas.
> May it form a platform of radiance
> Extending beyond boundless realms,
> And in boundless buddha-lands
> Be received and used for buddha-work,
> Universally perfuming all sentient beings,
> So that all may arouse the aspiration for bodhi.

Having made offerings, with all reverence.

## Section 8: Repentance and Vows

### Part 1: Repentance

On behalf of all sentient beings throughout the dharma-realm, may all the three obstructions be severed. With utmost sincerity, I repent:

"I, bhikṣu [name], repent with utmost sincerity. The buddhas of the ten directions truly see and know: I and all sentient beings are originally pure in nature. The dwelling-place of the buddhas, named 'Eternally Quiescent Light,' pervades every instant and all dharmas. Yet I have not understood this. Falsely conceiving 'self' and 'other,' amidst the dharma of equality I have given rise to discrimination; amidst the pure mind I have generated attachment. Through this inversion, through the causes and conditions of the five desires, I have cycled through birth and death, traversing the three realms. Persisting in this continuity, with no thought of escape, I have committed the most extreme evil karma: the four pārājikas, the five heinous crimes, the icchantika transgressions, slandering the Mahāyāna, reviling and destroying the Three Treasures..."

[The confession continues with a comprehensive catalogue of transgressions, concluding:] "Now I first awaken and am filled with great shame and great terror. The World-Honored Ones of the ten directions and Amitābha Buddha have long since given rise to the mind of great compassion for me. Through immeasurable kalpas, for the sake of liberating me, they cultivated the path of bodhi without sparing their lives, and have now attained buddhahood with great compassion fully perfected. They can truly serve as the savior and protector of all. I, having committed evil, will certainly fall into the three evil destinies. May they arise in compassion and accept my repentance, so that heavy offenses may be eradicated, all evils eliminated, and the causes of birth in this sahā world permanently exhausted, so that I may be reborn in the buddhas' Pure Land as I have vowed."

"I, bhikṣu [name], make this vow with utmost sincerity: May all the merit of the three karmas of body, speech, and mind of those who cultivate the pure practice together with me adorn the pure vow... May we, at the moment of death, know in advance that the end is near, with all obstacles removed, mindfulness bright, body without illness, mind without inversion, and face Amitābha and his retinue. Joyful and happy, in a single instant may we be reborn in the Land of Ultimate Bliss. Upon arrival, may we see ourselves born within a lotus flower. ## Section 9: Circumambulation and Sūtra-Recitation

Namo Buddha. Namo Dharma. Namo Saṃgha. Namo Śākyamuni Buddha. Namo Lokeśvararāja Buddha. Namo Amitābha Buddha. Namo Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva. Namo Mahāsthāmaprāpta Bodhisattva. Namo Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva. Namo Samantabhadra Bodhisattva. Namo Pure Ocean-Wide Assembly of Great Bodhisattvas.

> Taking refuge in the Buddha, may all sentient beings
> Comprehend the great Way and arouse the unsurpassed mind.
>
> Taking refuge in the Dharma, may all sentient beings
> Enter deeply into the sūtra-treasury with wisdom like the ocean.
>
> Taking refuge in the Saṃgha, may all sentient beings
> Lead the great assembly, with all things unobstructed. Homage to the holy assembly.

Announce to the assembly: Listen to the Tathāgata's verses from the sūtra:

> Why do you not strive for what is good?
> Mindfulness of the Way is natural of itself.
> Each of you should diligently advance,
> Striving to seek it for yourselves.
> You will certainly transcend and depart,
> To be reborn in the Land of Peace and Nourishment.
> Cutting horizontally across the five evil destinies,
> The evil paths will naturally close.
> Ascending the Way without limit,
> Easy to go, yet few go there.
> Why not abandon worldly affairs
> And diligently practice to seek the Way?
> Each will attain long life,
> Longevity and joy without limit.

## Section 10: Seated Meditation

When the above activities are completed, one should sit on a cord-bed in one place, facing west, for ease of visualization and to symbolize facing [the Pure Land] directly. Sit in full-lotus posture, with neck and spine aligned, neither raised nor hunched. Regulate the breath and settle the mind.

Among the contemplation-gates taught in sūtras and treatises, there are very many. A beginner cannot possibly practice them all. I now briefly present two methods, from which one may choose according to suitability; it is not necessary to use both. Those who are already skilled in other visualizations may use them freely, as long as they do not depart from the Pure Land dharma-gate.

### Method 1: General Pure Land Visualization

After sitting, one should imagine that, by the power of one's present cultivation, one has been reborn in the Land of Ultimate Bliss. Immediately arouse the mind of being there and visualize oneself sitting in full-lotus posture within a lotus flower. Visualize the lotus closing. Visualize it opening. When the flower opens, five hundred colors of light come to illuminate one's body. Visualize one's eyes opening. Visualize seeing the Buddha, the bodhisattvas, and the land. Visualize sitting before the Buddha and hearing the wondrous Dharma, and hearing all sounds teaching the Dharma one most wishes to hear. What one hears must accord with the twelve divisions of scripture. When performing this visualization, one must be extremely firm, not allowing the mind to scatter. The mental image should be as clear as what the eyes directly see. After a long time, rise.

### Method 2: Contemplation of the White Ūrṇā

Directly visualize Amitābha Buddha: a sixteen-foot golden body seated on a lotus flower. Focus the mind exclusively on the white ūrṇā between the eyebrows. This curl of hair is one *zhang* and five feet long, five inches in circumference, with eight facets on its exterior. It is hollow inside, spiraling to the right, resting between the eyebrows. It is brilliantly pure and luminous beyond description, radiating against the golden countenance with clear distinction.

When performing this visualization, stop the mind, fix the attention firmly, and do not shift. Furthermore, one should contemplate: what is visualized and what is thought are all due to causes and conditions, without real nature or characteristics; all are empty. [Yet they are perceptible,] like a face reflected in a mirror, like the moon reflected in water, like a dream, like an illusion. Though empty, they can still be seen. Both are manifestations of mind-nature. What exists is one's own mind. The mind does not know itself; the mind does not see itself. When the mind has mental images, this is delusion. When the mind is free of images, this is nirvāṇa. Whether mind has [images] or lacks them: both are called "having images," and both are called delusion, for one does not see the dharma-nature. Dharmas arising from causes and conditions are at once empty, provisional, and middle: not one, not different; not vertical, not horizontal; inconceivable. When mental images are stilled and quiescent, one can accomplish the samādhi of mindfulness of the Buddha.

After a long time, rise. [The section concludes with assurance that even if the samādhi is not fully attained within the retreat period, the merit of the attempt still eradicates immeasurable sins, and encouragement from the sūtras that even hearing the name of the white ūrṇā destroys immeasurable offenses.]
