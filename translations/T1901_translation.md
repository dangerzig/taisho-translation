# Ritual Procedure for Protecting Life and Releasing Living Beings

## *Hùmìng Fàngshēng Guǐyí Fǎ* (護命放生軌儀法)

Taishō 1901

Composed by Yìjìng (*Yìjìng*, 義淨, 635-713) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: Yìjìng was the great Tang-dynasty pilgrim, translator, and vinaya master who traveled to India via the maritime route. This text describes the proper monastic procedure for filtering water to protect insect life, based on Indian vinaya practice.*

Those who possess life all love their life, from the wise and worthy above to the crawling insects below. Those who face death all fear their death. For this reason, the Well-Gone One (*Shànshì*, 善逝, Sugata) cultivated compassion according to circumstances, not harming any sentient creature. Measuring one's own body as the standard for others, he applied neither knife nor staff. Only through great compassion did he proclaim and spread the dharma's conversion.

Regarding the protection of living beings, he was exceptionally earnest. As a disciple of the Buddha, one should accordingly follow this practice. Examining water for insects and filtering it: this is a fundamental procedure for the renunciant. Seeing danger and providing protection is the urgent rescue within compassion.

Since it is known that there are insects in the water, the vinaya texts command the making of a life-releasing vessel (*fàngshēng qì*, 放生器). But because this has been practiced in India for a long time and everyone there understands it, whereas in China it has not been previously known, the procedure must be explained in detail. Without a full account, there would be no way to understand.

The vessel may be made of iron, copper, clay, or wood. For clay, a handle should be attached; for iron and wood, make it accordingly. If one intends to carry it on one's person, it may be made of copper, holding just two or three shēng (about one liter). This is simply the small copper canister that has long been in use. Attach a copper cord so the hand can pass through. On one side of the bottom, attach a copper button that can fit the tip of the little finger. When going out to beg for alms, thread it on the left arm and cover it with the robe. Carry the bowl with the right hand. After receiving alms, upon arriving at a house, set down the food bowl. Take a clean cord about the thickness of thick chopsticks and, according to the depth of the well, tie it to the canister and draw water. Filter it through a small sieve. When enough has been measured, thread one end of the cord through the button and tie it firmly. Attach a small iron hook to the cord. When lifting by the hook, make sure it is stable. All of this should be prepared in advance; one must not search for supplies at the last moment. Then use the small sieve to cover the insects in the canister. Lower it slowly until it reaches the water. Release the hook and pull the cord so it tips over. Rinse it down two or three times, then draw it out of the well. This is the procedure for begging alms.

Alternatively, a copper or lacquered bowl with holes and a cord attached may also be used. For those in the monastery, use the standard iron canister and cover as described above, with only minor differences in arrangement. Attach an iron ring at the bottom side that three fingers can fit through. Insert the canister hook inside, lift by the cord, and attach a hook as before. Cover the insects inside and lower it into the water. Even for deep wells this method works. If keeping a separate cord would be troublesome, for deep wells one may separately store the water in a basin or take it to a river or pond. After pouring out the water, one should still rinse the vessel. This is the method.

The design of the filter sieve is described elsewhere. How can it be that above a well's mouth one simply turns a sieve over, with no life-releasing vessel at all, and claims to be protecting the precepts while disregarding the death of insects?

The sacred teaching of the Tathāgata takes compassion as its foundation. Among the precepts he established, offenses are classified as intrinsic (*xìng*, 性) or prohibitive (*zhē*, 遮). Prohibitive offenses are measured against circumstances in accord with the sūtras. Intrinsic offenses, by principle, should be regarded as serious. Among intrinsic offenses, killing is foremost. Therefore, the wise person should especially preserve and protect life. If one considers this a trivial matter, what could possibly be considered serious?

If one can follow the teaching and make this vessel, in the present life one will receive the karmic fruit of long life, and in the next life one will be reborn in the Pure Land. In the divine land of China, with more than four hundred cities and renunciants numbering in the tens of thousands, those who attend to the matter of filtering water are few. Habituated to custom and born into convention, they lightly regard the Buddha's teaching. One cannot go door to door to teach by word of mouth; one hopes that practitioners will instruct one another.

Even if one has studied and mastered the Tripiṭaka, if one sits in realization of the four dhyānas, if one continually contemplates non-arising, or if one purifies the mind in the principle of emptiness, if one does not protect life and follow the teaching in practice, one will ultimately not escape the Buddha's censure. The first of the ten evil offenses: who will bear it in your place?

Consider: if someone sees a butcher leading a sheep into the monastery, perhaps only a few animals, and releases them to live freely, the assembly gathers to watch and snaps their fingers in approval, calling it good. Yet who realizes that using unfiltered water in one's room kills a thousand or ten thousand lives each day? Since one knows that the principle and the teaching are not to be taken lightly, one should filter carefully and examine closely. Benefit yourself and benefit others; protect well and think well.

Furthermore, there are those who have others plow fields and plant crops, calculating small profit but not seeing the great fault. Creatures of water and land are both harmed, and lives killed are without number. Such offenses and blame: what can be done about them? It is as though one's hands are bound at the gate of the underworld, left to be dealt with by others. As the sūtra says: "A person who kills living beings shall fall into the hells, the hungry ghost realm, and the animal realm. Even if one obtains human rebirth, one will have a short life and many illnesses." Alas, such suffering! Who shall bear it?

If there is anyone who can do this: excellent, truly excellent! This may be called planting together the cause of compassionate mindfulness in the final age of Śākyamuni's dharma, and together realizing the fruit of non-arising when Maitreya first accomplishes buddhahood. The details are as described in other transmissions and need not be elaborated here.
