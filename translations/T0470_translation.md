# 佛說文殊師利巡行經
## Sūtra of Mañjuśrī's Rounds Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0470

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流支)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in the city of Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus. At that time, the World-Honored One, in the afternoon, came out from his quarters and sat in an open area outside. The great assembly surrounded him, reverently making offerings, and he taught the Dharma for them.

At that time, the youth Mañjuśrī was making his rounds through the places where all five hundred bhikṣus were walking and staying. He went in due order until he arrived at the Elder Śāriputra's place. He saw the Elder Śāriputra sitting alone in a place, his body upright, absorbed in dhyāna contemplation. When the youth Mañjuśrī saw the Elder Śāriputra, he spoke to him: "Venerable Śāriputra! Are you absorbed in dhyāna?"

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Yes, Mañjuśrī."

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! Is it that what was not yet quiescent, you wish to make quiescent, and so you enter dhyāna? Or is it that what was already quiescent, you further quiescent, and so you enter dhyāna? Venerable Śāriputra! On what kind of dhyāna do you rely? Do you rely on the past, the future, or the present? Do you rely on the internal or external when you enter dhyāna? Furthermore, Śāriputra! Do you rely on body-dhyāna or mind-dhyāna?"

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī! The meaning of my dhyāna is this: all those who practice seeing the Dharma and delighting in it, all those whose minds are undistracted, abide in correct mindfulness in this way."

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! Have you attained that dharma? What kind of dharma is it? Is it seeing the Dharma and delighting in its practice? Is it not seeing the Dharma and delighting in its practice?"

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī! I have not attained that dharma, so what dharma is there that either sees the Dharma and delights in its practice, or does not see the Dharma and delights in its practice? Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata taught the dharma of freedom from desire for those of the śrāvaka vehicle. I rely upon that dharma and enter dhyāna in this way."

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! What is this dharma of freedom from desire that the Tathāgata taught for those of the śrāvaka vehicle, which you, Venerable Śāriputra, rely upon and practice?"

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī! A bhikṣu who thus relies upon past practice, relies upon future practice, relies upon present practice, and even relies upon mind-practice: these should be known. Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata taught this dharma of freedom from desire for those of the śrāvaka vehicle, and I follow that dharma and rely upon that dharma in my practice."

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! If you speak thus, relying on past practice, relying on future practice, relying on present practice, and even relying on mind-practice, free from desire: then, Venerable Śāriputra, if those dharmas do not exist in the past Tathāgata, do not exist in the future Tathāgata, and do not exist in the present Tathāgata, then these dharmas are likewise non-existent. Venerable Śāriputra! How can you then say: 'I rely on past practice, rely on future practice, rely on present practice'? Because there are no dharmas, there is also nothing to rely upon.

"Furthermore, Venerable Śāriputra! The past Tathāgata, the future Tathāgata, and the present Tathāgata: no one causes them to abide, and there is no place where they abide. If there is no abiding, then reliance cannot be found.

"Furthermore, Venerable Śāriputra! If a person says: 'The past, future, and present Tathāgata has reliance or non-reliance,' such a person slanders the Tathāgata. Why? Suchness is without thought and also without what is thought. Suchness does not retrogress. Suchness is without marks.

"Furthermore, Venerable Śāriputra! The suchness of the past cannot be found. The suchness of the future cannot be found. The suchness of the present cannot be found. Even the suchness of the mind cannot be found. These should be known.

"Furthermore, Venerable Śāriputra! There is nothing further outside of suchness that can be revealed or spoken."

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī! All the Buddha Tathāgatas, having abided in suchness, then teach the Dharma."

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! Suchness is non-existent. How could the Tathāgata, having abided in suchness, then teach the Dharma? Venerable Śāriputra! That dharma is also non-existent. How could the Tathāgata, having abided in suchness, then teach the Dharma? The Tathāgata is also non-existent. Where is the Tathāgata who, having abided in suchness, then teaches the Dharma? All dharmas cannot be found. The Buddha Tathāgatas also cannot be found. Furthermore, the obtainable and the unobtainable: these two are both unobtainable. The Tathāgata neither teaches nor does not teach. Why? Venerable Śāriputra! It cannot be said of the Tathāgata, who is without speech, 'This is the Tathāgata.'"

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī! Who then will be able to accept such a dharma?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! If a person does not grasp the conditioned dharma-realm and does not aspire to nirvāṇa, such a person can accept this dharma. If a person does not attain dharmas of the past and does not know those dharmas, does not attain dharmas of the future or present and does not know those dharmas, such a person can accept this dharma. If one neither sees defilement nor sees purity, and has no mind that grasps, such a person can accept this dharma. If one neither practices self nor practices non-self, and neither grasps nor abandons, such a person can accept this dharma. Such a person can know the meaning that has been spoken here."

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī! What then is known?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! There is nothing here that can be spoken, nor anything to ask about. What then is known?"

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī! This Dharma you teach is extremely profound. Those who have faith in this Dharma are very few indeed. Mañjuśrī! Even for arhats, those on the path of learning and those beyond learning, this is not their sphere of understanding. How much less so for all deluded ordinary beings!"

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! Just so, just so! Even for arhats, this is not their sphere of understanding. Why? Because arhats have no spheres of understanding. Arhats have no abiding and no place: this is what is called arhat. It cannot be spoken of: this is what is called arhat. Because there is nothing to be said: this is what is called arhat. Why? Without the conditioned and without abiding: this is what is called arhat. Where then is the arhat's sphere of understanding? Arhats are neither name nor form. Deluded ordinary beings discriminate between name and form. Arhats do not discriminate and know name and form: this is what is called arhat. Arhats do not discriminate name and do not discriminate form. Deluded ordinary beings also cannot be found; the dharmas of ordinary beings also cannot be found. Arhats also cannot be found; the dharmas of arhats also cannot be found. If they cannot be found, then there is no discrimination. If there is no discrimination, then there is no activity. If there is no activity, then there is no conceptual proliferation. If there is no conceptual proliferation, then there is quiescence. Thus there is no activity and no conceptual proliferation. A person who is quiescent does not grasp existence and does not grasp non-existence, neither existent nor non-existent: thus they do not grasp. If one does not grasp, then there is nothing obtained. Such a person, free from all obtaining, without mind and apart from mind, abides in the dharma of the śrāvaka. This should be known."

At that time, when the youth Mañjuśrī had finished teaching this Dharma, those five hundred bhikṣus rose from their seats, turned away, and departed, saying: "We do not wish to see the body of the youth Mañjuśrī! We do not wish to hear the name of the youth Mañjuśrī! In whatever direction, in whatever place the youth Mañjuśrī dwells, that place should also be abandoned. Why? This Mañjuśrī is different from our holy life, and therefore should be abandoned."

At that time, the Elder Śāriputra said to the youth Mañjuśrī: "Mañjuśrī! In teaching the meaning of this Dharma, was it not your intention to cause sentient beings to understand the Dharma's meaning?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Just so, just so! Venerable Śāriputra!"

The Elder Śāriputra said: "Then why have these five hundred bhikṣus risen from their seats, disparaging and leaving with slander?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! If these bhikṣus say: 'We do not wish to see the body of the youth Mañjuśrī! We do not wish to hear the name of the youth Mañjuśrī! In whatever direction, in whatever place the youth Mañjuśrī dwells, that place should also be abandoned,' those who speak thus: well done, well done! Venerable Śāriputra! These bhikṣus have spoken well. Why? Because the youth Mañjuśrī does not exist and cannot be found. If he is non-existent and cannot be found, then he cannot be seen and cannot be heard. In whatever direction, in whatever place the youth Mañjuśrī dwells, that place should also be abandoned: those who speak thus, they speak as though the dwelling place of Mañjuśrī is also non-existent. If it is non-existent, then one cannot approach it and cannot abandon it."

At that time, when the youth Mañjuśrī had taught this Dharma, the five hundred bhikṣus, having heard it, turned back around. Having turned back, they faced the youth Mañjuśrī and said: "Mañjuśrī teaches this kind of Dharma, and we are not able to understand it."

Mañjuśrī said: "Well done, well done! You bhikṣus, disciples of the Tathāgata and those of the śrāvaka vehicle, should study in this way. Bhikṣus! This kind of Dharma is neither known by consciousness nor known by wisdom. Why? Because the dharma-realm is thus by nature. The dharma-realm is thus without thought and without retrogression. Since that dharma is without thought and without retrogression, it is neither known by consciousness nor known by wisdom. What is neither known by consciousness nor known by wisdom is not something that can be thought about. You bhikṣus, disciples of the Tathāgata and those of the śrāvaka vehicle, should study in this way. If you study in this way, the Buddha says such persons have attained the most supreme Dharma. They are the world's field of merit and worthy of offerings."

When this Dharma was taught, among those five hundred bhikṣus, four hundred bhikṣus did not grasp any dharma, exhausted all their bonds and outflows, and their minds were liberated. One hundred bhikṣus gave rise to evil minds, and their own bodies were about to fall into the great hells.

At that time, the Elder Śāriputra said to the youth Mañjuśrī: "Mañjuśrī! Your teaching of the Dharma has not protected sentient beings, and you have lost these one hundred bhikṣus."

At that time, the World-Honored One told the Elder Śāriputra: "Śāriputra! Do not speak thus. Why? Śāriputra! These one hundred bhikṣus will fall into the Great Screaming Hell, but after receiving a single touch of suffering there, they will be born in the Tuṣita Heaven in the same community, because they have heard this Dharma. Śāriputra! If these bhikṣus had not heard this Dharma-gate, they would have been destined to fall into the hells. After a full kalpa, they would have been born among humans. Because they have heard this Dharma-gate, the karma that should have caused them to suffer in the hells for a full kalpa will instead be reduced to a brief experience. Śāriputra! These one hundred bhikṣus, at the first assembly of Maitreya Tathāgata, will become śrāvakas, realize arhatship, and exhaust all outflows. Thus, Śāriputra! The merit of hearing this Dharma-gate surpasses the cultivation of the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables, and the four formless samāpattis. Why? If one does not hear this Dharma-gate, one cannot be liberated from birth and death. I say that such a person cannot be freed from birth, old age, sickness, death, sorrow, pain, grief, lamentation, and distress."

At that time, the Elder Śāriputra said to the youth Mañjuśrī: "How truly rare! Mañjuśrī is well able to teach this Dharma-gate and mature sentient beings."

Mañjuśrī said: "Venerable Śāriputra! Suchness is not diminished and suchness is not increased. The dharma-realm is not diminished and the dharma-realm is not increased. The realm of sentient beings is not diminished and not increased. Why? These are merely words. There is no person to rely upon and no place to rely upon; neither reliance nor non-reliance. Venerable Śāriputra! Such non-reliance is bodhi. Such bodhi is liberation. If one relies on a dharma, that is discrimination. If one knows that it is neither made nor not-made, that is nirvāṇa."

At that time, the World-Honored One told the Elder Śāriputra: "Just so, just so! Śāriputra! As the youth Mañjuśrī has said: suchness is not diminished and suchness is not increased. The dharma-realm is not diminished and the dharma-realm is not increased. The realm of sentient beings is not diminished and not increased: neither defiled nor purified."

At that time, the World-Honored One, to clarify this meaning, spoke again in verse:

> "Speaking of the dharmas of past, future,
> And present worlds:
> Words are not the meaning;
> This is neither with marks nor without marks.
> Whether with marks or without marks,
> There is no discrimination at all.
> Through discrimination one grasps;
> Through discrimination there are no marks.
> If one discriminates the conditioned,
> Then one discriminates nirvāṇa.
> Both of these are the work of Māra:
> The wise know this.
> The aggregates, sense-bases, and elements are mere names,
> Without arising, ceasing, or marks.
> If one contemplates with discrimination,
> That is not true contemplation.
> The wise do not discriminate;
> Their sphere of practice is like space.
> If one discriminates, one grasps;
> Without discrimination, one does not grasp.
> Discrimination and grasping lead to bondage;
> Without discrimination, there is liberation.
> If one knows the Dharma in this way,
> That person is called wise.
> Such a person attains exhaustion;
> This is called non-discriminating wisdom.
> Because there is wisdom, one speaks of wisdom,
> Yet both wisdom and its expression are empty.
> If a person knows in this way,
> That person is called wise.
> If one were to fill the three-thousand worlds with treasures
> And give them away, the merit of such giving,
> Compared to a person who hears this Dharma:
> That person's merit surpasses it.
> Generosity, moral discipline, patience,
> Supernatural powers without obstruction:
> Even if practiced for billions of kalpas,
> This cannot compare to hearing this sūtra.
> If one knows this Dharma-gate,
> Spoken by the Perfectly Awakened One,
> Having heard this sūtra,
> All will attain Tathāgatahood."

When the Tathāgata had finished teaching this Dharma-gate, ten thousand sentient beings were freed from dust and defilement and attained the pure Dharma-eye regarding all dharmas. Five hundred bhikṣus gave rise to the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. At that time, the World-Honored One gave a prediction to the five hundred bhikṣus, saying: "All of you bhikṣus, in the Star-Simile Kalpa, will all attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, all bearing the same name: Dharma-Flower Tathāgata, Perfectly Awakened."

When the World-Honored One had finished speaking, the youth Mañjuśrī, the Elder Śāriputra, devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and the rest, having heard the Buddha's teaching, joyfully practiced it.
