# 彌勒菩薩所問本願經
## Sūtra on the Original Vow Asked about by the Bodhisattva Maitreya

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0349

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the kingdom of Vaiśālī, in the Fearsome Tree Forest on Mount Wondrous Flower, at a gathering place of deer, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, five hundred in number. All were worthy sages who had attained spiritual penetration, venerable bhikṣus. Their names were: the worthy Ajñāta-kauṇḍinya, the worthy Aśvajit, the worthy Vāṣpa, the worthy Mahānāma, the worthy Bhadrika, the worthy Vigatamala, the worthy Pūrṇa, the worthy Gavāṃpati, the worthy Mṛgaśrī, the worthy Uruvilvā Kāśyapa, the worthy Nadī Kāśyapa, the worthy Gayā Kāśyapa, the worthy Mahākāśyapa, the worthy Subhūti, the worthy Piṇḍola, the worthy Mukharāja, the worthy Nandika, the worthy Ānanda, the worthy Rāhula, and the worthy Ānanda. Such were the five hundred bhikṣus. There were also five hundred bodhisattvas, like Maitreya and others. Their names were: the bodhisattva Increasing Intention, the bodhisattva Firm Intention, the bodhisattva Eloquent Accumulation, the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, the bodhisattva Mahāsthāmaprāpta, the bodhisattva Crystal Auspiciousness, the bodhisattva Soft Auspiciousness, the bodhisattva Supernatural-Power Flower, the bodhisattva Emptiness, the bodhisattva Faith-Purity Joy, the bodhisattva Root-Land, the bodhisattva Renown-Land, the bodhisattva Gentle Voice, the bodhisattva Pure Land, the bodhisattva Mountain Accumulation, the bodhisattva Completeness, and the bodhisattva Root Auspiciousness. Such were the five hundred bodhisattvas.

At that time, the bodhisattva Maitreya rose from his seat, adjusted his robes, knelt with palms joined, and addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask a question. Only if the God among Gods permits will I dare to ask."

The Buddha told the bodhisattva Maitreya: "I shall hear your question. Ask whatever you wish. The Tathāgata shall respond according to your wish and dispel your doubts, causing your mind to rejoice."

Thereupon the bodhisattva Maitreya, having received permission to ask, leaped with joy and addressed the World-Honored One: "How many practices does a bodhisattva have that cause them to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions?"

The Buddha told the bodhisattva Maitreya: "Excellent, excellent! Maitreya, you have great compassion for many, bringing ease and comfort to many, with pity for all devas and humans, that you should generate the intention to ask the Tathāgata about such a meaning. Listen carefully! Constantly reflect upon it!"

Maitreya said: "Yes, World-Honored One! I receive the teaching and listen."

The Buddha said: "Maitreya! A bodhisattva has one practice that causes them to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What is this one? It is the quiescent and equanimous mind of the Way. This is the one practice."

The Buddha said to the bodhisattva Maitreya: "There are further two practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the two? First, abiding in samādhi without giving rise to anything. Second, through skillful means, discerning all that is seen. These are the two practices."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "A bodhisattva has further three practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the three? First, attaining the dharma of great compassion. Second, regarding emptiness, having nothing to practice. Third, regarding what is known, having nothing to be mindful of. These are the three practices."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "A bodhisattva has further four practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the four? First, abiding in the precepts. Second, having no doubt regarding any dharma. Third, delighting in dwelling in seclusion. Fourth, viewing all with equanimity. These are the four practices."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "A bodhisattva has further five practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the five? First, always establishing virtue and righteousness. Second, not seeking out the faults of others. Third, examining one's own conduct. Fourth, always delighting in the Dharma. Fifth, not thinking of oneself but always rescuing others. These are the five practices."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "A bodhisattva has further six practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the six? First, not being stingy or greedy. Second, eliminating a depraved and evil mind. Third, being without delusion. Fourth, having no harsh speech. Fifth, having a mind like empty space. Sixth, taking emptiness as one's dwelling. These are the six practices."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "A bodhisattva has further seven practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the seven? First, having the intention of skillful means. Second, being able to discern the various Dharma-jewels. Third, always being diligent. Fourth, always being joyful. Fifth, attaining faith-patience. Sixth, being skilled in understanding samādhi. Seventh, possessing comprehensive wisdom and insight. These are the seven practices."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "A bodhisattva has further eight practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the eight? First, attaining right view. Second, right mindfulness. Third, right speech. Fourth, right governance. Fifth, right action. Sixth, right effort. Seventh, right intention. Eighth, right concentration. These are the eight practices."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "A bodhisattva has further nine practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the nine? First, the bodhisattva, having been freed from desire and being far from all evil, unwholesome dharmas, without conception or discrimination, attains quiescent concentration and joy, practicing the first single-pointed mind. Second, having eliminated conception and discrimination, with internal tranquility, the mind becomes one, without conception or discrimination, and one attains concentration. The mind is joyful, practicing the second single-pointed mind. Third, departing from the joy of observation, always being in quiescent concentration, the body attains ease, as the sages describe; what is observed in mind and intention does not arise, practicing the third single-pointed mind. Fourth, suffering and pleasure have already been cut off; joy and grief have all ceased. What is observed is neither suffering nor pleasure; the intention is pure, attaining the fourth single-pointed mind. Fifth, surpassing the perception of form. Sixth, having no more perceptions of speech. Seventh, no longer contemplating manifold perceptions; one entirely enters the wisdom of immeasurable empty space. Eighth, completely surpassing the wisdom of immeasurable empty space, one enters the practice of immeasurable consciousness-knowing. Ninth, completely surpassing the wisdom of consciousness-knowing, having no more perception of existence or nonexistence, completely surpassing the wisdom of non-consciousness, one enters the practice of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, not seeing any perception, attaining the quiescent samādhi. These are the nine practices."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "A bodhisattva has further ten practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not follow evil companions. What are the ten? First, attaining the vajra samādhi. Second, the samādhi of benefiting wherever one abides. Third, attaining the samādhi of skillful instruction. Fourth, attaining the samādhi that masters mindful and non-mindful states. Fifth, attaining the samādhi that pervades the world. Sixth, attaining the samādhi of equanimity toward suffering and pleasure. Seventh, attaining the jewel-moon samādhi. Eighth, attaining the moonlight samādhi. Ninth, attaining the illumination samādhi. Tenth, attaining the samādhi of twofold quiescence, fully accomplished in all dharmas. Maitreya! These are the bodhisattva's ten practices that cause one to abandon all evil destinies and not fall among evil companions."

Thereupon the bodhisattva Maitreya praised the Buddha in verse:

> "The World-Honored One originally practiced generosity,
> Giving wife, children, food, and drink,
> Not begrudging head or eyes:
> The Buddha's virtue of pāramitā is without limit.
> Guarding the precepts without transgression,
> Like the *hè* bird that loves its feathers,
> Upholding precepts with no one their equal:
> The merit of pāramitā is without limit.
> Having manifested the power of patience,
> Equalizing all suffering and pleasure,
> Patient endurance is a great power:
> The Buddha's virtue of pāramitā is without limit.
> Having understood the power of diligence,
> The supreme virtue counters all harm.
> Diligence is the great attainment:
> The Buddha's diligent pāramitā is without limit.
> Having cut off all evil,
> The guide delights in single-pointed mind.
> Great wisdom's quiescence is power:
> The Buddha's pure pāramitā is without limit.
> Pure wisdom, sovereign and free,
> Naturally without anything arising,
> Wisdom is always foremost:
> The Buddha's illuminating pāramitā is without limit.
> Wisdom conquered Māra's host;
> Beneath the tree, great wisdom was attained.
> The supreme meaning, free of all defilements:
> The Buddha's power conquered Māra.
> The World-Honored One turned the Dharma-wheel,
> With the great lion's roar of his body,
> Striking fear in all the heterodox:
> The Buddha's wisdom surpasses their virtue.
> His form is wondrous, with no one its equal;
> His precept-virtue and wisdom,
> His diligence, crossing to all shores:
> The Buddha's Way surpasses all virtue.
> Difficult to compare, impossible to liken,
> The supreme, great wisdom
> Always expounds the jewels of the Dharma,
> With light guiding the assembly."

At that time, the worthy Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Unprecedented, World-Honored One! The bodhisattva Maitreya's vow is fully accomplished. His teaching of the Dharma is without deficiency. His Dharma words and phrases are even and equal. The Dharma phrases he teaches are without bondage or attachment. His Dharma discourse is completed without confusion."

The Buddha said: "It is so, it is so! Ānanda! As you have said, the bodhisattva Maitreya's eloquence is fully accomplished, and the sūtra-dharma he teaches is without deficiency."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! The bodhisattva Maitreya did not praise me in verse only now. Long ago in the past, ten incalculable kalpas ago, there was a Buddha named Flame-Light Supreme Resounding King Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, Accomplished in Wisdom and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, Honored One among the Heavens. At that time, there was a brahmin elder's son named Virtuous Conduct. He went out from a pleasure garden and from afar saw the Tathāgata walking in meditation, his body's light and color displaying immeasurable transformations. Seeing this, he thought: 'How excellent! Unprecedented! The Tathāgata's body is inconceivable, so majestic, with such wondrous light and color, awesome spiritual radiance, and the virtues of auspiciousness as his adornment. I wish that in a future life I may obtain a body endowed with such light and color, awesome spiritual radiance, and the virtues of auspiciousness as adornment.' Having made this wish, he prostrated on the ground, and from his heart he thought: 'If in the future I shall attain the dharma-body and become a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, may the Tathāgata step over my body.' At that time, the World-Honored One, Flame-Light Supreme Resounding King Tathāgata, knowing the thoughts in the mind of the brahmin elder's son Virtuous Conduct, stepped over his body. No sooner had he stepped over it than the young man attained the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas. Thereupon the Buddha looked back and told his attendant: 'The reason I stepped over the body of the brahmin elder's son Virtuous Conduct was to immediately enable him to attain the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas, so that his eyes gained penetrating vision, his ears gained penetrating hearing, he could know others' thoughts, he could know his own past lives, his body could fly, and his spiritual powers were complete.' No sooner had the Buddha stepped over the body of the brahmin Virtuous Conduct than he achieved mastery of all forms of wisdom, fully accomplishing the five supernatural powers without any loss. He then praised the Buddha in verse:

> "'Going and coming through the world to the ten directions,
> The Honored One among Humans has no equal.
> Devoted solely to the Way, surpassing all practices,
> I bow my head to the Awakened Guide.
> Surpassing all the world's lights,
> And the radiance of maṇi-jewels and flames,
> The Buddha's radiance is the most supreme.
> I bow my head to the Awakened Guide.
> Like the lion's single roar,
> Before which all lesser beasts submit,
> The Buddha's Dharma teaching is also like this,
> Subduing all the heterodox.
> The mark between the brows is clear and pure,
> With awesome power immeasurable like heaped snow.
> Its light illuminates the three realms.
> The Buddha in the world has no equal.
> Beneath the Sage's feet, wheel-marks arise,
> Their wondrous wheels having a thousand spokes.
> This earth and these mountains
> Cannot shake the Unsurpassed One.'"

At that time, the Buddha told the worthy Ānanda: "Would you like to know who the brahmin elder's son Virtuous Conduct was at that time? He is the present bodhisattva Maitreya."

The worthy Ānanda then addressed the Buddha: "The bodhisattva Maitreya attained the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas so long ago. Why has he not swiftly attained unsurpassed, true, perfect awakening?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "A bodhisattva, for four reasons, does not take up perfect awakening. What are the four? First, purifying the buddha-land. Second, protecting the buddha-land. Third, purifying all beings. Fourth, protecting all beings. These are the four. When the bodhisattva Maitreya sought Buddhahood, it was because of these four things that he did not take up Buddhahood."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! When I originally sought Buddhahood, I also wished to purify the buddha-land, purify all beings, protect the buddha-land, and protect all beings. Maitreya generated the aspiration forty-two kalpas before I did. I generated the aspiration for the Way after him. Yet in this Fortunate Kalpa, through great diligence, I leaped beyond nine kalpas and attained the unsurpassed, true Way of perfect awakening."

The Buddha told the worthy Ānanda: "I attained the most perfect awakening through ten things. What are the ten? First, giving up all my possessions without attachment. Second, my wife. Third, my children. Fourth, my head and eyes. Fifth, my hands and feet. Sixth, my kingdom. Seventh, my precious treasures and wealth. Eighth, my marrow and brain. Ninth, my blood and flesh. Tenth, not begrudging my own body and life. Ānanda! Through these ten things I swiftly attained the Way of the Buddha."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "There are further ten things through which one swiftly attains the Way of the Buddha. What are the ten? First, establishing oneself in the Dharma of precepts and virtue. Second, always practicing patience. Third, always being diligent. Fourth, always being single-minded. Fifth, always practicing the pāramitā of wisdom. Sixth, not abandoning anything. Seventh, having attained patient endurance, being equal toward all. Eighth, not fixating on emptiness. Ninth, attaining the patient endurance of the empty Dharma. Tenth, attaining the dharma of marklessness. Ānanda! Through these ten things I personally attained the Way of the Buddha."

The Buddha said to the worthy Ānanda: "When I originally sought the Way of the Buddha, the hardships were without number. Only then did I attain the unsurpassed, true Way. The matters involved were not just one."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Long ago in the past, there was a crown prince named All-Revealing Meaning, handsome and beautiful. He went out from a pleasure garden and on the road saw a person gravely ill and in distress. Seeing this, he was moved to pity and asked the sick person: 'What medicine would cure your illness?' The sick person answered: 'Only the blood of the prince's body can heal my illness.' At that time, the prince immediately took a sharp blade, pierced his body and drew blood, and gave it to the sick person. He gave it with wholehearted sincerity, without any regret."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "That crown prince All-Revealing Meaning was none other than myself. Ānanda! The water of the four great oceans could be measured, but the blood I have given cannot be reckoned. Why is this? Because I sought perfect awakening."

The Buddha said to the worthy Ānanda: "Long ago in the past, there was a crown prince named Lotus King, handsome and beautiful, of awesome and majestic spiritual power. He went out from a pleasure garden and on the road saw a person whose body was afflicted with leprosy. Seeing this, he was moved to pity and asked the sick person: 'What medicine would cure your illness?' The sick person answered: 'If I could have the marrow from the prince's body to apply to mine, my illness would be healed.' At that time, the prince immediately broke open his bones, obtained the marrow, and gave it to the sick person. He gave it joyfully, without any regret. That crown prince was none other than myself."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "The water of the four great oceans could be measured, but the marrow I have given in generosity cannot be reckoned."

The Buddha said to the worthy Ānanda: "Long ago in the past, there was a king named Moonlight, handsome and beautiful, of awesome and majestic spiritual power. He went out from his palace and on the road saw a blind man, poor, hungry, and begging along the road. The blind man approached the king and said: 'The king alone is honored, noble, safe, and happy. I alone am poor, and moreover I am blind.' At that time, King Moonlight, seeing this blind man, shed tears of pity and said to him: 'What medicine would cure your illness?' The blind man answered: 'Only the king's eyes can heal my illness and enable me to see.' At that time, King Moonlight took out both of his own eyes and gave them to the blind man. His mind was peaceful, without a single thought of regret. That King Moonlight was none other than myself."

The Buddha said: "Mount Sumeru could be weighed and measured, but the eyes I have given in generosity cannot be reckoned."

The Buddha said to the worthy Ānanda: "When the bodhisattva Maitreya originally sought the Way, he did not give away his ears, nose, head, eyes, hands, feet, body, life, precious treasures, cities, wife, children, or kingdom to achieve the Way of the Buddha. Rather, solely through the practice of skillful means, peace, and happiness did he attain the unsurpassed, true Way."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "By what skillful means did the bodhisattva Maitreya attain the Way of the Buddha?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "The bodhisattva Maitreya, six times each day and night, properly adjusted his robes, knelt with palms joined on the ground, and facing the ten directions, spoke this verse:

> "'I repent of all my faults,
> I encourage and assist in all practices of virtue,
> I take refuge and pay homage to all the Buddhas,
> That I may attain the supreme wisdom.'"

The Buddha said to the worthy Ānanda: "The bodhisattva Maitreya, through this skillful means, attained the unsurpassed, true Way of perfect awakening. Ānanda! The original vow of the bodhisattva Maitreya in seeking the Way was this: 'When I become a Buddha, may the people of my land be free from all stains and blemishes, may their desire, anger, and delusion not be great, and may they earnestly practice the ten wholesome deeds. Only then will I take up unsurpassed, perfect awakening.'"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "In the future, the people of the world will be free from stains and blemishes, will practice the ten wholesome deeds, and will not be burdened in mind by desire, anger, or delusion. At precisely that time, Maitreya will attain the unsurpassed, true Way of perfect awakening. Why is this? It is what the bodhisattva Maitreya's original vow has brought about."

The Buddha said to the worthy Ānanda: "When I originally sought the bodhisattva Way, I wished to protect all beings and cause all to become pure. I chose to abide amid the five turbidities, amid desire, anger, and delusion, delighting in birth and death. Why is this? Because these people largely practice what is unlawful, taking wrong for right, following deviant paths, robbing and harming one another, being unfilial to parents, always thinking evil. They have evil intentions toward their brothers, wives, children, relatives, and others. They slight their teachers and elders. They constantly commit offenses. They are stained and corrupt, devouring one another. I vowed to become a Buddha in just such an age. Whether in counties, kingdoms, mounds, villages, or cities, there was nothing but talk of all manner of evil, of robbing and harming one another, striking with tiles and stones, beating with staves. People would gather and revile one another, then return to their homes and prepare meals, putting poison in the food to harm others. They gave rise to stained and corrupt thoughts, slandering one another. They concealed their own faults, only to expose each other's, with no more goodness of intention."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Through great compassion, universally mindful of all, I teach the sūtra-dharma for the sake of such people."

The worthy Ānanda, hearing the Buddha teach this, addressed the Buddha: "Unprecedented! God among Gods, Tathāgata, Samyak-saṃbuddha! You are able to endure such hardship, broadly extending the great intention, taming the depraved and evil, causing them to be accomplished. You remove their heavy burdens, fully equipping them with the jewel of the Dharma, and for the sake of such people, teach the sūtra-dharma."

The Buddha said: "It is so, Ānanda! As you have said, the Buddha can endure this. Precisely because of this, as a Tathāgata, Samyak-saṃbuddha, I teach and transform the obstinate, removing the darkness for all. By the merit of the Buddha-Dharma, fully accomplished, I teach the sūtra-dharma for these people."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Hearing that the Tathāgata's steadfast and weighty diligence, and his equanimous mind, are such as this, every hair on my body stands on end. What is the name of this sūtra? How should it be practiced?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Original Vow'; uphold it. It is also called 'The Original Practice of Maitreya, the Questions of the Compassionate One'; uphold it well."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, the bodhisattva Maitreya, the worthy Ānanda, the worthy Mahākāśyapa, and the other great disciples, together with all the bodhisattvas and all those in the assembly, as well as the devas, nāgas, spirits, gandharvas, and worldly humans, having heard the sūtra, rejoiced and came forward to pay homage to the Buddha.
