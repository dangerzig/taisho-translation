# 佛說心明經
## Sūtra of Mind-Light, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0569

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with five hundred bhikṣus and the fourfold assembly. At dawn the Buddha put on his robe, took his bowl, and went to a certain village to beg for food. The devas, nāgas, and spirits attended him from above. He arrived at the gate of a brahmin's house and stood outside. The Buddha emitted a great radiance that illuminated the ten directions.

At that time the brahmin's wife was at the stove cooking rice. Seeing the light shine upon her body, her body was at ease, and she was freed from countless anxieties. She thought to herself: "This radiance is unlike that of the sun or moon, of Śakra or Brahmā, or of the four kings and all the devas. My body is suffused with joy beyond measure." She turned around and saw the Buddha, dignified and extraordinarily beautiful, like the moon among the stars, his marvelous marks towering, his excellent features complete, his faculties calm and settled. There was no decline, and he was established in the highest serenity, having attained the foremost concentration. He was like the rising sun appearing over a mountain ridge, like a wheel-turning king gracing his great hall attended by ministers, like Śakra enthroned in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, like Lord Brahmā in the seventh abode, like snow on a high mountain brilliantly shining for all to see. Her joy redoubled, and she thought further: "Today I have beheld the Buddha and his disciples, truly fulfilling my past-life aspirations. I wish to offer food to the Perfectly Awakened One. But my foolish husband does not believe in the Way and its virtue. His mind is set on heterodox doubts and the sixty-two views. If he sees what I offer, he will surely grow resentful. Through past-life faults I have lost the bold and valiant male body and fallen into the form of a woman, bound and controlled by another. I wish to offer to the Holy One but am not free to act on my own. I should take care to placate him. What should I do?" She then strained the rice, took one ladle of rice-water, and offered it to the Buddha. Through the Buddha's supernatural power, the bowl spontaneously filled with food of a hundred flavors. The Buddha then gave a dedication, speaking in verse:

> "Though one gave a hundred horses
> With gold and silver saddles and bridles,
> Bestowing them upon another,
> It would not equal a ladle of rice-water.
> Though one gave carts of the seven treasures
> Loaded full with rare and precious things,
> Offering a ladle of rice-water to the Buddha,
> The merit surpasses all of that.
> Though one gave a hundred white elephants
> Adorned with bright pearl necklaces,
> Offering the Buddha one ladle of rice-water,
> The merit exceeds even that.
> Like a holy wheel-turning king's
> Consort, the Universal Worthy Queen,
> Beautiful beyond compare,
> Her body adorned with the seven treasures:
> If such wondrous beauties,
> A hundred of each,
> Were all given away,
> It would not equal one ladle of rice-water."

At that time the brahmin was standing quietly nearby and listening. Hearing the Buddha's praise, his heart was filled with doubt. He came forward and asked the Buddha: "What is one ladle of rice-water worth, that you should praise it so? Gifts of abundant treasures, elephants, horses, and carriages should not be disparaged, yet you say they do not equal a ladle of rice-water. This rice-water is not worth a single coin, yet you praise it as many billions of times greater. Who would believe this?"

Then the World-Honored One immediately revealed his broad and long tongue, covering his face and reaching up to the Brahmā heaven. He told the brahmin: "Through countless billions of kalpas, I have always practiced sincerity and the six perfections, giving away everything for the peace of all, possessing and not begrudging. It is through this that I obtained this tongue. Could it have been obtained through false speech? I wish to ask you, and you must answer truthfully. Have you ever traveled between Śrāvastī and Rājagṛha and along the road seen a tree called the nyagrodha, whose shade shelters five hundred carriages?"

He replied: "Indeed, there is such a tree. I have seen it myself."

The World-Honored One asked again: "How large is its seed?"

He answered: "Shaped like a mustard seed."

The Buddha told the brahmin: "Then you are speaking with a forked tongue. If the seed is truly like a mustard seed, how can the tree be so great?"

He replied: "It is truly so. I do not dare deceive."

The Buddha told him again: "The seed is like mustard, yet it produces a vast and great tree. The earth's generative power has nowhere it cannot reach, and what it shelters is immense. How much more so the Tathāgata, the Unsurpassed One, Truly and Perfectly Awakened, whose boundless gathering of merit surpasses all! With his precepts, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge and vision of liberation, with his great compassion and vast pity reaching out to save all, the merit of offering food to him is beyond reckoning." The brahmin fell silent and had no reply.

Then the Buddha smiled. Five-colored light issued from his mouth and illuminated the ten directions. Beings of the five destinies were affected: the devas' desires stilled, the human heart rejoiced, hungry ghosts were sated, the torments of Naraka ceased, and animals' minds opened and their offenses were removed. Following the light, they all came to the Buddha's presence.

Whenever the Buddhas smile, there are always auspicious signs. When giving a bodhisattva prediction, the light illuminates the ten directions and enters through the crown. When giving a pratyekabuddha prediction, the light enters through the face. When giving a śrāvaka prediction, the light enters through the shoulders. When speaking of rebirth in heaven, the light enters through the navel. When speaking of descent into the human realm, the light enters through the knees. When speaking of the three lower destinies, it enters through the soles of the feet.

The Buddhas' joy is not born of desire, not of anger, not of ignorance, not of heedlessness, not of profit-seeking, not of glory, and not of wealth. Now the Buddha, with equal compassion for all beings, smiled a great smile of compassion, free of these seven.

The worthy Ānanda, who understood the seven qualities (knowing the Dharma, understanding the meaning, discerning the time, comprehending moderation, knowing the assembly, knowing himself, and recognizing the capacities of others) rose from his seat, straightened his robes, and knelt: "Why has the Buddha smiled? May you explain its meaning."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Did you see the brahmin's wife generate a great resolve?"

He replied: "I have seen it."

The Buddha said: "When this woman's life ends, she shall transform her female form and become a man. She shall be born in the heavens as the most honored among the devas. When she descends to the human world, she shall be the foremost among humans. She shall understand the profound and wonderful Dharma: that it is like an illusion, like a transformation, like the moon in water, like a shadow, like an echo, like a mirage. After thirty kalpas, she shall become a Buddha named Cittaprabha Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One."

The brahmin's heart was subdued. He prostrated himself on the ground and reproached himself in repentance: "I am like a foolish child, ignorant and in darkness. I harbored doubt and uncertainty, not recognizing the Great Sage, and spoke coarse words from my mouth. I beg forgiveness for my offense."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Since you have seen your own fault and taken refuge in the Buddha, repenting your transgressions before the Way, the Dharma, and the Vinaya, your offenses shall diminish and your merit shall grow daily."

The brahmin then requested: "May you extend your great compassion, your kindness, and your protection, and allow me to leave the household." The Buddha immediately accepted him as a śramaṇa. His hair and beard were removed and Dharma robes appeared upon his body. At that time the World-Honored One taught the Four Noble Truths: suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. The brahmin leaped with joy, and his defilements were exhausted and his mind liberated.

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the worthy Ānanda, the fourfold assembly, the devas, humans, nāgas, and spirits all generated the resolve for the Way and departed with joy, bowing their heads.
