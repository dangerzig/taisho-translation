# 佛說分別經
## Sūtra of Distinctions, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0738

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada. Early in the morning he adjusted his robes and sat in dignified composure.

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Inform the bhikṣus that all should listen in quiet attentiveness. I shall now teach you about the suffering that people undergo in life."

Ānanda rose from his seat, adjusted his robes, bowed to the Buddha, and addressed the Buddha: "We gladly wish to hear."

The Buddha said: "People have six deceptions by which they harm and cheat themselves. What are the six? The eye is deceived by form, the ear is deceived by sound, the nose is deceived by scent, the mouth is deceived by taste, the body is deceived by fine and smooth sensations, and the mind falls into wrong thoughts and is deceived by wrong thoughts. These are the six deceptions. They cause people to fall into the evil paths with no prospect of escape. Only the wise can truly awaken to this!"

The Buddha said: "From three transgressions, people obtain three sufferings. What are the three transgressions? First, the body transgresses through killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct. Second, the mouth transgresses through divisive speech, harsh abuse, false speech, and frivolous talk. Third, the mind transgresses through greed, anger, and delusion. Because of these three transgressions, one falls into the hells, the realm of hungry ghosts, and the realm of animals. These are the three sufferings. Only the wise awaken to them."

The Buddha said: "People have six indulgences, and fall into eighteen kinds of suffering. What are the six indulgences? The eye indulges in entering into form, the ear indulges in entering into sound, the nose indulges in entering into scent, the mouth indulges in entering into taste, the body indulges in entering into fine and smooth sensations, and the mind indulges in entering into wrong thoughts. These are the six indulgences. They are both what one receives and what causes decline. Because of them, one falls into the eighteen hells, where suffering and pain last long with no prospect of escape."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "If people serve the Buddha and receive the precepts, can they obtain escape from this suffering and pain?"

The Buddha said: "There are people who serve the Buddha and receive the precepts and obtain merit beyond measure and beyond comparison. There are also people who serve the Buddha yet fall into the gravest sins."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "Serving the Buddha and receiving the Buddha's precepts should bring merit. How can one instead incur deep sin? What is the reason? I wish to hear the meaning of this."

The Buddha said: "There are people who serve the Buddha, uphold the sūtras and precepts, and are diligent without transgression. They obtain merit beyond measure and beyond comparison."

The Buddha said: "There are people who serve the Buddha and receive the precepts but do not uphold them, who cannot be diligent in dhyāna concentration and contemplation, who merely claim the name of serving the Buddha while devoting themselves to wrong pursuits, greedy and insatiable, not knowing when to stop. Indulging in lust and sensual desire, delighting in singing and dancing, addicted to the taste of wine, they give themselves over to license. Though they claim to serve the Buddha, their transgressions are immeasurable. Because of this, they fall for a long time into the three evil paths, where the suffering is manifold and escape is hard to achieve."

The Buddha said: "There are three grades of those who serve the Buddha. The first grade serve the Buddha as disciples of Māra. The second grade serve the Buddha as heavenly beings. The third grade serve the Buddha as disciples of the Buddha."

"What does it mean to serve the Buddha as a disciple of Māra?"

The Buddha said: "Though one receives the Buddha's precepts, if the mind delights in wrong pursuits such as divination, inquiring about curses, performing rituals of exorcism and prayer, and believing in household spirits and patriarchs, not believing in what is right and true, not knowing the consequences of sin and evil, merely claiming the name of serving the Buddha while always associating with what is deviant: at death one falls into the Avīci hell, where suffering lasts for a long time. After a long time one emerges, only to become a subject of Māra's realm, flattering, deceitful, and bewitching, extremely difficult to liberate. Such people, through the residual merit of past lives, temporarily gain a moment of seeing the right path, but their minds are dim and confused, unable to understand what was planted in the past, and they must enter again into wrong views without end. This is what it means to serve the Buddha as a disciple of Māra."

"What does it mean to serve the Buddha as a heavenly being?"

"One receives and upholds the five precepts, practices the ten wholesome deeds, and in life after life does not transgress. One believes that there are consequences for sin and merit, that as one acts so one receives. After life ends, one is born in the heavens. This is what it means to serve the Buddha as a heavenly being."

"What does it mean to serve the Buddha as a disciple of the Buddha?"

"One upholds the right precepts, broadly studies the sūtras and precepts, cultivates the highest wisdom, understands the suffering of the three realms, and the mind does not delight in or cling to them. Wishing to attain liberation, one practices the four immeasurables and the six perfections. With compassion for all sentient beings, wishing to bring them peace and deliverance, not clinging to body or life, knowing that death is followed by rebirth, one seeks to increase merit for the long term and does not engage in wrong pursuits. This is what it means to serve the Buddha as a disciple of the Buddha."

The Buddha said: "After my parinirvāṇa, within a thousand years, the way of Māra will flourish. The age will be greatly evil, the nation will have no stable ruler, and the people will have no stable dwelling. People from distant lands will enter the central country, plundering and killing with cruelty and violence, without law or principle. At such a time, the semblance-dharma will flourish."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What is the semblance-dharma?"

The Buddha said: "In the future, bhikṣus will not uphold the right Dharma. They will take wives and raise children without any sense of shame. They will plow fields and plant crops as their regular occupation, no longer studying, no longer sitting in dhyāna or practicing. They will delight in worldly customs, regarding them as refined and elegant. They will look upon each other with affected airs, high and low alike following the same ways. They will brush aside and dismiss the foundations of teaching that leads to liberation from the world. Confused by sensual desire, without fear of sin, when someone who knows the Dharma speaks true words and shows the right Dharma, they will harbor jealousy and enmity and wish to destroy that person. They will spread criticisms, picking apart strengths and weaknesses, slandering and driving the person out, leaving them without recourse. Because of this, the great Dharma will gradually diminish."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "At such a time, will there be any who uphold the Dharma?"

The Buddha said: "Many will serve the Buddha, and there will also be those who go forth from the household life. But they will not uphold the precepts. They will be jealous of one another. Those who understand the meaning will be few; most will not comprehend."

Ānanda said: "At such a time, which country will be the worst, with the least faith and practice?"

The Buddha said: "In the land of China there will be a thousand bhikṣus gathered in the great country, all having fallen into Māra's domain. Among them, the wise, perhaps one or two, will be true disciples of the Buddha. Those born in the six heavens will also be very, very few. Those in Māra's domain will be very, very many."

The Buddha said: "After my parinirvāṇa, there will also be many followers of other teachings who come seeking my path. Those who accept them should examine them carefully and thoroughly for three months, to know their aspiration and whether they can practice pure conduct, being humble, content, and of few desires, not engaging in defiled conduct. Then they may be accepted. First confer the ten wholesome precepts, and after a full three years, when they are accustomed to the intention of the path and do not transgress in evil matters, then further confer the two hundred and forty precepts. As for the observances of deportment, if they diligently guard and practice them, all these lead toward liberation. This is what Maitreya will establish. Through this they will attain liberation, according with the path."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "As the Buddha has taught, I receive it all with the utmost respect. I shall transmit it to those who come after, so that the Buddha's vast Dharma will not be cut off."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "All that you have received, before and after, you have threaded through your heart. I also know that you have faith in and protect the Buddha-Dharma."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "If in the future there are people who believe in and delight in the Dharma, who sincerely wish to seek it, who sever worldly ties and leave conventional ways to follow the right path, yet at that time there is no enlightened teacher to transmit the precepts and instructions to them: if a single person were to write out the precepts and rules and give them to such a person, could that person thereby be ordained and practice the path?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "All must be able to do so. Those who know the Dharma of prohibitions may then confer the precepts. One cannot receive the precepts through written words alone and consider it the proper method. Why? The Buddha is the great wisdom of the heavens above and the earth below, the great liberator of the heavens above and the earth below, the great illumination of the heavens above and the earth below. The teaching cannot be recklessly transmitted in a way that loses its meaning. All must be clear about the precept-dharma and the rules of prohibition, thoroughly practiced in every matter, and only then may they confer the precepts upon others. Those who are not clear about the essential matters of the Dharma, precepts, prohibitions, and rules, yet recklessly confer the precept-dharma upon others, violate the Buddha's sincere trust and treat what is wrong as right. The sin is great, not small. One should be extremely careful."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "In the final age of the world, if there are people who sincerely and wholeheartedly grow weary of suffering and wish to seek liberation, yet there is no Buddha in the world, by what means shall their aspirations be fulfilled?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "One should take them to someone who is clear about the precept-dharma and well-versed in the essential matters of deportment and the rules of prohibition. In this way it is proper to ordain them, and they too can attain liberation. If one who is not clear about these things confers ordination upon another, both become lost and confused on the path, muddled without end. How could they ever attain liberation?"

The Buddha said: "In the future there will be bhikṣus who cannot purify themselves. They will take wives and raise children, their conduct defiled and impure, greedy for offerings, not believing in the consequences of sin and merit, yet hoping for ease and happiness. It will be hard for them to attain liberation. This is indeed pitiable."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "In such a future age, those who follow the path and don the robes all do so through the Buddha's spiritual power. Such people have already obtained the semblance of the right and true causes and conditions, and through these should attain liberation. Why then would some not believe, and, violating the Buddha's clear teaching, must again undergo countless kalpas of suffering?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "These are all people who, in previous lives through countless kalpas, had fallen into prolonged suffering. While in that place of suffering, they repented and vowed to do good, and through that they were able to attain liberation. Because of the merit of that moment of repentance, they accordingly obtained merit, which followed them to be reborn in the latter age as human beings, briefly encountering the Buddha's sūtras. Furthermore, they were able to shave their heads and become bhikṣus. But their fundamental consciousness had not yet been disciplined; their minds wavered and were dim, unable to understand their past. Therefore they remain defiled, mostly unable to leave worldly ways, not encountering clear wisdom. After such a time, they must again fall into the most extreme suffering and undergo countless kalpas of punishment."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! You have gone forth from the household life, leaving behind wife and children, abandoning worldly conduct, and becoming śramaṇas. You should cultivate the practice of the precepts, following the method of the arhats. Better to have molten copper poured into one's mouth, burning through one's belly and intestines, than to accept the faithful offerings of others without virtue. Better to have one's hands cut off with a sharp blade and one's body dismembered than to accept the faithful offerings of others without virtue. A person who lacks virtue yet accepts the faithful offerings of others must fall into sinful suffering for many kalpas. After a long, long time they emerge, and through the residual dregs of merit they obtain a human body, only to have to repay each debt one by one. Some repay as slaves, some repay as children, some repay as parents."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What does it mean to repay debts?"

The Buddha said: "Some serve as slaves, and the master beats them unreasonably, yet the slave accepts it without resentment. They work diligently without fearing toil, and they cherish the master's possessions, not daring to waste them. This is a slave repaying debts in the present life. In past lives, they received the faithful offerings of others but did not cultivate merit. When their punishment was complete, they came to repay the debt. Because they still have their original consciousness, they harbor no resentment and merely accept it.

"What does it mean to repay debts as a child? The child earns wealth, and the parents spend it without limit, yet the child's heart feels no regret. This is a child repaying debts.

"What does it mean to repay debts as a parent? The parents earn wealth, and the child freely spends it. The parents feel no reluctance and allow what is needed. All these are debts being repaid through the causes and conditions of past consciousness. Therefore there is no feeling of reluctance. In all these cases of repaying debts, the causes and conditions bring them together; when the account is settled, they disperse again. There is no permanent abiding. The wise awaken to this and therefore do not act in this way. Only the path and its virtue can be preserved for a long time. In my past lives, I too served as a slave, child, and parent repaying debts, times beyond count. All had their temporary causes and conditions, and escape was difficult. Now that I have attained the path, my present parents are all connected through the causes and conditions of the path and its virtue from past lives, not through debt-repayment. My parents in life after life released me and allowed me to study the path. Through kalpas of diligent effort, I have now accomplished Buddhahood. All this is due to the kindness of parents. Those who wish to study the path must be diligent and filial. If one fails in either of these, one loses the human seed and will not recover it for many kalpas. In the final age of the world, one should practice in accordance with what is proper. When one encounters the path of the sūtras, one must be diligent. When one encounters a Buddha-age, one must sincerely receive the teaching and take it to heart. When one encounters a wise person, one must diligently inquire and respectfully receive instruction. Why? Because a human body is hard to obtain, complete sense-faculties are hard to have, talent and intelligence are hard to achieve, a Buddha is hard to encounter, and the sūtras are hard to hear. Therefore one should be diligent."

The Buddha said: "After my parinirvāṇa, there will be the age of the five corruptions. At such a time, in the land of China, the works of Māra will flourish, obstructing and blocking the right path. Though sūtras and Dharma exist, few will study them, and of those who study, few will practice. Bhikṣus in that age will rarely be able to maintain purity. Most will have defiled and worldly habits: seeking fine clothing, learning worldly eloquence, pursuing worldly proprieties and expenses, gathering in cliques and following one another, indulging the heart and mind, seeking worldly fame and reputation. They will teach people to enter the Dharma, ordaining them as disciples, but will not teach them to guard against Māra or follow the right path. As for the work of liberation from the world, they will not study or seek clear wisdom. They will consider themselves greatly virtuous yet will not guard the sense-gates. Though they have obtained a human birth, it is merely borrowed for a time. Thinking themselves long-lived, they do not know the great reckoning that awaits them. They must afterwards undergo suffering without end, turning upside-down and topsy-turvy, in the host of Māra. How lamentable! Bhikṣus, having already obtained a human body, with sense-faculties complete, and having encountered the Buddha's sūtras and precepts, you should diligently practice and recite them. Once you lose the root of humanity, it will be hard to obtain it again. A Buddha-age is hard to encounter, and the Dharma of the sūtras is hard to hear. Each of you should reflect upon this."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, all the bhikṣus sat in dignified composure, reflecting upon themselves, and immediately attained arhatship.
