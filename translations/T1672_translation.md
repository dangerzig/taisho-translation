# Essential Dharma Verses Spoken by Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna for King Śatavāhana

## *Lóngshù Púsà Wéi Chántuójiā Wáng Shuōfǎ Yàojì* (龍樹菩薩為禪陀迦王說法要偈)

Taishō 1672

Composed by Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna (*Lóngshù Púsà*, 龍樹菩薩). Translated by Guṇavarman (*Qúnà Bámó*, 求那跋摩) of the Liú-Sòng dynasty. A didactic letter in verse.

*Note: This is a second Chinese translation of Nāgārjuna's celebrated "Letter to a Friend" (Suhṛllekha), here addressed to King Śatavāhana (Chántuójiā). A parallel version translated by Saṅghavarman exists as T1673. This translation by Guṇavarman (367–431) is somewhat longer and more detailed than the Saṅghavarman version, with expanded treatments of heavenly pleasures, hell sufferings, hungry-ghost torments, and the contemplation of the body's impurity. The letter covers the full range of Buddhist practice from basic ethics through dependent origination to the aspiration for buddhahood, all presented as personal advice from a spiritual teacher to a royal patron.*

> King Śatavāhana, you should know:
> Birth-and-death, suffering, and affliction have many faults,
> All covered and obstructed by ignorance.
> I wish to bring benefit to you.

> Just as one carves and paints a buddha-image,
> The wise who see it should bow in reverence.
> I, relying on the Tathāgata, speak the true dharma;
> Great king, you too should deeply trust and receive it.

> Though you have earlier heard the Muni's words,
> If you now listen and receive them, you will further discern.
> Just as a lotus pool of pure color
> Shines with increased brilliance when moonlight descends upon it.

> The Buddha teaches that the six recollections should be cultivated:
> The Three Jewels, generosity, precepts, and the heavens.
> Practice the ten wholesome deeds and purify the three karmas;
> Keep far from wine, heedlessness, and wrong livelihood.

> Contemplate how body, life, and wealth swiftly decay;
> Give to the fields of merit and relieve the destitute.
> Generosity is the most firm, without equal,
> The foremost and most intimate companion.

> Diligently cultivate pure precepts and remove all blemish;
> Do not seek or wish for any realm of existence.
> Just as the great earth nurtures all things,
> Precepts likewise give rise to all that is wholesome.

> Cultivate patience with gentleness and abandon anger;
> The Buddha says this practice is the most supreme.
> Likewise diligence, meditation, and wisdom:
> With these six practices complete, one transcends birth-and-death.

> If one can honor one's father and mother at home,
> This is itself the supreme field of merit.
> In the present life, great fame spreads far;
> In the future, the reward of merit is beyond measure.

> Killing, stealing, lust, deception, indulging in wine,
> Carved beds, lofty and broad, and perfumed incense,
> Singing, dancing, and eating at improper times:
> All such evils should be kept far away.
> If even for a short time one cultivates these precepts,
> One will surely receive heavenly joy and ascend to nirvāṇa.

> Miserliness, jealousy, craving, guile, and deception,
> Delusion, inversion, and laziness:
> All such unwholesome dharmas,
> Great king, you should observe and swiftly abandon.

> Beauty, honor, and the five pleasures:
> Know they are perilous and decaying like bubbles.
> Do not rely on such unreliable things;
> Heedless indulgence gives rise to all suffering.
> To increase all good and realize the sweet dew,
> One should keep far away, as from discarding poison.

> One who diligently abandons anger and pride
> Is like clouds dispersing to reveal the autumn moon,
> Like Aṅgulimāla and Nanda,
> And like the worthy ones Kṣema and the other sages.

> The Tathāgata says there are three kinds of speech:
> Pleasing, truthful, and false.
> Pleasing speech is like flowers; truthful speech like honey;
> False speech is base and foul, like dung and filth.
> One should cultivate the first two kinds
> And swiftly sever and abandon the false.

> The four dharmas of going from light to light:
> O king, you should distinguish them and reflect carefully.
> Two kinds lead into light and should be cultivated;
> If heading toward dark delusion, one should swiftly abandon them.

> The *āmra* fruit has four kinds of transformation;
> People are similarly difficult to distinguish.
> One should observe deeply with wisdom;
> If truly worthy and good, one should draw near.

> Though you see a woman of supreme beauty,
> You should regard her as your own mother, sister, or daughter.
> If craving desire and defiling love should arise,
> You should rightly cultivate the contemplation of impurity.

> The mind is restless and should be restrained,
> As one protects one's life and treasures.
> If desire arises, one should be alarmed,
> As one fears swords and fierce beasts.

> Desire is profitless, like a venomous enemy:
> Such are the words the Muni has spoken.
> The cycle of birth-and-death is worse than prison bonds;
> One should diligently seek liberation.

> The six sense-gates are restless, racing toward their objects;
> One should gather and hold them without heedlessness.
> If one can thus restrain the faculties,
> This surpasses a valiant general crushing a mighty foe.

> This body is impure; nine orifices flow endlessly,
> Like rivers and seas without end.
> Thin skin covers and conceals, seeming pure,
> As if adorned with jeweled garlands.
> The wise discern and recognize its deception,
> And knowing it is false, they cast it aside.

> Like one with scabies approaching a fierce flame,
> First briefly pleased, but afterward suffering increases:
> The thought of craving desire is just like this,
> Pleasant at first but in the end bringing much affliction.

> Seeing the body's true characteristics as all impure
> Is itself contemplating emptiness and non-self.
> If one can cultivate this contemplation,
> Among all beneficial practices it is the most supreme.

> Though one has beauty, clan, and wide learning,
> Without precepts and wisdom one is like an animal.
> Though lowly and poor, with little learning,
> One who cultivates precepts and wisdom is called a superior person.
> The eight worldly winds cannot be avoided,
> But if one can sever them, that is truly without equal.

> For the sake of śramaṇas, brāhmaṇas,
> Parents, wives, children, and kin,
> Do not follow their wishes and accept their words
> To broadly create unwholesome, unlawful deeds.
> If for their sake you generate transgressions,
> In the future, the great suffering will be borne by you alone.

> One who creates many evils does not receive immediate retribution,
> Not like the mutual wounding of swords.
> At the end of life, the marks of sin all appear together;
> One then enters hell and suffers every torment.

> Faith, precepts, generosity, learning, wisdom, shame, and conscience:
> These seven dharmas are called the noble treasury,
> True and without compare, as the Muni has said,
> Surpassing all the world's precious jewels.
> Great king, if you gather these supreme treasures,
> Before long you too will realize the fruit of the site of awakening.

> Gambling, heavy drinking, fondness for music,
> Laziness, heedless pride, and evil companions,
> Acting rashly at improper times:
> These seven dharmas should be kept far away.

> Contentment is the foremost of all wealth:
> Such are the words the World-Honored One has spoken.
> The contented, though poor, may be called rich;
> Those with wealth but much desire are called poor.
> If wealth and enterprise only increase suffering,
> It is like a many-headed dragon, each head adding venom.

> Regard fine food as medicine;
> With wisdom-water, sprinkle and purify it.
> Though one should eat to sustain this body,
> Do not crave flavors and increase in pride.
> Toward all defiling desires, give rise to revulsion;
> Diligently seek the unsurpassed path of nirvāṇa.

> Harmonize the body and bring it to ease;
> Then one should cultivate fasting and the precepts.
> A single night is divided into five periods;
> In two of them one should rest in sleep.
> In the early, middle, and late night, contemplate birth-and-death;
> Diligently seek the crossing; do not let time pass in vain.

> The four immeasurable meditations should be cultivated;
> This is called opening the path to the Brahmā heaven.
> If one concentrates and binds the mind to the four dhyānas,
> At life's end one will surely be born in that heavenly realm.

> All conditioned things are transient and impermanent,
> Suffering, empty, decaying, and not solid,
> Without self, without joy, not pure:
> All these are called the remedial dharmas.

> If one deeply contemplates this dharma-gate,
> In future lives one will always abide in an honored position.
> Cultivating the five precepts and severing the five wrongs:
> This too is what the great king should bear in mind.

> Just as a pinch of salt placed in the Ganges
> Cannot make the water taste salty,
> So a tiny evil encountering abundant good
> Is dissolved and scattered, just like this.

> If the five wrongs increase and rob one's merit,
> O king, you should eliminate them and not let them grow.
> Faith and the other five faculties are the source of all good;
> These should be cultivated until they flourish.
> The eight sufferings, such as birth, constantly blaze;
> One should hold the water of wisdom and sprinkle to extinguish them.

> Wishing to seek heavenly joy and nirvāṇa,
> One should diligently cultivate right knowledge and right view.
> Though one has sharp intelligence, if entering a wrong path,
> Subtle and wondrous merit is forever lost.

> The four inversions harm all that is wholesome;
> Therefore contemplate them and do not let them arise.
> Form is not the self; the self is not form;
> Within the self there is no form; within form there is no self.
> With respect to form, generate these four kinds of mind;
> For the remaining aggregates, it is likewise.
> These twenty types of mind are called "inversions";
> If one can sever and eliminate them, this is the most supreme.

> Dharmas do not arise of themselves, nor from primordial darkness,
> Nor from a creator god, nor from time.
> All arise from ignorance, craving, and karma;
> If causes and conditions are absent, they are destroyed.

> Great king, having known these causes,
> You should light the lamp of wisdom and shatter the darkness of delusion.
> The view of a self in the body, clinging to rules, and doubt:
> These three can obstruct the undefiled path.

> O king, if you destroy and scatter them,
> The holy dharma of liberation shall become manifest.
> Like a blind person asking about the characteristics of water,
> In a hundred million kalpas one cannot understand.
> Seeking nirvāṇa is just like this:
> Only through one's own diligence can one later realize it.

> Wishing to rely on kin and acquaintances
> To obtain it: this is extremely difficult.
> Therefore, great king, be diligent;
> Only afterward can you realize quiescence.

> Generosity, precepts, wide learning, and meditation:
> Through these one gradually approaches the four noble truths.
> The lord of people should cultivate the light of wisdom,
> Practicing these three dharmas to seek liberation.
> If one can cultivate this most supreme vehicle,
> It encompasses all other forms of good.

> Great king, you should contemplate mindfulness of the body:
> The World-Honored One has called it the path of purity.
> Without this mindfulness, unwholesome thoughts increase;
> Therefore one should diligently cultivate it.

> Human life is brief and does not long endure,
> Like a bubble on water, arising and swiftly vanishing.
> Between the outbreath and the inbreath, between sleep and waking,
> Thought after thought constantly decays.
> Before long one shall see it ground to nothing:
> Skin and flesh, rotting and stinking, most detestable,
> Bruised, swollen, decayed, with pus and blood flowing,
> Worms and maggots feeding until nothing remains,
> Hair, nails, and teeth each scattered apart,
> Blown by wind and dried by sun until utterly gone.

> Know that this body is not solid,
> A mass of immeasurable sufferings gathered together.
> Therefore all worthy, holy, and wise persons
> Contemplate these faults and cast the body aside.

> Mount Sumeru, the great sea, and all rivers:
> When seven suns shine together, all melt and dry up.
> Even such solid things are destroyed and ruined;
> How much more this perilous, fragile body!

> When impermanence arrives, there is no rescue or protection;
> It cannot be relied on or pursued.
> Therefore, great king, constantly contemplate with care;
> Swiftly give rise to revulsion and seek the supreme dharma.

> The human body is hard to obtain; the dharma is hard to hear,
> Like a blind turtle encountering a floating log's hole.
> Having obtained such a rare body,
> One should diligently listen to the true dharma.
> To obtain this wondrous body and create evil deeds
> Is like filling a jeweled vessel with poison.

> Born in a central land, encountering a good teacher,
> With single-minded resolve, giving rise to right vows,
> Having long planted merit and possessing all faculties:
> O king, you now fully possess all these virtues.

> If one further draws near to wise persons,
> The Buddha says this is the pure holy life.
> Therefore one should delight in following them;
> All buddhas realize nirvāṇa through this.

> Having encountered the subtle and pure dharma,
> One should set one's aspiration on the path free from desire.
> In birth-and-death, perilous and difficult, suffering is immeasurable;
> Even speaking through endless kalpas cannot exhaust it.
> I now briefly explain it for the king;
> You should listen carefully and reflect well.

> The three realms revolve without end;
> Father, mother, wife, and child are mutually conditioned.
> Enemy and kin, hatred and love have no fixed place,
> Like a whirling fire-wheel, when will it end?

> From beginningless birth-and-death throughout the ages,
> If one reckons the mother's milk consumed, it surpasses the great sea.
> If one does not diligently realize the wisdom of emptiness,
> In the future one will drink yet more without limit.

> Circling through the five destinies, passing through human and heavenly realms:
> If one piled the bones of a single person, they would exceed Mount Sumeru.
> The tears of grief from loving separations
> Could not be matched by rivers and seas.
> If one counted the parents of a single person,
> They would exceed the grasses and trees of the world.

> Though one receives the five pleasures of heavenly joy,
> In the end one falls back into the suffering of evil destinies.
> The lifespan of the devas is extremely long;
> The pleasures during that time are beyond description.
> Singing and dancing, flowing with wondrous sounds,
> Mournful tones harmonious and refined, supremely clear and far-reaching,
> Marvelous forms of extraordinary beauty,
> Surrounded and attended in mutual delight,
> A hundred flavors of splendid banquets, all fully provided,
> Whatever is wished for naturally arrives.
> Jeweled pools, fragrant and pure, always filled with water,
> Ringed and covered with wondrous flowers,
> Birds of varied colors gathering upon them,
> Their mournful calls harmonizing in distant song.
> The devas sport and bathe within:
> Such joys are beyond description.

> When merit is exhausted and death approaches, the five signs of decay appear;
> The suffering born at that time surpasses the former joy.
> Therefore, though there is the delight of heavenly maidens,
> The wise, seeing this, have already given rise to revulsion.

> Though dwelling in jeweled towers and lofty pavilions,
> One must surely fall back to the rank and foul.
> Though roaming in the heavenly Nandana Garden,
> One will return and enter the Sword-Forest.
> Though bathing in the heavenly Mandārava Pool,
> One will at last fall into the Ash-River Hell.
> Though holding the rank of a wheel-turning emperor,
> One becomes a servant, driven and ordered.
> Though receiving the desireless joys of the Brahmā heaven,
> One falls back into the fierce suffering of Avīci.
> Though residing in a heavenly palace of full radiance,
> Afterward one enters the darkness of hell.

> The Black-Cord Hell, the Reviving Hell,
> Burning, hacking, flaying, piercing, and the Unrelenting:
> These eight hells constantly blaze,
> All the evil karmic retribution of beings.

> Some suffer great agony as if being pressed for oil;
> Some have their bodies crushed to powder-fine dust.
> Some have their limbs separated and scattered;
> Others are flayed, stripped, and boiled.
> Some have molten copper poured into their mouths;
> Others are split apart by iron presses.
> Iron dogs race to devour and feed on them;
> Iron crows gather to tear and gnaw.
> Every kind of venomous creature bites and chews;
> Or one is impaled on a burning copper pillar.
> Great fires blaze fiercely, burning through entirely;
> Due to karmic bonds, there is no escape.
> Cauldrons of boiling water surge to great heights;
> Sinners are cast headlong within.

> Human life is perilous and swiftly passing,
> Like the time of a single breath among the devas.
> If a person in this brief life
> Hears of the sufferings above and feels no alarm,
> Know that such a mind is extremely hard,
> Like vajra, difficult to destroy.

> If merely seeing a painting or hearing another's words,
> Or reflecting on it from reading sūtras,
> Even knowing of such things is already hard to endure;
> How much more to experience it oneself!

> In the Unrelenting Hell without rescue,
> The sufferings within are beyond exhaustion.
> If a person in a single day
> Were pierced by three hundred lances in the body,
> Compared to a single moment's suffering in Avīci,
> Not a hundred-thousandth, not a millionth, could compare.
> Enduring this great suffering for an entire kalpa,
> Only when the karmic bonds are exhausted is one finally freed.

> From whom does such suffering arise?
> All from the unwholesome actions of the three karmas.
> Great king, though you now have no such affliction,
> If you do not cultivate the causes, you will fall.

> In the animal realm, suffering is immeasurable:
> Some are bound, beaten, and whipped.
> Without faith, precepts, or learning,
> They constantly harbor evil minds and devour one another.
> Some are killed for their pearls, feathers, horns, or tusks,
> Their bones, hair, hides, or flesh, bringing cruel harm.
> Ridden and driven by people, without freedom,
> They constantly suffer from stones, swords, and staves.

> In the hungry-ghost realm, suffering is likewise:
> Whatever is needed and desired does not come as wished.
> Tormented by hunger, thirst, cold, and heat,
> Exhaustion and similar sufferings are beyond measure.

> Bellies as large as mountains, throats as thin as needles,
> Feces, urine, pus, and blood beyond description.
> Naked, draped in hair, most hideous,
> Like palmyra trees scorched and shorn.
> From their mouths at night great fires blaze;
> Worms race to devour and feed.
> Feces, urine, filth, and impurities:
> In a hundred million kalpas they cannot obtain even these.
> If by searching they find a small portion,
> Others rob and seize it, and it is soon lost.

> In the cool autumn moonlight they suffer blazing heat;
> In the warm spring sunlight, cold suffering deepens.
> If they approach a garden, all fruits vanish;
> If they reach a clear stream, it turns to dry waste.
> Due to karmic bonds, their lifespans are long,
> Lasting fifteen thousand years.
> Receiving every kind of torment without respite:
> All this is the retribution of the hungry-ghost realm.
> The Perfectly Awakened One says the cause of this suffering
> Is called the karma of miserliness, greed, and jealousy.

> If a deva's merit is exhausted but some good remains,
> Through this one may become a king among humans.
> If afterward one is heedless and all merit is exhausted,
> One must surely fall into the three evil destinies without doubt.

> Some are born among the asuras and give rise to arrogance;
> Anger, jealousy, greed, and harm increase their afflictions.
> Though the devas have wholesome roots and practices,
> Through miserliness and jealousy they lose benefit and joy.
> Therefore know that the fetter of jealousy
> Is a deeply evil dharma that should be cast aside.

> Great king, you now fully know
> The many faults and sufferings of birth-and-death.
> You should diligently cultivate the supramundane good,
> As the thirsty seek drink and one rescues a burning head.
> If you increase diligence and sever all existences,
> Among all that is wholesome, this is the most supreme.

> Diligently uphold precepts and cultivate meditation and wisdom;
> Tame and subdue the mind and seek nirvāṇa.
> Nirvāṇa is subtle and wondrous, beyond all characteristics,
> Without birth, old age, death, or decline,
> Without mountains, rivers, sun, or moon.
> Therefore one should swiftly realize and know it.

> If one wishes to realize the wisdom that needs no teacher,
> One should exclusively cultivate the seven factors of awakening.
> If one boards the ship of these awakening-factors,
> The great sea of birth-and-death is easily crossed.

> The fourteen questions the Buddha does not explain:
> Simply give rise to faith and do not doubt.
> Only with a right mind, diligently and energetically,
> Firmly cultivate all wholesome dharmas.

> Ignorance conditions formations; consciousness, name-and-form,
> The six sense-bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, existence:
> Existence conditions birth; birth conditions death.
> If birth-and-death and their causal conditions are exhausted,
> One who rightly contemplates the twelve links
> Is one who sees the Holy Lion.

> If one wishes to progressively see the four noble truths,
> One should diligently cultivate the eightfold noble path.
> Though dwelling in honor amid the five pleasures,
> One can still attain the holy path and sever all fetters.
> This fruit cannot be sought from another;
> Only when the mind itself accords can one realize it.

> I have spoken of the many sufferings and nirvāṇa,
> Wishing to moisten and benefit the great king.
> You should not give rise to a mind of fear,
> But diligently recite, practice, and cultivate all good.
> The mind is the root of all dharmas;
> If the mind is first tamed, all tasks are accomplished.

> I have briefly explained the essentials of the dharma;
> O king, you should not give rise to a mind of complacency.
> If there is one of great wisdom who further expounds,
> You should also listen and receive with all your heart.

> O king, you are now called a great dharma-vessel;
> If you broadly hear the dharma, the benefit will surely be great.
> If you see anyone cultivating the three karmas of goodness,
> You should deeply assist them and give rise to sympathetic joy.
> The good one practices and the rejoicing in it:
> Transfer all such merit.

> O king, you should emulate the worthy and holy ones,
> As Avalokiteśvara (*Guānyīn*, 觀音) and others deliver beings.
> In the future you will surely accomplish perfect awakening,
> In a land free of birth, old age, and the three poisons.

> Great king, if you cultivate the above-described good,
> Your fine reputation will spread far.
> Then, with this, teach and transform others,
> Universally causing all to accomplish perfect awakening.

> The swift river of afflictions sweeps away beings;
> It is a cause of deep dread and blazing suffering.
> Wishing to extinguish all such afflictions,
> One should cultivate the truth of true liberation,
> Departing from all worldly nominal dharmas,
> And attaining the pure, immovable place.

> If there is a woman who harbors a harmful mind,
> Such a wife should be kept far away.
> But if there is one chaste, harmonious, loving, and respectful,
> Humble and diligent as a maidservant,
> Always regarded as a friend, sister, or mother:
> Such a one should be honored like the household deity.

> The dharma I have spoken is exactly thus;
> O king, day and night, diligently practice it.
