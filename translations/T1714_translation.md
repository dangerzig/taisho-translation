# 般若波羅蜜多心經註解
## Annotated Commentary on the Prajñāpāramitā Heart Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1714

Translated from the Chinese. Ming dynasty, annotated by Zongle (宗泐) and Ruqi (如玘).

---

## Imperial Preface to the Heart Sūtra

Heaven and earth were established long ago, and the myriad things are fully provided. The ruler governs the people, and the law by which the ruler nurtures the people is that very law. The three bonds and five constants are shown to all under heaven, and the five punishments are used to supplement them. Yet there are those who are fierce and obstinate and do not follow these teachings, and who ever tend to rush into fire or plunge into the abyss, never reflecting upon themselves. Such fierce and obstinate people are not found in China alone; everywhere under heaven it is the same.

Long ago, in the western regions, a buddha was born, whose name was Śākyamuni. As a buddha, his practice was profound and his vows weighty, consistent from beginning to end. Thus he transcended the world and escaped from the realms of suffering. His teaching was one of benevolence, compassion, and patient endurance, striving to illuminate the mind and establish one's destiny. He held to this Way and practiced it, intending that all people should be like this, benefiting and aiding the multitudes of beings.

People of the present day do not know the purpose of the Buddha. They constantly say that the Dharma is empty and unreal: "How can it guide the superior person and instruct the lesser?" But as I say, it is not so. The Buddha's teaching is real and not empty. Its true purpose is to dispel the emptiness of delusion and establish the reality of one's original nature. The Buddha especially exerted himself in ascetic practice, making his teaching outwardly distinct in name, speaking of the suffering of sentient beings.

When the Buddha was alive, those who attended and listened to him were all persons of keen intelligence. What he expounded was the principle of the three bonds and five constants. After hearing it, each person obtained blessings. After the Buddha's entry into cessation, his Dharma flowed into China. Among those of keen intelligence, some would expound the lesser fruits of human and heavenly rebirths, yet they were still able to transform the fierce and obstinate toward goodness. How much more those of keen intelligence who know the Mahāyāna and recognize its essential meaning!

Now, the Heart Sūtra constantly speaks of "emptiness" and does not speak of "reality." But the emptiness it speaks of is the emptiness of characteristics. Apart from this emptiness, what remains is the original nature. Therefore, the empty characteristics are six: the mouth has the empty characteristic of speech, the eyes have the empty characteristic of form, the ears have the empty characteristic of hearing, the nose has the empty characteristic of smell, the tongue has the empty characteristic of taste, and the body has the empty characteristic of pleasure. These six empty characteristics are not the emptiness of true characteristics; they are the empty characteristics produced by deluded thinking. These empty characteristics delude worldly people and bring calamity from ancient times to the present, causing ever deeper downfall, the extent of which is unknown.

These empty characteristics caused former emperors to be deluded, nearly losing their domains: King Mu of Zhou, Emperor Wu of Han, Emperor Xuanzong of Tang, Emperor Wu of the Liang dynasty, Emperor Taiwu of the Northern Wei, Li Houzhu, and Emperor Huizong of Song. These several emperors neglected their states and ignored governance. Among them, Emperor Wu of the Liang and Huizong of Song went so far as to lose their lives, all because of deluded fantasies of flying up to the heavens or entering the buddha-lands. Yet the buddha-lands are not remote or vague. Such happiness exists in the human world, but people are too greedy to notice it, and moreover they grasp at pleasure.

How does it exist in the human world? Consider: those who are sovereigns, kings, or nobles, if they do not act wrongly and can skillfully safeguard this state, is this not the buddha-land? If they cannot safeguard it and act falsely, using deluded thinking, they then enter the realm of emptiness. Hence these empty characteristics arise. When the wealthy are entangled by them, sensuality and lust arise together, and wealth is lost. When the poor are entangled by them, all manner of deceit arises, and life is lost. When those who are on the verge of virtue but not yet virtuous are entangled, they cannot be called people of benevolence and integrity. When monks and Daoists are entangled, they cannot establish their original nature or perceive the essential meaning.

Therefore, the title of this sūtra calls it the "Heart Sūtra" precisely because it seeks to remove wrong thoughts from the mind and return to the right path. How could the Buddha's teaching be false? I have specifically written this so that the discerning may observe the canopy of heaven and earth, the cycles of sun and moon, and discern which to take between emptiness and reality, and how to preserve life. If one takes by the right way and preserves by the right method, is not the Buddha-Dharma excellent! Is not the subtlety of form and emptiness marvelous!

According to the translation by Śikṣānanda, the World-Honored One was on Vulture Peak, entering the samādhi of deep radiance called "Teaching the Right Dharma." Śāriputra addressed Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, saying: "If there is anyone who wishes to practice the profound prajñā Dharma-gate, how should they practice?" Thereupon Avalokiteśvara spoke this sūtra. This sūtra is the quintessence of the great Prajñāpāramitā spoken by the World-Honored One. Therefore one should know that what the bodhisattva speaks is what the Buddha speaks. It was transmitted to China in five translations. We now follow the translation of Xuanzang, because it circulates most widely in China.

"Prajñā" is a Sanskrit word, meaning "wisdom" in Chinese. "Pāramitā" means "reaching the other shore" in Chinese. Sentient beings, by being deluded about the nature of wisdom, dwell in birth and death: this is called "this shore." Bodhisattvas, by cultivating prajñā and awakening to the nature of wisdom, reach nirvāṇa: this is called "the other shore." "Heart" means the heart of prajñā. This prajñā-heart is originally present in every person. The purpose of speaking this sūtra is to cause sentient beings to cut off deluded thoughts and reveal their original nature.

"Sūtra" means "Dharma" and "constant." This sūtra takes a single Dharma as its name, true reality as its substance, contemplative illumination as its principle, crossing beyond suffering as its function, and the Mahāyāna as its doctrinal classification. These five are all the essential meaning of what the sūtra teaches. The single Dharma is prajñāpāramitā. True reality is "the empty characteristics of all dharmas." Contemplative illumination is "illuminating and seeing that the five aggregates are all empty." Crossing beyond suffering is "crossing beyond all suffering and distress." The Mahāyāna is "the profound prajñā practiced by the bodhisattva."

**"When Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara was practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā,"**

"Avalokiteśvara" is the bodhisattva who cultivates prajñā. "Prajñāpāramitā" is the Dharma cultivated by the bodhisattva. The bodhisattva uses the contemplative wisdom of prajñā to illuminate and realize that one's own mind is pure, perfectly complete, and unobstructed; therefore the name "Avalokiteśvara" (Freely Perceiving). This is self-practice. Furthermore, with compassion for sentient beings in the world who undergo suffering, the bodhisattva causes them to cultivate this Dharma, change evil and move toward good, leave suffering and attain happiness, all without obstruction. This is transforming others. "Bodhisattva" is an abbreviation of the Sanskrit "bodhisattva," meaning "one who awakens sentient beings" in Chinese. The abbreviated form is used here. "Practice" means cultivation. "Profound prajñā" is the prajñā of true reality. It is not what those of beginner's mind with shallow wisdom can contemplate; therefore it is called "profound." "Time" refers to the time when the bodhisattva cultivates prajñā.

**"He illuminated and saw that the five aggregates are all empty, and crossed beyond all suffering and distress."**

"Illuminated" means contemplated. The "five aggregates" are form, feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness. "Aggregate" means accumulation. "Empty" means truly empty. "Form" is the physical body. "Feeling" is reception. "Perception" is thought. "Mental formations" is creation. "Consciousness" is discrimination. Consciousness is the mind-king; feeling, perception, and mental formations are the mental factors. "Crossing beyond" means liberation. "All suffering and distress" refers to the various sufferings of the world. Because the bodhisattva illuminated the five aggregates as empty and quiescent, he was freed from the suffering of birth and death. Further, with compassion for sentient beings lost in delusion, their thinking inverted and deluded, who go against principle and violate propriety, who are disloyal and unfilial, and who through the ten evils and five heinous acts bring about various sufferings, the bodhisattva spoke this prajñā Dharma-gate to cause them to cultivate it and all attain liberation. The above passage was composed by Ānanda when he compiled the treasury of the Dharma, narrating the meritorious practice of Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara in liberating beings. This is the specific introduction. The following passage, beginning with Śāriputra, is Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara's answer to Śāriputra's question.

**"Śāriputra!"**

Śāriputra was a disciple of the Buddha, foremost in wisdom. Because he asked on behalf of the assembly, the bodhisattva called his name to address him.

**"Form is not different from emptiness, emptiness is not different from form. Form is precisely emptiness, emptiness is precisely form. Feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness are also like this."**

"Form" is the illusory form of the four great elements. "Emptiness" is the true emptiness of prajñā. Sentient beings, by being deluded about true emptiness, undergo illusory form. This is like water becoming ice. The bodhisattva, by cultivating the contemplative wisdom of prajñā, illuminates and realizes that illusory form is precisely true emptiness. This is like melting ice into water. Since form and emptiness are not different in substance, therefore it says "form is not different from emptiness, emptiness is not different from form," just as ice is not different from water and water is not different from ice. Furthermore, fearing that sentient beings of dull faculties might not understand and still cling to a dual view of form and emptiness, it says "form is precisely emptiness, emptiness is precisely form," just as ice is precisely water and water is precisely ice. As for feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness, none are otherwise. This is the essential point of the entire sūtra and the heart of prajñā.

**"Śāriputra! The empty characteristics of all these dharmas are unborn and unperishing, neither defiled nor pure, neither increasing nor decreasing."**

"All these dharmas" refers to the five aggregates above. "Empty characteristics" means the true reality of true emptiness. The bodhisattva further told Śāriputra: "Since all dharmas are understood to be, in their own substance, the true reality of true emptiness, the substance of true reality is fundamentally without birth or destruction. Since there is no birth or destruction, how can there be defilement or purity? Since there is no defilement or purity, how can there be increase or decrease?"

**"Therefore, in emptiness there is no form, no feeling, perception, mental formations, or consciousness."**

Within this true reality of true emptiness, since birth, destruction, defilement, purity, increase, and decrease cannot be found, therefore it concludes by saying there is no form, no feeling, perception, mental formations, or consciousness. "No" means empty.

**"No eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind. No form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or mental objects."**

Within the true reality of true emptiness, since there are no five aggregates, there are also no six sense-faculties or six sense-objects. This empties the twelve āyatanas.

**"No eye-realm, and so on up to no mind-consciousness realm."**

Since there are no twelve āyatanas, there are also no eighteen dhātus. The eighteen dhātus are the six sense-faculties, six sense-objects, and six consciousnesses. "And so on" indicates that the beginning and end are given while the middle is abbreviated. The above five aggregates, twelve āyatanas, and eighteen dhātus do not go beyond the two dharmas of form and mind. For those deeply deluded about mind, the five aggregates are taught. For those deeply deluded about form, the twelve āyatanas are taught. For those deluded about both form and mind, the eighteen dhātus are taught. Among the above three categories, practitioners may cultivate any single one according to their capacity and thereby gain entry to awakening.

**"No ignorance and no extinction of ignorance, and so on up to no aging-and-death and no extinction of aging-and-death."**

This empties the twelve links of dependent origination. "Ignorance" means darkness of delusion: having no clear understanding of one's original nature. This does not mean a blank unconsciousness, but rather a forced awareness that goes against principle. "No ignorance" and so forth means that the bodhisattva, using the wisdom of prajñā to contemplate this ignorance, sees that its nature is fundamentally empty and without characteristics of birth or destruction. Therefore it says "no ignorance and no extinction of ignorance." "And so on up to no aging-and-death and no extinction of aging-and-death" has the same meaning as above; the beginning and end are stated while the middle is abbreviated.

The aspect of arising is as the Lotus Sūtra says: "Ignorance conditions mental formations, and so on up to birth conditions aging-and-death." The aspect of cessation is as the sūtra says: "When ignorance ceases, mental formations cease, and so on up to when birth ceases, aging-and-death ceases."

The twelve links of dependent origination are also called the twelve limbs of existence. The first is ignorance, also called affliction. The second is mental formations, meaning the creation of various karmas. These two limbs are causes created in the past. The third is consciousness, meaning the arising of deluded thought, the first entry into the mother's womb. The fourth is name-and-form, meaning the arising of the various sense-faculties and bodily form after entering the womb. The fifth is the six sense-gates, meaning the completion of the six sense-faculties within the womb. The sixth is contact, meaning the contact of the six sense-faculties with the six sense-objects after leaving the womb. The seventh is feeling, meaning the reception of good and bad worldly experiences. These five limbs are the fruits received in the present. The eighth is craving, meaning attachment to the five desires and other objects. The ninth is grasping, meaning the arising of a mind that seizes upon various objects. The tenth is becoming, meaning that the causes of conditioned existence can bring about future fruits. These three limbs are the causes created in the present. The eleventh is birth, meaning receiving the body of the five aggregates in the future. The twelfth is aging-and-death, meaning the future body grows old and dies. These two limbs are the fruits to be received in the next life.

These twelve links of dependent origination span the causes and effects of three lifetimes. They revolve in mutual dependence like a turning wheel, without rest. All sentient beings are deluded about this and do not understand it, which is truly lamentable. This is originally the object contemplated by those of the pratyekabuddha vehicle. The Mahāyāna bodhisattva thoroughly illuminates this object as entirely without real nature; therefore it says "no."

**"No suffering, accumulation, cessation, or path."**

"No suffering, accumulation, cessation, or path" means contemplating the Four Noble Truths as pure. Suffering is the bitter fruit of birth and death. Accumulation is the cause of suffering through affliction and karma. These two belong to the worldly dharmas. Cessation is the blissful fruit of nirvāṇa. Path is the cause of bliss through the path-factors. These two belong to the world-transcending dharmas. The purpose of teaching these Four Noble Truths is to cause sentient beings to know suffering, cut off accumulation, aspire to cessation, and cultivate the path, so as to leave suffering and attain happiness. This is originally the object contemplated by those of the śrāvaka vehicle. The Mahāyāna bodhisattva illuminates and realizes that the substance of this object is empty and quiescent; therefore it says "no."

**"No wisdom and no attainment."**

"Wisdom" is the wisdom of prajñā. The Mahāyāna bodhisattva uses wisdom to illuminate objects. Since the five aggregates and the Four Noble Truths and other dharmas are all nonexistent, both person and dharma are empty, and object and knowledge are both extinguished. Like a disease gone and the medicine forgotten; therefore it says "no wisdom and no attainment."

**"Because there is nothing to be attained,"**

This statement connects the preceding and introduces what follows.

**"The bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind without obstruction. Because there is no obstruction, there is no fear. Departing far from inverted dream-thinking, one reaches ultimate nirvāṇa."**

"Bodhisattva" is the person who relies. "Prajñāpāramitā" is the Dharma relied upon. When the bodhisattva relies on this prajñā Dharma-gate to cultivate and practice, accomplishment is complete and principle is manifest. Therefore the mind is without karmic bonds. Because there are no karmic bonds, there is no fear of birth and death. Since there is no fear of birth and death, there are no inverted afflictions. When these three obstructions are emptied, the three virtues are manifested. Therefore it says "reaches ultimate nirvāṇa."

"Nirvāṇa" is the Sanskrit *mahāparinirvāṇa*, meaning "great cessation-and-crossing" in Chinese. "Great" is the Dharma-body. "Cessation" is liberation. "Crossing" is prajñā. These three virtues are not something separate; they are precisely the three obstructions. In delusion, they are the three obstructions; in awakening, they are the three virtues. As it is said: birth and death are precisely the Dharma-body, afflictions are precisely prajñā, karmic bonds are precisely liberation. Yet although the obstructions are precisely the virtues, without the function of prajñā, the virtues cannot be manifested. It is like polishing a mirror: when the dust is gone, the brightness appears. This is what is meant.

**"The buddhas of the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."**

"The three times" means past, future, and present. "Anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi" means "unsurpassed, complete, perfect awakening" in Chinese. This says that it is not only the bodhisattva who cultivates and realizes in this way; all the buddhas without exception cultivate prajñā and thereby attain perfect awakening.

**"Therefore know that prajñāpāramitā is the great spiritual mantra, the great illumination mantra, the unsurpassed mantra, the unequaled mantra."**

The preceding is the explicit teaching of prajñā; what follows is the esoteric teaching of prajñā. Since prajñā was already taught explicitly, why teach it esoterically as well? This is because the faculties of sentient beings are not the same, and their modes of entry differ. The four kinds of mantra describe the function of prajñā: able to destroy māra-obstacles, it is called the great spiritual mantra; able to extinguish the darkness of ignorance, it is called the great illumination mantra; able to reveal the ultimate principle, it is called the unsurpassed mantra; the most subtle and wondrous fruition of awakening, to which nothing is equal, it is called the unequaled mantra.

**"It can remove all suffering; this is true and not false."**

This concludes that the function of prajñā is vast, removing suffering and obtaining happiness, decidedly and without doubt, causing all sentient beings to receive it with faith and put it into practice.

**"Therefore the prajñāpāramitā mantra is spoken. The mantra is as follows:"**

*[Dhāraṇī]*

*Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā*

The above is the esoteric teaching of prajñā. This is one of the five categories that are not translated. Mantras are the secret words of the buddhas, not knowable to ordinary beings. The commentary on the Lotus Sūtra says: "Mantras are the names of kings among ghosts and spirits. When one pronounces the king's name, the subordinates respect their lord; therefore mantras can subdue all demons and malevolent spirits." Alternatively, it is said: "A mantra is like a secret password in an army. When the password matches, one passes without challenge." Again, "a mantra is a vow, like a wasp that raises a caterpillar, wishing it to become like itself. When buddhas and bodhisattvas speak mantras, they vow that all sentient beings may, like themselves, attain perfect awakening." Those who can recite this mantra will find that all their vows are fulfilled.
