# Outer Commentary on the Diamond Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra, Fascicle Two

## *Jīngāng Bōrě Bōluómì Jīng Zhuàn Wàizhuàn Juàn Xià* (金剛般若波羅蜜經傳外傳卷下)

Taishō 2742

Author not attributed.

*Note: This is the second fascicle of an anonymous Chinese commentary on the Diamond Sūtra that critically examines and debates the interpretations found in the Indian commentaries (zhuàn, 傳) by Asaṅga, Vasubandhu, and others, particularly as preserved in Bodhiruci's Chinese translations. The "outer commentary" (wàizhuàn, 外傳) positions itself as going beyond those inherited traditions to propose its own reading. The text engages deeply with Yogācāra epistemology (the three natures, two selflessnesses, eight consciousnesses) and the nature of the Buddha's teaching, and includes an extended discussion of the nine similes at the end of the Diamond Sūtra ("like a star, a visual distortion, a lamp, an illusion, dewdrops, a bubble, a dream, lightning, and a cloud"). The author argues these nine similes comprehensively illustrate the three kinds of naturelessness (sān wúxìng, 三無性) of all phenomena, rejecting more limited interpretations. The text also addresses the five vehicles of Buddhist teaching and the question of how teachings of different depth relate to one another.*

---

"Dharma-selflessness": if there is the view of "that self" or "my self," this view is what is subsumed under "views." When the bodhisattva enters the samādhi of correspondence, discrimination no longer operates. This very contemplation serves as the preliminary means of entry: the bodhisattva's non-discrimination of persons, non-discrimination of all dharmas, and the two kinds of wishing and gathering the scattered constitute the skillful means of all abidings. "Not abiding in the thought of dharmas" demonstrates non-discrimination. This accords with the commentary's (*zhuàn*) overall concluding exhortation to cultivate dharma-emptiness without abiding in dharma-emptiness.

This roughly corresponds [to the commentary], yet the points of knowledge, views, and faith are not clear [in the commentary]. Why? Because [the commentary] says: "To demonstrate the superior mind and superior wisdom, within non-discrimination there are knowledge, views, and conviction." According to [the commentary], "knowledge" relies on śamatha (calm abiding); "views" relies on vipaśyanā (insight); the "conviction" of these two relies on samādhi. "Conviction" is precisely "faith" (*xìn*). Śamatha is the superior mind; vipaśyanā is the superior wisdom; samādhi is the sovereign freedom of śamatha and vipaśyanā [together].

According to [the present text], these three pertain only to the direct practitioner's path of cultivating and subduing [through] the Mahāyāna. They do not pertain to the sudden-awakening practitioner who abides in the supreme vehicle. [The commentarial tradition] finds the three incalculable kalpas (*sān sēngqí*, 三僧祇) difficult to understand and difficult to enter; [for the present tradition], in a single kṣaṇa (moment) it is easy to realize and easy to attain. This is treated fully in the commentary under the passage "I cause all to enter."

Vasubandhu (*Tiānqīn*, 天親) takes "knowledge" to be mundane wisdom, "views" to be ultimate-truth wisdom, and "faith" to be samādhi. This misses the sūtra's purport. According to [the present analysis], seeing dharmas (*jiàn fǎ*, 見法) does not yet see true suchness; how much less does seeing the self! With mundane wisdom as "knowledge," what is known: existence or non-existence? With ultimate-truth wisdom as "views," what is seen: the true or the conventional? With samādhi as "faith," what is believed: abiding or separation?

If there is no existence or non-existence, what is called mundane wisdom? If there is no true or conventional, what constitutes ultimate-truth [wisdom]? If there is neither abiding nor separation, who enters samādhi? Fundamentally, concentration gives rise to wisdom: this is the common gateway of the three piṭakas. If we follow this terminology, then faith should come first and knowledge and views afterward; one cannot say "knowledge, views, faith."

---

According to this sūtra text: it proclaims the essence of the ever-abiding wisdom-fruit, the ultimate of the true and the transformation-bodies. The six similes and the three meanings, the "present contemplation" (*xiànguān*, 現觀), are brilliantly clear. The ten stages of the sage and three stages of the worthy are clearly set forth in terms of self-cultivation.

The Tathāgata raises this correct entry to exhort those of the true vehicle. This is not a separate teaching on concentration and wisdom. Rather, it employs only the three kinds of naturelessness (*sān wúxìng*, 三無性) to exclusively remove self-nature (*zìxìng*, 自性). Although we speak of the "two selves," ultimately it is a single emptiness.

The imagined self-characteristic (*biànjì wǒ xiāng*, 遍計我相) has a deluded existence; being deluded, one merely "knows." The dependent self-view (*yītā wǒ jiàn*, 依他我見) has a provisional existence; being provisional, one must "see." The perfected self-nature (*zhēnshí wǒ xìng*, 真實我性) has a verified existence; being verified, one can "believe." In this way, not abiding in the characteristics of non-characteristics, one sees the nature: this is the characteristic-aspect (*xiāng fēn*, 相分) that can enable awakening and entry, taken as a whole.

This characteristic is itself without self-nature; hence it is said "then it is not" (*zé fēi*, 則非). Yet it is still the habitual energy (*xíqì*, 習氣) of the self; hence it is said "is named" (*shì míng*, 是名). This constitutes non-abiding in dharma-emptiness. As treated fully in the commentary.

---

If all buddhas' teaching of the dharma does not refer to the transformation-body (*huàshēn*, 化身), then who taught what is called "transformation" by Śākyamuni Tathāgata?

According to [the present text]: the Buddha's body, since it is a manifestation brought about by merit, and the teaching of the dharma has nothing to do with [a physical] Buddha-body, this is the eternally abiding fundamental dharma. It is the dharma one obtains by oneself. The transformation-body is a reflection, an image, a contributory condition. It has never attained the Way; it has never spoken. Even granting it had dharma, it still could not express it, because the dharma-nature cannot be verbalized.

Therefore, those who uphold the sūtra are exhorted: in expounding it, do not generate the thought "I am teaching," and do not generate the characteristic of "conveying meaning." It is like someone who mistakes south for north and calls it "not-north"; that person will never awaken. But if one awakens by oneself, it is also not due to another. This is the meaning of "no teaching."

Moreover, the Tathāgata's expounding and teaching have always been the collective minds of the assembly's bodhisattvas transmitting and spreading. How could it be one's own power? All the more so since the speaker and the listener are both one's own mind, and what is practiced and verified is entirely without self-nature, like empty space having no self-nature of world-systems or atoms. Therefore:

> When I sat at the place of the Way, wisdom could not be obtained.
> With an empty fist I deceived small children, and thereby ferried across all beings.

According to [the present analysis]: this refers to the initial insight of bodhi (*pútí*, 菩提), the subsequent mind of naturelessness. Whether expounding the dharma-gates of conventional truth or teaching the contemplation of true-suchness principle, all of it is conditioned by those consciousnesses and is truly without self-mind. This is "being without defilement," and this "cannot be called teaching." One who teaches in this way is called one who "truly teaches."

This is why holding even a single four-line verse surpasses even the bodhisattva's perfection of giving (*dàn dù*, 檀度). The reason lies precisely here.

Asaṅga (*Wúzhuó*, 無著) says: "Not being defiled in birth-and-death." Vasubandhu says: "Not abiding in nirvāṇa." These two complement each other as inner and outer, both serving to remove doubt.

---

### The Nine Similes

According to [the present text], the four-line verse has three levels: (1) pointing to dharmas and raising similes, (2) [three are similes], (3) exhortation to practice. Maitreya (*Mílè*, 彌勒) says: "Not conditioned, not apart from; the nirvāṇa of all tathāgatas." This resolves the doubt: if one always teaches the dharma, who enters nirvāṇa? The answer: the Buddha attains nirvāṇa; the transformation-body teaches the dharma. The transformation-body's teaching is not the unconditioned. The Buddha's attaining nirvāṇa is not the conditioned.

Yet "not abiding in either the conditioned or the unconditioned" is the exhaustive dharma-gate; this is merely one particular practice of the bodhisattva, and cannot be used to characterize the Tathāgata.

The reward-body Buddha (*bào fó*, 報佛) has a beginning but no end. If he entered nirvāṇa, how would he differ from the transformation-body? Then the Tathāgata neither comes nor teaches, as fully [discussed] in the commentary above. How can one use the bodhisattva's mental measure to say the Buddha does not abide in the conditioned or unconditioned?

As the Vimalakīrti Sūtra (*Jìng Míng*, 淨名) says: "I contemplate the Tathāgata: in the prior limit there is no coming, in the present there is no abiding, in the posterior limit there is no going... not the conditioned, not the unconditioned... cannot be known through consciousness, cannot be known through wisdom. This is called correct contemplation; this is called seeing the Buddha."

Therefore: when teaching according to the capacity [of beings], one says "not abiding in birth-and-death or nirvāṇa," because the two natures cannot be obtained. When speaking of contemplating the Buddha for oneself, one says "apart from the conditioned and the unconditioned," because those two are mental measures. With neither past nor future coming or going, who is the speaker in the middle? Yet [others] use atoms and world-systems to interpret the pure empty space! Is this not the utmost confusion?

If [one follows] their interpretation, one would have to say: "A single bodhisattva's nirvāṇa is not conditioned, not apart [from the conditioned]." Why? Because "exhaustion and non-exhaustion" are, as the perfumed-rice (*xiāngjī*, 香積) bodhisattva says, not [limited to] bodhisattvas.

---

According to [the present analysis], "nirvāṇa" here means "quiescence and cessation." How could existence and non-existence fail to be quiescent and ceased? In the wisdom of dharma-selflessness, one has already departed from the unconditioned of true suchness as self-nature. How much more so with the most pure awakening, which is further removed from the wisdom of dharma-selflessness and yet still preserves non-abiding in existence and non-existence!

As stated in the commentary above, in the section on the fruit and its severance, [the present text] correctly interprets Maitreya: with nine things illustrated by nine dharmas, combined into three groups. (1) Essence: consciousness, sense-objects, and sense-faculties. (2) Function: "vessel" (environment), body, and sensation. (3) Temporal process: past, present, and future. The [Indian] treatise says: "seeing-aspect" is the seeing-division, the receiving consciousness; "characteristic-aspect" is the characteristic-division, the received consciousness; "consciousness" is the body, the self of the receiver. This is also not contradictory.

Thus: consciousness is discrimination; a star has luminosity. When the full moon shines clearly, small stars become dim; when the great sun blazes, even large stars disappear. Therefore: when the wisdoms of hearing, thinking, and cultivation arise as discrimination, they are subtle. When correct wisdom arises, those consciousnesses cease. This is the "same in opposition-removal."

The six sense-object characteristics appear, and the six consciousnesses arise: like "flowers in the sky" appearing before the eyes, which the eye-disease can produce. Characteristics arise from seeing: this is the "same in arising from views."

The six sense-faculties are formed from the harmonious combination of earth, water, fire, and wind, like a lamp's illumination arising from the combination of oil, stand, wick, and fire: this is the "same in conditional and provisional [arising]."

The "vessel" (environment) is the dependent reward, received and used from others, like a magical illusion, which is a deluded realm arising from dependent conditions, and like medicinal herbs arising [from conditions]: this is the "same in not arising from self."

The body is the direct reward, formed from the seeds of karma, like dew that accumulates from moisture due to the night's coolness: this is the "same in dependent arising."

Sensation means the three feelings, experienced through the direct and dependent rewards, like bubbles arising from the agitation of water by wind and rain: this is the "same in arising from contact."

Past karmic conditions, present thoughts and reflections, like deluded scenes in a dream that one knows upon waking: this is the "same in retrospective conditioning."

The present is provisionally existent, ceasing moment by moment without pause, like lightning that barely appears before it is gone, renewing and passing away ceaselessly: this is the "same in swiftness."

Seeds in relation to the reward: the reward is in the birth-to-come; clouds in relation to rain: the rain is not yet present: this is the "same in future existence."

Through these nine contemplations, the Buddha can attain sovereign freedom within conditioned dharmas. Therefore the verse says:

> Contemplating characteristics, enjoyment, and use; contemplating the affairs of the three times:
> Within conditioned dharmas, one attains unstained sovereign freedom.

According to [the present text]: attaining sovereign freedom in the conditioned does not [apply to] the unconditioned. Why? Because these nine kinds contemplate only the conditioned.

The preceding verse says: "Through wondrous wisdom and correct contemplation." But it is not clear whether this wisdom belongs to the bodhisattva or is attributed to the Tathāgata. If it belongs to the bodhisattva, then one cannot say "the nirvāṇa of all tathāgatas." If it is the Tathāgata's, then one cannot say "one should contemplate in this way." When master and disciple have not yet clarified this, how can the text and meaning become clear?

---

If these nine similes do not comprehensively demonstrate the three kinds of naturelessness, and instead are nine separate contemplations, then one does not know how to perform "contemplation like a star." A star: in darkness it has luminosity, in brightness it does not appear. Does one arouse the characteristic of darkness or the mind of brightness? If one arouses the bright wisdom, this constitutes removing the star-contemplation; one cannot say "like a star." Again, "like an eye-disease" (*yì*, 翳): the objects arise from the disease. If one knows they arise but does not remove [the disease], then the disease-objects constantly arise. If one removes the disease-objects, this constitutes removing the disease-contemplation; one cannot say "like a disease." Again, "like a lamp": a lamp arises from multiple conditions; sense-faculties arise from the four great elements. If one knows they arise from conditions, what is there to attain? If, because they arise from conditions, the lamp has no self-nature, this constitutes removing the lamp-contemplation; one cannot say "like a lamp." The remaining six similes can all be refuted in the same way.

Therefore, know that the exhortation to practice dharma-emptiness, with similes revealing naturelessness, is the true interpretation. These nine kinds are all the eighteen elements (*shíbā jiè*, 十八界), their enjoyment, and their karmic processes. There is no mention of the eight consciousnesses and mental factors. Even granting that contemplation of the nine kinds is accomplished, this is only the emptiness of persons, because it exclusively removes only the conditioned.

It does not encompass all dharmas exhaustively, because among all dharmas there are six kinds of unconditioned. This "all dharmas" (*yīqiè fǎ*) encompasses the hundred dharmas and accomplishes the two selflessnesses.

---

Asaṅga says: "It demonstrates four kinds of conditioned characteristics: (1) the characteristic of self-nature, (2) the characteristic of the flavor of attachment to what is abided in, (3) the characteristic of conformity with faults, (4) the characteristic of conformity with liberation." Although the respective categories of four and nine differ, the heading "conditioned" does not differ. Yet personal and dharma self-views being like eye-diseases should be the most apt [comparison] among the similes, but [this interpretation] does not extend to all the similes. Moreover, "conforming to the selflessness of persons and dharmas through grasping, thereby attaining liberation": it is known that [once] the processes of the three times have been penetrated, one comprehends selflessness. This demonstrates the characteristic of conformity with liberation.

The remaining interpretations are roughly the same as Vasubandhu's. According to [the present analysis], in the section on liberation: there are no persons and no dharmas, all collectively named "conditioned," which contradicts the hundred dharmas and selflessness. Why? The characteristic of self-nature, for instance, is a shared characteristic (*gòng xiāng*, 共相); seeing, consciousness, and true suchness are also shared characteristics of all dharmas. Are those not self-natures? If they have no self-nature, then one cannot speak of six kinds of unconditioned [dharmas]. Since they are distinctly different, each having self-nature, this is clear.

Thus, within the characteristic of self-nature, there are fully the aspects of both existence and non-existence. How can one say only "characteristics of the conditioned"? Moreover, the characteristic of liberation is the two selflessnesses. This liberation is beyond both the conditioned and the unconditioned; it does not belong within the conditioned and unconditioned. Since even the unconditioned is itself liberated from, how can it be included within the four [aspects of] the conditioned? How great is this discrepancy in names and meanings!

Therefore, know that grasping at the four characteristics and what is done by the four characteristics are all without self-nature: this is fully treated in the commentary. The root text says: "All that is made by harmonious combination constitutes the conditioned and the unconditioned." The major Mahāyāna sūtras often use these similes, sometimes one or two, sometimes seven, nine, or ten, and sometimes extensively, as in the Vimalakīrti Sūtra, which uses every simile to illustrate the body; the *Sūtra of Sublime Virtue* uses them to illustrate sentient beings; Dharmodgata (*Tánwújié*, 曇無竭) uses them to illustrate the Tathāgata. How can stars and eye-diseases be fixed as illustrations of the characteristic-and-seeing [divisions] alone?

Therefore, know that using easily understood emptinesses to illustrate the hard-to-understand emptiness: the emptiness of atoms, world-systems, and the true and responsive two bodies is itself without self-nature; how much more so all dharmas! Therefore these nine similes comprehensively demonstrate the three kinds of naturelessness; all are without inherent nature. This is the correct interpretation.

**Question:** I have heard about mind and mental factors, and about the six kinds of unconditioned within the five-eye realm, the eleven kinds of form understood within the diagram of the eight consciousnesses. But the twenty-four dharmas not associated with mind: please set them forth.

**Answer:** The dharmas not associated with mind are briefly of twenty-four kinds: (1) attainment, (2) life-faculty, (3) same-class community, (4) the nature of the ordinary being, (5) the attainment of no-thought, (6) the attainment of cessation, (7) the state of no-thought, (8) name-bodies, (9) phrase-bodies, (10) syllable-bodies, (11) arising, (12) aging, (13) abiding, (14) impermanence, (15) transmigration, (16) definite differentiation, (17) correspondence, (18) swiftness, (19) sequential order, (20) direction, (21) time, (22) number, (23) the nature of combination, (24) the nature of non-combination.

---

**Question:** If, when teaching the dharma, one brings out contradictions among sūtras and argues with an evil mind, one falls into the four evil destinies, as the Buddha has clearly stated. How then can one criticize various sūtras and commentaries, and the interpretations of Asaṅga and Vasubandhu, and call one's own commentary correct while slandering the sages? Will one not fall into the great pit? How can one be so confident?

**Answer:** According to the Lotus Sūtra (*Fǎhuá Jīng*, 法華經): "Sentient beings have dull faculties and are blinded by attachment to pleasures and delusion. For such beings, how can they be ferried across? I would rather not teach the dharma and quickly enter nirvāṇa. Then I recalled the skillful means practiced by the past buddhas, and [realized that] the Way I have now attained should also be taught as three vehicles."

Because there are five kinds of capacity, the teaching has depth and shallowness. Thus the last of the five is solely the buddha-vehicle; the first four are all not the buddha-dharma. Some people confuse the human vehicle with the heavenly; some doubt the first four because of the fifth; some doubt the fifth because of the first four; and so the five may be confused with one another in twenty-five layers of contradiction and obstruction. But this is teaching according to capacity, without obstruction: how can there be contradiction?

Moreover, within each vehicle there is depth and shallowness, and from this, confusion arises and the net of doubt multiplies. Is it that the sincere words are insufficiently deep, or merely that the perceiver's insight is shallow?

For instance, in the minor contemplation of impurity (*bùjìng guān*, 不淨觀), there may arise both correct and mistaken mental images. If during the impurity-contemplation one sees the Buddha's radiant form, the contemplation and the image contradict: this is a deviant contemplation. How much more so if, while contemplating the Buddha, one sees impure images, and yet calls this "correct contemplation"!

It is like the case of a person of the śrāvaka capacity: if one teaches them the Mahāyāna, they will slander it and not accept it, falling into the great pit. This is why, for three times seven days after [his awakening], the Buddha deliberated and did not teach the single vehicle, precisely for this reason. How much more so [should one be careful] when one is genuinely teaching the buddha-vehicle, and yet mixes it with the isolated teaching of śrāvaka and human-and-heavenly dharma. Is this not confusion?

Those who, having arrived at the true teaching, the pure and wondrous awakening of naturelessness and non-attainment, which is beyond the five vehicles, still mix it with the notions of "signlessness" and "non-arising" from other contexts: even that is highly improper. How much more so [mixing it with] the conventional-truth [notions of] suffering, emptiness, impermanence, non-self, the conditioned and the unconditioned, and interpreting "present contemplation" (*xiànguān*) through them! How lamentable! How deeply confused!

How do we know this? According to the Compendium of the Mahāyāna (*Shè Lùn*, 攝論): "Those who have not yet attained the true wisdom of awakening, within the consciousness-only [framework], by means of teaching and reasoning, should come to know it by inference." And the commentary by Asvabhāva (*Wúxìng*, 無性) says: "Through the authoritative teaching and through inferential reasoning, although not yet having verified it, one should be able to infer the true knowledge of consciousness-only." Therefore "authoritative teaching" means the sūtras of definitive meaning. What is not authoritative teaching is the sūtras of non-definitive meaning.

Just as in the world, measures and weights are standardized throughout the realm, and the regulations and laws are uniform across the four seas, yet some use short cloth and long rulers, exceeding the established standards, and this deviation from the norm arises from willful non-compliance and non-calibration. Having themselves not complied and not calibrated, they then blame those who do comply and calibrate! Is this not improper?

Therefore, know that teaching according to capacity is fundamentally without contradiction. It is the willful, deviant, foolish interpretation that creates discrepancy. Therefore: bringing out contradictions among sūtras is Māra gaining an advantage, and one brings the three evil destinies upon oneself. Hence: "Arguing with a good mind, one is born in the five pure-abode heavens. Arguing with an evil mind, one falls into the four evil destinies." This commentary (*zhuàn*) solely illuminates awakening and entry, accomplishing perfect awakening. Any [interpretation] that violates this contemplation is what [this commentary] does not rely upon.

The cognition and awakening that are the true exposition are still to be regarded as supplementary designations (*zēng yǔ*, 增語). How much more so one's own directly experienced reality (*zì xīn xiànliàng*, 自心現量), which is to be taken as the supreme truth! This is why there are points of agreement and disagreement [with the earlier commentaries]: the reason lies precisely here.

Therefore: a single proclamation of the pure awakening yields immeasurable merit and wisdom, because it can refute all deviant and deluded [views]. A single compliance with [mere] bodhisattva-level [interpretation] yields immeasurable transgression, because it obscures the perfect illumination. Not only does it obstruct oneself, it also misleads all beings.

Therefore Asaṅga says:

> Inferior persons, regarding this deep and great dharma, cannot awaken to it or aspire toward it.
> Most people in the world are like this; therefore this dharma falls into waste and neglect.

Truly, the buddhas' descent into the world is rare and distant, hard to encounter. Even if one does encounter them, hearing the dharma is harder still. Even having heard, hearing the profound [teaching] is harder yet. Even having heard the profound, faith and understanding are still harder. Even with faith and understanding, awakening and entry are the hardest of all.

Thus, awakening and entry, or non-awakening, lies in one's own diligence or lack thereof, not in hearing or speaking. May all fellow practitioners neither belittle themselves nor abandon themselves! As the verse says:

> Gathering and seeking all desires without satiation,
> Gathering desires, not yet satisfied, and life comes to an end.
> Wife, children, treasures, and fame and rank:
> At the moment of death, none follow along.
> Only giving, precepts, and heedfulness:
> In this life and the next, they are one's companions.
