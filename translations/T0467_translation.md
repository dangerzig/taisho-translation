# 大乘伽耶山頂經
## The Mahāyāna Gayāśīrṣa Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0467

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流志)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time the Bhagavān was dwelling at the Mountaintop Monastery in the city of Gayā, together with a great assembly of one thousand bhikṣus. All of them had formerly been matted-hair brahmins and were now arhats who had exhausted all outflows, completed what was to be done, cast off heavy burdens, attained their own benefit, severed all bonds of existence, and were correctly liberated through right knowledge. Having attained mastery of mind, they had reached the other shore. He was also accompanied by an immeasurable assembly of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

At that time, the World-Honored One, not long after attaining true enlightenment, sat silently in samādhi, contemplating the dharma-realm. He thought thus: "I have realized bodhi, attained sacred wisdom, completed what should be done, cast off heavy burdens, escaped the wilderness of birth and death, abandoned ignorance and attained the light of wisdom, pulled out the poison arrow, exhausted the thirst of craving, realized the dharma-realm, struck the Dharma drum, blown the Dharma conch, raised the Dharma banner, abandoned the eye of birth and death and spoken of the Dharma eye, closed the paths of evil and opened all wholesome paths, abandoned barren fields and shown the fields of merit. Now as I carefully examine such dharmas, who is able to realize them now, has already realized them, or will realize them? Is it the body that realizes, or is it the mind? If the body realizes, then the body is dull and insensate, without awareness or thought, like grass and trees, walls and tiles, formed from the four great elements and born from father and mother: a dharma of impermanence, decay, dissolution, and destruction that can only be sustained through conditions such as ointments, washing, clothing, and food. If the mind realizes, then mind is like a magical illusion, without characteristics, without form, with no place of reliance and no capacity for reception. Moreover, bodhi is merely a name established in accordance with worldly convention. It has no sound, no form or color, no real substance, no characteristic appearance, no coming and no going, neither emerging nor entering. It transcends the three realms and has no location. It cannot be seen or heard, cannot be recollected or thought about. It is beyond the domain of conceptual proliferation and removed from the locus of mental grasping, with no point of entry and no written expression. It cannot be shaken or established. It cuts off all paths of language. Yet to speak of 'realizing now,' 'having realized,' or 'will realize' is nothing but naming and false discrimination. It is unborn, unarisen, without inherent nature, ungrasped, unspeakable, and not to be clung to. Within it there is truly no one who has become truly enlightened, is becoming enlightened, or will become enlightened. If one can thus be without realization and without attainment, only then is it called attaining true enlightenment. Why? Because bodhi is apart from all characteristics of change and movement."

At that time, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva, knowing the Buddha's thought, spoke these words: "World-Honored One, if bodhi has such characteristics, how should good men and good women who have aroused the mind of bodhi abide?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī, as the characteristics of bodhi are, so should they abide."

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "World-Honored One, what are the characteristics of bodhi?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī, the characteristics of bodhi uniquely transcend the three realms. Although following worldly convention they have a name, they are far removed from all sounds and verbal expressions. When the assembly of bodhisattvas sets out for bodhi, from the initial arousal of mind there is nowhere to set out toward. Therefore, Mañjuśrī, good men and good women should abide in bodhi with a mind far removed from setting out toward anything. Mañjuśrī, if bodhisattvas can set out toward 'nowhere to set out toward,' then this is setting out on the path to bodhi.

"Mañjuśrī, to set out toward the absence of inherent nature is to set out toward bodhi. To set out toward the absence of any location is to set out toward bodhi. To set out toward the nature of the dharma-realm is to set out toward bodhi. To set out toward non-attachment to all dharmas is to set out toward bodhi. To set out toward the absence of distinction in true reality is to set out toward bodhi. To set out toward what is like a reflection in a mirror, a shadow in light, the moon in water, or a mirage in heat is to set out toward bodhi."

At that time in the assembly there was a devaputra named Pure Moon Radiance, who addressed Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva saying: "Great Being, what practice do bodhisattva-mahāsattvas cultivate, and relying on what do they cultivate?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "Devaputra, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas cultivate the practice of great compassion, relying on all sentient beings."

Devaputra Pure Moon again asked: "Upon what does a bodhisattva's great compassion arise?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "A bodhisattva's great compassion arises based on a mind free from deceit and flattery."

He asked again: "Upon what does a mind free from deceit and flattery arise?"

The answer was: "A mind free from deceit and flattery arises based on a mind of equality toward all sentient beings."

He asked again: "Upon what does a mind of equality toward all sentient beings arise?"

The answer was: "It arises based on a mind that enters the nature of dharmas that is neither one nor different."

He asked again: "Upon what does a mind that enters the nature of dharmas that is neither one nor different arise?"

The answer was: "It arises based on a mind of deep faith."

He asked again: "Upon what does a mind of deep faith arise?"

The answer was: "It arises based on the mind of bodhi."

He asked again: "Upon what does the mind of bodhi arise?"

The answer was: "It arises based on the six pāramitās."

He asked again: "Upon what do the six pāramitās arise?"

The answer was: "They arise based on skillful means and wisdom."

He asked again: "Upon what do skillful means and wisdom arise?"

The answer was: "They arise based on non-negligence."

He asked again: "Upon what does non-negligence arise?"

The answer was: "It arises based on three kinds of pure conduct."

He asked again: "Upon what do the three kinds of pure conduct arise?"

The answer was: "They arise based on the ten wholesome paths of action."

He asked again: "Upon what do the ten wholesome paths of action arise?"

The answer was: "They arise based on maintaining pure precepts."

He asked again: "Upon what does maintaining pure precepts arise?"

The answer was: "It arises based on proper reflection."

He asked again: "Upon what does proper reflection arise?"

The answer was: "It arises based on a mind of contemplative observation."

He asked again: "Upon what does a mind of contemplative observation arise?"

The answer was: "It arises from retentive mindfulness that does not forget."

At that time, Devaputra Pure Moon Radiance again asked Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Great Being, how many kinds of arousing the mind of bodhi do bodhisattvas have, and how are they fulfilled in cause and fruit?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "Devaputra, bodhisattvas arouse the mind of bodhi in four kinds, which are fulfilled in cause and fruit. What are the four? First, the initial arousal of mind. Second, the arousal of mind at the stage of understanding and practice. Third, the arousal of mind at the stage of non-retrogression. Fourth, the arousal of mind at the stage of one more lifetime before buddhahood. You should know that the initial arousal of mind is the cause of the stage of understanding and practice. The arousal at the stage of understanding and practice is the cause of non-retrogression. The arousal at the stage of non-retrogression is the cause of one more lifetime. The arousal at the stage of one more lifetime is the cause of omniscience.

"Furthermore, devaputra, you should know that the initial arousal of mind is like planting a seed in a field. The arousal at the stage of understanding and practice is like the gradual growth of the sprout. The arousal at the stage of non-retrogression is like the successive emergence of branches, leaves, flowers, and fruit. The arousal at the stage of one more lifetime is like the fruit ripening.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first arousal of mind is like a cart-builder first gathering materials. The second arousal is like obtaining the materials and separately refining each one. The third arousal is like the craftsman completing the construction of the cart. The fourth arousal is like using the cart to carry heavy loads over great distances.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first arousal of mind is like the new moon. The second arousal is like the moon from the fifth to the seventh night. The third arousal is like the moon on the tenth night. The fourth arousal is like the moon on the fourteenth night. You should know that the wisdom of the Tathāgata is like the bright moon on the fifteenth night, when all its light and beauty are perfectly full.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first arousal of mind surpasses the stage of the śrāvaka. The second surpasses the stage of the pratyekabuddha. The third surpasses the indeterminate stage. The fourth abides in the determinate stage.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first arousal of mind is like a person first learning the alphabet. The second arousal is like the learner gradually understanding analysis. The third arousal is like one who has studied long and become skilled in calculation. The fourth arousal is like one whose study is complete and who thoroughly comprehends the treatises.

"Furthermore, devaputra, at the first arousal of mind the bodhisattva abides in cause. At the second the bodhisattva abides in wisdom. At the third the bodhisattva abides in elimination. At the fourth the bodhisattva abides in fruit.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first arousal of mind is subsumed by cause. The second is subsumed by wisdom. The third is subsumed by elimination. The fourth is subsumed by fruit.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first arousal of mind proceeds from cause. The second proceeds from wisdom. The third proceeds from elimination. The fourth proceeds from fruit.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first arousal of mind pertains to the differentiation of cause. The second pertains to the differentiation of wisdom. The third pertains to the differentiation of elimination. The fourth pertains to the differentiation of fruit.

"Furthermore, devaputra, the first arousal of mind is like gathering various medicines. The second arousal is like discerning the properties of the medicines. The third arousal is like compounding the medicine according to the illness. The fourth arousal is like taking the medicine and being cured.

"Furthermore, devaputra, at the first arousal of mind one is born into the family of the Dharma King. At the second one studies the methods of the Dharma King. At the third one's studies achieve understanding. At the fourth one's studies achieve mastery."

At that time in the assembly there was a devaputra named Resolute Radiance, who addressed Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva saying: "Great Being, what is the swift path of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas by which they swiftly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "Devaputra, the swift path of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas is of two kinds, by which they swiftly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. What are the two? First, the path of means. Second, the path of prajñā. The path of means gathers all wholesome dharmas. The path of prajñā understands and discriminates. The path of means does not abandon sentient beings. The path of prajñā can relinquish all dharmas. The path of means knows the combination of dharmas. The path of prajñā knows non-combination. The path of means can serve as cause and condition. The path of prajñā can lead to cessation. The path of means can know the distinct characteristics of all dharmas. The path of prajñā can know the principle of non-distinction in the dharma-realm. The path of means can fully adorn all buddha-lands. The path of prajñā can know the equality of all buddha-lands. The path of means can know the different faculties and dispositions of sentient beings. The path of prajñā can know that faculties and dispositions are empty and without existence. The path of means leads bodhisattvas to the site of enlightenment. The path of prajñā enables bodhisattvas to attain the absence of awareness.

"Devaputra, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas also have two kinds of swift path. What are the two? First, the path of accumulation. Second, the path of discernment. The path of accumulation refers to the five pāramitās such as giving. The path of discernment refers to the prajñā pāramitā. The path with attachment and the path without attachment, the path with outflows and the path without outflows, should all be explained similarly.

"There are also two kinds of swift path. What are the two? First, the limited path. Second, the unlimited path. The limited path refers to the stage with characteristics. The unlimited path refers to the stage without characteristics.

"There are also two kinds of swift path: the path of wisdom and the path of elimination. The path of wisdom extends from the first ground to the seventh ground. The path of elimination extends from the eighth ground to the tenth ground."

At that time in the assembly there was a bodhisattva named Fearless-Wisdom-Faith, who addressed Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva saying: "Great Being, what is the meaning known by bodhisattva-mahāsattvas? What is the wisdom cultivated by bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "Good man, meaning is not compounded, but wisdom is compounded."

Bodhisattva Fearless-Wisdom-Faith said: "Great Being, for what reason is meaning not compounded while wisdom is compounded?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "Good man, meaning is the unconditioned. The unconditioned is then not meaning, and within non-meaning there is no dharma that is either compounded or not compounded. Meaning is without change, without real substance, neither grasped nor relinquished; all should be spoken of in this way. Good man, wisdom is called the path, and the path is compounded with mind, not uncompounded.

"Furthermore, good man, wisdom is only compounded, not uncompounded."

Bodhisattva Fearless-Wisdom-Faith said: "Great Being, for what reason is wisdom only compounded and not uncompounded?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "Good man, wisdom skillfully contemplates the aggregates, sense-bases, and elements; skillfully contemplates dependent arising; and skillfully contemplates the possible and the impossible. For this reason it is only compounded, not uncompounded.

"Furthermore, good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have ten kinds of wisdom. What are the ten? First, wisdom of cause. Second, wisdom of fruit. Third, wisdom of meaning. Fourth, wisdom of means. Fifth, wisdom of prajñā. Sixth, wisdom of gathering. Seventh, wisdom of the pāramitās. Eighth, wisdom of great compassion. Ninth, wisdom of teaching and transforming sentient beings. Tenth, wisdom of non-attachment to all dharmas. Good man, these are called the ten kinds of wisdom of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have ten kinds of arousing. What are the ten? First, arousing of body, for purifying bodily karma on behalf of all sentient beings. Second, arousing of speech, for purifying verbal karma on behalf of all sentient beings. Third, arousing of mind, for purifying mental karma on behalf of all sentient beings. Fourth, internal arousing, for non-attachment to one's own body toward all sentient beings. Fifth, external arousing, for practicing equality toward all sentient beings. Sixth, arousing of wisdom, for cultivating the wisdom of all buddhas. Seventh, arousing of lands, for manifesting the meritorious adornments of all buddha-lands. Eighth, arousing of teaching sentient beings, for knowing the medicines for all afflictions. Ninth, arousing of truth, for accomplishing the assembly of the determinate. Tenth, arousing of the fulfillment of unconditioned wisdom, for having no attachment of mind in all three realms. Good man, these are called the ten kinds of arousing of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have ten kinds of practice. What are the ten? First, the practice of the pāramitās. Second, the practice of gathering beings. Third, the practice of prajñā. Fourth, the practice of skillful means. Fifth, the practice of great compassion. Sixth, the practice of accumulating resources of wisdom. Seventh, the practice of accumulating resources of knowledge. Eighth, the practice of purifying faith. Ninth, the practice of entering all truths. Tenth, the practice of not discriminating between objects of love and aversion. Good man, these are called the ten kinds of practice of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have ten kinds of inexhaustible contemplation. What are the ten? First, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of body. Second, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of objects. Third, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of dharmas. Fourth, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of craving. Fifth, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of views. Sixth, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of accumulations. Seventh, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of grasping. Eighth, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of non-attachment. Ninth, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of correspondence. Tenth, contemplation of the inexhaustibility of the self-nature of consciousness at the site of enlightenment. Good man, these are called the ten kinds of inexhaustible contemplation of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have ten kinds of taming practice. What are the ten? First, the practice of taming miserliness, for giving generously like rain. Second, the practice of taming transgression, for the purity of the three karmas. Third, the practice of taming anger, for cultivating loving-kindness. Fourth, the practice of taming laziness, for untiring pursuit of the Dharma. Fifth, the practice of taming unwholesomeness, for attaining dhyāna, liberation, and supernatural powers. Sixth, the practice of taming ignorance, for giving rise to the resources of determinate and skillful wisdom. Seventh, the practice of taming afflictions, for perfecting the resources of omniscience. Eighth, the practice of taming inversion, for bringing forth the true and non-inverted path of accumulation. Ninth, the practice of taming lack of mastery, for mastery over timely and untimely circumstances. Tenth, the practice of taming attachment to self, for contemplating selflessness in all dharmas. Good man, these are called the ten kinds of taming practice of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have ten kinds of stages of tranquility. What are the ten? First, the stage of bodily tranquility, for abandoning the three kinds of unwholesome bodily karma. Second, the stage of verbal tranquility, for purifying the four kinds of verbal karma. Third, the stage of mental tranquility, for permanently abandoning the three kinds of evil mental conduct. Fourth, the stage of internal tranquility, for not being attached to one's own body. Fifth, the stage of external tranquility, for not being attached to any dharma. Sixth, the stage of tranquility in wisdom resources, for not being attached to the path one practices. Seventh, the stage of tranquility without conceit, for contemplating the self-nature of sacred wisdom. Eighth, the stage of tranquility of supernatural powers reaching the ultimate limit, for giving rise to the prajñā pāramitā. Ninth, the stage of tranquility in the cessation of conceptual proliferation, for not deceiving any sentient being. Tenth, the stage of tranquility without concern for body and mind, for teaching sentient beings through great compassion. Good man, these are called the ten kinds of stages of tranquility of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who practice authentically can attain bodhi; those who do not practice authentically cannot attain it. Those who practice authentically act in accordance with what they say. Those who do not practice authentically merely speak words without being able to have faith, accept, or cultivate.

"Furthermore, good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have two kinds of authentic practice. What are the two? First, authentic practice of the path. Second, authentic practice of elimination.

"Good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas also have two kinds of authentic practice. What are the two? First, authentic practice of self-taming. Second, authentic practice of teaching sentient beings.

"Good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas also have two kinds of authentic practice. What are the two? First, authentic practice of wisdom with effort. Second, authentic practice of wisdom without effort.

"Good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas also have two kinds of authentic practice. What are the two? First, authentic practice of skillfully establishing the stages. Second, authentic practice of skillfully contemplating the non-distinction of the stages.

"Good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas also have two kinds of authentic practice. What are the two? First, authentic practice of skillfully abandoning the faults of each stage. Second, authentic practice of skillfully perfecting the virtues of each stage.

"Good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas also have two kinds of authentic practice. What are the two? First, authentic practice of skillfully explaining the stages of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha. Second, authentic practice of skillfully explaining the irreversible Dharma of the bodhi of all buddhas.

"Good man, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have such immeasurable and boundless dharmas of authentic practice. Those who can practice authentically in this way should know that such persons will soon attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Bodhisattva-mahāsattvas should diligently study this."

At that time, the World-Honored One praised Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva saying: "Excellent, excellent! Mañjuśrī, you have spoken these words well."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva, Bodhisattva Fearless-Wisdom-Faith, Devaputra Pure Moon Radiance, Devaputra Resolute Radiance, and the rest of the assembly, all the devas, humans, asuras, and others in the world, all rejoiced greatly, accepted with faith, and put it into practice.
