# 佛說大迦葉本經
## Sūtra of Mahākāśyapa's Origin, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0496

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the city of Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak Mountain.

At that time, within the city there was a powerful and wealthy brahmin named Nyagrodha, whose riches were beyond counting: gold, silver, the seven treasures, fields, houses, cattle, and horses, all beyond measure. The brahmin had a son named Pippali, a student of the path. He renounced sixty kośas of gold, treasures, and fine goods, as well as a thousand yoke of oxen, and abandoned his virtuous wife, the most beautiful woman in the world, whose face was radiant and wondrous beyond compare. In his mind he thought: "I shall pursue the world's arhats and study to cultivate the pure holy life." He went to the shrine of the Many-Sons Spirit, beneath a medicinal tree, and ate its fruit. At that time, the World-Honored One was turning the great Dharma wheel. When the Dharma wheel had been turned, he withdrew with the assembly of bhikṣus and went to the shrine of the Many-Sons Spirit, where he stopped at its monastery.

At that time, as Pippali the student was standing in the night just before dawn, he saw from afar the World-Honored One among the grove of trees, his radiance shining far, his majestic brilliance reaching everywhere. The student then thought: "This grove of trees, as dawn approaches, is illuminated by a majestic divine radiance, immeasurable in its light, extraordinarily sublime. In this grove there must be either a king of deer, or a great lion, or a deva and great spirit with supernatural powers and miraculous transformations. This is certain beyond doubt. I should go and look."

Then Pippali the student rose from his seat and went to the grove of trees. From afar he saw the World-Honored One, with his hundredfold radiance, the marks on the soles of his feet, and all his excellent features complete. At once he exclaimed in admiration: "The ancient divine sages of our tradition left behind scriptures describing the thirty-two marks of a great person, each distinct and complete, leading to one of two destinies: if remaining in the household life, one becomes a cakravartin king, sovereign of the four continents, who selects the essential teachings and governs with right Dharma, possessing swords, spears, and weapons but never employing them; if going forth from home, renouncing kingdom and kingship, one becomes a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Vidyācaraṇasaṃpanna, Sugata, Lokavid, Anuttarapuruṣa, Puruṣadamyasārathi, Śāstā Devamanuṣyāṇāṃ, Buddha, Bhagavān. I should go to pay my respects."

Pippali the student went to the Buddha's dwelling. He beheld the World-Honored One seated among the trees with dignified composure, like a great statue wrought of the seven treasures, majestic in virtue, all his faculties tranquil, his mind of the path serene and still. Having attained the utmost calm, suddenly pure, he had crossed to the other shore, like a golden mountain, like Mount Sumeru the king of mountains, like a person in the darkness of night with a great torch blazing. He was like a dragon in a deep pool of cool, clear water. The thirty-two marks adorned his body. Like a great mountain king with a great fire blazing at its summit; like the sun emerging from a ridge of peaks, its radiance illuminating all; like the full moon, brilliant alone among the stars; like a cakravartin king surrounded by his retinue. The eighty minor marks were spread across his body, like various flowers, each in full bloom, a hundred thousand rays of light emanating from his holy form. When Pippali the student saw the Buddha thus, his heart was filled with joy, as when one in darkness sees light. He went to the World-Honored One, greeted him courteously, exchanged words, announced his own name, and sat to one side.

The Buddha taught him the sūtra, explaining its many meanings and distinguishing its wisdom. He spoke of the teaching of the World-Honored One with its instructive and joyful words: generosity, keeping precepts, the disease of desire, removing the mind of affliction, the superiority of leaving home, the turmoil of the various classes, and prescribing medicine according to the illness. The Honored One saw that his mind was responsive, with a soft mind, a doubting mind, a joyful and trusting mind, a mind of sin and merit, a level and equanimous mind: matching mind with mind, he taught the Dharma for him, as is the way of all Buddhas. Examining his root disposition, he explained and distinguished suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. Right there on his seat, free from dust and far from defilement, the Dharma-eye arose in him. In the present he attained liberation. He perceived the source of the Dharma, distinguished the sūtras, pulled out his doubt, and stood established in the fruit of realization. Having received the teaching and its wisdom, he gained courageous power. Rising from his seat, he straightened his robes, placed his right knee on the ground, touched his head to the Buddha's feet, and said: "When I first arrived, as I beheld the soles of the Honored One's feet and announced my name, as I contemplated the Buddha's excellent marks, my heart leaped with joy, and I forgot my reverence."

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Mahākāśyapa: "Therefore, worthy one, from this day forward, whatever clan-son goes anywhere, his mind should be like the moon, as moonlight illuminates. May the radiance and brilliance of the clan shine thus! A clan-son opens his eyes and walks. Thus, Kāśyapa! From this day, whatever clan-son disciplines his mind and practices, let him be like the full moon traveling through the sky. Therefore, Kāśyapa! From this day, wherever a clan-son goes, let him discipline his mind and practice like the sunlight illuminating the world. That clan-son never closes his eyes; let him discipline his mind like the sunlight, just so."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "From this day forth, discipline your mind and practice like a bee. Wherever you go, you will open up much. Like a bee gathering the flavor of various flowers without wilting their color or fragrance: if a clan-son disciplines his mind and practices, from this day forth, let his conduct be just so."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "From this day onward, discipline your mind and practice: be like earth, water, fire, and wind. When receiving what is pure, do not rejoice; when receiving what is impure, excrement, urine, pus, blood, dead snakes, dead people, and filthy matter, do not grieve. When receiving flowers, incense, gold, silver, the seven treasures, and the five colors, do not delight. Neither increase nor decrease. Let a clan-son discipline his mind and practice just so: praised and extolled, at ease and joyful, one does not delight; reviled and slandered, in suffering and distress, one does not grieve."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "From this day forth, let a clan-son discipline his mind and practice like a cleaning cloth: it wipes what is clean and also wipes what is unclean, excrement, urine, snot, spittle, pus, blood, dead dogs, dead snakes, dead people, and filthy matter. It does not rejoice in the clean or grieve over the unclean. If a clan-son disciplines his mind and practices, let him be just so."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "From this day onward, discipline your mind and practice like a broom: sweeping what is clean and sweeping what is unclean."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "From this day onward, let a clan-son discipline his mind and practice like the child of a lowly undertaker, who always walks with head bowed. Wherever he goes, he always keeps his hands close. Like a naked person, ashamed that the body is uncovered, living in the world merely to stay alive, not speaking of his original caste, whether acceptable or unacceptable, never advertising himself. If a clan-son disciplines his mind and practices, let him be just so."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "From this day onward, let a clan-son discipline his mind and practice like a dehorned ox. Like an ox with its horns cut off, gentle and docile, not craving the four necessities: it goes to a crossroads, and at the crossroads there are no gates, yet its life is endangered. If a clan-son disciplines his mind and practices, let him be just so."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "From this day onward, discipline your mind and practice like an iron cauldron and like lamps. The cauldron and lamps are pierced through and full of holes, yet filled with oil. As a person with clear eyes observes from one side, the oil leaks from the holes of the cauldron and lamps, dripping to the ground. If a clan-son examines the body's impermanence, composed of the four great elements, pierced through with nine holes, all discharging impurities, he does not crave or delight in the body, and does not regard it as marvelous."

Thereupon, Mahākāśyapa, having heard from the World-Honored One the practice taught through the simile of the moon, received and recited it, and contemplated the eight gates of liberation. The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Go to the Radiant Tree." Kāśyapa responded: "Yes, World-Honored One!" He rose from his seat and stood behind the Buddha.

Then the Buddha and Mahākāśyapa left that grove of trees and went to a different grove. Then they departed again and sat under a different tree. He told Mahākāśyapa: "Beneath this tree, spread a seat for the Tathāgata. My body is weary and my back aches." Kāśyapa received the instruction and quickly, quickly spread a seat for the Buddha, making it neat and proper. When the seat was prepared, he said to the Great Sage: "The seat is ready. May the Honored One be seated."

The Buddha then sat down and told Kāśyapa: "This ground is soft, smooth, and fine."

Kāśyapa said: "Indeed, World-Honored One! That ground or this ground, this human body must in the end return to this ground. Indeed, World-Honored One! All returns to cessation and extinction. Hold the mind in patience, as this ground does. My Dharma robe is also soft and fine. May the Buddha, out of compassion, kindly accept it."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "If I were to accept the soft crimson robe, what would you wear?"

Kāśyapa said to the Buddha: "The ancient World-Honored Ones praised it thus: 'If a clan-son wears the cast-off garments of the dead from the charnel grounds, the patched robe of rags, this brings peace to the devas and the people of the world.'"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent, Kāśyapa! You have great compassion and bring peace and security to many. To wear the patched rag-robe is what the ancient Buddhas praised. Kāśyapa! Rise, and quickly fetch water. I am very hungry and thirsty and wish to drink water."

"Yes, World-Honored One!" He received the instruction, rose, touched his forehead to the Buddha's feet, circumambulated the Buddha three times, and quickly, quickly went to fetch water.

The bhikṣus saw him and at once asked: "You are an elder, yet you are not proud. You are not sorrowful. You have stilled all violent desires and entered the abode of nothingness. Why then have you become a śramaṇa and received the full precepts? Where are you going now?"

Kāśyapa replied: "Go to the Buddha and raise this matter with the Great Sage yourselves. He will explain and distinguish it all for you."

At that time, the worthy Mahākāśyapa fetched water and brought it to offer to the Buddha. The Buddha received it. When he had finished drinking the water, he gave the remaining water to Kāśyapa. Kāśyapa received it, set it to one side, knelt on his right knee with palms joined, straightened his robes, and said to the World-Honored One: "When I went to fetch water, I saw the disciples and the bhikṣus, who asked me: 'You are an elder, yet you are not proud, not sorrowful, not foolish or deluded, and have stilled the four things and the violence of desires.' I renounced sixty kośas of gold, wondrous treasures, a thousand yoke of oxen, and abandoned my wife, the most beautiful woman in the world. Whoever in the world can become an arhat, I would follow them. Now the disciples and the bhikṣus have all come and asked me about this. I was formerly at the shrine of the Many-Sons Spirit, beneath the medicinal tree, eating its fruit. At that time I had not yet received the full precepts from the World-Honored One. At that time, the World-Honored One was traveling in the city of Rājagṛha, and I was in the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove. One morning, I put on my robe, took my bowl, and entered the city to beg for alms. I saw a great palace with a thousand rays of light emerging from it. At that time, the World-Honored One was in the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove in Rājagṛha. When I saw the World-Honored One, I recalled thinking it was the sun rising again, or a great deva, and night had suddenly become day. Then, in the late afternoon, the Buddha arose from his meditation, like the palace of the moon shining with great radiance, eclipsing the light of all suns and moons, like a great torch illuminating the darkness. The Buddha among the assembly of disciples, with his majestic divine radiance, was likewise. He was teaching the sūtra Dharma for the bhikṣus, like a cakravartin king with his sons and retinue, together with countless bhikṣus. At that time, looking until death I could not see among the assembly of bhikṣus anyone capable of receiving, anyone who could be a teacher. Only the Tathāgata, who has come forth into the world, promoted the teaching, and will enter nirvāṇa: now the bhikṣus have come to ask me."

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Kāśyapa: "Many bhikṣus do not understand good and evil, awakening and non-awakening, and do not comprehend the field of merit. Among the places of the Dharma, there are many profound meanings. This is called foremost. Kāśyapa is the most honored: not proud, level and without sorrow, having stilled the four things and the violence of desires, foremost in accomplishment, receiving the full precepts."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the worthy Kāśyapa and all the bhikṣus were filled with joy.
