# T1557 阿毘曇五法行經

# Abhidharma Sūtra on the Five Dharma Categories

*Note: This text was translated by An Shigao (Ān Shìgāo 安世高) during the Later Han dynasty. It is an early Abhidharma catechetical manual that systematically defines the five dharma categories (wǔfǎ 五法): form (sè 色), mind (yì 意/心), mental factors (suǒniàn fǎ 所念法), factors dissociated from mind (biélí yì xíng 別離意行), and the unconditioned (wúwéi 無為). The text opens with the four noble truths and their "knowledge-understanding" (xiá kě 黠可) analysis, presents the sixteen aspects of the four truths, and then proceeds to define each dharma category in detail: the four great elements, the five sense faculties, their objects, the six consciousnesses, mental factors (feeling, perception, formation, intention, desire, concentration, wisdom, faith, vigor, etc.), the three wholesome roots and three unwholesome roots, the five indeterminate roots, the nine fetters (jiǔjié 九結), the seven types of pride, the three wrong views, the seven latent tendencies (qīshǐ 七使), and the ten kinds of knowledge. An Shigao's archaic Chinese translation style is characteristically terse.*

---

## The Four Noble Truths

Understanding the dharma of suffering, and understanding the understanding of the dharma of suffering. Understanding the dharma of accumulation, and understanding the understanding of the dharma of accumulation. Understanding the dharma of cessation, and understanding the understanding of the dharma of cessation. Understanding the dharma of the path, and understanding the understanding of the dharma of the path.

The dharma of suffering means that the body and all things must decline, age, and die; this is suffering. The deluded person thinks they can be permanently preserved; this is what they call happiness. Understanding this as suffering, one no longer turns toward birth and death; this is understanding the dharma of suffering.

The dharma of accumulation means accumulating desire, accumulating acquisition, accumulating lust, accumulating anger, accumulating delusion, accumulating liking, accumulating what is pleasing. Understanding is: such is accumulation. From accumulation one reaches cessation, and then one no longer desires; this is understanding the dharma of accumulation.

The dharma of cessation means that persons and things will eventually dissipate and be completely extinguished. Having attained something, one feels no delight; having lost something, one feels no sorrow; this is understanding the dharma of cessation.

The dharma of the path means: practicing the path, one attains the path. Doing good, one ascends to heaven; doing evil, one enters evil destinies. Understanding the path means knowing to abandon evil and pursue good; this is understanding the dharma of the path.

"Understanding" (xiá 黠) means awakening. "Comprehension" (kě 可) means knowing. Originally not knowing: this is suffering. Suffering is one mental aspect; knowing it as suffering is the second mental aspect. Accumulation is one mental aspect; knowing it as accumulation is the second. Cessation is one; knowing it as cessation is the second. The path is one; knowing it as the path is the second. These eight mental aspects are external.

## The Sixteen Aspects

Impermanence, suffering, emptiness, non-self. The original accumulation arises from causes and conditions. Cessation. Calming, accordance with mind, the essential, the path, dwelling, contemplation.

Cessation: how is cessation of suffering, emptiness, and non-self attained? Cessation comes from suffering; from suffering one attains cessation. Through cessation one attains emptiness; attaining emptiness one knows "this is not my self." These four mental aspects constitute the aspects following the truth of suffering.

What is suffering? Everything within birth and death is suffering. Encountering what one desires and also what one does not desire: what a person's various desires obtain, and also what is not desired, meaning what the person's mind finds disagreeable: all of this is suffering. Craving arises from accumulation. Following the aspect of impermanence, one seeks the cessation of suffering through accumulation.

What is "dharma"? It means: by such causes and conditions one does this, one obtains this; this is "dharma." What should be identified has already been identified. To withdraw the mind, what should be severed. Among the four truths, the truth of suffering and the truth of accumulation serve as evidence that there is a path. Seeing suffering, one knows it arises from accumulation. Seeing accumulation, one knows suffering. Seeing the truth of cessation, one knows impermanence.

What is "comprehension"? It means delighting in the path and not forgetting the path, constantly seeking the path, and taking the path as what one comprehends. What is "understanding"? Constantly inquiring about the path is understanding. Having inquired, being able to receive and practice: this is understanding. Accumulation is likewise. Cessation and extinction are likewise. The path is likewise.

Suffering is the bondage of unwholesome dharma. Understanding is the thirty-seven factors of awakening. Comprehension is practice; practice is practicing the path. Accumulation is likewise; cessation is likewise; the path is likewise. All are for the sake of advancement.

## The Four Truths Explained in Detail

**First: Suffering.** What is suffering? All that is evil and disagreeable to the mind is suffering. Having identified suffering, what one does not desire, one then practices the path without departing; this is comprehension. Suffering in arising has a root. What is the root of suffering? It comes from the myriad things. If the myriad things did not exist, then one would neither exhaust oneself nor exhaust others, nor grieve for oneself nor grieve for others, and there would be no suffering.

**Second: Accumulation.** What is accumulation? The mind following craving is accumulation. Sever craving and there is no accumulation. What practice severs craving? All things arise from causes and conditions; sever the causes and conditions and they no longer arise. How does one sever causes and conditions? By directing the mind to contemplate the path. Once the mind is directed to contemplate the path, the mind cannot have two thoughts simultaneously and is thus on the path. This is accumulation.

**Third: Cessation.** The dharma of suffering is the myriad things. When they decay, one obtains sorrow; having obtained sorrow, one ages; having aged, one becomes sick, emaciated, and dies. This is called the external dharma of suffering and cessation. Why is it called the external dharma of suffering and cessation? Because one's own transgressions have not yet been removed. Why have one's transgressions not been removed? Because birth and death have not yet been extinguished. Why have birth and death not been extinguished? Because the mind is not unified. Why is the mind not unified? Because one has not entered dhyāna and abandoned distractions. Why has one not entered dhyāna and abandoned distractions? Because one has not accepted and practiced according to the Buddha's words. This is called external cessation. What is internal cessation? The mind enters concentration. Once it enters concentration, other thoughts cannot arise. Once other thoughts cannot arise, the fetters are extinguished. Once the fetters are extinguished, transgressions are exhausted. Once transgressions are exhausted, there is complete cessation and nothing remains. This is called internal cessation.

**Fourth: The Path.** What is the path? The path of suffering and the comprehensible mind is named as eight kinds. What are the eight kinds? As the ānāpāna-smṛti (mindfulness of breathing) explains the eight practices of the mind: one does not fall into birth and death but enters the path. Having entered the path, one severs the three things above. What three things? Suffering, accumulation, and cessation. Having severed suffering, accumulation, and cessation, one becomes settled. Once settled, wherever one is directed, one attains the path. What is attaining the path? When suffering is extinguished and no longer arises, this is attainment.

## The Five Dharma Categories

There are five dharma categories. What are the five? First, form (sè 色); second, mind (yì 意); third, mental factors (suǒniàn 所念); fourth, factors dissociated from mind (biélí yì xíng 別離意行); fifth, the unconditioned (wúwéi 無為).

### 1. Form

What is form? All form is within the four great elements and arises from the four great elements. What are the four great elements? The earth element, water element, fire element, and wind element; they also arise from the conditions of the four elements. What are the derivative forms? The eye faculty, ear faculty, nose faculty, tongue faculty, and body faculty; form, sound, scent, taste, and tangibles, each at a single base without interaction.

### 2. Mind

What is mind? What is meant by mind is mental consciousness. What is this? It is the six consciousness-groups. What are the six consciousnesses? Eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, and mental consciousness.

### 3. Mental Factors

What are the mental factors? They are the dharmas that are contemplated and that accompany the mind. What are they? Feeling, perception, formation, sensation, mindfulness, desire, mental concentration, and understanding. Also faith, vigor, reflection, mindfulness, craving, non-craving, wholesome roots, unwholesome roots, indeterminate roots, all fetters and bonds including effort, and the arising of understanding, insight, and essentials, as well as all such dharmas that accompany the mind. These are called "mental factors."

### 4. Factors Dissociated from Mind

What are the factors dissociated from mind? They are the dharmas that are separate and do not accompany the mind. What are they? Attainment, the attainment of non-perception cessation, the attainment of the cessation of perception and feeling, the non-perception attainment, the lesser-grade suffering attainment, place-attainment, species-attainment, the entrance into birth-aging-cessation, impermanence, name, word, sentence, and conformity, as well as other such dharmas dissociated from mind. These are called "factors dissociated from mind."

### 5. The Unconditioned

What is the unconditioned? Space (kōng 空), cessation through deliberation not yet free of attachment (miè wèi lí 滅未離), and cessation not requiring further reception (miè bù xū shòu 滅不須受).

## Definitions of the Elements and Faculties

The earth element is what is solid. The water element is what is moist. The fire element is what is hot. The wind element is what moves.

The eye faculty is what enables the eye-consciousness to engage with visible form. The ear faculty is what enables the ear-consciousness to engage with visible form. Likewise for the nose, tongue, and body faculties.

Form is: whatever is beautiful, ugly, or intermediate in color. These form-characteristics are cognized by one consciousness above all: the eye-consciousness engaging with form, which becomes mental consciousness engaging with it. Form is thus known by two consciousnesses: eye and mental. Sound, scent, taste, and tangibles are similarly each known by two consciousnesses: the respective sense-consciousness and the mental consciousness. Mental dharmas at the mental base do not engage with form. What are they? They are the dharma-conceptions to which consciousness is attached. These include what is wholesome and unwholesome in the body, and they are always cognized by one consciousness: the mental consciousness.

## The Six Consciousnesses

Eye-consciousness is: dependent on the eye, with form as condition, one knows. Ear-consciousness: dependent on the ear faculty, with sound as condition, one knows. Nose, tongue, body, and mental consciousnesses are defined analogously.

## Mental Factors Defined

**Feeling** (tòng 痛): What is feeling? It is pleasure. It is also of three grades: slight, much, and immeasurable.

**Perception** (xiǎng 想): What is perception? It is cognizing an object.

**Formation** (xíng 行): What is formation? It is action. It is also of three grades: wholesome, unwholesome, and indeterminate concerning merit and demerit.

**Desire** (lè 願樂): What is this? It is the three meetings. Also of three grades: wholesome desire, unwholesome desire, and neither-wholesome-nor-unwholesome desire.

**Mindfulness**, **intention** (yì 意), **delight** (kě yì 可意), **one-pointedness** (yī yì 一意), **understanding** (xiá 黠) as observing dharmas, **faith** (xìn 信) as mental assent, **vigor** (jìn 進) as observing mindfulness, **reflection** (jì 計), **sustained mindfulness** (niàn 念), and the distinction between them are each briefly defined.

**Craving** (tān 貪): Not following wholesome dharmas, not believing in sincerity, not practicing what should be practiced.

**Non-craving** (bù tān 不貪): Following wholesome dharmas, having faith, being sincere, practicing what should be practiced.

**Three wholesome roots**: absence of craving, absence of anger, absence of delusion.

**Three unwholesome roots**: craving, anger, delusion.

**Five indeterminate roots**: attachment-indeterminacy, pride-indeterminacy, delusion-indeterminacy, doubt-indeterminacy, and action-indeterminacy.

## The Nine Fetters

What are the fetters? There are nine fetters: (1) the fetter of attachment-mindfulness (craving); (2) the fetter of aversion; (3) the fetter of pride; (4) the fetter of delusion; (5) the fetter of wrong views; (6) the fetter of lost vows (appropriation); (7) the fetter of doubt; (8) the fetter of jealousy; (9) the fetter of miserliness.

**The fetter of pride** has seven kinds: (1) pride (regarding those inferior as inferior and those equal as equal); (2) over-pride (regarding equals as superior and superiors as equal); (3) self-pride; (4) pride of self-estimation (regarding the five aggregates as "my self"); (5) deceptive pride (claiming attainment not yet attained, knowledge not yet known, exhaustion not yet exhausted); (6) inferior pride (when one is far inferior but estimates oneself as only slightly inferior); (7) wrong pride (the unworthy estimating oneself as worthy). These are the seven kinds of pride.

**The fetter of wrong views** has three kinds: (1) the view of a self in the body (satkāya-dṛṣṭi); (2) extreme views (eternalism and annihilationism); (3) wrong views proper (which are the root of contention and destroy merit).

**The fetter of lost vows** (appropriation) has two kinds: (1) the appropriation of the aggregates (esteeming the five aggregates as supreme); (2) the appropriation of precepts and practices.

## The Seven Latent Tendencies

There are seven latent tendencies: (1) the tendency of desire (five kinds, severed by seeing each of the four truths and by cultivation); (2) the tendency of aversion (likewise five kinds); (3) the tendency of desire for the world (ten kinds across the form and formless realms); (4) the tendency of pride (fifteen kinds across the three realms and five paths of severance); (5) the tendency of delusion (fifteen kinds); (6) the tendency of wrong views (thirty-six kinds across the three realms); (7) the tendency of doubt (twelve kinds across the three realms).

These are the defilements and their torments. Sometimes there are defilements without torments; aside from the defilements, the remaining disturbing mental dharmas are called "torments, not defilements." Those that arise are eight: (1) drowsiness; (2) torpor; (3) pleasure; (4) doubt; (5) leaning; (6) indulgence; (7) lack of shame before others; (8) lack of self-shame.

## The Ten Kinds of Knowledge

What are the kinds of understanding? There are ten: (1) dharma-knowledge; (2) inferential knowledge; (3) knowledge of others' minds; (4) conventional knowledge; (5) knowledge of suffering; (6) knowledge of accumulation; (7) knowledge of cessation; (8) knowledge of the path; (9) knowledge of exhaustion; (10) knowledge of the unconditioned (non-arising).

**Dharma-knowledge**: In the desire realm of birth and death, understanding without any fetter regarding the desires, their origin, their cessation, and the path of destroying them, and also understanding without fetter regarding the dharma-ground.

**Inferential knowledge**: In the form and formless realms, understanding without any fetter regarding the actions, their origin, their cessation, and the path of severing them.

**Knowledge of others' minds**: Understanding of the actions, merits, and combinations that others have attained and not relinquished, that are always present and always recollected without being forgotten, practiced for the sake of others and out of kindness.

**Conventional knowledge**: Worldly practical understanding.

**Knowledge of suffering**: Contemplating the five aggregates as impermanent, suffering, empty, and not-self, without any fetter.

**Knowledge of accumulation**: Contemplating the worldly origin, habits, arising, causes, and conditions, without any fetter.

**Knowledge of cessation**: Understanding cessation as cessation, the supreme essential contemplation without fetter.

**Knowledge of the path**: The path as it should be received, the contemplation of the desire for liberation, without fetter.

**Knowledge of exhaustion**: Having identified suffering, relinquished accumulation, realized cessation, and practiced the path: from this understanding, seeing, knowing, and mental attainment in correspondence. This is knowledge of exhaustion.

**Knowledge of the unconditioned**: Suffering has been experienced and need not be experienced again. Accumulation is complete and need not be completed again. Cessation has been realized and need not be realized again. The path has been practiced and need not be practiced again. From this understanding, seeing, knowing, and mental attainment. This is knowledge of the unconditioned.

## Conclusion

The eight contacts are: the understanding-comprehension of the dharma of suffering, the understanding-comprehension of the analogy of suffering, the understanding-comprehension of the dharma of accumulation, the understanding-comprehension of the analogy of accumulation, the understanding-comprehension of the dharma of cessation, the understanding-comprehension of the analogy of cessation, the understanding-comprehension of the dharma of the path, the understanding-comprehension of the analogy of the path.

**Space**: void, without anything, without attachment, without form. **Cessation not yet free of attachment**: having ceased, one no longer experiences again, no longer attaches. **Cessation (unconditioned)**: transcending the world, the unconditioned.

This completes the explanation of the five dharma categories and their practices.
