# 菩提心義
## The Meaning of the Bodhi-Mind

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1953

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

The mind of bodhi is the foundation for attaining buddhahood. Its characteristics when aroused are fully described in the various sūtras. No great matter of causes and conditions surpasses this. If one wishes to correctly cultivate awakening, one must not fail to know it. According to what has been seen and heard, I shall briefly distinguish its characteristics. The meaning of the bodhi-mind is analyzed through five gates: (1) explaining the name and meaning, (2) identifying the substance and nature, (3) distinguishing unity and difference, (4) clarifying the characteristics and features, and (5) describing practice and vows.

### First: Explaining the Name and Meaning

The Sanskrit word "bodhi" is translated here as "awakening." When sentient beings are covered by confusion, this is called "non-awakening." Now, encountering a good friend who opens up and dispels the darkness of ignorance, one eliminates the covering of confusion and gives rise to the mind that seeks awakening. This mind that seeks awakening is called the "bodhi-mind." To arouse the mind seeking bodhi is called the bodhi-mind. This is a dependent-determinative compound.

If awakened, it is called "awakening"; when confused, it is "non-awakening." It is like a confused person who is confused precisely because of direction: sentient beings are the same. They are confused precisely because of awakening. If one departs from awakening, then there is no non-awakening. Strictly speaking, bodhi and mind cannot be made into two. "Bodhi and mind" is then a contradictory compound.

### Second: Identifying the Substance and Nature

This is as explained in the *Yifu*.

### Third: Distinguishing Unity and Difference

Mind and bodhi are in nature without duality. Because they are non-dual, they cannot be discriminated. This is from the perspective of the gate where the principle-substance is without differentiation. Therefore the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* says: "As with mind, so with buddha. As with buddha, so with sentient beings. Mind, buddha, and sentient beings: these three are without differentiation." The *Ākāśagarbha Sūtra*, fascicle four, also says: "The World-Honored One constantly teaches that the Dharma is inexhaustible. Sentient beings and empty space, the bodhi-mind, the buddha-dharma..."

If one follows the gate of progressive cultivation and the arousal of practice and vows, then one arouses the deluded mind to seek bodhi, and this is called the bodhi-mind. Therefore the *Awakening of Faith* says: "How does the perfuming occur such that the arising of pure dharmas is uninterrupted? It is because there is the dharma of true suchness. It can perfume ignorance. Through the power of perfuming as a condition, it causes the deluded mind to become weary of the suffering of birth and death and to delight in seeking nirvāṇa. Because the deluded mind has the conditions of weariness and seeking, it then perfumes true suchness. One trusts one's own nature, knows the mind's deluded stirring, and knows there are no objects before it. One cultivates the method of separation. Because one truly knows there are no objects before it, through various expedient means one arouses practices of accordance. Not grasping, not thinking, and so on, until through the power of long perfuming, ignorance is extinguished. Because ignorance is extinguished, the mind has no arising. Because the mind has no arising, the realm of objects is also extinguished. Because both causes and conditions are extinguished, the characteristics of mind are entirely exhausted. This is called attaining nirvāṇa and accomplishing the natural activity." And so forth. This, then, is abandoning the false and accomplishing the true. By arousing the deluded mind, one seeks to arouse the true awakening. This means true and false are different. Yet in reality, apart from the true there is no false; the substance of the false is itself the true. Because non-awakening is itself original awakening. This is why they are not different.

### Fourth: Clarifying the Characteristics and Features

The characteristics and features of arousing the bodhi-mind are twofold: (1) clarifying the characteristics of practice-stages, and (2) distinguishing the characteristics of function and efficacy.

**The characteristics of practice-stages:** The Tripiṭaka Master Long-Ear said: "At the initial stage of the practice-seed nature, the arousal of the mind has three kinds. First, arousal through imagined concept. Second, arousal through light concept. Third, arousal through faith concept."

First, arousal through imagined concept occurs through three kinds of power: (1) the power of good friends, meaning good teachers; (2) the power of practice, meaning receiving the moral code; (3) the power of the Dharma, which is both general and specific. The general cause is the nature of the tathāgata-garbha's inner perfuming. The specific causes are the five faculties such as faith. Through these three powers, one provisionally arouses the concept of seeking bodhi. Benefiting oneself and benefiting others, one gradually cultivates. Like a light feather without any attachment, this is called arousal through light concept. After gradual cultivation, when faith-mind is purified and one enters the ten abodes, this is called arousal through faith concept.

The *Awakening of Faith* says: "The arousal of the mind has three kinds. First, the arousal of mind through accomplished faith. Second, the arousal of mind through understanding and practice. Third, the arousal of mind through realization."

First, the arousal of mind through accomplished faith: this refers to sentient beings of the unfixed assembly. Through the power of perfuming roots of goodness, they trust in karmic retribution, are able to practice the ten wholesome deeds, become weary of the suffering of birth and death, and aspire to seek unsurpassed bodhi. And so on, until: "After ten thousand kalpas, faith-mind is accomplished. All buddhas and bodhisattvas teach them to arouse the mind. Or, through great compassion, they arouse the mind on their own. Or, because the correct Dharma is about to perish, through the condition of protecting the Dharma, they arouse the mind on their own and enter the ten abodes." This corresponds to the arousal through faith concept above.

The *Yingluo Sūtra* says: "This bodhisattva of faith-concept, for ten thousand kalpas, practices the Dharma of the ten precepts and should enter the ten levels of faith-mind, entering the first abode-stage." This is the abode of arousing the mind.

The *Renwang Sūtra* says: "The practice-seed nature includes the ten levels of faith. It already transcends the good grounds of the Two Vehicles."

The *Awakening of Faith* further says: "What kind of mind is aroused? In brief, there are three. First, the straightforward mind, because it correctly contemplates the dharma of true suchness. Second, the profound mind, because it delights in accumulating all wholesome practices. Third, the great compassion mind, because it wishes to remove the suffering of all sentient beings." And so on, until: "Because the bodhisattva arouses this mind, he attains a partial vision of the dharma-body. Because he sees the dharma-body, in accordance with the power of his vows, he can manifest eight kinds of forms to benefit sentient beings." And so forth.

Second, the arousal of mind through understanding and practice: one should know that this is of a still higher order. "Because this bodhisattva, since the beginning of correct faith, during the first incalculable kalpa, as it approaches completion, in the dharma of true suchness, deep understanding manifests before him. The practices he cultivates are free from characteristics." And so forth.

Third, the arousal of mind through realization: "From the pure-mind ground up to the ultimate ground of the bodhisattva, what realm is realized? The so-called true suchness." And so on, until: "Furthermore, the characteristics of this bodhisattva's aroused mind have three kinds of subtle aspects. First, the true mind, the name of non-discrimination. Second, the mind of expedient means, which naturally and universally performs actions that benefit sentient beings. Third, the ālaya-consciousness mind, which subtly arises and ceases." And so forth.

**Distinguishing the characteristics of function and efficacy:** The *Vimalakīrti Sūtra* says: "If one wishes to attain the Buddha-body and cut off all the diseases of sentient beings, one should arouse the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi." The *Avataṃsaka*, fascicle seventy-eight, says: "The bodhi-mind is like a seed, because it can produce all buddha-dharmas. The bodhi-mind is like a fertile field, because it can nurture the pure white dharmas of sentient beings. The bodhi-mind is like the great earth, because it can support all worlds. The bodhi-mind is like pure water, because it can wash away the stains of all afflictions. The bodhi-mind is like a great wind, because it goes throughout the world without obstruction. The bodhi-mind is like a blazing fire, because it can burn the firewood of all views. The bodhi-mind is like a pure sun, universally illuminating all worlds. The bodhi-mind is like a full moon, because all pure white dharmas are perfectly complete. The bodhi-mind is like a bright lamp, because it can emit various kinds of pure radiance. The bodhi-mind is like a pure eye, because it universally sees all places of safety and danger. The bodhi-mind is like a great road, because it universally enables entry into the city of great wisdom. The bodhi-mind is like a correct ford, because it enables one to leave behind all wrong dharmas."

And further: "Good men, if anyone arouses the mind of anuttarā-samyak-sa���bodhi, they have already produced immeasurable merit and can universally gather the entire way of all-knowledge. Good men, it is like a person who obtains the fearlessness-medicine and is freed from five kinds of dread. What are the five? Fire cannot burn, poison cannot harm, blades cannot wound, water cannot sweep away, and smoke cannot suffocate. The bodhisattva-mahāsattva is also like this. Obtaining the medicine of the all-knowing bodhi-mind: the fire of desire cannot burn, the poison of anger cannot harm, the blade of delusion cannot wound, the current of existence cannot sweep away, and the smoke of discursive thought cannot harm. Good men, it is like a person who obtains the medicine of liberation and never encounters accidental calamity. The bodhisattva-mahāsattva is also like this. Obtaining the medicine of the bodhi-mind's liberative wisdom, one is forever freed from all the accidental calamities of birth and death. Good men, it is like a person who holds the great mahā-aṅga medicine: poisonous snakes, smelling its scent, all flee far away. The bodhisattva-mahāsattva is also like this. Holding the great aṅga medicine of the bodhi-mind, all the evil poisonous snakes of afflictions, upon smelling its scent, are entirely scattered and extinguished."

### Fifth: Describing Practice and Vows

The *Xianyang* says: "The mundane arousal of mind means that, before a wise witness, one arouses the great vow." And further: "From this day forward, I arouse the unsurpassed bodhi-mind. Because I wish to benefit all sentient beings, from now on, whatever I cultivate of the six pāramitās is all for the purpose of realizing unsurpassed bodhi. I now, together with all bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, renounce the household life in harmony. I pray the honored ones will bear witness: I am a bodhisattva."

The *Mahāvairocana Sūtra Commentary* says: "To arouse the bodhi-mind means to give rise to a definite and determined vow, single-mindedly aspiring to all-knowledge, and certainly to universally deliver all sentient beings of the dharmadhātu. This mind is like a banner and standard; it is the forerunner of all practices. Like a seed, it is the root of myriad virtues. If one does not arouse this mind, it is as if the kalala-embryo has not yet formed: what would the womb of great compassion nourish?" It further says: "The sun symbolizes the originally pure bodhi-mind, which is the self-substance of Vairocana. The moon symbolizes bodhi-practice. On the fifteenth of the bright fortnight, all practices are perfectly complete, symbolizing the accomplishment of bodhi. On the fifteenth of the dark fortnight, all practices cease, symbolizing parinirvāṇa. The rising and setting in between symbolizes skillful means and expedient adaptations." And so forth.

The *Sūtra on Arousing the Bodhi-Mind* says: "If a bodhisattva draws near to good teachers, makes offerings to all buddhas, cultivates roots of goodness, aspires to the supreme Dharma, has a mind that is always gentle, is able to endure suffering, has compassion that is sincere and profound, has a deep mind of equanimity, trusts in and delights in the Great Vehicle, and seeks the wisdom of the Buddha: if a person can possess these ten qualities, then he is able to arouse the unsurpassed bodhi-mind."

"Furthermore, there are four conditions that can arouse this mind. First, contemplating the buddhas. Second, contemplating the faults of one's own body. Third, compassion for sentient beings. Fourth, seeking the supreme fruit."

First, contemplating the buddhas: "The buddhas of the three times, at the very beginning, aroused the mind while still possessing the nature of afflictions, just as I do now. They aroused great, luminous wisdom. From the shell of ignorance, they established the supreme mind. They accumulated arduous practices and crossed the ocean of birth and death. They relinquished body, life, and wealth and sought all-knowledge. Now they have all accomplished it. If this bodhi can be attained, I too should attain it." Therefore one arouses the bodhi-mind.

Second, contemplating the faults of the body: "I observe my own body, with nine apertures constantly flowing with foul and impure substances, and generate weariness and detachment. Furthermore, I observe the five aggregates and four great elements, all of which can create immeasurable evil karma. Possessing greed, anger, delusion, and immeasurable afflictions, they are like bubbles and foam, impermanent from thought to thought. Seeking to abandon and leave them," one arouses the bodhi-mind.

Third, compassion for sentient beings: "Seeing all sentient beings bound by ignorance, entangled in manifold suffering, accumulating unwholesome karma, undergoing great and intense suffering, abandoning the correct Dharma, trusting and accepting deviant paths, sinking in the river of afflictions, not seeking liberation, creating ever more evil: out of compassion for them," one arouses the bodhi-mind.

Fourth, seeking the supreme fruit: "Seeing all tathāgatas adorned with the marks and excellences, possessing precepts, concentration, wisdom, and the purity of knowing and seeing, the ten powers, the fearlessnesses, the three kinds of mindfulness of great compassion, possessing all-knowledge, caring for sentient beings, abiding in the eternal dharma-body, pure and undefiled: in order to cultivate these," one arouses the bodhi-mind.

It further says: "One who arouses the bodhi-mind should first firmly establish the correct vow, namely the four universal vows and the like, establishing one's resolve with strength and firmness, making great essential pledges, and constantly cultivating the correct practices, namely the six pāramitās and the like." Therefore, in the *Buddhakṣetra Sūtra*, Ākāśarāja made his pledge, saying: "Before the great assembly, I arouse the bodhi-mind. I vow to deliver all sentient beings and free them from all suffering. I vow that from now on, if I have any defiled, angry, jealous, arrogant, or desirous mind, this would be to deceive the buddhas of the ten directions and the buddhas now present." And further: "By the truth of these sincere words, may the earth quake in six ways. If my words are untrue, may the four great elements be overturned." And so forth.

The *Sūtra on Arousing the Bodhi-Mind* further says: "Establishing the definite vow is upheld by five things. First, it can make the mind firm. Second, it can subdue afflictions. Third, it can ward off heedlessness. Fourth, it can destroy the five coverings. Fifth, it can cause diligent cultivation of the six pāramitās." And so forth.

If one fully possesses such vows, with firmness and courage cultivating giving, precepts, patience, vigor, concentration, wisdom, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity, without retrogression: this is called truly arousing the bodhi-mind.

The *Vimalakīrti Sūtra* says: "One who arouses the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi: this is precisely leaving home; this is precisely the complete precepts."
