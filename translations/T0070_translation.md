# 數經
## Sūtra on Counting

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0070

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin, translated by Fǎjù (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavat was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Eastern Park, in Mṛgāra's Hall. At that time, the brāhmaṇa Gaṇaka, after his midday meal, was walking about and came to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he exchanged courteous greetings with the World-Honored One. After exchanging greetings, he sat to one side. Having sat to one side, the brāhmaṇa Gaṇaka addressed the World-Honored One: "Gautama! I wish to ask something. Please hear my question."

"Ask as you please, brāhmaṇa, according to your wish."

"Gautama! This Mṛgāra's Hall was built step by step, completed step by step. Gautama! This Mṛgāra's Hall, starting from the first step, has stairways going up, then two, then three, then four. Gautama! In this way, this Mṛgāra's Hall is ascended step by step. Gautama! Those who train elephants teach step by step, learn step by step, namely with the goad in hand. Gautama! Those who train horses teach step by step, learn step by step, namely with the bridle. Gautama! Those of the kṣatriya class teach step by step, learn step by step, namely in wielding bow and arrow. Gautama! We brāhmaṇas teach step by step, learn step by step, namely the study of poetic verses. Gautama! We study counting, and by counting we earn our living. When we have students in counting, the young ones are first taught one and two, then two and three, two and three, then up to ten, then a hundred, then ever larger numbers. Thus, Gautama, we study counting, and by counting we earn our living, teaching step by step, learning step by step, namely the study of numbers. Śramaṇa Gautama! In your Dharma and discipline, how do you teach and what is learned, that it may be known?"

"You who know counting, Moggallāna! If I were to say this, would you say it is a step-by-step teaching? Step-by-step instruction, step-by-step training in conduct and precepts. Moggallāna! If I were to say this, would you not say it is step by step? In my Dharma and discipline, why is it so? In my Dharma and discipline, Moggallāna, there is step-by-step instruction, step-by-step conduct, step-by-step training in precepts. Moggallāna! When a bhikṣu is a beginner who has not been long in the order and has newly come to this Dharma and discipline, not yet having received the Tathāgata's instruction, this bhikṣu must first purify his bodily conduct, then purify his verbal and mental conduct. Moggallāna! When a bhikṣu has purified his bodily conduct, and his verbal and mental conduct, the Tathāgata, the supreme charioteer, trains him further.

"This bhikṣu should internally observe the body in the body, its characteristics and activities, up to feelings, mind, and dharmas, observing dharmas in dharmas, their characteristics and activities. Moggallāna! When a bhikṣu internally observes the body in the body, its characteristics and activities, up to feelings, mind, and dharmas in dharmas, their characteristics and activities, the Tathāgata, the supreme charioteer, trains him further.

"This bhikṣu should guard the doors of the sense faculties, protect his mind, protect mindful awareness, and together practice diligence. When the eye sees forms, he should not grasp at signs or secondary characteristics. Because of the predominant condition, he guards the eye faculty, free from the shameful states of covetousness, sorrow, and mental anguish, free from evil and unwholesome dharmas. In this he trains to guard the eye faculty. Likewise with the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind faculties: when the mind cognizes dharmas, he should not grasp at signs or secondary characteristics. Because of the predominant condition, guarding the mind faculty, free from the shameful states of covetousness, sorrow, and mental anguish, free from unwholesome dharmas dwelling in the mind, in this he trains to guard the mind faculty. Moggallāna! When a bhikṣu is endowed with guarding the doors of the sense faculties, protects his mind, his mind undefiled, protects mindful awareness, his mind together with mindfulness practicing diligence equally, when the eye sees forms it neither grasps signs nor secondary characteristics. Because of the predominant condition, endowed with guarding the eye faculty, free from the shameful states of covetousness, sorrow, and mental anguish, free from evil and unwholesome dharmas, in this he trains to guard the eye faculty. Likewise with the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind cognizing dharmas, neither grasping signs, up to training to guard the mind faculty. The Tathāgata, the supreme charioteer, trains him further.

"This bhikṣu, in all that has passed and all that comes, should practice clear comprehension: whether bending or stretching, wearing the saṅghāṭī robe, carrying the bowl, whether walking, standing, sitting, lying down, waking, speaking, or remaining silent, he should practice clear comprehension. Moggallāna! When a bhikṣu, in all that has passed, up to practicing clear comprehension, the Tathāgata, the supreme charioteer, trains him further.

"This bhikṣu, knowing the right bed and seat, should accept it. Whether in a secluded place, beneath a tree, in an empty quiet place, among mountain caves, sitting in the open, on a grass mat, in the forest, or among tombs. In that secluded place, he spreads out his sitting cloth, sits cross-legged, straightens his body, his aspiring mind with mindfulness foremost. He removes covetousness from the mind, abides without ill will, does not covet others' possessions, thinking, 'May others' things become mine.' He purifies the mind of covetousness. Likewise with ill will, sloth and torpor, restlessness and shame. He removes doubt and covetousness, departs from wrong views, departs from doubt, departs from all hesitation regarding dharmas, and purifies the mind of doubt. Having abandoned the five hindrances, those mental fetters, with wisdom he is freed from sensual desire, up to dwelling in the four dhyānas. Thus, Moggallāna, a bhikṣu is freed from sensual desire, up to dwelling in the four dhyānas. Thus, Moggallāna, the Tathāgata greatly benefits the bhikṣu who is a beginner, namely in instruction and training.

"Moggallāna! Those bhikṣus who are senior, recognized by kings, dwelling without negligence, practicing the holy life, the Tathāgata, the supreme charioteer, trains them further, namely until the complete exhaustion of outflows. All śramaṇa disciples of Gautama are instructed and trained in this way, until they are completely near nirvāṇa. Yet, Moggallāna, it is not the case that all do; some do not, some do."

"Gautama! What cause, what condition is there, that there is nirvāṇa and there is the path to nirvāṇa, and the śramaṇa Gautama is present to instruct, yet one bhikṣu, so instructed and trained, fully reaches nirvāṇa, while another does not?"

"Therefore, Moggallāna, I shall ask you in return, and you may answer as you please. Moggallāna, what do you think? Do you know well the road to Rājagṛha?"

"Indeed, Gautama, I know well the road to Rājagṛha."

"Suppose a person came, wishing to go to Rājagṛha to see the king, and came to you saying: 'The brāhmaṇa Moggallāna, skilled in counting, knows the road to Rājagṛha well. I wish to go to Rājagṛha to see the king; I ask the road.' You would say: 'Take this road and go straight. Going straight, you will reach that village; reaching that village, you will come to that place. Thus, step by step, you will reach Rājagṛha. In Rājagṛha there are delightful parks, delightful forests, delightful grounds, delightful ponds, and cool flowing rivers, peaceful and delightful. You should know this and see this.' He would accept your instruction and receive your direction. But having received the instruction, he takes a wrong road and goes in the opposite direction. He would neither know nor see the delightful parks of Rājagṛha, up to its peace and delight. Then suppose another person came, having business with the king, wishing to go to Rājagṛha, and came to you saying: 'The brāhmaṇa Moggallāna, skilled in counting, knows the road to Rājagṛha well. I wish to go to Rājagṛha; I now ask you the road.' You would say: 'Take this road and go straight. Going straight, you will reach that village; reaching that village, you will come to that place. Step by step, you will reach Rājagṛha. In Rājagṛha there are delightful parks, up to peace and delight. He would know it and see it.' Now, Moggallāna, what cause, what condition is there, that there is Rājagṛha and there is the road to Rājagṛha, and you are present to instruct, yet the first person, having received instruction, does not follow it but takes a wrong road and goes in the opposite direction, and neither knows nor sees the delightful parks of Rājagṛha, up to its peace and delight? While the second person, following the instruction, receiving the direction, taking the road, step by step reaches Rājagṛha and knows and sees the delightful parks, up to its peace and delight?"

"Gautama! What can I do about it? Rājagṛha exists and the road to Rājagṛha exists, and I am present to instruct. Yet the first person, as instructed, does not receive the instruction, takes a wrong road and goes in the opposite direction, and neither knows nor sees the delightful parks of Rājagṛha, up to its peace and delight. While the second person, following the instruction, receiving the direction, taking the road, step by step reaches Rājagṛha and knows and sees the delightful parks, up to its peace and delight."

"In the same way, Moggallāna, what can I do about it? Nirvāṇa exists and the path to nirvāṇa exists, and I am present to instruct. Yet one bhikṣu, so instructed and directed, fully reaches nirvāṇa, while another does not. Moggallāna! That bhikṣu who accepts the instruction, who in the World-Honored One's community accepts the instruction and receives the prediction, fully exhausts all outflows."

"Excellent, Gautama! Excellent, Gautama! Just as, Gautama, in very good soil there is a grove of śāla trees, and the keeper of that śāla grove is diligent and unwearying. With his own strength he tends the roots of those śāla trees, timely digging and inspecting them, placing manure within, watering them. If there are gaps, he fills them with earth. If there are weeds nearby, he pulls them out and discards them. If there are tangled vines, corrupt, crooked, and bent, he uproots them all and discards them, removing the new shoots, letting the mature ones grow fully. He timely waters them, places manure within, and waters them again. Thus, Gautama, that grove of śāla trees on good soil in later times will grow exceedingly great. In the same way, Gautama, those who are deceitful and hypocritical, without faith, negligent, confused and unsettled in mind, of poor wisdom, distracted, with unsteady faculties, and not diligent in precept-conduct, not thoroughly discerning in the practice of the śramaṇa: the śramaṇa Gautama does not dwell with them, does not stay with such people. Why is this? Gautama, such people corrupt the holy life. Gautama, those who are without deceit, without crooked intention, faithful, diligent, with minds constantly settled, suited for concentration-wisdom, respectful and obedient in training in precepts, and thoroughly discerning in the practice of the śramaṇa: the śramaṇa Gautama, without attachment, dwells with such people. Why is this? Gautama, such people accord with the Dharma purity of the holy life. Just as, Gautama, among all root fragrances, kālānusāri is the foremost. Why is this? Gautama, because kālānusāri is the chief of all root fragrances. Just as, Gautama, among all heartwood fragrances, red candana is the chief. Why is this? Gautama, because among all heartwood fragrances, red candana is the chief. Just as, Gautama, among all water flowers, the blue utpala is the chief. Why is this? Gautama, because among all water flowers, the blue utpala is the chief. Just as, Gautama, among all land flowers, sumanā and vārṣikī are the chief. Why is this? Gautama, because among all land flowers, vārṣikī is the chief. Just as, Gautama, among all discourses in the world, the discourse of the śramaṇa Gautama is the foremost. Why is this? Because the discourse of the śramaṇa Gautama can encompass all heterodox teachings. Therefore, World-Honored One! I now take refuge in the Dharma and the Sangha of bhikṣus. World-Honored One! From this day forward, I hold to being an upāsaka. From this day forth, for as long as life lasts, I abandon the taking of life. I now take refuge."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The brāhmaṇa Gaṇaka Moggallāna, who was always counting, hearing what the Buddha had spoken, rejoiced and was delighted.
