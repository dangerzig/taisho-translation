# 息諍因緣經
## Sūtra on the Causes of Ceasing Quarrels

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0085

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the World-Honored One was in the village of Sāmagāma, spending the rains retreat. The assembly of bhikṣus were also spending the rains retreat not far from the Buddha. At that time, there was a śramaṇa named Cunda who was spending the rains retreat in the Jālūka Forest. In that place, there was a non-Buddhist, the Nirgrantha Jñātaputra, a most evil person who suddenly died. The Nirgrantha had followers who wished to stir up quarrels with the śramaṇas, saying: "Our doctrine and discipline, only we ourselves know; it is not for you to know. Your doctrine and discipline, only you yourselves know; it is not for us to know either. What we hold is entirely reasonable; what you hold is entirely unreasonable. Harmony is ours; disharmony is yours. Whatever you say, if the first words are right, the later words are wrong; if the later words are right, the first words are wrong. All your speech has no meaning and no value. Though you speak at length, you cannot achieve liberation, nor can you know the supreme ultimate truth. What the śramaṇas teach is different from our doctrine. How can you cause us to accept it?" The Nirgrantha followers deliberately spoke thus to cause destruction, wishing to stir up quarrels, to diminish strength and ability, using afflictive conditions to destroy the Dharma of peace and happiness. For these reasons, they wished to create unwholesome karma within the śramaṇas' pure Dharma, stirring up quarrels and various destructions, giving rise to arrogant thoughts of claiming superiority, saying: "Whatever the śramaṇas teach is neither doctrine nor discipline, not correctly understood, unable to bring liberation, unable to progress on the path of awakening. Your teacher is not a Tathāgata, not an arhat, not one of right and universal awakening."

At that time, when the śramaṇa Cunda's rains retreat was complete and his robe-making finished, he departed from that place, donned his robes, took his bowl, and traveled from place to place until he reached the village of Sāmagāma. Having arrived, he put away his robes and bowl, washed his feet, and went to where the Venerable Ānanda was. He prostrated at Ānanda's feet and withdrew to stand to one side. The Venerable Ānanda then asked the śramaṇa Cunda: "Cunda! Where did you spend the rains retreat? And for what reason have you come here?"

Cunda said: "Venerable one! I spent the rains retreat in a bad place, the Jālūka Forest. There a non-Buddhist, the Nirgrantha Jñātaputra, a most evil person, suddenly died. The Nirgrantha's followers wished to stir up quarrels with the śramaṇas, even to the point of saying, 'Your teacher is not a Tathāgata, not an arhat, not one of right and universal awakening.' For this reason, I have come here."

Ānanda said: "Cunda! I am aware of this matter. Because the Buddha, the World-Honored One, is not among the assembly, non-Buddhists wish to stir up quarrels. But you at this time must not engage in quarrels with them. If quarrels arise, you will not be able to benefit many people, and will cause many people to produce various sufferings, and even all the assemblies of gods and humans will gain no benefit and will all produce suffering. Cunda, regarding what you have reported, only the Buddha, the World-Honored One, knows and sees all of this. Let me now go with you to see the Buddha and present these matters fully."

Cunda said: "Excellent, Venerable one! If I can see the Buddha, I will obtain great benefit. Moreover, in the Buddha's presence, I may hear the profound and correct Dharma."

Then the Venerable Ānanda went with the śramaṇa Cunda to where the Buddha was. Having arrived, they each prostrated at the Buddha's feet and withdrew to stand to one side. The Venerable Ānanda then addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! This śramaṇa Cunda spent the rains retreat in the Jālūka Forest. There a non-Buddhist, the Nirgrantha Jñātaputra, a most evil person, suddenly died. The Nirgrantha's followers wished to stir up quarrels with the śramaṇas, even to the point of saying, 'Your teacher is not a Tathāgata, not an arhat, not one of right and universal awakening.' For this reason he came and told me. World-Honored One! At that time I said to Cunda: 'Because the Buddha, the World-Honored One, is not among the assembly, non-Buddhists wish to stir up quarrels. But you at this time must not engage in quarrels with them. If quarrels arise, you will not be able to benefit many people, and will cause many people to produce various sufferings, and even all the assemblies of gods and humans will gain no benefit and will all produce suffering.'"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "What reason did you see to say, 'The Buddha is not among the assembly, and non-Buddhists stir up quarrels'?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! At one time, I saw two bhikṣus whom the Buddha had praised. These two bhikṣus were skilled in maintaining the Vinaya and proceeded in orderly deportment, practicing in one place. When I saw them, I said to them: 'You two bhikṣus should not display such deportment. The Buddha is not among the assembly, and if non-Buddhists see such display, they may stir up quarrels. If quarrels arise, many people, and even all the assemblies of gods and humans, will gain no benefit and will all produce suffering.' I recall this event, and therefore I spoke to Cunda in this way."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "What do you think about this?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I cannot fully understand this matter."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Through my own wisdom I have realized correct awakening, and in the interim I have widely taught various dharmas, namely the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right exertions, the four bases of supernatural power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, and the noble Eightfold Path. These and other dharmas I have proclaimed, all known and seen. Ānanda! Many non-Buddhists stir up quarrels with the bhikṣu assembly. Can you understand the causes of these quarrels?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I cannot."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "There are bhikṣus whose morality sometimes increases and sometimes decreases, whose deportment sometimes increases and sometimes decreases. Ānanda! If morality and deportment increase and decrease, one loses the constant Dharma. Wherever one goes, non-Buddhists see this and stir up quarrels. If quarrels arise, many people, and even all the assemblies of gods and humans, gain no benefit and all produce suffering. Why? Ānanda! The non-Buddhist Nirgranthas are not omniscient and not truthful; in all places and all views they are impure. Therefore they constantly delight in stirring up quarrels. Ānanda! You should know that when quarrels arise among the bhikṣu assembly, there are various roots of quarreling."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Excellent, World-Honored One! Excellent, Well-Gone One! Now is the right time. May the Buddha proclaim the roots of quarreling, so that the bhikṣus, having heard it, may remember and always be free from faults."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Listen carefully! Apply right attention and remember well; I shall now teach you. Ānanda! If a bhikṣu gives rise to anger and resentment, because of anger and resentment they do not generate respect, reverence, or similar feelings toward their teachers and elders, nor are they able to serve and make offerings to them. Because they do not respect their teachers and elders, they do not see the Dharma. Because they do not see the Dharma, they do not observe correctly within the bhikṣu assembly. Because they do not observe correctly, quarrels arise. Because quarrels arise, many people, and even all the assemblies of gods and humans, gain no benefit and all produce suffering.

"Furthermore, there are bhikṣus who internally and externally reckon things as truly existing, making fixed observations, accumulating wrong attention, delighting in evil and extremely evil mental fabrications, energetically pursuing wrong exertion, producing wrong and false speech, and giving rise to quarrels. With such wrong mindfulness and incorrect knowledge, being out of accord, they cannot extinguish the causes of quarrels.

"Ānanda! Such things are the roots of quarreling, and therefore produce various sufferings. The causes of suffering are these: first giving rise to anger and resentment, and from this further giving rise to concealment, deceit, flattery, jealousy, miserliness, shamelessness, lack of moral dread, incorrect knowledge, attachment to views, and various wrong and false memories. Because of these various evil causes, one does not generate respect, reverence, or similar feelings toward teachers and elders, nor is one able to serve and make offerings to them. Because one does not respect teachers and elders, one does not see the Dharma. Because one does not see the Dharma, one does not observe correctly within the bhikṣu assembly. Because one does not observe correctly, quarrels arise.

"Ānanda! As previously stated, such causes are all roots of quarreling. For these reasons, when quarrels arise, there are seven methods for settling disputes. As I have taught, Ānanda, whatever disputes have arisen or have not yet arisen can all be settled and extinguished. What are the seven? They are: the face-to-face procedure, the procedure of recollection, the procedure for one recovered from madness, resolution by confession, resolution by majority, resolution for the blameworthy, and covering over as with grass. These are the seven.

"What is the face-to-face procedure for settling disputes? Ānanda, this means: one person teaches the Dharma and Vinaya to one person, one person teaches two, one person teaches many, one person teaches the great assembly: thus the face-to-face procedure has four aspects. Two persons teach the Dharma and Vinaya to one person, two teach two, two teach many, two teach the great assembly: thus the face-to-face procedure has four aspects. Many persons teach the Dharma and Vinaya to one person, many teach two, many teach many, many teach the great assembly: thus the face-to-face procedure has four aspects. The great assembly teaches the Dharma and Vinaya to one person, the great assembly teaches two, the great assembly teaches many, the great assembly teaches the great assembly: thus the face-to-face procedure has four aspects. This is called the face-to-face procedure for settling disputes. Through this method, disputes can be settled and extinguished.

"What is the procedure of recollection for settling disputes? Ānanda, suppose a bhikṣu has committed an offense but does not remember it. Other bhikṣus say to him: 'You have committed such-and-such an offense. You should recollect it. Go before the great assembly, seek the assembly's compassion, and perform the procedure of recollection.' That bhikṣu then enters the assembly, seeks the assembly's compassion, and performs the procedure of recollection. The great assembly, following the Great Teacher's instruction, performs the procedure of recollection for him. When that bhikṣu has been released from the offense, the dispute is settled and extinguished. This is called the procedure of recollection for settling disputes. Through this method, disputes can be settled and extinguished.

"What is the procedure for one recovered from madness for settling disputes? Ānanda, suppose a bhikṣu is insane, with a disordered mind and afflicted by suffering. Though he may have heard much, he cannot practice in accord. Regarding the Dharma he has heard, he contrarily claims it was wrongly taught, and having said this, he leaves the assembly. Later, that bhikṣu recovers his original mind. Other bhikṣus say to him: 'You committed such-and-such an offense. You should enter the assembly, seek the assembly's compassion, and perform the procedure for recovery from madness.' That bhikṣu enters the assembly, seeks the assembly's compassion, and performs the procedure for recovery from madness. The great assembly, following the Great Teacher's instruction, performs the procedure for recovery from madness for him. When that bhikṣu has been released from the offense, the dispute is settled and extinguished. This is called the procedure for recovery from madness for settling disputes. Through this method, disputes can be settled and extinguished.

"What is the resolution by confession for settling disputes? Ānanda, suppose a bhikṣu does not understand the characteristics of an offense, yet claims broad knowledge and understanding, and further declares before the assembly: 'Venerable ones! Gains are difficult to obtain.' Other bhikṣus say to him: 'You do not understand the characteristics of offenses, yet you claim broad knowledge and understanding. You have committed such-and-such an offense. You should go before the assembly, seek the assembly's compassion, and confess your offense.' That bhikṣu enters the assembly, seeks the assembly's compassion, and confesses of his own accord. The great assembly, following the Great Teacher's instruction, applies the resolution by confession. When that bhikṣu has been released from the offense, the dispute is settled and extinguished. This is called the resolution by confession for settling disputes. Through this method, disputes can be settled and extinguished.

"What is the resolution by majority for settling disputes? Ānanda, suppose two bhikṣus dwelling together suddenly have a dispute arise between them, producing various words and extensively arguing, each insisting on their own position. Some say 'this is the Dharma,' some say 'this is not the Dharma'; some say 'this is the Vinaya,' some say 'this is not the Vinaya'; some say 'this is an offense,' some say 'this is not an offense.' When the dispute arises between these two bhikṣus, if they can settle it, that is good. If they cannot, the two bhikṣus leave their original dwelling and argue in another place. Having left, if they can settle it on the way, that is good. If they cannot, then many bhikṣus together work to settle the dispute: some analyze and explain based on the sūtras, some analyze and explain based on the Vinaya, some analyze and explain based on the Mātṛkā. Because many people analyze and explain, the dispute between the two bhikṣus is settled and extinguished. This is called the resolution by majority for settling disputes. Through this method, disputes can be settled and extinguished.

"What is the resolution for the blameworthy for settling disputes? Ānanda, suppose a bhikṣu has committed an offense and knows of the transgression. He may tell another person or he may not, but having reflected on it himself, he goes to another bhikṣu, removes his sandals, bares one shoulder, kneels on his right knee before that bhikṣu, and states his own name and clan three times: 'I have committed such-and-such an offense and dare not conceal it. I come to you, Venerable one, seeking compassionate repentance. I pray the Venerable one will bestow joy and forgiveness.' The bhikṣu then accepts the repentance. When that bhikṣu has been purified, the other says: 'Do you see the characteristics of this offense?' The bhikṣu answers: 'I have seen the characteristics of this offense.' The other says: 'You should henceforth maintain the Vinaya as it should be.' The bhikṣu answers: 'I shall henceforth maintain the Vinaya as it should be.' This is said three times. This is called the resolution for the blameworthy for settling disputes. Through this method, disputes can be settled and extinguished.

"What is the covering over as with grass for settling disputes? Ānanda, suppose many bhikṣus are dwelling together and mutual quarrels arise, splitting into two parties. In one party, the senior members gather with senior members, those who know the Dharma gather with those who know the Dharma, and the leaders gather with leaders. In this party, a bhikṣu, for the sake of material gains, stirs up a dispute. Having stirred up the dispute, he addresses his own assembly: 'At a certain layperson's house, gains are easily obtained. I spoke there according to Dharma and Vinaya. Other bhikṣus, for this reason, quarreled with me. Venerable ones! If because of this matter I have committed an offense of quarreling, I pray the Venerable ones will forgive me this offense. Even my intentional visit to the layperson's house, I ask you to hear my repentance and pardon, since it was a minor transgression. From now on I shall not do such things. If I do, I shall seek compassionate repentance before the Venerable ones. I will not dare to conceal anything I have done.' When that bhikṣu makes this repentance, only one bhikṣu in his own party does not permit the repentance. That bhikṣu then goes to the other party, removes his sandals, bares one shoulder, and proceeding from the most senior elder, inquires after each in order. He then returns before the senior elder, kneels on his right knee, joins his palms, and addresses the assembly: 'Venerable ones! At a certain layperson's house, gains are easily obtained. I spoke there according to Dharma and Vinaya. Other bhikṣus, for this reason, quarreled with me. I sought repentance in my own assembly as prescribed, but one bhikṣu in my assembly would not permit the repentance. Therefore I have come here to seek compassionate repentance. Venerable ones! If because of this matter I have committed an offense of quarreling, I pray the Venerable ones will forgive me this offense. Even my coming from the layperson's house, I ask you to hear my repentance and pardon, since it was a minor transgression. I now seek compassionate repentance before the Venerable ones. I will not dare to conceal anything I have done. I pray the Venerable ones will permit my repentance and bestow joy and forgiveness.' The assembly then permits the repentance. When that bhikṣu has been purified, the senior elder says: 'Do you see the characteristics of this offense?' The bhikṣu answers: 'I have seen the characteristics of this offense.' The senior elder says: 'You should henceforth maintain the Vinaya as it should be.' The bhikṣu answers: 'I shall henceforth maintain the Vinaya as it should be.' This is said three times. In the other party also, just as in this party, the senior members gather with senior members, those who know the Dharma with those who know the Dharma, and the leaders with leaders. In that party also, a bhikṣu for the sake of material gains stirs up a dispute, and the process of question and answer up to the second and third time is fully as described above. Ānanda! When those bhikṣus, knowing their own offenses, go back and forth confessing and repenting, and upon meeting each other show respect and greet one another, the causes of quarreling are extinguished, all arguments are stopped, and there is no longer any matter about which to make distinctions. This is called the covering over as with grass for settling disputes. Through this method, disputes can be settled and extinguished. Ānanda! These seven methods for settling disputes, you bhikṣus should remember them.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! There are six practices of harmony. Listen carefully, apply right attention, and remember well; I shall now teach you. What are the six? First, in bodily action, one practices compassionate and harmonious deeds, always cultivating the brahma conduct purely before the Buddha, respecting and reverencing the correct Dharma, practicing in accord with principle, and dwelling in harmony with the bhikṣu assembly. This is called the practice of bodily harmony. Second, in verbal action, one speaks compassionate and harmonious words without any contention. This is called the practice of verbal harmony. Third, in mental action, one gives rise to compassionate and harmonious thoughts without any opposition. This is called the practice of mental harmony. Fourth, whatever Dharma benefits and worldly gains one receives are shared equally. If one goes with bowl in hand to beg from place to place, whatever food and drink one obtains, one reports it to the assembly and shares it equally with the assembly, not using it privately in secret. If the assembly knows, then it is in accord with the brahma conduct. This is called the practice of harmony in sharing gains. Fifth, regarding the precepts, they are neither broken nor interrupted; the power of the precepts is firm, free from defilement, pure. Knowing the right time and place, universally and equally, one is worthy to receive the food offerings of donors. Pure precepts are cultivated together, known together, and practiced together in the brahma conduct. This is called the practice of harmony in precepts. Sixth, when one sees the noble wisdom that leads to realization and the path of liberation, reaching the complete end of suffering, and when one has truly seen such characteristics, one practices together, knows together, and cultivates the brahma conduct together. This is called the practice of harmony in views. These are the six practices of harmony.

"Ānanda! As previously taught regarding the roots of quarreling, you bhikṣus should cut them off. Regarding the seven methods of settling disputes, you should understand them fully, so that whatever disputes have arisen or have not yet arisen can all be settled and extinguished. Then together you should cultivate the six practices of harmony. If you bhikṣus practice in this way, then in the east, west, south, and north, wherever you go, whether walking or resting, you bhikṣus will all obtain peace and happiness, free from all disputes. Even after my nirvāṇa, you bhikṣus will likewise be able, in all places, to always obtain peace and happiness, just as when I am presently in the world teaching the Dharma and guiding sentient beings, without any difference."
