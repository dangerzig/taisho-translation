# 佛說諫王經
## Sūtra of Admonishing the King, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0514

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Juqu Jingsheng (沮渠京聲)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the king, named Prasenajit, had been out touring the borders of his kingdom, and on his way back passed by the Buddha's dwelling. His body was covered with dust. He removed his sword, put aside his parasol, and made obeisance to the Buddha. The Heavenly Honored One said: "Be seated." The king sat.

The Buddha asked the king: "Where have you come from, Your Majesty, that your body is covered with dust?" The king moved his seat back, prostrated, and replied: "Indeed! I have been touring the kingdom's borders where there have been calamities and strange occurrences."

The Buddha told the king: "Your Majesty, you should govern with the correct Dharma, without losing due measure. Always nurture the people with a compassionate mind; by this you will attain sovereignty. To be a king, all comes from wholesome deeds performed in past lives. In administering the affairs of the people, you must not be partial or unjust. If the ministers, officials, and all the common people harbor grievances, if the king's governance is not equitable and the whole realm is resentful, then upon death the soul will always enter the Great Mountain Hell. Though you repent afterward, it will be too late. If the king governs the country with equity and due measure, the ministers and people will praise his virtue, and all within the four seas will be of one heart. The devas, nāgas, and spirits will all hear of the king's goodness. Upon death, he will ascend to the heavens, and there will be no regret thereafter. Your Majesty, do not be fond of lust and licentiousness, which bring ruin upon yourself. Do not, with an angry mind, harm or destroy anything. You should accept the admonitions of loyal ministers who are forthright and upright. When speaking with others, always be broad-minded and gentle, and do not scorch them with heat.

"You should treat the people of the country with four attitudes. What are the four? Give provisions in a timely manner. Speak to them with a harmonious mind. Share your treasures with the people. Look after the aged, the sick, and the orphaned and widowed. If Your Majesty acts in this way, the country will be at peace, and you will obtain the corresponding merit. At the end of your life, you will ascend to the heavens, and your wishes will be naturally fulfilled. Your Majesty, you cannot always remain in a position of freedom. Though people respect and fear you and take this as happiness, the prized elephants, fine horses, jeweled carriages, worthy ministers, the multitude of officials who escort you before and behind, the treasures within the inner treasury, and the hundred goods in the storehouses: all will decay, and none will endure.

"Youth inevitably leads to old age; the strong and healthy will certainly fall ill. All who have blood must die. Treasures, wives, children, family within and without cannot be kept forever. It is like a person who in a dream sees palaces, fine gardens, trees, flowers, fruits, pools, and flowing springs, and sports within them with boundless delight. Upon waking, all vanishes; one does not know where it has gone. All that exists in the world is like a person's dream.

"Has Your Majesty seen a tree with flowers and fruit? The flowers and fruit cannot stay on the tree forever. The green, green leaves will wilt and fall. Heavenly crowns, headbands, embroidered robes, and fine garments cannot always remain beautiful. Flowing water cannot always be full. A fire set on the open plain blazes with fierce flames, but before long it dies. Violent wind, sudden rain, thunder and lightning: in a brief moment, they vanish. When the sun is about to rise, the stars lose their brilliance. The blazing sun illuminates the world, but before long comes darkness. Impermanence in the world is just like this. Consider: if from the four directions, great mountains of stone came, above and below, and six mountains converged and simultaneously crushed together, then among the people and creatures and all beings with blood within, whether noble or lowly, all would be ground to pieces.

"There are four things that cannot be stopped: old age arrives and the body withers; sickness comes and the mind is tormented; the body dies and the spirit departs; and all that one possesses in treasures must be abandoned and cannot be kept. When even one of these four arrives, it cannot be escaped; there is no place to flee. It is not something the mouth can beg, plead, or talk one's way out of, nor can it be bought off with riches or resolved by entreaty. At that time, the prized elephants, fine horses, treasures, strong soldiers, ministers, officials, and escorts before and behind: which of them can push these aside for the king? Has Your Majesty seen a lion, the mightiest of beasts? When from afar it sees a herd of deer and wishes to seize its prey, it pounces, snatches, tears, and devours the flesh. Can such pain be described in words? Death is like the lion seizing the deer. When a person's life is about to end, the body is not at ease. The blood and veins waste away, the complexion changes. As the days grow short, the five organs fail and one does not desire food or drink. Though there be mantras, good physicians, and excellent medicine, none can bring a cure. The mouth speaks falsely; whatever one asks for, the family gives. The whole body is in pain, as if being tortured. Hands and feet flail, the joints feel as though they will come apart. The mouth is dry, the breath is strained, the body is emaciated and utterly feeble, unable to rise or sit without another's help. Even when given good medicine, porridge, or delicacies, someone must place them in one's mouth. The suffering must reach its extremity: the sinews and veins are about to rupture. There is only outgoing breath and no returning breath. The lips are parched and cracked. The vital energy is exhausted, and foul energy takes its place. The tongue gradually retracts; the face and eyes lose their color. The ears and nose close up; one can no longer hear sounds or smell fragrances. Hands and feet become rigid, the sinews tighten. The mouth is clenched; one wishes to speak but cannot. The hands grasp at the air, searching beside oneself. Cold sweat and tears stream forth without ceasing. The mind and awareness are fixed on pain; consciousness gradually fades and is extinguished, and there is no more knowledge. The warmth departs, the body grows cold, and the soul is gone. All the treasures, parents, brothers, wives and children, family within and without, acquaintances, servants: all must be abandoned. Alone one departs, following one's deeds, not knowing where one will arrive. Though the world has its pleasures, one cannot stay long.

"Your Majesty, at that time, what will you have to rely on? Only filial devotion, compassionate care of your parents, serving the lofty and pure śramaṇas, and when you see ordinary elderly people, showing them reverence. Share your treasures with the people in a spirit of common joy. With a compassionate mind, bestow generosity upon the people. Do not, by slanderous words, harm and destroy people's lives. The proper way for a king is to proclaim the noble path, to teach the people to do good, to guard a single-pointed mind, and to keep the mind fixed on the Three Honored Ones. If Your Majesty acts in this way, the noble ones will praise you. The devas, nāgas, and spirits will protect your kingdom. In life you will have glory and honor; in death you will ascend to the heavens.

"When the body dies and the spirit departs, what will you have to rely on? Only good deeds. When a fire blazes fiercely, one relies on water to extinguish it. The hungry and thirsty rely on water and grain. The aged rely on their staff. The blind rely on those who can see. In the dark one relies on a lamp. The severely ill rely on good physicians and medicine. A ship at sea amid fierce wind and waves relies on oars and rudders. When bandits line the road, one relies on a hiding place. When the body dies and the spirit departs, one relies on the cultivation of good, just as when one encounters each of these dangers, one relies on the corresponding means to rescue oneself and bring safety to the family. Your Majesty, do not take pleasure in eating delicacies to the full, or in traveling at your leisure, thinking these can always be had. Satiation will give way to emptiness; it cannot always be had. Fine fragrances to anoint the body, filling the nostrils with their scent; pearls and jeweled necklaces dazzling the eyes; fine flowers from land and water as ornaments; garments of golden thread: white woolen robes, embroidered robes, multi-colored robes, finest robes, thin cotton robes, delicate robes, brocade and silk robes: all these are impermanent and cannot endure.

"Palace towers and high terraces, splendid halls and mansions; beds and frames of gold, silver, and the seven treasures, with fine carpets and cushions of the softest silk to ease the body; curtains of the seven treasures woven with brocade and embroidery; columns, beams, halls, and doors carved with elaborate patterns; burning various incenses while sporting within: all these are impermanent and cannot endure.

"Zithers, pipes, flutes, and various instruments gathered together; singing and dancing performances filling the ears with beautiful sound and describable delight: these too are impermanent, like illusions and dreams that cannot endure. Elephants, horses, and jeweled carriages, a dazzling spectacle: when the king goes forth, bells are struck and drums beaten, escorts before and behind. The king rides in a canopied chariot with attendants holding banners, with kingfisher feathers and bustard plumes, their shafts carved with patterns, to sweep away the dust. The roads are paved and cleared, with vermilion-painted railings. All the people who look on are filled with awe. Fine flowers and famous incense are all brought to welcome the king. They chant blessings of ten thousand years. Yet this too is hard to preserve.

"Has Your Majesty seen a person at the point of death? All the family, within and without, gather around, beating their chests and crying out to heaven: 'What can be done?' Sobbing and choking, tears streaming in torrents: 'Alas! How painful! The spirit departs alone. You abandon us, and what shall we do?' All who hear it are grief-stricken; all who see it are moved to sorrow. The body is carried out of the city and left in the open field. Birds and beasts snatch and eat it. The worms inside the body eat its flesh in turn. The sun scorches and the wind dries the bones. The kings of old, in all their honor and magnificence, resplendent and powerful, were just like Your Majesty, yet today they have vanished and are seen no more. These are the clear proofs of impermanence. It has always been so since ancient times; how much more so today. Your Majesty, reflect deeply on this. Do not think of lust and licentiousness. Do not accept flattery and convict the innocent. Accept loyal admonitions and govern with due measure. Fear the harsh torments of the hells. All creatures with blood all crave to live; you should not kill them."

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the king's mind was immediately awakened, and he wished to become a disciple. He then received the five precepts, touched his forehead to the ground, and made obeisance to the Buddha.
