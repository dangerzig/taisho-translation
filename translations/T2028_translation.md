# Sūtra on the Bhikṣu Kātyāyana's Account of the Coming Transformations

## *Jiādīng Bǐqiū Shuō Dānglái Biàn Jīng* (迦丁比丘說當來變經)

Taishō 2028

Translator unknown.

*Note: This is a vivid prophecy of dharma-decline (saddharmavipralopa) attributed to the bhikṣu Kātyāyana (here called Jiādīng). It presents a harrowing vision of the future corruption of the Buddhist saṃgha: monks who pursue wealth and power, abandon meditation and vinaya, forge alliances with secular authorities, exploit donors' offerings, engage in commerce and family life, and persecute those who genuinely keep the precepts. The text prophesies three kings who will destroy kingdoms and persecute the saṃgha, followed by a final great assembly at Kauśāmbī where the last monks gather to recite the Prātimokṣa. At this assembly, an arhat named Sudāsravā (Xūtuóliú) is murdered by a violent monk, and a dharma-protecting yakṣa kills the murderer in turn, triggering cosmic earthquakes, weeping from the gods up to the twenty-eighth heaven, and the final extinction of the Dharma. The text belongs to a genre of Buddhist apocalyptic literature warning practitioners to practice diligently while the Dharma still exists.*

---

At that time the bhikṣu Kātyāyana (*Jiādīng*, 迦丁) addressed the assembly: "All of you, listen quietly to what I now speak. From beginning, middle, to end, my words accord with what the Buddha has taught, without error or discrepancy. In the world to come, there will be evil transformations that are greatly fearsome. If you wish to know, I shall speak of them. The events to come are of great terror. You should diligently increase your cultivation and practice vigor. I have received the Buddha's kindness and now obtained peace and security. All of you who have gone forth should follow the Buddha's teaching."

"When human lifespan is a hundred years, with few exceeding it and many falling short, in the world to come evil dharmas will flourish. Evil bhikṣus will appear and destroy the Buddha-dharma."

"When the Dharma is about to end, people's minds will be weak and feeble, without aspiration or purpose, occupied only with jealousy. They will slander one another, be attached to written words, devoted to paper and ink, using these to glorify themselves, considering this superior. If there are bhikṣus who receive oral transmission from their teachers, who recite and penetrate the sūtras, who analyze and explain the meaning of sentences, and who teach others by distinguishing the divisions of doctrine, they will instead be looked down upon. These two groups of students will quarrel with each other, saying: 'What I teach is right; what you teach is wrong,' all competing for wealth and possessions. Their minds and mouths will contradict each other. Foolish people who do not understand the meaning of the sūtras will explain them in reversed ways. Both teachers and students will be inverted. Their speech will be improper and the sūtras mixed up with stanzas in error. Having studied little, they will harbor only arrogance, looking down on others. In the world to come, such people will be extremely numerous."

"They will be disrespectful toward their teachers and elders, no longer serving them. They will tell their three teachers: 'What I say is true and real; what you say is false and fraudulent.' Such bhikṣus will personally wear lay clothing and practice what laypeople do. They will not delight in quiet, secluded places. In the bustle and noise of human society, they will be covetous and attached, unable to leave it behind."

"Although they build buddha-stūpas and monasteries in human settlements, they will be mutually jealous. When monks from the four directions come to stay, they must report to the monastery head before they can remain. Even if allowed to stay, inwardly they will harbor resentment and displeasure. When such monks leave, all will rejoice together. The bhikṣus dwelling in the Buddha's monasteries will also envy one another: envying wealth, envying good looks, envying offerings, envying noble birth, envying learning of sūtras, hoarding them and not transmitting them. They will chase after nobles and those with power and influence. Their minds will be corrupted, causing the Buddha-dharma to collapse."

"They will accumulate money and property, slaves and livestock, cultivate gardens and orchards, considering these of the highest importance. Inside and out they will have changed. They will force themselves into the kaṣāya robe and shave their heads, that is all. Day and night they will assiduously attend to government affairs. They will chase after ministers, officials, and those with power and influence, serving them like slaves."

"The households of donors, out of reverence for the Three Treasures, will deprive their own wives and children, not daring to feed or clothe them, in order to build buddha-stūpas and monasteries, provide monks' quarters, robes, bedding, and everything needed in abundance. Still fearing insufficiency, they will take out their household's most precious treasures and place them in the stūpas. The śramaṇas dwelling in those monasteries will chase after officials and dignitaries, seeking their respect, and will take what the donors placed in the stūpas and offer it to the officials. The officials, covetous of wealth, will not investigate the source, thinking it good to receive more. Because of such greed and jealousy, after death they will fall into hell. Those bhikṣus, greedy for fame and profit, will take the property of the Three Treasures and falsely call it acts of kindness. Because of this sin, after death they will fall into hell."

"Some bhikṣus will commit many evils with their own bodies, and use the money of the Three Treasures to bribe others, seeking release from punishment. Some bhikṣus and laypeople, greedy for the wealth of the Three Treasures, will forcibly seize it and carry it away. All such people will enter hell."

"Those who serve as dharma-masters, vinaya-masters, or masters of the four Āgamas will each form intimate friendships with laypeople who have power and influence. They will sit on laypeople's seats, whisper in confidence, gossiping about others' good and bad qualities. Relying on laypeople and depending on their power, whoever displeases them they will endanger and harm. When they teach the Dharma, they will call the deviant correct and the correct deviant. Those who act in this way are called those who create unlawful divisions in the world."

"The Buddha has the Tripiṭaka. Those who serve as teachers will hoard the sūtras and not transmit them, nor teach their disciples. Why? Because they fear that if their disciples know as much as they do, they will look down on their teachers. Therefore they keep the sūtras secret. As for the disciples: what does it mean to have gone forth? Without learning or knowledge, they have gone forth in vain. This causes the disciples to harbor resentment and anger, to look down upon their teachers, with no more distinction of senior and junior. Their speech becomes shallow and coarse."

"Such bhikṣus will engage in evil conduct. Māra and his retinue will all take advantage of them, and they will fall into evil realms. They will praise themselves, saying: 'Who is my equal?' Their minds will turn to evil; the three poisons will blaze fiercely, and they cannot restrain themselves. Greedy for offerings and donations of wealth, they will not avoid the consequences of sin and merit, thinking whatever they get is good. Because of offerings, they will quarrel with each other. Outwardly wearing the Dharma robes, they will not accord with the rules of conduct. Coming and going, they will not follow the Dharma's teaching. Shaking their heads and admiring their own reflections, they will be infatuated with sensual desire, worse than ordinary people."

"They will buy cheap and sell dear, or lend at double interest, devoted to greed for profit. The dharma of a bhikṣu's conduct they will abandon entirely. When they obtain even a small gain, they will be delighted. If bhikṣus gather for seated meditation or doctrinal study, they will not be able to endure it and will flee far away. Greedy for wealth, they will go out in all four directions seeking it, never feeling fatigued. They will deceive the common people without any satisfaction."

"If there are stūpas, monasteries, monks' quarters, and bedding in prosperous places, they will compete to enter them. Outwardly they will appear to keep the precepts while inwardly harboring deceit. People will see them and pay respect; gradually, day by day, they will pursue laypeople, serving and providing for them. The laypeople, delighted, will in turn look up to them, praising one another and saying: 'This bhikṣu's conduct is pure and perfect.' They do not know that inwardly it is entirely false."

"They will gather many disciples and novices, slaves and servants. They will go in all four directions seeking and accumulating without satisfaction, saying: 'I keep the precepts; my years of ordination are not yet fulfilled,' and so they will lead retinues. The precepts and Dharma will be loosely worn, and they will not believe in the consequences of sin and merit. Their speech will be fierce and aggressive. They will enjoy the company of drunkards and consort with licentious women, conducting business with them. Or they will enter the palaces and inner chambers of officials, coming and going to enrich themselves."

"Or they will join in gatherings of people, delighting in various activities. In a hundred kinds of livelihood, they will gossip about affairs of state, discuss politics, debate military affairs and the outcomes of battles, talk about bandits and thieves, discuss food and drink, or talk about women and desire. Day and night they will discuss worldly and deviant matters. Good teachers and virtuous friends they will refuse to associate with. Instead, they will form close ties with butchers, wicked robbers, drunkards, brigands, traders, and those who engage in sexual misconduct. Drinking and rising together, their minds will gradually become attached and they will adopt those people's behavior."

"Or they will attend to household affairs, or run errands for laypeople regardless of distance. Such people do not know the true Dharma, yet they are made śramaṇas, which is called 'destroying the Dharma.' They are unfilial to parents and elders. If they see bhikṣus who keep the precepts, pure and lofty brahmacārins, they will curse and humiliate them, without any compassion. They will harm living beings, plunder others' wealth, seduce others' wives, engage in false speech. Their chambers will be licentious and disordered, with no distinction of senior and junior."

"If there are pure bhikṣuṇīs, they will pursue them with lustful intent, destroy their precepts and conduct. Those of this sort, in the present life, will be bound and imprisoned by the king's law, subjected to the five punishments."

"If any king, minister, or common person commits such evils, it will cause the wind and rain to be untimely, the five grains not to ripen, the people to be impoverished, bandits to rise everywhere, military campaigns to be constant, the masses to flee in all directions, the five clans to be scattered, and villages and settlements to be emptied."

"At that time, the people of the world, destitute of clothing and food, will become śramaṇas to escape worldly suffering. Having become śramaṇas, they will destroy the Buddha-dharma, disrespect seniors and juniors, gather together evil people, unite in purpose and will, and become companions. They will praise one another, saying: 'That one's precept-conduct is pure; that one practices meditation.' The people, hearing and seeing this, will think it true."

"When donors invite them to chant sūtras and teach the Dharma, they will secretly lie naked [in their quarters] and refuse to attend. But when donations are distributed, they will compete for them. They will seek offerings of robes, bedding, food, and drink without satisfaction. They will gather many disciples but not instruct them in the Dharma. They will not teach the Buddha's correct precepts. The disciples will not understand precept-conduct, meditation, or the method of cultivating the Way."

"Those who are teachers will be occupied with gatherings, discussing worldly and unlawful matters as their regular business. Such people receive others' offerings, but the merit of the giver is small and the receiver incurs misfortune."

"If the four assemblies gather for a sūtra lecture, they will not care to listen. But if laypeople come wishing to hear the sūtras, they will compete to welcome and receive them, providing for all their needs, never failing to please them. If someone speaks of the prohibitory precepts, they will quarrel together, and even past midnight they will say: 'Everyone is tired. Just mention the four [grave offenses].' But if they are attending to laypeople's affairs, they feel no fatigue at all. If the pure precepts or sūtra-dharma are being discussed, they do not wish to hear it."

"Bhikṣus of the future who wish to throw the Dharma into confusion will compete to build buddha-monasteries and hold gatherings everywhere. On the fifteenth day, those who are supposed to recite the Prātimokṣa, though they gather, will only quarrel and not recite the precepts or discuss the Dharma. The devas and people, seeing the saṃgha assembled, will come wishing to hear the Dharma. But they will hear only quarreling, and will say to one another: 'We came for the Dharma, but instead heard quarreling. What is there to seek here?' Displeased in their hearts, they will leave in sorrow, thinking: 'Before long, the Buddha-dharma will be extinguished.'"

"The devas, nāgas, spirits, yakṣas, and all the good ghosts and deities will all be grieved and dejected. They will no longer protect the Buddha-dharma and the saṃgha, but will let evil ghosts suck their blood, making them all sickly, of pallid complexion and diminished strength, their faces withered and devoid of any majestic virtue. All of this is due to such causes."

"If bhikṣus fall ill, they will not care for one another; their hearts will dread and despise the sick, hoping they will die. Then the sick bhikṣu, having no one to care for him, will die. When the Buddha-dharma is about to be extinguished, bhikṣus and laypeople will all be miserly and greedy, accumulating property and refusing to give. They will covet only to receive."

"The monastery stewards will not believe in sin and merit. With no distinction of senior and junior, they will not distinguish between the property of the Three Treasures. They will use it without principle: perhaps sharing it with women, perhaps mingling with laypeople in promiscuous dwelling. They will drink wine, sing, and dance, delighting in mutual entertainment, no different from common people. They will envy each other. Because of this, property will dwindle and enterprises will fail."

"Such bhikṣus, because of lustful desire, will delight in household life and not find it wearisome. They will love to form bonds with young women. Why? The mind of a young woman is especially deep and intense; when she first gets a man, her heart becomes attached and neither wishes to abandon the other. Or [bhikṣus] will live together with licentious women and set up households; or bhikṣuṇīs will become householders."

"If there is a bhikṣu who stores nothing, living on alms alone, all the precept-breaking bhikṣus will join in hating him and not wanting to see him. They will tell the donors: 'This bhikṣu is inwardly deceitful, outwardly displaying precept-keeping. He is not worth giving to.' In the present age, if there is a precept-breaking bhikṣu, all join in despising him. In the future age, if they see a pure precept-keeping bhikṣu, they will instead join in hating him. In the present age, bhikṣus who transgress the precepts in private fear being seen. In the future age, if there is a bhikṣu who upholds the precepts and practices meditation, he will fear being seen by others. Why? Because in the future age, people will hate those who keep the precepts."

"Bhikṣus of the future will be aggressive, greedy for profit, and without shame. Such people, in that foolish age, will all be revered. Seeing those who keep the precepts, people will instead slander and despise them. All the myriad things in the world are treasures, but because people do not recognize them, they vanish. The Buddha-dharma is the same. Because of this, the true Dharma gradually disappears. Because it is not respected, the Dharma is completely extinguished."

"It is like a great ship: the more it is loaded, the heavier it becomes, until it sinks. In the world to come, many bhikṣus, deluded by offerings, will cause the Dharma to be destroyed and sunk. If there are bhikṣus who uphold the precepts and guard the vinaya, the Dharma will endure for a long time."

"It is like a lion king who, though dead and lying on the ground, is approached by no bird or beast. After ten days, worms are born within his body and eat his flesh, destroying his form. Though the Buddha has entered nirvāṇa, the true Dharma continues. Brahmā, Māra, the host of sages, and all heterodox paths cannot destroy the Buddha-dharma. In the future, there will be people of no practice who will enter the Buddha-dharma, seek to become śramaṇas, and destroy the Buddha-dharma, slandering one another. Those who study the Tripiṭaka will mutually envy one another. Because of this envy, the Buddha-dharma will quickly be extinguished. If you wish to protect the Buddha-dharma, you must remove arrogance and abandon jealousy."

"Bhikṣus of the future who are jealous of the Buddha-dharma will be like pigs wallowing in filth, not knowing their own stench. They will bump into everything, like unbridled donkeys. Heedless of the precepts, they will drink wine and eat meat without regard for proper times. They will form intimate friendships, dig through people's walls, plunder their property, accept deposits and then cheat the owners, confirm false documents, and consider it fortunate to gain more."

"When the saṃgha gathers for monastic business, the junior monks will scold and curse the seniors. The seniors, silent and sorrowful, will leave. At that time the junior monks will not restrain themselves with propriety and decorum. Those worthy of being teachers will no longer be accepted."

"When bhikṣus have such quarrels, the Dharma will be near its end. The world will be in turmoil, with universal unrest. Rulers will levy taxes without end. Bandits will rise everywhere, plundering the people's property and turning it over to the king. The king, receiving this wealth, will only encourage the thieves further. Government will collapse; people will kill each other without accountability. The people will be impoverished; deceit and cunning will flourish. The rains will be untimely; the five grains expensive. Royal merchants will trade across borders. Those who plow and plant will reap meager harvests. Day and night they will toil, their bodies and mouths unsatisfied. Unable to pay taxes, the masses will wail in distress, unable to sustain themselves."

"At that time, bhikṣus too will plow fields and go out trading, managing both official and private affairs, no different from laypeople. How deeply lamentable!"

### The Three Kings and the Final Assembly

"In the world to come, three kings will arise and destroy the world. One is named Yēláinà (*Yēláinà*, 耶來那), who will be in the southern region, in the central country. One king will arise in the north, in the land of Jìn. One king is named Jiānqiū (*Jiānqiū*, 揵秋)."

"When the Buddha-dharma is about to end, these three kings will arise in the land of Jìn. They will destroy kingdoms, slaughter the people, demolish stūpas and monastic gardens, humiliate śramaṇas, and subject them to the five punishments. They will also lead armies to invade the land of India, destroying its territory and causing great carnage."

"At that time, the king of the central country will raise armies and destroy the land of Jìn, its ministers and people, and they will return to their own territory. At that time, the śramaṇas of the land of Jìn, pressed by the government, will either die, return to lay life, flee in all directions toward India (some arriving, some not), or be too blind, deaf, lame, emaciated, old, or sick to travel, and will be killed by the government."

"At that time, the king of the central country will revere the Buddha-dharma and the saṃgha, giving generously to all. If he sees precept-breakers, he will admonish them. At that time, there will be a senior bhikṣu named Shīyīqiú (*Shīyīqiú*, 尸依仇), fully versed in the Tripiṭaka, who will teach the Dharma for the king. The king, greatly delighted, will invite the entire saṃgha to the country of Kauśāmbī (*Jūshǎnmí*, 拘睒彌) and hold a Pañcavārṣika assembly, inviting all of Śākyamuni's disciples remaining in Jambudvīpa to gather together."

"At that time, a hundred thousand bhikṣus will attend this final great assembly. It is called the 'final assembly' because the Buddha-dharma is approaching its end and there will be no further gathering."

"At that time, those in the assembly will ask one another: 'Where are your preceptors and ācāryas?' Each will answer: 'Our teachers died of illness on the road; were killed by bandits; were too feeble to continue; some say they arrived.' All will be stricken with grief, raising their voices and weeping aloud."

"At this time, on the night of the fifteenth, there will be a great evil wind and violent rain. During the recitation of the two hundred and fifty precepts, some will hear and some will not. The bhikṣus will then quarrel together, shouting loudly."

"The senior bhikṣu will admonish the assembly: 'Honored ones, speak softly! For the sake of the Dharma, do not speak in confusion. I will resolve your doubts according to the bhikṣu-dharma. Now all of Śākyamuni's disciples in Jambudvīpa have come to this assembly. This is the final assembly. What I have studied I will study no more. I beg you all to be silent and listen to what I say.'"

"Then there will be another bhikṣu, intelligent and wise, deeply attained in dhyāna, who will address the assembly: 'All the meditations I have entered are now fully accomplished. In this great assembly of a hundred thousand bhikṣus, those who wish to hear the Dharma and the precepts and are able to practice them: I shall teach. May all the disciples of Śākyamuni be silent and listen attentively.'"

"Then there will be a bhikṣu named Sudāsravā (*Xūtuóliú*, 須陀流), who has attained arhatship. He will rise from his seat, with single-pointed mind, join his palms, bow at the feet of the senior, and make the lion's roar: 'I wish to speak the sūtras. May the assembly be quiet. What the sūtras teach, I have fully studied it, without error. I shall never again have doubt. As the Buddha has said [about the preeminence of the senior disciple]...'"

"The senior's disciple, named Shàngtóu (*Shàngtóu*, 上頭), who is foremost in rank but violent and evil in disposition, will rise from his seat and say to Sudāsravā: 'What do you know? You do not understand the sūtras, Dharma, or vinaya!' When the senior wishes to speak, they will compete to speak over him."

"At that time, Shàngtóu will take an iron pestle and strike Sudāsravā dead. But Sudāsravā has already crossed beyond birth-and-death."

"Among the assembly, there will be precept-keeping bhikṣus who will silently rise and leave."

"At that time, a dharma-protecting yakṣa who believes in the Buddha will also take an iron pestle and strike the bhikṣu Shàngtóu dead."

"At that moment, the earth will quake in six ways. In the sky, voices will spontaneously cry out in lamentation. From the four directions, thunder will crash and rain will fall. From the four directions, foul vapors will fill the sky, with thunder resounding on all sides."

"At that time, all sentient beings, seeing these portents, will face one another and raise their voices in mournful weeping, all saying to each other: 'Today is the final end. The Buddha-dharma is now extinguished!' Up to the twenty-eighth heaven, the formless heavens, the nāgas, asuras, filling the sky, all will raise their voices in great lamentation, casting their bodies to the ground."

"Then the dharma-protecting rākṣasas and the dharma-protecting yakṣas will all throw their bodies to the ground, crying out these words: 'From this day forth, the world will never again hear the sound of the two hundred and fifty precepts being recited! Bhikṣus will never again practice them! The world is orphaned; beings have lost their eyes. How swift! Alas, the world will not last long!'"

"'People will kill one another with no one to stop them, just like wild beasts. The practice of Dharma has fallen; the Dharma drum has split; the gate of sweet dew has been shut. The Dharma teachers' lives have been extinguished. The Dharma torch has gone out; the Dharma wheel has toppled. The twelve divisions of the canon have been scattered and destroyed. The Dharma wheel has snapped; the Dharma water has ceased. The Dharma ocean has dried up; the Dharma mountain has collapsed.'"

"'In the mountain valleys, there are no more vigorous bhikṣus practicing seated meditation. The devas and good spirits, seeing the mountains and valleys empty with none to revere, will all cast their bodies down [in grief]. The people will be blind and in darkness, with no Dharma to practice.'"

"At that time, all the hosts of Māra will be greatly delighted. Wearing fine robes and ornaments, they will congratulate one another: 'The Buddha-dharma has been extinguished! Our heterodox dharma can now flourish!' They will celebrate together."

The bhikṣu Kātyāyana addressed his disciples: "The evil transformations to come are as I have described. Today, because the Buddha-dharma still continues, it is fitting to practice it diligently."

"The Buddha's true Dharma is like a deep marsh of grasses. Sentient beings depend upon it, lacking nothing. If the marsh dries up, sentient beings will be starved and destitute, falling into birth-and-death. It is like a merchant who is not diligent in managing his affairs: his wealth and goods diminish daily, and he cannot sustain his parents, wife, and children. Body and mind are scorched, and regret is of no avail."

"Now while the true Dharma still exists, it is supremely possible to practice it. Take whatever path you wish. If you are heedless and unwilling to practice vigor, you will go forth in vain, gaining nothing, and be drowned in the three evil destinies. Regret then will be of no avail."

"Now, you four assemblies, think of the Buddha's weighty kindness! Revere your superiors as fathers, cherish your juniors as children. While your four elements are strong and your minds resolute, diligently practice vigor, and you can cross beyond suffering. If instead, one day, body and mind grow feeble, and old age and sickness trample upon you, regret will be of no avail."

"Now the lands and people have not yet reached the extremity of evil; military campaigns have not yet arisen; the people are at peace; grain is plentiful and cheap; alms are easily obtained. Be mindful, practice vigor, and you will have no later regret."

At that time, the disciples, hearing this teaching, all bowed their heads to the ground at the feet of the great sage. Their minds were filled with terror; their bodies trembled. Weeping and sobbing, they said: "In the future world, when the Dharma is completely extinguished, what will be the state of mind of those who witness this world? How could they bear to see it! Today, hearing this account, our hearts are shattered. How will the people of that future world, encountering such evils, not be torn into a hundred pieces in body and mind?"

Then the disciples suddenly reflected together. With utmost sincerity they prostrated on the ground and said with one voice to their teacher: "We are deeply terrified! How can we attain the Way and be freed from this suffering, so as not to encounter such calamity?"

The great sage replied: "The Way is neither far nor near. If you diligently seek it, you will attain it. There is no before or after."

"This sūtra is called the 'Record of the Great Sage Kātyāyana's Prophecy of the Coming Secret Transformations.' Diligently practice vigor, and you can be freed from this suffering."

At that time, the disciples, hearing the sūtra, wept with flowing tears, bowed their heads, and practiced accordingly.
