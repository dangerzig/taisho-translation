## Fascicle 1

### Sūtra on the Eight Contemplations of Aniruddha, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was at Mount Śimaku, beneath the Śiṃśapā trees. The Venerable Aniruddha, sitting in contemplation in the Śūnya Marshes, reflected thus: "The dharma of the path is of few desires; many desires are not the path. The dharma of the path is contentment; dissatisfaction is not the path. The dharma of the path is seclusion; delight in crowds is not the path. The dharma of the path is diligence; laziness is not the path. The dharma of the path is restraint of mind; dissipation is not the path. The dharma of the path is concentration of mind; scattered thoughts are not the path. The dharma of the path is wisdom; the darkness of ignorance is not the path."

The Buddha, with his holy mind, knew these thoughts in advance. Like a strong man who bends and extends his arm in an instant, he flew to Aniruddha's presence and praised him, saying: "Excellent, excellent! Aniruddha! What you contemplate are the contemplations of a great being. Hear my words to you on the eight contemplations of a great being; reflect well upon them and practice them. You should learn the four dhyānas, examining the mind and observing the dharma without interruption, and you will surely gain great benefit and not fail in your aspirations.

"What are the four dhyānas? Having abandoned desires, evil, and unwholesome dharmas, with the mind suffused with joy and happiness—this is the practice of the first dhyāna. Having abandoned evil thoughts and maintaining single-pointed focus, without relying on joy—this is the practice of the second dhyāna. With joy stilled, observing the dharma as it is, perceiving suffering and pleasure—this is the practice of the third dhyāna. Again abandoning suffering and pleasure, with sorrow and joy both severed, abiding in purity—this is the practice of the fourth dhyāna.

"Having learned these, then practice the eight contemplations of a great being. The four dhyānas are the method of examining the mind; the practice swiftly bears fruit, benefit and wishes are quickly attained, and one no longer falls into interruption.

"As for few desires: its meaning is like a king who has a border official who oversees treasure chests filled with fine colored robes, yet you yourself take pleasure in wearing coarse and tattered garments. With few desires and contentment, dwelling in seclusion, with diligence, restraint of mind, concentration of mind, and wisdom—having abandoned the household life, without frivolity or arrogance, without deviation or error—you will surely arrive at safety and reach the gate of nirvāṇa. These are the eight contemplations of a great being.

"For the practice of the four dhyānas, examining the mind and observing the dharma—its meaning is like a king who has a border official who oversees the kitchen and regulates the five flavors, yet you yourself take pleasure in begging for food, obtaining just enough to sustain the body without seeking gratification. Its meaning is like a king who has towers and pavilions for pleasure and viewing, yet you yourself take pleasure in secluded diligence among mountains, marshes, and trees, free from worldly desire. Its meaning is like a king who has a border official who oversees fine medicines as well as milk, ghee, clarified butter, and rock honey, yet when you yourself fall ill you take pleasure in using urine as medicine, obtaining just enough to remove affliction.

"By practicing the eight contemplations, reflecting on the four dhyānas, being diligent without deficiency, with a mind free from deviation and error—you will surely arrive at safety and reach the gate of nirvāṇa."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, he returned to Śimaku and addressed his disciples: "The path requires few desires; do not have many desires. The path requires contentment; do not hoard and accumulate. The path requires seclusion; do not delight in large assemblies. The path requires diligence; do not be lazy. The path requires restraint of mind; do not be dissipated. The path requires concentration of mind; do not have scattered thoughts. The path requires wisdom; do not dwell in the darkness of ignorance.

"A bhikṣu who has few desires is excellent. This means that one personally has few desires without causing others to know of one's few desires—the meaning should accord with this.

"A bhikṣu's contentment means: with respect to one's begging bowl, robes, bed and bedding, and medicine for illness, one is satisfied with what food one obtains and does not hoard or accumulate—the meaning should accord with this.

"A bhikṣu's seclusion means: avoiding human society, not entering large assemblies, dwelling far away among mountains, marshes, rocks, and trees. If there come the four kinds of followers—kings or great ministers—who come to ask about the dharma, one explains purity to them and wishes them to depart quickly. Like a poor person who owes a debt to a wealthy household and is dragged about by the creditor, wishing to leave but finding no pleasure in it, secretly hiding and fleeing far away—the meaning should accord with this.

"A bhikṣu's diligence means: cutting off what is contrary to the dharma, diligently practicing the way of the scriptures without ever growing weary, walking and sitting and lying down in the early, middle, and late watches of the night, constantly wakening the mind and purifying thought so as to remove the five hindrances—the meaning should accord with this.

"A bhikṣu's restraint of mind means: abandoning desires and evil dharmas, sitting in contemplation and observation, cutting through suffering and pleasure, attaining the practice of the four dhyānas—the meaning should accord with this.

"A bhikṣu's concentration of mind means: constantly single-minded, observing the body, observing the mind, observing the dharma, not relying on conditioned practice, gathering the mind toward the path and abandoning thoughts of delusion and affliction—the meaning should accord with this.

"A bhikṣu's wisdom means: knowing the four noble truths—suffering, arising, cessation, and path.

"What is the noble truth of suffering? The suffering of birth, the suffering of aging, the suffering of illness, the suffering of death, the suffering of grief, lamentation, and affliction, the suffering of separation from loved ones, the suffering of meeting with those one hates, the suffering of not obtaining what one desires, the suffering of the five aggregates of clinging. The suffering of birth means: according to one's karma and wherever one falls, one receives a womb and takes form; when born one emerges with body manifest, with the faculties entering, receiving, and growing. Aging means: when a person's faculties ripen and the body changes—hair turns white and teeth fall out, sinews slacken and skin wrinkles, one bends over and leans on a staff. Illness means: what human beings bring about through their evil deeds—carbuncles, sores, festering wounds, epilepsy, madness, and prolonged illness, over a hundred kinds. Death means: the passing of a person's life and the dissolution of the body—warmth departs, breath ceases, and the spirit takes its leave. All of these are suffering.

"What is the noble truth of arising? The mind delights in pleasure and joy, giving rise to affection and love; the will abides in craving, causing further outflows; the various activities flourish, and through attachment one is bound. What is called craving: the eye craves form, the ear craves sound, the nose craves fragrance, the tongue craves flavor, the body craves smooth textures, the mind craves its objects. One merely observes their apparent permanence and pleasant quality and, resting in them, takes them as beneficial—calling them 'mine' and binding oneself through attachment. Because of this, among the five aggregates—form, sensation, perception, volition, and consciousness—craving flourishes; seeing permanence and delighting in pleasure, one calls them 'mine' and binds oneself through attachment. What is called form: the spirit receives the energies of earth, water, fire, and wind, which transform into the body. Through what one loves and is attached to, the eye cognizes form, the ear cognizes sound, the nose cognizes fragrance, the tongue cognizes flavor, the body cognizes smooth textures, the mind cognizes dharmas through attachment—this is confirmed as the noble truth of arising.

"What is the noble truth of cessation? Not receiving and not entering; craving extinguished without remainder; the bonds of attachment already dissolved—as one who sees through wisdom: there is no longer any remainder whatsoever. Worldly people see nothing; they are bound by the five aggregates. When the reckoning is complete and the bonds of craving are wholly dissolved, having seen through wisdom that they are impermanent, suffering, empty, and not-self, they are severed—this is the noble truth of cessation.

"What is the noble truth of the path? It is the eightfold noble path: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right discipline, right livelihood, right intention, right concentration.

"What is right view? Right view has two kinds: worldly and transcendent. Knowing that there is humaneness and righteousness; knowing that there are fathers and mothers; knowing that there are śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas; knowing that there are true persons who have attained the path; knowing that there is the present life and the next life; knowing that there are good and evil, offense and merit—with this conduct as evidence as one passes from here to there: this is worldly right view. Having understood the four noble truths—suffering, arising, cessation, and path—having attained vision through wisdom, seeing emptiness, purity, and not-self: this is the right view of the path.

"Right thought also has two kinds: thinking of learning and inquiry, thinking of harmony and respect, thinking of precept and carefulness, thinking of non-harm—this is worldly right thought; thinking of renunciation, thinking of patient silence, thinking of extinguishing craving and ending attachment—this is the right thought of the path.

"Right speech also has two kinds: not speaking with a forked tongue, not speaking abusively, not speaking falsely, not speaking frivolously—this is worldly right speech; departing from the four offenses of the mouth, speaking the words of the dharma, with the mind not fabricating, completely without remainder—this is the right speech of the path.

"Right action also has two kinds: performing good bodily actions, speaking good words, holding good mental intentions—this is worldly right action; with body and mouth diligent, with mind contemplating emptiness and purity, dissolving and extinguishing all attachment—this is the right action of the path.

"Right discipline also has two kinds: not killing, stealing, or engaging in sexual misconduct; not being arrogant; cultivating virtue and guarding oneself—this is worldly right discipline; departing from the three bodily evils, cutting off suffering and its arising, extinguishing craving and seeking liberation—this is the right discipline of the path.

"Right livelihood also has two kinds: seeking wealth through the path without greedy or improper gain, without deceiving people—this is worldly right livelihood; departing from wrong livelihood, abandoning worldly divination and prognostication, not violating the prohibitions of the path—this is the right livelihood of the path.

"Right intention also has two kinds: not being jealous, not being angry, not following wrong views—this is worldly right intention; departing from the three mental evils, practicing the four right strivings, pure and unconditioned—this is the right intention of the path.

"Right concentration also has two kinds: with a nature that is pure and harmonious, guarding goodness and settled in security, with a mind that is not crooked—this is worldly right concentration; attaining the four mental foundations, contemplating emptiness, the signless, and the wishless, seeing the source of nirvāṇa—this is the right concentration of the path. This is the noble truth of the path.

"When a bhikṣu abandons the household life, renounces affection and love, rests in stillness and reflects on the path with nothing yearned after, the mind not following desire, pure and without obstruction—this is the dharma of the path; the meaning should accord with this."

The Venerable Aniruddha, having heard the Buddha's explanation of the sūtra, with his mind opened and illuminated, received and practiced it. In three months his āsravas were exhausted and his mind was liberated; he attained the three knowledges as confirmation. Having realized he had become an arhat, he then spoke these verses:

> One given to desire without satisfaction, delighting in crowds with a dissipated mind—
> Such practice leads to suffering; cultivating evil, one is bound to much.
> Few desires, knowing the practice of the path, knowing shame and not parading oneself—
> This dharma leads to purity; departing from evil, one attains liberation from the world.
> The mind of the path does not cling to life, nor does it delight in death and parting—
> With a concentration like empty space, all sufferings can be endured in their time.
> Having received the Buddha's teaching and charge, guarding practice and abandoning desire and evil,
> The afflictions of the body already cast aside, gaining benefit, one enters the unconditioned.
> Having myself arrived at the three knowledges, the root of affection and love already pulled out—
> At the settlement of Vaiśālī, in the bamboo grove, I shall enter parinirvāṇa.
