# 佛說老母女六英經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha Concerning the Old Woman and Six Glories

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0560

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

The Buddha is the World-Honored One, his virtuous merit towering and immense. With compassion he thinks of all sentient beings, sorrowful on their behalf. At this time, accompanied by his disciples and great sages, he stopped at a place where pleasant music sounded and gave forth extensive teachings.

There was a woman, impoverished, old, and hunchbacked. She knelt before the Buddha and asked: "The five aggregates and the six deteriorations have assembled in my body. Who is responsible for all of this? From where do they come? To where do they return? I pray that the World-Honored One will reflect on this for me!"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! You should understand how subtle this is. As for the causal conditions of all phenomena, those who truly understand are rare. It is like making fire with a drill: two pieces of wood are rubbed against each other. The fire does not come from the drill, nor does it come from the fire-stick. The fire emerges from between them, blazing brilliantly and brightly; it then burns the wood until the wood is utterly consumed and nothing remains. Likewise, when a drum is beaten, its sound is mournful and stirring. The sound does not come from the leather, nor does it come from the drumstick. All phenomena are like this: they arise through mutual causal conditions. Likewise with rain from the sky: wind, clouds, thunder, and lightning all come together to make rain; it is not solely due to the power of the dragon. All phenomena are like this; the teaching is also like this. It is like a painter who blends and mixes colors, applies paint to a plain surface, and no form comes about without the combining of causes and conditions; nothing arises through a single force alone."

When the woman heard this teaching, she was overjoyed and deeply moved. She immediately attained dharmakṣānti, and her body was no longer exhausted.

Ānanda then spoke up: "Who is this woman? She questioned the Buddha about the origin of all phenomena in birth and death, and her mind opened and her understanding was resolved, so that she immediately attained the true path."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Listen to what I say. In a past age there was a Buddha named Krakucchanda. At that time this woman was my own mother. When I went forth to practice the path, she, out of attachment and longing, shut herself away; in her grief and longing she did not eat for an entire day. Because of this bond of love and attachment, she was poor for five hundred lifetimes. Now that I have attained Buddhahood, all blessings have arrived in full, and innumerable sentient beings stand pure before the Buddha. After sixty billion kalpas have passed, she will attain Buddhahood. Her name will be Sarva, the name of her land will be Many Flowers, and the name of the kalpa will be Lichan. The people of that time will have food and clothing like those in Trāyastriṃśa Heaven; each will live for one kalpa without hardship or toil."

At that time the woman, along with gods, nāgas, and yakṣas, heard the Buddha's teaching and were all greatly joyful. Eighty-seven thousand people all attained the aspiration for the true and correct path, paid homage to the Buddha, and departed.

---

**Translator's notes:** The "six deteriorations" (六衰) in the woman's question refers to the six sense-spheres understood as sources of decline and suffering, parallel to the five aggregates (五陰, *pañcaskandha*). The Buddha named Krakucchanda (拘樓秦) is the first of the seven past Buddhas and the twenty-eighth in the longer reckoning. The "dragon" (龍, *nāga*) controlling rain reflects standard Chinese Buddhist cosmology. The future Buddha-name Sarva (薩婆) renders a Sanskrit term meaning "all" or "everything"; the land-name "Many Flowers" (多華) and kalpa-name Lichan (禮禪) appear to be either transliterations or abbreviated translations of Sanskrit originals not recoverable from this text alone.
