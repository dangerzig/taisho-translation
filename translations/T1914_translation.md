# General Meaning of Calming and Contemplation

## *Zhǐguān Dàyì* (止觀大意)

Taishō 1914

Composed by Zhànrán (*Zhànrán*, 湛然) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a condensed summary of the Tiāntái system of meditation and doctrine by Jīngxī Zhànrán (711-782 CE), the ninth patriarch of the Tiāntái school and its most important systematizer after Zhìyǐ himself. The text was written at the request of the court official Lǐ Huá, who wished to know the "general meaning" of calming and contemplation (zhǐguān). Zhànrán provides a remarkably compressed synopsis of Zhìyǐ's monumental Mohe Zhǐguān (Great Calming and Contemplation, T1911), the foundational Tiāntái meditation manual of over 300 fascicle-pages. Zhànrán covers: the Tiāntái lineage from Nāgārjuna through Huìwén, Nányuè, and Zhìyǐ; the "five-fold profound interpretation" (wǔchóng xuánjiě) used for all sūtras; the "ten meanings" (shíyì) for doctrinal exegesis; the overall structure of the Mohe Zhǐguān (the first two fascicles giving a brief outline, fascicles three through ten the detailed practice); the four kinds of samādhi; the twenty-five preparatory conditions (in five groups: five prerequisites, five desires to be rejected, five coverings to be abandoned, five activities to be regulated, five practices to be cultivated); and the ten objects of contemplation (the "ten-vehicle" method). For the ten vehicles, Zhànrán explains how they apply differentially to practitioners of superior, middling, and inferior faculties, with superior practitioners needing only the first (contemplation of the inconceivable object) and inferior practitioners requiring all ten. The text is an invaluable introduction to Tiāntái meditation theory.*

## The Tiāntái Doctrinal and Contemplative System

I shall briefly describe the gates of teaching and contemplation in general outline. Our school's doctrinal lineage begins with Nāgārjuna as its founding patriarch. Huìwén merely practiced inner contemplation of seeing and hearing, nothing more. When it reached Nányuè [Huìsī] and Tiāntái [Zhìyǐ], through the Lotus Samādhi, they opened the dhāraṇī, expanded the gates of meaning, and perfected the methods of contemplation. In elucidating sūtras, all are explained through the five-fold profound interpretation and the ten meanings of comprehensive integration. For the contemplative method, the five preparatory stages and the ten vehicles of practice are employed.

### The Five-Fold Profound Interpretation

The five-fold interpretation is applied to the opening of every sūtra: (1) explaining the name, (2) discerning the essence, (3) clarifying the purport, (4) distinguishing the function, and (5) classifying the teaching. For all sūtras before the *Lotus Sūtra*, the five-fold interpretation is coarse. Upon reaching the *Lotus Sūtra*, all five are wondrous.

### The Ten Meanings of Doctrinal Exegesis

(1) First, clarify that the principle of the Way is quiescent, beyond expression, inconceivable. Within the single quiescent principle, without dividing it, one distinguishes and opens the various truths: the four kinds of four truths, the seven kinds of two truths, the five kinds of three truths, and so on. (2) The teaching that expresses [this principle]. (3) Resolving contradictions among sūtras and treatises using the four siddhānta. (4) Skillfully breaking attachments. (5) Establishing correct dharma-gates, correlating practice stages. (6) Unrestricted application of any single statement, horizontally and vertically. (7) Organizing chapters and sections. (8) Phrase-by-phrase commentary on the sūtra text. (9) Translation of technical terms. (10) For each phrase, integrating principle-contemplation so that the contemplation accords with the sūtra.

## Structure of the Mohe Zhǐguān

The ten fascicles of the *Mohe Zhǐguān* are broadly divided into two parts. The first two fascicles briefly explain the essential framework. The latter eight fascicles extensively clarify the aspects of practice.

### The Brief Outline (Fascicles 1-2)

The brief section opens into five: (1) arousing the perfect aspiration, (2) cultivating perfect practice, (3) realizing the perfect fruit, (4) arising the eight teachings, and (5) returning to the three virtues.

**Arousing the perfect aspiration** (Fascicle 1): This is explained through the four universal vows (*sìhóngyuàn*), the four noble truths, and the six identities (*liùjí*). The four vows are the pledges that are aroused; the four truths are the objects upon which they depend; the six identities are the stages through which one passes. The four vows are:

- "Sentient beings are boundless; I vow to save them all" (based on the truth of suffering).
- "Afflictions are inexhaustible; I vow to sever them all" (based on the truth of the origin).
- "Dharma-gates are infinite; I vow to know them all" (based on the truth of the path).
- "The Buddha-way is supreme; I vow to accomplish it" (based on the truth of cessation).

The six identities: (1) identity in principle, (2) identity in name, (3) identity in contemplative practice, (4) identity in resemblance, (5) identity in partial realization, and (6) ultimate identity. Because of "identity," first and last are both [the same]; because of "six," first and last do not overlap. The principle is the same, hence "identity"; the stages differ, hence "six."

**Cultivating perfect practice** and the remaining four sections are in Fascicle 2. Perfect practice comprises the four kinds of samādhi that encompass all practices: (1) constantly sitting (based on the *Mañjuśrī Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra*, also called the single-practice samādhi, ninety days per session); (2) constantly walking (based on the *Pratyutpanna Samādhi Sūtra*, also called the samādhi of the Buddha standing before one, ninety days per session); (3) half-walking, half-sitting (based on the *Lotus Sūtra* and *Vaipulya Sūtra*); (4) neither-walking-nor-sitting (also called "following one's own intention," applicable during all four deportments and all activities).

---

### The Detailed Practice (Fascicles 3-10)

Fascicle 3 explains the name of calming and contemplation, discerns its essence, shows how the essence encompasses all dharmas, and determines which dharmas are partial and which are perfect.

**Fascicle 4: The Twenty-Five Preparatory Conditions** (in five groups):

(1) *Five prerequisites:* sufficient clothing and food (to remove anxiety); pure precepts (to remove causes for the evil paths); quiet dwelling (to remove bustle); ceasing all extraneous affairs; having a good spiritual friend.

(2) *Five desires to be rejected:* form, sound, smell, taste, and touch.

(3) *Five coverings to be abandoned:* lustful desire, anger, drowsiness, agitation-and-regret, and doubt.

(4) *Five activities to be regulated:* body, breath, mind (the three inner adjustments); sleep and food (the two outer adjustments).

(5) *Five dharmas to be practiced:* aspiration, mindfulness, diligence, skillful wisdom, and single-mindedness.

---

### The Ten Vehicles of Contemplation (Fascicles 5-10)

The correct contemplation consists of ten methods. Without these ten, it is called "a broken donkey-cart." Although all ten are constantly perfect, practitioners have three levels of capacity:

- **Superior faculty:** only the first method is needed.
- **Middle faculty:** two or up to seven methods.
- **Inferior faculty:** all ten are needed.

**1. Contemplation of the inconceivable object** (for superior faculty practitioners):

The object contemplated is the aggregates, elements, and sense-bases, which do not go beyond form and mind. Form is created from mind and is entirely mind. As the sūtra says: "The three realms have no other dharma; it is only the one mind that creates." This creating mind fully possesses all dharmas: contaminated, uncontaminated, neither contaminated nor uncontaminated; causes, effects, neither cause nor effect. As the sūtra says: "Mind, Buddha, and sentient beings: these three are without difference."

In each and every mind there are all minds; in each and every mote of dust there are all motes of dust; in each and every mind there are all motes of dust; in each and every mote of dust there are all minds; in each and every mote of dust there are all lands. All dharmas, all motes of dust, all lands and bodies: their essence is clearly present yet without self-nature. Having no self-nature, they have always followed things in transformation. Therefore they mutually interpenetrate, yet phenomena are always distinct.

To contemplate in this way is called contemplating the nature of mind. Following conditions yet unchanging, it is called "nature." Unchanging yet following conditions, it is called "mind." As the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* says: "To be able to contemplate the nature of mind is called the supreme concentration. The supreme concentration is called the first meaning. The first meaning is called Buddha-nature. Buddha-nature is called Vairocana."

If a person of superior faculty practices this single contemplation, horizontally and vertically encompassing all, they immediately recognize the signless amid signs clearly present, break through fundamental ignorance, and ascend to the first abiding (*bhūmi*).

**2. Arousing the compassionate mind:** If contemplating the object does not yield awakening, one adds the arousal of aspiration.

**3. Stabilizing the mind:** Using the dharma-realm as the object of stabilization, using quiescence and illumination as the means.

**4. Breaking dharmas universally:** Using the gate of non-arising to universally break all delusions, then extending this to all other gates.

**5. Recognizing the open and obstructed:** Knowing that birth-and-death and afflictions are "obstructed" and bodhi and nirvāṇa are "open," and further recognizing that obstruction can arise within the open (which must be broken) and openness within the obstructed (which must be preserved).

**6. Adjusting the path-factors:** Trying all seven categories of path-factors (the four foundations of mindfulness, four right efforts, four bases of power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold path) and adjusting them to suit one's practice.

**7. Counteractive aids:** When the main practice does not succeed, applying remedial practices such as the six pāramitās to counteract the six heavy coverings (miserliness, immorality, anger, laziness, distraction, and delusion).

**8. Knowing the stages:** To prevent the pride of thinking one has already attained what one has not, one must know the stages and positions clearly.

**9. Patient endurance:** When entering the outer stage of ordinary practice, one faces the external temptation of fame and profit and the internal arising of past karmic obstructions. One must endure without being moved.

**10. Abandoning dharma-attachment:** Having attained the resemblance-stage with the mutual functioning of the six faculties, having broken both [types of] delusion and permanently free from falling into suffering, one must not cling to this attainment. Clinging to the resemblance-stage is called "falling from the peak." Abandoning this attachment, one enters the copper-wheel [stage], the ten abodes, and manifests in a hundred worlds.

---

From Fascicle 5 through Fascicle 7, the text describes the correct practice from the initial aspiration through the first abiding. From Fascicle 8 onward, it describes the arising of latent habitual patterns (afflictions, illness, karmic impressions, māra-events, dhyāna-states, and views) during contemplation, and how to handle them with the ten-vehicle contemplation.

---

The contemplative method of our school and the stages for entering the Way differ somewhat from other accounts, because they are grounded in the sūtras and form actual practice-stages. Following inner contemplation of the Way, outwardly supported by the gates of doctrine: if one practices accordingly, one will certainly not pass through in vain. Even if in this life one does not attain [the fruit], the seeds planted will be strong.

The original text runs to over three hundred pages. This summary is unavoidably incomplete in many places. Although I have humbly complied with the stern command, I truly fear I have missed the profound intent of the Great Master. For all that is lacking, I dare to hope for generous forbearance.
