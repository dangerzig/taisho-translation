# 佛說舍衛國王十夢經
## Sūtra on the Ten Dreams of the King of Śrāvastī Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0147

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the monastery. At that time, there was a king named Prasenajit, who dreamed of ten things during the night. First, he saw three cauldrons: the two on either side were boiling with steam passing back and forth between them, yet it did not enter the empty middle cauldron. Second, the king dreamed of a horse that was eating from its mouth and also eating from its rump. Third, he saw large trees bearing flowers. Fourth, he saw small trees bearing fruit. Fifth, he saw a person cutting a rope, with a goat behind the person eating the rope. Sixth, he saw barbarians sitting on gold and silver beds, eating from gold and silver vessels. Seventh, he saw a large cow nursing from a small calf. Eighth, he saw a herd of bulls coming from all four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight; just as they were about to clash, the bulls were nowhere to be found. Ninth, he saw a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges. Tenth, he saw a stream of water flowing bright red. When the king awoke from his dream, he was frightened, fearing for the loss of his kingdom and his life. The next morning, he summoned his attendants, ministers, and various non-Buddhist practitioners: "Last night I dreamed of ten things. Who can interpret them?" One of the non-Buddhist practitioners said: "I can interpret them, but I fear the king will not be pleased to hear it." The king said: "Speak, according to what you know." The non-Buddhist practitioner said: "The king will lose his kingdom and his life." The king was greatly alarmed. Displeased, the non-Buddhist practitioner said: "It can be averted. You should kill the crown prince and the honored queen, and all the treasury's contents should be burned as a sacrifice to heaven. If the king can do this, he may be spared."

When the king heard the practitioner's words, he was filled with worry, alarm, and sorrow. He withdrew into his purification chamber to reflect on the matter. The king had a queen named Māllī, who came to the purification chamber and asked: "Why has the king entered the purification chamber during the daytime? Your complexion is troubled and you are so worried. Who has offended the king?" The king replied: "Do not ask about this matter. It would only frighten you. It is not something you would understand." The queen said: "I am part of the king's own body. In urgent matters, you should tell me." The king then told his queen: "Last night I dreamed of ten things. I asked the non-Buddhist practitioners to interpret my dreams, and they said such things. I am extremely frightened and do not know what to do." The queen said: "Do not worry! It is like a person buying gold who rubs it on a touchstone to see its quality. The Buddha is now nearby at the monastery. You may go and ask the Buddha. All calamities and portents can then be understood."

The next morning, the king summoned his attendants and ministers, several thousand in number, and went to where the Buddha was. He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet. The king said: "Last night I dreamed of ten things. I beseech the Buddha to interpret them for me." The Buddha told the king: "Do not worry! What the king dreamed pertains only to transformations of the future age."

The Buddha said: "The king dreamed of three cauldrons, the two on either side boiling with steam passing back and forth, yet not entering the empty middle cauldron: in the future age, people will no longer provide for the poor, their relatives, or the lonely and destitute. Those who are both wealthy will exchange gifts among themselves. The king's first dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of a horse eating from its mouth and also from its rump: in the future age, high ministers and officials will draw provisions from the government and also take from the people, both the honest and the dishonest. The king's second dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of large trees bearing flowers: in the future age, people not yet thirty years old will have white hair growing on their heads. The king's third dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of small trees bearing fruit: in the future age, women not yet fifteen years old will marry and carry children, without any sense of shame. The king's fourth dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of a person cutting a rope with a goat behind eating the rope: in the future age, when a husband is away on service or traveling on business, his wife will bring another man to share her bed and consume the husband's wealth. The king's fifth dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of barbarians sitting on fine gold and silver beds, eating from gold and silver vessels: in the future age, the noble will become base and the base will become noble. The base will sit in the seats of honor and eat the finest delicacies. Gentlemen will eat chaff and bran, while petty men will eat fine grain. The king's sixth dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of a large cow nursing from a small calf: in the future age, a mother will act as a matchmaker for her daughter, bringing another man to share the daughter's room, while the mother guards the door and takes the earnings to sustain herself. The king's seventh dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of a herd of bulls coming from all four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight, but just as they were about to clash the bulls were nowhere to be found: in the future age, people will be licentious and greedy, keeping many wives. Kings, ministers, common people, and elders will all rejoice greatly. Clouds will gather as if rain is coming, but in a moment they will each scatter and disperse. At that time, seeing such portents, the rain spirits will seek to make people fear the prohibitions of heaven and earth, so that they will stop their licentiousness and greed, keep to one wife, and have compassionate hearts, removing harm to their wives. The king's eighth dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges: in the future age, people within the central lands will not respect their elders, while those of the border countries will respect the young. The king's ninth dream pertains precisely to this. The king dreamed of a great stream of water flowing bright red: in the future age, high ministers and officials will not know contentment in their desires. They will raise armies and gather troops to attack one another, killing the people until blood flows bright red. The king's tenth dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm. These dreams all pertain to events of the future age to come."

The king then knelt and said: "Having received the Buddha's teaching, my heart is immediately filled with joy. It is like a person holding a small vessel to receive oil: when the oil is too much and the vessel too small, one seeks a larger vessel. Obtaining the larger vessel and pouring the oil into it, one is immediately at ease and free from fear." The king then bowed his head twice, placed his head at the Buddha's feet, and departed. He returned to his palace, bestowed additional rewards upon his chief queen, and confiscated the stipends of all the ministers and officials. He never again trusted the words of the brāhmaṇas.
