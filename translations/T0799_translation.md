# 佛說略教誡經
## Sūtra on the Brief Admonition Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0799

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, the Garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with an immeasurable assembly of bhikṣus.

At that time, the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "You should know this! Within my Dharma there is the matter of living with few desires and contentment. My disciples shave their heads, dye their robes, and carry their bowls from house to house, sustaining themselves by begging for food; this is something that the foolish people of the world hold in contempt. If there is a faithful son of good family who renounces the household life and goes forth, practicing in this manner; not because he is compelled by danger from kings, not out of fear of bandits, debt, terror, or inability to survive, but solely because he has aroused the aspiration to turn away with revulsion from birth, old age, sickness, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and distress; wishing to cut off the aggregates of suffering and the bondage of the afflictions to their very end, and seeking liberation; is it not for this very reason that you have gone forth?"

The bhikṣus then said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! It is so, it is so! We have gone forth seeking liberation."

"You bhikṣus! There is a certain class of wicked bhikṣus who, although they have gone forth, are by nature given over to greed and defilement. They become deeply attached to the objects of the five sensual pleasures; they give rise to ill will and generate unwholesome thought-impulses; their minds are perpetually distracted and they do not apply themselves with diligence; they are constantly filled with deluded thoughts and do not cultivate the gateway of meditative concentration; they pursue external objects and delight in inferior matters; they do not aspire to superior practice, and in the end they attain nothing.

"To what may such a wicked person be compared? Bhikṣus, hear this parable. It is like a piece of wood from a funeral pyre in an open field: both ends have been burned and the middle is fouled with impurity; this wood is of no use to either the people of the village or the people of the open fields. I now use this simile for that class of lazy and foolish persons who have gone forth, having abandoned the various pleasures of worldly life, yet who likewise do not cultivate the virtuous aims of the śramaṇa life. They constantly generate three kinds of unwholesome reflection: reflection on the five sensual pleasures, reflection on ill will and harm, and reflection on deception and fraud.

"From what do these three kinds of unwholesome reflection arise? Know that they all arise with ignorance as their cause. At the dissolution of the body and the end of life, such a one falls into the three evil destinations.

"Therefore, you should diligently cultivate practice and eradicate ignorance. I am the Teacher; you are my disciples. I have now spoken for you the essential and concise matters of my teaching. Out of great compassion, out of tender care, for your benefit, and for your supreme happiness; as I have spoken, so should you practice. Whether dwelling in mountain forests, in araṇya hermitages, beneath trees, or in the open air; reflect on this carefully, do not be heedless, and do not let regret arise in your minds at a later time! Practice as I have taught, and you shall attain liberation."

When the World-Honored One had spoken these words, the assembly of bhikṣus rejoiced and respectfully put them into practice.
