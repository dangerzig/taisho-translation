# 佛說演道俗業經

## The Sūtra on Expounding the Path and Worldly Occupations

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0820

Translated from the Chinese. Western Qin dynasty, translated by Shèngjiān (聖堅).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the state of Śrāvastī, in Jetavana, the park of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty monks, innumerable bodhisattvas, the fourfold assembly, and a gathering of devas, nāgas, spirits, and asuras. At that time, Anāthapiṇḍada and five hundred householders came forth from the city of Śrāvastī, went to where the Buddha was, prostrated at his feet, and sat to one side. With palms joined, he asked the Buddha: "For those who dwell as householders and manage wealth, how many grades are there? For those who leave home to cultivate the path, how do their practices differ? What dharma should one follow to swiftly attain the unsurpassed, true, and correct path? And how should one best transform sentient beings?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent question! This opens the way for those in darkness and for students of generosity to come."

The Buddha said: "There are three grades of wealth: first, lower wealth; second, middle wealth; third, upper wealth. What is lower wealth? There are people who manage property and accumulate money, yet dare not spend on food or clothing, do not cultivate the sūtras and precepts, cannot filially serve and provide for their parents, take no pleasure in giving timely support to their wives and children so that they are well-nourished and satisfied, and provide their servants and laborers with clothes that barely cover their bodies and food that merely fills their bellies. They embrace ignorance and cling to their possessions like bees guarding their honey. They do not believe in the ancient sages, do not honor worthy ones, śramaṇas, or practitioners, and take no joy in generosity or the cultivation of merit. They think of themselves as permanent, never considering that retribution will come, that what comes together must scatter, and that fortune and misfortune follow of themselves. They covet their worldly position, unaware of distress and resentment, and in an instant pass away and enter the gates of hell. Their bodies depend on food, and the four great elements are vigorous yet poisonous; their spirits merely lodge within, given names by convention. They are weak like a transformation, fragile and insecure. Not understanding impermanence, they rely on worldly glory; their hearts harbor ten thousand worries, yet they think they will live forever. They cling to self and mine, not comprehending that all is empty. The three realms are themselves unreal; how much more so people and things? Hurrying and confused, they are swept away by greed, lust, and jealousy.

"Those who practice as follows constitute the middle grade of wealth: they serve and provide for their parents with peaceable and sincere hearts; their words upon leaving and returning never lose their gentle expression; they attend to their parents morning and evening with care and reverence, mindful of the boundless kindness of their two parents. They provide for their wives and children with timely food and clothing; affection flows between them and they share in joy. Wives and children treated thus will never act improperly. They look after servants, relatives, and laborers, never letting them go hungry. Yet they do not believe that after death they will be born again; they think death is annihilation, a return to formlessness. They serve their parents out of gratitude for the kindness of nursing and nurture, they provide for wives and children from attachment to affection, and they care for servants to gain their labor. But they cannot revere śramaṇas and practitioners, and refuse to do good deeds or bestow merit. In the future, however, they will receive blessings that set them apart from the multitude. This is called middle wealth."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Ever mindful of nursing and nurture,
> Filially serving both parents,
> Providing for wife and children
> In timely fashion without neglect;
> To servants and laborers
> Giving comfort, never cruelty,
> So those below all obey
> And when sent on tasks do not resist.
> They do not believe in future birth;
> Hearing of it, they are startled and displeased,
> Reckoning that the body is permanent,
> Existing forever and never perishing.
> The three realms are like magical illusions;
> You should recognize the meaning of these words:
> The merits and demerits you create
> Follow from their origins and are received."

The Buddha further told the householder: "The occupation of upper wealth means that a person who possesses resources is able to clothe and feed themselves, filially serve their parents without missing the proper seasons, always attend to their expressions so they harbor no sorrow, avoid transgressing the precepts when going out and not violate propriety when coming in, keep their conduct pure and unstained, respectfully honor their elders and show deference to the wise, learn from those of broad knowledge with an equitable and unbiased mind, and extend support to those who are lowly, poor, and distressed. They generously provide for wife and children, keeping them well supplied; remove wrong thoughts and govern with uprightness; give measured care to servants so they lack nothing; and never beat or curse them, but treat them with compassion. They revere the ancient sages, accomplished scholars, upright persons, and śramaṇas of wisdom who practice in accord with the Dharma. Day and night they practice propriety without failing to be mindful, give what is lacking so others may accomplish merit and virtue, freely expound the sūtras and also transform the ignorant. With skillful means and never missing the right moment, they bring peace to themselves and protect all sentient beings. Just as a cow eats grass and produces milk, milk produces curds, curds produce butter, and butter produces ghee, the finest and most subtle of all, so too such persons bring peace to themselves and compassion to the ten directions, showing abundant loving-kindness and bringing abundant peace, so that all devas and humans receive deliverance. Such a person is the most honored, unsurpassed, without equal, without peer, the great hero of the world, walking alone and without companion."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "If one has various occupations of wealth
> And thereby clothes and feeds oneself well,
> Providing for and honoring parents,
> Never losing a pleasant expression;
> Going out, one does not violate the precepts,
> Returning, one does not breach propriety;
> Conduct is always pure and clean,
> Following the Dharma without straying.
> Offering respect to elders,
> Showing deference to the wise,
> Learning from those of broad knowledge,
> With an equitable mind, not craving what is wrong.
> Timely providing for wife and children,
> Each obtaining their proper share,
> Compassionately giving to servants,
> Clothing and food always plentiful.
> Revering śramaṇas and scholars,
> Making offerings of generosity and support,
> Receiving the deep and wondrous Dharma from them,
> Casting off the ignorance of the blind and deaf.
> Showing compassion for people of the ten directions,
> Not acting solely for oneself,
> Always bringing peace to oneself,
> And also resolving all afflictions.
> Like butter and ghee
> Originally coming from grass,
> Able to bring peace to the body,
> The body at ease, free of disease.
> With universal compassion for all beings,
> The heart always impartial,
> Through these four immeasurable practices,
> One swiftly attains supreme buddhahood."

The Buddha told the householder: "Renunciant practice has three grades: first, śrāvaka; second, pratyekabuddha; third, Mahāyāna. What is the śrāvaka? Fearing suffering and loathing the body, they contemplate the hardship of immeasurable births and deaths and the affliction of cyclic existence. They regard the body as an enemy, the four great elements as poisonous snakes, and the five aggregates as bandits. They sit in meditation, count their breaths in ānāpāna practice, and contemplate the foul and impure form of the body. They fear the origin of desire in form, and they dread sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness; they dread the suffering of the hells, the distress of hungry ghosts, the entanglements of the animal realm, the difficulties of the human realm, and separations in the heavens, all incalculable, cycling without end like prisoners in a jail. They wish to sever the toil of birth and death and seek the peace of unconditioned nirvāṇa. But they act only for themselves, with no thought for sentient beings. They constantly maintain a small compassion and do not arouse great pity. They rely on sound and do not understand the wisdom of emptiness. The three realms are like illusions, yet they merely seek to save themselves without concern for kindness and compassion. This is the śrāvaka's learning."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Fearing immeasurable birth and death
> And the hardship of cyclic existence,
> Their hearts are filled with dread,
> Wishing only for their own peace.
> Sitting in meditation counting breaths,
> With concentrated intent on ānāpāna,
> Contemplating the foulness within the body,
> The various kinds of impurity.
> Abandoning the form of the three realms,
> Cutting off desire to gain their peace,
> Unable to cultivate great compassion,
> They aspire only to the joy of nirvāṇa."

The Buddha told the householder: "The pratyekabuddha originally aroused the great aspiration for the bodhisattva's work of generosity, morality, patience, vigor, concentration, and wisdom, yet with expectant thoughts they seek to become exalted, honored above and below the heavens, with all taking refuge in them, endowed with the thirty-two marks and eighty excellent characteristics, with majestic spiritual power, towering and imposing, surpassed by none. They do not understand that the Tathāgata manifests his physical form because people in the world are deluded and do not recognize the great path; he cuts off the stream of birth and death, but cannot reverse the source, and so he exhausts the root of birth and death by manifesting a body adorned with excellent characteristics, using words and teachings to transform the deluded and reveal great illumination. Yet they cling to the excellent marks, thinking them truly real. Although they practice the four immeasurables, four kinds of gratitude, and the six perfections, and contemplate the twelve links of dependent arising seeking to uproot their source, they do not understand that fundamentally there is nothing. They aspire to the great path, yet even if they were to accumulate merit as vast as the realm of space, they could not reach buddhahood. Why? Because they do not penetrate. What is meant by not penetrating? In generosity, morality, patience, vigor, concentration, and wisdom, and in the four immeasurables and four kinds of gratitude, they have expectations. One should aspire to rescue all beings in the five destinies from birth and death, understand emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness in all dharmas, and comprehend that all dharmas are like illusions, transformations, dreams, mirages, shadows, echoes, plantain trees, and bubbles, all without inherent existence, and that the wisdom of the path is formless, equal to empty space, neither increasing nor decreasing, universally delivering all beings."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Originally arousing the bodhisattva's aspiration,
> Aspiring to the great Mahāyāna work,
> But only wishing to possess a buddha's body,
> Not understanding that there is nothing to grasp.
> Generosity, morality, patience,
> Vigor, meditation, and wisdom,
> The four immeasurables, gratitude, six perfections,
> Seeking only their own unconditioned peace;
> Admiring the thirty-two marks,
> The eighty excellent characteristics, towering and majestic,
> Honored above and below the heavens,
> Freed from the five aggregates and six declines.
> They only examine the coarse aspects,
> Unable to observe the deep and subtle;
> Though wishing to deliver the ten directions,
> Their minds and mouths contradict each other.
> Not understanding that all is like illusions,
> Bubbles, foam, and mirages,
> Plantain trees, dreams, and shadows,
> Their false thoughts are very many.
> Even if they perform meritorious deeds
> As numerous as the sands of the Ganges,
> With hearts aspiring to the unsurpassed truth,
> They do not know how to vanquish the māras."

The Buddha told the householder: "Those who study the Mahāyāna arouse the aspiration for unsurpassed, true, and correct awakening. They practice great loving-kindness as vast as empty space and cultivate great compassion without partiality. They do not worry about their own bodies but think only of the five destinies, wishing to bring peace to all sentient beings. They cultivate the four immeasurable minds of loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimous protection. They practice benevolence, love, benefit, righteousness, and impartial aid, rescuing all in the ten directions. Through generosity, morality, patience, vigor, concentration, and the limitless perfection of wisdom, without any expectation, they give to all classes of sentient beings. Contemplating the cyclic coming and going in the three realms, the toil and hardship beyond all reckoning, they think of all beings as a father, a mother, a child, or as their own body without any difference, and shed tears for them, wishing to deliver them from affliction to the great path."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Arousing the great unsurpassed aspiration,
> Practicing kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity,
> With great pity vast as empty space,
> Acting impartially and without preference;
> Establishing virtue not for oneself,
> But giving only for the ten directions,
> Delivering all living beings,
> Bringing them to the wisdom of the great path.

"Furthermore, there are four things that bring one to the Mahāyāna: first, practicing generosity to relieve all who are destitute; second, not discriminating between noble and lowly, and not having thoughts of favoritism; third, in all that one gives, having no expectations and not seeking repayment; fourth, dedicating this merit to all sentient beings."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Giving generously to relieve the poor,
> Without thoughts of high or low,
> With aspiring wisdom free of expectation,
> Not seeking to receive repayment;
> Showing compassion for all beings
> Coming and going in cyclic existence,
> Dedicating this merit to all,
> Leading them entirely to the great path."

The Buddha told the householder: "Upholding the precepts has four aspects that swiftly bring the Mahāyāna: first, guarding mouth and body so the mind does not think of wrong; second, in all coming and going, never failing in propriety; third, not wishing to be reborn as a heavenly being, a wheel-turning king, Śakra, or Brahmā; fourth, dedicating these precepts to benefit all sentient beings."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always guarding body, mouth, and mind,
> With a heart as firm as a great mountain;
> In all going out and coming in,
> Never once failing in propriety;
> Not wishing for birth in the heavens,
> As Śakra, Brahmā, or a wheel-turning king,
> But taking this upright practice
> To benefit all people."

The Buddha told the householder: "Patience has four aspects that swiftly bring the Mahāyāna: first, when cursed, not minding the sounds; second, when struck, regarding it as if directed at something formless; third, when humiliated, regarding it as wind blowing past; fourth, when harmed, always harboring great compassion."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "When struck or cursed, remain silent,
> Reflecting that the body is fundamentally formless;
> If thoughts of resentment arise,
> The mind immediately returns to stillness.
> With harmonious heart and pleasant expression,
> All people will show respect;
> Through this one attains buddhahood,
> The radiance of the thirty-two marks."

The Buddha told the householder: "Vigor has four aspects: first, day and night upholding the Dharma without ever being lax; second, preferring to lose one's life rather than violate the teachings of the path; third, diligently chanting the profound scriptures without weariness; fourth, broadly seeking to rescue all who are in danger and distress. These are the four." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Day and night upholding the great Dharma,
> Never once forgetting;
> Preferring to lose one's own life
> Rather than daring to violate the path's teachings;
> Chanting and studying the profound sūtras
> Without ever growing weary;
> Rescuing all who are in danger and distress,
> Without letting the heart harbor resentment."

The Buddha told the householder: "Meditation has four aspects: first, delighting in diligent cultivation in quiet solitude; second, stilling body, mouth, and mind so they are not disturbed; third, even amid a crowd, always being able to compose oneself; fourth, the mind being vast and open, attached to nothing."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always delighting in diligent cultivation,
> Aspiring to quiet solitude;
> Stilling body, mouth, and mind,
> Never thinking of commotion;
> Even in the midst of clamor and confusion,
> The mind is settled without sudden change;
> With one-pointed mind seeing the ten directions,
> The wisdom of the path gives rise to supernatural powers."

The Buddha told the householder: "Wisdom has four aspects: first, understanding that the body is empty, a combination of the four great elements which, when scattered and destroyed, has no master or name; second, all that is born in the three realms is produced by the mind, and the mind is like an illusion that conjures up various forms; third, knowing that the five aggregates fundamentally have no abode, and only through attachment do such feelings arise; fourth, comprehending that the twelve links of dependent arising have no fundamental source, but arise through the encounter of conditions. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Fully understanding that the body is empty,
> A combination of the four great elements,
> When scattered it has no abode;
> It arises from the mind.
> The five aggregates fundamentally have no root,
> Named only through attachment;
> The twelve links have no beginning;
> Understanding this, one reaches great peace."

The Buddha told the householder: "Wisdom further has six aspects: first, understanding that form is like a heap of foam; second, comprehending that sensation is like a water bubble; third, knowing that perception is like a mirage; fourth, understanding that formations are like a plantain tree; fifth, discerning that consciousness is like an illusion; sixth, knowing that the mind-spirit is like a shadow or an echo, and reckoning it to the origin, all is empty, entirely without any abode."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Understanding form as a heap of foam,
> Sensation as a water bubble,
> Perception as a mirage,
> Formations as a plantain tree,
> Comprehending consciousness as an illusion;
> In the three realms nothing is good.
> Discerning all as empty and nonexistent,
> Then one reaches the great path."

The Buddha told the householder: "Loving-kindness has four aspects: first, extending loving-kindness to the ten directions; second, like a mother nurturing her child; third, showing the utmost pity and care; fourth, regarding all as one's own body without any difference. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Extending loving-kindness to the ten directions,
> Like a mother nursing her infant,
> Always harboring the utmost pity,
> Regarding all as oneself without difference."

The Buddha told the householder: "Compassion has four aspects: first, showing pity; second, shedding tears for their sake; third, wishing to take their punishment upon one's own body; fourth, offering one's life to save them.

"Sympathetic joy has four aspects: first, a harmonious expression; second, kind words; third, expounding the sūtras; fourth, explaining the meaning.

"Equanimous protection has four aspects: first, teaching others to abandon evil and pursue good; second, constantly instructing and guiding others to take refuge in the Three Jewels; third, causing others to arouse the aspiration for the path; fourth, transforming all sentient beings. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Showing pity and shedding tears for them,
> Wishing to take their punishment upon oneself,
> Offering one's life to save them
> Without harboring resentment.
> With harmonious expression and kind words,
> Expounding the Dharma and explaining its meaning,
> Teaching others to abandon evil and pursue good,
> Guiding them to take refuge in the Three Jewels."

The Buddha told the householder: "There are four dharmas that swiftly bring the unsurpassed, true, and correct path: first, understanding emptiness, learning without seeking anything; second, signlessness, without any expectation; third, wishlessness, not yearning for any place of birth; fourth, always equalizing the work of the three vehicles, with no past, future, or present. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Understanding emptiness, seeking nothing,
> Signless, without expectation of reward,
> Not yearning for any place of birth,
> Always equalizing the practice of the three times."

The Buddha told the householder: "There are four dharmas that swiftly bring the path of buddhahood: first, all things are fundamentally pure; second, comprehending that the myriad phenomena are entirely like magical transformations; third, birth, death, and cessation all arise from conditions; fourth, reckoning those conditions to the source, they too are fundamentally without form."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "All things are fundamentally pure;
> Comprehend phenomena as magical transformations;
> Birth and death arise from conditions,
> And those conditions are fundamentally without form."

The Buddha told the householder: "There are six dharmas that swiftly bring perfect awakening: first, the body always practices loving-kindness, without enmity or binding; second, the mouth always practices loving-kindness, expounding the meaning of deep wisdom; third, the mind is kind, benevolent, harmonious, and gentle, showing compassion for the ten directions; fourth, guarding the precepts without producing thoughts, seeking the work of the Mahāyāna; fifth, with right contemplation seeing the ten directions as empty, the path and worldly life being non-dual; sixth, providing food to those in need and rescuing those in danger and distress. These are the six."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The body always practices a compassionate heart,
> Never once forming enmity or binding;
> The mouth constantly cultivates words of compassion,
> Expounding the deep meaning of wisdom.
> The mind is harmonious, benevolent, and gentle,
> Showing compassion for all the ten directions;
> Guarding the precepts without arising thoughts,
> With right contemplation, the ten directions are empty."

The Buddha told the householder: "There are four things that swiftly bring the path of buddhahood: first, practicing vigor without attachment to anything; second, teaching and transforming sentient beings so that the mind of the path is never broken; third, traveling through birth and death without aversion or weariness; fourth, great loving-kindness and great compassion that never abandon skillful wisdom. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Vigorous without attachment,
> Teaching and transforming without ever ceasing,
> Neither averse to nor weary of birth and death,
> Never abandoning skillful wisdom."

The Buddha told the householder: "Transforming sentient beings has four aspects: first, for those who do not believe in birth and death, using present evidence of fortune and misfortune to instruct them; second, for those who do not believe in the Three Jewels, revealing the great path; third, for those confused and following wrong paths, pointing them to the three vehicles, explaining that the Buddha's path is uniquely honored and without equal; fourth, explaining that all that exists in the three realms is entirely like magical transformations, with nothing truly real. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "For those who don't believe, show the fortune and misfortune of birth and death;
> For those fallen into wrong views, reveal the great path;
> The Buddha's path is uniquely honored and without equal;
> The three realms are entirely empty, like magical transformations."

The Buddha told the householder: "Transformation further has seven aspects: first, teaching the miserly to practice generosity; second, instructing the wrongdoer to uphold the precepts; third, advising the angry to practice patience; fourth, urging the lazy to practice vigor; fifth, teaching the scattered-minded to practice concentration; sixth, teaching the ignorant to study the limitless perfection of wisdom; seventh, knowing when to display skillful means in accord with the occasion. These are the seven."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Teaching the miserly to give,
> Instructing wrongdoers to uphold precepts,
> Advising the angry to practice patience,
> Urging the lazy to practice vigor,
> Teaching the scattered to compose the mind,
> Teaching the ignorant to study and learn,
> With the limitless perfection of wisdom,
> Using skillful means in accord with the time."

The Bodhisattva Timely asked the Buddha: "Why are there upper, middle, and lower grades of practitioner, rather than all equally reaching the Mahāyāna?"

The Buddha said: "Practitioners' minds differ in scope, their understanding varies in depth and shallowness, and their aspirations differ in quality. Therefore the three vehicles are taught. Fundamentally there are not three; they are provisional illustrations. Suppose there were a person who served as a great minister of the realm, wise and intelligent, highly valued by the king, entrusted with all affairs of state without any suspicion. This great minister had three friends: first, the crown prince; second, a nobleman; third, a commoner. The minister, while governing state affairs, made some errors. Others secretly went to the king and accused him of plotting rebellion. The king, hearing this, grew suspicious and asked his ministers: 'What punishment should he receive?' The ministers, seizing the opportunity, each proposed severe punishment: some said to behead him, some to cut off his hands and feet, some to cut off his ears and nose, gouge out his eyes, and remove his tongue. The king, seeing that the ministers' proposed punishments were excessive, said: 'Not so! This man is brilliant and wise. He made a small mistake, and this is not warranted. He should be taken and imprisoned.' The ministers obeyed and dared not say more. He ordered an officer: 'Quickly issue a writ to arrest the minister and confine him in prison.'

"At that time, the commoner friend heard of this and was grieved. Wishing to get him out of prison but lacking the power to do so, he could only provide the clothing, food, and necessities he lacked, bringing them every day, but he could not prevent him from being punished. The nobleman then heard, and his heart was pained. He went to the prison and persuaded the jailers not to flog him, so his suffering would ease, but he could not get him out of prison. When the crown prince heard, he was dismayed: 'This is my close relative and he has committed no grave offense. The ministers despised him and slandered him to the king. This should not have been done.' He went to the king, laid out the full details, and argued there was no treason, saying: 'For my sake, please pardon his punishment.' The king, out of love for his son, immediately pardoned the minister, released him from prison, had him appear before the king, and restored him to his former duties.

"The king represents the Tathāgata. The crown prince represents the perfection of wisdom and the skillful means of the bodhisattva, who has attained the patient acceptance of the unborn and, through the appropriate use of skillful wisdom, is able to liberate beings from the prison of the three realms and attain buddhahood, broadly saving all sentient beings. The nobleman friend represents the practice of pure morality, which frees one from the three evil paths without escaping the three realms; it can bring the blessings of heavenly or human birth but cannot bring one to the path. The commoner friend represents the work of generosity, which can only free one from the realm of hungry ghosts but not from the afflictions of hell or the animal realm. Why? As one sows, so does one reap. Those who arouse the aspiration for unsurpassed, true, and correct awakening, who cultivate great compassion and limitless great pity, and who transform all beings, therefore attain the path of buddhahood. Those who originally studied the great path but could not penetrate the profound Dharma, and who did not understand how to advance or retreat, stopping midway and abandoning the effort, therefore become pratyekabuddhas. Those who fear the difficulty of birth and death and cyclic existence, wishing only to save themselves without thought for those who suffer, therefore fall to the śrāvaka level. Each obtains according to their original practice."

When this sūtra was spoken, the householder Anāthapiṇḍada and the five hundred householders all aroused the aspiration for unsurpassed, true, and correct awakening. Several thousand people distanced themselves from defilement, and the Dharma-eye arose in them. Harps and musical instruments sounded without being played, and birds and beasts cried out in harmonious voices. At that time, all rejoiced and took refuge in the Buddha.

The householder further asked: "For those beginning to study the path, what should their initial aspiration be?"

The Buddha said: "First one should practice the five precepts and take refuge in the Three. What are the five precepts? First, with a compassionate and benevolent heart, not to kill; second, with integrity and restraint, not to steal; third, with chastity and purity, not to engage in sexual misconduct; fourth, with sincerity and harmony, not to deceive; fifth, with clarity of purpose, not to become intoxicated. What are the three refuges? First, taking refuge in the Buddha, the unsurpassed truth; second, taking refuge in the Dharma, to govern one's own mind; third, taking refuge in the Sangha, the noblest community, which is vast as the great ocean, encompassing all. Furthermore there are four stages: first, stream-entry; second, once-returning; third, non-returning; fourth, arhatship. Through the pratyekabuddha path to the Buddha's unsurpassed great path, birth in the heavens or among humans all arises from these. Next one practices the four immeasurables, four kinds of gratitude, four kinds of eloquence, the six limitless perfections, and great loving-kindness and great compassion, and attains the great path. With knowledge of the past that is limitless and vision of the future that is boundless, teaching the ten directions, what wisdom could fail to reach?"

Ānanda asked: "What is this sūtra called? How should it be practiced?"

The Buddha said: "It is called 'Explaining the Three Grades of Wealth for Householders and the Unsurpassed Truth of Renunciant Practice.' Its essential title is 'Expounding the Path and Worldly Occupations.'"

When the Buddha had finished speaking, the worthy Ānanda, the householder Anāthapiṇḍada, and the five hundred laymen all rejoiced.
