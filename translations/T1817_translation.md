# Brief Elucidation and Praise of the Final Verse of the Prajñāpāramitā

## *Lüèmíng Bōrě Mòhòu Yīsòng Zànshu* (略明般若末後一頌讚述)

Taishō 1817

Composed by Yìjìng (*Yìjìng*, 義淨) of the Táng dynasty. A verse commentary.

*Note: This is a short but intellectually dense composition by the great Tang-dynasty translator-monk Yìjìng (635–713), written after he completed his translation of Asaṅga's verse commentary on the Prajñāpāramitā. It elucidates the famous closing verse of the Diamond Sūtra: "All conditioned dharmas are like stars, cataracts, lamps, illusions, dew, bubbles, dreams, lightning, clouds: one should contemplate them in this way." Yìjìng structures his commentary as nine paired sections, each pairing a doctrinal "thesis" (lùn) on a Buddhist concept with a poetic "simile" (yù) explaining the corresponding image. The text also provides a valuable summary of the Yogācāra and Mādhyamika interpretive traditions as they existed in seventh-century India.*

Yìjìng, having completed translating Bodhisattva Asaṅga's (*Wúzhuó*, 無著) *Commentary on the Prajñāpāramitā in Verse*, examined how the great being analyzed the nine similes. It may be said that the literary expression is profoundly deep and the principles and meanings are mysteriously concise. Unless one were near the stage of Supreme Joy, who could produce such brilliant wisdom?

In the Western Regions it has been transmitted that Bodhisattva Asaṅga formerly received these eighty verses personally from the Honored Maitreya (*Císhì*, 慈氏) in Tuṣita Heaven (*Dōushìduō Tiān*, 覩史多天), opening the essential gate of prajñā, according with the principles of the Yogācāra school, and clarifying the meaning of consciousness-only. This caused the teaching to flow through India like the golden crow blazing brilliantly over Fúsāng, and the meaning to be proclaimed in the Divine Land (China) like the jade rabbit's light floating above the Snow Mountains.

Now, regarding the *Vajracchedikā* (*Néngduàn Jīngāng*, 能斷金剛), in the West there are many commentaries. Examining their origins, these verses are the earliest, and it was the great being Vasubandhu (*Shìqīn*, 世親) who personally composed its commentary. Although this was already translated in China, the meaning was somewhat incomplete. Therefore I personally verified it in the lecture hall, again examining its subtleties in detail, elegantly according with the intent of the commentary, and again translated the original sūtra.

Bodhisattva Vasubandhu also composed a commentary on the Prajñāpāramitā through seven gates of meaning. At Nālandā (*Nàlàndà*, 那爛陀) monastery this treatise was widely transmitted, but because the treasury of meaning is deep and profound, seekers could not fathom it. There was Dharma Master Siṃhacandra (*Shīzǐ Yuè*, 師子月) who composed a sub-commentary on this treatise, and also in East India a greatly learned layman named Candragomin (*Yuèguān*, 月官), who examined all the schools and also composed a meaning-commentary. All of these without exception accord in meaning with the three natures and differ from the Mādhyamika.

There are also other commentaries which only follow Nāgārjuna (*Lóngměng*, 龍猛) and do not accord with Yogācāra. Yogācāra holds that the ultimate is existent and the conventional is nonexistent, taking the three natures as fundamental. Mādhyamika holds that the ultimate is nonexistent and the conventional is existent, truly taking the two truths as primary. The great teaching of prajñā contains both meanings. This has caused in the Eastern Land the Way to divide between north and south, and in the West the meaning to be separated between existence and emptiness. Having recognized these fundamental frameworks, in principle they cannot be mixed together; each accords with the sage's intent, and truly it is difficult to set them in opposition.

Now, the final verse says: "All conditioned dharmas are like stars, cataracts, lamps, illusions, dew, bubbles, dreams, lightning, clouds: one should contemplate them in this way." Since the commentary text is obscure, I fear seekers may still be confused. Therefore, based on the twice-nine thesis-and-simile pairs, I offer these eighteen compositions, hoping that those who read them in the morning may find ease, so that there may be no hindrance to dissolving mental stagnation, like a luminous pearl personally bestowed, forever free of the confusion of grasping at swords.

Now, the nine matters such as views are the domain of birth-and-death. The great being comprehends them as like stars and so on, and therefore abides in them without attachment. Wisdom does not dwell at the ford of birth-and-death, like a red lotus upon rippling water. Compassion does not abide in parinirvāṇa, like white jade sinking in the depths. In response to the non-abiding nirvāṇa, these nine similes are set forth as follows.

---

### 1. Thesis on Views

Contemplating views as like stars: through the long night, falsely grasped.

> The mind of views, a long night's scene;
> False grasping, long in darkness spread.
> When the three minds have just arisen,
> Both divisions are entirely removed.

### Simile of Stars

Using stars to illustrate views: present at night, absent at dawn.

> The elm-star throughout the night scene shines;
> The willow's hue at dawn spreads wide.
> A thousand rays of light, once dawn arises,
> In one moment are entirely removed.

---

### 2. Thesis on Objects

Saying objects are like cataracts: deludedly seeing flowers in the sky.

> Object-forms are nothing but obstructions;
> Deludedly one sees the flaws of dust.
> But through the power of true wisdom,
> One no longer sees the mad flowers.

### Simile of Cataracts

Using cataracts to illustrate objects: originally nonexistent, yet deludedly grasped.

> The great void originally has no obstructions;
> The pure realm by nature lacks all flaws.
> But through the power of cataracts in the eye,
> One comes to see flowers in the sky.

---

### 3. Thesis on Consciousness

Saying consciousness is like a lamp: arising and arising without cessation.

> Views arising, arising without end;
> Delusions born, born without limit.
> Detained within the palace of death,
> Truly due to the power of reliance on consciousness.

### Simile of Lamps

Using lamps to illustrate consciousness: flame upon flame, endlessly.

> Emitting flames, flaming without end;
> Spreading light, shining without limit.
> Blazing and bright throughout the night,
> Truly due to the power of oil.

---

### 4. Thesis on Realms

Saying realms are like illusions: arising through karma and eventually ceasing.

> Karma's power adorns places with skill;
> Through delusion arise myriad forms.
> What is grasped is certainly not real;
> The substance of realms is entirely false.

### Simile of Illusions

Using illusions to illustrate realms: created by the craftsman, but ultimately nonexistent.

> The illusionist, skillful in expedient means,
> Fashions various forms and appearances.
> Their very substance is certainly not real;
> All that is seen is entirely false.

---

### 5. Thesis on the Body

Describing the body as like dew: its substance not long enduring.

> Casting the body at the edge of lofty peaks,
> Entrusting life amid the river of death.
> Always dwelling in the drifting night,
> Just like the wind of bellows.

### Simile of Dew

Using dew to illustrate the body: encountering wind, it must fall.

> Round pearls resting at the tips of grass,
> Tear-drops dwelling amid the flowers.
> Sorrowful, stirring in the forest night,
> Rustling, all following the wind.

---

### 6. Thesis on Experience

Presenting experience as like bubbles: dependent on faculty, object, and consciousness.

> Within the world, faculties and forms accumulate;
> Relying on these, mental activity proceeds.
> Truly due to the conjunction of the three contacts,
> The three feelings come to arise.

### Simile of Bubbles

Using bubbles to illustrate experience: created from drops, water, and wind.

> In the pool, still water collects;
> Drops fall and wind moves.
> But due to the conjunction of these three,
> Myriad bubbles come to arise.

---

### 7. Thesis on the Past

Describing the past as like dreams: arising from thought.

> Though the past has no objects,
> Deliberate thought still communicates.
> Thus within the square inch of the mind,
> One again sees the appearance of the nine cities.

### Simile of Dreams

Using dreams to illustrate the past: arising from memory.

> During the day one encounters many objects;
> In the good night, memory communicates.
> Thus within sleep,
> One again recalls the appearances of former times.

---

### 8. Thesis on the Present

Discussing the present as like lightning: existing only momentarily.

> Myriad phenomena like lightning across the fields;
> The four marks like streaming light.
> How could one know it is merely a single thought-moment?
> Deludedly one reckons there are three constants.

### Simile of Lightning

Using lightning to illustrate the present: in a flash it is gone.

> Crashing thunder rumbles across the four fields;
> Striking lightning moves with a thousand lights.
> In a kṣaṇa there exists a single thought;
> That very substance is itself impermanent.

---

### 9. Thesis on the Future

Discussing the future as like clouds: the root consciousness able to hold its seeds.

> The storehouse-consciousness has no initial knot;
> Waves of emotion fly forth afterward.
> Truly because there is craving and love,
> It carries seeds; what doubt is there?

### Simile of Clouds

Using clouds to illustrate the future: their substance necessarily containing moisture.

> Dense clouds gather and knot;
> Brilliant light and wind fly.
> Their radiant beauty seems so lovable;
> That they contain moisture is beyond doubt.

---

### Summary Verse

Furthermore, with a single composition gathering the theses and similes, summarizing the essential meaning:

> The mind of views, when wisdom eliminates it, is like stars extinguished;
> Deluded objects, when truth dispels them, are like cataracts removed.
> Perception arises from consciousness like a lamp's flame;
> Realms exist through thought like an illusionist's chariot.
> The single body briefly appears like morning dew;
> The three feelings suddenly manifest, as empty as bubbles.
> Recollecting the past, empty dreams arise;
> Grasping at the present, rushing lightning unfolds.
> Knowing that rain-moisture dwells within clouds,
> Future seeds forever reside in the storehouse-consciousness.

### Closing Verse on the Three Natures

Below, separately based on the three natures, three bodies, conventional and ultimate, and prajñā, to illuminate contemplative practice through the nine similes explaining the nine matters:

> Having thoroughly contemplated that the world of birth is entirely like this,
> The wise should devote themselves to the true and permanent.
> The true and permanent is truly not arcane:
> The perfected nature is right before one's eyes.
> Awakening to the emptiness of both, snake and rope are exhausted;
> Understanding the one is not nonexistent, like the moon suspended in a mirror.
> When the mirror-moon is suspended, truly there is no anxiety;
> One simply creates it as a place for generating merit through other conditions.
> In consciousness-only, the initial mind briefly has a support;
> In true suchness, the subsequent thought has no ground.
> No ground is precisely prajñā;
> Transcending the conventional, both true and false are extinguished.
> The ford of merit, like a raft: to discard or not discard?
> Compassion and wisdom follow beings: to relinquish or not relinquish?
