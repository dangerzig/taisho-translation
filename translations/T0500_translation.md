# 羅云忍辱經
## Sūtra on Rāhula's Patience

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0500

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Faju (法炬)

---

Ānanda said: "I heard this from the Buddha."

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada. Śāriputra and Rāhula went together at dawn, wearing their Dharma robes and carrying their alms-bowls, to enter the city to beg for food. At that time, a ruffian encountered the two worthies and thought to himself: "The foremost disciple of the śramaṇa Gautama is going on his alms round with Rāhula." He then gave rise to a malicious intention, took sand and dirt from the ground, put it in Śāriputra's bowl, and struck Rāhula on the head.

The teacher, seeing blood streaming down Rāhula's face, said: "As a disciple of the Buddha, be careful not to harbor poison. You should regard all sentient beings with a compassionate and merciful mind. The World-Honored One always says: 'Patience is the greatest joy; only the wise are capable of it.' I have heard the Buddha's precepts and will not violate them for the rest of my life. I control my own mind and hold patience as a treasure. One who indulges the mind in evil is like one who throws oneself into fire. One who is arrogant and self-regarding, the fool calls strong, not reckoning the disaster that will come back to harm him. The calamity of an indulged mind is heavier than Mount Sumeru. Even if one spent all the years of one's life to pay for the evil, not one-sixteenth of it would be diminished. A fool who acts with evil toward a pure and precept-holding śramaṇa is like one who walks into the wind holding a torch; if the madman does not let go, he will surely burn himself. A wretched person who harbors poison and considers himself wise is like a bhikṣu who trusts in the four fruits of the śramaṇa path. As a disciple of the Buddha, one should always subdue the mind; when evil arises, extinguish it immediately. This is the foremost among the courageous. Heavenly spirits and emperors, though said to have great power, are not the equal of one who endures evil. That power is unsurpassed."

Rāhula, seeing the blood streaming down across his face, washed the blood at the water's edge and spoke to himself: "My pain is but a moment; what of his long suffering! That person is evil, and that place is also evil. I harbor no resentment; what more can my compassion do for him? The Buddha is my honored one; he taught me great loving-kindness. The wild and perverse, whose intentions tend toward cruelty and violence: the śramaṇa silently endures, thereby achieving lofty virtue. The violent are like wolves; fools respect them. The śramaṇa guards his patience, and the wild fools despise him. That person is evil, but how can I become evil in response? The wheel of rebirth is endless; how could things always be one way? I wish to use the Buddha's sūtras of ultimate truth to instruct and admonish the foolish and deluded, but it is like taking a sharp sword to cut a stinking corpse: the corpse does not know pain. It is not that the sword is not sharp; it is that the dead corpse has no awareness. To feed heavenly ambrosia to a pig in a latrine: the pig would abandon it and run away. It is not that the ambrosia is not fine; it is that the filthy creature does not value it. To use the Buddha's true words to instruct the violent fools of the world: is it not the same?"

Teacher and disciple returned together. After the meal, they washed their bowls, rinsed their hands and mouths, and both went to the Buddha's presence, prostrating at the Buddha's feet. Śāriputra sat to the side and recounted the entire matter to the Buddha from beginning to end. The World-Honored One said: "When the evil mind arises, it is the decline of oneself. The ruffian, at the end of his life, by midnight shall enter the Avīci Hell. The hell-wardens will inflict torment, with every kind of poison. After eighty-four thousand years, his life there will finally end. His soul will then receive the body of a venomous python. So heavy is the poison that it will harm his own body. When it ends, it begins again: he will then receive the form of a viper, and will eat sand and dirt for ten thousand years before it is finished. Because he directed an angry mind toward one who upholds the precepts, he receives a venomous body. Because he threw sand and dirt into the bowl, world after world he will eat sand and dirt until he dies. When the punishment is complete and he is released, he will obtain birth as a human. While his mother carries him, she will always have severe illness, and the household will decline daily. The child born will be dull and stupid, completely without hands or feet. His relatives will be alarmed and astonished, and the entire clan will say: 'What is this monstrosity? This has come as an ill omen.' They will immediately take and abandon it at a crossroads. Passersby, coming and going, will all be shocked; some will throw tiles and stones at it, some will strike it with knives and staves, all hitting its head, trampling its brain. In extreme suffering, within a fortnight it will die. After death, the soul will again be reborn, and again it will be without hands or feet, dull and stupid as before, for five hundred lifetimes, until the heavy penalty is finished. Afterward, when born as a human, he will always suffer from headaches."

The World-Honored One further said: "Śāriputra! One who lives in the world and does not practice patience, wherever they are born, will not encounter a Buddha's world. They will be far from the Dharma and distant from the Saṅgha, always in the three evil paths, ending and beginning again, each time for an entire kalpa. If by some residual merit they emerge as a human, their disposition will always be foolish, cruelty and violence will follow them, they will be jealous of the noble and slander the most honored. They will be ugly in appearance, despised by all. Wherever they are born they will always be poor. In service they will not attain rank. Their wishes will run contrary to their intentions. The heavenly spirits and noble sages will not protect or aid them. At night they will always have evil dreams, with monstrous and uncanny happenings from beginning to end, unforeseen calamities arising from all sides. Wherever they dwell there will be no peace, and the mind will always be fearful. All of this is because one has not endured and subdued the evil mind; that is what causes it to be so. One who patiently endures evil will always be at peace wherever born. All calamities will be extinguished. Wishes will always be fulfilled. One's countenance will be radiant, one's body strong with few illnesses. One's wealth and honor will be abundant and respected. All of this comes from patience, from loving-kindness and generosity in aiding all beings. The blessing of patience brings peace to oneself and one's kin; the family and clan will be harmonious and flourishing, and there will never be a lack of joy. The wise, seeing deeply, restrain and subdue their minds. The mind misleads people, destroying families and endangering lives. It leads to punishment by the king's law, to being roasted in the hells, or to becoming a hungry ghost or an animal. All is the fault of the mind."

The World-Honored One further said: "Better to have a sharp sword pierce one's belly and slice one's flesh, or to throw oneself into fire, than to tread the path of evil. Better to carry Mount Sumeru upon one's back, crushed unto death, and cast into the great sea to be swallowed by fish and turtles, than to do evil. If one does not understand this meaning, be careful not to speak rashly. The Buddha's clear Dharma runs counter to the ways of the world. What the world treasures, the path disdains. The pure and the turbid flow in separate streams; the wise and the foolish tend in different directions. The loyal and the treacherous are foes to each other; the crooked are always jealous of the upright. Therefore those who are slaves to desire do not appreciate my practice of desirelessness. Better to swallow burning coals than to slander the Three Honored Ones. The radiance of patience surpasses the sun and moon. The strength of nāgas and elephants may be called powerful, but compared to patience, it falls short ten-thousandfold. The brilliance of the seven treasures is valued by worldly people, yet it invites worry and brings calamity and misfortune. Patience as a treasure brings eternal safety. Though giving to the ten directions brings great merit, that merit does not compare to patience. One who cherishes patience and practices loving-kindness will have no enemies, world after world. The mind within will be tranquil, forever free from poisonous harm. There is nothing in the world to rely upon; only patience can be trusted. Patience is a peaceful dwelling where calamity and misfortune do not arise. Patience is divine armor that no weapon can penetrate. Patience is a great vessel that can carry one across difficulties. Patience is an excellent medicine that can save all lives. What wish does a patient person not obtain? If one wishes to become a flying emperor ruling the four continents, the second-heaven Lord Śakra, or even a being of the sixth heaven, with limitless life, a body fragrant and pure, with all wishes naturally fulfilled as though taking things from one's own home: this can be achieved. If one aspires to the four pure fruits of a śramaṇa, one can attain them; it depends on where one directs oneself. My own attainment of Buddhahood, revered by all the devas, walking alone supreme through the three realms: it was all brought about by the power of patience."

The Buddha told all the śramaṇas: "You should recite the Patience Sūtra, never forgetting it for even a moment. Cherish it, learn it, recite it, and proclaim it. Proclaim the virtue of patience, to aid all sentient beings."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, all the śramaṇas greatly rejoiced, made obeisance, and departed.
