# 寶授菩薩菩提行經
## Sūtra on the Bodhi-Practice of Ratnākaradatta Bodhisattva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0488

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmabhadra (法賢)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time the Buddha was dwelling in the great city of Vaiśālī, in the great forest, in the hall with the peaked roof, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. All were great arhats who had exhausted all outflows, were free from all afflictions, had attained their own benefit, severed all bonds of existence, cast off heavy burdens, and completed what was to be done. Like great nāga kings, their minds were well liberated and their wisdom well liberated. Having attained profound liberation, their minds had reached the true path. Having tamed their faculties, their deportment was refined and careful. Only one, the person of the Venerable Ānanda, had received the Buddha's prediction and upheld the treasury of Dharma.

He was also accompanied by a great assembly of one thousand bodhisattvas, all of whom had attained the stage of non-retrogression, were endowed with dhāraṇī, the patience of equality in dharmas, were great in wisdom and endowed with great faith. Their words and deeds were in accord, and they had severed all doubts. Their faces were full and without frowning; they were always greatly joyful and endowed with great diligence. They were sons of the Dharma King who knew the self-nature of dharmas and taught the Dharma without weariness. The Dharma they taught was free from conceptual proliferation. They transformed sentient beings and awakened them to buddha-wisdom. Whatever benefit they brought was never in vain. They had attained great patience, had departed from the dharmas of inverted states, had fulfilled the ten grounds, and knew well the three times. They clearly understood self-nature as neither arising nor ceasing, had permanently severed all bonds, and were complete in dignified deportment. They had realized the samādhis of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. Although they had transcended birth and death, they continued to move through the cycle of rebirth. They took no pleasure in the practices of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas, but solely aroused the mind of great bodhi and taught the Dharma according to the capacities of beings. Their names were:

Maitreya Bodhisattva, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Dialectical Accumulation Bodhisattva, Jewel-Hand Bodhisattva, Fragrant Light Bodhisattva, Boundless Light Bodhisattva, Obstacle-Removing Bodhisattva, Supreme-Meaning-Mind Bodhisattva, Attained-Light-King Bodhisattva, Severer-of-All-Darkness Bodhisattva, Sarvāviṣamadarśī Bodhisattva, Inner Practice Bodhisattva, Boundless Mind Bodhisattva, Endowed-with-Great-Diligent-Stride-Mind Bodhisattva, Jewel Ocean Bodhisattva, Adornment King Bodhisattva, Endowed-with-Great-Supernatural-Power-King Bodhisattva, Unhindered Stride Bodhisattva, Supreme Mind Bodhisattva, Universally Full Bodhisattva, Anāvaraṇadarśī Bodhisattva, Ever-Joyful Bodhisattva, Supreme Golden Light Bodhisattva, Contemplator-of-All-Dharma-Mind Bodhisattva, Aśubhaṃdharanārkusumita Bodhisattva, Heap-of-Heads Bodhisattva, Worry-Free Auspicious Bodhisattva, Sumeru Treasury Bodhisattva, Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Fragrance-Sovereign-King Bodhisattva, Joy-King Bodhisattva, Boundless Wisdom-Treasury Bodhisattva, Upholder-of-All-Wondrous-Dharma-Treasury Bodhisattva, and Lion's Roar Bodhisattva. These and other great bodhisattva-mahāsattvas served as the leaders of the assembly.

At that time the World-Honored One, when the time for meals arrived, put on his robe and took his bowl. Surrounded by two thousand bhikṣus, he entered the great city of Vaiśālī to beg for food. When the World-Honored One entered the city gate, by the power of great compassion he displayed supernatural powers and radiated a great light illuminating everything. The great city of Vaiśālī was transformed into lapis lazuli, and the crossroads were all made pure. Those sentient beings who were touched by the light gained sight if blind, hearing if deaf, speech if mute, and right mindfulness if confused. Celestial flowers rained down like a shower, filling the city. Celestial music sounded of itself, with wonderful tones clear and resonant. From the Avīci Hell below to the Akaniṣṭha Heaven above, the beings within all experienced only great bliss.

At that time, King Siṃhahṛṣṭa of the great city of Vaiśālī had a son named Ratnākaradatta, only three years old. His wet-nurse held him upon the palace terrace. Ratnākaradatta suddenly saw the radiant auspicious signs and also heard of the various extraordinary events in the city. He quickly descended from her arms to the ground, stood upright before the wet-nurse, and spoke this verse:

> "Whose power of majestic virtue
> has manifested these rare signs,
> like the light of a hundred billion suns
> illuminating the three-thousandfold world,
> wherein all evil destinies
> have been entirely purified?
> Such supernatural powers as these:
> mother, tell me quickly.
>
> "Devas rain down wondrous flowers
> scattered throughout the buddha-land,
> forming wondrous parasol canopies
> suspended in the sky.
> Exotic birds from ten directions
> fly singing and gather here.
> Men and women all rejoice greatly,
> adorned in extraordinary ways.
>
> "The blind now see again;
> the deaf can hear once more.
> The mute have gained speech;
> the confused have gained right mindfulness.
> All that was ugly and coarse
> has been transformed into wondrous appearances.
> All unwholesome people
> have given rise to minds of loving-kindness and compassion.
>
> "Who is it moving through the world
> displaying such supernatural powers?
> This is the most supreme event.
> Mother, tell me quickly."

At that time the wet-nurse answered the boy Ratnākaradatta in verse:

> "A treasure of merit has appeared,
> pure and beyond thought.
> Pure and supreme in conduct,
> stainless, without increase or decrease.
>
> "The World-Honored One is the eye of the world.
> He regards beings as his own kin,
> transforming the world through his deeds,
> without distinction of near or far.
>
> "He has come begging for food.
> He does not abide in worldly marks.
> He is not stained by worldly dharmas,
> like a lotus flower in water.
>
> "He can sever the doubts of beings.
> With loving-kindness and compassion he ever benefits them.
> Whatever sufferings beings have,
> only the Buddha can remove them all.
>
> "When praised, he does not rejoice.
> When slandered, he does not grow angry.
> Without obstruction or hindrance,
> he moves through the world like a pure wind.
>
> "The one who comes is the World's Teacher,
> the most supreme, great Dharma King,
> who well understands the most profound Dharma
> and clearly knows the ultimate truth.
>
> "He ever teaches the Middle Way,
> whose words are free from grasping and rejecting.
> His Dharma phrases are most tranquil,
> far removed from all locations.
>
> "His accumulated wisdom is without the mark of self.
> He is the most supreme, great gathering of merit.
> The Well-Gone, Knower of the World:
> the one who comes bears marks beyond measure.
>
> "The Buddha's body is like Mount Sumeru,
> lofty and magnificent, without equal.
> He is also like a nyagrodha tree,
> proportionate above and below.
> His color is like true gold,
> gleaming and brilliant.
> Pure as crystal,
> and also like the autumn full moon.
>
> "His head is round like a canopy;
> his hair is black and dark blue.
> His topknot is like a spiral whorl,
> each strand turning to the right.
> His face is like the full moon,
> serene and pure.
> The white hair-tuft between his brows
> turns to the right and coils.
>
> "His eyes are like blue lotuses,
> bright and keenly observing.
> His lips are like bimba fruit;
> his teeth are white, straight, and close-set.
> His tongue is like a lotus petal,
> long and broad, covering his face.
> His nose is high and straight;
> his forehead is broad and even.
> His eyebrows are dark blue,
> extending far to his ears.
> His ears are perfectly shaped,
> their lobes hanging to his shoulders.
>
> "The Tathāgata's brahma-voice
> is clear and resonant like a kalaviṅka,
> beautiful, soft, and gentle.
> Beings who hear it rejoice.
>
> "All the kiṃnaras,
> peacocks, geese, parrots,
> mynas, cuckoos,
> kuṇālas, mandarin ducks,
> jīvañjīvas, and pairs,
> and all such sounds,
> together with all celestial music
> and every beautiful sound,
> none can equal the Tathāgata:
> not even one part in sixteen.
>
> "His neck is slender yet full;
> his two arms are straight.
> His fingers are long and slender,
> bearing the marks of wheel and conch,
> soft as cotton down,
> with nails the color of red copper.
> His chest is broad, even, and straight;
> his navel-whorl is deep and hidden.
> His male organ, like that of a royal horse,
> is concealed and not visible.
> Down to the soles of his two feet,
> their color is like red lotus,
> level, full, and soft,
> bearing the pattern of the thousand-spoked wheel,
> together with the hook and banner.
>
> "All these marks are complete,
> each adorned by a hundred merits.
> He is a powerful great hero,
> the teacher of all the world,
> ever making the lion's roar,
> teaching the Dharma of non-duality.
>
> "All his words
> are true and without error,
> loving, gentle, and agreeable.
> Beings who hear them rejoice.
>
> "He awakens sentient beings
> and inspires them according to their capacities.
> His meaningful merit and virtue
> are supreme and foremost.
>
> "With such adornments complete,
> he is called the Buddha, the World-Honored One.
> Throughout the worlds of the ten directions,
> no ordinary or sacred being can compare."

At that time, the boy Ratnākaradatta, having heard the wet-nurse's verses praising the Buddha, addressed his mother: "How can I come to see the Buddha?"

The Buddha, knowing his intention, quickly went to the palace gate and appeared standing there. The wet-nurse pointed and said: "That is the Buddha." The boy Ratnākaradatta then saw the World-Honored One from the palace terrace, raised his hands in homage, and thought:

"If any being were to see the Tathāgata with such complete marks of merit and not arouse the mind of great bodhi, it would be difficult for them to gain their own benefit." He further reflected: "Throughout hundreds of thousands of koṭis of kalpas it is difficult to encounter a buddha. That I have encountered one today is extremely rare. I should offer this body in worship." Then the boy, holding a thousand-petaled golden lotus in his hand, threw himself down from the palace terrace. Sustained by the Buddha's power, the boy remained in mid-air, where he offered the golden lotus to the Buddha. Then the golden lotus left the boy's hand and in the sky above the Buddha transformed into a flower canopy, adorned with many jewels, supremely wondrous and foremost.

At that time the boy Ratnākaradatta, in mid-air, joined his palms toward the Buddha and spoke in verse:

> "The lotus I have offered
> is not to sever afflictions
> or for any dharma at all,
> but solely for the bodhi of the Buddha.
>
> "As bodhi is unborn,
> neither existent nor non-existent,
> neither grasped nor relinquished,
> I am manifested by the Buddha.
>
> "Not what the deluded grasp at:
> marks and marklessness are equal.
> I am free from all marks
> in making offerings to the Buddha, the World-Honored One.
>
> "Whatever merit is gained
> is also free from all marks.
> In offering this flower today,
> I do not aspire to the two vehicles.
> By means of the foremost vehicle,
> may I ever turn throughout the buddha-lands."

At that time the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana, standing to the Buddha's right, seeing this, spoke in verse asking the boy Ratnākaradatta:

> "You thus faithfully and reverently
> make offerings to the Buddha Śākyamuni.
> Yet with an inverted mind, how
> do you say bodhi is unborn?"

At that time Ratnākaradatta replied to the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana in verse:

> "All dharmas are originally unborn.
> What is given is empty and unconditioned.
> The nature of dharmas is originally thus.
> How could anything be born?
>
> "Shaven-headed, wearing the kaṣāya,
> you abide in the mark of arhatship.
> If you cannot even understand emptiness,
> how could you comprehend buddha-wisdom?
>
> "If you have false thinking
> and offer to a hundred billion buddhas,
> though you offer to such buddhas,
> in truth it is no offering.
>
> "Venerable One, even until now
> you have not yet severed false thinking.
> What can your mind do
> to call the markless 'having marks'?"

At that time the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana further addressed the boy: "Has the Tathāgata not realized unsurpassed, perfect enlightenment? Does he also not teach the Dharma?"

The boy Ratnākaradatta said: "One of great wisdom does not abide in the mark of bodhi or the mark of the Tathāgata. The nature of all dharmas is unconditioned; dharmas are fundamentally without arising. If one understands in this way, one then knows the nature of dharmas. One is not alarmed or frightened; one abandons the distinction of near and far. Without coming and without going, without conduct and without marks, one does not abide in the Buddha-Dharma, does not abide in the dharma of the pratyekabuddha, does not abide in the dharma of the śrāvaka, and also does not abide in the dharma of greed, does not abide in the dharma of aversion, does not abide in the dharma of delusion, and does not even abide in the dharmas of ignorance and afflictions of deluded sentient beings. Nor does one abide in form or formlessness, perception or non-perception, marks or marklessness, purity or impurity, or body, speech, and mind, or equality or inequality. One does not abide in any dharma whatsoever."

At that time the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana asked again: "Ratnākaradatta, has the Tathāgata perhaps not realized anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?"

The boy said: "No. If there were something realized, that would mean abiding in the mark of the Tathāgata, in the mark of bodhi, in the mark of liberation. If one abides in such marks, that is delusion."

Mahāmaudgalyāyana said: "Boy, I too am without marks. You say I have marks." Mahāmaudgalyāyana further said: "Boy, what I asked earlier was in terms of conventional truth."

The boy said: "Mahāmaudgalyāyana, all sentient beings are deluded and confused by falsehood, and their faculties arise without being tamed."

Mahāmaudgalyāyana said: "If sentient beings are fully endowed with falsehood, then dharmas too are false. If they are false, how can you teach?"

The boy said: "Mahāmaudgalyāyana, to teach the Dharma without marks: that is called teaching the Dharma. Whoever teaches in this way has nothing arrived at, nothing realized, nothing known, and nothing seen."

Mahāmaudgalyāyana said: "Boy, if that is so, why do you now make offerings to the Tathāgata?"

The boy said: "Mahāmaudgalyāyana, if you think in this way, you see the mark of the Tathāgata and the mark of the giver."

At that time Mahāmaudgalyāyana, having heard the boy's words, fell silent.

Then the boy further said: "Mahāmaudgalyāyana, if any sentient being has such views with marks, they cannot attain liberation, cannot gain their own benefit, and are far from the Tathāgata's tranquil nirvāṇa. They will certainly set out on the śrāvaka vehicle."

At that time Mahāmaudgalyāyana spoke in verse:

> "Though the boy is young in years,
> his wisdom is like the great ocean.
> Over how long a time
> did you master the Dharma of non-arising?"

At that time the boy Ratnākaradatta replied to Mahāmaudgalyāyana in verse:

> "What is learned is itself not learning;
> all learning is without self-nature.
> The greatly wise learn in this way,
> and my learning is likewise.
>
> "What you have asked me
> is attached to the mark of sentient beings.
> Sentient beings are fundamentally without marks;
> dharmas cannot be found.
>
> "To speak of the mark of bodhi
> is a deluded and incorrect view.
> Venerable One, how can you
> still abide in these views?
>
> "The wise, regarding all views,
> see them all as entirely pure.
> Buddha-dharma, dharmas of delusion,
> and all the various dharmas:
> to contemplate them all as empty
> is to know the nature of dharmas.
>
> "If one abides in marks of existence or nonexistence
> and seeks to realize bodhi,
> since dharmas are fundamentally neither existent nor nonexistent,
> how can bodhi be attained?
>
> "The Dharma that is spoken is boundless;
> sentient beings are likewise.
> Not abiding in differentiated marks:
> this itself is called nirvāṇa.
>
> "Moving thus through the cycle of rebirth,
> teacher and disciple are both without substance.
> Within this markless Dharma,
> the wise are not deluded.
>
> "The deluded speak of attainment and realization.
> They all abide in the cycle of rebirth.
> Ignorance grows ever stronger for them:
> this is being possessed by Māra.
>
> "Sitting peacefully at the site of enlightenment
> is to demonstrate conventional truth.
> What all the buddhas realize
> is neither conventional nor tranquil.
>
> "Bodhi cannot be spoken of;
> it is far from the seen and the unseen.
> Whoever sees this truth
> can understand the wondrous Dharma."

At that time the Venerable Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, from what time has this boy Ratnākaradatta cultivated the Dharma?"

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, when I first aroused the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, this boy Ratnākaradatta had already realized the patience of non-arising for three hundred thousand kalpas.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, when in the past I received the prediction from Dīpaṃkara Buddha, I first realized the patience of non-arising. At that time, Ratnākaradatta was already a great bodhisattva in that Dharma, foremost in understanding emptiness."

Śāriputra further said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, for what cause and condition has the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta, after so long a time, not yet realized anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "You should take this question and ask the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta yourself. He will certainly explain it for you."

Then Śāriputra, following the Buddha's sacred instruction, asked the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta: "Why have you not yet become a buddha?"

The Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta said: "Venerable One, because anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi cannot be found, I have not become a buddha."

Śāriputra said: "Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta, what do you think? Has the Tathāgata become a buddha? Does he not bear marks?"

The Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta said: "If the Tathāgata had something to realize regarding bodhi, that would be grasping at marks. If one grasps at marks, that is false thinking."

Śāriputra said: "Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta, since the time you speak of, in what patience and what practice have you abided, and by what Dharma do you transform beings in equality?"

The Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta said: "I do not abide in even a single dharma; how much less in four? Śāriputra, do not think that I have any Dharma to teach, or that I realize bodhi, or that I am a Tathāgata, or that I have attained liberation."

Śāriputra said: "How rare, good man! If you can understand the Dharma in this way, you should leave home in the presence of the Buddha."

At that time the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta spoke in verse:

> "Those who leave home
> are mostly attached to the mark of renunciation.
> Their minds are deluded by false thinking
> and they claim to have attained something.
>
> "Clinging to the view of phenomena,
> cultivating the cause of giving,
> seeking the fruit of the unconditioned:
> what they realize is itself conditioned.
>
> "Not understanding the ground of marklessness,
> they see arising and non-arising.
> The marks of attainment and non-attainment:
> they claim to have tasted ambrosia.
>
> "Such persons, within the Buddha-Dharma,
> are called destroyers of the Dharma.
> The Tathāgata, the Lion of the Śākyas,
> teaches a Dharma that is tranquil and without marks.
>
> "Not abiding in mind or non-mind,
> not abiding in nature or non-nature.
> Whoever sees it taught in this way:
> this is seeing the Buddha's teaching.
>
> "Whoever sees with marks
> has impure vision.
> The view of self is not liberation;
> the wise should not practice thus.
>
> "The view of self is delusion,
> clinging to the view of permanence in marks.
> With self-generated views of marks,
> they claim to have attained nirvāṇa.
>
> "Not recognizing the nature of dreams and illusions,
> and turning away from emptiness and marklessness:
> the Buddha says such persons
> are greatly lacking in wisdom.
>
> "Furthermore, taming the faculties,
> keeping precepts, attached to dhyāna concentration,
> giving rise to false thinking,
> abiding in marks, deluded in seeking fruits:
> these are thieves within the Dharma.
> The wise should know this.
>
> "With all such various dharmas,
> what benefit would my leaving home bring?
> The dharma-realm is originally serene;
> dharmas are without distinction.
> Like an echo in a valley,
> the wise do not see marks.
>
> "One should abide in thusness,
> with no other nature or marks.
> If one understands dharmas in this way,
> why abide in the mark of renunciation?"

At that time Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said to the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta: "What do you say bodhi is?"

The Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta said: "Departing from all language is called bodhi."

Mañjuśrī said: "How can you make such a statement?"

The Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta said: "Dharma is fundamentally without words. That is why I make this statement."

Mañjuśrī said: "What should be taught to a bodhisattva on the first ground? How should they be instructed to practice?"

The Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta said: "One should teach thus: without severing desire and aversion, without abandoning delusion, without severing afflictions, and so on up to the five aggregates and six sense-bases. Furthermore, one should not be doubtful about wisdom or delusion, should not be mindful of the Buddha, should not think about the Dharma, should not make offerings to the Saṃgha, should not keep precepts, should not seek tranquility from friends, and should not even transcend difficulties. Mañjuśrī, one should teach the Dharma in this way to bodhisattvas on the first ground and instruct them to practice thus. What do you think? One also should not abide in any mark regarding these dharmas. If one abides in marks, one is abiding in dharmas, and that is delusion giving rise to the dharmas of arising and ceasing. If one teaches these dharmas without any doubt, one then knows the nature of the dharma-realm. Whoever can understand the nature of dharmas in this way deserves to be called one who teaches bodhi. Mañjuśrī, if any bodhisattva, hearing this Dharma, is neither alarmed nor frightened, know that they have attained non-retrogression."

At that time, eight bhikṣus in the assembly, suddenly hearing this teaching of markless true Dharma, were displeased in their minds. They left the Dharma assembly, vomited blood, died, and all fell into the Avīci great hell.

At that time Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how is it that these eight bhikṣus, hearing this true Dharma, manifested such a great evil sign?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī, do not speak thus. These bhikṣus, for ten thousand kalpas, never heard the Dharma and never drew near to spiritual friends. Therefore, hearing this true Dharma today, they were displeased. Mañjuśrī, in a future age, these eight bhikṣus, while in the Avīci Hell, will suddenly recall the true Dharma. They will immediately die there and be reborn in Tuṣita Heaven as devaputras, or be reborn in the human world as wheel-turning kings. After sixty-eight kalpas of receiving and serving ten nayutas of buddhas, at the end of that period a buddha will appear in the world named Stainless Light Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened. That Stainless Light Buddha, just as I today dwell in the great city of Vaiśālī, will widely teach the Dharma and bestow predictions for devas and humans. At that time, those devaputras, hearing the teaching and the predictions from afar with their celestial ears, will go together with eighty thousand devaputras to that buddha's presence. Having arrived, they will scatter celestial flowers throughout the great city of Vaiśālī, make offerings and pay reverence, sit to one side, and address the Buddha: 'World-Honored One, we rejoice and wish to hear the true Dharma. May the Buddha teach us the practice of bodhi.' Then Stainless Light Tathāgata will teach the true Dharma and cause them to arouse the mind of great bodhi. As soon as those devaputras arouse the mind, they will all attain non-retrogression regarding anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. At that time, eighty-four thousand people in the great city of Vaiśālī will also attain non-retrogression regarding anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, and another twelve hundred people will become free from dust, removed from defilement, and attain the pure Dharma-eye."

Then the World-Honored One told Mañjuśrī: "Even if a bodhisattva were to practice the six pāramitās for hundreds of thousands of kalpas without the wisdom of skillful means, it would not compare to briefly hearing this true Dharma. Why? The merit of hearing this true Dharma is immeasurable. How much more so the merit of one who delights in hearing and receiving it, and who even copies, upholds, recites, and widely teaches it to others. Mañjuśrī, if there are those who seek the fruit of the arhat, and those who seek the fruit of the pratyekabuddha, they should not study this Dharma. But those who seek anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi should study this Dharma."

At that time, the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta, knowing that the Buddha, the World-Honored One, and the assembly of bhikṣus had nowhere yet to eat, told the wet-nurse: "Please go quickly into the palace and bring food to offer to the Buddha and the Saṃgha." The wet-nurse quickly brought a hundred flavors of food and drink, filling a single vessel, and gave it to the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta. Having received the food, the bodhisattva made this vow before the Buddha: "As the Tathāgata has taught, all dharmas are inexhaustible. If these words are true, may this food also be inexhaustible, so that the assembly of bhikṣus may all be fully satisfied."

Then the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta filled a single bowl with food and offered it to the Buddha. He then told the bhikṣus: "Venerable Ones, out of compassion for me, each of you please accept this food." He further said: "What I give is given neither through body nor through mind. It is free from the three karmas; it does not seek any reward. It does not abide in the conditioned or the unconditioned. It is not attached to worldly dharmas. Nor does it abide in the śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, or buddha-bodhi."

At that time not one bhikṣu extended his bowl to accept the food. The Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta said: "Venerable Ones, please accept this food. You delight in begging for food, and I now delight in giving. I seek nothing from you." Then the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta made another vow: "The Buddha's words are true. Just as Mañjuśrī and hundreds of thousands of koṭis of bodhisattvas will, in a future age, in the buddha-land of the Buddha Adorned-with-Merit-King, all become buddhas, each bearing the same name. If this is true, may the food in this vessel fill the bowls of all the bhikṣus, and may the food in this vessel be inexhaustible." By the power of this vow, food spontaneously filled the bowl of every bhikṣu.

Then the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta further distributed the remaining food in the vessel to all the people of the great city of Vaiśālī, satisfying them all. Still the food in the vessel was not exhausted.

At that time the World-Honored One told the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta: "There are five kinds of treasures that can make a bodhisattva's practice of giving pure. What are the five? First, giving without any expectation. Second, giving with a mind free from attachment. Third, not giving rise to marks in giving. Fourth, not seeing the reward of giving. Fifth, not causing the recipient to give anything in return."

The Buddha said: "There are also four kinds of treasures that a bodhisattva who practices giving should always bear in mind. What are the four? First, always being mindful of the samādhi of emptiness. Second, always being mindful of the Buddha. Third, always being mindful of great compassion. Fourth, always being mindful that one does not seek any reward for oneself. If a bodhisattva practices giving in this way, it is pure giving."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "This Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta will, in a future age, after thirty kalpas have passed, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and be called Unfailing-Power-and-Renown Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, Possessed of Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Men, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One, appearing in the world. That buddha's assembly will contain boundless bodhisattvas, all abiding on the ground of non-retrogression, with limitless majestic power and immeasurable lifespans."

At that time the World-Honored One and the great assembly of bhikṣus, having accepted the food, returned to their original dwelling.

Then Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what is the name of this sūtra, and how should we uphold it?"

The Buddha said: "This sūtra is called 'The Practice of Bodhi.' It is also called 'All Dharmas as the Foremost.' Uphold it in this way."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the Bodhisattva Ratnākaradatta and the entire great assembly of devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and others, having heard what the Buddha taught, all rejoiced greatly, accepted with faith, and put it into practice.
