# 佛說決定義經

## The Sūtra on Definitive Meaning Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0762

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Fǎxián (法賢).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī at the Jetavana, the park of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of 1,250 monks.

At that time, the Bhagavān addressed the monks: "I shall now proclaim to you the profound definitive correct meaning (good at the beginning, good in the middle, good at the end; deep in meaning, pure and unmixed, complete, pure and white, bearing the marks of the holy life). Listen attentively and reflect well upon it!"

The monks addressed the Buddha: "Excellent, Bhagavān! What is called the definitive correct meaning? We wish to hear it. We humbly ask the Bhagavān to explain it for us."

The Buddha then told the monks: "The definitive meaning refers to: the five aggregates, the five aggregates of clinging, the eighteen elements, the twelve sense-bases, the twelve links of dependent origination, the four noble truths, the twenty-two faculties, the ten powers of the Tathāgata, the four kinds of fearlessness, the four dhyānas, the four formless absorptions, the four immeasurable practices, the four unobstructed knowledges, the four samādhi-perceptions, the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of supernatural power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold noble path. These dharmas are called the definitive correct meaning.

"The five aggregates are: the aggregate of form, the aggregate of feeling, the aggregate of perception, the aggregate of formations, and the aggregate of consciousness. These are called the five aggregates.

"The five aggregates of clinging are: the clinging-aggregate of form, the clinging-aggregate of feeling, the clinging-aggregate of perception, the clinging-aggregate of formations, and the clinging-aggregate of consciousness. These are called the five aggregates of clinging.

"The eighteen elements are: the eye-element, the form-element, the eye-consciousness element; the ear-element, the sound-element, the ear-consciousness element; the nose-element, the smell-element, the nose-consciousness element; the tongue-element, the taste-element, the tongue-consciousness element; the body-element, the touch-element, the body-consciousness element; the mind-element, the dharma-element, and the mind-consciousness element. These are called the eighteen elements.

"The twelve sense-bases are: the internal eye-base, the external form-base; the internal ear-base, the external sound-base; the internal nose-base, the external smell-base; the internal tongue-base, the external taste-base; the internal body-base, the external touch-base; the internal mind-base, and the external dharma-base. These are called the twelve sense-bases.

"The twelve links of dependent origination are: conditioned by ignorance, formations arise; conditioned by formations, consciousness arises; conditioned by consciousness, name-and-form arises; conditioned by name-and-form, the six sense-bases arise; conditioned by the six sense-bases, contact arises; conditioned by contact, feeling arises; conditioned by feeling, craving arises; conditioned by craving, clinging arises; conditioned by clinging, becoming arises; conditioned by becoming, birth arises; conditioned by birth, old age, death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, and distress arise. Thus the entire great mass of suffering is assembled.

"Because of these conditions, there is the aggregate-dharma. If the conditions do not exist, the aggregate-dharma ceases. That is to say: when ignorance ceases, formations cease; when formations cease, consciousness ceases; when consciousness ceases, name-and-form ceases; when name-and-form ceases, the six sense-bases cease; when the six sense-bases cease, contact ceases; when contact ceases, feeling ceases; when feeling ceases, craving ceases; when craving ceases, clinging ceases; when clinging ceases, becoming ceases; when becoming ceases, birth ceases; when birth ceases, old age, death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, and distress cease. Thus the entire great mass of suffering ceases.

"These dharmas arise and cease because ignorance serves as their condition. What is this condition? It means: not understanding the past, not understanding the future, not understanding the present; not understanding internal dharmas, not understanding external dharmas, not understanding both internal and external dharmas; not understanding wholesome karma, not understanding unwholesome karma, not understanding both wholesome and unwholesome karma; not understanding causes, not understanding effects, not understanding both causes and effects; not understanding already-arisen conditioned dharmas, not understanding not-yet-arisen conditioned dharmas, not understanding presently-arising conditioned dharmas; not understanding the Buddha, not understanding the Dharma, not understanding the Saṅgha; not understanding the dharma of suffering, not understanding the dharma of origination, not understanding the dharma of cessation, not understanding the dharma of the path; not understanding wholesome dharmas, not understanding unwholesome dharmas; not understanding sinful dharmas, not understanding meritorious dharmas; not understanding what should be practiced, not understanding what should not be practiced; not understanding superior and inferior dharmas; not understanding conditioned and unconditioned dharmas. Because of such failures to understand, one cannot give rise to signless wisdom regarding the dharmas of dark and bright and so forth.

"Because of this ignorance and failure to understand, there is transgression, obstructing the wisdom of reality, and one cannot realize the quiescent nirvāṇa. This ignorance is by nature confused and dark, purely a dharma of delusion, like a blind person who walks only on dark paths, gathering dust and defilements, diminishing wisdom, and unable to escape from the dharmas of obstruction. This ignorance is an obstacle to knowledge. Ignorance is pain; ignorance is a vessel of poison; ignorance is the binding of ropes; ignorance is the root of poison; ignorance is the tree of poison; ignorance is the root affliction; ignorance is the basis of subsidiary afflictions; ignorance is the place of inversion; ignorance is the room of darkness; ignorance is the darkness of delusion. Meanings such as these are what is called ignorance.

"Because of this as a condition, the dharma of formations arises. Formations are of three kinds: bodily formations, verbal formations, and mental formations. Bodily formations: the in-breath and out-breath depend upon and belong to the body. Because they depend on the body, the in-breath and out-breath are called bodily formations. Verbal formations: deliberation and discrimination; these discriminative and deliberative words are therefore called verbal formations. Mental formations: the mind and its mental factors of greed, hatred, delusion, and so forth depend upon and belong to the mind. Because they depend on the mind, they are called mental formations.

"There are further three kinds of formations: meritorious formations, demeritorious formations, and imperturbable formations. These are called the three formations.

"Conditioned by formations, the dharma of consciousness arises. Consciousness is of six kinds: eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, and mind-consciousness. These are called the six consciousnesses.

"Conditioned by consciousness, name-and-form arises. Name-and-form refers to: the aggregate of form, the aggregate of feeling, the aggregate of perception, the aggregate of formations, and the aggregate of consciousness. Form refers to the internal five sense-objects, all constituted by the four great elements. The four great elements are: earth, water, fire, and wind. Earth's nature is solidity and heaviness; water's nature is flowing and moistening; warmth is fire; movement is wind. The five sense-objects constituted by these four great elements are called the aggregate of form. Among these aggregate-dharmas, four aggregates are called 'name' and one aggregate is called 'form.' These five aggregates are called name-and-form.

"Conditioned by name-and-form, the six sense-bases arise. The six sense-bases are the six internal bases: the eye-base, ear-base, nose-base, tongue-base, body-base, and mind-base. These are called the six sense-bases.

"Conditioned by the six sense-bases, the dharma of contact arises. Contact is of six kinds: eye-contact, ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, and mind-contact. These are called contact.

"Conditioned by contact, the dharma of feeling arises. Feeling is of three kinds: painful feeling, pleasant feeling, and neutral feeling. In this way, eye-contact gives rise to these three feelings, and so on through mind-contact likewise. This is called feeling.

"Conditioned by feeling, the dharma of craving arises. Craving is of six kinds: the eye observing forms, the ear listening to sounds, the nose smelling scents, the tongue knowing tastes, the body sensing touch, and the mind discriminating dharmas. Because of craving for these six, this is called craving.

"Conditioned by craving, the dharma of clinging arises. Clinging is of four kinds: clinging to sensual desires, clinging to views, clinging to rules and rituals, and clinging to the doctrine of self. Because craving intensifies, this is called clinging.

"Conditioned by clinging, the dharma of becoming arises. Becoming is of three kinds: desire-realm becoming, form-realm becoming, and formless-realm becoming.

"Desire-realm becoming refers to: the ten evil destinies, and the human and heavenly realms.

"The ten evil destinies are the eight hells: (1) Saṃjīva (Reviving), (2) Kālasūtra (Black Cord), (3) Saṃghāta (Crushing), (4) Raurava (Screaming), (5) Mahāraurava (Great Screaming), (6) Tapana (Scorching), (7) Pratāpana (Great Scorching), (8) Avīci (Uninterrupted), (9) the animal realm, and (10) the hungry ghost realm. These ten are called the evil destinies.

"The human realm refers to the four great continents: Jambudvīpa in the south, Pūrvavideha in the east, Aparagodānīya in the west, and Uttarakuru in the north. Jambudvīpa measures 7,000 yojanas in length and breadth; this continent is broad in the north and narrow in the south, like the shape of a chariot. Pūrvavideha measures 8,000 yojanas; that continent's shape resembles a half-moon. Aparagodānīya measures 9,000 yojanas; that continent's shape resembles a full moon. Uttarakuru measures 10,000 yojanas; that continent's shape is a square with straight sides, like a pond. These four continents are called the human realm.

"The heavenly realm: the six heavens of the desire-realm, namely: the Heaven of the Four Kings, Trāyastriṃśa, Yāma, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, and Paranirmitavaśavartin. These are called the six desire-realm heavens. All these destinies are called desire-realm becoming.

"Form-realm becoming consists of eighteen heavens: Brahma-pāriṣadya, Brahma-purohita, Mahābrahmā, Ābhā, Apramāṇābhā, Ābhāsvara, Śubha, Apramāṇaśubha, Śubhakṛtsna, Anabhraka, Puṇyaprasava, Bṛhatphala, Asaṃjñisattva, Avṛha, Atapa, Sudṛśa, Sudarśana, and Akaniṣṭha. These heavens are called form-realm becoming.

"Formless-realm becoming consists of four heavens: the Sphere of Infinite Space, the Sphere of Infinite Consciousness, the Sphere of Nothingness, and the Sphere of Neither Perception nor Non-Perception. These heavens are called formless-realm becoming. These three kinds of becoming are called becoming.

"Conditioned by becoming, the dharma of birth arises. Birth means that sentient beings, having abandoned these aggregates, according to their karmic retribution, in the various realms and destinies, the appearance of aggregates again manifests. This is called birth.

"Conditioned by birth, old age and death come into being. Old age means that the aggregate-dharmas of sentient beings ripen; the mind and consciousness become confused; much is forgotten; the hair turns white and the face wrinkles; bodily strength weakens; one relies on a staff to move about; one pants and groans; strength gradually diminishes; and the sense-faculties decay. This is called old age. What then is death? It means that sentient beings, according to their accumulated karma, when their lifespan is exhausted, consciousness abandons what it grasps, the life-faculty expires, and the aggregates disperse. This is called death. Old age has no fixed characteristic; it is established in conjunction with death. The two dharmas are combined as one; therefore they are called old age and death. These are called the twelve links of conditioned dharmas.

"What then are the four noble truths? They are: the truth of suffering, the truth of origination, the truth of cessation, and the truth of the path. The truth of suffering: the suffering of birth, old age, sickness, death, separation from what is loved, encounter with what is hated, not getting what one wants, and the five aggregates of clinging. Such sufferings are called the truth of suffering. The truth of origination: craving. Because of this craving, attachment arises. Because of attachment, one creates karma and moistens rebirth. The accumulated cause is called the truth of origination. The truth of cessation: the complete exhaustion of craving and all remaining afflictions, realizing the principle of quiescent extinction. This is called the truth of cessation. The truth of the path: the eightfold noble path, namely: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration. This eightfold noble path is called the truth of the path. These four truths, being observable by noble wisdom, are called the four noble truths.

"What then are the twenty-two faculties? They are: the eye-faculty, ear-faculty, nose-faculty, tongue-faculty, body-faculty, male-faculty, female-faculty, life-faculty, mind-faculty, pleasant-feeling faculty, painful-feeling faculty, joy-faculty, sorrow-faculty, equanimity-faculty, faith-faculty, effort-faculty, mindfulness-faculty, concentration-faculty, wisdom-faculty, the faculty of 'I shall come to know what is not yet known,' the faculty of perfect knowledge, and the faculty of one who has fully known. These are called the twenty-two faculties.

"What then are the ten powers? The Tathāgata's wisdom, regarding the dharma of what is possible and impossible for all sentient beings, knows as it truly is. This is called the Tathāgata's first power: the wisdom-power of knowing what is possible and impossible.

"The Tathāgata further, regarding all karmic actions of sentient beings in the past, present, and future, wherever they arise, the causes, conditions, and results: the Buddha, by his wisdom-power, fully knows them all. This is the Tathāgata's second power: the wisdom-power of knowing karmic retribution.

"The Tathāgata further, regarding all dharmas of meditation, liberation, samādhi, and samāpatti, and the dharma of exhausting all outflows: the Buddha, by his wisdom-power, knows as it truly is. This is the Tathāgata's third power: the power of concentration.

"The Tathāgata further, regarding the various distinctions between superior and inferior faculties of sentient beings: the Buddha, by his wisdom-power, knows as it truly is. This is the Tathāgata's fourth power: the wisdom-power of knowing superior and inferior faculties.

"The Tathāgata further, regarding all the various kinds of aspirations and inclinations of sentient beings: the Buddha, by his wisdom-power, knows as it truly is. This is the Tathāgata's fifth power: the wisdom-power of knowing aspirations and inclinations.

"The Tathāgata further, regarding all the various distinctions of realms and destinies of sentient beings: the Buddha, by his wisdom-power, knows as it truly is. This is the Tathāgata's sixth power: the wisdom-power of knowing realms and destinies.

"The Tathāgata further, regarding all the desires and inclinations of sentient beings: the Buddha, by his wisdom-power, knows as it truly is. This is the Tathāgata's seventh power: the wisdom-power of knowing desires.

"The Tathāgata further, regarding the immeasurably many past lives, all the various matters of sentient beings, namely: one life, ten lives, a hundred lives, a thousand lives, a hundred thousand lives, up to countless hundreds of thousands of millions of lives; through countless kalpas of formation and destruction; the sentient beings therein, dying here and being born there, dying there and being born here; their clans, noble or lowly, their names, food and drink, suffering and happiness, long or short lifespans, with such and such characteristics, such and such causes and conditions: such matters through immeasurable past lives the Buddha, by his wisdom-power, is fully able to know. This is the Tathāgata's eighth power: the wisdom-power of knowing past lives.

"The Tathāgata further, with the purified divine eye, observes sentient beings, noble and lowly, superior and inferior, beautiful and ugly, being born and dying, born in good destinies or falling into evil destinies. Those sentient beings who have committed unwholesome bodily actions, performed wrong conduct; who have committed unwholesome verbal actions, slandering the noble ones; who have committed unwholesome mental actions, giving rise to wrong views: because of these causes and conditions, after death they fall into the evil destinies. And those sentient beings who have performed wholesome bodily actions, not engaging in wrong conduct; wholesome verbal actions, not slandering the noble ones; wholesome mental actions, giving rise to right views: because of these causes and conditions, after death they are born in the human and heavenly realms. Such matters the Tathāgata's divine eye is fully able to observe and see. This is the Tathāgata's ninth power: the wisdom-power of the divine eye.

"Further, the Tathāgata knows that for those sentient beings whose outflows are exhausted, who have realized the outflow-free liberation, the liberation of wisdom, who by their own supernatural power have realized such dharmas: 'My births are exhausted, the holy life has been lived, what was to be done has been done, I shall not be subject to further becoming.' Such dharmas the Buddha, by his wisdom-power, is fully able to know. This is the Tathāgata's tenth power: the wisdom-power of knowing the exhaustion of outflows. By these ten powers, the Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened One, makes the lion's roar amid the great assembly and turns the great Dharma-wheel.

"What then are the four kinds of fearlessness? The Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened One, proclaims amid the great assembly: 'By my own wisdom, riding the path of reality, I have come to attain perfect awakening, and I make the lion's roar and turn the wondrous Dharma-wheel. There is no śramaṇa, brāhmaṇa, deva, human, māra, or Brahmā who can equal me.' This is the Tathāgata's fearlessness of all-knowledge.

"The Tathāgata has attained the supreme fruit of merit at the place of peaceful, blissful quiescence. All outflows are exhausted; habitual tendencies have perished. This is the Tathāgata's fearlessness of exhausted outflows.

"The Tathāgata further, for all the śrāvaka assembly, reveals the path of suffering, teaching how to abandon afflictions and reach the end of suffering. This is the Tathāgata's fearlessness of proclaiming the path out of suffering.

"The Tathāgata further, for all the śrāvakas, explains all obstructing dharmas and enables them to escape. This is the Tathāgata's fearlessness of proclaiming obstructing dharmas. These are called the four kinds of fearlessness.

"What then are the four dhyānas? Separated from desires and all defiled dharmas, having eliminated doubting and discriminating thoughts: this is called the first dhyāna, the concentration of joy and bliss born of separation.

"Further, having separated from desire and eliminated discriminating thoughts, when all external dharmas are completely extinguished and one is internally settled in concentration, gathering both internal and external dharmas so that all return to unity: this is called the second dhyāna, the concentration of joy and bliss born of concentration.

"Further, abandoning joy, bliss, and the perception of deliberation, one experiences bodily bliss without receiving anything: this is called the third dhyāna, the concentration of wondrous bliss free of joy.

"Further, abandoning the dharmas of suffering and pleasure, with no thought of sorrow or joy, all dharmas are pure: this is called the fourth dhyāna, the concentration of pure equanimity and mindfulness. These are called the four dhyānas.

"What then are the four formless absorptions? Cultivating that absorption, one becomes weary of the lower form-characteristics and delights in the formless above. Because there is no form, there is only empty space; one contemplates empty space as having no limit. Perceiving thus, this is called the absorption of the Sphere of Infinite Space.

"Further, having departed from the object of infinite space, one further perceives that the perceiving consciousness is also infinite. This is called the absorption of the Sphere of Infinite Consciousness.

"Further, having departed from the perceiving consciousness, one further perceives that both the perceiver and the perceived are utterly nonexistent. This is called the absorption of the Sphere of Nothingness.

"Further, having departed from nothingness, one further perceives that there is no coarse perception, yet fine perception is not absent. This is called the absorption of the Sphere of Neither Perception nor Non-Perception. These are called the four formless absorptions.

"What then are the four immeasurable practices? They are: immeasurable loving-kindness, immeasurable compassion, immeasurable sympathetic joy, and immeasurable equanimity. If a monk possesses great loving-kindness, toward all sentient beings, without enmity or favoritism, he is equally able to give joy. Constantly practicing such an immeasurable mind of loving-kindness, this is called the immeasurable practice of loving-kindness.

"If a monk possesses great compassion, toward all sentient beings, without enmity or favoritism, he is equally able to remove suffering. Constantly practicing such an immeasurable mind of compassion, this is called the immeasurable practice of compassion.

"If a monk possesses great sympathetic joy, toward all sentient beings, without enmity or favoritism, he equally bestows gladness. Constantly practicing such an immeasurable mind of sympathetic joy, this is called the immeasurable practice of sympathetic joy.

"If a monk, toward all sentient beings, without enmity or favoritism, abides in equanimity, constantly practicing thus, this is called the immeasurable practice of equanimity. These are called the four immeasurable practices.

"What then are the four unobstructed knowledges? They are: unobstructed knowledge of expression, unobstructed knowledge of eloquence, unobstructed knowledge of dharma, and unobstructed knowledge of meaning. Unobstructed expression means: taking sound as its object, among all sounds, speech and expression are unobstructed. This is called unobstructed knowledge of expression. Unobstructed eloquence means: the four kinds and seven kinds of eloquence; whenever there is debate, one is swift and unhindered. This is called unobstructed knowledge of eloquence. Unobstructed dharma means: taking sentences and phrases as its object, whatever the teaching, there is nothing one does not penetrate. This is called unobstructed knowledge of dharma. Unobstructed meaning means: taking meaning as its object, whatever meaning is expressed, there is nothing one does not comprehend. This is called unobstructed knowledge of meaning. These are called the four unobstructed knowledges.

"What then are the four samādhi-perceptions? If a monk contemplates thus: 'This body of mine, from the crown of the head to the soles of the feet, consists of hair, body-hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, and various impure substances combined together. It is like a granary packed with stores of grain: a person entering it can clearly distinguish what is rice and what is wheat. Such an assemblage is called a granary.' The monk's contemplative perception of his own body is likewise. One who performs this contemplation is able to cut off the dharma of craving. This is called the first samādhi-perception.

"Further, after the monk has contemplated the body, in samādhi he attains the flavor of tranquility. He further contemplates: 'The pleasant flavor obtained in this samādhi is also without perception.' It is like a lotus that grows from the water; the lotus has no perception: 'I grow from the water and experience coolness.' The monk's contemplation is likewise. One who can contemplate thus immediately attains the flavor of dharma-vision and tranquility. This is called the second samādhi-perception.

"Further, in samādhi, the monk performs the perception of luminosity: contemplating like the sun, there is no remaining darkness. Contemplating thus, continuously without interruption, day and night, light and dark, all are without difference. Performing this luminous perception, it is pure and unmixed. It is like an autumn when clouds, haze, and darkness obscure the sun's light so it does not appear; if a person with clear eyes contemplates the sun's light, it is pure and unmixed. The monk's contemplation is likewise. One who can contemplate thus immediately attains the wisdom of luminosity appearing before them. This is called the third samādhi-perception.

"Further, in samādhi, the monk contemplates thus: cutting off suffering and pleasure, up to sorrow and joy, with only equanimity and mindfulness, the two kinds of purity. It is like a person skilled at driving a chariot, proceeding without obstruction on level ground. The monk's contemplation is likewise. One who can contemplate thus immediately attains the benefit of wisdom appearing before them. This is called the fourth samādhi-perception. These are called the four samādhi-perceptions.

"What then are the four foundations of mindfulness? They are: body, feeling, mind, and dharmas. If a monk contemplates the body as impure, filled with filth and foulness, without anything real: such contemplation is called the foundation of mindfulness of the body.

"Further, contemplating all feelings, both internal and external, as entirely suffering: such contemplation is called the foundation of mindfulness of feeling.

"Further, contemplating the mind and its mental factors as entirely impermanent: such contemplation is called the foundation of mindfulness of the mind.

"Further, contemplating both internal and external dharmas, not generating the perception of self in these dharmas: such contemplation is called the foundation of mindfulness of dharmas. These are called the four foundations of mindfulness.

"What then are the four right efforts? Already-arisen unwholesome dharmas: one should arouse vigor to diligently eliminate and cut them off, causing them all to cease. This is the first right effort.

"Further, not-yet-arisen unwholesome dharmas: one should arouse vigor to guard against and cut them off, causing them not to arise. This is the second right effort.

"Further, not-yet-arisen wholesome dharmas: one should arouse vigor to cause all wholesome dharmas to arise and grow. This is the third right effort.

"Further, already-arisen wholesome dharmas: one should arouse vigor to cause them to increase, become firm, and reach fulfillment. This is the fourth right effort. These are called the four right efforts.

"What then are the four bases of supernatural power? They are: aspiration, effort, mind, and investigation. If a monk, regarding defiled dharmas, abandons deluded thoughts, and regarding wholesome dharmas, arouses aspiration, advancing in goodness without weariness: this is called the base of supernatural power of aspiration.

"Further, in wholesome dharmas, diligently cultivating the practices, correctly practicing without regression: this is called the base of supernatural power of effort.

"Further, in all dharmas, abandoning wrong thinking, the mind correctly discriminating: this is called the base of supernatural power of mind.

"Further, with correct wisdom, contemplating the inner body, finding no self in the inner body; further contemplating external objects, finding no dharma in external objects; the essences of both self and dharma being nonexistent, approaching and realizing the twofold emptiness: this is called the base of supernatural power of investigation. These are called the four bases of supernatural power.

"What then are the five faculties? They are: the faculty of faith, the faculty of effort, the faculty of mindfulness, the faculty of concentration, and the faculty of wisdom. These are called the five faculties.

"What then are the five powers? They are: the power of faith, the power of effort, the power of mindfulness, the power of concentration, and the power of wisdom. These are called the five powers.

"What then are the seven factors of awakening? They are: the factor of investigation of dharmas, the factor of effort, the factor of joy, the factor of tranquility, the factor of equanimity, the factor of mindfulness, and the factor of concentration. These are called the seven factors of awakening.

"What then is the eightfold noble path? It is: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration. Right view means: believing in the dharma of giving, believing in father and mother, in wholesome actions and unwholesome actions. Such wholesome and unwholesome karma will have its fruits and retribution. There is this world and the next. In these worlds there are sentient beings and there are arhats who, having practiced correctly, by their own supernatural power see such dharmas: 'My births are exhausted, the holy life has been lived, what was to be done has been done, I shall not be subject to further becoming.' Such matters are called right view.

"What is right intention? Abandoning wrong thinking (wrong thinking means: giving rise to incorrect views, arousing greed, hatred, and delusion). Being far removed from these is called right intention.

"What is right speech? Abandoning false speech, frivolous talk, divisive speech, harsh speech, and the like. This is called right speech.

"What is right action? Abandoning killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, and the like. This is called right action.

"What is right livelihood? Possessing right view, leaving home in the true Dharma, wearing the Dharma-robes, abandoning all wrong conduct, having faith in the true Dharma, and in all matters through to the use of food, drink, sitting, and lying, all according to the true Dharma. This is called right livelihood.

"What is right effort? Abandoning wrong exertion. In the true Dharma, arousing correct exertion. This is called right effort.

"What is right mindfulness? Abandoning wrong mindfulness. Constantly mindful of the true Dharma, remembering it in the mind without forgetting. This is called right mindfulness.

"What is right concentration? When the mind and mental factors do not give rise to distraction, free from all clinging conditions, with definitive and correct contemplation in śamatha and vipaśyanā. This is called right concentration. These are called the eightfold noble path."

When the Bhagavān had spoken this teaching, he further told the assembly of monks: "You should know that what I have proclaimed, the definitive correct Dharma, is good at the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end; deep in meaning, pure and unmixed, pure and perfectly complete."

At that time the assembly of monks, having heard what the Buddha taught, were all greatly delighted and faithfully received and practiced it.
