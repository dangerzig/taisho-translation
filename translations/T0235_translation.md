# 金剛般若波羅蜜經
## The Diamond Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0235

Translated from the Chinese. Later Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus. At that time, when the meal hour came, the World-Honored One put on his robe and took up his bowl, and entered the great city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. Within the city, he begged in sequence from house to house, then returned to his place. When the meal was finished, he put away his robe and bowl, washed his feet, spread his seat, and sat down.

At that time, the Elder Subhūti, who was in the great assembly, rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms reverently, and addressed the Buddha: "How rare, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata skillfully protects and is mindful of all bodhisattvas, and skillfully entrusts and instructs all bodhisattvas. World-Honored One! When good men and good women have aroused the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, how should they abide? How should they subdue their minds?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Subhūti! Just as you have said: 'The Tathāgata skillfully protects and is mindful of all bodhisattvas, and skillfully entrusts and instructs all bodhisattvas.' Listen attentively now, and I shall explain it for you. When good men and good women arouse the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, they should abide thus and subdue their minds thus."

"Yes, World-Honored One! We delight in hearing."

The Buddha told Subhūti: "All bodhisattva-mahāsattvas should subdue their minds thus: 'All the various kinds of sentient beings, whether born from eggs, born from wombs, born from moisture, or born by transformation; whether having form or without form; whether having perception or without perception or neither having perception nor not having perception: I shall cause them all to enter the nirvāṇa without remainder and bring them to final liberation.' Yet when immeasurable, countless, boundless sentient beings are thus brought to liberation, in truth no sentient being is brought to liberation. Why? Subhūti! If a bodhisattva has the mark of a self, the mark of a person, the mark of a sentient being, or the mark of a lifespan, then he is not a bodhisattva.

"Furthermore, Subhūti! A bodhisattva should practice giving without abiding in anything. This means giving without abiding in form, and giving without abiding in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas. Subhūti! A bodhisattva should give in this way, not abiding in marks. Why? If a bodhisattva gives without abiding in marks, the merit of that is inconceivable.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the space of the eastern direction be conceived of?"

"No, World-Honored One!"

"Subhūti! Can the space of the south, west, north, the four intermediate directions, above, and below be conceived of?"

"No, World-Honored One!"

"Subhūti! The merit of a bodhisattva who gives without abiding in marks is just as inconceivable as that. Subhūti! Bodhisattvas should abide only as instructed.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the Tathāgata be seen by means of bodily marks?"

"No, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata cannot be seen by means of bodily marks. Why? What the Tathāgata calls bodily marks are not bodily marks."

The Buddha told Subhūti: "All that has marks is illusory. If one sees that all marks are not marks, then one sees the Tathāgata."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Will there be sentient beings who, hearing these words and phrases, can give rise to true faith?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "Do not say such a thing. After the Tathāgata's passing, in the last five hundred years, there will be those who uphold the precepts and cultivate merit who are able to give rise to faith in these phrases and take them as real. You should know that such people have planted good roots not merely with one Buddha, two Buddhas, or three, four, or five Buddhas, but have already planted all manner of good roots with immeasurable thousands of myriads of Buddhas. Those who, hearing these phrases, give rise to even a single thought of pure faith: Subhūti! The Tathāgata fully knows and fully sees that such sentient beings obtain such immeasurable merit. Why? Because these sentient beings no longer have the mark of a self, the mark of a person, the mark of a sentient being, or the mark of a lifespan.

"They have no dharma-mark, and also no non-dharma-mark. Why? If the minds of these sentient beings grasp at marks, then they are attached to self, person, sentient being, and lifespan.

"If they grasp the mark of dharmas, they are attached to self, person, sentient being, and lifespan. Why? If they grasp the mark of non-dharmas, they are attached to self, person, sentient being, and lifespan. Therefore, one should not grasp dharmas, and one should not grasp non-dharmas. For this reason, the Tathāgata always says: 'You bhikṣus should know that the Dharma I teach is like a raft. Even the Dharma should be abandoned, how much more non-dharma.'

"Subhūti! What do you think? Has the Tathāgata attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi? Has the Tathāgata taught any dharma?"

Subhūti said: "As I understand the meaning of what the Buddha has said, there is no fixed dharma called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, and there is no fixed dharma the Tathāgata could teach. Why? The dharma the Tathāgata teaches cannot be grasped and cannot be spoken. It is not a dharma and not a non-dharma. Why is this? All worthies and sages differ through the unconditioned dharma."

"Subhūti! What do you think? If a person filled the three-thousand great-thousand world system with the seven treasures and gave them as alms, would the merit obtained by that person be great or not?"

Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One! Why? This merit is not the nature of merit; therefore the Tathāgata says the merit is great."

"If again there is a person who receives and upholds even as few as four lines of verse from this sūtra and explains them for others, that person's merit surpasses the former. Why? Subhūti! All Buddhas and the dharma of all Buddhas' anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi all come forth from this sūtra. Subhūti! What is called the Buddha-Dharma is not the Buddha-Dharma.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can a stream-enterer have the thought 'I have attained the fruit of stream-entry'?"

Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Why? 'Stream-enterer' means 'one who has entered the stream,' yet there is nothing entered. One does not enter form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas. This is called stream-entry."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can a once-returner have the thought 'I have attained the fruit of once-returning'?"

Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Why? 'Once-returner' means 'one who goes and comes once,' yet in truth there is no going or coming. This is called once-returning."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can a non-returner have the thought 'I have attained the fruit of non-returning'?"

Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Why? 'Non-returner' means 'one who does not come,' yet in truth there is no not-coming. Therefore it is called non-returning."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can an arhat have the thought 'I have attained the path of the arhat'?"

Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Why? In truth there is no dharma called 'arhat.' World-Honored One! If an arhat had the thought 'I have attained the path of the arhat,' then he would be attached to self, person, sentient being, and lifespan. World-Honored One! The Buddha has said that I have attained the samādhi of non-contention, that I am foremost among all people, the foremost arhat free from desire. Yet I do not have the thought: 'I am an arhat free from desire.' World-Honored One! If I had the thought 'I have attained the path of the arhat,' the World-Honored One would not say that Subhūti delights in the practice of araṇya. Because Subhūti in truth does not practice anything, he is called 'Subhūti who delights in the practice of araṇya.'"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "What do you think? Did the Tathāgata, when he was with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, obtain any dharma?"

"World-Honored One! When the Tathāgata was with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, he truly obtained no dharma."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does a bodhisattva adorn buddha-lands?"

"No, World-Honored One! Why? To adorn buddha-lands is not to adorn; it is called adorning."

"Therefore, Subhūti, all bodhisattva-mahāsattvas should give rise to a pure mind in this way. They should not give rise to a mind that abides in form. They should not give rise to a mind that abides in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas. They should give rise to a mind that abides in nothing.

"Subhūti! Suppose there were a person with a body as large as Mount Sumeru, the king of mountains. What do you think? Would that body be large?"

Subhūti said: "Very large, World-Honored One! Why? The Buddha says that which is not a body is called a great body."

"Subhūti! If there were as many Ganges Rivers as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, what do you think? Would the grains of sand in all those Ganges Rivers be many?"

Subhūti said: "Very many, World-Honored One! The Ganges Rivers alone would already be countless, not to mention their sands."

"Subhūti! I now tell you truthfully: if a good man or good woman filled three-thousand great-thousand world systems as numerous as all the grains of sand in all those Ganges Rivers with the seven treasures and gave them as alms, would the merit obtained be great?"

Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "If a good man or good woman receives and upholds even four lines of verse from this sūtra and explains them for others, that merit surpasses the former.

"Furthermore, Subhūti! Wherever this sūtra is taught, even as few as four lines of verse, you should know that this place is one that all the world's devas, humans, and asuras should make offerings to, as if it were a Buddha's stūpa or temple. How much more so for a person who can fully receive, uphold, read, and recite it! Subhūti! You should know that such a person has accomplished the most supreme, foremost, and rare dharma. Wherever this sūtra is found, there the Buddha is present, together with his honored disciples."

At that time, Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What should this sūtra be called? How should we receive and uphold it?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "This sūtra is called the Diamond Prajñāpāramitā. By this name you should receive and uphold it. Why? Subhūti! The Buddha teaches that prajñāpāramitā is not prajñāpāramitā. Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata have any dharma that he teaches?"

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has nothing that he teaches."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Are the motes of dust in the three-thousand great-thousand world system many or not?"

Subhūti said: "Very many, World-Honored One!"

"Subhūti! The Tathāgata says these motes of dust are not motes of dust; they are called motes of dust. The Tathāgata says the world is not a world; it is called a world.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the Tathāgata be seen by means of the thirty-two marks?"

"No, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata cannot be seen by means of the thirty-two marks. Why? The Tathāgata says the thirty-two marks are not marks; they are called the thirty-two marks."

"Subhūti! If a good man or good woman gave as many lives as there are grains of sand in the Ganges River as alms, and if again there were a person who received and upheld even four lines of verse from this sūtra and explained them for others, that person's merit would be far greater."

At that time, Subhūti, having heard this sūtra spoken, deeply understood its meaning and purport. He wept with tears and addressed the Buddha: "How rare, World-Honored One! The Buddha has spoken such a profoundly deep sūtra. From the time I first attained the wisdom-eye until now, I have never heard such a sūtra. World-Honored One! If there is a person who, hearing this sūtra, gives rise to a pure mind of faith, that person gives rise to the true mark of reality. You should know that such a person has accomplished the foremost and most rare merit. World-Honored One! This true mark of reality is not a mark; therefore the Tathāgata calls it the true mark of reality. World-Honored One! That I now hear this sūtra, and believe, understand, receive, and uphold it: this is not difficult. If in a future age, in the last five hundred years, there are sentient beings who hear this sūtra and believe, understand, receive, and uphold it, such persons shall be the foremost and most rare. Why? Such persons have no mark of a self, no mark of a person, no mark of a sentient being, no mark of a lifespan. Why is this? The mark of a self is not a mark; the mark of a person, the mark of a sentient being, the mark of a lifespan are all not marks. Why? To be apart from all marks is to be called all the Buddhas."

The Buddha told Subhūti: "So it is, so it is! If there is a person who, hearing this sūtra, is not alarmed, not frightened, not afraid, you should know that such a person is most rare. Why? Subhūti! What the Tathāgata calls the foremost pāramitā is not the foremost pāramitā; it is called the foremost pāramitā. Subhūti! The patience pāramitā: the Tathāgata says it is not the patience pāramitā. Why? Subhūti! In the past, when King Kalirāja cut my body to pieces, at that time I had no mark of a self, no mark of a person, no mark of a sentient being, no mark of a lifespan. Why? In those past times, when my limbs were being severed joint by joint, if I had had the mark of a self, the mark of a person, the mark of a sentient being, or the mark of a lifespan, I would have given rise to anger and hatred. Subhūti! I further recall that in the past, for five hundred lifetimes, I was the sage of patience. During all those lifetimes, I had no mark of a self, no mark of a person, no mark of a sentient being, no mark of a lifespan. Therefore, Subhūti! A bodhisattva should leave behind all marks and arouse the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. One should not give rise to a mind that abides in form, nor give rise to a mind that abides in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas. One should give rise to a mind that abides in nothing. If the mind has any abiding, that is not abiding. Therefore, the Buddha says that a bodhisattva's mind should not abide in form when giving.

"Subhūti! A bodhisattva, for the benefit of all sentient beings, should give in this way. The Tathāgata says: 'All marks are not marks.' He also says: 'All sentient beings are not sentient beings.'

"Subhūti! The Tathāgata is one who speaks truly, speaks honestly, speaks things as they are, does not speak deceptively, and does not speak inconsistently.

"Subhūti! The dharma the Tathāgata has obtained: this dharma is neither real nor unreal. Subhūti! If a bodhisattva's mind abides in a dharma when giving, it is like a person entering darkness, who can see nothing. If a bodhisattva's mind does not abide in any dharma when giving, it is like a person with eyes seeing all manner of things in the bright light of the sun.

"Subhūti! In a future age, if there are good men and good women who are able to receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra, the Tathāgata, with the Buddha's wisdom, will fully know and fully see that such persons will all accomplish immeasurable, boundless merit.

"Subhūti! If a good man or good woman, in the morning, gave as many bodies as there are grains of sand in the Ganges River; at midday again gave as many bodies as there are grains of sand in the Ganges River; and in the evening again gave as many bodies as there are grains of sand in the Ganges River; giving one's body in this way for immeasurable hundreds of thousands of millions of billions of kalpas: if again there were a person who, hearing this sūtra, gave rise to a mind of faith without opposition, that person's merit would surpass the former, not to mention copying, receiving, upholding, reading, reciting, and explaining it for others.

"Subhūti! To speak of it in summary: this sūtra possesses inconceivable, immeasurable, boundless merit. The Tathāgata has spoken it for those who have set forth in the Great Vehicle and for those who have set forth in the Supreme Vehicle. If there is a person who can receive, uphold, read, recite, and broadly explain it for others, the Tathāgata fully knows and fully sees that such a person will accomplish immeasurable, inestimable, boundless, inconceivable merit. Such persons bear the Tathāgata's anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Why? Subhūti! Those who delight in lesser dharmas are attached to the view of a self, the view of a person, the view of a sentient being, and the view of a lifespan, and therefore cannot listen to, receive, read, recite, or explain this sūtra for others.

"Subhūti! In any place where this sūtra is found, all the world's devas, humans, and asuras should make offerings to it. You should know that such a place is a stūpa, and all should reverently worship it, circumambulate it, and scatter flowers and incense upon it.

"Furthermore, Subhūti! If a good man or good woman receives, upholds, reads, and recites this sūtra, and is despised by others, this person's prior karmic offenses from previous lives, which should have caused him to fall into the evil destinies, are now, because of being despised by people in this present life, extinguished. That person shall attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

"Subhūti! I recall the past, immeasurable asaṃkhyeya kalpas ago, before the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, when I encountered eight hundred and forty thousand myriads of koṭīs of nayutas of Buddhas, and made offerings to and served every one of them, missing none. Yet if there is a person in the final age who can receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra, the merit obtained will not equal even a hundredth, a thousandth-of-a-myriad-of-a-hundred-millionth part of the merit of my offerings to all those Buddhas, nor can it be reached by calculation or comparison.

"Subhūti! If a good man or good woman in the final age receives, upholds, reads, and recites this sūtra, and I were to speak fully of the merit obtained, some who heard it might go mad with disbelief, their minds confused and doubting. Subhūti! You should know that the meaning of this sūtra is inconceivable, and its karmic fruit is also inconceivable."

At that time, Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! When good men and good women have aroused the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, how should they abide? How should they subdue their minds?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "Good men and good women who arouse the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi should give rise to this thought: 'I should bring all sentient beings to final liberation. Yet when all sentient beings have been brought to final liberation, in truth not a single sentient being has been brought to final liberation.' Why? Subhūti! If a bodhisattva has the mark of a self, the mark of a person, the mark of a sentient being, or the mark of a lifespan, then he is not a bodhisattva. Why is this? Subhūti! In truth there is no dharma that arouses the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

"Subhūti! What do you think? When the Tathāgata was with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, was there any dharma by which he attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?"

"No, World-Honored One! As I understand the meaning of what the Buddha has said, when the Buddha was with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, there was no dharma by which he attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

The Buddha said: "So it is, so it is! Subhūti! In truth there is no dharma by which the Tathāgata attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Subhūti! If there were a dharma by which the Tathāgata attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, the Buddha Dīpaṃkara would not have given me the prediction: 'In a future life you shall become a Buddha, named Śākyamuni.' Because in truth there is no dharma that attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, the Buddha Dīpaṃkara gave me this prediction, saying: 'In a future life you shall become a Buddha, named Śākyamuni.' Why? 'Tathāgata' means the suchness of all dharmas.

"If a person says 'The Tathāgata attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi,' Subhūti, in truth there is no dharma by which the Buddha attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Subhūti! The anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi that the Tathāgata attained: in this there is neither real nor unreal. Therefore the Tathāgata says: 'All dharmas are the Buddha-Dharma.' Subhūti! What are called 'all dharmas' are not all dharmas; therefore they are called 'all dharmas.'

"Subhūti! It is like a person's body being tall and large."

Subhūti said: "World-Honored One! When the Tathāgata says 'a person's body is tall and large,' this is not a great body; it is called a great body."

"Subhūti! A bodhisattva is also like this. If a bodhisattva says 'I shall bring immeasurable sentient beings to final liberation,' then he is not called a bodhisattva. Why? Subhūti! In truth there is no dharma called 'bodhisattva.' Therefore the Buddha says: 'All dharmas have no self, no person, no sentient being, no lifespan.' Subhūti! If a bodhisattva says 'I shall adorn buddha-lands,' that one is not called a bodhisattva. Why? The Tathāgata says that adorning buddha-lands is not adorning; it is called adorning. Subhūti! If a bodhisattva penetrates the selflessness of self and dharmas, the Tathāgata says such a one is truly called a bodhisattva.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata have a fleshly eye?"

"So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a fleshly eye."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata have a divine eye?"

"So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a divine eye."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata have a wisdom eye?"

"So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a wisdom eye."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata have a Dharma eye?"

"So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a Dharma eye."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata have a Buddha eye?"

"So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a Buddha eye."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Has the Buddha said that the sand in the Ganges River is sand?"

"So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata says it is sand."

"Subhūti! What do you think? If in one Ganges River there is as much sand as there is in the Ganges, and there were as many Ganges Rivers as all those grains of sand, and the number of buddha-worlds equaled the number of all the grains of sand in all those Ganges Rivers, would that be many?"

"Very many, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "In all those lands, all the many kinds of minds of all sentient beings are fully known to the Tathāgata. Why? The Tathāgata says that all minds are not minds; they are called minds. Why is this? Subhūti! The past mind cannot be obtained; the present mind cannot be obtained; the future mind cannot be obtained.

"Subhūti! What do you think? If a person filled the three-thousand great-thousand world system with the seven treasures and gave them as alms, would that person, because of this cause and condition, gain much merit?"

"So it is, World-Honored One! Because of this cause and condition, that person would gain very much merit."

"Subhūti! If merit were real, the Tathāgata would not say that the merit gained is great. Because merit is non-existent, the Tathāgata says the merit gained is great.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the Buddha be seen through a complete physical body?"

"No, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata should not be seen through a complete physical body. Why? The Tathāgata says the complete physical body is not a complete physical body; it is called a complete physical body."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the Tathāgata be seen through complete marks?"

"No, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata should not be seen through complete marks. Why? The Tathāgata says that complete marks are not complete; they are called complete marks."

"Subhūti! Do not say the Tathāgata has this thought: 'I have some dharma to teach.' Do not have this thought. Why? If a person says 'The Tathāgata has some dharma to teach,' that person is slandering the Buddha, unable to understand what I have said. Subhūti! Teaching the Dharma means there is no dharma that can be taught; this is called teaching the Dharma."

At that time, the Venerable Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! In a future age, will there be sentient beings who, hearing this Dharma spoken, will give rise to a mind of faith?"

The Buddha said: "Subhūti! They are neither sentient beings nor not sentient beings. Why? Subhūti! 'Sentient beings, sentient beings': the Tathāgata says they are not sentient beings; they are called sentient beings."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Did the Buddha attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi as having nothing attained?"

"So it is, so it is! Subhūti! With respect to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, I have not even the slightest dharma that can be attained. This is called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

"Furthermore, Subhūti! This dharma is equal, with nothing high or low. This is called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Through no-self, no-person, no-sentient-being, no-lifespan, cultivating all wholesome dharmas, one attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Subhūti! What are called 'wholesome dharmas' the Tathāgata says are not wholesome dharmas; they are called wholesome dharmas.

"Subhūti! If all the Mount Sumerus in the three-thousand great-thousand world system were heaped up as seven treasures, and a person used them to give as alms; yet another person received, upheld, read, recited, and explained for others even four lines of verse from this Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra, the merit of the former would not equal even a hundredth part of the latter, or a thousandth-of-a-myriad-of-a-hundred-millionth part, nor could it be reached by calculation or comparison.

"Subhūti! What do you think? You should not say the Tathāgata has this thought: 'I should liberate sentient beings.' Subhūti! Do not have this thought. Why? In truth there are no sentient beings for the Tathāgata to liberate. If there were sentient beings for the Tathāgata to liberate, then the Tathāgata would have a self, a person, a sentient being, a lifespan. Subhūti! When the Tathāgata says 'there is a self,' there is no self, yet ordinary people take it as having a self. Subhūti! Ordinary people: the Tathāgata says they are not ordinary people.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the Tathāgata be seen through the thirty-two marks?"

Subhūti said: "So it is, so it is! One sees the Tathāgata through the thirty-two marks."

The Buddha said: "Subhūti! If one sees the Tathāgata through the thirty-two marks, then a wheel-turning sage-king would be a Tathāgata."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! As I understand the meaning of what the Buddha has said, one should not see the Tathāgata through the thirty-two marks."

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke a verse:

> "If one sees me through form,
> or seeks me through sound,
> that person walks a deviant path
> and cannot see the Tathāgata.

"Subhūti! If you have this thought, 'The Tathāgata did not attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi through complete marks': Subhūti! Do not have this thought: 'The Tathāgata did not attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi through complete marks.'

"Subhūti! If you have this thought, 'Those who arouse the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi teach that all dharmas are cut off and annihilated': do not have this thought. Why? Those who arouse the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi do not teach that dharmas have the mark of annihilation.

"Subhūti! If a bodhisattva filled world systems as numerous as the sands of the Ganges River with the seven treasures and gave them as alms; yet another person understood that all dharmas are without self and thereby achieved patience, this bodhisattva's merit would surpass that of the former. Subhūti! This is because bodhisattvas do not receive merit."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why do bodhisattvas not receive merit?"

"Subhūti! Bodhisattvas should not be attached to the merit they create. Therefore it is said they do not receive merit.

"Subhūti! If a person says 'The Tathāgata comes, goes, sits, or lies down,' that person does not understand the meaning of what I have said. Why? The Tathāgata does not come from anywhere, nor does he go anywhere. Therefore he is called Tathāgata.

"Subhūti! If a good man or good woman ground the three-thousand great-thousand world system into motes of dust, what do you think? Would those motes of dust be many?"

"Very many, World-Honored One! Why? If those motes of dust were real, the Buddha would not call them motes of dust. Why is this? The Buddha says motes of dust are not motes of dust; they are called motes of dust. World-Honored One! The Tathāgata's three-thousand great-thousand world system is not a world; it is called a world. Why? If the world were real, it would be a composite unity. The Tathāgata says a composite unity is not a composite unity; it is called a composite unity."

"Subhūti! A composite unity is unspeakable, yet ordinary people are greedily attached to such things.

"Subhūti! If a person says 'The Buddha teaches the view of a self, the view of a person, the view of a sentient being, the view of a lifespan': Subhūti! What do you think? Does that person understand the meaning of what I have said?"

"World-Honored One! That person does not understand the meaning of what the Tathāgata has said. Why? The World-Honored One says the view of a self, the view of a person, the view of a sentient being, and the view of a lifespan are not the view of a self, not the view of a person, not the view of a sentient being, and not the view of a lifespan. They are called the view of a self, the view of a person, the view of a sentient being, and the view of a lifespan."

"Subhūti! Those who arouse the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, regarding all dharmas, should know thus, see thus, and believe and understand thus, without giving rise to the mark of dharmas. Subhūti! What is called the mark of dharmas, the Tathāgata says, is not the mark of dharmas; it is called the mark of dharmas.

"Subhūti! If a person filled immeasurable asaṃkhyeya world systems with the seven treasures and gave them as alms; yet a good man or good woman who arouses the bodhisattva-mind upholds even four lines of verse from this sūtra, receives, upholds, reads, recites, and explains it for others, that person's merit surpasses the former. How should one explain it for others? By not grasping at marks, with suchness unmoving. Why?

> One should contemplate them thus."

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, the Elder Subhūti and all the bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, and all the world's devas, humans, and asuras, hearing what the Buddha had said, all rejoiced greatly, believed, accepted, and put it into practice.
