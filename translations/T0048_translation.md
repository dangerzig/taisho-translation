# 是法非法經
## Sūtra on Dharma and Non-Dharma

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0048

Translated by An Shigao during the Later Han dynasty

---

## Fascicle 1: Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on Dharma and Non-Dharma

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the park of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus, and the bhikṣus responded: "So be it." The bhikṣus listened to the Buddha. The Buddha said: "There is the dharma of the worthy one; bhikṣus, listen as I speak. There is also the dharma of the unworthy one. You should listen carefully, listen well, know it thoroughly, and reflect upon it thoroughly as I speak." The bhikṣus said "so be it" and received the teaching from the Buddha, and the Buddha then spoke thus.

"What, bhikṣus, is the dharma of the unworthy one? If a bhikṣu is of noble lineage, delights in the path, and desires to cultivate the path, yet there are other fellow-student bhikṣus not of noble lineage, and on account of his noble lineage this bhikṣu becomes prideful about himself and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one.

"What is the dharma of the worthy one? The worthy one studies and reflects: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on noble lineage. I am able to sever desire, to sever anger, to sever delusion.' Or perhaps there is a bhikṣu not of a noble family who yet has skillful means to receive the dharma, speaks in accordance with the dharma, practices as required, and follows the dharma in practice — on account of this he gains renown. He practices in accordance with the dharma and truly follows the dharma; he does not exalt himself, nor does he disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu has a highly handsome appearance, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of his handsome appearance exalts himself and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one. The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one reflects: 'A handsome appearance — it is not necessary for me to rely on this. I am able to sever desire, to sever anger, to sever delusion.' Or perhaps there is a bhikṣu who is not handsome, but who follows and practices the dharma to whatever extent he can, thereby gaining praise and renown. He receives the truth of the dharma and practices following the dharma, does not exalt himself nor disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu speaks well and expounds with skill, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of his fine speech and skilled exposition exalts himself and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one. The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one studies and reflects: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on fine words, nor on knowing how to expound beautifully. I am able to sever desire, to sever anger, to sever delusion.'

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu whose speech is not fine and who does not expound with skill nonetheless receives teaching in accordance with the dharma and follows it in practice to whatever extent he can, thereby gaining respect and renown. This is from receiving and practicing the truth of the dharma. He does not exalt himself nor disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or there is heard of a certain bhikṣu who is of advanced age, with many acquaintances, whose connections are wealthy and prosperous, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of his advanced age and many acquaintances exalts himself with pride and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply reflects and considers: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on advanced age, nor on many acquaintances, nor on obtaining much merit. I am able to sever desire, to sever anger, to sever delusion.'

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu is not of advanced age, has few acquaintances, and has not obtained much merit, but simply receives the dharma, wishes to follow the dharma, and practices following the dharma to whatever extent he can, thereby gaining renown. He truly follows the dharma, does not exalt himself, is not prideful, nor does he disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or there is heard of a certain bhikṣu who has learned the sūtras and can expound them, who knows the Vinaya, has penetrating insight, and thorough mastery of the scriptures, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of his penetrating insight and thorough mastery of the scriptures exalts himself with pride and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply studies and reflects: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on penetrating insight, nor on thorough mastery of the scriptures. I am able to sever desire, to sever anger, to sever delusion.'

"Or perhaps there is a bhikṣu who has no penetrating insight, nor thorough mastery of the scriptures, but simply receives and follows the dharma, rightly seeking to practice following the dharma, thereby gaining respect and renown. He upholds the dharma and truly practices in accord with the dharma, does not exalt himself, is not prideful, nor does he disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu seeks alms for himself without relying on acquaintances, visits no more than seven households, sits in one place, eats at one time, and takes nothing afterward, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of eating once and taking nothing afterward exalts himself with pride and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one. The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply studies: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on eating once and taking nothing afterward. I am able to sever desire, to sever anger, to sever delusion.' Or perhaps a bhikṣu does not eat only once nor refrain from taking food afterward, but simply receives and follows the dharma, rightly receives and follows the dharma, thereby gaining respect and renown. He truly follows the dharma, does not exalt himself, is not prideful, nor does he disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu dwells on bare ground among burial mounds, known only for his three robes, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of this reputation exalts himself with pride and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply studies and reflects: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on the reputation of the three robes. I am able to sever desire, to sever anger, to sever delusion.' Or perhaps a bhikṣu has no such reputation for the three robes, but simply receives and follows the dharma, rightly receives and follows the dharma, thereby gaining respect and renown. He truly follows the dharma, does not exalt himself, is not prideful, nor does he disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu dwells in the open air, or beneath trees, or in desolate marshes among burial mounds, with whatever bedding is at hand, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of this exalts himself with pride and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply studies and reflects: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on dwelling in the open air, beneath trees, or in desolate marshes. I am able to sever desire, to sever anger, to sever delusion.'

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu has none of the above-mentioned practices, but simply receives and follows the dharma, rightly receives and follows the dharma, thereby gaining respect and renown. He truly follows the dharma, does not exalt himself, is not prideful, nor does he disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu has already attained the first dhyāna, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of the first dhyāna exalts himself with pride and disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply practices the first dhyāna; as the Buddha has said, one knows this for oneself. One receives the truth of this dharma, does not exalt oneself, is not prideful, nor does one disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu possesses the virtue of the second dhyāna, or possesses the virtue of the third dhyāna, or possesses the virtue of the fourth dhyāna — as has been said regarding the first dhyāna — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu comprehends the mental cultivation of emptiness, or comprehends the mental cultivation of consciousness, or comprehends the mental cultivation of impermanence, or comprehends the mental cultivation of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, surpassing other bhikṣus, and on account of attaining the practice of neither-perception-nor-non-perception exalts himself with pride and also disparages others — this is the dharma of the unworthy one. The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply studies and reflects upon neither-perception and the practice of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. As the Buddha has said, one reflects: 'I possess this.' One receives and truly follows the dharma, does not exalt oneself, is not prideful, nor does one disparage others — this is the dharma of the worthy one."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! I have spoken the dharma of the worthy one, and I have also spoken the dharma of the unworthy one. Bhikṣus, you should reflect on this for yourselves. You should also reflect upon the dharma of the worthy one. Having reflected on the dharma of the unworthy one, you should practice the dharma of the worthy one, abandon the dharma of the unworthy one, and receive the dharma of the worthy one. Bhikṣus who follow the dharma should practice accordingly."

The Buddha spoke thus. The bhikṣus received it, took it to heart, and put it into practice.
