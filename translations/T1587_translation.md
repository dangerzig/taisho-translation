# 轉識論
## Treatise on the Transformation of Consciousness

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1587

Translated from the Chinese. Chen dynasty, translated by Paramārtha (真諦).

---

The transformation of consciousness is of two kinds: first, it transforms as sentient beings; second, it transforms as dharmas. All objects of cognition do not go beyond these two. These two are in reality non-existent; it is merely consciousness that transforms to produce these two appearances.

Next, the cognizing consciousness is explained as being of three kinds: first, the resultant-retribution consciousness, which is the ālaya-vijñāna; second, the appropriating consciousness, which is the ādāna-vijñāna; third, the object-consciousness, which is the six consciousnesses.

**The resultant-retribution consciousness**: because it is drawn by the karma of afflictions, it is called "resultant retribution." It is also called the "root consciousness," because it is the basis upon which all seeds of conditioned dharmas depend. It is also called the "abode consciousness," because it is the dwelling place of all seeds. It is also called the "store consciousness," because it is the place where all seeds are hidden.

Question: What are its marks and what are its objects?

Answer: Its marks and objects cannot be differentiated; they are of one essence, without difference.

Question: If so, how does one know it exists?

Answer: By its functions one knows this consciousness exists. This consciousness can produce all the functions of afflictions, karma, and resultant retribution, just like ignorance. When this ignorance arises, can its marks and objects be differentiated? If they could be differentiated, it would not be called ignorance. If they cannot be differentiated, then it should not exist. Yet it does exist and is not non-existent. It is also by the functions of desire, anger, and the like that one knows ignorance exists. The root consciousness is likewise: though its marks and objects are without difference, by its functions one knows that it exists.

Within this consciousness, there are eight kinds of distinctions, such as the basis and so forth, as fully explained in the "Treatise on the Meaning of the Nine Consciousnesses." It is also associated with five kinds of mental factors: (1) contact, (2) attention, (3) feeling, (4) deliberation, and (5) perception. Contact is produced by the combination of the three: faculty, object, and consciousness. The mind's constant motion is called attention. Feeling is exclusively equanimity-feeling. Deliberation is the weighing of what is practicable and what is not, causing the mind to become crooked or straight, and is called deliberation. Attention is like a horse walking; deliberation is like the rider. The horse merely walks straight, unable to avoid or approach right and wrong; because of the rider, the horse is caused to leave wrong and approach right. Deliberation is likewise: it can cause attention to depart from aimless wandering.

This consciousness and its mental factors are exclusively of the nature of morally neutral. Thought after thought, they constantly flow like the flowing of water waves. The root consciousness is like the stream; the five factors are like the waves. Even upon attaining the fruit of arhatship, this flowing-wave dharma has still not been extinguished. This is called the first consciousness.

**Depending on this consciousness, there is the second appropriating consciousness.** This consciousness takes appropriation as its essence and is associated with the four afflictions: (1) ignorance, (2) self-view, (3) self-conceit, and (4) self-love. This consciousness is called obscured-indeterminate. It is also associated with five kinds of mental factors, the same names as the preceding, but the preceding are subtle while this is coarse. This consciousness and its associated dharmas are ultimately extinguished at the stage of arhatship and are also entirely extinguished upon entering the attainment of the cessation of feeling and perception. If seeing the truth severs the afflictions of consciousness and its mental factors, then through attaining the supramundane path of the sixteen aspects, they are ultimately extinguished. The remainder not yet exhausted belongs only to deliberation. This is called the second consciousness.

**The third object-consciousness**: consciousness transforms to resemble objects, further producing six kinds of consciousness that transform to resemble objects, as already explained above. Its essence pervades the three natures. It is associated with ten kinds of mental factors, as well as the ten wholesome and unwholesome, the major and minor afflictions, and possesses the three kinds of feeling.

The ten mental factors are: the first five, such as contact, are as before, but these are the coarsest. The latter five are: (1) desire, (2) understanding, (3) recollection, (4) concentration, and (5) wisdom. Among these, "understanding" is what was formerly called "comprehension" (vimuktika).

The ten wholesome factors are: (1) faith, (2) modesty, (3) shame, (4) non-greed, (5) non-hatred, (6) diligence, (7) ease, (8) non-heedlessness, (9) non-injury, and (10) equanimity. These ten pervade all minds of the three realms and the undefiled mental factors, and are called the "great ground." These are wholesome by self-nature. The reverse of these ten constitutes what is evil by self-nature.

The major afflictions are ten kinds: (1) desire, (2) anger, (3) delusion, (4) conceit, (5) the five views, and (10) doubt. The minor afflictions are twenty-four kinds: (1) resentful anger, (2) harboring grudges, (3) concealment, (4) not relinquishing evil, (5) jealousy, (6) stinginess, (7) deception, (8) flattery, (9) extreme intoxication, (10) injury, (11) absence of modesty, (12) absence of shame, (13) lack of ease, (14) agitation, (15) lack of faith, (16) laziness, (17) heedlessness, (18) loss of recollection, (19) distraction, (20) lack of understanding, (21) worry and regret, (22) drowsiness, (23) applied thought, and (24) sustained thought.

Among these minor afflictions, there are two kinds: (1) those that pervasively accompany attention, and (2) those that do not pervasively accompany.

The five consciousnesses, together with the sixth mental consciousness, the root consciousness, and the appropriating consciousness: within these three roots, following causes and conditions, they sometimes arise simultaneously or sometimes arise in succession. With attention as the cause and external objects as the condition, consciousness arises. If one first directs attention to apprehend the two objects of form and sound, then afterward the two consciousnesses of eye and ear arise simultaneously and apprehend the two objects. If one directs attention to go to a certain place to see forms, hear sounds, and smell odors, then likewise three consciousnesses arise simultaneously to apprehend three objects. Up to all five consciousnesses arising simultaneously, it is likewise. Or they may arise in sequence, one after another, with only one consciousness arising and apprehending only one object. All follows causes and conditions; therefore they are not the same.

Thus these seven consciousnesses all arise in mutual correspondence within the ālaya consciousness, like various reflections simultaneously appearing in a mirror, or like various waves converging upon a single body of water.

Question: In what states does this mental consciousness not arise?

Answer: Apart from the attainment of cessation, the realm of non-perception beings, deep dreamless sleep, intoxicated stupor, complete cessation of mind, and momentary death: apart from these six states, in all other states it is always present.

Thus the transformation of consciousness does not depart from two meanings: (1) what discriminates, and (2) what is discriminated. Since what is discriminated is non-existent, what discriminates is also non-existent. With no object to be apprehended, consciousness cannot arise. For this reason, the meaning of consciousness-only is established.

What does it mean to establish the meaning of consciousness-only? The original intention is to remove both object and mind. Now that the objective realm is non-existent, consciousness-only is also extinguished. This is to say that the meaning of consciousness-only is established. This constitutes the pure aspect, because afflictions and objects are both non-existent.

Furthermore, the meaning of consciousness-only being established means: it is the consciousness that is the seed of all dharmas. This consciousness, in such and such a way, creates and turns about in activity. Whether in relation to self or others, mutually following one another, it gives rise to various discriminations and what is discriminated. By this meaning, apart from consciousness, no external matters can be established. This constitutes the impure aspect, where only the preceding object is removed but consciousness has not yet been eliminated.

Commentary: "It is the consciousness that is the seed of all dharmas" refers to the ālaya consciousness. It is the seed of all dharmas and the seed of the remaining seven consciousnesses. Because it can produce immeasurable dharmas of its own kind, it is collectively called the "consciousness that is the seed of all dharmas."

"In such and such a way" means: because these consciousnesses can turn about and create immeasurable consciousness-dharmas, sometimes transforming to become sense-faculties, sometimes transforming to become objects, sometimes transforming to become a self, and sometimes transforming to become consciousness. Such various differences are solely the creation of consciousness: this is called "in such and such a way creating and turning about."

"Whether in relation to self or others, mutually following one another": with respect to self, it transforms into the five aggregates, whether as the form aggregate up to the consciousness aggregate. With respect to others, it transforms into enemies, friends, and neutral persons, all various and different. Looking back at one's own five aggregates, they are called "other." Thus self and other mutually transform, one after another in different ways, which is called "mutually following one another."

All the various creations are entirely consciousness; there is no separate objective realm. "Giving rise to various discriminations" and so forth: within each and every consciousness, there is both subject and object. The subject of discrimination is consciousness; the object of discrimination is the objective realm. The subject belongs to the dependent nature (paratantra); the object belongs to the discriminated nature (parikalpita). Therefore it says "giving rise to various discriminations and what is discriminated."

By such a meaning, apart from consciousness, there is no separate object; only the meaning of consciousness-only is established. Since the elimination of consciousness has not yet been made clear, confused delusion has not been removed; therefore it is called the impure aspect.

Question: Removing the object and retaining the consciousness, one may indeed call it consciousness-only. But since both object and consciousness are removed, what consciousness can be established?

Answer: Establishing consciousness-only is a first step of removing the object and retaining the mind. But as a final conclusion, removing the object is for the purpose of emptying the mind: this is its true intention. Therefore, when both object and consciousness are extinguished, that is the establishment of its meaning. When both object and consciousness are extinguished, this is the true nature. The true nature is the amala-vijñāna (pure consciousness). As a final conclusion, one may also say this is the amala-vijñāna.

*Record*: Through two kinds of habitual residue from former karma and two kinds of latent tendencies, these can serve as the truth of the origin of suffering, establishing birth-and-death. The two kinds of habitual residue from former karma are the seeds of all karmas: (1) habitual residue of former karma, and (2) the habitual residue of grasping former karma. The habitual residue of former karma is what is discriminated and belongs to the discriminated nature. The habitual residue of grasping former karma is what discriminates and belongs to the dependent nature. What is discriminated is the object; what discriminates is consciousness. These two kinds of karma are called the seeming truth of the origin, and can produce the arising of the five aggregates.

The two kinds of latent tendencies are the afflictions: (1) mark-latent-tendencies, and (2) coarse-heavy latent-tendencies. "Marks" refers to the essence of affliction, which is the dependent nature, because it gathers the preceding appearances. "Coarse and heavy" refers to the objective realm of affliction, which is the discriminated nature, because the objective realm is coarse and manifest. These two kinds of affliction are called the true truth of the origin, and can accumulate so as to bring about the future five aggregates.

Through these two kinds of seeming and true truths of the origin: if the former karma is exhausted and one receives a different retribution, birth-and-death can be established.

Commentary: The two kinds of habitual residue from former karma: within each single seed there are two meanings. What is discriminated is the habitual residue of former karma; what discriminates is the habitual residue of grasping former karma. What is discriminated is the discriminated nature: it serves as the Dharma-gate of the seed that gives rise to birth, and therefore this Dharma-gate is named "habitual residue of former karma," having a name but no essence. What discriminates is the dependent nature: it is precisely the seed that gives rise to karma, named "habitual residue of grasping former karma," having an essence but not being truly real.

The two kinds of latent tendencies are likewise: within each single affliction there are two meanings. What is discriminated is the coarse-heavy latent tendency, serving as the Dharma-gate of the arising of affliction, having a name but no essence. What discriminates is precisely the essence of affliction, also existent but not truly real, being of the dependent nature. However, the discriminated and dependent natures discussed here have different names from those in the system of the three non-natures. In the three non-natures, the discriminated nature is called "mark-category" and the dependent nature is called "coarse and heavy." Because the discriminated nature, in its own essence, has its mark-category and can serve as the Dharma-gate of affliction, it is called "affliction." The dependent nature is precisely the essence of affliction, which can obtain the retribution of birth-and-death, and is therefore called "coarse and heavy." Here, in the present context, because the discriminated nature's mark-category is coarse and manifest, it is called "coarse and heavy." Because the dependent nature can grasp the preceding mark-category, it is called "marks." Each has its own meaning. If one wishes to transpose the names in this context to those of the three non-natures, that too works.

*Record*: Thus, discrimination upon discrimination: if one discriminates such and such a category, these categories are called the discriminated nature. This has only a name, and the essence revealed by the name is in reality non-existent. This essence being in reality non-existent, this discriminator arises because of another, and is designated the dependent nature. These preceding and following two natures have never been separate: this is the true and real nature. If they were separate, the meaning of consciousness-only would not be established, because there would be a difference between object and consciousness. Because they are not separate, consciousness-only is without objective realm. Without objective realm, consciousness also becomes non-existent. Because object is non-existent and consciousness is non-existent, the meaning of consciousness-only is thus truly established.

Therefore, the preceding nature with respect to the following nature is neither one nor different. If they were definitively one or different, there would be faults. What faults? If the discriminated nature were definitively one with the dependent nature, then since the discriminated nature is definitively and permanently non-existent and is not subsumed under the five Dharma-treasuries, the dependent nature should also be permanently non-existent. If so, there would be no birth-and-death, liberation, wholesome, unwholesome, vinaya, or Dharma. This would be unacceptable. Since it is not like this, the discriminated nature and the dependent nature cannot be definitively one. If they were definitively different, then the discriminated nature could not remove the dependent nature. Since by observing that the discriminated nature is utterly non-existent, one then sees that the dependent nature is also utterly non-existent, they cannot be definitively different. Furthermore, if the discriminated nature were definitively different from the dependent nature, the essence of the discriminated nature should be definitively existent, not said to be permanently non-existent. The existent can be different from the non-existent; what ground is there for discussion of difference? Therefore one says only "neither one nor different"; one cannot definitively assert one or different.

Just as impermanence and conditioned dharmas also cannot be definitively said to be one or different. "Before non-existent, after non-existent" is the meaning of impermanence. The five aggregates are conditioned dharmas. If impermanence were definitively one with conditioned dharmas, since impermanence is non-existence, all dharmas would be non-existent. Since they are not all non-existent, they cannot be definitively one. If definitively different, then when observing impermanence, it should not penetrate conditioned dharmas. Since it does penetrate, they cannot be definitively different. This too is neither one nor different.

Likewise for all dharmas. For example, form and a vase are also neither one nor different. If form were definitively one with the vase, then since fragrance and the rest would not constitute a vase, the vase would then be truly real. If form were definitively different from the vase, seeing form should not penetrate the vase. Therefore they are not definitively one or different. Both explanations are likewise: if one does not see the discriminated nature, one does not see the dependent nature. Therefore, neither one nor different.

All dharmas have only these three natures, and the classification of dharmas is thereby exhaustive. The Tathāgata teaches sentient beings that all dharmas are without nature; this too is of three kinds. The three natures are as explained above. The first two are conventional truth; the last is ultimate truth. The conventional and ultimate truths encompass all dharmas exhaustively.

The three non-natures do not depart from the preceding three natures. The discriminated nature is called the "non-nature of marks," because it is without essential marks. The dependent nature is called the "non-nature of arising," because its essence, cause, and fruit are all non-existent. Its essence resembles the mark of an object; the object is the discriminated nature. Since the discriminated is non-existent, the essence is also non-existent. The cause is also non-existent because: originally it was the discriminated nature as object that could give rise to consciousness as fruit. Since the objective realm is non-existent, how can it produce fruit? Just as a seed can produce a sprout, if the seed is non-existent, from what does the sprout emerge? Therefore it is without arising.

The true-reality nature is called the "non-nature of nature," being without existent-nature and without non-existent-nature. On account of persons and dharmas, it is without existent-nature. On account of the twofold emptiness, it is without non-existent-nature. That is, it is neither existent-nature nor non-existent-nature; therefore the term "non-nature" is repeated as "the non-nature of nature."

These three non-natures are the true reality of all dharmas. Because they are free from existence, they are called permanent. To reveal these three non-natures, the meaning of consciousness-only is taught.

If a person cultivating the path has wisdom that has not yet settled in this meaning of consciousness-only, the afflictions produced by the latent tendencies of the two grasping cannot be extinguished and separated, because the root has not been destroyed. For this reason, the one vehicle is established, causing all to study the bodhisattva path.

If one says that only consciousness exists and presently gives rise to this grasping: if one has not yet departed from this grasping, one cannot enter into consciousness-only. If the wise person no longer takes this object as a condition, both cease to appear. At that time, the practitioner is called "one who has entered consciousness-only." Why? Because through the maturity of cultivating contemplation, confused grasping is exhausted. This is called "nothing to be attained, neither mind nor object." This wisdom is called "supramundane non-discriminating wisdom," which is the non-differentiation of object and wisdom, called "wisdom of suchness." It is also called "turning of the basis": relinquishing the basis of birth-and-death and depending only on the principle of suchness, because both coarse-heaviness and grasping are completely exhausted. Coarse-heaviness is the discriminated nature; grasping is the dependent nature. Both are completely exhausted.

This is called the undefiled realm. This is called the inconceivable. This is called the true and real good. This is called the permanent-abiding fruit. This is called supramundane bliss. This is called the liberation-body. Among the three bodies, this is the Dharma-body.

Commentary: "The afflictions produced by the latent tendencies of the two grasping cannot be extinguished" means: the latent afflictions of the two grasping of views and thinking serve as seeds that produce immeasurable mental formations above. All of them have the root consciousness as their root. While the root has not been destroyed, the branches have not been exhausted. As the *Śrīmālā Sūtra* says: "If the ground of the abiding of ignorance is not severed, it is not ultimate. If the boundless four abiding-grounds are not severed, it is not ultimate."

"If the wise person no longer takes this object as a condition, both cease to appear": this object is the object of consciousness-only, the scattering of consciousness-only. Because there is no object, consciousness is non-existent. Since this consciousness is non-existent, the mind that cognizes consciousness-only is also non-existent. Therefore it says "both cease to appear." These two refer only to the objects manifested by the two consciousnesses. Since the preceding object was already non-existent from the start, this is called the ultimate turning of consciousness.
