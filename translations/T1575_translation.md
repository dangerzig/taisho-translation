# 六十頌如理論
## Sixty Verses on Reasoning

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1575

Composed by Nāgārjuna (龍樹). Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護).

---

> Homage to the Lord of Silence of the three times,
> Who proclaimed the words of the true Dharma of dependent origination.
> If one understands that dharmas are free from what dependent origination produces,
> All Dharma-practices are likewise freed.
>
> Apart from the two extremes of existence and non-existence,
> The wise have no object of reliance.
> Profound and without any object,
> The meaning of dependent origination is established.
>
> If one claims dharmas have no nature,
> Then various faults arise.
> The wise should, in accord with reasoning,
> Investigate whether dharmas have nature.
>
> If one truly obtains an existent nature,
> As the foolish discriminate,
> Without nature there would be no cause:
> How could the meaning of liberation be established?
>
> One cannot say there is nature;
> One cannot say there is no nature.
> Understanding nature and non-nature,
> The greatly wise speak in accord with reasoning.
>
> Do not regard nirvāṇa and birth-and-death
> As having different natures.
> It is not that nirvāṇa and birth-and-death
> Are two natures with distinctions.
>
> Birth-and-death and nirvāṇa:
> Both are without any existence.
> If one understands birth-and-death,
> This itself is nirvāṇa.
>
> Refuting the view that birth has nature,
> Discrimination of cessation is likewise.
> Like the deeds performed by an illusion,
> Cessation is manifestly without reality.
>
> If cessation has something destroyed,
> Know that it is conditioned.
> Even present dharmas cannot be obtained:
> How much less can one know destroyed dharmas?
>
> Those aggregates do not cease;
> When defilements are exhausted, that is nirvāṇa.
> If one understands the nature of cessation,
> That person attains liberation.
>
> If arising dharmas and ceasing dharmas
> Are both unobtainable,
> Then what right wisdom observes
> Is that they arise from dependent origination through ignorance.
>
> If one sees dharmas as quiescent,
> All activities are likewise.
> Knowing this supreme Dharma,
> One attains Dharma-wisdom without bounds.
>
> Dependent origination cannot be seen:
> This meaning is not a non-seeing.
> The subtle nature herein
> Is not a discrimination of dependent origination.
>
> What the Buddha, the Perfectly Awakened One, proclaimed
> Is that existence is proclaimed, not without cause.
> If one exhausts the source of afflictions,
> One destroys the characteristics of the cycle.
>
> All dharmas proceed determinately:
> Seeing existence, acting, and grasping.
> How are the former and later limits
> Established from conditions?
>
> How can what was formerly born
> Later turn into something different?
> Therefore the former and later boundaries
> Are like what the worldly see in illusions.
>
> How can an illusion be born?
> How can there be attachment to it?
> Fools within an illusion
> Seek the illusion as real.
>
> The former limit is not the later limit;
> Through clinging to views, one does not let go.
> The wise observe nature and non-nature
> As illusions, mirages, and reflections.
>
> If one says birth is not cessation,
> This is a conditioned discrimination.
> Yet the wheel of dependent origination
> Turns without any appearance.
>
> Whether already born or not yet born,
> Their self-nature is unborn.
> If self-nature is unborn,
> How can the name "birth" be obtained?
>
> When the cause is quiescent, dharmas are exhausted;
> This exhaustion cannot be obtained.
> If self-nature has no exhaustion,
> How can the name "exhaustion" be established?
>
> Not the slightest dharma can be born;
> Not the slightest dharma can cease.
> The two paths of birth and cessation
> Appear in accordance with affairs and meaning.
>
> Knowing birth means knowing cessation;
> Knowing cessation means knowing impermanence.
> If the nature of impermanence is known,
> One does not reach the bottom of dharmas.
>
> All dharmas arise from conditions;
> Though born, they are immediately free from cessation.
> Like one who reaches the other shore
> And then sees the vast ocean.
>
> If one's own mind does not understand,
> Ordinary beings grasp at the nature of self.
> In the confusion of nature and non-nature,
> Various faults arise.
>
> All dharmas are impermanent,
> Suffering, empty, and without self.
> Herein, seeing dharmas as free,
> The wise observe nature and non-nature.
>
> Without abiding, without any object,
> Without root, and not established,
> Born from the seed of ignorance,
> Free from beginning, middle, and end.
>
> The great evil city of foolish darkness
> Is like a plantain, without substance,
> Like the city of the gandharvas:
> All are illusions seen by the world.
>
> This world was first proclaimed by Brahmā;
> The Buddha correctly and truly proclaimed it.
> The later sages are without error;
> Their proclamations are without difference.
>
> The world is darkened by delusion;
> Love's continuity flows in cycles.
> The wise understand all forms of love
> And explain them with equanimity.
>
> First, one says all dharmas exist;
> Within existence, one seeks real nature.
> Later, seeking nature is also nothing;
> Then, without attachment, nature is freed.
>
> If one does not understand the meaning of freedom,
> One becomes attached to whatever is heard.
> The meritorious deeds one has performed
> Are destroyed by the foolish themselves.
>
> As was previously spoken with equanimity,
> Those karmic actions are true and real.
> If self-nature is understood,
> This is called the unborn.
>
> What I have thus proclaimed
> All relies on the Buddha's words and teachings.
> As he expounded,
> The dharmas of the skandhas, āyatanas, and dhātus,
> The great elements and consciousness —
> All that was proclaimed is entirely equal.
> When that wisdom is directly realized,
> There is no error and no discrimination.
> If this one thing is as it truly is,
> The Buddha calls it nirvāṇa.
> This is supreme, without error;
> Without wisdom, there is only discrimination.
> If the mind is distracted,
> It provides an opening for the māras.
>
> If one is truly freed from faults,
> Then there is nothing born.
> Such is the condition of ignorance:
> The Buddha proclaimed it for the world.
>
> If the world is without discrimination,
> How is this "unborn"?
> If ignorance can be extinguished,
> Once extinguished, there is no arising.
> The names "birth" and "cessation" are contradictory;
> Without wisdom, discrimination arises.
>
> With a cause there is birth;
> Without conditions there is no abiding.
> If apart from conditions there were nature,
> How could this existence be obtained?
>
> If one can grasp an existent nature,
> Then one speaks of birth and abiding.
> Herein doubts multiply further,
> Claiming there are dharmas that can abide.
>
> If bodhi can be realized,
> Then one speaks of permanence everywhere.
> If abiding-nature can be grasped,
> This claim entails further arising.
>
> If one says dharmas are truly real,
> The unwise make such claims.
> If one says dharmas have a location,
> Even grasping cannot be obtained.
>
> Dharmas are unborn and without self;
> Wisdom enters into the real nature.
> The characteristics of permanent and impermanent
> All arise from views produced by mind.
>
> If one establishes many natures,
> One seeks to establish them as real natures.
> How is it that this does not
> Constantly produce faults?
>
> If one establishes a single nature,
> What is sought is like the moon in water:
> Neither real nor unreal,
> All arise from views produced by mind.
>
> The dharmas of craving and aversion are extremely heavy;
> From them arise clinging to views.
> Through disputation one establishes [positions];
> Separating from nature, one clings to the real.
>
> Those causes give rise to views;
> From views, afflictions are born.
> If this is rightly understood,
> Views and afflictions are both exhausted.
>
> One should know dharmas are impermanent,
> Appearing because they arise from conditions.
> What arises from conditions is also unborn:
> This is the supreme true statement.
>
> Sentient beings, with wrong and deluded wisdom,
> Conceive the unreal as real.
> They raise disputes against others,
> While they themselves turn in confusion.
>
> One's own position cannot be established;
> How can the other's position exist?
> When both positions are non-existent,
> The wise understand there is no dispute.
>
> If there is the slightest dharma to rely on,
> Afflictions are like poisonous snakes.
> If there is no stillness and no movement,
> The mind then has no object of reliance.
>
> Afflictions are like poisonous snakes,
> Producing extremely grave faults.
> When covered by the poison of afflictions,
> How can one see the mind?
>
> As when the foolish see a reflection
> And wrongly conceive it as real:
> The bondage of the world is likewise —
> Wisdom is ensnared by the net of delusion.
>
> Nature, likened to a reflection,
> Is not the domain of the unwise eye.
> Great wisdom is originally unborn:
> The subtle domain of conception.
>
> Attachment to form characterizes the ordinary person;
> Freedom from craving characterizes the lesser sage.
> Understanding the self-nature of form:
> This is the supreme wisdom.
>
> If one is attached to wholesome dharmas,
> It is like a confusion free from craving;
> It is like seeing an illusory person
> And, apart from what is done, seeking its substance.
>
> Knowing this meaning as a fault,
> One does not observe nature or non-nature.
> Afflictions cannot be obtained;
> The light of nature destroys wrong wisdom.
>
> Wisdom, free from defilement, is pure,
> Yet there is no purity to rely on.
> If there is reliance, there is defilement;
> That purity again produces faults.
>
> The extremely evil dharma of afflictions:
> If one sees its self-nature as free,
> Then the mind is without disturbance,
> And one crosses the ocean of birth-and-death.
>
> This wholesome Dharma, the nectar of immortality,
> Arises from great compassion.
> Relying on the Tathāgata's proclamation,
> Without limit or discrimination.

> What is herein difficult to express:
> Following the wise person's view, accomplishment is attained.
> The wise follow and observe the gate of accordance;
> All thus turns from great compassion.
>
> The true nature within all dharmas:
> The wise observe it fittingly, in accord with reasoning.
> From this, faith arises in the direction one faces,
> Pulling sentient beings free from all suffering.
>
> This meaning is profound and also vast;
> I have praised it for the sake of supreme benefit.
> As the words of the greatly wise have now been proclaimed,
> The delusion of self and others is entirely destroyed.
>
> Having destroyed that delusion and affliction,
> All actions are freed from māra-obstacles.
> From this the gates of fortunate destinies are opened:
> How could the matter of liberation be lost?
>
> Those who uphold the pure precepts are reborn in heaven;
> This is a definitive and true statement.
> Even those who break the precepts, if they abide in right view,
> Though the precepts are damaged, their view is not damaged.
>
> Seeds grow and this is not without meaning;
> Seeing benefit and advantage, one acts broadly with generosity.
> If great compassion were not the primary cause,
> How could the wise give rise to the desire for Dharma?
