# 薩鉢多酥哩踰捺野經
## Sūtra of the Seven Suns

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0030

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmabhadra (法賢)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Blessed One was dwelling in the land of Vaiśālī, at the Kūṭāgāraśālā by the Markaṭahrada. At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus, saying: "Listen well and attend carefully! All conditioned phenomena are impermanent, subject to arising and ceasing — without solidity, without substance; not ultimate, not to be relied upon, not to be rejoiced in. You should know this well, and strive with diligent effort to seek liberation. Bhikṣus! Time does not long abide; moment by moment it passes away. After this, when the final age of the kalpa draws near, the heavens will withhold their rain, and drought will grip the human world. All the trees and forests on the great earth, the hundred grains and crops, all flowers and fruits — all shall wither and dry up, and none shall come to fruition. Bhikṣus, you should know: all conditioned phenomena are impermanent, subject to arising and ceasing — without solidity, without substance; not ultimate, not to be relied upon, not to be rejoiced in. Therefore, you ought to strive with diligent effort to seek liberation. Bhikṣus! When the final age of the kalpa comes, a second sun will appear, blazing upon the world. The heat will intensify, and all the trees and forests of the great earth — their roots, stems, branches, and leaves — will all be destroyed and utterly annihilated. Bhikṣus! Such is impermanence; it cannot long be preserved. Therefore you should now strive diligently and quickly seek liberation.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! When the final age of the kalpa comes, a third sun will appear, blazing upon the world. The heat, surpassing what came before, will destroy things still more severely. All the small rivers and streams of the great earth, all springs and sources, shall be exhausted without a single drop remaining. Bhikṣus, you should know: in the face of such impermanence, who can escape it? Therefore I now earnestly exhort you: quickly seek liberation. Furthermore, bhikṣus! When the final age of the kalpa comes, a fourth sun will appear, blazing upon the world. The heat, surpassing what came before, shall cause all the great lakes free from affliction and the four great rivers of the earth — the Gaṅgā, the Sindhu, the Sītā, and the Vakṣu — all to dry up without a single drop remaining. Bhikṣus, you should know: in the face of such impermanence, who can escape it? Reflect within yourselves; be no longer heedless, and quickly seek liberation.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! When the final age of the kalpa comes, a fifth sun will appear, blazing upon the world. The heat, surpassing what came before, shall cause the waters of the great ocean of the earth gradually to diminish — by one hundred yojanas, two hundred yojanas, three hundred yojanas, up to one thousand yojanas. As the sun blazes ever more fiercely and the heavenly rains fail ever more utterly, the ocean diminishes further: to two thousand yojanas, three thousand yojanas, and on to seven thousand yojanas. After such reduction, the remaining water in the ocean still measures seven thousand yojanas.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! These seven thousand yojanas of ocean water shall gradually diminish further — to six thousand yojanas, five thousand yojanas, four thousand yojanas, and on until only seven hundred yojanas remain.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! These seven hundred yojanas of ocean water shall gradually diminish further — to six hundred yojanas, five hundred yojanas, four hundred yojanas, and on until only seven yojanas remain.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! These seven yojanas of ocean water shall gradually diminish further — to six yojanas, five yojanas, four yojanas, and on until only seven krośas remain.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! These seven krośas of ocean water shall gradually diminish further — to six krośas, five krośas, four krośas, and on until only seven tāla trees' depth remains.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! This water of seven tāla trees' depth shall gradually diminish further — to six tāla trees, five tāla trees, four tāla trees, and on until only seven men's height remains.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! This water of seven men's height shall gradually diminish further — to six men's height, five men's height, four men's height, and on to one man's height; then to a man's neck, to a man's armpit, to a man's navel, to a man's waist, to a man's knees, to a man's ankles — and then to the depth of a single finger-breadth, whereupon it instantly dries up entirely. In this world there remains only the great earth with its mountains and rivers, and nothing else whatsoever."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Conditioned phenomena are impermanent; arising and ceasing, they do not abide. Without substance, without solidity — therefore, swiftly seek liberation from this.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! When the final age of the kalpa comes, a sixth sun will appear. The great earth blazes with heat, and rocks and mountains are melted and dissolved. A faint smoke rises from the king of mountains, Sumeru — even as black smoke rises from a kiln firing tiles, so likewise it is. In this world there is nothing fit to behold. Bhikṣus! Such is impermanence — arising and ceasing, it does not abide. You should therefore strive with diligent effort, abandon the mind of craving and attachment, and quickly seek liberation.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! When the final age of the kalpa comes, a seventh sun will appear. The heat reaches its extreme, and suddenly fire breaks out — as if burning ten bundles of firewood, then a hundred bundles, a thousand bundles, ten thousand bundles, hundreds of thousands of millions of bundles — until the small thousandfold world-system and all the six desire heavens become an aggregate of fire. When the desire realm has been burned, fire also spontaneously arises in the first dhyāna Brahmā world, burning and destroying all the palaces of the world-vessel — those rising one hundred yojanas in height, or two hundred yojanas, three hundred yojanas, up to seven thousand yojanas in height — all such palaces become an aggregate of fire. Approaching even the second dhyāna, the newly born Brahmā beings dwelling there see the flames blazing below and are seized with great terror, seeking to escape the calamity of fire. At that time, the elder Brahmās of the second dhyāna spoke, saying: 'You have come to be born in our realm through the merit of past virtuous deeds. Simply set your mind at ease and do not give rise to terror and fear. The fire of the lower realms below will soon be extinguished.' Having spoken thus, suddenly a great wind arose and blew away the smoke and flames, and the ashes as well — all gone, with nothing remaining. It is as when a person lights a lamp with clarified butter in the midst of empty space: after the flame is extinguished, there is not the slightest trace of black smoke or ash. So too it is after that fire is extinguished."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Conditioned phenomena arise and cease without abiding — they are phenomena of inversion, without solidity, without substance; not ultimate, not to be rejoiced in. Far removed from craving and attachment, quickly seek liberation. If there were no craving and attachment, what cause, what condition would there be for the great earth and the heavens to be destroyed and to cease to appear?"

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! In a past age there appeared a Buddha named Sunetra Tathāgata, with immeasurable and innumerable assemblies of śrāvakas, as well as Brahmā gods, non-attachment outsiders possessed of supernatural powers, and multitudes of other communities. That Tathāgata Sunetra, for the sake of the śrāvakas, expounded the profoundly deep, definitive, and pure brahma-conduct of the Dharma. At that time, all the bhikṣus attained perfect fulfillment in all the precepts and each practiced according to his inclination: some cultivated the four immeasurable contemplations, severed the defilements of the lower realm of desire, and were born in the Brahmā world; others were inclined to be born in the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven, or to be born in the Nirmāṇarati heaven, or to be born in the Tuṣita heaven, or to be born in the Yāma heaven, or to be born in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, or to be born in the Cāturmahārājakāyika heaven, or to be born in a kṣatriya family, or to be born in a brahmin family, or to be born in the family of a householder or elder. At that time Tathāgata Sunetra thought: 'I shall now cause this assembly of śrāvakas, by practicing together and being born together, and through the same spiritual power, to enter into the second dhyāna of loving-kindness (maitrī).' Having thus thought, he himself entered into the second dhyāna of loving-kindness. At that time, the immeasurable hundreds of thousands of śrāvakas and non-Buddhist practitioners all cultivated that samādhi, and all were born together above the Brahmā heavens."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! The Tathāgata Sunetra of the distant past is no other than myself. Though those beings were born in the second dhyāna and escaped the calamity of fire in the lower realms, they had not abandoned the seeds of craving, aversion, and delusion, and therefore could not attain liberation from birth, old age, sickness, and death. Bhikṣus! The Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One, teacher of gods and humans, has far removed himself from all afflictions such as craving, aversion, and delusion, and has attained liberation from birth, old age, sickness, and death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, and despair. He further proclaimed, for the Brahmā gods and the assembly of śrāvakas, the pure Dharma practice in accordance with what was fitting. Having each heard it, some severed all conceptually-generated and innately-arisen afflictions pertaining to desire, and attained the fruit of anāgāmin; some severed all conceptually-generated and six grades of innately-arisen afflictions, and attained the fruit of sakṛdāgāmin; some, though having severed all conceptually-generated afflictions yet not having severed the innately-arisen, attained the fruit of śrotāpatti, and after passing through seven existences among gods and humans, became arhats."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Such is the going forth from home life, such is the brahma-conduct, such is the attainment of the fruits — whereby one departs from the bounds of suffering and attains liberation. Now, with resolute intention, abandon craving and attachment, and set forth upon the path to awakening."

At that time, when the World-Honored One had expounded this Dharma, the bhikṣus who had heard the Buddha's teaching were filled with great joy, and with faith and reverence they received it and put it into practice.
