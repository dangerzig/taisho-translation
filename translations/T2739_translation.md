# Interlinear Notes on the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra

## *Jīngāng Bōrě Jīng Jiāzhù* (金剛般若經挾註)

Taishō 2739

Author unknown.

*Note: This is a fragmentary text presenting the latter portion of the Diamond Sūtra (Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā, T235, Kumārajīva's translation) with brief interlinear annotations (jiāzhù) inserted between the sūtra phrases. Such interlinear note editions were a common format in Chinese Buddhism, where short glosses, section markers, or explanatory phrases were written in smaller characters between the lines of the main text. In the present extraction, the annotations and sūtra text are merged together, making them difficult to distinguish. The text covers roughly the second half of the Diamond Sūtra, beginning from the discussion of "seeing all characteristics as non-characteristics" and proceeding through the major themes: the four fruits of the śrāvaka path (stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, arhat), the adornment of buddha-lands, the superiority of the sūtra's merit over material giving, the famous verse on not seeking the Tathāgata through form or sound, the teaching that all dharmas are buddha-dharmas, the five eyes of the Tathāgata, the unreality of past, present, and future mind, and the final teaching that the Tathāgata does not liberate any sentient being. The text breaks off abruptly near the end.*

---

[The text begins mid-sūtra:]

"To see all characteristics as non-characteristics is to see the Tathāgata." Subhūti addressed the Buddha, saying: "World-Honored One." [Section marker.] "... can they give rise to true faith?" "Do not speak thus. Those who maintain precepts and cultivate merit, with regard to these phrases, can give rise to faith. You should know that such persons have not planted wholesome roots with just one buddha or two buddhas, but have planted various wholesome roots with immeasurable thousands of myriads of buddhas. With regard to these phrases, if they give rise to even a single thought of pure faith, the Tathāgata fully knows and fully sees. These sentient beings obtain [immeasurable merit]."

"Why? Because these sentient beings have no mark of self, no mark of person, no mark of sentient being, no mark of life-span. They have no mark of dharmas, nor any mark of non-dharmas. Why? If they grasp the mark of dharmas, that is attachment to self, person, sentient being, and life-span. If they grasp the mark of non-dharmas, that is also attachment to self, person, sentient being, and life-span. Therefore, one should not grasp dharmas, nor should one grasp non-dharmas. The Tathāgata always says: 'You bhikṣus, know that the Dharma I teach is like a raft. Even the Dharma should be relinquished; how much more so what is not the Dharma.'"

"Subhūti, what do you think? Has the Tathāgata attained anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi?" Subhūti [said]: "There is no fixed dharma called anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi that the Tathāgata could teach." "Why? Because the Dharma taught by the Tathāgata cannot be grasped and cannot be spoken. Why? All worthies and sages are distinguished by the unconditioned dharma."

"Subhūti, what do you think? If a person were to fill the three-thousand great-thousand world-system with the seven treasures and use them for giving, would the merit obtained by that person be great or not?" Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One. Why? This merit is not the nature of merit itself. Therefore the Tathāgata says the merit is great."

"If again there were a person who, within this sūtra, received, upheld, and even so much as a four-line verse, and explained it for others, that person's merit would surpass the former. Why? Subhūti, all buddhas, and all buddhas' dharma of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi, all come forth from this sūtra. Subhūti, what is called 'buddha-dharma' is not buddha-dharma."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can a stream-enterer (*srotāpanna*) think, 'I have attained the fruit of stream-entry'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One. Why? The stream-enterer is called 'one who enters the stream,' yet there is nothing entered. Not entering form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas: this is called a stream-enterer."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can a once-returner (*sakṛdāgāmin*) think, 'I have attained the fruit of once-returning'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One. Why? The once-returner is called 'one who comes and goes once,' yet in reality there is no coming or going. This is called a once-returner."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can a non-returner (*anāgāmin*) think, 'I have attained the fruit of non-returning'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One. Why? The non-returner is called 'one who does not come,' yet in reality there is no coming. Therefore it is called a non-returner."

"Subhūti, what do you think? Can an arhat think, 'I have attained the way of arhatship'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One. Why? There is in reality no dharma called 'arhat.' World-Honored One, if an arhat were to think, 'I have attained the way of arhatship,' that would be attachment to self, person, sentient being, and life-span. World-Honored One, the Buddha has said that I have attained the samādhi of non-contention and am foremost among people, the foremost arhat free from desire. Yet I do not think, 'I am an arhat free from desire.' World-Honored One, if I were to think, 'I have attained the way of arhatship,' the World-Honored One would not say that Subhūti delights in the practice of araṇya (*āraṇyaka*). Because Subhūti in reality has no practice, he is called Subhūti who delights in the araṇya practice."

"The Buddha told Subhūti: 'What do you think? When the Tathāgata was formerly with Dīpaṃkara Buddha, did he obtain any dharma?' 'World-Honored One, when the Tathāgata was formerly with Dīpaṃkara Buddha, in regard to dharma there was truly nothing obtained.'"

"Subhūti, what do you think? Does a bodhisattva adorn buddha-lands?" "No, World-Honored One. Why? To adorn buddha-lands is not adornment; it is called adornment." "Therefore, Subhūti, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva should give rise to a pure mind in this way: one should not abide in form to give rise to mind, nor should one abide in sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas to give rise to mind. One should give rise to a mind that abides nowhere."

"Subhūti, suppose there were a person whose body was like Mount Sumeru, the king of mountains. What do you think? Would that body be great?" Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One. Why? The Buddha says 'not-body'; this is called a great body."

[The sūtra continues through the themes of the Ganges River sands, the superiority of sūtra-merit over material giving, the naming of the sūtra as "Diamond Prajñāpāramitā," the non-existence of the Tathāgata's teaching, the nature of the thirty-two marks, Subhūti's tears upon hearing the sūtra, the pāramitā of patience and the story of King Kalirāja, the five eyes of the Tathāgata, the unreality of past, present, and future mind, the impossibility of seeing the Tathāgata through physical marks, and the famous verse:]

> "If one seeks me through form,
> Or seeks me through sound,
> This person walks the wrong path
> And cannot see the Tathāgata."

[The text continues through the remaining sections on the bodhisattva's attainment, the equality of all dharmas, the Tathāgata's non-liberation of sentient beings, and the teaching that ordinary persons are not ordinary persons. The text breaks off abruptly near the end of the sūtra.]
