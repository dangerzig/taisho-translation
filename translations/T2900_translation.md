# Sūtra on Purity in the Observance of Fasting

## *Zhāifǎ Qīngjìng Jīng* (齋法清淨經)

Taishō 2900

Translator unknown. An apocryphal text.

Thus have I heard. At one time the Buddha was in the Bamboo Grove Monastery (*Zhúlín Jīngshè*, 竹林精舍). Together with two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, and all the devas, nāgas, the eightfold assembly of ghosts and spirits, and bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, they gathered in assembly.

At that time, Mahāmaudgalyāyana (*Dà Mùjiānlián*, 大目揵連) rose from his seat, bowed to the Buddha, and said: "World-Honored One, once in the past when I entered the city to beg for alms, on the road I encountered several thousand people. Their heads were as large as great mountains and their bellies as large as Mount Sumeru (*Xūmí*, 須彌), but their throats were as thin as needles. When they walked, the sound was like five hundred carriages. Smoke and flames poured from their mouths. What sin caused this? I pray the World-Honored One will explain it for me."

The Buddha said: "How bitter! How bitter!" He said to Maudgalyāyana: "These hungry ghosts (*èguǐ*, 餓鬼), in their previous lives, prepared food for fasting ceremonies, and after eating, they broke the fast afterward. They received such karmic retribution. Those who eat the fasting food of others without observing the fast themselves also receive this retribution. After a fast is completed, any remaining food must not be casually given to those who are not observing the fast. After the fasting meal is finished, if the donor announces and asks, requesting the monks' joyful approval, the remaining food may be given by the monks to their disciples. Only after joyful approval may it be used as desired. If there is no such request, both the monks and the donor incur offense."

Maudgalyāyana replied: "It is truly as the holy teaching says."

The Buddha said: "When any patron sets up a dharma-assembly and provides a fasting meal, those who are observing the fast may be given food. Those not observing the fast must not casually eat it. Eating such food incurs immeasurable and boundless offense. If one is able to observe the fast for a single day, one gains surplus provisions for six hundred thousand lifetimes. If one does not observe the fast yet eats fasting food, for six hundred thousand lifetimes one falls among the hungry ghosts. Why? Because this food offered in faith is weighty. Better to swallow a ball of hot iron than to eat this food. Swallowing hot iron lasts but an instant; eating others' food offered in faith brings prolonged and bitter suffering. For five million lifetimes one suffers the misery of a hungry ghost.

"Wherever a dharma-assembly is held, all should prepare the fast according to proper procedure. One must not secretly carry away any leftovers to give to relatives, friends, or members of one's clan. If one secretly carries away leftovers, for five million lifetimes one will be in hell, where hot iron wheels will pass over both sides of the body, causing burning agony and great suffering.

"All fasting food must be treated with care. Not a single grain of rice or wheat should be wasted, for each transforms into a ball of hot iron. Furthermore, if any donor wishes to set up a merit assembly and someone first tastes the food beforehand, it all becomes leftover food. Such an assembly is pointless and would have been better left undone. Why? Because the devas, ghosts, and spirits are not pleased. A person who first samples the food will also suffer the misery of a hungry ghost for five million lifetimes.

"From now on, those who wish to obtain merit should prepare everything in purity according to the proper method as described above. Wash the body and hands. Husks and bran should first be set aside in a hidden place, covered and stored. One must not taste or sample beforehand. If the fast is prepared according to proper procedure, one can obtain merit and benefit. The devas will rejoice, the hundred spirits will be delighted, and the benevolent deities will protect you. The sūtra does not speak falsely. Karmic reward is like a shadow and an echo.

"These hungry ghosts originally came from the human realm. With impure hands they touched the monks' robes and bowls. With impure hands they contaminated the pure food of the śramaṇas. With impure hands they added impure things to the monks' food. They gave impure food to the sangha and to their parents. For the next five hundred lifetimes they fell among the hungry ghosts, constantly eating impurities. When they wished to approach a latrine to eat feces, the guardian spirit of the latrine, holding an iron staff, blocked the hungry ghosts from approaching. They subsist on pus, blood, mucus, and spittle, and the dregs of filthy liquids. They constantly attend the impure blood and filth of women in childbirth, using it as sustenance. They further undergo five million lifetimes among pigs and dung beetles, constantly eating foul and impure things. Suffering such bitterness through accumulated lifetimes, for tens of millions of kalpas there is no prospect of escape. It is difficult to attain liberation; the pain is beyond words.

"If one touches one's body, the private parts of a man or woman, and with that hand touches the pure food of śramaṇas or parents, or gives impure food to śramaṇas or parents, one will incur this calamity. All those with fleshly eyes do not know the consequences of sin and merit.

"From now on, those who wish to assist the sangha in preparing food should handle the sangha's utensils with clean body and hands. With clean hands, measure the rice and handle the flour, and use clean water to make the food. The merit obtained will be immeasurable.

"From now on, broadly make this known to all so they may hear it. Practice as has been taught."

Maudgalyāyana said to the Buddha: "I shall disseminate this and dare not forget. I shall practice this with wholehearted devotion."
