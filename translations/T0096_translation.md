# 治意經
## Sūtra on Governing the Mind

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 96

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

The Buddha said: "Having fully practiced ānāpānasmṛti in accordance with the Dharma, one who wishes to learn step by step — just as the Buddha has taught, to attain illumination in the world, like clouds dispersing and the moon emerging to stand revealed — whether standing with body established and mind established, or sitting and lying down, it is likewise so. The bhikṣu who has thus established the mind will certainly benefit before and after; having benefited before and after, aberrant mental states no longer appear. Having stilled the mind, one also guards against the six sources of decline. Guarding constantly without interruption, one comes to know the unconditioned body.

"If with a mind that is wholly attentive one constantly guards the body — ceasing desire, neither desiring existence, neither doubting existence nor doubting non-existence — practicing step by step through all one's doubts, one will quickly cross beyond birth and death. If the mind is alert and knows concentration, joy, and purity, and one continuously contemplates the Dharma, one can cross beyond aging and illness. Thus, alarmed by illness, the diligent practitioner who governs their own mind can sever the fetters of birth and aging and in this very life exhaust suffering.

"Being watchful, one listens; what was sleep becomes awakening; watchfulness overcomes drowsiness; being watchful, there is no fear. Having diligently overcome sleep, studying day and night, having sought the amṛta, one attains the cessation of suffering.

"One who has this benefit takes refuge in the Buddha; in the middle of the night the mind rests constantly on the Buddha; having awakened, one attains Awakening — this is always the way for disciples of the Buddha. If in the middle of the night one is constantly mindful of the Dharma — likewise for the Saṅgha, likewise for practicing the precepts, likewise for dāna, likewise for guarding the body, likewise for practicing dhyāna, likewise for not harming others, likewise for composing the mind, likewise for emptiness — having awakened, one is able to awaken; this is always the way for disciples of the Buddha, those who throughout do not fall into discursive thought."

A question was raised: "What is meant by 'coming to know the unconditioned body'?" The master said: "Nirvāṇa is the unconditioned body."

"What is 'sequential practice'?" "It means: whatever one encounters now, one should immediately eliminate it, then eliminate the next — this is sequential practice."

"What is 'continuously contemplating the Dharma'?" "It means: when the six āyatanas arise, one should immediately examine and assess — this is continuously contemplating the Dharma."

"What is 'the practitioner governing their own mind'?" "It means: when teaching others to be diligent, one must first practice with one's own mind; guarding one's own body, guarding one's own mind — placing oneself within merit; because the merit of the heavenly realms is not yet fulfilled, when the merit of self-guarding is fulfilled, one attains dhyāna."
