# 犍陀國王經
## Sūtra of King Gandhāra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0506

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time there was a king named Gandhāra, who served a brahmin. The brahmin dwelt in the mountains, where he had planted many fruit trees. One time, a woodcutter damaged his fruit trees. When the brahmin saw this, he brought the woodcutter before the king and said: "This man has acted outrageously, destroying my fruit trees. Your Majesty should punish him with death." The king, who served the brahmin with reverence and dared not go against him, had the tree-destroyer executed.

Not long after, there was an ox that ate someone's rice. The owner chased and beat the ox, breaking off one of its horns. Blood streamed down its face, and the pain was unbearable. The ox went directly to the king and said: "I was truly at fault, eating this man's rice. But now he has beaten me and broken my horn." The rice-owner also followed and arrived at the king's court. The king, who understood the language of birds and beasts, told the ox: "I shall punish him with death for your sake." The ox immediately replied: "Even if you kill this man now, it still cannot make me stop hurting. You should simply issue an order that henceforth no one may treat others as he has treated me."

The king was moved and reflected: "I serve this brahmin, and merely on account of fruit trees he made me kill a person. He is not as good as this ox." He then summoned the brahmin and asked: "By following this path of yours, what blessings does it bring?"

The brahmin replied: "It can ward off disaster, bring fortune, wealth, nobility, and long life."

The king asked again: "Can it free one from the cycle of birth and death?"

The brahmin replied: "It cannot free one from birth and death."

The king thought to himself: "What use is this path to follow?" He then commanded his ministers to prepare the royal carriage and went to the Buddha's presence. He prostrated with his five limbs touching the ground, made obeisance to the Buddha, and said: "I have heard that the Buddha's path is the most honored, majestic in its transforming teaching, liberating countless beings throughout the world. I wish to receive the words of the Dharma, so as to reform my conduct." The Buddha then gave the king the five precepts and the ten wholesome actions, and taught him that among all things in heaven, earth, and the human world, there is nothing born that does not die.

The king, touching his forehead to the ground in obeisance, addressed the Buddha: "Now that I have received these honored Dharma-precepts, what blessings shall I obtain?"

The Buddha said: "Through generosity and upholding the precepts, one obtains blessings in this present life. Through patience, diligence, single-mindedness, and wisdom, one's virtue is immeasurable. Afterward one ascends to the heavens. One may also become a wheel-turning king. One may also attain the unconditioned path of transcending the world." The Buddha then manifested for the king his excellent marks, spiritual authority, and brilliant radiance. The king immediately rejoiced, his mind was opened, and he attained the fruit of stream-entry.

Ānanda, straightening his robes and touching his forehead to the ground, made obeisance to the Buddha and said: "What was the original connection between this king and the ox? The ox spoke, and the king's mind was immediately opened. He abandoned the brahmin and followed the Buddha's path. Upon seeing the Buddha and hearing the Dharma, he immediately attained the fruit of the path."

The Buddha said: "Long ago, in the time of the Buddha Kanakamuni, the king and the ox were brothers. They were upāsakas who together observed the precepts for one day and one night. The king guarded the Dharma with diligence and without laxity; at the end of his life he ascended to heaven, and when his heavenly life ended, he was reborn as a king. The ox at that time broke the fast by eating at night, and afterward received the retribution for that fault. When the punishment was complete, he became an ox for a hundred lifetimes. Yet he still retained his awareness from past lives, and so he came to awaken the king's mind. Seven days later, the ox's life ended and he was reborn in heaven."

The Buddha said: "Disciples of the four groups! The observance of fasting and precepts must not be violated."

The bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, devas, nāgas, and spirits, hearing the sūtra, rejoiced, and came forward to make obeisance to the Buddha before departing.
