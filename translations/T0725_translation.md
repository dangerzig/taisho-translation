# 佛說六道伽陀經
## Verses on the Six Destinies, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0725

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmadeva (法天)

---

> I pay homage to all the Buddhas
> And to the assembly of bodhisattvas.
> May right wisdom be opened;
> I call to mind the Buddha's merit.
> Taking refuge in the Honored One of the Three Realms,
> The three karmas of body, speech, and mind,
> Whether good or evil in their deeds:
> For them I make this distinction.
> Those beings receive their retribution;
> There is no master or sovereign over it.
> O Honored One among the three realms,
> May you arise in compassion and wisdom,
> And broadly teach the world.
> I now hear what was spoken.
> Observing transmigration through the cycle,
> Contemplating karmic retribution:
> The Buddha has taught the causes of the evil paths;
> Greed, anger, and delusion are the root.

> If a person commits killing,
> That karma follows and binds them.
> They will certainly fall into the Saṃjīva hell
> For five hundred years before gaining release.
> Those in the Saṃjīva hell
> Are born and die again and again;
> Therefore it is called "Reviving."
> Parents and kinfolk,
> Family and good friends:
> If one deceives, slights, or despises them,
> One falls into the Kālasūtra hell.
> Blazing, rough, and coarse ropes
> Bind the bodies of beings;
> Like a saw cutting through wood,
> Therefore it is called "Black Thread."
> If one falls into the Tapana hell,
> Fierce flames compete to burn;
> Flame upon flame without cease
> Drives the sinful beings
> To rush and gather in one place.
> Deep is the suffering of burning;
> Therefore it is called "Scorching."
> Those who cultivate unlawful deeds
> And disturb the great assembly
> Are said to have no end to their punishment;
> They fall into the Pratāpana hell.
> Having entered this evil path,
> They are cooked in the most fierce fire.
> Long is the burning suffering;
> This is called "Extreme Scorching."
> Pigs, sheep, wolves, rabbits, and the like,
> And other living creatures:
> Those who commit such killing
> Fall into the Saṃghāta hell.
> Having fallen into that evil destiny,
> Mountains crush their bodies to pieces;
> The agony is unbearable,
> Therefore it is called "Crushing."
> If with body, speech, and mind
> One gives rise to afflictions,
> Deceiving sentient beings,
> One falls into the Raurava hell.
> Having entered that evil path,
> Violent fire burns their bodies;
> Their cries of anguish never cease,
> Therefore it is called "Screaming."
> Saints, pure practitioners, and teachers,
> And all their wealth and treasures:
> If one covets or steals these,
> One falls into the Mahāraurava hell.
> The great fire of stolen karma
> Burns their bodies,
> And they cry out with a great voice;
> This is called "Great Screaming."
> Those who have done great merit,
> And one's parents and the like:
> If one slanders them and repays kindness with harm,
> One will certainly fall into the Avīci hell.
> The instruments of punishment crush bone and marrow;
> Suffering is continuous without interruption.
> Body, life, and retribution are all thus;
> Therefore it is called "Without Interruption."
> Mutual love, hatred, and jealousy,
> Along with mutual killing and harm:
> One obtains a body in that evil destiny
> With hands bearing blade-like claws.
> These iron claws
> Are sixteen finger-widths long.
> With violently blazing bodies
> They scratch and tear at each other;
> Therefore it is called "Blade Claws."
> The foolish who commit sexual misconduct
> Climb the iron-thorned trees;
> Great iron-toothed demons,
> Ugly and with blazing bodies,
> Torment them without end.
> There are also iron crows and birds,
> Foxes and vicious beasts
> That devour those beings.
> There is also the Forest of Sword Leaves
> That cuts and slices the sinners;
> They cry out in terrible voices,
> Their agony unbearable.
> Those who speak deceit and falsehood
> Swallow hot iron pellets
> And drink molten copper,
> Again and again without rest.
> Those who scorn and cheat others:
> Iron-toothed evil beasts
> Come and eat the flesh from their bodies;
> They suffer for koṭīs of years.
> Those who love unlawful deeds
> Fall into the river of molten copper;
> Greatly blazing molten copper
> Boils them as they float and sink.
> Those who are themselves deluded
> And urge others to unlawful deeds
> Go to the Iron Wheel hell
> Where an iron fire-wheel
> Grinds their bodies countless times;
> Or they are ground by an iron millstone,
> Or they ascend the mountain of knives.
> Those who teach a false path,
> Destroying the right Dharma as wrong:
> The road is lined with sharp blades
> And they are made to walk back and forth.
> The power of karma makes their bodies large;
> They are driven and gathered in one place.
> Mountains of rock close in from all sides,
> Like a fingernail crushing a louse.
> Those who destroy the cultivation of practice
> And depart from the cause of right awakening,
> With biased clinging and perverted minds,
> Will certainly enter the Augmented hell.
> Violent, evil worms and larvae
> Constantly dwell in filth and excrement;
> When sinners walk therein,
> They devour their feet.
> In planting, pressing oil, and such activities,
> Many small crawling creatures are killed;
> One lies in iron troughs repeatedly
> And is beaten with hot iron rods.
> If one gives rise to extreme anger
> And commits such evil sins,
> At death one falls into Yama's realm
> And fully suffers all manner of pain.
> Break the bonds of evil seeds;
> With body, speech, and mind all pure,
> The wise should not commit sins.
> The appearance of the hells is thus.

*End of the section on the hells.*

> Cattle, donkeys, apes, and monkeys,
> Doves, pigeons, geese, and ducks:
> Through anger, greed, and lust,
> One receives retribution such as this.
> Jackals, wolves, fierce tigers, and leopards,
> Vipers, scorpions, and poisonous snakes:
> Through deep anger, arrogance, and pride,
> One receives retribution such as this.
> Crows, magpies, hawks, and vultures,
> Centipedes and various insects,
> Bears, cats, cattle, horses,
> Dragons, fish, and other creatures:
> Such animal births
> Are from increasing the three evil karmas.
> Falling into Yama's realm,
> One receives retribution such as this.

*End of the section on animals.*

> Obstructing others' meritorious giving,
> Stealing food and drink:
> One falls among the pūtana ghosts,
> Starving and becoming a hungry ghost.
> The foolish, miserly, and base,
> Arrogant and lacking in propriety,
> Seeking food without shame:
> At death they become great goiter-ghosts.
> Those who do not practice generosity themselves
> And urge others to be miserly
> Fall among the hungry ghosts
> With large bellies and throats like needles.
> Those deeply stingy and miserly
> Guard their wealth like their own eyes;
> To the poor, the ill, to the Buddha and Saṃgha,
> They cannot give even the smallest portion.
> They do not use it themselves,
> Nor can parents obtain any.
> At death they become hungry ghost bodies
> And never encounter even a drop of water.
> If one steals others' property
> And, after giving, the heart regrets,
> One also falls as a hungry ghost,
> Constantly eating pus and spittle.
> If with the mouth one speaks evil words,
> Slandering the worthy and good,
> One falls among those hungry ghosts
> Whose mouths blaze with fire.
> Through stinginess and quarreling,
> With evil intent eyeing others' wealth,
> One falls among those hungry ghosts
> Who only slightly obtain sacrificial food,
> Or who enter villages
> And find discarded food,
> Vomit, and saliva,
> Which they ever treat as fine fare.
> Those who are themselves stingy and obstructive,
> Who come between others and their giving,
> Become kumbhāṇḍa ghosts
> With ugly forms dripping with pus and blood.
> Those who slaughter living beings,
> Eating them themselves and giving to others,
> Fall as rākṣasa ghosts
> Who love to eat garlands, incense, and flesh.
> Though they give food and drink,
> They harbor a small portion of anger;
> They fall as gandharva ghosts
> Whose music is loved by the devas.
> Those who love divisive speech,
> Stirring up strife through anger,
> Fall as piśāca ghosts
> With ugly heads and faces.
> Though they love to practice generosity,
> They constantly cause others distress;
> They fall into that evil destiny
> As mātṛ-ghosts.
> Those who fiercely kill themselves
> And teach others fierce killing
> Fall as yakṣa bodies,
> Also violent and fierce.
> Parents, teachers, and elders,
> Whose wishes one constantly violates:
> One falls into the yakṣa palace,
> Courageous yet violent and savage.
> The retribution of stinginess and anger
> Is as hungry ghosts and yakṣas.
> Suffering and pleasure follow one's own cause;
> Do not commit any evil.

*End of the section on hungry ghosts.*

> Devas, asuras, and humans
> Differ in merit and lifespan.
> Those who joyfully seek birth in heaven
> Should firmly uphold the eight precepts of fasting;
> They will have long lives of happiness
> And be far from the suffering of illness.
> If one breaks and violates the precepts
> But has a small portion of joy in heavenly birth,
> One falls among the asuras
> With bhūtas as one's retinue.
> Though one does not steal others' wealth,
> If one does not give even a little,
> Being stingy and broadly miserly,
> One becomes a wealth-guarding ghost.
> Neither stealing nor giving,
> Neither stingy nor greedy:
> One certainly obtains a human body,
> But with great hardship in obtaining clothing and food.
> Neither stealing, nor greedy, nor angry,
> Content with one's lot and at peace:
> If one gives fine food to saints and sages,
> One is born in the human world
> With a long life, good complexion, and strength,
> Auspicious and free from illness.
> If one constantly gives food
> With pure faith and a sense of shame,
> One obtains a beautiful and honorable form,
> With abundant wealth and livestock.
> If one gives fields and residences
> And builds monasteries,
> Bringing joy to others' hearts,
> Then in one's own body and others',
> Every wish is fulfilled.
> If one gives shoes and sandals
> In offering to the Buddha and Saṃgha,
> One travels in comfort
> And always obtains carriages and mounts.
> If in the wilderness
> One provides water and digs wells,
> And further creates shade,
> So that people are free from weariness and thirst,
> One obtains wonderful flowers,
> Auspicious and broadly adorned,
> With a soft, rounded, and perfect body.
> If to a teacher of the Dharma
> One gives servants and attendants,
> One is later born as a human
> Bright and possessed of great wisdom.
> If one gives medicine,
> One is freed from all illness.
> If one gives lamps and lights,
> One's eyes are always clear and pure.
> If one gives music,
> One's speech is beautiful and wondrous.
> If one gives bedding,
> One's body will know ease and comfort.
> If one gives food to animals,
> One gains long life, good complexion, and strength.
> If one allows women to go forth,
> One's family will grow and increase.
> If one gives fields and land,
> One obtains flowers, fruit, and flowing springs.
> If one fears the cycle of rebirth,
> One should draw near to saints and sages,
> Offering whatever they need,
> Diligent and always respectful.
> Skillfully breaking through afflictions,
> One will obtain the fruit of happiness.
> With compassion and without miserliness,
> Setting aside one's own to give to others:
> One will certainly obtain the fruit of abundance;
> Clothing and food will be plentiful of themselves.
> As in the four seasons,
> Giving at the appropriate time,
> With reverence and proper ceremony,
> One obtains all blessings.
> Stealing others' property
> And using it for one's own needs
> Violates true emptiness;
> Afflictions are not abandoned.
> Without generating a mind of attachment to characteristics,
> Practicing generosity in accordance with principle:
> The fruit of such giving
> Is the unconditioned, great peace and happiness.
> As when a person is free from sexual desire
> And no sons are born,
> One goes far to a distant place
> Without thought or entanglement.
> If a person indulges in sexual desire,
> With a mind that never turns back,
> Mad and self-indulgent,
> One falls forever into the three evil paths.
> Rejecting women and womanly things,
> Upholding precepts and thinning deluded love,
> At the time of death
> It is like dispersing poisonous vapors.
> If one practices the cause of right awakening,
> Without abandoning the holy life,
> Through diligence one obtains auspiciousness,
> And devas and humans constantly make offerings.
> Firm and unconfused,
> Without drink, intoxicants, or false speech,
> Always speaking truth,
> One obtains fame and happiness.
> Preparing various foods and drinks
> To offer to the harmonious assembly,
> One obtains a good retinue,
> All born together in the Unshakable Land.
> Mutually attending to one another,
> With joyful minds without disagreement,
> Contemplating the emptiness of truth,
> Without deceit or inversion:
> All suffering is exhausted,
> And there is happiness and liberation.
> If one delights in idle chatter,
> Singing, dancing, and foolish attachment,
> Arrogant in one's fine appearance,
> Scorning the poor and humble:
> One receives a hunched and bent body,
> Mute and ugly in form,
> Constantly bound by illness,
> With rude and impolite speech.
> Sinful suffering only deepens;
> There is no cause for gaining happiness.
> A tranquil and serene mind
> Gives birth to all goodness.
> Retribution is never false;
> Swiftly accomplish the path free from suffering.

*End of the section on the human realm.*

> Through deceit and violating the precepts,
> Poisonous harm and deep quarreling,
> Broadly indulging in ignorance,
> One will certainly fall into the asura destiny.

*End of the section on asuras.*

> Abandoning fame, profit, and pleasure,
> Parting from relatives and kin,
> Upholding precepts at the middle or lower level,
> One is born in the Cāturmahārājika heaven.
> Making various offerings
> To parents and kinfolk,
> Avoiding quarrels and observing the discipline,
> One is born in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven.
> With loving-kindness and joy, without killing,
> A pleasant countenance free from love and hatred,
> Purely upholding the precepts,
> One is born in the Yāma realm.
> Being learned and upholding the Dharma's teachings,
> Cultivating wisdom and seeking liberation,
> Accumulating virtue with perfect deportment,
> One is born in the Tuṣita heaven.
> If a person goes forth from the household life
> And gives generously while firmly upholding the precepts,
> Bringing great happiness to others,
> One is born in the Nirmāṇarati heaven.
> Those of the highest faculties
> Who uphold the highest precepts,
> Whose merit surpasses those before,
> Are born in the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven.
> Upholding precepts leads to birth in the heavens;
> Meditative concentration is likewise.
> If wisdom is cultivated through practice,
> It draws one forth and further generates wisdom.
> The retribution of wholesome and evil karma:
> What I have spoken is without error.
> Through goodness one obtains happiness;
> Through evil one obtains suffering.
> Old age, sickness, and death revolve;
> Retribution is just like this.
> Carefully observe these three:
> Do not love them; one must let them go.
> Seek merit and depart from sin.
> Penetrating form and sound,
> Understanding the true meaning,
> One will certainly reach the great liberation.
