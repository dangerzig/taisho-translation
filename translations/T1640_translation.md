# 提婆菩薩釋楞伽經中外道小乘涅槃論
## Bodhisattva Āryadeva's Explanation of the Non-Buddhist and Hīnayāna Views of Nirvāṇa in the Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1640

Translated from the Chinese. Composed by Āryadeva (提婆). Later Wei dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流支).

---

Question: What are the views of nirvāṇa held by the non-Buddhists?

Answer: There are twenty kinds of nirvāṇa propounded by the non-Buddhists, which are the false discriminations of these non-Buddhists. Such causes as these can produce the six destinies. The Tathāgata, in order to block such wrong views, therefore taught the correct meaning of the causes and effects of nirvāṇa. What are the twenty?

First, the Hīnayāna non-Buddhist theorist. Second, the Direction theorist. Third, the Wind theorist. Fourth, the Veda theorist. Fifth, the Īśāna theorist. Sixth, the Naked Ascetic non-Buddhist theorist. Seventh, the Vaiśeṣika theorist. Eighth, the Ascetic-Practice theorist. Ninth, the Women's Retinue theorist. Tenth, the Practice-of-Austerities theorist. Eleventh, the Pure-Eye theorist. Twelfth, the Mātara theorist. Thirteenth, the Nirgrantha theorist. Fourteenth, the Sāṃkhya theorist. Fifteenth, the Maheśvara theorist. Sixteenth, the No-Cause theorist. Seventeenth, the Time theorist. Eighteenth, the Water-Drinking theorist. Nineteenth, the Mouth-Power theorist. Twentieth, the Primordial-Egg theorist.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that when all the aggregates of sensation are exhausted, like a lamp being extinguished, like a seed being destroyed, or like wind ceasing, this is called nirvāṇa?

Answer: The first, the Hīnayāna non-Buddhist theorist, says this.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that direction is nirvāṇa?

Answer: The second non-Buddhist, the Direction theorist, says that in the very beginning the directions were produced. From the directions, the people of the world were produced. From people, heaven and earth were produced. When heaven and earth are destroyed and dissolved, they return to that place, and this is called nirvāṇa. Therefore the Direction theorist says that direction is permanent and is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that wind is the cause of nirvāṇa?

Answer: The third non-Buddhist, the Wind-Ṛṣi theorist, says that wind can produce and sustain living beings, and wind can kill living beings. Wind creates all things, and wind can destroy all things. They call wind nirvāṇa. Therefore the Wind-Ṛṣi theorist says that wind is permanent and is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that Brahmā is the cause of nirvāṇa?

Answer: The fourth non-Buddhist, the Veda theorist, says that from the navel of Nārāyaṇa a great lotus was produced. From the lotus, Brahmā the Forefather was produced. That Brahmā created all sentient and insentient things. From Brahmā's mouth, brāhmaṇas were produced. From his two arms, kṣatriyas were produced. From his two thighs, vaiśyas were produced. From his two heels, śūdras were produced. The entire great earth is the arena for cultivating merit and precepts, producing all flowers, grasses, and other items for offerings. He also created the birds and beasts of the mountains and wilds, and among humans, pigs, sheep, donkeys, and horses. Within this arena, by killing and offering to Brahmā, one is born in that place, and this is called nirvāṇa. Therefore the Veda theorist says that Brahmā is permanent and is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that seeing no distinctions, and seeing permanence and impermanence, is nirvāṇa?

Answer: The fifth non-Buddhist, the Īśāna theorist and his followers, say this. The Revered One of the Īśāna theorists has a form that cannot be seen and pervades all places. Though without form, he can produce all sentient and insentient things among the myriad phenomena. This they call nirvāṇa. Therefore the Īśāna theorist and his followers say that Īśāna is permanent and is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists, by discrimination, see various different characteristics and call this nirvāṇa?

Answer: The sixth, the Naked Ascetic non-Buddhist theorist, says this.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that seeing the individual and universal characteristics of all dharmas is nirvāṇa?

Answer: The seventh non-Buddhist, the Vaiśeṣika theorist, says this: Earth, water, fire, wind, space, atoms, substance, quality, action, and universality are ten kinds of dharmas that are permanent. Through their combination, all sentient and insentient things of the world are produced. From two atoms, in succession, all dharmas are produced. Without the one who combines, there is no combination; without combination, there is dispersal. Dispersal is nirvāṇa. Therefore the Vaiśeṣika theorist says that atoms are permanent, that they produce all things, and that this is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that when the body is exhausted and merit is exhausted, this is called nirvāṇa?

Answer: The eighth, the Ascetic-Practice theorist, says this.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that the transformation of inherent nature and personal destiny is nirvāṇa?

Answer: The ninth non-Buddhist, the Women's Retinue theorist, says that Maheśvara created eight women. The first is named Aditi, the second Diti, the third Surabhī, the fourth Vinatā, the fifth Kapilā, the sixth Manu, the seventh Irā, and the eighth Kadrū. Aditi gave birth to the devas. Diti produced the asuras. Surabhī gave birth to the nāgas. Vinatā gave birth to the birds. Kapilā gave birth to four-footed creatures. Manu gave birth to humans. Irā gave birth to all grains and seeds. Kadrū gave birth to all snakes, scorpions, mosquitoes, gadflies, flies, fleas, centipedes, and the like. To know this is called nirvāṇa. Therefore the Women's Retinue theorist says that women are permanent and are the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that when transgressions, merit, and virtue are all exhausted, this is called nirvāṇa?

Answer: The tenth non-Buddhist, the Practice-of-Austerities theorist, says this.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that when afflictions are exhausted, reliance upon wisdom is nirvāṇa?

Answer: The eleventh non-Buddhist, the Pure-Eye theorist, says this.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that seeing a sovereign god create sentient beings is nirvāṇa?

Answer: The twelfth non-Buddhist, the Mātara theorist, conveys that the Nārāyaṇa theorist says: "I create all things. Among all sentient beings, I am the most supreme. I produce all sentient and insentient things in the world. I am the great Mount Sumeru King among all mountains. I am the great ocean among all waters. I am grain among all medicines. I am the ṛṣi Kapila among all ṛṣis. If a person with sincere mind makes offerings to me with water, grass, flowers, and fruits, I shall not abandon that person, and that person shall not lose me." The Mātara theorist says that the Nārāyaṇa theorist declares: "All things are produced from me and created by me. To return and dissolve into that place is called nirvāṇa." Therefore he is called permanent and is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that sentient beings mutually cause each other's arising and call this nirvāṇa?

Answer: The thirteenth non-Buddhist, the Nirgrantha theorist, says this: In the beginning, there was born one male together with one female. Those two, through their union, were able to produce all sentient and insentient things. Later, when they separate and dissolve, returning to that place is called nirvāṇa. Therefore the Nirgrantha theorist says that the union of male and female, producing all things, is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that realizing the path of truth is the cause of nirvāṇa?

Answer: The fourteenth non-Buddhist, the Sāṃkhya theorist, speaks of twenty-five tattvas. Primal nature as cause produces all sentient beings and is the cause of nirvāṇa. Primal nature is permanent. From primal nature arises the great principle. From the great principle arises the ego. From the ego arises intellect. From intellect arise the five subtle elements. From the five subtle elements arise the five sense faculties. From the five sense faculties arise the five action faculties. From the five action faculties arise the five gross elements. Therefore the treatise says: according to whatever nature one cultivates, practicing the twenty-five tattvas, truly knowing that from primal nature one arises and returns to primal nature, one can escape all birth and death and attain nirvāṇa. Thus, since all sentient beings arise from primal nature, the non-Buddhist Sāṃkhya says that primal nature is permanent, that it produces all dharmas, and that it is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that there is a maker and things made, and that their combination is nirvāṇa?

Answer: The fifteenth non-Buddhist, the Maheśvara theorist, says this: The effect is made by Nārāyaṇa; Brahmā is the cause. Maheśvara is one substance with three aspects, namely Brahmā, Nārāyaṇa, and Maheśvara. Earth is the foundation; the lord of the earth is Maheśvara. Among the three realms, all sentient and insentient things are born from Maheśvara. The body of Maheśvara: space is the head, earth is the body, water is the urine, mountains are the excrement, all sentient beings are the worms in the belly, wind is the life-force, fire is the warmth, and transgressions and merit are karma. These eight constitute the body of Maheśvara. The sovereign god is the cause of arising and ceasing. All things arise from the sovereign god and cease in the sovereign god; this is called nirvāṇa. Therefore the Maheśvara theorist says that the sovereign god is permanent, that he produces all things, and that he is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that all things arise spontaneously and call this nirvāṇa?

Answer: The sixteenth non-Buddhist, the No-Cause theorist, says this: Without cause and without conditions, all things arise. There is no cause of defilement and no cause of purification. Their treatise says: "Just as the thorns of brambles are sharp like needles without anyone making them, and the various painted colors of peacocks are all without anyone making them, they exist spontaneously and do not arise from causes." This they call nirvāṇa. Therefore the No-Cause theorist says that spontaneity is permanent, that it produces all things, and that it is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that all things are made by time and call this nirvāṇa?

Answer: The seventeenth non-Buddhist, the Time theorist, says this: Time ripens all things; time creates all things; time disperses all things. Therefore their treatise says: "Even if one is struck by a hundred arrows, when the time has not come, one does not die. When the time arrives, a mere touch of a small blade of grass brings death. All things are born in time; all things ripen in time; all things perish in time. Time cannot be surpassed." Therefore the Time theorist says that time is permanent, that it produces all things, and that it is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that seeing something existent is nirvāṇa?

Answer: The eighteenth non-Buddhist, the Water-Drinking theorist, says this: Water is the root of all things. Water can produce heaven and earth, and produce all sentient and insentient things. From the lowest Avīci Hell to the highest Akaniṣṭha Heaven, water is the master of all. Water can produce things; water can destroy things. This they call nirvāṇa. Therefore the non-Buddhist Water-Drinking theorist says that water is permanent and is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that seeing something nonexistent is the cause of nirvāṇa?

Answer: The nineteenth non-Buddhist, the Mouth-Power theorist, says that space is the cause of all things. In the very beginning, space was produced. From space arose wind; from wind arose fire; from fire arose warmth; warmth produced water; and water, freezing solid, formed earth. From earth arose various medicinal herbs; from various medicinal herbs arose the five grains; and from the five grains arose life. Therefore their treatise says: "Life is food." In the end, all returns and dissolves into space, and this is called nirvāṇa. Therefore the non-Buddhist Mouth-Power theorist says that space is permanent and is the cause of nirvāṇa.

---

Question: Which non-Buddhists say that seeing both existent and nonexistent things is the cause of nirvāṇa?

Answer: The twentieth non-Buddhist, the Primordial-Egg theorist, says: Originally there were no sun, moon, stars, space, or earth; there was only the great water. At that time, a great egg was produced, like a hen's egg, golden all around. When the time was ripe, it split into two halves: one half above became heaven, one half below became earth. In the space between the two, Brahmā was born, called the Forefather of all sentient beings. He created all sentient and insentient things. When these sentient and insentient things disperse and dissolve, returning to that place is called nirvāṇa. Therefore the non-Buddhist Primordial-Egg theorist says that the great egg, which produced Brahmā, is permanent and is the cause of nirvāṇa.
