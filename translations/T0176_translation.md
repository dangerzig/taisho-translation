# 師子月佛本生經
## Sūtra of the Previous Life of Siṃhacandra Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0176

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the city of Rājagṛha, in the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and one hundred bodhisattvas.

At that time, among the assembly there was a bodhisattva bhikṣu named Vasumitra, who wandered through the Bamboo Grove, climbing up and down trees, making sounds like a monkey, or holding three bells and performing the nāṭya dance. At that time, elders and travelers gathered in crowds to watch. When the crowd had assembled, he rose into the air, leaped to the treetops, and made the cry of a macaque. Eighty-four thousand golden macaques from Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa gathered at the bodhisattva's place, and the bodhisattva performed various transformations to delight them. At that time, everyone in the assembly said: "These śramaṇas of the Śākya clan are like performers, deceiving people with illusions. Their conduct is wicked, and no one trusts them. They even engage in improper behavior with birds and beasts." Such slanderous talk spread throughout Rājagṛha.

A certain brāhmaṇa went up to the great King Bimbisāra and said: "The śramaṇas of the Śākya clan are performing improper acts, even doing the nāṭya dance with birds and beasts." When the king heard this, he looked with displeasure upon the Śākya disciples and immediately ordered the elder Kalandaka: "These śramaṇas of the Śākya clan have gathered many macaques in your garden. What are they doing? Does the Tathāgata know of this?" The elder reported to the king: "Vasumitra performs transformative deeds, making all the macaques joyful. The devas rain flowers as offerings. As for what he is doing, your servant does not know."

At that time, the great King Bimbisāra rode his finest elephant, with attendants before and behind, and went to the Buddha's dwelling. He arrived at the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove and dismounted from his elephant. From afar he saw the World-Honored One on the upper story of a pavilion. His body was the color of purple gold, his proportioned frame a full jōjana and six, seated upon a seven-treasured lotus. The thirty-two major marks and eighty minor marks all radiated light, like a purple-gold mountain set within blazing fire, ringed by golden radiance, so that the entire assembly appeared golden. The venerable Vasumitra and the eighty-four thousand macaques likewise appeared golden.

At that time, the macaques, seeing the great king arrive, some sang, some danced, beat drums, blew conches, and performed various transformations. Some among them gathered flowers and offered them to the king. When the great king saw this, he went with the assembly to the Buddha's presence, bowed to the Buddha, circumambulated three times to the right, sat to one side, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What past merit do these macaques possess that their bodies are golden? And what past offense caused them to be born as animals? As for the venerable Vasumitra, what past merit did he plant that he was born in an elder's family, left the household life in faith to study the path? And what past offense accounts for his being born in human form with all faculties complete, yet not upholding the precepts, keeping company with macaques, with his singing and speech all sounding like a macaque, so that the non-Buddhist paths mock us? May the Honored One of Heaven, in compassion for us, explain and teach this so that we may understand."

The Buddha told the great king: "Listen carefully! Listen carefully! Reflect well upon this! I shall explain it for you in full. Long ago, in the past, through countless billions of kalpas, before that time, a Buddha appeared in the world named Dīpaṃkara, complete in the ten titles. After that Buddha's parinirvāṇa, there were bhikṣus who practiced the Buddha's Dharma in the mountains and marshlands. They cultivated the twelve dhūtaguṇa of the āraṇyaka and upheld the precepts as carefully as a person protects his eyes. Because of this, they attained the path of the arhat, with the three knowledges, six supernatural powers, and the eight liberations. At that time, in the empty marshland there was a macaque who came to the arhat's place. Seeing the arhat sitting in meditation, the macaque took the arhat's sitting cloth, draped it like a kāṣāya, and in the manner of a śramaṇa, bared its right shoulder, held an incense burner in its hands, and circumambulated the bhikṣu. At that time, the bhikṣu arose from samādhi and saw that this macaque had a good and wholesome heart. He snapped his fingers and told the macaque: 'Dharma-child! You should now generate the aspiration for the supreme path.' When the macaque heard this, it leaped with joy, prostrated with all five limbs on the ground, and paid respect to the bhikṣu. Then it gathered flowers and scattered them upon the bhikṣu.

"At that time, the bhikṣu taught the macaque the three refuges, saying: 'Dharma-child! You should now follow the Dharma of the Buddhas of the three times and request to receive the three refuges and the five precepts.' The macaque then stood up, joined its palms, and said: 'Great Virtuous One! Please keep me in mind. I now wish to take refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha.' The bhikṣu said: 'You should take refuge in the Buddha, take refuge in the Dharma, take refuge in the Sangha.' A second and third time he spoke likewise: 'The refuge in the Buddha is complete. The refuge in the Dharma is complete. The refuge in the Sangha is complete.' A second and third time he spoke likewise. 'Next you should repent.' He told the macaque: 'In your past lives, through countless kalpas, you have committed the defilements of greed, anger, delusion, wrong views, jealousy, arrogance, and slander. You have broken the precepts and committed evil deeds, fulfilling the ten evils, the five heinous acts, slandering the vaipulya sūtras, violating bhikṣuṇīs, stealing Sangha property, and committing countless grave offenses. My own births are exhausted, and I shall not receive further existence. A great arhat can remove the immeasurable grave offenses of sentient beings. Why is this? Because from my very first birth, I was born together with great compassion. The sages of the three times are all likewise: they too are born in the world together with great compassion.' In this earnest manner, three times he taught the macaque to confess and repent. When the repentance was complete, he told the macaque: 'Dharma-child! You are now purified. This is called the poṣadha. From this day until the end of your life, receive the precept of not killing. The Buddhas of the three times and the arhats forever abstain from killing, with body, speech, and mind purified. You should likewise.'

"At that time, the macaque said to the arhat: 'I vow to become a Buddha. Following the great virtuous one's words, from this day until I attain Buddhahood, I shall never take a life.' When the arhat heard the macaque's words, his body and mind were filled with joy, and he immediately conferred the five precepts: 'Dharma-child! From this day until the end of your life, follow the Buddha's Dharma. The Buddhas of the three times and all the śrāvakas have purified their bodily actions and never take life, upholding the precept of not killing. You should likewise, upholding the precept of not killing until the end of your life. Can you accept and uphold this?' The macaque answered: 'I can uphold it.' Next it received the precepts of not stealing, not engaging in sexual misconduct, not speaking falsely, and not drinking alcohol, each in the same manner as above. When the precepts had been received, the arhat said: 'You should now make a vow. You are an animal, and your present body obstructs the path, but exert yourself diligently and seek the supreme, perfect enlightenment.'

"At that time, having made its vow, the macaque leaped with joy, ran up a high mountain, climbed a tree and danced, then fell to the ground and died. Because the arhat had conferred the five precepts, the animal's karma was broken, and at death the macaque was immediately reborn in the Tuṣita heaven. There it encountered a bodhisattva of one more birth, who taught it the aspiration for the supreme path. It then took celestial flowers and descended to the empty marshland to make offerings to the arhat. When the arhat saw it, he smiled and said: 'Heavenly King! The retribution of good and evil follows like a shadow following the body; it never parts from you.' The arhat then spoke this verse:

> "Karma adorns the body,
> Following it wherever it goes.
> It is not lost, like a bond of debt;
> Karma is like one who owes a debt.
> That you are now born in heaven
> Is due to the karma of the five precepts.
> Your former birth as a macaque
> Came from the nature of broken precepts.
> Upholding precepts is the ladder to heaven;
> Breaking precepts becomes a cauldron of boiling.
> I have seen those who uphold precepts:
> Light and splendor adorn their bodies.
> In pavilions of the seven treasures,
> Devas serve as their attendants.
> Jeweled beds with curtains,
> Maṇi flowers and necklaces;
> They encounter the future Buddha,
> Delighting in hearing the supreme Dharma.
> I have seen those who break precepts:
> They fall into the hells.
> Iron plows till their tongues;
> They lie upon iron beds.
> Molten copper flows from all sides,
> Burning and boiling, destroying their bodies.
> Or they dwell on Sword Mountain,
> In forests of blades and pools of boiling filth,
> In the ash river and freezing hells,
> Swallowing iron pellets and drinking molten copper.
> Such sufferings as these
> Are their constant adornments.
> If you wish to escape these many difficulties,
> Not falling into the three evil paths,
> Traveling the road of devas and humans,
> And transcending to attain nirvāṇa,
> You should diligently uphold the pure precepts,
> Practice generosity and cultivate a pure livelihood."

"When the arhat had spoken this verse, he fell silent. The macaque devaputra said: 'Great Virtuous One! In my former life, what offense did I commit to be born among macaques? And what merit allowed me to encounter the Great Virtuous One, be freed from the animal realm, and be reborn in heaven?' The arhat answered: 'Listen carefully now! Reflect well upon this! Long ago in the past, in this Jambudvīpa, a Buddha appeared in the world named Ratnamati Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Perfectly Enlightened One, complete in the ten titles, who appeared in the world. After the three kinds of demonstration and his parinirvāṇa, during the age of the semblance Dharma, there was a bhikṣu named Padmagarbha, who was closely associated with kings, elders, and householders. He practiced wrong livelihood and flattery, and did not uphold the precepts. When his body perished and his life ended, because of his deceit, as swiftly as a strong man bends and extends his arm, he fell into the Avīci great hell. His body spread like an opening lotus, filling all eighteen divisions of the hell. Hot iron pellets rained down and entered through his crown. Hundreds of thousands of raging fires and hot iron wheels descended from the sky, immeasurable and boundless. The lifespan in Avīci hell is one kalpa. When that kalpa was exhausted, he was reborn again. In this way he passed through all the great hells for a full eighty-four thousand kalpas. Emerging from hell, he fell into the realm of hungry ghosts, swallowing molten copper and eating hot iron pellets. After eighty-four thousand years among the hungry ghosts, for five hundred lifetimes he was always an ox. For another five hundred lifetimes he was born among camels. For another five hundred lifetimes he was born among swine. For another five hundred lifetimes he was born among dogs. For another five hundred lifetimes he was born among macaques. Because in the past he had made offerings to a precept-keeping bhikṣu and made weighty vows, he has now encountered me again, been bathed in purification, and been reborn in heaven. The precept-keeping bhikṣu is my present self. The negligent bhikṣu is your present self.'

"At that time, the macaque devaputra, hearing these words, was alarmed, his hair standing on end. He repented his past offenses and returned to heaven. The Buddha told the great king: 'Although that macaque was an animal, having once seen an arhat and received the three refuges and the five precepts, by the merit of this it transcended a thousand kalpas of extremely grave evil karma and was reborn in heaven. It encountered a bodhisattva of one more birth, and from then on encountered countless Buddhas, purified its brahma-practice, perfected the six pāramitās, dwelt in the śūraṅgama samādhi, and abided in the stage of non-retrogression. In its final birth, after Maitreya, it shall attain the supreme, perfect enlightenment. The king shall be named Ratnaprabha. The land shall be as pure as the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. All sentient beings born in that land shall practice the ten wholesome deeds and keep the precepts without fault. The Buddha shall be named Siṃhacandra Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Perfectly Enlightened One, Possessed of Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone One, Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Men, Teacher of Devas and Humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One. If any sentient being hears that Buddha's name, in every life they shall always be free from birth as an animal and shall be relieved of the offenses of immeasurable kalpas of birth and death.'"

The Buddha told the great king: "Do you wish to know who the Siṃhacandra Buddha of that land will be? It is the bhikṣu Vasumitra, here in this assembly." When Bimbisāra heard this, he immediately stood up, joined his palms, sweat covering his body, tears of remorse streaming down, and rebuked himself. He prostrated before Vasumitra with his face to the ground, touched his feet in respect, and repented his former offenses. The Buddha told the great king: "Do you wish to know who these eighty-four thousand golden macaques are? In the time of Krakucchanda Buddha, in the countries of Vārāṇasī and Kauśāmbī, there were eighty-four thousand bhikṣuṇīs who engaged in improper conduct, sending messages to laymen, violating grave prohibitions, adorning their bodies like gandharva maidens without any shame, wearing lust as their necklaces, using their transgressions as garlands of flowers, raising the banner of arrogance, beating the drum of conceit, strumming the lute of heedlessness, singing songs of praise for wicked voices, wild and foolish like mad macaques, and regarding good and virtuous bhikṣus as enemies.

"At that time, in that world there was a bhikṣuṇī named Sukhāvāsa, who had attained the path of the arhat, with the three knowledges, six supernatural powers, and the eight liberations. She went to those bhikṣuṇīs and said: 'Sisters! The World-Honored One, when he was in the world, always spoke this verse:

> "If a bhikṣuṇī
> Does not cultivate the eight forms of respect,
> She is not a daughter of the Śākya clan
> But is like a caṇḍāla.
> If a bhikṣuṇī
> Heedlessly violates the eight grave precepts,
> Know that such a one is
> The greatest thief among devas and humans.
> She shall dwell forever in Avīci hell,
> Passing through all eighteen divisions.
> The remaining three evil paths
> Shall be her garden and grove,
> For hundreds of thousands of immeasurable kalpas,
> Never hearing the name of the Three Treasures.
> She shall also swallow burning iron pellets,
> In freezing cold, embracing copper pillars.
> When such offenses are exhausted,
> She shall be born as a pigeon or dove,
> A venomous snake, a rat, a weasel,
> A centipede, a millipede, and such.
> Through all these various kinds of creatures,
> She must pass."'

"When the bhikṣuṇīs heard the arhat bhikṣuṇī speak this verse, they were filled with anger and cursed her with harsh words: 'This old macaque, where did she come from? Speaking evil and telling lies, fabricating tales of hell!' The arhat, seeing these wicked people generating unwholesome minds, arose with compassion, and her body rose into the sky, where she performed the eighteen transformations. When those wicked ones saw these transformations, each removed her golden bracelets and scattered them upon the arhat bhikṣuṇī, saying: 'May we be born life after life with golden bodies. We now fully repent all the evil we have done. We only beg that you, in compassion and pity for us, accept our offerings.' The arhat bhikṣuṇī then descended from the sky and received the various offerings of those wicked women. When those wicked ones died, they fell into Avīci hell. Their bodies spread like opening lotuses, filling all the hells, and they passed in turn through all the remaining eighteen great hells. The lifespan in each hell was exactly one great kalpa. In this way, through ninety-two kalpas they constantly dwelt in hell. Emerging from hell, for five hundred lifetimes they were always hungry ghosts. Emerging from the hungry ghost realm, for one thousand lifetimes they were always macaques with golden bodies. Great king! Know that the eighty-four thousand bhikṣuṇīs who broke the precepts and cursed the arhat are now the eighty-four thousand golden macaques in this assembly. Those who made offerings to those wicked bhikṣuṇīs are now the great king himself. Because of their habitual tendencies from past lives, these macaques bring flowers and incense to make offerings to the great king. Those who in the past defiled the precepts of those bhikṣuṇīs are now Kokālika and the king's five hundred eunuchs." The Buddha told the great king: "One must be careful with the karma of body, speech, and mind." At that time, the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "The precepts are the medicine of sweet dew;
> Those who take it never grow old or die.
> The virtue of precepts is a reliable support;
> Meritorious reward always follows you.
> Upholding precepts brings peace and safety;
> In every birth, there are no calamities.
> One shall also see the Buddhas,
> Receive the Dharma, and attain liberation.
> Breaking precepts leads to hell,
> Just as with these macaques.
> In every birth, one is lowly and base;
> The suffering of hell cuts to the bone.
> Great king, listen carefully!
> Cease evil and cultivate all that is good."

At that time, King Bimbisāra, hearing this verse, repented before the Buddha, ashamed and rebuking himself, and his mind was suddenly opened. He attained the fruit of the non-returner. Eight thousand of the king's retinue asked the king's permission to go forth. The king granted it. The Buddha said: "Welcome, bhikṣus!" Their hair fell away of itself and kāṣāyas appeared on their bodies. They immediately became śramaṇas, prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and before they raised their heads they had attained the path of the arhat, with the three knowledges, six supernatural powers, and the eight liberations. The remaining sixteen thousand of the king's retinue all generated the aspiration for the supreme, perfect enlightenment. Eighty thousand devas also generated the aspiration. The eighty-four thousand golden macaques, hearing the causes and conditions of their past, were ashamed and rebuked themselves. They circumambulated the Buddha a thousand times, repented before the Buddha, and each generated the aspiration for supreme enlightenment. According to their respective lifespans, when each died, they were to be reborn in the Tuṣita heaven, encounter Maitreya, make further progress, and attain the stage of non-retrogression.

At that time, the venerable Mahākāśyapa, seeing these events, told the great assembly: "The bodhisattva's practice is so pure that it can even cause animals to generate the aspiration for the path. If Vasumitra can accomplish such great Buddha-work, how much more so for other bodhisattvas of immeasurable power and virtue?"

At that time, the devaputras, mountain spirits, earth spirits, devas, nāgas, and the eight classes of protectors, seeing the macaques generate the aspiration for enlightenment, knowing they would be reborn in heaven and attain the stage of non-retrogression, were joyful in their hearts and said to the Buddha: "When will these macaques attain Buddhahood?"

The Buddha told the great assembly: "After one hundred million billions of nayutas of asaṃkhyeya Ganges-river-sands of kalpas, in a kalpa named Great Light, they shall attain Buddhahood. Eighty-four thousand Buddhas shall appear in succession in that single kalpa, all sharing the same name: Universal Golden Light King Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly Enlightened One, Possessed of Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone One, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Men, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One."

When the great assembly heard the World-Honored One bestow the prediction of enlightenment upon the macaques, they all removed their finest necklaces from their bodies and offered them to the Tathāgata and the sangha of bhikṣus. With one voice they praised the World-Honored One's immeasurable virtue: "The Tathāgata appeared in the world precisely for the sake of these macaques and their kind. Excellent! World-Honored One! If macaques who hear the Dharma can attain Buddhahood, how much more so shall we in a future life!"

When the great assembly heard what the Buddha taught, they joyfully accepted it, put it into practice, bowed, and withdrew.
