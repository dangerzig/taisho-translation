# 佛說鬼子母經
## Sūtra of the Mother of Ghost-Children Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1262

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

The Buddha was traveling in the great kingdom of Dou. At that time, in that kingdom there was a mother with many children whose nature was extremely evil. She constantly stole other people's children and devoured them. The families of the lost children did not know who had taken them, and they wept and wailed in the streets. People gathered to discuss the matter, and this had been going on for more than a day.

Ānanda and the bhikṣus went out and continually encountered weeping people. When they returned, they discussed together, feeling sorrow for the bereaved families. The Buddha immediately knew what the bhikṣus were discussing, and he went to where they were.

The Buddha asked the bhikṣus: "What were you just discussing?" The bhikṣus and Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Just now, when we went out on our begging rounds, we saw many weeping people in the streets. We asked those who were weeping: 'Why are you crying?' They replied: 'Our children have been taken alive, and we do not know where their bodies are.' Those weeping like this were not from just one family; all had lost children."

The Buddha then explained to Ānanda and the bhikṣus: "The one who steals children in this kingdom is no ordinary person; she is the Mother of Ghost-Children. In this life she has been born as a human, but she habitually steals other people's children. This mother has a thousand sons: five hundred sons in heaven and five hundred sons in the world. All thousand sons are ghost kings, and each king commands tens of thousands of ghosts. Thus five hundred ghost kings in heaven torment the devas, and five hundred ghost kings in the world torment emperors and common people. Even the heavens can do nothing about these five hundred ghost kings."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "The Mother of Ghost-Children has come to this kingdom. Can we not now command her to stop stealing children?"

The Buddha said: "Very good! We can cause her to never steal children again."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What skillful means should we use to make her stop stealing children?"

The Buddha then told Ānanda: "Go to the place where this mother dwells. All the bhikṣus together should watch for this mother to go out. After she leaves, gather up all her children and bring them back to the monastery, hiding them there." The bhikṣus went at once. They watched for the mother to go out, then followed behind and gathered up her children. They obtained a thousand children and hid them in the monastery.

The mother then went out again to steal more children. When she returned to her dwelling and did not see her own children, she set down the other person's child and dared not kill it. She went searching for her children, but throughout her dwelling she could not find them. She went out into the streets and searched the entire city but could not find them. She went outside the city to search but could not find them. She came back into the city and walked the streets weeping. After ten days like this, the mother went mad. With disheveled hair she entered the marketplace, wailing, beating her breast, throwing herself on the ground, and crying aloud to heaven, speaking wildly, unable to eat or drink.

The Buddha sent a bhikṣu to see her. Meeting the mother, he asked: "Why are you in the marketplace with disheveled hair, weeping?"

The mother replied to the bhikṣu: "So many of my children have been taken. That is why I weep."

The bhikṣu said: "Do you want to get your children back?"

The mother replied: "I want to get them back."

The bhikṣu said: "If you truly want them back, there is a buddha here. Go and ask the Buddha. The Buddha knows the affairs of the future and the past. If you go to him, you can get your children back." Hearing these words, the mother was delighted and her mind opened. She followed the bhikṣu. Arriving at the Buddha's place, she joyfully came forward and bowed to the Buddha.

The Buddha asked the mother: "Why were you weeping in the marketplace?"

The mother replied: "Because my children have been taken."

The Buddha asked the mother: "Where did you go that you lost your children?"

The mother was silent and did not speak.

The Buddha asked again: "Where did you go and where did you return from?"

She was silent, without a word.

The mother knew that stealing children was evil. She immediately rose and bowed to the Buddha, her face touching the ground: "I was foolish and ignorant."

The Buddha then asked: "You have children. Do you love them?"

The mother said: "I have children. Whether sitting or standing, I always want them by my side."

The Buddha then asked: "You have children and you love them. Why then do you daily steal other people's children? Other people have children and love them just as you love yours. The families of lost children also walk the streets weeping, just as you do. Yet you steal their children and devour them. After death you will fall into the Great Mountain Hell."

Hearing these words, the mother was struck with terror.

The Buddha then asked: "Do you wish to get your children back?"

The mother immediately rose and pressed her face to the ground: "I beg your compassion."

The Buddha then told the mother: "If your children are alive, can you repent?" If you can repent, I shall return your children to you."

The mother said: "I can repent."

The Buddha said: "If you can repent, what form shall your repentance take?"

The mother said: "I shall listen to the Buddha's admonition and shall follow the Buddha's words in repenting. If the Buddha returns my children, I shall never dare to depart from the Buddha's instructions."

The Buddha said: "Is what you say truly so?"

The mother said: "What I say is truly as the Buddha's words."

The Buddha then conferred upon her the five precepts: first, not to kill; second, not to steal; third, not to commit sexual misconduct; fourth, not to speak falsely; fifth, not to drink alcohol. She accepted them all, and all her children were returned.

The Buddha then explained to her: "You have a thousand sons." He spoke all thousand sons' names. "Five hundred sons are in heaven, all ghost kings, leading ghost retinues who maliciously torment the celestial beings. Five hundred sons are in the world, tormenting emperors and common people. Your sons, as ghost kings, each command tens of thousands of ghosts. Thus your five hundred sons and their ghost retinues are beyond counting. They are extremely malicious and evil. Some falsely claim to be tree spirits, some claim to be earth spirits, some claim to be water spirits, some impersonate brothers, wives, or children to cause resentment within families, some claim to be sea spirits, some claim to be spirits of ships and carriages, some claim to be spirits of houses and dwellings, some claim to be spirits of the night and darkness, some cause people to have dreams and awaken, some cause people to feel terror and create monstrous omens, some falsely claim that someone died under such-and-such a star, or claim that someone died of such-and-such a disease. In such ways they confuse and disturb people, appearing in no single fixed place. They are extremely malicious and evil. Through such false claims, they cause people to make blood sacrifices, slaughtering animals. People do not know that they prepare food and drink for these ghosts, yet these ghosts do not even eat the food. They are simply malicious, wishing to cause people to commit killing, wishing to cause people to fall into hell. They certainly do not eat it. They are merely pleased when they see people making sacrifices. Such ghosts cannot protect or preserve human life; they only increase sins. Foolish people do not understand this and are impoverished because of ghosts."

The Mother of Ghost-Children, hearing the Buddha speak these words, immediately single-mindedly repented and attained the fruit of stream-entry, knowing the affairs of the past and future. Kneeling, she addressed the Buddha: "I was foolish and ignorant. Life after life I have committed such evil. Now I have been able to uphold the precepts and contemplate the Middle Way. My mind sees through everything clearly; I see all my thousand children again. Now I truly know: the Buddha's words, wherever they reach, are perfectly accomplished. I beseech the Buddha to have compassion on me! I wish to dwell beside the Buddha's monastery. I wish to call my thousand princely sons. I wish to have them make vows with the Buddha. I wish to repay the kindness of all beings in heaven and on earth."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! That you have such an intention is very good." The Buddha said: "From this day forward, you shall speak these words and dwell beside the Buddha's monastery. The people of this kingdom who have no children and come seeking children: you shall give them children, as they wish. I shall command the kṣatriya families to follow and protect people, and they must not recklessly torment them."

Those who wish to make requests of the Mother of Ghost-Children: her son is named Maṇibhadra, and her elder sister is named Jaṭānī. All ghosts in heaven and on earth are under Maṇibhadra's command. He governs all ships, carriages, livelihoods, and wealth within the four seas; all belong to Maṇibhadra. Maṇibhadra made vows with the Buddha and received the precepts, and he is in charge of protecting people's property. Jaṭānī is in charge of childbirth; when people give birth, she rescues them. There is a heavenly king named Vaiśravaṇa, who governs the four quarters of heaven and earth and protects human life. When going out or coming in, one should always make requests of Vaiśravaṇa. There is a great ghost king named Asura, who governs all nāga kings and all poisonous vapors. If people make requests of him, he causes poison not to touch them. When making requests, one should do so with a compassionate mind. There is no need to make offerings of thanks, nor any need to offer them flesh to eat. Those who make requests simply ask for whatever a person seeks, and it is given. There is absolutely nothing demanded in return, nor do they blame people. People should go before Maṇibhadra and bow in homage.
