# 佛說薩羅國經
## Sūtra of the Land of Sāla, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0520

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Long ago, there was a great country named Sāla, whose territory was vast and whose capital was located in a pure and splendid place with many people. The population was wealthy and prosperous, clever and refined, fond of ornament and literary embellishment. The land produced many precious treasures and fine goods of every color. The city walls, towers, streets, gates, and dwellings were inlaid with gold and silver, beautiful in their proportions. Around the city were bathing pools in which lotus flowers grew. Ducks and geese, mandarin ducks, cuckoos, birds of paradise, peacocks, parrots, cranes, and cormorants flew about chasing one another, all in the pools, resting day and night and calling in harmony. Men and women amused themselves with singing and entertainment, never growing weary. Their happiness was beyond words. They were proud and self-indulgent, knowing nothing of the Buddha-Dharma. Each took delight in their own pleasures, considering them unmatched in the world.

The Buddha was at the Jetavana garden in Śrāvastī. The Buddha saw that this country was so absorbed in pleasure, not reflecting on the suffering of impermanence, birth, and death, greedy for sensual desires without cease. The Buddha thought that in that country, the cycle of birth and death would only increase. Without the exercise of skillful wisdom, who could save them? He then devised expedient means to open their minds: "Whatever has form and appearance should be met with emptiness. Whatever brings pleasure should be met with suffering. Through skillful practice, following their inclinations, I shall free them from false perception and consciousness." The Buddha then manifested his supernatural power of the samādhi of sovereign mastery, emitting a great radiance that illuminated all things without exception. He moved the eight directions and all beings, human and non-human. The devas, nāga spirits, and all followed in attendance behind him. Śakra and Brahmā held precious canopies of the seven treasures and offered them to the Buddha. Maitreya, Mañjuśrī, Maudgalyāyana, Rāhula, Ānanda, Revata, Śāriputra, and all the disciples and bodhisattvas, innumerable thousands, all followed in his retinue and went to that country. The hundred birds and animals called together in harmony. The singing and musical instruments sounded without being played. Withered trees sprouted new growth. In the rivers, canals, and seas, the turtles, tortoises, and water-creatures all rejoiced. The three-thousand lands all shook greatly. From the ground, lotus flowers as large as chariot wheels arose, composed of precious treasures and lapis lazuli interwoven, their colors wondrous, their radiance dazzling to the eye.

The Buddha emitted radiance that illuminated all things, and in all the dark and painful places, everything was opened and freed without obstruction. The king, great ministers, elders, householders, the prince of the inner palace, noblewomen, and beautiful attendants, great and small throughout the country, were all struck with awe: "What has happened today that such things appear? We have always taken our pleasure within the palace, entertained by the five pleasures, with palace ladies playing music for us, enjoying happiness without compare. What we now see is something rare in all the world."

At that time, the earth-spirit Pṛthivī surged up from the ground and appeared before the king's palace, praising the Buddha's merit: "The World-Honored One's manifestations and radiance surpass the sun and moon. The devas revere him; Śakra and Brahmā honor him. The defilements of the world are removed, and he delivers people from birth and death. Those who see the Buddha have their sins eliminated. Those who make offerings to the Buddha receive immeasurable merit. By planting the roots of virtue, they will be reborn in the heavens. To give rise to a single thought of wisdom yields limitless gain. You should go and see the Buddha, and receive his Dharma teachings."

The king's mind then gradually opened, and he was filled with inner joy: "The Tathāgata has descended with his divine presence into our country's territory. All beings will receive deliverance." Not only himself, but all his retinue and all the others went out from the city, overjoyed and elated.

Through the Buddha's supernatural power, the entire territory of the country was brilliantly illuminated, clear and boundless. The king and the people all went to see the Buddha. How fortunate! The roots of merit and wholesome intentions had been born! They beheld the Buddha's honored countenance, radiant with golden light, awe-inspiring and glorious, adorned with auspicious marks, his features bright and clear, his mind serene and tranquil. The king and the people all came forward and bowed to the Buddha, touched their foreheads to the ground, circumambulated the Honored One three times, and stood before him. They addressed the Buddha, confessing: "We have long been immersed in the turbid waters of sensual craving, deluded by sounds and forms, and have not seen the Tathāgata to make offerings and ask about the Dharma."

The Buddha said: "Very good! King, ministers, and people, is the prince of the inner palace well?"

The king said: "Through the kindness of the Buddha, all is as it should be."

The Buddha said: "King, you have been immersed in sensual desires, indulging your mind without restraint. You levy taxes on wealth and treasures, fine foods and delicacies, gardens and bathing pools for amusement without limit. You do not reflect on impermanence. How does this benefit you, even one part in ten thousand? A person bound by desire, not reflecting on future lives, will thereby go to the mud-prisons and the realm of animals. All because of insatiable craving: the body is burned and maimed, hungry without food, thirsty without water, wandering in grief and misery, suffering in turn, all because of heedless and perverse minds that commit evil. In the world, though one is wealthy and holds a high position in a deep palace, all of this is impermanent, like having awakened from a dream. When one contemplates one's life, one departs alone; not a single thing follows."

The king then knelt before the Buddha and said: "World-Honored One! By what means can I be freed from these sins? The Buddha is the Dharma-King, the refuge of all. The Buddha is the true and honored one, the lord whom all beings look up to. May you lift me up and deliver me from this suffering."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! King, establish the root of faith. By practicing four things you may be freed from sin: first, give of what you have without attachment; second, diminish desire and anger, and reduce gluttonous eating; third, hear the Buddha's sūtras and precepts, and receive them with faith, not violating them; fourth, revere the Dharma teachers and cultivate good spiritual friends. These are the four things by which you may attain purity."

The king's mind then wished to practice great generosity. He came forward, bowed to the Honored One, joined his palms, and said to the Buddha: "May the Honored One graciously condescend to come to the palace for a modest meal." The Buddha remained silent, having accepted the invitation.

The king returned to the palace and commanded his ministers and officials: "The Buddha is as rare to encounter as the udumbara flower. Now that we have met him, we should make excellent offerings. Bring out all the flowers, incense, banners, pennants, and music. Adorn the palace and sweep it clean. Throughout the city streets, hang banners and canopies." The queens of the inner palace and all the people received the king's orders. They arranged the seats, and the head chef was ordered to prepare foods of a hundred flavors, fragrant, harmonious, and sweet. They then went to welcome the Buddha: "The offerings are prepared. May the Honored One condescend to come."

The Buddha then arrived at the appointed time and took his seat. He manifested his supernatural power, moving the entire assembly. The fourfold assembly of disciples, hundreds of thousands of devas and humans, and all the nāga spirits and gandharvas, with their retinues, all followed the Buddha. The Four Heavenly Kings led the way, and Śakra and Brahmā held canopies. The bodhisattva mahāsattvas attended the Buddha as he entered the city. When the Buddha stepped upon the threshold, the territory shook. The blind, the deaf, the mute, the poisoned, and the wasted by illness were all made whole and restored as before. The harps and instruments sounded without being played. The Buddha emitted radiance illuminating all the palaces and dwellings, and the walls and buildings all became lapis lazuli. Within and without, everything was transparent, and all beheld the Buddha.

The Buddha ascended to the hall and took the lion-seat. The king, the prince, the ministers, and the people then served the meal, pouring the drinks with their own hands. When the meal was finished, water was offered for washing. After all the people had taken their seats, the king took a small table and sat before the Buddha to hear the sūtra. The Buddha turned the Dharma wheel and taught non-retrogression. The king was overjoyed and offered a robe to the Buddha, worth ten million and rare in all the world. The robe he scattered hung suspended in the sky and, above the Buddha, transformed into a flower canopy, with open-lattice work of the seven treasures and hanging pearls. From the pearls a radiance was emitted, illuminating the innumerable buddha-lands of the ten directions. The king, the ministers, the people, the prince of the inner palace, the queen, and the beautiful attendants, more than ten thousand in all, seeing these transformations, all leaped with joy. They all gave rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed, true, and right awakening. Eight hundred devas attained the patience of non-arising dharmas. Five thousand bodhisattvas were established in non-retrogression. Innumerable thousands of people all planted the roots of virtue, and when their lives ended, they were all reborn in the heavens above.

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "This king has made offerings to more than five hundred Buddhas. He has practiced generosity with his treasures, and equally practiced compassion. He has honored the Dharma without weariness. In a future kalpa, he will become a Buddha named Huiguang Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha. The people of this country and all the queens will, through his merit, all attain Buddhahood."

When the king heard the prediction, he leaped into the sky, his body rising to a height of one hundred and forty fathoms. He descended and praised the Buddha's merit: "The Buddha is supremely honored and is the foundation for all. His virtue is like heaven: nothing is not covered by it!"

Eight thousand people of the country, hearing the prediction given to the king, gave rise to pure minds and vowed to become bodhisattvas.

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the entire assembly, both human and non-human, were all filled with joy. Such were the skillful means and transformations of the World-Honored One.
