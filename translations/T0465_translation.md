# 伽耶山頂經
## Sūtra of Gayāśīrṣa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0465

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流支)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavat was staying at the city of Gayā, at the stūpa on the summit of Mount Gayā, having newly attained enlightenment. He was together with a full assembly of one thousand great monks, all of whom had formerly been braided-hair ascetics. They had done what was to be done, accomplished what was to be accomplished, laid down the heavy burden, attained their own benefit, exhausted all bonds of existence, been liberated through right knowledge, and gained complete mastery of mind. They had all reached the other shore and were all arhats. There were also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, immeasurable and boundless, all gathered from the worlds of the ten directions. They possessed great awesome virtue, had all attained the various forms of patient endurance, the various dhāraṇīs, the various profound samādhis, and were endowed with all supernatural powers. Their leaders were the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, the Bodhisattva Mahāsthāmaprāpta, the Bodhisattva Gandhahastī, the Bodhisattva Vīradatta, the Bodhisattva Vīryaprajñā, and others. Such bodhisattva-mahāsattvas were immeasurable in number. They were surrounded by a great assembly of devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, and non-humans.

At that time the World-Honored One, alone in stillness with no one else present, entered the profound samādhi of all buddhas, contemplated the dharma-realm, and thought: "I have attained supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment (anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi), attained omniscience, accomplished what was to be done, removed the heavy burden, crossed all the dangerous paths of existence, extinguished ignorance and attained true knowledge, removed all arrows and cut off the thirst of craving, completed the Dharma-vessel, beaten the Dharma-drum, blown the Dharma-conch, raised the Dharma-banner, reversed the seed of birth-and-death, and revealed the nature of nirvāṇa. I have closed off the wrong path and opened the right road, departed from the field of wrongdoing and revealed the field of merit.

"I should now contemplate that Dharma. Who attains supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment? With what wisdom does one attain supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment? What is the Dharma that constitutes the realization of supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment?

"Is it attained through the body? Or through the mind? If through the body, then the body is without knowledge and without awareness, like grass, like wood, like a clod of earth, like a shadow, without any cognition. It is composed of the four great elements, born from father and mother, impermanent by nature. It depends on clothing, food, bedding, and bathing for its survival, and this thing must inevitably decay and be destroyed.

"If through the mind, then the mind is like an illusion, arising from conditions, without location, without characteristics, without substance, without anything at all.

"Bodhi is only a name, spoken through worldly convention. It is without sound, without form, without accomplishment, without action, without entry. It cannot be seen, cannot be relied upon. The path of coming and going is cut off. It transcends all speech and language. It goes beyond the three realms. It is without seeing, without hearing, without sensation, without attachment, without observation, free from conceptual elaboration, without dispute, without indication, not to be observed, not to be seen, without echo, without words, beyond the path of language.

"If one who can realize bodhi does so: with what wisdom? As for the one who realizes bodhi and the Dharma of bodhi that is realized, all such dharmas are merely names, merely conventionally designated, merely composite designations, dependently designated according to worldly convention, discriminated without discrimination, nominally established yet unestablished, without substance and apart from substance, ungrasped and inexpressible, without attachment. In that, there is no person who realizes, no means of realization, and no dharma to be realized. To penetrate in this way is what is called attaining supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment: without difference and apart from difference, without the characteristics of bodhi."

At that time the Dharma-prince Mañjuśrī was standing to the Buddha's right in the great assembly, holding a great jeweled canopy to shade the Buddha. Mañjuśrī silently perceived the World-Honored One's thought and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If the characteristics of bodhi are like this, how should good men and good women generate the aspiration for bodhi and abide in it?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Good men and good women should generate their aspiration and abide in accordance with those very characteristics of bodhi."

Mañjuśrī said: "World-Honored One! How should one understand the characteristics of bodhi?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The characteristics of bodhi transcend the three realms, go beyond all worldly conventional names, words, and language, and go beyond all echoes. There is no aspiration generated and no generating: the cessation of all generating is the generating of the aspiration for bodhi and abiding therein.

"Therefore, Mañjuśrī, when bodhisattva-mahāsattvas transcend all generating, that is generating the aspiration and abiding in it. Mañjuśrī, non-generating is generating the aspiration for bodhi and abiding in it. Mañjuśrī, generating the aspiration for bodhi abides in no thing: that is generating the aspiration for bodhi and abiding in it. Mañjuśrī, generating the aspiration for bodhi abides without obstruction: that is generating the aspiration for bodhi and abiding in it. Mañjuśrī, generating the aspiration for bodhi abides as the nature of dharmas (dharmatā): that is generating the aspiration for bodhi and abiding in it. Mañjuśrī, generating the aspiration for bodhi does not cling to any dharma: that is generating the aspiration for bodhi and abiding in it. Mañjuśrī, generating the aspiration for bodhi does not destroy the true limit of reality (bhūtakoṭi): that is generating the aspiration for bodhi and abiding in it. Mañjuśrī, generating the aspiration for bodhi neither shifts nor increases, is neither different nor one: that is generating the aspiration for bodhi and abiding in it. Mañjuśrī, generating the aspiration for bodhi is like an image in a mirror, like a heat-mirage, like a shadow, like an echo, like space, like the moon reflected in water. One should generate the aspiration for bodhi and abide in this way."

At that time in the assembly there was a deva-son named Moon-Pure-Light-Virtue, who had attained the non-retrogressive aspiration for supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment. He asked Mañjuśrī: "What dharma do the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas first contemplate in order to practice the bodhisattva-path? And on what dharma do they rely in order to practice the bodhisattva-path?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes great compassion as its root, and is for the sake of all sentient beings."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the great compassion of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The great compassion of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes the straightforward mind as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the straightforward mind of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The straightforward mind of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes the mind of equality toward all sentient beings as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the mind of equality toward all sentient beings of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The mind of equality toward all sentient beings of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes the practice of non-discrimination and freedom from discrimination as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the practice of non-discrimination and freedom from discrimination of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The practice of non-discrimination and freedom from discrimination of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes the deeply pure mind as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the deeply pure mind of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The deeply pure mind of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes the aspiration for supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the aspiration for supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The aspiration for supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes the six pāramitās as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the six pāramitās of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The six pāramitās of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas take skillful wisdom as their root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the skillful wisdom of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The skillful wisdom of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes non-negligence as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the non-negligence of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The non-negligence of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes the three kinds of wholesome conduct as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the three kinds of wholesome conduct of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The three kinds of wholesome conduct of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas take the ten wholesome paths of action as their root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the ten wholesome paths of action of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The ten wholesome paths of action of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas take moral discipline as their root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the moral discipline of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The moral discipline of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes right recollection as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the right recollection of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The right recollection of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes right contemplation as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the root of the right contemplation of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The right contemplation of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas takes firm, unforgetting mindfulness as its root."

The deva-son asked Mañjuśrī: "How many kinds of mind do the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas possess that can bring about the accomplishment of causes and the accomplishment of fruits?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The bodhisattva-mahāsattvas possess four kinds of mind that can accomplish causes and accomplish fruits. What are the four? First, the initial aspiration-mind. Second, the practice aspiration-mind. Third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind. Fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind is like planting various seeds. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, is like the sprout growing and increasing. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, is like the stem, leaves, flowers, and fruit first coming to completion. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, is like the fruit being ripe and ready for use.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind is like the cart-maker's wisdom of gathering materials. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, is like the wisdom of cutting and preparing the timber. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, is like the wisdom of fitting and assembling the timber. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, is like the wisdom of the completed cart, ready for transport.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind is like the newly risen moon. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, is like the moon on the fifth day. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, is like the moon on the tenth day. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, is like the moon on the fourteenth day. The wisdom of a tathāgata is like the moon on the fifteenth day.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind can transcend the śrāvaka stage. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, can transcend the pratyekabuddha stage. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, can transcend the indeterminate stage. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, abides firmly in the determinate stage.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind is like the wisdom of learning the first chapter. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, is like the wisdom of distinguishing various chapters. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, is like the wisdom of mathematics and calculation. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, is like the wisdom of mastering all the treatises.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind arises from causes. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, arises from wisdom. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, arises from cutting off. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, arises from fruit.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind belongs to the category of causes. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, belongs to the category of wisdom. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, belongs to the category of cutting off. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, belongs to the category of fruit.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind is born from causes. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, is born from wisdom. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, is born from cutting off. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, is born from fruit.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind is differentiated by causes. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, is differentiated by wisdom. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, is differentiated by cutting off. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, is differentiated by fruit.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind is like the skillful means of gathering medicinal herbs. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, is like the skillful means of distinguishing medicinal herbs. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, is like the skillful means of the sick person taking the medicine. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, is like the skillful means of the illness being cured.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the initial aspiration-mind is learning to be born in the Dharma-king's family. The second, the practice aspiration-mind, is learning the Dharma-king's methods. The third, the non-retrogressive aspiration-mind, is being fully able to learn the Dharma-king's methods. The fourth, the one-birth-supplement aspiration-mind, is learning the Dharma-king's methods and attaining mastery."

At that time in the great assembly there was a deva-son named Dīpaṅkaraprabhamukha, who had attained the non-retrogressive aspiration for supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment. The deva-son Dīpaṅkaraprabhamukha asked the Dharma-prince Mañjuśrī: "What is the ultimate abbreviated path for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas? Through this abbreviated path, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas swiftly attain supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment."

Mañjuśrī answered: "Deva-son! The abbreviated path for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas is of two kinds. Through these two paths, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas swiftly attain supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment. What are the two? First, the path of skillful means; second, the path of wisdom. Skillful means is knowing how to gather wholesome dharmas. Wisdom is the knowledge of knowing all dharmas as they truly are. Furthermore, skillful means is observing all sentient beings, while wisdom is the knowledge of being free from all dharmas. Furthermore, skillful means is knowing the correspondence of all dharmas, while wisdom is the knowledge of knowing the non-correspondence of all dharmas. Furthermore, skillful means is observing the path of causes, while wisdom is the knowledge of extinguishing the path of causes. Furthermore, skillful means is knowing the differentiation of all dharmas, while wisdom is the knowledge of knowing the non-differentiation of all dharmas. Furthermore, skillful means is adorning buddha-lands, while wisdom is the knowledge of the equality and non-differentiation of adorning buddha-lands. Furthermore, skillful means is entering the faculties and dispositions of all sentient beings, while wisdom is the knowledge of not perceiving sentient beings. Furthermore, skillful means is arriving at the place of awakening, while wisdom is the knowledge of being able to realize all the buddha-dharmas of awakening.

"Furthermore, deva-son, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have another two kinds of abbreviated path through which they swiftly attain supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment. What are the two? First, the assisting path; second, the cutting-off path. The assisting path is the five pāramitās, and the cutting-off path is the prajñā-pāramitā.

"There are another two kinds of abbreviated path. What are the two? First, the obstructed path; second, the unobstructed path. The obstructed path is the five pāramitās, and the unobstructed path is the prajñā-pāramitā.

"There are another two kinds of abbreviated path. What are the two? First, the path with outflows; second, the path without outflows. The path with outflows is the five pāramitās, and the path without outflows is the prajñā-pāramitā.

"There are another two kinds of abbreviated path. What are the two? First, the limited path; second, the unlimited path. The limited path is discriminating with the grasping of characteristics. The unlimited path is discriminating without the grasping of characteristics.

"There are another two kinds of abbreviated path. What are the two? First, the wisdom-path; second, the cutting-off path. The wisdom-path refers to the stages from the first ground up to the seventh ground. The cutting-off path refers to the stages from the eighth ground up to the tenth ground."

At that time in the assembly there was a bodhisattva-mahāsattva named Vīryaprajñā, who asked the Dharma-prince Mañjuśrī: "What is meant by 'meaning' for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas? And what is meant by 'wisdom' for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Good man! 'Meaning' (artha) is that which does not correspond. 'Wisdom' (jñāna) is that which corresponds."

The Bodhisattva Vīryaprajñā said: "Mañjuśrī! In what sense does 'meaning' not correspond? And in what sense does 'wisdom' correspond?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Good man! 'Meaning' denotes the unconditioned. With that meaning, there is not a single dharma that corresponds with it, and not a single dharma that does not correspond with it. Why? Because it is without change and without characteristics. 'Meaning' is such that there is not a single dharma that corresponds with it and not a single dharma that does not correspond with it, because fundamentally meaning is not established. Therefore there is not a single dharma that corresponds with it and not a single dharma that does not correspond with it. 'Meaning' neither shifts nor increases; there is not a single dharma that corresponds with it and not a single dharma that does not correspond with it.

"Good man! 'Wisdom' denotes the path! The path corresponds with the mind, and it is not the case that it does not correspond. Good man, for this reason, wisdom corresponds and it is not the case that it does not correspond.

"Furthermore, good man, wisdom corresponds with cutting off. Therefore, good man, wisdom is a corresponding dharma, not a non-corresponding dharma.

"Furthermore, good man, wisdom means skillfully contemplating the five aggregates, the twelve sense-gates, the eighteen elements, the twelve links of dependent arising, and the possible and impossible. Good man, for this reason, wisdom corresponds and it is not the case that it does not correspond.

"Furthermore, good man, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas possess ten kinds of wisdom. What are the ten? First, the wisdom of causes. Second, the wisdom of fruits. Third, the wisdom of meaning. Fourth, the wisdom of skillful means. Fifth, the wisdom of insight. Sixth, the wisdom of gathering. Seventh, the wisdom of the pāramitās. Eighth, the wisdom of great compassion. Ninth, the wisdom of teaching and transforming sentient beings. Tenth, the wisdom of non-attachment to all dharmas. Good man, these are the ten kinds of wisdom of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas possess ten kinds of generating. What are the ten? First, generating of the body, in order to cause the bodily actions of all sentient beings to be purified. Second, generating of speech, in order to cause the verbal actions of all sentient beings to be purified. Third, generating of mind, in order to cause the mental actions of all sentient beings to be purified. Fourth, inner generating, because one does not falsely discriminate among all sentient beings. Fifth, outer generating, because one practices with equality toward all sentient beings. Sixth, generating of wisdom, because one perfects the pure wisdom of a buddha. Seventh, generating of pure lands, because one reveals the merit and adornments of all buddha-lands. Eighth, generating of teaching sentient beings, because one knows the medicine for all afflictions. Ninth, generating of reality, because one accomplishes the assembly of the determined. Tenth, the generating of the mind that fulfills unconditioned wisdom, because one does not attach to the three realms. Good man, these are the ten kinds of generating of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas possess ten kinds of practice. What are the ten? First, the practice of the pāramitās. Second, the practice of the four means of attraction. Third, the practice of insight. Fourth, the practice of skillful means. Fifth, the practice of great compassion. Sixth, the practice of seeking dharmas that assist insight. Seventh, the practice of seeking dharmas that assist wisdom. Eighth, the practice of mental purification. Ninth, the practice of contemplating the truths. Tenth, the practice of non-attachment to all pleasant and unpleasant things. Good man, these are the ten kinds of practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas possess eleven kinds of inexhaustible contemplation. What are the eleven? First, the inexhaustible contemplation of the body. Second, the inexhaustible contemplation of objects. Third, the inexhaustible contemplation of afflictions. Fourth, the inexhaustible contemplation of dharmas. Fifth, the inexhaustible contemplation of craving. Sixth, the inexhaustible contemplation of views. Seventh, the inexhaustible contemplation of aids to the path. Eighth, the inexhaustible contemplation of grasping. Ninth, the inexhaustible contemplation of non-attachment. Tenth, the inexhaustible contemplation of correspondence. Eleventh, the inexhaustible contemplation of the wisdom-nature of the place of awakening. Good man, these are the eleven kinds of inexhaustible contemplation of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas possess ten kinds of antidote-dharma. What are the ten? First, antidote for the miserly and greedy mind, by raining the rain of generosity. Second, antidote for the mind that violates precepts, by purifying the three kinds of action of body, speech, and mind. Third, antidote for the angry mind, by cultivating the purified practice of great loving-kindness and compassion. Fourth, antidote for the lazy mind, by seeking all buddha-dharmas without fatigue or weariness. Fifth, antidote for the mind of unwholesome thoughts, by attaining the meditation concentrations and liberations and becoming masterful in them. Sixth, antidote for the deluded mind, by producing the skillful means that assist decisive wisdom. Seventh, antidote for the mind of afflictions, by producing dharmas that assist the path. Eighth, antidote for the inverted path, by gathering the aids to the true path and producing the non-inverted path. Ninth, antidote for the mind that lacks mastery, by attaining mastery over the Dharma in timely and untimely circumstances. Tenth, antidote for the perception of self, by contemplating all dharmas as being without self. Good man, these are the ten kinds of antidote-dharma of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas possess ten kinds of ground of tranquility. What are the ten? First, tranquility of body, by having departed from the three kinds of unwholesome bodily action. Second, tranquility of speech, by having purified the four kinds of verbal action. Third, tranquility of mind, by having departed from the three kinds of mental misconduct. Fourth, inner tranquility, by not being attached to one's own body. Fifth, tranquility regarding external objects, by not being attached to any dharma. Sixth, tranquility regarding the merits of wisdom, by not being attached to the path. Seventh, supreme tranquility, by contemplating the holy stages as they truly are. Eighth, tranquility regarding the future, by the insight-assisted practice of the other shore. Ninth, tranquility in worldly conduct, by not deceiving any sentient being. Tenth, tranquility in not sparing body or mind, by teaching and transforming all sentient beings with a mind of great loving-kindness and compassion. Good man, these are the ten kinds of ground of tranquility of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas attain enlightenment through authentic practice, not through inauthentic practice. Good man, what is meant by authentic practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas? Good man, authentic practice means practicing as one teaches. Inauthentic practice means merely having words without being able to practice authentically.

"Furthermore, good man, there are two additional kinds of authentic practice for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. What are the two? First, the authentic practice of the path of wisdom. Second, the authentic practice of the path of cutting off. Good man, these are the two kinds of authentic practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, there are two additional kinds of authentic practice for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. What are the two? First, the authentic practice of disciplining oneself. Second, the authentic practice of teaching and transforming sentient beings. Good man, these are the two kinds of authentic practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, there are two additional kinds of authentic practice for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. What are the two? First, the authentic practice of effortful wisdom. Second, the authentic practice of effortless wisdom. Good man, these are the two kinds of authentic practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, there are two additional kinds of authentic practice for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. What are the two? First, the authentic practice of skillfully knowing and distinguishing the various grounds. Second, the authentic practice of skillfully knowing the skillful means of non-differentiation among the various grounds. Good man, these are the two kinds of authentic practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, there are two additional kinds of authentic practice for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. What are the two? First, the authentic practice of departing from the faults of the various grounds. Second, the authentic practice of skillfully knowing the skillful means of progressing from ground to ground. Good man, these are the two kinds of authentic practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Furthermore, good man, there are two additional kinds of authentic practice for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. What are the two? First, the authentic practice of being able to teach the grounds of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Second, the authentic practice of skillfully knowing the skillful means of non-retrogression from buddha-awakening. Good man, these are the two kinds of authentic practice of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

"Good man, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have such immeasurable and boundless authentic practices as these. The bodhisattva-mahāsattvas should study authentic practice in this way. If the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas are able to practice authentically in this way, they will swiftly attain supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment without difficulty."

At that time the Buddha praised the Dharma-prince Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! Mañjuśrī! You have now skillfully taught the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas the fundamental path of their practice. It is truly as you have spoken."

When this Dharma was taught, ten thousand bodhisattvas attained the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas. The Dharma-prince Mañjuśrī and all the assembly of the world, including devas, humans, and asuras, hearing what the Buddha had taught, all rejoiced greatly, believed, accepted, and put it into practice.
