# 佛說旃陀越國王經
## Sūtra of King Candraprabha, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0518

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Juqu Jingsheng (沮渠京聲)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time, there was a king named Candraprabha who followed the brahmanical path. When the king governed the affairs of the country, he always employed brahmins.

The king had a junior queen who was especially cherished and treasured, and at that time she was pregnant. The other queens were jealous and envious of her. They bribed a brahmin with gold and had him slander her to the king, saying: "This woman is wicked. If she bears a son, he will surely be a calamity for the kingdom."

When the king heard this, he was deeply troubled and unhappy. He asked the brahmin: "What should be done?"

The brahmin said: "The only thing to do is to kill them both."

The king said: "A human life is of the utmost weight. How can they be killed?"

The brahmin replied: "If you do not kill them, there will surely be the worry of losing the kingdom and your own life. The disaster will be no small matter."

The king then heeded his words. The queen was wrongfully killed and buried in a tomb.

The child was later born within the tomb. His mother's body was half-preserved, and the child was able to drink from her breast. This continued for three years, until the tomb collapsed and caved in. The child then was able to come out. He played with the birds and beasts, and at night returned to sleep in the tomb.

When the child was six years old, the Buddha, with his universal compassion, reflected on the child's hardship, living among the birds and beasts. He transformed himself into a śramaṇa, put on the robes, and went to call upon the child, asking: "Whose child are you? Where do you live?"

The child joyfully replied: "I have no home. I only sleep in this tomb. I beg to follow the renunciant."

The Buddha said: "If you follow me, what will you do?"

The child replied: "Whether for good or for ill, I shall follow the renunciant to the end."

The Buddha then brought the child to the Jetavana Monastery. When the child saw the deportment and decorum of the bhikṣus, he was greatly delighted and said to the Buddha: "I wish to become a bhikṣu."

The Buddha granted his request, and with his hand rubbed the child's head. The hair fell away, and a kāṣāya robe appeared naturally upon his body. He was given the name Sudā. He received the honored precepts from the Buddha and practiced with diligence and zeal, his mind never slackening. Within seven days he attained the fruit of arhatship.

The Buddha said to Sudā: "You have received the honored precepts from the Buddha and pulled up the very root of desire. You have attained freedom over birth and death. Now you should go and save King Candraprabha."

Sudā received the Buddha's instruction, touched his head to the ground, and made obeisance to the Buddha. He traveled to that country and stood at the palace gate, requesting an audience with the king. The attendants reported to the king: "A renunciant is outside and wishes to see the king."

When the king heard this, he came out to meet him and asked: "I have something that greatly troubles me. What should I do?"

The renunciant said: "What troubles you?"

The king said: "I am growing old and my time will soon pass. The kingdom has no heir to continue the line. This is what grieves me."

When the renunciant heard the king's words, he did not answer at first, but only smiled. The king grew angry and said: "I spoke to the renunciant and you did not answer me, but instead just smiled." He then wanted to have him seized and killed. Sudā, perceiving his intent, rose lightly and flew, hovering in the sky. He divided his body and reassembled it, appearing and vanishing without obstruction. When the king saw his supernatural transformations, he was terrified and repented, saying: "I was truly foolish and could not distinguish the true from the false. I beg the great spirit to return and allow me to take refuge."

Sudā then descended from the sky and stood before the king, saying: "If you can take refuge, that is excellent! You should take refuge in the Buddha. The Buddha is my great teacher, the honored one of the three realms, who delivers and liberates all beings." The king then ordered his ministers to prepare the carriages; they would go to the Buddha's dwelling. Sudā, by his powers of the path, in the time it takes to extend an arm, brought the king and the people to the Buddha's dwelling. They touched their heads to the ground, made obeisance to the Buddha, took refuge in the Three Honored Ones, and requested the five precepts, becoming upāsakas.

The Buddha said to the king: "Do you wish to know about the bhikṣu Sudā? He is the son of the woman you had slandered and killed on the brahmin's word, the pregnant queen. After his mother died, the child was born within the tomb. In the tomb, his mother's body was half-preserved, and he was able to drink from her breast for six years. Then he followed me to practice the path, and he has arrived at this attainment."

When the king heard the Buddha's words, his fear was even greater, and he could not contain himself.

The Buddha said: "Long ago, in the time of Krakucchanda Buddha, there was a king named Buddhadāsa. The king and three hundred million people in his country all followed the king in making offerings to the Three Honored Ones. At that time, there was an ordinary man, destitute and without livelihood, who constantly hired himself out to tend cattle for wealthy families, herding several hundred head. Seeing the king and the people making offerings to the bhikṣu sangha, he asked: 'What are you doing?' The people answered: 'We are making offerings to the Three Honored Ones, and in the future we will receive their merit.' He then asked: 'What kind of merit?' The people replied: 'If a person makes offerings to the Three Honored Ones with a pure heart, in future lives wherever they are born they will be peaceful, happy, and honored, without any hardship.' He then thought: 'I am destitute. I only hire myself out to herd cattle. I have no food of my own. How can I make an offering?' He then thought: 'I can only go back and take the cow's milk, boil it into curds and ghee, and offer it to the bhikṣus with a pure heart.' The bhikṣu sangha blessed him, saying: 'May you, in lifetime after lifetime, wherever you are born, receive the corresponding merit.' From then on, through successive births and deaths, he always received the merit of this: sometimes reborn above among the devas, sometimes below as a king or nobleman.

"Later, when he was a king, he went out hunting and saw that a man in his country had a fine milch cow that was pregnant. The king ordered his men to take the cow and slaughter it. The queen said to the king: 'Do not let them kill the calf.' The cow's owner then retrieved the calf and raised it with care. The owner raged: 'May the king suffer as this cow has!' Afterward, the cow's owner's spirit was reborn as the king's son. Before the child was born, his mother was killed by the king. The one you should know as Sudā is precisely this child. The mother of Sudā who was wrongfully killed is the queen of that time. The brahmin who slandered her is the cow's owner of that time. The reason Sudā was born in the tomb and his mother's body was half-preserved, so that he could drink from her breast and grow to adulthood, is because in a past life he offered curds and ghee to the bhikṣu sangha."

The Buddha said: "Sin and merit have their echo and response, as a shadow follows a form. There has never been a case where one did good and did not receive merit, or committed evil and did not suffer calamity."

When the king heard the Buddha teach the sūtra, his mind was opened and he immediately attained the fruit of stream-entry. All the people of the country followed the king in receiving the five precepts and practicing the ten virtues, taking refuge in the Three Honored Ones. Among them, some attained stream-entry, some once-returning, some non-returning, and some arhatship. The fourfold assembly of disciples, the heavenly nāgas and spirits, having heard the sūtra, were filled with joy. They came forward, made obeisance to the Buddha, and departed.
