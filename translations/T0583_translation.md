# 佛說黑氏梵志經
## Sūtra of the Brahmin Kṛṣṇa, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0583

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling along the bank of the Nairañjanā River. He stayed there for one month, performed the eighteen transformations, and converted the three Kāśyapa brothers and their thousand disciples. He then traveled to the city of Rājagṛha, where he stayed for one year, teaching and instructing the people of the land, expounding the Dharma for them. Having completed two years since first attaining buddhahood, he then went to Śrāvastī, where he spread and flourished the transformative teaching, liberating devas, humans, and the people of the world.

At that time on Mount Gandhamādana there was a brahmin named Kṛṣṇa. He had attained the four dhyānas in full and possessed the five supernatural powers: clairvoyance, clairaudience, the ability to fly, insight into one's own thoughts, and knowledge of others' past lives. When he lectured on the sūtras, he moved Śakra, Brahmā, the four heavenly kings, all the ghosts, spirits, nāgas, and King Yama, who all came to listen. His words were refined and wonderful, his voice harmonious like Brahmā's. Day after day they came to learn from him without wearying. His voice carried far, and beings from all around came to listen.

At that time King Yama, sitting and hearing the Dharma teaching, shed tears like rain. He raised his eyes to look and became even more sorrowful.

Then the brahmin asked King Yama: "Why do you weep with tears flowing like rain?"

King Yama answered: "What must be said should be truthful; one cannot speak falsely. Your teaching of the sūtras is eloquent and fluent, the meaning and principle are marvelously wonderful, like a lotus flower, like a bright moon-pearl. Yet your life is about to end; only seven days remain. I fear that suddenly you will pass into the next life. This is why I weep and cannot contain myself. Moreover, when your life ends, you will fall into Naraka, within my domain. Now I take refuge in you and singlemindedly receive your teaching, but soon I must take you and subject you to interrogation and the five torments. Thinking of this deeply only increases my sorrow, beyond any comparison."

The brahmin was startled and pondered in his mind. He replied to King Yama: "I have attained the four dhyānas and accomplished the five supernatural powers. I walk alone through the four continents and ascend to Brahmā's heaven without hindrance. Since I have committed no offense, how should I fall into Naraka in Yama's domain?"

King Yama said: "At the time of your death, you will encounter an adverse circumstance. You will give rise to anger and hatred, and your mind will wish to do harm. You will lose the principles of your original practice, and therefore you will fall into Yama's domain."

Hearing this, the brahmin was suddenly filled with anxiety. He did not know what plan to devise or what expedient could save him from this difficulty. Distressed and bewildered, his heart felt as though it were on fire. He could not sit or stand at ease, and he sighed deeply.

Śakra, Brahmā, the four kings, and all the spirits asked: "Why are you uneasy and sighing so deeply?"

The brahmin replied: "My life is about to end; only seven days remain. Moreover, I will encounter an adverse circumstance that will disturb my good mind, and because of this I fear I will fall into the evil destinies. This is why I toss and turn and cannot contain myself."

At that time on Mount Gandhamādana there were good spirits who frequently visited the Buddha's presence to receive and learn the sūtra teachings. They said to the brahmin: "A Buddha has arisen in the world. Do you not know this?"

The brahmin replied: "I am immersed in worldly ways. How could I know?"

The spirit then said: "The Buddha is the savior of all in the three realms. He liberates those not yet liberated, frees those not yet freed, brings peace to those not yet at peace, and rescues all from peril, leading them to the eternal, tranquil path of the unconditioned. Why do you not go to the Buddha? You can be freed from your sorrows and attain lasting serenity, uniting with the Way of merit."

Hearing this, the brahmin was delighted and overjoyed, like one in darkness who sees the light. With each hand he took a beautiful flowering tree, one of paulownia and one of silk-tree, and flew to the Buddha's presence. Just before he arrived, the Buddha told Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "The World-Honored One's great compassion and boundless pity never abandon those who should be saved."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The tide flows straight along the cliffs,
> Never once transgressing its ancient bounds.
> Should a water-spirit cause disorder,
> It would violate the ancient flow.
> The Buddha contemplates original emptiness
> And observes those who should be saved,
> Causing all to be rescued and delivered:
> None are ever overlooked or lost."

Then the brahmin flew to the Buddha's presence and hovered in the sky, facing the Buddha with palms joined in reverence.

The Buddha said to the brahmin called Kṛṣṇa: "Let go! Let go!"

The brahmin assented: "As the World-Honored One instructs."

He let go of the paulownia tree in his right hand and planted it to the Buddha's right.

Again the Buddha said: "Let go! Let go!"

The brahmin let go of the silk-tree held in his left hand and planted it to the Buddha's left.

The Buddha said yet again: "Let go! Let go!"

The brahmin said: "I had only two trees. I have let them go to the Buddha's left and right. I stand here empty-handed. What more should I let go of?"

The Buddha said to the brahmin: "The Buddha does not tell you to let go of what is in your hands. What the Buddha says to let go of is: let go of the before, let go of the after, and let go of the middle, so that there is nowhere to abide. Only then will you cross beyond the many afflictions of birth and death."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "You should let go of the beginning,
> And also let go of the end.
> In the middle, there is nowhere to abide;
> Only then do you cross the source of birth and death.
> Within, there are no six sense-entries;
> Without, decline cannot advance.
> Release and set down the six sense-faculties,
> And swiftly accomplish the unconditioned."

When the brahmin Kṛṣṇa heard the Buddha's teaching, he thought to himself: "I do not see a self or a 'mine'; thus I understand that the mind is nothing. The mind is originally nothing. Applying the medicine to match the illness, my lowly mind is opened and freed. Like a blind man gaining eyes, a deaf man gaining hearing: this is truly universal insight, the true omniscience. Now I have met the Buddha, whose merit is beyond reproach."

He immediately came down, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, and stood to one side. The Buddha taught in accordance with his mind's capacity, clearly showing the place of enlightenment and expounding the three gates of liberation. He then abided at the stage of non-retrogression, without a single sorrow. He praised the Buddha's merit and spoke in verse:

> "His radiance surpasses the sun and moon;
> His wisdom is like the great ocean.
> His great compassion and boundless pity
> Are joyfully borne by all in the ten directions.
> Beings have flowed through the three realms
> For immeasurable hundreds of millions of ages.
> He applies the medicine of Dharma to match each illness,
> Proclaiming and spreading his great eloquence.
> Though he appears to enter birth and death,
> In his circling there is no coming or going.
> He urges and transforms beings to be diligent,
> For none can take another's place in karmic retribution.
> Strive hard and be diligent!
> Do not be ruined by desire.
> Subdue decline and remove the four māras,
> And the Way is accomplished without obstruction."

The brahmin said to the Buddha: "I have been lost for a very long time! May you have pity on me and allow me to become a śramaṇa!"

The Buddha immediately consented. His hair fell away of itself, a kāṣāya robe appeared on his body, and his deportment was orderly and composed. He became a śramaṇa. He went to King Yama and said to him: "You once told me I had only seven days to live and would fall into Naraka. Now I have become a śramaṇa. My supernatural powers are complete, all my outflows are exhausted, and I have crossed the four floods. All my afflictions are permanently removed, like a great encircling fortress. My lifespan has been extended sevenfold, seven times seven days. All sufferings are extinguished, and I have transcended the heterodox arts. I abide freely in the world, for countless kalpas more."

King Yama replied: "You were fortunate to have remaining merit that allowed you to encounter the Buddha. He applied the Dharma-medicine to match your illness, extinguishing lust, anger, and delusion. Your supernatural powers are fully accomplished, and within and without there is no doubt. Had it not been so, you would have been like a rat seized by a cat, like grain struck by disaster, dragged away by your offenses; like a fish swallowing the hook and bait, you would have fallen into Naraka with no prospect of release. Now you are forever free, and I rejoice on your behalf." When these words were spoken, countless people all generated the aspiration for the Way.

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the bhikṣus, bodhisattvas, the śramaṇa Kṛṣṇa, the devas, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, asuras, and the people of the world were all filled with delight. They bowed and departed.
