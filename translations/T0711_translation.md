# 大乘舍黎娑擔摩經
## Mahāyāna Śālistamba Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0711

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the World-Honored One was in Rājagṛha on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, twelve hundred and fifty in number. There was also a great assembly of bodhisattva mahāsattvas, with the Bodhisattva Maitreya at their head.

At that time the World-Honored One, having observed the śālistamba, addressed the bhikṣus: "If any bhikṣu can see and understand the twelve links of dependent arising, this is called seeing the Dharma. Having seen this Dharma, one has seen the Buddha." After the World-Honored One had spoken thus, he remained silent.

At that time Śāriputra thought to himself: "My World-Honored One has taught this dharma. What does it mean? How should it be understood?" He then went to the Bodhisattva Maitreya's place. Arriving, they met, exchanged gentle words and greetings, and Śāriputra sat down with the Bodhisattva Maitreya beneath a great rock.

Then Śāriputra said to Maitreya: "Just now the World-Honored One taught the bhikṣus the Śālistamba Sūtra, saying: 'If any bhikṣu can see the twelve links of dependent arising, this is called seeing the Dharma. Having seen this Dharma, one has seen the Buddha.' Bodhisattva, I do not understand this meaning. What are the twelve links of dependent arising called? What is the Dharma? What is the Buddha? I humbly wish the Bodhisattva to explain this briefly!"

At that time the Bodhisattva Maitreya said to the Venerable Śāriputra: "The Tathāgata, the Dharma King, possessing omniscience, teaches what is appropriate, profound, and subtle. You now ask me, and I shall explain briefly. Śāriputra! As the World-Honored One has said: 'If any bhikṣu can see and understand the twelve links of dependent arising, this is called seeing the Dharma. If one sees this Dharma, one has seen the Buddha.' Śāriputra! The twelve links of dependent arising are: conditioned by ignorance, formations; conditioned by formations, consciousness; conditioned by consciousness, name-and-form; conditioned by name-and-form, the six sense bases; conditioned by the six sense bases, contact; conditioned by contact, feeling; conditioned by feeling, craving; conditioned by craving, grasping; conditioned by grasping, becoming; conditioned by becoming, birth; conditioned by birth, old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish. Thus what arises is merely the arising of the great mass of suffering. Śāriputra! When ignorance ceases, formations cease; when formations cease, consciousness ceases; when consciousness ceases, name-and-form ceases; when name-and-form ceases, the six sense bases cease; when the six sense bases cease, contact ceases; when contact ceases, feeling ceases; when feeling ceases, craving ceases; when craving ceases, grasping ceases; when grasping ceases, becoming ceases; when becoming ceases, birth ceases; when birth ceases, old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish cease. Thus what ceases is the cessation of the great mass of suffering. Śāriputra! This is what the World-Honored One has taught as the twelve links of dependent arising."

Śāriputra asked: "What is the Dharma?"

The Bodhisattva replied: "The noble eightfold path is called the Dharma, namely: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration. Śāriputra! The fruition of this eightfold path is nirvāṇa. Therefore the World-Honored One has briefly called it the Dharma."

Śāriputra asked: "What is the Buddha?"

The Bodhisattva replied: "One who knows all dharmas is called the Buddha. Having obtained the holy wisdom eye, seeing the dharmas of the factors of bodhi, one thereby realizes the Dharma-body.

"Furthermore, how does one see the twelve links? As the Buddha has said: 'If a person constantly sees these twelve links as unborn, unceasing, uncreated, unconditioned, without grasping, without attachment, as they truly are, without inversion, quiescent, without fear, as the great sage teaches, inexhaustible, at rest, and entirely without inherent nature, that person sees the Dharma. If one can thus constantly see them as unborn, unceasing, uncreated, unconditioned, without grasping, without attachment, as they truly are, without inversion, quiescent, without fear, as the great sage teaches, inexhaustible, at rest, and sees the Dharma as without inherent nature, that person sees the unsurpassed Dharma-body.' The Buddha is one who has attained right Dharma, right wisdom, and the samādhi of cessation."

Śāriputra further asked: "For what reason are they called the twelve links?"

The Bodhisattva replied: "Because they have causes and conditions, they are called the twelve links. Śāriputra! This dharma is neither cause nor condition, yet it is also not apart from cause and condition; it exists through conditions. I shall now briefly explain its characteristics. Whether the Tathāgata appears in the world or does not appear, this dharma of causes and conditions permanently abides, equal, real, and not false; it is the true and real dharma, free from inversion.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, dependent arising has two meanings. What are the two? First, from causes; second, from conditions. These two meanings are divided into external and internal. What arises from external causes? Just as from a seed arises a sprout, from the sprout a seedling, from the seedling a stem, from the stem branches and leaves, from branches and leaves flower and fruit. Without a seed there is no seedling, and so on until without a flower there is no fruit. When there is a seed, a seedling and stem arise, and so on until when there is a flower, fruit invariably follows. Śāriputra! That seed does not think, 'I can produce a sprout.' Nor does the sprout think, 'I can produce a seedling and stem.' So on until the flower does not think, 'I can produce fruit,' nor does the fruit think, 'I arise from the flower.' This is the visible dharma of external arising from causes.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! What arises from external conditions is the conjunction of six elements. What are the six? Earth, water, fire, wind, space, and time. The earth element provides stability, the water element provides moisture, the fire element provides warmth, the wind element causes movement, the space element provides non-obstruction, and the time element brings about fruition. When these six elements are each in conjunction, the seed produces a sprout, seedling, flower, and fruit without exception. If one of these six elements is not in conjunction, the seed does not grow, and so on until flower and fruit cannot be obtained. Yet these six elements each have no self. Earth does not say, 'I am able to provide stability.' Water does not say, 'I am able to provide moisture.' Fire does not say, 'I am able to provide warmth.' Wind does not say, 'I am able to cause movement.' Space does not say, 'I am able to provide non-obstruction.' Time does not say, 'I am able to bring about fruition.' Yet the seed does not say, 'I am able to produce a sprout.' Nor does the sprout say, 'I arise from the various conditions.' These sprouts and so forth are not self-produced, not produced by another, not produced by both self and other together, not created by a creator god, not transformed by time, not produced by conditions alone, not produced by a single thing, and not produced without a cause. Yet earth, water, fire, wind, space, time, the seed, flower, and fruit: from these things arise, neither the same nor separate, without exhaustion or destruction.

"This external arising from conditions further has five meanings. What are the five? Not permanent, not annihilated, not destroyed though ceasing, small cause producing great result, and mutually serving as conditions. How is it not permanent? Because the seed and the sprout have different names and characteristics. How is it not annihilated? Because from the seed there is a sprout, and from the sprout branches and leaves. How is it not destroyed though ceasing? 'Not destroyed' means that although the seed decays and seemingly perishes, it is 'not destroyed' because the seed transmits itself and produces a sprout. How does a small cause produce great result? Because one seed as the cause produces abundant fruit. How do they mutually serve as conditions? Because from the seed there is a sprout, and so on until flower and fruit, in similar succession, again become a seed.

"Furthermore, what are the internal twelve links? These twelve links again have two meanings. What are the two? First, from causes; second, from conditions. What is arising from causes? Because of ignorance, formations arise, and so on until birth gives rise to old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish. Without ignorance, formations cannot be established, and so on until without old age and death, there is no grief, lamentation, suffering, or anguish. Yet ignorance does not think, 'I can produce formations.' Formations do not think, 'I arise from ignorance.' So on until birth does not think, 'I can produce old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish.' Old age and death do not think, 'I arise from birth.' This is called the characteristic of arising from causes.

"What is arising from conditions? It is because of the conjunction of six elements. What are the six? Earth, water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness. When these six elements are in conjunction, the body is born. What is the earth element? That which is solid in the body is called the earth element. What moistens is called the water element. What warms is called the fire element. The in-breath and out-breath is called the wind element. What is unobstructed in the body is called the space element. Eye-consciousness through to the eighth consciousness is called the consciousness element. When these six elements are in conjunction and none is diminished, the body is formed. If these conditions are diminished, the body is not formed. The earth element does not think, 'I am able to be solid.' The water element does not think, 'I am able to be moist.' The fire element does not think, 'I am able to be warm.' The wind element does not think, 'I am able to breathe in and out.' The space element does not think, 'I am able to be unobstructed.' Consciousness does not think, 'I am able to accomplish.' The body does not think, 'I arise from the various conditions.' Yet without these conditions, the body cannot be established. The earth element has no self, no person, no sentient being, no life-force; it is neither male nor female, and neither self nor other. So too with water, fire, wind, space, and the consciousness element: they have no self, no person, no sentient being, no life-force; they are neither male nor female, and neither self nor other. Furthermore, if within these six elements one generates a perception of unity, a perception of the ordinary, a perception of permanence, a perception of reality, a perception of durability, a perception of pleasure, a perception of self, a perception of person, a perception of sentient being, a perception of life-force, a perception of moving creatures: because of lacking wisdom and generating such various perceptions, this is called ignorance. Because of ignorance, greed, anger, and delusion arise. Conditioned by ignorance, formations arise; formations likewise attach to conventional names and produce deluded thoughts, which are called consciousness. Consciousness produces name-and-form. Name-and-form produces the six sense bases. The six sense bases produce contact. Contact produces feeling. Feeling produces craving. Craving produces grasping. Grasping produces becoming. Because of becoming, future aggregates are produced, which is called birth. After birth, deterioration is called old age. The destruction of the aggregates is called death. Ignorance and delusion produce grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish. The five senses encountering harm is called bodily suffering. The mind being ill at ease is called mental suffering. The mass of suffering gathers, constantly dwelling in darkness: this is called ignorance. Creating and producing is called formations. Discriminating is called consciousness. Establishing characteristics is called name-and-form. The six sense gates are called the six sense bases. Encountering objects is called contact. Experiencing suffering and pleasure is called feeling. Hunger and thirst is called craving. Pursuing and seeking is called grasping. Producing further karma is called becoming. The arising of future aggregates is called birth. The ripening of the aggregates is called old age. Their destruction is called death. Brooding with fear is called grief. Painful cutting is called lamentation. The mass of suffering is called suffering. Weariness and disturbance is called anguish. Mistaking truth for falsehood, taking wrong views for right views: because of this lack of wisdom, it is called ignorance. Formations are of three kinds: meritorious formations, demeritorious formations, and imperturbable formations. Performing meritorious formations produces the wisdom of meritorious formations; performing demeritorious formations produces the wisdom of demeritorious formations; performing imperturbable formations produces the wisdom of imperturbable formations. So on down to old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish. These twelve links each have their causes and results. They are not permanent, not annihilated, not conditioned yet not apart from the conditioned, not exhaustible, not subject to the cessation of desire, not subject to destruction. They exist from the beginning, their arising never interrupted, like a river flowing swiftly with no break in its current."

At that time the Bodhisattva Maitreya further said to the Venerable Śāriputra: "The twelve links of dependent arising are further increased by four conditions: ignorance, craving, karma, and consciousness. The consciousness seed has its own nature as the cause; karma serves as the field; ignorance and craving, the afflictions, serve as the covering. The consciousness seed then sprouts. Karma provides the field for consciousness; craving provides the moisture for consciousness; ignorance provides the covering. Thus consciousness is accomplished. Yet karma does not think, 'I serve as the field for the consciousness seed.' Craving does not think, 'I serve as the moisture for the consciousness seed.' Ignorance does not think, 'I serve as the covering for the consciousness seed.' Because of these conditions, the consciousness seed is accomplished. Consciousness does not think, 'I arise from the various conditions.' Furthermore, karma serves as the field for consciousness, craving as moisture, and ignorance as covering. Thus the seed grows and becomes the sprout of name-and-form within the mother's womb. That sprout of name-and-form is not self-produced, not produced by another, not produced by both self and other together, not created by a creator god, not transformed by time, not produced by conditions alone, not produced by a single thing, and not produced without a cause. This dharma truly arises from the conjunction of parents and the various conditions. Yet the sprout of name-and-form fundamentally has no owner, no grasping and no releasing; its own nature and conditions are like empty space and magical illusion.

"Furthermore, eye-consciousness arises from five kinds of conditions. What are the five? The eye, form, light, space, and attention. From these five conditions eye-consciousness arises. The eye serves as the basis, form as the object, light enables illumination, space enables non-obstruction, and attention initiates activity. From these conditions eye-consciousness arises. If the eye, form, light, space, and attention are not in conjunction, eye-consciousness does not arise. Yet the eye does not think, 'I serve as the basis for eye-consciousness.' Form does not think, 'I serve as the object for consciousness.' Light does not think, 'I enable consciousness to illuminate.' Space does not think, 'I enable consciousness to be unobstructed.' Attention does not think, 'I enable consciousness to initiate activity.' Consciousness does not think, 'I arise from the various conditions.' Yet eye-consciousness truly arises from the conjunction of the various conditions. So too the successive arising of consciousness from the other sense faculties should be explained in the same way.

"Furthermore, no dharma passes from this world to the next world. It is only through karmic results, causes, conditions, and deluded thought that things arise. Again, it is like a clear mirror reflecting a face: the face appears in the mirror, yet in reality no face enters the mirror; it is through deluded thought and conditions that it appears. Again, Śāriputra, it is like the full moon high in the sky, forty-two thousand yojanas from the earth, yet its reflection appears in all the waters below. The moon does not sink there and arise in this water; it also appears through deluded thought and conditions. Again, it is like obtaining fire: given fuel it burns, and when the fuel is exhausted it is extinguished."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, no sentient being passes from this world to the next, nor from the next world back to this. It is only through the conjunction of karmic bonds that the consciousness seed everywhere obtains birth. Lodging in the mother's womb, the sprout of name-and-form arises. This dharma of causes and conditions fundamentally has no owner, no self, no grasping, no releasing; it is like empty space, like magical illusion, without any real dharma. Yet the retribution of wholesome and unwholesome karma does not perish.

"Furthermore, the twelve links of dependent arising are also explained through five aspects. What are the five? Impermanence, non-annihilation, non-destruction, small cause producing great result, and resemblance. How is it impermanent? Because when one aggregate ceases another aggregate arises; cessation is not arising, and arising is not cessation; because arising and cessation are different, it is called impermanent. How is it not annihilated? It is like the rising and falling of a scale: when this ceases, that arises; therefore it is called non-annihilation; thus seeing truly as it is. How is it not destroyed? Because the causal karma performed by sentient beings is never destroyed. How does a small cause produce great result? Because whatever cause is created is like tending a field; through single-minded diligent effort, the fruit obtained is abundant. How is it resemblance? Because the causal karma one creates does not yield a different result; therefore it is called resemblance.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, as the Buddha has said: 'If someone can contemplate the twelve links of dependent arising, this is called right contemplation and right wisdom.' What is right contemplation and right wisdom? It is contemplating the arising in the three times of past, future, and present without generating thoughts of existence or non-existence, without coming from anywhere or going to any place. If śramaṇas, brahmins, and worldly people can contemplate this dharma as unborn, unceasing, uncreated, unconditioned, without grasping, without releasing, not inverted, quiescent, and at rest, without inherent nature, and if they can see the Dharma in this way, tranquil and fully understanding, without illness, without wound, in the space of a blink of an eye the view of self is immediately eliminated, like cutting the crown of a tāla tree so that it cannot grow again. This is to attain the dharma of non-arising and non-ceasing. Śāriputra! If there are sentient beings who can endure this dharma, then the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Samyaksaṃbuddha, endowed with perfect knowledge and conduct, the Sugata, the knower of the world, the unsurpassed one, the tamer of persons, the teacher of devas and humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One, will certainly bestow upon them the prediction of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi."

When the Bodhisattva Maitreya had finished speaking this dharma, the Venerable Śāriputra, along with the devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and the entire assembly, joyfully received the teaching, bowed in reverence, and departed.
