# 海印三昧論
## Treatise on the Ocean-Seal Samādhi

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1889

Translated from the Chinese. Silla, composed by Myŏnghyo (明皛).

---

The great Way is boundless; it cannot be sought through narrow practice. The ultimate principle is deep and abstruse; it cannot be known through shallow wisdom. If one enters the Way with a confined disposition, the Way does not admit confined entry. If one seeks the principle by grasping at teachings, the principle does not yield to grasping. Only through boundless and great practice can one begin to merit the name "Way." Only through penetrating, empty, and perfect wisdom can one truly encounter the principle. Therefore I invite the noble persons of the future to share in the spirit of entrusting oneself to the fish-trap while forgetting the fish.

> Homage to the Three Jewels throughout
> The dharmadhātu of the ten directions.
> I wish to repay the Buddha's kindness
> By briefly expounding the meaning of the sūtras,
> Universally causing all classes of sentient beings
> To attain the bliss of great nirvāṇa.
> May compassion empower and protect,
> Fulfilling my original vow.

### The Ocean-Seal Samādhi

The treatise states: There is a great samādhi called the Ocean Seal. It swiftly causes practitioners to reach the stage of non-retrogression. Using small expedient means, one obtains great benefit. Within this seal, the essential meaning is expressed in verse as a dhāraṇī. It comprehensively holds the essential meaning of all sūtras. It universally contains all the merit of all buddhas.

This dhāraṇī has, in brief, two kinds: dharma and meaning.

Within dharma there are three: substance, wisdom, and function.

What is called "substance" is the fundamental single mind of all sentient beings. This mind is originally and by nature pure, neither increasing nor decreasing, neither arising nor ceasing, vast and boundless like empty space. It comprehensively holds all mundane and supramundane dharmas because there is nothing it does not encompass.

What is called "wisdom" is the twofold wisdom of the tathāgata-ground: the wisdom of principle and the wisdom of discernment. No principle goes unilluminated; no phenomenon goes uncomprehended. It comprehensively holds all boundless merit because there is nothing it does not encompass.

What is called "function" is the twofold body of the tathāgata-ground: the responsive body and the transformation body. They universally respond throughout the dharmadhātu, teaching and transforming sentient beings, inexhaustible in their transformative function to the end of the future. They comprehensively hold all mundane and supramundane dharmas because there is nothing they do not encompass.

What is called "meaning" is the vast and wondrous meaning revealed by the perfect teaching of the Mahāyāna. It comprehensively holds the secret Dharma-treasury of all buddhas of the ten directions and three times because there is nothing it does not encompass.

These two kinds of dhāraṇī gates each encompass immeasurable Dharma-gates as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. They mutually contain each other without obstruction, interpenetrating and mutually entering. Entering any single gate, one penetrates everywhere, comprehending all Dharma-gates as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. One enters the realm of the buddhas and attains fearlessness. As a verse of the sūtra says:

> Within one, the immeasurable is understood;
> Within the immeasurable, the one is understood.
> They arise in mutual dependence, not as real;
> The wise are without fear.

These two kinds of dhāraṇī gates, and even the immeasurable Dharma-gates as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, all enter within the ocean-seal samādhi. It is like all the waters under heaven flowing into the great ocean, which receives every stream. The ocean-seal samādhi is also like this: it takes in all the waters of the sūtras. It is like the sun, moon, stars, palaces of the devas, the armies of the asuras, and the hosts led by Śakra Devānām Indra in the sky: all appear as reflections in the waters of the great ocean. The ocean-seal samādhi is also like this: the immeasurable and boundless buddhas and bodhisattva assemblies of the dharmadhātu all appear within it.

### The Dhāraṇī Diagram

The meaning of the dhāraṇī should be understood through the following verse. If one wishes to read it, one begins from the character at the central corner of the seal and follows the turns of the seal's text, circling around until reaching "nirvāṇa" at the end. The verse states:

*[Dhāraṇī diagram: a grid of Chinese characters arranged in a spiral pattern, to be read from the center outward.]*

The verse, read from the center:

> Birth-and-death and nirvāṇa are not in different places;
> Affliction and bodhi are in substance non-dual.
> Nirvāṇa is intimate, yet no person recognizes it;
> Bodhi is near, yet it is extremely hard to see.
> Body and mind are originally without arising or ceasing;
> All dharmas are also like this.
> No arising, no ceasing, no place of abiding:
> This is the substance of bodhi and nirvāṇa.
> The wise, within one, understand all;
> Within all dharmas, they understand the one.
> Immeasurable dharmas are precisely one dharma;
> One dharma is precisely immeasurable dharmas.
> One buddha-land fills the ten-directional worlds;
> Yet the original form of one world is not enlarged.
> One buddha-realm contains the worlds of the ten directions;
> Yet all those worlds are not weighed down.
> A single mote of dust contains the ten-directional worlds;
> Within every mote, it is likewise.
> Yet one mote is not made more vast;
> All worlds remain in their original aspect, ever thus.
> Immeasurable, countless, vastly great kalpas:
> The wise understand them as a single thought.
> A single thought is never extended to become long;
> Long kalpas are never compressed to become short.
> Traveling everywhere to the ten directions seeking buddhahood,
> One does not know that body and mind have been buddhas all along.
> In the past, with vigor one renounced birth and death,
> Not knowing that birth and death are precisely nirvāṇa.

### Explanation

The treatise states: This Dharma-gate can open the wisdom-eye of all bodhisattvas and can fulfill the aspirations of all bodhisattvas. All practitioners who wish to be born in the Pure Land should rely upon this gate and practice it, thought after thought, in their studies. At all times they should not allow any interruption. Those who ride this Dharma use small expedient means to swiftly reach the buddha-fruit. It is like a person who boards a ship to cross the ocean to the other shore: catching a favorable wind, with small expedient means one swiftly reaches the other shore. Those who ride this Dharma, whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down, at all times constantly enter the samādhi of cessation. Those who ride this Dharma will before long attain the ground of unobstructed patience. There are such various benefits that even if all buddhas were to dwell for innumerable kalpas, they could not fully describe them. The secret Dharma-treasury of the buddhas of the three times is nothing beyond this dhāraṇī verse.

If there are practitioners who wish to swiftly advance toward unsurpassed bodhi, they should first arouse vast and wondrous vows. They should generate a mind of great compassion toward all sentient beings. They should regard good teachers as difficult to encounter. They should revere the Three Jewels and destroy arrogance. Relying on the Mahāyāna sūtras, they should sincerely repent of all karmic obstructions. After that, they should single-mindedly seek out great good teachers and ask about the essentials of the Dharma. Having heard the teaching, they should concentrate their thoughts in contemplation. They should practice as taught, at all times without interruption of mind. If one can do this, before long one will enter the dhāraṇī gate.

As described above, the ocean-seal samādhi comes from the Ten Grounds Chapter of the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*. As for the form of the seal, it was arranged according to meaning. As for the verse text, it summarizes the essential meaning of the entire sūtra in verse form.

Why does the path of the seal have no beginning or end? To reveal that the one path transcends the boundaries of the three times, because neither the prior limit nor the posterior limit can be grasped.

Why does the path of the seal have turns and curves? To reveal that the one path follows conditions and accords with capacities, manifesting differences in response to the three vehicle-practices.

For what reason do fifty-six corners together form the seal? To reveal the forty mind-stages before the grounds, the ten grounds, equal awakening, and wondrous awakening. These fifty-two stages of persons all rely upon the one path to cultivate the four means of attraction and deliver sentient beings. Fifty-two corners represent these fifty-two kinds of persons. The remaining four corners represent the four means of attraction.

At the stage of the ten levels of faith, one looks up in faith at the one path and the four means of attraction. At the stage of the ten levels of understanding, one has faith in and comprehends the one path and the four means of attraction. At the stage of the ten levels of practice, one has faith in and carries out the one path and the four kinds of attraction-practice. At the stage of the ten levels of dedication, understanding and practice are both complete, and one carries out the one path and the four kinds of attraction-practice. At the stage of the ten grounds and the stage of equal awakening, one directly realizes the one path and practices the four means of attraction. At the stage of wondrous awakening, one has ultimately and perfectly realized the original source of the one path and, to the end of the future, uses the four means of attraction to transform sentient beings.

Why do the verses have fourteen characters both vertically and horizontally? To reveal that although a bodhisattva's practices are immeasurable, they do not depart from the ten pāramitās and the four means of attraction-practice. All bodhisattvas throughout all worlds of the ten directions without limit all rely upon these practices as their foundation.

Why do the verses have a beginning and an end? To comfort and reassure timid bodhisattvas: though the Buddha-Way is long, if cultivation is uninterrupted, one will ultimately attain buddhahood; do not retreat from practice.

Why are both the beginning and the end of the verse situated in the center? To reveal that the right contemplation initially cultivated by the beginner is no different from the right contemplative wisdom of the Tathāgata.

Why, when read vertically, horizontally, or around the four sides in sequence, does one always lose the sentences? To reveal that the profoundly deep meaning of this dhāraṇī cannot be known by those of eternalist or nihilist views or by those who hold extreme views.

Why, when following from the center, does one find the sentences? To reveal that the profoundly deep meaning of this dhāraṇī can only be known through the right contemplation of the Middle Way.

Why is there no verse outside the seal? To reveal that although the Buddha's teachings surpass dust and sand in number, they do not depart from the one path. Outside the one path, there is no further dharma.

Why is the meaning within the verse not explained? To cause practitioners to obtain much understanding of meaning from a small amount of text. Fearing that practitioners would abandon the root to chase after branches, becoming greedy for many sentences and words and losing the great benefit. As the sūtra says: "It is better, with little learning, to have much understanding of the flavor of meaning, than to desire much learning without comprehending the meaning." To test whether practitioners have obtained much or little benefit, whether their faculties are deep or shallow, and whether their practice is mature or raw. To cause sentient beings of narrow vision and arrogance to generate a mind of seriousness toward the correct Dharma.

This deep meaning is not the domain of nine kinds of persons. What are the nine? First, it is not the domain of ordinary people. Second, it is not the domain of worldly cleverness and eloquence. Third, it is not the domain of those who discriminate the characteristics of dharmas. Fourth, it is not the domain of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Fifth, it is not the domain of practitioners who cling to the view of emptiness. Sixth, it is not the domain of distracted minds of much learning. Seventh, it is not the domain of sentient beings who hold extreme views. Eighth, it is not the domain of sentient beings who hold nihilist views. Ninth, it is not the domain of sentient beings who hold eternalist views. Such persons mutually disparage one another, saying: "My wisdom is superior; that one's wisdom is certainly weak. Only I alone can fully comprehend the buddha-dharma." Such persons are truly pitiable. From a body of five feet, they raise up a grand sense of self. With a mind the size of a square inch, they presume to measure the limits of empty space. They are like small children using a ladle-cup to scoop the ocean, claiming: "Only I can measure the extent of the great ocean." Sentient beings of narrow vision who presume to measure the buddha-dharma are also like this. If such people do not destroy their arrogance and do not repent of their original minds, they are difficult to teach and instruct. Even if they recite with their mouths, they will not comprehend the deep principle. Even if they hear much, they will only increase their arrogance. Their toil will be without benefit. As a verse of the sūtra says:

> Like a poor person
> Who day and night counts another's treasures,
> Yet lacks half a coin himself:
> Much learning is also like this.

There are such various kinds of sentient beings. Wishing to protect them, I do not further explain the meaning, fearing that their wrong views would only increase, causing them to sink forever in the sea of suffering with no prospect of escape. As a verse of the sūtra says:

> Because those who break the Dharma lack faith,
> They fall into the three evil destinies.
> I would rather not teach the Dharma
> And swiftly enter nirvāṇa.

As stated above, the meaning of this dhāraṇī is the domain of boundless practice and dharmadhātu-equal wisdom. As a verse of the sūtra says:

> One who roams the dharmadhātu in mind, as in empty space:
> That person can know the realm of the buddhas.

For what reason is the original preserved? To reveal that the correct Dharma ultimately abides permanently, relied upon by all buddhas of the three times. It is like the ink-line of a carpenter, which is a permanent standard that past and future craftsmen alike rely upon. Furthermore, to cause nominal bodhisattvas to abandon the branches and seek the root, penetrating to the original source. To cause the correct Dharma to abide permanently without destruction, the light of the Dharma to shine constantly and never cease, destroying the darkness of ignorance for sentient beings. To cause sentient beings, in every place they are born, to never break the lineage of the buddha-seed: for these reasons the original is preserved.

The essential meaning of the sūtras has been briefly described thus. To exhaust the original source is something only the buddhas can fully fathom. I can only look up in faith, understand, and praise the teaching.

Wishing to arouse the great vow, I offer praise in verse:

> The buddha-dharma is supremely vast,
> Its measure equal to empty space.
> The meaning I have described
> Is like a fraction of a single pore.
> The merit I have described
> I universally bestow upon all classes of sentient beings.
> May they swiftly ascend to the ten grounds
> And together accomplish the buddha-fruit.
