# 前世三轉經
## Sūtra of Three Transformations in Past Lives

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0178

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Fajù (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the Anāthapiṇḍada Monastery, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats. All were great sages, supreme among the honored. All knew the thoughts of others, save only Ānanda, who had not yet attained this.

At that time, the Buddha came out of the monastery and sat in open space. Countless hundreds of thousands of assembly members surrounded him as he taught the Dharma, together with seventy thousand bodhisattvas who had all attained the dhāraṇīs. At that time, the Buddha's face shone with a radiance surpassing its usual brilliance. Light issued from his face and illuminated all the worlds. The fragrance spread likewise. Then the Buddha smiled, and five-colored light issued from his mouth, ascending as high as the Brahma heaven. When the Buddhas, Honored Ones among the Honored, bestow predictions upon śrāvaka disciples, the light goes forth and illuminates the realm of the Four Heavenly Kings, then returns, circles the Buddha three times, and enters through the soles of his feet. When the Buddhas, Honored Ones among the Honored, bestow the prediction of the pratyekabuddha path, the light issues from his mouth, illuminates the Parāṇirmita heaven, circles the Buddha three times, and enters through the navel. When the Buddhas, Honored Ones among the Honored, bestow the prediction of the Buddha path, the light illuminates the Brahma heaven, circles the Buddha three times, and enters through the crown of his head. At that time, all the earth spirits simultaneously raised their voices: "The Buddha thus proclaims three kinds of predictions, whether of the past, the future, or the present." The spirits of the sky, the devas, the Four Heavenly Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, up to the Brahma heaven, all raised their voices: "He thus proclaims three kinds of predictions, past, future, and present: predictions of the śrāvaka ground, predictions of the pratyekabuddha ground, and predictions of the Buddha ground." At that time, all the Brahma devas descended, and the beings from as high as the Trāyastriṃśa heaven all came down. Then, a countless assembly of hundreds of thousands gathered: bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās.

The worthy Ānanda at that time knew seven things. What are the seven? First, he knew the meaning. Second, he understood the Dharma. Third, he discerned the right time. Fourth, he comprehended the proper measure. Fifth, he knew the assembly. Sixth, he knew his own affairs. Seventh, he knew the affairs of others. The worthy Ānanda rose from his seat, straightened his robes, knelt, joined his palms, and praised the Buddha in verse:

> "He who has attained the pure eye of wisdom, bright and fine,
> Whose honored faculties are still, whose concentration is boundless,
> Whose radiance shines far, golden in color,
> Whose divine illumination: who will be granted his words? Pray explain.
> Who now generates the aspiration for the Buddha path?
> Who sits beneath the tree and vanquishes Māra?
> Who now takes the supreme benefit of the Buddha's path?
> O Moon-Faced One, pray tell us: why do you smile?
> When the Buddha, the World-Honored One, smiles,
> The light from his face surpasses all others.
> Now, with this assembly of hundreds of thousands,
> The Buddha, with this cause, should explain."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "O Honored One among the Honored! Those who have lust, anger, and delusion laugh on account of form, sound, smell, taste, touch, and mental objects. The Honored One has severed lust, anger, and delusion. For what reason does he smile? O Honored One! This is not a question asked by Śāriputra, nor by Mahāmaudgalyāyana, Mahākāśyapa, Uruvilvā-Kāśyapa, Gayā-Kāśyapa, Nadī-Kāśyapa, or Śāriputra. The Buddhas, Honored Ones among the Honored, possess six unshared qualities. What are the six? First, the Buddhas have unobstructed wisdom regarding the past. Second, they have unobstructed wisdom regarding the future. Third, they have unobstructed wisdom regarding the present. Fourth, the Buddhas have the wisdom of bodily action. Fifth, the Buddhas have the wisdom of verbal expression. Sixth, the Buddhas have the wisdom of mental intention. These are the six. There is nothing the Buddhas do not see or hear. The Buddhas expound the wisdom of the Buddha path!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "In a past age, there was a country named Utpalavṛtta. The country was wealthy, prosperous, and flourishing, the five grains were abundant, and the people were at peace. Their number was beyond reckoning." The Buddha said to Ānanda: "At that time, in the country of Utpalavṛtta, there was a king named Prāsena, who was supreme among all the countries. He governed with the right Dharma and caused people to act justly. The rod and staff were not employed. At that time, the people's lifespan was twenty thousand years. When the king's life ended, there was in that country a courtesan, a woman of surpassing beauty, with a fine countenance, exquisitely lovely. This woman went to the home of another family. The woman of that household had just given birth to a boy. She was pulling the child by the arm. The courtesan asked: 'What are you doing, pulling the child?' She answered: 'I am starving and wish to eat him.' She asked: 'Is there no food you can eat?' She answered: 'There is none.' The courtesan said: 'Wait a moment. I will go and bring you food.' She answered: 'Before you even leave the door, I shall starve to death. How can I wait for you to bring food?' The courtesan thought: 'If I take the child away, the mother will starve to death. If I leave, she will eat the child. What can I do to save both mother and child?' The courtesan then took a sharp knife and cut off both her breasts, giving them to the woman, who ate them. The courtesan asked: 'Are you full?' She answered: 'I am.' Blood flowed from both of the courtesan's breasts, streaming to the ground, as she returned home.

"At that time, a man came to the courtesan's house, intending to engage in improper conduct. When he saw her, he said: 'Who has taken this fine woman's breasts and cut them like this?' He was filled with compassion, and regarding her as a sister, his desire ceased. The man then asked: 'Sister! Who did this to you?' She answered: 'No one harmed me. I went to another's home. The woman there had just given birth, and was pulling her child. I asked what she was doing. She said she was starving and wished to eat her child. I asked if she had no food. She said she had none. I said I would bring food. She said that before I even left the door, she would die. I thought: if I took the child, the mother would die; if I left, she would eat the child. So I cut off my breasts and gave them to her.' When the man heard this, he collapsed to the ground in a faint. The courtesan sprinkled water on him, and after a long while he revived. The man said: 'Sister, will you demonstrate to me the truth of your words?' The courtesan said: 'I will.' The man said: 'I have never seen such a difficult feat. If your words are truly sincere and not false, may your breasts be restored as before.' At that very moment, the woman's breasts were restored as before, without any scar.

"Śakra, with his divine eye, saw the courtesan and said: 'This fine courtesan practices generosity and merit to such a degree; I fear she will come and take my throne.' He then transformed into a brāhmaṇa, carrying a jeweled staff, a pitcher of washing water, and a golden walking stick. He went begging and arrived at the courtesan's house, saying: 'Give me alms.' The woman filled a golden bowl with food and offered it to the brāhmaṇa. The brāhmaṇa stepped back and refused to accept. The woman asked: 'Why do you step back and refuse the food?' He answered: 'I do not need food. I heard that you gave your breasts in charity. Is this true?' She answered: 'It is true.' The brāhmaṇa then asked in verse:

> "'What is the wish you seek? To become Śakra or Brahmā?
> Do you seek a king of many treasures? Such a wish would be as difficult as this.'

"The beautiful woman answered in verse:

> "'O brāhmaṇa! I seek the unconditioned,
> Where there is no birth, old age, sickness, or death,
> No sorrow, no grief, a place of purity:
> That, O brāhmaṇa, is what I seek.'

"The brāhmaṇa said: 'Let me ask you: when you gave your breasts in charity, did your mind waver at all?' The woman answered: 'Brāhmaṇa! I shall demonstrate my sincerity to you.' He said: 'Demonstrate it.' The woman said: 'If my sincerity in giving my breasts was true and my mind did not waver, may my woman's body be transformed into that of a man.' No sooner had she spoken than she was transformed into a man.

"When the king of Utpalavṛtta had ruled for five thousand years, he died. His close ministers and attendants, hearing that the courtesan's body had been transformed into a man, thought: 'We should enthrone this one as king, for such a ruler would govern with the right Dharma.' They together enthroned him as king. The rod and staff were not employed. He governed with the right Dharma and delighted in generosity, placing gold, silver, and treasures at the four gates of the city and in every street and alley. Whatever people desired, whether food, drink, water, clothing, incense, flowers, lodging, seats, dwellings, gold, silver, treasures, luminous pearls, jade, lapis lazuli, crystal, coral, or agate, was given to them. He taught all the people of the realm to observe the eight precepts. Under such governance for five hundred years, the land needed no plowing: rice grew of itself, clean, fragrant, and delicious, without any coarseness. When cut today, it grew back tomorrow, springing up of its own accord. When the rice was taken, the stalks immediately vanished. The taste of the food encompassed every flavor. After eating, one's teeth did not decay, nor did one grow old, fall ill, or become bent. One's complexion remained unchanged, and all were of equal stature. Their fortune and appearance were also equal, like those of the people in the continent of Uttarakuru. The king then thought: 'That I give to people is not so remarkable. If I were to give to birds and beasts, that would truly be difficult.' He then anointed his body with fragrant ghee and entered the mountains to a secluded place. He lay upon a cliff. All kinds of birds came and ate his body, and he died.

"He was then reborn in a brāhmaṇa family of great wealth, with countless gold, silver, and treasures. When ten months were full, he was born, of exquisite beauty beyond compare. As soon as he was born, four attendant women raised him: the first was in charge of wiping his body, the second of bathing, the third of nursing, and the fourth of carrying him. The child grew up. The four women attended him together and arranged that he should never see the outside world. Five hundred silk-clad maidens served and entertained him. He secretly slipped out and went to the marketplace, where he saw merchants, traders, and beggars, the poor and destitute. He was filled with compassion and said: 'If these people were wealthy, they would not have to trade and beg.' He then spoke this verse:

> "'How is it with my body and mind? So firm and yet unbroken.
> While I myself dwell in ease and comfort,
> I see all the people toiling in hardship.'

"He rushed home and told his parents: 'I wish to shave my head and enter the mountains, to dwell beneath the trees in a secluded place.' His parents refused. Why? 'We have only you, our single child, and we love you dearly. We prayed to the mountains, rivers, sun, moon, and all the devas, and at last obtained you, our only son. If we cannot see you, we shall grieve and die.' They would not allow him to leave. The boy then lay on the ground and did not eat or drink for one, two, three, four, or five days. His relatives and acquaintances heard that the boy wished to study the path and shave his head, but his parents would not allow it, so he lay on the bare ground without food or drink for five days. They all went to him and counseled: 'Young man! Why do you not rise, bathe, eat, and adorn yourself? If you wish to shave your head and study the path in the forest, why not do so?' The boy did not answer. They spoke thus for three days. Then the relatives all went to the parents and said: 'Let him go and study the path. If he is happy, he can come and visit from time to time. If he is not, he can return home.' The parents said: 'Very well.' With their permission, the boy nourished himself for six or seven days, circumambulated his parents three times, bowed to them, and entered a secluded place to sit and practice the path. Afterward, he went deeper into the great forest.

"Within the forest, two practitioners were seated, having attained the five supernatural powers. They were of utmost sincerity in seeking the path and had abandoned desire. The boy went to their place and asked: 'What are you doing here?' They answered: 'We are sitting in the open, meditating and contemplating the path, enduring hardship for the sake of all people.' The boy said: 'I too shall sit in the open, meditating and contemplating the path, for the sake of all people.' The two practitioners said: 'Good!' The boy then sat beneath a great tree in the forest, practicing with diligence for the sake of all people. He attained the five supernatural powers, and his progress surpassed that of the two practitioners. He was a great sage, supreme among the honored. In the forest there was a pregnant tigress. According to the practice of the forest sages, one only ate fruit that had fallen from the trees of its own accord, never picking from the branches. The practitioners went together to gather fallen fruit, and then saw the pregnant tigress. The young practitioner said to the two: 'This tigress will give birth before long. She has gone days without food. I fear she will eat her own cubs. Who is able to offer his body to her?' The Bodhisattva Maitreya said: 'I shall offer my body to her.' After gathering fruit and returning, they saw that the tigress had given birth and was starving, about to devour her cubs. The young practitioner said to the two: 'The tigress has given birth and is starving. She is about to eat her cubs. Who is able to offer his body to her?' They all went together to the starving tigress. The tigress opened her eyes and bared her teeth at them. The two practitioners were both terrified and flew up into the sky. The young one said: 'Practitioner! Is this your sincerity? Just now you said you would offer your body to the hungry tigress, yet now you fly up into the sky?' One of the practitioners wept with pity and looked around, but there was nothing to be found. The young practitioner took a sharp knife, pierced his right arm, and let the blood flow. In this manner he pierced himself in seven places, letting the blood flow into the tigress's mouth. The tigress drank the blood. Then he threw his own body to feed the starving tigress, and died."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Do you wish to know who the beautiful courtesan of that time was? It was my present self. The one who was enthroned as king was also my present self. The brāhmaṇa's son who threw his body to the hungry tigress was also my present self. The two practitioners are Kāśyapa and the Bodhisattva Maitreya."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Because I practiced with such diligence, I surpassed Maitreya by nine kalpas and attained Buddhahood before him. Thus, Ānanda! For sixty kalpas I practiced the path with great hardship, giving my hands, feet, nose, ears, head, eyes, flesh, wife, sons, daughters, fine clothing, and food and drink. Because of this, I vanquished six billion māras, and through thirty-four billion such efforts I attained the Buddha path."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "If all people knew the merit of generosity as I know it, then even a person in dire poverty who had obtained a single meal to sustain his life, such that without it he would die, would still not eat it himself but would give it to a person of virtue. Ānanda! I recall the generosity of my past lives. Because of this, my face shines with radiant brilliance, and the light of my smile issued from my mouth and illuminated the three-thousand great-thousand world system."

When the Buddha taught this sūtra, four thousand two hundred bhikṣus generated the mind free of residual attachment and attained liberation. Eighty nayutas of devas and humans generated the aspiration for the supreme, perfect, true path. Seventy thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of the unborn dharmas. Thus spoke the Buddha. The worthy Ānanda and the entire assembly, including all the devas, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, and people of the world, were all joyful. They came forward, bowed to the Buddha, and departed.
