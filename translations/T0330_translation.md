# 佛說菩薩修行經
## Sūtra on Bodhisattva Practice Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0330

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Bai Fazhu (白法祖)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty great bhikṣus and five thousand bodhisattvas. All were honored bodhisattvas who had penetrated spiritual powers, who by skillful wisdom performed transformations, who traveled through the three-thousand worlds, and who universally benefited all beings, none of whom did not receive their aid. At that time, within the great city of Śrāvastī, there was a wealthy elder named Virāḍa, who, together with five hundred great elders of that city, all of one mind and shared aspiration, went forth from the great city of Śrāvastī and proceeded to the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada. They came before the Buddha, prostrated, circumambulated the Buddha three times, inquired after the World-Honored One's well-being, and sat to one side.

At that time, the World-Honored One, with his limitless penetrating wisdom, asked Vīradatta and the elders, sons of good family: "What aspiration have you given rise to, that you come to the Tathāgata?"

Then Vīradatta and the elders addressed the Buddha: "We, World-Honored One, gathered together in a quiet place, and in discussion the thought arose: 'A Buddha's appearance in the world is rare, and the human body is likewise; to attain liberation from the world is also extremely difficult.' We privately discussed: 'By which vehicle shall we reach nirvāṇa? Should we take nirvāṇa by the vehicle of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas? Or should we attain liberation through the Great Vehicle of universal wisdom?' Then we all raised our minds and declared: 'We aspire to nirvāṇa through the unsurpassed vehicle, not through the liberation of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas.' We, World-Honored One, aspire to give rise to the mind of unsurpassed, peerless, right intention. For this reason we have come to pay respects to the Tathāgata. How, World-Honored One, should a bodhisattva-mahāsattva whose inner nature always aspires to unsurpassed, perfect, true, most honored awakening study the Dharma? How should one practice and abide? May the Tathāgata extend his wisdom and universal compassion, and with boundless mercy, dispel and reveal our doubts."

At that time, the World-Honored One told Vīradatta: "Excellent! Excellent! Great elders, you are able to change your worldly ways, abandon the world's glory and pleasures, give rise to the aspiration for the unsurpassed, true path, and come to visit the Tathāgata. Furthermore, Vīradatta and all of you, listen diligently and reflect. I shall now explain: the practices of a bodhisattva-mahāsattva for attaining unsurpassed, equal, most perfect enlightenment, and the aspirations one should make, along with the dharmas of awakening."

The elder Vīradatta and the five hundred all joined their palms, received the teaching, and listened.

Then the Buddha said: "Elders! A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who has set forth in practice, wishing to attain unsurpassed, true, equal, most perfect enlightenment, should turn their mind toward sentient beings and establish the vast, universal, boundless great loving-kindness. They should train their aspirations, practice diligently without abandoning, advance in study without forgetting. This is how one accords with the unsurpassed path of awakening. Furthermore, elders! There are sentient beings who, through the evil of body, speech, and mind, and through their wrong conduct, fall into the hells upon death. Therefore, elders! When heaven and earth converge and suffering gathers, toward all beings of the various wrong views, one should maintain a mind of great loving-kindness and diligently cultivate great compassion. One should guard, train, and practice, concentrating one's effort in this way. One's body should not be attached to clothing, food, or drink, and the mind should not covet any gains. One should joyfully give away all one's treasures. Thinking of those sentient beings, one should carefully practice moral discipline, patience, diligence, concentration, and wisdom. Thus, elders! A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who wishes to give rise to the aspiration for the unsurpassed, true path should train in contemplating dharmas and accord with bodily practice."

Then Vīradatta and the elders said: "We, World-Honored One, shall cultivate the three purities of body, four of speech, and three of mind. How should a bodhisattva-mahāsattva contemplate the dharmas of bodily practice?"

Then the World-Honored One told Vīradatta and the others: "Thus, elders! A bodhisattva-mahāsattva has forty-two matters by which to contemplate the body. Having made this contemplation, one is freed from the entanglements of perception. The bondage and attachment of body, mind, consciousness to self, the craving for body and life-span, and all turbid defilements should thereby be entirely eliminated."

Then Vīradatta and the elders received the teaching and listened.

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva should contemplate: the body is filthy, fundamentally impure. The body is fetid, a mere accumulation of rot. The body is precarious and fragile, destined to disintegrate. The body has no strength, destined to crumble and scatter. The body is like an illusion, the great elements transforming. The body oozes foul discharge from its nine orifices. The body blazes with the raging fire of lust. The body is scorched by the fire of the poison of anger. The body is shrouded in the darkness of the poison of delusion. The body is a net, bound by the fetters of attachment. The body is like a sore, entangled in countless ailments. The body is prone to four hundred and four diseases. The body is a filthy dwelling that harbors all manner of worms. The body is impermanent, passing away and returning to dust. The body is dull and foolish, not understanding the dharma of its own nature. The body is precarious and decrepit, its deterioration not far off. The body is unreliable, always harboring many worries. The body has no firmness; when old age arrives, suffering is extreme. The body has no support; all its adornment is deception and fraud. The body is hard to satisfy; its craving is insatiable. The body is a nest for attachment to forms. The body is deluded by craving, infatuated with the five pleasures. The body is dark, the mind clinging to joy and delight. The body has no fixed abode; birth and death take various forms. The body's consciousness harbors lowly thoughts. The body has no true friends; even the most nourished will part. The body will be food that foxes devour and wolves contend over. The body is a mechanism of countless turnings. The body depends on food and drink to sustain it. The body is unbearable to look at, full of pus, blood, and stench. The body will be destroyed, tending toward the law of impermanence. The body is like an enemy, always causing harm. The body is feverish and distressed, always bearing worry and entanglement. The body is an accumulation of calamity, deceived by the five aggregates. The body is a vessel of suffering; birth and death bring intense pain. The body is not self, merely an accumulation of conditions. The body has no life-force; whether male or female, all will disperse. The body is empty; the faculties merely receive sense impressions. The body is unreal, like an illusion. The body is false, appearing like a dream. The body is deceptive, like a mirage. The body is fraudulent, like an echo or a reflection. These, elders, are the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's forty-two matters for contemplating the dharmas of bodily practice. One who does not contemplate may become attached to the body, and the mind and consciousness will arise and cease on account of it. If a bodhisattva contemplates in this way, the craving and attachment to body and life, the love of self, the defilements of doubt and inversion, and all pleasures and notions of permanence will be entirely eliminated. Guarding one's aspiration and maintaining single-mindedness, not sparing one's years and life-span, one will thus swiftly perfect the six pāramitās. This is what is meant, elders, by the bodhisattva-mahāsattva who, having fulfilled the six virtues, spreads the teaching through skillful transformations, and swiftly attains unsurpassed, most perfect enlightenment."

Then the World-Honored One further elaborated on the dharmas of bodily practice, and proclaimed in verse:

> To be born human is exceedingly rare;
> Do not use the body to create evil deeds.
> Inevitably one dies, cast upon the burial mound,
> Where foxes and wolves feast, or it rots away.

> Falsely deceived, the fool is always deluded,
> Solely raising thoughts of craving for form and desire.
> This body seeks without end,
> Day and night enduring every pain.

> Through manifold sufferings, affliction is formed;
> The body, an abscess, brims with impurity.
> Ever oppressed by hunger and thirst,
> What wise person would cling to such a life?

> Ever indulging the body, never sated,
> Forcibly nurturing it as if a dear friend,
> For the sake of forms one commits all manner of sins,
> And thereby receives the torments of hell.

> The body cannot endure like vajra;
> Do not use it to create evil karma.
> Though one survives long, one will surely die;
> Now give rise to faith and mindfulness of the Buddha's world.

> Though you long nourish and sustain this body
> With fine foods and fragrant flowers,
> Inevitably comes hunger, nothing is permanent;
> Though you strive, what benefit is there?

> After an age of kalpas, one may again encounter
> The heroic, honored one, a Buddha in the world.
> Always give rise to faith, commit no wrongdoing,
> Lest you fall into the three realms and suffer bitter poison.

> Even with a lifespan of a billion thousand years,
> Exert yourself diligently as if putting out a fire.
> How much more so for one whose life is but a hundred years,
> Who, arrogant and indulgent, creates the disasters of hell!

> If one thinks with notions of "I" and "mine,"
> Know that obtaining a human body is exceedingly rare.
> Always indulging at will in the five pleasures,
> Entertaining oneself for now, who knows what comes after?

> Such pleasures do not last forever;
> The manifold bitter poisons are not far off.
> One should swiftly abandon all stinginess and craving;
> Then one may attain the great fortune of merit.

> Wealth and non-wealth are like a dream,
> Forcibly deceiving all beings with falsehood.
> In a moment one has it, then it is gone;
> The wise do not cling to riches.

> Like an illusory conjuration that deceives with form,
> Displaying false flowers of vivid color,
> These desires and riches, who do they deceive?
> The foolish and turbid, deluded, fall into inversion.

> Through manifold suffering one obtains meritorious wealth,
> Yet for the body's sake one clings with thought and perception.
> Wealth and non-wealth are at the mercy of the five claimants;
> What wisdom is there in being deluded by riches?

> Foolishly following after wife and children,
> The king's power forcibly seizes your gathered wealth.
> Awakening to impermanence and understanding this,
> One will never again set one's mind on profiting one's household.

> Love's gatherings bring only suffering and affliction;
> Do not be deluded and attached to the prison of the household.
> Parents, wealth, body, wife, and children
> Are all left behind; you alone must face your deeds.

> Those who crave and cling without self-awareness,
> Fearing only that their wealth will perish with them,
> The foolish and stubborn exert themselves for wealth;
> The wise and discerning have faith and are free from craving.

> The stingy and faithless are not worth following;
> They utterly debase themselves like children and servants.
> Outwardly parched by craving, inwardly burning with flattery,
> The noble sages do not praise such people.

> Discussing books and composing verses,
> They deceive the multitudes like a seductive woman.
> Their minds are coarse and violent in nature;
> The stingy are mostly given to jealousy.

> Greedy as wolves, with no true friends,
> They put on humility and force themselves upon others.
> Only for wealth's sake they pursue suffering;
> The wise and discerning should not trust them.

> Compliant with wealth, they engage in these affairs,
> Even devising minds of poison and harm.
> Therefore the wise should examine and reflect,
> Abandoning stinginess, jealousy, and wrong deeds.

> Gold, pearls, jewels, and precious rarities
> Are obtained through the causes of meritorious fortune;
> On their account arise disputes and litigation.
> Control the mind and set it right with the Dharma.

> In time one may encounter the heroic, honored one,
> Maitreya Buddha, the supreme Tathāgata;
> Then there will be a land of golden jewels,
> But who knows whether one will be reborn there?

> The five sensual pleasures are pure falsehood,
> Deceiving the minds of the foolish and deluded.
> Desire is like the fierce heat of summer,
> Sitting upon a mirage, one wearies in vain.

> Craving for visible forms deceives oneself;
> Lust arises, one is intoxicated, losing purpose and will.
> Following desires, one falls into inversion.
> When will one ever encounter a Buddha's world?

> In ninety-one kalpas,
> A world may produce a Buddha, a Most Honored Awakened One.
> Mount Sumeru itself burns and is destroyed;
> Afterward, by what cause will one encounter him again?

> The seas, ponds, and pools dry up and are exhausted,
> Heaven and earth are scorched, utterly nothing remains.
> Desire blazes just like this;
> What wisdom is there in clinging to desire?

> The wise and discerning, those of brilliant insight,
> Should perceive and know, abiding in cessation.
> What is there to crave? What is there to delight in?
> Understanding this meaning, one does not enter the net.

> Contemplative practice is the foremost among dharmas;
> Do not be attached to this corpse-like prison.
> Those attached to love, with craving and turbid minds,
> Cannot escape the suffering and disaster of the hells.

> Having wife and children, craving and parting,
> The deeds one has done, one alone must bear.
> Alone one proceeds, following bitter poison;
> There is no one to take one's pain.

> In these three realms, nothing is more tormenting
> Than wife and children.
> Originally loved and intended for happiness,
> They turn into the root of worry, wrongdoing, and affliction.

> On account of them, one receives the suffering of the three evil paths,
> The bitter, sour, and wretched pains of existence.
> If one is tormented by these roots of affliction,
> Neither wife nor children come to one's rescue.

> Do not create evil deeds for the sake of father
> Or mother or any kin.
> From the pain of Avīci there is no deliverance;
> Rather, act in accordance with your own conduct.

> The wardens of King Yama's hells
> Do not inquire about father or mother,
> Brothers, wife, children, or friends;
> They only reckon the good and evil of one's own body.

> Having obtained this human body,
> Having encountered it, do not think of evil.
> Cut off and extinguish all calamitous wrongdoing;
> Reform and discard all past unwholesome deeds.

> Already defiled, guard and cover yourself;
> Do not believe that deeds have no consequences.
> The Dharma King will expose and explain them all,
> Distinguishing and clarifying the path of pure practice.

> The body reaps what its deeds have sown;
> Giving the mind free rein, one falls into affliction.
> What the body has created, it reaps in calamity,
> Just as a shadow follows a form.

> When one undergoes suffering and pain,
> Neither parents nor kin can grant reprieve.
> Not even the dearest friend can take one's place.
> Therefore the wise do not cling to desire.

> If one wishes to escape the harsh poisons of hell,
> And all the bonds, cangues, chains, and fetters,
> One should diligently give up and abandon desire,
> And swiftly practice the teachings of the World-Honored Hero.

> The household blazes fiercely, root of much affliction,
> Like a fire arising and burning without cease.
> What wise person would delight in this,
> Rubbing up against a great fire in the midst of danger?

> The householder worries about profit and gain,
> Dwelling in worldly affairs, managing wife and children.
> With these ten thousand kinds of anxious concerns,
> What wisdom is there in not leaving the household?

> The teaching of the Ten-Powered One is supremely delightful,
> Requiring no planting or sowing, no pulling of roots.
> Foolish children have no such aspiration;
> They are only deluded by the household, and fall into the hells.

> Between heaven and earth, the most deluded
> Are those who raise thoughts of "my wife and children."
> The foolish and stubborn think they will last forever,
> Not knowing this body is but an illusion and transformation.

When the World-Honored One taught this Dharma, the five hundred elders led by Vīradatta immediately attained the gentle and compliant Dharma-patience. Having attained this patience, they were complete in spiritual powers, penetrating knowledge of past and future. Their noble wisdom was vast and wondrous, their insight unobstructed. Clearly perceiving where the aspirations and desires of all sentient beings tend, wishing to awaken the minds of all in the assembly to contemplation, they then spoke in verse:

> How joyful! What great benefit,
> The most supreme of all benefits!
> Those who give rise to aspiration and practice,
> Seeking the Buddha's path as bodhisattvas.

> The mind of the Great Vehicle is delightful,
> Desiring only to bring peace to all beings.
> Building bridges for all people,
> Those who delight in the Great Vehicle.

> Sentient beings love and delight in such ones;
> All joyfully gaze upon their countenances.
> Those who give rise to aspiration,
> Seeking the bodhisattva path.

> Those who give rise to the bodhi-mind,
> Planting virtue in the field of merit,
> Who deeply delight in the bodhisattva way,
> Become the light of the three realms.

> The noble bodhisattva-mind
> Surpasses the minds of all beings.
> Complete in all things,
> It can ferry all beings across.

> How fortunate we are to gain this benefit,
> Delighting in giving rise to this mind.
> We have encountered the Buddha Munīndra's world,
> The lion, the Most Perfectly Awakened.

> We have attained the hearing of this Dharma,
> The bodhisattva's contemplation of bodily dharmas.
> Our aspirations delight in the Great Vehicle,
> And we have obtained the gentle and compliant patience.

Then the Buddha smiled. When the World-Honored One smiled, light of five colors issued forth, blazing from his mouth, brilliant and radiant, each color distinct, reaching immeasurable realms of light pervading the worlds of the ten directions. Its majestic brilliance outshone and eclipsed the luminance of all the palaces of Śakra, Brahmā, the sun, the moon, the heavenly māras.

When the Buddha smiled and this covering light appeared, the devas, nāgas, spirits, and the seventy-two thousand people of the world, seeing the Buddha's spiritual brilliance and its wondrous transformations, also each felt themselves bathed in the Tathāgata's radiance, their bodies nurtured and at peace. Each upon their seat suddenly attained the Dharma-delight patience of non-arising, the patience of that which arises from nowhere. The remaining countless beings all gave rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed, true enlightenment. Then the radiant flames circled the Buddha's body three times, and the majestic light suddenly entered through the crown of his head.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "The Buddhas, Tathāgatas, appear in the world to bring peace and ferry beings across. Their teachings of the path are vast and flowing, and they never smile without cause. What is the reason today that you manifest this majestic countenance and smile with delight? How excellent, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata's condescending virtue shows compassion for all the immeasurable devas, humans, and all sentient beings of the world, causing them all to attain peace, and ferrying across even animals, birds, and crawling insects, none of whom are not saved. May the Buddha explain and reveal the meaning of his smile."

Then the World-Honored One told Ānanda: "Did you see the elder Vīradatta and the five hundred?"

"Indeed, I have seen them."

The World-Honored One said: "These elders, in the presence of past Buddhas, planted the roots of many virtues and gave rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed, true enlightenment. Thus, Ānanda! The elder Vīradatta and the five hundred, after seventy-six kalpas more, will not fall into the three sufferings. Thereafter they will all become Buddhas in the same kalpa. The kalpa will be named Valiant, and they will all share the same title: Puṣpagarbharāja Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Master of the Way, Teacher of Devas and Humans, World-Honored Buddha. Each will ferry across beings beyond measure."

Then Ānanda again addressed the Buddha: "Indeed, World-Honored One! How profound and wondrous, unprecedented! The Tathāgata has expounded this vast and essential Dharma of boundless depth. What is this sūtra called? How should it be upheld?"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! This sūtra is called 'Bodhisattva Practice.' It is also called 'The Sūtra on Contemplating Bodily Practice as Asked by the Great Being Vīradatta.' Furthermore, Ānanda! This essential dharma of contemplation is what all the Buddhas of the past, future, and present have relied upon to attain the path and broadly teach. My own attainment of Buddhahood, with the marks of my body, transforming those in birth and death, is also due to this dharma. You should properly copy, uphold, recite, read, expound, and reveal it to all."

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, the Venerable Ānanda, the great being Vīradatta and the five hundred, and all the devas, nāgas, spirits, and people of the world, hearing the sūtra, rejoiced, all rose with palms joined, and made obeisance to the Buddha.
