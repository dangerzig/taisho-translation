# Upadeśa on the Sūtra of Turning the Dharma-Wheel

## *Zhuǎn Fǎlún Jīng Yōupōtíshě* (轉法輪經憂波提舍)

Taishō 1533

Composed by Vasubandhu (*Tiānqīn Púsà*, 天親菩薩). Translated by Vimokṣaprajñāṛṣi (*Pímù Zhìxiān*, 毘目智仙) of the Eastern Wèi dynasty [541 CE].

*Note: This is Vasubandhu's commentary (upadeśa) on the Dharmacakrapravartana Sūtra (Sūtra of the Turning of the Dharma-Wheel), the Buddha's first sermon at the Deer Park in Sārnāth. It was translated by Vimokṣaprajñāṛṣi and his disciple Gautama Prajñāruci in 541 CE at the Jīnhuá Monastery in Yè city, at the request of the military official Gāo Zhòngmì. The treatise takes the form of fourteen questions and answers, systematically examining: why the Buddha spoke this sūtra, the meaning of "Bhagavat" (World-Honored One), why the teaching was given at Rājagṛha and Vulture Peak, the meaning of "Tathāgata," the meaning of "dharma-wheel," the three turnings and twelve aspects of the four noble truths, the reconciliation of "turning" with the Mahāyāna teaching that all dharmas are unborn and non-turning, how the Buddha can "sustain" sentient beings and dharmas when both are ultimately empty, why the Buddha chose the Deer Park at Vārāṇasī, the setting of the first teaching, the number of beings who attained the path, and the meaning of the two kinds of "sustaining" (sentient-being sustaining and dharma sustaining). The text includes extensive citations from the Vaipulya Sūtra (Guǎngpǔ Jīng) and the Anavataptanāgarāja Sūtra, as well as verses in the standard Indian śāstra format. It represents the Yogācāra-Vaibhāṣika exegetical tradition applied to the foundational teaching of the four noble truths.*

---

## Translator's Colophon

The Dharmacakrapravartana Sūtra is what the Tathāgata first taught. The *upadeśa* is the name of the gate of meaning; it was elucidated by Vasubandhu Bodhisattva. For whom did the Buddha teach? For Kauṇḍinya and the others. For the meaning to travel to this land, a proper person was needed. Gāo Zhòngmì, the Wèi Cavalry Grand General with the rank of Directorate General and Censor-in-Chief, of Bóhǎi, earnestly sought the way of meaning, selected the genuine, and sifted out the false. He therefore invited Dharma Master Vimokṣaprajñāṛṣi and his disciple Gautama Prajñāruci, at the Jīnhuá Monastery in Yè city, to produce this gate of meaning, the *upadeśa*. In the third year of the Xīnghé era [541 CE], the year of the Great Beam, in the month when the Rooster is established, the first day being *gēngzǐ*, on the eleventh day of translation, in 3,942 words. The śramaṇa Tánlín collated and recorded.

## The Sūtra Text

Thus have I heard: At one time, the Bhagavat was dwelling on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in the city of Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus and great bodhisattvas. At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the Bodhisattva Jñānasāgaravādivaibhāṣika ["Wisdom-Ocean Eloquent Debater"]: "Jñānasāgaravādivaibhāṣika! There are two kinds of sustaining by which the Tathāgata turns the dharma-wheel. What are the two? First, sentient-being sustaining; second, dharma sustaining." [The Buddha then says:] "These two kinds of sustaining, by which the Tathāgata turns the dharma-wheel, are as taught throughout this sūtra."

## The Fourteen Questions

This dharma-wheel, this supreme sūtra: for what reason did that Muni-King, inconceivable, immeasurable, inexpressible, beyond comparison, like empty space, neither permanent nor annihilated, according with dependent origination, quiescent, supremely quiescent, most supremely quiescent, foremost in quiescence, true, non-deceptive Tathāgata, turn the supreme dharma-wheel and teach this sūtra? This is praised by the Tathāgata's śrāvaka disciples, their disciples, and the sages. For this reason, I now explain it.

How do I explain it? The Tathāgata of immeasurable merit, the great Muni-King: (1) For what reason is it called a "supreme sūtra"? (2) For what reason is he called "World-Honored One" (*Bhagavat*)? (4) Why did the Tathāgata turn this dharma-wheel with two kinds of sustaining at Rājagṛha on Vulture Peak, and not elsewhere? (5) For what reason is he called "Tathāgata"? (6) For what reason is it called a "dharma-wheel"? (7) Furthermore, with how many turnings and how many aspects does the World-Honored One turn the dharma-wheel? (8) Furthermore, the World-Honored One here speaks of "turning," yet in the dharma-gate of non-arising, he says that all dharmas neither turn nor revolve; one should know they are ultimately non-arising. If this does turn, how can one avoid [contradicting] that sūtra? That sūtra need not be avoided. (9) Furthermore, if it is said that sentient-being sustaining and dharma sustaining [are taught], then how [can this be reconciled with] the Prajñāpāramitā, in which the Tathāgata tells Subhūti: "Even if the Tathāgata were to speak for a kalpa about 'sentient beings, sentient beings,' is there any sentient being that arises or perishes?" Subhūti says: "No, World-Honored One. All sentient beings have been pure from beginningless time." And the Tathāgata further says in the Vimalakīrtinirdeśa Sūtra: "If one abides in the conception of dharmas, this is a great disease." If both sentient beings and dharmas are ultimately unobtainable, then what does the World-Honored One sustain in turning the dharma-wheel? This requires explanation. (10) Furthermore, for what reason did the World-Honored One abandon the broad, various, and magnificent places adorned with flowers and trees where immeasurable distinguished persons gather in great assemblies, and instead turn the dharma-wheel at Vārāṇasī, a place of few people, in the Deer Park under the shade of a pāṭali tree? This reason also requires explanation. (11) Furthermore, where did the World-Honored One first sit to turn the dharma-wheel? (12) Furthermore, when the World-Honored One turned the dharma-wheel, how many sentient beings abandoned evil and practiced good? (13) In brief, how are sentient-being sustaining and dharma sustaining demonstrated? (14) All these are difficult questions; the explanations follow below.

### Question 1: Why This Sūtra Is Supreme

That foremost, stainless, vast, immeasurable, inconceivable, indestructible, profoundly unshakable, perfectly awakened World-Honored One has already taught this sūtra; why does he also further teach this supreme, stainless, vast, immeasurable [dharma-wheel] praised by all beings of the three realms?

The World-Honored One, observing that sentient beings in the assembly, including devas, asuras, humans, nāgas, yakṣas, kumbhāṇḍas and others, upon hearing of the "turning of the dharma-wheel," might give rise to doubt, not knowing how many kinds of sustaining the World-Honored One employs in turning the dharma-wheel. Observing the doubts in sentient beings' minds, in order to sever those doubts, he therefore taught the two kinds of sustaining in turning the dharma-wheel. In verse:

> The humans and devas of the world / observe the Lord of the Dharma with doubting minds.
> To sever the meaning of their doubt, / he taught this sūtra.

Furthermore, the World-Honored One, out of the power of great compassion, to benefit sentient beings, taught this sūtra. In verse:

> Knowing the world is without self, / like an illusion, like a gandharva-city,
> [Yet] with sentient-being sustaining and dharma sustaining, / the Tathāgata teaches out of great compassion.

Demonstrating his own power, the meaning that he alone can teach: in the world, there is no other who can sustain [beings]; only the Buddha can perform the two kinds of sustaining. There is no other person who can turn the dharma-wheel as I turn it. Furthermore, in verse:

> Not in the palaces of heaven, / not in the dwelling of asuras,
> Not in the abodes of humans and nāgas / is there such a being:
> Foremost, immeasurable, beyond faults, / extinguishing the three sufferings,
> Reverenced by devas and humans, / who well turns the foremost wheel.

Furthermore, [the Buddha taught this] because only after immeasurable suffering and immeasurable accumulation does one attain *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi*. Lest beginning bodhisattvas, hearing this, become timid and weak, the Tathāgata, to remove their timidity, demonstrates this meaning: the stainless, perfect awakening, [attained] after immeasurable suffering and immeasurable accumulation, the fruit of *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi*, the dharma-wheel of immeasurable merit. In verse:

> Gold, jewels, pearls and the like, / wife, children, kingdom given in charity,
> Head, limbs, eyes, bone, and marrow: / these are the supreme gifts of the body.
> Through various sufferings, maintaining precepts, / the rare buddha-body is attained.
> Its merit is immeasurable; / it is shown to those who doubt and fear.

Furthermore, for those who have not yet aroused the aspiration for bodhi and are [only] in the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha vehicles, who wish to enter nirvāṇa and abandon the Mahāyāna: this meaning is demonstrated. If śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas enter nirvāṇa and abandon [the path], they will not again turn the supreme dharma-wheel. In verse:

> Those of small mind who lack compassion / wish to enter the two [inferior] nirvāṇas.
> The Muni teaches this sūtra / to cause them to abide in the foremost vehicle.

Furthermore, to benefit those of merit and bring them joy, this meaning is demonstrated: the foremost, incomparable turner of the dharma-wheel in all the world is none other than my teacher. In verse:

> Those who have taken refuge in the Buddha, / those who take refuge now, and those who will:
> The Muni rejoices in those persons / and teaches this sūtra.

Furthermore, to draw those who rely on heterodox teachers toward benefit, this meaning is demonstrated: the stainless merit adorns the wondrous body that turns the dharma-wheel. Your teacher cannot compare; your teacher cannot enable you to obtain the stainless wholesome dharmas. In verse:

> Seeing the world relying on evil teachers, / the Tathāgata,
> To draw those persons, / teaches this sūtra-treasure.

Furthermore, to pacify the pride of all-knowledge and bring benefit, this meaning is demonstrated: "I, the omniscient one, now newly turn the supreme dharma-wheel. How can you [claim to be] an omniscient person?" In verse:

> The Buddha first turned the dharma-wheel, / able to eliminate the errors of permanence and annihilation.
> One who cannot turn the stainless wheel / is not an omniscient one.

Furthermore, to benefit those seeking the vast, supreme fruit, the unsurpassed field of merit, demonstrating the inconceivable fruition it can bestow: if one can turn the supreme dharma-wheel, gifts to such a person yield great rewards. In verse:

> If there is a person who can turn / the supreme dharma-wheel,
> Even a small gift to such a person / yields incomparable reward.

### Question 5: The Meaning of "Tathāgata"

The meaning is now explained. "Coming as [reality] truly [is]" is why he is called Tathāgata (*Rúlái*). What dharma is called "thus" (*rú*, *tathā*)? Nirvāṇa is called "thus." Sentient beings and dharmas are not "thus" [i.e., they lack the nature of nirvāṇa]. As the World-Honored One said: "Bhikṣus! The foremost noble truth, the non-deceptive dharma, is called nirvāṇa." "Knowing" (*lái*, *āgata*) is called "coming." The word "come" (*lái*) in the usage of etymological analysis means "cognizing." As worldly people say: "This person was born as a knower." This clarifies wisdom being complete. The meaning of "coming" is thus. Nirvāṇa is "thus" (*tathā*); knowing and understanding is "coming" (*āgata*); perfectly awakening to nirvāṇa, he is called Tathāgata.

Furthermore: emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness are called "thus"; going in accord with all those practices, he is called Tathāgata. Furthermore: the four noble truths are called "thus"; no other person sees them. Going in accord with all those practices, he is called Tathāgata. Furthermore: all such buddha-dharmas are called "thus"; that one who has come to this is called Tathāgata. Furthermore: "thus" refers to the six pāramitās: giving, morality, patience, effort, concentration, and wisdom. Perfectly awakening to them and coming, he is called Tathāgata.

Reality, relinquishment, quiescence, wisdom, and peaceful abiding are "thus." Coming in accord with that supreme, perfect, complete knowledge, he is called Tathāgata. All such bodhisattva grounds: Joy, Freedom from Defilement, Radiance, Blazing, Hard to Conquer, Manifest, Far-Reaching, Immovable, Good Wisdom, and Cloud of Dharma, these ten are called "thus." Coming in accord with that supreme, perfect, complete knowledge, he is called Tathāgata. Coming by way of the eightfold path, he is called Tathāgata. Coming with the feet of prajñāpāramitā and the feet of skillful means, he is called Tathāgata. Or he may be called "Thus-Gone" (*Rúqù*). "Thus-Gone" means: or he teaches according to "thus," hence "Thus-Gone." Furthermore, "Thus-Gone" means: having gone, he does not return, hence "Thus-Gone."

### Question 6: The Meaning of "Dharma-Wheel"

The meaning is now explained. The Dharma itself is the wheel; hence it is called "dharma-wheel." Just as in the world, a copper vessel that is a vase is called a "copper vase," and a wooden object that is a wheel is called a "wooden wheel"; so too, the Dharma that is the wheel is called the "dharma-wheel."

What is this Dharma? It refers to the thirty-seven factors of awakening (*bodhipakṣa dharma*). This Dharma is the wheel; hence "dharma-wheel." Furthermore, the meaning of awakening to the self-nature of all dharmas is the meaning of the dharma-wheel. Furthermore, the meaning of the supreme adornment of all dharmas, and the meaning of accepting and relinquishing, are also called "dharma-wheel." What is relinquished? The conditioned (*saṃskṛta*). What is accepted? Nirvāṇa. Furthermore, it is able to destroy all afflictions; therefore it is called a "wheel": like the wheel of time, like the wheel of a dharma-king (as a wheel-turning king's wheel), and like the wheel of the constellations that illuminates all the world.

Furthermore, the dharma-wheel is the wheel of neither permanence nor annihilation, not fixed in either extreme. Furthermore, the wheel of non-arising, as in dependent origination. Furthermore, the wheel of non-duality, as eye and form, and so on up to mind and dharmas, are non-dual. The wheel of unobtainability, because dharmas of the three times are unobtainable. Furthermore, the wheel of emptiness, because it is free from all views. Furthermore, the wheel of signlessness, because it contemplates all signs and is free from signs. Furthermore, the wheel of wishlessness, because it is free from the three realms. The wheel of non-discrimination of all discriminations, because all dharmas are non-discriminated.

Furthermore, the World-Honored One, in the Anavataptanāgarāja Sūtra, told the Nāga King: "Noble-Faced Nāga King! The dharma-wheel is the practice of the non-destruction of reality; thus it is called a wheel, because the three times are equal. The wheel of no self-nature, because it is free from the two views of existence and non-existence. Furthermore, the wheel of detachment, because the body is without defilement. Furthermore, the wheel of non-attachment, because it is free from mind, mental factors, and consciousness. The wheel of no location, because it abandons all conditioned existence. Furthermore, the wheel of reality, because of the vision of great reality. Furthermore, the wheel of truth, because correct cultivation is indestructible. Furthermore, the wheel of inexhaustibility, because it demonstrates inexhaustibility. Furthermore, the wheel of the dharma-realm, because all dharmas are practiced. Furthermore, the wheel of the true limit, because the prior and posterior limits are not limits. Furthermore, the wheel of suchness, because the self-nature of dharmas is without self-nature. The wheel of the unconditioned, because all doubts are definitively examined. Furthermore, the wheel of permanence, because of the gathering of the noble nature. Furthermore, the wheel of emptiness, because internal and external things are not seen. Furthermore, the wheel of signlessness, because all signs are not discriminated. Furthermore, the wheel of wishlessness, because all dharmas are not clung to. Furthermore, the wheel of the unconditioned, because all verbal expressions are empty and inexpressible." Such are all the meanings of the dharma-wheel as taught by the World-Honored One.

### Question 7: The Three Turnings and Twelve Aspects

The meaning is now explained. The dharma-wheel has three turnings with twelve aspects.

"This is the noble truth of suffering; this is the noble truth of the origin; this is the noble truth of cessation; this is the noble truth of the path to the cessation of suffering." This is the first turning.

"This noble truth of suffering should be known; this origin should be abandoned; this cessation should be realized; this path should be cultivated." This is the second turning.

"This noble truth of suffering has been known; this origin has been abandoned; this cessation has been realized; this path has been cultivated." This is the third turning.

Thus the three turnings are explained. Suffering-knowledge, origin-knowledge, cessation-knowledge, path-knowledge. The suffering truth has three turnings of knowledge; likewise the origin truth, cessation truth, and path truth each have three turnings of knowledge. Thus there are twelve aspects. Why? Because the different aspects of knowledge differ in the suffering truth (three turnings), the origin truth, the cessation truth, and the path truth (each three turnings). Thus there are twelve aspects.

What is called "suffering"? It refers to the five aggregates (*skandha*). The suffering-characteristic of the five aggregates is called "suffering." That suffering-characteristic is empty; penetrating this emptiness is called the noble truth of suffering-knowledge. The causes of the five aggregates, the tendencies of craving and the causes of views, are called "the origin." Not discriminating, not grasping, not touching the causes of craving and views: this is called the noble truth of origin-knowledge. When the five aggregates are ultimately and completely extinguished, the prior limit does not come, the posterior limit does not go, and the middle limit is unobtainable: this is called "cessation." Knowing it as such is called the noble truth of cessation-knowledge. When the path is attained, one relies on suffering-knowledge, origin-knowledge, and cessation-knowledge; their equal characteristic, their non-dual knowledge, is called the noble truth of the path to the cessation of suffering.

### Question 8: Reconciling "Turning" with Non-Arising

The meaning is now explained. That [teaching of non-arising] is spoken from the perspective of ultimate truth (*paramārthasatya*); this [teaching of turning] is spoken from the perspective of conventional truth (*saṃvṛtisatya*). Furthermore, this is spoken for this particular occasion; this is spoken as an antidote for those who have faith. Furthermore, this is spoken for beginning bodhisattvas; those who have attained the great grounds do not dispute this.

---

### Question 9: How the Buddha Sustains When Beings and Dharmas Are Empty

The meaning is now explained. The Buddha, out of great compassion, without grasping sentient beings and without grasping dharmas, constantly sustains sentient beings and dharmas and turns the dharma-wheel. Furthermore, the World-Honored One says in the Nāgarāja Sūtra: "It turns like empty space turns; this is called the turning of the dharma-wheel." Furthermore, this is the World-Honored One's skillful means: all dharmas are nameless, yet he designates them with names and teaches them. Therefore the verse says:

> All dharmas are nameless; / designating names, he teaches the Dharma.

The World-Honored One, by his nature, does not grasp sentient beings, yet he treats sentient beings and teaches them the Dharma. Though he does not grasp dharmas, he constantly and broadly teaches all dharmas. Furthermore, the Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra and the Vimalakīrtinirdeśa Sūtra say: "In order to know the ultimate truth, one teaches the conventional truth; thus there is no fault."

### Question 10: Why the Deer Park at Vārāṇasī

The meaning is now explained. The World-Honored One, in past lives, had already performed giving in sixty thousand billion *nayuta* assemblies at that place. He had already made offerings to sixty thousand billion *nayuta* buddhas at that place. Ninety-one billion buddhas had already turned the dharma-wheel at that place. That place has always been frequented by quiescent sages. Because of such great merits, the World-Honored One turned the dharma-wheel at that place. As the Vaipulya Sūtra says in verse:

> "In sixty thousand billion *nayuta* assemblies I gave;
> I made offerings to sixty thousand billion *nayuta* buddhas.
> Vārāṇasī is the supreme place, with supreme ancient sages;
> The foremost devas, nāgas, and others constantly praise the place of Dharma-teaching.
> Ninety-one billion [buddhas] in the past: I recall the supreme, unsurpassed [ones]
> Who in this wondrous grove turned the unsurpassed dharma-wheel.
> Here dwell *nayutas* of quiescent, supreme sages,
> Always in the Deer Park; hence it is called 'the Sage's Place.'
> In such a supreme grove, the unsurpassed dharma-wheel is turned."

### Question 11: Where the Buddha First Sat

The meaning is now explained. The World-Honored One sat at the Great Circular Hall, upon a lion-throne adorned with immeasurable pure, wondrous, and precious jewels, and turned the dharma-wheel. Where is this stated? The Vaipulya Sūtra says: "Bhikṣus! The earth-devas, knowing that the dharma-wheel would be turned at Vārāṇasī with great benefit, erected a Great Circular Hall, variously adorned, broad and magnificent, seven hundred *yojanas* in extent. The devas of the sky adorned it with canopies, banners, and pennants. Above in the sky, the desire-realm devas offered eighty-four thousand lion-thrones to the Tathāgata. Having offered them, each individually requested: 'May the Tathāgata sit upon my throne and turn the dharma-wheel!' Each deva saw the World-Honored One sitting upon the throne he had offered, turning the dharma-wheel. Thus the World-Honored One fulfilled the wishes of all the devas."

---

### Question 12: How Many Beings Attained the Path

The meaning is now explained. Kauṇḍinya and the others, five bhikṣus. Furthermore, sixty billion devas. Furthermore, eighty billion form-realm devas. Furthermore, eighty-four thousand billion humans. Where is this stated? The Vaipulya Sūtra says in verse:

> "Ājñāta-Kauṇḍinya and the rest, these five bhikṣus,
> Sixty billion devas all attained the pure Dharma-eye.
> Eighty billion form-realm devas purified the supreme Dharma-eye.
> Humans who purified the supreme Dharma-eye: eighty-four thousand billion."

---

### Question 13: The Two Kinds of Sustaining

In brief, sentient-being sustaining demonstrates the teaching about sentient beings; dharma sustaining demonstrates the teaching of the Dharma. Furthermore, sentient-being sustaining demonstrates knowledge of the eighty-four thousand types of sentient beings' mental dispositions; dharma sustaining demonstrates knowledge of the eighty-four thousand aggregates of Dharma that illuminate and greatly benefit [beings]. Furthermore, sentient-being sustaining demonstrates the equality of sentient beings; dharma sustaining demonstrates the equality of dharmas. Furthermore, both of these are demonstrated from the perspective of conventional truth.
