# 慈氏菩薩所說大乘緣生稻𦼮喻經
## The Mahāyāna Sūtra on Dependent Arising Using the Analogy of Rice Seedlings, Spoken by Maitreya Bodhisattva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0710

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Amoghavajra (不空).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling on Vulture Peak Mountain in the city of Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty monks and a host of great bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

At that time, the Elder Śāriputra went to the place where the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya was walking in meditation. The Bodhisattva Maitreya and Śāriputra sat down together on a flat rock. Then the Elder Śāriputra asked the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya: "Today the World-Honored One, observing the rice seedlings, told the monks this: 'You monks! Whoever sees dependent arising sees the Dharma. Whoever sees the Dharma sees the Buddha.' Having said this, the Bhagavān fell silent. What is the meaning of what the Tathāgata said in this sūtra? What is dependent arising? What is the Dharma? What is the Buddha? How does seeing dependent arising mean seeing the Dharma? How does seeing the Dharma mean seeing the Buddha?"

After these words were spoken, the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya said to Śāriputra: "The Bhagavān has always taught the monks this meaning: 'Whoever sees dependent arising sees the Dharma; whoever sees the Dharma sees the Buddha.' What is dependent arising? It is this: conditioned by ignorance there are volitional formations; conditioned by volitional formations there is consciousness; conditioned by consciousness there is name-and-form; conditioned by name-and-form there are the six sense-bases; conditioned by the six sense-bases there is contact; conditioned by contact there is feeling; conditioned by feeling there is craving; conditioned by craving there is grasping; conditioned by grasping there is becoming; conditioned by becoming there is birth; conditioned by birth there are old age and death. The Tathāgata taught this as dependent arising. What is the Dharma? The Noble Eightfold Path, as the Tathāgata taught it in brief, whose fruit is the attainment of nirvāṇa: this is called the Dharma. What is the Buddha? One who awakens to all dharmas, who realizes nirvāṇa through the eye of holy wisdom, who sees and practices the teachings that lead to bodhi: this is called the Buddha.

"How does one see dependent arising? The Tathāgata taught that this dependent arising is permanent, without a person, without a self, without a being, without a life-span, free from inversion, unborn, uncreated, unconditioned, without opposition, without obstruction. Seeing its own nature as quiescent: this is seeing the Dharma. If one sees such characteristics as permanence, without a person, without a self, without a being, without a life-span, free from inversion, unborn, without opposition, without obstruction: this is seeing the Dharma. From this point onward, one sees the Dharma-body and sees the Tathāgata, realizing right knowledge directly."

Śāriputra further asked: "What is the meaning of 'dependent arising'?"

Maitreya answered: "Having causes, having conditions, not being without causes and conditions: this is called dependent arising. Within this, the Tathāgata taught the characteristics of dependent arising in brief. Because of this cause, this fruit is produced. Whether tathāgatas appear in the world or do not appear, the nature of dharmas, the abiding of dharmas, the station of dharmas, the conformity to dependent arising, suchness, non-inverted suchness, non-different suchness, reality, non-difference, non-inversion, non-confusion: these are all just so.

"Furthermore, dependent arising arises from two kinds of causes. What are the two? First, dependent on inherent causes; second, dependent on conditions. The dharmas of dependent arising should be understood as twofold: external and internal. What is external dependent arising dependent on inherent causes? From a seed an sprout is born; from a sprout a leaf is born; from a leaf a branch is born; from a branch a stem is born; from a stem a trunk is born; from a trunk a flower is born; from a flower a fruit is born. If there is no seed, no sprout can come forth; and so on up to: without a flower, no fruit can come forth. Because there is a seed, a sprout is born; and so on up to: because there is a flower, fruit is born. The seed does not have the thought: 'I can produce a sprout.' The sprout does not have the thought: 'I was born from a seed.' And so on up to: the flower does not have the thought: 'I can produce fruit.' The fruit does not have the thought: 'I was born from a flower.' Yet because there is a seed, a sprout is born; and so on up to: because there is a flower, fruit is born. Thus should external dependent arising be understood as dependent on inherent causes.

"How is external dependent arising dependent on conditions? It is because the six elements combine that dependent arising is dependent on conditions. What are the six elements? Earth, water, fire, wind, space, and time combine in dependent arising, dependent on conditions. What is the earth element, and what are the water, fire, wind, space, and time elements? That which enables the seed to be supported is called the earth element. That which moistens the seed is called the water element. That which matures the seed is called the fire element. That which enables the seed to grow is called the wind element. That which enables the seed to be unobstructed is called the space element. That which causes the seed to change and develop is called the time element. If there are no conditions, a seed will not produce a sprout. If the earth element is not lacking, and the water, fire, wind, space, and time elements are not lacking, then when all combine together, the seed produces a sprout. The earth element does not have the thought: 'I can support the seed.' The water element does not have the thought: 'I can moisten the seed.' The fire element does not have the thought: 'I can mature the seed.' The wind element does not have the thought: 'I can enable the seed to grow.' The space element does not have the thought: 'I can enable the seed to be unobstructed.' The time element does not have the thought: 'I can cause the seed to change and develop.' The seed also does not have the thought: 'I produce a sprout from conditions.' Yet, given these conditions, the seed produces a sprout. The sprout is not self-made, not made by another, not made by both together, not made by Maheśvara, not made by temporal change, not born from an inherent nature, not dependent on an agent, and not produced without cause. Thus, because of the earth, water, fire, wind, space, and time elements combining, the seed produces a sprout.

"This external dependent arising should be understood through five characteristics: it is not permanent, not annihilated, not transferred, its cause is small but its fruit is great, and it continues in similar kind. How is it not permanent? Because the seed and the sprout are different. It is not the case that the seed itself is the sprout, nor is it by the destruction of the seed that the sprout comes to be. Rather, as the seed deteriorates, the sprout is born. Because the seed deteriorates and the sprout is born, it is not permanent. How is it not annihilated? The seed does not first perish and then the sprout arise; nor does the sprout arise without the seed's dissolution. In this way, the seed dissolves and in combination the sprout arises: this is called not being annihilated. As for not being transferred: the seed and the sprout are different. As for the cause being small and the fruit great: the seed is small, but the fruit is plentiful. As for continuing in similar kind: whatever type of seed is planted, that same type of fruit is harvested. Thus should the five characteristics of external dependent arising be understood.

"How does internal dependent arising come about? It arises from two kinds of conditions. What are the two kinds? First, dependent on inherent causes; second, dependent on conditions. How is internal dependent arising dependent on inherent causes? Conditioned by ignorance there are volitional formations, and so on up to: conditioned by birth there are old age and death. If there is no ignorance, then there are no volitional formations. Yet where there is ignorance, there are volitional formations, and so on up to: where there is birth, there are old age and death. Ignorance does not have the thought: 'I can produce volitional formations.' Volitional formations do not have the thought: 'I was born from ignorance.' And so on up to: birth does not have the thought: 'I can produce old age and death.' Yet where there is ignorance, volitional formations arise, and so on up to: where there is birth, old age and death arise. Thus should internal dependent arising dependent on inherent causes be understood.

"How is internal dependent arising dependent on conditions? It arises from the combination of six elements. What are the six elements that combine? Earth, water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness combine in dependent arising, dependent on conditions. What is the earth element? That which enables the body to aggregate as a solid substance is called the earth element. What is the water element? That which enables the body to cohere is called the water element. What is the fire element? That which enables the digestion of food and drink within the body is called the fire element. What is the wind element? That which enables the inhalation and exhalation within the body is called the wind element. What is the space element? That which produces the cavities and openings within the body is called the space element. What is the consciousness element? That which turns name-and-form like a bundle of reeds, the five sense-consciousnesses together with the defiled mental consciousness, is called the consciousness element. If the six elements are absent, the body cannot be formed. If the internal earth element is not lacking, and the water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness elements are not lacking, then when all combine together, the body is produced. The earth element does not think: 'I enable the body to aggregate as a solid substance.' The water element does not think: 'I enable the body to cohere.' The fire element does not think: 'I enable the digestion of food and drink within the body.' The wind element does not think: 'I enable the inhalation and exhalation within the body.' The space element does not think: 'I produce the cavities and openings within the body.' The consciousness element also does not think: 'I turn name-and-form like a bundle of reeds.' The body itself also does not think: 'I have been produced by conditions.' Yet, given these conditions, the body is produced. This earth element is without self, without person, without life, without life-span, without mind-born being, without youth, without woman, without man, without non-male-non-female, without 'I,' and without anything else. The water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness elements are likewise without self, without person, without life, without life-span, without mind-born being, without youth, without woman, without man, without non-male-non-female, without 'I,' and without anything else.

"What is ignorance? With regard to these six elements, giving rise to a perception of unity, a perception of combination, a perception of permanence, a perception of solidity, a perception of constancy, a perception of pleasure, a perception of stillness, a perception of a sentient being, a perception of life, a perception of life-span, a perception of a mind-born being, a perception of a youth, a perception of 'I' as an agent: the arising of such various forms of unknowing is called ignorance. In the domain of ignorance, craving, aversion, and delusion arise. From craving, aversion, and delusion, volitional formations arise. With regard to those objects, what is designated is consciousness. With that consciousness, four aggregates arise. The sense-bases on which name-and-form depends constitute the six sense-bases. The conjunction of three things is called contact. Contact gives rise to feeling. Being attached to feeling gives rise to craving. When craving becomes extensive, it is called grasping. Grasping in turn produces becoming. Karmic becoming, acting as cause, produces the aggregates. When the aggregates mature, it is called old age. When the aggregates disintegrate, it is called death. Being confused and attached to craving, being afflicted: this is called grief. Recalling past events with mournful words and lamentation is called weeping. What is associated with the five sense-consciousnesses is called suffering. Displeasure of mind is called sorrow. Accompanying afflictions are called distress. Delusion is called ignorance; creating is called volitional formations; discerning is called consciousness; mutual support is called name-and-form; being the locus of dependence is called the six sense-bases; contacting objects is called contact; experiencing is called feeling; thirsting is called craving; clinging is called grasping; grasping producing further existence is called becoming; being able to produce is called birth; the maturing of faculties is called old age; disintegration is called death; grief is called sorrow; sighing is called lamentation; displeasure of mind is called mental anguish; physical oppression is called suffering; things not going as wished is called distress. Not cultivating the true practice is called wrong practice; unknowing is called ignorance. Because there is ignorance, one creates various formations: meritorious approach-formations, non-meritorious approach-formations, and imperturbable approach-formations. Because one produces meritorious and non-meritorious approach-formations, there arises consciousness; therefore it is said that ignorance conditions volitional formations. Because one produces non-meritorious approach-formations, there is also consciousness; therefore it is said that volitional formations condition consciousness. Because one produces imperturbable approach-formations, there is also consciousness; therefore it is said that consciousness conditions name-and-form. When name-and-form increases and develops, the six sense-gates are produced; therefore it is said that name-and-form conditions the six sense-bases. When the body of the six sense-bases functions, contact arises; therefore it is said that the six sense-bases condition contact. Corresponding types of contact produce corresponding types of feeling; therefore contact conditions feeling. Attachment and delight in the distinctions of feeling give rise to craving; therefore feeling conditions craving. Through attachment to the pleasures of craving, craving is not relinquished, and one repeatedly seeks and desires; therefore craving conditions grasping. Through such seeking, further becoming arises, producing karma of body, speech, and mind; therefore grasping conditions becoming. From karma the five aggregates turn; this is called becoming conditioning birth. From birth the aggregates mature, decay, and perish; this is called birth conditioning old age and death. Thus are the twelve links of dependent arising. Mutually serving as causes, they are not permanent, not fabricated, not deliberate, and there is no arising, no exhaustion, no separation from desire, and no cessation; they flow in an unbroken stream from beginningless time, revolving like the swift current of a river.

"Even though dependent arising flows in an unbroken stream like the swift current of a river, these twelve branches of dependent arising are turned by the combination of four factors. What are the four? They are: ignorance, craving, karma, and consciousness. Consciousness serves as the seed by its own nature; karma serves as the field by its own nature; ignorance and craving serve as the afflictions by their own nature. Karma and afflictions produce the seed of consciousness. In this way, karma and consciousness create the field for the seed; craving and consciousness moisten the seed; ignorance and consciousness cause the seed to germinate. Karma does not have the thought: 'I provide a field for the seed of consciousness.' Craving does not have the thought: 'I moisten the seed of consciousness.' Ignorance does not have the thought: 'I cause the seed of consciousness to germinate.' The seed of consciousness also does not have the thought: 'I am produced from conditions.' Yet truly, the seed of consciousness is established by karma and afflictions, moistened by craving, and covered with the soil of ignorance, giving rise to the sprout of name-and-form. The sprout of name-and-form is not self-made, not made by another, not made by both together, not made by Maheśvara, not made by temporal change, not born from an inherent nature, not dependent on an inherent cause, and not produced without cause. Yet, from the union of father and mother at the appropriate time, and from the conjunction of other appropriate conditions, the seed of consciousness gives rise to the sprout of name-and-form in the mother's womb in an unbroken continuum.

"Without a master, without a self, without a bestower, in dharmas that are like illusions, when causes and conditions are not lacking, eye-consciousness arises from five conditions. What are the five? The eye conditioned by form, light, space, and from these the arising of attention. The eye serves as the support for consciousness, form serves as the object, light serves to illuminate, space serves to be unobstructed, and from these the arising of attention serves as deliberation. If conditions are lacking, eye-consciousness does not arise. If the internal eye-base is lacking, or if form, light, space, or attention is lacking, then eye-consciousness does not arise. When these five conditions are not lacking, and all combine, eye-consciousness can arise. The eye does not have the thought: 'I serve as the support for eye-consciousness.' Form does not have the thought: 'I serve as the object for eye-consciousness.' Light does not have the thought: 'I serve as the illuminating condition for eye-consciousness.' Space does not have the thought: 'I serve as the unobstructed condition for eye-consciousness.' The arising of attention does not have the thought: 'I serve as deliberation for eye-consciousness.' Eye-consciousness also does not have the thought: 'I am produced from many conditions.' Yet, given many conditions, eye-consciousness arises. The remaining four sense-faculties should be understood in the same way.

"In truth, no dharma transfers from this world to another. Yet there is the manifestation of karmic retribution, because causes and conditions are not lacking. It is like a clear mirror that reflects the image of a face. The face-image does not transfer into the mirror, yet the image appears in the mirror because causes and conditions are not lacking. In the same way, it is not the case that something ceases here and goes to another place. There is the attraction of karmic results because causes and conditions are not lacking. It is like the disk of the moon, which is forty thousand yojanas from the earth, yet when there is even a small amount of water in a vessel, the image of the moon appears in it. The moon does not actually descend from there to appear in the small amount of water in the vessel, yet given the combination of conditions, the reflection appears. In the same way, nothing ceases here and is born in another place. There is the mutual attraction of karmic retribution because causes and conditions are not lacking. It is like fire, which does not arise without fuel; with fuel, fire arises. Karma and afflictions produce the seed of consciousness, and from its place of production, name-and-form sprout and continue. Thus, without a master, without a self, in dharmas without an owner, which are mutually conditions for one another, like illusions in their nature, when causes and conditions are not lacking, dependent arising proceeds.

"The five characteristics of internal dependent arising should be understood as follows: not permanent, not annihilated, not transferred, cause small but fruit great, and continuing in similar kind. How is it not permanent? The aggregates at death on this side are different from the aggregates at birth on that side. It is not the case that the dying aggregates themselves become the birth aggregates. Yet when the dying aggregates cease, the birth aggregates arise; therefore it is not permanent. How is it not annihilated? It is not the case that the dying aggregates first perish and then the birth aggregates arise; nor is it the case that without the dying aggregates perishing the birth aggregates arise. Rather, at the very time the dying aggregates cease, the intermediate-state aggregates arise, like a scale that neither dips nor rises; this is called not being annihilated. How is it not transferred? Before the arising of a different type, it is called not transferred. How is the cause small but the fruit great? In this body one performs a small amount of good or evil karma, but in the next life one receives an abundant good or evil retribution. How does it continue in similar kind? Just as in this present body one performs karma, so in the next life one receives its retribution. If one cultivates this dependent arising with the eye of right wisdom over a long period of time, seeing it as without a person, without a self, non-inverted, unborn, unceasing, uncreated, unconditioned, unobstructed, without an object, quiescent, without fear, not to be taken away, inexhaustible, like an illusion, empty and quiescent in its own nature, not solid, like a wound, like a boil, obstructed, impermanent in nature, suffering in nature, empty in nature, without self in nature, then one will not course in the past, thinking: 'Did I exist in the past? Who was I in the past? How did I exist in the past?' Nor will one course in the future, thinking: 'Will I exist in the future? Who will I be in the future? How will I exist in the future? Will I not exist in the future? Who says I will not exist in the future? How will I not exist in the future?' Nor will one course in the present, thinking: 'Do I exist now? Who says I exist now? How do I exist now? When this being dies here, where does it go?' All the various views of śramaṇas, brahmins, and worldly people, namely the fetters of self-view, the fetters of being-view, the fetters of life-span view, the fetters of all views, the fetters of hope and auspiciousness: when one accords with right view, at that time all these fetters are severed, and one attains omniscience. Like cutting down a palmyra tree, having the nature of nothingness, one enters into the ultimate meaning. Throughout all destinies, over a long period of time, one realizes non-arising and non-ceasing, attains the accomplishment of patience, and broadly performs boundless deeds for the benefit and happiness of sentient beings. If any good man or good woman, within this sūtra, even for a brief moment, carefully contemplates and examines the meaning of dependent arising, they can immediately extinguish the extremely heavy karmic obstructions accumulated from beginningless time, broadly accumulate merit, penetrate wisdom, permanently sever wrong views, and teach the Dharma without fear. O Elder Śāriputra! The Bhagavān bestows upon such good men and good women the prediction of supreme, perfect, great enlightenment (anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi)."

The Elder Śāriputra, together with the devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, and non-humans, having heard the Bodhisattva Maitreya speak this sūtra, were filled with great joy and delight. Rising from their seats, they prostrated at the feet of the Bodhisattva Maitreya, and joyfully put it into practice.
