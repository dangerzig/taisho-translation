# 佛說內身觀章句經
## Sūtra of Verses on Contemplation of the Inner Body, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0610

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

> Let all be singleminded in heart
> And listen to these fine instructive words.
> This is the Way through which the Buddha attained the path;
> Hear now this sūtra.
> That emptiness is not annihilation,
> And all formations are impermanent:
> That formations do not perish and decay,
> The Buddha has lectured and taught in the sūtras.
> Profound and subtle, hard to perceive,
> Not ordinary lines and sentences:
> One who penetrates them and can expound them
> Is therefore called a teacher.
> From the root, preserving the root,
> There is an accumulation of created dharmas.
> Through wisdom one removes and discards them:
> This is what the supreme ones have lectured and taught.
> From causes and conditions there is existence;
> All formations are ultimately without existence.
> There is also what was removed in past lives;
> Without knowledge, those formations continue.
> As one might wish to expound the body:
> The kinsman of the sun has lectured on it.
> Countless great calamities
> All return and flow into the body.
> Compiled and established as in the original text,
> Expounding the essential words of the assembly,
> In elegant five-character lines,
> With clear vows to exhort and encourage.
> With verses of complete stanzas,
> Paired syllables and balanced sounds:
> Like flowers and fruits mixed together,
> Hear now my instruction.

The body is not a person, not a life-force, not a man, not a being. Whether in its form or without charm, these things are all utterly absent there.

> The body is created and comes to be;
> Having come to be, it decays and perishes.
> It has no strength and all must end;
> Like foam that is trampled and crushed.
> Without strength, it is impermanent;
> Being impermanent, it is without happiness.
> Being impermanent, it is also suffering;
> Not self, the body is not the self.
> The body is not permanent, and it is suffering;
> It is not what should be taken as a body.
> If the form, charm, and self existed,
> The body would have something to possess.
> But since there is utterly no substance,
> There is both existence and perishing.
> The wise one considers beginning and end:
> What substance is there already possessed?
> Since there is no owner,
> One does not attain freedom of one's own accord.
> If one examines and reckons thus,
> What substance or self is there to possess?
> This substance and body are empty;
> That there is a substance or self to possess is false.
> It exists in dependence on causes and conditions;
> The body is not created by a departing mind,
> Nor created by a multitude of persons,
> Nor created by the body itself.
> There is utterly no creator of the body,
> Yet the body is not uncreated.
> Since the body has no creator,
> From that one may obtain the truth.

> It is not merely joints and bones.
> It did not come from a previous existence ready-made,
> Nor was the body created by heaven,
> Nor was it a city of the gods' creation.
> It is not without formations, not without a root,
> Not without cause and self-existent.
> This body has its origins;
> There are matters and there are things.
> There is a root and a source of arising.
> Gradually it comes into being:
> At root there is delusion and craving,
> Mind together with craving and the outflows.
> There are also bonds and fetters;
> Action in two classes constitutes the outflows.
> These are the root, as well as other things.
> The world and what is received arise from conditions.
> Through these, this body
> Gradually brings about existence.

> In the beginning there is a drop of semen;
> The semen gradually coagulates.
> Then it grows into pairs of limbs;
> From pairs it gradually thickens.
> In order come the four limbs,
> And then the head, the fifth.
> Various bones are accumulated;
> Through formations they come to completion.
> The head: nine bones form the skull.
> The bridge of the nose is two bones.
> The tooth-roots are thirty-two;
> The teeth are thirty-two bones.
> The neck, together with the ear-roots,
> The nose and upper palate,
> The heart, the throat, and the larynx:
> In total there are eighty bones.
> The jawbone and the neck have four;
> The gullet also has four bones.
> The left arm and the right arm
> Have in total fifty bones.
> As for the left side of the ribs,
> There should be thirteen;
> And the right side of the ribs likewise
> Should have thirteen ribs:
> These make forty-eight.
> Three groups of three are linked together;
> Two groups of two are linked together.
> The rest are not linked;
> The body thereby has its strength.
> Like bound reeds that have no strength,
> The spine has thirty-two vertebrae.
> The buttocks and the waist have three.
> As for the left thigh,
> There are twenty-five bones;
> The right thigh likewise
> Has twenty-five bones.
> The shoulder and thigh joints have four bones.
> In total there are three hundred and twenty.
> Expounding and naming them is the truth.
> The Buddha, the kinsman of the sun, has severed the lineage of rebirth.

> Those segments are all assembled together;
> The sense-faculties are sewn together.
> It is not a wound, yet it is wrapped up;
> Flesh and blood are smeared and plastered on,
> Like a wooden puppet worked with strings:
> Just as an illusionist creates an illusion,
> So too this skeleton of joints.
> Bound together with sinews,
> The bones are assembled thus
> And taken to be this physical form.
> The foolish are all attached to it;
> The wise are not attached.
> Raw hide conceals and covers it.
> The nine openings are great wounds,
> Leaking and oozing all around:
> An impure, rotting, stinking place.
> The mouth is like an opening
> Filled with various grains.
> Such is this body.

> And further with its various organs:
> Hair of the head, hair of the body, nails, and teeth,
> Dust, grime, and skin,
> Joints and marrow,
> In total sinews and veins,
> Stomach, heart, and spleen,
> Large intestine and small intestine,
> Liver, lungs, and kidneys,
> Fat, grease, and feces,
> Tears, saliva, and sweat,
> Nasal mucus, membranes, and blood,
> Cold, heat, subcutaneous fat, and urine,
> And further the membranes:
> All are sunk and submerged within.
> Like mud plastered on an old ox,
> Once formed, one does not even know it.

> The inner and outer of the body:
> The city, with bones for walls,
> Adorned and plastered with flesh and blood,
> To be destroyed by the enemy.
> Always both within and without,
> Those calamities number in the hundreds.
> Because they crave the flesh,
> From without come disasters too.
> All bring many troubles:
> This body decays, rots, and crumbles.
> It is like an ancient, crumbling city,
> Tended day and night.
> It crumbles and crumbles again,
> Like a pit, like an empty ruin.
> Always thieves dwell within it,
> Seizing it and deceiving the person.
> The body is a den of poisonous serpents,
> And those poisonous serpents are of extreme venom.
> Always they dwell within the body:
> Delight, anger, poison, deceit, and wrongdoing,
> And the great poison of dishonesty and discord.
> Even if one lives a full hundred years,
> Always in harmony and peace,
> One outburst of rage leaves no recompense,
> And in a moment peace is gone again.
> This body is disaster and calamity,
> With resentment and fear.
> Like a tiger roaming the empty wilds,
> There is fear and much terror.
> All thoughts look upward in supplication:
> It is the vessel of all suffering,
> And the site of every grievous matter.
> This body is where the master receives,
> Sunk in the dharma of suffering:
> The abode of every illness,
> Subject to the dharma of old age and death.
> The body increases love and affection;
> It suffers from cold and heat.
> If the wind is out of harmony,
> Illness arises accordingly.
> It ruins and destroys the sense-faculties;
> Swiftly, like thieves, it harms the person.
> Old age and death press and oppress,
> Like a pile of snow meeting fire:
> Swiftly it dissolves and is released.
> Such and countless more
> Are the body's many disasters.
> All that I have expounded
> Has not yet been fully complete.
> In summary, it is every suffering.
> The rotting body is not real.
> It is formed through many causes and conditions;
> A great delusion serves as its frame.

## Chapter on the Eleven Causes and Conditions

The Buddha said: "For a practitioner there are eleven causes and conditions whose elimination governs the Way and prevents one from falling into the evil paths. Those who do not know them are called ignorant of all things. First is great gatherings, meaning crowds of people. Second is eating too much, meaning all delicacies and also overeating. Third is too much activity, meaning too many undertakings. Fourth is too much noise, meaning too much talking. Fifth is too much sleep. Sixth is social gatherings, meaning during meditation. Seventh is habitual activity, meaning too many affairs. Eighth is loving the body. Ninth is frivolity, meaning improper speech. Tenth is greed, meaning excessive desire. Eleventh is not dwelling in a good place, meaning among evil people. A practitioner should sever these eleven causes and conditions, and the Way will be swiftly attained."
