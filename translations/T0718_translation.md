# 佛說分別緣生經
## Sūtra of Distinguishing Dependent Arising, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0718

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmadeva (法天)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was at the shore of Uruvilvā Pool, beside the Nairañjanā River, beneath the bodhi tree. Not long after attaining buddhahood, he was staying there alone. A thought arose in his mind: "The dharma of suffering in the world cannot be escaped by anyone, cannot be feared by anyone; it is definitively real. To contemplate it thus is of great benefit. The dharma of pleasure in the world is likewise: it cannot be escaped by anyone, cannot be wearied of by anyone; it is definitively real. To contemplate it thus is of great benefit."

He further reflected: "Among all the beings in the world, whether devas, humans, māras, brahmās, śramaṇas, or brahmins, none can understand this dharma. Yet if there is someone who can skillfully reflect, who awakens to suffering and pleasure, who thus comprehends that this is not the ultimate dharma, who constantly contemplates it and practices in accordance with the Dharma, that person will attain the complete dharmas of morality, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and the knowledge and vision of liberation. All the Buddhas of the past and future have fully awakened to the suffering and pleasure of the world, understanding each one, practicing in accordance with the Dharma, and by the power of their own practice attaining perfect awakening. Why? Because this dharma is unprecedented; there has been no one who could understand it. All the Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas of the past have each understood this dharma, practiced in accordance with the Dharma, and thereby attained perfect awakening. The Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas of the future will likewise each understand these dharmas of worldly suffering and pleasure, practice in accordance with the Dharma, and thereby perfect the fruit of the path."

At that time, the Lord of the Sahā World, Mahābrahmā Deva King, by the Buddha's spiritual power, knew the Buddha's thought. In the time it takes a strong person to bend and extend their arm, he left the Brahmā realm and came to the Buddha's place. Having paid his respects, he stood before the Buddha and said: "World-Honored One! What the Buddha has been reflecting upon is indeed so, indeed so. The suffering and pleasure of the world cannot be escaped by anyone; this is of great benefit. So too with the past and the future. Among devas, humans, māras, and brahmās, only the Buddha, the World-Honored One, can understand each one, whether increasing or decreasing, whether wholesome or unwholesome, and can fully discriminate the dharma of dependent arising. By the Buddha's power of wisdom, he truly understands."

The Buddha said: "Brahmā King! Indeed, indeed. The sentient beings of the world, regarding all dharmas, are without wisdom and without understanding; they cannot comprehend. They are covered and enclosed by the darkness of ignorance and delusion. This is called ignorance. From the condition of ignorance arise formations. Formations are of three kinds: body, speech, and mind. From the condition of formations arises consciousness. Consciousness is of six kinds: eye-consciousness, ear-consciousness, nose-consciousness, tongue-consciousness, body-consciousness, and mind-consciousness. From the condition of consciousness arise name and form. 'Name' refers to what is apart from form. Name is of four kinds: feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. 'Form' refers to the four great elements; all form dharmas arise from the four great elements. These two, the form aggregate and the name aggregate, are called name-and-form. From the condition of name-and-form arise the six sense bases. The internal six sense bases are of six kinds: the eye base, the ear base, the nose base, the tongue base, the body base, and the mind base. From the condition of the six sense bases arises contact. Contact is of six kinds: eye-contact, ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, and mind-contact. From the condition of contact arises feeling. Feeling is of three kinds: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling. From the condition of feeling arises craving. Craving is of three kinds: craving for the desire realm, craving for the form realm, and craving for the formless realm. From the condition of craving arises grasping. Grasping is of four kinds: grasping at desires, grasping at views, grasping at rules and vows, and grasping at a doctrine of self. From the condition of grasping arises becoming. Becoming is of three kinds: becoming in the desire realm, becoming in the form realm, and becoming in the formless realm. From the condition of becoming there is the dharma of birth. Birth means that within the realm of sentient beings, the aggregates arise in various and distinct ways, arising differently and ceasing differently, constantly changing. Based on birth, there are aggregates, elements, and sense bases, extending to the life faculty and other dharmas; this is called birth. From the condition of birth there are old age and death. Old age means that the mind and consciousness become dim and confused, the hair turns white and the face becomes wrinkled, strength and vigor decline, one breathes with difficulty, the body becomes weak and frail, and all the faculties deteriorate and decay; this is called old age. What is the characteristic of death? Death means that sentient beings in the various realms and destinies all return to impermanence; the span of their life reaches its end; they relinquish warmth and contact; when the life faculty has ceased, the aggregates are also relinquished, and the four great elements disperse; this is called death. As explained above, old age and death are these two dharmas.

"What has been explained is called the dharma of distinguishing dependent arising. If sentient beings can understand it, they will attain the complete fivefold Dharma-body."

At that time the Brahmā King, having heard the Buddha's teaching on the dharma of dependent arising, bowed to the Buddha, withdrew, and returned to the Brahmā realm.
