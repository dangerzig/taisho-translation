# 佛說鴦掘摩經
## Sūtra of Aṅgulimāla Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0118

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus. In the city of Śrāvastī there was a certain brahmin who was broadly learned in the three Vedas without any doubt or obstruction, and fully versed in the five classics, able to answer any question posed to him. He excelled in his teaching hall, and none failed to look up to him with respect. The elders of the country consulted him, and the multitude of scholars regarded him as their patriarch. His disciples were numerous, numbering five hundred. His foremost student was named Aṅgulimāla. He was of imposing bearing, fierce and strong, his strength surpassing that of mighty warriors. With his hands he could catch birds in flight, and on foot he could outrun galloping horses. He was intelligent, eloquent, and of a gentle and refined disposition, calm, sharp-witted, and thoroughly capable, without any hesitation. His appearance was the finest, and his teacher regarded him with special esteem.

The teacher's wife, admiring him and watching for when her husband was away, went to Aṅgulimāla and said to him: "Seeing your fine countenance, you have a most distinguished bearing. Considering our ages, they are not so far apart. Would you not share in pleasure and delight with me?"

Aṅgulimāla, hearing this, was alarmed and terrified, his hair standing on end. He knelt and replied: "Madam, you are like a mother to me, and the teacher is like a father. That you would lower yourself to make such a request, I dare not accept. My heart cannot consent to this; it is most contrary to the Dharma."

The teacher's wife said: "To give food to the hungry and water to the thirsty, what is contrary to the Dharma in that? To give warm clothing to those who are cold and cool relief to those who are hot, what is contrary to the Dharma in that? To cover the naked and rescue those in danger, what is contrary to the Dharma in that?"

Aṅgulimāla replied: "To rush to those in distress and give generous relief to the destitute, there is truly nothing contrary to the Dharma in that. But madam, you are a mother, cherished by the teacher. To indulge in lust and cling to passion, to transgress in such an improper way, would be like a snake whose body harbors poison within fine clothes."

When the teacher's wife heard this, she was filled with shame and resentment. She went back and deliberately disheveled herself, tearing her garments, smeared her face with turmeric, feigned distress, and lay down in apparent anguish. When her husband returned from his outing, he asked: "What has happened? What misfortune? Who has harassed you?"

His wife made a false accusation: "That student you always praise as intelligent, gentle, chaste, and flawless in conduct: while you were away this morning, he came and seized me, attempting to commit a vile outrage. I did not comply, and he assaulted and humiliated me, throwing me to the ground. Because of this disgrace I cannot get up."

Hearing this, the teacher was indignant and furiously angry. He wanted to inflict punishment and chastise this violent crime, but he reflected that his student was so powerful that he could not be subdued by force. Then he thought to remain silent, but deeply considered how this indecency had defiled his household and overturned proper order. Wavering back and forth, what was he to do? At last he sighed with distress and said: "I shall subtly alter my usual teaching and give him a perverse instruction, commanding him to kill people up to a hundred, stringing one finger from each on a garland for his forehead. The crime of killing is the greatest of all crimes! Without inflicting direct punishment, he will certainly meet his own destruction. In this life he will face ruin, and after death he will fall into hell. I cannot simply let him go, for that would only let the transgression grow worse."

Thereupon the teacher summoned Aṅgulimāla and told him: "Your intelligence and learning are thorough and complete. You have entered the inner sanctum and excelled beyond all others. There is only one practice you have not yet carried out."

Aṅgulimāla stepped forward and said: "I wish to hear your instruction."

The teacher said: "If you wish to swiftly attain completion, you should take up a sharp sword. At dawn, at the crossroads, personally kill a hundred people. Take one finger from each as an adornment. By midday, let the hundred fingers be complete. If you diligently follow this, then your path of virtue will be perfected." He then gave him a sword.

Aṅgulimāla received the sword and, hearing the instruction, was shocked and terrified, his heart filled with anguish and sorrow. "If I disobey the teacher's command, I am not a filial disciple. But if I obey and carry it out, I fear I will fall into wrongdoing." Receiving the sword, he withdrew, weeping as he said: "Purifying and cultivating the holy life is the way of the brahmin. Filially serving one's parents is the way of the brahmin. Cultivating all manner of good is the way of the brahmin. Not going astray but returning to what is right is the way of the brahmin. Being gentle and benevolent is the way of the brahmin. Extending great loving-kindness equally in the four directions is the way of the brahmin. Attaining the five supernatural powers is the way of the brahmin. Ascending to the Brahmā heaven is the way of the brahmin. Now to violently cut down and kill, that is contrary to the Dharma and contrary to principle. Pacing back and forth in distress and anguish, what am I to do?"

He then went to a tree at the side of the crossroads, grieving with furious agitation. Evil spirits aided the calamity, confounding and disordering his mind. With eyes blazing and roaring, he looked about in every direction like a demonic lion, like a tiger or a wolf. He leapt and charged about, his appearance terrifying. Travelers approaching from every direction, all heading toward the city, he struck down with his long sword, killing many. None did not scatter in terror; whoever encountered him had no escape. Those coming and going were unaware of what awaited them. Countless people cried out in anguish and entered the royal palace to report: "There is a rebel outlaw blocking the main road, harming many people! We beseech Your Majesty to eliminate this threat for the people!"

At that time, the bhikṣus entered the city to collect alms and saw the people reporting in such terror. After collecting alms and finishing their meal, they went to where the Buddha was, bowed their heads at his feet, and said to the World-Honored One: "We saw the people of the country going to the palace gate to report a great outlaw named Aṅgulimāla, who holds a sharp sword and has endangered many. His body and hands are stained with blood, and the roads are deserted."

Then the World-Honored One told the bhikṣus: "You all stay here. I shall go and save him." The Buddha rose from his seat and went straight to that place. On the road he met herdsmen and people carrying loads and driving carts. Farmers and village folk said to the World-Honored One: "Great Sage, wherever you are heading, do not take this road! Ahead there is a rebel outlaw who has cut off the four crossroads. The slaughter is terrible. Please change your route, and moreover you are walking alone without any attendants."

The World-Honored One said: "Even if all three realms were filled with bandits, I would take no notice of them. How much less one bandit?"

Aṅgulimāla's mother, wondering why her son had not returned, and knowing that the time for eating had come, feared he must be hungry. She brought food and left the city to feed him. As the day was approaching midday and the hundred fingers were not yet complete, Aṅgulimāla feared the sun would pass its zenith and his task would remain unfinished. He was about to turn and kill his own mother to make up the number. The Buddha thought: "If Aṅgulimāla kills his mother, his downward path will not stop midway, and his offenses will be beyond rescue." The Buddha then swiftly appeared and stood before him.

At that moment, Aṅgulimāla saw the Buddha and abandoned his mother. With the stride of a lion he went to meet the World-Honored One, thinking to himself: "When ten people or a hundred people see me charging, they scatter and dare not face me. I have always exerted my might and done as I pleased unchallenged. Yet here is this śramaṇa coming alone! Now I shall surely take his life." He then took up his sword and rushed toward the Buddha, but could not advance. He exhausted his strength running but still could not reach him. Then he thought: "I have leapt across rivers and freed myself from every bond. I have charged with valor and never met my match. Heavy gates and solid fortresses I have always opened. Yet this śramaṇa walks slowly, barely moving, and I cannot catch up. Though I exhaust all my might, I can never draw near."

Aṅgulimāla called to the Buddha: "Śramaṇa, stop!"

The Buddha told the outlaw: "I stopped long ago. It is you who have not yet stopped."

Then Aṅgulimāla spoke these verses from afar:

> "What do you mean, O silent sage, saying you have already stopped?
> Where does this statement lead? Do you say that I have not stopped?
> How is it that you, the Buddha, stand still, saying your body does not move,
> Yet say that I am thus? Please explain the meaning of this."

Thereupon the World-Honored One spoke these verses in reply to Aṅgulimāla:

> "Aṅgulimāla, hear how the Buddha has stopped: the World-Honored One has removed your faults.
> You run with thoughts devoid of wisdom; I am settled, for you have not stopped.
> I dwell peacefully in the three liberations, delighting in the Dharma and cultivating the holy life.
> You alone are driven by deluded thoughts; harboring harm, you have not yet stopped."
> The Great Sage of boundless wisdom praised stillness at the crossroads.
> Hearing the teaching on his offenses, Aṅgulimāla listened and took in the meaning of the Dharma.

Thereupon Aṅgulimāla's mind was immediately awakened. He cast down his sword, bowed his head, and threw himself upon the ground: "I beseech the World-Honored One to forgive my delusion! I committed harm and collected fingers, yet I wished to see the path. By the grace of your compassionate teaching, I beg you to pardon my offenses. Have pity and receive me, allowing me to go forth and receive the complete precepts."

The Buddha then admitted him, and he immediately became a śramaṇa.

At that time, the World-Honored One, majestic in his awesome spiritual power and radiant with the light of wisdom, sat cross-legged, with the worthy Aṅgulimāla attending at his side, and returned to the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. Aṅgulimāla, transformed by the teaching and trusted by the Blessed One, was also embraced and supported by the senior disciples. That son of good family shaved off his hair and beard, donned the Dharma robes, and with household faith renounced the household life for the path. He fully and ultimately completed the unsurpassed holy life, attained the six supernatural powers, and realized that birth and death were exhausted. Proclaiming his pure virtue, he declared that what was to be done had been done. Understanding the root of name and form, he immediately attained arhatship.

At that time, King Prasenajit, with his four divisions of elephant, horse, infantry, and cavalry, set out in full array to campaign against the vile outlaw. His body was weary and covered with dust. He went to where the Buddha was and bowed his head at the Buddha's feet. The Buddha asked the king: "From where have you come, with your body covered in dust?"

The king said to the Buddha: "Indeed, World-Honored One! There is a great outlaw named Aṅgulimāla, fierce and violent, harboring harm, who has cut off the four crossroads and wielded a sharp blade, wounding and killing the people. Therefore I have marshaled the four divisions of my forces and set out to hunt him down."

At that time, Aṅgulimāla was in the assembly, not far from the Buddha. The Buddha told the king: "Aṅgulimāla is here. He has already shaved off his hair and beard and is now a bhikṣu. What would you do about that?"

The king said to the Buddha: "Since he has set his resolve upon the path, there is nothing further to be done. I shall provide him with robes, food, bedding, and medicine for illness for the rest of his life."

He then asked the World-Honored One: "Indeed, Great Sage, can a man of such fierce and violent wrongdoing truly attain the path and practice the meaning of stillness? Where is he now?"

The Buddha told the king: "He is sitting nearby."

The king saw him from a distance, and his heart was immediately filled with fear, his hair standing on end. The Buddha said: "Great king! Do not be afraid, do not be alarmed! He is now benevolent and worthy, with no more thoughts of wrongdoing."

The king approached and paid his respects, saying: "Worthy one! Are you Aṅgulimāla?"

He answered: "I am."

The king asked: "What is your family name?"

He said: "Gagga."

The king asked: "What does Gagga mean?"

He said: "It is my father's original surname."

The king said: "Worthy son of Gagga, accept my offerings: robes, food, bedding, and medicine for illness, each for the rest of your life." He then made the offerings. The king, having received permission, bowed his head in farewell and praised the World-Honored One: "He can tame the untamed, he can perfect the imperfect. Dwelling peacefully and extending great compassion, there is no one he does not guide to the path. He has subdued this dangerous outlaw and brought him into the assembly of the Dharma, and has also enabled the people to attain this peace and composure. My country has many affairs, and I wish to take my leave."

The Buddha said: "You may go. Follow what you uphold."

The king bowed at the Buddha's feet and returned to his palace.

At that time, the worthy Aṅgulimāla, dwelling in seclusion and wearing patched robes, at dawn took up his bowl and entered the city of Śrāvastī to collect alms everywhere. He saw a certain household where a pregnant woman, her months complete, was having difficulty in labor and was taking refuge in him. She asked Aṅgulimāla: "Where are you going? Please grant me your aid."

Aṅgulimāla received his offerings, left the city, finished his meal, washed, put away his bowl, and sat alone in respectful meditation. He then went to the Buddha, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, and said to the World-Honored One: "This morning at dawn I put on my robes, took up my bowl, and entered the city to collect alms. I saw a woman about to give birth whose labor was difficult. She was frightened and begged for help."

The Buddha told Aṅgulimāla: "Go quickly and say to that woman: 'As Aṅgulimāla speaks truthfully and without falsehood: since I was born, I have never killed a living being. If this is truly so, may you, sister, give birth safely and without distress.'"

Aṅgulimāla said to the Buddha: "I have committed offenses beyond counting. I killed ninety-nine people, lacking only one to make a hundred. If I were to make this declaration, would it not be speaking with a double tongue?"

The World-Honored One said: "Your former life was a different existence; your present life is not the same. This statement is therefore truthful and is not a falsehood. Use it in this way to rescue that woman from her plight."

He then respectfully followed the holy instruction and went to where the woman was. As the Buddha had instructed, he said: "As I speak truthfully and without falsehood: since I was born, I have never killed a living being. If this is truly so, may you, elder sister, be safe and at peace in your delivery." Before he had even finished speaking, the woman gave birth smoothly, and the child was also safe.

At that time, when Aṅgulimāla entered the city of Śrāvastī, a group of small boys saw him collecting alms. Some threw tiles and stones at him, some shot arrows at him, some struck him with knives, and some beat him with sticks. The worthy Aṅgulimāla, his head broken and his body injured, his robes torn to shreds, returned to where the Buddha was, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, and before the Buddha spoke these verses:

> "I was formerly a bandit; 'Finger-Garland' was my name, known everywhere.
> The great flood has now dried up, and I take refuge in the Perfectly Awakened One.
> Through this I have perfected patience, and the Buddha's teaching has transformed me.
> I hear the sūtras always at the proper time; therefore I am free from stumbling.
> Now I have taken refuge in the Buddha and received the precepts of the true Dharma.
> Having attained the three penetrations, I follow the teaching of all the Buddhas.
> Formerly I was violent, harboring fierce poison, and harmed the lives of many beings.
> Though in the past I endangered many, now my name is 'Harmless.'
> The offenses I committed with body and mouth, the mind that harbored killing and harm:
> Since I no longer endanger others, I have never again encountered calamity.
> Furthermore, there is no more past. Upholding this Dharma, I dwell in stillness.
> Though I should bear the name of one fierce and violent, through self-discipline I have become benevolent and worthy.
> With just one act of taming and composure, like a hook that tames elephants,
> The Tathāgata has perfected me, without sword and without staff.
> One who was formerly heedless, but afterward is able to restrain himself:
> He illuminates the world, like the sun emerging from clouds.
> Even if one has committed many evils, if one does not cut off the many good virtues,
> He illuminates the world, like clouds dispersing to reveal the sun.
> If a newly ordained bhikṣu diligently cultivates the Buddha's teaching,
> His light illuminates the world, like the moon when it is full.
> One who has committed many offenses will head toward the evil destinies,
> But no longer finding hardship difficult, taking food without attachment,
> Neither seeking for life nor having ever found virtue in death,
> Only awaiting the time and the day, the mind always set on concentration.
> Thus has Aṅgulimāla already attained arhatship.
> Before the World-Honored One, the Buddha, he himself spoke these verses."

The Buddha spoke thus. The worthy Aṅgulimāla and the assembly of bhikṣus, having heard the sūtra, joyfully practiced in accordance.
