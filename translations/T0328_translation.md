# 佛說須賴經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on Sulai

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0328

Translated from the Chinese. Cao Wei dynasty, translated by Bái Yán (白延)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus and five thousand bodhisattvas.

At that time, in the city of Śrāvastī there was an extremely poor man named Sulai. He had obtained a firm and unshakable resolve. He had faith in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha, and had taken refuge in the Three Honored Ones. He upheld the pure precepts and cultivated the ten wholesome actions. He possessed the mind of the four immeasurables and worked tirelessly to aid others. His inner nature was pure, regarding suffering and happiness as equal. He wholeheartedly sought the Buddha's unsurpassed great Way, reflecting on his practice and reciting day and night. Through skillful means he guided and benefited all people and creatures, content in poverty and guarding himself, taking the Dharma as his joy.

At this time, Śakra, lord of the devas, with his divine eye saw Sulai's extraordinary merit. What Sulai had learned never confused him. He was broadly conversant with all the sūtras, had no deviant conduct, and was composed whether sitting or standing. In walking, standing, lying down, and waking, he never lost proper deportment. He had few desires, was easy to satisfy, had no craving for gain, was straightforward and spoke skillfully, his words trustworthy and unembellished. He fasted and lived frugally, ate moderately and wore coarse garments, using tree leaves as vessels and straw as his mat. He stored nothing and had no possessions. The king and all the people without exception loved and respected him. Day and night he would visit the Buddha three times to inquire about the Dharma. The Buddha, knowing his mind, would always grant him an audience. Whenever Sulai went to see the Buddha, hundreds of people constantly accompanied him. Wherever he went, whether resting, rising, or walking, devas and humans guarded him. Śakra thought to himself: "This man's precept-practice is perfectly complete. I fear he will take my position. I should descend and test him to determine what path he truly seeks."

Śakra then conjured several people to kick and curse Sulai, speaking without restraint. They also threw stones at him and beat him with sticks. Sulai, with the power of patient endurance and compassionate humaneness, neither grew angry nor resentful. Śakra then conjured several people who said to Sulai: "Sir, we have truly been watching you. If you wish, we shall kill those men for you. What say you?"

Sulai answered: "What you propose is not a wholesome dharma. Even if those people were to cut my body with knives, I would bear no grudge, let alone for mere blows and curses. Why is this? Wrongdoing and merit have two fruits: those who plant merit are born in the heavens above; those who commit wrongs fall into the evil destinies. Therefore, one should not bear grudges, how much less wish to harm others!"

At this, Sulai spoke a verse:

> "Those who plant evil seeds
> thereby fall among the guilty.
> Those who plant good roots
> will surely reap the fruit of merit.
> Perceive evil with right contemplation;
> one should discern with wisdom.
> Evil seeds are not good roots;
> planting virtue yields no evil fruit.
> Guarding the three and being free from evil,
> body, speech, and mind always good:
> the superior person endures without resentment;
> the wise one accepts without transgressing.
> Purifying the three to become clean,
> body, speech, and mind without blemish:
> encourage the practice of merit and virtue,
> and fulfilling one's vows, one shall always be at peace."

The conjured people were unable to make the poor man of the land, Sulai, harbor even the slightest harmful thought, and so they withdrew. Śakra then placed gold and silver before him and had the conjured people say to Sulai: "Sir, take this treasure. You can use it freely to give alms and create merit. You can also have fine clothes and good food. How can you bear such poverty?"

Sulai said: "My own past deeds are such that I should bear this poverty and suffering. If I greedily take this treasure, the suffering to come will be far worse."

The conjured people said: "Just enjoy yourself and be happy for the rest of your life. How can you know what comes after?"

Sulai answered: "These are not the words of wisdom. One who, within a single lifetime, does not think ahead will afterward receive great punishment, and this is what the wise fear. To greedily acquire hoarded wealth and take what is not one's own is a crime equal to theft, and the wise are ashamed of it. To love one's body, calculate one's lifespan, and imagine one will never die, committing the offense of taking what is not given: the wise do not do this. The wise calculate that the body and life are impermanent and that the myriad things are not truly one's own. What they value is the Way alone, and so they have no craving or contention, but simply guard what is good."

He then spoke a verse:

> "Hoarding a billion treasures
> without giving, at death one will regret.
> The wise call this true poverty;
> one should recognize this supreme truth.
> Eating in moderation, not attached to flavors,
> sincere, trustworthy, and fond of giving,
> possessing wisdom and foresight: this is wealth.
> The Buddha says this is constant peace.
> To be able to stop and not do evil,
> guarding the Way, pure within and without,
> needing no precepts yet naturally restrained:
> to have transgressed is not worthy.
> One should accept what is bright as one's teacher;
> do not follow what fools praise.
> The praise of fools leads into darkness;
> a bright teacher brings one nearer to purity."

The conjured people were unable to make Sulai harbor any greedy thought, and so withdrew. Śakra then conjured again, carrying pearls worth several hundred million, and went to Sulai saying: "I have a legal dispute with someone, and the case has been brought before the king. I secretly ask you to serve as my witness. I offer you these pearls; please help me with a single word in my favor."

Sulai answered: "Why do you speak of this? This is what I fear most. I would never dare to deceive. Why? One who speaks falsely first deceives himself, next deceives heaven, and also deceives the Dharma. It makes one's mouth stink, one's words go unheeded, and one is often slandered. The mind is always haggard. Heaven takes no thought of such a person: one's complexion changes, merit fades, and one's good name is ruined. Such a person has been thwarted by the sages and worthies of past ages. Having lost the root of virtue, one produces all manner of evil, blocking the road of goodness and throwing oneself into the darkness of deviance. This in future lives invites calamity and punishment."

He then spoke a verse:

> "All deception produces evil fruit;
> it causes one's mouth to constantly stink.
> Having completely forgotten one's former words,
> one enters deviance with no right path.
> Deceiving oneself and also deceiving heaven,
> deceiving the Dharma is self-enmity.
> One is always suspected by all;
> what benefit has deception for anyone?
> Deception is the root of all evil;
> it cuts off one's wholesome conduct.
> Therefore it brings a mass of suffering;
> what benefit has false speech for anyone?
> Even if one were given all the gold, silver,
> and pearls that fill the world,
> what guardian of the Dharma's precepts
> would ever be deceived by treasure?"

The conjured people were unable to make Sulai speak falsely, and withdrew at once. Śakra returned and told his consort: "You should try taking my retinue of court maidens, along with several celestial musicians and serving women, to see Sulai and turn his mind, to corrupt his pure practice. Let us see whether he has any lust or not."

The heavenly queen received the order and at once set forth with the court maidens. In the stillness of the night, they came to where Sulai dwelt. From the air, they spoke in beguiling words: "Sir, please rise! Heaven has sent us to attend upon you. Our forms are beautiful, our garments bright, with necklaces, precious jeweled ornaments, and sandalwood fragrance. Neither old nor young, we are in the prime of youth, lovely, radiant, and fair. Will you not gaze upon us? It is your good fortune that brings us here to serve you."

Sulai looked up and answered: "You are all loved by hell-dwellers, animals, and hungry ghosts, but not delighted in by the devas and those of higher wisdom. All that you possess, I see as dream-images and fragrances, as the foam and bubbles of the sea. I see your forms as bone-frames, smeared with flesh, irrigated with blood, wound with sinew, wrapped in hide, covered with skin to conceal the filth and stain, like an artisan's puppet that deceives the eyes and ears. Your prime of youth, I see as impermanent, destined for decay and dissolution, subject to the law of separation. I have already awakened to wrongdoing and merit. I have not a hair's breadth of affection. You truly corrupt people's good minds and cannot establish people's intentions; you only defile pure practice. You yourselves are linked to stench and rot. Pursuing desire brings no joy but only causes suffering. You delude yourselves and fall into darkness, entering the three evil paths. Desire is not a wholesome root. Quarreling, terror, and anger, confusion, turbidity, and disorder all arise from desire. The madness of delusion brings calamity, and because of these forms one's body is ruined. Outwardly collecting, inwardly burning, one never sees the fruit of merit. Having lost one's original nature, one is born among the animals. Afterward one becomes an ox, horse, mule, donkey, camel, dog, pig, chicken, or goose: all caused by desire. One distances oneself from the sages and worthies, losing the Way of faith, precepts, learning, giving, and wisdom. Following desire for a single thought, not considering the afterlife: desire constantly corrupts the aspiration for the Way. How much more for everything else!"

He then spoke a verse:

> "Desire's filth is stench and rot, alone in pain, far from peace and joy.
> Throwing oneself into the guilt of hungry ghosts, heedless, one always follows evil.
> Without cause it is the root of chaos, far from the Dharma, going astray.
> Heedless, bringing reversal and confusion; when desire is strong, delusion only deepens.
> Craving and hatred destroy good intentions; deviant thoughts bring constant fear.
> Inwardly burning like an outward sore, sitting in desire one loses the root of faith.
> Already it is the root of unwholesomeness, like a female demon devouring the liver.
> Dwelling in decline, serving evil spirits: for desire one becomes like a madman.
> Lust makes one an ox, sheep, pig, donkey, horse, elephant, or camel,
> long enduring the suffering of animal form. Therefore the wise abandon desire.
> Abandoning faith, precepts, learning, and virtue, one casts oneself far into evil paths.
> For lust, people become nets of delusion. Stay far from women and quickly be free!
> Even if beings with forms as lovely as yours filled this entire Jambudvīpa,
> one whose mind stands firm like a mountain: what deva-spirit could topple it?"

At that time, the heavenly queen and the court maidens were unable to make the poor Sulai harbor any lustful thought. They returned to the heavenly palace and spoke a verse to Śakra:

> "This man's learning is deep, and he sees the truth.
> He knows the evil of women's wiles and speaks of it.
> He has already abandoned form and is free from desire.
> Where there is no wound, poison cannot enter."

At this, Śakra became more alarmed, his body-hair standing on end. He thought: "This man's practice is perfectly pure and he is not deviant. He will surely take my position." He then descended himself to Sulai and spoke a verse of inquiry:

> "Sulai, what do you seek,
> that your precept-purity is so heroic?
> Please say: do you wish for kingship,
> or to be the sun and moon, Śakra, or Brahmā?"

Sulai answered Śakra in verse:

> "Rulers, sun and moon, devas,
> Śakra and Brahmā: though honored,
> they are impermanent, all like illusions.
> Since they are not yet liberated, what brightness have they?
> What I desire and delight in is non-arising,
> free from the afflictions of old age and death.
> The boundary of hatred, love, enmity, and suffering:
> I vow to ferry all beings in the three realms across."

Śakra's mind opened and he was filled with great delight. He then spoke a verse praising Sulai:

> "Excellent! You who feel compassion for the world,
> may you quickly be delivered and have your wish fulfilled.
> You shall remove the defilements of the three ages;
> devas and humans will surely receive your grace."

Then Sulai was walking through the marketplace of the city of Śrāvastī, and right there in the market he found a precious jewel from a former kalpa, its value equal to all the world. He held it up in his hand and called out: "Now, whoever in the country of Śrāvastī is the most extremely poor, I give this jewel to them." At that time, in the country there were former elders and wealthy merchants, numbering several thousand, who immediately ran to Sulai and each spoke, saying: "We are destitute! Please have pity and help us." And countless other extremely poor people also rushed forward, begging for the jewel.

Sulai answered: "Sirs, you are not poor. In this city there is one who is extremely poor, and I shall go give this jewel to him."

The crowd asked: "Looking at this city, who is the most extremely poor?"

Sulai answered: "King Prasenajit is the poorest in the land."

The people said: "Do not speak such words! How could a king ever be poor? The treasures and rare goods in the royal treasury are beyond reckoning."

Sulai answered the crowd in verse:

> "Though one may heap up riches, desire and seeking are insatiable,
> like water flowing day and night. Such people are still poor.
> Greed grows without thought of suffering; deviant conduct without shame.
> From this life to the next: this is extreme poverty."

Then Sulai, together with the great multitude of people, went to the royal palace gate. At that time, King Prasenajit was in his main hall, having just arrested more than five hundred elders, all guilty of offenses. The king wished to make them produce large amounts of treasure to redeem their crimes. An attendant reported to the king that Sulai was outside. The king at once invited him in for an audience. Sulai entered and said to the king: "The other day I was walking through the marketplace of Śrāvastī and found this Bright Moon Pearl. I wished to give it to the poorest person. Looking throughout this land, none is more extremely poor than the king. I wish the great king to accept this treasure."

Hearing these words, the king had a look of shame and answered: "Sulai! Am I truly as poor as you?"

Sulai said: "The king is far poorer than I."

The king said: "Please explain your meaning."

Sulai, before the great assembly, spoke a verse for the king:

> "Wealth, craved for day and night, insatiable; as a ruler, creating harm, the pain burns later.
> Preserving oneself as if one would never die, not thinking of the future: this is extreme poverty, devoid of Dharma-practice.
> Always having a compassionate mind, not indolent, keeping far from sensuality, drawing near to the worthy, and knowing contentment,
> not fond of seeking much, not hoarding: this person is born wise, without enmity or evil.
> Holding rank but abandoning righteousness for wrongdoing, using wealth to intimidate the people and impose punishments,
> at ease yet forgetting danger, indulging desires: when one deceives others and dies in distress, regret is too late.
> Honest, trustworthy, pure, and firm in knowledge; moderate, knowing sufficiency, hearing and not forgetting;
> always knowing without fear, delighting in practice: this is what it means to not be poor, as the sages say.
> Already wealthy and honored yet not thinking of giving, thinking life can last forever yet destroyed in the present,
> greedy, turbid, confused, like a drunken elephant, mind blocked and unaware: this is what it means to be poor.
> Having faith in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha, practicing with respect, forgiving oneself, bringing peace to others, teaching well,
> not foolish, not angry, not self-indulgent, governing oneself with the Dharma: this is constant wealth.
> Fire burns grass and trees without knowing sufficiency; all streams flow to the sea without ever filling or resting;
> sun and moon tire not of illuminating the four regions: craving continues until old age and death, yet desire for more never ceases.
> Fire, though fiercely hot, does not burn long; wealth and honor are impermanent, like floating clouds.
> Therefore the wise desire neither heavenly nor kingly station. The fire of wisdom burns away evil, leaving no more smoke."

King Prasenajit said to Sulai: "Who can bear witness that I am poor and you are rich?"

Sulai answered: "Has the great king not heard? In this world there exists a Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed Worthy, Tamer of the Dharma, Teacher of Devas and Humans, known as the Buddha, the World-Honored One. What he has seen is true, and he can manifest and bear witness to the essential truth. He is not far from here, but nearby in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park."

The king said: "I am most fortunate to be able to see this Honored One."

Sulai said: "This sage-teacher can serve as witness."

The king said: "Please invite the Buddha to come. Whatever the Buddha determines, I shall accept as correct."

At this, Sulai spoke a verse:

> "The Buddha's wisdom sees all everywhere;
> no need for the great king's invitation.
> What my mind now contemplates,
> he has already seen and will surely come with compassion.
> His spiritual penetration is holy and undefiled;
> what the world creates, one receives hereafter.
> The Buddha has no uncertain wisdom;
> he will surely come. Let the king not doubt.
> He is always equal, without hatred or affection,
> feeling compassion for humans and non-humans alike.
> Even if far away in another land,
> simply recollect his Dharma-words.
> I wish the great king to have faith.
> The Buddha is the lord of compassion.
> If you turn your mind toward him with utmost sincerity,
> the World Hero will come before long.
> Set out fragrant flowers and treasures,
> pearl necklaces, silk canopies, and banners.
> Let the musicians tune the five sounds:
> make offerings to the Buddha, bright and splendid."

Sulai then joined his palms, bared his right shoulder, placed both knees on the ground, prostrated, and facing toward the Buddha from afar, spoke a verse:

> "The Buddha knows all people's minds
> and sees with illumination all who are utterly sincere.
> I beg you to hear my sincere intention
> and manifest your spiritual power before me."

At this, the earth shook greatly. The Buddha, with five hundred bhikṣus and two hundred bodhisattvas, appeared through transformation, rising from the ground into the king's hall. Śakra, Brahmā, the Four Heavenly Kings, and countless hundreds of thousands of devas all came following the Buddha. The king and all his officials and people, seeing the Buddha manifest his spiritual powers, were all awestruck. With increased reverence, they bowed at the Buddha's feet. Thousands of people, inspired by this reverence, aroused the aspiration to become bodhisattvas. Then the poor man of the land, Sulai, bowed before the Buddha's feet, stepped back, and addressed the Buddha: "I was walking through this city and found a Bright Moon golden pearl, whose value equals all of Jambudvīpa. I wished to give it to the poorest person. Looking throughout this land, the king alone is the most extremely poor. Why? His craving and desire are insatiable. Taxation and confiscation never cease; oppression and harassment never cease. The entire nation's people are exhausted, the weak are pressed into hard labor, the middle class is injured in their uprightness, and below there is toil and disturbance. Above, the king is bound by desire, never reflecting on impermanence, never following right governance. Therefore I offered him this Bright Moon Pearl, but he would not accept it, and instead challenged me regarding proof of poverty and wealth. Therefore I wish to see the Tathāgata, who opens and guides without exception and protects without limit. I beg you to resolve this matter and clarify its meaning."

The Buddha said: "You are wealthy, Sulai! Your words are utterly sincere. Great king, do not doubt this."

The king said: "Yes, World-Honored One! With your words of true righteousness, please enlighten one so benighted."

The Buddha, through skillful means, carefully considering the king's feelings, manifested the meaning and said: "King, listen carefully! There is also a way of reckoning by which the king is wealthy and Sulai has nothing. And there is also an essential way of reckoning by which Sulai is wealthy and the king cannot match him. What is called the king's wealth is calculated by the nation's possessions: gold, silver, jade, crystal, lapis lazuli, pearls, coral, mother-of-pearl, agate, elephants, horses, palaces, and all abundant goods governed at will. This is the king's wealth, which Sulai does not have. But consider Sulai's virtuous character and right marks: his generosity, his complete precepts, his patient endurance, vigor, wisdom, non-negligence, and wholesome practices in good order; his compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity; his love and respect for the Three Treasures; his deep study, pure intention, honest faith, and sense of shame; his seven kinds of wealth, fully complete. This is Sulai's wealth, which the king cannot match. Even if the great king's entire realm of people and subjects were all as wealthy as the Śākyan Mahānāma, and you combined all their wealth to compare with Sulai's wealth of the Way and its virtue, it would not equal even a hundredth, a thousandth, or a hundred-billion-millionth part. No comparison can express this."

The king said: "Excellent! Excellent! As the World-Honored One has said. I am already fortunate. Within my kingdom there exists such a person, one who upholds the Dharma and possesses supreme wealth."

The Buddha said: "Indeed, there are many true practitioners within the king's realm."

At that time, King Prasenajit rose and stood before the Buddha and praised Sulai, saying:

> "I honor you together with the Buddha.
> Through you I have become refined.
> I offer you the kingdom.
> From now on, you shall be my teacher.
> Long have I been arrogant with the precepts,
> sitting on the throne, far from the right Dharma.
> Now hearing Sulai's words,
> I vow to carefully cultivate the pure practice.
> Because of my craving for wealth and profit,
> I bore grudges against five hundred people and imprisoned them.
> Now I pardon them, for craving brings no benefit.
> Mindful of your kindness, which is hard to forget."

Then the five hundred imprisoned people heard the king pardon their offenses. They all thought of Sulai's kindness. Weary of impermanent suffering and having gained the thought of desirelessness, they all rose and bowed to the Buddha, and also bowed to Sulai. The king was delighted and praised him again:

> "You are truly wealthy; I am truly poor.
> Sulai's words are wonderfully true.
> I now proclaim throughout the land:
> no one may call this man poor."

After the people heard the king's decree, they all called Sulai wealthy, and the name "poor" was no more. Sulai rose, straightened his garments, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "Now this great assembly has gathered. Excellent, World-Honored One! Please speak the Dharma-words, so that this multitude may not have seen the Buddha in vain."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Listen to what I say and reflect upon it well. There are four dharmas, sons and daughters of good family, for seeing the Buddha. What are the four? Faith, delight, joy, and reverence. These are the four.

"There are a further four dharmas by which one may see the Buddha. What are the four? Having seen the Buddha's form and marks, one immediately arouses the aspiration for the Way and vows that in a future life one will obtain such a body. Having single-mindedly reflected upon the Buddha's words, one is always utterly sincere. Having felt compassion for all people and beings, one's mind no longer wavers. Having taken delight in one's practice and never severing the Three Treasures. These are the four.

"There are further four dharmas that constitute the awakened mind. What are the four? Regarding form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness as having nothing to observe; what one sees turning to emptiness; what one perceives, one immediately understands. These are the four.

"There are a further four dharmas by which seeing the Buddha leads toward purity. What are the four? Not calculating self and other, so as to eliminate inner and outer; not calculating permanence, so as to eliminate the notion of lifespan; not calculating annihilation, so as to abandon habitual practices; using the Buddha's eye to see and awaken the mind that is always asleep. These are the four.

"There are a further four dharmas by which seeing the Buddha leads toward purity. What are the four? None of the dharmas can be grasped; using right samādhi as the practice of purity; having studied and accomplished unsurpassed wisdom; through skillful means, seeing purity and liberation. These are the four. Sons and daughters of good family! Having seen the Tathāgata is to be heading toward purity."

When the Buddha spoke this, seven hundred bhikṣus attained liberation of mind through the destruction of the outflows. The bodhisattvas in the assembly all attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas. Countless thousands of people all established roots of virtue. When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, he rose from his seat, and together with the disciples and bodhisattvas, manifested spiritual powers and flew away like the king of phoenixes, returning to the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park.

At that time, King Prasenajit said to Sulai: "Sir, whenever you wish to visit the Buddha, please inform me so that I may follow you."

Sulai said: "I also wish that the great king would command all the women of the inner palace, the crown prince, the officials, as well as the government officers and people of the land, that those who do not go to see the Buddha should have their offense recorded by the officials. Why? A bodhisattva is not concerned only for himself but for all humans and non-humans. A bodhisattva in a great assembly is all the more magnificent in awesome spiritual power."

The king said: "I wish to hear about the bodhisattva's leading of great assemblies. How does this work?"

Sulai answered: "All sentient beings the bodhisattva is able to gather and unite as followers. Through generosity, people gladly follow, and the stingy can be transformed into lovers of giving. Through the bodhisattva's upholding of precepts, people gladly follow, and those without faith are transformed to believe in karmic retribution. Through the bodhisattva's patient endurance, people gladly follow, and the angry are transformed to be free from grudges and resentment. Through the bodhisattva's vigor, people gladly follow, and the weak are transformed to establish vigor. Through the bodhisattva's practice of dhyāna, people gladly follow, and the scattered-minded are transformed to guard single-pointedness. Through the bodhisattva's wisdom, people gladly follow, and the foolish are transformed to gain right wisdom. Through the bodhisattva's practice of loving-kindness, people gladly follow, and the unkind are transformed to have compassionate hearts. Through the bodhisattva's practice of compassion, people gladly follow, entering the suffering of birth and death without wearying of the right practice. Through the bodhisattva's practice of sympathetic joy, people gladly follow, and those lost in worry are transformed to delight in the Dharma. Through the bodhisattva's practice of equanimity, people gladly follow, comforted, encouraged, and assisted, causing people to enter the Dharma. Thus, great king, virtuous conduct is not of one kind alone.

"There are a further four things that serve as the good reception of people. First, protecting sentient beings without abandoning them. Second, practicing all roots of virtue with purity. Third, choosing good vows so that the buddha-land is free from the three poisons. Fourth, through emptiness, wishlessness, and signlessness, escaping from Māra's net and its bonds. The bodhisattva always acts with gentleness, transforming the stubborn and never forgetting the Great Vehicle. He delights in dwelling in mountains and marshes, never through criticism or severance destroying the merit and virtue of former lives. His good roots daily increase. He universally cultivates all practices, perfecting the Dharma of the Way, the thirty-seven factors of awakening. Through these the bodhisattva gathers and wins the people."

King Prasenajit was filled with great delight and a wholesome mind arose in him. He took the fine garment he was wearing, worth ten million, and offered it to Sulai. Sulai did not accept it, saying: "Since the great king's mind is delighted, what difference is there between giving and receiving?" The king was displeased. Sulai then said: "I have my own coarse garment, and wearing it gives me great delight. What need do I have to worry about clothing?"

The king said: "Why is that?"

Sulai answered: "Sometimes I take off my garment and hang it on a tree and leave it, going away for one day or up to seven days. No one takes it out of greed, and I too have no feelings of attachment. I never look back at that garment. A bodhisattva always wears garments such as these: he himself has no attachment, and he also causes others not to be greedy."

The king said: "I wish that you, sir, would have pity on me, and step upon this garment with your feet, so that I may gain immeasurable merit through the long night." Sulai acceded to the king's wish and stepped upon the garment.

The king said: "This garment now belongs to Sulai. Where should I place it?"

Sulai answered: "Great king, you should look throughout this land for those whose bodies are exposed and unclothed, and give this garment to them."

The king at once ordered his attendants to take the garment out and bestow it upon the poor. The poor people who received the king's fine garment all rejoiced and thought of Sulai: "How can we repay his kindness? Sulai's sincere intention..." Through the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, a voice from the sky spoke a verse:

> "Not with fragrant flowers or treasures,
> nor with sweet and fine foods,
> can one repay this kindness of compassion.
> Simply arouse the aspiration for the Way.
> The person of perfection does not crave honor,
> nor delight in rare and extraordinary things.
> To repay them, one should seek buddhahood,
> with the Great Vehicle and the four immeasurables."

Then Sulai, at the time of the sun's decline, together with the great multitude of people, and the king also with all the women of the inner palace, officials, and people, all went together to visit the Buddha. At that time, the faithful men and women of Śrāvastī, numbering a billion in all, along with the poor people who had been given the fine garment, hearing that Sulai was going to the Buddha, all followed him.

At that time, Śakra, lord of the devas, descended from his heaven to Śrāvastī and along the way created a great hall like the palace of the Heaven of the Thirty-Three. He created trees of the seven treasures and beneath the trees prepared a lion-throne for the Buddha, symmetrically proportioned and spread over with many kinds of silk. He commanded twelve thousand celestial maidens to stand in rows nearby, playing a hundred kinds of music as an offering to the Buddha. The Buddha knew the great assembly had all arrived and at once rose and went to the great hall, sitting upon the celestial lion-throne. The Buddha's body emitted radiance illuminating heaven and earth, and flowers scattered from the sky, falling like rain.

Śakra told his son: "Kauśika! I have prepared a seat for the Buddha. You should create seats for the bodhisattvas and true practitioners." The prince then created more than sixty thousand seats, all splendidly adorned, covered with celestial five-colored cushions. He then spoke a verse:

> "True practitioners, all bodhisattvas,
> please come and sit upon these seats.
> These transformations delight the devas.
> We always wish to gather before the Buddha."

When all the bodhisattvas and great disciples had settled in their seats, the celestial musicians of the Pañcaśikha devas ordered their celestial attendants to tune more than five hundred lutes and make the music harmonious, then advance and sing. "When the Buddha and Sulai come, there will surely be honored devas accompanying them, and we should withdraw." They then tuned their minds and sang a hymn:

> "Wisdom-practice through a hundred kalpas; wisdom flourishing, giving without measure.
> Wisdom-precepts guarding body and speech: bow to the Unsurpassed Sage.
> Human patience, never transgressing; vigor, human power most strong.
> Humaneness opening the gate of samādhi and wisdom: bow to the Hero of the Three Realms.
> Having clearly severed lust, anger, and delusion, all exhausted and extinguished, without blemish.
> Having attained it himself and then bestowed it on others: bow to the Teacher of Devas and Humans.
> Through wisdom's contemplation, the three attachments are removed, not craving the world's glory.
> Calm and peaceful, free from grief and fear: bow to this King of the Dharma.
> When Māra's devas offered three women, the aspiration for the Way was not toppled.
> Unattached, incapable of being defiled: bow to the One of Supreme Purity.
> Wondrous marks, thirty-two, with many fine features adorning the body.
> Eight kinds of voices, heard without exception: bow to the Deva among Devas.
> Where he walks, the ground shows his marks. Fearless, his awesome power shakes from afar.
> Teeth even, a halo round his shoulders: bow to the Divine One among the Śākyas.
> I praise the King of the Ten Powers. My solitary joy in giving is sincere.
> Taking refuge in the Buddha, gaining merit: may I hereafter become like the World-Honored One."

At that time, Sulai, with the great multitude of people and also many thousands of devas, all came to the Buddha and bowed at the Buddha's feet. Each sat to one side. King Prasenajit and his retinue bowed before the Buddha's feet and stood to one side. The king then personally took a seat and said to Sulai: "Sir, please sit upon this seat." Among the devas in the assembly, those who had not yet seen Sulai thought: "This poor man: what merit does he have, that he should be so honored?" Śakra, knowing the devas' thoughts, said: "You who rely on merit should not look down upon this man. Why? I can bear witness: this man's observance of the precepts and devotion to the Dharma is difficult to match. Just wait a moment and you shall see his virtue."

Sulai, wishing to open the minds of all the devas, then addressed the Buddha: "I only wish, World-Honored One, that you would explain how a bodhisattva obtains awesome spiritual power and honor, and arrives at the unsurpassed, true, and right Way."

At that time, the Buddha emitted light from his body. The flames touched Sulai, and his appearance became a hundred-thousand-fold more magnificent than Śakra's. The devas' minds were all delighted. They knew he was no ordinary man, and they all scattered celestial flowers upon him.

The Buddha told Sulai: "A bodhisattva who is of high station does not become arrogant, and who appears humble and lowly is able to cause others not to crave wealth and honor, nor bear grudges against poverty. This is pure virtue. Being in a position of power and prosperity, one can lead others to develop the intention of generosity. Being wise yet appearing foolish, one can cause the foolish to quickly attain wisdom. This is pure virtue. Being able to control the mind, like you, Sulai, manifesting extreme poverty while guarding the precepts and following the Dharma, being honored by all brahmins, householders, and people: this is pure understanding of skillful means."

The Venerable Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "How long has Sulai been a practitioner?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "His practice is exceedingly long. He has already served many hundreds of thousands of koṭīs of Buddhas. He has now attained the three kinds of patience and broadly accomplished all wisdom. He follows all goodness in his practice, yet manifests himself as poor and lowly. His intention is this pure."

Ānanda said: "Are there now others who wish to seek buddhahood?"

The Buddha said: "Seven thousand among the desire-realm devas, twelve thousand among the form-realm devas, and thousands among humans have all aroused the aspiration for the unsurpassed, true, right Way."

Ānanda then asked: "How long before Sulai becomes a Buddha? What will his Buddha-name be? What will his land be like?"

The Buddha spoke this verse for Ānanda:

> "Ānanda, listen as I explain
> the original aspiration of Sulai.
> He protects people, bears no enmity, is good.
> His virtue is vast, always giving generously.
> Since he first aroused the aspiration,
> the number of lives is hard to recount.
> Making offerings to Buddhas without wearying,
> upholding the Dharma and guarding it without forgetting.
> He studied the six perfections without limit,
> advancing on the Way, delighting in its long duration.
> His pure conduct was never defiled;
> guarding the Dharma, his wisdom never wavered.
> With all his practices, aspiration, and mindfulness complete,
> he awakened and stood on the stages of the Way.
> Having crossed beyond all the nets of deviance,
> his nature good, awakening to inner truth,
> he renounced the world's eight concerns:
> gain and loss, blame and praise, each in the mind.
> He views all equally,
> like space, without obstruction.
> He loves the Dharma and practices without weariness,
> guarding patience, with compassion as his constant.
> Loving others as he loves himself,
> sacrificing his body to bring peace to all beings.
> Whatever habitual tendencies remain, he teaches others to overcome.
> With thought matured, he explains the truth of meaning.
> His awakened mind never parts from the Dharma;
> understanding emptiness, he guides toward the two liberations.
> The three kinds of patience complete, thought without grasping,
> he studies the Dharma and knows what can be practiced.
> Wherever he goes, he always opens and guides.
> All receive his kindness.
> Wherever a land or settlement flourishes,
> he goes to that place,
> proclaiming transformation as the Buddha intends,
> universally teaching all devas and humans.
> After my entry into nirvāṇa,
> in the final age, when Sulai's life ends,
> he will be reborn in the delightful land of the east,
> in the domain of the Buddha Akṣobhya,
> and after three more incalculable kalpas,
> his practices complete, his great vow fulfilled,
> he will attain buddhahood and remove the world's deviance,
> bringing peace and liberation throughout the ten directions.
> His title will naturally be 'Divine General.'
> His name will be World-Honored King.
> His beginning will be like the Buddha Akṣobhya,
> liberating beings beyond measure.
> The name of his world will be 'Virtue-Transformation Accomplished.'
> Evil will be extinguished and good righteousness will flourish.
> That Buddha will dwell for tens of millions of years,
> and his sangha will be beyond reckoning.
> In that time, those seeking arhatship will be few;
> those seeking buddhahood will be many.
> The people of that age will have spiritual powers and strength,
> vigor, merit-practice, and clarity.
> In that world, the five kinds of sound
> will broadcast the Buddha's Dharma universally.
> There will be no Māra to corrupt the good;
> with right faith, one will be freed from the afflictions of deviance.
> After that Buddha's parinirvāṇa,
> eighty-four thousand people
> will cause the supreme Dharma to flourish and illuminate the world,
> causing conduct to be free from contention.
> Those whom Sulai taught and transformed,
> many of whom vowed for the Great Vehicle,
> will all gather and be born in that world.
> Those he has already liberated are beyond count."

The Buddha predicted Sulai's future. At that time, the devas, humans, ghosts, and nāgas seated in the assembly all offered fine garments to Sulai. The sounds of joy were heard throughout the three-thousand worlds. Hundreds of thousands of millions of people came and bowed to him. The Buddha broadly expounded the manifold essentials of the Dharma, explaining the practice of the Three Vehicles.

King Prasenajit moved from his seat, joined his palms, stood, and addressed the Buddha: "I have been intoxicated by the wealth of my kingdom through greed and turbidity. Proud and indulgent, I have engaged in dangerous conduct. Having heard Sulai's wondrous words, I now know myself to be the most impoverished of all. I now wish to abdicate the kingdom and entrust it to the crown prince, to receive the precepts of the World-Honored One, and to become a bhikṣu in the Buddha's sangha, guarding the gardens and serving as an attendant. Of my present wealth and treasures, I shall make three portions: one portion I shall offer to the Buddha's disciples; a second I shall give to the people of the land; and a third I shall keep in the state treasury. Having received the Buddha's grace, I am no longer attached to the wealth and treasures of this present life. I give all this merit to all sentient beings, and vow to attain the aspiration for the unsurpassed, true, and right Way."

At that time, seated in the assembly were five hundred elders and householders, five hundred brahmins, and five hundred minor officials. Hearing the king's vow, like the roar of a lion, they all aroused the aspiration for the unsurpassed, true, and right Way. All wished to renounce desire and, with household faith, leave the household life to become śramaṇas. With the exception of three hundred of them, the Buddha ordained all the rest as śramaṇas.

Then Sulai withdrew to his seat, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "I also wish to follow the Sage and Teacher, and beg all the Buddhas presently in the ten directions to have compassion and allow me to become a śramaṇa. Sulai's power of wisdom has also been accomplished by the Tathāgata." He then entered samādhi, and all the Buddhas presently in the ten directions appeared, each together with their bhikṣu sangha. Sulai then rose and bowed to the Buddhas of the ten directions, and was ordained as a śramaṇa. He further begged: "I wish that all the World-Honored Ones would compassionately accept me and cause me to become established."

All the Buddhas of the ten directions together with Śākyamuni each extended their right arms and touched Sulai's head. The arms of all the Buddhas each appeared without obstructing one another. At that time, radiance illuminated the three-thousand worlds. Celestial flowers rained down. The Venerable Sulai's Dharma-robes naturally appeared on his body, properly worn and evenly arranged, his deportment composed and dignified. At the moment when Sulai received ordination, five hundred bhikṣus had their outflows exhausted and their minds liberated, free from all remaining bonds, and a thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of faith.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Receive this sūtra of prediction and ordination, uphold it, and expound its meaning for all. Why? In the final age, people will mostly be caught in the net of deviant beliefs. It is for this reason that I have become a Buddha in their midst, to transform their evil intentions and cause them to see the right path, causing them, like Sulai, to enter from faith into wisdom. In this world I universally expound the Dharma. Not until every last person is liberated will I ever abandon them. Therefore, Ānanda, you should transmit this Dharma and cause people to have faith and delight, constantly studying and listening throughout the day so that their understanding is opened. Through this, one attains the essential."

When the Buddha finished speaking the sūtra, all rejoiced: the Bhikṣu Sulai, all the bhikṣu sangha, Śakra lord of the devas, King Prasenajit, devas, humans, and asuras all delighted in hearing it.
