# 佛說長者子制經
## Sūtra of Zhi, Son of the Elder, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0526

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Rājagṛha, on Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain, together with five hundred śramaṇas, all of whom were arhats. At dawn they all donned their kāṣāya robes, took their bowls, and entered the city to beg for food.

At that time, in the city there was a wealthy and distinguished household of the four castes, foremost in the country. Their dwelling was on high and dry ground, with fine buildings and towers, encircled by walls with seven gates. The head of this eminent household was named Dānnikānai. He had one son named Zhi, who was sixteen years old. His father Dānnikānai had passed away, so Zhi lived alone with his mother.

When the Buddha came to their house to beg for food, Zhi was standing inside the third gate. From afar he saw the Buddha coming, and thought: "How supremely handsome and beautiful this person is! Beautiful as a bright moon-pearl, radiant as the sun and moon, his complexion like gold, like the full moon on the fifteenth day."

The Buddha stood outside the gate. Zhi ran in and said to his mother: "I have seen a person coming who is supremely handsome and marvelous, without equal in all the world. Since I was born I have never seen anyone like this. He is now standing at the gate, wishing to beg for food."

Zhi said to his mother: "We should give to him."

His mother was very stingy and refused to give. Zhi said: "Mother, please have compassion for my sake and give to this person. To give to this person is like a sick person finding a good physician." Zhi added: "To give to this person, whose name is known throughout heaven and earth; this person is still standing outside now."

His mother still refused. Zhi repeatedly implored her without ceasing. His mother grew angry and said to Zhi: "You pester me without stop and make me flustered!" She said: "This person has not come to beg for food; he only wants to deceive and mock you. You are a foolish little child; what do you know? If you don't stop asking, you'll get a beating from me before you stop!"

The Buddha, knowing in advance that his mother was stingy, released his divine radiance, which shone through all seven gates. Zhi, seeing the Buddha's radiance, was filled with great joy. He went again to his mother and said: "Foolish people do not understand generosity. It is like a blind person falling into fire, suffering only because they have no eyes. People of the world, on account of stinginess and greed, who do not believe in generosity, will later receive their calamity. Those whose minds think evil, whose mouths speak evil, whose bodies do evil, who are foolish and do not believe in the Buddha, do not believe in the sūtras, do not believe in the bhikṣu sangha, after death will all fall into the hells, among hungry ghosts and animals. Those who are not stingy or greedy, who believe in generosity, will later receive their reward. Those whose bodies do good, whose mouths speak good, and whose minds think good, will receive their own blessings. One's household should always give. Following the wise makes one wise."

Zhi said: "If mother will not give, then bring me my food portion for today. I would rather go without food for a day. Please, out of compassion for me, bring it quickly. I wish to give it to this person, and I fear he will leave me. If this person leaves me, he will be hard to encounter again."

His mother again refused. Zhi then went himself to take his food portion, and also took the fine clothing he wore. He went to the Buddha, bowed with his head at the Buddha's feet, then stood aside with palms joined, and said to the Buddha: "I offer all the clothing and food I have. May it please the Buddha to accept them." The Buddha was silent and did not respond.

Zhi again addressed the Buddha: "The Buddha is the teacher of all beings in heaven and on earth. Please have compassion and deliver us. May you accept this for my sake, so that I may obtain merit." He asked three times in this way, and the Buddha then accepted. Zhi was filled with great joy.

The Buddha said to Zhi: "Today you have overcome all the stingy. By offering the Buddha clothing and food today, may all the wishes in your heart be fulfilled."

Zhi was filled with great joy, and again addressed the Buddha: "What I wish for is not to become the second-heaven Lord Śakra of Trāyastriṃśa, nor to become the seventh-heaven King Brahmā, nor to be wealthy and distinguished life after life. I only wish that my wisdom and radiance may be like the Buddha's."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! May all your wishes be fulfilled."

Lord Śakra of the second heaven, hearing Zhi's words, stood behind the Buddha with palms joined. Śakra then spoke before the Buddha, saying to Zhi: "You have offered only a small amount of food and clothing to the Buddha, and now you wish to become a Buddha? You cannot attain this! After ten kalpas, a hundred kalpas, a thousand kalpas, ten thousand kalpas, a hundred million kalpas, you still will not be able to become a Buddha."

Zhi replied to Lord Śakra: "I do not expect to become a Buddha merely from one offering of food. I will always maintain a wholesome mind with diligence and without slacking, seeking buddhahood without ceasing, and in the end I shall become a Buddha."

Lord Śakra replied to Zhi: "Even if you offered the four treasures in heaps as great as Mount Sumeru, and with a compassionate mind thought of all the people of the world, for ten million kalpas, a hundred million kalpas, you still could not attain buddhahood."

Zhi replied to Lord Śakra: "It is like a craftsman who takes an axe and enters the forest to seek timber. He will select the best and straightest, and only take what pleases him. I do not expect to become a Buddha merely from one offering of food and clothing."

Lord Śakra again said to Zhi: "To seek buddhahood is extremely arduous. Would it not be better to seek to become the seventh-heaven Brahmā, or the second-heaven Lord Śakra? To seek to become a Buddha is very difficult to attain."

Zhi replied to Lord Śakra: "Can you see into Jambudvīpa?"

Lord Śakra said: "I can."

Zhi said: "Fill it entirely with a great fire reaching up to heaven. I would cast my body into it, and still I would seek buddhahood without ceasing."

Lord Śakra said: "If you fill Jambudvīpa with fire, there are people, animals, and crawling creatures within it. You must not kill them. If your mouth speaks evil and your mind thinks evil, you will fall into the hells after death. How then could you become a Buddha?"

Zhi replied to Lord Śakra: "The people and crawling creatures of the world I entrust with a compassionate heart to Maitreya Buddha. Maitreya Buddha will deliver them. My quest for the buddha-path will never cease."

Zhi further said to Lord Śakra: "The seventh-heaven Brahmā and the second-heaven Lord Śakra, and the places where they dwell, will in the end decay and perish. All must return to death; none can escape death."

Lord Śakra then joined his palms and said to Zhi: "Your resolve is so firm, your determination to seek buddhahood without ceasing; in the end you shall become a Buddha."

Zhi replied to Lord Śakra: "Do not take pride in your heavenly position and be conceited. There are those who surpass you. With my wholesome mind, with diligence and without slacking, seeking buddhahood without ceasing, in the end I shall become a Buddha." Lord Śakra then fell silent, with nothing more to say.

The Buddha then said to Zhi: "In your past lives you have served sixty thousand Buddhas. You have always wished to become a Buddha. You have always maintained a diligent mind in serving the Buddhas. After two hundred billion kalpas, you will no longer fall into the hells, among hungry ghosts, or among animals. When the two hundred billion kalpas are complete, you shall become a cakravartin king, and ten billion minor kingdoms shall all be subject to you. You shall rule the four continents and travel by flight. Your conduct shall be righteous. When your life ends, you shall be reborn in the seventh Brahmā heaven and the fourth Tuṣita heaven. When your heavenly lifespan ends, you shall again be reborn as a cakravartin king. When that life ends, you shall again ascend to the seventh Brahmā heaven. Thus ascending and descending for a hundred billion kalpas, when this is complete, you shall become a Buddha named Sumerikālā. You shall deliver the people of heaven and earth. When you descend to the earth, your radiance shall illuminate twelve thousand billion worlds, reaching up to the twenty-eighth heaven. Heaven and earth shall quake greatly. Night shall be bright as when the sun rises. The people's lifespan shall be seven thousand billion years. Your body shall be two hundred zhang tall. There shall be eleven thousand cities, each four hundred and eighty li in extent. The cities shall all be adorned with carvings of gold, silver, lapis lazuli, crystal, coral, mother-of-pearl, and agate, the seven treasures.

"When you become a Buddha, you shall hold two great assemblies to teach the sūtras. At the first assembly, six thousand billion śramaṇas shall all attain arhatship. At the second assembly, four thousand billion śramaṇas shall all attain arhatship. At that time, among the people there shall be no thieves. Men and women shall all be of one mind, and none shall do evil. There shall be no mountains, forests, or valleys; the earth shall be level and smooth. The people shall have no illness or suffering, and all shall be happy and at peace, each keeping to their dwelling. The heavens shall rain three times a day, just enough to settle the dust. When the people wish to discuss anything, they shall all gather together. In summer it shall not be hot, and in winter it shall not be cold, but always pleasantly moderate.

"Your teaching hall shall be named Nanditā. If any man or woman keeps the precepts, practices generosity, and makes offerings to Buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, and arhats, and seeks to become a Buddha, they shall all attain buddhahood. The path must not go unsought. In all the eight directions, above and below, there is nothing that can exhaust it. The Buddha's wisdom is thus. The people of the world do not know where birth comes from, nor where death leads."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, Zhi and all the bhikṣus bowed with their heads at the Buddha's feet, made obeisance, and departed.
