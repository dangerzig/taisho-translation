# Treatise on the Wheel of the Tenets of the Different Schools

## *Yìbù Zōnglún Lùn* (異部宗輪論)

Taishō 2031

Composed by Bodhisattva Vasumitra (*Shìyǒu*, 世友). Translated by Xuanzang (*Xuánzàng*, 玄奘) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is one of the most important sources for the history of early Buddhist sectarian divisions. Vasumitra systematically catalogs how the original saṃgha split into the Mahāsāṃghika and Sthavira (Elder) lineages, and then further into eighteen or twenty schools, detailing their distinctive doctrinal positions. It parallels T2032 (Treatise on the Eighteen Schools) but provides substantially more doctrinal detail. Xuanzang's colophon notes this was a retranslation checked against multiple Sanskrit manuscripts.*

---

> After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, just over a hundred years,
> Divergent schools arose in the holy teaching, leading to what was not beneficial.
> As they successively held to different views, various schools arose accordingly.
> Relying on their own Āgamas, I shall state their tenets to bring weariness [of error].
> The great bodhisattva Vasumitra, endowed with great wisdom and awakened understanding,
> A true bhikṣu of the Śākya lineage, contemplated that time with discernment.
> Observing equally all beings in the world, tossed about by various views,
> Breaking apart the words of the Sage: the tenets of each school shall now be stated.
> One should carefully examine the Buddha's teaching, relying on the noble truths as basis,
> Like panning for gold in sand, selecting what is truly real.

Thus have I heard transmitted. After the World-Honored One, the Buddha, entered parinirvāṇa, more than a hundred years had passed since the time of the sages, like the sun long set. In the land of Magadha, in the city of Kusumapura, there was a king called Aśoka (*Wúyōu*, 無憂), who ruled over Jambudvīpa, sheltered under a single white parasol, his transforming influence reaching both humans and spirits. At this time, the great assembly of the buddha-dharma first divided. It was because of four assemblies jointly debating the "five matters" (*wǔ shì*, 五事) of Mahādeva (*Dàtiān*, 大天), on which they could not agree, that they split into two schools: (1) the Mahāsāṃghika (*Dàzhòng bù*, 大眾部), and (2) the Sthavira (*Shàngzuò bù*, 上座部). What were the four assemblies? (1) The Dragon-Elephant Assembly. (2) The Border Assembly. (3) The Learned Assembly. (4) The Great Virtue Assembly. As for the five matters, they are stated in this verse:

> "The remainder," "seduced by others," "ignorance," "doubt," "entry caused by another,"
> "Arising of the path through sound": this is the true teaching of the Buddha.

---

### The Mahāsāṃghika Schools

Later, within this second century, three schools branched out from the Mahāsāṃghika: (1) the Ekavyāvahārika (*Yīshuō bù*, 一說部), (2) the Lokottaravāda (*Shuō Chūshì bù*, 說出世部), and (3) the Kaukkuṭika (*Jīyìn bù*, 雞胤部). Next, within this second century, one more school emerged from the Mahāsāṃghika, called the Bahuśrutīya (*Duōwén bù*, 多聞部). Then, within this second century, yet another school emerged from the Mahāsāṃghika, called the Prajñaptivāda (*Shuōjiǎ bù*, 說假部). At the end of the second century, there was a renunciant who had been an adherent of an outside path, who abandoned the heterodox and returned to the orthodox, also named Mahādeva. He went forth and received full ordination within the Mahāsāṃghika. Being learned and energetic, he dwelt on Mount Caityaka (*Zhìduō shān*, 制多山). Together with the monks of that school, he again debated the five matters in detail. Due to the resulting discord, they divided into three schools: (1) the Caityaka (*Zhìduō shān bù*, 制多山部), (2) the Aparaśaila (*Xīshān zhù bù*, 西山住部), and (3) the Uttaraśaila (*Běishān zhù bù*, 北山住部).

Thus the Mahāsāṃghika underwent four or five divisions. Counting the root and branches separately, they comprise nine schools: (1) Mahāsāṃghika, (2) Ekavyāvahārika, (3) Lokottaravāda, (4) Kaukkuṭika, (5) Bahuśrutīya, (6) Prajñaptivāda, (7) Caityaka, (8) Aparaśaila, (9) Uttaraśaila.

---

### The Sthavira Schools

The Sthavira school remained of one flavor and harmonious for that period. At the beginning of the third century, a minor dispute arose and they split into two schools: (1) the Sarvāstivāda (*Shuō Yīqiè Yǒu bù*, 說一切有部), also called the Hetuvāda (*Shuōyīn bù*, 說因部); and (2) the original Sthavira school, which then took the name Haimavata (*Xuěshān bù*, 雪山部).

Later, within this third century, from the Sarvāstivāda branched one school, the Vātsīputrīya (*Dúzǐ bù*, 犢子部). Then, within this third century, from the Vātsīputrīya branched four schools: (1) Dharmottarīya (*Fǎshàng bù*, 法上部), (2) Bhadrayānīya (*Xiánzhòu bù*, 賢胄部), (3) Saṃmitīya (*Zhèngliàng bù*, 正量部), (4) Ṣaṇṇagarika (*Mìlín shān bù*, 密林山部). Then, within this third century, from the Sarvāstivāda emerged another school, the Mahīśāsaka (*Huàdì bù*, 化地部). Then, within this third century, from the Mahīśāsaka branched one school, the Dharmaguptaka (*Fǎzàng bù*, 法藏部), who claimed: "We inherit the lineage of Master Maudgalyāyana (*Cǎishū shì*, 採菽氏)." At the end of the third century, from the Sarvāstivāda emerged another school, the Kāśyapīya (*Yǐnguāng bù*, 飲光部), also called the Suvarṣaka (*Shàn suì bù*, 善歲部). At the beginning of the fourth century, from the Sarvāstivāda emerged yet another school, the Sautrāntika (*Jīngliàng bù*, 經量部), also called the Saṃkrāntivāda (*Shuōzhuǎn bù*, 說轉部), who claimed: "We take Ānanda (*Qìngxǐ*, 慶喜) as our master."

Thus the Sthavira underwent seven or eight divisions. Counting the root and branches separately, they comprise eleven schools: (1) Sarvāstivāda, (2) Haimavata, (3) Vātsīputrīya, (4) Dharmottarīya, (5) Bhadrayānīya, (6) Saṃmitīya, (7) Ṣaṇṇagarika, (8) Mahīśāsaka, (9) Dharmaguptaka, (10) Kāśyapīya, (11) Sautrāntika.

---

### Doctrinal Positions of the Schools

**The shared root tenets of the Mahāsāṃghika, Ekavyāvahārika, Lokottaravāda, and Kaukkuṭika:**

These four schools jointly held: All buddhas and world-honored ones are entirely supramundane. All tathāgatas have no dharmas with outflows. All the tathāgata's words turn the dharma-wheel. The Buddha teaches all dharmas with a single sound. All that the World-Honored One says is in accord with the true meaning. The tathāgata's form-body is truly without limit. The tathāgata's awesome power is also without limit. The buddhas' life-spans are also without limit. The Buddha's work of converting beings and generating pure faith in them is without satiation. The Buddha has no sleep or dreams. When the Tathāgata answers questions, he does not need to deliberate. The Buddha at all times does not utter names and the like, because he is constantly in samādhi. Yet sentient beings perceive him as speaking names and the like, and they rejoice and leap with delight. In a single moment of mind, he knows all dharmas. In a single moment of mind, the associated prajñā knows all dharmas. All buddhas' cognition of exhaustion (*jìn zhì*, 盡智) and cognition of non-arising (*wúshēng zhì*, 無生智) constantly and perpetually turn, even until parinirvāṇa.

All bodhisattvas, when entering the mother's womb, do not take the kalala, arbuda, peśī, or ghana as their own body. All bodhisattvas enter the mother's womb in the form of a white elephant. All bodhisattvas exit the mother's womb from the right side. No bodhisattva arouses thoughts of desire, anger, or harm. Bodhisattvas, wishing to benefit sentient beings, vow to be born in evil destinies and can go there at will. With the marginal cognition of a single moment's insight, they comprehensively know the various characteristics and differences of the four noble truths.

The five sense-consciousnesses have both defilement and freedom from defilement. The form and formless realms possess all six consciousnesses. The five material sense-faculties have flesh as their substance. The eye does not see forms; the ear does not hear sounds; the nose does not smell odors; the tongue does not taste flavors; the body does not feel touch. In the state of meditative equipoise, there is the uttering of words, and there is also the taming of the mind, and there is also pure mental attention. One whose task is done cannot receive dharmas [anew].

All stream-enterers' mental and mental-factor dharmas can know their own nature. There are arhats who are seduced by others. There is still ignorance and doubt [in arhats]. Others cause one to enter awakening. The path arises through sound. Suffering can lead to the path; the word "suffering" can assist it. Wisdom is the preparatory practice that can extinguish all suffering and can also lead to bliss. Suffering is also food. One can also dwell long in the eighth stage, and even at the stage of the nature-abiding dharmas, all can be said to have retrogression. Stream-enterers have the meaning of retrogression; arhats do not.

There is no mundane right view. There is no mundane faculty of faith. There are no morally neutral dharmas. When one enters the certainty of the correct (*zhèngxìng lí shēng*, 正性離生), one can be said to sever all fetters. Stream-enterers can commit all evils except the five heinous acts. All sūtras spoken by the Buddha are of definitive meaning (*liǎoyì*, 了義).

Unconditioned dharmas are of nine kinds: (1) cessation through discrimination (*zémiè*, 擇滅), (2) cessation not through discrimination (*fēi zémiè*, 非擇滅), (3) space (*xūkōng*, 虛空), (4) the base of boundless space, (5) the base of boundless consciousness, (6) the base of nothingness, (7) the base of neither perception nor non-perception, (8) the nature of the links of dependent arising, (9) the nature of the factors of the noble path.

The nature of the mind is originally pure; it is defiled by adventitious afflictions, and so is called impure. The latent dispositions (*suímiǎn*, 隨眠) are neither mind nor mental factors; they also have no object. The latent dispositions are different from the active afflictions (*chán*, 纏); the active afflictions are different from the latent dispositions. The latent dispositions should be said to be unassociated with mind; the active afflictions are associated with mind.

Past and future are not real entities. All dharma-bases are neither objects of knowledge, nor of cognition or measurement, nor of penetrating comprehension. There is utterly no intermediate existence (*zhōng yǒu*, 中有). Stream-enterers also attain the dhyānas.

Such are the shared root tenets. The divergent branch tenets of these four schools include: Just as the noble truths have various characteristics and differences, so there are separate insights for each. Some dharmas are self-made, some made by others, some made by both, some arise from conditions. It is possible for two minds to arise simultaneously at one time. The path and afflictions can be simultaneously present. Karma and its ripened fruit can operate simultaneously. The seed is the sprout itself. Material faculties and great elements have the meaning of transformation, but mental and mental-factor dharmas do not. Mind pervades the body; mind extends or contracts in accordance with its basis and object.

Such are the branch tenets, whose differences have immeasurable aspects.

**The Bahuśrutīya's shared root tenets:** The Buddha's five kinds of speech are supramundane teaching: (1) impermanence, (2) suffering, (3) emptiness, (4) non-self, (5) the peace of nirvāṇa. Because these five can lead to the path of liberation. The Tathāgata's other speech is mundane teaching. There are arhats who are seduced by others; there is still ignorance and doubt; others cause one to enter; the path arises through sound. Their remaining positions mostly agree with the Sarvāstivāda.

**The Prajñaptivāda's shared root tenets:** Suffering is not the aggregates themselves. The twelve sense-bases are not truly real. The conditioned factors are mutually dependent, combined and interrelated; they are provisionally designated as "suffering." There is no agency of a person. There is no untimely death. Prior karma is the basis of what is obtained; the increase of karma is the cause. There is a turning of ripened fruits. One attains the noble path through merit. The path cannot be cultivated; the path cannot be destroyed. Their remaining positions mostly agree with the Mahāsāṃghika.

**The shared root tenets of the Caityaka, Aparaśaila, and Uttaraśaila:** Bodhisattvas are not freed from the evil destinies. Offerings made at stūpas do not yield great fruit. There are arhats who are seduced by others. Regarding these five matters and other doctrinal points, their positions mostly agree with the Mahāsāṃghika.

---

**The Sarvāstivāda's shared root tenets:**

The Sarvāstivāda holds that all existents are subsumed under two categories: (1) name (*míng*, 名), and (2) form (*sè*, 色). Past and future entities are also truly real. All dharma-bases are objects of knowledge, cognition, and penetrating comprehension. The characteristics of arising, aging, abiding, and impermanence are subsumed under the aggregate of formations not associated with mind. Conditioned events have three kinds; unconditioned events also have three kinds. The three conditioned characteristics have separate real substance. Three truths are conditioned; one truth is unconditioned.

One gains insight into the four noble truths gradually. Relying on the two samādhis of emptiness and wishlessness, one can enter the certainty of the correct. One enters the certainty of the correct by contemplating the formations of the desire realm. If one has already entered the certainty of the correct, for fifteen mind-moments one is called "one proceeding toward [the fruit]"; at the sixteenth mind-moment, one is called "one abiding in the fruit." The worldly supreme dharmas (*shì dì yī fǎ*, 世第一法) are of one mind-moment and three grades. The worldly supreme dharmas definitely cannot retrogress.

Stream-enterers have no meaning of retrogression; arhats have the meaning of retrogression. Not all arhats attain the cognition of non-arising. Ordinary beings can sever desire-realm craving and anger. There are non-Buddhist practitioners who can attain the five superknowledges. There are also those in the heavens who maintain the brahma-conduct. The awakening factors (*juézhī*, 覺支) can be attained in seven of the attainments, but not in the remaining attainments. All dhyānas are subsumed under the foundations of mindfulness.

Without relying on the dhyānas, one can enter the certainty of the correct and also attain arhatship. If one relies on a form-realm or formless-realm body, although one can attain arhatship, one cannot enter the certainty of the correct. Relying on a desire-realm body, one can not only enter the certainty of the correct but also attain arhatship. In the northern continent of Uttarakuru, there is no one who separates from defilement. Noble ones are not born there or in the heaven of no-thought. The four fruits of the śramaṇa are not necessarily attained gradually. If one has previously entered the certainty of the correct, relying on the mundane path one can attain the fruits of once-returner and non-returner.

One can say that the four foundations of mindfulness can subsume all dharmas. All latent dispositions are mental factors, associated with mind, and have objects. All latent dispositions are subsumed under the active afflictions, but not all active afflictions are subsumed under the latent dispositions. The nature of the links of dependent arising is definitely conditioned. There are also links of dependent arising that turn in accordance with an arhat. There are arhats who increase their store of meritorious karma.

Only in the desire and form realms is there definitely intermediate existence. The five sense-consciousnesses have both defilement and freedom from defilement, but they grasp only the specific characteristic and are solely non-discriminating. Mental and mental-factor dharmas each have real individual substance. Mind and mental factors definitely have objects. A nature does not associate with itself; mind does not associate with mind.

There is mundane right view. There is a mundane faculty of faith. There are morally neutral dharmas. All arhats also have dharmas that are neither learning nor beyond-learning. All arhats attain the dhyānas, though not all can manifest them. There are arhats who still receive the results of prior karma. There are ordinary beings who die while abiding in a wholesome mind. While in the state of meditative equipoise, one definitely does not die.

The liberation of the Buddha and the two vehicles is no different. The noble paths of the three vehicles each have differences. The Buddha's compassion and kindness do not take sentient beings as their object. One who grasps at real sentient beings cannot attain liberation. Bodhisattvas should be called ordinary beings. Their fetters are not yet severed. If they have not yet entered the certainty of the correct, they have not yet transcended the ground of ordinary beings.

Sentient beings are merely provisional designations based on the presently existing appropriated continuum. All conditioned factors are momentary. There is definitely no dharma whatsoever that can transfer from a previous life to a later life; there are only conventional persons (*bǔtègāluó*, 補特伽羅) that are said to transmigrate.

The assemblage of formations during life is itself extinguished without remainder. There are no transforming aggregates. There are supramundane dhyānas. Vitarka also has an undefiled [kind]. There are wholesome dharmas that have causes. In the state of meditative equipoise, there is no one who utters words.

The eightfold noble path is the true dharma-wheel. Not all tathāgata speech constitutes turning the dharma-wheel. The Buddha cannot teach all dharmas with a single sound. The World-Honored One also has words that are not in accord with the true meaning. Not all sūtras spoken by the Buddha are of definitive meaning; the Buddha himself states that there are sūtras of non-definitive meaning.

These and the like are called the shared root tenets and the divergent branch tenets, whose categories are without limit.

**The Haimavata's shared root tenets:** Bodhisattvas are still ordinary beings. Bodhisattvas, when entering the womb, do not arouse craving. No non-Buddhist practitioner can attain the five superknowledges. Nor are there those in the heavens who maintain the brahma-conduct. There are arhats who are seduced by others; there is still ignorance and doubt; others cause entry; the path arises through sound. Their remaining positions mostly agree with the Sarvāstivāda.

**The Vātsīputrīya's shared root tenets:** The person (*pudgala*, *bǔtègāluó*, 補特伽羅) is neither identical to the aggregates nor separate from them. The person is provisionally designated in dependence on the aggregates, sense-bases, and elements. Conditioned factors have temporary abiding and also have momentary cessation. If, apart from the person, no dharma can transfer from a previous life to a later life, then in dependence on the person, transmigration can be spoken of. There are also non-Buddhist practitioners who can attain the five superknowledges. The five sense-consciousnesses are neither defiled nor free from defilement. Severing the fetters of the desire realm to be abandoned through cultivation is called separation from desire, not those abandoned through insight. Patience (*rěn*) is the very name of "characteristics." The worldly supreme dharmas are called "that which can proceed toward the certainty of the correct." If one has already entered the certainty of the correct, for twelve mind-moments one is called "proceeding toward"; at the thirteenth mind-moment, one is called "abiding in the fruit." There are many such differences. Due to their interpretation of a single verse, their tenets differ. From this school branched four sub-schools: the Dharmottarīya, Bhadrayānīya, Saṃmitīya, and Ṣaṇṇagarika. The verse they interpret is:

> "The already liberated falls again;
> Having fallen through craving, one returns once more.
> Attaining peace and the delight of joy,
> Following delight, one proceeds to bliss."

**The Mahīśāsaka's shared root tenets:** Past and future do not exist; the present and the unconditioned do exist. One gains insight into the four noble truths simultaneously. When one sees the truth of suffering, one can see all the truths; only one who has already seen them can see in this way. The latent dispositions are neither mind nor mental factors, and have no object. Latent dispositions and active afflictions are different. The latent dispositions by nature are unassociated with mind; the active afflictions by nature are associated with mind. Ordinary beings do not sever desire-realm craving and anger. No non-Buddhist practitioner can attain the five superknowledges. Nor are there those in the heavens who maintain the brahma-conduct. There is definitely no intermediate existence. No arhat increases their store of meritorious karma. The five sense-consciousnesses have both defilement and freedom from defilement. All six consciousnesses are accompanied by vitarka and vicāra. There is also a "level-headed" person (*qíshǒu bǔtègāluó*, 齊首補特伽羅). There is mundane right view. There is no mundane faculty of faith. There are no supramundane dhyānas, nor any undefiled vitarka and vicāra. Unless there is a cause, stream-enterers can retrogress. Arhats definitely do not retrogress. The factors of the path are all subsumed under the foundations of mindfulness.

Unconditioned dharmas are of nine kinds: (1) cessation through discrimination, (2) cessation not through discrimination, (3) space, (4) immovability, (5) the true suchness of wholesome dharmas, (6) the true suchness of unwholesome dharmas, (7) the true suchness of neutral dharmas, (8) the true suchness of the factors of the path, (9) the true suchness of the links of dependent arising.

Entering the womb is the beginning; death is the end. Material faculties and great elements all undergo transformation; mental and mental-factor dharmas also undergo transformation. The Buddha is included within the saṃgha. Therefore, one who gives to the saṃgha obtains great fruit; giving separately to the Buddha does not [surpass it]. The liberation of the Buddha and the two vehicles is one and the same path, one and the same liberation. All conditioned factors are momentary. There is definitely no dharma whatsoever that can transfer from a previous to a later life.

These are their shared root tenets. Their divergent branch tenets include: Affirming that past and future truly exist. There is also intermediate existence. All dharma-bases are objects of knowledge and cognition. Karma is truly mental volition (*cetanā*); there is no bodily or verbal karma accompanied by vitarka and vicāra. Great world-periods of kalpas have abiding. Offerings at stūpas yield small fruit. The latent dispositions by nature constantly abide in the present. The aggregates, sense-bases, and elements also constantly abide in the present. The branch tenets of this school, due to their interpretation of a single verse, had different positions. That verse says:

> "Five dharmas definitely bind; all sufferings arise from them:
> Namely ignorance, craving, the five views, and karma."

**The Dharmaguptaka's shared root tenets:** Although the Buddha is included within the saṃgha, giving separately to the Buddha yields greater fruit than giving to the saṃgha. Offerings at stūpas yield vast fruit. The liberation of the Buddha and the two vehicles is one, but the noble paths are different. No non-Buddhist practitioner can attain the five superknowledges. The bodies of all arhats are entirely without outflows. Their remaining positions mostly agree with the Mahāsāṃghika.

**The Kāśyapīya's shared root tenets:** Whatever dharma has been severed and comprehensively known, that does not exist. Whatever has not been severed and not comprehensively known, that exists. If karma's fruit has already ripened, it does not exist; if not yet ripened, it exists. Some conditioned factors have the past as their cause, but none have the future as their cause. All conditioned factors are momentary. Dharmas at the learner stage have ripened fruit. Their remaining positions mostly agree with the Dharmaguptaka.

**The Sautrāntika's shared root tenets:** They hold that the aggregates can transfer from a previous life to a later life. They establish the doctrine of transmigration, [maintaining] that apart from the noble path, there are aggregates that are permanently extinguished. There are root aggregates, and there is a single-flavor aggregate. Even in the stage of an ordinary being, there are already holy dharmas present. They assert a real person (*pudgala*) in the ultimate sense. Their remaining positions mostly agree with the Sarvāstivāda.

> Having thoroughly examined many Sanskrit manuscripts, I retranslate the *Treatise on the Wheel of Tenets*.
> The wording is fitting and the meaning without error; the wise should diligently study it.
