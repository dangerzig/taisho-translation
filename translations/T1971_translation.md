# 淨土境觀要門
## Essential Gate of Object and Contemplation in the Pure Land

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1971

Translated from the Chinese. Yuan dynasty, composed by Huaize (懷則).

---

The Pure Land Dharma-gate is the essential path for those of the final age to escape birth-and-death. It is the vessel that cuts horizontally across the five destinies. Once one is born in that land, one never retrogresses. With a body like that of the devas, one flies freely, and clothing and food come of themselves. One joins the pure ocean-assembly, constantly sees the Buddha, hears the Dharma, and swiftly enters the stages of the noble ones. There are no tigers, wolves, lions, mosquitoes, gnats, fleas, or lice to cause the torment of sentient beings. There is no cold, heat, wind, or rain of the non-sentient to cause affliction. Born from a lotus, with a lifespan without limit, there are none of the sufferings of birth, old age, illness, and death, but only the experience of all manner of bliss. Therefore it is called the World of Ultimate Bliss.

For this reason, our teacher Śākyamuni Tathāgata, wishing to cause the deluded sentient beings of this land to escape all suffering, opened the gate of suppression and restraint. Amitābha, the compassionate father, shows the path of gathering and receiving. Therefore, with earnest words and fervent exhortation, the Buddha especially praises the Pure Land and broadly urges rebirth there. The reason is precisely this.

Thus, in the Western Heaven and this land, sages and worthies, monastics and laypeople, who have directed their aspirations and vows and at death's moment have seen the Buddha and obtained rebirth, are recorded in the canonical records in numbers too great to count.

However, ordinary people of base capacity, attached to coarse and defiled forms and sounds, are willingly content to drift through birth-and-death without seeking liberation. It is like entering a city on business: if one does not prepare a place to rest in advance, when evening falls, one will have nowhere to lodge. Deeply lamentable and sorrowful indeed!

Yet among the superior good people, many are those who, weary of suffering and seeking bliss, arouse the mind and recite the Buddha's name. But those who understand object-and-contemplation are few. Therefore I take up the brush and briefly write a general outline, so that those who have faith and delight may, through this, gain entry to the gate of the path, and not labor in vain at ascetic practice.

The *Contemplation of Immeasurable Life Sūtra* says: "If one wishes to be born wholeheartedly in the Western Land, one should first contemplate a sixteen-foot image standing upon the waters of a pond." As stated previously, the body's dimensions are boundless and are beyond the capacity of an ordinary person's mental power. Thus the eighty thousand marks and excellences can only be seen by those at the ten stages of faith; they are not the object of contemplation for an ordinary beginner. Therefore one is instructed to contemplate the sixteen-foot body. The body has thirty-two marks, which cannot all be contemplated at once. One must enter through a single mark. Simply contemplate the white hair-curl between the brows, and the thirty-two marks will naturally become manifest. Once the contemplation is well practiced, one may then change and contemplate the remaining bodily marks without hindrance.

When contemplating this mark, it must be extremely clear. The mark is between the two brows, white as shell-jewel or snow, one zhang and five chi in length, five cun in circumference. On the outside it has eight facets; on the inside it is hollow and penetrating. It curls to the right in a spiral, brilliantly reflecting the golden countenance, with distinct and clear boundaries. Bright, pure, and translucent, it defies description.

To contemplate this mark, one must first understand that the ten thousand dharmas are mind-only and all is consciousness-only. Therefore the sūtra says: "The mind encompasses the great void; its measure extends throughout realms like sand-grains." It also says: "The mind is like a master painter, creating all the five aggregates. Throughout all the worlds, there is nothing not created by the mind."

Thus the Pure Land's dependent retribution of national lands, jeweled trees, jeweled ground, jeweled pools, and the ocean-assembly of Amitābha's proper retribution body with its thirty-two marks are all originally possessed by my mind, all created by my mind, not obtained from another, not coming from outside. One who can understand this may then discuss contemplating the Buddha through the mind.

Therefore one can say "mind-only Pure Land, inherent-nature Amitābha."

The *Contemplation Sūtra* says: "All buddhas, as tathāgatas, are Dharma-realm bodies that enter the mind-contemplation of all sentient beings," and so on up to the passage about the eighty subsidiary marks. The Great Master of Tiantai interpreted this in two ways: first, explained in terms of the sympathetic response and mutual interaction; second, explained in terms of understanding entering into correspondence.

Without the first interpretation, contemplation would not be contemplation of the Buddha. Without the second interpretation, there would be a Buddha outside the mind. Thus, "understanding entering into correspondence" is precisely the mind; "sympathetic response and mutual interaction" is precisely contemplating the Buddha.

As for the interpretation of "this mind creates the Buddha; this mind is the Buddha": from the side of cultivating contemplation, it is called "the mind creates"; from the side of original possession, it is called "the mind is." This passage appears in the eighth contemplation (the image contemplation), but its meaning pervades from beginning to end. By this pattern, one should say: "This mind creates the sun; this mind is the sun," and so on up to "this mind creates Mahāsthāmaprāpta; this mind is Mahāsthāmaprāpta." Even within the nine grades, whatever object one contemplates, all are likewise.

Now, "contemplating the Buddha through the mind" is also called "contemplating the Buddha on the basis of the mind." "On the basis of the mind" speaks from the side of relying on the Buddha. "Through the mind" speaks from the side of original possession: it is because of possession that it is "through." Each raises one aspect, but the meaning necessarily includes both.

This object-and-contemplation, though many have spoken of it, none have hit the mark. I shall first cite the old explanations, evaluate them, and then correctly present the meaning.

Dharma Master Jingue said: "Gather the Buddha back to the mind, and then use contemplation; this is called contemplating the Buddha." I say: if one sends the thought to the Western Land while the object is here in the Eastern Land, then object and contemplation are already misaligned. How could one be born there? This also risks falling into the error of directly contemplating the mind alone.

Dharma Master Guangzhi said: "Gather the mind back to the Buddha; this is called contemplating the Buddha." But this amounts to directly contemplating only the Buddha. Why then did the patriarch name "mind-contemplation" as the essential principle?

According to the views of these two masters, one must first understand that the ten thousand dharmas are mind-only, and only then can one contemplate the mind. Or first understand that the ten thousand dharmas are Buddha-only, and then contemplate the Buddha. This is the same as the practice of directly contemplating the three paths in the constantly-sitting samādhi and the like. It amounts to directly contemplating the mind or directly contemplating the Buddha.

The two masters debated without ceasing and sought a decision from the patriarch Siming. The patriarch combined both positions, saying: it is not gathering the Buddha back to the mind; it is not gathering the mind back to the Buddha. It is contemplating the Buddha on the basis of the mind.

How so? Amitābha's Pure Land is originally possessed by my mind. Therefore, relying upon the fruit-Buddha's thirty-two marks, one perfumes one's own mind's originally possessed Dharma-body nature-essence. When the contemplative wisdom is accomplished, it naturally manifests. Therefore the *Miao Zong Ji* says: "Relying upon that dependent and proper retribution to perfume the mind-nature, the mind-nature is easily activated." This is precisely this meaning.

Question: "Contemplating the Buddha on the basis of the mind": is this only the contemplated object, or does it also include the contemplating contemplation?

Answer: The single word "contemplation" is the contemplating contemplation; the two words "mind" and "Buddha" are the contemplated object. If so, is this contemplated object deluded or true? Answer: The contemplation of object as discussed in our school is forever different from all other explanations. Directly contemplating the true mind and true Buddha belongs exclusively to the buddha-realm. Therefore, whenever we speak of contemplating the mind or contemplating the Buddha, both belong to the deluded object. The intention is to understand that delusion is itself truth. One need not destroy delusion and only then reveal truth.

Those of other schools who directly contemplate the true: for them, delusion must be destroyed before the true principle can appear. This is the meaning of "contemplating principle to sever the nine realms," which amounts to destroying the cultivation-evil of the nine realms and revealing the nature-goodness of the buddha-realm. This is a dharma of severance, belonging to the partial and preliminary Separate Teaching, and is not the perfectly sudden wondrous contemplation.

Question: Since it is said to be "contemplating the Buddha on the basis of the mind," the Buddha is a fruit-person, so how can it be deluded? If it is deluded, Amitābha the World-Honored One should be an ordinary being!

Answer: For a beginning practitioner, as long as external objects have not been forgotten, seeing another Buddha is nothing but delusion and also external skandha-entry. Thus the fault lies with ourselves; how does it concern the Buddha?

Question: Whatever object is contemplated falls within either inside or outside. The mind belongs to the inside; the Buddha belongs to the outside. Now, saying "contemplate the Buddha on the basis of the mind," could this mean contemplating both inside and outside? If so, one would have to look up to contemplate the Buddha and look down to examine the mind, like straddling a threshold with both feet, or like a rat looking in two directions. Ultimately, how does one focus the mind?

Answer: This is especially hard to get right. I shall first cite one school's error and then present the correct meaning.

The initial view: the predecessors said that the fixed object belongs to the external, and the contemplation belongs to mind-only. But this explanation has two errors. First, it precisely falls into the error of contemplating both inside and outside. Second, "the contemplation belongs to mind-only" risks confusing it with the true mind. For the beginning practitioner, one must contemplate, on the basis of the deluded mind, the response-body of Amitābha, and thereby reveal the true Buddha-body. Only then does one avoid these errors.

Although the patriarch used the phrase "mind-only contemplation established," this means precisely that one uses as one's object only what is created by the deluded mind, and applies the threefold contemplation to it. When the contemplation is accomplished, the true Buddha then appears. Therefore this explanation is greatly mistaken.

Now the correct meaning: one must know that our mind is not confined to any direction. As the sūtra passages cited above show, Jingxi says: "The word 'mind-only,' how can it mean only the true mind? One must know that the mind of afflictions pervades everywhere." You do not even know that the mind of afflictions pervades; how can you understand that the form of birth-and-death pervades? Why does form pervade? Because form is mind.

If so, one need not gather the Buddha back to the Eastern Land or to within the five-skandha substance, nor need one look up to contemplate the Buddha or look down to examine the mind. Now I determine it thus: the fixed object belongs to the external; the object is precisely the mind. One need not gather the Buddha back to the mind to call it "contemplating the Buddha on the basis of the mind."

Understood in this way, one not only deeply grasps the Buddha's intent but also completely transcends ordinary thinking. Moreover, the Buddha himself cited a simile, saying: "It is like holding a bright mirror and seeing one's own face." The image in the mirror is outside; how could one gather it back into one's body for it to be one's own image? This also draws the mind toward that place as a cause of rebirth. Though there is no fixed direction, in delusion it becomes localized. Now, with wondrous understanding, one dissolves this localized mind, and precisely with regard to the Buddha, one accomplishes the three truths and threefold contemplation.

Thus the mirror is the simile for the contemplation method; holding it is the simile for cultivating contemplation; seeing the image is the simile for accomplishing the contemplation, which means seeing the Buddha of one's own nature. Before the contemplation is accomplished, the image being outside is a simile for the mind. Why must one gather it back into one's own body? This simile is perfectly clear; people simply fail to understand it.

Question: "Contemplating the Buddha on the basis of the mind": the fixed object being external and yet belonging to delusion, this meaning is clear and beyond doubt. How does one apply the threefold contemplation to this deluded object and accomplish the wondrous three truths? Please show the marks.

Answer: When contemplating the white hair-curl, with one mind and one intention, concentrating the thought without shifting, clearly and distinctly, one can understand this object as complete with all dharmas. Is this mark obtained from my body? Is it obtained from my mind? The Buddha is not obtained from my body, nor from my mind. Not from my body do I obtain the Buddha's mind; not from my mind do I obtain the Buddha's form. Why? If it were mind, the Buddha would be without mind. If it were form, the Buddha would be without form. One cannot seek the three kinds of bodhi by means of form and mind.

Since the contemplated object is empty, the contemplating contemplation is also quiescent. Both subject and object are extinguished, not falling into conceptual thinking. Though emptiness cannot be obtained, following the thought it is immediately seen, like a mirror reflecting a face, the image appearing therein. It is also like a bhikṣu contemplating bones giving rise to various lights: there is no one who brings these, nor is there actually such a bone. It is all created by the mind. Everything is like an illusion, like a transformation. The Buddha has never actually come, nor have I actually gone anywhere. The mind does not know the mind of itself; the mind does not see the mind of itself. When the mind has thoughts, it is delusion. When free of thoughts, it is nirvāṇa. This Dharma cannot be demonstrated; all is made by conceptual thought. Even if there is thought, it too is nothing, empty.

The threefold contemplation is discussed from the side of contemplating. The three truths are discussed from the side of what is revealed. Truth and contemplation are non-dual; subject and object are one suchness. Therefore the patriarch says: "Three truths, three contemplations; three is not three, three and one, one and three, without any fixed locus. Truth and contemplation are different in name but the same in essence. Therefore subject and object are two yet not two." This is precisely the meaning.

Question: One-mind threefold contemplation and three-truths single-object, not before and not after: this is what constitutes the perfectly sudden samādhi. But the above discussion seems sequential; where is the meaning of "one mind"?

Answer: Though the explanation is sequential, in practice they are simultaneous. For example, illuminating this white hair-curl is precisely my mind. Outside the mind there is no dharma; each dharma is ungraspable: this is emptiness. Its mark is vividly present: this is the provisional. The provisional is the object; emptiness is the contemplation. Clearly penetrating without being stained by the object is the extinguishing of the provisional. Clearly penetrating without being purified by wisdom is the extinguishing of emptiness. Neither stained nor purified, with object and contemplation both extinguished and subject and object simultaneously vanished: this is the middle way. What "before and after" is there?

If one discusses the contemplation of the provisional, it is also like this. The white hair-curl is vividly present, like bones giving rise to light: this is the provisional. Its mark is ungraspable: this is emptiness. "There is no one who brings it, nor is there actually such a bone": this is the middle. If one discusses the contemplation of the middle, understanding this white hair-curl as neither empty nor provisional: "When the mind has thoughts, it is delusion; when free of thoughts, it is nirvāṇa. This Dharma cannot be demonstrated; all is made by conceptual thought": this is the extinguishing of the two extremes. Since both extremes are extinguished, both are necessarily illuminated. Because both are illuminated, emptiness and the provisional are vividly present. Extinguishing and illuminating are simultaneous; there can be no before and after.

Thus, when one emptiness is all emptiness, the threefold contemplation is entirely emptiness. When one provisional is all provisional, the threefold contemplation is entirely provisional. When one middle is all middle, the threefold contemplation is entirely middle. Not before, not after, transcending thought, transcending discourse.

Question: The Pure Land's dependent and proper retribution is ten trillion lands away. How can one say "mind-only Pure Land, inherent-nature Amitābha"? And the sūtra says: "Amitābha Buddha is not far from here." How is this?

Answer: This meaning must be explained in terms of the three truths and threefold contemplation; only then will the doubt be resolved. How? From the side of not losing its own essence, where East and West are vividly distinct: what prevents it from being ten trillion lands away? This is the wondrous provisional. From the side of sharing a single nature-essence, without even a hair's breadth of separation: this is the wondrous emptiness. From the side of being neither one nor different, where both marks vanish: this is the wondrous middle.

It is also because "one is all," so it is not prevented from being far. "All is one," so it is not prevented from being near. "Neither one nor all," so it is neither far nor near. By this pattern, grasping and releasing, not-grasping and not-releasing, combining and separating, not-combining and not-separating, and so forth, are all likewise. This is detailed in the first six chapters of the *Mohe zhiguan*, where, relying on the sūtras, the wondrous understanding is broadly opened, and all dharmas are discussed as originally true, being nothing other than the wondrous Dharma of the three truths.

Because of this possession in principle, there is function in practice. Because it is the provisional, not losing its own essence, though not far, it is far: one is reborn in that land. Again, because it is emptiness and shares a single nature-essence, through the Buddha's power, through the power of samādhi, through the ability to see in a single thought, through the mind being in concentration, as in the snap of a finger, though not near, it is near: in reality one does not go there at all. Both extremes being simultaneously extinguished: neither born nor not-born. Since phenomena are identical with principle, they become wondrous phenomena.

But in the Buddha's time, faculties were sharp: raising any single one necessarily included all three. Therefore the shorter Amitābha Sūtra speaks exclusively of the distance, while the Sūtra of Sixteen Contemplations speaks exclusively of the nearness. Since far and near are simultaneously illuminated, far and near are necessarily simultaneously quiescent. Thus: near and far, neither near nor far. Born is non-born; non-born is born; neither born nor not-born.

People today follow the words and generate understanding, partially grasping one side, unable to achieve a complete understanding. Therefore the *Treatise on Ten Doubts* says: "People today, hearing 'born,' take it as born; hearing 'not born,' take it as not born." This is precisely the fault being criticized. Alas!

If one does not discuss the three dharmas of principle-possession and yet discusses the three dharmas of practical function, one incurs the fault of "dharmas arising from the mind" and also draws the criticism of "contemplating principle to sever the nine realms." How can this be called a perfectly sudden Dharma-gate?

If this were not so, why does the *Miao Zong Ji* cite the *Pratyutpanna Samādhi Sūtra*'s three powers as evidence? First, the Buddha's power. Second, the power of samādhi. These two, are they not practical function? Third, the power of original meritorious qualities. Is this not principle-possession?

However, practitioners of the final age who wish to establish their practice and undertake cultivation must select the gate of entering principle and the locus for initiating contemplation. Because sentient beings are in delusion and have not yet awakened to principle, it is only in the seventh chapter on correct cultivation that the ten objects such as the skandhas are specifically selected as what is to be contemplated. The present case is the same as what has been discussed above. The reason lies precisely here. Not to see this intent, would that not be confusion?

Question: How do the "mind-only Pure Land" in the phrase "mind-only" and the "inherent-nature" in "inherent-nature Amitābha" compare with the "mind-only" in the "only color, only mind" of the *Ten Gates of Non-Duality*? Are they the same or different?

Answer: "Mind-only Pure Land" is the contemplated skandha-object. "Inherent-nature Amitābha" is the revealed Dharma-gate and is also the contemplating contemplation. Each raises one side, with the intention of mutual disclosure. As for the *Ten Gates of Non-Duality*, it discusses "mind-only" in terms of the three truths, wondrous form and wondrous mind: "Neither form nor mind" is emptiness. "Yet form, yet mind" is the provisional. "Only form, only mind" is the middle. This is discussed directly in terms of the absolute middle way, and is called wondrous form and wondrous mind. How can it be spoken of in the same breath as the contemplated skandha-mind of "mind-only Pure Land"? Examine the *Jin Bi* and *Zhi Yao* carefully, and you will see the right and wrong for yourself.

If one contemplates the bodily marks of the two bodhisattvas, since they are similar to those of Amitābha, one must make distinctions. The sūtra says: "Simply observe the marks of the head to know whether it is Avalokiteśvara or Mahāsthāmaprāpta." The head-marks are: Avalokiteśvara has a fleshy protuberance on the crown, like an unopened lotus, bright and pure, of a fresh red hue. Mahāsthāmaprāpta has a jeweled vase upon the fleshy protuberance, filled with all manner of radiance.

When reciting the name of this bodhisattva, one should contemplate this mark. When reciting the name of the pure ocean-assembly, one should visualize the three vehicles and all the devas surrounding the one Buddha and two bodhisattvas. The marks of object-and-contemplation discussed here can all be known by the patterns given above.

If one does not carefully distinguish, how can it be called perfect cultivation? Therefore, unable to refrain, I have written this down with a trusting brush. My aged eyes are dim and blurred, and I have been unable to check the texts; there are many omissions. May the reader forgive me. Written three days before the winter solstice of the third year of Zhida (1310 CE).
