# 佛說出生菩提心經

## The Sūtra on Generating the Bodhicitta

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0837

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, translated by Jñānagupta (闍那崛多).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Rājagṛha at the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove, together with a great assembly of a hundred thousand monks, and also with an immeasurable, incalculable, indescribable number of great bodhisattvas who had gathered from the ten directions.

At that time, in the great city of Rājagṛha there was a brāhmaṇa of the Mahākāśyapa clan. While sleeping he dreamed that within Jambudvīpa there was a great lotus, with a thousand petals, its radiance illuminating the entire great trichiliocosm, supremely wondrous and composed of the seven treasures. Within the lotus he saw a moon-disk, and within the moon-disk he saw a man emitting great radiance that illuminated everything. All the sentient beings in the four continents who saw this light felt great joy, leaping with immeasurable delight, and all experienced bliss.

When the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa awoke, he reflected on his dream. With joy and wonder he thought: "What was the cause of this? What event occurred? I have seen this unprecedented sign, something never before heard of, such as I dreamed." Having thought thus, he felt great joy at what he had never seen before. He further reflected: "There is the śramaṇa Gautama. I have heard that he practiced austerities for six years, conquered the māra-hosts, realized great bodhi, turned the wondrous Dharma-wheel, defeated all the heterodox teachers, and is praised by the wise, being clever and skillful in knowing the marks of all things. I should now go to the śramaṇa Gautama and ask him about this dream."

When the night had passed, the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa left the city of Rājagṛha and went to the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove. Having arrived at the Buddha's presence, he prostrated at the Buddha's feet and stood to one side. Standing to one side, he told the Buddha what he had seen in his dream.

When the brāhmaṇa had finished describing his dream, the World-Honored One said to the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa: "Good man! There are four kinds of auspicious dreams by which one obtains the supreme Dharma. What are the four? If while sleeping one dreams of a lotus flower, or dreams of a parasol, or dreams of a moon-disk, or dreams of a buddha-form, having seen these, one should congratulate oneself on encountering the supreme Dharma."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke in verse:

> "If one in sleep dreams of a lotus flower,
> Or in a dream sees a parasol,
> Or in a dream sees a moon-disk,
> One shall obtain great benefit.
> If one dreams of a buddha-image,
> A body adorned with all the marks,
> Beings who see this should rejoice,
> Knowing they shall surely become a tamer of beings."

When the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa heard this verse, he said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is this great benefit? How can sentient beings obtain this benefit and seek the path of bodhi?"

The Buddha told the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa: "The great benefit is omniscience; that is the benefit."

The brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! This benefit of omniscience: by what causes and conditions can it be obtained?"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse for the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa:

> "I now teach the great benefit;
> Brāhmaṇa, listen well.
> If the causes of benefit come together,
> One shall become the Honored Among Bipeds.
> If one wishes to become a wheel-turning king,
> Sovereign over the four continents,
> Beings who wish to become such
> Must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If one wishes to become Brahmā King,
> Sovereign among the multitudes,
> Beings who wish to become such
> Must arouse the bodhicitta.
> In the desire realm, the form realm,
> The formless realm, and above,
> Beings who wish to be born there
> Must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to become a caravan leader,
> A guide among merchants,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to become a great light
> That dispels all darkness,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish all inversions
> And the three realms of existence,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish all coverings and obstacles
> And all unwholesome dharmas,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish ignorance
> And sever the net of craving and attachment,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish existence and craving,
> And to extinguish both defilement and freedom from defilement,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish the pride of self
> And the pride of form and self,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to be free from arrogance,
> The pride of health, of long life, and of self,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish the pride of old age,
> The pride of permanence within impermanence,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish the pride of learning
> And the pride of keeping precepts,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish the pride of forest-dwelling
> And the pride of alms-begging and such practices,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish the pride of associates
> And the pride of wearing rag-robes,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish the pride of supernatural powers
> And the purity of eating one meal a day,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish all pride,
> All the prides of the conditioned,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to make offerings to buddhas
> Who have already entered nirvāṇa,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to make offerings to buddhas,
> To all the tathāgatas,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to turn the Dharma-wheel
> That no one in the world can turn,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to extinguish what should be extinguished
> And to contemplate what should be contemplated,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to practice the brahma-conduct
> Supreme in its beginning, middle, and end,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to gather all vigor
> While passing through the realms of existence,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to teach that all formations are suffering,
> Seeing sentient beings suffer,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to teach beings
> That all dharmas are without self,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to turn the Dharma-wheel
> And to touch supreme bodhi,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> If sentient beings
> Wish to teach the peace of nirvāṇa
> And to realize supreme bodhi,
> They must arouse the bodhicitta.
> Such merits as these
> Can be obtained by those who arouse the aspiration.
> Brāhmaṇa, having heard this,
> You should practice the path of bodhi."

When the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa heard this verse, he said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How much accumulation of merit should one who arouses the bodhicitta gather?"

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke in verse to the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa:

> "If all sentient beings in this buddha-field
> Were established in faith and morality,
> That supreme and great accumulation of merit
> Would not equal one-sixteenth of the aspiration for the path.
> If all sentient beings in this buddha-field
> Were established in faith and Dharma-practice,
> That supreme and great accumulation of merit
> Would not equal one-sixteenth of the aspiration for the path.
> If in buddha-fields as numerous as the sands of the Ganges
> One everywhere built monasteries seeking merit
> And further built stūpas as tall as Mount Sumeru,
> It would not equal one-sixteenth of the aspiration for the path.
> If in buddha-fields as numerous as the sands of the Ganges
> One everywhere gave away the seven treasures,
> That supreme and great accumulation of merit
> Would not equal one-sixteenth of the aspiration for the path.
> If one built stūpas as high and vast as the Iron-Ring Mountains,
> Immeasurably many for all the buddhas,
> The merit sought by such beings
> Would not equal one-sixteenth of the aspiration for the path.
> If sentient beings for a full kalpa
> Constantly carried burdens upon their heads and shoulders,
> That most supreme accumulation of merit
> Would not equal one-sixteenth of the aspiration for the path.
> Such people obtain the supreme Dharma;
> If they seek bodhi to benefit sentient beings,
> Among all beings they are the most supreme.
> This is beyond comparison; how much more beyond anything above it?
> Therefore, having heard these dharmas,
> The wise should always arouse a mind that delights in the Dharma;
> They shall obtain a boundless great accumulation of merit
> And swiftly realize the unsurpassed path."

The brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Can those who have thus aroused the bodhicitta retrogress?"

The Buddha told the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa: "Those who have thus aroused the bodhicitta do not retrogress from liberation. However, in terms of specific matters, there are three kinds of bodhi. What are the three? The bodhi of the śrāvaka, the bodhi of the pratyekabuddha, and anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

"Great brāhmaṇa! What is the bodhi of the śrāvaka? If a good man or good woman arouses the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi but does not teach others to arouse the bodhicitta and does not establish others in it, does not explain such sūtras for others, does not uphold them themselves or broadly explain their meaning for others, and though there are persons worth befriending they do not serve or provide for them, and whether people come or not they show no respect and feel no sympathetic joy toward them, yet through these causes and conditions their minds attain liberation: brāhmaṇa, this is called the bodhi of the śrāvaka.

"Furthermore, what is the bodhi of the pratyekabuddha? If a good man or good woman arouses the bodhicitta themselves but does not teach others to arouse the bodhicitta and does not establish others in it, does not explain such sūtras for others, does not uphold them themselves or broadly explain them for others, does not befriend such persons or serve and provide for them, and whether people come or not they show no respect and no sympathetic joy, yet through these causes and conditions their minds realize the bodhi of the pratyekabuddha: this is therefore called the pratyekabuddha path.

"Furthermore, what is anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi? If a good man or good woman arouses the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, and also teaches others to arouse this aspiration, establishes them in it, explains such sūtras for others, has them all uphold them, befriends such persons, serves and provides for them, and whether people come or not shows respect and sympathetic joy to all, attaining liberation that benefits both self and others, for the benefit of many, for the happiness of many, out of compassion for the world, for the benefit and happiness of devas and humans: this is called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. For what reason is it called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi? Because above it there is nothing superior to seek; therefore it is called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke in verse:

> "Arousing the bodhicitta oneself
> But not teaching others to uphold it,
> Through the power of one's own mind,
> One later enters nirvāṇa.
> Striving only for one's own benefit,
> Not teaching others to uphold it,
> One is therefore called a śramaṇa,
> The Buddha's child, a supreme teacher.
> That one arouses the bodhicitta
> And transforms beings with joy;
> Therefore one attains the path oneself,
> And the karmic result should be known thus.
> Accomplishing oneself but not others,
> A field of merit among the seers,
> One is called a pratyekabuddha;
> Brāhmaṇa, you should know this.
> Arousing the bodhicitta oneself
> And further liberating many beings,
> Bringing benefit to the world,
> One is called a buddha, the guide.
> Accomplishing one's own benefit
> And further causing others to be liberated,
> With no difference between self and other,
> This is called inconceivable."

The brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Is there any difference between liberation and liberation?"

The Buddha said: "Brāhmaṇa! Between liberation and liberation there is no difference; between path and path there is no difference; between vehicle and vehicle there is a difference. It is like a royal road: some travel by elephant carriage, some by horse carriage, some by donkey carriage; they proceed in order along that road and all arrive at the same city. Brāhmaṇa! What do you think? Is there a difference between these vehicles?"

The brāhmaṇa said: "Venerable World-Honored One! Indeed, there is a real difference between those vehicles."

The Buddha said: "Just so, just so. Brāhmaṇa! Between the śrāvaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, and the vehicle of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi there is a difference; between the path and liberation there is no difference. Brāhmaṇa! It is like the Ganges River, where three kinds of people cross from this shore to the far shore. The first person makes a raft of grass and, relying on it, crosses over. The second person, relying on a leather sack or a leather boat, crosses over. The third person builds a great ship, boards it, and enters the river, carrying a hundred thousand people in the ship. This third person further instructs their eldest son to look after and guard the ferry: 'Carry all beings who come from this shore to the far shore, for the benefit of many.' Brāhmaṇa! What do you think? Is there any difference between the far shores?"

The brāhmaṇa said: "No, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha asked further: "Brāhmaṇa! What do you think? Is there a difference between the vehicles by which they crossed?"

The brāhmaṇa said: "There is indeed a real difference between the vehicles."

The Buddha said: "Just so, just so. Brāhmaṇa! Between the śrāvaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, and the vehicle of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi there is indeed a difference. Brāhmaṇa! Like the first person who relies on a grass raft to cross from this shore to the far shore, alone and without a second, so should the bodhi of the śrāvaka be understood. Like the second person who relies on a leather sack or leather boat to cross from this shore to the far shore, so should the bodhi of the pratyekabuddha be understood. Brāhmaṇa! Like the third person who builds a great ship and, together with many people, crosses from this shore to the far shore, so should the bodhi of the Tathāgata be understood."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke in verse:

> "The path and liberation are unsurpassed;
> All the vehicles have differences.
> The wise should thus weigh and consider
> And choose the most supreme, the highest vehicle.
> The teachings of all dharmas are like this;
> The Perfectly Awakened One speaks these words.
> Having discerned among the dharmas,
> The superior one should study accordingly."

The brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How should bodhisattva mahāsattvas practice, and how should they abide in mindfulness, to reach the Mahāyāna?"

The Buddha told the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa: "Brāhmaṇa! Listen to this meaning. If bodhisattva mahāsattvas practice in accordance with mindfulness, they shall reach the Mahāyāna. Brāhmaṇa! If a good man or good woman arouses the bodhicitta themselves and also teaches others to arouse the bodhicitta, delights in practice themselves, encourages others to practice and establishes them in it, and further explains the meaning of such sūtras for them, yet such persons do not come to be befriended, served, and attended to, then one should embrace them through the four means of attraction. What are the four? Generosity, kind speech, beneficial action, and shared endeavor."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke in verse:

> "Various kinds of great generosity,
> Giving all that one possesses,
> Wishing to embrace others:
> The bodhisattva is fearless.
> Showing and guiding the path of approach,
> When beings do not come of their own accord,
> With wondrous and kind words
> One should comfort them again and again.
> For the peace and happiness of self and others,
> In the wholesome places where they are born,
> Day and night one constantly follows
> Such sentient beings.
> Teaching the faithless to have faith,
> Establishing the immoral in morality,
> Teaching the miserly to give:
> All this is skillful benefit.
> Teaching people to practice bodhi,
> Firm and ever vigorous,
> Sharing in beneficial work:
> The wise practice as taught.
> Such a wise one
> Is a guide among bodhisattvas.
> What the wise one practices
> Is to always delight in the Mahāyāna Dharma.
> Courageous and most supreme,
> The wise should study this;
> Through that supreme Dharma,
> One reaches the most supreme far shore."

At that time, the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa spoke to the Buddha in verse:

> "O Venerable One, show those practices
> Of the bodhisattva guides;
> I shall study their practice
> And reach the Honored Among Bipeds.
> Explain for me those practices,
> And the basis upon which they rest;
> Bodhi is deep and vast;
> With compassion, please explain."

At that time, the World-Honored One told the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa: "Excellent, brāhmaṇa! Bodhisattvas have three kinds of practice. What are the three? The heavenly practice, the brahma-practice, and the holy practice. Brāhmaṇa! What is the heavenly practice? If a good man or good woman, with compassionate bodily action, compassionate mental action, and compassionate verbal action, fills the immeasurable worlds of the eastern direction with the practice of compassion, and having pervaded those, further enters the south, west, and north, the four intermediate directions, above and below, with compassionate bodily action, compassionate mental action, and compassionate verbal action universally pervading and filling all: this is called the heavenly practice. What is the brahma-practice? It is the four immeasurables. What are the four? Loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. This is called the brahma-practice. Brāhmaṇa! What is the holy practice? It is the three gates of liberation. What are the three? Emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. This is called the holy practice."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke in verse:

> "Courageously practicing with vigor,
> The bodhisattva guides:
> Those who possess the heavenly practice
> Are people who delight in bodhi.
> The holy practice and the brahma-practice:
> These are what the holy ones have taught.
> Those who cultivate these practices
> Are people who attain the immovable."

At that time, the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa spoke to the Buddha in verse:

> "I delight in the profound bodhi
> And now ask the great guide:
> In future times,
> How shall these practices be gathered?
> For the sake of future sentient beings
> I ask the World-Honored One.
> Regarding the bodhi of the Buddha,
> My mind makes no distinction;
> I wish to arouse the aspiration for the path
> To benefit sentient beings."

At that time, the World-Honored One replied to the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa in verse:

> "This sūtra is taught
> To arouse the great bodhi.
> Brāhmaṇa! In the Buddha's bodhi
> There has never been any distinction.
> This sūtra is taught
> To arouse the great bodhi;
> It can sever all doubts,
> Answering the questions of sentient beings.
> This sūtra is taught
> To arouse the great bodhi;
> It can sever all doubts,
> In accord with beings' desires.
> Those who hear this sūtra
> In future lives
> Shall practice great generosity
> And reach the far shore of giving.
> Those who hear this sūtra
> In future lives
> Shall guard the precepts without deficiency
> And reach the far shore of morality.
> Those who hear this sūtra
> In future lives
> Shall practice patience for beings
> And reach the far shore of patience.
> Those who hear this sūtra
> In future lives
> Shall practice vigor for beings
> And reach the far shore of vigor.
> Those who hear this sūtra
> In future lives
> Shall constantly enter the dhyānas
> And reach the far shore of meditation.
> Those who hear this sūtra
> In future lives
> Shall seek supreme wisdom for beings
> And reach the far shore of wisdom.
> Those who have previously made offerings
> And shown compassion for beings
> Shall hear this sūtra,
> And in later lives it shall come into their hands.
> Monks dwelling in the forest
> Who aspire to the Buddha's bodhi,
> Hearing this sūtra,
> Shall afterward be the first to attain it.
> Hundreds of millions of past buddhas
> Have upheld this sūtra
> For the benefit of bodhisattvas,
> To arouse their aspiration and desire.
> If there is a brāhmaṇa
> Who delights in the Buddha's bodhi,
> When at that time faith is obtained,
> This sūtra shall come into their hands.
> I see those sentient beings
> And know all their practices;
> I also know their names,
> And my vision is without obstruction.
> I have not told everything completely,
> Fearing it might confuse future people;
> Fearing they might generate faults,
> Therefore I have taught only a portion."

At that time, the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa spoke to the Buddha in verse:

> "Venerable One, this is well upheld;
> It arouses the vast great aspiration.
> A great hero of this world
> I shall before long become.
> What past and future
> Guides have taught,
> For the sake of producing their benefit,
> I shall abide in bodhi."

At that time, the World-Honored One replied to the brāhmaṇa in verse:

> "Those who abide in this wisdom:
> For whom was it spoken?
> Having known their minds and practices,
> I now shall give them a prophecy.
> Those who hear this sūtra,
> Now present before me,
> In later lives
> This sūtra shall appear before them.
> If there are women
> Who copy this sūtra,
> This sūtra shall come into their hands
> And generate the great bodhi.
> I have already said before:
> Monks who delight in forest-dwelling,
> When this sūtra comes into their hands,
> Shall afterward have it appear before them.
> A monk who hears this sūtra
> Weeps and sheds tears:
> 'What deeds did I do before
> That I gain this benefit in this life?
> Regarding such a sūtra as this,
> I never before gave it proper thought;
> I have already received a prophecy.
> What karma brought this fruit?
> In the past I was a brāhmaṇa
> Who lived depending on a monk;
> At that time the monk was negligent
> And recited this sūtra.
> The brāhmaṇa heard it then,
> And when the time came went out to beg for food,
> Weeping as he walked away,
> At that moment making this vow:
> Regarding this sūtra,
> Its meaning and its words,
> In future lives I shall bear witness to it
> And also protect and guard it.'
> Through the fruit of those good deeds,
> In the very last age of the future,
> He obtains this sūtra
> And holds it in his hands.
> At that time a monk
> Weeps with tear-filled eyes;
> When at that time he repents,
> He later obtains this sūtra-dharma.
> When former karma is exhausted,
> At that time a sign appears:
> In a sleeping dream
> One obtains this sūtra.
> In the cycling of birth and death,
> The deception and great terror,
> It is through Amitābha's
> Vow-power that such fruit arises.
> Monks who break the precepts,
> Despised and looked down upon by others,
> With such manifold faults,
> Cycling through great terror:
> Through hearing this sūtra
> Despite their many evils,
> They shall reach that far shore
> And afterward always shine."

At that time, the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa spoke to the Buddha in verse:

> "This great sūtra
> Arouses the great aspiration for the path.
> For my sake and for the future,
> Please explain it broadly."

At that time, the World-Honored One replied in verse to the brāhmaṇa of the Kāśyapa clan:

> "All the sounds of the future,
> Even what I myself have taught,
> This is a vast sūtra;
> Therefore you should know.
> This great sūtra that is taught
> Shall become an Āgama,
> Shall become a secret treasury,
> What the śrāvakas study.
> What is taught in this place,
> And what other attainers of the path teach:
> This is the mother of all sūtras;
> Brāhmaṇa, know it thus.
> At that time, monks,
> After my passing,
> The Saṃyukta and Dīrgha Āgamas
> And the one called the Madhyama Āgama,
> At that time shall be Āgamas;
> The one complete in number is called Ekottara.
> There shall also be miscellaneous sūtras,
> The casket-treasury spoken by śrāvakas;
> There shall also be the Vinaya
> And also the Abhidharma.
> Whether through the three casket-treasuries
> One gains the name of monk:
> Eighty-four thousand
> Collections of Dharma I have taught.
> All emerge from this;
> It is called the most supreme sūtra.
> Herein the śrāvaka is taught,
> And the solitary awakened one is taught;
> The root of all wisdom,
> An inconceivable sūtra.
> Whatever exists in the world,
> What has not yet appeared in the three realms,
> The root of all merit,
> Comes from arousing the bodhicitta.
> The merits of generosity and morality,
> The practice of patience and vigor,
> The supreme merits of meditation:
> All are well taught in this sūtra.
> The supreme merits of wisdom,
> Liberation, patience, and quiescence:
> All are shown herein;
> All are well taught in this sūtra.
> Suffering, its origin, and the path,
> Cessation appearing herein:
> All dharmas are buddha-dharmas;
> This is what this sūtra teaches.
> The impermanence of all suffering is taught,
> And the dharma of non-self is taught,
> And the peace of nirvāṇa is taught
> Within this sūtra.
> Herein is taught the śrāvaka
> And the causes and conditions of their abiding;
> The Mahāyāna, this sūtra,
> Embraces all dharmas.
> All dharmas are vast and great,
> Contained within the bodhicitta;
> This is the most supreme scripture,
> A universal sūtra.
> To see the buddhas face to face,
> And to hear them teach the Dharma,
> To hear it in their very presence:
> This comes from this sūtra.
> Among the beings of the three realms,
> Few hear this sūtra;
> Having heard it, they give rise to love and joy,
> Seeking the Buddha-vehicle."

The brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa said to the Buddha: "How extraordinary, World-Honored One! If sentient beings lack wisdom and, hearing such an unsurpassed and boundless sūtra, cannot give rise to firm desire for this Dharma, then, Venerable World-Honored One, for what reason do such beings, despite the existence of such a wondrous Dharma, let it pass in vain?"

The Buddha told the brāhmaṇa: "In this great trichiliocosm there are a hundred koṭis of māra-palaces, and each māra has koṭis of māra-retinues surrounding him. Those māras constantly and diligently seek by skillful means to extinguish this sūtra, creating various causes and conditions, and through those conditions they create obstacles wherever this sūtra appears. Why? If all the sentient beings in the great trichiliocosm were to attain the arhat fruit, and yet a good man or good woman hears this sūtra and arouses the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi: brāhmaṇa, for this reason, koṭis of māras diligently seek by skillful means to extinguish this sūtra. Why? Brāhmaṇa! This sūtra is the root and source of the nature of all dharmas. For this reason, koṭis of māras diligently seek by skillful means to extinguish this sūtra."

The Buddha then told the brāhmaṇa: "There is now a sūtra called 'Destroying the Assembly of Māras.' If you uphold, read, and recite it, you can destroy the assembly of the Māra heaven. Brāhmaṇa! It is like when the sun appears and can dispel all darkness and gloom. Just so, brāhmaṇa, when the sūtra 'Destroying the Assembly of Māras' is recited, all the māras are hidden from view. Brāhmaṇa! What is called the sūtra 'Destroying the Assembly of Māras'?" At that time, the World-Honored One spoke the dhāraṇī:

*Tadyathā: anumodha-bodhi-samata-pavana-tad-avahuta-utavahuta-tattra-avahuta niḥsaṅga-māra-parā-bhata-rabhata-hūta-ruṃga-magha-māna-bhiri-mama-su-makhe-ri-bhaga-māvid-arā-māda-rā-krū-ma-ala-mira-ekakṣa-ranā-ayu.*

"Brāhmaṇa! This is the dhāraṇī. The buddhas and world-honored ones of the past, future, and present all teach this sūtra 'Destroying the Assembly of Māras.' Brāhmaṇa! When this sūtra is recited, all the māra-palaces tremble, shake, greatly shake, sway, and greatly sway. All the māras tumble and fall from their seats and cannot speak. Why? Because they constantly bring harm to many, constantly cause suffering and distress to many, and cause them to lose their benefit. For this reason, they now experience such a terrifying retribution. Just as the Buddha, the World-Honored One, always brings joy to all sentient beings, through loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity, all those pāpīyas are made to feel terror. Brāhmaṇa! Furthermore, if anyone can transmit this sūtra on generating the bodhicitta, no obstacle can affect that person. Neither devas, nāgas, yakṣas, humans, non-humans, māras, māra-sons, māra-retinues, water, fire, swords, staffs, evil practitioners, evil beasts, nor any suffering of body or mind can possibly befall them. Why? Because those good men and good women constantly bring benefit and happiness to many, constantly show compassion to many, and protect devas and humans. This should be understood through the power of their compassionate practice. Brāhmaṇa! Because those good men and good women do not perform evil actions of body, do not perform evil actions of speech, and do not perform evil actions of mind, those sufferings do not oppress their bodies, nor do they oppress their minds. Brāhmaṇa! Through this cause and condition, all suffering is extinguished."

At that time, the World-Honored One restated this meaning in verse:

> "The māras are diligent against this sūtra,
> Wishing to destroy and extinguish it;
> Therefore this sūtra
> Is now spoken by the Tamer.
> It causes all the māras to be confused and bewildered,
> Gathered together, sitting and trembling,
> Looking at one another, unable to speak:
> This is the fruit of their evil conduct.
> Because they terrify sentient beings
> And constantly harbor evil intentions,
> They tumble and fall:
> Therefore they receive such present retribution.
> Those who unite compassion and kindness,
> Who speak with joyful hearts,
> When they teach the Dharma impartially,
> All evil intentions are scattered.
> Vanquishing the kings of māras
> And scattering the māra-armies,
> Yakṣas and various ghosts
> Naturally fall of themselves.
> Their force and weapons do no harm;
> Water and fire cannot flood or burn them;
> Spells, curses, and poisons
> Cannot cause them any injury.
> Oppression of body and oppression of mind:
> These have never occurred for them.
> Through the vows they constantly make
> And abiding thus in body and speech,
> Closing the gates to all evil paths,
> Far from all difficulties,
> All māras are utterly destroyed:
> This is taught through this sūtra.
> Skillful wisdom of all dharmas:
> If one wishes to reach the far shore,
> One must hear this sūtra;
> Having heard it, one can study.
> If one can study this sūtra,
> A fearless bodhisattva,
> The most supreme enlightenment of bodhi:
> This is the phrase pointing toward bodhi."

When the Buddha taught this sūtra, the brāhmaṇa Kāśyapa, the great assembly, the gandharvas, devas, humans, asuras, and others, having heard what the Buddha taught, rejoiced and practiced accordingly.
