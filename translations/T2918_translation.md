# Sūtra on the Śākya's Conversion of the Foolish through Contemplation

## *Shìjiā Guānhuà Huányú Jīng* (釋家觀化還愚經)

Taishō 2918

Translator unknown. An apocryphal text.

Long ago in the kingdom of Śrāvastī (*Shèwèi*, 舍衛) there was a poor household. The husband and wife were miserly and wicked, having no faith in the way and its virtues. The Buddha, pitying their foolishness, manifested as a śramaṇa and went to their door to beg for alms. At the time the husband was away. His wife cursed and reviled the monk without any sense of propriety.

The śramaṇa said: "I am a practitioner of the way. I beg for food to sustain myself. I do not mind your curses and abuse. I only hope for one meal."

The housewife said: "Even if you were to drop dead standing there, you could not get food. How much less should you, in full health, expect food from me! Just stop wasting time and leave at once."

Thereupon the śramaṇa stood still before her, rolled up his eyes, exhaled his last breath, and appeared to die standing. His body bloated and swelled. Worms emerged from his nose and mouth. His belly burst, his intestines rotted, and impure fluids flowed everywhere.

The wife, seeing this, screamed in terror and fled in fright. Thereupon the practitioner suddenly vanished. He went several miles away and sat beneath a tree to rest.

When the husband returned home, he met his wife on the road and was surprised at her terror. His wife told him: "A śramaṇa frightened me like this." The husband flew into a rage and asked where the monk was. The wife said: "He has already left, though surely not far."

The husband immediately took up his bow and strapped on his sword and followed the tracks in pursuit. Drawing his bow and unsheathing his sword, he charged straight ahead, intending to cut down the practitioner. The practitioner then transformed, creating a city of lapis lazuli (*liúlí*, 瑠璃) that surrounded him on all sides. The man circled the city several times but could not enter. He asked the practitioner why he would not open the gate.

The practitioner said: "If you wish the gate to open, cast away your bow and sword."

The man thought to himself: "I should comply with his words. Once I get inside, I can strike him with my fists." He then threw down his bow and sword. But the gate still did not open.

He said to the practitioner: "I have already cast away my bow and sword. Why does the gate not open?"

The practitioner said: "I told you to cast away the bow and sword of evil intent within your heart, not the bow and sword in your hands."

At this the man's heart was startled and his body trembled. He realized: "This practitioner is a divine sage who can perceive even my thoughts." He immediately prostrated, knocking his head to the ground in repentance, and said to the practitioner: "I have a wretched wife who failed to recognize a true person and thus turned me into an enemy. Please extend a small measure of compassion and do not abandon me. I wish to bring her here and persuade her to cultivate the way."

He rose and returned home. His wife asked: "Where is the śramaṇa?" The husband explained the practitioner's divine virtue in full and said: "He is there in the countryside. You should go yourself to repent and extinguish your sins."

Thereupon husband and wife went to the practitioner. They prostrated with all five limbs touching the ground in repentance and vowed to become his disciples. Kneeling for a long time, they asked: "Practitioner, your divine transformations and holy attainments are truly extraordinary. You have a city of lapis lazuli, firm and difficult to scale. Your will is bright and your intention is settled, forever free from anxiety and trouble. What way and what virtue have you practiced to bring about such divine wonders?"

The practitioner replied: "I have broadly studied without tiring and upheld the dharma without slackening. Through vigor, maintaining the precepts, and wisdom, I am not lax. Because of these, I have attained the way and arrived at nirvāṇa (*nìhuán*, 泥洹)."

Thereupon the practitioner spoke in verse:

> Much learning, firmly upheld;
> The dharma upheld becomes a wall.
> Vigor is hard to breach or destroy;
> From this, precepts and wisdom are accomplished.
>
> Much learning makes the will bright;
> Once bright, wisdom increases.
> With wisdom one broadly understands meaning;
> Seeing meaning, one practices the dharma in peace.
>
> Much learning can remove sorrow
> And through concentration bring joy.
> Skillfully teaching the sweet-dew dharma,
> One personally knows the attainment of nirvāṇa.
>
> Learning is knowing the dharma and discipline,
> Resolving doubt and perceiving causes.
> Through learning one abandons what is not dharma
> And proceeds to the deathless state.

When the practitioner finished speaking the verses, he revealed the Buddha's radiant form. Blazing and brilliant, the light illuminated heaven and earth. The husband and wife were astonished, their spirits trembling with awe. Transforming their enmity and cleansing their hearts, they struck their heads upon the ground. They destroyed two billion evils and attained the fruit of stream-entry (*xūtuóhuán*, 須陀洹).
