# 大乘四法經釋
## Commentary on the Mahāyāna Sūtra of the Four Dharmas

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1535

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

First, establishing the school: The doctrinal views of the world are of two kinds in total: external and internal. Although the external doctrinal views are many, they do not go beyond two kinds, namely annihilationism and eternalism, as explained extensively in the treatises. As for the internal doctrinal views, during the Great Teacher's lifetime, all studied under one teacher without any differences. After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, the schools of the Great Vehicle and the Small Vehicle divided into many groups. The doctrinal views of the Small Vehicle number twenty-two, as each is explained extensively in the *Samayabhedoparacanacakra*. The doctrinal views of the Great Vehicle are divided into three categories: first, the School of Complete Emptiness of the Supreme Meaning; second, the Consciousness-Only Middle Way; third, Perfect Fusion of Dharma-Nature. The views of these three schools are each explained extensively, as in the *Madhyamaka*, the *Extensive*, the *Hundred*, the *Thirty Verses*, and other treatises. As for this sūtra, there was a bodhisattva named Vasubandhu, whose stage was that of the preliminary practices, who composed a treatise-commentary on it. Therefore one should know that it belongs to the Consciousness-Only Middle Way school.

Second, clarifying the vehicle: The Tathāgata, out of great compassion, receives and guides those lost in confusion. According to the capacities of beings, he established the teachings. Some speak of three, others of five. By "three" is meant: first, the Śrāvakayāna; second, the Pratyekabuddhayāna; third, the Bodhisattvayāna. By "five" is meant these three together with the Devayāna and the Manuṣyayāna. The practices of these five vehicles are extensively distinguished in other sūtras and treatises. As for this sūtra, since it exclusively explains the practices of the bodhisattva in every case, it is Great and not Small. Therefore one should know that it belongs to the Mahāyāna.

Third, clarifying the division: Although the verbal teachings proclaimed by all buddhas, bodhisattvas, and śrāvakas are many, when classified by kind they number twelve divisions, namely sūtra and so forth, with the characteristics of each explained elsewhere. As for this sūtra: since it was proclaimed without request, it belongs to the udāna division. Since "not forsaking the aspiration for unsurpassed bodhi" and the like are the bodhisattva's vast practices, it belongs to the vaipulya division. Since it contains gāthās, it belongs to the geya division. Since it is of definitive meaning, it belongs to the upadeśa division. Since it contains similes, it belongs to the avadāna division. Therefore one should know that it belongs to five divisions.

Fourth, determining the piṭaka: The twelve divisions of teaching described above, when summarized, belong to the three piṭakas. First, the Sūtrapiṭaka: in Sanskrit, *sūtra* means "connecting and stringing together"; what it conveys is the training in samādhi. Because it accords with principle and accords with the faculties of beings, it benefits others. "Piṭaka" means "basket." Second, the Vinayapiṭaka: *vinaya* means "discipline and subjugation"; the training in śīla it contains harmonizes the three modes of action and subjugates evil conduct, thereby bringing benefit. Third, the Abhidharmapiṭaka: *abhidharma* means "that which faces the Dharma"; the training in prajñā it conveys faces toward nirvāṇa and contemplates the Four Noble Truths, thereby bringing benefit. How are the divisions such as sūtra classified within the three piṭakas? The *Abhidharmasamuccaya* states: "Sūtra, geya, vyākaraṇa, gāthā, and udāna belong to the Śrāvaka Sūtrapiṭaka. Nidāna, avadāna, itivṛttaka, jātaka, and their subcategories are called the Vinayapiṭaka. Vaipulya and adbhutadharma belong to the Bodhisattva Sūtrapiṭaka." If solely of definitive meaning, they also belong to the Abhidharmapiṭaka. One should reflect on this as appropriate.

Next, the commentary on the main text of the sūtra is divided into two sections: first, commentary on the sūtra's title; second, commentary on the sūtra proper.

First, the commentary on the title: In all sacred teachings, names are always established according to four kinds: person, place, dharma, and simile. This sūtra is named according to dharma. The word "great" has seven meanings of greatness: first, greatness of object, since the vast realms of difficult and arduous practices expounded in the *Prajñāpāramitā* and other sūtras are the objects of the bodhisattva's contemplation; second, greatness of practice, since one extensively cultivates practices for both self and others; third, greatness of wisdom, since one can understand the twofold selflessness of persons and dharmas; fourth, greatness of vigor, since one practices difficult practices for three incalculable kalpas; fifth, greatness of skillful means, since one abides neither in saṃsāra nor in nirvāṇa; sixth, greatness of activity, since to the very end of saṃsāra one can perform all the activities of the buddhas; seventh, greatness of accomplishment, since one can accomplish the great qualities of the ten powers, fearlessnesses, and unshared dharmas. Through these seven greatnesses it surpasses the two vehicles; hence the word "great." The word "vehicle" means conveyance. Saṃsāra is this shore, nirvāṇa is the far shore, and sentient beings are in the middle current. The four kinds of Dharma-ship described in this sūtra convey sentient beings across the ocean of saṃsāra and bring them to nirvāṇa; hence the name "vehicle." The word "four" is a numeral. Why is the number specified? This is explained in the treatise itself. The word "dharma" means "that which guides and upholds." The four kinds of practice taught in the sūtra below serve as the norms for the bodhisattva. The word "sūtra": in Sanskrit it is *sūtrāṃ*, and it has four meanings: basis, thread, wellspring, and canon. Here we take the meaning of "thread": just as a thread strings flowers so the wind does not scatter them, the teaching strings together meanings so that heterodox views cannot remove them. As the sūtra says, "Upholding the Vinaya is like weaving cloth; with the teaching one gathers living beings and enables them to attain the holy fruits." The teaching is thus named by the metaphor of a thread. If we speak in terms of true meaning, there are five senses: first, like a spring; second, like a chalk-line; third, a garland; fourth, that which gives birth; fifth, that which reveals. If one uses the meanings of the Chinese character for "canon": constancy, normativity, and pathway, because it does not change through past and present, because it determines right from wrong, and because it provides a thoroughfare for understanding principles. The *Mahāyānasūtrālaṃkāra* says: "Indicating place and characteristics, the Dharma and its meaning are called sūtra." This concludes the commentary on the title.
