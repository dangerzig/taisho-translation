# 菩薩投身飴餓虎起塔因緣經
## Sūtra of the Causes and Conditions of Erecting a Stūpa Where the Bodhisattva Offered His Body to Feed the Hungry Tigress

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0172

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Liang dynasty, translated by Fasheng (法盛)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the country of Gandhāra, at the great city of Vaiśravaṇapura, beneath the shade of a mountain cliff north of the city. He was teaching the Dharma for the king, ministers, people, and the devas, nāgas, the eight classes of protectors, humans, and non-humans, converting countless beings. When the teaching was nearly complete, the Buddha smiled, and from his mouth emanated fragrant light. The light was of nine colors, illuminating all countries everywhere; the fragrance spread likewise. At that time, the great assembly saw the light and smelled the fragrance, and all were greatly joyful. The light then circled the Buddha seven times and re-entered his mouth.

At that time, Ānanda straightened his robes, knelt, joined his palms, and said to the Buddha: "Now the World-Honored One has manifested this wondrous auspicious sign. There must surely be a cause and condition, of great benefit, and sentient beings will receive its blessings. May the Honored One of Heaven explain the cause and condition."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "As you say, the secret words of all Buddhas have great causes and conditions behind every sign they manifest. Do you wish to hear?"

Ānanda said: "Yes! O Honored One among the Honored!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Nine kalpas ago in the past, there was no Buddha in the world. There was a great country named Gandhamati. The king was named Gandhaśrī. His queen was named Kṣamāmukhī. The crown prince was named Candanamati. The country was vast and broad, prosperous, peaceful, and richly populated. The people's lifespan was one thousand five hundred years. Due to the prince's merit, the world was at peace. There were no thieves or robbers. The people lived in harmony and did not attack one another. The prince was benevolent, compassionate, intelligent, and wise. He was thoroughly learned in all the scriptures and in the arts and ceremonies of the ninety-six kinds of paths, mastering them all. From a young age, he always delighted in giving, sparing nothing of his body, life, or possessions. He cherished all beings more than a newborn child, and his great compassion covered all equally, without distinction. He was filial to his parents, observing all proper rites.

"At that time, the royal father built a garden not far from the city for the prince. The garden was eight yojanas on each side, planted with rows of flowers and fruit trees, with rare birds and exotic fowl, clean and beautifully adorned. Everywhere there were flowing springs and bathing pools. In the pools there were always utpala flowers, padma flowers, kumuda flowers, puṇḍarīka flowers, and various other kinds of red and white lotus flowers. Peacocks, wild geese, night herons, and mandarin ducks played within them. It was cool, fragrant, pure, and supremely wonderful.

"At that time, the prince, together with his ministers, officials, consorts, and court ladies, with attendants before and behind, went to the garden for seven days of leisure, and then returned to the palace.

"At that time, the poor, orphaned, elderly, and diseased people within the kingdom heard that the prince was returning. They all came and stood along the roadside, stretching out their hands toward the prince. When the prince saw them, he gave away his bodily ornaments, clothing, gold coins, silver coins, carriages, elephants, and horses, distributing everything. By the time he reached the city gate, nothing remained, yet there were still many poor people, and he regretted that he could not provide for them all. The prince returned to the palace and thought of the poor, so grieved that he could not eat.

"The king asked the prince: 'What troubles you?' The prince answered: 'Recently, when I went out for leisure, I saw the poor along the roadside, seeking what they lacked. I gave them all that I had on my person, yet it was still not enough. That is why I grieve. Now I wish to ask the great king to let me distribute the treasures of the central treasury to provide for the whole land. I wonder whether the great king will grant my wish.' The king said: 'The state treasury guards against emergencies and should not be used privately.'

"Therefore the prince's wish was not fulfilled, and his grief doubled. The prince had a minister named Jaya, who saw that the prince was not eating and was filled with sorrow and distress. He knelt, joined his palms, and said to the prince: 'Your servant has ten thousand gold coins, which I offer to your highness. Use them as you wish. Please do not grieve over poverty, and eat as before. If the coins are not enough, I shall sell my own body to serve your highness.' Thereupon Jaya presented the ten thousand gold coins to the prince. The prince sent someone to take the coins outside the city and distribute them to the poor. When all ten thousand were spent, it was still not enough. The messenger returned and reported to the prince: 'The gold coins are exhausted, but there are still many poor people.' The prince then had his attendants count his private stores and obtained another ten thousand gold coins, but even after distributing these to the poor, it was still not sufficient.

"The prince thought to himself: 'The suffering of people all arises from the suffering of poverty and unfulfilled desires. I should now sell my own beloved body to relieve their suffering and bring them ease.' Having reflected thus, he removed his precious garments, put on plain old clothes, and quietly left the palace. He went to another country called Vaitiśa and sold himself to a brāhmaṇa, obtaining one thousand gold coins, which he then distributed to the poor.

"The brāhmaṇa then ordered his slave to take a cart into the mountains, chop firewood, and sell it at the market. After some time, when he went to cut wood again, he found in the mountains a piece of ox-head sandalwood weighing one hundred jin.

"At that time, the king of that country had long suffered from leprosy. Physicians and spells could not cure him. The king grew angry and said: 'What use are physicians? Every illness in the world has a corresponding medicine for its cure. Why is my illness alone without remedy?' He ordered the physicians to be arrested and publicly executed in the marketplace. At that time, one physician kowtowed before the king and said: 'The medicine to cure the king's illness is extremely rare in the world. Though its name is known, I have never seen it.' The king said: 'What is this medicine called?' He answered: 'It is called ox-head sandalwood.' The king said: 'People's merit and misfortune differ according to their deeds. There may be a person of good fortune who possesses this medicine.' He immediately issued a proclamation throughout the land: whoever had this medicine would receive half the kingdom in exchange.

"The brāhmaṇa then called his slave and said: 'In the past, selling firewood brought you only a meager income, but now there is wealth and honor to be had: the king is ill, and he offers half the kingdom for ox-head sandalwood. You should now take this sandalwood and present it to the king. You will surely obtain what you desire, and I will share in this good fortune.' The slave then took the ox-head sandalwood and presented it to the king. When the king received it and ground it to apply to his body, the leprosy was immediately cured. The king was overjoyed, and all the ministers and people of the kingdom shared in the celebration. He summoned his ministers and held a great feast, pardoned prisoners, distributed gifts to the poor, and all rejoiced together, high and low alike. The king ordered his chief minister to divide half the palace, as well as half the people, gold, silver, treasures, coins, grain, silk, servants, carriages, elephants, horses, cattle, and sheep under his domain. He prepared one hundred adorned treasure carriages, one thousand mounted riders, music and entertainment, incense, flowers, banners, jeweled canopies, and a feast of a hundred delicacies to welcome the slave back to the country. The king then invited him to sit together on the jeweled throne, with music and entertainment, food and drink and merriment.

"The king asked the slave: 'I see that your merit, virtue, and bearing are extraordinary in this world. How did you come to be in such a lowly position? I wish to hear your story.' The slave said: 'Very well! Since you wish to hear, I shall tell you. As you suspect, I am not originally a slave. Have you ever heard that the king of the country of Gandhamati has a crown prince named Candanamati, who delights in generosity?' He answered: 'I have heard of him many times, but have never seen him.' He said: 'I am he.'

"When the king heard this, he was even more deeply respectful, and asked: 'How did you come to this?' The prince said: 'I delight in giving. I exhausted the wealth of my country, yet it was not enough. There were still many poor people, and my original vow was unfulfilled. Therefore I left my country and sold myself.' The king said: 'People receive the fruits of their past deeds. Those who cultivate good enjoy happiness; those who do evil receive suffering. This is not something you brought about, nor was it given by your parents. Why then would you diminish the great hope of a nation and risk such danger and hardship? Such a thing is rare in the world. You must have some extraordinary purpose. Please explain your intention.' The prince answered: 'My original vow was to liberate all beings. I practice the pāramitās, aspiring to enlightenment.' The king said: 'Excellent! I deeply rejoice in this.' The prince said to the king: 'I now return the kingdom to you. I ask only one favor, which I hope you will not refuse.' He answered: 'What do you wish?' The prince said: 'I wish to use the coins and treasures from the central treasury to provide for the poor, orphaned, aged, frail, and diseased of the whole world, distributing gifts freely for fifty days. The merit of this I will share with you.' The king said: 'Excellent! The coins and treasures are yours to distribute. The half kingdom I bestowed is your rightful share; I dare not take it back.' The prince said: 'Good! You give me your wealth, and I offer the kingdom back to you. I delight in giving, and you delight in the kingdom. We are different in nature, and our aspirations differ.' The king said: 'Your practice is profound beyond my reach. When you attain the path, I pray you will deliver me as well.'

"The prince then sent envoys to proclaim throughout all the neighboring countries that all who were poor, orphaned, aged, frail, or ill should come and gather. At that time, the prince had the treasuries opened, and the wealth was transported to level ground and distributed to the poor for fifty days. The poor became wealthy, and all rejoiced.

"After the prince had entrusted the kingdom and departed, the ministers were alarmed and wept, reporting to the king: 'Last night the prince suddenly disappeared. We do not know where he is.' When the king heard this, he fell from his throne, faint and unable to recognize anyone. The queen and the consorts, court ladies, ministers, officials, and people were all alarmed and distressed, crying out and weeping, rushing out in four directions to search for the prince. The queen, fearing she had lost her son, was frantic as if mad. She and the consorts, with robes gathered and hair disheveled, ran out of the city, rushing east and west, searching for the prince. The king, fearing the queen's grief might be fatal, took his ministers and prepared his carriage, went out of the city to search for the queen and for news of the prince. Ten li from the capital, in the empty marshlands, he found the queen with several court ladies, beating their chests and weeping, hair tangled and eyes swollen, pushing through tall grasses, searching for the prince. When the king saw this, his grief deepened. He went forward, took the queen's hand, and with tears flowing, counseled her: 'Our son's merit and virtue are great. He is filial and generous, and has no enmity with anyone. He distributed all the wealth of the kingdom, and it was still not enough. He always felt regret that he had nothing left to give. He has surely gone to another country to seek wealth for giving, or has sold himself to provide for the destitute. Let us return to the palace together and do not grieve so. I will send envoys to inquire in all the countries, and we will surely get our son back.' The queen cursed: 'It is because the king was stingy and guarded his wealth, not caring for his son. Can coins now serve as a son?' The king said: 'My fault came first; what good is regret now? Let us return to the palace. I promise we shall not lose our son. I will personally go out in four directions to search, and I will surely bring him back.' The queen said through her tears: 'Now that I have lost my son, what is the use of living? I would rather die here than return empty-handed. I know my son does not care for hunger or thirst, and though he suffers illness, he does not count it hardship. What would I rely on if I return to an empty palace?'

"Then the prince's consort, her hair disheveled and tangled, cried out to heaven and beat the ground, looking in all four directions but not seeing the prince. She cried out to heaven and beat her head, swallowed her tears, and said: 'Heaven and earth, sun and moon, parents and spirits! If I have committed any offense, I now fully repent. May I be reunited with my husband soon.' The king then forcibly led the queen and the prince's consort to the carriage and returned to the palace.

"At that time, the prince was far away in another country. His eyes, hands, and feet twitched three times, and his heart was anxious and fearful, as though he had lost something. He took his leave of the king and returned to his home country. The king ordered his attendants to prepare one hundred adorned treasure carriages, one thousand mounted riders, ten thousand gold coins, and one hundred thousand silver coins. Five hundred of the king's chief ministers each contributed ten thousand gold coins and one hundred thousand silver coins as farewell gifts for the prince. The king and his ministers, numbering ten thousand, escorted the prince to the border. A great assembly was held in joyful parting, and then they bid farewell. The prince thought: 'Since I was young, my feet have never moved without reason, and my eyes have never twitched without cause. When I left the country, I did not take leave of my parents. Surely my parents, ministers, and people are anxious and grieving, fearing they have lost me. I must hurry back so they know I am safe.' He further thought: 'The road is long and far, and I cannot arrive quickly. I fear my parents' sorrow is so heavy that they may lose their lives. What means can I use to send word quickly?' At that time, there was a bird that could speak human language. It said to the prince: 'Your virtue is so great and your kindness so widespread; what need is there to worry? What do you wish to do? I shall help.' The prince answered: 'I wish to entrust you with one task. I hope you will not refuse.' The bird said: 'I accept!' The prince said: 'Please deliver a letter to my royal father.' The bird said: 'We should hurry; now is the time.' The prince wrote a letter and gave it to the bird. The bird carried the letter in its beak and flew to the home country, placing the letter before the king. The king opened and read the letter, learned that the prince was safe, and was greatly overjoyed. He immediately went into the inner palace and said to the queen: 'As I told you, I knew we would not lose our son. Within a few days, we will surely see him again.' When the queen heard this, it was as though she had come back from the dead. She clapped her hands and exclaimed: 'May all people under heaven be safe and happy! May all their wishes be fulfilled, and may their lifespans be without limit!'

"At that time, all the ministers, nobles, men, women, and people, great and small, throughout the country, hearing that the prince was returning, all cried out 'Ten thousand years!' The king, together with his ministers, numbering tens of thousands, prepared his carriage and retinue and went out to welcome the prince. When they met on the road, the prince saw his father, immediately descended from his treasure carriage, went forward, prostrated at his feet, and said to the king: 'This unfilial son has caused your honored self trouble, disturbed the entire kingdom, and begs your forgiveness.' The king said: 'Very good!' Father and son met, with both sorrow and joy mingled together. They returned to the palace, and all the people of the kingdom rejoiced. Poor people from distant countries, hearing that the prince had returned with great wealth, all came from afar to beg from him. The prince had his men carry coins and goods to level ground at the main crossroads and distributed to the poor. For an entire year, not a single day passed without distribution, and all who came from the four directions received what they wished.

"At that time, the royal father and the ministers said to the prince: 'From this day forward, the treasures of the national storehouse are at your disposal. Do not hesitate.' The virtue of generosity was honored far and near, and even enemies and evildoers, hearing of the prince's merit, naturally turned to goodness.

"At that time, there was a sage possessing the five supernatural powers, named Vīrya, who with five hundred disciples dwelt in a great cave on this very mountain, practicing meditation and cultivating the path, aspiring to enlightenment, seeking to overcome all suffering, and teaching all under heaven to cultivate goodness. At that time, Prince Candanamati brought various kinds of food and drink of a hundred flavors up the mountain to make offerings to the sages.

"The sage then blessed the prince and taught him the Dharma. The prince's heart was joyful, and his mind was set on the unconditioned. He did not wish to return to the kingdom. He regarded the palace as a hell, his wife and family as shackles, and the five desires and pleasures as hell. Having reflected thus, he removed his ornaments and fine garments, and entrusted his carriages, horses, and attendants to his ministers, sending them back to the kingdom. The prince then donned a deerskin robe and remained in the mountains, studying the path under the sage, mastering all the teachings.

"The prince's minister returned to the kingdom and reported to the king: 'The prince went up the mountain to make offerings to the sages. He has stayed there to study the path and refuses to return to the palace. He has mastered all the scriptures and arts and insists he will not return.' The king said: 'How bitter this is! People who have sons expect joy, relying on them in old age to benefit the kingdom and ward off calamity. Yet this son of mine has always brought me worry and grief. He does not desire wealth and honor, nor does he cherish his family. What path can such a troublesome child attain?' He summoned his ministers to discuss the matter. The ministers said: 'The prince loves the path and does not covet worldly glory. His mind delights in the unconditioned. Since he will not return, there is nothing we can do. The king should send an envoy to determine his intentions. If he truly will not return, we must make other arrangements.' The king then sent an envoy to ask the prince: 'I await you as a thirsty person longs for water. Why do you remain in the mountains and not return? The queen and consorts weep and gaze down the road, grieving and unable to bear it. A son should bring peace to his parents; you should not persist in this. Please return with the messenger!' The messenger received his orders and conveyed them thus. The prince answered: 'All things are impermanent. The body does not last long. The joy of family, when parted, turns to suffering. Life and death are determined by heaven, and we cannot control them. When impermanence comes, even father and son cannot save each other. I now seek the unconditioned to overcome all suffering. When I attain the path, I will first deliver my parents. This place is not far; I will still come to pay my respects from time to time. My resolve is now fixed. The king should make other plans and establish a new heir.' The reply was brought back to the king, reporting everything. The king then gathered his ministers and appointed a new crown prince.

"Then the queen, together with the prince's consorts and court ladies and their retinue, brought the prince's clothing and ornaments, various sweet fruits, food, drink, incense, flowers, and music, and with attendants before and behind went up the mountain to the prince's dwelling, offering a feast to the assembled sages. The queen then tried to bring the prince back, saying: 'One plants grain against famine, digs a well against thirst, builds walls against enemies, and raises a son to care for one in old age. If you do not return to the kingdom, I cannot survive.' The prince knelt and said to the queen: 'To leave home and dwell in the mountains, to change one's form and clothing, is like saliva that has left the mouth: it cannot be taken back. A recluse has nothing to offer the kingdom. The matter is settled and cannot be changed. I would rather be broken to pieces here than return. I beg my mother to return for now, and I will come to visit before long.'

"The queen and the prince's consorts, seeing that the prince's resolve was utterly firm and that he had no intention of returning, wept in grief and sorrow as they went back along the road. The king, who had only hoped the queen would bring the prince back, went out with his ministers to welcome them, but saw only the queen and the prince's consorts with disheveled hair, beating their chests and crying out as they returned empty-handed. The king's unhappiness deepened. Among the ministers and the multitude of ten thousand, there was none who did not weep. They returned to the palace. The king then consoled the queen and the prince's wife: 'Our son loves the path; such a one is rare in the world. His kindness and charity are universal, and all have received his grace. He is a treasure of this nation, not an ordinary vessel. Now he delights in dwelling in the mountains to cultivate his aspiration. As long as he is safe, we will see each other from time to time. He is not far away. We can send food and drink and maintain communication. This can bring us comfort.'

"The queen, having received the king's counsel, found some relief from her grief. From time to time she sent people with food, drink, sweet fruits, and various delicacies to the mountain to provide for the prince. This continued for many years, and the prince also came down from time to time to visit his parents, and then returned to the mountain to cultivate the path.

"Below the mountain was a sheer cliff with a deep ravine. At the bottom, a tigress had just given birth to seven cubs. At that time, a great snow fell from heaven. The tigress, holding her cubs, had gone many days without being able to seek food, fearing the cubs would freeze to death, so she stayed and guarded them despite her hunger. The snow fell without ceasing. Mother and cubs were starving and near death. Driven by the fire of hunger, the tigress was about to devour her own cubs. At that time, the sages on the mountain saw this and urged one another: 'Who is able to give up his body to save sentient beings? Now is the time.' When the prince heard this, he exclaimed: 'Excellent! My vow is fulfilled!' He went to the edge of the cliff and looked down. He saw the tigress holding her cubs, covered by snow, and a great compassion arose in his heart. Standing atop the mountain, he entered a still and profound samādhi and attained the pure patience of the unborn dharmas. He saw the events of countless past kalpas, and the future likewise. He then returned and said to his teacher and the five hundred fellow students: 'I now give up my body. I pray you all rejoice with me.' The teacher said: 'You have studied the path only a short time. Your knowledge and insight are not yet broad. Why would you suddenly end your life and give up your beloved body?' The prince answered: 'In the past, I made a vow to give up a thousand bodies. I have already given nine hundred and ninety-nine. Today's offering completes the thousand. That is why I give my body. I pray my teacher will rejoice.' The teacher said: 'Your aspiration is lofty and wondrous; none can match it. When you attain the path first, do not forget me.' The prince took his leave of the teacher.

"Then the great teacher and five hundred immortal sages, with eyes full of tears, escorted the prince to the edge of the cliff. At that time, the elder Pūrṇa, with five hundred men and women in his company, had brought food and drink up the mountain to make offerings. Seeing the prince about to give up his body, they wept with sorrow and also followed the prince to the cliff's edge.

"The prince then, before the assembly, made a great vow: 'I now give up my body to save the lives of sentient beings. May the merit of this swiftly bring me to enlightenment, the diamond body, the eternal bliss and purity of the unconditioned Dharma-body. May those not yet delivered be delivered. May those not yet liberated be liberated. May those not yet at peace be at peace. This body of mine is impermanent, a mass of suffering and poisons. This body is impure, its nine orifices always flowing. It is stung and bitten by the four great venomous serpents and pursued and wounded by the five sword-wielding robbers. Such a body is faithless: though one feeds it fine food and the five desires, after death it repays no kindness but instead leads to hell and immeasurable suffering. The human body should only be made to endure hardship, and should not be given pleasure.'

"After the prince had thoroughly rebuked his body for its many faults, he further made this vow: 'I now give my flesh and blood to save this starving tigress. As for the relics and bones that remain, my parents will surely erect a stūpa in the future. May all sentient beings who suffer bodily illness or disease, or whose past karmic conditions prevent them from being cured by medicine, acupuncture, or cauterization, come to this stūpa and make offerings with a sincere mind. According to the severity of their illness, within one hundred days they shall surely be healed. If this is true and not false, may the devas rain fragrant flowers.' In response, the devas rained mandārava flowers, and the earth shook.

"The prince then removed his deerskin robe, wrapped it around his head and eyes, pressed his palms together, and cast himself down before the tigress. The tigress ate the bodhisattva's flesh, and both mother and cubs survived. At that time, the people on the cliff looked down and saw the prince devoured by the tigress, his bones and flesh scattered. They cried out in grief, their voices shaking the mountain. Some beat their chests and threw themselves on the ground, rolling back and forth. Some entered meditation. Some kowtowed and repented before the prince.

"At that time, the devas of the Śuddhāvāsa heavens, the Lord Śakra, the Four Heavenly Kings, and the devas of the sun and moon, numbering tens of millions, all generated the aspiration for supreme enlightenment. They performed music, burned incense, scattered flowers, and offered mandārava flowers to the prince, proclaiming: 'Excellent! O Mahāsattva! Before long, you shall sit upon the seat of enlightenment.' Having proclaimed this three times, they each returned to their heavenly palaces. The five hundred sages all generated the aspiration for the supreme, perfect, true path, and the great teacher of the sages attained the patience of the unborn.

"The next day, the king and queen sent a messenger with food and drink up the mountain for the prince. When they arrived at his usual stone dwelling, they saw only his bedding, deerskin robe, parasol, alms bowl, staff, water bottle, and washing vessel in the room. The prince was nowhere to be seen. They asked everyone around, but no one answered. They only saw the sages in groups of ten or five, weeping and facing one another. When they reached the great teacher, they found him sitting with his hand propping his cheek, eyes full of tears, sighing and moaning. They questioned everyone around, but no one would answer. The messengers, filled with dread, distributed the food to the sages and rushed back to report everything to the queen. The queen asked: 'You did not see my son? Did you see the sages?' They answered: 'We only saw the sages in groups of ten or five, weeping and facing one another.' The queen cried: 'Calamity! My son is dead!' She beat her chest, screamed, and ran to the king. When the king heard, he fell from his throne, faint and unable to recognize anyone. His ministers, numbering ten thousand, gathered at the king's side, kowtowed, and counseled: 'The prince is in the mountains; the truth is not yet known. Why grieve so? May the king rest for a moment.'

"Then the king, the queen, consorts, court ladies, ministers, officials, and people, gathering their robes and going barefoot, ran up the mountain.

"At that time, the elder Pūrṇa also came to meet the king and reported: 'Yesterday the prince cast himself from the cliff and offered his flesh to the tigress. Now only his bones remain, scattered on the ground.' The elder then led the king to where the prince's body lay. The king, the queen, consorts, court ladies, ministers, officials, and people all wailed in grief, their voices shaking the mountain valley. The king and queen threw themselves upon the prince's body, their hearts breaking, fainting and unable to recognize anyone. The consort came forward, held his head, and smoothed the prince's hair, her heart shattered. She wept and cried: 'How cruel my fate, that my lord should be lost to me! Today the parting is forever, and I shall never see him again. I would rather that my own body be ground to dust than that my lord should have perished so suddenly. The prince is dead; what use is my life?'

"Then the ministers said to the king: 'The prince gave his body in his vow to liberate all beings. The demon of impermanence has seized him. Before the body decays, we should make offerings.' They collected the bones, brought them out of the ravine to level ground, and piled up sandalwood, various fragrant woods, incense, ghee and oil, silk canopies, banners, and pennants, and cremated the prince. They collected the relics, placed them in a precious vessel, and right there erected a stūpa of the seven treasures, adorned with various precious objects. On all four sides of the stūpa, for ten li in each direction, they planted rows of flowers and fruit trees, and built flowing springs and bathing pools, beautifully clean and pure. The king permanently stationed performers of the four kinds to make offerings and entertain at this stūpa day and night."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The prince of that time is my present self. The royal father of that time is my present father Śuddhodana. The queen of that time is my present mother Māyā. The consort of that time is now Gopī. The minister Jaya of that time is now Ānanda. The great teacher of the sages on the mountain of that time is now Maitreya. The king of Vaitiśa is now Nanda. The brāhmaṇa of that time is now Rāhula. From long ago, Maitreya Bodhisattva has always been my teacher. But because I practiced giving unstintingly of my body and life to save sentient beings, I surpassed my teacher by a span of nine kalpas, and have now attained Buddhahood to deliver beings without limit."

When the Buddha taught this, eighty-four thousand devas, nāgas, and humans all generated the aspiration for the supreme, impartial path, and eight thousand bhikṣus exhausted their defilements, severed their bonds, and attained the path of the arhat. The king, together with his ministers, devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits, hearing what the Buddha had taught, were all greatly joyful. They bowed to the Buddha and departed.

---

At that time, the king, having heard the Buddha's teaching, erected a great stūpa at that very place, named the "Stūpa Where the Bodhisattva Offered His Body to the Hungry Tigress." It stands to this day. East of the stūpa, at the foot of the mountain, there are monks' quarters, a lecture hall, and a monastery, where five thousand monks always reside. The fourfold offerings were provided by Fasheng.

At that time, I saw that people from various countries who had leprosy, madness, deafness, blindness, lameness, or various other diseases all came to this stūpa. They burned incense, lit lamps, plastered the ground with fragrant clay, cleaned and swept, and kowtowed in repentance. All their illnesses were healed. Those who had been cured departed, and others continually arrived. There were always more than a hundred people, regardless of rank, and this has never ceased.
