# 佛說文殊師利淨律經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Pure Discipline of Mañjuśrī

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0460

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

## Chapter One: The Meaning of Ultimate Truth

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus and thirty-two thousand bodhisattvas. At that time, the World-Honored One, surrounded by an immeasurable retinue of hundreds of thousands, was expounding the sūtras for them.

There was a deva-prince named Tranquil-Sound-of-Discipline, who was in the assembly. He rose from his seat, straightened his garments, knelt, joined his palms, and addressed the World-Honored One: "Where is Mañjuśrī now? All in this assembly, the four groups, the devas, nāgas, ghosts and spirits, Śakra, Brahmā, and the Four Kings, are all longing to behold that true practitioner, to consult him, listen to his wondrous words, and receive the meaning of the sūtras."

The Buddha said: "To the east of here, past ten thousand buddha-lands, there is a world called Jewel Clan. The Buddha there is named Jewel-Flower Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened. He is presently expounding the Way's teachings. Mañjuśrī is there, proclaiming for all the great bodhisattvas what cannot be reached through other means."

The deva-prince addressed the Buddha: "I only wish the Great Sage would extend his compassion and awesome power to cause Mañjuśrī to condescend to come here. Why? Whatever sūtras and Dharma Mañjuśrī expounds, he opens up all entanglements and obstructions, leaving nothing that is not brilliantly clear, surpassing the levels of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. When Mañjuśrī expounds the great Dharma, all Māras are subdued, all those of deviant confusion gain no advantage, and all followers of heterodox paths come to take refuge. Those who are proud no longer harbor self-importance. Those who have not yet aroused the aspiration all arouse the mind for the Way. Those who have aroused the mind for the Way become established in non-retrogression. All who should receive it bow their heads. All who should be guided and governed are firmly held. The Tathāgatas, the Truly Enlightened, also praise him: 'Because of this sage's teaching, the true Dharma is caused to long endure. Setting aside the Tathāgata himself, there is no other honored one with wisdom and eloquence who proclaims the canonical teachings like Mañjuśrī.'"

At this, the World-Honored One, seeing what the deva-prince Tranquil-Sound-of-Discipline had set forth, and for the sake of all beings, sent forth great compassion and emitted from between his eyebrows the light of his hair-mark. This illumination universally shone upon all the three-thousand great-thousand buddha-lands, penetrating and reaching throughout ten thousand buddha-lands. Its great radiance brilliantly illuminated the world of Jewel Clan.

At that time, the bodhisattvas of that buddha-land came before their Buddha and asked: "What spiritual response brings about this auspicious sign?" The Jewel-Flower Tathāgata told the bodhisattvas: "To the west of here, past ten thousand buddha-lands, there is a world called Endurance. Its Buddha is named Śākyamuni Tathāgata, Truly Enlightened, Perfectly Awakened. He is presently expounding the Dharma and has emitted light from between his eyebrows, illuminating ten thousand buddha-lands and shining brilliantly upon this land." The bodhisattvas asked: "World-Honored One, why has he emitted this light?" The Buddha said: "Immeasurable koṭīs of hundreds of thousands of bodhisattvas are assembled in that buddha-land. Śakra, Brahmā, the world-protectors, and the four groups are all eagerly longing for Mañjuśrī, wishing to see him, to consult him on the sūtras and Dharma, and to bow their heads to him. They have all addressed their Buddha, who has sent forth this radiance to invite Mañjuśrī from afar."

The Jewel-Flower Tathāgata told Mañjuśrī: "You should go to that land. The Tathāgata Śākyamuni is longingly waiting for you. The entire assembly impatiently anticipates seeing you, bowing their heads, eager to listen and receive your teaching." Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "I already know the auspicious response of this light."

At that time, Mañjuśrī and ten thousand bodhisattvas bowed to the Jewel-Flower Buddha, circumambulated him three times to the right, and in the time it takes a strong man to bend and extend his arm, vanished from the Jewel Clan land and stood in the empty sky above the World of Endurance, not manifesting his body. Flowers rained down upon the entire great assembly, reaching up to the knees. All in the assembly marveled at this unprecedented sight and addressed the Buddha: "What is this auspicious sign that flowers rain down?"

The Buddha told the sons and daughters of good family: "This is Mañjuśrī, who together with ten thousand bodhisattvas has come as requested. He is in the sky above, raining down these flowers as offerings to the Buddha and the assembly." They all said: "We wish to see Mañjuśrī and the bodhisattvas. If we could personally behold such a true practitioner, it would be a most joyous occasion, rare and hard to encounter." Before they had finished speaking, Mañjuśrī and the ten thousand bodhisattvas immediately manifested their bodies, bowed at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated him seven times to the right, and each through their own awesome spiritual power created great lotus flowers and seated themselves upon them.

The deva-prince Tranquil-Sound-of-Discipline addressed the Buddha: "Please issue a holy instruction, asking Mañjuśrī to expound the Way's transforming teaching. The assembly is eagerly longing to hear his instruction." The Buddha told the deva-prince: "Consult your own mind and feel free to inquire."

Tranquil-Sound-of-Discipline then addressed Mañjuśrī: "What is so extraordinary and surpassing about the Jewel-Flower Buddha's land, that you, sir, delight in dwelling and traveling there?"

Mañjuśrī said: "There one neither arouses craving nor extinguishes it, neither gives rise to anger nor exhausts it, neither builds up delusion nor removes it, neither creates afflictions nor destroys them. Why? What has no arising also has no exhaustion."

He was asked: "In that Buddha's teaching of the Dharma, what does he arouse? What does he extinguish?" The answer: "That which is originally pure has no arising or extinguishing, no producing or ending. Why? The sentient beings of that land understand the meaning of ultimate truth as their foundation. They do not take conditioned arising as primary."

He was asked: "What is meant by 'ultimate truth as the foundation'? What is meant by 'conditioned arising as primary'?" The answer: "In its meaning, there is no arising and no destroying, no characteristic and yet not characteristicless, neither a single characteristic nor apart from characteristics, nor manifesting characteristics. There is no one who sees, and yet it is not without seeing, nor is there careful seeing. There is nothing that ends and no one who can end it. Since there is nothing to end, there is nothing that can be ended. This is the meaning of ultimate truth. Deva-prince, it means that there is no mind. Where there is no original mind, one does not instruct others, does not exist on this shore, does not cross to the other shore, and does not stand in midstream. This is the meaning of ultimate truth. Deva-prince, it means that there are no words or letters, and this is the noble truth. Why? As the Buddha has said: all sounds and voices are entirely false."

The deva-prince then asked: "Is what the Tathāgata teaches not also deceptive?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "What the Tathāgata teaches is neither sincere nor deceptive. Why? The Tathāgata's mind does not abide in either category. Regarding both conditioned and unconditioned dharmas, there are no words. For this reason there is neither sincerity nor deception. What does the deva-prince think? If a transformation created by the Tathāgata were to teach something, would it be real or false?"

He answered: "Neither sincere nor deceptive. Why? The Tathāgata's transformation does not possess the four great elements, nor any actual substance."

Mañjuśrī replied: "Just so, deva-prince! All dharmas are also like the naturally so activity of a transformation. What the Tathāgata understands has no achievement and no abiding. For this reason, the Dharma he proclaims is neither sincere nor deceptive, returning to non-duality."

He was asked: "What is meant by the Tathāgata's teaching of the meaning of ultimate truth?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "The meaning of ultimate truth cannot be spoken. Why? Its import has no words, no speech, and cannot be apprehended."

When this teaching on the meaning of ultimate truth was spoken, five hundred bhikṣus had their outflows exhausted and their minds liberated. Countless thousands of people were freed from dust and stain, and the Dharma-eye of all dharmas became pure for them. Twelve thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas.

## Chapter Two: The Noble Truth

Tranquil-Sound-of-Discipline asked Mañjuśrī: "The meaning of ultimate truth is extremely difficult to understand."

Mañjuśrī answered: "Just so, deva-prince! For the indolent, the meaning of ultimate truth is extremely difficult to understand."

He was asked: "What does it mean for a bhikṣu to be vigorous?"

The answer: "Not cutting off or eliminating anything, not cultivating or realizing anything: this is a bhikṣu's practice of the right meaning. Why? If one says to oneself, 'I cut off this; I eliminate that; I cultivate this; I realize that,' this is the thought of destruction, which is erroneous and negligent, compounded with the totality of all formations. Moreover, to calculate in this way is not right vigor."

He was asked: "What then is right vigor?"

The answer: "When the equality of the originally unborn and the dharma-realm are equal, the five heinous acts are also thus. When the originally unborn and the dharma-realm are equal, the sixty-two wrong views are also thus, as are the dharmas of ordinary beings, the dharmas of learning and non-learning, the dharmas of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, and the Buddha-dharma. All are thus equal, as equal as the Buddha-dharma. The dharmas of birth and death, the dharma of nirvāṇa, craving, afflictions, contention, and delusion are also thus. A bhikṣu who vigorously practices in this way is truly vigorous."

He was asked: "What does it mean that the Buddha-dharma is equal to the meaning of craving and afflictions, and to the affairs of contention and delusion?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Because emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness are equal. Why? Emptiness makes no distinctions and has no diversity. Just as, deva-prince, the emptiness inside a clay vessel and the emptiness inside a jeweled vessel are both equally empty, without any diversity, and cannot be called two: so it is, deva-prince, the emptiness of craving and the emptiness of contention and delusion, up to the emptiness of the Way, are all equally empty without diversity, and cannot be named as two."

The deva-prince then asked: "What does it mean for a bodhisattva to practice the noble truth?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "If a bodhisattva did not practice the true truth, on what basis would he teach the Dharma to śrāvakas? Why? The bodhisattva's practice of truth encompasses much and protects many; the śrāvaka's has no such protection. The bodhisattva's practice of truth is vast and without limit; the śrāvaka's is partial and confined. The bodhisattva's practice of truth protects sentient beings while at the ultimate limit making no realization. The bodhisattva's practice of truth employs skillful means without forsaking either birth-and-death or the gate of nirvāṇa. The bodhisattva's practice of truth broadly contemplates all the dharmas of all Buddhas. Just as, deva-prince, there is a man who secretly abandons his great teacher and flees, running alone without companions, his heart filled with fear. He traverses a vast wilderness and does not dare return. The śrāvaka is like this: his mind is filled with alarm, fearing birth and death, not protecting sentient beings, unable to endure traveling through the afflictions of all lives and deaths, practicing truth alone without protecting the Buddha-Dharma, lacking skillful means, without the companion of wisdom. Is this not so? But just as, deva-prince, that great teacher obtains abundant profit and bears immeasurable precious jewels and rare treasures, which he bestows upon the many merchants as they cross the vast wilderness: the bodhisattva is also like this. Like a great teacher, his accumulated practices are immeasurable, his Way-treasures are without limit. He cultivates great loving-kindness and unlimited compassion. The wisdom of ultimate truth benefits all. His immeasurable eloquence is his proud wealth. He travels from one buddha-land to another, practicing the six perfections without limit and gathering the four kinds of compassionate action. To rescue the endangered and save sentient beings, he returns to birth and death. Through skillful means he practices the noble truth, liberating all who have not been liberated, unraveling all who have not been unraveled, traveling through the three realms walking alone without companions, opening and transforming those who have not yet heard, causing them to enter the Great Vehicle.

"Just as, deva-prince, a dirty, worn-out garment: if one perfumes it with the flowers of the thought-free tree, yellow and white and sumanas, the fragrance will not last long and will soon fade. The śrāvakas' and pratyekabuddhas' practice of truth is thin and slight, just like this. They enter nirvāṇa midway without fulfilling their vows, never reaching the fragrance of the Buddha's morality, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge-and-vision of liberation. They are also unable to subdue the desires of affliction and obstruction.

"Just as, deva-prince, a fine and delicate garment worth a hundred thousand, perfumed for hundreds of thousands of years with celestial and supremely rare jeweled flowers: that garment will always be fragrant, its scent flowing universally, gloriously redolent, never fading. Devas and worldly humans all love and delight in it. The bodhisattva is like this. Through incalculable kalpas he practices the Dharma-fragrance of truth, never fulfilling his vows midway by entering nirvāṇa, but constantly emanating the fragrance of the Buddha's unsurpassed Way: morality, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge-and-vision of liberation. He subdues the desires of affliction and obstruction, traveling among the heavens and in the human world. Devas, nāgas, ghosts and spirits, asuras, gentlemen, and common people all revere and honor him, and those who wish to see him are always broadly saved and liberated."

The deva-prince Tranquil-Sound-of-Discipline then asked Mañjuśrī: "In the land of the Jewel-Flower Tathāgata, the Truly Enlightened, what is the assembly of śrāvakas like?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "They do not rely on devoted faith and do not follow others' teachings. They do not practice within the Dharma and do not destroy the dharma-realm. They are not the eight correct, and they are apart from the eight deviant. They are not stream-enterers, yet they have completely crossed over all the evil destinies of fear. They are not once-returners, yet they come to transform sentient beings. They are not non-returners, yet among all dharmas they neither come nor go. They are not arhats, yet they receive the offerings of the entire three-thousand world system.

"They have not departed from desire, yet they do not see desire as a problem. They have not departed from anger, yet they do not see wrath and resentment as scorching. Toward sentient beings they harbor no harmful intent and no worry. They have not departed from delusion, yet they do not regard ignorance as dangerous. They extinguish darkness and all dharmas. They have not departed from afflictions yet are diligently vigorous, transforming and removing the desires of all sentient beings. Having attained the lofty stage, they have nothing from which they arise, yet they manifest arising in existence. With respect to all thoughts, they open and transform sentient beings. They do not calculate self, others, or lifespan. They receive nothing and abandon nothing.

"They purify the virtue of the World-Honored Ones' offerings from all people and citizens. Without intention or thought they cultivate the foundations of mindfulness. They practice the four right efforts without arising or ceasing. They practice the four bases of spiritual power, with body and mind serene. They follow the five faculties and comprehend the original source of all sentient beings. They practice the five powers and subdue afflictions. They are mindful of the factors of enlightenment and comprehend the wisdom of equality. They purely cultivate the Way's teaching, abandoning deviant paths. They realize the Way's instruction yet do not attain the unconditioned. They travel in quiescent stillness while practicing at the ultimate limit. They contemplate what is to be contemplated and thereby enter the dharma-realm. They extinguish ignorance and exhaust delusion. They arouse holy wisdom, the unsurpassed and true. They remove the three liberations through the gate of transcendence. With the fleshly eye they see all sentient beings.

"With regard to all buddha-lands and the people transformed by all Buddhas, they use the divine eye to see in all five destinies the beings circling through birth and death: the people, worms, crawling creatures, all that walks, breathes, and has form, and where they go when reborn. With the wisdom-eye they discern the minds and thoughts of all sentient beings. With the Dharma-eye they see the three worlds and three ages, and what all living beings can practice. With the Buddha-eye they clearly observe all dharmas. The treasury of the Dharma, the secret scriptures, and the illumination of the holy: with the divine ear they hear from afar the sūtras and Dharma proclaimed by all Buddhas. With the wisdom of non-thought they know and recall the countless kalpas of the past and all that has been experienced. With spiritual powers they travel to immeasurable buddha-lands, pervading them without exception. They exhaust all the outflows yet do not attain the nirvāṇa without remainder.

"They manifest form yet have no material body. They give teachings yet do not employ words or letters. They have thought yet harbor no mental conceptualization. They display countenance and beautiful appearance, adorned with marks and numerous fine features, adorning their bodies with merit, their awesome spiritual power extraordinary with none who can match them. Their renown flows universally, their meritorious deeds are made known, penetrating the three ages without obstruction. Through the fragrance of praising wisdom, they perfume their own bodies. With regard to worldly dharmas, they have no attachment. They are not defiled by afflictions. Harsh speech and coarse words cannot diminish them. Through spiritual penetration they entertain themselves. Through extensive learning without satiation, they proclaim eloquence as a lion's roar. With the light of wisdom they illumine everything, with the sage's brilliant comprehension. As thunder, they eliminate the darkness of all who are blocked, hidden, and ignorant. Their teachings are inexhaustible. They comprehend the power of total retention and what the Buddha contemplates, realms that śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas cannot know.

"They constantly see all Buddhas. Their awakened mind is like the ocean. Their samādhi is like Mount Sumeru. Their patient endurance and gentleness are equal to the earth. Their heroic strength subdues Māra and his retinue and casts aside all heterodox paths. They are at ease like Śakra, lord of the devas. Like Brahmā, their minds are free and without equal. Seeking to compare them, one finds no comparison and no equal. Like empty space, they cannot be likened to anything, pervading everything, entering everything. Deva-prince, you should know that in the land of the Jewel-Flower Tathāgata, the merits of the śrāvaka assembly surpass even what I have praised, and are beyond all reckoning."

When Mañjuśrī spoke these words, five hundred bhikṣus, five hundred bhikṣuṇīs, five hundred upāsakas, five hundred upāsikās, and five thousand deva-princes who had not yet attained realization aroused their minds and addressed the Buddha, the World-Honored One: "We wish to be reborn in the land of the Jewel-Flower Buddha and become śrāvakas there." Mañjuśrī replied: "Sons and daughters of good family! One cannot be reborn in that buddha-land while harboring the mind of a śrāvaka. You should arouse the mind of the great Way, and then you will reach that land." They immediately accepted the teaching and all aroused the aspiration for the unsurpassed, true Way. The Buddha then predicted for them all: "You shall be reborn in that land."

## Chapter Three: Understanding Discipline

The deva-prince Tranquil-Sound-of-Discipline then asked Mañjuśrī: "What is the discipline of the śrāvaka? What is the discipline of the bodhisattva?"

The answer: "Accepting instruction and fearing the difficulties of the three realms, finding them wearisome: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. Protecting beings through the immeasurable turnings of birth and death, urging peace for all people, citizens, crawling creatures, all that breathes and squirms, opening and guiding the three realms and cutting through the net of doubt and the attachment to concepts: this is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Being weary and loathing the accumulation of virtue, becoming lax and unable to advance: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. Establishing meritorious deeds without wearying of any practice, benefiting sentient beings and thereby attaining salvation: this is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Extinguishing the desires of one's own afflictions, which one's own body finds loathsome: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. Combating the afflictions, cravings, and attachments of all sentient beings: this is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Not seeing the mental actions, thoughts, and differing aspirations of all devas: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. With one's eyes seeing the faculties and the destinations of the mind in the three-thousand great-thousand buddha-lands: this is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Only being able to examine the actions of one's own mind: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. Universally seeing the abodes and mental thoughts of all the Buddhas in the ten directions and of all sentient beings: this is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Only illuminating one's own body's dispositions and tendencies: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. Illuminating the practices of all people, worms, and crawling creatures, their mental thoughts, the dwellings of the three realms, each with its own origin and end: this is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Finding it difficult to maintain and protect against all Māras: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. Subduing and transforming all the Māras and their retinue in the three-thousand great-thousand worlds, destroying all Māra-deeds and enabling them to receive the true Dharma: this is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Like shattering a vessel of broken clay or stone that cannot be reassembled: one of small aspiration enters nirvāṇa thus, without advancing to the true and right. This is the śrāvaka's discipline. Like a golden vessel that, though broken, is never discarded but can be reassembled as a precious vessel: the great practitioner manifests cessation, yet his deep-wisdom Dharma-body endures forever, not increasing or diminishing, continuing to manifest in the three realms. This is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Like a great fire burning mountain forests and trees, scorching everything, with birds and beasts fleeing in all directions: one of small aspiration is like this, fearing the difficulties of the three realms and hiding in nirvāṇa. This is the śrāvaka's discipline. Delighting in birth and death, walking alone through the three realms with a mind free from timidity, joyfully entertaining the Way's Dharma-joy, exhorting and transforming sentient beings, like an enjoyable park and garden, with flowers and fruit flourishing and bringing much delight: this is the bodhisattva's discipline.

"Being unable to sever the difficulties of obstruction and entanglement, and having a fixed abode: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. Wearing away all hindrances and afflictions, with never a fixed abode: this is the bodhisattva's discipline. In summary, having limitations, binding oneself, and being accomplished with limited virtue in morality, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge-and-vision of liberation, unable to fully accomplish the boundless great Way: this is the śrāvaka's discipline. Reaching to the profound and distant, with an aspiration like empty space and immeasurable merits, with morality, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge-and-vision of liberation beyond all reckoning: this is the bodhisattva's discipline."

At that time, the World-Honored One praised Mañjuśrī: "Excellent! Excellent! How well you have explained the discipline of all bodhisattvas. Mañjuśrī, listen. I shall use a parable to further clarify, so that this meaning may be broadly and thoroughly understood. Suppose there are two people. One praises the water in an ox's hoofprint. The other rises and praises the vast accumulation of water in the great ocean. What do you think? Could the person who praises the water in the ox's hoofprint go on praising it for long?"

He answered: "The water in an ox's hoofprint is exceedingly little, not worthy of praise."

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! The awesome spiritual power seen in the śrāvaka's discipline is also like this, like the water in an ox's hoofprint, not worthy of praise. And the person who rises to praise the great ocean: how would that be?"

He answered: "Very great, very great, O Deva among Devas! The great ocean is boundless, its limits cannot be fixed, its depth and breadth incalculable."

The Buddha said: "The bodhisattva's discipline should be viewed in this way, like rivers and oceans, beyond all measure."

When the Buddha spoke this, twenty-two thousand people attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas. With one voice they all exclaimed: "We, World-Honored One, shall study this discipline of the bodhisattva and open and guide innumerable people."

The deva-prince Tranquil-Sound-of-Discipline then asked Mañjuśrī: "What discipline does Mañjuśrī study? Do you cultivate the discipline of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha, or the bodhisattva's discipline?"

Mañjuśrī replied: "What does the deva-prince think? The great ocean: what water does it accept? What water does it reject?" He answered: "The great ocean rejects no water."

He replied: "Just so, deva-prince! The bodhisattva's discipline is like the great ocean, which does not refuse filth. All disciplines of the ten directions flow into it. The disciplines of śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and all sentient beings are opened and transformed, and through this discipline one universally travels."

The deva-prince then asked: "Mañjuśrī! What you call 'discipline': what does it mean?" The answer: "What is called discipline means opening, guiding, and transforming craving and afflictions. Hence it is called discipline. It means comprehending desire. Hence it is called discipline."

The deva-prince then asked: "What does it mean to open and guide craving and afflictions? What does it mean to comprehend desire?"

The answer: "When thoughts and concepts calculate a self, when one dwells in views without abandoning delusion, when one does not discard the ignorance that is the root of folly, and when one engages in dualistic action and gives rise to afflictions: discerning all this is what is called 'comprehending desire.' If one cultivates the practice of non-craving thoughts, purely guiding and according with non-self, not abiding in views, abandoning delusion, casting aside the darkness of ignorance and folly, not engaging in dualistic action, so that afflictions do not arise and there is no contention, and having no contention one reaches ultimate peace: this is called the discipline that opens and transforms afflictions.

"Just as, deva-prince, there is a healer who clearly knows the varieties of venomous snakes and uses incantations to remove the poison: so the practitioner, if able to discern the origin and end of afflictions as being without any root or source, is then able to extinguish craving and afflictions."

The deva-prince then asked: "What is the discipline that opens and transforms the origin and end of afflictions?"

The answer: "Regarding the origin and end of all thoughts and concepts: where there are no thoughts, there is no contention. Where there is no contention, there is no attachment. Where there is no attachment, there is no reliance. Where there is no reliance, there is no abiding. Where there is no abiding, there is no agitation. Where there is no agitation, one is ultimately taught and obtains liberation. This is called discipline. If, deva-prince, with the holy wisdom of the worthy and the profound and subtle insight, one comprehends that craving and afflictions are at root empty and illusory, with nothing that actually exists, no permanent lord and nothing they belong to, coming from nowhere and going nowhere, having no location and no direction, neither inside nor outside nor in between, neither accumulated nor with form or image or shape: this is comprehending the root of craving and afflictions."

The deva-prince then asked: "Are afflictions liberated through something real or through something unreal?"

The answer: "Just as there is a person lying asleep who in a dream is bitten by a venomous snake. The person feels pain and cannot bear it, and immediately takes an antidote medicine. The poison is destroyed and the pain ceases. What does the deva-prince think? Was that person truly bitten by a venomous snake, or was it an illusory event?"

He answered: "It was illusory and cannot be called real."

He then asked: "If it was illusory, why was the poison cured by medicine?"

He answered: "The dream was illusory, an empty and unreal dream. Being bitten by the poison was illusory, and the removal of the poison was also thus: there was nothing actually removed."

Mañjuśrī replied: "The sages' understanding of emptiness, and their opening and transforming of all craving and afflictions, is also like this. As the deva-prince asked, 'Is the opening and transforming of craving and afflictions real or unreal?' To comprehend this meaning: just as my body is reckoned to have no body, craving and afflictions truly have no craving or affliction, and it is also like this. If my body were a real body, then craving and afflictions would also be permanent. The reason that afflictions have no afflictions is that my own body has no body. For this reason, there is no one who can open and transform afflictions. Why? All dharmas are entirely quiescent, for they have no arising. All dharmas are serene, for they cannot be grasped. All dharmas are silent, for they have no destination. All dharmas are exhausted, for they have no accumulation. All dharmas are inexhaustible, for they have no arising. All dharmas do not arise, for they have no accomplishment. All dharmas have no accomplishment, for they have no creation. All dharmas have no action, for they have no purpose. All dharmas have no purpose, for they have no self. All dharmas have no self, for they have no master. All dharmas have no master, for they are like empty space. All dharmas have no attachment, for they come from nowhere. All dharmas come from nowhere, for they have no abiding. All dharmas have no abiding, for they receive nothing. All dharmas receive nothing, for they are attached to nothing. Therefore, deva-prince, one is ultimately transformed and this becomes the Dharma-discipline, yet there is nothing actually transformed."

## Chapter Four: The Gate of the Way

The deva-prince then asked: "What is the gate and the foundation of all dharmas?"

The answer: "Thoughts that are not in accord with the Way are the gateway's foundation. Thoughts that cycle through birth and death yet accord with the meaning of the Way are nirvāṇa. Failure to practice vigor is the gate of obstruction. Vigorous practice is the gate of the factors of the Way. Doubt is the gate of the hindrances. Diligent cultivation of liberation is the gate of non-obstruction. Thoughts of conceptual attachment are the gate of afflictions. Non-thought, non-delusion, and non-craving are the gate of freedom from afflictions. Scattered and many thoughts, the gate of false concepts. Quiescent practice is the gate of serenity. The sixty-two views are the gate of pride. Cultivating emptiness and the signless is the gate of non-pride. Following evil friends is the gate of evil wrongdoing. Following good friends is the gate of wholesome dharmas. The affairs of deviant views are the gate of affliction. The meaning of right view is the gate of security. The affairs of stinginess and greed are the gate of poverty. The meaning of generosity is the gate of great wealth.

"Violating the precepts leads to the gate of the evil destinies. Cultivating the prohibitory precepts leads to the gate of rebirth in all good places. Delighting in contention is the gate of losing the Dharma. Practicing patience is the gate of reaching the extraordinary and surpassing. Being indolent is the gate of the mind's defilements. Following vigor is the gate of non-defilement. Self-indulgence is the gate of mental confusion. Single-mindedness is the gate of mental stability. Wrong knowledge and the delusion of foolish darkness are like the gate of oxen and sheep. Cultivating wisdom, with the thirty-seven factors of awakening as the Dharma's foundation: this is the lion's gate.

"To be fully endowed: practicing loving-kindness is the gate of non-harm. Practicing compassion is the gate of a harmonious and refined will, one's nature gentle and free from flattery. Practicing sympathetic joy is the gate of delight in the Dharma's joy. Practicing equanimity is the gate of non-partiality, neither increasing nor decreasing. Practicing the four foundations of mindfulness is the gate of not losing past merit. The four right efforts are the gate of accordance with equality. The four bases of spiritual power are the gate of lightness of mind and body. The five faculties, with the meaning of devoted faith as the foundational gate. The five powers are the gate of not being stained by afflictions and cravings. The seven factors of awakening, having fully comprehended the gate of the wisdom of equality. The Eightfold Path, having abandoned all deviant and heterodox paths: this is the gate beyond delusion.

"Furthermore, deva-prince, consider the bodhisattva's gate as the foundation for all the buddhadharmas. It protects all dharmas: this is the gate of sovereign command over the Dharma. Skillful means comprehend what has a place and what has no place: this is the gate of understanding everywhere. The perfection of wisdom-pāramitā penetrates and knows the mental thoughts of all sentient beings: this is the gate of according with the other shore of liberation. The six perfections gather the six objects of desire and render them without a fixed place: this is the gate of the Great Vehicle. Contemplating and seeking emptiness, the three realms being like illusions and all lives like dreams: this is the gate of wisdom and illumination. All dharmas are originally unborn: the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, clearly penetrating the naturally so, with nothing that is not comprehended, its wisdom depending on no other's illumination."

The deva-prince then asked: "Mañjuśrī! What is the gate of the dharma-realm?"

The answer: "The dharma-realm is the universal gate."

He then asked: "What does the dharma-realm encompass?"

The answer: "What all sentient beings encompass is called the dharma-realm."

He asked: "Does the dharma-realm have any boundary?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "Does empty space have any boundary?"

He replied: "No, Mañjuśrī!"

The answer: "Just as empty space has no boundary, the dharma-realm is likewise without boundary."

The deva-prince then asked: "Can one make distinctions within the dharma-realm?"

The answer: "The dharma-realm cannot be distinguished."

The deva-prince then asked: "Sir! For what reason do you understand and illumine all dharmas so as to be able to comprehend such eloquence?"

Mañjuśrī said: "What does the deva-prince think? An echo that responds to a call, producing a sound: by what understanding of dharmas does it do this?"

The deva-prince replied: "The echo does not understand any dharmas. It is through the coming together of conditions that the echo arises."

He replied: "Just so, deva-prince! The bodhisattva teaches only because of the conditions of sentient beings."

The deva-prince then asked: "Sir! Where do you abide when you teach?"

The answer: "The Tathāgata's transformation abides and teaches. My abiding and teaching is just like this. If one says, 'The Tathāgata's transformation teaches from no abiding,' then just like the Tathāgata's transformation, which teaches from non-abiding, my own proclamation is also like this."

"If, Mañjuśrī, you abide in no dharma whatsoever and yet teach, then where do you abide to accomplish the unsurpassed, true, and right Way, becoming the Most Perfectly Awakened One?"

The answer: "I abide in the five heinous acts and thereby accomplish the unsurpassed, true, and right Way."

He then asked Mañjuśrī: "Where do the five heinous acts abide?"

The answer: "The five heinous acts have no root and no abiding."

He then asked: "Has the Tathāgata not said that one who commits the heinous acts falls into uninterrupted hell?"

The answer: "Just so, deva-prince! As the Buddha has said, one who commits the heinous acts falls into hell. But if a bodhisattva abides in these five heinous acts, he swiftly attains the unsurpassed, true, and right Way. What are the five? If a bodhisattva diligently and wholeheartedly arouses the aspiration for the great Way, departing from the mind of the lesser vehicle without ever falling into the grounds of the śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha: this is the first heinous act. If one arouses the mind of broad generosity, giving away everything one has without any attachment, never associating with stinginess and greed: this is the second heinous act. If one arouses the mind of compassion, vowing 'I shall liberate all sentient beings' without ever flagging midway: this is the third heinous act. If one sees that all dharmas have no arising and immediately attains the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, never again associating with the sixty-two doubts and wrong views: this is the fourth heinous act. If, regarding what should be known and seen, what should be cut off, what should be proclaimed, and what should be awakened to, one knows, sees, and awakens in a single moment of arising thought, penetrating everything without exception and yet abiding nowhere, accomplishing all-knowledge without attachment to the three realms: this is the fifth heinous act."

Mañjuśrī told the deva-prince: "When a bodhisattva abides in these five heinous acts, only then does he swiftly accomplish the unsurpassed, true, and right Way, becoming the Most Perfectly Awakened One."

The deva-prince then asked: "What you have said means that the heinous does not become heinous, and compliance does not become compliance?"

The answer: "If pure gold and a wish-fulfilling jewel fell into filth, would they merge with it?"

He answered: "They would not merge. Why? Because these substances are genuine and do not merge with what is false."

Mañjuśrī said: "The human mind is originally pure. Even if placed amid defilement, there is no blemish, just as the light of the sun does not merge with darkness, and just as a lotus flower is not soiled by mud. It is like empty space, which nothing can pollute. One who wishes to practice the Dharma and arouses the bodhisattva's mind, dwelling amid the heinous acts, is not shaken. To open and transform the heinous is then called compliance. The mind is originally pure and does not merge with defilement. Why? If they merged, they could not be separated again. Water and mud still do not truly merge. How much less would the mind, which is originally pure and formless, merge with the formed?"
