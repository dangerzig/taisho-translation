# 佛說四品法門經
## Sūtra of the Four Categories of Dharma-Gates, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0776

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmabhadra (法賢)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly.

At that time the Venerable Ānanda was alone in a quiet room, and this thought arose in his mind: "All the terrors in the world, and even the disasters, obstacles, illnesses, and faults, whether already arisen or yet to arise, all belong to the foolish. The wise have none of these."

After the Venerable Ānanda had reflected on this matter, he rose from his seat and went to where the Buddha was. Having arrived, he bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, completed his respects, stood to one side, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! While alone in the quiet room, this thought arose in my mind: 'All the terrors and such things in the world belong to the foolish; the wise have none of these.' I pray the World-Honored One will explain this for me."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Listen carefully now, and I shall explain it for you."

Ānanda said: "Yes indeed, World-Honored One! I wish to hear with joy."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! All the terrors and such things in the world, whether already arisen or yet to arise, such as disasters, obstacles, illnesses, and faults, all belong to the foolish; the wise have none of these. Ānanda! It is as if someone piled up dry reeds and they were set ablaze by fire: the terrors and such things of the foolish person are just like this. Ānanda! Thus in past, future, and present worlds, the foolish person has disasters, while the wise person has no disasters; the foolish person has obstacles, while the wise person has no obstacles; the foolish person has illnesses, while the wise person has no illnesses; the foolish person has faults, while the wise person has no faults. Ānanda! You should know that these are the dharmas of the foolish and the dharmas of the wise. Having understood the dharmas of the foolish, one should distance oneself from them and practice the dharmas of the wise. Thus, Ānanda, you should study."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is meant by a foolish person?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "A foolish person is one who is foolish through lack of understanding. The foolish person of the world does not understand the Dharma and is therefore called foolish. What is it that one does not understand? One does not understand the four categories of the dharma-realm. These are: not understanding the dharma of elements, not understanding the dharma of sense-bases, not understanding the dharma of dependent arising, and not understanding the dharma of what is possible and impossible. Ānanda! One who cannot penetrate such dharmas is therefore called a foolish person."

Ānanda said: "World-Honored One! Just so, just so. One who does not understand the four dharmas is called a foolish person. And how is one called a wise person?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "A wise person is one who discriminates among dharmas, skillfully understanding what is right and what is wrong, and is therefore called wise. What does one skillfully understand? One skillfully understands the dharma-realm, also in four categories: skillfully understanding the dharma of elements, skillfully understanding the dharma of sense-bases, skillfully understanding the dharma of dependent arising, and skillfully understanding the dharma of what is possible and impossible. One who can skillfully understand such dharmas is therefore called a wise person."

Ānanda again addressed the Buddha: "One who skillfully understands these dharmas is called wise. What dharma of elements does this wise person understand?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "You ask well, and I shall explain it for you. The dharma of elements has many kinds. These are: the eye-element, the form-element, and the eye-consciousness element; the ear-element, the sound-element, and the ear-consciousness element; the nose-element, the scent-element, and the nose-consciousness element; the tongue-element, the taste-element, and the tongue-consciousness element; the body-element, the touch-element, and the body-consciousness element; the mind-element, the dharma-element, and the mind-consciousness element. These eighteen elements the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are six elements: the earth-element, the water-element, the fire-element, the wind-element, the space-element, and the consciousness-element. These six elements the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are six elements: the joy-element, the pleasure-element, the suffering-element, the equanimity-element, the affliction-element, and the ignorance-element. These six elements the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are six elements: the element of desire, the element of anger, the element of non-anger, the element of killing, the element of non-killing, and the element of renunciation. These six elements the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are four elements: the feeling-element, the perception-element, the formations-element, and the consciousness-element. These four elements the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are three realms: the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm. These three realms the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are three realms: the lower realm, the middle realm, and the upper realm. These three realms the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are three realms: the wholesome realm, the unwholesome realm, and the neutral realm. These three realms the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are three realms: the realm of the learner, the realm of the non-learner, and the realm of neither-learner-nor-non-learner. These three realms the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are two realms: the realm with outflows and the realm without outflows. These two realms the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Furthermore there are two realms: the conditioned realm and the unconditioned realm. These two realms the wise person understands as they truly are.

"Ānanda! Such elements as these the wise person skillfully understands."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Having thus understood the dharma of elements as they truly are, how does the wise person then understand the dharma of sense-bases?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The dharma of sense-bases consists of the twelve sense-bases: the eye-base, the form-base, the ear-base, the sound-base, the nose-base, the scent-base, the tongue-base, the taste-base, the body-base, the touch-base, the mind-base, and the dharma-base. These twelve sense-bases the wise person understands as they truly are."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Having thus understood the twelve sense-bases, how does the wise person then understand the dharma of dependent arising?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The wise person should know that the twelve links of dependent arising arise from conditions. Because of conditions there are the various dharmas. What are these conditions? Ignorance conditions formations; formations condition consciousness; consciousness conditions name-and-form; name-and-form conditions the six sense-entries; the six sense-entries condition contact; contact conditions feeling; feeling conditions craving; craving conditions grasping; grasping conditions becoming; becoming conditions birth; birth conditions old age and death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, and affliction. Thus is assembled one great mass of suffering. If one understands the dharma of dependent arising as described above, one knows that the nature of conditions is empty: when conditions gather, there is existence; when conditions disperse, there is nothing. Because conditioned dharmas are without substance, there are no dharmas. That is: when ignorance ceases, formations cease; when formations cease, consciousness ceases; when consciousness ceases, name-and-form ceases; when name-and-form ceases, the six sense-entries cease; when the six sense-entries cease, contact ceases; when contact ceases, feeling ceases; when feeling ceases, craving ceases; when craving ceases, grasping ceases; when grasping ceases, becoming ceases; when becoming ceases, birth ceases; when birth ceases, old age and death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, and affliction all cease. Thus is extinguished one great mass of suffering. Such dharmas of arising and ceasing the wise person should understand as they truly are."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Having truly understood the dharma of dependent arising, how does the wise person then understand the dharma of what is possible and impossible?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "What is impossible means: if one creates unwholesome karma of body, speech, and mind, yet obtains a pleasant and wholesome result, this is impossible. If one creates wholesome karma of body, speech, and mind, yet obtains an unwholesome result, this is also impossible. What is possible means: if one creates wholesome karma of body, speech, and mind, and obtains a pleasant and excellent result, this is possible. If one creates unwholesome karma of body, speech, and mind, and receives an unwholesome result, this is also possible.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, what is impossible means: if one creates unwholesome karma of body, speech, and mind, and then hopes for a supreme result, and by this cause is born among devas or humans after death, this is impossible. If one creates wholesome karma of body, speech, and mind, yet falls into the evil destinies, this is also impossible. What is possible means: if one creates wholesome karma of body, speech, and mind, and is born among devas and humans, this is possible. If one creates evil karma and falls into the evil destinies, this is also possible.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, for two Buddhas to appear in the world at the same time is impossible. One Buddha appearing in the world: this is possible. For two wheel-turning kings to appear at the same time is also impossible. One wheel-turning king appearing: this is possible. Likewise, for a woman to become a wheel-turning king governing the world, or to become a lord of the Four Heavenly Kings, or a lord of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, or a Great Brahmā King, or to accomplish pratyekabuddha awakening or unsurpassed perfect awakening: all these are impossible. If a man possesses the marks of a great being and is adorned with merit and wisdom, and becomes a wheel-turning king appearing in the world, or even attains unsurpassed perfect awakening: this is possible.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, for a person of right views to kill a father, kill a mother, kill an arhat, disrupt the harmony of the Saṃgha, or shed the Buddha's blood, committing such five grave offenses: this is impossible. For an ordinary, foolish person of wrong views to commit the five grave offenses: this is possible.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, for a person of right views who has received the complete precepts, having violated the precepts, to then slander one's ācārya, saying 'This ācārya does not understand the Dharma of the precepts and furthermore cannot receive and uphold the precepts; I shall find another ācārya from whom to receive and study the precepts,' and then going to other śramaṇas and brahmins, selecting a teacher and finding there a person who, seeing śramaṇas and brahmins engaging in idle discourse, follows those who lack proper deportment, relying on them as one's ācārya, and then seeking quiescence and liberation from the three realms of existence: this is impossible. For a foolish person of wrong views to do such seeking: this is possible.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, for a person who has not severed the root afflictions and the secondary afflictions, and because of these afflictions has diminished wholesome roots, obstructed wisdom, and obscured the truly quiescent dharma of nirvāṇa, for such a person to understand the four foundations of mindfulness: this is impossible. If a person has severed the root afflictions and the secondary afflictions, and skillfully understands the dharma of the four foundations of mindfulness, seeking to progress toward nirvāṇa: this is possible.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, for a person who has not severed the afflictions to skillfully understand the four foundations of mindfulness, to realize the seven factors of awakening, and to progress toward nirvāṇa: this is impossible. If a person has severed the afflictions, understands the four foundations of mindfulness, realizes the seven factors of awakening, and progresses toward nirvāṇa: this is possible.

"Furthermore, Ānanda, for a person who has not severed the afflictions to understand the four foundations of mindfulness, realize the seven factors of awakening, reach the end of suffering, progress toward nirvāṇa, accomplish pratyekabuddha awakening, or even attain unsurpassed, perfect awakening: this is impossible. If one has severed the afflictions, understood the four foundations of mindfulness, realized the seven factors of awakening, reached the end of suffering, progressed toward nirvāṇa, and accomplished pratyekabuddha awakening or even unsurpassed, perfect awakening: this is possible."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Thus the wise person truly understands the dharma of what is possible and impossible. All of you should study it in accord with principle."

At that time Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Hearing such a Dharma as I have never heard before, I have tasted the flavor of amṛta and am deeply gladdened. World-Honored One! What should this sūtra be called, and how should we receive and uphold it?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Four Categories of Dharma-Gates.' It is also called 'The Dharma Mirror.' It is also called 'The Drum of Amṛta.' It is also called 'The Many Elements.' By these names you should receive and uphold it."

At that time the Venerable Ānanda and the great assembly, hearing what the Buddha had taught, were all greatly filled with joy. They received it with faith and practiced accordingly.
