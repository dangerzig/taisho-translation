# 佛說文殊師利行經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Practice of Mañjuśrī

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0471

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, translated by Jñānagupta (豆那掘多)

---

I, Jñānagupta, pay homage to Vairocana Tathāgata, the great ocean of wisdom.

Thus have I heard.

At one time the Bhagavān was dwelling at Rājagṛha on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus. All were great arhats who had exhausted all outflows, were free of all afflictions, possessed the three knowledges and six supernatural powers, were endowed with the eight liberations, and whose wisdom-minds were unobstructed and perfectly pure. Together with these five hundred bhikṣus, each seated cross-legged in their own cell, body and mind tranquil, they were in the correct absorption of samādhi. At that time, Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva, wishing to reveal his own method of practice and enable the assembly to hear and know it for their great benefit, rose first and went from cell to cell in order, observing each one. He then saw the Venerable Śāriputra alone in one cell, his body folded, seated cross-legged, absorbed in samādhi. When Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva saw this, he neither awakened him nor went elsewhere, but continued observing the remaining cells, proceeding in this way until dawn when the sun first appeared. At that time Śāriputra and the five hundred bhikṣus had all emerged from meditation. These bhikṣus, together with other bhikṣus who had come from various directions, all gathered in a great assembly.

Then the World-Honored One, at that time, rose from his seat and walked with body upright, moving at a measured and easy pace like a lion king. He emerged from his own cell, and a seat was spread for him. The entire great assembly surrounded him on left and right, mindful of the World-Honored One with reverence, not daring to step before him. The World-Honored One, seated as the supreme leader among the great assembly, with majestic radiance like a golden mountain, riding on clouds of great compassion, rained down the rain of Dharma.

Then Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva, in the midst of the great assembly, asked the Venerable Śāriputra, speaking these words: "Earlier, when I went observing through all the cells, I saw you alone in one cell, seated cross-legged with your body folded. Were you sitting in meditation at that time, or not?"

The Venerable Śāriputra replied to Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "At that time I was indeed sitting in meditation."

Then Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva further asked Śāriputra: "What do you think? Were you sitting in meditation to remove something not yet cut off, or to further remove something already cut off? Were you meditating by relying on dharmas of past, present, or future? By relying on the five aggregates of form, sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness? By relying on the faculties and consciousnesses of eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind? By relying on the six sense-objects of form, sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas? By relying on the three realms of desire, form, and formlessness? By relying on dharmas that are internal, external, or both? By relying on body, mind, or the name-and-form of body and mind? I have asked you about all these dharmas. You should answer quickly: upon what were you relying when you sat in meditation?"

Then the Venerable Śāriputra replied to Mañjuśrī: "Respected one, I now directly see the dharmas of joyful practice, and I sit in meditation because my mindfulness does not forget them."

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva further asked Śāriputra: "Śāriputra, do there truly exist dharmas that can be directly seen, whose joyful practice can be mindfully not forgotten?"

Śāriputra said: "Respected Mañjuśrī, such dharmas of joyful practice I truly do not see. Respected Mañjuśrī, although I do not see such dharmas of joyful practice, the Buddha, the World-Honored One, once taught the Dharma of tranquil concentration to all the assemblies of śrāvakas. It is this Dharma that I follow and practice."

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva further asked the Venerable Śāriputra: "What dharmas did the Tathāgata teach the assemblies of śrāvakas as tranquil concentration, which you follow and practice?"

Śāriputra said: "Respected Mañjuśrī, there is a bhikṣu who practices by relying on dharmas of the past, future, and present, and who, to put it briefly, practices by relying on dharmas of mind and mental factors. It is this practice that the Buddha, the World-Honored One, taught the assemblies of śrāvakas as tranquil concentration, and this is what I follow and practice."

Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva further asked the Venerable Śāriputra: "You say that the Tathāgata once taught the assemblies of śrāvakas to practice by relying on the three times and on mind and mental factors, and that you follow and practice this. This is not so. Why? Within the past there is presently no Tathāgata. In the future there is presently no Tathāgata. And in the present there is no such Tathāgata. If this is so, then if one seeks the body of the Tathāgata throughout all dharmas, it cannot be found. How then can you say, 'I practice by relying on dharmas of past, future, and present'? Śāriputra, the past limit, the future and present limits: that is not this, and this is not that. Each is separate and distinct. They do not act for one another. There is no location, no reliance, no abiding, and no one who abides. No place of reliance can be found. Furthermore, Śāriputra, if anyone says that within the past, future, or present one can speak of a place of reliance or no place of reliance within true reality, know that such persons are slandering the Tathāgata and incur grave transgressions. Why? True reality has no recollection and no mindfulness, and is also without any falling. It has no form or color, no characteristic or appearance that can be found. Śāriputra, within true reality, dharmas of past, future, and present truly cannot be found. To put it briefly, dharmas of mind and mental factors also cannot be found. Apart from true reality, not a single dharma can be found. Therefore it is called emptiness. Because empty, there are no dharmas and nothing that can be pointed out and explained."

Then the Venerable Śāriputra asked Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva: "Does the Tathāgata not abide in true reality when he teaches the Dharma?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva replied to the Venerable Śāriputra: "Śāriputra, within true reality, what location is there that would allow the Tathāgata to abide in true reality and teach dharmas? Śāriputra, dharmas are fundamentally without self-nature. How could the Tathāgata abide in true reality and then teach dharmas? Not only are dharmas nonexistent; the Tathāgata too is nonexistent. Since both are nonexistent, how can one say that the Tathāgata abides in true reality and teaches dharmas? Why? All dharmas cannot be found. The Tathāgata is likewise truly unfindable. The nature of what is taught is also like this: unfindable within time, unfindable outside of time, and unfindable in both time and non-time. The Tathāgata cannot be made manifest either in the moment of speaking or the moment of not speaking. Why? Śāriputra, the Tathāgata has cut off all paths of language. He is unconditioned, without action, and without any establishment."

Then the Venerable Śāriputra further asked Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Mañjuśrī, given what you have said, who in this situation would be a fitting vessel for the Dharma?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva replied to the Venerable Śāriputra: "Śāriputra, if there is a person who can overturn worldly convention and yet also does not enter nirvāṇa, that person is a fitting vessel for the Dharma. If one can neither realize nor explain dharmas of the past, neither realize nor explain dharmas of the future, and neither realize nor explain dharmas of the present, that person is a fitting vessel for the Dharma. One who has neither the view of afflictions nor the view of purity, neither the view of the conditioned nor the view of the unconditioned, is a fitting vessel for this teaching. One who is without self and without non-self, who in the midst of activity neither grasps nor rejects, is a fitting vessel for this teaching. Such a person, though able to listen and receive, does not take what is said here as a definitive and ultimate teaching."

Then the Venerable Śāriputra further asked Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Given what you have said, how should one practice within this meaning? How should one be instructed to abide?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva addressed the Venerable Śāriputra: "Śāriputra, if within this meaning there were something that could be said, then one could ask, 'How should one be instructed to abide?' Since within this meaning there is nothing to be said and all mental activities are cut off, how can you ask, 'How should one be instructed to abide?'"

Then the Venerable Śāriputra addressed Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Respected Mañjuśrī, this meaning is extremely profound. Within this meaning, few are those who realize it and few are those who can uphold it. Why? All those who are still in training and all the arhats are still bewildered and lost within this realm. How much more so ordinary beings! How could they know and understand this extremely profound meaning?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Śāriputra, the arhats have no ground or stage within this meaning. The reason the arhats have no findable ground to abide in is that because of non-abiding they are called arhats. Because of non-attainment they are called arhats. Because the path of language is cut off, they are called arhats. Because the path of language is cut off, whatever ground the arhat practices has no place of realization. Because there is no place of realization, whatever ground the arhat practices receives its name through the unconditioned. Because it is unarisen, it is called unconditioned. There is no agent and no abode. How then can one say the arhat has a ground of attainment? The arhats are not called arhats by virtue of name, nor by virtue of form. It is only ordinary beings who make false discriminations within name and form. Such name and form are in truth without discrimination. All the arhats know in this way and do not give rise to discrimination. Therefore the arhats are called arhats neither by virtue of name nor by virtue of form. There are no ordinary beings and no dharmas of ordinary beings, no arhats and no dharmas of arhats that can be found. Therefore the arhats do not make discriminations. Because they are without action, there is no place of practice and no agent; this itself is tranquil concentration. One does not make it into existence, does not make it into non-existence, and does not make it into neither existence nor non-existence. If non-action and the unconditioned are unfindable within it, then one attains freedom from all existence. Without mind and without any findable practice, one can be said to be definitively and rightly established in the fruit of the contemplative."

At that time, when Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva spoke thus, five hundred bhikṣus in the great assembly rose from their seats and loudly proclaimed before the World-Honored One: "From now on we need never see Mañjuśrī's body again, nor hear his name. Such a place should be quickly abandoned. Wherever Mañjuśrī dwells, we should never go. Why? How can Mañjuśrī teach that affliction and liberation have one single characteristic?" Having proclaimed this, the five hundred bhikṣus all turned their backs and left the assembly. They further thought: "How is it that we who joyfully study and practice the brahma-conduct within the Dharma the Buddha himself taught have today suddenly heard such wretched Dharma?"

Then the Venerable Śāriputra, seeing this, addressed Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva: "Mañjuśrī, when you teach this Dharma, do you not wish to enable sentient beings to definitively know such Dharma?"

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "Indeed so, indeed so."

The Venerable Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī, if that is so, why did these five hundred bhikṣus rise from their seats, vilify and slander what you taught, and loudly proclaim before the Buddha, 'We need not see Mañjuśrī, nor hear his name. This place should be abandoned. Wherever Mañjuśrī dwells, we should not go.' Having proclaimed this, they left the assembly."

Then Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva praised the Venerable Śāriputra saying: "Excellent, excellent! Śāriputra, you have well spoken of the proclamation of those bhikṣus. Why? Because Mañjuśrī is truly unfindable. If Mañjuśrī is truly unfindable, then he also cannot be seen and cannot be heard. Likewise, that place too should be abandoned. Why? Wherever Mañjuśrī dwells, that place and Mañjuśrī are both without anything at all. What is without anything at all not only should not be approached; it may well be abandoned."

At that time, when Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva spoke thus, the five hundred bhikṣus returned to the assembly and addressed Mañjuśrī: "What the Respected One has taught is not for our sake. How could we understand what you have taught?"

Then Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva praised the bhikṣus saying: "Excellent, excellent! Indeed so, indeed so! The Tathāgata, the World-Honored One, and the assemblies of śrāvakas should act thus within this Dharma: they need not seek to know it. Bhikṣus, within this Dharma you too should act thus: you need not seek to know it, yet also should not refuse to know it. Why? Such Dharma is itself the permanent abiding, also called the dharma-realm. If it is the permanent abiding and the dharma-realm, it is without recollection and without mindfulness. What is without recollection and mindfulness is entirely without realization and without non-realization. What is without non-realization is also not non-realization, not non-recollection, not non-mindfulness. Whoever knows in this way is called the Tathāgata's true śrāvaka disciple, is called the most supreme, and deserves to be called 'worthy of offerings.'"

At that time, when Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva spoke these words, among the five hundred bhikṣus, four hundred attained liberation of mind through the dharmas without outflows. One hundred bhikṣus, increasing in vilification and arousing a mind of slander, in their present bodies fell alive into the great hells.

Then the Venerable Śāriputra addressed Mañjuśrī the Youthful Bodhisattva: "Mañjuśrī, why did you not teach the Dharma in accord with sentient beings? You have caused these one hundred bhikṣus to regress and fall."

Then the World-Honored One addressed the Venerable Śāriputra: "Śāriputra, do not speak thus. Why? Śāriputra, if these one hundred bhikṣus had never heard this profound original Dharma, know that they would certainly have fallen into the great hells and suffered for an entire kalpa. After leaving the hells, only then would they have attained a human body and the human path. Because these bhikṣus heard this original Dharma of profound meaning, the grave evil karma and heavy transgressions for which they should have suffered in the great hells for an entire kalpa have been reduced. They have entered the Great Screaming Hell, and upon being touched once, they will immediately be reborn in Tuṣita Heaven to enjoy celestial pleasures. You should know, Śāriputra, that because these bhikṣus heard this Dharma, their many transgressions were swiftly eliminated, and they received only brief and light suffering. You should know, Śāriputra, that these one hundred bhikṣus will attain the fruit of arhatship among the śrāvaka assembly at the first Dharma assembly when Maitreya Bodhisattva descends to be born and attains the way. They will exhaust all outflows, be free of all afflictions, possess the three knowledges and six supernatural powers, and be endowed with the eight liberations. Both bodily and mental afflictions will be completely exhausted.

"Therefore, Śāriputra, it is better to listen to this original Dharma sūtra with a doubtful mind than to accomplish the four dhyāna concentrations and the four immeasurable minds, or even to fully accomplish the four formless concentrations. Why? Although one who accomplishes such dharmas does not hear this profound original Dharma, they cannot attain liberation from afflictions, from birth, old age, sickness, death, grief, and lamentation. It is out of compassion for such beings that I teach this original Dharma."

Then the Venerable Śāriputra addressed Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "How rare, how rare! Mañjuśrī, you have indeed well taught this original Dharma, wishing to teach and transform all sentient beings."

Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva said: "Śāriputra, true reality neither increases nor decreases. The dharma-realm neither increases nor decreases. The realm of sentient beings also neither increases nor decreases. Why? Such dharmas are merely spoken of in words, with nothing that can be found. That is not this, and this is not that. The self itself is without a self. What place of reliance is there? Therefore, Śāriputra, bodhi is itself liberation. Why? The wisdom of all dharmas has no place of difference; it is neither made nor unmade. Whoever knows in this way is called one who has entered nirvāṇa."

Then the World-Honored One addressed the Venerable Śāriputra: "Śāriputra, indeed so, indeed so, as Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva has spoken. Within true reality there is neither increase nor decrease. The dharma-realm and the realm of sentient beings also neither increase nor decrease. One neither receives afflictions nor receives liberation."

Then the World-Honored One, having spoken these words, wishing to further clarify the true meaning, spoke again in wonderful verses:

> Dharmas of past, present, and future
> are merely words without reality.
> If one is at the place of truth,
> there is one characteristic without distinction.
>
> If without characteristics and without discrimination,
> this itself is having the true characteristic.
> Without characteristics, without discrimination:
> discrimination too is without characteristics.
>
> If one does not make discriminations,
> one does not conceive of nirvāṇa.
> Both of these are the work of Māra;
> the wise should know this.
>
> Within the aggregates, elements, and sense-bases,
> though I teach with names and words,
> there is nothing born and no name-bearer:
> those two return to one characteristic.
>
> When one rouses the mind to discriminate correctly,
> that itself becomes wrong thought.
> Wondrous wisdom is without discrimination,
> because it has the practice of emptiness.
>
> Discrimination has deliberation;
> non-discrimination has no thought.
> Conceptualization itself is characteristics;
> non-conceptualization attains nirvāṇa.
>
> Whoever can know in this way
> is called one of great wisdom.
> Therefore one who exhausts all wisdom
> attains wisdom without discrimination.
>
> Wisdom can speak of wisdom,
> yet what wisdom speaks returns to emptiness.
> Whoever can endure within this
> is called one of great wisdom.
>
> Suppose one fills the three-thousandfold world
> with seven treasures and offers them:
> whoever patiently trusts in this Dharma,
> their merit is supreme.
>
> Suppose throughout a hundred million kalpas
> one perfects the merit of giving, precepts,
> patience, diligence, supernatural powers,
> and analytical eloquence:
> it does not compare to upholding this sūtra.
>
> Whoever upholds this sūtra,
> the Perfectly Enlightened One declares
> that by the power of this sūtra's merit,
> they will all attain buddhahood.

When the World-Honored One spoke the verses of this original Dharma sūtra, ten thousand beings of various kinds became free from dust, removed from defilement, and attained the pure Dharma-eye. Five hundred bhikṣus attained liberation of mind through the dharmas without outflows. Eighty thousand devaputras of the desire realm who had not yet aroused the mind all aroused the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. The World-Honored One then bestowed a prediction upon them: "You will all, in the Constellation Kalpa, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, all bearing the same name: Dharma-Blossom Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, Mañjuśrī the Youthful, the Venerable Śāriputra, the five hundred bhikṣus, devas, nāgas, the eightfold assembly, and all the spirits, having heard what the Buddha taught, rejoiced and put it into practice.
