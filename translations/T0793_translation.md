# 佛說佛醫經
## The Buddha Speaks the Sūtra of the Buddha as Physician

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0793

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhu Lüyan (竺律炎) and Zhi Yue (支越)

---

Within the human body there are originally four kinds of illness. The first is earth; the second is water; the third is fire; the fourth is wind. When wind increases, qi arises. When fire increases, heat arises. When water increases, cold arises. When earth increases, strength becomes excessive. From these four kinds of illness originally arise four hundred and four diseases. Earth corresponds to the body, water to the mouth, fire to the eyes, and wind to the ears. When fire is deficient and cold is excessive, the eyes grow dim.

In spring, during the first, second, and third months, cold is prevalent. In summer, during the fourth, fifth, and sixth months, wind is prevalent. In autumn, during the seventh, eighth, and ninth months, heat is prevalent. In winter, during the tenth, eleventh, and twelfth months, there is both wind and cold. Why is cold prevalent in spring? Because all things are coming to life; as cold emerges, cold is prevalent. Why is wind prevalent in summer? Because all things flourish and bloom, and yin and yang gather together; therefore wind is prevalent. Why is heat prevalent in autumn? Because all things ripen and mature; therefore heat is prevalent. Why is there both wind and cold in winter? Because all things come to their end and heat departs; therefore there is wind and cold.

During the third, fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh months, one may lie down to sleep. Why? Because wind is prevalent and the body is relaxed. During the eighth, ninth, tenth, eleventh, twelfth, first, and second months, one should not lie down to sleep. Why? Because cold is prevalent and the body contracts.

In the three months of spring there is cold; one should not eat wheat or beans, but should eat rice, ghee, and various warming foods. In the three months of summer there is wind; one should not eat taro, beans, or wheat, but should eat rice, milk, and curds. In the three months of autumn there is heat; one should not eat rice or ghee, but should eat fine grain, parched meal, honey, millet, and other such things. In the three months of winter there is wind and cold, and yang arises as yin gathers; one should eat rice, peas, broth, and ghee. Sometimes when one lies down, wind arises and then subsides. Sometimes when one lies down, fire arises and then subsides. Cold arises and then subsides.

People fall ill from ten causes and conditions. The first is sitting too long without eating. The second is eating without moderation. The third is worry. The fourth is exhaustion. The fifth is sexual indulgence. The sixth is anger. The seventh is restraining the bowels. The eighth is restraining the bladder. The ninth is suppressing upward wind. The tenth is suppressing downward wind. From these ten causes and conditions, illness arises.

The Buddha said: "There are nine causes and conditions by which those whose lives should not yet end come to a premature end. The first is eating what should not be eaten. The second is eating without measure. The third is eating unfamiliar food. The fourth is not allowing food to be digested. The fifth is suppressing what should be released. The sixth is not keeping the precepts. The seventh is associating with evil companions. The eighth is entering towns at improper times and not conducting oneself properly. The ninth is not avoiding what should be avoided. By these nine causes and conditions, human life comes to a premature end.

"Eating what should not be eaten means eating what is disagreeable, or eating out of accord with the four seasons, or eating again when one has just eaten. This is eating what should not be eaten. Eating without measure means not knowing moderation, eating excessively beyond one's fill. This is eating without measure. Eating unfamiliar food means eating at irregular times, or when traveling to another country, not knowing the local customs of food, and not gradually easing into the diet. This is eating unfamiliar food. Not allowing food to be digested means eating more food before the previous meal has been digested, or after taking purgative medicine, eating before the purging is complete. This is not allowing food to be digested. Suppressing what should be released means not going immediately when the need for bowel or bladder arises, or suppressing belching, flatulence, and other such urges. This is suppressing what should be released. Not keeping the precepts means violating the five precepts. In the present world, stealing or violating others' wives can lead to being apprehended by the authorities, where one may be punished, executed, beaten to death, starved to death, or perhaps released only to be killed by an enemy, or one may die of terror and anguish. This is not keeping the precepts. Associating with evil companions means that when others commit evil, it comes to involve oneself as well. Why? Because one has not separated from evil companions. Evil people do not consider that one will share their punishment. This is associating with evil companions. Entering towns at improper times and not conducting oneself properly means going about at dawn or dusk, when there may be ghosts contending and fighting, or officers in pursuit whom one does not avoid, or entering others' homes and looking at what should not be looked at, listening to what should not be listened to, doing what should not be done, and thinking what should not be thought. This is entering at improper times and not conducting oneself properly. Not avoiding what should be avoided means not avoiding unruly cattle, horses, rabid dogs, venomous snakes, insects, water, fire, pits, wells, runaway carts, galloping horses, drawn swords, drunkards, and evil people, and so forth. This is not avoiding what should be avoided.

"By these nine causes and conditions, human life ends before its time. The wise should recognize and avoid these. Having avoided them, one gains two blessings: first, one obtains long life; and second, one is able to hear the good words of the path and to practice the path for a long time."

The Buddha said: "There are four kinds of food. The first is the food of thinking of one's children. The second is the food of three hundred spears. The third is the food of worms hatching from leather. The fourth is the food of disaster.

"The food of thinking of one's children means that when a person craves the flavor and eats meat, one should reflect and reckon: 'This meat is all from beings who in my past lives were my father, mother, brothers, wife, children, and relatives, and it is because of this that I have not escaped birth-and-death!' Having attained this understanding, one ceases to crave. This is the food of thinking of one's children.

"The food of three hundred spears means that each mouthful of food follows taste but is accompanied by the thought of its consequences, which are innumerable. If one can cease to be attached to taste, one can attain liberation. Furthermore, a spear thrust at a person destroys the body. Having given rise to such thought and thinking further of the innumerable sufferings one must undergo, this is the food of three hundred spears.

"The food of worms hatching from leather means that when a person craves taste and also worries about the myriad things and household affairs, these cravings bore through one's mind, and the mind produces ten thousand schemes to go out and seek. This is the food of worms hatching from leather.

"The food of disaster means that the entire course of birth-and-death consists entirely of disaster-food, like fire burning all things. Everything a person does will eventually come back to torment the body, more severely than fire burning all things; therefore it is called disaster. The reason these are called 'food' is that they are what the mind craves and feeds upon; therefore they are called food.

"A person eating meat is like eating one's own children. All animals have been my father, mother, brothers, wife, and children, innumerably. Furthermore, there are six reasons not to eat meat: first, one must not kill by oneself; second, one must not order another to kill; third, one must not be complicit in killing; fourth, one must not watch killing; fifth, one must not hear killing; sixth, one must not suspect that killing was done on one's behalf. If these six intentions are absent, one may eat meat. If one does not eat it, it is because of these six suspicions. A person who is able to refrain from eating meat obtains the blessing of being free from terror."

The Buddha said: "Excessive eating brings five offenses. The first is much sleepiness. The second is much illness. The third is much lust. The fourth is being unable to recite the sūtras. The fifth is much attachment to the world. Why is this? A person of greed understands the flavor of sensuality. A person of anger understands the flavor of aggression. A person of delusion understands the flavor of food. The Vinaya says: 'A person who craves flavor, craving flavor upon flavor, is reborn without obtaining fine flavor.'"

The Buddha said: "Eating once a day is for the purpose of severing birth-and-death. Yet one still follows craving and cannot practice the path. It is for the sake of gaining the divine eye that one comes to know for oneself where one has come from and where one will go to. A person who does not contemplate death, who eats excessively and constantly thinks of women, falls into the hundred and forty evils. All of this is on account of food. Committing the ten evils, in a future life one loses the human form and falls among the animals. Having then obtained a human body, one suffers hunger and thirst until blood flows, anger and irritation arise toward other beings, and from within attachment desire is born. The Buddha says that great merit belongs to one who, being hungry oneself, gives food to another so that the other's life is sustained. This is great merit. In a future life one will have abundant food and drink. If one lacks anger, yet has nothing to give, and gives yet does not obtain the result, but merely follows the mind in craving and lust, and has nothing to give, merely obtaining what the mind desires: this is not what belongs to me. From a single coin upward, one must not take what is not given, for acting on craving amounts to nothing but tormenting oneself and creating offenses. A practitioner of the path should have no worry or sorrow. Worry follows anger; sorrow follows craving. We all have a year of death, a month of death, a day of death, an hour of death. Yet we do not know it, do not fear it, do not practice the path, and do not keep the precepts. We run east and west, worrying about copper and iron, worrying about fields, houses, and servants. This only increases people's vexation and deepens their suffering. It is the planting of the habits of the animal realm."

The Buddha said: "People pursuing a livelihood are like bees making honey. They gather from many flowers, toiling and laboring for days until it is complete, and then someone comes and takes it away. They have toiled in vain, unable even to provide for themselves. People pursue things, thinking and worrying; whether they have them or lack them, there is hunger, thirst, toil, and hardship. They accumulate wealth, and before they die, the five sharers divide it: water, fire, thieves, officials, and illness. Most things do not go as one wishes. After one dies, others obtain it all. One's own body must bear the karmic consequences, and the poisonous suffering is beyond words.

"The five sharers are: first, fire's share; second, water's share; third, thieves' share; fourth, the officials' share; and fifth, the share of poor relatives. How can one be free from worry over possessions? A person who does not reckon with these five sharers' worries, whose suffering is extreme yet who does not let go: such worry and suffering have ten thousand forms, knotting up within the belly, keeping one far from the path and distant from the Dharma.

"When a person according to Dharma conducts trade and makes a profit, one should not be elated. When one does not make a profit, one also should not worry. All of this is brought about by the destiny of past lives. A person who has craving, because of that craving, does not obtain the benefit. Even if one obtained all the wealth in the world, one could not boldly use it for oneself; nor would it follow one when one departs. It only increases people's bonds, only brings suffering and vexation, only plants the causes and conditions of future lives. Causes and conditions are like fire: fire spares nothing from burning. We are not aware of this. The wise do not dare to recklessly stir, knowing it only increases suffering and plants offenses."
