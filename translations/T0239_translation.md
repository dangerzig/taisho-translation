# 佛說能斷金剛般若波羅蜜多經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0239

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yìjìng (義淨)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the great city of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus, as well as a great assembly of bodhisattvas. At that time, in the early morning, the World-Honored One put on his robe and took up his bowl and entered the city to beg for food. Having begged in sequence, he returned to his original place. When the meal was finished, he put away his robe and bowl, washed his feet, sat cross-legged on the previously arranged seat in proper posture, and dwelt in right mindfulness. Then the bhikṣus came to the Buddha's place, bowed at his feet, circumambulated him three times to the right, and withdrew to sit to one side.

At that time, the Elder Subhūti, who was in the great assembly, receiving the Buddha's spiritual power, rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms reverently, and addressed the Buddha: "How rare, World-Honored One! How rare, Well-Gone One! The Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, is able to benefit all bodhisattvas with the supreme benefit, and able to entrust all bodhisattvas with the supreme entrustment. World-Honored One! If there are those who have set out on the bodhisattva vehicle, how should they abide? How should they practice? How should they bring their minds under control?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "Excellent! Excellent! So it is, so it is! As you have said: 'The Tathāgata benefits all bodhisattvas with the supreme benefit, and entrusts all bodhisattvas with the supreme entrustment.' Subhūti! Listen attentively and apply your mind most thoroughly. I shall explain it for you in detail. Those who have set out on the bodhisattva vehicle should abide thus, practice thus, and bring their minds under control thus." Subhūti said: "Yes, World-Honored One! We delight in hearing."

The Buddha told Subhūti: "Those who have set out on the bodhisattva vehicle should give rise to this thought: 'All the various kinds of sentient beings, whether born from eggs, wombs, moisture, or transformation; whether with form or without form, with perception or without perception, neither with perception nor without perception, throughout all the worlds: all such beings as these, I shall cause to enter the nirvāṇa without remainder and bring them to final liberation.' Though immeasurable sentient beings are thus caused to realize perfect quiescence, not a single sentient being enters perfect quiescence. Why? Subhūti! If a bodhisattva has the concept of sentient beings, then he is not called a bodhisattva. Why is this? Because of having the concept of a self, the concept of sentient beings, the concept of a lifespan, and the concept of further seeking a destiny.

"Furthermore, Subhūti! A bodhisattva should practice giving without abiding in anything. Without abiding anywhere, one should practice giving. Without abiding in form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas, one should practice giving. Subhūti! When a bodhisattva gives in this way, one should not even abide in the concept of marks. Why? By giving without abiding, the accumulation of merit is immeasurable.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the space of the eastern direction be known and measured?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One!" "Can the space of the south, west, north, the four intermediate directions, above, below, all ten directions be known and measured?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One!" "Subhūti! The merit obtained by a bodhisattva who gives without abiding is equally unknowable and immeasurable."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the Tathāgata be observed by means of complete and supreme marks?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! One should not observe the Tathāgata by means of supreme marks. Why? The Tathāgata says that supreme marks are not supreme marks."

"Subhūti! All that has supreme marks is entirely illusory. If there are no supreme marks, then there is no illusion. Therefore, one should observe the Tathāgata through supreme marks as markless." Subhūti said: "World-Honored One! In a future age, in the last five hundred years, when the true Dharma is perishing, will there be sentient beings who, hearing this sūtra, give rise to true faith?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "Do not say such a thing as 'Will there be sentient beings in a future age, in the last five hundred years, when the true Dharma is perishing, who hear this sūtra and give rise to true faith?' Subhūti! In the age to come, there will be bodhisattvas endowed with morality, endowed with virtue, endowed with wisdom. Those bodhisattvas will not have served and made offerings to merely one Buddha and planted good roots. They will have practiced service and planted good roots with immeasurable hundreds of thousands of Buddhas. Such people will be able to give rise to a single thought of faith regarding this sūtra.

"Subhūti! The Tathāgata fully knows and fully sees such people. Those bodhisattvas will produce and gather immeasurable accumulations of merit. Why? Because those bodhisattvas have no concept of a self, no concept of sentient beings, no concept of a lifespan, no concept of further seeking a destiny.

"Those bodhisattvas have no concept of dharmas, no concept of non-dharmas, no concept, and no non-concept. Why? If those bodhisattvas had a concept of dharmas, they would have grasping at a self, at sentient beings, at a lifespan, at further seeking a destiny. If they had a concept of non-dharmas, they would also have grasping at a self, at sentient beings, at a lifespan, at further seeking a destiny. Subhūti! Therefore, a bodhisattva should not grasp at dharmas and should not grasp at non-dharmas. For this reason, the Tathāgata's hidden meaning is proclaimed in the Dharma-gate of the raft analogy: those with wisdom should abandon even dharmas, how much more non-dharmas.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Has the Tathāgata realized anything regarding unsurpassed bodhi? Is there any dharma that he teaches?" Subhūti said: "As I understand the meaning of what the Buddha has said, the Tathāgata has truly realized nothing regarding unsurpassed bodhi, and there is nothing he teaches. Why? The dharma the Buddha teaches cannot be grasped and cannot be spoken. It is neither a dharma nor a non-dharma. Why? Because all noble ones are manifested through the unconditioned."

"Subhūti! What do you think? If a good man or good woman filled the three-thousand great-thousand world system with the seven treasures and gave them as alms, would the merit obtained be great?" Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One! Why? This accumulation of merit is not an accumulation; therefore the Tathāgata says it is an accumulation of merit, an accumulation of merit."

"Subhūti! If a good man or good woman filled the three-thousand great-thousand world system with the seven treasures and gave them as alms; yet if there were another person who could receive and uphold even a single four-line verse from this sūtra, and who himself upheld it and explained it for others, the accumulation of merit produced by this cause and condition would be far, far greater than the former, immeasurable and countless. Why? Subhūti! The unsurpassed, perfect enlightenment of all Tathāgatas comes forth from this sūtra. All Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, are born from this sūtra. Therefore, Subhūti! The Buddha-Dharma: the Tathāgata says it is not the Buddha-Dharma; this is called the Buddha-Dharma.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can a stream-enterer have the thought 'I have attained the fruit of stream-entry'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Why? A stream-enterer has no dharma to enter; therefore he is called a stream-enterer. He does not enter form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas; therefore he is called a stream-enterer. World-Honored One! If a stream-enterer had the thought 'I have attained the fruit of stream-entry,' then there would be grasping at a self, at sentient beings, at a lifespan, at further seeking a destiny."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can a once-returner have the thought 'I have attained the fruit of once-returning'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Why? Because there is not even the slightest dharma that realizes the nature of once-returning; therefore it is called once-returning."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can a non-returner have the thought 'I have attained the fruit of non-returning'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Why? Because there is not even the slightest dharma that realizes the nature of non-returning; therefore it is called non-returning."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can an arhat have the thought 'I have attained the fruit of arhatship'?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Because there is not even the slightest dharma called 'arhat.' World-Honored One! If an arhat had the thought 'I have attained the fruit of arhatship,' then there would be grasping at a self, at sentient beings, at a lifespan, at further seeking a destiny. World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has said that I have attained the abiding of non-contention and am foremost. World-Honored One! I am an arhat free from attachment to desire, yet I have never had the thought 'I am an arhat.' World-Honored One! If I had the thought 'I have attained arhatship,' the Tathāgata would not say that Subhūti has attained the abiding of non-contention, that he is foremost. Because there is nowhere to abide at all, it is said that I have attained the abiding of non-contention, attained the abiding of non-contention."

"Subhūti! What do you think? When the Tathāgata was formerly with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, was there any dharma that could be grasped?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! When the Tathāgata was with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, there was truly nothing that could be grasped."

"Subhūti! If a bodhisattva says 'I shall accomplish the adornment of buddha-lands,' this is a false statement. Why? The adornment of buddha-lands: the Tathāgata says it is not adornment; for this reason it is called the adornment of buddha-lands. Therefore, Subhūti! A bodhisattva, without abiding in anything, without abiding anywhere, without abiding in form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas, should give rise to the mind. One should give rise to a mind that does not abide in anything; one should give rise to a mind that does not abide anywhere; one should give rise to a mind that does not abide in form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas.

"Subhūti! Suppose there were a person with a body like Mount Sumeru, the king of mountains. What do you think? Would that body be large?" Subhūti said: "Very large, World-Honored One! Why? That great body the Tathāgata has called a non-body. Because it is non-existent, it is called a body."

"Subhūti! What do you think? If there were as many Ganges Rivers as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, would the sand in all those rivers be many?" Subhūti said: "Very many, World-Honored One! The rivers alone would already be countless, not to mention the sand."

"Subhūti! I now tell you truthfully: if there were a person who filled world systems as numerous as those sands with treasures and offered them to the Tathāgata, would the merit obtained be great?" Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One!"

"Subhūti! If there were another person who received and upheld a single verse from this sūtra and also explained it for others, this accumulation of merit would surpass the former, immeasurable and boundless.

"Subhūti! In whatever land this Dharma-gate is found and explained for others, even as few as four lines of verse, you should know that this place is a caitya. All devas, humans, and asuras should circumambulate it to the right and pay reverence. How much more so for one who can fully receive, uphold, read, and recite it! You should know that such a person has accomplished the most supreme, foremost, and rare dharma. Moreover, in that place the Buddha is present, together with honored disciples.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Is there any dharma at all that the Tathāgata teaches?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! There is not the slightest dharma that the Tathāgata teaches."

"Subhūti! Are the motes of dust in the three-thousand great-thousand world system many?" Subhūti said: "Very many, World-Honored One! Why? All the motes of dust: the Buddha says they are not dust; therefore they are called motes of dust. All these worlds: the Buddha says they are not worlds; therefore they are called worlds."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the Tathāgata be observed by means of the thirty-two marks of a great man?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! One should not observe the Tathāgata by means of the thirty-two marks. Why? The thirty-two marks: the Buddha says they are not marks; therefore they are called the marks of a great man."

"Subhūti! If there were a man or woman who gave as many lives as there are sands in the Ganges as alms; yet if there were another person who received and upheld a single verse from this sūtra and also explained it for others, that person's merit would surpass the former, immeasurable and countless."

At that time, Subhūti, having heard this sūtra spoken, deeply understood its meaning and purport. He wept with tears and addressed the Buddha: "How rare, World-Honored One! Since the time I first developed wisdom, I have never heard such a profound sūtra as this. World-Honored One! What should this sūtra be called? How should we receive and uphold it?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "This sūtra is called the Prajñāpāramitā. It should be upheld in this way. Why? The Buddha teaches that prajñāpāramitā is not prajñāpāramitā."

"World-Honored One! If there is a person who, hearing this sūtra, gives rise to the concept of reality, you should know that such a person is the most supreme and rare. World-Honored One! This concept of reality is not a concept of reality; therefore the Tathāgata calls it the concept of reality, the concept of reality. World-Honored One! That I hear this sūtra and give rise to faith and understanding in the mind: this is not rare. If in a future age there are those who hear this sūtra and are able to receive and uphold it, such persons shall be the foremost and most rare. Why? Those persons have no concept of a self, no concept of sentient beings, no concept of a lifespan, no concept of further seeking a destiny. Why is this? World-Honored One! The concept of a self, the concept of sentient beings, the concept of a lifespan, the concept of further seeking a destiny are all not concepts. Why is this? Because all Buddhas and World-Honored Ones are apart from all concepts."

"Subhūti! So it is, so it is! If there is a person who, hearing this sūtra, is not alarmed, not frightened, not afraid, you should know that such a person is the foremost and most rare. Why? Subhūti! This most supreme pāramitā is the pāramitā spoken by the Tathāgata. What the Tathāgata speaks is what countless Buddhas also proclaim; therefore it is called the most supreme pāramitā.

"Subhūti! The Tathāgata teaches that the patience pāramitā is not the patience pāramitā. Why? In the past, when King Kaliṅga severed my limbs, I had no concept of a self, no concept of sentient beings, no concept of a lifespan, no concept of further seeking a destiny. I was without such concepts, and also not without concepts. Why is this? If I had had such concepts, I would have given rise to anger and hatred. Subhūti! I further recall the past, for five hundred lifetimes, when I was the sage of patience. During those times, I had no such concepts as these. Therefore, one should leave behind all concepts and set out with the mind for unsurpassed bodhi. One should not abide in form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas, but should give rise to one's mind abiding in nothing at all. One should not abide in dharmas, and one should not abide in non-dharmas, but should give rise to one's mind. Why? If there is any abiding, that is not abiding. Therefore the Buddha says: 'A bodhisattva should practice giving without any abiding.'

"Subhūti! A bodhisattva, for the benefit of all sentient beings, should give in this way. This concept of sentient beings is not a concept; those sentient beings are not sentient beings. Why? Because all Buddhas and Tathāgatas are apart from all concepts. Subhūti! The Tathāgata is one who speaks truly, speaks things as they are, does not speak deceptively, and does not speak inconsistently.

"Subhūti! The dharma the Tathāgata has realized and the dharma he teaches: these are neither real nor false. Subhūti! If a bodhisattva's mind abides in things when giving, it is like a person entering darkness, who can see nothing. If one gives without abiding in things, it is like a person with eyes seeing all manner of colors in the bright light of the sun. Therefore, a bodhisattva should practice giving without abiding in things.

"Subhūti! If there are good men and good women who are able to receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra and explain it for others, the Tathāgata, with the wisdom-eye, fully knows and fully sees such persons, who will produce and gather immeasurable accumulations of merit.

"Subhūti! If a good man or good woman, in the morning, gave as many bodies as there are sands in the Ganges; at midday again gave as many bodies; and in the evening again gave as many bodies; giving one's body in this way for immeasurable hundreds of thousands of millions of billions of kalpas: yet if there were another person who, hearing this sūtra, did not give rise to slander, that person's merit would surpass the former, not to mention copying, receiving, upholding, reading, reciting, and explaining it for others.

"Subhūti! This sūtra possesses inconceivable, immeasurable, boundless merit. The Tathāgata has spoken it for those who have set out on the Great Vehicle and for those who have set out on the Supreme Vehicle. If there is a person who can receive, uphold, read, recite, and broadly explain it for others, the Tathāgata fully knows and fully sees that such a person will accomplish immeasurable, inestimable, inconceivable accumulations of meritorious deeds. You should know that such a person bears on their shoulders the Tathāgata's unsurpassed bodhi. Why? Subhūti! Those who delight in lesser dharmas are attached to the view of a self, the view of sentient beings, the view of a lifespan, the view of further seeking a destiny. For such persons to be able to read, recite, receive, and uphold this sūtra is impossible.

"Subhūti! In whatever place this sūtra is found, you should know that this place is a caitya, and all the world's devas, humans, and asuras should reverently worship it, circumambulate it, and make offerings with incense and flowers to that place.

"Subhūti! If a good man or good woman receives, upholds, reads, recites, and explains this sūtra, and at that time is disparaged by others, why is this? Subhūti! You should know that in previous lives this person created evil karma that should have caused him to fall into the evil destinies. Through being disparaged in this present life, this is a good thing, for it exhausts the evil karma and swiftly leads to bodhi.

"Subhūti! I recall the past, beyond countless kalpas, before the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, when I encountered eighty-four hundred million nayutas of Buddhas and made offerings to and served every one of them without any lapse. Yet if there is a person in the last five hundred years, when the true Dharma is perishing, who can receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra, understand its meaning, and broadly explain it for others, the merit obtained, compared with my former merit, would surpass it: the former could not reach even a hundredth, a thousandth-of-a-myriad-of-a-hundred-millionth part, nor could it be reached by calculation, force, comparison, number, cause, or even analogy. Subhūti! If I were to fully describe the merit of receiving, upholding, reading, and reciting this sūtra, some who heard it might go mad, confused and disbelieving. Subhūti! You should know that this sūtra is inconceivable, and those who receive and uphold it should look forward to the inconceivable accumulation of merit it produces."

Again, Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If there are those who have set out on the bodhisattva vehicle, how should they abide? How should they practice? How should they bring their minds under control?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "Those who have set out on the bodhisattva vehicle should give rise to this thought: 'I shall liberate all sentient beings and cause them all to enter the nirvāṇa without remainder. Though there are such immeasurable sentient beings who realize perfect quiescence, not a single sentient being realizes perfect quiescence.' Why? Subhūti! If a bodhisattva has the concept of sentient beings, then he is not called a bodhisattva. Why is this? Subhūti! In truth there is no dharma that can be named 'one who has set out on the bodhisattva vehicle.'

"Subhūti! What do you think? When the Tathāgata was with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, was there any dharma that he realized?" Subhūti said: "When the Tathāgata was with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, there was no dharma that he realized to attain bodhi."

The Buddha said: "So it is, so it is! Subhūti! In truth there is no dharma by which the Tathāgata, when with the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, had any realization to attain great bodhi. If he had realized a dharma, the Buddha Dīpaṃkara would not have given me the prediction: 'Young man! In a future life you shall become a Buddha, named Śākyamuni.' Because there was nothing attained, the Buddha Dīpaṃkara gave me the prediction that I would become a Buddha named Śākyamuni. Why? Subhūti! 'Tathāgata' is another name for the true nature of suchness.

"Subhūti! If one says 'The Tathāgata realized unsurpassed, perfect enlightenment,' this is a false statement. Why? In truth there is no dharma by which the Tathāgata realized unsurpassed, perfect enlightenment. Subhūti! The dharma of perfect enlightenment attained by the Tathāgata is neither real nor unreal. Therefore the Buddha says: 'All dharmas are the Buddha-Dharma.' Subhūti! 'All dharmas, all dharmas': the Tathāgata says they are non-dharmas; therefore the Tathāgata says all dharmas are the Buddha-Dharma.

"Subhūti! It is like a man whose body is tall and large."

Subhūti said: "World-Honored One! What the Tathāgata calls 'a great body' is called a non-body; this is called a great body."

The Buddha told Subhūti: "So it is, so it is! If a bodhisattva says 'I shall liberate sentient beings and bring them to quiescence,' then he is not called a bodhisattva. Subhūti! Is there any dharma at all called 'bodhisattva'?" He answered: "No, World-Honored One!"

"Subhūti! Therefore the Tathāgata says: 'All dharmas are without a self, without sentient beings, without a lifespan, without further seeking a destiny.'

"Subhūti! If a bodhisattva says 'I shall accomplish the supreme adornment of buddha-lands, supreme adornment of buddha-lands,' the Tathāgata says this is not supreme adornment; therefore the Tathāgata says it is called supreme adornment. Subhūti! If there is one who believes and understands that all dharmas are without nature, all dharmas are without nature, the Tathāgata says such a one is truly called a bodhisattva, a bodhisattva.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata have a fleshly eye?" Subhūti said: "So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a fleshly eye."

"Does the Tathāgata have a divine eye?" "So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a divine eye."

"Does the Tathāgata have a wisdom eye?" "So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a wisdom eye."

"Does the Tathāgata have a Dharma eye?" "So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a Dharma eye."

"Does the Tathāgata have a Buddha eye?" "So it is, World-Honored One! The Tathāgata has a Buddha eye."

"Subhūti! What do you think? If there were as many Ganges Rivers as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and world systems as numerous as all those sands, would that be many?" Subhūti said: "Very many, World-Honored One!"

"Subhūti! Among all the sentient beings in these world systems, with their various natures and dispositions, the flow and turning of their minds: I fully know all of this. Why? Subhūti! The dhāraṇī of the mind: the Tathāgata says it is without grasping. Because there is no grasping, the mind flows and turns. Why? Subhūti! The past mind cannot be obtained; the future mind cannot be obtained; the present mind cannot be obtained.

"Subhūti! What do you think? If a person filled the three-thousand great-thousand world system with the seven treasures and gave them as alms, would that person's merit be great?" Subhūti said: "Very great, World-Honored One!"

"Subhūti! If this accumulation of merit were truly an accumulation, the Tathāgata would not say it is an accumulation of merit, an accumulation of merit.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Can the Tathāgata be observed through a complete physical body?" "No, World-Honored One! One should not observe the Tathāgata through a complete physical body. Why? A complete physical body, a complete physical body: the Tathāgata says it is not complete; therefore it is called a complete physical body."

"Subhūti! Can the Tathāgata be observed through complete marks?" "No, World-Honored One! One should not observe the Tathāgata through complete marks. Why? All complete marks: the Tathāgata says they are not complete marks; therefore the Tathāgata says they are called complete marks."

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata have this thought: 'I teach the Dharma'? You should not have such a view. If one says 'The Tathāgata has some dharma that he teaches,' that is to slander me. Why? 'Teaching the Dharma, teaching the Dharma': there is no dharma that can be taught; this is called teaching the Dharma."

Subhūti addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! In a future age, will there be sentient beings who, hearing this Dharma spoken, will give rise to a mind of faith?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "Those who give rise to faith are neither sentient beings nor not sentient beings. Why? 'Sentient beings, sentient beings': the Tathāgata says they are not sentient beings; this is called sentient beings.

"Subhūti! What do you think? When the Buddha attained unsurpassed, perfect enlightenment, was there any dharma at all that he realized?" Subhūti said: "In truth there is no dharma that the Buddha realized."

The Buddha told Subhūti: "So it is, so it is! In this there is not the slightest dharma that can be obtained; therefore it is called unsurpassed, perfect bodhi. Subhūti! This dharma is equal, with nothing high or low; therefore it is called unsurpassed, perfect bodhi. Through no-self, no-sentient-being, no-lifespan, no-further-seeking-a-destiny, its nature being equal, it is called unsurpassed, perfect bodhi. All wholesome dharmas are perfectly awakened to; therefore it is called unsurpassed, perfect, right awakening. Subhūti! Wholesome dharmas: the Tathāgata says they are non-dharmas; therefore they are called wholesome dharmas.

"Subhūti! If all the Mount Sumerus in the three-thousand great-thousand world system were gathered into a heap of the seven treasures, and a person gave this as alms; yet if another person received and upheld even a single four-line verse from this sūtra, whether himself receiving and upholding it or explaining it for others: comparing the former accumulation of merit with this accumulation of merit, even dividing the latter into a hundredth part, the former could not equal even one part, or a thousandth, a hundred-millionth, a calculable part, a force part, a numerical part, a causal part, nor could even an analogy equal one part.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Does the Tathāgata liberate sentient beings? You should not have this view: 'The Tathāgata liberates sentient beings.' Why? There has never been a single sentient being whom the Tathāgata has liberated. If there were sentient beings for the Tathāgata to liberate, the Tathāgata would have a view of self, a view of sentient beings, a view of a lifespan, a view of further seeking a destiny. Subhūti! The grasping at 'I' and the like: the Tathāgata says it is not grasping, yet foolish ordinary people falsely engage in this grasping. Subhūti! Foolish ordinary sentient beings: the Tathāgata says they are not sentient beings; therefore they are called foolish ordinary sentient beings.

"Subhūti! What do you think? Should the Tathāgata be observed through complete marks?" "No, World-Honored One! One should not observe the Tathāgata through complete marks."

"Subhūti! If one observes the Tathāgata through complete marks, then a wheel-turning sage-king would be a Tathāgata. Therefore, one should not observe the Tathāgata through complete marks. One should observe the Tathāgata through marks being markless."

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke a verse:

> "If one sees me through form,
> or seeks me through sound,
> that person has arisen in a wrong view
> and cannot see me.
> One should contemplate the nature of the Buddha-Dharma,
> which is the guide's Dharma-body.
> The Dharma-nature cannot be known through perception;
> therefore such a one cannot comprehend."

"Subhūti! 'For those who have set out on the bodhisattva vehicle, is their dharma one of annihilation?' You should not have this view. Why? For those who set out on the bodhisattva vehicle, their dharma is not lost.

"Subhūti! If there were a man or woman who filled world systems as numerous as the sands of the Ganges with the seven treasures and gave them as alms; yet if there were another person who, regarding the principle of non-self, within the dharma of non-arising, attained patient understanding, the accumulation of merit produced would be far, far greater than the former, immeasurable and countless.

"Subhūti! A bodhisattva should not grasp at the accumulation of merit." Subhūti said: "Does a bodhisattva really not take the accumulation of merit?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "One should rightly take it; one should not take it transgressively. Therefore it is called taking.

"Subhūti! If one says 'The Tathāgata comes, goes, sits, or lies down,' that person does not understand the meaning of what I have said. Why? Subhūti! There is absolutely no coming or going; therefore he is called Tathāgata.

"Subhūti! If there were a man or woman who ground the earth of the three-thousand great-thousand world system into ink-dust: Subhūti! What do you think? Would that collection of minute particles be many?" Subhūti said: "Very many, World-Honored One! Why? If the nature of that collection were real, the Tathāgata would not call it a collection of minute particles, a collection of minute particles. Why? The collection of minute particles: the World-Honored One says it is not a collection of minute particles; therefore it is called a collection of minute particles. World-Honored One! The Tathāgata's three-thousand great-thousand world system is called a non-world; therefore it is called the three-thousand great-thousand world system. Why? If the world were real, the Tathāgata would have a grasping at composites. The Buddha says grasping at composites is not grasping at composites; therefore it is called grasping at composites."

"Subhūti! This grasping at composites is a worldly expression. Its essential nature truly cannot be spoken of; it is only a false grasping by foolish ordinary beings.

"Subhūti! If one says 'The Buddha teaches the view of a self, the view of sentient beings, the view of a lifespan, the view of further seeking a destiny': is this correct speech or not?" Subhūti said: "No, World-Honored One! Why? If there were a view of self that the Tathāgata teaches, it would be a non-view; therefore it is called a view of self."

"Subhūti! Those who have set out on the bodhisattva vehicle, regarding all dharmas, should know thus, see thus, and understand thus. One who understands thus should not even abide in the concept of dharmas. Why? Subhūti! The concept of dharmas, the concept of dharmas: the Tathāgata says it is not a concept; therefore it is called the concept of dharmas, the concept of dharmas.

"Subhūti! If there were a person who filled immeasurable, countless world systems with the seven treasures and gave them as alms; yet if there were another person who could receive, uphold, read, and recite even four lines of verse from this sūtra, making them well understood, and broadly explained their meaning correctly for others, the accumulation of merit produced by this cause and condition would be far, far greater than the former, immeasurable and countless. How should one explain it correctly? There is no dharma that can be spoken; this is called correct explanation."

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke a verse:

> "All conditioned dharmas
> are like stars, cataracts, lamps, illusions,
> dew, bubbles, dreams, lightning, clouds.
> One should contemplate them thus."

When the Bhagavān had finished speaking this sūtra, the Elder Subhūti and all the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, the bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, and all the world's devas, humans, and asuras, all rejoiced greatly, believed, accepted, and put it into practice.
