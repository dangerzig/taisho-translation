# 佛說放鉢經
## Sūtra of the Released Bowl, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0629

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

The Buddha was in Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery, together with bodhisattvas, innumerable bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, heavenly kings, Śakra, Brahmā, asuras, ghosts, spirits, nāgas, and all manner of human and non-human beings, innumerable, all gathered and seated. The Buddha taught the dharma of the bodhisattva: through innumerable kalpas of diligent hardship in the hells, among animals and pretas, and through all manner of toil, he practiced generosity in the ten directions, giving gold, silver, precious treasures, carriages, horses, servants, even his wife, children, head, eyes, and flesh, sparing nothing, giving to people of the ten directions because of their sufferings. At that time, two hundred deva-sons from the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, who in former lives had practiced the bodhisattva path but whose resolve was not yet firm, having heard at the Buddha's place about the diligent hardship of seeking the Way, all thought the Way was extremely difficult to attain, and their minds turned to seeking the paths of the arhat and the pratyekabuddha. The Buddha knew the intentions of these deva-sons were about to turn, and so he conjured a person of unparalleled beauty, who came bearing food of a hundred flavors to the Buddha's place, knelt, joined his palms, bowed, and said: "May the Buddha compassionately accept this food." The Buddha then accepted it.

A bodhisattva sitting in the assembly before the Buddha, named Mañjuśrī, said to the Buddha: "One should remember the kindness of old." All the bodhisattvas in the assembly heard this and asked one another: "Mañjuśrī! What kindness did he bestow upon the Buddha in a former life, that he now also wishes to receive the Buddha's food?" The Buddha then released the bowl to the ground, and it descended into the earth, passing all the way down to the buddha-land of the Buddha Raivīraya, in the land called Padmaśa. The bowl hung suspended in space, visible. The bodhisattvas of that land saw it, rose, knelt, and said to the Buddha: "Where has this suspended bowl come from, that it neither falls to the ground?" That Buddha said: "Wait a moment, and you shall see the awesome spiritual transformations of a bodhisattva."

At that time, the Buddha Śākyamuni said to Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "Go and search for the bowl." Mahāmaudgalyāyana immediately entered eight thousand samādhis and passed through eight thousand buddha-lands searching, but did not find it. He returned and said to the Buddha: "I searched for the bowl but have no idea where it is."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "You go and search for it." Śāriputra entered ten thousand samādhis and descended through ten thousand buddha-lands searching for the bowl, but did not obtain it. He returned and said to the Buddha: "I descended through ten thousand buddha-lands searching for the bowl and could not find it at all."

The Buddha then directed Mahākāśyapa to go search for the bowl. Mahākāśyapa entered twelve thousand samādhis and descended through twelve thousand buddha-lands searching for the bowl, but did not obtain it. He returned and said to the Buddha: "I searched for the bowl but could not find it at all."

Śāriputra said to the Bodhisattva Maitreya: "Benevolent One! Your great talent is supreme, your merit is already complete, your wisdom is fully furnished, and you are the next to become a Buddha. You should know where the bowl is."

The Bodhisattva Maitreya said to Śāriputra: "Although I am next to become a Buddha, with merit complete and practice fulfilled, I am not equal to the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī. Suppose all the things and plants of ten directions and Ganges-bank-sand-grain buddha-lands, and bodhisattvas as numerous as all of those, were to try to know what the Buddha contemplates in a single step: they could not know. Mañjuśrī knows the profound samādhi. Only Mañjuśrī can know where the Buddha's bowl is."

Śāriputra then rose, went before the Buddha, knelt with palms joined, and said: "May the Buddha direct the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī to go search for the bowl."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Go and retrieve the bowl."

Mañjuśrī thought to himself: "Śāriputra shall see that without rising from my seat I will bring the bowl back." He then entered samādhi. Just as when the sun rises its radiance illuminates everything, so when a bodhisattva enters samādhi, he reaches all places throughout the ten directions. Mañjuśrī extended his hand from within his kaṣāya downward, passing through ten buddha-lands. From each joint of his fingers he emitted ten million rays of light. At the tip of each ray of light was a lotus flower, and upon each lotus flower sat a bodhisattva, all appearing as Mañjuśrī. In the lower lands where there were Buddhas, the bodhisattvas upon the lotus flowers each conveyed the voice of the Buddha Śākyamuni to pay respects to those Buddhas, and also conveyed the voice of Mañjuśrī to bow to those Buddhas from afar.

Mañjuśrī's hand reached all the way to the buddha-land of the Buddha Raivīraya. The bodhisattvas of that land said to the Buddha: "What is this hand? We can see neither its edge nor its limit." The Buddha Raivīraya said to the bodhisattvas: "Above, past innumerable buddha-lands, there is a land called Sahālokadhātu. Its Buddha is named Śākyamuni. Before him sits a bodhisattva named Mañjuśrī, the most honored and radiant, whose wisdom is unmatched. He creates transformations such as this." The bodhisattvas said to the Buddha: "We now wish to see the Buddha Śākyamuni and Mañjuśrī together."

The Buddha Raivīraya then emitted billions of rays of light from his forehead, illuminating the innumerable buddha-lands between them, reaching all the way to the Śākyamuni land. The bodhisattvas asked the Buddha: "Now there is a great fiery light and smoke appearing. In a moment fire will come forth. Is this the fire of the hells?" The Buddha said: "Do not speak thus. This is not the fire of the hells. The land of Śākyamuni is called Sahālokadhātu; this is the fire of that world." The bodhisattvas asked the Buddha: "Why is the land called Sahālokadhātu, and why is there this fire?" The Buddha said to the bodhisattvas: "Sahālokadhātu is a land mixed with evil and the three poisons: lust, anger, and delusion." The bodhisattvas said to the Buddha: "The bodhisattvas of Sahālokadhātu who practice patience without anger: is that not admirable?" The Buddha said to the bodhisattvas: "The people of Śākyamuni's land curse, abuse, belittle, and beat the bodhisattvas. Yet the bodhisattvas endure with patience and never give rise to anger. They compassionately pity the people of the ten directions and wish for their liberation. All this is the effect of the bodhisattvas' awesome spiritual power and the grace of their patience. Therefore the land is called Sahā." The bodhisattvas said to the Buddha: "We rejoice greatly to hear this, and we are grateful not to have been born in the wretched place of Śākyamuni's land." The Buddha Raivīraya said to the bodhisattvas: "Do not speak thus. You cannot understand the intention of the bodhisattvas in Sahālokadhātu."

The Buddha said: "Let me explain to you. In the east, there is a Buddha named Manaḥprabha, in a land called Aparimita-pariśodhana. The bodhisattvas in that land who practice the six perfections to their full completion cannot match the bodhisattvas of Sahālokadhātu who practice the six perfections for a single day and night, while thinking of the suffering throughout the ten directions and wishing for all to be liberated. Why? Because the practice in Sahālokadhātu is so arduous. It is as if, when a buddha-land is being destroyed, a great fire burns the land, and a person wearing new clothes enters the fire from the east and passes through to the west, and his body emerges unburned. Is this not difficult?" The bodhisattvas said: "Extremely difficult, O Deva among devas!" The Buddha said: "The bodhisattvas of Sahālokadhātu, in a single day and night of practice, being cursed, abused, and belittled, yet enduring with patience and never giving rise to anger: the difficulty of this is like that of a person walking through fire with his body unburned, and indeed surpasses it a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a ten-thousandfold, a hundred-million-fold." Twenty thousand bodhisattvas then came forward and said to the Buddha: "We wish to ascend to Sahālokadhātu and make offerings to the Buddha Śākyamuni and the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī and the others." The Buddha Raivīraya said to the bodhisattvas: "If you wish to go to Sahālokadhātu, first discipline your minds. Just as the earth receives fragrant flowers and fine things without delight, and receives feces, urine, nasal mucus, and saliva, pus, blood, and foul discharges without anger." The Buddha said: "Why do I say this to you? In the land of the Buddha Śākyamuni, there are bodhisattvas who in former lives made many offerings to the Buddhas. Some of them have sharp temperaments: their intentions are good but their speech is urgent and harsh, because of the evils of the present age."

The bodhisattvas said to the Buddha: "The bodhisattvas of Sahālokadhātu, who in former lives made many offerings to the Buddhas and have sought the Way for a long time, why were they born in the wretched and evil place of Sahālokadhātu?" The Buddha said to the bodhisattvas: "There are two causes for being born in Sahālokadhātu in this present life. First, in former lives they practiced alongside the Buddha Śākyamuni in seeking buddhahood, and therefore life after life they follow him. Second, some bodhisattvas had unwholesome karma from past lives not yet exhausted, and so were born in this evil age."

The bodhisattvas said to the Buddha: "Now these bodhisattvas who are born in Sahālokadhātu, by what causes and conditions can they eliminate the unwholesome karma of past lives?" The Buddha said: "If a good man or good woman born in Sahālokadhātu seeks the bodhisattva path and is born into a poor household, this very fact eliminates the unwholesome karma of past lives. If they have many illnesses, this further eliminates the unwholesome karma of past lives. If they encounter the illness, suffering, and death of parents, brothers, spouse, or children, and grieve with sorrow, this further eliminates the calamities of past lives. If they encounter government officials, fear, and terror, and are forced to abandon parents, household, and property and flee in anguish, this further eliminates the calamities and evils of past lives. If they suddenly lose their livelihood and fall into poverty and hardship, this further eliminates the calamities and evils of past lives. If they are born in an evil country that is attacked and defeated by another, and they flee in anguish and despair, this further eliminates the calamities and evils of past lives. If they are born among wretched and evil people, lowly and poor, with ugly features, deformed, blind, or deaf, and unable to keep up with others, and are despised by parents, brothers, spouse, children, and all their kin, and this person grieves, this further eliminates the calamities and evils of past lives. If they hear of a wholesome path and rejoice, wishing to find a wise teacher to instruct them in the sūtras and open their minds, but cannot find a wise teacher and grieve, this further eliminates the calamities and evils of past lives. If they hear from afar that in a distant place there is a teacher of lofty wisdom who has penetrated all things, and they wish to go and study under him, but their body is ill, their hands and feet are paralyzed and cannot move, their funds are scarce, and they have no companions, and so cannot travel, and they think of this and grieve, this further eliminates the calamities and evils of past lives. If a person goes in search of a good teacher and wishes to study and receive the sūtras, and the teacher is greatly learned and knows the essentials of the Way, but the disciple is foolish and dull-witted, unable to understand, and grieves, this further eliminates the calamities and evils of past lives. If a good teacher wishes to instruct a disciple in worldly matters and explain the Way of the sūtras, but the disciple is foolish and cannot be patient, and leaves the teacher, and later thinks back on the teacher's Dharma and precepts with great joy, and then regrets and grieves, this further eliminates the calamities and evils of past lives. If a seeker of the bodhisattva path, while sleeping or dreaming, sees an enemy pursuing him with weapons, and is terrified in the dream, this further eliminates the unwholesome karma of past lives. If a good man or good woman of a bodhisattva household has unwholesome karma from past lives not yet exhausted, who at death would enter the hells to suffer for a kalpa, but who obtains a good teacher and repents for one day and one night, their headaches, fevers, and all illnesses are entirely eliminated, and they will no longer enter the hells."

When the Buddha Raivīraya had just finished speaking, Mañjuśrī reached down and grasped the bowl. The land of the Buddha Raivīraya, the innumerable buddha-lands between, and above to the land of the Buddha Śākyamuni all shook greatly, causing all to be startled and afraid. Śāriputra rose, knelt with palms joined before the Buddha, and said: "For what cause and condition does the earth shake like this? All are alarmed and frightened."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "The earth shakes now because Mañjuśrī is reaching for the bowl; therefore it trembles."

Śāriputra asked the Buddha: "Where is the bowl now?"

The Buddha said: "The bowl has gone far below, past innumerable buddha-lands. There is a Buddha named Raivīraya, in a land called Padmaśa. The bowl is there."

Śāriputra said to the Buddha: "All the bodhisattvas, arhats, devas, humans, asuras, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas now wish to see the lower land of the Buddha Raivīraya and the buddha-lands between, and to see Mañjuśrī's transformation as he retrieves the bowl."

The Buddha then emitted billions of rays of light from beneath his feet, illuminating the innumerable buddha-lands of the ten directions, so that all could see the land of the Buddha Raivīraya and the bodhisattvas, and could see Mañjuśrī's transformation as he retrieved the bowl. At that time the bodhisattvas, devas, humans, asuras, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas were all filled with great joy. The arhats were all deeply moved and wept, each saying: "The bodhisattvas can produce such transformations and accomplish such deeds as these. How much more so the awesome spiritual radiance of the Buddha, which is unmatched! We would rather enter the hells for a hundred kalpas, and after emerging, hear the bodhisattva dharma and practice it. Why should we worry about not fulfilling our aspiration?"

The bodhisattvas of the land of the Buddha Raivīraya, and bodhisattvas from the innumerable buddha-lands between, all came up to the place of the Buddha Śākyamuni. Each bodhisattva thought: "We shall go to the place of the Buddha Śākyamuni to make offerings." Some scattered flowers covering an entire buddha-land; some scattered incense; some scattered celestial garments; some scattered gold, silver, and precious treasures; some made the sound of music. From one buddha-land, all such bodhisattvas came forward, placed their heads upon the ground, bowed to the Buddha, and then sat down.

The Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī retrieved the bowl and emerged into the assembly. The bodhisattvas, arhats, devas, humans, asuras, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas were all filled with joy. Śāriputra rose, knelt with palms joined before the Buddha, and said: "What kindness did Mañjuśrī bestow upon the Buddha? For what reason did he say, 'One should remember the kindness of old'?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "In a former life, innumerable kalpas ago, there was a Buddha named Ratnadhvaja. He had sixty thousand bhikṣu arhats and seven hundred and twenty million bodhisattvas. Among them was a bodhisattva named Jñānāraya, who one morning entered the city to beg for alms and obtained a full bowl. As he was walking along the street, a wet-nurse carrying a householder's son, named Vimalaprabhu, saw the śramaṇa holding his bowl. The child reached down, and the nurse carried him to the śramaṇa. The śramaṇa gave a honey cake to the child. The child ate it and found it delicious, and followed the śramaṇa away, while the nurse followed to watch over him. When the child had eaten it all, he turned around, wanting to go back. The śramaṇa took out another cake and gave it to him. The child ate the cake and followed the śramaṇa out of the city to the Buddha's place. There the child saw the Buddha, beautiful in form, endowed with the thirty-two marks and the eighty minor excellences, gazing upon him without wearying. He saw the bodhisattvas and bhikṣus and was filled with great joy. The śramaṇa then taught the child to wash his hands and rinse his mouth, and gave the child the bowl and cake to offer to the Buddha: 'You now have peace, and afterward you shall receive merit from this.' The child took the bowl and cake, went before the Buddha, and with his hands placed the cake into the Buddha's bowl. He then passed it to the bodhisattvas and the bhikṣu saṃgha, and all were fully satisfied. After they had eaten, the bowl and cake remained as before. Thus for seven days the child offered food to the Buddha, the bodhisattvas, and the bhikṣu saṃgha. The child was overjoyed and declared: 'Each day I take a single cake to feed the Buddha, the bodhisattvas, and the bhikṣu saṃgha. Seven days of feeding being complete, I shall certainly receive merit. By this single act of merit, I shall attain buddhahood.'"

The Buddha said to the bodhisattvas and arhats: "This is the kindness of that former time. The Bodhisattva Jñānāraya is now Mañjuśrī. The child Vimalaprabhu was I myself. Now that I have attained buddhahood, endowed with the thirty-two marks and the eighty minor excellences, with awesome spiritual honor, liberating all sentient beings of the ten directions: all of this is due to the kindness of Mañjuśrī, who was originally my teacher. In the past, the innumerable Buddhas who have gone before were all disciples of Mañjuśrī, and those who are to come in the future have also been brought about through his awesome spiritual grace. Just as in the worldly life a child has a father and mother, Mañjuśrī is the father and mother of the Buddha-path."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the two hundred bodhisattvas in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven thought to themselves: "The Buddha was originally taught and transformed by Mañjuśrī, who caused him to cultivate merit and become a Buddha. Why then has Mañjuśrī, who stands before the Buddha, not yet become a Buddha himself?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī has penetrated deeply into skillful means and broadly transforms sentient beings. Therefore he has not yet taken the Way for himself."

The Buddha told the bodhisattvas and the bhikṣu fourfold assembly: "The two hundred celestial bodhisattvas who earlier wished to turn back and take the two vehicles, having now seen the transformations of Mañjuśrī and the kindness I owe, have all newly aroused the supreme aspiration and cultivated the bodhisattva path. In future lives, all shall become Buddhas."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the bodhisattvas, the bhikṣu saṃgha, the devas, humans, asuras, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas were all filled with great joy. They rose and bowed to the Buddha.
