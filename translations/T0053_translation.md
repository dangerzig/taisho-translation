## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra on the Aggregate of Suffering, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavān was dwelling in Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the bhikkhus had gathered after the midday meal to discuss the Dhamma, all assembled together for some minor matter. At that time, various wanderers of other schools were roaming about after the midday meal and came to where those bhikkhus were. Having arrived, they exchanged face-to-face greetings with the bhikkhus; having exchanged greetings, they sat down to one side. Those wanderers of other schools, having sat down to one side, said to the bhikkhus:

"Friends! The ascetic Gautama teaches sensual pleasures with wisdom, and teaches form and feeling with wisdom. Friends! We too teach sensual pleasures with wisdom, and teach form and feeling with wisdom. So, friends — what difference is there, what distinction, what variation? The ascetic Gautama and we alike possess wisdom."

At that time, when the bhikkhus heard what those wanderers of other schools had said, they neither approved nor disparaged it. Without approving or disparaging, they rose from their seats and departed. "Having now heard these words, we should question the World-honored One and fully understand their meaning." They then went to where the World-honored One was; having arrived, they paid homage at the feet of the World-honored One and sat down to one side. Those bhikkhus, having sat down to one side, reported to the World-honored One in full all that had been discussed with the wanderers of other schools. Having thus reported to the World-honored One, the World-honored One said to the bhikkhus:

"Bhikkhus! At that time you should have said to those wanderers of other schools: 'What is the gratification of sensual pleasures? What is the danger therein? What is the escape from them? What is the gratification of form? What is the danger therein? What is the escape from it? What is the gratification of feeling? What is the danger therein? What is the escape from it?' Bhikkhus! This is how they should have been addressed. Upon hearing it, those wanderers of other schools would look at one another in bewilderment, would seek further arguments, and would surely become angry. Having become angry, they would fall silent — their faces displeased, their limbs ill at ease — and would turn away their faces, unable to answer. Changing their expressions, they would rise silently from their seats and depart.

"Why? Because I do not see in this world — among its gods, Māra, Brahmā, among ascetics and brahmins, the multitude of gods and humans — any being who, having heard my teaching, could equal my knowledge of this meaning, save for the Tathāgata himself, disciples of the Tathāgata, or those who have heard it here from them.

"What is the gratification of sensual pleasures? It is said: dependent upon the five strands of sensual pleasure, whatever pleasure or joy arises — this is the gratification of sensual pleasures. Yet therein there is much danger.

"What is the danger of sensual pleasures? A son of good family sustains his livelihood through some craft — whether by farming, by trading, by clerical work, by learning arithmetic, by learning calculation, by learning the composition of verses, by learning the Ślokas, by teaching writing, or by serving in official positions. He is oppressed by cold and oppressed by heat; he endures hunger and thirst, bitten by mosquitoes, horseflies, flies, and fleas, enduring all this while seeking wealth. That son of good family rises thus, goes thus, strives thus. Yet if despite all this he cannot obtain wealth, he grieves and laments, weeps and beats his breast, and in his delusion says: 'I have acted foolishly; I have gained nothing.' If he does not give up but persists in diligent effort, he may then succeed. Having obtained wealth, he guards it carefully and stores it away, thinking: 'May this wealth of mine not be seized by a king, not be stolen by thieves, not be burned by fire, not decay, and may the profit I have gained not be lost.' Yet while guarding his wealth, it may be seized by a king, stolen by thieves, or destroyed by fire; or the profit he sought may not be gained. He then grieves and laments, weeps and beats his breast, increasing his delusion.

"Furthermore, what he has long cherished, delighted in, and held dear through many a long night is destroyed and lost through adversity. This is the present aggregate of suffering — owing to sensual pleasures, conditioned by sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures; such is the causal condition of sensual pleasures.

"Beings, owing to sensual pleasures, conditioned by sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures: mothers quarrel with sons, sons with mothers, fathers with sons, sons with fathers, brothers quarrel with sisters, sisters with brothers. In their disputes and quarrels, mothers speak ill of sons, sons of mothers, fathers of sons, sons of fathers, brothers of sisters, sisters of brothers — how much more so among people in general? This is the present aggregate of suffering, owing to sensual pleasures, conditioned by sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures.

"Beings, owing to sensual pleasures, conditioned by sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures: kings quarrel with kings, brahmins with brahmins, householders with householders, and artisans of low station quarrel with one another; each takes up various weapons of combat — fists, stones, blades, or staves — and some die therein, suffering the agony of death. This is the present aggregate of suffering, owing to sensual pleasures, conditioned by sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures.

"Beings, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures: they don armor and take up bows and arrows, or don leather armor and carry sharp swords, massing together in battle. In that battle, some fight with elephants, some on horseback, some by chariot, some as foot soldiers, some with women fighters, some as warriors — and some die therein, suffering the agony of death. This is the present aggregate of suffering, owing to sensual pleasures, conditioned by sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures.

"Beings, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures: donning armor and carrying sharp swords, they approach mighty walled fortresses to besiege them. There they blow conch shells, beat drums, raise battle cries; they strike with hammers, thrust with halberds, swing battle-axes, hurl sharp discs, shoot arrows at one another, rain down stones, fire crossbows, pour down molten bronze — and some die therein, suffering the agony of death. This is the present aggregate of suffering, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures.

"Beings, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures: they come to royal cities and towns, pierce walls and break open storehouses, steal others' property, block others' roads, destroy cities, plunder villages, and kill people. Being seized by the authorities, they are subjected to various tortures: some have their heads severed; some have their hands and feet cut off; some have their ears cut off, or their noses, or both ears and nose; some have their topknots cut off, or their hair, or both topknot and hair; some are confined in boxes; some are wrapped in cloth and slain; some are dragged over sand and gravel; some are placed on straw; some are forced into the mouth of an iron donkey; some into the mouth of an iron lion; some are placed in a bronze cauldron, others in an iron cauldron; some are cut to pieces; some are pierced with sharp forks; some are laid on beds of hot iron and sprinkled with boiling oil; some are placed in a mortar and pounded with an iron pestle; some are set upon by serpents; some are beaten with cudgels; some are bound and rolled up; and some are brought to the stake and beheaded with a blade. This is the aggregate of suffering in this very body, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures.

"Beings, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures: committing evil deeds of body, speech, and mind, they fall ill and suffer — lying on a bed, on a seat, or in the shade, with pain pervading the body, in extreme suffering and extreme pain, no longer wishing to live and longing for death. Those evil deeds of body, speech, and mind — at the time of death they hang inverted and fall downward, just as at dusk, when the sun is setting between great mountains, the shadow of those mountains falls inverted downward. Thus those evil deeds of body, speech, and mind — at the moment of death they hang inverted and fall downward. He thinks: 'These evil deeds of body, speech, and mind hang inverted downward. From the beginning I have not cultivated virtue, have not accumulated merit; I have done much evil — I have gone toward evil, toward greed, toward violence; I have not performed meritorious deeds, not performed good deeds, and have not taken refuge. I shall surely fall to that destination.' Then remorse arises; with remorse, the death is not good and the rebirth is not good either. This is the present aggregate of suffering, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures.

"Beings, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures: having committed evil deeds of body, speech, and mind, when the body breaks up at death, they are born in an evil destination, in niraya. This is the aggregate of suffering pertaining to the body, owing to sensual pleasures, arising from sensual pleasures. This is the danger of sensual pleasures.

"What is the escape from sensual pleasures? If there is one who has desire and longing for sensual pleasures, they should stop this desire and longing, transcend the desire for sensual pleasures, and abandon this desire — this is the escape from sensual pleasures.

"Those ascetics and brahmins who do not know as it truly is the gratification in sensual pleasures, the danger therein, and the escape from them — how could they themselves abandon sensual pleasures? How could they lead others to abandon them? And those who live together with them — how could they abandon sensual pleasures? This is impossible. Those ascetics and brahmins who do know as it truly is the gratification in sensual pleasures, the danger, and the escape — they themselves can abandon sensual pleasures, they can lead others to abandon them, and those who live together with them can abandon sensual pleasures — this is possible.

"What is the gratification of form? If a woman of the kṣatriya class, a brahmin woman, an artisan woman, or a woman of common birth, at the age of fourteen or fifteen, is at that time endowed with her full beauty — at that time there is pleasure and joy in her form. This is the gratification of form. Yet therein there is much danger.

"What is the danger of form? Imagine seeing that same woman grown old and decrepit, advanced in years, with teeth fallen out and hair fallen away, her back bent, leaning on a staff, moving with difficulty. What do you think — has not the earlier beautiful form now deteriorated?"

"Indeed, World-honored One!"

"Furthermore, imagine seeing that same woman afflicted with illness, lying on a bed, on a seat, or in the shade, with pain pervading her body, in extreme suffering and extreme affliction, not wishing to live and longing for death. What do you think — has not the earlier beautiful form now deteriorated?"

"Indeed, World-honored One!"

"Furthermore, imagine seeing that same woman dead, from one day to seven days, pecked by crows, pecked by hawks, eaten by dogs, eaten by foxes, burned by fire, buried, or consumed by worms. What do you think — has not the earlier beautiful form now deteriorated?"

"Indeed, World-honored One!"

"Furthermore, imagine seeing the corpse of that same woman — bones discolored and bluish, consumed by worms, gnawed away, then bleached white. What do you think — has not the earlier beautiful form now deteriorated?"

"Indeed, World-honored One!"

"Furthermore, imagine seeing the corpse of that same woman with no skin or flesh remaining, held together only by tendons. What do you think — has not the earlier beautiful form now deteriorated?"

"Indeed, World-honored One!"

"Furthermore, imagine seeing the corpse of that same woman with the bones of every joint scattered and dispersed in separate places — the foot bones in one place, the shoulder bones, the thigh bones, the waist bones, the spinal bones, the scapulae, the neck bones, and the skull each lying in a different place. What do you think — has not the earlier beautiful form now deteriorated?"

"Indeed, World-honored One!"

"Furthermore, imagine seeing the bones of that same woman — pure white as a conch shell, or the color of a blue pigeon, or reddish and moist, or rotten and crumbled. What do you think — has not the earlier beautiful form now deteriorated?"

"Indeed, World-honored One!"

"This is the danger of form. What is the escape from form? If there is desire and longing for form, one should stop this desire and longing, transcend all desire and longing, and abandon this form — this is the escape from form.

"Those ascetics and brahmins who do not know as it truly is the gratification in form, the danger therein, and the escape from it — how could they themselves abandon form? How could they lead others to abandon it? And those who live together with them — how could they abandon form? This is impossible. Those ascetics and brahmins who do know as it truly is the gratification in form, the danger, the abandonment, and the escape — they themselves can abandon form, they can lead others to abandon it, and those who live together with them can abandon form — this is possible.

"What is the gratification of feeling? A bhikkhu who is liberated from sensual pleasures enters and abides in the four dhyānas in right attainment. Abiding therein, he neither harms himself nor harms others; being free from harm, he attains pleasure therein. Why? I say that one who is without anger attains pleasant feeling — this is the gratification of feeling.

"What is the danger of feeling? Feeling is impermanent, suffering, and subject to cessation — this is the danger of feeling.

"What is the escape from feeling? Regarding the desire and longing for feeling — one should stop this desire and longing and transcend desire and longing — this is the escape from feeling.

"Those ascetics and brahmins who do not know as it truly is the gratification in feeling, the danger, and the escape from feeling — how could they themselves abandon feeling? How could they lead others to abandon it? And those who live together with them — how could they abandon feeling? This is impossible. Those ascetics and brahmins who do know as it truly is the gratification in feeling, the danger, and the abandonment — they themselves can abandon feeling, they can lead others to abandon it, and those who live together with them can abandon feeling — this is possible."

Thus spoke the Buddha. Those bhikkhus, having heard what the World-honored One had said, rejoiced and were glad.
