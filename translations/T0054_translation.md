# 釋摩男本四子經
## 釋摩男本四子經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0054

Translated from the Chinese. 吳 支謙譯

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of the Śākyas, traveling to the Nyagrodha Grove in the land of Kapilavastu, and sat beneath a tree. At that time there was a Śākyan named Mahānāma who came before the Buddha, placed his head and face upon the Buddha's feet in reverence, and addressed the Buddha, saying: "I have always heard the Buddha's words and held them in my heart. I have heard the Buddha teach that the human mind has three dispositions: the disposition of lust, the disposition of anger, and the disposition of delusion. Since hearing this, I have always kept it in mind, and I think to myself: 'When there is no disposition of lust, the mind itself becomes correct; when there is no disposition of anger, the mind itself becomes correct; when there is no disposition of delusion, the mind itself becomes correct.' I think to myself: 'I always uphold these three without wavering in mind—yet why do I still not understand?'"

The Buddha said: "If one who has relinquished the minds of lust, anger, and delusion—why would he still dwell together with wife and children? It is because of a mind of greed. A wise person reflects for himself: 'Though there may be slight suffering, in the long run there is great happiness. Dwelling with wife and children is momentary pleasure, but in the long run it brings great suffering.'"

"Is it the case that wise persons know that in the world pleasure is little and suffering is great?"

The Buddha said: "Those bhikṣus who have attained the path of the arhat know that in the world pleasure is little and suffering is great. The reason I sought the path of Buddhahood was simply that I thought of how the world has little pleasure and much suffering. When I was a bodhisattva, I always thought of how there is little pleasure and much suffering."

Mahānāma said: "Is it only the Buddha and the arhats who have this thought?"

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "Listen to what I say and hold it in your heart. What is pleasure for people in the world? There are five pleasures that people are greedy for and delight in. The eyes are greedy for beautiful forms—this lodges in the mind, and day and night one thinks of it, becoming attached through greed for beautiful forms. The ears hear pleasant sounds; the nose smells sweet fragrances; the tongue delights in fine tastes; the body receives softness and comfort—these all lodge in the mind, becoming attached through greed. These five things—all people of the world are greedy for them, and all the world's pleasurable attachments arise from these five things. Do you know how much sorrow comes forth from them?

"People in the world—some earn their living by farming, some by craftsmanship, some by trading in the marketplace, some by serving as officials, some by raising livestock, some by painting. These people endure cold when it is cold, endure heat when it is hot, endure suffering in suffering, endure hunger when hungry, endure thirst when thirsty—all because of the mind of greed, all enduring cold, heat, hunger, and thirst. They lament to themselves: 'I have worked for my livelihood for so many years, suffering to the point of death, yet I have obtained no wealth; I dwell together with cold and hardship, and sometimes fall ill and waste away.'"

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "This is the first suffering. The second matter concerns the mind of lustful greed. Among people, some farm, some are craftsmen, some trade in the marketplace, some are officials, some raise livestock, some are painters—they go about earning their living, enduring cold, heat, hunger, and thirst, amassing wealth through greed. Having obtained wealth, they then harbor anxiety and fear: fearing that the authorities may seize their wealth, or that fire may break out and burn their wealth, or that a ship may sink and their wealth be lost, or that bandits may rob them of their wealth, or that trade may cost them their wealth, or that poor relatives may poison them, or that their own children may squander their wealth. Such people constantly dwell together with heavy anxiety, harboring worry day and night with no time of release. Among people, some travel with their wealth and encounter officials, or encounter flood and fire, or sell goods and are not repaid, or bury their wealth in the ground and forget the place, or someone comes to slander and falsely accuse them, or their own children spend their father's wealth. Such a person thinks to himself: 'From youth I worked for my livelihood, enduring cold, heat, hunger, and thirst, enduring diligent hardship to amass wealth—and now it is lost again.' From such anxious thoughts one may fall ill or die—all on account of wealth and money. All of this is brought about by the mind of greed and the five pleasures. This is the second suffering.

"The third matter: in the world, because of money, fathers quarrel with sons, brothers quarrel with brothers, husbands quarrel with wives; friends and acquaintances quarrel with one another, or all within and without a household quarrel, speaking ill of each other behind their backs. All of this is brought about by greed and pleasure. In the world, because of wealth, kings fight with kings, renunciants fight with renunciants, farmers fight with farmers, craftsmen fight with craftsmen—all because of wealth, they curse each other with their mouths, beat each other with staves, hack each other with knives, or wound and kill each other. All of this is brought about by greed. This is the third suffering.

"The fourth matter: people in the world join the military, receive government pay—this is publicly known—and must go into battle with life and death uncertain, all because of the mind of greed. Having received government pay, they cannot rest, and so they fight. Their heads are wounded or severed, their arms are wounded or severed, their legs are wounded or severed—each taking the other's life. All of this is brought about by greed. This is the fourth suffering.

"The fifth matter: people in the world with a mind of greed travel at night to break through the walls of others' homes, or rob people on the road, or assault walled cities. When caught by the authorities, they may be beheaded, or have their hands cut off, or have their feet cut off, or be bound and exposed on a frame, or have their flesh cut away, or be burned with fire, or have their foreheads struck with a great maul, or be cut in two at the waist. All of this is brought about by the mind of greed. This is the fifth suffering.

"People in the world, because of wealth, successively deceive one another—deceiving with their mouths, deceiving with their bodies, deceiving with their minds—at the time thinking themselves justified, without fault or transgression. But they do not know that calamity and poison await them hereafter, and they shall enter hell. As for the wise, or śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, they reflect for themselves: 'Are the five pleasures of the world the more abundant? Or is suffering and sorrow the more abundant?'"

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "When I was a bodhisattva, I always thought of how the world has little pleasure and much suffering, and for this reason I sought the path of the unconditioned. Those who would say the world has pleasure do not know the way of birth and death. If in the world there are those who are wise and virtuous, whose heart and mind harbor no intent of greed, and who further wish to teach others not to be greedy—this is the greatest virtue."

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "I once went to the kingdom of Rājagṛha, where there was a mountain called the Saptaparṇaguhā. I saw various kinds of Nigaṇṭhas: some walked with their hair unbound, some walked bent over, some sat upon the ground, some lay upon the ground—their bodies without clothing, all covered in deerskin. The Buddha saw them from afar and went forward to speak with the Nigaṇṭhas: 'What is the reason you walk about with your hair unbound? What is the reason you sit and lie upon the ground without clothing or covering, tormenting yourselves like this?' The Nigaṇṭhas replied to the Buddha: 'It is because of evil deeds we committed in past lives that we suffer such afflictions in this life. It is because the evil deeds are not yet exhausted.' The Buddha said: 'How do you come to know about the deeds of past lives? Did you hear it from others? Or do you know it yourselves?' The Nigaṇṭhas said: 'We neither know it, nor have heard it, nor have received it from a teacher.' The Buddha said: 'Through this affliction and suffering, do you attain liberation from birth and death? If you have neither heard it from others nor received it from a teacher, are you not tormenting yourselves in vain? It would be better to abandon what you are doing and come to follow the Buddha's path.' The Buddha said: 'I merely feel concern for your bodies and think of your descendants—in future lives they will all continue this practice and imitate what you are all doing.' The Nigaṇṭhas all grew angry at the Buddha's words and said: 'King Bimbisāra has brought this śramaṇa Gautama into the kingdom.' The Buddha told the Nigaṇṭhas: 'Do not be angry! King Bimbisāra has received my sūtras and perhaps does not dare to speak recklessly.' The Buddha told the Nigaṇṭhas: 'Could you sit upright for seven days and seven nights without eating, drinking, or speaking—is that pleasure? Or is the pleasure of the king's palaces and music pleasure?' The Nigaṇṭhas said: 'Śramaṇa Gautama! That is pleasure.' The Buddha said: 'Why is it pleasure? Why does King Bimbisāra's kingdom not have pleasure?' The Nigaṇṭhas said: 'We have few worries, and for this reason, Śramaṇa Gautama, our way surpasses King Bimbisāra's.' The Buddha told the Nigaṇṭhas: 'King Bimbisāra has the mind of lust, the mind of anger, the mind of delusion; he also wishes to subjugate his courtiers and further wishes to subjugate the common people beyond his borders, calculating day and night: whom should I govern? Whom should I bind?' The Buddha said: 'Those possessed of lust both wish to kill themselves and to kill others; those possessed of anger both wish to kill themselves and to kill others; those possessed of delusion both wish to kill themselves and to kill others.' All the Nigaṇṭhas came forward before the Buddha and addressed him, saying: 'We too have no disposition of lust, no disposition of anger, no disposition of delusion—may we become śramaṇas?' The Buddha said: 'You should return home and inform your parents.' The Nigaṇṭhas said: 'We have already left home to cultivate the Way and have taken leave of our parents.' The Buddha said: 'Then you should receive the five precepts and return.' The Nigaṇṭhas all received the five precepts: the first, not killing; the second, not stealing; the third, not violating the wives and daughters of others; the fourth, not deceiving; the fifth, not drinking intoxicants. Having received the five precepts, the Nigaṇṭhas put on clothing, bound up their hair, and walked with proper deportment, each returning to their own home."

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "Having heard this sūtra on the minds of lust, anger, and delusion, and having said that you uphold the Buddha's teaching—if you deeply contemplate these five matters, would you not thereby surpass the ordinary people of the world?"

Mahānāma said: "I will return home to contemplate and recite this scripture, and each day I shall come to the Buddha."

Mahānāma then made obeisance to the Buddha and departed.
