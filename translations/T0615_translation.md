# 菩薩訶色欲法經
## Sūtra of the Bodhisattva's Reproach of Sensual Desire

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 615

Translated from the Chinese. Later Qin, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Female beauty is the shackles and chains of the world; ordinary beings cling to it with longing and cannot extricate themselves. Female beauty is a grave affliction of the world; ordinary beings are beset by it and cannot escape even until death. Female beauty is the misfortune and calamity of the world; ordinary beings who encounter it meet with every manner of suffering.

A practitioner who has succeeded in abandoning it and then turns back to dwell upon it again is like one who escapes from prison and then longs to return; like one restored from madness who takes delight in it once more; like one who recovers from illness and then desires to fall sick again. The wise are angered by this, knowing that such madness and stumbling means death is not far off!

Ordinary beings prize sensual beauty and willingly become its servants, racing about for their whole lives and toiling on its behalf. Even if they were to be cut apart piece by piece on the chopping block, or assailed from all sides by blades and arrows, they would gladly endure it, not regarding it as a misfortune. Madmen who delight in their madness — there is nothing more extreme than this. If a practitioner is able to abandon it and give it no thought, this is to break the shackles and slip the chains, to abhor madness and be averse to illness, to be free from misfortune and calamity, to dwell in peace and good fortune, to escape the prison-house forever without affliction.

The nature of women: their words are like honey, yet their hearts are like poison. They are like a deep and still pool, clear as a mirror, yet in which a sea-dragon lurks; like a mountain of gold with a treasure-cave, yet in which a lion makes its lair. One should know that this danger must not be approached. Discord in the household arises from women; the ruin of one's clan and family is women's doing. Truly they are hidden thieves who extinguish the bright light of human wisdom; they are also a hunting enclosure from which few escape. They are like a tall net in which a flock of birds becomes trapped and cannot beat their wings to fly; like a fine-meshed net into which a school of fish swims, only to have their bellies gutted and their flesh laid upon the chopping block; like a dark pit into which the sightless fall, like moths rushing into flame. Therefore the wise, knowing this, keep their distance and do not suffer its harm; regarding it with loathing and contempt, they do not allow themselves to be deluded by such things.
