# 佛說阿難分別經
## Sūtra of Ānanda's Distinctions, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0495

Translated from the Chinese. Qifu Qin dynasty, translated by Fajian (法堅)

---

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "There are people who serve the Buddha and obtain wealth and success, and there are those who suffer decline and misfortune. Why is there such inequality? May the God Among Gods explain this universally for us."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "There are people who serve the Buddha. They receive the precepts from a wise teacher, with dedicated faith and without transgression. They practice diligently and do not lose what they have received. Their images are kept bright and clean; morning and evening they bow in reverence, respectfully lighting lamps. They make pure offerings in a proper place, never violating the prohibitions of the path. They observe the purificatory precepts without weariness, and their hearts are filled with joy. They are always protected by the devas and good spirits. Everything they undertake succeeds, and their blessings increase a hundredfold. They are respected by the heavenly nāgas, spirits, and all people. In the end, they will surely attain Buddhahood. These are the good men and good women who are true disciples of the Buddha.

"There are people who serve the Buddha but have not found a good teacher and have not encountered the sūtras and their teachings. They have merely received the precepts in name. They have the title of precept-holder but are confused and without faith, violating the precepts and the Vinaya. They half-believe and half-doubt, their minds wavering. They have no sūtras or images. They do not burn incense or make obeisance daily, and they constantly harbor anger. With vile speech they curse and revile others. Moreover, they do not observe the six purificatory days, and they kill living creatures with their own hands. They do not respect the Buddha's sūtras, keeping them in worn baskets along with clothing and bedding, or placing them on the beds of their wives and children, or hanging them on the wall, without any thought of offering or honor, treating them no differently from ordinary worldly books. If they fall ill, they doubt and do not believe, and instead summon shamans and diviners, consulting oracles and making petitions, sacrificing to deviant spirits. The heavenly spirits depart from them and they receive no good protection. Demons and malevolent beings press in daily; evil ghosts throng at their gates, causing them to decline and suffer misfortune, so that nothing they undertake succeeds. In this present life they are sinners, not disciples of the Buddha. Upon death they will enter the mud-prisons, where they will be beaten and punished. Because of their sins, they experience decline and misfortune in this life, and afterward will suffer further calamity. The pain of the dead spirit is excruciating beyond words.

"Foolish people, blind and benighted, do not reflect on the causes and conditions of past lives, on where the spirit goes, or on the root from which it came. They say that serving the Buddha has caused their decline and misfortune. They feel no shame for the lack of merit in their past conduct, but blame the sages and fault heaven. The people of the world are so confused and deluded. Those who lack understanding are full of doubt and hesitation; their faith is not firm, their minds are unsettled. In advancing and retreating they lose all principle, betraying the Buddha's kindness without any thought of repayment. In the end they are caught and bound by the three evil destinies. They create their own fortune and misfortune. The causes and conditions of sin are seeds that bear fruit; one cannot go against them. The ten evils are enemies; the ten virtues are true friends. To pacify the spirit and attain the path, all arises from goodness. Goodness is a great suit of armor; one need not fear swords or weapons. Those who can maintain faith will have peace and harmony within their household. Blessings arrive naturally; they are not bestowed by spirits. As for those who still do not believe now: in future lives, their suffering will be even worse!"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! Good and evil follow a person as a shadow follows a form; they cannot be escaped. The matter of sin and merit is also just like this. Do not harbor doubt and thereby cast yourself into the evil destinies! With true faith and without delusion, wherever one is, there is always peace. The Buddha's words are perfectly true; he never deceives anyone."

The Buddha further told Ānanda: "The Buddha does not speak with two tongues. The sūtra Dharma is difficult to hear. You have merit from past lives and are now able to attend the Buddha. You should be mindful of repaying this kindness. Spread the Dharma teaching widely, revealing it to the people, serving as a field of merit for them. Those who have faith will obtain its benefits, and in future lives they will be free from worry."

Ānanda received the teaching, practiced it, and spread it universally.

Ānanda again addressed the Buddha: "If a person does not kill with their own hand but orders another to kill, is the one who does not kill with their own hand without sin?"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! The one who orders another to kill bears a heavier sin than one who kills with their own hand. Why? Because the one ordered may be a servant, a foolish or lowly person who does not understand the law of sin, or may be compelled by government officials to act, not of their own will. Though they incur sin, the circumstances and intent are different, and the gravity varies. But the one who orders the killing knows it is wrong and deliberately commits it, harboring treacherous malice, wickedly directing the deed with no compassionate mind. They deceive and defraud the Three Honored Ones and also naturally commit the five heinous sins: harming life and cutting short others' existence. Their sin is the greatest! Retribution follows in kind; lifetime after lifetime they receive calamity, repaying each other in an endless cycle with no cessation. In this present life they will have no peace and will frequently encounter disaster and misfortune. Upon death they will enter the hells, and when they emerge from human form they will fall among the animals, to be slaughtered and butchered by people, suffering through the three evil destinies and the eight difficulties for vast eons, providing their flesh to others with no end in sight. They will suffer bodily hardship, eating grass and drinking from springs. The animals that exist in this present world were all, in past lives when they were human, violent and lawless, secretly harming and injuring life. Through their disbelief they have come to this. Lifetime after lifetime they are enemies, repaying one another: the spirit is the same though the form is different. Their sin is as deep as this."

Ānanda again addressed the Buddha: "What sin befalls those in the world, including disciples of the Buddha, who harbor ill will toward their teacher or toward persons of virtue?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "As a human being, one should love and delight in the goodness of others and take goodness as one's friend; one must not be envious of it. A person who harbors ill will toward a person of virtue or toward a good teacher: this is no different from harboring ill will toward the Buddha. It would be better to take a crossbow of ten thousand stones and shoot oneself."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Would shooting oneself be painful or not?"

Ānanda said: "Extremely painful! Extremely painful, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said: "A person who harbors ill will toward a person of virtue or toward their teacher suffers pain far worse than being shot by a crossbow. As a disciple, one must not slight or show contempt for one's teacher, nor harbor ill will toward persons of virtue. One should regard them as one regards the Buddha, and must not be envious of their goodness. A person who has the virtue of keeping the precepts moves the devas; the heavenly nāgas, spirits, and all beings revere and honor them. It would be better to throw oneself into fire, or to have one's flesh cut with a sharp blade, than to be envious of another's goodness. The sin of envy is not small. Be careful! Be careful!"

Ānanda said: "As for the teacher, can a teacher act wrongly toward a disciple? Can a small fault become a great one? Can one be without fault?"

The Buddha said: "No, this too is very grave, very grave! The bond between teacher and disciple is a natural bond of righteousness. They should make mutual commitments and regard the other as themselves. What one does not wish for oneself, one should not impose on others. They should broadly uphold the rites and Vinaya and not allow resentment or quarreling. The disciple likewise. When both sides are sincere in righteousness, the teacher should act as a teacher and the disciple as a disciple. They must not slander each other. Be careful not to harbor poison; a small grudge becomes a great one and burns oneself in the end. I observe that in the final age, the wicked people, the monks of Māra's age, will only think of others' faults and not restrain their own evil. They will envy the worthy and be jealous of the good, with no thought of practicing the path. Since they cannot do it themselves, they will destroy and defame others, cutting off their aspiration for the path and preventing them from practicing. Greedy for worldly affairs, they will accumulate wealth to their own ruin, and upon death will fall into the evil hells. Things should never come to this. What is there to seek in the world? Be mindful of repaying the Buddha's kindness, and lead one another with the path. The path must be studied; it saves the spirit from suffering and transcends birth and death. When you see the worthy, do not be arrogant; when you see the good, do not slander them. Do not use a small fault to convict someone of a great crime. To go against the Dharma and lose all principle: there is no greater sin. Sin and merit have their evidence. How can one not be careful!"

Ānanda again addressed the Buddha: "In the final age, how should disciples manage their household affairs and adapt to circumstances?"

The Buddha said: "Receive the Buddha's prohibitions and precepts, and with faith practice them. Do not think of deceit or falsehood. Practice filial devotion with reverence and caution! Respectfully take refuge in the Three Honored Ones, nourish your parents, and be faithful. One may engage in worldly affairs, but one must not adopt worldly intentions."

Ānanda said: "What are worldly affairs, and what are worldly intentions, God Among Gods?"

The Buddha said: "As a disciple of the Buddha, one may engage in trade for profit, using fair measures and honest scales, not cheating the people. One's conduct should accord with principle and not violate the natural order. Funerals, relocations, and marriages: these are worldly affairs.

"As for worldly intentions: as a disciple of the Buddha, one must not practice divination, petition spirits, use talismans or spells, employ sorcery, or sacrifice and make ritual appeals. Nor should one select auspicious times and days. One who has received the Buddha's five precepts is a person of merit and virtue. Whatever one undertakes, one should invoke the Three Honored Ones. The protection of the path is the strongest. Even if the devas, heavenly nāgas, and spirits all appear, none will fail to submit in reverence. When the precepts are valued, one is honored; wherever one goes, there is good fortune. How could there be any taboo or inauspicious thing?

"Those who lack understanding bring curses upon themselves. Good and evil are created by one's own mind. Fortune and misfortune come from oneself, as a shadow follows a form, as an echo follows a sound. The virtue of precept-practice responds naturally. The devas will protect one; one's wishes will not go unfulfilled. The ten directions are moved, and one shares in the virtue of heaven. The merit is towering; all the sages praise it, and it is difficult to measure! The wise one who understands fate will never deviate from the right path for as long as they live. Follow the Buddha's teaching well, and you may attain the path that transcends the world."

Ānanda, having heard what the Buddha taught, again straightened his robe and touched his head to the ground: "Indeed, World-Honored One! We are fortunate to have encountered the Tathāgata, whose kindness is vast and whose compassion is great, who takes pity on all beings, serving as a field of merit for them, enabling them to escape suffering."

The Buddha said: "Those who truly believe are few. This world is full of evil, and beings curse one another. How pitiful! If there are believers, perhaps one or two. Alas, the world's evil has become this wretched! After the Buddha passes into nirvāṇa, the sūtras and Dharma will gradually decline and be extinguished. Alas! How painful! What will there be to rely on?"

Ānanda shed tears, and speaking on this occasion, said:

> The Buddha is the protector of the three realms,
> His kindness vast, his compassion universal.
> The world is corrupt and does not see the Buddha;
> To encounter the Dharma is likewise rare.
> How painful for those who do not recognize it,
> Blind and benighted, unable to discern the true.
> How painful that it has come to this!
> Their sins have made it so.
> Those with past merit who encounter the Dharma
> Are perhaps one or two.
> The sūtras and teachings gradually vanish:
> What then will there be to rely on?
> The Buddha's kindness is not lacking in greatness;
> The fault lies with sentient beings.
> The Dharma drum shakes the three-thousand worlds:
> How is it that they do not hear?
> The world is turbid, full of evil people,
> With inverted views, fallen into confusion.
> They flatter and fawn, despise and slander the sages,
> With perverse charm they destroy one another.
> They do not believe there is a Buddha in the world,
> Saying the Buddha is not the great path.
> Such people, whether human or inhuman,
> Create for themselves the root of all sins.
> When life ends, they go without choosing;
> Swords and blades carve up their bodies.
> Hungry ghosts delight in cutting and killing;
> Boiling cauldrons surge around them.
> The lustful and licentious embrace bronze pillars;
> Great fires burn them in turn.
> Those who falsely slander the pure and lofty
> Have their tongues pulled out with iron hooks.
> Drunk and dissolute, without propriety,
> Confused and deluded, they lose the human path.
> Upon death they enter the hells,
> Where molten copper is poured into their mouths.
> Encountering every kind of peril,
> The pain of the poison is beyond words.
> If they are reborn as humans,
> It is among the lowly and destitute.
> Not killing brings long life,
> Free from illness, always strong and healthy.
> Not stealing brings great wealth in future lives,
> With riches always in abundance.
> Not committing sexual misconduct brings fragrant purity,
> The body fresh and sweetly scented,
> Its radiance always resplendent;
> In the highest case, one becomes a heavenly king.
> Sincere and without deceit,
> One is honored and served by all.
> Not intoxicated, one gains clarity,
> Revered for virtue and wisdom.
> The five blessings transcend and emerge from the Dharma;
> Devas and humans are of the same company.
> What is born is increased a hundred million-fold;
> The noble truth is exceedingly clear.
> In the final age, the wicked people
> Do not believe, full of doubt.
> Foolish and stupid, unable to discern the path,
> Their sins are deep, and they plunge into darkness.
> They obstruct the sages and defame right awakening;
> Upon death they enter the great iron city,
> Where the consciousness dwells within,
> An iron wheel mounted on the head.
> They seek death but cannot die;
> In an instant they are already transformed.
> Spears and halberds pierce and impale them;
> Their bodies are constantly maimed and dismembered.
> Alas, that the world has come to this,
> Turning from the right to trust in ghosts and spirits,
> Performing divinations and consulting oracles,
> Sacrificing and harming without benevolence.
> Upon death they fall into the eighteen hells,
> Passing through the Hell of Black Ropes.
> The eight difficulties stand as the boundary;
> To regain a human body is hard indeed.
> If ever they are reborn as humans,
> It is among barbarians without morality,
> Foolish and witless, without faculties,
> Lame, crippled, mute, unable to speak,
> Dim and confused, unable to understand things,
> Evil dragging evil in mutual bondage.
> Turning and turning in the throng of creatures,
> In the forms of birds, beasts, and livestock,
> Slaughtered and butchered by humans,
> Skinned and cut at the throat,
> Repaying the debts of past enmity,
> Giving their flesh to others in return.
> Without the path, one falls into the evil destinies;
> To seek liberation is exceedingly difficult.
> A human body is hard to obtain;
> The Buddha's sūtras are hard to hear.
> The World-Honored One is the refuge of all,
> The supreme one of the three realms.
> He has bestowed the Dharma of sweet dew,
> Causing all people universally to practice it.
> Alas! He who has attained wisdom,
> Out of pity for all living beings,
> Opens his words and reveals the true path;
> The wise one thereby escapes suffering.
> The fortunate one proceeds toward the goal,
> Sees the truth, studies, and achieves the fruit.
> Take refuge in the great field of protection;
> Plant your seeds in the deathless ground.
> Kindness and compassion surpass nothing else.
> The World-Protector turns the Dharma wheel.
> May all people everywhere
> Drink the nectar of sweet dew.
> The ship of wisdom reaches the other shore;
> The Dharma bell leads the great thousand worlds.
> Self and other are without duality;
> May we give rise to the unsurpassed truth.

When Ānanda had spoken thus, all the members of the great assembly understood with faith. The sound of the great armor of saṃnāha, the nectar of the teaching, perfumed the three-thousand worlds. From this they attained liberation; the ground of the path was revealed and opened, serving as a bridge for all. The kings, ministers, people, heavenly nāgas, and spirits, hearing what was recorded in the sūtra and what Ānanda had spoken, were both grieved and joyful. They touched their foreheads to the Buddha's feet and also made obeisance to Ānanda, received the teaching, and departed.
