# 佛說濟諸方等學經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on Aiding the Study of All Vaipulya Teachings

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0274

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at Rājagṛha on Vulture Peak, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus: sixty thousand bhikṣus, eighty billion bodhisattvas, and six million upāsakas of the kingdom of Magadha. As the time of his final nirvāṇa approached, in the last year, he arrived at Rājagṛha at the beginning of the new year.

At that time, the World-Honored One entered samādhi in proper form, adorning the entire trichiliocosm with hanging silk banners and pennants, erecting various canopied standards, scattering flowers, burning incense, and spreading lotus flowers, each with a thousand petals. From throughout the trichiliocosm, billions upon billions of Brahmā kings, each with countless hundreds of thousands of retainers, came to the Buddha's abode, prostrated at his feet, withdrew to stand to one side, and with palms joined took refuge.

The Pure Abode devas of this great chiliocosm, the august and majestic Great Divine Wondrous Heaven Kings, the dragon kings, spirit kings, asura kings, garuḍa kings, kiṃnara kings, and mahoraga kings, each with countless billions of hundreds of thousands of attendants, came to the Buddha's abode, prostrated at his feet, withdrew to stand to one side, and with palms joined took refuge.

From the ten directions, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, supremely sacred, transcendent, lofty, and noble, came to the Buddha's abode, prostrated at his feet, moved to stand to one side, and with palms joined took refuge.

All those who had come to the assembly at that time filled the entirety of this trichiliocosm, reaching up to the thirty-second heaven, with no empty space where even a staff could fit or a needle could enter, so dense was the gathering. The devas of immeasurable divine power, the dragons, spirits, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas each stood in their places, gazing upon the Buddha's honored countenance. Their bodies were each different, but their minds were one. They set aside all conditioned causes, and with equal minds took refuge in the Buddha.

At that time, the World-Honored One, calm and composed, arose from samādhi and three times surveyed the assembled congregation. Having surveyed them three times, he three times examined himself with the lion's stretch. Having stretched three times, he three times extended his tongue. Having extended his tongue three times, he three times covered the trichiliocosm with his tongue, leaving nothing uncovered, and his brilliant radiance illuminated the ten directions. Then the Great Sage retracted his tongue and again gazed upon all those who had come to the assembly. All those assembled then rose to stand, prostrated in reverence, and took refuge in the Buddha.

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya: "Ajita! You should know that the Perfectly Enlightened One will not be long before entering nirvāṇa. If you have questions to ask, now is the time; you should inquire of the Tathāgata, lest you later regret, thinking: 'I was face to face with the Great Sage; why did I not resolve my doubts?'"

The Bodhisattva Maitreya addressed the Buddha, saying: "The World-Honored One knows the proper time. He has penetrated the six superknowledges and all the perfections, and there is nothing he has not expounded to resolve all doubts. Excellent, World-Honored One! As befits the occasion, please proclaim the sūtra-path, so that this eye of the Dharma may long endure."

At that time, the Great Divine Wondrous Heaven King, together with eighty billion Pure Abode celestial beings in his retinue, proceeded to the Buddha's abode. Having prostrated at his feet and with palms joined taken refuge, he addressed the Buddha, saying: "World-Honored One! This sūtra is entitled 'Aiding the Study of All Vaipulya Teachings,' and was taught by all past buddhas, tathāgatas, worthy ones, and perfectly enlightened ones. May the Great Sage today bestow his compassion and once again, for the benefit of distracted minds, teach it with great sympathy and great peace, so that the training of the Tathāgata's Dharma may long endure."

At that time, the Buddha silently assented to what the Great Divine Wondrous Heaven King had requested. Seeing that the Buddha had silently assented, the Heaven King withdrew to stand to one side.

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the Bodhisattva Maitreya: "Ajita, you should know: all past tathāgatas, worthy ones, and perfectly enlightened ones, without exception, have taught the essential points of this 'Aiding the Study of All Vaipulya' sūtra. The buddhas of the future and present, and the tathāgatas and worthy ones of the ten directions, all likewise teach this. Those who do not proclaim it slander the Buddha, violate the sūtras, and defame the noble Sangha. Why is this? If in teaching this sūtra one thinks it is without benefit or deems it incomplete, that constitutes slandering the Buddha, violating the sūtras, and reviling the noble Sangha. Those who defame the Three Jewels will surely fall into hell. Therefore, Maitreya, if sons and daughters of good family who study the bodhisattva vehicle have thoroughly mastered the various sūtras and other distinct teachings, only then may they analyze and expound the meaning of the sūtras. If one wishes to be complete and without omission in the texts, one should follow the orthodox canonical teaching and not damage oneself. Moreover, Maitreya, you should concentrate your mind and contemplate this teaching, so that in the final age you may protect the true Dharma."

Maitreya replied: "Yes, World-Honored One! Truly, as the Sage instructs, I shall transmit the divine intent and dare not disobey."

The Buddha further addressed the Bodhisattva Maitreya: "From the night when the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, attained the unsurpassed, true, and perfect enlightenment as the Supreme Perfectly Enlightened One until the day and night when he enters the nirvāṇa-realm without remainder, has the Tathāgata ever had any deficiency, any error or forgetfulness of mind? Has he ever proclaimed evil thoughts, faults, or blemished conduct?"

Maitreya replied: "No, World-Honored One!"

He further said to Ajita: "From the night when the Tathāgata became the Supreme Perfectly Enlightened One until the day of nirvāṇa, everything he has taught has been true, sincere, timely, appropriate, and without falsehood."

The Buddha said: "There are foolish people who cannot discern the Tathāgata's skillful means and expedient devices, who do not recognize his true and sincere words, but instead transmit deluded speech of their own invention: 'This accords with the meaning; that does not accord.' By such comparisons they slander the true path. Those who slander the sūtras thereby slander the Buddha. I say that such persons will surely fall into hell."

The Buddha said to Maitreya: "In the final age to come, during the five turbidities, there will be those of the four groups: bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, who aspire to study the bodhisattva path and honor their original vows, but whose minds are those of non-Buddhist externalists. They serve the Tathāgata, see the Dharma and Vinaya that the Buddha has taught, leave the household life, and practice as śramaṇas with humble and respectful dispositions. Yet what these people seek is only profit and offerings. They destroy good dispositions, are greedy and jealous regarding their households, disrupt their own family affairs, have unpleasant countenances, aspire to petty goals, are by nature unable to understand, cannot be broad-minded or at ease, do not overcome sensual desires, and are greedy and anxious in their many pursuits.

"They are far removed from all dharma-gates, practices, samādhis, and right concentrations, and constantly dwell in flattery and deceit. Their minds think one thing while their words and deeds differ. They are greedy for family status and rely on the powerful. When they see those who are wise and who clearly understand the Dharma-treasury, they call them ignorant. Knowing nothing themselves, they praise their own wisdom. Possessing no intelligence, they laud their own cleverness. Relying on the Buddha's path to establish their views, claiming to treat all beings with equal compassion and to generate great sympathy, they also do not forget or abandon skillful means. Relying on the Buddha's power alone, they deviate from what has been taught and delight in their own studies. Some even say: 'If there are sūtras that speak of śrāvaka matters, those who practice the bodhisattva path should not study them, nor should they listen to them. These are not our Dharma, not our path-meaning; they are the practices of śrāvakas. Those cultivating the bodhisattva path must be careful not to study them. The dharma of pratyekabuddhas is likewise; be careful not to listen to it.'"

The Buddha said to Ajita: "The Buddha, to generate faith and joy, teaches the devas, and according to the occasion teaches the Dharma. He teaches the dragons, spirits, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, gandharvas, humans and non-humans, according to their basic practices and the liberation for which each is suited. He reaches out to all ten directions, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, guiding and benefiting sentient beings, each according to their basic practices, teaching as suits their liberation. Based on these dispositions, he proclaims the sūtra-path and liberates all, enabling them to attain the path."

The Buddha addressed Maitreya: "In such an age, within the bodhisattva studies, those who harbor fixed views, who among bodhisattvas are rigid and difficult to transform, evil and violent, given to false speech and double-tongued talk, of little learning and scant wisdom, will proclaim the Buddha's path divided into two parts: 'If you wish to be a bodhisattva, you should study this teaching, and you should not study that.' Harboring such thoughts, they slander the Buddha, revile the sūtras, and stir up discord in the noble Sangha. When their lives end and their bodies perish, they fall into hell. There they are cooked, and must pass through countless kalpas of suffering, and their offenses will still not be exhausted. Emerging from hell, they will be reborn in impoverished families. The hardships they must subsequently endure are extremely distant and difficult to measure; only then will they receive a prediction of buddhahood. Although they receive such a prediction, it will be in a world of five turbidities that they become buddhas, just as I myself originally encountered difficulties over long and incalculable kalpas in a world of five turbidities to become the Supreme Perfectly Enlightened One. Those who hear their words and faithfully listen and accept, who sincerely reflect upon and follow their teaching, will likewise fall into evil destinies. Why? Because following evil companions leads to these calamities."

The Buddha addressed Ajita: "In the distant and immeasurable past, there was a buddha named Stainless-Flame Achievement-of-Merits Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One, who appeared in the world for eighty koṭi-nayuta years, teaching the Dharma-path for all sentient beings. At that time there was a bhikṣu named Pure Life, who was the chief Dharma-lecturer. He taught a great many teachings with soft and beautiful words and true eloquence, encouraging and inspiring countless people and displaying the universal teaching to the great joy of many. The Stainless-Flame Achievement-of-Merits Tathāgata then entrusted the bhikṣu Pure Life to protect the true Dharma in the final age. After entrusting him, the Tathāgata entered nirvāṇa.

"After the Tathāgata entered nirvāṇa, that bhikṣu for hundreds of millions of years upheld the Dharma and proclaimed the Buddha's teaching. He obtained thorough penetration of the essential points of this 'Aiding the Study of All Vaipulya' sūtra, and proclaimed it for the transformation and awakening of all, following the hearts of sentient beings and what they delighted in and needed for liberation. He traveled throughout eighty thousand cities, observing the fundamental aspirations of sentient beings, satisfying them and removing their hunger and the hardships of their afflictions.

"At that time in that world there was a great city-state called Bhadra. With its eighty billion households, he observed their fundamental natures and taught the Dharma accordingly. At that time, the eighty billion households in the city joyfully received his teaching. Of these, one hundred million families developed the aspiration for bodhi, and seventy-nine billion were established in the śrāvaka vehicle. Then the bhikṣu Pure Life, together with ten thousand bodhisattvas of one mind, set out for the place of practice.

"Shortly after he had departed, there was a bhikṣu named Dharma, who had been traveling about and came from a distant land. In the city of Bhadra, he upheld over a thousand volumes of Vaipulya sūtras and had attained the four dhyānas, considering himself accomplished. The bhikṣu Pure Life had studied fourteen billion volumes of Vaipulya sūtras and additionally cultivated six million volumes of other sūtras.

"At that time, the bhikṣu Dharma in the city of Bhadra proclaimed only one type of Dharma-teaching. He did not know how to observe fundamental dispositions and teach the sūtra-Dharma according to the occasion. He could not awaken to or penetrate the dharma-realm, but taught only through the emptiness teaching, saying that all dharmas are empty and utterly non-existent. Everything he taught concerned only emptiness. He said there was no sin or merit, and he made light of all practices. He further declared: 'What I now teach is entirely the Buddha's teaching. What the bhikṣu Pure Life teaches is impure, attached to miscellaneous trivialities. His nature is not pure; he is a defiled person. Who gave this bhikṣu the name Pure Life? He is truly not pure! Why? Because this bhikṣu accepts various flowers and, having accepted them, consumes them himself. He likewise accepts fragrant incense and various perfumes and consumes them himself. His learning is foolish and deluded; he knows nothing. I have long cultivated the pure practice. He is newly ordained and has only recently received the precepts. Do not trust him!' Moreover, this bhikṣu was lax and indulgent. Although the people mistakenly revered him, they were single-mindedly devoted to the bhikṣu Pure Life. The bhikṣu Dharma slandered and defamed him, speaking ill of his conduct, but the people did not believe him and did not follow his teaching.

"Because the bhikṣu Dharma harbored a poisonous mind and slandered a wise person, when his life ended he fell into hell for eighty kalpas. Because he had slandered the Buddha and reviled the true Dharma, he spent seventy kalpas in hell and was additionally confused and deluded for sixty kalpas. After this duration was completed, he finally encountered his former practices at the abode of a buddha named Fragrant-Jewel-Radiance Tathāgata. That buddha taught him and re-aroused his aspiration for the path. He fell among animals for nine hundred thousand lifetimes. After emerging from animal rebirth, he was born among humans for six hundred thousand lifetimes, always in poverty and distress. In every life he was born mute and without a tongue. Thereafter, in subsequent lifetimes, he encountered sixty-three koṭi-nayutas of buddhas, and in the presence of all these buddhas he was always a Dharma-teacher, possessing the five superknowledges in every life. He was always instructed by the buddhas, and in all cases he understood the dharmas and expounded the meaning of purity."

The Buddha addressed Ajita: "Would you like to know who the bhikṣu Pure Life of that time was? Do not think otherwise. Why? He is the present Amitābha Buddha. And the bhikṣu Dharma was none other than myself. In that past life, I experienced such things as these."

The Buddha addressed Ajita: "The various dharmas and teachings are difficult to comprehend, most difficult to comprehend. Their meaning is as subtle as this. It is for this reason that I, in a world of five turbidities, attained perfect enlightenment."

The Buddha said to Ajita: "If there are bodhisattvas who teach the Dharma divided into two categories, because of this they will become buddhas in a world of five turbidities. Moreover, in their buddha-lands there will be many māras who repeatedly create disturbances. And when they achieve buddhahood and teach the sūtras, the māras will likewise harass and disturb them."

At that time, all who had come to the assembly sighed sorrowfully, tears streaming down their faces, and each said: "May no student proclaim the Dharma in two categories, expounding the sūtra-meaning in a partisan way! May none be one-sided and obstinate, willful and indulgent in self-righteousness, reviling a Dharma-teaching bhikṣu, creating divisions before bhikṣus and householder students, saying what is not Dharma is Dharma!"

At that time in the assembly, one hundred bodhisattvas rose from their seats, moved to sit upon the ground, and wept with sighing. At that time, the World-Honored One, knowing the thoughts of these bodhisattvas, deliberately asked: "Sons of good family! Why do you sit upon the ground, sighing and weeping?"

They immediately addressed the Buddha: "We, World-Honored One, each recognize that we ourselves have committed such transgressions."

The Buddha said: "It is so, it is so, as you, sons of good family, now recognize. In the time of Dīpaṃkara Buddha, there was a bodhisattva named Accumulated Wisdom. After the Buddha's nirvāṇa, he served as a Dharma-teacher. All of you, good people, sought his faults and flaws. From that time until now, you have never again met with the buddhas, have not recognized the mind of the path, have not obtained dhāraṇī, and have not attained samādhi. Yet in previous lives, sons of good family, you also joined with me in generating the aspiration for the path.

"Moreover, sons of good family, in the final era of this Bhadra-kalpa, the last buddha will be named Rucika. In his time you will at last attain the patient acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas. Then, after three more incalculable kalpas, you will attain the unsurpassed, true, and perfect enlightenment as Supreme Perfectly Enlightened Ones. Therefore, sons of good family, when bodhisattvas see other bodhisattvas, they should not generate thoughts of difference or seek each other's faults. When seeing a bodhisattva, one should think: 'Now I behold a buddha-temple; I see a perfectly enlightened buddha standing before me.' Suppose, sons of good family, a bodhisattva sees another bodhisattva and generates a different thought, not regarding them as a buddha: this is self-injury. Therefore, do not harbor resentment. If one sees even an initial-aspiration bodhisattva and does not regard them as a buddha, this immediately constitutes an offense against all the buddhas, world-honored ones, presently existing in incalculable, limitless worlds throughout the ten directions.

"Moreover, sons of good family, the Buddha now foresees that in the final age, those who attain dhāraṇī or samādhi do so entirely through the spiritual power of the Tathāgata. Therefore, sons of good family, if one slanders a Dharma-teacher and seeks their faults, this constitutes slandering the Tathāgata. After the Tathāgata's nirvāṇa, if there is someone who clearly understands what is timely and appropriate, and who teaches the essential Dharma according to the hearts and fundamental faith-practices of sentient beings, and if in an assembly there is a bodhisattva student who, upon hearing the sūtra even once, instantly memorizes and recites it with even a moment's hair-raising response, then one should know that such a person is sustained by the sacred intent of the Tathāgata.

"In that time there will be foolish and ignorant people without bodhisattva practice who call themselves bodhisattvas. Among bodhisattvas they will be rigid and self-willed, discriminating among the sūtras, concealing others' merits while claiming only their own attainments, making two divisions. They constantly say: 'What does this person know? What does that person understand?'"

The Buddha addressed Maitreya: "In my present life, I proclaim the verse-teachings. When I practiced the bodhisattva path, I gave away my head, eyes, limbs, flesh, wife, children, and kingdom. The Buddha remembers all of this: for the sake of a single verse, I gave away the entire world. Yet these deluded ones, seeking profit and offerings, do not go to pay respect to those who proclaim the Buddha's teaching. They do not follow the Buddha's teaching and deviate from the path's principles."

The Buddha said: "Ajita! One should receive this Dharma with clarity and harmony, not through contention.

"Ajita! Observe how these beings, habituated to faults, generate anger, not carefully reflecting upon or understanding the meaning. When I accomplished my cultivation and became the Supreme Perfectly Enlightened One, proclaiming the Buddha's wisdom and teaching the Dharma for others, some said: 'Do not transmit this teaching,' seeking only profit and offerings in that era. That bhikṣu Dharma taught over a thousand volumes of Vaipulya sūtras, aroused the four dhyānas, and because of this he encountered such difficulties. How much worse for those who speak falsely, deviate from meaning and principle, and do not follow the path's teaching!

"If there is a bhikṣu who honors the Vaipulya teaching and proclaims the Vaipulya treasury, who has deliberately generated the aspiration for the path, but then generates a mind of slander against a Dharma-teacher, he thereby slanders the sūtras of all the buddhas, harboring great arrogance and seeking others' faults and flaws. The Buddha does not say that such a person will reach the ultimate end of birth and death. Such persons will surely fall into hell. Why? If one seeks the faults of a Dharma-teacher, it constitutes contempt for the Buddha and aversion to the sūtra-Dharma. Showing disrespect to a Dharma-teacher is showing disrespect to the Buddha. If one does not wish to see a Dharma-teacher, that person does not wish to see the Buddha. If one defames a Dharma-teacher, one defames all the buddhas. If one generates a harmful mind and evil thoughts toward even an initial-aspiration bodhisattva, then for each moment of that harmful intent, one loses one's aspiration for the path for a corresponding number of kalpas. With the evil eye looking at a bodhisattva, for a corresponding number of lifetimes one will be born blind in every life. With the mouth proclaiming slander and speaking ill of a Dharma-teacher, for a corresponding number of kalpas one will be mute and without a tongue."

The Buddha addressed Ajita: "The Buddha surveys everything and sees that among bodhisattvas, there is no other dharma that more swiftly causes one to fall into evil destinies than having a malicious mind toward other bodhisattvas. Conceiving notions of self and personhood, greedily abiding in the pursuit of Dharma: through this one falls into evil destinies."

The Buddha said: "Ajita! The Tathāgata does not say that one who proclaims sūtras while following inverted teachings, dwelling in birth and death with self-indulgent abandon, abiding in notions of self and personhood while practicing various dharmas, is a bodhisattva. Because of this, such persons fall into hell. Therefore, Ajita, bodhisattvas should follow and practice the six pāramitās, for they enable the accomplishment of the unsurpassed, true wisdom of the Buddha.

"There are foolish people who declare with their own mouths: 'Bodhisattvas should study only the Prajñāpāramitā. The other sūtras are not pāramitās,' disparaging and belittling them. What does Ajita think? When in a past life the king gave away his head for others, was he without wisdom?"

Maitreya answered: "No, World-Honored One! The Buddha's cultivation of the six pāramitās in former lives was undertaken with sincerity and truth."

"Can one attain buddhahood without practicing these?"

Maitreya replied: "No, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said to Ajita: "You yourself in former lives practiced the Dāna-pāramitā for sixty kalpas, and the Śīla and Kṣānti pāramitās likewise. The Dhyāna-pāramitā and Prajñā-pāramitā were each practiced for sixty kalpas. Yet the foolish declare: 'One should reach buddhahood through the single practice of Prajñāpāramitā.' Following inverted views, they have no aspiration. Such people, though they speak with their mouths, do not practice with purity. They speak empty words contrary to the teaching, constantly dwelling in attachment, hoping for emptiness but unwilling to act. They merely speak, considering themselves supreme. Though they travel far, they are greedy, jealous, and envious, valuing family status and personal connections. I myself would rather give my head to others than assume the position of a wheel-turning sage king. Yet these people, attached to robes, food, and offerings, enter other people's homes and speak such words: 'The path should be practiced like this. Practicing in this way, the principle is thus; there is no other meaning.' Many Dharma-teachers do not understand this teaching, and for the sake of profit and offerings they generate many harmful intentions, thinking ill of Dharma-teachers."

The Buddha said: "Ajita! The Buddha does not consider that those who love their lives, who are foolish and without the path, truly study the path. Even for a hundred kalpas they will not attain the patient acceptance of gentle compliance, let alone achieve the Buddha-path."

The Buddha said: "Ajita! Those who practice flattery and deceit, who are stingy, greedy, and jealous, who do not practice reverence, who lack wisdom, who are jealous of others' status, and who create two divisions in their pursuit of the path: these foolish people consider their own intelligence superior to the Buddha's wisdom. Why? Because they arbitrarily declare: 'The Tathāgata's Dharma-teaching does not say such things. These are śrāvaka matters; the Buddha also does not teach śrāvaka dharmas.'"

At that time, the Buddha addressed the Elder Subhūti: "Is the Prajñāpāramitā of one essence, without a second practice?"

Subhūti replied: "Yes, Heavenly among the Heavenly!"

He further asked: "Subhūti! In the bodhisattva's practice, does one abandon all expectation and hope, with no attachment?"

"Yes, World-Honored One!"

He further asked: "Subhūti! In the bodhisattva's cultivation, does one abandon all distraction without negligence?"

"Yes, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha addressed Subhūti: "Have you seen practitioners who do not cultivate themselves and know no shame? Desiring to gain the reward of merit and achievement without effort, they will only encounter poverty and distress. Desiring status and family connections and hoping to gain support: how foolish they are!"

The Buddha further addressed Ajita: "Even if a bodhisattva hears that all dharmas are śrāvaka dharmas, one should not be frightened. If one hears that all dharmas are pratyekabuddha dharmas, or that all dharmas are buddha-dharmas, one should not be frightened. Do not harbor expectations or attachments. If one hears that all dharmas are ordinary person dharmas, or that all dharmas are affliction dharmas, one should not be frightened. If one hears that all dharmas are anger dharmas, or that all dharmas are dharmas without anger, one should not be frightened. Whether receiving or not receiving, acting or not acting, covering or not covering, pure or impure, with mind or without mind, with thought or without thought, with sin or without sin, with merit or without merit, with retribution or without retribution, with peace or without peace, with liberation or without liberation, diligent or negligent, with practice or without practice, cultivating noble dharmas or without noble dharmas, tranquil or not tranquil, receiving or not receiving, sincere or deceptive, mindful or not mindful, abiding or not abiding: regarding all these dharmas, one should have no fear whatsoever.

"When the Buddha formerly studied beneath the Bodhi Tree, the dharmas he penetrated to become the Supreme Perfectly Enlightened One encompassed all sentient beings' realms, entirely without inversion, clearly comprehending all dharmas. All are natural, without attachment, without location. Therefore, for the sake of worldly beings, the Buddha analyzes and teaches that the Tathāgata's truth does not designate the dharmas, nor is there any contention. It is for this reason that bodhisattvas attain the sacred light and limitless Dharma-radiance, arouse dhāraṇī, and uphold the Dharma-seal. For the sake of all dharmas, there is neither high nor low."

The Buddha said to Ajita: "The Buddha, for the sake of the Dharma, proclaims the sūtra-path throughout the four continents. Each sentient being thinks: 'The Tathāgata teaches the sūtra-meaning in this manner for me.' This extends to the twenty-fourth Akaniṣṭha heaven. He then teaches the second continent up to the twenty-fourth heaven. Throughout the trichiliocosm it is likewise: all sentient beings think, 'The Tathāgata was born here and turns the Dharma-wheel, teaching the sūtra-Dharma.' In this manner, through countless expedient means, reaching incalculable, boundless worlds, he teaches and transforms sentient beings, each according to their fundamental practices.

"The buddha-work I accomplish each morning in the places I visit is without limit. At midday and afternoon it is likewise. The Tathāgata ceaselessly carries on in this way, his eyes without obstruction, universally seeing the realms of all sentient beings. All buddha-lands are thus without limit, and the domains of all buddhas are likewise.

"Bodhisattvas should study this meaning, abiding in the place where all dharmas are falsely discriminated and there transforming beings. If one abides in the buddhas with expectations and hopes, one has already established oneself in slandering the buddhas. Having established oneself in slandering the buddhas, one has then established oneself on a dangerous path. Falling upon a dangerous path, one falls into evil destinies. Abiding in evil destinies, one abides in the place of contention over dharmas. Therefore, Ajita, one should protect the Tathāgata's skillful means. The Buddha himself teaches this Dharma according to the occasion."

At this, Mañjuśrī addressed the Bodhisattva Gathering-of-Merit, the Bodhisattva Non-Deluded-Vision, the Bodhisattva One-Eloquence-Mind, the Bodhisattva Well-Knowing-Expressed-Mind, the Bodhisattva Reproving-Eloquence, the Bodhisattva Joy-King, the Bodhisattva Hair-Rising-Fearless-Practice-Free-from-Enmity, the Bodhisattva Mind-Aspiring-to-Immeasurable-Buddha-Lands, the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, the Bodhisattva Fragrant-Hands, the Bodhisattva Removing-All-Coverings, the Bodhisattva Not-Placing-Far-Away, the Bodhisattva United-Hundred-Thousand-Virtues, the Bodhisattva Majestic-Sound, the Bodhisattva Mind-Not-Abandoning-All-Wisdoms, the Bodhisattva Proclaimed-Fame-Heroic-Banner, the Bodhisattva Seeking-All-Meanings, the Bodhisattva Practice-Not-Leaving-Buddha-Realm, the Bodhisattva Surpassing-Moon-Palace-Blazing-Radiance, and the Bodhisattva Adorning-All-Great-Realms. Mañjuśrī addressed these twenty bodhisattvas and then further addressed the Buddha, saying: "It is so, it is so! World-Honored One! Truly as the Sage teaches. I traveled eastward past sixty Ganges-sands of buddha-lands, paying homage to all the world-honored ones and hearing their Dharma-teaching, and it was the same as today. In the buddha-lands of the west, south, north, the four intermediate directions, above, and below, it was likewise. After traveling for seven days, I proceeded further but saw no other buddhas. Then I returned and came to this land to prostrate and listen to the sūtras."

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed Mañjuśrī: "You should observe the Tathāgata's sacred wisdom, which is immeasurable. The realms of all the buddhas are inconceivable, august and majestic. What the Tathāgata has entered is without equal and without peer, and there is nothing it does not completely pervade. Yet there are foolish and ignorant people who do not understand the meaning and principle, who arbitrarily say: 'The Prajñāpāramitā is the Tathāgata's practice; it is the unsurpassed teaching of all tathāgatas. The rest of the sūtras are not the Buddha's words.'

"Mañjuśrī! The bodhisattva's practice is the practice without confluence, and this is the true practice of a great being. The practice without equal is the bodhisattva's practice. The practice without reception is the bodhisattva's practice. The practice without attachment, and likewise without non-attachment, is the bodhisattva's practice. Thus, Mañjuśrī, the bodhisattva's practice is without arrogance. I analyze and teach it according to the occasion. The dharmas are difficult to see and difficult to comprehend. Therefore, Mañjuśrī, all wise ones should cultivate tranquil practice without negligence, following the firm practice of the righteous, always harboring a compassionate mind without doing harm through anger. Abiding in all dharmas and cultivating the practice of equality accords with the Buddha's teaching.

"The Buddha, over incalculable kalpas, has followed and practiced this path and proclaims it through skillful means. Yet people without wisdom harbor various views and fall into great difficulties, generating criticism and slander, slandering the sūtra-path, saying: 'This is not what the Tathāgata proclaimed.' Those who revile the Dharma do not realize their subtle failings; they fall because they do not guard their mouths. Some think in their minds, 'This matter is excellent' or 'That is not excellent,' and thereby slander the Dharma. Having slandered the Dharma, they slander the Buddha. Having slandered the Buddha, they defame the noble Sangha.

"Arbitrarily saying, 'This matter is appropriate; that matter is not appropriate,' constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Saying, 'This was taught for bodhisattvas; this teaching is for śrāvakas,' constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Saying, 'Bodhisattvas should study this dharma; they should abandon that dharma and should not study it,' falsely speaking thus, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Saying, 'So-and-so has eloquence; so-and-so lacks eloquence; so-and-so is naturally talented; so-and-so is naturally dull,' making such unfounded criticisms about the sūtra-teaching, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Saying, 'When one encounters the Buddha's time, one can obtain dhāraṇī; when one does not encounter the Buddha's time, one cannot obtain dhāraṇī,' constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Saying, 'Although one has cultivated and attained dhāraṇī, the dhāraṇī so attained is not necessarily pure,' constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Seeking a Dharma-teacher's faults, scrutinizing their practices and conduct for deficiencies, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Not believing that a Dharma-teacher's practices are complete, or even if the Dharma-teacher does not accord with proper deportment, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Proclaiming a Dharma-teacher's negligent deeds, racing about on one's own unable to concentrate, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Being deficient in propriety, deviating from sūtras and violating principles, not upholding the precepts, being agitated and tumultuous, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Proclaiming some meaning or principle without broad understanding, making unfounded pronouncements, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Speaking unclearly, with unpolished eloquence, while wishing to transmit the path's teaching, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Not having studied diligently from the beginning, knowing very little, with understanding that is not broad, while wishing to proclaim the path's teaching, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Thinking in one's mind, 'So-and-so does not know their limitations, does not understand timeliness; I should teach and transform them, leading them to the ultimate meaning,' constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Generating thoughts without protecting the Buddha's teaching, harboring dangerous and harmful intentions, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Each obtaining one volume of sūtra text and teaching, each studying their own portion, contending over meaning while losing the meaning, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Each memorizing one verse and contending over its meaning, each claiming theirs is right and the other is wrong, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Saying, 'So-and-so has faith and joy; so-and-so is not devout; so-and-so will attain liberation; so-and-so will not attain liberation,' constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"When teaching the Dharma, if what one says differs from the meaning, claiming this is not what so-and-so taught, deliberately coming to disrupt the gathering with idle talk, constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Saying, 'This person should practice; that person should not practice; so-and-so is compliant; so-and-so is not compliant,' constitutes slandering the Dharma.

"Saying, 'So-and-so understands timeliness; so-and-so does not understand timeliness,' deviating from meaning and principle, not following the path's principles, constitutes slandering the Dharma."

The Buddha addressed Mañjuśrī: "Whatever the śrāvaka assembly conveys and analyzes, and whatever the bodhisattvas of intelligence and eloquence proclaim, all is sustained by the Tathāgata's spiritual power and sacred intent."

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata's path-teaching is timely and appropriate; his vow is as such. These foolish people think only of reviling, seeking others' strengths and weaknesses. Not following the Buddha's teaching, they oppose the Tathāgata and slander the Dharma-teachers they encounter. All such persons are confused by Māra. Relying on the pursuit of profit and offerings, they generate such intentions and will fall into evil destinies. They protect their household connections and those upon whom they depend, yet they fail to protect the Tathāgata's teaching. They rely on powerful and prestigious persons: kings, elders, and brahmins. If someone comes to question the meaning and they recite and explain the doctrine, all together they praise: 'Well spoken! What you know is excellent, just as the Buddha taught. What you proclaim has great meaning and principle.' All encourage and support one another, and through this encouragement, they fall into slandering the Dharma. Being of the same party, coming and going together, afterward when they die, they all fall into evil destinies together."

The Buddha further addressed Mañjuśrī: "If there is a bodhisattva who is attached to various practices, I do not call this person a bodhisattva."

The Buddha said: "Ajita! Those who rely on family status and powerful positions, the Buddha does not consider these to be pure. Those who proclaim the sūtra-meaning in two categories, whose words and deeds differ, the Buddha does not say they will attain liberation; they will surely fall into hell. Even if someone specializes in one category and says the meaning and principle are ultimate and unsurpassable, the Buddha does not say this person has transcended the difficulties of evil destinies and various afflictions. Those given to much speech who delight in clamorous crowds, the Buddha does not consider this as befitting pure conduct."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "What the Buddha proclaims to rectify all inversions encompasses dharma-gates as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. The dharma-gates he proclaims for those attached to emptiness are likewise as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. For those of false discrimination who reckon there is a person, the dharma-gate teachings are again as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. For sentient beings, for the deeds of no-thought and the sources of all-thought, for the deeds of no-aspiration and the foundations of all-aspiration, the dharma-gates he teaches are again as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. For persons and non-persons, for life and non-life, for longevity and non-longevity, for desire and non-desire, for greed and non-greed, for the conditioned and unconditioned; for those who are diligent in following the eternal, for those who are not diligent and think only of annihilation; for those who delight in worldly convention and those who seek transcendence; for the gate of desire, the gate of anger, and the gate of delusion: the Tathāgata enables all to remove these gates, and therefore teaches the dharma-gates."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "If one cultivates the practice of equality and is forever without attachment, then everything accords with the Prajñāpāramitā teaching. If one teaches that wisdoms are each different, with the mind fixed on seeing and hearing, expounding what does not accord with wisdom, this constitutes slandering the Tathāgata."

At this, Mañjuśrī further asked the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Are these persons led astray by evil companions, receiving mistaken teachings, and so generating such slander? By what means can they confess in this present life and remove their transgressions?"

The World-Honored One addressed Mañjuśrī: "If such a person, for seven years, confesses and takes responsibility six times daily, day and night, then afterward the transgression of slander may be removed, gradually dissolving. They must then additionally undergo fifteen kalpas of practice before attaining the patient acceptance of dharmas."

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! If a bodhisattva learns the essential points of this sūtra but cannot comprehend them and rashly proclaims them, desiring to explain these matters is most difficult; the Dharma of words and letters scatters the meaning without flowing clearly. Therefore, a bodhisattva should first study thoroughly and truly, and only then proclaim it. Studying in this way, one does not harm oneself."

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! A bodhisattva has four dharmas for clearly comprehending all practices. What are the four? First, an equal mind of sympathy for all sentient beings. Second, equal understanding of all dharmas without partiality. Third, equality regarding the path's meaning, not relying on the deviant or the correct. Fourth, equal speech without harboring false discrimination. These are the four. If one does not understand these four equalities and rashly speaks, one injures oneself.

"If sons and daughters of good family abide in four dharmas, they do not harm themselves. What are the four? First, not harboring a harmful mind toward sentient beings. Second, not contending with Dharma-teachers. Third, if one's own understanding is limited, not reviling others who possess broad and penetrating wisdom. Fourth, thinking in one's mind: 'All of these meanings are what the Buddha has taught; therefore I should respectfully honor them with humility and compliance.' These are the four dharmas for not harming oneself."

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! If there is a bodhisattva who fills buddha-lands throughout Ganges-sands of the ten directions with the seven treasures, and for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges makes offerings daily to the buddhas of the ten directions, each day doing so, giving and making offerings to the buddhas without rest, and if another person receives the essential points of this 'Aiding the Study of All Vaipulya' sūtra, writes it down on bamboo or silk, and proclaims it even once, the merit of the latter surpasses the former's offering to all buddhas. Why? The merit of generosity, moral discipline, patience, diligence, single-mindedness, and wisdom cannot match the wholesome deeds of this person. The essential points of this 'Aiding the Study of All Vaipulya' sūtra: for a bodhisattva who studies this, the merit is without equal, walking alone without peer."

When this sūtra was taught, bodhisattvas as numerous as thirty Ganges-sands all attained the patient acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas. Bodhisattvas as numerous as seventy Ganges-sands were all established in the stage of non-retrogression and will attain the unsurpassed, true, and perfect enlightenment. Sixty billion koṭi hundreds of thousands of beings born in this trichiliocosm, hearing this sūtra, all together encouraged one another and generated the aspiration for the path. All at once their understanding penetrated and they transcended the difficulty of eighty kalpas of birth and death, and they all simultaneously attained non-retrogression.

The Buddha taught thus. The bhikṣus and bodhisattvas, and the entire assembly of devas, dragons, spirits, gandharvas, asuras, and all the people of the world, hearing what the Buddha taught, were all filled with joy, made obeisance, and departed.
