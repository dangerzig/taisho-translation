# 佛五百弟子自說本起經
## Sūtra of the Buddha's Five Hundred Disciples Each Speaking of Their Past Lives

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0199

Translated during the Western Jin dynasty by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護).

---

## Fascicle One

Now the Nāga King Anavatapta was one who, in the time when the Buddha was in the world, had received the prediction of bodhisattvahood. He possessed fierce and mighty virtue and dwelt upon the heights of Mount Kunlun. The palace and jeweled halls where this nāga resided were the source of the five rivers, which he oversaw and governed. There was a pool of water with eight flavors, where flowers of seven colors grew. Those who drank this water would recall their past lives. At that time, the Nāga King invited the World-Honored Buddha and the five hundred foremost disciples. When the meal was finished, seated upon lotus flowers, they recounted their past lives and the merit and offenses they had committed. All of these arose from the most subtle causes and gradually brought about their karmic retribution. Through kalpas and eons, none had been able to save themselves; only through the fortune of encountering a perfectly awakened one were they able to cross beyond the world. Each composed verses expressing what they had realized, as follows:

### Chapter 1: Mahākāśyapa

> The Buddha, supreme among humans, the Dharma's charioteer,
> Having severed the bonds of the prison of fetters, dwelt in Śrāvastī.
> All his faculties at peace, his virtue towering and majestic,
> The Tathāgata himself addressed his bhikṣus:
> "There is a place where gods and spirits take delight,
> With various flowers beyond all number.
> Four great rivers surge forth toward the four directions;
> All those flowing rivers return to the sea.
> Sindhu and the Lion River, where humans cannot reach, only supernatural powers can go;
> Flying swiftly, one surpasses all. Come quickly, let us visit that deep flowing pool."
> The bhikṣus said: "Very well, we obey." The great and penetrating foremost disciples,
> Hearing the Honored One's command, mounted their supernatural powers.
> Like the king of geese leading a flock of geese,
> They traveled and journeyed to the river.
> Joyfully observing their companions delighting together,
> The Buddha, God among Gods, was likewise:
> Together with his disciples he soared aloft.
> When the Buddha arrived, he told his disciples:
> "Do you recall what you experienced in past lives?
> Speak for me, each of you, of what practices you undertook
> And thereby obtained immeasurable merit."
> That venerable Kāśyapa, disciple of the Buddha,
> Like a lion traversing the deep mountains,
> Wherever he passes, none dare stand against him,
> Then spoke of his deeds in past lives:
> "Gathering wild oats from the field,
> I made a small offering to a pratyekabuddha,
> Whose liberated mind was joyful, without outflows,
> Who served the practice of emptiness with a tranquil mind.
> At that time, my mind made this vow,
> And I immediately contemplated the supreme Dharma:
> 'May I be united with such a person.'
> From that life I was reborn in Uttarakuru.
> Because of the merit from that karmic connection,
> I was reborn a thousand times in Uttarakuru,
> And then was born in the Heaven of Supreme Life,
> Where I was the most distinguished, without equal.
> Through the merit of those deeds of virtue,
> I was also reborn a thousand times in Trāyastriṃśa,
> Adorned with various flowers, fragrant jeweled garlands,
> My body subtle, wonderful, and free.
> When my heavenly lifespan ended,
> I was then reborn again in Uttarakuru,
> Because of the vow I made in a past life,
> Through the karmic connection of that meritorious deed.
> Born into a wealthy brāhmaṇa family,
> With property and occupations beyond number,
> Amid the five pleasures I was without craving.
> This Buddha is without equal.
> The Great Compassionate One expounds the Dharma;
> With the powers, single-minded, he settles the faculties.
> The seven factors of awakening and the Eightfold Path:
> Through these I attained this Dharma.
> I then exhausted all outflows, holding a lamp in hand.
> Together with this assembly in the final gathering,
> United in right practice, free from the deviant:
> What the Buddha, the Tathāgata, teaches is good.
> Those who uphold the precepts attain what they aspire to;
> Whatever their minds wish, they obtain.
> In this my final body, all is fulfilled:
> I have ended birth-and-death and uprooted its base.
> I have severed and removed all bonds of craving;
> I am a Dharma-prince of the Buddha.
> Foremost in contentment, always contemplating the Way,
> My mind empty, pure, without attachment,
> My resolve firm and unshakable,
> Like a great mountain that cannot be moved."

> Thus the Venerable Kāśyapa,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the great pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past karmic connections.

### Chapter 2: Śāriputra

> "When I was a sage dwelling in seclusion,
> I saw a śramaṇa there,
> A venerable pratyekabuddha
> Wearing a crimson-dyed robe.
> Seeing him, my heart rejoiced.
> I washed his garments for him,
> And also sewed a kaṣāya,
> And repeatedly bowed in reverence.
> He then had compassion for me
> And flew up into the sky,
> Above and below producing water and fire,
> And in an instant vanished.
> I then joined my palms
> And made this vow in my heart:
> 'May I attain such
> Brilliant and great wisdom.
> Let me not be born into a noble house,
> Nor into a low-born family,
> But always born into a middle family,
> With the aspiration to become a śramaṇa.'
> Through this merit,
> For five hundred lives
> I always obtained a human body,
> And life after life became a śramaṇa.
> In this final life,
> I again obtained a human birth
> And had the fortune to see the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior.
> I then became a śramaṇa
> Under the Lion of the Śākyas.
> Having accomplished arhatship,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> Now, before the World-Honored One,
> Among the bhikṣu assembly,
> He declares my wisdom as foremost,
> Turning the wheel of the correct Dharma."

> Śāriputra, the wise one,
> Before the bhikṣu assembly,
> At the great pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 3: Mahāmaudgalyāyana

> "When I was a sage dwelling in seclusion,
> Living among the forest trees,
> A person came to me there
> Seeking to become a śramaṇa.
> I shaved off his hair,
> Washed his garments,
> Sewed and dyed them,
> And in my heart rejoiced.
> He then withdrew to one side
> And sat in full lotus posture.
> He then attained pratyekabuddhahood
> And flew into the sky.
> I then made this vow: 'May I attain supernatural powers;
> May I become like this,
> With great strength and great supernatural powers.'
> Through this meritorious deed,
> Wherever I was born,
> In the heavens or among humans,
> The merit I had created was luminous.
> In this final life,
> Having obtained a human body,
> I had the fortune to see the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior.
> I became a śramaṇa
> Under the Lion of the Śākyas,
> And attained arhatship,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> The good I did was very little,
> Yet I received immeasurable peace.
> I also did unwholesome deeds;
> Now I shall speak of them, so listen.
> East of Rājagṛha I was born
> As the son of a distinguished man.
> Going outside to play,
> I begged for food at others' homes.
> I saw my father and mother
> Together enjoying themselves.
> Seeing this, they beat me
> And cursed and drove me away.
> It was only to provide a living,
> But the deed my body did not perform.
> I fell into the Hell of Black Ropes,
> Suffering beyond all reckoning.
> Because of the remaining consequences of that evil,
> In this final life,
> The followers of other heterodox paths
> Beat my body to pieces like reeds.
> Through this illness I shall
> End my life and enter nirvāṇa.
> The remaining consequences of those deeds
> Will then be fully exhausted.
> Therefore, with a joyful heart,
> One should serve one's parents with utmost filial devotion.
> Through the merit of a joyful heart,
> One surpasses even the heavens."

> Thus the Venerable Kolita,
> Among the bhikṣu assembly,
> At the great pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past karmic connections.

### Chapter 4: Revata

> "Once long ago I went to a monastery
> And saw a place that was unclean.
> I took a broom
> And swept that monastery clean.
> When I beheld the monastery was pure,
> My heart was filled with great joy:
> 'May I be free from dust and defilement,
> As clean as this monastery.'
> Through this merit,
> Wherever I was born,
> My complexion was radiant and beautiful,
> With a fine appearance hard to match.
> With the remaining blessings from that fortune,
> In this final life,
> My parents named me
> 'Purified' (Revata).
> Among my kinfolk,
> My birth was also pure.
> All loved and revered me;
> Those who saw me were never satiated.
> I had the fortune to see the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior.
> Having attained arhatship,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> The wish I had made,
> That I be free from dust and defilement:
> Now I am a stainless arhat,
> Without outflows, my task accomplished.
> Even if one were to sweep
> The entire world clean,
> It would not compare to being free from desire
> And sweeping clean one's own walking path.
> Even sweeping the whole earth
> And a practitioner's walking path
> Would not compare to sweeping one step of ground
> For the saṃgha of the four directions.
> Even sweeping
> All the monasteries that fill the earth
> Would not compare to sweeping one step of ground
> In a temple of the Buddha.
> The merit I created with my body,
> By this I know the difference.
> One should sweep the Buddha's temple
> With a joyful heart.
> From this one may understand
> That the merit of the Perfectly Awakened One is lofty.
> One should make offerings at the Buddha's temple;
> The blessing obtained is exceedingly great.
> O honored ones, I recall
> The good deeds I once performed.
> Having reaped their fruit,
> They brought delight and peaceful bliss.
> Therefore, for the Buddha's temple,
> One should joyfully make offerings with a pure heart.
> O honored ones, this is the foremost
> Field of merit, without superior.
> Through making offerings to it,
> One obtains immeasurable peace
> And destroys and removes entirely
> All lust, anger, and delusion.
> Do not make light of it, thinking your joyful heart
> Will yield only thin and meager merit,
> When directed toward the Tathāgata, the Perfectly Awakened One,
> And toward the disciples of the Buddha."

> Thus Revata,
> Before the bhikṣus,
> At the great pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past causes.

### Chapter 5: Sumanā

> "Once long ago I went out on a tour,
> Together with friends and kinfolk.
> On my head I wore adornments,
> And at my ears I wore sumanā flowers.
> In the time of Vipaśyin, the Buddha of supernatural powers,
> A great monastery was established there.
> From afar I saw the people
> Dwelling together and making offerings.
> My friends all set out from home,
> Each bringing fine flowers,
> All with pure hearts
> Scattering them as offerings at that buddha's temple.
> When I saw their generous giving,
> I too was first inspired.
> I then gathered flowers from the grove
> And offered them to the buddha's temple.
> Wherever I was born, I fell not into evil destinies,
> But ascended to heaven or descended as a human.
> Through this root of virtue,
> The good I had done was luminous.
> Later I met the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The unsurpassed guide.
> Having realized arhatship,
> Cool and quenched, I attained nirvāṇa.
> I offered but a single flower,
> Yet gained a hundred thousand years
> Of heavenly self-delight;
> The remaining merit brought nirvāṇa.
> If only I had known from the start
> That the Buddha's merit is immeasurable,
> I would have erected stūpas and temples,
> And the merit would have been without limit.
> Yet even without full joy of heart,
> The merit was still considerable.
> The Tathāgata, the Perfectly Awakened One,
> And the disciples of the Buddha:
> I recall
> The meritorious deeds I performed with my body.
> Now I have obtained their true reward,
> Delightful and blissfully peaceful.
> Through the deeds I performed,
> Beginning and end are severed, no more rebirth.
> Without outflows, without attachment,
> Cool and quenched, I attained nirvāṇa.
> The five destinies are finished for me;
> Never again shall I enter a womb.
> This is my final life;
> Hereafter, I shall not arise again.
> Liberated from the root of birth-and-death,
> I have crossed the sea of existence.
> Through this karmic connection,
> I am named Sumanā."

> At that time the elder Sumanā,
> Present in the assembly of monks,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 6: Sroṇa Koṭiviṃśa

> "In the time of Vipaśyin Buddha,
> In the land of Bandhumatī,
> I originally built one chamber
> For the saṃgha of the four directions.
> I also offered bedding and furnishings,
> All given in generosity.
> Having given with a joyful heart,
> I made this vow at once:
> 'May I see a perfectly awakened one
> And become a śramaṇa,
> Attaining the supreme unconditioned,
> Cool and quenched, in perfect nirvāṇa.'
> Through the root of this merit,
> For ninety-one kalpas I was at peace,
> Endowed with natural clear sight,
> In the heavens and in the world.
> With the remaining meritorious blessings,
> In this present final life,
> I was born to a powerful elder's family,
> Proud and noble, with no siblings.
> Born, I was revered by my father,
> Who gave me his instructions:
> 'I give you, my son,
> Treasures of billions of kinds.'
> On the soles of my feet grew unusual hair,
> Naturally four inches long.
> My body was soft, fine, and lovely;
> I walked in comfort, unharmed.
> For ninety past kalpas
> And again as many more,
> I have no memory
> Of ever stepping on bare ground.
> In this present final life,
> Having again obtained a human body,
> I accomplished non-attachment,
> Cool and quenched, in nirvāṇa.
> The Buddha, the Universal Seer, declared me
> Foremost in diligent effort.
> Liberated, outflows exhausted,
> I have attained the unshakable stage."

> Thus this one of the Koliya clan,
> In the midst of the assembly,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past merit.

### Chapter 7: Vaṅgīsa

> "I did not understand merit,
> Nor did I originally understand its meaning.
> Seeing the temple of Vipaśyin Buddha,
> I made offerings and attended to it.
> A golden temple of refined purple-gold color,
> With banners, parasols, fragrant flowers:
> Seeing offerings made to stūpas and temples,
> I was reborn in good destinies.
> Always amid gods and humans,
> My deeds were luminous.
> For ninety-one kalpas,
> I never fell into evil destinies.
> Having done but a little merit,
> I obtained abundant peace.
> Having attained non-attachment,
> Nirvāṇa, cool and refreshing.
> If only I had known from the start
> That the Buddha's merit is thus,
> I would always have made offerings at stūpas and temples,
> And the merit obtained would surpass this.
> Therefore, knowing clearly
> That the Perfectly Awakened One's virtue is vast and great,
> One should make offerings at stūpas and temples;
> The merit is without end.
> The Buddha, the Universal Seer, declared me
> Foremost in delight in the sūtras,
> Learned in many kinds,
> With eloquence truly accomplished."

> At that time the elder Vaṅgīsa,
> Present in the assembly of monks,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 8: Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja

> "When I was with my parents,
> Born as the foremost among their sons,
> I respectfully served my father
> And was filial in caring for my mother.
> For my parents, siblings,
> Servants, attendants, and workers,
> I would tell my parents
> About food and drink in due season.
> But then I arose a mind of greed and jealousy,
> Thinking they should not eat my parents' food.
> With angry, slanderous words,
> I claimed I could get food and wealth.
> Because of this sin,
> I fell into the great mountain hell.
> Burned and roasted in the Hell of Black Ropes,
> I suffered beyond all reckoning.
> After emerging from hell,
> Wherever I was born,
> I always suffered great hunger and thirst,
> And in hardship died of starvation.
> In this present final life,
> Having again obtained a human body,
> I had the fortune to see the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior.
> Under the Lion of the Śākyas,
> I became a silent contemplative.
> Having attained the path of non-attachment,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> O honored ones, I now possess
> Supernatural powers and the ability to fly,
> Yet I must enter a cave
> Before I can obtain food.
> Therefore, with a joyful heart,
> One should make offerings to one's parents.
> Single-mindedly bowing in reverence,
> The blessings obtained are immeasurable.
> O honored ones, I recall
> And cut away the evil deeds I committed.
> One reaps what one has sown:
> Merit and offenses cannot be separated."

> Piṇḍola, who dwells behind closed doors,
> Present at that time in the assembly,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 9: Svāgata

> "Once I was a distinguished man's son,
> In the land of Bandhumatī,
> Of noble family with much wealth and treasure,
> Surrounded by kinfolk.
> On every side, near the king,
> In delight without limit,
> Handsome, a joy to behold,
> My complexion hard to match.
> One time I went out in splendid array,
> With my retinue leading and following,
> Wishing to tour and observe everywhere,
> Accompanied by many ladies.
> During that outing,
> I saw a śramaṇa of tranquil appearance,
> Practicing the rites of calm abiding,
> His body clad in a crimson-red robe.
> When I saw that śramaṇa,
> An evil thought arose in me.
> I despised his appearance
> And was angry and displeased.
> 'Why has he shaved off his hair?
> His face is dark and ugly.
> Covered in sores and scabs,
> Emaciated in body and mind alike!'
> Because of this sin,
> These evil words spoken by my mouth,
> After my life ended,
> I fell into hell.
> Emerging from hell, my complexion was dark and ugly,
> Covered in sores and scabs,
> Emaciated in body and mind.
> Holding a clay bowl, I begged for alms,
> Wearing the clothes of the dead.
> My garments were worn, coarse, and filthy;
> Wherever I stayed, there was no peace.
> Wherever I went,
> Begging to sustain my life,
> People wielding sticks drove me away and shouted;
> I was despised and humiliated.
> Like this for five hundred lives,
> Wherever I was born,
> I was destitute and always starving,
> Suffering and dying of hunger.
> Then I saw the Perfectly Awakened One,
> Surrounded by the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> Together with a great assembly,
> Expounding the words of sweet dew.
> As soon as I saw the great assembly,
> I quickly ran toward them,
> Hoping among them
> To find food and provisions.
> Arriving, I saw the great assembly,
> All seated, wishing to hear the Dharma.
> I did not obtain my original wish;
> There was no one offering food.
> Then the Great Compassionate One,
> The Tathāgata, addressed me:
> 'Honored one, welcome!
> Come and sit upon this seat.'
> I was immediately overjoyed,
> And single-mindedly with palms joined,
> I bowed my head at the World-Honored One's feet
> And sat to one side.
> Then the Honored Great Compassionate One,
> Gautama, of utmost mercy,
> In order explained and separately set forth
> The Four Noble Truths for me.
> The Able One shaved off my hair,
> And through this I saw the path.
> The Buddha made me a contemplative,
> And there I attained supernatural powers.
> Because of this, my name is
> Called Svāgata.
> For this reason the Buddha declared me
> Foremost in right concentration.
> The Buddha, the valiant and great honored one,
> The world-hero, supreme of all,
> With supernatural powers and limitless compassion,
> Liberated me from all suffering."

> Thus the Venerable Svāgata,
> Among the assembly of monks,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 10: Nanda

> "East of the city of Rājagṛha,
> I was once a wealthy and distinguished man.
> At a time when grain was scarce and expensive,
> A practitioner wandered there.
> When I was eating alone,
> A good practitioner came,
> A pratyekabuddha who had realized awakening,
> Free and without outflows.
> A mind of greed and jealousy arose,
> And my heart was set on evil:
> 'Now this bhikṣu has come;
> How can I feed him along with everyone else?'
> Then, thinking of food,
> I mixed in horse dung.
> After the practitioner ate it,
> He immediately died.
> After my own life ended,
> I fell into hell for a very long time,
> In the Saṃghāta and Screaming hells,
> Life after life, boiled and roasted.
> Emerging from hell,
> I again obtained a human body,
> But was always afflicted with many illnesses,
> Dying in torment and distress.
> Like this for five hundred lives,
> Wherever I was born,
> I was always sick and destitute,
> Dying in anguish.
> In this present final life,
> Having been born among humans,
> I again met the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior.
> I left home as a śramaṇa,
> Receiving the Dharma of the Lion of the Śākyas.
> Having attained the arhat's path,
> Cool and quenched, I took nirvāṇa.
> O honored ones, I now possess
> Supernatural powers, without outflows,
> Yet my body is afflicted with many illnesses,
> And wherever I am, there is no peace.
> I now fully recall
> The deeds I committed in past lives.
> One reaps their fruits;
> Merit and offenses cannot be separated."

> Thus the Venerable Nanda,
> Among the bhikṣu assembly,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 11: Yaśas

> "Once there was a practitioner
> Who entered a village to beg for alms.
> He saw the body of a dead woman,
> Blue, bloated, and foul-smelling.
> He sat in full lotus posture,
> Contemplating impermanence and change,
> Examining the decomposition and impurity,
> Single-mindedly studying concentration.
> Then, upon that very seat,
> There was a faint, subtle sound.
> Hearing it, frightened,
> He emerged from his single-pointed focus.
> He saw the dead belly burst open,
> The foul discharge and impurity,
> Flowing from every orifice,
> The stench unbearable.
> Intestines, stomach, and five organs exposed,
> Heart and liver all scattered.
> Innumerable insects:
> Having observed this, he quieted his mind.
> He examined the outer dead body
> And inwardly examined his own body:
> 'That is thus, and I am likewise;
> In essence, all is empty and void.'
> Rising from samādhi,
> He practiced without negligence,
> Neither going out on alms rounds
> Nor thinking of food and drink.
> 'If I enter a village
> To seek food and drink,
> Though I see beautiful forms,
> I shall contemplate them as foul discharge.
> Observing all those bodily forms
> As no different from a corpse,
> Examining the root of decay and ruin,
> I shall find no delight in any of it.'
> Through this contemplation,
> I became free from craving and desire.
> Practicing the four brahmavihāras,
> Deeply contemplating without frivolity.
> After that life ended,
> I was reborn in the Brahmā heaven.
> When my Brahmā lifespan was exhausted,
> I descended and was born in Vārāṇasī,
> Into a powerful and distinguished elder's family,
> Born as his son.
> Revered by all,
> In right concentration, immeasurably profound.
> By day I always practiced;
> At night I did not sleep.
> Seeing the many women,
> I regarded them equally as rotting heaps.
> Those who lay sleeping on drums as pillows,
> Those who held harps as performers,
> Their instruments scattered on the ground,
> Dreaming and talking in their sleep.
> Then, withdrawing to reflect,
> I recalled the meritorious practices of past lives
> And recognized the place of impurity
> That I had experienced before.
> Having observed this,
> I aspired to a mind free from desire.
> I was then compelled by this.
> O honored ones, I departed.
> Rising from my bed,
> I descended the hall and fled.
> The gods had compassion for me,
> And the gates opened of themselves.
> I then left the city
> And went to the side of a flowing river.
> Gazing at the far bank,
> I saw a śramaṇa with tranquil faculties.
> Seeing this great contemplative,
> I raised my voice and cried out,
> Declaring my distress:
> 'O supernatural one, I have renounced desire!'
> The World-Honored One, with a deep, gentle voice,
> In response to my words of anguish, said:
> 'Young man, come, do not be afraid.
> Here there is no distress.
> Let your mind release all suffering
> And cross to the other shore.'
> I went to where the Great Compassionate One was,
> The World-Honored One, without compare,
> Supremely wondrous, without equal.
> Like one who is hungry and thirsty,
> Hearing the teaching, I understood its meaning;
> Indeed, I grasped its meaning.
> There I saw the truth of the path.
> I asked the Buddha to let me leave home.
> Gautama, the Great Compassionate,
> Permitted me to become a śramaṇa.
> That very night,
> As dawn approached,
> All outflows were exhausted,
> And cool and quenched, I attained nirvāṇa.
> These were my past-life
> Good deeds.
> This is my final life;
> I have attained the sweet-dew path."

> Thus the worthy Yaśas,
> Son of a distinguished man, possessing supernatural powers,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 12: Śrīla

> "Once, in the city of Vārāṇasī,
> After Kāśyapa Buddha's parinirvāṇa,
> King Kṛkin erected a stūpa,
> Made of the seven precious substances, exceedingly great.
> At that time, the king's eldest prince:
> I then, for that honored buddha,
> Was the first to erect the central pillar.
> Through this merit,
> Wherever I was born,
> In the heavens or among humans,
> My blessings were naturally manifest.
> Wherever I was born,
> In that kingdom there was great prosperity.
> Wealth beyond all counting,
> I always delighted in great generosity.
> For five hundred lives,
> I gave without sparing.
> I provided for all people,
> Contemplatives and brāhmaṇas.
> Through the practice of the pratyekabuddha path,
> Free from craving, without outflows,
> With a pure and joyful heart,
> I made offerings to five hundred of them.
> Through this merit,
> In this present final life,
> I was born into the noble Śākya clan.
> At the moment of my birth, I spoke these words:
> 'Are there treasures in this house,
> Coins and goods?
> I shall give them all away,
> To save and relieve the poor.
> I give without weariness,
> Saving all the lowly.
> When I see the return of good faith and good deeds,
> How could I not be generous?'
> My family, hearing my words,
> Was anxious and alarmed.
> They scattered in all eight directions;
> My nurses all fled.
> My mother, out of love and affection,
> Immediately said to me:
> 'Are you a god, a human, a ghost, or a spirit?
> Why do you speak with such urgency?'
> I then replied: 'I am a human, not a ghost.
> Recalling my past-life practice of giving,
> I wish to be generous to people.'
> When my mother heard these words,
> She leaped with joy, without fear,
> And permitted and encouraged me,
> Letting me give as I wished.
> My family and kinfolk were many;
> My mother instructed them to care for me.
> Revered by all who loved me,
> Those who saw me were delighted.
> When I was born, my family prospered.
> Because of this, the contemplatives
> Named me Śrīla.
> There I gave generously,
> Supplying all the poor.
> Meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> I renounced the household for the Way.
> Born, and the household prospered;
> Touching the ground, I could speak.
> Therefore I am called Śrī,
> My name spreading naturally.
> Born to a household without craving,
> And also without fear,
> Through faith I left home to study the Way,
> Fully endowed with supernatural powers.
> Honored by the king of the country,
> By great ministers and all the people,
> I received abundant offerings of clothing and food,
> Bedding and all comforts."

> Thus Śrīla,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 13: Bākula

> "Once I was a medicine seller
> In the land of Bandhumatī,
> In the time of Vipaśyin Buddha.
> I reverently served the bhikṣu-saṃgha.
> When there were those who were ill,
> I administered medicine to cure them.
> I supplied all kinds of herbal medicine
> And gave them to the bhikṣus.
> For one year I ensured the saṃgha
> Lacked for nothing.
> At that time I gave to the śramaṇas
> A single myrobalan fruit.
> For ninety-one kalpas,
> I never fell into evil destinies.
> In the heavens and among humans,
> My blessings were naturally manifest.
> The merit I created was little,
> Yet the blessings I received were immeasurable.
> From giving one myrobalan fruit,
> For a long, long time I was born in good destinies.
> With the remaining blessings,
> I again obtained a human body,
> And met the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The sole guide without equal.
> I have no memory
> Of having ever received offerings from any district or county.
> O honored ones, in two nights
> I realized the three kinds of penetrating wisdom.
> I always wore coarse and poor garments,
> The five-patch paṃśukūla robe.
> I renounced the household and studied the Way,
> Delighting in dwelling in seclusion.
> I lived to one hundred and sixty years.
> In all this time, I was without stain or turbidity.
> I never had an illness;
> Wherever I was born, there was always peace.
> The Buddha, the Universal Seer, taught the Dharma:
> Foremost in having few desires, without drowsiness.
> See the one who gave medicine:
> Such is the breadth of his merit.
> Now I fully recall
> The small merit I planted of old.
> I have reaped all its fruits,
> Delightful and peacefully blissful."

> At that time the worthy Bākula,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 14: Mahākauṣṭhila

> "Once I was a leather worker.
> My livelihood was peaceful.
> At a time when grain was very expensive,
> I softened hides to make leather.
> I once obtained a fine piece of hide
> And boiled it until it was excellent.
> At that time a śramaṇa came
> Begging for food.
> Seeing him, I immediately rejoiced
> And shared my portion with him in giving.
> When that contemplative had eaten,
> He flew up into the sky.
> Seeing the practitioner, I leaped with joy,
> And immediately with palms joined, I bowed toward him,
> Reverently going wherever
> He traveled, following him.
> With a heart of joy and expansive mind,
> I then made this vow:
> 'May I attain what he has,
> And always be with honored ones.
> May I attain the Dharma-body
> As this practitioner has.
> May my body be likewise,
> Swiftly fulfilling my true aspiration.'
> What I gave had no fine form,
> And its smell was foul.
> Without fragrance, without flavor:
> Such was my offering.
> The merit I created was little,
> Yet I received immeasurable peace.
> In the heavens and among humans,
> My blessings were naturally manifest.
> In this present final life,
> I again obtained a human body
> And met the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior.
> The wish I originally made,
> To see the World-Honored One, the supreme person:
> All has been fulfilled,
> And cool and quenched, I attained nirvāṇa.
> Now I fully recall
> The meritorious deeds I performed.
> I have reaped all their fruits,
> Received with delight and joy."

> Thus that great honored one,
> Named Mahākauṣṭhila, the Great Penetrating,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 15: Uruvilvā Kāśyapa

> "Two guides,
> Of the same kind, brothers both,
> Saw that a stūpa of Kāśyapa Buddha
> Had crumbled and collapsed.
> They gathered a company of merchants
> And repaired and rebuilt the stūpa.
> At that time, the two brothers
> Together raised and erected the central pillar.
> Through the root of this merit,
> They were born in heaven for a long time,
> Then returned to be born in the human world,
> Into a powerful and distinguished clan.
> Before meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> They left home and studied heterodox paths,
> By the waters of Nairañjanā,
> Long practicing as matted-hair ascetics.
> The World-Honored One, without equal,
> In compassion and pity for us,
> By the side of the Ganges River,
> Moved and transformed us with miraculous displays.
> When we witnessed these transformations,
> We asked the Buddha to let us shave our hair.
> The Great Honored One, in compassion and pity,
> Permitted us to leave home.
> Making offerings to the Buddha's stūpa and temple,
> Bowing in reverence before it:
> Through this, we and all the people
> Attained coolness and nirvāṇa."

> The Venerable Uruvilvā Kāśyapa
> And the Venerable Nadī Kāśyapa,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of their past deeds.

### Chapter 16: Gayā Kāśyapa

> "Once I was a perfume seller.
> Having obtained fragrance, I sold it.
> A young woman
> Came to my perfume shop.
> Her appearance was beautiful.
> Seeing her approach me,
> I seized her and played with her,
> Desire and intention fixed upon her.
> I did not violate her body,
> Nor did I join with her.
> I only grasped her arm,
> Molesting another's woman.
> Because of this evil deed,
> After death I fell into hell.
> Returning to a human body,
> My right arm naturally withered.
> Like this for five hundred lives,
> Wherever I was born, it was the same:
> My right arm always withered,
> Painful and most inconvenient.
> O honored ones, recall this:
> The sin committed was but slight,
> Yet the misfortune reaped was very great.
> Good and evil cannot be separated.
> Meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> I renounced the household as a śramaṇa.
> Having attained arhatship,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> O honored ones, I now possess
> Supernatural powers and freedom,
> Yet even now my right arm
> Is not as useful as my left.
> If a man
> Delights in violating others,
> After death he falls into hell,
> And the suffering is extremely fierce.
> One should not transgress against outward forms,
> As one would cast away a blazing fire.
> The wise, those who have awakened and understood,
> Already know contentment.
> If one sees another's woman,
> One should practice the contemplation of impurity.
> I have gone through hells,
> Suffering beyond all reckoning.
> When I committed this sin,
> I thought it was not worth mentioning.
> Yet I reaped all its fruits:
> Merit and offenses cannot be separated.
> Meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior,
> I have attained non-attachment,
> Cool and quenched, in nirvāṇa.
> This is my final birth;
> I have attained the sweet-dew phrase.
> Having been freed from all suffering,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa."

> Thus the Venerable Gayā Kāśyapa,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 17: Jyotiṣka

> "In the time of Vipaśyin, the World-Honored One,
> In the city of Bandhumatī,
> There was a wealthy elder
> Named Anagāṇa.
> At that time, the Buddha's retinue
> Numbered six hundred and twenty thousand.
> He invited the Venerable Vipaśyin
> And the assembly for a three-month offering.
> I was lord of Bandhumatī;
> I offered to the Honored One among humans.
> Each day the food was rare and fine,
> Making offerings to the Buddha's disciples.
> Thus was my offering to the Buddha
> In Bandhumatī.
> At the time of the final offering,
> King Bandhumatī wished to arise
> And offer fine food and drink,
> Robes and bedding,
> And construct a wonderful altar.
> This is what the king erected.
> He offered all comforts,
> Seats numbering hundreds of thousands,
> To each and every bhikṣu,
> Giving generously to their satisfaction.
> That king's final
> Offering was thus,
> Serving the limitless hero,
> The guide of supernatural powers.
> When I then saw those offerings,
> Bedding and comforts,
> Robes, food, and drink given freely,
> Seats all fully provided,
> At that time the honored heavenly king,
> Śakra, came to me.
> The heavenly lord said to me: 'I shall be your companion.'
> He immediately conjured an altar,
> Delightful and magnificent as heaven,
> Set with heavenly seats
> And supplied with heavenly food and drink.
> At that time, the World-Honored Buddha,
> Vipaśyin, without equal,
> Received my offering for a full month,
> He and his disciples.
> With heavenly food and drink,
> I made offerings to the guide.
> I offered heavenly robes and garments
> To the great person and his disciples.
> Through this merit,
> The blessings I received were immeasurable.
> For ninety-one kalpas,
> I never fell into evil destinies.
> The merit I created was luminous,
> In the heavens and in the world.
> I served the great sage,
> Vipaśyin, the limitless honored one.
> In this present final life,
> I was born in the city of Rājagṛha,
> In the palace of King Bimbisāra,
> A wealthy family with immeasurable treasures.
> Loved by King Bimbisāra,
> Revered by all,
> Honored and served by the multitude,
> By ministers and people alike.
> I had heavenly music;
> In this world I enjoyed freely.
> Born into the world with a human body,
> Heavenly music delighted me.
> Then the Buddha of great wisdom,
> The guide without superior,
> Came to Rājagṛha.
> The guide, in compassion and pity,
> When I heard that the Great Wise One
> Had come to the Royal City,
> My heart rejoiced and leaped with joy,
> And I went to the honored World-Honored One.
> From afar I saw the World's Radiance,
> His light shining forth, illuminating all.
> I then descended from my chariot
> And walked on foot to the Buddha.
> Joyfully I went forward,
> Bowed my head at the feet of the Supreme One.
> When I had finished bowing to the Tathāgata,
> I sat to one side.
> Long had I yearned for the True Hero;
> Now at last I saw the Great Person,
> The guide, the light among humans,
> Who has subdued Māra's net.
> The World-Honored One, without superior,
> Immediately had compassion for me.
> He explained the Four Noble Truths,
> Teaching their foundation as appropriate.
> Then the Limitless Compassionate One,
> The World-Honored One, spoke thus.
> The Great Penetrating One wished to leave home
> And receive the great precepts.
> Immediately the Buddha of great wisdom,
> Without equal,
> Said: 'Come, bhikṣu!'
> And I was fully accomplished as a śramaṇa.
> Through this earnest diligence,
> With firm effort and settled mind,
> I encountered the place of sweet dew,
> The unconditioned, arising and unshakable.
> Meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior,
> I accomplished arhatship,
> Cool and quenched, in nirvāṇa.
> O honored ones, I recall
> The evil deeds my body committed in the past.
> I have received all their fruits,
> With delight and blissful peace.
> Widely practiced and circulating,
> Freed from birth, old age, sickness, and death,
> Liberated from all afflictions,
> From sorrow and from weeping."

> Thus the Venerable Jyotiṣka,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 18: Rāṣṭrapāla

> "There was a king named Śuddhavinaya.
> That king had a son
> Named Rāṣṭrapālabandhu,
> The king's youngest son.
> In the auspicious time of Kāśyapa Buddha,
> A great stūpa was erected.
> Wishing to honor his royal father's wishes,
> He made the finial for the central pillar.
> With a joyful and leaping heart,
> He erected the dew-catching basin,
> Vowing: 'May I become a śramaṇa,
> United with the Perfectly Awakened One.'
> Through this merit,
> Wherever I was born,
> In the heavens and among humans,
> My virtue was naturally manifest.
> This is my final birth,
> In the land of Thullakotthita.
> Born to a distinguished family,
> With only a single daughter.
> Loved by all,
> Thus was the king Koravya.
> He was my kinsman,
> And the kingdom likewise.
> Handsome and exceedingly fine,
> My countenance like a blooming flower.
> Amid human delights I freely enjoyed,
> Indulging every desire.
> With a delighted heart, the World-Honored One
> Came to Thullakotthita.
> Seeing him, my heart rejoiced,
> And I sought to become a śramaṇa.
> The meritorious roots of past lives
> Brought a transformation beyond compare.
> In compassion and pity for me,
> He then spoke these words:
> 'The buddhas' true teaching is:
> Without the consent of one's parents,
> One may not become a śramaṇa.
> Noble son, go and report to them yourself.'
> I immediately returned home
> And addressed my parents:
> 'Father and mother, please allow me
> To leave home as a śramaṇa.'
> When my parents heard my words,
> Their grief was beyond measure:
> 'Even if our son were dying,
> We would not wish to be parted from him.'
> I then refused food and drink,
> Single-minded, finding no joy,
> My aspiration set on the pure Dharma,
> Wishing to become a śramaṇa.
> Refusing food and drink,
> I lay withered on the bare ground:
> 'If they do not permit me,
> I shall die here.'
> For six days without food or drink,
> Single-minded, finding no joy,
> My aspiration set on the pure Dharma,
> Wishing to become a śramaṇa.
> Then my close friends and acquaintances
> Went and said to my parents:
> 'It would be well to let him go.
> What use is a dead man's body?
> If he can find happiness
> As a śramaṇa, he will still be here.
> While alive, you can visit him;
> If he dies, what then?'
> Then my parents and their friends
> Together came out, their voices full of sorrow:
> 'If you become a śramaṇa,
> Come and visit me. That I permit.'
> Then my close friends and acquaintances
> Went and told me:
> 'Your parents have permitted you.
> Wise one, become a śramaṇa.'
> My parents made one condition:
> 'If you become a śramaṇa,
> Come often to visit us;
> Then, my child, I permit you to leave home.'
> Hearing these joyful words,
> I nourished myself and regained strength.
> I went to where the World-Honored One was
> And addressed the Buddha:
> 'I have received permission.'
> I then received the Honored One's teaching.
> The World-Honored One shaved my hair
> And made me a śramaṇa.
> Because of offering the dew-catching basin,
> I received abundant peace.
> In the heavens and in the world,
> My merit was naturally manifest.
> The Buddha, the Universal Seer, declared me
> Foremost in delight in forest seclusion.
> Having attained arhatship,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> Therefore, with a joyful heart,
> One should turn with gladness toward the Great Compassionate One.
> One should make offerings at stūpas and temples
> And be freed from great fear."

> The great Venerable Rāṣṭrapāla,
> Dwelling in seclusion, wearing the five-patch robe,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 19: Gavāṃpati

> "Once in the city of Rājagṛha,
> I was a wealthy and great distinguished man.
> Five hundred practitioners
> Stayed at my house for one year.
> Five hundred elders
> All came to visit.
> At that time the practitioners
> Each went to one household for food.
> Like me and my household,
> Whatever food we cooked,
> For each and every bhikṣu
> The offering was likewise.
> The senior practitioner
> Distributed to the elders.
> The supreme honored practitioner
> Had this in mind.
> To feed the five hundred people,
> Bean soup was poured on top.
> Such were the provisions I prepared,
> Feeding the bhikṣus thus.
> On the second day of giving
> To those bhikṣus,
> I then arose a thought
> Of greed, jealousy, and ill will:
> 'It is already hard to feed my children,
> My wife, sisters,
> Brothers, and kinfolk,
> With this food offering.
> How much more these bhikṣus!
> I must provide for three months.
> Feeding five hundred people
> Will greatly diminish my household.'
> I wished to make the bhikṣus
> Die by some stratagem:
> 'If they die,
> They will not cost me anything.'
> Having conceived this evil thought,
> I mixed horse dung into the food
> And used it to feed them,
> Thinking: 'Kill them without any trouble.'
> Having eaten this food,
> They fell gravely ill,
> Their intestines and stomachs ravaged,
> Their five organs fatally injured.
> The practitioners who delighted in the Dharma and had attained the Way
> Thereupon died.
> The gods and spirits
> Together raised their voices, saying:
> 'This elder is greatly wicked!
> He has harmed and killed practitioners,
> Venerable pratyekabuddhas,
> Cool and without outflows.'
> When I heard what was said,
> I reflected in bitter anguish and sorrow:
> 'My sins are immeasurable;
> I have killed good practitioners.'
> My kinfolk, hearing this,
> Were all filled with grief and worry.
> They assembled all the practitioners
> And before them repented and confessed.
> Taking refuge in the practitioners,
> Having repented and confessed,
> They invited the five hundred practitioners
> And made offerings of food to them.
> Again repenting and confessing,
> Taking refuge in the practitioners,
> When the offering of food was complete,
> I made this vow in my heart:
> 'May I be united
> With such honored ones,
> And as they have attained liberation,
> May my mind be freed likewise.
> Wherever I am born,
> Let me not be in poverty.
> Let me never give rise
> To thoughts of greed, jealousy, and ill will.'
> Having killed the pratyekabuddhas,
> Having committed this grave offense,
> After my life ended,
> I fell into the Avīci hell.
> Suffering pain beyond all thousands,
> Anguish beyond description.
> Returning to a human body,
> My life was short and passed swiftly.
> Wherever I gained wealth and power,
> Honored and served by many,
> My intestines and stomachs would always rot,
> And then I would die.
> I abandoned household life
> And as a śramaṇa desired nothing.
> Diligently practicing the Buddha's teaching,
> I severed all desire.
> Were I now to relinquish this body,
> Approaching parinirvāṇa,
> My intestines, stomach, and five organs
> Would each burst and rot.
> The evil I committed,
> Harming a bhikṣu with an evil mind:
> The remaining karmic consequences of those offenses
> Will at last come to an end.
> The evil my body committed
> And the good I also practiced:
> I receive the fruits of both.
> Good and evil alike bear their consequences."

> Born in Śrāvastī,
> Gavāṃpati of great supernatural powers,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 20: Saṃkicca Kāśyapa

> "There were some bhikṣu monks
> Throughout seven full years.
> At that time, grain and rice were expensive,
> And famine caused great fear.
> I was allotted one person to support,
> A wonderful practitioner of Magadha,
> A venerable pratyekabuddha,
> Cool and without outflows.
> At that time I arose
> An evil intention in my mind:
> 'What shall I use
> To feed this bhikṣu?'
> I then set aside the food,
> Letting it breed worms and turn foul.
> I went off to inspect my workers
> And only afterward made the offering.
> Because of this sin,
> After death I fell into hell,
> The Saṃghāta, burned and roasted,
> Suffering beyond description.
> Emerging from hell,
> Wherever I was born,
> By many stratagems
> I sought food but could not obtain it.
> This is my final life;
> I have returned to the human world.
> Meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The unsurpassed guide.
> Through faith I left home.
> Destroying and exhausting all outflows,
> I have attained non-attachment,
> Cool and quenched, in nirvāṇa.
> O honored ones, I now possess
> Supernatural powers and am always free,
> Yet when I seek food by various means,
> I cannot obtain it.
> I travel far, avoiding the main roads,
> Wearied beyond description,
> And only then do I obtain by fortune
> Food and provisions."

> The Venerable Saṃkicca Kāśyapa, great and penetrating,
> Renowned for his deeds,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 21: Cūḍapanthaka

> "In a former life long ago,
> I was once a swineherd.
> By the side of a river,
> I bound the mouths of the pigs.
> Trying to cross at midstream,
> I alone managed to reach the other side,
> But the pigs could not breathe
> And all drowned midstream.
> At that time my livelihood was ruined,
> And I had nothing to depend on.
> A sage then came to that place,
> With compassion from the crown of his head.
> He exhorted and taught me,
> Shaving off my hair,
> Explaining and instructing in the good discipline,
> The practice of signless samādhi.
> After that life ended,
> I was reborn in heaven.
> When my heavenly lifespan was exhausted,
> I again became a practitioner.
> Meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> I renounced the household as a contemplative.
> But wherever I was, my mind was clouded;
> I would learn a sūtra and immediately forget it.
> I recited a single verse,
> And it took me three months to memorize it.
> Studying and chanting four lines,
> I severed all craving and desire."

> At that time the World-Honored One asked him,
> And Cūḍapanthaka spoke
> Of his past deeds, good and evil,
> At the pool of Anavatapta.

### Chapter 22: Sarvadatta

> "After Kāśyapa Buddha's parinirvāṇa,
> I was one of his later disciples,
> Broadly learned, knowing the three ages.
> But I was always secretive and grudging with the sūtra-dharma.
> I would not teach the bhikṣus,
> Nor would I share with others,
> Lest others who begged for my knowledge
> Might become my equals.
> If a bhikṣu came
> To my dwelling to ask about matters,
> I would deceive and mislead him,
> And he would leave with resentment and rancor unresolved.
> The practitioners left in anger,
> Cursing and reviling:
> 'Why are you so jealous that you will not teach the Dharma?
> O honored one, is it for your benefit?'
> When my life was nearing its end,
> My heart was filled with self-reproach:
> 'I have never taught the Dharma;
> This is a great unwholesomeness.'
> Knowing my life was ending,
> With only seven days remaining,
> I gathered the assembly of monks
> And immediately taught the Dharma.
> Day and night I expounded the essentials,
> Eradicating greed and jealousy.
> Before I had finished teaching,
> I died there.
> As I had analyzed and taught it,
> Those who heard it found it supremely wonderful.
> They received the teaching, contemplated its meaning,
> And in turn exhorted and taught one another.
> The Dharma I taught was but little,
> And the gathering lasted but seven days.
> Through this I was reborn in heaven,
> With heavenly music to delight me.
> When my heavenly lifespan ended,
> I returned to the human world.
> In Kapilavastu,
> I was born into the Śākya royal family.
> Handsome, revered by all who saw me,
> Loved and delighted in by all,
> With great wealth and limitless treasure,
> I gave universally, liberating without limit.
> Seeing the sons of noble families
> All coming to renounce the household,
> I aspired to be a contemplative,
> Abandoning household, desire, and wealth.
> The World-Honored One, without equal,
> In compassion and pity for me,
> Again and again urged and encouraged me,
> Exhorting and guiding me to leave home.
> I then respectfully followed the Buddha,
> The unsurpassed joyful teaching.
> O honored ones, I myself
> Practiced giving for seven years.
> Having given in this way
> Throughout those seven years,
> I then became a contemplative,
> Receiving the supreme wisdom-teaching.
> Seven years is a long time,
> And human life is very short.
> If one gives today,
> Who can guarantee their life tomorrow?
> Because of this past experience,
> I immediately became a contemplative.
> O honored ones, within seven days
> Of leaving home and shaving my head,
> Through faith becoming a śramaṇa,
> Practicing the Buddha's Dharma in this body:
> For twenty-five years
> My mind was settled like water.
> Then, on this wretched and evil path,
> Thoughts of household life arose.
> I followed the practice of diminishing deeds
> And did not savor the sweet dew.
> Feeling great shame,
> I sought the limitless benefit.
> To disgrace my kinfolk
> Would only bring universal enmity.
> To do such a thing would be wrong,
> And there should be no resentment.
> Having gone forth and committed to contemplative stillness,
> How could I return to thoughts of the household?
> Thoughts of family, lineage, and desire,
> And craving for wealth and profit:
> I must sever these attachments;
> I shall never abandon the precepts.
> Better to let my body perish,
> My life despised:
> I shall take up the great sword.
> What use is this life?
> I grasped the sharp sword
> And cut away the causal conditions,
> And having severed the impurities,
> My mind was then liberated.
> Single-minded, I attained liberation,
> Gradually bringing about quiescence.
> I reached the fruit of loving-kindness
> And swiftly encountered the Dharma's radiance.
> As my life nears its end,
> I teach the honored and wonderful Dharma.
> Through these past deeds,
> With settled mind I cross beyond, immeasurably."

> The Śākya son of great supernatural powers,
> Of weak faculties, Sarvadatta,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 23: Anuruddha

> "Once long ago, when I was without food,
> I gave at that time.
> I encountered a śramaṇa,
> The great and penetrating Vāsiṣṭha.
> Because of this, I was born into the Śākya clan,
> Named Anuruddha.
> Delighting in meritorious pleasures,
> Entertained by performers and music.
> When I saw the Perfectly Awakened One,
> I rejoiced and admired the World-Honored One.
> Seeing him, my heart leaped with joy.
> I renounced the household as a contemplative.
> Through past-life practice of diligent effort,
> With skillful means, always firm and strong,
> I attained the three kinds of penetrating wisdom,
> Fully, as the Buddha taught.
> I recall my past lives,
> The deeds I performed and experienced.
> In the Trāyastriṃśa heaven,
> I dwelt for seven lifetimes.
> Seven times I returned to the human world,
> In the human realm, ever more powerful and honored.
> Born to wealthy and noble families,
> Gold, pearls, and treasures arising naturally.
> Seven times there, seven times here,
> In birth-and-death, fourteen in all:
> I recall and know all of them,
> The deeds of past lives.
> Such are the fruits of giving.
> I never had a miserly or jealous thought.
> Wherever I was born,
> I always sought freedom from birth-and-death."

> At that time the Venerable Anuruddha,
> Among the assembly of monks,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 24: Mṛgāra's Son

> "Once I was walking my brave dog
> And went to a medicine shop.
> A venerable pratyekabuddha
> Had fallen ill.
> I provided him with medicine
> And cared for him for seven days.
> After seven days, the honored one
> Flew up into the sky.
> When I saw this, I told
> My household servants:
> 'The World-Honored One has arrived.
> Thus should one leave home and study.'
> When I heard what my servant said
> About the pratyekabuddha flying,
> My spirit leaped with joy,
> And single-mindedly with palms joined, I bowed toward him.
> Because of this joyful mind
> And the giving of medicine,
> In the heavens and among humans,
> My merit was naturally manifest.
> In this present final life,
> I again obtained a human body
> And met the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The guide without superior.
> Under the Lion of the Śākyas,
> I left home as a contemplative.
> Having attained non-attachment,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> In the past, through this,
> I received very abundant offerings:
> Robes, food and drink,
> Bedding and all comforts.
> Garments sewn for me,
> Because I gave medicine:
> Medicine comes to me from the four directions,
> And I lack for nothing.
> A heavenly being went and told
> King Bimbisāra:
> 'You should give medicine
> To Mṛgāra's Son.
> Your kingdom will then prosper greatly,
> And medicines will flourish abundantly.'
> He sent the royal physician Jīvaka
> Bearing medicine for the Deer's Son.
> Medicine came from all four directions,
> All converging upon me.
> At that time, King Bimbisāra
> Sent with great supernatural powers.
> Coming to bestow upon me
> A fully appointed, comfortable hall,
> Sufficient for the entire bhikṣu-saṃgha
> Of one thousand two hundred and fifty."

> That bhikṣu, Mṛgāra's Son,
> Of the six supernatural powers and great supernatural abilities,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 25: Rāhula

> "Once long ago I was a king,
> Ruling the kingdom of Magadha.
> The people were very numerous;
> I decided affairs with justice and reason.
> At that time there was a sage
> Who drank water from someone's ditch.
> He then came to my presence
> And addressed me thus:
> 'Great King, I am a thief.
> Lacking drink, I took water without leave.
> You should punish me
> As you would one who steals.'
> I then replied: 'Sage, you hold the Dharma as medicine.
> I freely grant you permission.
> Go now, and follow your wish.'
> 'Great King, I am plagued by doubt.
> The fault and bond cannot be removed.
> You should punish me,
> So that my karmic offense may be dissolved.'
> I then ordered him placed in the garden
> But forgot about him for six days.
> After six days had passed,
> He had received neither food nor drink.
> Because of this karmic connection,
> Though I never had an evil intention,
> I fell into the Burning and Black Rope hells,
> And went through sixty thousand years.
> When that was finished, with remaining consequences,
> In this present final life,
> While in my mother's womb,
> Six years passed before I was born.
> I never gave rise to a confused thought;
> My body and mouth committed no offense.
> Yet I reaped these fruits:
> Merit and offenses cannot be separated."

> Thus the Venerable Rāhula,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 26: Nandika

> "In the time of Vipaśyin Buddha,
> I donated a warm bathing-room.
> After one bathing of the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> I made this vow:
> 'May I be united with such
> An honored assembly.
> Life after life, may I attain coolness,
> Free from desire, without dust or defilement,
> Handsome, always gentle and fine,
> Pure as a wondrous flower.'
> After that life ended,
> I was reborn in heaven.
> In the heavens and among humans,
> My appearance was fine and handsome.
> Wherever I was born,
> I dwelt in power and honor.
> After that life ended,
> I returned to the human world.
> Gods and humans alike,
> Seeing me, were never satiated.
> Seeing a pratyekabuddha's stūpa,
> I repaired it, plastering and setting it right,
> Adorning it with fresh whitewash,
> And above it hung banners and canopies.
> I then made this vow,
> Wishing to attain the marks of excellence:
> A body of refined purple-gold,
> Magnificent and without compare.
> Through the merit of these deeds,
> I was born in Vārāṇasī,
> In the Kṛtavanī clan,
> Born as a son without anger or harm.
> Seeing the stūpa of Kāśyapa Buddha,
> My heart was filled with joy.
> I went to that temple
> And erected a dew-catching basin.
> Through this offering to the stūpa,
> And repairing and adorning the stūpa,
> And erecting the pillar and basin,
> The merit received was immeasurable.
> With the remaining blessings,
> In this present final life,
> I was born into the Śākya royal family
> And became the Buddha's younger brother.
> My body naturally possesses
> The marks of a great person,
> Majestically adorned, thirty
> Evenly distributed marks of excellence.
> The Buddha, the Universal Seer, declared me
> Foremost in fine appearance.
> Having exhausted all outflows,
> I have attained the sweet-dew phrase."

> Nandika, his parents' son,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 27: Bhadrika

> "Once, when grain and rice were expensive,
> And famine caused great fear,
> There were five hundred bhikṣus
> Seeking food; I gave to them.
> All the elders
> Gave generously to the practitioners.
> On alms rounds they obtained food
> And brought it to give to me.
> Whether coarse or fine, whatever food they received,
> They always shared a portion with me,
> Without being able to know me,
> Yet always following my words.
> When people came
> Seeking food and provisions,
> I at that time made my own effort
> And departed from that place.
> At that time, each ran off.
> From afar they searched,
> Chasing me with all their might
> But unable to catch me.
> I crossed a flowing river
> And then sat to one side,
> Looking all around in the four directions:
> I was alone, in stillness, with no one else.
> 'Today I shall eat alone,
> Soft and fine, fragrant and delicious.
> Filled and satisfied,
> At last I shall find peace.'
> There was then a bhikṣu,
> A world-honored pratyekabuddha,
> Of towering majestic power,
> Who had eliminated birth-and-death without remainder.
> My thoughts were always preoccupied:
> 'Poverty is bitter and extremely hard.
> In past lives I did not cultivate merit;
> That is why I am now poor.'
> I then arose a pure heart,
> And with leaping joy I thought:
> 'I should give to this bhikṣu;
> He is an original blessed one.'
> At that time the World-Honored One accepted
> That food.
> Out of compassion and pity for me,
> He flew into the sky.
> I then made this vow: 'May I never again be poor.
> In future lives, may I be born to a powerful and wealthy family,
> Handsome as a wondrous flower.
> United with such honored ones,
> Life after life may I always meet them.
> May I inherit this Dharma
> As the honored one has attained.'
> Through the merit of these deeds,
> I received peace for a long, long time.
> In the heavens and among humans,
> The merit I created was self-evident.
> I was even a king,
> Among gods and humans, innumerable times.
> I never fell into evil destinies,
> Nor was there any karmic offense.
> With the remaining blessings,
> In this present final life,
> I was born to a powerful and wealthy family,
> Of the great Śākya lineage.
> At that time, the World-Honored Buddha
> Came to my birthplace.
> I then became a contemplative,
> Together with my kinfolk.
> The wish I originally made
> Was always fulfilled as desired.
> Having attained non-attachment,
> Cool and quenched, in nirvāṇa."

> Renouncing power to become a śramaṇa,
> Bhadrika received the Buddha's teaching.
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> He spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 28: Revata Khadiravaniya

> "In the time of Krakucchanda Buddha,
> Once, someone was building a stūpa.
> I was present at that place;
> The temple was exceedingly high and great.
> During the construction of this stūpa,
> I criticized it with my mouth:
> 'This stūpa is far too large!
> When will it ever be finished?
> One should do merit gradually,
> Building it up in this way.
> It would not require so much labor,
> And the stūpa would be completed quickly.'
> Because of speaking those stingy words,
> Having committed this verbal offense,
> When my life ended,
> I fell into hell.
> Emerging from hell,
> Short, small, dark, and ugly,
> Wherever I was born,
> I was slighted by all.
> In the time of Kāśyapa Buddha,
> I was a small red bird
> In the midst of Vārāṇasī,
> Flying among the thickets of trees.
> I beheld the World's Radiance,
> Surrounded by bhikṣus.
> I then followed the Buddha in reverence,
> And from my mouth came a mournful sound.
> When the World-Honored Buddha traveled
> In the land of Vārāṇasī,
> I always followed him in and out,
> Constantly circling and calling mournfully.
> Through the merit of these deeds,
> I returned and obtained a human body.
> Meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The unsurpassed guide.
> I went forth as a contemplative
> Under the Lion of the Śākyas.
> Having attained non-attachment,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> An arhat, free and at ease,
> With the six supernatural powers and great abilities,
> I am named 'Dharma-holder,'
> True and endowed with eloquence.
> When any assembly gathers
> And hears my voice,
> Gods and humans alike,
> All are delighted.
> The sin I committed was but small,
> And the merit I created was not great.
> Yet I reaped the fruits of both:
> The two, offense and merit."

> The Venerable Revata Khadiravaniya,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 29: Madhuvāsiṣṭha

> "Once in Vaiśālī,
> I was a great monkey.
> I went and took the Buddha's bowl.
> The bhikṣus, seeing this, scolded me:
> 'You might break the Buddha's bowl!'
> The World-Honored One told the bhikṣus:
> 'Bhikṣus, do not scold him.
> He will never break the bowl.'
> I then took the Buddha's bowl
> And gently carried it up a tree,
> Filled it with honey,
> And then came down from the tree.
> Holding the bowl brimming with honey,
> I offered it to the World-Honored One.
> But there were insects and impurities in the honey;
> The Perfectly Awakened One would not accept it.
> The Buddha, seeing in the bowl
> Dead bees mixed with the honey,
> I then carefully picked them out
> And again offered the bowl to the Buddha.
> At that time, the World's Radiance
> Still would not accept it.
> I then washed it clean with water
> And once more bowed and offered it.
> I poured water over it
> And filled a different bowl,
> Having made the offering to the honored Buddha,
> My heart leaped with joy.
> The World-Honored One, without equal,
> At that time delivered the dead bees,
> Accepted the bowl of honey,
> And consumed it with his disciples.
> I was then greatly overjoyed,
> With palms joined, facing the Buddha,
> Focused before the Dharma King,
> My heart always diligent.
> There I made this vow: 'May I obtain a human body.
> May I be born in the time of a world-honored one
> And attain the supreme meaning.'
> Through the merit of these deeds,
> I thereby obtained a human body.
> Meeting the Perfectly Awakened One,
> The unsurpassed guide.
> I went forth as a śramaṇa,
> Attending the Lion of the Śākyas.
> Having attained non-attachment,
> Cool and quenched, I entered nirvāṇa.
> A self-mastered arhat,
> With the six supernatural powers and great abilities,
> I am named 'He Who Produced Honey,'
> As the bhikṣus also know.
> Knowing the merit I created before,
> I now receive reverence.
> Together with several hundred bhikṣus,
> I travel and wander about.
> When on a destitute road,
> The bhikṣu-saṃgha is hungry and thirsty,
> My heart simply makes a wish:
> 'I wish to obtain honey-drink.'
> Knowing my heart's thought,
> People immediately come from afar,
> Bringing honey and fine food
> To offer to me.
> I then receive it;
> It naturally comes in great abundance and excellence.
> I give it to the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> And delightfully, all are satisfied.
> From the time I was born,
> Through the monkey's deed,
> Though the path to liberation had no clear route,
> I attained the sweet-dew phrase.
> As I originally vowed,
> All was fulfilled as desired.
> Making offerings to the World-Honored Buddha,
> Whatever I sought was obtained in full.
> O honored ones, I always recall
> The meritorious deeds I performed.
> I have reaped all their fruits,
> With delight, peace, and auspicious bliss."

> Thus the Venerable Madhuvāsiṣṭha,
> Among the bhikṣu-saṃgha,
> At the pool of Anavatapta,
> Spoke of his past deeds.

### Chapter 30: The World-Honored One

> Victorious over all, universally radiant,
> Supreme in all the world,
> Having removed and exhausted all defilements,
> Subduing all assemblies.
> With all-penetrating wisdom and universal sight,
> The great person pervades all things,
> Having crossed beyond all enmity and fear.
> The Dharma-vessel ferries to the other shore.
> Understanding all who are to be transformed,
> Joyfully having compassion for the world,
> Pitying and liberating sentient beings,
> Saving all through righteousness.
> Removing all people's bonds,
> Completely untying all fetters:
> Supreme among all people,
> He teaches the Dharma as the eye of the world.
> The great person of limitless wisdom,
> The great hero of utmost renown,
> The great light of limitless Dharma,
> Who has crossed to the ultimate Dharma.
> With great power he transforms the unwise,
> Opening and teaching with great luminous wisdom,
> Joyfully encouraging the multitudes,
> The great physician who encompasses all.
> The World-Honored One who destroys all fear,
> The unsurpassed one who removes all sorrow,
> The Buddha, the benevolent one who liberates
> From the great prison's chains.
> The great dragon, the great lion,
> The unattached, the great bhikṣu,
> The World-Honored One of great wisdom,
> Who saves from all worldly toil.
> Diligent, with great power,
> Skillful, greatly firm and strong,
> He subdues all gods and people.
> The great Way: quiescent, peaceful, secure.
> The Buddha, great God among Gods:
> All ghosts and spirits
> Bow to his feet of wisdom.
> The Buddha, appearing out of compassion for the world,
> Always amid the great round of birth-and-death,
> Destroys and breaks the net of snares.
> With supernatural powers and limitless compassion,
> He liberates from the great prison.
> The great dragon, the great god among humans,
> First among all assemblies,
> With vast and limitless giving,
> He has attained the great peaceful path.
> The elder, the sage among sages,
> Who has crossed beyond all honored dharmas,
> Who accomplishes great disciples:
> The guide, whose virtue is supreme.
> The World-Honored One, highest of all,
> The unsurpassed one who removes sorrow.
> Of all he has liberated, the greatest:
> The honored one of all excellent marks.
> He has severed all attachment to form and desire,
> Uprooting all bonds of affection.
> At that time, sojourning at the Nāga King's
> Great pool of Anavatapta,
> All his tasks accomplished,
> He rose into the sky.
> Surrounded by his assembly of disciples,
> In serene silence there were five hundred.
> With compassion and utmost pity,
> Tenderly protecting all people,
> He observed the assembly of bhikṣus
> And himself spoke these words:
> "Listen clearly to what I say
> Of what was done in past lives,
> The deeds my body once committed,
> And the remaining consequences I now receive.
> In a past life of mine,
> I was a person named Vānara.
> I slandered a spotless and pure
> Good and wonderful pratyekabuddha.
> The people gathered in a great crowd
> And bound the good and wonderful sage,
> Placed him in shackles and chains,
> And brought him out like a condemned prisoner.
> When I saw the śramaṇa
> Bound and suffering,
> My heart was moved with compassion,
> And I went and freed him.
> Because of this sin,
> I fell into hell for a very long time.
> Later, born in the human world,
> I was always slandered by others.
> Because of the remaining consequences of this,
> In this present final life,
> The woman Sundarī and the heterodox practitioners
> Together plotted and slandered me.
> I was once a brāhmaṇa,
> Broadly learned and versed in the arts.
> I had five hundred students
> And taught among the thickets of trees.
> At that time a sage of great supernatural powers,
> A bhikṣu with the five supernatural powers, came.
> When I saw the practitioner arrive,
> I slandered and proclaimed his faults:
> 'This sage is deeply lustful,'
> I proclaimed from high in the trees.
> When my students heard this,
> They imitated and spread my words.
> At that time, all the students,
> Going from house to house begging,
> Slandered the sage before the multitude,
> Saying he was defiled with desire.
> Because of this offense,
> The woman Sundarī,
> The Buddha, and the five hundred disciples
> Were all together slandered.
> Though the Buddha is the all-knowing one,
> There are false and groundless slanders.
> Those who know this, revile the disciples:
> 'Are these truly śramaṇas?'
> Having committed this offense,
> I fell into evil destinies.
> Born in the Mountain Hell,
> The suffering was extremely fierce.
> Because of the remaining consequences,
> The woman Ciñcamāṇavikā,
> Before a great assembly,
> Falsely accused the Buddha.
> Once I was one of three brothers.
> We quarreled over money and property.
> I pushed and threw one into a deep ravine,
> And crushed him with a stone, killing him.
> Because of this offense,
> I fell into the Mountain Hell,
> Burned and roasted in the Black Ropes,
> The bitter pain extremely fierce.
> Because of the remaining consequences,
> Devadatta hurled a stone at me.
> That stone fell
> And injured the Buddha's toe.
> Boarding a ship to cross the ocean,
> Together we wished to cross the deep water.
> At that time, aboard the ship,
> I drew a sword and killed a merchant.
> Because of committing this offense,
> I fell into hell.
> Because of the remaining consequences,
> An iron thorn appeared before the Buddha.
> Once I lived among the fish-sellers,
> Born as a fisherman's son.
> When there were those who caught and killed fish,
> I gave rise to a thought of approval.
> Because of this offense,
> I fell into the Mountain Hell,
> Burned and roasted in the Black Ropes,
> The suffering extremely fierce.
> When King Virūḍhaka
> Slaughtered the Śākya clan,
> Because of the remaining consequences of this,
> I now have headaches.
> In the time of the World-Honored Vipaśyin,
> I cursed and reviled his disciple,
> Saying he should not eat fine rice,
> And should be made to eat raw barley.
> Because of this offense,
> Having uttered evil words with my mouth,
> I fell into the Black Rope Hell,
> Suffering beyond all reckoning.
> Because of the remaining consequences,
> A resentful brāhmaṇa
> Once invited me,
> And for three months I ate barley.
> Once I was a physician.
> Treating a distinguished man's son,
> I compounded the medicine incorrectly,
> Causing the illness to worsen.
> Because of committing this offense,
> I fell into hell and suffered greatly.
> Because of the remaining consequences,
> I now have dysentery.
> Once in a past life,
> I was a wrestler.
> In combat with a strong man,
> I injured and killed one who had a buddha-son.
> Because of committing this offense,
> I suffered beyond all measure.
> Because of the remaining consequences,
> My ribs and sides now ache.
> Regarding Nandīpāla,
> Who disparaged Kāśyapa Buddha,
> Because of seeing that śramaṇa,
> He said: 'He will not attain the Buddha-path.'"
