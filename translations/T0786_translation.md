# 佛說木槵子經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on Wooden Soap-berry Beads

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 786

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was sojourning on Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain in Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of 1,250 bhikṣus and innumerable bodhisattvas of far-reaching renown, revered by gods and humans alike. At that time, the king of the Nanda kingdom, named Virūḍhaka, sent an envoy to where the Buddha was. The envoy bowed his head to the feet of the Buddha and said: "World-Honored One! My kingdom is small and peripheral. Year after year there have been raids and marauding bandits; the prices of the five grains have surged; disease is rampant; the people suffer hardship; and I am never able to sleep in peace. The Dharma treasury of the Tathāgata is vast and profound in all its dimensions, but I am burdened with worries and affairs of state and cannot devote myself to practice. I humbly beseech the World-Honored One, in his special compassionate mercy, to grant me an essential teaching, so that I may easily practice day and night, and in future lives be far removed from all suffering."

The Buddha said to the king: "If one wishes to extinguish the obstruction of afflictions and the obstruction of karmic retribution, one should string together 108 soap-berry seeds and always keep them with oneself. Whether walking, sitting, or lying down, one should constantly maintain a sincere and undistracted mind, reciting the names of the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha, and with each recitation passing one soap-berry bead. In this way, gradually counting through the soap-berry beads—ten, twenty, a hundred, a thousand, up to millions—if one is able to complete 200,000 recitations with body and mind undistracted and free from all flattery and crookedness, at death one will be reborn in the Third Yāma Heaven, where food and clothing arise spontaneously and one always abides in happiness and ease. If moreover one is able to complete 1,000,000 recitations, one will sever and eliminate the 108 karmic bonds, and will for the first time be said to have turned one's back on the stream of birth and death and to be heading toward nirvāṇa, having permanently severed the roots of affliction and attained the unsurpassed fruit."

The envoy returned and reported these words to the king. The king was greatly delighted and, bowing from afar in the direction of the World-Honored One with his head prostrated to the ground, said: "Excellent! I shall practice accordingly." He then ordered his officials and people to prepare soap-berry rosaries, making one thousand sets, and distributed one set each to all his relatives and members of the royal family. The king constantly recited and contemplated, and even when personally engaged in military campaigns did not abandon this practice. He also thought to himself: "The World-Honored One's great compassion responds universally to all. If through this virtue I am able to escape long drowning in the sea of suffering, may the Tathāgata appear before me in person and teach the Dharma to me." This wish and aspiration pressed upon his mind, and for three days he did not eat.

The Buddha then manifested his form and, together with his retinue, came to the king's palace, and said to the king: "The bhikṣu Śāṭu recited the names of the Three Jewels for ten years and attained the fruit of a once-returner (sakṛdāgāmin). Gradually continuing the practice, he is now in the Universally Fragrant World, where he has become a pratyekabuddha." Upon hearing this, the king redoubled his practice.

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "How much more so for one who is able to recite the names of the Three Jewels tens of thousands of times! Even one who merely hears the name of such a person and gives rise to a single thought of sympathetic joy will, in all future rebirths, constantly hear of the ten wholesome deeds."

When this teaching was spoken, the great assembly rejoiced, and all vowed to practice accordingly.
