# 聖無動尊一字出生八大童子祕要法品
## Secret Essential Dharma Chapter on the Eight Great Youths Born from the One Syllable of the Holy Acalanātha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1204

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

Vajrapāṇi said: "The thoughts of all sentient beings differ; some are compliant and some are defiant. Therefore the Tathāgata manifests both compassionate and wrathful forms, benefiting beings according to their dispositions." Explanation: Because all buddhas, with great compassion, pity sentient beings, they use gentleness to guide the compliant, and severity to restrain the defiant. When the Buddha abides in the wrathful samādhi, all buddhas of the ten directions jointly enter the wrathful samādhi. How could it be that when Vairocana manifests a vidyārāja from within signlessness, the other buddhas and bodhisattvas would not also manifest wrathful forms? Therefore the tathāgatas of the four directions manifest their teaching-command bodies to subdue all māras. Yet they do not personally follow the practitioner constantly; this is because their original vows differ. Trailokyavijaya Bodhisattva subdues the heavenly māras and the three poisons of greed, anger, and delusion across the three times. Kuṇḍalī Bodhisattva tames the constant-companion māra called Vināyaka, and human māras. Yamāntaka subdues dragon māras and various enemies. Vajrayakṣa tames ghost māras and the ignorant. Such various functions cannot be fully described.

Acala Vidyārāja constantly follows the practitioner. Whatever obstacles are created by devas, Vināyakas, dragons, or ghosts are all annihilated at once. Since all buddhas and bodhisattvas who abide in the same samādhi can serve as messengers in accordance with Acala's great vow, there was the Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra, who was able to penetrate the way of the vidyās. He examined the essential texts of the secret treasury and found that Acala Vidyārāja possesses a one-syllable heart secret mantra, and eight syllables adorning that one syllable. The eight syllables are the homage formula, and the syllable *Haṃ* is the honored deity.

If that is so, how could the eight syllables be without form? It is because the eight syllables of "Namaḥ" and the rest each individually pay homage to the single syllable *Haṃ*. Therefore from those eight syllables arise eight attendant messengers who surround the honored deity *Haṃ*. These are the Eight Great Youths:

1. Prajñāprabhā Bodhisattva (Wisdom-Light)
2. Prajñāmuditā Bodhisattva (Wisdom-Joy)
3. Anavatapta Bodhisattva (Without Heat)
4. Guṇanidarśa Bodhisattva (Pointing-to-Virtue)
5. Ukubhāga (Transcending Worldly Abode)
6. Viśuddha-bhikṣu (Pure Bhikṣu)
7. Kiṃkara (Obedient Servant)
8. Ceṭaka (Pacifier of Disasters)

These attendants correspond to the Four Wisdoms and Four Pāramitās. Because they intimately follow and obey the teaching-command of Mahāvairocana, they manifest these forms surrounding the Holy Acalanātha. Their significance is revealed in each of their individual mantras.

Explanation: The eastern Vajra Family is the gate of bodhicitta; its light is likened to sunlight. Therefore the attendant born from it is named Wisdom-Light. The southern Jewel Family is the gate of merit. Therefore the attendant born from it is named Wisdom-Joy, because the twofold adornment of merit and wisdom brings joy. The western Lotus Family is the gate of wisdom. Therefore the attendant who appears from it is named Anavatapta. This means "without heat," representing the lotus born from pool-water, pure and undefiled. The northern Karma Family is the gate of vigor. Therefore the attendant born from it is named Pointing-to-Virtue. This family points to the virtue of the preceding three families; through vigor, one attains the fruit, hence the name.

The Vajra Pāramitā is the practice of bodhicitta. Therefore the attendant who appears from it is named Ukubhāga, meaning "transcending worldly abode." The Jewel Pāramitā is the practice of the merit-mind. Therefore the attendant born from it is named Pure Bhikṣu, representing the bhikṣu's ability to guard the dharma-treasure. The Dharma Pāramitā is the practice of the compassion-mind. Therefore the attendant who appears from it is named Kiṃkara, meaning "obedient." The Karma Pāramitā is the practice of the skillful-means-mind. Therefore the attendant born from it is named Ceṭaka, meaning "pacifier of disasters." Because the bodhisattva manifests a wrathful form through skillful means. The basis of the Eight Youths has now been fully explained.

If practitioners wish to attain both present and future siddhi, they should, in the course of their practice, call upon each youth by name, request rescue and protection, and form the mudrā while reciting the mantra. This will accomplish all supreme worldly siddhi.

The mantra of the Youth Prajñāprabhā Bodhisattva:

*Oṃ vajra-mati jvāla bodhi-citta na*

The mantra of the Bodhisattva Prajñāmuditā:

*Oṃ ratna-mati sādhu mahā-maṇi mo*

The mantra of the Bodhisattva Anavatapta:

*Oṃ padma-suvadāha praśamana mahā-dharma saṃ*

The mantra of the Youth Guṇanidarśa Bodhisattva:

*Oṃ karma mahā-vīrya paripūraka maṃ*

The mantra of the Youth Ukubhāga:

*Oṃ vajra-sattva-bha ukubhāga mahā-saukhyatā*

The mantra of the attendant Pure Bhikṣu:

*Oṃ maṇi-viśuddha dharma-kuru rakṣa dharma-va*

The mantra of the Youth Kiṃkara:

*Oṃ dharma kiṃkara tiṣṭha vajra*

The mantra of the Youth Ceṭaka:

*Oṃ karma ceṭaka hūṃ hūṃ phaṭ naṃ*

Next, the mantra of the principal deity:

*Oṃ acala-caṇḍa haṃ*

Next, the method for painting the image.

The practitioner should select a clean and quiet place where neither humans nor non-humans may observe. The practitioner and the painter should both be pure, wearing fresh clean garments. On white cotton cloth, a clean board, or clean cloth, have the principal deity and the Eight Great Youths painted.

The form of Wisdom-Light: slightly wrathful, wearing a celestial crown, body of white-yellow color. The right hand holds a five-pronged vajra; the left hand holds a lotus upon which rests a moon-disc. He wears a kāṣāya and is adorned with necklaces and various ornaments.

Next, the Bodhisattva Wisdom-Joy: compassionate face, displaying a gentle smile, color like a red lotus. The left hand holds a maṇi; the right hand holds a three-pronged hook.

The Bodhisattva Anavatapta has the form of Brahmā, color like true gold, wearing a garuḍa upon the crown. The left hand holds a lotus; the right hand holds a single-pronged vajra. He rides upon a nāga king.

The Bodhisattva Pointing-to-Virtue has the form of a yakṣa, color like space, with three eyes, wearing armor and a helmet. The left hand holds a wheel; the right hand holds a trident.

Ukubhāga wears a five-pronged crown, displaying a violently wrathful aspect, body the color of gold. The right hand holds a vajra; the left hand forms a fist mudrā.

Pure Bhikṣu has a shaved head and wears a dharma-kāṣāya draped and knotted over the left shoulder. The left hand holds a sūtra text; the right hand holds a five-pronged vajra at the heart. The right shoulder is bare. Around the waist is wound a red cloth. His face is neither young nor old. His eyes are like blue lotuses. His upper teeth protrude over the lower lip.

Next, Kiṃkara has the form of a fifteen-year-old youth, wearing a lotus crown, body of white flesh-color. Both hands are joined in añjali, and between the two thumbs and index fingers is horizontally inserted a single-pronged vajra. He is beautifully adorned with heavenly garments and a kāṣāya.

Next, Ceṭaka also has the form of a youth, color like a red lotus, hair bound in five topknots. The left hand holds a vajra; the right hand holds a vajra-staff. Being wrathful and fierce by nature, he does not wear a kāṣāya; a heavenly garment is wrapped about his neck and shoulders.

The method for painting the image has now been fully explained.

Next, the method of offering.

When the practitioner wishes to present offerings, they must first generate the bodhicitta. What is bodhicitta? It is giving rise to the aspiration to save sentient beings, contemplating the suffering of sentient beings, and giving rise to the mind of rescue and deliverance. When one gives rise to this aspiration, Acala Vidyārāja and the Eight Great Youths, as unbidden teachers, emerge from one's own mind to protect and sustain the practitioner, and one should then make offerings.

Dwell in a mountain forest or secluded place, burn incense, scatter flowers, and uphold and recite the true words. When one practices thus, the attendants will appear in person and serve as one wishes.

One must first bow to the four directional tathāgatas and make great vows.

Bow to the east and make this aspiration: "Sentient beings are boundless; I vow to liberate them all." This is the vow of Great Mirror Wisdom, the gate of bodhicitta. Bodhicitta is the mind that liberates sentient beings. Therefore, by making this vow and bowing, until the attainment of buddhahood one will constantly receive the empowerment of Vajrasattva, bringing bodhicitta to perfect fulfillment.

Next, bow to the south and make this aspiration: "Merit and wisdom are boundless; I vow to gather them all." This is the cause of Equality Wisdom, the gate of merit and wisdom. Because these two provisions of merit and wisdom benefit sentient beings, by making this vow and bowing, until the attainment of buddhahood, at each stage one will constantly receive from Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva the bestowal of abhiṣeka, bringing merit and wisdom to perfect fulfillment.

Next, bow to the west and make this aspiration: "Dharma-gates are boundless; I vow to study them all." This is the vow that is the cause of Wondrous Observation Wisdom, the gate of wisdom. Because one thereby becomes a great dharma-king, by making this vow and bowing, until the attainment of buddhahood one will receive the empowerment of Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, bringing wisdom to perfect fulfillment.

Next, bow to the north and make this aspiration: "Tathāgatas are boundless; I vow to serve them all." This is the cause of Action-Accomplishing Wisdom, the gate of activity. Because one performs all tasks before all buddhas, by making this vow and bowing, until the attainment of buddhahood one will receive the empowerment of Vajrakarma Bodhisattva, accomplishing vast offering-activities in all buddha-worlds.

Next, bow to Vairocana Tathāgata and make this statement: "Unsurpassed bodhi I vow to realize." This is the cause of Dharmadhātu-Essence Wisdom, the realization of the position of Mahāvairocana-Samantabhadra, pervading the dharmadhātu and further benefiting sentient beings. Therefore this vow is made. At that time all buddhas of the ten directions simultaneously bear witness, praising: "Excellent!" Therefore all bodhisattvas and holy beings will jointly guard and protect such a one, who will before long attain the unsurpassed path.

The general vow having been explained, all other particular vows should be stated as one wishes, and the roots of merit thus generated should be dedicated to the equal benefit of self and others.

Furthermore, one should not eat impure foods. The mind should be free of worry and sorrow, and one should not give rise to the afflictions of greed, anger, and delusion. One should not delight in worldly pleasures but deeply cultivate meditation.

Such a practitioner is the world's great happiness, is a teacher of sentient beings, and with samādhi manifesting before them, they will swiftly realize unsurpassed samyak-saṃbodhi.
