# Sūtra on Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja's Teaching of the Dharma for King Udayana

## *Bīntóulú Tūluóshé Wèi Yōutuóyán Wáng Shuōfǎ Jīng* (賓頭盧突羅闍為優陀延王說法經)

Taishō 1690

Translated by Guṇabhadra (*Qiúnà Bátuóluó*, 求那跋陀羅) of the Liu Song dynasty.

*Note: This is a celebrated early text in which the arhat Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja (formerly the son of the prime minister) teaches King Udayana of Kauśāmbī through an extended series of vivid parables on the dangers of sensual pleasure and the impermanence of worldly glory. The text culminates in the famous parable of the man in the well, one of the most widely known Buddhist allegories: a man fleeing an elephant (impermanence) descends into a well (the body) by tree roots (lifespan) gnawed by two mice (day and night), with four snakes (the four elements), a dragon below (death), bees stinging (evil thoughts), fire burning the tree (old age), and three drops of honey falling into his mouth (the five desires). The sūtra is notable for its rhetorical power and its catalog of kings destroyed by desire.*

---

> The taste of sensual pleasure is exceedingly small;
> The grief and suffering it brings is exceedingly great.
> Therefore the wise should cultivate skillful means,
> Swiftly separating from all desires, diligently practicing the pure conduct.

I once heard: the prince of a thousand blessings, named Udayana (*Yōutuóyán*, 優陀延), succeeded to his father's throne and dwelt in the city of Kauśāmbī (*Jūshěmí*, 拘舍彌). That city was extraordinarily fine, broad, and magnificently pure, with dazzling palace-towers adorned in exquisite beauty, windows and lattices open and spacious, interlaced with pearl nets. Thousands upon thousands of towers and pavilions adorned the city. Streets and lanes were aligned, roads and paths orderly, marketplaces full and brimming, with an abundance of precious treasures.

Around the city were fine parks with verdant trees and luxuriant flowers and fruit, pure flowing springs producing various lotuses, blue, yellow, red, and white, their colors reflecting one another. Geese and mandarin ducks, peacocks and parrots, kalaviṅka birds and jīvañjīvaka birds sang in harmony like the sound of music. In its magnificence and glory, it was like the Kailāsa Mountain King with its towering cliffs adorning itself, and resembled the City of Joy where Indra dwells.

King Udayana was handsome in form with full marks of majesty, brilliant and wise, of matchless martial valor, and proficient in all skills without exception. He was skilled at enchanting elephants, making all mountain-elephants gather at his call, and could control them so that they all became tame. He was also skilled at playing the lute, harmonious and measured, with proper tones in accord, so that birds and beasts danced. He could compound fragrant pills to subdue enemies, so that all who smelled the fragrance came to submit. He was skilled at carving and painting, capturing likenesses perfectly, making portraits indistinguishable from the real form. He was fully accomplished in all sixty-two arts. In clothing and food he did not favor extravagance; he cherished the poor, respected the elderly, cared for the common people, governed the country by the correct dharma without tiring day or night, and followed the rites and laws entirely according to ancient standards, like the wise kings of old. The country was rich, the people prosperous, the treasuries overflowing. People of merit were born in his kingdom; receiving the king's moral influence, all cultivated goodness. They were broadly learned in the scriptures, clearly understood the treatises, and were practiced in all worldly classics. They were brave and mighty. All this was because the king, in previous lives, had planted roots of merit at the shrine of a pratyekabuddha and received such a reward. The king's awesome virtue was feared and respected by neighboring countries, and his transforming influence was heard throughout the world.

---

At that time, the prime minister's son, named Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja (*Bīntóulú Tūluóshé*, 賓���盧突羅闍), was of extraordinary beauty, rarely seen in the world, brilliant and wise, broadly learned and widely knowledgeable, benevolent and compassionate, his aspiration set on relieving suffering. He exhorted the people of the kingdom to cultivate the ten wholesome actions, believed in and delighted in the Three Treasures, left home to study the Way, and attained the complete fruit [of arhatship]. Wandering and teaching, he returned to Kauśāmbī, wishing to convert his relatives. He went about begging for food. When begging was done, beneath a tree in the grove he sat in full-lotus posture, contemplating in samādhi.

At that time, a man who recognized Piṇḍola came and reported to the king: "The former prime minister's son Piṇḍola is now nearby, beneath a tree in this grove." The king heard this with delight, his heart filled with reverence. He ordered his carriage prepared and, with his palace women, retinue, and attendants, proceeded to the venerable one's abode.

After exchanging greetings, [Piṇḍola] invited the king to sit. The king then thought: "Whatever doubts I have, I shall now ask him." He said: "Piṇḍola! You and I have known each other since childhood. Your ancestors served for generations as prime minister, brilliant and wise, always serving as the national preceptor. Now that we meet, I wish to ask about my doubts. I mean no offense. Will you explain for me?"

The venerable one replied: "Ask whatever you wish. I shall analyze and explain it for the king."

The king asked in verse:

> "All people in the world are attached to the five desires,
> Indulging their passions and living in abandon, amusing themselves thereby.
> You now dwell alone in the wilderness,
> Having abandoned love and affection. What glory or joy do you have?"

The venerable one answered: "I contemplate all causes and conditions as entirely impermanent; therefore I left home. Having severed emotional attachments, I delight in the forest thickets, like a wild deer. With single-minded diligence I have permanently severed the afflictions. With the axe of wisdom I have cut the branches of the tree of craving; my mind has no lingering attachment, and the poisonous fruit has vanished. The swift current of all the fetters, the cascading river of birth-and-death: I have already crossed it. There is no more grief or distress. It is like a bird freed from a net, soaring far into the sky: this is called liberation."

The king, hearing these words, said to Piṇḍola: "Today my power can subdue all countries. My majestic virtue shines brilliantly like the blazing sun. I wear a heavenly crown and jeweled ornaments, attended by palace women, like Indra, Lord of the Gods. You dwell alone. Do you not envy me?"

The venerable one replied: "I have no envious mind."

The king asked again: "Why do you not wish to envy me?"

The venerable one replied: "Today the mud of my desires has dried up. All fetters and bonds have been liberated. Even the wondrous celestial maidens of Indra could not arouse envy in me; how much less your crude and impure human attendants! What wise person, having freed himself from Māra's bonds, crossed the shore of birth-and-death, attained the pure eye of wisdom, and destroyed the darkness of ignorance, would envy a king? What person with clear eyes would envy the blind? What healthy person would envy the sick? What innocent person would envy a prisoner? What wealthy person would envy the destitute? What noble person would envy a slave? What wise person would envy the foolish? What brave person would envy the weak?"

The king, hearing this, felt vexed in his heart and said: "Your analogies are extremely harsh! Am I truly so inferior?"

The venerable one replied: "The king lacks the eye of wisdom, is afflicted by the sickness of defilement, swept away and submerged by the swift current of the four graspings, has lost his vigor and cannot be diligent. Being so childishly foolish, not knowing the noble truths, sunk and drifting in the ocean of suffering: this is the king's portion. To generate the thought of something rare in the five desires: such a thought truly goes against the pure practice."

The king asked again: "What fault is there, that you say it goes against [the pure practice]?"

The venerable one replied: "These five desires are the root of all sufferings. They harm the roots of merit of sentient beings like hail harms seedlings. They sting sentient beings more painfully than poisonous snakes. They are like a blazing fire that can burn up merit. They are like a mirage that deceives the foolish. They are like magical illusions that bewilder and confuse. Desire feigns friendliness but is worse than an enemy. Desire is like an old ox mired in filthy mud. Desire is like a great net that enmeshes the three realms. Desire is like a sword's edge, difficult to tread upon. Desire can bind, imprison, and kill sentient beings. All faults arise from desire."

"In ancient times there was the god Vasu, who, because of desire, was bound by the asura Bali and cast into boiling water. The god Bala destroyed the asura's city walls, annihilated his people, and caused suffering to the line of King Subāhu. The eight kings of the Puṇḍi lineage and their hundred sons were all slain. The asura Vitrā harmed the thousand-eyed [Indra]. Rāma harmed the ten-headed rākṣasa and many billions of rākṣasas. Rāmāyaṇa harmed King Indratanaya. King Mārici annihilated the lineage of Tamāracī. King Kātivī was killed by the brahmin Yama-Nī. Vinaśana harmed the retinue of Dhṛtarāṣṭra. The five sons of Pāṇḍu killed eighteen hundred million people. [Many other kings and lineages were destroyed.] All these kings, because of desire, mutually destroyed one another."

Then he spoke in verse:

> "Though the royal throne is grand, its succession never pauses;
> As swift and fleeting as a flash of lightning, in an instant it returns to dust.
> The royal seat, supremely rich and indulgent: the fool's affection delights in it,
> But when decline and death arrive, the suffering surpasses that of the lowliest.
> A king occupies the highest seat, his fame filling the four directions,
> Handsome and lovable, adorning himself in every way,
> Like one about to die who wears flower garlands and ornaments:
> The remaining time is not long. The royal seat is just the same.
> A king is like a crow, constantly harboring all kinds of fear;
> Whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down, at all times,
> Among both intimates and strangers, he always has a doubtful and fearful mind.
> Ministers, palace women, queens, elephants, horses, and treasures,
> The land and all possessions: all are the king's property,
> But when kings abandon their lives, all is left behind with none to follow.
> Human kings, heavenly kings, and asura kings,
> By power they oppress the people; with axes and halberds they harm one another,
> Not recognizing the suffering of impermanence, they only increase greed, jealousy, and vexation.
> Like a splendid flower grove with a golden serpent sleeping within:
> A fool, thinking it a treasure, wraps it up and brings it home;
> The serpent wakes and unleashes its venomous fire, burning down the house.
> The royal seat is like the flower grove; calamity is like the golden serpent.
> The fool considers it precious; the wise find no delight in it.
> Like a lump of meat placed at a crossroads:
> Foxes, wolves, crows, and vultures compete to fight over it.
> The royal seat is just the same: all compete to seize it.
> Birds and beasts with beaks and claws scratch and fight with one another;
> Kings with swords and spears harm each other, fighting over glory and position.
> They are no different from those birds and beasts, equally foolish.
> I would rather eat ashes and earth, and subsist on grasses and vegetables.
> This body is like a boil or sore, destined to burst and rot.
> Why, for this body's sake, do you create so many evil deeds?
> Like eating a kiṃpā fruit, fully endowed with fragrance and flavor,
> But when the fruit is digested, the entire body rots and decays:
> The royal seat is like that fruit; when lost, it produces only suffering.
> Like a land where pestilence and disease spread:
> The wise and worthy should swiftly flee far away.
> If one does not flee, it is like holding a torch against the wind:
> If one does not let go, one will certainly burn oneself.
> Like drinking salt water: there is never a time of satisfaction.
> Like the ten-headed rākṣasa [Rāvaṇa],
> Whose city and retinue, because of desire,
> Were destroyed without remainder.
> And like King Jayanta, with his hundred brothers,
> Who, because of desire, were all destroyed.
> The Solar Dynasty's King Pāṇḍava and Dhṛtarāṣṭra,
> And all such kings, were all destroyed by desire."

"Know that a kingdom is like a net, like a snare, like deep mire, like a whirlpool, like ocean waves, like a forest on fire, like a crumbling cliff, like a hell. What wise person would delight in attachment to it? With such great suffering, what wise person would generate a thought of happiness?"

"Great King! Alas, how strange! To be deceived to this extent! To be deluded to this extent! Like an empty fist deceiving a small child: swiftly and without pause, like a magical illusion. The five desires deceive in just the same way."

"Like a monkey on a mountaintop who sees clouds spreading below and takes them to be solid ground, and thinking it is earth, leaps with his body, falling a hundred fathoms from the cliff, losing his life, entirely shattered."

"Or like a jackal who sees a kiṃśuka tree whose fruit resembles meat. When the fruit falls to the ground, the jackal goes to eat it, discovers it is not meat, and then thinks: 'This one is not meat; the ones up in the tree must surely be meat.' And so he waits beneath the tree and is thereby trapped."

"The five desires deceive the king in just the same way. Like a merchant who deceives people with counterfeit pearls. Like a foolish infant who craves the taste of a happy-ball, and someone comes deceiving him with a mud ball, which he takes to be real, running after it in exhaustion until he obtains only a mud ball. Like a mirage in the heat that deceives the thirsty fool."

"Like a crowd of people who set up a magic post before them, which makes the crowd see all kinds of things; if the post is pulled up, the images vanish at once. Like a painter or a maker of mechanical puppets."

"Like a dog barking at a well, seeing its own reflection, eyes angry and fur bristling, thinking the reflection at the bottom of the well wants to fight with it, and in senseless rage it leaps into the well and dies."

"Great King! Please observe carefully. What among the five desires endures forever? What royal position stays for long? No honored power or authority can abide. What territory is not destroyed? What treasure is not scattered and lost? What pleasure remains constant and unchanging? Whatever is conferred by suffering must receive decline. What meeting is not followed by parting? All five desires are suffering by nature; happiness arises only from delusion."

### The Parable of the Man in the Well

The venerable one said: "Great King! I shall now briefly explain a parable for the king, concerning the faults of attachment to the taste of birth-and-death. May the king listen with his whole heart."

"Once there was a man traveling on a desolate road. He encountered a great fierce elephant, which pursued him. In terror he ran desperately with nowhere to turn for refuge. He saw a dry well with a tree growing beside it, and he descended by the tree's roots into the well to hide. There were a white mouse and a black mouse gnawing at the tree roots. On the four walls of the well were four venomous snakes seeking to sting him. Below the well was a great poisonous dragon. He feared the four snakes on the sides and the dragon below; the tree he clung to had its roots shaking. From the tree above, three drops of honey fell into his mouth. At that moment, shaking the tree dislodged a beehive; the bees scattered and stung the man. Then a wildfire arose and began to burn the tree."

"Great King, you should know: that man's suffering was beyond all reckoning."

The king, filled with worry and aversion, said: "The taste that man received was exceedingly small, and his suffering was exceedingly great. What he tasted was like the water in an ox's hoofprint; his suffering was like the great ocean. The taste was like a mustard seed; the suffering like Mount Sumeru. The taste was like a firefly; the suffering like the sun and moon. Like the hole in a lotus root compared to the great void; like a mosquito compared to a garuḍa bird. The disparity between taste and suffering is thus."

The venerable one said: "Great King! The desolate wilderness is a metaphor for birth-and-death. The man is a metaphor for the ordinary person. The elephant is a metaphor for impermanence. The dry well is a metaphor for the human body. The tree roots are a metaphor for the human lifespan. The white and black mice are a metaphor for day and night. Their gnawing of the roots is a metaphor for the cessation of each moment. The four venomous snakes are a metaphor for the four great elements. The honey is a metaphor for the five desires. The bees are a metaphor for evil and unwholesome thoughts. The wildfire is a metaphor for old age. The poisonous dragon below is a metaphor for death."

"Therefore know: the taste of desire is exceedingly small, while the suffering is exceedingly great. Birth, old age, sickness, and death have power over every person. People of the world toil in body and mind without a place of refuge, oppressed by all sufferings, as swift as lightning. This is cause for grief, not for attachment."

"Great King! My words to you today, though coarse and harsh, are truly beneficial."

The king, hearing these words, felt his hair stand on end. Grief and joy mingled together, and tears streamed down his face. He rose, joined his palms, and prostrated with his whole body on the ground, saying to the venerable one: "In my childish foolishness I have no wisdom. In my lowliness I spoke those mad words. Please allow me to repent of such mad speech."

The venerable one said: "I have gone forth with patience; there is nothing I do not endure. My mind is pure, like the autumn moon, clear and without clouds or mist. Now that the king has repented, may the great king, like the Lord of the Gods, attain a vision of the path."

The king, greatly delighted, bowed with his retinue and returned to the palace.
