# 佛說胞胎經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Embryo in the Womb

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0317

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park. At that time the Elder Nanda, while sitting in quiet contemplation, arose and went to the Buddha. Together with five hundred bhikṣus, they all went to the Buddha's abode, prostrated at his feet, and sat to one side.

The Buddha addressed Nanda and the bhikṣus: "I shall now teach you a sūtra. Its opening words are wholesome, its middle words are wholesome, and its concluding words are wholesome. Its meaning is analyzed, subtle, and complete, purely cultivating the holy life. I shall teach you about how a person encounters a mother's womb and receives the embryonic body. Listen carefully! Reflect well upon it!"

"Yes, World-Honored One!" The Elder Nanda received the instruction and listened.

The Buddha addressed Nanda: "Why does a mother not conceive? In this case, the father and mother give rise to a mind of passionate desire. When causes and conditions come together in union, and the mother has fine and wholesome mental dispositions, and the spirit that comes to be born arrives at the proper time, yet the mother has an irregularity in her menses, or the father has an irregularity while the mother does not, or the father is pure while the mother is not, or the mother is pure while the father is not, or the mother's womb has reached its limit: in such cases she does not conceive. Regarding the womb's conditions: sometimes it becomes cold; sometimes sounds are too close, which destroy the essence; sometimes it is too full; sometimes it is as if from medicine; or like the center of a fruit; or like a long-pepper seed; or like an unripe fruit; or like a bird's eye; or like an iṣā eye; or like a śākhā eye; or like a jūkā eye; or like the pupil of an eye; or like a leaf; or gathered together like grime. In these conditions, sometimes it is too deep, sometimes too high; sometimes the womb is not a proper vessel; sometimes sounds are too close; or it is hard like a kernel or a pearl; or it has been eaten by worms; or it leans to the left or to the right; or it is excessively clear; or it is overly violent; or it is unbalanced, leaning left when it should lean right; or it is like a water vessel; or like a fruit; or like a wolf's tracks; or it has various flaws; or it is afflicted with various colds; or there is excessive heat. Or the father and mother are humble while the incoming spirit is noble; or the incoming spirit is noble while the father and mother are humble. For these reasons they do not match. When their conduct and aspirations are equal, when both are noble or both are humble, when their minds are the same without difference, then the spirit enters the mother's womb. Why does a mother not conceive? When none of the various mixed and discordant conditions mentioned above are present, when the intentions and conduct are equal, when both are noble or both are humble, when the karmic conditions of past lives mean they should bear a child, and the incoming spirit is destined to encounter the parents and become their child, but at that time the spirit or the arriving consciousness harbors two minds, with differing thoughts, then the conditions do not harmonize, and there is no entry into the womb."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "How does one come to enter the mother's womb? Those of meager merit generate this thought: 'There is cold wind and rain falling now, and a great crowd is coming to beat and harm me. I should flee into a great pile of grass, or into a pile of leaves and various vegetation, or into a ravine or deep valley, or climb to a high summit where none can reach me, and escape the cold wind, the great rain, and the crowd.' Thus the person enters a house. Those of ample merit and great power think to themselves: 'Now there is cold wind and great rain and a great crowd. I should enter a house, go up to a great lecture hall, ascend to a fine pavilion, and sit upon a couch or throne.'"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "When the spirit enters the mother's womb, the thoughts are many and varied, each different."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "When the spirit enters the womb, the embryonic receptacle is formed. This embryo is formed from the impure essences of the father and mother, which are not separate. The impure essences of the father and mother are also relied upon. Through the coming together of causes and conditions, the embryo is received. Therefore, it is neither solely from the father and mother nor separate from the father and mother. It is like, Ānanda, a vessel of curds: if you place milk in a vessel that holds curds, through the conditions of holding curds, butter may be produced. But if it is left alone, butter will not form. Butter does not come from curds alone, nor is it separate from curds. Through the coming together of causes and conditions, butter is obtained. Thus, Ānanda, the body is not formed from the impure essences of the father and mother alone, nor is it formed apart from the father and mother. Through the father and mother as conditions, the embryo is formed."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "It is like green grass upon which insects are born. The insects do not come from the grass, nor are they apart from the grass. Depending on the green grass as a cause and condition, through their coming together, insects are born. Thus, from among such conditions, the insects naturally arise. So too, Ānanda, the body is not formed from the impure essences of the father and mother alone, nor apart from those essences. Through the father and mother as conditions, the embryo is formed. It is like, Ānanda, grain from which insects are born. The insects do not come from the grain alone, nor are they separate from the grain. Through the grain as a cause and condition, insects are born. Through this coming together, they naturally arise. So too, Ānanda, the body is not from the parents' impure essences alone, nor apart from them. Through the parents as conditions, the embryo is formed, and the sense faculties and the four great elements are established. It is like, Ānanda, the bāla fruit from which insects are born. The insects do not come from the bāla fruit alone, nor are they separate from it. Through the bāla fruit as a condition, they naturally arise. So too, Ānanda, the body is not from the parents' impure essences alone, nor apart from them. Through the parents as conditions, the embryo is formed, and the sense faculties and the four great elements are established. It is like, Ānanda, curds from which insects are born. The insects do not come from the curds alone, nor are they separate from the curds. Through the curds as a condition, they naturally arise. So too, Ānanda, the body is not from the parents' impure essences alone, nor apart from them. Through the parents as conditions, the embryo is formed, and the sense faculties and the four great elements are established. Through the parents as conditions, the earth element is established: this refers to all that is solid. What is soft and moist is the water element. What is hot and warm is the fire element. What is breath and air is the wind element. Suppose, Ānanda, the embryo formed through the parents' conditions were to consist only of the earth element. The water element would cause it to decay, just as parched grain or skin dissolves when it encounters moisture. Suppose the embryo were to consist only of the water element, without the earth element: because it would be as thin as liquid, like oil or water, it could not cohere. Again, Ānanda, the water element relies on the earth element so as not to decay, and the earth element relies on the water element so as not to scatter. Suppose, Ānanda, the embryo formed through the parents' conditions consisted of the earth element and the water element, but the fire element had nothing to rely upon: then it would wither and rot. Just as in the fifth month of summer, when the heat is intense, meat decays because of the fire element, becoming foul and putrid. Thus, Ānanda, if the embryo formed through the parents' conditions consisted of the earth element and the water element, without the fire element, it would not rot, decay, or perish. Suppose, Ānanda, the embryo consisted of the earth element, the water element, and the fire element, but had no wind element: without the wind element established, it could not grow or develop. Furthermore, Ānanda, the spirit dwelling within, through its merits and transgressions, gives rise to the four great elements. The earth element holds everything together; the water element differentiates; the fire element gives its designation; and the wind element enables growth and development, leading to the accomplished form."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "It is like the lotus root growing in a pond: pure and complete, its flower in bud, not yet open. The wind blows it open and causes it to grow and develop and become fully formed. So too, Ānanda, the spirit dwelling within, through its merits and transgressions, gives rise to the four great elements. The earth element is established for support; the water element holds it together; the fire element differentiates; the wind element gives its designation, and through the wind it grows and develops. Gradually becoming complete: this is not solely because of the parents' embryonic conditions. It is also the spirit of the person being reborn. It is not the parents' merit alone, not the father's body alone, not the mother's body alone. Through causes and conditions they come together. Not through empty causes and conditions alone, not through a multiplicity of conditions, not through other conditions. And yet, through mutual giving and shared aspiration, they come together to form the embryonic body within the womb.

"It is like, Ānanda, the seeds of the five grains and plants that are complete, not rotten, not worm-eaten. When the earth is plowed, turned, and enriched, and the seeds are sown, they grow lush and fine. What does Ānanda think? Can the seeds stand alone? Do they require earth, water, and designation to form roots, stems, branches, leaves, flowers, and fruit?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "No, Heavenly among the Heavenly!"

The Buddha said: "So too, Ānanda, it is not from the parents' seminal essences alone that the embryonic body is formed. It is not from the parents' body alone, nor from empty causes and conditions alone. Through causes and conditions coming together, through the four great elements equally uniting and equally manifesting, the embryonic body is formed within the womb.

"It is like, Ānanda, a person with good eyes who takes a maṇi jewel or a fire-crystal and holds it toward the bright sun at high noon, and places dry cow dung, or mugwort, or cloth beneath it. Immediately fire appears and flames are produced. Now that fire does not come from the sun alone, does not come from the maṇi jewel, the fire-crystal, the mugwort, or the cloth alone, nor is it separate from them. Again, Ānanda, through causes and conditions coming together, through conditions arriving equally, with nothing more or less, fire is produced. The embryonic body is likewise: it is not from the father and mother alone, nor apart from them. And yet, through the parents' impure essences as conditions, the embryonic body is formed. Through this, form is accomplished, as well as sensation, perception, volition, and consciousness. Through this, name and designation are obtained, and through conditions, the name is obtained. From the fundamental form, the designation 'name-and-form' arises. Again, Ānanda, regarding what arises from conditions: I do not praise the coming and going of birth and death."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "It is like a small sore or wound that festers: this is not something people enjoy. How much less a large one? Even a small opening or flaw is repugnant; how much more a large one? So too, Ānanda, even a small amount of cycling through birth and death is not something I praise. How much more so a long duration? Why? All the afflictions of cycling through birth and death are extremely toilsome. Who would take pleasure in a foul-smelling place, entering the mother's embryonic womb?"

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "At the first seven-day period, the embryo is received in the mother's womb. How does it naturally form? When first settling and not yet complete, the embryo naturally likewise takes seven days in its place, remaining without increase or decrease. Gradually it heats, gradually it solidifies, and the earth element is established. What is soft and moist becomes the water element. What is warm within becomes the fire element. What circulates through it becomes the wind element. In the second seven-day period, a wind called 'Turning' gently arises and blows, causing the embryo to move to the left or right side. It gathers the body into the embryonic form, like the cream atop curds. The essence gradually solidifies, and it is likewise. During those seven days, it further transforms and ripens. What is solid establishes the earth element; what is soft and moist becomes the water element; what is warm becomes the fire element; and what circulates within becomes the wind element."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the third seven-day period, within the womb inside the mother's belly, a wind called 'Sound-Gate' arises and blows, causing the embryonic body to further coagulate and solidify. What does this coagulation resemble? It is like placing one's finger into an abscess of pus and flesh that has deteriorated. The essence transforms thus. During those seven days, it transforms and ripens. What is solid becomes the earth element; what is soft and moist becomes the water element; what is warm becomes the fire element; and what circulates within becomes the wind element."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the fourth seven-day period, within the womb inside the mother's belly, a wind arises called 'Nourishment.' It blows the embryonic body, causing it to further solidify. What does this solidity resemble? It is like a blood-bearing creature that has an offspring called 'cluster,' boneless and untrustworthy in its firmness, yet solid in this way. During those seven days, it transforms and ripens. What is solid becomes the earth element; what is soft and moist becomes the water element; what is warm becomes the fire element; and what circulates within becomes the wind element."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the fifth seven-day period, within the womb inside the mother's belly, a wind arises called 'Guiding.' It blows the solid essence, transforming it into bodily form, producing five auspicious protuberances: two knees, two shoulders, and one head. Just as in springtime when rain falls from the sky, nourishing the branches and leaves of the trees, so it is with the embryo. Within the mother's womb, the five protuberances form: two knees, two shoulders, and the head."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the sixth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Water.' It blows the embryonic body, causing it to transform further, forming four more auspicious protuberances: two knee-joints and two elbow-joints."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the seventh seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Revolving.' It blows, causing the transformation of four more auspicious protuberances: two hand-buds and two arm-buds, gradually lengthening, soft and tender. Just as foam dries out, so the embryonic body's four protuberances, the two hands and two feet, appear as bud-formations."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the eighth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Counter-Revolving.' It blows the embryonic body, producing twenty protuberances: ten toe-joints and ten finger-joints. Just as rain falls from the sky, causing branching twigs to grow ever more lush, so the embryonic body within the womb develops twenty segments: ten toes and ten fingers."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the ninth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, blowing and transforming the nine openings: two eyes, two ears, two nostrils, the mouth, and the two lower orifices."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the tenth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Boil-Shortening.' It blows the embryonic body with rapid, violent, and very strong force. During those seven days, at night on the seventh day, a wind arises naturally, called 'Universal Gate,' which arranges the body as if it were strong and solid, complete with sounds."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the eleventh seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Arranging-and-Destroying.' It blows the embryonic body, arranging its form, settling what is scattered, and causing the mother to pace about restlessly, with agitation and discomfort. She moves sluggishly, enjoys laughter, likes to play and joke, sing and dance, and the wind causes tears to flow. Thus, while dwelling in the mother's womb, the embryo delights in stretching its hands and feet. When the embryo turns into position, as it forms, the scattered parts come together. There is a wind called 'Pillar-Turning' that goes toward the top of the head, scatters the crown, and causes the embryo to turn upside down. It is like a blacksmith's bellows blowing from above, causing it to turn. Thus, Ānanda, the Pillar-Turning wind rises to the neck. From the neck it scatters and turns, going back and forth. The wind circling at the neck opens the throat, the mouth, and the navel in the middle of the body, and the fingers, causing them to become perforated. Its action gradually leads to their full formation."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twelfth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Skin-Face.' It blows the embryonic body, forming the intestines and stomach, the left and right forms. Just as a lotus root is embedded in the earth, so the intestines are formed and rely upon the body in the same way. Eighteen cavities, channels, grooves, and passages are formed. During those seven days, a wind arises naturally, called 'Casting-off-Hair.' It blows forth the tongue, opens the eyes, and forms one hundred joints in the body, bringing them to full completion, not lacking in anything. They rely upon the body, producing eleven thousand one hundred joints."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the thirteenth seven-day period, within the womb, the embryo begins to sense the body's weakness and also hunger and thirst. Whatever the mother eats and drinks enters the child's body. The child in the womb, whatever the mother eats and drinks, relies on the mother for its growth and nourishment."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the fourteenth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Channel-Thread-Gate.' It blows the embryo's body and produces ninety thousand sinews: twenty-two thousand five hundred in the front of the body, twenty-two thousand five hundred in the back, twenty-two thousand five hundred on the left side, and twenty-two thousand five hundred on the right side."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the fifteenth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Red Lotus' and 'Wave-Cloud.' It blows the child's body and establishes twenty vessels: five vessels extend to the front of the body, five to the back, five to the left, and five to the right. Within these vessels there are countless varieties of many colors, each with its own name. These are called 'Strength,' 'Standing,' 'Firmness,' and 'Power.' Among the colors, some are blue, some white; white turns to red, red turns to white; some white becomes yellow; some are of the color of milky tea, or of butter, or of curds-oil. There are raw mixtures and cooked mixtures. Each of the twenty vessels has forty subsidiary vessels, totaling eight hundred: two hundred in the front of the body, two hundred in the back, two hundred on the left, and two hundred on the right. Of these, two are chief and two are secondary."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Each of the eight hundred vessels has ten thousand subsidiary vessels, totaling eighty thousand. Twenty thousand are in the chest and abdomen, twenty thousand in the back, twenty thousand on the left, and twenty thousand on the right. These eighty thousand vessels have countless hollow spaces beyond reckoning. There is one space, then two, then three, up to seven. Just as a lotus stem has many openings, growing from one to two to three, up to seven openings, so too, Ānanda, the eighty thousand vessels are likewise, with countless root-hollows beyond reckoning: one, then two, then three, up to seven." The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "These vessels are interconnected with the pores of the body."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the sixteenth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Immeasurable.' It blows the child's body, properly arranging the bones and joints, each settling into its place. It opens the two eyes, the two ears, the nostrils, the mouth, and the neck. It circulates throughout, settling the heart, enabling food and drink to flow without obstruction. All the established openings flow in and out, back and forth, following the body without error. If all is complete, there is no hindrance. Just as, Ānanda, a potter or a potter's apprentice kneads the clay and adjusts it well, fashioning the vessel form, patting and beating it into shape, mending the top and bottom so it does not leak, and placing it in its proper location: so too, Ānanda, through the karmic conditions of merit and transgression, a wind naturally transforms the bodily form, opening the eyes, ears, nose, mouth, throat, and neck, opening the root of the mind, enabling all food and drink to flow through, with all openings passing in and out without obstruction, settling the food and drink."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the seventeenth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Yak-Face.' It blows the child's body, opening the eyes and making them clean, bright, and lustrous; and the two ear openings, the nose, and the mouth are all made clean, lustrous, and without blemish. Just as, Ānanda, a mirror-polisher's apprentice takes a dirty mirror and scrapes, polishes, and rubs it, using oil to bring out its brightness and remove its blemishes, until it shines inside and out: so too, Ānanda, through the karmic conditions of merit and transgression, a wind naturally opens the eyes, ears, nose, and mouth, making them clean, clear, and without blemish."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the eighteenth seven-day period, within the womb, all the various blemishes are entirely removed and made pure. Just as a moon-city or a person's palace has a wind called 'Great-Firm-Strong,' and this wind is extremely powerful, spinning and blowing the palace, lifting and carrying it about, and making it naturally pure and complete without blemish: so too with the embryo. Within the mother's womb, all the incoming essences are blown by the wind, naturally becoming bright and clear, complete and fully formed."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the nineteenth seven-day period, the embryo obtains four faculties: the eye faculty, the ear faculty, the nose faculty, and the tongue faculty. From the time of first entering the mother's womb, it already possesses three faculties: the body faculty, the mind faculty, and the life faculty."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twentieth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Leather-Straps.' It blows the small child's body, beginning with the left foot to produce bone-joints, then supporting the right foot to blow and form bones. Four bones at each knee; two bones at each kneecap; three bones in the neck; eighteen bones in the back; eighteen bones in the ribs; thirteen bones in each palm; twenty bones each in the left and right feet; four bones at each elbow-joint; two bones at the hip joints; two bones in the shoulders; eighteen bones in the cervical area; three bones around each ear; thirty-two bones for the teeth in the mouth; four bones in the skull. Just as, Ānanda, a mechanical-figure artisan or a painter makes a wooden person, joining all the parts together, first preparing the timber, assembling and securing it, tying it with cords and connecting the joints, pressing it into shape with a frame, linking it with ropes, and thereby forming an image no different from a real person: so too, Ānanda, through the transformations of merit and transgression, a wind naturally blows and forms the appearance, transforming it into bone-joints through the transformation of causes and conditions. During these twenty seven-day periods, within the body, there arise at the proper time two hundred tiny bones mingled with flesh."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twenty-first seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'All-That-Exists.' It blows the child's body, causing muscle and flesh to grow. Just as, Ānanda, a skilled potter makes fine pottery vessels, jars, pots, and vases, bringing them to full completion: so too, Ānanda, the wind called 'All-That-Exists' blows the child's body, causing muscle and flesh to grow in the same way."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twenty-second seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Crossing-Over-Evil.' It blows the child's body, causing blood to be produced."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twenty-third seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Needle-Hole-Pure.' It blows the child's body, causing skin to gradually grow and become complete."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twenty-fourth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Firm-Holding.' It blows the child's body, spreading the skin and making it even and uniform."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twenty-fifth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Heard-Existing-Holding.' It blows the child's body, sweeping away the flesh and making it entirely smooth and lustrous."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twenty-sixth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally and blows the child's body. If in former lives one performed evil deeds and various misfortunes, practicing the ten evils, or being stingy and greedy, hoarding wealth and unable to give, or not receiving the teachings of former sages, teachers, and parents: then what should have been long and pure instead becomes short and small; what should have been large and robust instead becomes thin and frail; what should have been pure and tall instead becomes coarse and thick; what should have had much purity instead has little; what should have had little instead has much; what should have been clean instead becomes filthy; what should have been filthy instead becomes clean; what should have been male instead becomes non-male; what was not desired as male instead becomes a violent male; what was sought is not obtained; what was not desired comes of its own accord; what should have been black instead becomes yellow; what should have been yellow instead becomes black."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "Whatever evil one has sown in past lives naturally comes to fruition. One may be born blind, deaf, mute, or foolish; the body may be covered with sores and ulcers; one may be born without eyes, unable to speak, with all orifices blocked, lame, bald, or afflicted. What one has done in the past naturally comes back, and one's parents feel aversion. This deviates from the Dharma's meaning. Why? Because, Ānanda, it is the unwholesome deeds of past lives that produce these results."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "If that person in former lives practiced many virtues, did not transgress the various evils, and was inclined toward wholesome deeds, namely the ten virtuous actions, delighting in generosity without stingy defilement, receiving the instructions of former sages, teachers, and parents: then the joints of the body will be long where they should be long, clean and pure; will be bright where they should be bright; will be large and pure where they should be; will be fine and small where they should be; will have much purity where much is needed, and little where little is needed; will be smooth and lustrous where required; will be small and patient where needed; will be male where male is appropriate; will have a pleasing voice; will have wished-for adornments; will be black where black is appropriate; and will speak as desired. Thus, Ānanda, according to the merits sown in past lives, all naturally results in being pleasing to all beings, of upright and fine appearance, supreme among forms. Whatever the body, speech, and mind seek, wish, and aspire to, all is obtained as desired. Why? Because, Ānanda, the deeds of past lives naturally bring their results."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "If it is a boy, he settles in the mother's right side in the cross-legged position, with the palms of his hands against his face, his back outward, facing toward his mother, below the undigested food and above the digested food, bound by five cords as if in a leather sack. If it is a girl, she is in the mother's left side in the cross-legged position, with her palms against her face, below the undigested food and above the digested food, bound by five cords as if in a leather sack. If the mother eats too much, the child is unsettled. If she eats too little, the child is unsettled. If the food is too greasy, the child is unsettled. If there is no oil in the food, the child is unsettled. In great heat or great cold, when desires are pursued or not pursued, sweet, sour, coarse, or fine, if the food is excessive or insufficient and unbalanced, the child is unsettled. If the mother indulges excessively in sexual activity, the child is unsettled. If she is out in the wind excessively, the child is unsettled. If she walks, runs, or moves about too much, or climbs trees, the child is unsettled."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "The child in the mother's womb endures such hardship, distress, and afflictions, such numerous difficulties! Yet ordinary people say they are born in a place of ease. If even this is so, how much more the hardships and suffering of the evil destinies? These sufferings and difficulties are beyond comparison. Who would take pleasure in the mother's womb?"

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twenty-eighth seven-day period, within the womb, eight kinds of thoughts arise: the thought of riding in a vehicle, the thought of a park, the thought of a pavilion, the thought of sightseeing, the thought of a couch or throne, the thought of a flowing river, the thought of a spring, and the thought of a bathing pool."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the twenty-ninth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, called 'Marrow-Middle.' It maintains the skin, making it clean and pure. The complexion becomes fixed according to past-life deeds. If one performed black deeds in past lives, the color manifests as black, the body like lacquer. If one performed deeds that were neither white nor black, the color manifests as neither white nor black, with a uniform appearance. If one performed plain deeds without luster, the color manifests as plain without luster, uniform throughout the body. If one performed white deeds, the face and body are uniformly white. If one performed yellow deeds, the face and body are uniformly yellow. Ānanda! In this world, people have six kinds of complexion, each according to what was sown in past lives, and naturally they receive it."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the thirtieth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally and blows the child's body, causing hair to grow. According to past-life deeds, the child's hair may be perfectly black, fine, and marvelous beyond measure, or it may grow yellow and unpleasing."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the thirty-first seven-day period, within the womb, the child's body grows larger and becomes complete.

"In the thirty-second seven-day period, the child's body is self-formed and lacks nothing. In the thirty-third, thirty-fourth, thirty-fifth, and thirty-sixth seven-day periods, the child's body is fully formed, with solid bones and joints. While in the womb, it takes no pleasure in this."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the thirty-seventh seven-day period, within the womb, thoughts arise naturally: as if trapped in a net, wishing to escape; an impure thought; a prison thought; a dark and gloomy thought. The child takes no pleasure in this."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In the thirty-eighth seven-day period, within the womb, a wind arises naturally, a flower-wind called 'Where-Does-It-Tend.' It turns the child's body and directs it toward the place of birth, lowering the two hands to face the birth-passage, following the fruition of its karmic conditions. If in former lives one committed evil deeds, at the time of birth the feet turn back and the hands and feet are reversed, endangering the mother, who may even lose her life. The mother is distressed and pained beyond measure. If in former lives one performed meritorious deeds leading to a long life, then the child does not turn back, the life is not cut short midway, and the mother is spared suffering and immeasurable affliction. During this thirty-eighth seven-day period, the child endures great suffering, immeasurable distress, sorrow, and unhappiness."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "The suffering of birth and death is extremely toilsome. When a person is born, whether male or female, upon falling to the ground, the pain is beyond words. How truly unwholesome it is! The distress and bitterness! When received by cloth that touches its body, or by a blanket and placed in its resting spot, whether on a bed, or placed upon the ground, whether covered or exposed, whether in heat or cold: because of these things, the child undergoes suffering and torment beyond description. It is as if, Ānanda, a snake or cow's hide is hung somewhere: if on a wall, it transforms into worms that eat the hide. If on a tree, or on shoots of grass, or by a pool of water, or even suspended in empty space, wherever it rests, worms arise from it and eat its form. Wherever it is placed, worms are born and eat its form. When the child is first born and received by hands, the pain and distress are beyond measure. Whether received by cloth that touches it, and so on, as described before. As the body gradually grows larger, there is hunger, thirst, cold, and heat. The mother, with tender care, moves the child from wet to dry places, nurturing and removing its impurities. This is what the ancient sages' Dharma and Vinaya call the mother's grace of nursing."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "With such hardship as this, who would take pleasure in dwelling in the parents' embryonic womb? Shortly after the child is born, it is fed rice-gruel to nourish its body. The body immediately produces eighty thousand kinds of worms that surround it, moving and feeding on the child's body. The worms at the hair-roots are called 'Tongue-Licking,' dwelling at the hair-roots and feeding on the hair. The names of the worms are found in the *Yogācārabhūmi*. The first is called 'Tongue-Licking,' the second 'Heavy-Licking,' and a third kind on the head is called 'Firm-Damage-and-Harm.'"

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "A person's body endures such suffering. Eighty thousand kinds of worms feed on the body day and night, causing the person to be exhausted, weak, and depleted. The body falls ill, becoming alternately cold and hot, with afflictions and suffering beyond count. The body is agitated with extreme suffering, hunger is extreme, walking is extreme, standing is extreme. If the body falls ill, one must seek medicine to cure the disease. The suffering in the mother's womb is beyond words. Once born as a human being, one lives at most a hundred years, or longer or shorter. Within a hundred years, one passes through a hundred springs, a hundred summers, a hundred autumns, and a hundred winters. Within a hundred years, one passes through one thousand two hundred months: three months of spring, three of summer, three of autumn, and three of winter. Within a hundred years, dividing the days of light and darkness, one passes through two thousand four hundred and fifteen days: six hundred and fifteen days of spring, six hundred and fifteen of summer, six hundred and fifteen of autumn, and six hundred and fifteen of winter. Within a hundred years, one eats seventy-two thousand meals: eighteen thousand in spring, eighteen thousand in summer, eighteen thousand in autumn, and eighteen thousand in winter. Sometimes one does not eat due to displeasure; sometimes due to anger; sometimes due to poverty; sometimes due to being occupied with work; sometimes due to intoxication and heedlessness; sometimes due to observing a fast. All of these are included within the seventy-two thousand meals. Thus, Ānanda, with such hardship, distress, and affliction, who would take pleasure in dwelling in the mother's womb? Amid such numerous afflictions, one is never at peace, bound by various conditions. Sometimes the eyes are in pain; sometimes the ears, nose, mouth, tongue, or teeth are in pain; the kneecaps, feet, throat, shortness of breath, the waist, spine, arms, elbows, curled wrists: pains in every hundred joints. Wind-cold, various fevers, scabies, leprosy, hemorrhoids, sores, abscesses, jaundice, coughing and hiccups, madness, blindness, deafness, muteness, foolishness, tumors, incontinence, distended abdomen, diarrhea, bodily swelling. Thus, Ānanda, if one of the four great elements increases, a hundred diseases arise. If wind increases excessively, a hundred diseases arise. If heat increases, a hundred diseases arise. If cold increases, a hundred diseases arise. If food increases, a hundred diseases increase. When three things combine (wind, cold, and heat) together, four hundred and four diseases arise simultaneously. How much more the other countless afflictions! Sometimes one's hands or feet are cut off, or the ears or nose. Sometimes one is beheaded, or chained and whipped, beaten with staves, and flogged, locked in prison, tortured, and punished. Sometimes one fears other people; sometimes one fears non-human beings, the hells, the hungry ghosts, or animals. The hardship of the desolate wilderness, mosquitoes, gadflies, lice, fleas, and the stings of bees. The terror of tigers, wolves, lions, snakes, and vipers. Reckoning it all, the suffering is beyond words. Many are the things one seeks, and many the roots of hardship planted. What is not obtained causes sorrow. What one aspires to and desires does not accord with one's wishes. What one has obtained must then be guarded, and the toil of livelihood is hard. What one acquires brings insatiable craving, and the vexation of worldly dust creates many hindrances."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "To speak briefly: the five aggregates are suffering. All the sense-fields, all the declines, perceptions, and many thoughts give rise to suffering. From these arise pride and self-aggrandizement. The willful mind races about and is never at rest. Each and every meaning should be observed as it truly is. Like a wheel, one does not remain in one place, whether lying down, rising, on a bed, on the ground, singing, dancing, or playing: one should observe the thought of suffering. Even when walking, sitting, rising, or stepping, one should constantly contemplate suffering. The distresses and afflictions are beyond count; not a single thing is truly pleasant. In all the places one traverses, one should not give rise to the thought of ease. If the mind is stuck and seated without moving, not on a bed or couch, one should still know this is suffering."

Ānanda said: "Do not give rise to the thought of ease."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "If one abides in propriety without rest, there are still countless, immeasurable sufferings. Yet the mind thinks to itself and calls it peaceful and not suffering. Thus, Ānanda, birth and death are difficult to find pleasant. There are, when reckoned, two kinds of affliction: observing one's own body as suffering, and suffering for the sake of others. Observing these two meanings, one should examine oneself: 'Although I have left the household life, by what means shall I attain wisdom, realize the fruit of the path, and gain peace and freedom from affliction?' Regarding the food, clothing, bedding, and medicine one receives from donors, one should enable those donors to obtain great fruition, great radiance, and limitless universal benefit."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "One should study in this way. What does Ānanda think? Is form permanent or impermanent?"

Ānanda answered: "It is impermanent, Heavenly among the Heavenly!"

"If it is impermanent, is it suffering or not suffering?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "It is great suffering, Heavenly among the Heavenly!"

"Furthermore, being impermanent, it must be parted from; the dharma does not endure forever. When noble disciples hear this meaning expounded, would they generate the thought: 'There is a self, there is a me, this is mine'?"

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "No, Heavenly among the Heavenly!"

"Are form, sensation, perception, volition, and consciousness permanent or impermanent?"

He answered: "Impermanent."

He said: "If they are impermanent, are they suffering or peace? When noble disciples hear this expounded, would they think: 'There is a self, there is a me, this is mine'?"

He answered: "No, Heavenly among the Heavenly!"

"Therefore, Ānanda, regarding all form, whether past, future, or present, internal or external, coarse or fine, subtle or defiled, distant or near: there is no self, no other, and it is not my body. The wise and clear-sighted should observe it as equal, should they not? If, Ānanda, noble disciples grow weary of form, of sensation, perception, volition, and consciousness, and if they can grow weary, they leave behind the dust of defilement. Leaving behind the dust of defilement, they cross over. If their aspiration is for crossing over, they attain the crossing-over vision of wisdom. They exhaust birth and death, proclaim the holy life. What the body should accomplish is accomplished, and they cross to the other shore, manifesting here and now."

When the Buddha taught this sūtra, the Elder Ānanda attained the pure Dharma-eye. The five hundred bhikṣus achieved the exhaustion of all defilements and the liberation of mind. The Elder Ānanda, the five hundred disciples, and all the devas, dragons, and spirits, hearing the sūtra, were filled with joy.
