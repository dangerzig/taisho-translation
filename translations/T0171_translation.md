# 太子須大拏經
## Sūtra of Prince Sudāna

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0171

Translated during the Western Qin dynasty by Shèngjiān (聖堅).

---

## Fascicle One

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park. He was together with an innumerable assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās. Seated in the midst of the fourfold assembly, the Buddha smiled, and five-colored light issued from his mouth. Ānanda rose from his seat, adjusted his robes, joined his palms, knelt, and addressed the Buddha: "In the more than twenty years I have attended the Buddha, I have never seen the Buddha smile as he does today. Is the Buddha now thinking of the buddhas of the past, the future, or the present? Is there a particular intention? I wish to hear about it."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "I am not thinking of the buddhas of past, future, or present. I am merely recalling my practice of dāna-pāramitā in a past life, during innumerable asaṃkhyeya-kalpas."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What was this practice of dāna-pāramitā?"

The Buddha said: "Long ago, in the incalculable past, there was a great kingdom called Yepō. Its king was named Śibi. He governed the kingdom with right Dharma, never wronging the people. The king had four thousand ministers, ruled over sixty lesser kingdoms and eight hundred villages, and possessed five hundred great white elephants. The king had twenty thousand consorts but had no son. The king himself prayed to the various deities and to the mountains and rivers, and his consort then became aware she was with child. The king personally attended to the consort's bedding, food, and drink, making everything soft and fine. When ten months were full, she gave birth to a prince. The twenty thousand consorts in the palace, hearing of the prince's birth, all rejoiced and leaped with joy, and milk spontaneously flowed from their breasts. For this reason, they named the prince Sudāna. Four nurses cared for the prince: one to nurse him, one to hold him, one to bathe him, and one to take him out to play. When the prince reached the age of sixteen, he was fully accomplished in writing, arithmetic, archery, charioteering, and all the rites and music. The prince served his parents as one would serve heavenly deities. The king built a separate palace for the prince.

"From childhood, the prince always delighted in giving. To all the people of the land, the birds in the sky, and the beasts on the ground, he wished that all beings might always receive his blessings. Fools are miserly and unwilling to give; in their delusion they deceive themselves to no benefit. The wise, living in the world, know that giving is meritorious. Those who practice giving are praised by all the buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, and arhats of the past, future, and present.

"As the prince grew older, the great king took a bride for him. Her name was Māndhī, and she was a king's daughter. Her beauty was without equal, and she adorned herself with fine lapis lazuli, gold, silver, and various precious jewels. The prince had one son and one daughter. The prince reflected: 'I wish to practice dāna-pāramitā.' He addressed the king: 'I wish to go out for a tour of observation.' The king consented. The prince then left the city. The heavenly king Śakra descended and transformed himself into poor, deaf, blind, and mute people, all standing along the roadside. When the prince saw them, he immediately turned his chariot and returned to the palace, greatly distressed and unhappy. The king asked the prince: 'You went out on a tour and have returned. Why are you unhappy?' The prince replied: 'When I went out, I saw all the poor, deaf, blind, and mute people. This is why I am distressed. I wish to ask one favor of the king. I wonder whether the great king will grant it.' The king answered: 'What do you wish? Whatever you ask for is yours. I will not go against your wishes.' The prince said: 'I wish to take all the precious treasures from the great king's treasury and place them outside the four city gates and in the marketplace, to distribute as gifts, giving to all who ask without refusing anyone.' The king said to the prince: 'Do as you wish; I will not oppose you.' The prince then ordered his attendant ministers to transport the treasures and place them outside the four city gates and in the marketplace, distributing them to all who wished, refusing no one. In the eight directions, above and below, there was no one who had not heard of the prince's merit. People came from a hundred leagues away, from a thousand leagues, and from ten thousand leagues and beyond. Those who wanted food were fed; those who wanted clothing were given clothing; those who wanted gold, silver, and precious jewels were given as they wished. Whatever they desired, none were refused.

"At that time, a rival enemy kingdom heard that the prince delighted in giving, refusing no one who asked. They assembled their ministers and various priests and deliberated together: 'The king of Yepō has a white elephant that walks upon lotus flowers, named Sudānta, of great strength and prowess in battle. Whenever they war against the other kingdoms, this elephant always prevails. Who can go and ask for it?' The ministers all said: 'No one can obtain it.' Among them were eight priests who addressed the king: 'We can go and ask for it. Provide us with provisions.' The king supplied them. The king then said: 'If you can obtain the elephant, I shall reward you greatly.'

"The eight priests then set forth carrying their staffs, traversing mountains and rivers far away until they reached the kingdom of Yepō. Arriving at the prince's palace gate, they all planted their staffs, raised one foot, and stood facing the gate. The gatekeeper went in and reported to the prince: 'Outside there are priests, all standing with their staffs, each with one foot raised. They say: "We have come from afar and wish to make a request."' When the prince heard this, he was greatly delighted. He went out to welcome them, bowed before them as a son would before his father, and inquired of their journey: 'Where have you come from? Was the journey not exhausting? What do you seek, and why do you stand on one foot?' The eight priests said: 'We have heard that the prince delights in giving, refusing no one who asks. The prince's name is renowned in the eight directions, reaching up to heaven and down to the underworld. The merit of your generosity is immeasurable; far and near, all sing your praises and have heard of you. What people say of the prince is truly not false. You are now a son among gods and humans, and the words of one among gods and humans are never deceitful. If the prince can truly give without refusing anyone, we wish to ask for the white elephant that walks upon lotus flowers.' The prince then led them to the elephant stable and told them to take an elephant. The eight priests said: 'We specifically wish to have the white elephant that walks upon lotus flowers, the one named Sudānta.' The prince said: 'This great white elephant is my royal father's most cherished possession. The king regards the white elephant as he regards me; I cannot give it to you. If I were to give it, I would go against my royal father's wishes, and he might even banish me from the kingdom on account of this elephant.' But the prince then reflected: 'I previously made a vow that in all my giving I would refuse no one. If I do not give it now, I would violate my own resolve. If I do not give this elephant, how shall I ever attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?' He said: 'Very well! I joyfully give it to you.' He then ordered his attendants to saddle the elephant with its golden trappings and bring it out quickly. With his left hand the prince poured water over the priests' hands, and with his right hand he led the elephant and handed it to them. The eight men, having received the elephant, pronounced blessings over the prince. When the blessings were finished, they mounted the white elephant one after another and departed joyfully.

"The prince said to the priests: 'Go quickly. If the king learns of this, he may pursue you and take it back.' The eight priests then departed swiftly.

"When the ministers of the kingdom heard that the prince had given the white elephant to the enemy, they were all greatly alarmed. They fell from their seats in distress and said: 'The kingdom depends solely on this elephant to repel its enemies.' The ministers all went to address the king: 'The prince has given the kingdom's most important war-elephant to the enemy.' The king was shocked. The ministers further addressed the king: 'The reason your majesty rules the realm is because of this elephant. This elephant surpasses the strength of sixty elephants, and the prince has given it to our enemy. We fear we shall lose the kingdom. What shall be done? The prince gives away at will like this, and the treasury grows emptier by the day. We fear he will give away the entire kingdom, including his wife and children.' When the king heard these words, he became even more distressed.

"The king summoned a minister and asked: 'Has the prince truly given the white elephant to our enemy?' The minister answered: 'He has indeed given it.' When the king heard his minister's words, he was even more shocked. He fell from his seat and lost consciousness. They sprinkled cold water on him, and after a long while he revived. The twenty thousand consorts were likewise all distressed. The king deliberated with his ministers: 'What shall we do about the prince?' One minister said: 'He who set foot in the elephant stable should have his feet cut off; he who led the elephant by hand should have his hands cut off; he who looked upon the elephant should have his eyes gouged out.' Some said: 'His head should be cut off.' The ministers all deliberated and spoke in this manner. When the king heard these words, he was greatly distressed and said to the ministers: 'My son greatly loves the Way and delights in giving to people. How could I imprison or confine him?' Among them was one great minister who objected to the others' proposals. The king had only this one son and cherished him dearly. How could they wish to mutilate him and harbor such thoughts? The great minister addressed the king: 'I would not dare suggest that the great king imprison or confine the prince. Rather, banish him from the kingdom and send him into the wilderness and mountains for twelve years. That should make him feel shame.'

"The king then followed this great minister's advice. He dispatched a messenger to question the prince: 'Did you give the white elephant to our enemy?' The prince replied: 'I did indeed give it.' The king asked: 'Why did you give my white elephant to our enemy without telling me?' The prince replied: 'You previously gave your own command that in all my giving I should refuse no one. That is why I did not report to the king.' The king said: 'The previous arrangement was meant for precious treasures. What business had the white elephant in it?' The prince answered: 'These are all possessions belonging to the king. How can the elephant alone be excluded?' The king said to the prince: 'Leave the kingdom at once. You are banished to Mount Daṇḍaka for twelve years.' The prince replied: 'I dare not disobey the great king's command. I only ask to be allowed to give for seven days more, to fulfill my humble wish, and then I shall leave the kingdom.' The king said: 'It is precisely because of your excessive giving, emptying the national treasury and losing our war-elephant, that I am banishing you. You may not stay another seven days of giving. Leave quickly; I will not allow it.' The prince said: 'I dare not disobey the great king's command. I have my own private wealth. I wish to be allowed to give it away, and when it is gone, I shall leave. I shall not trouble the national treasury again.' The twenty thousand consorts all went before the king and petitioned him to allow the prince to give for seven days and then depart. The king consented.

"The prince then ordered his attendants to make a proclamation far and wide: any who wished for wealth should come to the palace gate and take whatever they desired. People's wealth cannot be kept forever; it will eventually be scattered and lost. People from all four directions came to the gate. The prince prepared food for them and distributed precious treasures as they wished, and they departed satisfied. After seven days the wealth was exhausted. The poor became rich, and all the people rejoiced.

"The prince said to his wife: 'Rise quickly and listen to me. The great king is now banishing me to Mount Daṇḍaka for twelve years.' When Māndhī heard the prince's words, she started up in alarm and said to the prince: 'What offense have you committed that the king should do this?' The prince answered: 'Because my giving was too excessive, emptying the national treasury, and because I gave the mighty white elephant to our enemy. The king and his ministers are angry about this and are banishing me.' Māndhī said: 'May the kingdom prosper, and may the great king, all the ministers, officials, and people, great and small, enjoy boundless wealth and happiness. We need only exert ourselves together in the mountains and diligently seek the Way.' The prince said: 'Life in the mountains is a fearful place; it is hard to bear. Tigers, wolves, and fierce beasts are terrifying. You are accustomed to luxury; how could you endure it? In the palace, your clothing is soft and fine, your quarters have curtains and canopies, your food and drink are sweet and delicious, and all you desire is provided. In the mountains, you would sleep on grass mats and eat wild fruits. How could you find joy in that? Moreover, there is much wind and rain, thunder, lightning, mist, and dew, enough to make one's hair stand on end. When cold, it is bitterly cold; when hot, it is scorching hot. Among the trees there is no shelter, and the ground has thorns, gravel, and venomous creatures. How could you endure it?' Māndhī said: 'What good would these fine silks, curtains, canopies, and delicious foods be to me if I were separated from the prince? I could never bear to be far from you. I shall certainly follow the prince. A king's banner is his standard, fire's banner is its smoke, and a woman's banner is her husband. I depend solely on the prince. The prince is my lord. When the prince was in the kingdom and gave to people from all directions, I always shared in it with you. Now that the prince is going far away, if someone comes to beg, how shall I respond? If I heard someone come to seek the prince and the prince were gone, I would certainly die of grief.' The prince said: 'I delight in giving and refuse no one. If someone asks me for our son or daughter, I cannot refuse. If you cannot obey my wishes, then you would disturb my resolve, and you should not come.' Māndhī said: 'I consent to follow the prince. Give without ceasing. In all the world, there has never been one who gives as the prince does.' The prince said: 'If you can do so, that is very good.'

"The prince, with his wife and two children, went to his mother to bid farewell. He addressed his mother: 'I beg you to counsel the great king often: govern the kingdom with right Dharma, and do not wrong the people.' When his mother heard the prince's farewell, she was seized with grief and wept. She said to those beside her: 'My body is like stone and my heart like steel, yet I have served the great king without ever committing an offense. Now I have only one son and he is leaving me. How can my heart not break and I not die? When my child was in my womb, like the leaves of a tree he grew day and night. I raised my son until he was grown, and now he is leaving me. All the other consorts will surely be pleased, for the king will no longer honor me. If heaven does not go against my wish, let my son swiftly return to the kingdom.' The prince, with his wife and two children, all bowed before his parents and departed.

"The twenty thousand consorts each gave the prince a strand of pearls. The four thousand ministers fashioned flowers of the seven precious substances and presented them to the prince. The prince exited through the north gate of the inner palace. He distributed all the jewels and pearl-flowers to the people of the four directions, and they were immediately exhausted. Officials and people, great and small, numbering tens of millions, all came to send off the prince. They whispered to one another: 'The prince is a good man; he is the spirit of the kingdom. How could his parents banish such a precious son?' All who watched felt pity for him. Outside the city, the prince sat beneath a tree and bade farewell to those who had come to see him off, saying they should return from here. The officials and people, great and small, wept as they went home.

"The prince, his wife, and two children rode together in a single chariot, which he drove himself. After traveling a great distance, they rested beneath a tree. A brāhmaṇa came and asked for the horses. The prince immediately unhitched the chariot and gave him the horses. He placed his two children in the chariot, his wife pushed from behind, and he himself stepped into the shafts and pulled the chariot forward on foot. They had gone a little further when another brāhmaṇa came and asked for the chariot. The prince immediately gave him the chariot. They went a little further and met yet another brāhmaṇa who asked for something. The prince said: 'It is not that I begrudge you anything; my possessions are all gone.' The brāhmaṇa said: 'If you have no possessions, give me the clothes on your body.' The prince immediately removed his precious garments and gave them to him, putting on a worn robe instead. They went further and met another brāhmaṇa who asked for something. The prince gave him his wife's garments. They went further still and met another brāhmaṇa. The prince gave him the clothing of both children. The prince had given away the chariot, horses, money, clothing, and garments until there was nothing left. Not once did he feel a moment's regret, not even as much as a hair's breadth. The prince carried his son on his back, and his wife carried their daughter, and they walked on. The prince, his wife, and their two children, with cheerful faces, joyfully followed one another into the mountains.

"Mount Daṇḍaka was more than six thousand leagues from the kingdom of Yepō. As they traveled further from the kingdom, passing through empty marshlands, they suffered greatly from hunger and thirst. The heavenly king Śakra of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven then conjured in the marshland a city with markets, streets, lanes, music, clothing, food, and drink. People from the city came out to welcome the prince, inviting him to stay and eat and enjoy himself. Māndhī said to the prince: 'The journey has been extremely tiring. May we rest here for a while?' The prince said: 'My royal father banished me to Mount Daṇḍaka. To linger here would violate my father's command; that would be unfilial.' They then left the city, and when they looked back, it had vanished. They traveled on until they reached Mount Daṇḍaka. At the foot of the mountain was a great body of water, deep and impossible to cross. Māndhī said to the prince: 'Let us stop here for now and wait until the water recedes before crossing.' The prince said: 'My royal father banished me to Mount Daṇḍaka. To stop here would violate my father's command; that would be unfilial.' The prince then entered the samādhi of loving-kindness, and a great mountain rose up within the water to dam and divert it. The prince and his wife then hitched up their garments and crossed. After crossing, the prince thought: 'If I simply leave, the water will flood and drown the people and all the insects and creatures.' The prince then turned back and said to the water: 'Flow again as before. But if anyone wishes to come to where I am, let them all be able to cross.' As soon as the prince spoke, the water resumed flowing as before.

"When they arrived at Mount Daṇḍaka, the prince saw the mountains steep and towering, the trees lush and dense, the many birds singing mournfully, the flowing springs, clear pools, fine water, and sweet fruits. Wild ducks, geese, egrets, kingfishers, and mandarin ducks were abundant in great variety. The prince said to his wife: 'Look at the trees in this mountain, reaching to the sky, unbroken and unharmed. We shall drink this fine spring water and eat these sweet fruits. There are also practitioners of the Way in this mountain.' When the prince entered the mountain, the birds and beasts all rejoiced greatly and came to welcome him.

"On the mountain was an ascetic named Accuta, five hundred years old, possessed of the most wondrous virtue. The prince bowed to him and stood to one side, saying: 'Here in the mountain, is there a good place with sweet fruits and spring water where we might stay?' Accuta said: 'This entire mountain is blessed ground; you may stay anywhere.' The ascetic then said: 'This mountain is a place of purity. How is it that you bring a wife and children if you wish to practice the Way?' Before the prince could answer, Māndhī asked the ascetic: 'How many years have you been practicing the Way here?' The ascetic answered: 'I have been in this mountain for four or five hundred years.' Māndhī said: 'If one reckons in terms of self and person, when will one ever attain the Way? Though you have been long in this mountain, you are no different from the trees. Only one who does not reckon in terms of self and person can attain the Way.' The ascetic said: 'Truly, I did not know this.'

"The prince then asked the ascetic: 'Have you perhaps heard of Prince Sudāna of the kingdom of Yepō?' The ascetic said: 'I have heard of him many times, but I have never seen him.' The prince said: 'I am Prince Sudāna.' The ascetic asked: 'What do you seek?' The prince answered: 'I wish to seek the Mahāyāna path.' The ascetic said: 'The prince's merit is such that it will not be long before you attain the Mahāyāna path. When the prince attains the unsurpassed true path of awakening, I shall become your foremost disciple in supernatural powers.' The ascetic then pointed out where the prince should stay. The prince then followed the ascetic's example: he bound up his hair in a topknot, took spring water and wild fruits for food and drink, gathered firewood, and built a small grass hut. He also built one for Māndhī and one for the two children, three grass huts in all. The boy was named Jāli, seven years old; he wore grass garments and followed his father in and out. The girl was named Kṛṣṇājinā, six years old; she wore deerskin garments and followed her mother in and out. The birds and beasts of the mountain all rejoiced and gathered around the prince.

"After the prince had stayed but one night, the empty pools in the mountain all produced spring water, the withered trees all put forth flowers and leaves, all the venomous creatures and beasts were eliminated, those that preyed on one another all began to eat grass, the various fruit trees naturally flourished, and the many birds sang together in harmony. Māndhī took charge of gathering fruits to feed the prince and the children. The two children also would leave their parents and play by the water's edge with the birds and beasts, sometimes staying overnight. Once the boy Jāli was riding on a lion's back in play. The lion leaped, and Jāli fell to the ground, cutting his face so that blood flowed. A monkey then took tree leaves and wiped the blood from his face, then led him to the water's edge and washed it with water. The prince, sitting in the distance, also saw this and said: 'Even the birds and beasts have such a heart.'

"At that time, in the kingdom of Kuru, there was a poor brāhmaṇa who at age forty had finally taken a wife. His wife was exceedingly beautiful. The brāhmaṇa had twelve marks of ugliness: his body was black as lacquer, his face had three creases, his nose was flat and squat, his two eyes were blue-green, his face was wrinkled, his lips drooped, his speech stammered, his belly was large and his hips protruding, his legs were twisted, and his head was bald. He looked like a ghost. His wife detested the sight of him and cursed him to die. When his wife went to draw water, she encountered young men who mocked her husband's appearance and teased her, asking: 'You are so extraordinarily beautiful. How can you be this man's wife?' The wife said to the young men: 'This old man whose head is white as frost on a tree, morning and night I wish for him to die. But what can I do if he refuses to die?' The wife then carried the water home, weeping, and said to her husband: 'When I went to draw water just now, the young men all mocked me. You must get me servants. If I had servants, I would no longer have to go draw water myself, and people would no longer laugh at me.' The husband said: 'I am extremely poor. Where could I get servants?' The wife said: 'If you will not get me servants, I shall leave and no longer live with you.' The wife continued: 'I have always heard that Prince Sudāna was banished to Mount Daṇḍaka because of his excessive giving. He has a son and a daughter. You can go and ask for them.' The husband said: 'Mount Daṇḍaka is more than six thousand leagues from here. I have never traveled in the mountains. Where would I even begin to search?' The wife said: 'If you will not get me servants, I shall cut my own throat and die.' The husband said: 'I would rather die myself than let you die.' He continued: 'If you want me to go, you must give me provisions.' The wife said: 'Just go; there are no provisions.' The brāhmaṇa prepared his own provisions and set off on the road.

"The brāhmaṇa went directly to the kingdom of Yepō and arrived outside the king's palace gate. He asked the gatekeeper: 'Where is Prince Sudāna now?' The gatekeeper then went in and reported to the king: 'Outside, a brāhmaṇa has come asking after the prince.' When the king heard that someone was asking for the prince, he was both moved and angry, saying: 'It is because of people like this that my prince was banished. And now another one has come?' The king then said by way of analogy: 'It is like a fire already blazing, to which one adds more fuel. My sorrow is like a blazing fire, and this person coming to ask about the prince is like adding fuel.' The brāhmaṇa said: 'I have come from far away. I have heard of the prince's name, which reaches up to heaven and down to the underworld. The prince gives without refusing anyone. That is why I have come from afar, hoping to receive something.' The king said: 'The prince lives alone in the deep mountains, in great poverty. What could he possibly give you?' The brāhmaṇa said: 'Though the prince may have nothing, I merely wish to see him.' The king then sent someone to show him the way.

"The brāhmaṇa then set out for Mount Daṇḍaka. When he reached the great water, he simply thought of the prince and was able to cross. The brāhmaṇa then entered the mountains. He encountered a hunter, whom he asked: 'You are in these mountains. Have you seen Prince Sudāna?' The hunter knew well that the prince had been banished to the mountain because of his giving to brāhmaṇas. The hunter then seized the brāhmaṇa, bound him to a tree, and beat him with a whip until his body was covered in wounds. He cursed him: 'I want to shoot your belly and eat your flesh. Why do you ask about the prince?' The brāhmaṇa thought: 'Am I to be killed by this man? I must devise a deception.' He said: 'You should question me first.' The hunter asked: 'What do you wish to say?' The brāhmaṇa said: 'The royal father misses his son and sent me to summon the prince back to the kingdom.' The hunter then immediately released him, apologizing: 'Truly, I did not know.' He then pointed out the way. The brāhmaṇa then arrived at the prince's dwelling.

"When the prince saw the brāhmaṇa approaching from a distance, he was greatly delighted. He went out to welcome him and bowed before him, inquiring of his journey: 'Where have you come from? Was the journey not exhausting? What do you seek?' The brāhmaṇa said: 'I have come from far away. My whole body aches, and I am terribly hungry and thirsty.' The prince then invited the brāhmaṇa in to sit, and brought out fruits and water and placed them before him. After the brāhmaṇa had drunk the water and eaten the fruits, he said to the prince: 'I am from the kingdom of Kuru. I have long heard that the prince delights in giving, and his name is famous in the ten directions. I am very poor and wish to ask the prince for something.' The prince said: 'It is not that I begrudge you anything. All I had has been given away; I have nothing to give you.' The brāhmaṇa said: 'If you have nothing, give me your two children to serve as my attendants and care for me in my old age.' He repeated this three times. The prince said: 'You have come from so far away, wishing to have my son and daughter. How can I refuse you?'

"At that time, the two children were out playing. The prince called them and said: 'This brāhmaṇa has come from far away to ask for you. I have already promised him. Go with him.' The two children ran under their father's arms. Through their tears they said: 'We have seen many brāhmaṇas, but never one like this. This is not a brāhmaṇa; he is a ghost! Now our mother has gone to gather fruits and has not yet returned, and our father is giving us to a ghost to eat. We shall certainly die. When our mother returns and cannot find us, she will be like a cow searching for her calf, weeping and wailing in sorrow.' The prince said: 'I have already promised him. How can I take it back? He is a brāhmaṇa, not a ghost. He will never eat you. Go with him.' The brāhmaṇa said: 'I wish to leave now. If their mother returns, I will no longer be able to leave. Give them to me with a good heart. If the mother comes, she will only ruin your good intention.' The prince answered: 'Since I was born, I have never had any regret about giving.'

"The prince then poured water over the brāhmaṇa's hands, and led the two children by hand and gave them to him. The earth quaked. The two children would not go. They returned to their father and knelt before him, saying: 'What sins did we commit in our past lives that we must now suffer this? Born of royal lineage, we are made into someone's slaves. We confess our faults to our father. Through this karmic connection, may our sins be extinguished and blessings arise, and in life after life may we never encounter this again.' The prince said to his children: 'In all the world, those bound by affection must part. All things are impermanent; what can be held forever? When I attain the unsurpassed and equal path of awakening, I shall save you then.' The two children said to their father: 'Give our mother our regards. We now part forever, regretting that we cannot say farewell face to face. This is our past karma that we must suffer. Think of our mother, who will grieve and toil in sorrow at losing us.' The brāhmaṇa said: 'I am old and frail. The little children will each run away to their mother. How can I manage them? You must bind them and hand them to me.' The prince then held his two children's hands behind their backs and let the brāhmaṇa bind them, tying them together and holding the end of the rope. The two children would not go. He beat them with a whip until blood flowed on the ground. When the prince saw this, tears fell to the earth, and the ground seethed. The prince and all the birds and beasts escorted the two children. When they could see them no more, they turned back. All the birds and beasts followed the prince back to the place where the children used to play, crying and wailing, rolling on the ground in grief.

"The brāhmaṇa took the two children away. Along the road, the children wound their rope around trees and refused to go, hoping their mother would come. The brāhmaṇa beat them with a whip. The two children said: 'Do not hit us anymore. We will go.' They raised their faces to the sky and cried: 'Mountain spirits, tree spirits, have pity on us. We are now being taken far away to be slaves. We could not say farewell to our mother. Tell our mother to leave the fruits and come quickly to see us.' In the mountain, their mother felt her left foot itch, her right eye twitch, and milk flowed from both breasts. She thought: 'This has never happened before. What do I need these fruits for? I should go back and see my children. Something must have happened.' She then abandoned the fruits and returned.

"At that time, the heavenly king Śakra of the Second Heaven knew that the prince had given away his children. Fearing that his wife would undermine his resolve, Śakra transformed himself into a lion and crouched in the middle of the path. Māndhī said to the lion: 'You are the king of beasts, and I am also a king's child. We share this mountain. Please move aside a little so I may pass. I have two small children who have had nothing to eat since morning and are only waiting for me.' The lion, knowing the brāhmaṇa had gone far away, then rose and moved aside, letting her pass. When she returned and saw the prince sitting alone, the children were nowhere to be seen. She went to her grass hut and searched but did not find them. She went to the children's hut and looked but did not find them. She went to the water's edge where the children always played, but still did not find them. She only saw the birds and beasts they had played with: the deer, the lion, and the monkeys, all throwing themselves on the ground before Māndhī, crying out. The pool where they had played had run dry. Māndhī then returned to the prince and asked: 'Where are the two children?' The prince did not answer. Māndhī said again: 'When the children used to see me returning from a distance with fruits, they would run toward me, tumble to the ground and leap up again, calling out: "Mother has come home to see us!" When we sat together, they were always at my side, and if they saw dust on my body they would brush it off. Now I do not see the children, and they do not come to me. To whom have you given them? Not seeing them, my heart is broken. Tell me quickly where they are, or I shall go mad.' She repeated this three times. The prince did not answer. Māndhī grew even more distressed and said: 'Not seeing the two children is already unbearable, but the prince's silence makes me even more distraught.' The prince then said: 'A brāhmaṇa from the kingdom of Kuru came and asked me for the two children, and I gave them to him.' When she heard the prince's words, Māndhī collapsed on the ground like a great mountain crumbling, rolling and weeping without cease.

"The prince said: 'Stop now. Do you remember the vow we made in the past in the time of Dīpaṃkara Buddha? I was then a brāhmaṇa's son named Megha. You were a brāhmaṇa's daughter named Sudarā. You held seven lotus stems, and I held five hundred silver coins. I bought the flowers from you, wishing to scatter them before the Buddha. You entrusted two stems to me to offer to the Buddha, and made a wish, saying: "In future lives, may I always be your wife, whether in fortune or misfortune, never to be parted." At that time I made an agreement with you: "If you wish to be my wife, you must follow my wishes. In all my giving, I will refuse no one, except that I will not give away my parents. All other giving must be according to my wishes." At that time you agreed. Now I have given away our children, and yet you disturb my resolve?' When Māndhī heard the prince's words, her mind opened and she understood. She recognized her past life and consented to follow the prince's giving, so that he might swiftly attain what his heart desired.

"The heavenly king Śakra, seeing the prince give in this manner, descended to test the prince and learn what he sought. He transformed himself into a brāhmaṇa who also had twelve marks of ugliness. He came before the prince and said: 'I have always heard that the prince delights in giving, refusing no one who asks. That is why I have come. I wish to ask for your wife.' The prince said: 'Very well! My wife can indeed be given.' Māndhī said: 'If you give me away, who will attend to the prince?' The prince said: 'If I do not give you, how shall I ever attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?' The prince poured water over the brāhmaṇa's hands and led his wife to him and gave her. When Śakra saw that the prince had no regret whatsoever, all the gods praised his goodness, and heaven and earth shook mightily. The brāhmaṇa then took the wife and departed, but after walking seven steps, he brought her back and entrusted her to the prince: 'Do not give her away again.' The prince said: 'Why will you not take her? Is there something wrong with her? Among all people's wives, this wife is the best. She is the daughter of a king; her father had only this one daughter. For my sake, she has thrown herself into boiling water and fire, eaten coarse and vile food without ever avoiding it. In all that she does, she is diligent, and her appearance is beautiful. Take her now, and my heart will be glad.'

"The brāhmaṇa said to the prince: 'I am not a brāhmaṇa. I am the heavenly king Śakra, come to test you. What do you wish for?' He then resumed his true form, noble and wonderfully beautiful. Māndhī bowed to him and asked for three wishes: 'First, let the brāhmaṇa bring my two children back and sell them in our own kingdom. Second, let my two children not suffer hunger or thirst. Third, let me and the prince return to the kingdom soon.' The heavenly king Śakra said: 'It shall be as you wish.' The prince said: 'I wish that all sentient beings may be liberated and freed from the suffering of birth, old age, sickness, and death.' The heavenly king Śakra said: 'How great this wish is, lofty and unsurpassed! If you wished to be reborn in heaven as king of the sun or moon, or as sovereign of the world, or to have your lifespan extended, I could grant you all of that. But supreme among the three realms is beyond my power.' The prince said: 'For now, I wish to attain great wealth and to always delight in giving, even more than before. And I wish that my royal father and all his ministers may wish to see me.' The heavenly king Śakra said: 'It shall certainly be as you wish.' In an instant he vanished.

"The brāhmaṇa of Kuru took the children home. His wife came out and cursed him: 'How dare you show your face here! These children are of royal blood! You have no compassion, beating them so their bodies are covered in sores and pus and blood. Go sell them quickly and get proper servants.' The husband obeyed his wife and went to sell them. The heavenly king Śakra took charge of disrupting the marketplace, declaring: 'These children are too precious; no one can buy them.' When the children were hungry and thirsty, heaven sent a natural energy to fill and satisfy them. The heavenly king Śakra then altered the brāhmaṇa's course so that he went to the kingdom of Yepō. The ministers and people of the kingdom recognized the prince's children, the king's grandchildren, and everyone, great and small, grieved. The ministers asked: 'Where did you get these children?' The brāhmaṇa said: 'I begged for them myself. Why do you question me?' The ministers said: 'You have come into our kingdom; we have a right to question you.' The ministers and people wished to take the children from the brāhmaṇa by force. Among them was an elder who counseled against it: 'This is the result of the prince's resolve to give. To take them by force now would surely go against the prince's original intention. It would be better to report to the king. If the king knows, he will redeem them himself.' So they stopped.

"The ministers reported to the king: 'The great king's two grandchildren are being offered for sale by the brāhmaṇa.' The king was greatly alarmed and immediately summoned the brāhmaṇa, ordering him to bring the children into the palace. The king, the consorts, all the ministers, and the palace ladies, seeing the two children from afar, all choked with emotion. The king asked the brāhmaṇa: 'How did you come to have these children?' The brāhmaṇa answered: 'I begged for them from the prince.' The king called the two children, wanting to embrace them, but they both wept and would not go to him. The king asked the brāhmaṇa: 'How much do you want for the children?' Before the brāhmaṇa could reply, the boy said: 'A boy is worth one thousand silver coins and a hundred bulls; a girl is worth two thousand gold coins and two hundred cows.' The king said: 'A boy is what people treasure most. Why should a boy cost less and a girl cost more?' The boy said: 'The consorts and ladies of the rear palace have no kinship with the king. Some come from humble origins, some were merely maids. Whoever pleases the king gains honor and nobility, receiving precious garments and a hundred delicacies. The king had only one son, and he banished him to the deep mountains. Day after day, the king enjoys himself with the palace ladies, without a single thought for his son. Therefore it is clear that a boy is cheap and a girl is precious.' When the king heard these words, he was seized with grief and wept profusely, saying: 'I have wronged you. Why will you not let me hold you? Are you angry with me? Are you afraid of the brāhmaṇa?' The boy said: 'I dare not blame the great king, nor am I afraid of the brāhmaṇa. Once I was the king's grandson, but now I am a slave. How can a slave come to a king's embrace? Therefore I dare not.' When the king heard the child's words, his grief doubled. He paid the brāhmaṇa the price as the boy had stated. He then called the two children to embrace them, and they came to him.

"The king held his two grandchildren, caressed them, and asked: 'What does your father eat and drink in the mountains? What does he wear?' The two children answered: 'He eats wild fruits and vegetables, and wears rough cloth. The many birds delight and entertain, and he has no worried heart.' The king then sent the brāhmaṇa away. The boy said to the king: 'This brāhmaṇa is terribly hungry and thirsty. Please give him a meal.' The king said: 'Are you not angry with him? Why do you ask for food for him?' The boy said: 'My father loves the Way and no longer has any possessions to give, so he gave us. That makes the brāhmaṇa our master. I have not yet had the chance to serve him, in keeping with my father's intention. How can I now see him go hungry and thirsty and have no compassion? My father even gave his children to a brāhmaṇa. Would the great king begrudge a single meal?' The king then gave the brāhmaṇa a meal. When the brāhmaṇa had finished eating, he departed happily.

"The king dispatched a messenger to bring the prince back quickly. The messenger received his orders and went to meet the prince. He was blocked by the water and could not cross, but when he merely thought of the prince, he was immediately able to pass. He conveyed the king's command to the prince: 'Return to the kingdom at once. The king wishes to see the prince.' The prince answered: 'The king banished me to the mountain for a term of twelve years. There is still one year remaining. When the term is complete, I shall return on my own.' The messenger returned and reported this to the king. The king then wrote a letter and had it delivered to the prince: 'You are a wise man. You should be able to bear going and also bear coming. Why are you angry and will not return? I need you even for food and drink.' The messenger brought the letter. When the prince received it, he placed his forehead on the ground in reverence, circumambulated it seven times, and then opened and read it. The birds and beasts of the mountain, hearing that the prince was about to return, leaped and rolled and threw themselves on the ground, crying out. The springs ran dry. The beasts ceased to nurse. All the birds sang mournfully, because they were losing the prince. The prince then put on his garments and, together with his wife, set off for home.

"The rival enemy kingdom heard that the prince was about to return. They immediately dispatched envoys bearing the white elephant adorned with a golden saddle and bridle, a golden bowl filled with silver grain and a silver bowl filled with golden grain, and met the prince along the way to return the elephant. They apologized, expressing their remorse: 'When we asked for the white elephant before, it was out of foolishness. Because of us, the prince was banished far away. Now hearing of your return, we are filled with joy. We hereby return the white elephant to the prince, and also present this golden and silver grain. We beg you to accept them, to atone for our offense.' The prince answered: 'Suppose a person prepared a feast of a hundred flavors and offered it to someone. If that person ate it and then vomited it on the ground, could the food, even if once fragrant and pure, be eaten again? My giving is like something vomited; I will never take it back. Ride the elephant back quickly and thank your king. I have troubled you to come all this way with your kind regards.' The envoys then rode the elephant back and reported to their king. Because of this elephant, the rival enemy kingdom was transformed into one of benevolence. The king and all his people generated the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

"The royal father rode an elephant out to welcome the prince. The prince came before him, bowed his head in reverence, and returned to the kingdom with the king. All the people of the kingdom rejoiced. They scattered flowers, burned incense, hung silk banners and canopies, sprinkled the ground with fragrant water, and awaited the prince. When the prince entered the palace, he went straight to his mother, bowed his head in reverence, and inquired after her health. The king then entrusted the royal treasury to the prince, and the prince gave as he wished, even more generously than before. Giving without ceasing, he thus attained buddhahood."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Such was my practice of giving in past lives. Prince Sudāna was none other than myself. His father the king at that time is now my present father, Śuddhodana. His mother at that time is now Māyā. His wife at that time is now Gopī. The mountain ascetic Accuta at that time is now Mahāmaudgalyāyana. The heavenly king Śakra at that time is now Śāriputra. The hunter at that time is now Ānanda. The boy Jāli at that time is now my present son, Rāhula. The girl Kṛṣṇājinā at that time is now the arhat, the mother of Mālī. The brāhmaṇa who begged for the children at that time is now Devadatta. The brāhmaṇa's wife is now Ciñcamāṇavikā. Through hardship like this for innumerable kalpas, and through goodness also for innumerable kalpas: one should take this sūtra and expound it for all śramaṇas. The bodhisattva's practice of dāna-pāramitā, the perfection of giving, was like this."
