# 佛說大自在天子因地經
## Sūtra of the Causal Ground of the Deva-Son Maheśvara, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0594

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana, at the time for eating, donned his robes and took his bowl. With the power of vigor he employed great supernatural powers and emitted innumerable rays of light, golden in color, illuminating all of space. With his supernatural power he further transformed his body, making it large or small, one or many, like a blazing flame, and went to Mount Kunlun. The mountain peaks were white as snow. Within the mountain's summit, the ground was adorned with gold, silver, lapis lazuli, pearls, agate, coral, maṇi gems, and all manner of precious treasures. There were palaces magnificently adorned with every treasure. Among them was the palace of Maheśvara, two yojanas wide and five yojanas high, brilliant and radiant, constantly guarded by sixty great spirits. Hundreds of thousands of celestial maidens surrounded it on four sides, playing seven kinds of music. On four sides of the palace there were seven halls, each one krośa wide, adorned with the seven treasures. Further, on each of the four sides there were bathing pools, filled to the brim with the pure water of ambrosia. In those pools grew white lotus flowers and kumuda blossoms, white as the heavenly moon, with gold, silver, and maṇi gems adorning their surfaces. The deva-son Maheśvara and Queen Umā were seated together upon a lion throne. The host of devas constantly gathered around them with reverence and offerings. Then the devas suddenly heard the sound of string music, the tones exquisitely refined. Hearing it, they became attached, and their minds were confused.

At that time, the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana, though he heard the music, subdued his mind through the wholesome roots of fearlessness and was attached to nothing. Like Mount Sumeru rising from the great ocean, he abode unmoved. He practiced the wondrous practice of vigor and received the offerings of all the world. His physical form was dignified and beautiful, his deportment complete, and he held his bowl in his hands. When Queen Umā suddenly saw him, doubt arose in her mind. She said to the deva-son: "Who is this person? He wears the Dharma-robes, is dignified and serene, his sense-faculties tamed, his deportment complete, his circular radiance shining like the newly risen sun, as tranquil and unmoved as Mount Sumeru."

When the deva heard these words, he observed the Venerable One and said: "Queen! Do you recognize him?"

The Queen replied: "I have never seen him before, nor do I know him now."

Maheśvara said: "This is a great man of merit, a disciple of the Buddha who has abandoned desire. He can destroy all faults, teach and transform sentient beings, and bring them peace and happiness. He has come here to beg for food."

When Queen Umā heard these words, she said to the deva: "What is his teacher like? What form and appearance does he possess? What virtue, power, and unprecedented qualities does he have? I wish to hear about him."

Maheśvara said: "The teacher of this man, for three incalculable kalpas, sought great bodhi, broadly cultivated the power of merit, and in this world accomplished what is difficult to accomplish. He practiced the perfection of generosity, giving food and drink, clothing, bedding, medicines, gold, silver, precious treasures, servants, carriages, cities, villages, great treasure-stores, and the position of a king. He even gave brahmins his wife, sons, and daughters. In his heart there was not the slightest particle of miserly affliction, nor was there even the name of craving and attachment. Further, out of compassion for sentient beings, he gave his head, eyes, marrow, brain, nose, tongue, and bodily flesh, without the slightest particle of any thought of pain, nor was there any semblance of falsehood. He practiced true generosity, seeking the fruit of great power in bodhi, and perfected the perfection of generosity. Further, for three incalculable kalpas, in order to deal with various objects and situations, he practiced the perfection of morality, firmly keeping the precepts. Through the authority of his moral conduct he always received the offerings of devas and humans. Furthermore, because enemies and friends were unequal and favorable and unfavorable circumstances differed, he practiced the perfection of patience, subduing his mind with the noble eightfold path, joyfully enduring with patience, and making his mind equal. Furthermore, for the sake of sentient beings of the superior, middle, and inferior classes, out of compassion and the wish to benefit and bring happiness to them, he practiced the perfection of vigor, exerting himself in diligent cultivation day and night. Furthermore, fearing that his mind might become scattered and that false attachments might cause regression, he practiced the perfection of meditation, making his mind resolute, serene, and at ease. Furthermore, in order to generate wisdom, he read, recited, and analyzed the profound and subtle sūtras, practicing the perfection of wisdom. Thus for three incalculable kalpas he practiced the six perfections, seeking the wisdom of omniscience. Now he has attained the complete fruition of buddhahood, has conquered all Māra kings, has realized the truth of cessation, and his form and mind are subtle and wondrous. He is endowed with the thirty-two marks and the eighty minor excellences, adorned with golden color, his circular radiance like the sun. In the three realms there is none his equal. He has escaped the difficulty of saṃsāra and abides in the peace, happiness, and liberation."

When Maheśvara had finished speaking, Queen Umā was delighted to hear and now understood the great fruits obtained through generosity and the other practices. She further asked: "What actions did you yourself cultivate in former lives as causes? Please grant me joy and explain briefly."

Then Maheśvara said: "Listen carefully; I shall explain for you. Queen! In the past, innumerable koṭis of kalpas ago, in the presence of a Buddha named Mahāmuni, I practiced generosity, made offerings, cultivated merit, kept the precepts, and perfumed my mind with wisdom. Since that time I have obtained the reward of sovereignty. In eight successive births I have attained eight kinds of sovereignty. Life after life I have practiced generosity, morality, and austerities, seeking mastery and sovereignty, and the joy of liberation up to ultimate cessation. Peace and happiness are likewise. Therefore I have practiced generosity, contemplated with wisdom, and guarded my practice singlemindedly without interruption." Having said this, Maheśvara, within his palace, filled his golden bowl with food of a hundred supreme flavors, wondrously fragrant, and went before Maudgalyāyana, offering it wholeheartedly.

The Venerable Maudgalyāyana received the bowl of food and rose into the air, about to return. Maheśvara, who possessed the knowledge of past lives, said to Maudgalyāyana: "Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana! Stay a moment and listen carefully to my words. An extremely long time ago, a Buddha appeared. In a past life I recall, upon this great earth I was its lord, able to create and destroy the world, the teacher of the three realms, with no one my equal. Listen to these words, and go singlemindedly to Mount Gandhamādana."

Then Mahāmaudgalyāyana employed his supernatural powers and rose into the air, going to Mount Gandhamādana. On one face of that mountain there was a sacred pool called Heavenly Water. In that pool he bathed. Having bathed, he sat atop Mount Gandhamādana and opened his golden bowl. The food was exquisite, perfect in color and fragrance, and had transformed into celestial food. After Maudgalyāyana ate, a celestial maiden offered him pure water. Having received it, Maudgalyāyana purified all five parts of his body, then sat upon that mountain, focused his mind, and entered concentration to contemplate and reflect: "Maheśvara said, 'An extremely long time ago, a Buddha appeared.' Through a thousand lifetimes I cannot know this; through a hundred thousand lifetimes I also cannot know; through koṭis of hundreds of thousands of lifetimes I still cannot know." Arising from his meditation, he went to Mount Mahendra and entered concentration to contemplate, but still could not know. He then entered the cave of the great king of Mount Mahendra and entered concentration to contemplate, but still could not know. From there he went to seventy mountains to contemplate in meditation, but still could not know. He then went to thirty-two continents to sit in stillness and contemplate, but still could not know. Maudgalyāyana said: "The words born of the wisdom of that Maheśvara are something that lesser śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas also cannot know." He then singlemindedly addressed the Elder Śāriputra: "Venerable One! Listen carefully! I went to the summit of Mount Kunlun and saw Maheśvara. He said to me: 'An extremely long time ago, a Buddha appeared in the world.' I cannot fathom the meaning of these words. Venerable One, you possess great wisdom, profound insight, and subtle penetration. You know the number of raindrops that fall in three years and three months. You know the number of drops in the water of the four great oceans. You know the minds and conduct of all the people of the four great continents. With such great wisdom, O son of the Buddha, you should be able to understand the meaning of these words. I humbly beg you, with compassion, to briefly reveal it."

Then the Elder Śāriputra, having heard these words, entered meditation and through a hundred thousand koṭis of lifetimes contemplated this matter, yet could not know. At that time, the Elder Mahākāśyapa said to Śāriputra: "The dharma spoken by Maheśvara is extremely profound in meaning, subtle and difficult to fathom. Only if you obtain the Buddha's power can you understand it. If you try to comprehend it by your own power, it is like trying to measure Mount Sumeru with an inch of grass."

Then Mahākāśyapa reflected: "To observe events of the extremely distant past as though in the palm of one's hand, even in the span of a lightning flash or a kṣaṇa, can only be known by the Buddha's great wisdom."

Thereupon Mahākāśyapa raised his voice, pure and stainless, and said to Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "The personal practice of Maheśvara is something no human or deva can comprehend. Only the omniscience of the one without equal in the three realms can bear witness to it. Why? Because the Blessed Buddha, throughout hundreds of thousands of koṭis of Ganges-sand-grain worlds, knows the minds, conduct, and dispositions of all sentient beings of the past and present, whether of superior, middle, or inferior capacity, as clearly as seeing the palm of his hand. This is what is called omniscience."

Then the Elders Mahāmaudgalyāyana, Śāriputra, and Mahākāśyapa together went to the Buddha's place, circumambulated the World-Honored One, prostrated with their five limbs touching the ground, bowed singlemindedly with reverence, and sat to one side.

At that time, the World-Honored One, knowing their thoughts, glanced at them and smiled. Then Mahāmaudgalyāyana praised the Buddha's merits: "The Buddha's face is dignified and beautiful, like a golden lotus opening. His compassionate countenance is pleasing, his merit-marks deep and abundant. His teeth are even and close-set, his body supremely wondrous. He emits great radiance of blue, yellow, red, and white, illuminating the entire world; mountains, rivers, and hidden valleys are all made bright. Even if hundreds of thousands of koṭis of suns and moons were gathered together, their radiance could not compare. He proclaims the cool flavor of the Dharma, moistening all the three realms. All sentient beings receive his benefit." Having praised him thus, he bowed with reverence and said: "World-Honored One! Once, bearing my bowl, I went to the palace of Maheśvara to beg for food. At that time, Maheśvara, seeing me hold my bowl, gave food in accordance with the Dharma and said to me: 'An extremely long time ago, a Buddha appeared.' I cannot penetrate the meaning of these words. The Buddha possesses omniscience, knowing the differences in the minds and conduct of sentient beings in all the nayutas of koṭis of Ganges-sand-grain worlds as clearly as seeing the palm of his hand. His tongue is broad and long, covering his entire face. I humbly beg the Muni to resolve this doubt."

At that time, the World-Honored One, in his subtle and wondrous Brahmā voice, said to Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "Listen carefully and reflect well upon it! I shall now explain it for you in detail. In the past, eighty-four thousand kalpas ago, there was a Buddha, a Perfectly Awakened One, who appeared in the world, named Guṇasāgara. There was a great city also called Guṇasāgara. In it was a brahmin named Śānta, who had two sons: the first named Śaṅkha and the second named Ruci. They gradually grew up, intelligent and wise. Both were weary of birth and death. They bowed to their parents and said: 'We wish to enter the mountains and practice the Way.' Their parents refused: 'You may not go.' Again they said: 'We must go to cultivate the Way.' Having asked three times, they left their parents and entered a mountain valley, where they lived in a grass hermitage.

"Eleven steps outside the hermitage lived an old brahmin who practiced the ways of the ṛṣis. There was also another person who lived nearby and practiced on his own. On a festival day, the four gathered together and asked one another about their respective aspirations. Ruci asked the old brahmin: 'What do you practice, and what reward do you seek?' The old brahmin said: 'I practice in order to obtain the body of Brahmā and a thousand years in heaven.' He asked the nearby practitioner: 'What do you practice, and what reward do you seek?' That one replied: 'I seek to be lord of the three realms and a thousand years in heaven.' The great ṛṣi Śaṅkha then said: 'I seek a thousand years in heaven and the love and respect of the world.' The ṛṣi Ruci, who possessed great wisdom, said to them: 'What you three seek, once accomplished, yields only worldly rewards. What I now seek is for all beings in the world, whether of form or formless, whether with two feet, four feet, many feet, or no feet, of superior, middle, or inferior class, all those who cycle through saṃsāra, to be liberated.'

"Later, the Buddha descended from heaven with innumerable hundreds of thousands of koṭis of nayutas of Śakras, Brahmās, humans, devas, and an assembly of both ordinary and noble beings surrounding him. That Buddha was seated upon a golden lotus throne, wearing red robes, his face like a full moon, emitting auspicious light of blue, yellow, red, and white, descending to the great earth and illuminating all sentient beings, purifying their minds. The four practitioners came to the Buddha's place. The old brahmin offered the Buddha flowers of white auspicious grass, bowed, and circumambulated, making this vow: 'By this merit, may I become lord of the Brahmā world, with five beautiful faces, and may I fulfill the wishes of innumerable sentient beings.' The next practitioner offered the Buddha a piece of iron and anointed the Buddha's feet with fragrant oil, making this vow: 'By this merit, may I obtain the body of Nārāyaṇa and become lord and master of the three realms.' Next, the ṛṣi Śaṅkha lit three lamps and offered three needles with a wholehearted offering, making this vow: 'I offer these three lamps and three needles. May I possess three eyes and obtain a trident. In life after life may I always practice generosity and fulfill wishes, becoming lord and master of the world, attaining the eight kinds of sovereignty, and accomplishing desire and happiness with a sharp and clear mind.' Then Ruci, whose topknot was supremely fine, his hair long and reddish in color, untied his topknot and spread his hair upon the road, saying to the World-Honored One: 'May the Buddha compassionately step upon my hair as he passes. The Buddha's two feet are unprecedented in the three realms, with the thousand-spoked wheel, net-markings, and the marks of the banner, pennant, and vajra, their patterns like embroidered paintings, wondrously and beautifully adorned!' The Buddha stepped across, and Ruci made this vow: 'By this merit, may I become a Buddha and save all sentient beings cycling through saṃsāra.'

"After these four had each made their vows, the old brahmin, through his various wholesome deeds, obtained the body of the Brahmā King, possessed five faces, and became lord of the three realms. Because he had offered the auspicious flower, he holds a celestial whisk in his hand. The next practitioner, through his wholesome roots, obtained boundless merit and the body of Nārāyaṇa. Because he had offered iron, he holds in his hand a wheel-treasure called Sudarśana, which can defeat all asuras. The third, the ṛṣi Śaṅkha: because he offered three lamps, three eyes appeared upon his face; because he offered three needles, he obtained a trident; because he circumambulated the World-Honored One, he gained the love and respect of the world and the eight kinds of sovereignty, becoming its lord and master, able to create and destroy the world. The fourth, Ruci: because he spread his hair as an offering, through the power of his vow he shall abandon desire, transcend the three realms, accomplish omniscience, and be called Devatideva."

At that time the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke in verse:

> With the most supreme auspicious flower, he offered and circumambulated;
> He shall obtain the body of the Brahmā King, called Lord of Sahā,
> With five faces beautifully adorned, holding a celestial whisk in his hand.
> By offering iron he gained merit: the deva Nārāyaṇa,
> Holding in his hand the Sudarśana wheel, able to defeat the asuras.
> He lit those three lamps, and with three needles made his offering:
> Three eyes appeared upon his face; in his hand he holds a trident.
> The great ṛṣi Ruci spread his hair as an offering;
> By this wondrous and wholesome cause, he shall attain the fruit of buddhahood.
> Maheśvara recalls the past, innumerable kalpas ago;
> The causes of their practice then shall afterward lead to buddhahood.
> Appearing in the world, it is beyond what śrāvakas can know;
> Only the Buddha, the supreme awakened one, can discern and distinguish it.

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Maudgalyāyana: "The birth of that Maheśvara into this body is truly rare. He descended from the Brahmā heaven to the human world. At that time, in a charnel ground, there was a hungry female ghost named Māyā. He consorted with the ghost, and the ghost became pregnant. He was born from the ghost's womb, and his body came forth with three eyes upon his face and radiance upon his body. When his mother saw him, she was terrified and fled. But because of his merit, his radiance illuminated the charnel ground and all its ghosts. At that time the ghosts, seeing light like the sun, were suspicious and frightened, and asked: 'Who are you?' He replied: 'I am Maheśvara, called Self-Born.' When the ghosts heard this, they bowed with reverence and praised him: 'Of supreme power and vigor, with wondrous form and appearance.' Devas and Brahmā himself also came to gaze upon him. The hosts of devas and humans, seeing that Brahmā possessed five heads, one of which was ugly and fearsome, were alarmed and distressed. They said to Maheśvara: 'Can you cut off that head for us?' Maheśvara said to the host of devas: 'If I cut off that head, I shall incur the sin of killing Brahmā.' The host of devas said: 'If there is any sin, we shall all share in bearing it.' Then Maheśvara consented and said: 'Very well.' He immediately transformed himself into a great eagle and with his talons plucked off that head. For this reason, in Maheśvara's hand he holds the head of Brahmā, and all devas, humans, and brahmins everywhere know of it."

When the Buddha had spoken these words, the Elder Mahāmaudgalyāyana and the others listened singlemindedly, received the teaching, and were all filled with great joy.
