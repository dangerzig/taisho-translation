# 佛說自誓三昧經
## Sūtra on the Self-Vow Samādhi

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0622

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the realm of Magadha, in the great grove near a brahmin hermitage, at a luminous, auspicious vihāra. The place of practice where he abided was called "Radiant Display of Solitary Realization." He had just attained buddhahood, and his light and radiance were exceedingly brilliant. Upon a naturally jeweled lotus seat, he was together with a great assembly of 32,000 bhikṣus, all of them arhats whose outflows were already exhausted. Their minds held all in recollection, their faculties were fully gathered, they were sovereign over the three times, and their spiritual powers were unobstructed. Like great dragons, they had accomplished all that was to be done. Their holy wisdom was complete, and they fathomed the origins of all sentient beings: the worthy Śāriputra, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, and the others. The bodhisattvas were innumerable, all endowed with inconceivable skillful conduct. They traveled through the treasuries of all the buddhas, transcended all the works of Māra, and extended loving-kindness and the six perfections across buddha-lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. Responding to the times, they rescued and aided sentient beings, bringing them to peace. They had long since transcended the teachings of mere renown: Avalokiteśvara, Maitreya, and those of like eminence.

Thereupon the Tathāgata entered the divinely tranquil, transformative-realization samādhi, which pervaded the buddha-worlds of all the lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. Before the Buddha's seat, a lotus seat suddenly sprang up of its own accord from the ground. Its pure fragrance illuminated the ten directions. The lotus had a thousand petals, and on each petal stood a transformation-bodhisattva, attending in auspicious order, hovering in the empty sky. Each from their position prostrated fully, circumambulated seven times to the right, then stood reverently before the Buddha. Together they raised their voices in a great sound and exclaimed in wonder: "So it is, World-Honored One! We, in our own buddha-lands, saw an auspicious, miraculous tree of transformation. When this tree first appeared, its light illuminated the buddha-worlds as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. The tree produced a great sound, its voice pure and clear, mournful and dignified, compassionate and harmonious, penetrating all hearts. Those who heard it were filled with joy, perfectly endowed with equanimity, and gave rise to the practice of the Great Vehicle, the six boundless perfections, and the thirty-seven factors of the path, completing all the buddha's works."

At that time, each tathāgata in all the lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges dispatched bodhisattvas to proclaim the Way's teaching and to glorify the Great Vehicle, telling their bodhisattvas: "From this buddha-land, traversing lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, there is a buddha-land called Sahā. Its buddha is named Śākyamuni Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Truly Awakened, Perfectly Enlightened. Through the Dharma-Vinaya, through spiritual powers, and through spoken teaching he performs the buddha's works." Each tathāgata held a thousand-petaled lotus in hand and presented it to the bodhisattvas, saying: "Bear my name and convey boundless reverence. We rejoice that this noble one has perfected his practice and completed his vow, his realization of the Way fully accomplished. Having descended into the five turbidities to bear the heavy burden for all beings, surpassing all stages and leaping ahead to stand before Maitreya, extending loving-kindness through the six perfections to universally aid all living beings, his conduct is light and nimble, his Way-teaching surpasses the ordinary. We now present these flowers as a dharma-offering. May all beings universally assemble at the place of the Way." Those bodhisattvas, empowered by the spiritual authority of their buddhas, suddenly vanished from their respective lands. Entering into the profoundly settled, tranquil samādhi, the contemplative samādhi, in a mere instant they all arrived in this world of patient endurance. Each left their seat, stood solemnly with reverence, paid homage with their five limbs to the ground, circumambulated seven times to the right, and returned to their original positions. With spiritual power they hovered majestically, their deportment grave and solemn, their dharma-robes perfectly arranged. Together they raised a great voice and addressed the Buddha: "Our world-honored ones, the tathāgatas of our native lands, convey boundless reverence. They rejoice that your meritorious practice is accomplished, your vow fulfilled, and your realization of the Way complete. Having descended into the five turbidities to bear the heavy burden for all, surpassing all stages and leaping ahead to stand before Maitreya, extending loving-kindness through the six perfections to universally aid all living beings, your conduct is light and nimble, your Way-teaching surpasses the ordinary. They present these flowers as a dharma-offering. May all beings universally assemble at the place of the Way."

The Buddha said: "Noble ones! We rejoice that those tathāgatas tirelessly teach with their wisdom. Their samādhi flows freely, their dharma-bodies are empty and pure, their wisdom is bright and broadly equipped. This and that are equally one. We delight in praising what we have heard, and rejoice boundlessly." At that time, Śākyamuni Tathāgata received the flowers in his hands and smiled with delight. Light issued from his mouth, illuminating in all directions the immeasurable buddha-worlds as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. He then scattered these flowers equally throughout all the immeasurable buddha-lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and with his light penetrated and pervaded all those worlds. Each and every sentient being, bathed in the Buddha's compassionate light, attained the contemplation of wisdom and came to know their former lives. The light radiated in succession, illuminating even down to the hells and the three evil destinies, the eight difficulties, and the heavenly and human realms. All were bathed in the compassionate light and all attained liberation. Hundreds of thousands of sentient beings all became of one mind and universally aroused the aspiration for supreme, perfect enlightenment. The light then returned, circled the Buddha's body three times, and disappeared into the crown of his head.

At that time, all the buddhas in all the lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges became mutually visible. By the spiritual power of all the buddhas, sentient beings everywhere were enabled to see the buddhas. When the display of transformations was complete, all returned to its ordinary state as before.

Thereupon a bodhisattva in the assembly named Xiánrú rose before the Buddha and praised him in verse:

> "Wondrous is the Great Sage's transformation,
> Compassionate toward all kinds of beings.
> Through countless kalpas,
> He accumulated meritorious deeds.
> Each and every meritorious deed
> Comprises hundreds and thousands,
> A hundred blessings completing a single mark.
> We bow to the Honored One of the three times.
> Wondrous is the Great Sage's transformation,
> His compassionate wisdom without limit.
> The Way's teaching is pure and noble:
> The Śākya Teacher, Deva among Devas.
> His great wisdom is high and unsurpassed,
> His Dharma-vessel ferries all beings across.
> His holy wisdom is pure and immeasurable.
> We bow to the Unsurpassed Honored One.
> Wondrous is the Great Sage's transformation,
> His compassionate light nourishing lands as many as the sands of the Ganges.
> The darkness of ignorance is entirely removed,
> The confused are awakened, turbidity turns to clarity.
> His beneficent rain falls as the occasion requires,
> Skillfully guiding all beings.
> The Dharma-bridge delivers all.
> We bow to the Honored One of the three realms."

Thereupon the Holy Teacher addressed Xiánrú: "The buddhas' dharma-smile has three causes and conditions. What are the three? The wisdom of sarvajña (omniscience) is deep, far-reaching, subtle, and wondrous. It brightly comprehends the three times and fathoms the origins of all sentient beings. Those in the three vehicles, each according to their particular practice and their root of faith being complete, advance toward their respective goals. There are bodhisattvas who hold to great vows, who don the armor of great virtue, who bear the heavy burden for all beings, who sever all courses of rebirth and become a bridge for the world. They single-mindedly pursue the six perfections without abandoning any being. They abide in the Way with ever-increasing courage and heroic diligence: giving without attachment to marks, upholding precepts with patient endurance protecting their practice, settled in dhyāna without distraction, with wisdom and knowledge clear and bright, advancing toward non-retrogression. These, Xiánrú, are all seen by the Buddha-eye, and each one receives a prediction. Not only one buddha gives the prediction; the buddhas presently abiding in the ten directions all give the prediction. This is the first cause and condition.

"Furthermore, Xiánrú, there are bodhisattvas who are approaching non-retrogression, who have accumulated the roots of virtue and perfected holy wisdom, who have made offerings to immeasurable buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. Each one has received a prediction; each prediction is mutually clear. Their buddha-lands are purified. They equally nourish sentient beings, all sharing one practice. They invite the buddhas, the sages and great benevolent ones. Upon the four swift currents they become a great dharma-vessel. They exhaust the ocean of the six desires and dry up the twelve gates. They enter the five destinies and purify the five eyes. Their spirit is concentrated in profound stillness, dwelling in the Tuṣita Palace. They gather bodhisattvas, practitioners, and noble ones to purify the practices of the three realms and turn the wheel of non-retrogression. The buddhas presently abiding in the ten directions, among the fourfold assembly, all jointly praise such bodhisattvas, extolling their virtue and declaring that they will soon descend to become buddhas, and that sentient beings in the ten directions will universally attain liberation. This is the second cause and condition.

"Furthermore, Xiánrú, there are bodhisattvas in the Tuṣita Palace who, having completed their celestial lifespan, are about to descend. They enter the ultimate, broad-display samādhi. The Śuddhāvāsa devas survey the great trichiliocosm, looking for a vast and spacious kingdom where the beings are gentle and mild, where there are kṣatriyas, brahmins, elders, and householders, and in which city, among the billions of places, there are those with the Way and virtue who are pure, honest, benevolent, harmonious, and compassionate. A wheel-turning sage-king in the central realm of Jambudvīpa: they convene and decide the descent of the divine one. Devas attend and follow. He appears dwelling in the palace, attended by ladies-in-waiting and guarded by the court. He appears to study and exhaust worldly knowledge, and then contemplates the four signs of impermanence. The Śuddhāvāsa devas encourage him to go forth from home. He enters the mountains to practice intensely, sits beneath the Bodhi Tree, shaves his hair, takes the self-vow and assumes the form of a bhikṣu. He cultivates the Dharma of the former buddhas, taking the Dharma as his teacher, with the Śuddhāvāsa devas as witnesses. In a single night he attains the three knowledges, vanquishes Māra and his hosts, and completes all the works of a buddha. The auspicious, miraculous tree sends forth its presence throughout all the buddha-worlds as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. Each tathāgata, among the eight classes of beings in their respective lands, praises the merit of the bodhisattva seated beneath the tree. Thus each dispatches bodhisattvas from their own lands to present flowers, convey reverence, and praise the Great Vehicle. In this way, Xiánrú, the buddhas presently abiding in the ten directions all know of this together, rejoicing for sentient beings who universally assemble at the place of the Way. This is the third cause and condition.

"All the bodhisattvas who have come are persons connected to the Tathāgata's original causes and conditions. Because of this teaching of the Dharma, they shall all attain the patience of the unborn. Some advance to the stage of a true youth, some to the stage of completed birth, and some toward non-retrogression. Therefore the Tathāgata gives each one a clear and complete prediction. You should know, noble ones, that a buddha does not smile without reason."

When these words were spoken, seven billion nayutas of bodhisattvas attained the stage of a true youth. Six billion nayutas of bodhisattvas attained the stage of completed birth. Three billion nayutas of bodhisattvas attained non-retrogression. Hundreds of thousands of bhikṣus attained arhatship. Nine billion nayutas of people attained the first fruit of the path. The devas of the three realms all attained the light of the Dharma.

Thereupon the Holy Teacher spoke in verse:

> "The dharma-realm, all is empty;
> The form-body, pure and true.
> Dhāraṇī transcends the boundless;
> Samādhi has no cause.
> The buddha-realm is not empty;
> Wisdom-purity is not 'existent.'
> In compassion for the world he reveals a subtle smile.
> Noble ones, receive this swiftly."

Thereupon a bodhisattva in the assembly named Bright-Seeing, Light-Worthy rose from his seat, arranged his robes, bared his right shoulder, lowered his right knee, knelt with palms joined, and addressed the Holy Teacher: "I wish to ask a question. May the Holy Teacher, with immeasurable wisdom, illuminate what has not yet been heard."

The World-Honored One said: "Excellent! Ask as you wish. I shall now expound the essentials in full for you."

The bodhisattva Bright-Seeing, Light-Worthy said: "What constitutes the bodhisattva's going forth from home, perfecting the realization of the Way, and attaining omniscience?"

Thereupon the Holy Teacher said: "Noble one, listen well and keep it in mind, reflecting thoroughly on its essentials. When the buddhas go forth from home, there is necessarily a catalyst. They sit in tranquil meditation, profoundly still, penetrating the subtle. When one concentrates the mind on the Way, there is inevitably a response. Those who respond are the Śuddhāvāsa devas and the Brahmā kings. Śakra is then directed to manifest the four signs of impermanence: old age, illness, death, and the renunciant. Through this, he teaches the realization of renouncing the suffering of desire. With the mind set on the Way and purity, one should dwell in mountains and marshes, practicing dhyāna with intense focus. When this aspiration first arises, the Four Kings and those below provide whatever is needed. Proceeding to the Bodhi Tree, recollecting the Dharma of the former buddhas' going forth, taking the Dharma as one's teacher and the Brahmā devas as witnesses, with the root of faith firmly established within, one practices the six perfections cultivated in past lives, the four immeasurables, the four kinds of kindness, the four dhyānas, the five supernatural powers, and skillful means as the occasion requires, as well as the thirty-seven factors of the path, completing all the buddha's works. From the first arousing of aspiration, one has never abandoned any being. Suddenly one awakens of oneself. Śakra then descends and presents a razor. The bodhisattva, holding his hair with his left hand and the razor with his right, makes this mental vow: 'I sever habitual suffering and defilement. I plant the root of non-attachment. I sever the stream of non-retrogression. I open the source of nirvāṇa. From the first arousing of aspiration, I have always been able to leave the household life. This resolve is firm, and my mind never wearies or flags. Deep, non-retrogressing faith is fully present, the evidence of realization complete, and the mind's intention clear.' Before the razor touches his hair, it suddenly falls away of its own accord, and the uṣṇīṣa naturally appears, bright and conspicuous. The bodhisattva reflects: 'When the former buddhas went forth and shaved their hair, there should have been a kāṣāya robe.' Just as this thought arises, the Śuddhāvāsa devas immediately bring a robe of naturally produced kārpāsa cotton from the Rūpadhātu heaven and present it to the bodhisattva: 'May the noble one accept this dharma-robe.' The bodhisattva accepts it and puts it on. The robe fits perfectly, and his deportment is solemn. Thereupon in all the immeasurable worlds as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, all the buddhas become mutually visible. Each sends a kāṣāya to present to the bodhisattva, and the bodhisattva receives the kāṣāyas of all the buddhas. By the manifested spiritual power of all the buddhas, all the kāṣāyas merge into a single robe, called the Sarvajña Buddha-Crown Supreme Garment. This robe is now in the Brahmā heaven. The reason the bodhisattva sits upright for six years is to complete past karmic connections. After six years, in the last watch of the night, the evidence of the precepts manifests. What is this evidence of the precepts? Aspiring to tranquil, secluded mountain marshes and receiving the Dharma; with divine, true, and far-reaching aspiration, upholding the precepts and practicing the Way; not sparing one's life, abandoning the body; regarding all things equally and not seeking material gain; guarding emptiness and practicing stillness; constantly contemplating the pure Dharma; practicing the pure four immeasurables of loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and protection; proclaiming the four kinds of kindness: generosity, affection, benefiting others, and equal conduct, endowed with boundless compassion; illuminating and comprehending the four dhyānas without blemish or cunning thought; withdrawing from eye and form, abandoning ear and sound, stopping nose and scent, cutting off mouth and taste, going beyond tactile pleasure, stilling mental objects; extinguishing the six desires, abandoning habitual patterns. Form ceases; form, sound, and scent cease; taste and pleasant taste cease; pleasant sensation ceases; when sensation is purified, form arises; sound of the ear is severed; the nose-faculty is stilled; the power of the mouth is removed; pleasure settles into stable awareness. Form is not form, neither not-form, not the extinction of form, not form as form; abandon the great elements, cast off habitual patterns. Sound is not sound, neither not-sound, not sound as sound; relinquish that reception, stop this arising, without object or counterpart, returning to the fundamental severance. Scent is not scent, neither not-scent, not scent as scent; the spirit is empty and pure, purifying the spiritual power. Taste is not taste, neither not-taste, not taste as taste; tranquil and subtle, strengthening the faculties' power. Pleasure is not pleasure, neither not-pleasure, not pleasure as pleasure; the four great elements are purified, purified in their fundamental abiding. Mind, thought, and consciousness: these condition seven dharmas. The seven dharmas purified, purifying their direction. Mind is no-mind, not mind as mind; thought is no-thought, not thought as thought; consciousness is no-consciousness, not not-consciousness, not consciousness as consciousness; neither thought nor consciousness: all united as one. Knowing the one, one thereby eliminates the one. Not within the one does one stir. Emptying the ground of the fundamental, purifying the direction of the fundamental, abandoning name and fame, relinquishing all entrances, without lineage or kind, not following the group, far from punishment and law, without self or ego, without person or lifespan, abandoning the quest for life, without any notion of the three realms, without consciousness, without self, without ego, without person, without life, without thought, without name, without lineage, without transformation, without number, without action, without coming from anywhere, without going anywhere, without arising, without ceasing, without body, without transgression, without mouth, without speech, without mind, without thought, without worldly affairs, without calculation, without conditions, without abiding. There is also no precept, and also no upholding. Without the thought of completion, without destruction. This is called the prohibitive precepts, the inner and outer pure precepts, the Buddha's prohibitive precepts: without blemish, and also without one who holds precepts. Without anger, without resentment: settled, at peace, and pure, one achieves the Way that transcends the world.

"The bodhisattva, the noble one, first sitting beneath the tree, initially purifying the evidence of the precepts, abandoning desire as the root of suffering, relinquishing scattered thoughts: no arising-perception, no movement-perception, no subtle perception, no self-perception, no other-perception, no middle perception, no this-and-that perception, no inner-outer perception, no Way-perception, no worldly perception, no cessation-perception. When cessation is no-perception, when no-perception too is nothing, when there is no perception of no-perception, when all is exhausted without the perception of exhaustion. In this way, noble one, the bodhisattva seated beneath the tree establishes and purifies the fundamental 1,800 precepts. The moment this enumeration is complete, the vajra seat suddenly splits open and rises from the ground, the palace of the Sixth Heaven Māra is greatly shaken, and the devas of the three realms are unsettled from their positions. They all come down together to the Bodhi Tree to attend and serve. Throughout all the worlds as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, a great voice suddenly sounds. Within that great voice, it proclaims: 'The bodhisattva seated beneath the tree shall this night attain realization.' Sentient beings each hear and see this. In this way, noble one, this is how the bodhisattva fully realizes the evidence of the precepts and attains omniscience: the three knowledges and six supernatural powers, the thirty-seven factors of the path, the eighteen unshared dharmas, the ten powers and spiritual penetrations, the four fearlessnesses, all universally complete. The great trichiliocosm quakes in six ways. Meritorious virtue vanquishes Māra. Light illuminates all the worlds as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. Sentient beings, bathed in this compassionate light, all at once find peace and universally arouse the aspiration for supreme, perfect enlightenment."

When the Buddha spoke this, 800 bhikṣus attained arhatship, 30,000 devas and humans attained the dharma-eye, 3,000 laymen attained the fruit of non-returning, the noble one Xiánrú and others all attained the stage of completed birth, and the entire assembly universally aroused the aspiration for supreme, perfect enlightenment.

Thereupon the Holy Teacher addressed Xiánrú: "Once, at a certain time, I was traveling in the kingdom of Kauśāmbī. At that time, Māra's hosts suddenly brought their followers into the great assembly. They wore strange garments and mingled with the assembly, constantly stirring up disputes and quarrels. They slandered each other, forming factions on both sides. The ordinary bhikṣus, not well versed in the Way, became angry and irritated. Each left the group and scattered. Only the arhats, the true ones, went off to the mountains and forests. At that time, Xiánrú, the three months of the summer retreat had passed and the year's end had come. On the fourteenth night of the pravāraṇa ceremony, when the morning star appeared, I instructed only Ānanda to strike the ghaṇṭā and spread the grass mats. Only with Ānanda did I observe the pravāraṇa. At that time, the Śuddhāvāsa devas in the empty sky addressed the Buddha: 'World-Honored One! The bhikṣu assembly has scattered. Now the Buddha observes the year-end ceremony, yet how solitary he is!' The Buddha told the devas: 'When I first went forth from home, you served as my witness. When I went to the Bodhi Tree, you again served as witness. Now I observe the year-end, and you again serve as witness. Throughout Jambudvīpa, traversing the three thousand worlds, in the immeasurable worlds, the tathāgatas who attain omniscience: from the time of their attaining buddhahood until their entry into nirvāṇa, you serve as threefold witness, with clear evidence complete. Know this, devas: the tathāgatas, with their Way-wisdom and spiritual penetrations, walk alone through the three realms, yet still require witnesses. How much more do all ordinary practitioners of the Way need a teacher!'

"He further told the Śuddhāvāsa devas: 'In the final age, many people will aspire to purity and integrity. Their minds set on the Way will be steadfast and clear, taking no pleasure in worldly life, dwelling in seclusion in mountains and forests. If they truly have the firm resolve to leave the household life, yet have no teacher or elder they can follow as their model, they should follow the example of Mahākāśyapa's method of going forth. Having gone far from desire, the abandonment of desire serves as proof. Having gone far from worldly life, the abandonment of worldly life serves as proof. Having gone far from fame, the abandonment of fame serves as proof. Having gone far from bodily form and convention, the forgetting of form serves as proof. Having gone far from inner and outer seeking, the abandonment of seeking serves as proof. In this way, devas, Mahākāśyapa, by these five proofs, shaved his hair, put on the kāṣāya, and took the Dharma of the naturally pure as his teacher. He moved the buddhas of the ten directions to compassion and made his own declaration, reverently following the Dharma of the buddhas' going forth, invoking the Śuddhāvāsa devas as witnesses. With the three proofs clearly established, he thereby became a bhikṣu. He practiced the twelve dhūta ascetic practices with total firmness, each one without the perception of arising as proof. At that time, Kāśyapa, beneath a tree, accomplished the five supernatural powers. Later, upon meeting the Buddha, the sixth was also complete. In this way, devas, bhikṣus of the final age should reflect well on this Dharma. They should not be arrogant, seeking fame and denigrating the assembly in hopes of receiving offerings. Nor should they carelessly adopt this method, turning their backs on the assembly and refusing a teacher. Regarding a teacher and this method: it means that when there are truly no bhikṣu sangha members who can serve as teachers, one may practice this method. If there is a sangha, one should seek the sangha as witness. The Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha, these Three Jewels, are equal to the Tathāgata. Therefore the tathāgatas honor the bhikṣu sangha who are clear in the vinaya as foremost. Know this, devas: within the bhikṣu sangha there are certainly practitioners of all three vehicles.'" When these words were spoken, the Śuddhāvāsa devas and the eight classes of beings, hearing the sūtra, were filled with joy. They paid homage and departed.
