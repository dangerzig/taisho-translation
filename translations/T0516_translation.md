# 佛說勝軍王所問經
## Sūtra of King Prasenajit's Questions, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0516

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time, in the land of Kośala there was a great king named Prasenajit. This king was honored and noble, with great majestic virtue, wealth, and sovereignty. The territory of his realm was vast and extensive. He was revered by all people, and his merit and virtue were such that he had long had deep faith and esteem for the Buddha-Dharma. The great king then mounted his jeweled carriage, accompanied by his ministers, attendants, and innumerable hundreds of thousands of brahmins and elders, all surrounding him. Various kinds of music led the way as he departed the land of Śrāvastī and went to the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, to the dwelling of the World-Honored Buddha, to pay reverence, make offerings, and receive the right Dharma.

At that time, as lord of the people, the Buddha taught him to always govern with right Dharma and not to follow non-Dharma. Why? Great King, you should know: if a king and his ministers turn their backs on right Dharma and follow non-Dharma, in this present life they will be disparaged by the people, and when their bodies perish and their lives end, they will not be reborn in a favorable destiny. If a king and his ministers abandon non-Dharma and follow right Dharma, in this present life they will be praised by the people, and when their bodies perish and their lives end, they will be reborn in the heavenly realm and receive the reward of a favorable rebirth, rich in happiness, sovereignty, and the love and reverence of devas and humans.

"Great King! It is like a person in the world who gives birth to one child. The parents love and cherish the child like a precious jewel, devising many means to ensure the child is always happy. When the child grows up, he too shows filial devotion. A king's compassionate love should be likewise. All the people should be regarded as an only child. The king loves and cares for them as a parent, always employing four dharmas to guide and teach them. These are: generosity, kind words, beneficial actions, and cooperation in common endeavor. By always practicing these four dharmas, all the people will submit. When the king observes the people with a compassionate mind as if they were children, those same people will regard the king as a parent.

"Furthermore, it is like a person who in a dream sees various things: rivers, springs, and pools; gardens, groves, flowers, and fruits; streets and roads, all splendidly adorned; all clean, pleasant, and delightful. All such things, upon waking, are completely nonexistent. All worldly dharmas are likewise, just like a dream, utterly without substance. Great King, you are the lord of men and receive three kinds of happiness: the happiness of wealth, the happiness of desire, and the happiness of sovereignty. You rule a great kingdom with many possessions: elephants, horses, carriages, gold, silver, precious treasures, and the contents of storehouses, as well as queens, attendants, ministers, and servants in great numbers. Your wealth and glory blaze without equal. Yet though all these possessions are many, they are not supreme. Why? Because they are dharmas of inversion that labor the mind and increase afflictions.

"Great King, you should know: all of these are impermanent dharmas of extinction. They are neither firm nor lasting, like water-froth, without substance. Therefore, great king, you should truly understand such things. You should always be awakened to worldly dharmas, be free from afflictions, and cultivate the practice that transcends the world.

"Furthermore, worldly dharmas are like a great tree. When its root is watered, it produces branches and leaves. When the branches and leaves flourish, it blossoms. Not long after blossoming, it bears fruit. When the fruit is ripe, its color, fragrance, and flavor are wonderful, and everyone delights in it. Then the tree is suddenly consumed by a great fire blazing on all four sides, its red flames so brilliant they eclipse the sun and moon, a single great light above and below in every direction. That lovely tree is utterly gone; only the fire appears. But that fire does not last long; it is soon extinguished by a great rain, with clouds, thunder, and lightning flashing together. At that time, the fire is utterly gone; only the great rain pours without cease. Yet the rain too does not last long and also stops. Great King, you should know: all worldly dharmas, as described above, are likewise: destroyed in an instant, utterly without substance. Though the king's domain is vast and possessions are many, the truth of their instant destruction is the same. Therefore, great king, toward impermanent dharmas do not generate thoughts of permanence. Toward dharmas that will end, do not hold the thought that they will not end. Moment by moment, contemplate that impermanence is encroaching. Abandon worldly dharmas, free yourself from attachments, cultivate the practice that transcends the world, and increase your wholesome roots.

"Great King! It is also like four great mountains descending from the four directions out of the sky. Those mountains are tall, vast, and each one solid. They fall upon Jambudvīpa, and all the grasses, trees, and forests in the land are completely destroyed without remainder. Even the powerful cannot rescue anyone. Great King! In this world there are four great terrors that likewise come to oppress, and all beings have nowhere to flee. Even the powerful cannot rescue anyone. What are the four terrors? First, the terror of wrong conduct. Second, the terror of old age. Third, the terror of sickness. Fourth, the terror of death. Great King! When wrong conduct arises, it destroys right conduct. When the terror of old age comes, it destroys the appearance of youth. When the terror of sickness comes, it destroys the dharma of well-being. When the terror of death comes, it destroys and extinguishes the lifespan. Great King! It is also like a lion, the king of beasts. When it enters a herd and seizes one beast for food, that beast cannot escape. Once in the lion's belly, nothing remains. Great King! The great power of impermanence is likewise toward all beings.

"Great King! When people in the world approach the end of life, they are first afflicted by illness, as if struck by a poisoned arrow. Their strength becomes weak, their sinews, bones, and limbs are all in pain. Their skin and flesh dry up, their hands and feet tremble, filth flows from their body. The eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and other faculties can no longer produce awareness. Objects do not appear; only the unwholesome karmic visions they themselves created appear before them. They are filled with great terror, with no one to rely on. Who will rescue them? Their parents and family surround them to no avail. Renowned physicians and fine medicines cannot cure them. The finest food and drink cannot be eaten. Moment by moment, the terror of impermanence arises. The ingoing and outgoing breaths grow ever fainter. In such illness and terror, only then does the mind turn to wish for wholesome karma. In a faint voice they tell their parents: 'I am now in great terror. Terrible visions appear before me. My life is about to end. Parents, do something to benefit me! Give to the Buddha and the Sangha. May they protect me.' Having spoken these words, in an instant the life is severed. This life ends, and another begins. According to one's own karma, one receives the corresponding reward.

"Great King, you should know: among the beings of the world, whether virtuous or not, whether superior or inferior, the fruit arises from one's own cause, and nothing is lost. Those who have performed wholesome karma have a place to return to, a refuge to rely on. At the time of death, they are free from terror. This life ends, and they are reborn in another place with a favorable reward. Therefore, great king, you should now abandon worldly dharmas, free yourself from attachments, cultivate the practice that transcends the world, and enter the gate of wholesome dharmas. Moment by moment, contemplate impermanence. If you do this, then among wholesome dharmas this is called diligence.

"Furthermore, great king! It is like a person in the world who enters a great mass of fire and must use skillful means to extinguish it immediately. One who is in the midst of fever must rely on what is pure and cool to awaken. When suffering from hunger and thirst, one relies on food and drink to be rescued. When afflicted with illness, one relies on good medicine to be cured. In danger and difficulty, one is delivered by powerful good spiritual friends. When suffering from poverty and hardship, one is rescued by obtaining great wealth. When entering a battlefield, one must wear brave, resolute, and firm armor to win the battle. When in every situation one is without support, without protection, alone, and in distress, finding a trusted friend provides a refuge.

"Great King! The wholesome dharmas that transcend the world are likewise. In the world, they correspond to the food, medicine, and friends described above; they can serve as a refuge and as protection. Great King! If a person does not cultivate the wholesome dharmas that transcend the world, they have nothing to rely on. At the time of death, they generate terror on their own. Who will rescue them? Having given up this body, they suffer on their own. Who will save them? For this reason, I speak truthfully.

"Great King! Swiftly, toward worldly dharmas, abandon the view of permanence and generate the perception of impermanence. Abandon the view of solidity and generate the perception of destruction. Like water-froth, they are without substance. You should mindfully cultivate the wholesome dharmas that transcend the world. What you yourself have done, in turn encourage others to do. In this way, among wholesome dharmas this is called diligence.

"Great King! Observe your own body. There is not even a small amount of happiness to be found. Though you may possess various kinds of the finest and most exquisite food and drink for nourishment, never once suffering from hunger or thirst, this can only temporarily sustain the life-faculty. When the lifespan reward is exhausted, the body immediately disintegrates and returns to the dharma of impermanence. Great King! Further observe your own body. Though you possess various kinds of the finest jeweled garments, ornaments, and adornments, and many kinds of stored goods and treasures, lacking nothing, with the four kinds of troops, elephants, horses, chariots, and infantry in great numbers, without equal: when the lifespan reward is exhausted, all returns to impermanence.

"Furthermore, great king! It is like a person in the world with great wealth, who day after day bathes in pure water, anoints the body with fragrant oil, adorns it with the finest garments, with marvelous flower garlands, pearl necklaces, earrings, bracelets, and similar ornaments, and then sits upon a jeweled seat with wealth, sovereignty, and majestic authority, surrounded by attendants, with a hundred thousand kinds of exquisite music. In the marvelous jeweled chambers, sandalwood, aloeswood, and other fine incenses are burned everywhere. They arise and cease, continuing in cycles without end. All of this is produced because ignorance is the cause. From this arise craving and the rest. If ignorance is extinguished, craving and the rest do not arise. When craving and the rest are extinguished, right practice arises. Free from all faults, this is called the dharma that transcends the world.

"Furthermore, great king! Regarding all worldly objects of perception, whether gained or lost, whether certain or uncertain, whether desirable or undesirable: when craving arises and knows no satisfaction, this is a great loss. But if toward the holy path and the dharma that transcends the world one has delight, aspiration, and insatiable desire, this is right practice and is of great benefit."

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> Great King, you should now know:
> That dharma of death is supremely evil.
> It can sever a person's lifespan
> And destroy and break apart the aggregates.
> This is a great terror;
> The world does not find it lovable.
> When that dharma of death comes,
> It pervades all things everywhere.
> The sky and the great ocean,
> Deep caves and high mountains,
> The great earth and all directions:
> There is no place to flee.
> Only those with wisdom
> Abide securely in the true Dharma.
> They are firm and immovable;
> Nothing whatsoever can destroy them.
> While the lifespan has not yet ended,
> One should generate great diligence,
> Broadly cultivate the many causes of goodness,
> And diligently practice the holy life.
> By the power of wholesome roots,
> One arrives at the realm of nirvāṇa.
> Having arrived at the realm of nirvāṇa,
> One is able to leave the terror of death far behind.

At that time, King Prasenajit of Kośala, having heard the World-Honored Buddha teach the wondrous Dharma through various skillful means and similes, was overjoyed. He expressed his reverence and praise, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, and returned to the royal palace.

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, all the great bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha taught, were greatly overjoyed. They received it with faith and practiced accordingly.
