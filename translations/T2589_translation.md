# 瑩山清規
## The Pure Rules of Keizan

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2589

Composed by Keizan Jōkin (瑩山紹瑾), 1324.

---

## Fascicle One: Preface and Daily Affairs

### Preface to the Pure Rules of Reverend Keizan

An ancient saying goes: "The human mind is like iron; the laws of governance are like a furnace." Well spoken indeed! When iron passes through a red-hot furnace, it can be made into all manner of implements, and its uses are inexhaustible. If it does not undergo this process, it remains stubbornly dull, no different from broken tiles and pebbles. The human mind is just the same. When it is rectified by the laws of governance, there is no partiality or selfishness. Otherwise, one mostly falls into one-sided crookedness. A person of crooked bias and a piece of stubborn, dull iron are equally useless.

"The laws of governance" do not refer only to royal edicts. The three thousand forms of dignified conduct and the eighty thousand subtle practices, these are the laws of governance of the Buddha's gate. The rites and proprieties of the Great Hero and the standards and guidelines of Eihei, these are the laws of governance of the patriarchs' gate. The Dharma that rectifies the human mind, whether called king, Buddha, or patriarch, these are different names for the same substance. One cannot be separated from it for even a moment. What can be departed from is not the true Dharma.

This winter, I traveled north a thousand *li* and ascended to the Cryptomeria Forest of Kayō, where I paid respects to the Abbot. I was fortunate to be permitted to join the assembly for the rains retreat. The pure regulations of the hall accord inside and out with those of the ancient Buddhas and former patriarchs, and they are well-ordered and admirable to behold. These are truly the laws of governance of the Buddhas and patriarchs, to which nothing can be added. How fitting it is that the ocean-like assembly is able to regulate their minds and become great vessels!

The assembly's officers told me: "These present pure regulations are what our great master Keizan practiced at this mountain at both the Tōkoku and the other site. The text in two fascicles accords in every article with the middle way of the Buddhas and patriarchs, without the slightest deviation of excess or deficiency. The Old Man of the Abbot's quarters once obtained them from an ancient chest and put them into practice throughout the hall, reviving the monastic community. Several years have passed since then. We surreptitiously copied them and hastily had them printed. The characters had only been copied once, and errors abounded; 'crow' became 'horse.' The arrangement was disordered, with earlier and later sections transposed, occurring frequently throughout. By chance we obtained a good copy, and through punctuation, collation, and comparison, we roughly restored it to its proper form. However, the writing is plain in style and somewhat rough. If it is not polished, those who see it may regard it lightly and not trust it. We request that you undertake the work of Zichan of Dongli."

I said: "Although the writing may seem rough, the meaning is self-evidently clear. Unrefined gold and uncut jade; those with discernment know them to be precious. How could one recklessly apply effort and thereby damage the genuine? To ornament the writing would be to lose its virtue. If I must choose, I will follow the rough and unadorned."

The assembly's officers all said: "Excellent! We shall have it re-carved for circulation. We request that you compose a preface to distinguish the beginning of the fascicles."

Alas! With my meager talent of a small measure, how could I possibly be adequate to this need? Yet Huang Siniang entrusted herself to Du Fu's poetry and became immortal; something envied through all ages. This is why I forget my presumption, and all three seals upon my lips are broken open.

I humbly observe: In the past, when Keizan passed away, this text became hidden. When this text was hidden, the monastic community declined. Now the Reverend has appeared and this text has been revealed. When this text is revealed, the monastic community flourishes. The appearance and passing of persons, the concealment and manifestation of texts; these truly determine the flourishing and decline of the monastic community. By committing them to the printing blocks, the wisdom-life of the text will not perish, and the Dharma-body of the person will endure forever. Among all those with aspiration for the ancestral gate, who would not rejoice and leap with joy?

Now I compose this preface, and I am the first to rejoice accordingly. In chanting it and practicing it, I shall inform those of like aspiration. Those of like aspiration will rejoice accordingly and in turn inform their fellow aspirants. Fifty successive transmissions, is this not solely the province of the *Lotus Sūtra*? Indeed, is this not also a *Lotus Sūtra* of the Zen garden? Such a Dharma as this, listen closely, listen closely!

Written on an auspicious day of the twelfth month of the year *tsuchinoe-uma*, the sixth year of Enpō [1678], composed by Wanan of Hakumanji.

## Fascicle Two: Monthly Affairs

**Sequential Order of Affairs**

### Daily Affairs, Part One

**Hour of the Dragon (7–9 AM): Morning Gruel**

The method of eating gruel: After the opening bell, the great assembly dons kāṣāyas and waits for the fish drum. At this mountain, the cloud-board before the storehouse is struck thirty-six times on the platform. Facing the Sangha hall, the cook burns incense and makes nine prostrations. Afterward, the attendants reverently send out the pure gruel. The fish drum sounds three rounds. At the beginning of the third round, the postulants and others stand in rows outside the threshold of the hall's front door, face the Holy Monk, make a deep bow of greeting, and stand with hands folded. The head of the procession calls out: "How are you?" The assembly of postulants call out in unison: "How are you?" drawing out the voice. They then bow again and disperse.

After the fish drum finishes sounding, the great assembly is not permitted to enter the hall. The board sounds one round, and the great assembly lowers their bowls. The Holy Monk's attendant enters the hall with palms joined and serves gruel to the Holy Monk. At the head of the board, the Holy Monk's attendant makes another bow with palms joined, goes to the side of the mallet, removes the cloth wrapper, bows with palms joined, makes a bow facing the front, and exits the hall, then goes to the attendant's lower seat.

Before the kitchen, the cloud-drum sounds three rounds. At the third round, the small bell before the hall sounds, and the abbot enters the hall. The great assembly descends from their platforms, bows in greeting, and exchanges salutations. When the master does not come to the hall, the great assembly does not descend from their platforms. The attendant goes to the master's desk. The rector enters the hall and strikes the mallet as usual.

Upon hearing the bowl-spreading mallet, the food-calling postulants enter the hall. After bowing before the Holy Monk, the master, and the head seat, they pass behind the Holy Monk and arrive at the south-side head of the board to bow. After the head seat has performed the food-offering prayer, the food is called out as usual. After the universal mallet, the postulants enter the hall, bow facing the front with folded hands, and two or three persons chant in unison. Afterward, the release-from-attendance bell is struck three times. The master exits. The pure-persons and others bow. The attendant follows behind in exiting. The administrators and attendants wait for the head seat and others to exit, bow, and disperse.

When the head seat exits the hall, the pure-persons all bow together. If the Dharma hall ascent board is hung, there is no release from attendance. When a donor provides a meal and the person invited ascends the hall, then there is release from attendance.

If after gruel there is sūtra chanting at the main hall or various halls, after the three bell-strikes, the bell is struck again; one round or three rounds, as circumstances require. If it happens to be sūtra chanting for imperial blessings, even though the three bell-strikes are not made, the bell must be struck three rounds, and sūtra chanting is done at the main hall.

After gruel, the great assembly may go freely to the various halls to burn incense.

**Hour of the Serpent (9–11 AM): Morning Meditation**

After gruel, there is a brief interval. The rector hangs the meditation board at the front of the hall. At the beginning of the serpent hour, the hall administrators, kitchen administrators, and postulants first inform the head seat. The assembly of the quarters all enters the hall, faces the wall, and sits in meditation. After three strikes, the head seat is also informed, and the board before the head seat's quarters is struck three times. The head seat enters the hall, burns incense, and makes one circuit of the hall, inspecting whether all have entered the hall.

The striking of the board before the storehouse comes after the extended three strikes of the board before the quarters. The boards in the east corridor, the mountain gate, the west corridor, and before the various quarters are struck before the board before the assembly's quarters. The board before the abbot's quarters is struck after the board before the head seat's quarters.

The length of incense during meditation and its beginning and end are managed by the Holy Monk's attendant. Generally, meditation is under the supervision of the head seat.

When the serpent-hour incense is about to finish, the Holy Monk's attendant bows to the head seat, this is the report to release from meditation. Then the cloud-board before the storehouse is struck three times. This is called the "fire board", it signals the end of meditation. The master, the head seat, and the great assembly simultaneously join palms, put on their socks, descend from their platforms, and wait for the meal time.

When the fire board sounds, [the attendant] proceeds from below the storehouse, passes before the various quarters, circles the monastery, and rings the fire bell.

**Hour of the Horse (11 AM–1 PM): Meal Time**

After the fire bell has circled the monastery, the drum before the storehouse is struck three times, announcing the meal time. Next, the great bell is struck eighteen times; this is called the "meal bell." During this interval, at the main hall, the Uṣṇīṣa-vijaya-dhāraṇī is recited seven times. This is called "midday."

After the chanting is finished, the great assembly enters the hall. The board before the storehouse is struck in extended fashion thirty-six times. The platform before the storehouse prepares the offering items. Facing the Sangha hall, the cook burns incense and makes nine prostrations. During the prostrations, the various attendants stand in two rows. After the prostrations, the offering items are reverently sent to the front of the Sangha hall. The offerings for the Buddha and the Holy Monk are offered together to all the honored ones. The hall custodian makes the Buddha offering, offers fragrant incense and worship, and makes three prostrations each. Before the earth deity, only incense is burned.

During the thirty-six board-strikes and the fish drum, the assembly both inside and outside the hall gathers outside the hall. The assembly outside stands in two groups, each facing the Holy Monk, bows, and takes their seats. At the beginning of the third round of the fish drum, the head seat enters the hall, bows to the Holy Monk, and takes his seat, entering from the south side of the front door.

After the fish drum, the cloud-board is struck in one extended round. The great assembly lowers their bowls. Next, the cloud-drum before the storehouse sounds three rounds. At the third round, the small bell before the hall sounds together, announcing that the master is entering the hall. The entire assembly, both inside and outside the hall, simultaneously descends from their platforms, exchanges salutations with the master, and bows. The master bows facing the front, bows toward his chair, turns in order, and exchanges salutations with the head seat. He bows and takes his chair. The head seat exchanges salutations and bows with the great assembly; all following the customary pattern. From here onward, the procedure follows that of the gruel time.

Generally, after gruel and after the meal, the assembly in the quarters returns to the quarters, bows, and drinks tea.

**Hour of the Goat (1–3 PM)**

If the board before the abbot's quarters sounds three rounds, it is known that there will be Dharma benefit. People may come for informal instruction as they wish, or the whole assembly may come for instruction. Whether wearing informal robes or formal robes is at each person's discretion. The informal greeting "How are you?" is offered. With empty mind, one listens to the Dharma; the various sūtras and records. When the assembly gathers as occasions require, each person bows. When dispersing, all bow together. If there is no urgent matter, one should not casually leave one's seat. Idle chatter is not permitted.

**Hour of the Monkey (3–5 PM): Afternoon**

From half of the monkey-hour incense to half of the rooster-hour incense, the boards before the various quarters are struck three times. The great assembly puts on informal robes and socks, enters the hall, faces the wall, and sits in meditation. At this time, the sitting cushions are set out and will remain until after the meal the next day when they are put away. This is the standing procedure.

**Hour of the Rooster (5–7 PM)**

When the time for release from attendance arrives, the Holy Monk's attendant bows and reports to the head seat. The head seat comes out of his quarters. Either before the head seat's quarters or before the assembly's quarters, the board is struck three times. The assembly outside the hall and those sitting in meditation put on their socks, don their kāṣāyas, come out, fold their bedding, and sit on the edge of their platforms.

A postulant goes up to the abbot's quarters and reports: "The release-from-attendance board is to be hung." The master says: "Release from attendance." The postulant bows, comes down, and hangs the release-from-attendance board before the hall, and puts up the warm curtains and so forth.

The head seat enters the hall, burns incense, and makes a circuit of the hall, then likewise sits on the edge of his platform. A postulant enters the hall and bows facing the front. He then bows to the head seat, stands with folded hands, and says: "The master releases from attendance." The head seat silently assents. The postulant bows, passes behind the shrine, arrives at the front, makes a deep bow, stands with hands folded, and calls out in a drawn-out voice: "Release from attendance!" He bows again, exits to the front of the hall, and strikes the small bell three times. This is called "release from attendance."

The great assembly simultaneously descends from their platforms, exchanges salutations, bows, and says: "Take care." After gruel, the bow at the assembly quarters is followed by: "The master releases from attendance."

Next, sūtra chanting at the main hall. The bell is struck one round or three rounds, according to the master's wishes. Either the Śūraṅgama dhāraṇī, throughout the year, or the Great Compassion dhāraṇī and the Disaster-Eliminating dhāraṇī; during the summer retreat.

Afterward, one returns to the quarters and bows. Before and after the release from attendance, when visiting the various quarters, one must always don the kāṣāya. This is the standing procedure. Whether for requesting leave, paying a visit, consulting about meditation, or reporting on matters, when going to the abbot's quarters or the quarters of the head seat and others, regardless of the time, one must don the kāṣāya.

**Hour of the Dog (7–9 PM)**

When the ant's waist [of the evening sky] is still visible, the board before the assembly's quarters is struck three times. The assembly dons kāṣāyas, enters the hall, goes to their respective bedding positions, faces the wall, and sits in meditation; as in the morning.

Upon hearing the board strike, the quarters master arranges the assembly's walking practice and waits for the next morning.

The bell is struck, and the following is chanted: "May the three paths and the eight difficulties cease their suffering and end their bitterness. May all in the ten-direction worlds, hearing the bell, awaken to the Way."

Then the bell is struck: eighteen slow strikes and eighteen fast strikes make one round; again eighteen slow and eighteen fast make two rounds. During the first round, the great assembly enters the hall. At the end of the second round, the head seat enters the hall, burns incense, and makes a circuit. The third round is as before. At the end of the third round, the master enters the hall, burns incense, and makes a circuit as in the morning.

After the three rounds are finished, the drum before the storehouse is struck three rounds. First, the drumstick is poised at the drum's lip three times. Then with the left stick a small strike, and with the right stick a large strike; three slow strikes. Then with the left stick a small strike, and with the right stick continuous strikes. After three such cycles, from slow to fast constitutes one round. As before, when three rounds are finished, the stick is poised at the drum's lip three times with one strike. This is called "one watch." Striking the board constitutes "one point." The first watch always has three strikes.

Next, around the middle of the dog hour, when it is about to pass, first strike the first watch, three points, three times. Afterward, the great bell is struck eighteen times; this is called the "settling bell." At this time, the master exits the hall. The head seat faces the wall, folds his kāṣāya, wraps it in his quilt, places his cloth on the function box, and dons his quilt and other items as he wishes. The Holy Monk's attendant lowers the lanterns before the Holy Monk and others. The great assembly exits the hall. Sitting in meditation is at one's discretion.

At the end of the dog hour: first watch, four points; first watch, five points; these are struck. At the beginning of the boar hour, the second watch, one point, is struck as before. After the second watch, two points, the fire bell is rung with the call "Watch the fire and candles!" circling the monastery from the main gate to the abbot's quarters, returning below the storehouse. Then the second watch, three points, is struck.

The head seat rests his head on his pillow and sleeps. The great assembly sleeps at the same time. Remaining seated [past this time] is impolite. One must not go to improper places at will. One should sleep inside the hall. This is approximately the middle of the boar hour.

One lies down at approximately the middle of the boar hour and rises at the middle of the tiger hour.

The procedure for sleeping: The entire assembly must lie on the right side. Do not lie on the left side, face up, or face down. Either make an external fist with both hands and place them under the chin with both legs slightly bent to sleep; or slightly bend the right hand and place it under the chin, straighten the left hand and place it on the left thigh; or place the left foot's big toe on the right inner ankle to sleep. Both right and left sleeves should be straight and extended while sleeping.

**Hours of the Rat and Ox (11 PM–3 AM): Sleeping Time**

The master may make a midnight circuit of the hall, inspecting the presence or absence of the assembly.

**Hour of the Tiger (3–5 AM), Half-Hour**

The drum before the storehouse strikes the fourth watch, three points. At this time, the fire bell is rung, circling the monastery. The boards before the various quarters are struck; fourth watch, with three points on the clappers. The board before the head seat's quarters sounds. The great assembly rises simultaneously and first sits. After the head seat has made his circuit of the hall, each person gets up and goes to the latrine, chews willow twigs, and washes their face.

The head seat rises first, folds his pillow, and strikes it [as a signal]. The great assembly, hearing this, rises and sits in meditation simultaneously. The head seat first washes his face, dons his kāṣāya, and makes a circuit of the hall, inspecting whether the great assembly has risen or not. This is between the fourth watch, four points, and five points.

The great assembly sits and waits for the master to make his circuit of the hall. The fifth watch, one point, sounds. The master enters the hall, burns incense, and makes a circuit, admonishing the great assembly against dozing while sitting. After the fifth watch, three points, the great bell is struck as at the evening bell.

After the late-night bell is finished, after the fifth watch, four points before the storehouse, the board before the storehouse sounds three rounds. This is called the "small awakening." The attendants all rise at once.

**End of the Hare Hour (5–7 AM)**

After the fifth watch, five points, the drum and board are struck with extended beating, three rounds. This is called the "great awakening." The great assembly folds their quilts, folds their bedding, wraps their quilts, places their cloths on the quilts, covers the quilts, puts on socks, and reverently places the kāṣāya on their heads. With palms joined, they recite the verse:

> Great indeed is the robe of liberation,
> The formless field of merit garment.
> Wearing it, I uphold the Tathāgata's teaching
> And broadly save all sentient beings.

After reciting this three times, they don the kāṣāya and sit on the edge of their platforms, waiting for gruel time. Going to the latrine to wash one's face and going to the privy to relieve oneself; each should wait for a gap among the great assembly and not compete. This is the standing procedure.

The conduct during the twelve hours is as detailed in the Rules for Pursuing the Way, the Rules for Attending Gruel and Meals, the Rules for Washing the Face, the Rules for Washing and Purifying, as well as the Quarters Regulations and the Rules for Attending the Teacher on the Great Matter of the Self, and so on. All should be thoroughly familiar with them.

*End of Daily Affairs*

### Monthly Affairs, Part Two

**First Day of the First Month**

At gruel time, the rector strikes the mallet and says: "Homage to the Bhagavān, the perfect Sūtras, and the great holy Bodhisattva Sangha; their merit is beyond thought." He also says: "This morning, [we pray for] boundless [blessings]. Relying reverently on the honored assembly, we recite the Ten Buddha Names as usual."

After gruel, the bell is struck three rounds, and sūtra chanting is performed at the main hall. A tablet for "the reigning Emperor" may be set up at the main position in front. The master burns incense before the three honored ones at the Buddha [altar] and returns to his position. Facing forward, the rector chants. The Mahā-dhāraṇī of Perfect Fulfillment, one recitation; the Disaster-Eliminating Dhāraṇī, three recitations; the great assembly joins palms and recites, not wearing head coverings. From the beginning of the Disaster-Eliminating Dhāraṇī to the beginning of the dedication, the master burns incense. Before the three honored Buddhas, all is solemnly adorned. The great assembly is reverent and awe-struck. This is the constant ritual procedure and must not be treated carelessly.

Next, the junior disciples pay respects to the master. After gruel and the sūtra chanting for imperial blessings, the attendant and junior disciples go up to the sleeping hall, invite the master out, and have him sit in his chair. The head attendant alone burns incense and offers congratulations, a full prostration of nine bows. Next, the training disciples or Dharma-relatives offer congratulations. If there are return bows, or if disciples who have entered the master's room burn incense and make nine bows, if they are disciples who have gone out to teach, they do not bow in a group. Next, novices, postulants, or the nuns' assembly offers congratulations. Then lay disciples offer congratulations.

Next is the Dharma hall ascent. With reverent blessings, congratulations and thanks are offered properly. Prohibited words are not spoken. Faults within the Sangha are not aired. Everything is solemnly proper, and all are awestruck. Hands are not tucked in sleeves with folded arms. Head coverings are not worn. The hall circuit must be made. The master has the attendant serve hot water. If eminent head seats or venerable elders are in the hall, the attendant brings the master's sitting cloth for him to perform the ceremony. This is the solemn protocol.

The ancient protocol: As on the first and fifteenth Dharma-seat ascents, the imperial blessing must not be omitted. Therefore, if the master is ill or traveling, the head seat substitutes in offering incense and ascending the seat. When the head seat ascends the seat, the abbot's attendant offers incense and burns it as for the master. Only the rector invites him. Since he acts as the master's substitute, it is like the head seat's four seasonal Dharma whisk ceremonies; toward both groups of the great assembly, he does not bow. However, if it would not be respectful, the head seat does not substitute. When a head seat appointed as head of the Sangha, or a retired abbot serving as head seat, is installed, the master commands him to ascend the seat. Without the command, he does not proceed.

After the seat ascent, the hall circuit is made. The head seat leads the great assembly into the hall. From the upper section, they stand in order. The attendant also enters the hall, standing at the rear door facing the Holy Monk's shrine, with south as the lower position. Next, the master enters the hall, burns incense, and makes a circuit from the upper section. He also circuits outside the hall, returns inside, bows facing the front, and stands before his chair. During the hall circuit, the great assembly bows with bent body and lowered head as the master passes.

Next, the administrators enter the hall, circuit from the upper section, bow to the master, then bow to the head seat, making a sequential circuit before exiting outside the hall. At this time, the attendant exits in succession from the lower end. The head attendant remains, bows facing the front, and invites [the master] to be seated. The great assembly bows together, exchanges salutations for the upper and lower sections, ascends the platforms, removes shoes, and takes their seats. They sit solemnly with folded hands at the chest, not wearing caps. The attendant burns incense and bows at the four places: the Holy Monk, upper section, lower section, and outside the hall. This is called "bowing at four places."

The bell before the hall strikes twice. The cup-bags are brought in. After the hot water is served, bowing again as before. The bell strikes twice again. The cup-bags are taken out. The release-from-attendance bell strikes three times. At the second strike, the great assembly joins palms. At the third strike, they exchange salutations and bows, descend from platforms, bow, and disperse.

When the master makes the hall circuit, a postulant and an attendant carry the incense box and follow him into the hall. When burning incense at the Buddha hall and various halls, the attendant carries it and follows.

**Second Day**

Earth Deity Gruel. Praising verse as on the first day: "This morning we have prepared a hall of bean gruel, offered to the earth deity of this mountain and the dragon king of this mountain. May their awesome radiance increase, may they protect the Dharma and bring peace to people. Relying reverently on the honored assembly, we recite the Ten Buddha Names as usual."

After gruel, following the release-from-attendance bell, after sūtra chanting at the main hall is finished, the bell is struck again and sūtra chanting is performed at the earth deity hall. The master burns incense three times, as at the main hall for the imperial blessing sūtra chanting. If the master does not come out, the superintendent or the prior substitutes in burning incense. If the administrators do not come out, the attendant substitutes. Even if the administrators burn incense, the attendant may also burn incense. If the attendant burns incense, the administrators do not burn incense. For the imperial blessing sūtra chanting, if the master does not come out, the senior administrator must burn incense and prostrate as the master would. If the administrators do not come out, the attendant substitutes in burning incense and prostrating.

If on the second day the entering-the-room board is hung, there is no release from attendance. When the entering-the-room drum sounds, the great assembly goes up to the abbot's quarters together, burns incense, and makes three prostrations. Regardless of administrators or chief officers, both groups, with the head seat as the senior, burn incense and stand in order, then step forward to enter the room.

The method of entering the room: The whole assembly each burns incense and makes three prostrations, then stands in line in the order of incense-burning, regardless of monastery rank or ordination seniority. After they stand in position, the attendant leads the group to face the edge of the master's seat and bow to the master. The head attendant alone goes outside the hall, bows to the head seat, steps forward, and says: "Please enter the room." The head seat enters the room, bows at the front edge of the seat, bows at the right corner of the meditation bench, turns right, bows, stands diagonally, and converses. When finished, he bows, and at the front edge of the seat makes a single prostration, that is, with the right hand he takes the sitting cloth, throws it directly on the ground, makes one prostration, rises, bows, and exits from the host's position. He bows to the next person. The next person steps forward, bows, and enters the room as before. Each person steps sideways to advance. When the assembly has finished entering the room, the attendant enters from the lower end, goes to the left corner of the meditation bench, converses, and exits from the guest's position, bowing to the next person. After the room entry is finished, the head attendant converses, makes a single prostration, and rolls up the mat. After the room entry is finished, the bell is struck. Sūtra chanting at the earth deity hall follows the regular pattern.

**Third Day**

Donor Gruel. Praising verse as before: "This morning, [we offer] a hall of pure gruel for the donors of this temple and the benefactors of the ten directions; may their merit and longevity be adorned. Relying reverently on the honored assembly, we recite the Ten Buddha Names as usual."

After gruel, sūtra chanting is performed at the main hall. There are no prostrations; only incense is burned properly. If administrators or attendants substitute for the master who does not come out, it is as at the earth deity hall.

Next, sūtra chanting below the storehouse. The bell is struck, and the rector leads the assembly to the storehouse area. The administrators receive the master upon entry. They also reverently escort him. The master takes the host's position. After the master enters, the administrators stand by the host's position and receive the great assembly.

This pattern applies from beginning to end. After the meal, the rector reports to the master and, upon receiving his command, hangs the recitation board.

At the afternoon hour, there is no meditation. When the Sangha hall's incense, flowers, lamps, and candles have been prepared, the rector has the hall administrators and kitchen administrators inform the master and the various quarters.

Starting from the abbot's quarters, the board is struck three times each. The earth deity hall, Buddha hall, and all other halls are all solemnly adorned. The master first goes down to the earth deity hall to burn incense, then to the main hall to burn incense and make three prostrations, then to the patriarchs' hall to burn incense. He then enters the hall. During this time, the great bell is slowly struck several times as the master proceeds to the hall.

The bell before the hall sounds three rounds. The great assembly stands in rows before the hall, with the south head seat as senior and the north superintendent as senior. The master enters either from the front or from the side door. The great assembly bows together. The master goes directly in, burns incense to the Holy Monk, and exits to stand by the host's position. When he bows, the great assembly bows together.

Two lower attendants step out from the group, one bows to the superintendent, one bows to the head seat, to report on the hall circuit and the serving of hot water. If there is no hot water service, this bow is omitted. This is the standing procedure.

Next, the rector steps out from the group, bows facing the front, and the great assembly bows together. They hold up joined palms for the recitation. That is, during the rector's one recitation of the Buddha's name, each person recites it ten times. Then the report to the dragons and devas is made. After the recitation, the rector bows, and the great assembly bows together. The rector returns to the assembly.

The master, the head seat, and the superintendent exchange salutations and bows, enter the hall, bow facing the front, turn in order, and stand before their chairs. The head seat leads his group into the hall, bows to the Holy Monk, turns in order, bows to the master, and goes before his position. The great assembly enters in groups of three, each forming one group, entering the hall in sequence from the upper section, standing in rows as they enter. The attendant enters from the lower position, forming one group to enter the hall, standing in rows at the rear door. The administrators form one group to enter the hall, making one circuit before exiting. When they exit, the attendant exits. The head attendant remains at the south side, advances to the front, bows, and the great assembly bows together. Everyone simultaneously removes their shoes and takes their seats.

Hot water is served as after a Dharma-seat ascent. If there is no hot water, after the recitation and bowing, the postulants are ordered to announce exemption from the hall circuit, and the release-from-attendance bell is struck. The great assembly each returns to their bedding positions and spreads their bedding. During the hall circuit, even if it is not one's own bedding position, one descends from the platform and spreads one's bedding. This is the constant rule. After spreading the bedding, "peaceful bowing" follows as usual. Afterward, incense is burned at the various halls as one wishes.

**Fourth Day**

Head-shaving and bathing. The meditation board is not hung. From the fourth day onward, regular gruel is served. The Ten Buddha Names are used without a praising verse. One simply says: "We humbly hope the Three Jewels will all grant their witness and knowledge. Relying reverently on the honored assembly, we recite..."

**Sixth Day**

**Seventh Day**

**Eighth Day**

**Tenth Day**

Dharma hall ascent. If abbreviated...

**Eleventh Day**

Request for instruction. The method of requesting instruction: After the meal, the attendant informs the rector, and the request-for-instruction board is hung. In the evening, the board at the abbot's quarters is struck three rounds. The great assembly, fully composed in deportment, goes up to the abbot's quarters. The west drum of the Dharma hall is struck in one extended round, like the informal instruction drum. The great assembly each burns incense facing the front, either two or three persons burn incense, and stands in order facing the front.

The head seat advances, bows facing the front, bows with body bent toward the corner of the meditation bench, and with folded hands says: "We, so-and-so, [acknowledge that] the matter of birth and death is great, impermanence is swift. We humbly hope the master will be compassionate and reveal the causes and conditions."

The master silently assents and descends from his seat. He makes three prostrations with the great assembly. After putting away the sitting cloth, he stands with folded hands and bowed body, saying: "In response to your request, I have soiled the ancestral style. I am ashamed before the ancient patriarchs and humbled before the great assembly."

He ascends the bench. The head seat comes to the corner of the meditation bench and raises the topic. The master raises it, comments, gives a capping phrase, and descends from the seat. The head seat returns to the front and makes prostrations of gratitude; three or six prostrations, as the occasion requires.

Next, after each person has burned incense, they stand in order and in turn bow facing the front, come to the corner of the meditation bench, and first give their capping phrase. After giving the capping phrase, they stand in a group on the west side. Then the head seat requests again as before, and the master assents as before. The ritual is as before. Requesting instruction on the first and sixth [of each ten-day period] was the ancient practice of Eihei-ji. At this mountain, we practice it on the third and first.

**Twelfth Day**

Entering the room. Or once a month. During the summer retreat, two or three times. Or there may be universal instruction followed by entering the room. Whether at dawn, at dusk, or even at midnight or early morning; whether in the sleeping hall, the illumination hall, or before the [Sangha] hall; the place is not fixed, the time is not fixed. Having the Way of the ancient Buddhas, this is the family style of Tendō.

The entering-the-room drum: The west drum of the Dharma hall is struck in three extended beats. Or the entering-the-room board before the hall is tapped. Or the board before the quarters is struck three times.

For universal instruction, the west drum is struck in five extended beats. The great assembly stands in a circle around the meditation chair, and with empty minds listens to the Dharma. After the universal instruction, the master taps the corner of the meditation bench with his whisk and says: "Please enter the room." Regardless of who it is, the person closest to the meditation bench advances to the corner of the bench, converses, and exits. Then the next person bows facing the front. The master descends from his seat.

Universal instruction may be on the third and eighth, or the timing may be unfixed; it is at the master's discretion.

This was the wish of the Venerable One.

**Twenty-third Day**

**[Rules for Special Meal Offerings:]**

When a meal offering is arranged at a specific time, whether a meal, gruel, or monetary gifts, the two groups confer as the occasion requires. Whether it is precept lectures, sūtra recitation, or sūtra chanting, either following the donor's wishes or responding to the donor's request, Dharma hall ascent and precept lectures are at the donor's request.

Feeding the Sangha and monetary gifts: the Three Jewels of the temple, all holy ones, sick monks, and monks on leave all receive them. Novices and postulants receive one-third of the monks' share.

When a donor requests a Dharma hall ascent, it must be at the midday meal time or after the meal. The Dharma hall is adorned properly. Below the steps, at the upper end of the mat, one dignified table is set with the offering items. At the lower end of the mat, another table is set with a pair of flower vases and one incense burner. A prostration mat is also spread. After three drum rolls, the master enters. When the master stands on the platform, the guest master leads the donor to burn incense and prostrate. At this time, the master offers incense. If a memorial is read, [the master] takes his seat. The guest master leads the donor to take a position at the administrators' upper shoulder to receive the donor's prostrations of gratitude. After the Dharma talk, the donor advances and makes a Chan prostration. If the donor is a layperson, the guest master leads them. If there is no guest master, the rector or attendant leads them. If the donor is a monastic, they simply advance, burn incense, and prostrate. The guest master leads them to their seat. If they are not a high official or venerable elder, they are not given a seat. If they are an ordinary monastic, no chair is set up. If there are no offering items, there is no upper table on the mat.

Officiant assignments for the mountain gate ceremony:

- So-and-so Senior Seat: burns incense
- So-and-so Senior Seat: purifying water
- So-and-so Senior Seat: scatters flowers
- So-and-so Senior Seat: fragrant water
- So-and-so Senior Seat: hand towel
- So-and-so Senior Seat: assistant
- So-and-so Senior Seat: hand towel
- So-and-so Senior Seat

"...all properly composed in deportment. Upon hearing the bell, assemble."

On the last day of the month, change "bright half" to "dark half." All else is the same as above. If a separate precept master and chanting master are invited, a duty board may also be posted.

Before the meal, the rector and the officiating monks go to the Buddha hall or the Dharma hall. The ceremonial space is adorned. The arrangement of chairs and tables follows the diagram and should be properly adorned. Afterward, purifying water is placed, and when sūtra chanting occurs, it is consecrated.

When the fire board sounds, the mirror board is collected. When the time comes after the meal, the rector has the hall administrator attendants report the roster to the various quarters. Each of the various quarters strikes the board before their quarters three times. Then the Dharma hall drum is struck in a proper measured cadence.

During this time, beginning with the head seat, each in order burns incense and bows. On open ground, that is, before the hall, they stand in rows. Taking the west pillar of the front face as the senior position, the west-south [side], regardless of the number, must have south and north facing each other. The head seat is senior. The assembly of monks, without distinguishing permanent residents from visiting monks, stands according to ordination seniority. On the east, the rector is senior. The officiating monks stand in order.

While the assembly stands in rows, the rector stands at the east side of the table, observing when the assembly has finished burning incense. He then spreads the prostration mat. After the attendant has burned incense, he stands on the east side, observing when the rector has spread the prostration mat, then goes to the abbot's quarters to escort the master out. At this time, the attendant stops the drum sound, and the great bell is struck in a proper measured cadence.

The master bows facing the front, burns incense, makes three full prostrations. The head seat bows at the upper shoulder. He stands [at the table]. At this time, the great assembly bows together. The rector joins palms and recites the outdoor verse. The great bell immediately stops. The great assembly all joins palms and recites the verse:

> "Subdue the power of Māra's armies,
> Remove the fetters without remainder.
> In the open ground, strike the gandhārī...
> Bhikṣus, hearing it, should assemble.
> All who wish to hear the Dharma,
> To cross the ocean of birth and death...
> Hearing this wondrous resonance,
> All should gather like clouds."

When the verse is finished, the rector strikes the hand-bell and leads the assembly into the hall. Before their respective seats, they stand in position, spread their sitting cloths, and make three simultaneous prostrations. On the cloths, they kneel with legs folded, join palms, and recite the verse:

> "Keeping precepts pure as the full moon,
> Body and speech luminous, without foul stain.
> The great assembly harmonious, without dispute...
> Only then may we practice the Poṣadha together."

When the verse is finished, the sitting cloths are collected and all rise. First the master takes his seat. Then the head seat and great assembly exchange salutations, bow, and sit in full lotus posture.

The rector and the five persons for purifying water, fragrant water, and two hand-towels do not take seats. The persons for the sacred chant, incense-burning, flower-scattering, and assistant, these four, fold their sitting cloths and take their positions.

The rector leads the four persons for purifying water and so forth. They advance to the front face, standing in position as shown in the diagram. The rector first bows, then moves to the rear and advances before the purifying water [person]. He bows, kneels with palms joined, and chants the purifying-water verse:

> "Water of eight meritorious qualities purifies all dust.
> Pouring over the palms removes defilement; the mind is unstained.
> Upholding the prohibitive precepts without deficiency...
> May all sentient beings likewise be so."

When the verse is finished, the right hand takes the purifying-water vessel. The left palm receives the pure water. Both hands wash each other. Then the right hand takes the hand towel and wipes dry. The right hand presses the table to stand, and he bows. At this time, the purifying-water person bows together. As before, advancing before the purifying water [person], the rector slowly steps sideways, advances before the fragrant-water [person]. The two bow together and kneel. The rector first chants the fragrant-water verse:

> "Fragrant water bathes and washes away all defilements.
> The Dharma-body is fully endowed with the five-part [incense].
> Prajñā's perfect illumination, liberation fulfilled...
> May the assembly of beings together realize the Dharma-realm's form."

When the verse is finished, the purifying-water person chants the purifying-water verse. The rector's left hand takes the fragrant-water vessel. The right palm receives the fragrant water. Both hands wash each other. Then the left hand takes the hand towel and wipes dry. He waits for the purifying-water person to finish washing. The left hand presses the table, and both stand together and bow.

The fragrant water and two hand-towel persons advance to the front. The verse is the same as before. The rector turns left, passes behind the hand-towel person, and returns to his original position. He spreads his sitting cloth and makes three prostrations, then stands. He waits for the hand-towel persons to finish the verse and return to their positions to bow.

The five persons all bow together, then slowly advance together near the table. Three persons kneel; the two hand-towel persons stand facing each other left and right. The rector joins palms and chants the bathing-tally verse. The officiating monks and the great assembly recite in unison:

> "All Buddhas, holy Sangha assembly,
> Bodhisattva assembly, harmonious...
> Fragrant-water bath, pure tallies...
> Poṣadha saves sentient beings."

The rector takes the tallies, goes to the place for purifying the tallies, and purifies them; that is, bathes the upper part in fragrant water and the lower part in purifying water. When the verse is finished, the two hand-towel persons take their hand towels from left and right. The five persons stand side by side and step back to the edge of the mat to bow.

First, the fragrant-water person advances near the mallet-side, bows, removes the cover, bathes the mallet handle with fragrant water, bows again, and replaces the cover. At this time, the purifying-water and fragrant-water persons advance in turn before the master.

The rector stands by the mallet, bows, removes the cover, places the tallies on his left shoulder, and waits for the great assembly to recite the verse and wash their hands. He strikes the mallet once and announces in a loud voice to the assembly:

"Bodhisattvas of the household and others, reflect: Our original teacher, Śākyamuni Buddha, the World-Honored One, told the bodhisattvas: 'I now, every half-month, personally recite the precepts of all Buddhas. All of you who have aroused the bodhisattva-mind should also recite them.' Even to the ten stages of aspiration, the ten stages of nurturing, the ten vajra stages, and the ten bhūmi, all bodhisattvas also recite them. Therefore, the precept-light issues from the mouth. Having conditions, it is not without cause. Therefore this light; it is not blue, yellow, red, white, or black; it is not form, not mind; not existent, not non-existent; not a dharma of cause and effect. It is the fundamental source of all Buddhas, the root of practicing the bodhisattva path, the root of the great assembly, all children of the Buddha. Therefore, the great assembly, all children of the Buddha, should receive and uphold it, should read and recite it."

"For this reason, I now practice Poṣadha with the assembly and recite the precepts of all Buddhas. With this merit, we benefit the eight classes of dragons and devas, the earth and monastery deities, dedicate it to the myriad spirits of the three realms, the true sovereigns of the ten directions. May His Majesty the Emperor's sagely transformation be without limit. May princes, officials, civil and military, and all people together accomplish the path of peach and plum, and all maintain the integrity of the sunflower and bean-leaf. May the monastery be tranquil, the assembly at peace. May the Buddha-sun increase in radiance, may the Dharma wheel turn constantly. May donors and faithful supporters increase in merit and wisdom. May preceptor-masters and parents perfect the seed-wisdom. May those who see, hear, and rejoice, the empty-space Dharma-realm, the three paths and eight difficulties, those with nature and without feeling, all abide in the proper Dharma-body."

"We vow together with all sentient beings, from this Jambudvīpa straight to the original..."

"Children of the Buddha, listen well. This treasury of bodhisattva precepts is recited together by all Buddhas and bodhisattvas of the three times. Among you, those who have not yet aroused the bodhi-mind and not yet received the bodhisattva precepts; please leave."

"Children of the Buddha, listen well. Among you, the assistants have already secured the area. Outside, the pure great bodhisattvas have already entered. Inside and outside is quiet; there are no difficulties. It is fitting to distribute the tallies."

"The assembly should single-mindedly contemplate the Poṣadha. May the upper, middle, and lower seats, each in proper order..."

Each, within the Buddha-Dharma, maintains purity, harmoniously practicing the Poṣadha together. Above, in accord with the Buddha's teaching; in the middle, repaying the four debts of gratitude; below, for the sake of all sentient beings. Each should..."

The rector advances before the master, makes three prostrations, collects his sitting cloth. Then he bows.

The sacred chant: "Children of the Buddha, listen well. The abbot will expound the precepts for the assembly."

**Dharma Lecture Ceremony**

"Namo. Protect and uphold the remaining Dharma, O Sixteen Great Arhats. Life after life, may we encounter and reverently bear [this teaching]."

"Respectfully announcing to our great benefactor teacher, Śākyamuni, World-Honored One. The Avataṃsaka, the Lotus, the eighty-thousand holy teachings. The sixteen arhats who protect and uphold the remaining Dharma. The ninety-nine koṭis of learners-beyond-learning, the holy assembly. Each and every atom, each and every hair-tip, a land-sea of the inexpressible and the inexpressible; the Three Jewels' realm."

"We declare: Śaṇavāsa's withdrawal from the world, though the stain of the degenerate age is to be regretted, the sixteen arhats' abiding in their regions is the great fortune of the disciples of the remaining Dharma. Mātaṅga and Zhu Falan came to China; Dengyō and Kōbō transmitted to Japan; all in repayment of their guardianship."

"Therefore, we disciples, tracing the stream to find the source, all is none other than the World-Honored One's vast grace. Smelling the fragrance and seeking the root, how could it not be the fragrant mind of the arhats? In reliance upon this, on this day we prepare incense and flowers, offering to the steadfast relics of the remains. At this seat, we prepare tea and medicine, offering to the arhats presently dwelling in the world. Various offering articles, strung precious gems, with the three karmas we reverently bow, manifesting the holy wealth."

"We humbly pray that the Three Jewels illumine what we disciples intend. This present lecture has a special purpose: merely praising one drop of the ocean of virtue, wishing to cross the perilous waves of the ocean of suffering."

"The two-times-eight Honored Ones: inwardly they conceal the bodhisattva's practice, outwardly they manifest the śrāvaka's form. Within an inch of ground, they accomplish the transformation of the Dharma-realm. Within a single thought, they respond to the donors' field. The Three Baskets, the twelve categories of scripture, the five sciences, the four Vedas; they realize their profound import, and their eloquence flows without obstruction. Their praise cannot be exhausted. Summarizing the essentials in five sections: (1) Clarifying their abodes and names. (2) Clarifying their benefit of prospering [the Dharma]. (3) Clarifying the benefit of the field of merit. (4) Clarifying the benefit of eliminating disasters. (5) Offering to the World-Honored One's relics."

"**First, Clarifying Their Abodes and Names:** The *Record of the Duration of the Dharma* states: The first honored one, Piṇḍola Bhāradvāja, abides in Western Godānīya. The second honored one, Kanakavatsa, abides in the Kingdom of Kaśmīra. The third honored one, Kanakabhāradvāja, abides in Eastern Pūrvavideha. The fourth honored one, Subinda, abides in Northern Uttarakuru. The fifth honored one, Nakula, abides in Southern Jambudvīpa. The sixth honored one, Bhadra, abides in Tāmradvīpa. The seventh honored one, Kālika, abides in Saṃghāṭa-dvīpa. The eighth honored one, Vajraputra, abides in Pratanadha-dvīpa. The ninth honored one, Jīvaka, abides in Mount Gandhamādana. The tenth honored one, Panthaka, abides in Trāyastriṃśa Heaven. The eleventh honored one, Rāhula, abides in Priyaṅgu-dvīpa. The twelfth honored one, Nāgasena, abides in Mount Pandava. The thirteenth honored one, Iṅgada, abides in Mount Vipulāpārśva. The fourteenth honored one, Vanavāsin, abides in Mount Sudarśana. The fifteenth honored one, Ajita, abides in Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa. The sixteenth honored one, Cūḍapanthaka, abides in Mount Dhārādhara."

"Each leads hundreds and thousands of great arhats as retinue, wandering and teaching in various regions, gathering and dispersing without fixed pattern. Some dwell in jeweled towers and golden pavilions, sitting alone in meditation and entering samādhi. Some reside on ocean shores and immortal caves, preaching the Dharma to birds and beasts. Some go to the heavenly realms to practice Poṣadha. Some roam the human world, expounding precepts and discourse."

"If one wishes to make invocations, one should burn incense and scatter flowers toward their abodes and perform prostrations. One invitation, one coming; three invitations, three comings. A hundred times, a thousand times; likewise. Like the moon floating on water, they never fail to appear."

"Therefore, the great assembly, relying on the communion, should recite the verse-hymn:"

> "Homage to the great arhats...
> The Dharma-nature, the cool moon.
> When sentient beings' mind-water is pure,
> The arhats' reflection appears within."

"**Second, Clarifying the Benefit of Prospering [the Dharma]:** When the Buddha, in the city of Kuśinagara, in the Śāla grove, was about to enter final nirvāṇa, he summoned Piṇḍola and the other sixteen arhats, touched their heads, and said: 'I entrust my unsurpassed true Dharma to you. After my extinction and before Maitreya, do not enter nirvāṇa. You should prosper my Dharma and benefit sentient beings. My conditions for transformation are exhausted; remaining in the world would be of no benefit.' Thereupon, the sixteen honored ones, hearing the golden-mouthed final entrustment, wept like small children, unwittingly dropped their single bowl, fainted, and fell to the ground. The Buddha consoled them again, and they revived as if from a swoon, restrained their tears, and received [the charge] atop their heads, not violating the Buddha's command... and so forth."

"Then, ever since Kāśyapa's form was concealed at Kukkuṭapāda Mountain and Ānanda's body was divided at the Ganges River, the four reliance-bodhisattvas have exerted their sūtra-protecting power, yet it does not compare to these arhats who, through supernatural power, extend their lives, awaiting from afar Maitreya's descent and birth; the final command faithfully maintained, able to protect the Buddha-Dharma of the final age."

"Therefore, when the lifespan of beings on this continent reaches ten years and the kalpa of swords arises, as the Buddha-Dharma gradually vanishes, when it increases to one hundred years, still the arhats will spread the true Dharma and save immeasurable beings. Especially, when King Puṣya destroyed Aśoka's stūpas, burned the Buddha's sūtras, and harmed monks and nuns, the very name of the Three Jewels was completely extinguished, after that evil king died, the arhats ascended to heaven, retrieved the sūtra scrolls, and came down to continue the already-extinguished traces. The Buddha-sun shone again. This is truly their vast grace."

"If it were not so, we would go from darkness into darkness, never seeing the form of the Three Jewels. From suffering we would encounter more suffering, never hearing the name of peace and bliss."

"Therefore, the great assembly, with joy and praise, should repay the grace of prospering [the Dharma]. The verse-hymn says:"

> "The Buddha's true Dharma has two aspects...
> Called the teaching and the realization, as its essence.
> When there are those who uphold and practice...
> Then it abides in the world."

"**Third, Clarifying the Benefit of the Field of Merit:** All arhats are the great field of merit for sentient beings. The retribution of harm and benefit does not wait for future lives. The proof of reward and punishment is before one's eyes. Troubling them brings calamity, earth rains down, burying the capital. Making offerings brings merit, jewels rain down, filling the king's palace. Those who see their images are forever free from poverty. Those who call their names are early granted protection. The *Record of the Duration of the Dharma* states: 'Kings, great ministers, elders, householders, men and women; when they establish a great feast and wish to give to the Sangha, the sixteen arhats and their retinues of great arhats conceal their holy demeanor, manifest various forms, and secretly receive the offerings, causing the donors to attain the supreme fruit of retribution'... and so forth."

"Accordingly, the people of Khotan established a great feast, invited the arhats, and sought water from the high plain. The sixteen arhats manifested as beggars, ascended the feast-mat, and spoke to each other: 'Mindful of the Buddha's command, we have come to answer your invitation. Do not refuse us.' Immediately, with their staff, they struck the ground, and following the staff, a sweet spring gushed forth."

"Also, in the city of brāhmaṇas, there was a poor woman who wept in shame at her karmic retribution. She had obtained only a small portion of rice and wished to offer it to the sixteen honored ones. The arhats, pitying her poverty, manifested as beggars and went to receive her meager offering. The poor woman's form was suddenly transformed; an emperor welcomed her as his consort... and so forth."

"Also, a householder of Hú province made offerings to the images of the sixteen honored ones, and his household's wealth nearly equaled that of Sudatta. In Yáng province, under a tree, there were sixteen shadow-images that on feast days emitted light. Those who made offerings were like a marketplace. Prayers were never empty."

"Now, we disciples, though merit within the conditioned realm is not our original intention, virtuous provisions are indeed essential."

"Therefore, the great assembly should take refuge in the sixteen arhats and pray for the benefit of the field of merit. The verse-hymn says:"

> "The sixteen great arhats...
> The unsurpassed, supreme field of merit.
> Those who reverently make offerings...
> Naturally obtain great benefit."

"Namo, with reverence and homage. To the sixteen arhats who benefit sentient beings."

"**Fourth, Clarifying the Benefit of Eliminating Disasters:** According to the intent of the *Consecration Sūtra*: If in future ages the four groups of disciples take refuge in the arhats and make the prostration of 'Namo,' a myriad koṭis of Ganges-sands of benevolent deities will invisibly guard this person in succession... and so forth."

"The Buddha praised the virtue of the sixteen great disciples, saying: 'When various difficulties are about to arise, one should call their names. Each will manifest awesome virtue, eliminating worry for people, removing all calamities. A hundred matters will be auspicious.' How much more so the sixteen honored ones, who specially received the Buddha's command, would their protection be scant? As with the honored one Rāhula, he is indeed such a person."

"Also, the *Sūtra of the Benevolent King's Prajñā* explains the secret method of protecting the nation, saying: 'One should invite images of five hundred arhats'... and so forth."

"Furthermore, when the asuras stormed the heavens and battled with Śakra, the Four Kings drew their shields and Śakra's banners flagged. At that time, Śakra donned the arhat's robe, and the asuras naturally retreated and scattered. From this we know: The elimination of calamities for humans and devas depends solely on the supernatural power of the arhats."

"We disciples, having traveled between Japan and China, seeking the Muni's white Dharma, going back and forth between the capital and the provinces, we rely on the black karma of the final age. The semblance of Poṣadha has not missed a half-month. The provision for donors has not lacked a single day. Those who contemplate the Buddha-Dharma all should be diligently encouraged. Those who receive the remaining Dharma all come to know [the arhats]. This is precisely the power of the arhats' protection. Moreover, is it not for the purpose of making the Dharma long abide?"

"Therefore, the great assembly, for the sake of eliminating disasters and bestowing joy, and making the Dharma long abide, should perform prostrations. The verse-hymn says:"

> "The two-times-eight great arhats...
> Are like the nation-protecting jewel.
> One recitation, one prostration...
> Can eliminate all calamities."

"Namo, with reverence and homage. To the sixteen arhats who benefit sentient beings."

"**Fifth, Offering to the World-Honored One's Relics:** The arhats presently dwell in the world, protecting the people and Dharma of the final age; because when the World-Honored One was in the world, he earnestly entrusted [them]. Yet, relying on the arhats to pity us is still a vast grace and virtue."

"Moreover, the indestructible vajra relics that were left behind benefit the deluded multitudes. How could we not offer gratitude in return? On the evening of the Twin Trees, the tears of compassion and great pity flowed. The bodhisattvas and śrāvakas were entrusted with our care. On the morning of the five turbidities, the adamantine bone-relics were left behind. Through supernatural transformation, they benefit [beings] and accomplish the Buddha's work."

"The sorrow of [the Buddha's] presence and extinction, the blessing of the living body and the broken body; these cannot be adequately expressed."

"Therefore, in order to look up to the inexhaustible benefit, we recite the verse-hymn and perform prostrations. The hymn says:"

> "We only pray that the holy and heavenly assembly
> Definitively witness and know us.
> Each arriving at their respective places of abiding...
> Again and again, descend in compassion to attend."

"Namo, with reverence and homage. To the relics of the [Buddha's] remains that benefit sentient beings."

Next, the universal dedication:

> "May this merit
> Be universally extended to all.
> May we and all sentient beings
> Together accomplish the Buddha Way."

Next, the six kinds of dedication. The rector alone chants them.

Then follows the offering sūtra chanting. Dedication as usual. After the sūtra chanting, three prostrations, and exit from the hall.

## Fascicle Three: Annual Observances

**First Day of the First Month.** At gruel time, five-flavored gruel must [be served]. The verse of praise is as usual. Tea is served at gruel time; this is under the management of the attendant. Greetings at the four places are as usual.

Next, the head seat goes to the Sangha hall annex and performs three prostrations of touching [foreheads to the ground] with the great assembly. This is the beginning of the formal courtesies. Next come the courtesies of the attendant and junior disciples. Next, those with aspiration to study offer courtesies. Next, the junior disciples of the abbot's chamber offer courtesies. Next, śrāmaṇeras and postulants offer courtesies.

Next is the regular Dharma hall ascent. The attendant's greeting is included; one must not forget the four seasonal and three seasonal [greetings]. After the Dharma hall ascent is finished, a prostration mat is laid crosswise at the foot of the steps. First, the administrative officers exchange personal courtesies, two full prostrations and three bows. Next, the senior officers and great assembly exchange courtesies as before. Next, the attendant's personal courtesies, a full prostration and three bows; with no return bow. Next, śrāmaṇeras and postulants offer three prostrations.

Next, the head seat leads the great assembly before the storehouse to perform three prostrations of touching [foreheads to the ground] with the various administrative officers. Next, the head seat stands before the hall in the host's position. The secretary and those below, along with the great assembly, perform three prostrations to the head seat. The head seat then leads the great assembly on a circuit of the hall and they stand in position. The attendant joins the great assembly and stands in position at the rear door. The administrative officers form a line in the same manner, enter the hall, burn incense, and perform a full prostration with three bows. Before the head seat, they perform three prostrations of touching [foreheads to the ground]; this is the return courtesy. They then make one circuit of the hall; this signifies the return courtesy to the great assembly.

Next, the abbot enters the hall, burns incense, performs a full prostration with three bows, and makes one circuit of the hall as a return courtesy to the great assembly, acknowledging their condescension. The attendant exits through the rear door, goes to the front of the hall, and stands in line. Before the chair, the abbot performs three prostrations of touching [foreheads to the ground] to the head seat, the great assembly, and the administrative officers, this is the return courtesy. When the abbot departs, the head seat alone sees the abbot off. At the threshold, they exchange greetings with expressions of humility, this is the return courtesy acknowledging the condescension.

Next, the head seat returns to his position and performs a full prostration with three bows with the great assembly. This morning, the head seat went down to the assembly hall annex to greet the great assembly; this is the return courtesy. The formal public courtesies are thus. This concludes the formal courtesies.

Next come individual personal courtesies. In the various quarters, corridors, and wherever is convenient, Dharma relatives, Dharma kin, personal teachers, and even fellow countrymen exchange courtesies as they wish. When visiting various quarters and the quarter master is away, one posts a small notice and returns. The notice reads: "Personal courtesies from so-and-so," or "Respectful visit from so-and-so," or "Prostrations offered by so-and-so," or "Bows from so-and-so." When the quarter master sees this notice, each returns the courtesies. When the quarter master is also away, another notice is posted in return reading: "Return courtesies from so-and-so," or "Return prostrations from so-and-so."

**Next, at the Great Hall: Prayers for the Emperor and New Year Ceremonies.** Two placards are hung on the left and right pillars. A large notice is posted on the front face. The adornments are done according to proper form. The abbot, both ranks, the corridors, and the hall front are notified. The bell is struck three rounds. When the great assembly has gathered and settled, the abbot burns incense and performs three prostrations. They chant the Sūtra of the Reigning Monarch and circumambulate three times.

The notice reads:

"The Mountain Gate. We humbly state: As the New Year inaugurates auspicious fortune, the radiance of the mountain gate is redoubled. All things are renewed; we celebrate the great peace of the imperial way. The rivers and lakes blend their flavors; the mountains and forests scatter their salt. The five grains ripen in abundance; the six flavors are harmoniously balanced. Take heed! When one person has blessings, the myriad people have reliance. Hidden virtue extends to both the unseen and manifest realms; the blessings of the Southern Mountains are profound. Manifest reward is bestowed through wind and rain; the shelter of the Northern Peaks reaches far.

Above: Brahmā, Śakra, the Four Kings, nāgas, devas, and the eight classes. Within the land of Japan, all great and small divine spirits. The local deities, the entire assembly of the hall's true sovereigns. The donors of this temple, the patrons of all halls, the donors of fields, those connected by karmic affinity whether ordained or lay, the entire assembly of the mountain, the natal stars and current year's governing constellation.

Below: down to the firm and solid earth spirit.

May the true, contemplative, pure contemplation cause each to clarify the correct eye. May the Brahma-voice, the voice of ocean tides, together shake the Dharma thunder. May all board the ship of prajñā, and may the wondrous merit of the Sūtra of the Reigning Monarch be broadly distributed. May they wear the seal of dhāraṇī, and universally bestow the accumulated blessings of the Universal Gate Manifestation."

A permanent annual regulation for the three mornings: the second day, the third day.

"The above is for the attention of the entire mountain. Fourth year of Genkō, this month and day. Sangha hall officer, bhikṣu so-and-so, recorded."

Each day, incense is burned and three prostrations are performed at the beginning and end. On the final day of dispersal, if there is a memorial, then after the chanting is completed and the abbot has prostrated, he kneels on his sitting cloth before the censer. The rector steps forward and reads the memorial:

"In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Northern Route, Noto Province, Kashima District, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Zen Monastery, founded by the fifty-fourth generation Dharma heir of Śākyamuni Buddha, the Dharma-transmitting śramaṇa Shōkin and others.

Now encountering the auspicious occasion of the Three Mornings, we reverently pray for the extension of the sacred lifespan. Over these three days, leading the assembly present, at the Precious Hall of the Awakened Emperor, the Supreme Hall, we have chanted the Sūtra of the Reigning Monarch in three fascicles to complete the New Year ceremonies.

The accumulated blessings thus collected are dedicated and offered to: Amaterasu Ōmikami, the founding deity of Japan; the Seven Generations of Heavenly Deities; the Five Generations of Earthly Deities; the ninety-sixth generation of human emperors, the current Emperor's natal star and this year's governing constellation; the seven luminaries, nine luminaries, and twenty-eight lunar mansions; the great divine spirits who guard the imperial capital; the great and small divine spirits of the five provinces and seven circuits; the Great Overseer of the Buddha-Dharma, the Great Avatar of Wondrous Principle of Hakusan; the two great bodhisattva shrines guarding the front and rear of this province; the various shrines of this district and this estate; the local earth spirit of this mountain; the dragon king of this mountain; this year's presiding spirits governing the balance of yin and yang, creation, good and evil, and discernment.

The First Shrine of the South, the Great Bodhisattva of Keta, with retinue. Shōhō Shichirō, the Great Bodhisattva Śūri of Authority, with retinue. Vaiśravaṇa. The Kāla Deva. The Blue-Faced Messenger of Service. The White-Robed Prince who follows and serves. The old guardian Inari Great Divine Spirit. The Great Bodhisattva Hachiman who protects Silla.

The extraordinary merit collected is transferred to: the donors of this temple, the patrons of all halls, the donors of fields, those connected by karmic affinity whether ordained or lay, the entire assembly of the mountain, their natal stars and this year's governing constellations. May their majestic light increase and their excellent virtues be perfected.

We pray: May the sacred lifespan be without limit, like the camellia and pine showing forms that resist aging. May the imperial virtue extend universally, and the blessings of mountains and seas leave nothing unreached. May all under heaven enjoy well-regulated wind and rain. May all the land enjoy abundance, prosperity, and good fortune. May the sun of the Buddha increase in radiance. May the wheel of the Dharma constantly turn. May the monastery's earth spirits protect the Dharma and bring peace to all people.

Respectfully submitted. May Your Majesty graciously accept this. May the Three Jewels clearly witness.

Respectfully submitted. Fourth year of Genkō, third day of the first month. Tōkoku, Shōkin and others, respectfully submitted."

Reverently. Memorial on yellow paper: "In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Noto Province, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Monastery, founded by Shōkin and others. Respectfully sealed. May Imperial Heaven graciously accept."

The prayers for the Emperor and New Year ceremonies are a great event in all monasteries under heaven. The entire assembly must attend. If anyone does not attend, the rector shall have an attendant urge them to gather. Buddha offerings should be prepared and placed before the Buddha. Rice cakes should be piled up. This year, for the first offering, fifty pieces. In subsequent years, the more the better to show earnestness. Tea and hot water should be offered before the Buddha. The abbot oversees this.

Both ranks should exchange greetings. The rector steps forward, burns incense, and first greets the left side of the stand, this is greeting the abbot. The abbot returns the greeting. For the incense burning, the attendant opens the incense box. After the incense is burned, the remaining incense is placed in the lid box. The administrative officers and senior officers, two persons, step forward and burn incense. Each subsequent person does the same. At the beginning of each round when the abbot and both ranks exchange greetings, the rector greets, this is the requesting gesture. After all persons have greeted, the rector and the junior senior officers step forward and greet.

At both the opening and the final dispersal, both ranks must burn incense. After burning incense, the abbot spreads his sitting cloth and performs three prostrations. Afterward, the rector leads the chanting. The abbot leads the assembly in circumambulation. The attendants collect the prostration mat. The abbot and great assembly do not wear head coverings and do not place hands in sleeves but keep palms together; this expresses earnest reverence. Perhaps refreshments are served. The three days proceed thus.

Next, on the first day, after the three strikes [signaling the meal's end], the abbot and great assembly enter the hall and ascend to their seats. Round mirrors, confections, and such have been arranged in advance at the hall front. After the entire assembly is seated, the senior administrative officer enters the hall, burns incense, and greets the four places. After the round mirrors, confections, and such have been distributed, refreshments are served. If, as in this mountain forest, refreshments are abbreviated, there is no objection. After the three days, the signal board is struck for seated meditation. Recitations during the three days must not be neglected.

**Fifteenth Day of the Second Month: Nirvāṇa Assembly.** Offerings are prepared according to one's means. In advance, all persons in the assembly, great and small, and all persons in the mountain each contribute seven coins for making offerings to the Nirvāṇa Buddha. This is noted on a tally. The storehouse encourages this and prepares the offerings. This is the old custom of Eihei-ji.

After the offerings are prepared, the Dharma hall ascent proceeds as usual. However, the incense dedication reads:

"In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Northern Route, Noto Province, Kashima District, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Monastery, founded by the fifty-fourth generation Dharma grandson of Śākyamuni Buddha, the newly-precept junior bhikṣu so-and-so. This single stick of incense is burned in the censer, reverently offered to our original teacher, the Tathāgata Śākyamuni, the great preceptor, to repay the kindness of the Dharma-milk."

Or, after the incense for praying for the Emperor and the mountain invocation, one also offers incense in homage to the Buddha. Although there are two styles, since the time of Tendō Nyojō, only the incense offering to the Buddha has been used. The attendant's greeting is included.

After the Dharma talk concludes, one must say: "I request the great assembly to proceed to the Buddha hall to burn incense and make prostrations." When the assembly has gathered, the abbot burns incense. The great assembly forms lines and transmits the offerings. The rector raises the Great Compassion Dhāraṇī. Or the Dharma drum is sounded. The senior administrative officer offers incense with each offering and passes it along. The abbot also offers incense and makes the offerings. After the offerings are complete, both ranks burn incense and greet. They stand to the abbot's left and right. The great assembly faces north and performs nine prostrations. During the prostrations, the chime is struck. After prostrating, they fold their sitting cloths. The abbot does not fold his sitting cloth but kneels, holding the censer and burning incense. The rector steps forward and reads the memorial:

"The pure Dharma-realm body is originally without arising or passing. Through the power of great compassion and vows, it manifests coming and going. We look upward and pray for illumination; we humbly request true compassion.

In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Northern Route, Noto Province, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Monastery, founded by so-and-so and others. On the fifteenth of this month, we reverently commemorate the occasion of our original teacher, the Tathāgata Śākyamuni, the great preceptor, entering parinirvāṇa. We have respectfully prepared incense, flowers, lamps, candles, tea, fruits, and delicacies to express our most earnest and devoted offering. We reverently gather the entire assembly present to chant the secret divine dhāraṇī. The extraordinary merit collected is offered above to repay the compassionate shelter.

We humbly state: The faint moonlight of Gṛdhrakūṭa, its dim radiance shining far. The remnant blossoms of the twin śāla trees of nirvāṇa, their lingering fragrance still rich. The salvific activity of nirvāṇa's eternal bliss continues to this very time. The virtuous function of the unconditioned true aspect extends to ages yet to come.

Therefore, with the Dharma-offering of the one-mind of the supreme vehicle, each face raises the offerings of the fifty-two categories. With the secret dhāraṇī of the Śūraṅgama of myriad practices, each performs the Buddha-work of different mouths with one voice.

We pray: May all in the Dharma-realm be startled by the announcement of immeasurable light and sound. May all beings partake of the Tathāgata's ever-present transforming activity.

Respectfully submitted. May the Honored One have compassion; may the wise and holy all bear witness.

Respectfully submitted. Fourth year of Genkō, fifteenth day of the second month. Fifty-fourth generation Dharma grandson, heir-bhikṣu so-and-so and others. Respectfully submitted."

Reverently. Memorial on yellow paper: "In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, such-and-such province, such-and-such route, such-and-such district, such-and-such village, the abbot so-and-so. Respectfully sealed. Nirvāṇa Tathāgata."

After reading the memorial, the chant leader initiates the chanting of sūtras as usual. The abbot folds his sitting cloth, rises, and begins the circumambulation. When the sūtra chanting is finished, he burns incense and performs three prostrations.

The rector's dedication reads: "The merit of chanting the secret divine dhāraṇī of the Śūraṅgama just now is offered in homage to the original teacher, the Tathāgata Śākyamuni, the great preceptor, who today entered parinirvāṇa, to repay the kindness of the Dharma-milk. The Buddhas of the ten directions and three times..." and so forth.

The three seasonal [observances] all use this dedication.

The Sangha hall and all quarters close their braziers. The great assembly attends without head coverings, with exposed clasped hands.

**End of the Second Month or Beginning of the Third Month: The Seasonal Day of Entering the Third Month.** This day is Qīngmíng [Clear and Bright]. Fire-prevention talismans are written. The seal of the Three Jewels is stamped. At the midday sūtra chanting, [talismans] for the donors, the various hermitages, and the various donor households [are distributed]. On this day, the entire country changes its fire. If drilling for new fire, elm and willow should be used. Elm for the drill and willow for the base, or willow for the drill and elm for the base. When the fire is changed, the fires of all halls and all hermitages and small dwellings in the mountain should all be changed.

**During the Third Month:** The number of monks granted hanging-bag registration is determined; about two or three persons. A person skilled and experienced in protocol is selected as the head of participants. First, prepare an official registration document for the new arrival. If accompanied by a letter of reference, the six recollections, and an ordination certificate, prepare a supplementary document. The guest prefect goes up to the abbot's quarters and obtains the hanging-bag registration slip. The slip reads:

"Mountain gate: Hanging-bag registration granted to the following new arrivals:

- So-and-so, Ascent to the Hall: from such-and-such country
- So-and-so, West Hall: formerly abbot of such-and-such temple
- So-and-so, Head Seat: former incumbent of such-and-such temple
- So-and-so, Comptroller: senior veteran of such-and-such temple
- So-and-so, Attendant: disciple of such-and-such preceptor

The above new arrivals are reported to the rector, who shall arrange for hanging-bag registration. The guest prefect knows all details."

The guest prefect, having received this slip, prepares the letters of reference and documents, and leads the new arrivals to the rector's quarters to meet the Sangha hall officer and the offering officer. They meet the rector. The rector supervises and arranges the hanging-bag registration, their return to the hall, and assignment to quarters.

Even if they are not former abbots, former incumbents, or senior veterans, if they are elders, senior monks, or persons of established virtue, they should be assigned quarters according to their standing and provided for as appropriate. This is the responsibility of the abbot, administrative officers, and rector.

Hanging-bag registration is limited to before the first day of the fourth month. This is the protocol of the Pure Rules.

**First Day of the Fourth Month:** The gate is closed in the morning and guest monks are not received. However, in recent practice, before the Buddha's Birthday Assembly, those of appropriate standing are admitted. This is abbreviated hanging-bag registration.

The procedure for abbreviated hanging-bag registration: The rector and guest prefect privately consult the abbot's quarters. Together with the abbot, they burn incense and prostrate. The rector leads [the new arrival] through the hanging-bag registration. The rector also leads them into the Sangha hall. They only burn incense and prostrate before the Holy Monk, circuit the hall, and at the position assigned by the rector perform three prostrations of touching foreheads. That is all.

The official registration document reads:

"The above-named so-and-so, a resident of such-and-such temple in such-and-such province. In such-and-such year, ascended such-and-such mountain. Had the head shaved in the chamber of the ācārya so-and-so. In such-and-such year, ascended Mount Hiei [or Tōdai-ji in Nara] to receive precepts at the ordination platform. Ordination certificate is attached. Also entered the assembly of so-and-so at such-and-such temple and received full ordination to become a monk. [Or: changed robes and converted schools.] Now, because the matter of birth and death is great, has specially come to seek refuge and reside. Humbly requesting compassion, may the Sangha hall Zen master grant acceptance.

Such-and-such year, month, day. New arrival, so-and-so. Statement."

The rector receives the statement and places it in the box in order. One prostration of touching foreheads; the farewell is abbreviated. The Sangha hall officer says: "Please, venerable, return to the hall."

The new arrival, having registered, folds the sitting cloth before the Sangha hall. Starting from the head seat's position, he circuits the hall once, then goes before the Holy Monk and makes a greeting. Then at the third position on the south side of the board at the front door, he sits in order.

The rector, according to ordination seniority, records the bed assignment on the bed register and has an attendant place the statement in the box. Following along, they enter the hall. The rector burns incense and greets before the Holy Monk. The new arrival stands up, stands before his bed, and exchanges greetings with the rector. The rector passes the registration statement in order. One prostration of touching foreheads. The registration proceeds in order. South, north, east, and west beds; the rector always takes the upper hand. Closer to the Holy Monk is senior.

After registration, the rector leads the new arrival up to the abbot's quarters for a congratulatory prostration. The attendant inquires in detail: how many new arrivals, how many entering-the-room disciples, how many tonsured junior disciples, how many Dharma relatives and Dharma kin. According to each person's standing, the protocol differs. New arrivals only perform a full prostration with three bows. If they are important Dharma relatives, two full prostrations with three bows, this is also the abbot's public courtesy. Higher Dharma kin, the abbot should not accept prostrations. Lower Dharma kin; two full prostrations with three bows, or three prostrations of touching foreheads. For closest Dharma kin, regardless of rank: administrative officers, senior officers, junior disciples, attendants, and servers all exchange personal courtesies. For higher Dharma kin, the abbot goes down to a lower place and returns the courtesy. A nearby upper quarter should be chosen for their accommodation. For guest monks of equal standing, all go to the quarters for hanging-bag registration.

First they find the quarter master and the quarter's head seat and exchange greetings. The new arrivals stand in order before the holy image, greet, and after circuiting the quarters, each goes to the reading bed and performs one prostration of touching foreheads. Then they arrange their belongings.

Next, the rector has the attendant notify the head seat, administrative officers, and the various quarters. Whether touching foreheads or greetings depends [on the case]. For those with abbreviated hanging-bag registration, the rector has the attendant notify the attendant's quarters and various quarters. Next, personal Dharma relatives and former residents exchange greetings and prostrations in order.

Within three days after registration, one should always remain in one's quarters or the Sangha hall, waiting to be invited for tea. One must not wander idly, lest others be sent to look for you.

After registration, the head seat, superintendent, and others invite the newly registered for tea; either in the illumination hall, the storehouse quarters, or their own quarters, wherever is convenient. The various quarter masters send a formal invitation on a small notice:

"Newly returned to the hall: so-and-so, head seat; so-and-so, senior seat.

This month, this day. Head seat bhikṣu so-and-so, with prostrations, respectfully invites."

**On the Tenth or Eleventh Day of the Fourth Month**, the abbot goes to the illumination hall or the sleeping hall to perform the gratitude tea for hanging-bag registration. The sleeping hall or illumination hall, depending on the number of people, is arranged with four-headed, six-headed, or eight-headed [seating]. Arrangements should be made carefully so as not to cause confusion.

First, a notice is posted:

"So-and-so, West Hall; so-and-so, Head Seat; so-and-so, Senior Seat; so-and-so, Attendant; so-and-so, Senior Seat; so-and-so, Senior Seat.

The Abbot, this morning after the midday meal, will proceed to the sleeping hall to specially serve tea. We humbly request the cloud assembly to grace us with your presence."

All those newly returned to the hall since the previous winter solstice are invited together. According to the number of persons, four-headed or six-headed seating is arranged. The accompanying seats include the current head seat and senior veterans, who are invited as circumstances warrant. One attendant, or if two, performs the courtesies. For six-headed or more, two must perform the courtesies. The two stand facing each other, greet, then separate left and right. At each seat head they greet, then return to facing each other and greet again; like the protocol for a formal reception.

From the end of summer until winter, for monks who registered during winter, in the days around the winter seasonal observance, three to five days, another round of gratitude tea for hanging-bag registration is performed.

**First Day of the Fourth Month.** After the Dharma hall ascent, during the hall circuit, the abbot and the head seat and great assembly perform three prostrations of touching foreheads. The beginning of each season is the same.

**Third Day of the Fourth Month.** A draft of the summer assembly's ordination-seniority board must be prepared. This is called the "draft roster." It is to determine the correct order of ordination seniority. The format reads:

"Japan, Noto Province, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Zen Monastery. The ordination seniority of the sea-assembly is as follows:

The Venerable Kauṇḍinya
The Abbot: first precepts in the first year of Shōgen, so-and-so, senior seat
First precepts in the first year of Shōō, so-and-so, senior seat.

The above is respectfully presented. If there are any errors, please direct corrections.

Respectfully stated. Fourth year of Genkō, third day of the fourth month. Sangha hall officer bhikṣu so-and-so, with prostrations."

From the third day, it is posted after the gruel meal is finished and taken down after the rest period. Thus for three days it is posted and then removed. If there are corrections from the assembly, they are recorded on the board accordingly.

**Seventh Day of the Fourth Month.** At gruel time, after the general signal, another single strike is sounded:

"Announcement to the great assembly: After the gruel meal, there is a general work request to gather flowers and adorn the Buddha niche. The senior officers of all quarters are to rise together. When the bell and drum sound, gather. Respectfully announced."

Another single strike. One circuit of the hall. Those outside the hall also circuit. Facing greetings, return to positions.

After gruel, the wooden fish before the storehouse is struck three rounds. Next the cloud drum is struck three rounds. The small bell at the hall front and the boards of all quarters all sound together. The entire assembly gathers at the Buddha hall. If the gathering is dispersed, they go to collect flowers. The elders arrange them by the niche. During the day, the Buddha niche is adorned and the area before the Buddha arranged. The hall keeper boils water from five kinds of wood gathered at dawn, places it in a clean bowl, and stands up the Nativity Buddha image. Two ladles, incense, and flowers are offered. The administrative officers prepare the offerings in advance.

> Homage to the Great Sage, the Bhagavān,

If an equal-standing head seat, only three prostrations of touching foreheads.

Then, according to the host position, they greet the head seat, perform three prostrations of touching foreheads, and escort the head seat out. Re-entering the hall, they burn incense at the four places and circuit, this is the companion's thanks. The attendant enters the trays and collects the cups. The bell is struck before and after. After the cups are out, the administrative officer burns incense at the center, one stick, and greets, signifying the ceremony is complete.

Then they wait for the informal talk drum. Going up to the abbot's quarters, the informal talk placard is hung.

The western drum of the Dharma hall is struck. The abbot, both ranks, and the great assembly all gather at once. It may be at the abbot's chair, or the illumination hall, or the sleeping hall, wherever is suitable. The attendant burns incense and both ranks greet. Zen inquiries and responses proceed as in a Dharma hall ascent. However, the essential instructions and detailed admonitions of the school tradition are delivered. At descending, he must say: "The night is deep and you have stood long. Take care of yourselves."

**Fifteenth Day.** At dawn, around the second or third watch of the fifth watch, during the dawn bell, the attendant, junior disciples, administrative officers, senior officers, and the great assembly go up to the abbot's quarters for congratulatory courtesies. If a notice exempting personal courtesies has been posted, one does not go up. In this mountain forest, since the paths are inconvenient, after the gruel meal, the attendant and junior disciples offer congratulatory courtesies. There are no public congratulatory courtesies from administrative officers and senior officers. Only after the Dharma hall ascent are the public congratulatory courtesies performed.

Next, for the Dharma hall ascent incense dedication: after being seated, the attendant burns incense. Leading the fellow officers, they greet from below the steps. Next the senior officers greet toward the edge of the mat. The great assembly greets universally together. Next the administrative officers greet toward the edge of the mat. If there is a west hall [resident], he turns slightly and greets. If the west hall position is held by the abbot's entering-the-room disciple, he greets toward the edge of the mat. If a currently-serving abbot of another temple is in the west hall position, he stands at the upper shoulder of the senior officers, slightly behind. If it is a retired abbot of the home temple, he is called "east hall" and stands at the upper shoulder of the administrative officers, slightly behind. If there is an abbot from another temple who comes for the Dharma hall ascent, the current abbot stands above the east hall. If there are two east halls, they stand according to position order. If it is a retired abbot from another temple, he is called "west hall." If two or more, they are arranged either by the order of their Dharma hall opening or by the order of succession. If currently serving, they stand above the west hall. For east hall and west hall residents, entering the hall first is acceptable. Even if entering at the same time as the great assembly, each stands at the upper shoulder of the administrative officers or senior officers in their proper line.

First the administrative officers, then the senior officers, and the great assembly each perform two full prostrations and three bows. Next, visiting abbots, east hall, and west hall residents; either greetings or touching foreheads, depending on the person. Meeting the abbot on his hall circuit, they exit from the rear door to the hall front and meet the head seat for congratulatory courtesies.

It is prepared in advance and adorned after the gruel meal.

The attendant also prepares an ordination-seniority board. It is placed high in the eastern section of the Dharma hall, facing the Dharma seat, before the gruel meal and before the Dharma hall ascent, with incense and flowers offered.

As for the ordination-seniority boards of various mountains, the two ranks are not listed by ordination order but are separately listed in two columns. Though listed separately, our temple follows the ordination-seniority board format of Tendō Nyojō: regardless of the two ranks or seniority of service, all are listed by ordination order.

The dimensions of the ordination-seniority board vary by location. Our temple's is neither too large nor too small, humbly modeled after Eihei-ji's board.

"In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Northern Route, Noto Province, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Zen Monastery, founded by so-and-so and others. Now at the time when the Dharma King restricts his steps, this being the day when the sons of Śākya protect life, the monastery, following the old custom, establishes the Śūraṅgama Victory Assembly to chant the secret divine dhāraṇī.

We humbly state: The Śūraṅgama Victory Assembly; our original teacher, the Tathāgata Śākyamuni, emitted jeweled light from the invisible-crown mark, extended the tongue-mark over the great-thousand sand-worlds, illuminated the buddha-lands of the ten directions, gathered all Buddhas, caused innumerable bodhisattvas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges to expound twenty-five kinds of perfect penetration. Uniquely, the attainment of Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva is especially the standard for awakening through hearing sound. Then he expounded the secret divine dhāraṇī, directly revealing the straight path of awakening.

The Śūraṅgama Victory Assembly originated from this assembly. The long-term dhāraṇī recitation is devoted to this dhāraṇī. The days are limited to one hundred. The number of persons is set. The buddhas and patriarchs had the old rule of ninety days' peaceful dwelling. The monastery establishes the victory assembly of three months of practice.

Respectfully submitted. May the buddhas and patriarchs, the wise and holy, illuminate and witness. May the nāgas, devas, and Dharma protectors guard and support.

Respectfully submitted. Fourth year of Genkō, fifteenth day of the fourth month. Fifty-fourth generation Dharma grandson, heir-bhikṣu so-and-so and others. Respectfully submitted."

"Original Teacher Tathāgata, Lord of Perfect Penetration.

In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Northern Route, Noto Province, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Monastery, founded by so-and-so and others. Respectfully sealed. Śūraṅgama Victory Assembly, Three Jewels of the Ten Directions."

After reading the memorial, the Śūraṅgama leader initiates the chanting, and the "Buddha Mother" [passage] is recited:

"At that time, the World-Honored One, from his flesh-crown, emitted hundred-jeweled radiance. Within the radiance appeared a thousand-petaled jeweled lotus. Within it sat a transformation Tathāgata, who from the crown emitted ten paths of hundred-jeweled radiance. In each radiance was universally manifested Ganges-sand numbers of vajra-holders, fearsome, mountain-wielding, brandishing pestles throughout all of space. The great assembly looked up with awe and love combined, seeking the Buddha's compassionate protection, single-mindedly listening as the Buddha, from the invisible crown mark, radiated light, and the Tathāgata proclaimed the divine dhāraṇī."

*Namas samanta-buddhānāṃ sugatāya oṃ samara samara...* [The Śūraṅgama Dhāraṇī follows.]

The comptroller burns incense, performs three prostrations of touching foreheads. The rector delivers the placard. The attendant delivers the whisk. The rector hangs the placard at the hall front. The whisk is delivered to the Holy Monk's attendant.

In the evening, the Dharma hall is adorned as for a Dharma hall ascent. The drum is struck, the assembly gathers, and greetings, questions, and discussion proceed as in a Dharma hall ascent. However, there is the abbot's greeting. On this day, although there should be a grand tea service in the Sangha hall, due to the Poṣadha, it cannot be omitted.

**Sixteenth Day.** The abbot, in the Cloud Hall, hosts a tea service for the administrative officers and senior officers. The tea notice, after the midday meal's three strikes, is presented to the head seat and administrative officers and posted at the hall front on the north side of the placard board. This is the attendant's responsibility. The notice reads:

"The Abbot, this morning after the midday meal, specially for the Head Seat, to mark the ceremony of commencing the retreat, with the administrative officers and great assembly as company."

When the time comes, the drum before the storehouse is struck. The assembly gathers. The head attendant performs the courtesies, as in the administrative officer's protocol, except without the abbot's greeting. But at the right side of the rear door there is the superintendent's seat, and at the right side of the front door there is the comptroller's seat. First greet the special seat, next greet the superintendent, next greet the comptroller, next greet the secretary. Circuit the hall once. Outside the hall, there may be temporary visitors circuiting; otherwise, all administrative officers and senior officers are received inside.

There is no protocol at the hall front.

Seat arrangement is thus prepared, with the illumination notice placed in advance.

After the courtesies, the special seat advances and performs two full prostrations with three bows; this is the tea gratitude. After the head seat departs, the comptroller and superintendent advance with congratulations and gratitude, performing two full prostrations with three bows and departing. The abbot escorts each, advancing, then returns inside. He sits again in the chair, accompanied by incense burning and hall circuit. After the cups are removed, one stick of incense is burned at the center stand. Three strikes of the storehouse drum. All descend from the platforms, universally bow and greet, and return to the abbot's quarters. The head seat, comptroller, and the great assembly below all go up to the abbot's quarters for congratulatory greetings. The abbot is in the western section of the Dharma hall, waiting for the great assembly to come up for greetings and disperse. The four seasonal [observances] are all the same. The attendant must invite the abbot to be seated.

Also in the Sangha hall, a grand tea service. The format is the same as above. When there is a visiting temple's west hall [resident], or when the placard head seat is specially invited, full courtesies should be extended in the invitation.

**Seventeenth Day.** The comptroller alone, or the superintendent alone, again hosts a tea service in the Cloud Hall. If the abbot does not perform the courtesies, the administrative officers also abbreviate. The notice is the same as the fourteenth day's public courtesy notice, with slight changes. It must be written in seven lines. Namely, the notice reads:

"Comptroller bhikṣu so-and-so, this morning after the midday meal, in the Cloud Hall, a tea service, specially for the Head Seat Zen master, to mark the congratulations for commencing the retreat. The great assembly as company.

This month, this day. Comptroller bhikṣu so-and-so. Respectfully announces."

The superintendent's notice is the same. However, it is presented at meal time. Before the wooden fish, he enters the hall, burns incense before the Holy Monk, performs three prostrations, circuits the hall once, and then posts the notice. The courtesies are exactly as the previous day. However, the rest-period bell is not struck; only three strikes of the cloud drum before the storehouse.

**Eighteenth Day.** The head seat performs courtesies, specially for the secretary. This also uses a formal statement. The paper is eight inches. The characters do not exceed five parts. The format and courtesies are exactly like the administrative officer's. But the secretary's invocation is reported at the south-side board head of the bed opposite the administrative officers for the meal. The statement is posted on the lower section board. The statement must be written in nine lines.

A "shell-leak" [seal] is also posted at the top of the statement. It must be on red paper, stamped on the statement. The notice reads:

"Head seat bhikṣu so-and-so, this morning after the midday meal, in the Cloud Hall, a tea service, specially for the Secretary Zen master, to mark the congratulations for commencing the retreat and dwelling together. We humbly request the administrative officers and great assembly to grace us with their company.

This month, this day. Head seat bhikṣu so-and-so. Respectfully announces."

The secretary, library master, and so forth perform courtesies on successive days. The secretary hosts specially for the library master. The library master then hosts specially for the head seat. If there are two head seats for front and rear halls, the front hall hosts specially for the rear hall. All use formal statements. The rector, guest prefect, and others perform courtesies in the rear quarters or illumination hall. During the summer, the quarter master of the assembly quarters performs daily courtesies. Fúróng [Dàokǎi] established free tea for the general assembly. Eihei-ji followed this practice at the sewing place, where the quarter master daily prepares hot water for the general assembly to drink as they wish. Our mountain also follows this practice.

However, for the daily hot water in the assembly quarters, on the first day, the *Guījìng Wén* [Mirror of the Way] is read. On the eleventh day, the Quarters Pure Rules are read. On the twenty-first day, the *Cān Dàjǐ* is read. Each month proceeds thus. During the summer, free days are permitted.

**Twentieth Day.** The board is struck. General work request for seated meditation.

**Fifth Day of the Fifth Month.** Called the Dragon Boat Festival. Dharma hall ascent. The storehouse provides refreshments, perhaps rice dumplings.

**First Day of the Sixth Month.** Called the Mid-Summer Seasonal Day. During the Dharma hall ascent, the reason for suspending seated meditation is announced. Then seated meditation is at one's discretion. The board is simply not struck.

The Śūraṅgama Assembly diagram is posted. Late-arriving assembly members take their original ordination position. If arriving after mid-summer, they again take the novice position. From the end of summer, they take their original ordination position.

At mid-summer, donors may be received. During the sixth month, there is a general work request to air the Sangha hall mats. The mallet is announced, the hall circuited, and the drum struck, as for the Buddha's Birthday general work request. The assembly quarters' mat airing is done at the same time.

From the first of the sixth month, because of perspiration, bathing is every other day. Or during six bathings, one bathing in between. Additionally, there are donor-sponsored bathing days. Also from the first of the sixth month, fans are used in the hall during meals. The method: a fan-stopping placard is hung on the left pillar beside the Holy Monk. After the general signal, the fanning begins. Two or four attendants enter the hall with fans, greet before the Holy Monk, and fan the upper and lower sections separately. After the water bucket is removed, the head attendant strikes the fan-stopping placard once with the fan handle. All attendants, hearing this, stop fanning. They greet as before at the center and exit the hall.

**First Day of the Seventh Month.** Beginning of the season. During the Sangha hall circuit, the abbot performs touching foreheads with the great assembly. From the first of the seventh month, the notice for the feeding of hungry ghosts, merit-connection sūtra reading, is posted in a convenient place. The notice reads:

"At the time of the Dharma King's lifting of the retreat and the monks' self-examination day, the practice has come full circle and the merit is accomplished. At this time, the Buddha expounded the Dharma at Trāyastriṃśa Heaven to repay his mother's kindness. Maudgalyāyana offered food in vessels to save his mother from suffering.

Alas! The Venerable Maudgalyāyana, having attained supernatural powers, saw where his mother had been reborn and rescued her. But we, with our Dharma-eyes blinded, cannot see where the benefactors of this life have been reborn, nor know what sufferings they endure or in which evil destiny they dwell. The Buddha said: People in this world create many sins for the sake of their children and fall into the three evil paths, enduring long suffering.

Thus, the suffering of both them and us is caused by our karma. If we do not rescue them, they will sink forever; when would it end? Therefore, each should abide in a mind of compassion and earnest grief, chanting Mahāyāna sūtras, secret dhāraṇī, and the precious names of the honored ones, transferring the merit to all beings in the ten realms, perfecting the ground of reward, and ceasing the afflictions of suffering. Items are listed below:

Sūtra of the Lotus of the Wondrous Dharma of the Great Vehicle
Brahmajāla Bodhisattva Precepts Sūtra
Ullambana Sūtra
Sūtra of the Buddha's Bequeathed Teaching
Such-and-such sūtra, such-and-such dhāraṇī

Fourth year of Genkō, seventh month. Sangha hall officer bhikṣu so-and-so, respectfully encourages."

**Thirteenth Day of the Seventh Month.** Assembly quarters sūtra chanting, as at the start of summer:

"We humbly state: The golden wind fans across the fields; the White Emperor presides over this quarter. This is the time when the Awakened Emperor lifts the retreat, the day when the Dharma-year has come full circle. Ninety days without difficulty. The entire assembly at peace. We chant the myriad virtues' great names and transfer the merit to the entire assembly of the hall's true sovereigns. We humbly rely on the great assembly's recitation."

In the evening, the feeding of hungry ghosts is offered. The storehouse manages this. Four large banners and twenty-five small banners. Silver coins, memorials, and such are all the rector's responsibility. The large banners stand at the four directions. The small banners stand on the offering plates. The small banners are of five colors. Inscriptions read: [as appropriate].

In the open ground before the hall, offering stands with paired offerings are set up; one of rice, one of confections. Bowls filled with water and bush clover.

When the time comes, the rector has the attendant announce the feeding of hungry ghosts. The bell is struck and the assembly gathers. The rector steps forward, burns incense, and requests the abbot and both ranks with greetings. After greetings, he also requests the one who will perform the food offering. The abbot or head seat or another elder leads this.

The rector chants the text. With a large fist, pressing the left hip, the right hand forms the giving mudrā. He consecrates the food and sprinkles water. For the water-sprinkling mantra, first the right hand forms the water mudrā; the thumb is held, the four fingers are extended together. During the seven-fold consecration, after the third time, the right hand takes the bush clover and sprinkles water. At the mouth-opening mantra, snap the fingers three times. At the giving mantra, take the rice and toss it onto the ground. At the summoning of the five Buddhas for witnessing, join palms and continue through the dedication.

"In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Northern Route, Noto Province, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Monastery, founded by so-and-so and others. On the fourteenth of this month, reverently encountering the time when the monks' self-examination and Dharma-year have come full circle, we respectfully lead the assembly present to the precious presence of the Awakened Emperor, to prepare a modest offering and universally bestow the merit of Ganges-sand [good deeds], adding Dharma-giving to rescue the various classes of beings.

We humbly state: The mountain of karma is deep and dark, the light of sun and moon cannot illuminate it. The ocean of suffering has treacherous waves, the power of the wise and holy cannot cross it. The ladder of wisdom seems without means; the boat of compassion seems to have lost its skill. Brahmā and Śakra cannot manage it; the Buddhas can only clasp their hands in helplessness.

The Buddha turned to skillful means and borrowed the spiritual power of the assembled monks. Maudgalyāyana aided his grieving mother and rescued her from the extreme suffering of starvation. How worthy is the spiritual power of peaceful dwelling practiced in harmony! It has already transcended the Buddha-wisdom of the Awakened Emperors of the three times. Without doubt, the accumulated merit of the harmonious sangha instantly extinguishes the aggregate of evil of those with fixed karmic suffering.

The Ullambana offering began from this. The great feeding of hungry ghosts is diligently practiced. Long has it been so. Therefore, the power of the sūtras and dhāraṇī gradually cultivated by each person over these days gradually startles the ocean of karmic suffering in this and other worlds. Tonight, in perfect and sudden thusness, the majestic dhāraṇī of the mind-king suddenly collapses the mountain of floating dust of delusive thought and actual suffering.

If so, then the present modest offering satisfies the starving classes of Ganges-sand worlds. All assembled beings receive the inexhaustible and boundless Dharma-offering, are instantly satisfied by the taste of the unconditioned true aspect, and swiftly roam in the ease of free delight. The living and the dead are equally guided; enemies and kin are universally benefited. The chanting is as follows:

Sūtra of the Lotus of the Wondrous Dharma of the Great Vehicle.
Such-and-such sūtra.

May they be satisfied by the Dharma-taste. May all generate right wisdom. May all heavy and light karmas be liberated. May the seen and unseen both benefit. May all together perfect the omniscient wisdom. The Buddhas of the ten directions and three times..." and so forth.

May they be satisfied by the Dharma-taste. May right wisdom be opened and developed. May all be broadly liberated."

**Eighteenth Day.** Those departing submit their requests. They go to the rector's quarters to request a certificate of summer completion. A small notice:

When registering at various mountains, one carries the summer-completion certificate for hanging-bag registration. In the Song dynasty, those without a certificate were not allowed to register. Although this custom is not yet practiced in Japan, it should be practiced at this mountain. Those who depart without requesting leave should not be granted re-entry. **Twenty-Eighth Day of the Eighth Month.** Anniversary of Eihei-ji [founder]. Offerings are prepared at the Patriarch's Hall, as for the Bodhidharma anniversary. The offerings are transmitted, incense is burned, and prostrations are made. The abbot kneels at the censer. The rector reads the memorial:

"In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Noto Province, Kashima District, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Monastery, founded by the newly-precept bhikṣu so-and-so and others. On the twenty-eighth of this month, we reverently commemorate the distant anniversary of Eihei-ji's Founding Master. We have respectfully prepared incense, flowers, coarse tea, and modest offerings, and chant the Buddha-Crown Śūraṅgama Divine Dhāraṇī. The vast virtue collected is transferred and offered to the Great Preceptor of Eihei-ji, to repay the kindness of the Dharma-milk.

We humbly state: The waters of the Dòng Cave flowed in reverse; the waves of the great ocean became thunder. The Yellow Dragon's lightning struck; the clouds and rain of all under heaven became nourishing moisture. One drop of the Cáo Source, when it touched down, the stream of Dharma branches flourished abundantly. The tender cassia of the twin trees gave shade, and their branches and boughs grew luxuriantly.

The house-style of the Five Houses, there was none he did not penetrate. The essentials of the Seven Schools, he comprehended them all. He widely studied under the eminent masters of both Japan and China, and extensively investigated the sūtras and teachings of both exoteric and esoteric [traditions]. A hero for a hundred generations. A model for a thousand ages. Is this not our progenitor of the land of the rising sun, the Founding Master of Eihei-ji?

Illuminating the first heaven, his brilliance exceeded that of the sun and moon in the eyes. Breaking through the great-thousand [worlds], he turned the Dharma wheel more wonderfully than the wheel-treasure. In Jambudvīpa, the Great Land of Japan, Noto Province, Kashima District, Sakai Estate, Tōkoku Mountain, Yōkō Monastery, founded by so-and-so and others. On the fifth of this month, we reverently commemorate the occasion of the passing of the Progenitor, the Great Master Bodhidharma. We have prepared incense, flowers, lamps, and candles to express our earnest offering. We prostrate as if in his presence. We chant the Great Buddha-Crown, Myriad-Practice Śūraṅgama Divine Dhāraṇī. The extraordinary merit collected is offered to repay the kindness of the Dharma-milk.

We humbly state: The sun of wisdom sank in the west on Vulture Peak; nearly a thousand years. The cloud of Dharma gathered in the east over the Divine Land, crossing more than a hundred thousand leagues. Thus the white horse first brought the auspicious sign to the Han dynasty. The red bird itself continued the propitious omen at the Wú court. Dragon-script was translated, and the sūtras and teachings were circulated. The sections of commentaries were as numerous as stars. The net of names and marks spread like clouds.

Yet what seemed similar was merely superficial, and the authentic was hard to distinguish from the counterfeit. Then our Progenitor, the Great Master Bodhidharma of Shàolín, whose compassionate heart embraced distant descendants, and whose master's command extended to remote corners; he did not shrink from the daunting waves of the great ocean and first traveled to the land of Liáng.

Not meeting accord with old Emperor Xiāo, he withdrew to the realm of Wèi. He crossed the river on a single reed and completed nine years at Shàolín. He solely transmitted the mind-seal and directly indicated the school's guiding principle.

The raising of the flower on the seat, Mahākāśyapa's orthodox transmission found its place. The standing in snow in the courtyard, Huìkě's attainment of the marrow was without doubt. The thunder of the Dharma shook throughout the nine provinces; the wind of the Way fanned across the four seas.

Yet when the day came that a single sandal was seen returning westward, how could we forget the diligence of arranging the pure meal while facing north? Now, looking up to the deep and weighty virtue of the Honored One, we remotely express our sincere ceremony as if in his presence. We humbly hope that he will not withhold his coming, and will graciously savor our modest offering.

We acknowledge that we are vessels as small as a ladle, with lives as tiny as ants. Though we bend the bow of Shí Gǒng, we cannot hit the target of the deer. Though we lower the hook of Xuánshā, we have never yet caught a fish. We humbly tend the distant flame of the transmitted lamp and offer it before the true presence of the ancient patriarch.

We humbly request that the great vow be directed and that true compassion be without obstruction. May he condescend to pull our ears and enable us to attain peace of mind. May we have clear understanding and swiftly turn the light around. Without relying on practice and realization, may we suddenly awaken and become Buddhas.

Then: May the river serve as a sash and the mountain as a crown, praying that the imperial treasure-throne north of the palace endure without end. May clouds be like cranes and rain like balm, nourishing the people of the southern fields with bountiful harvests.

Respectfully submitted. May the Progenitor graciously accept; may the Patriarchs witness.

Respectfully submitted. Fourth year of Genkō, fifth day of the tenth month. Twenty-seventh generation Dharma grandson, heir-bhikṣu so-and-so and others. Respectfully submitted."

We humbly state: The swarming sentient classes, without great compassion, there is no salvation. The benighted masses of beings, apart from the Buddha's sūtras, how could there be liberation? Ants drown in the water on the hands of one who holds a sūtra. Snails die under the staff of one who listens to the Dharma. Both are freed from the animal realm and reborn in Trāyastriṃśa Heaven.

How much more so when the five hundred holy ones were formerly five hundred bats, and ten thousand swimming fish are now ten thousand arhats. Karmic connections through eternal kalpas are never destroyed. Seeds planted over accumulated lifetimes come to fruition.
