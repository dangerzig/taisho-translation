# 普達王經
## Sūtra of King Pūrṇadatta

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0522

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Honored One was traveling in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jeta grove, in the Anāthapiṇḍada park, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time, there was a king of the land of Fuyān, named Pūrṇadatta, who governed the vassal states, and tribute came from all four directions. The king himself honored the Buddha and revered the Dharma, and had never been partial or unjust. He always had a compassionate mind, grieving for the foolish people who did not know the Three Honored Ones. Whenever a purificatory day arrived, he would ascend to a high terrace to burn incense, and then turn and touch his face to the ground, bowing in reverence. The ministers and people of the country found the king's behavior strange and discussed it among themselves: "The king holds the most honored position among all the people. Near and far submit to him in awe. His word is obeyed. What petition or desire could he have, that he humiliates his dignity, pressing his face to the ground?" The ministers discussed this repeatedly, wishing to remonstrate but not daring.

The king ordered his ministers to prepare the carriages for a journey. The king then set out with several thousand officials and people. He had barely left the palace when, unexpectedly, he saw a renunciant on the road. The king immediately descended from his carriage, removed his canopy, halted his retinue, and prostrated with his face to the ground, making obeisance to the renunciant. He then followed the renunciant back, arranged a meal, and thus did not complete his journey. The ministers then remonstrated: "The great king is of the highest honor! How can it be appropriate, on the open road, for you to prostrate with your face to the ground before a begging renunciant? In all the world, nothing is more precious than the head and face. Moreover, as the sovereign of the country, you are not like other men."

The king then commanded his ministers to obtain the heads of dead persons, as well as the heads of oxen, horses, pigs, and sheep. The ministers went out to search for them, and after some days, succeeded. They reported to the king: "As instructed, we have obtained heads of dead persons and of the six kinds of livestock."

The king said: "Sell them at the market." The ministers had the heads sold. The heads of oxen, horses, pigs, and sheep all found buyers, but the human heads remained unsold.

The king said: "Sell them at any price, cheap or dear, just so they sell. If they still do not sell, give them away to people." Thus some days passed. They could not be sold, and no one would take them even as a gift. The heads all swelled up and reeked, too foul to approach. The king then grew angry and said to his ministers: "Earlier you remonstrated with me, saying: 'The human head is the most precious; you must not disgrace it by pressing your face to the ground before a renunciant.' Yet now, when the livestock heads have all been sold, why will no one take the human heads, even as a gift?"

The king then commanded his ministers to prepare the carriages. They would go outside the city to the open fields, where the king had something to inquire about. The ministers and people were all shaken with trepidation, not knowing the king's purpose. The king then led his retinue out of the city. He told the assembled ministers: "Do you remember, in the time of my late father, a young boy who always held the parasol for him?"

The ministers replied: "We do indeed recall such a person."

The king said: "Where is this boy now?" They answered: "He died long ago, more than seventeen years past."

The king said: "What sort of person was this boy?"

They answered: "We always observed that he served the late king with purificatory fasting and respectful conduct, honest and self-restrained, never speaking what was improper."

The king said to his ministers: "If you now saw the clothing that this boy wore in his lifetime, would you recognize it?" The ministers replied: "Though it was long ago, we would still recognize it."

The king glanced at an attendant and ordered him to rush back to the inner treasury and find the deceased boy's clothing. In a moment the clothing arrived. The king said: "Is this the clothing?" The ministers replied: "It truly is."

The king said: "If you now saw the boy himself, would you recognize him?" The ministers were all silent. After a long pause, they answered: "We fear our eyes are dim. Seeing him suddenly, we might not be able to tell."

Just as the king was about to reveal the story of his own past life, the renunciant he had previously seen arrived. The king was overjoyed. He rose and prostrated with his face to the ground, making obeisance to the renunciant. The ministers were all delighted. The renunciant took his seat. The king, with palms joined, said: "Earlier, when I suddenly met the renunciant on the road, I turned back and followed him. I was then criticized and ridiculed by my ministers, who remonstrated: 'The head and face are the most precious parts of a person. Moreover, as the sovereign of the country, lord of all the people, to whom all the four directions pay homage, what petition or desire could you have, that you prostrate before a begging renunciant?' I then commanded that livestock heads and human heads be sold together at the market. The livestock heads all found buyers, but no one would buy the human heads; and no one would even take them as gifts, though these are what such people call precious. Today I have formally come out, also wishing to show them the whole story. If it please you, may the renunciant condescend to open the minds of the ignorant ministers and people of this country, so that they may know the true Dharma, and reveal a bridge for them."

The renunciant then told the ministers the story of the king's past life: "If you wish to know about the king, he was originally the parasol-bearing boy in the service of the late king. He once accompanied the late king in observing the purificatory fast for one day, practicing the right Dharma, keeping the mind pure and disciplined, not committing any evil. After his death, his spirit was reborn as a son to the king. The honor and eminence he now enjoys all come from the merit of observing the purificatory fast in his past life."

All the ministers, great and small, unanimously said: "We are fortunate to have encountered the renunciant. May you continue to have compassion on us in our ignorance. We beg to become your disciples."

The renunciant told the ministers and people: "I have a great teacher. You should go to him and ask your questions."

The ministers said: "We wish to hear about the great teacher and what he practices. Even if it costs us our lives, we wish to pay our respects to him in person and receive his Dharma teaching."

The renunciant said: "My teacher is called the Buddha. He can fly through the air, and a halo of light crowns his head. He can divide his body and reassemble it, transforming in ten thousand ways. He has thirty-two extraordinary marks and eighty refinements of beauty. He is the sovereign of heaven, earth, and the twelve thousand worlds. He walks alone in the three realms, with none his equal. His disciples are pure and are called śramaṇas. What he teaches delivers and liberates without fail."

The ministers then said to the renunciant: "Can the Buddha be seen?"

The renunciant replied: "Very good! I shall go and inform the Honored One."

The ministers asked the renunciant: "Where is the Buddha now? How far is it from here?"

The renunciant replied: "More than six thousand miles." In the time it took to speak these words, the renunciant flew to the land of Śrāvastī and informed the Buddha in full: "The people of that country are deeply pitiable. They now sincerely wish to see the Buddha. May you bestow your great compassion and reveal the true path."

The Buddha remained silent, and then called Ānanda: "Command the bhikṣus: tomorrow we shall go to the land of Fuyān."

Ānanda proclaimed the Buddha's instruction and then reported to the Buddha: "What should be the manner of our journey tomorrow?"

The Buddha said: "When we are about to arrive, I will manifest my supernatural power."

Then the Buddha, together with the bhikṣus, set out. When they were still some tens of miles away, the king and all the ministers, following the renunciant, went out of the city with flowers and incense to welcome the Buddha. Beholding the Buddha's majestic supernatural presence, they were at once joyful and awed. They prostrated with all five limbs and touched their foreheads to the ground in reverence, and welcomed the Buddha up into the palace to take his seat.

The king knelt and, with palms joined, said to the Buddha: "We have troubled the World-Honored One and the sangha to come from so far to this place."

The king and ministers were anxious lest their preparations prove insufficient. The Buddha, knowing their thoughts, said to Ānanda: "Tell the king not to worry about the preparations. Wherever the Buddha goes, how could anything be lacking?"

The king offered the meal with all his heart, pouring the drinks with his own hands. When the meal was finished, water for washing was served. When the blessings had been spoken, the Buddha smiled, and a five-colored light shone from his mouth. Ānanda straightened his robes, made obeisance to the Buddha, and said: "The Buddha does not smile without reason; there must be something he wishes to say."

The Buddha said: "Do you wish to know the story of King Pūrṇadatta and the renunciant?"

Ānanda said: "I wish to hear."

The Buddha said: "Long ago, in the time of Mahāmuni Buddha, the king was the son of a great family. His father made offerings to the Three Honored Ones. The father instructed his son to pass the incense. At that time, there was an attendant who, in his mind, looked down on the boy and did not give him the incense. The echo of sin and merit responded accordingly, and so this retribution was incurred. Though for a time he served as an attendant, he never forgot to practice the Dharma, and now he has become a king, governing the people. He should know how things come about and be careful never to be partial. The renunciant was originally the attendant of that time. At that time he did not receive the incense, yet though he did not receive it, his mind held no resentment. He immediately made a vow: 'If I attain the path, I shall save this person.' The merit and the vow came together in fruition, and now he has come to save the king and all the people."

When the king heard the Buddha explain the story of his past life, his mind was opened and he immediately attained the fruit of stream-entry. All the people of the country, hearing the sūtra, received the five precepts and the ten virtues, making this their constant practice.

At that time, the fourfold assembly of disciples, having heard the sūtra, were filled with joy. They came forward, made obeisance to the Buddha, and departed.
