# Annotated Essay on the Gold Lion of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra

## *Huáyán Jīng Jīn Shīzi Zhāng Zhù* (華嚴經金師子章註)

Taishō 1881

Original essay by Fǎzàng (*Fǎzàng*, 法藏) of the Tang dynasty. Annotated by Chéngqiān (*Chéngqiān*, 承遷) of the Song dynasty.

*Note: This text contains two layers: (1) the celebrated "Essay on the Gold Lion" (Jīn Shīzi Zhāng) by Fǎzàng (643-712 CE), the third patriarch of the Huáyán school, and (2) annotations by the Song-dynasty monk Chéngqiān. Fǎzàng's essay is one of the most famous short texts in Chinese Buddhist philosophy. According to tradition, Fǎzàng composed it at the request of Empress Wǔ Zétiān, using a golden lion statue in the palace as an analogy to explain the core doctrines of the Avataṃsaka (Huáyán) Sūtra. The gold represents the dharma-realm principle (lǐ), and the lion represents phenomenal things (shì). Through ten sections, Fǎzàng explains: (1) dependent origination, (2) the relationship of form and emptiness, (3) the three natures of Yogācāra, (4) the signlessness of reality, (5) the non-arising of dharmas, (6) the five teachings (a Huáyán doxography), (7) the ten mysterious gates (shíxuán mén, the hallmark Huáyán doctrine of the mutual interpenetration and infinite reflection of all phenomena), (8) the six characteristics (liùxiàng), (9) the attainment of bodhi, and (10) entry into nirvāṇa. Chéngqiān's annotations include a lengthy preface extolling the unique virtues of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra and the Huáyán school, followed by phrase-by-phrase glosses on Fǎzàng's text. The essay remains one of the most accessible introductions to Huáyán philosophy.*

## Preface to the Gold Lion Essay (by Chéngqiān)

[Chéngqiān's preface consists of a series of rhetorical questions extolling the unique features of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra and the Huáyán school:]

The dragon-elephants tread upon heaven and earth, opening the treasury of profundity and teaching the wondrous Dharma... Of all the teachings spoken by the Tathāgata, is there any teaching within which this [Huáyán] teaching is not found? Is there any teaching that, at the initial stage of the ten faiths, completes the work of all fifty-two stages and realizes the patience of non-arising? Is there any teaching that speaks of the "one" that encompasses all three worlds (sentient-being world, material world, and wisdom world)? Is there any sūtra whose setting is a pure land, wondrous and adorned? Is there any sūtra that does not proceed through principle alone but reveals the nature through phenomena?

Only this single sūtra possesses all these virtues...

The "Essay on the Gold Lion" (*Jīn Shīzi Zhāng*) was composed at the request of Empress Zétiān by the Great Master [Fǎzàng]. Its text is concise yet its meaning is deep; its words are few yet its purport is vast. It is like extracting the great sūtra-roll from a tiny mote of dust, or compressing the great void to fit within a single hair.

[The preface concludes with meditation instructions: sit in full or half-lotus posture, follow the breath, and practice the non-dual contemplation. "When a thought arises, follow it. Not arousing thoughts is itself having thoughts: when would birth-and-death ever cease? Arising thoughts and following them is non-thought: this is called great nirvāṇa."]

## Essay on the Gold Lion (by Fǎzàng)

The gold lion (*jīn shīzi*) serves as an analogy for the Dharma. Investigating its original source, it has [this significance]: the Tang master Xiánshǒu [Fǎzàng], facing the Sage-Emperor Zétiān, used it to demonstrate the essential purport of the great sūtra [Avataṃsaka]. The gold symbolizes the essence of the dharma-realm; the lion symbolizes the function of the dharma-realm. Now, following the Dharma through the analogy, I briefly open ten gates of analysis, so that principle and phenomena may be fused, and the one and the many may be unobstructed.

---

### 1. Clarifying Dependent Origination

The myriad dharmas have no self-nature; they are established through the coming together of conditions. Without causes and conditions, dharmas do not arise. As the sūtra says: "All dharmas arise from conditions; without conditions, they do not arise."

The gold has no self-nature (analogizing the true principle as unchanging). Following the conditions of the skilled artisan, the form of a lion arises (analogizing the true principle following conditions to produce phenomenal dharmas). Since the arising is solely through conditions, it is called "dependent origination." The gold symbolizes the one true nature; the lion symbolizes the phenomenal dharmas of dependent origination. Principle is originally without arising; following various conditions, it produces differentiated characteristics. Though characteristics arise in profusion, principle remains without arising.

---

### 2. Distinguishing Form and Emptiness

The lion's form is empty (analogizing form and emptiness). There is only the true gold (analogizing the reality of the nature). The lion does not [truly] exist (analogizing that dependently-arisen phenomenal forms are not truly existent). The gold-substance is not non-existent (analogizing that true emptiness permanently abides). Therefore it is called "form and emptiness."

Emptiness has no self-nature and is clarified in terms of form; it does not obstruct the existence of illusory phenomena. Form is clarified in terms of true emptiness; true emptiness is formless. The Dharma is originally beyond words, yet through words the Way is revealed.

---

### 3. The Three Natures

(1) The "universally imagined nature" (*parikalpita*): The lion is emotionally [perceived as] real. All sentient beings, from beginningless time, through habitual afflictions and confusion, universally imagine and calculate, [supposing] that dharmas outside the mind are real.

(2) The "other-dependent nature" (*paratantra*): The lion appears to exist. All sentient beings, relying on the true, give rise to the false; this seemingly-existent dharma, falsely grasped through dependence on others, is not real internally or externally.

(3) The "perfectly accomplished nature" (*pariniṣpanna*): The gold-nature does not change. Perfect and not diminished, accomplished and not increased: though the lion's form may differ, the gold does not change accordingly. Since both [aspects] are present, it constitutes the perfectly accomplished ultimate nature. As the sūtra says: "From the 'non-abiding' basis, all dharmas are established."

---

### 4. Revealing Signlessness

When the gold encompasses the lion completely (principle takes over phenomena), outside the gold there is no lion to be found (the dependently-arisen phenomenal dharma is inherently empty). Therefore it is called "signlessness."

---

### 5. Teaching Non-Arising

When one correctly sees the lion arising, it is only the gold that arises (true principle follows conditions to create phenomenal dharmas). Outside the gold there is not a single thing (apart from true principle there are no separate phenomenal characteristics). Though the lion has arising and ceasing, the gold-substance originally has no increase or decrease. Therefore it is called "non-arising."

---

### 6. The Five Teachings

**(1) The Śrāvaka Teaching:** This lion is a dharma of dependent origination, arising and ceasing moment by moment (analogizing all phenomenal dharmas arising from conditions, each without self-nature, without any cessation). There is truly no lion to be found: this is called the Śrāvaka Teaching.

**(2) The Mahāyāna Beginning Teaching:** This lion, being a dharma of dependent origination, has no self-nature whatsoever and is thoroughly and utterly empty.

**(3) The Mahāyāna Final Teaching:** Though thoroughly empty, this does not obstruct the clear presence of illusory dharmas. Though the gold is solely one, the lion's form still exists. The two characteristics of dependent arising and provisional existence both stand.

**(4) The Mahāyāna Sudden Teaching:** The two characteristics mutually negate each other and both vanish. Emotional fabrication does not remain; both [existence and emptiness] are without force. Emptiness and existence are both extinguished; the path of language is cut off; the mind has nowhere to rest. It directly discerns that the true nature is originally empty.

**(5) The One Vehicle Perfect Teaching:** Emotional [projections onto] the lion are exhausted; the substance is revealed, forming a complete whole. The great function arises in full abundance, and everything is entirely true. All the myriad phenomena are in profusion, yet each is in its own place without confusion. The one is all, and all are the same in having no [fixed] nature. The one is also all, and cause and effect are distinct. Force and function mutually embrace; extension and contraction are free. Therefore it is called the One Vehicle Perfect Teaching.

---

### 7. The Ten Mysterious Gates

**(1) Simultaneous complete correspondence:** Gold and lion are established simultaneously, perfectly complete.

**(2) Mutual containment of the one and many without sameness:** Gold and lion contain each other; the one and many are unobstructed; yet each abides in its own place.

**(3) Simultaneous establishment of the hidden and the manifest:** Seeing the lion, one sees only the lion and no gold (the lion is manifest, the gold is hidden). Seeing the gold, one sees only gold and no lion (the gold is manifest, the lion is hidden). Seeing both at once, both are manifest. The hidden is secret; the manifest is revealed.

**(4) The Indra's-net realm:** In the lion's eyes, ears, limbs, joints, and each individual hair, there is a complete gold lion. In each hair, all lions simultaneously and completely appear, with infinite lions in each hair. Layer upon layer, endlessly, like the heavenly pearls of Indra's net.

**(5) The pure and mixed storehouse of complete virtue:** If the eye encompasses the entire lion, everything is purely eye. If the ear encompasses the entire lion, everything is purely ear. When all faculties simultaneously encompass each other, each is both pure and mixed, forming the perfectly complete storehouse.

**(6) The mutual identity of all dharmas, freely:** Each and every hair, being gold, encompasses the entire lion. The eye is the ear, the ear is the nose: unobstructed and unhindered.

**(7) The establishment of the subtly fine in mutual containment:** Gold and lion, whether hidden or manifest, one or many, established or scattered, all exist simultaneously. Each is in the other; principal and companion illuminate each other. All manifested dharmas, like the ocean-seal, are clearly present.

**(8) Different accomplishment through the separation of dharmas across ten time-periods:** The lion is a conditioned dharma, arising and ceasing moment by moment. Within a kṣaṇa, three periods are distinguished: past, future, and present. Three threes make nine periods, which together with the one [moment that unifies them] form ten. Though the ten periods are separated, they mutually accomplish each other, interpenetrating without obstruction, together forming a single thought-moment.

**(9) Good accomplishment through the mind's turning:** Gold and lion, whether manifest or hidden, one or many, have no self-nature. Through the mind's turning, dharmas have no fixed nature but transform according to the mind.

**(10) Revealing dharmas through phenomena to generate understanding:** The lion represents ignorance; the gold-substance fully reveals the true nature. When principle and phenomena are combined, this illuminates the ālayavijñāna and generates correct understanding.

---

### 8. The Six Characteristics

The lion is the "total" characteristic. The five faculties being different is the "particular" characteristic. Sharing a single dependent origination is the "identical" characteristic. Eyes, ears, and so on not reaching each other is the "different" characteristic. The faculties combining is the "accomplished" characteristic. Each condition abiding in its own place is the "disintegrated" characteristic. This shows that no dharma in the dharma-realm stands alone: whenever one characteristic is raised, it possesses all six characteristics of dependent origination.

---

### 9. Attaining Bodhi

"Bodhi" means the Way and Awakening. When the eye sees the lion, all conditioned dharmas, without waiting to be destroyed, are originally quiescent (the lion's form is unreal; it is entirely gold and indestructible). Departing from all grasping and relinquishing, one enters the ocean of omniscient wisdom (*sarvajñā*) through this very path. Therefore it is called "the Way." Immediately understanding that the inversions from beginningless time were originally without reality: this is called "Awakening." Ultimately possessing all-knowledge (*sarvākārajñatā*): this is called "attaining bodhi."

---

### 10. Entering Nirvāṇa

Seeing that the two characteristics of lion and gold are both exhausted, afflictions do not arise. Whether beautiful or ugly appears before one, body and mind are at rest. The mind is calm like the ocean. Deluded thoughts are completely exhausted; there is no oppression. Liberated from entanglements, forever departing from the source of suffering: this is called "entering nirvāṇa."

Delusion exhausted, the whole is true; without any oppression, freely and spontaneously liberated from entanglements. Māras and afflictions cannot invade. Obstacles overcome, dharma-gates without impediment, tranquil and serene, always peaceful and joyful, benefiting sentient beings and delivering them from the source of suffering.

As Sēngzhào said: "Merit flows through ten thousand ages and permanently abides; the Way penetrates a hundred kalpas and grows ever more firm. The creation of a mountain depends on the first basket [of earth]; the cultivation of the path relies upon the first step. This is because the work of merit cannot be rotted."

The three-vehicle teaching has sequence and stages; the perfectly sudden principle is wholly interpenetrating and unobstructed. Understanding one dharma, one understands all dharmas. Within one practice, all practices are complete. Cause encompasses the ocean of fruition; fruition penetrates the source of cause. Fruition is the fruition of the cause; cause is the cause of the fruition. Equal and without duality. As the sūtra says: "The equal, true dharma-realm: no buddha, no sentient being."
