# 佛般泥洹經
## Sūtra of the Buddha's Parinirvāṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0005

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Bó Fǎzǔ (白法祖)

---

Uphold these seven sets of seven; forty-nine precepts in all; as all the waters of the world: small streams flow into great streams, great streams flow into rivers, and rivers flow into the sea. Bhikṣus! Be like water flowing into the sea; if you do not cease in the practice of the path, you will surely attain the path of arahantship."

Buddha departed from the kingdom of Rājagṛha and called to Ānanda: "Let us go to Pāṭaligāma." Ānanda replied: "Yes." They set out from Magadha, but had not yet reached Pāṭaligāma. Along the way was an intermediate village called Koṭigāma. When Buddha arrived there, he called to the assembly of bhikṣus: "All listen." The bhikṣus replied: "We receive your instruction."

Buddha said: "In the world there are four sufferings that the Buddha knows and people do not. Because people do not know them, birth and death do not cease and there is no time of rest. What are the four? The suffering of birth, the suffering of aging, the suffering of illness, and the suffering of death. Because people do not contemplate these four sufferings but endure them by force, birth and death are unceasing and have no time of rest. For this reason the Buddha has proclaimed these four sufferings to teach people. Although one has father, mother, wife, and children, one must part from all of them; they grieve and weep for one another without end. All that one hates is seen before one's eyes day after day. For this reason the Buddha has expounded the scriptures: one should depart from these four sufferings and uphold the eight precepts, for even the body may be wearied of."

Buddha said: "First: receive the words of the Buddha. Second: distance oneself from desire and attachment, take up the path, and be free from greed and contention. Third: do not speak falsehood, flowery speech, duplicitous speech, or harsh speech. Fourth: do not kill, steal, or violate another's spouse. Fifth: do not harbor jealousy, anger, or delusion. Sixth: sit and contemplate the four sufferings, holding them in mind. Seventh: contemplate that the body is altogether impure. Eighth: regard both birth-and-death and the body as equal to earth. Buddhas of the past also contemplated these four sufferings; Buddhas of the future will also contemplate these four sufferings. Buddhas of the past also set forth these eight precepts; Buddhas of the future will also set forth these eight precepts. You should contemplate the deep meaning of the Buddha's scriptures. Bhikṣus! Those who are mindful of father, mother, wife, and children, and who are mindful of worldly livelihood, cannot attain the path of transcendence. Those who delight in the worldly mind do not delight in the path. The path arises from the mind; those whose minds are correct can attain the path. Those whose minds are moderately rectified can ascend to the heavens. Those who are illumined by the scriptures can be reborn as human beings. One should cut off the paths to hell, the realm of animals, and the realm of hungry ghosts. The Buddha has rectified the path of birth and death for all the world. Bhikṣus, you should contemplate this."

Buddha departed from the village of Koṭigāma and called to Ānanda: "Let us go to Pāṭaligāma." Ānanda replied: "Yes." They thereupon set out. At that time the assembly of bhikṣus numbered one thousand two hundred and fifty. Buddha arrived at Pāṭaligāma and sat beneath a tree. The spirits of Pāṭaligāma thereupon went to inform the brahmin householders. All came out; some carrying seats, some carrying carpets, some carrying lamps; and all went to where Buddha was. They came forward, made obeisance to Buddha, and withdrew to sit at one side.

Buddha told the householders: "Among people in the world, those who are greedy and indulge themselves have five misfortunes. What are the five? First, their wealth and property diminish day by day. Second, they do not know the meaning of the path. Third, they are not respected by others and have regrets at the time of death. Fourth, their ugly name and evil reputation are heard far and wide throughout the world. Fifth, at death they enter the three evil paths of hell. Those who can subdue their minds and do not indulge themselves have five blessings. What are the five? First, their wealth and property increase day by day. Second, they have conduct in the path. Third, they are respected by others and have no regrets until death. Fourth, their good name and fine reputation are heard far and wide throughout the world. Fifth, at death they are born in a place of superior merit and virtue. Not indulging oneself brings these five blessings; contemplate this yourselves." When Buddha finished expounding the scriptures to the householders, all were joyful, made obeisance to Buddha, and departed.

Buddha proceeded to the village of Āmbaṭṭhikā and sat beneath a tree. With the eye of the path he beheld the celestial beings above, and charged the good and wholesome spirits to guard and protect this place. Rising from his seated meditation, Buddha emerged from Āmbaṭṭhikā and sat down elsewhere. The Venerable Ānanda, adjusting his robes, arose from his seat, bowed with his head to Buddha's feet, and stood to one side. Buddha asked Ānanda: "Who has planned this Pāṭaligāma and is building its walls?" He replied: "The great minister of Magadha, Varṣakāra, has planned and built this city, wishing to cut off and obstruct Vajjī."

Buddha said: "Excellent! Ānanda, such is the wisdom of Varṣakāra that he knows how to plan this. I have seen the wonderful celestials of Trāyastriṃśa Heaven together guarding this place. Wherever land is guarded and protected by the celestial beings of heaven, that land will surely be peaceful and prosperous. Moreover, this place is the center of heaven. The celestial ruler over the four quarters of this land is named Benevolent Intention. Where Benevolent Intention guards, the kingdom will long endure and grow ever more excellent, will surely have many sages and wise counselors, will be unsurpassed by other kingdoms, and none will be able to destroy it. When this city of Pāṭaliputra is to be destroyed, it will be through three things: first, great fire; second, great flood; third, those within conspiring with those without. Only by these means will this city be destroyed."

The great minister Varṣakāra, hearing that Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus had journeyed from Magadha and arrived here, thereupon departed from Pāṭaligāma with five hundred carriages in imposing royal splendor and went to where Buddha was. He came forward, made obeisance to Buddha, withdrew and sat to one side, and then addressed Buddha: "Tomorrow, may I invite you and the assembly of bhikṣus to take a meal at my residence?" Buddha silently gave no response. Varṣakāra asked this three times. In the Buddha's rule, silence is equivalent to assent. Varṣakāra then departed, prepared his residence, set out seats and beds for Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus, lit lamps and fires, and prepared the food.

The next day, Varṣakāra went to invite Buddha. Buddha went together with the one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. When the meal was finished, Buddha recited a benediction: "May you attain the path; do not take delight in the position of a royal minister. Although in this present life you cannot be separated from officials and government, because you have now provided a meal for the Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus, may you in future lives be freed from officials and government. Those of wisdom in this world should provide meals and food to worthy and virtuous men of the path. When men of the path recite benedictions, they do not abandon official service; but in seeking office one must not have a greedy mind, a cruel mind, an advancing mind, a pleasure-seeking mind, or a flattering mind. Departing from these five mental states in serving the government, one may proceed without misfortune, and after death may be freed from the sins of hell. Varṣakāra! Contemplate this yourself." The minister replied: "Yes, I receive your instruction." Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus all arose and departed. When Buddha left through the city gate, Varṣakāra followed behind, watching through which gate Buddha departed, wishing to name the gate through which Buddha had passed "the Buddha Gate," and the small stream he crossed "the Buddha Stream."

Buddha arrived at the riverside. At that time, many of the common people wished to cross; some on large vessels, some in small boats, some crossing on bamboo rafts. Buddha sat and thought: "When I had not yet become a Buddha, to cross this water I rode rafts and boats. Now I should no longer cross water by riding rafts and boats." Buddha reflected: "I am a teacher of crossing over; I enable people to attain the path of transcendence; I should no longer receive crossing from others." No sooner had he finished this reflection than all the bhikṣus had already crossed. Buddha called to Ānanda: "Let us go together to Koṭigāma." Ānanda replied: "Yes." Buddha then went together with the one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, all going to Koṭigāma. Buddha said to the bhikṣus: "All listen. Maintain a mind of loving-kindness toward all the world, without contention. Contemplate yourselves and know impermanence, and with wisdom be concerned about the body. Maintaining a mind of loving-kindness toward all the world without contention, and thus contemplating yourselves, you will thereby attain clarity. With clarity one departs from the conditions of greed and lust, anger, and delusion. When these three conditions depart, one attains the path of transcendence and is no longer subject to birth and death. The mind no longer runs about; with a unified mind there is nothing to which it clings; like a king who delights in solitary thought: among so many of his subjects, I alone am master. One who has attained the path, who has transcended the world, also thus contemplates the mind: there are countless thousands and ten-thousands of strands, yet now I am master of this mind, as a king governs and commands the people."

Buddha again departed from Koṭigāma and called to Ānanda: "Let us go together to the kingdom of Nādikā." Ānanda replied: "Yes." Buddha went together with the bhikṣus to the kingdom of Nādikā and sat beneath a tree. Buddha sent the assembly of bhikṣus to go on almsround in the kingdom of Nādikā. When they returned, they reported to Buddha: "The kingdom of Nādikā has many sick people, and many of the people have died. Among them are upāsakas named Xuanniao, Shixian, Chudong, Shixian, Shuxian, Kuaixian, Bozong, Jianzun, Diju, and Shangjing; these ten persons were all upāsakas who upheld the five precepts, and they have now all died." All the bhikṣus asked Buddha: "Of these upāsakas who have died, in which realm have they all been reborn?" Buddha said: "Xuanniao and the other ten persons, having died, are all established in the path of non-returning."

Buddha told the assembly of bhikṣus: "You have only seen ten people who died, but the Buddha with the divine eye has seen five hundred upāsakas who died; all of them have been born in the path of non-returning. Furthermore, there are two hundred upāsakas such as Nandita and others who throughout their lives had no condition of lust, no condition of anger, and no condition of delusion. At death they were all born in Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, attained the path of stream-entry, and after seven more deaths and seven more births will attain the path of arahantship. Xuanniao and the five hundred others have all attained the path of non-returning, and will of themselves in the heavens attain the arahant path." Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Having returned from almsround, why do you speak of these ten upāsakas to me? You deliberately wish to disturb the Buddha, thinking the Buddha does not wish to hear such ill news; but what should the Buddha fear? Those who are born must all die. All the Buddhas of past, future, and present have all entered parinirvāṇa. Now I have become the Buddha and will also enter parinirvāṇa. It was for this body that I became the Buddha, seeking Buddhahood for countless kalpas, bringing the path of birth and death to a halt, and becoming a Buddha to cut off the root of birth and death. Know that the origin of these people comes from delusion; from delusion come formations; from formations comes consciousness; from consciousness come name-and-form; from name-and-form come the six sense bases; from the six sense bases comes contact; from contact comes feeling; from feeling comes craving; from craving comes seeking; from seeking comes existence; from existence comes birth; from birth come aging-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, dissatisfaction, and distress; thus does the great mass of suffering arise and accumulate. The Buddha therefore contemplated the origin of birth and death; like a cart with wheels: the cart rolls on without rest. Because of delusion people undergo birth and death. By eliminating delusion, delusion ceases; with the cessation of delusion, formations cease; with the cessation of formations, consciousness ceases; with the cessation of consciousness, name-and-form ceases; with the cessation of name-and-form, the six sense bases cease; with the cessation of the six sense bases, contact ceases; with the cessation of contact, feeling ceases; with the cessation of feeling, craving ceases; with the cessation of craving, seeking ceases; with the cessation of seeking, existence ceases; with the cessation of existence, birth ceases; with the cessation of birth, aging-and-death ceases; with the cessation of aging-and-death, sorrow, lamentation, suffering, dissatisfaction, and distress cease; thus the entire great mass of suffering ceases and is extinguished. For this reason the Buddha first told you: because of delusion there is birth and death; those with wisdom maintain the path and are no longer subject to birth and death."

Buddha said: "You should be mindful of upholding the pure precepts of the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Holy Saṃgha; receive and practice the Buddha's scriptures in succession; constantly contemplate and rectify the mind, so that you will no longer undergo birth and death and the affliction of sorrow and weeping."

Buddha departed from the village of Nādikā and called to Ānanda: "Let us go to the kingdom of Vaiśālī." Ānanda replied: "Yes." Buddha departed from the village of Nādikā. Seven li before reaching the kingdom of Vaiśālī, Buddha stopped in a mango grove. There was a courtesan named Āmrapālī who had five hundred female disciples in the courtesan's art. Hearing within the city that Buddha had arrived and was staying in the mango grove, she instructed her five hundred female disciples to dress themselves elegantly and prepare their carriages. They departed from the city and went to where Buddha was, wishing to see Buddha and to bow before him. At that time, Buddha was in the mango grove together with a thousand bhikṣus, expounding the scriptures to the assembly. Buddha saw Āmrapālī coming together with her five hundred female disciples, all elegantly dressed. Buddha instructed the bhikṣus: "You have seen Āmrapālī coming together with her five hundred female disciples; all lower your heads and rectify your minds. Although they come elegantly dressed, they are like painted jars: the outside has fine painting, but within there is only impurity, sealed and knotted so that it cannot be opened; if one opens it, impurity and stench arise immediately. Āmrapālī and her kind are all of the painted-jar type. As for bhikṣus, you should see with the power of discernment. What is the power of discernment? Departing from evil and going toward good, not permitting the condition of lust; rather than ever follow the mind into evil, one should break one's own bones, shatter one's heart, and set one's body ablaze. It is not only the man of great physical strength who possesses great power; the one who can rectify his own mind surpasses the man of physical strength. The Buddha has been contending with the mind since time immemorial, through countless kalpas, not permitting himself to follow the mind, exerting diligent effort, thereby of himself achieving Buddhahood. Bhikṣus, you too should restrain and rectify your minds. The mind has long dwelt in impurity, but even now you can extract and lift it out yourselves. Contemplate yourselves; the body with its five organs can also be brought to a halt in the turning of birth and death. Viewed outwardly there is suffering; viewed inwardly there is also suffering. Rectify your minds."

Āmrapālī arrived, descended from her carriage, went to where Buddha was, made obeisance to Buddha, and withdrew to sit at one side. All the bhikṣus lowered their heads. Buddha said: "Why have you come?" Āmrapālī replied: "I have often heard that the Buddha is revered above all the heavenly beings, and so I have come to bow before him." Buddha said: "Āmrapālī! Do you delight in being a woman?" Āmrapālī replied: "Heaven has made me a woman; it is not that I delight in it." Buddha said: "If you do not delight in being a woman, who has caused you to keep five hundred female disciples in the courtesan's art?" Āmrapālī replied: "They are all poor people, and I support and care for them." Buddha said: "That is not so. You do not weary of the afflictions of womanhood; the monthly impurity, the bonds and blows and restrictions, the lack of freedom; you do not weary of your own body, and yet you further attach yourself to five hundred others." Āmrapālī replied: "My delusion has brought this about; a person of wisdom would not do this." Buddha said: "If it is truly so, that is good." Āmrapālī thereupon knelt and said to Buddha: "Tomorrow I invite Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus to a meal." Buddha silently gave no response. Āmrapālī was greatly joyful, thereupon arose, made obeisance to Buddha, and departed.


Not long after, the noble Licchavī householders of Vaiśālī heard that the Buddha had come together with the community of monks, and was staying in the Nāgī Garden seven leagues from the city. Availing themselves of royal ceremony, they all had their carriages splendidly arrayed and set out, wishing to pay reverence and make offerings to the Buddha. Among them, some rode blue horses in blue carriages, wearing blue garments beneath blue canopies, with blue banners and blue pennants, all their retinue in blue; some rode yellow horses in yellow carriages, wearing yellow garments beneath yellow canopies, with yellow banners and yellow pennants, all their retinue in yellow; some rode red horses in red carriages, wearing red garments beneath red canopies, with red banners and red pennants, all their retinue in red; some rode white horses in white carriages, wearing white garments beneath white canopies, with white banners and white pennants, all their retinue in white; some rode black horses in black carriages, wearing black garments beneath black canopies, with black banners and black pennants, all their retinue in black. The Buddha, seeing from afar many hundreds of thousands of carriages and riders approaching, at once told the monks: "If you wish to see the attendants and retinue of Śakra Devānām-Indra going forth and returning in the gardens of Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, they would be no different from these noble householders." When the householders arrived at the roadway, they all descended from their carriages and came to where the Buddha was. Those in front knelt before the Buddha, those in the middle all bowed their heads, and those at the rear merely joined their palms and sat. The Buddha asked: "Where have you come from?" The householders said: "Hearing that the Buddha is here, we have come out from the city to bow in reverence." Among them was one man named Biñja, who rose and came before the Buddha and gazed upon him intently. The Buddha asked: "Why do you gaze so?" Biñja said: "Throughout all of heaven above and earth below, all are moved by the Buddha. I gaze upon the Buddha and am without satiety." The Buddha said: "Biñja, you should indeed gaze upon the Buddha attentively. A Buddha appears only after a long, long time. Whenever a Buddha is present, you should receive the Buddha's teaching and guidance." Among the assembly, four or five hundred householders said: "Biñja possesses great virtue, to converse with the Buddha thus." Biñja said: "I heard of the Buddha's sūtras from afar. I have cherished them for a long time. Only today have I at last been able to behold him. I have a devoted and filial heart toward the Buddha." The Buddha said: "Few indeed in this world are like Biñja's kind; devoted and filial toward their teacher."

The Buddha told Biñja: "The Buddha appears in the world, knowing the path of birth and death in the world, expounding the sūtras to transform and enlighten heaven above and earth below, as well as ghosts and dragons; there are none who are not moved and stirred. This is the Buddha's first spiritual power. Those who read the Buddha's sūtras and through rectifying their own minds attain the Way; this is the Buddha's second spiritual power. The Buddha expounds the sūtras throughout the world; the wise are without exception gladdened by this, those who hear it are without exception gladdened, and those who study it teach one another in turn, guide one another, and rectify one another's minds; this is the Buddha's third spiritual power. Those who study the Buddha's sūtras are all filled with joy, like a fool who obtains gold. The highest wisdom attains the path of the Worthy Ones; the second may attain non-return; the third may attain once-return; the fourth may attain stream-entry; the fifth, those who uphold the five precepts of an upāsaka, may attain rebirth in heaven; those who uphold three precepts may attain rebirth as a human being. The Buddha appears in the world and thereby reveals this path; this is the Buddha's fourth spiritual power." The Buddha told Biñja: "You have come to gaze intently upon the Buddha, who so rarely appears that merely hearing the Buddha's name is already rare, and beholding the Buddha rarer still. In the assembly at this moment there are many hundreds of thousands of people, yet you alone question the Buddha while the others do not; this is the Buddha's fifth spiritual power." The Buddha told Biñja: "In this world, the wise are few and those without steadfast faithfulness are many. Those who receive the Buddha's path through the sūtras, who receive the good teachings of their teacher, who uphold the precepts and teachings of their teacher; there are no ghosts, spirits, or dragons who will not protect and watch over them; officials will not dare summon them capriciously. You should be devoted and filial toward your teacher. The teacher does not seek things from disciples. In the teacher's presence, show reverence to the teacher; behind his back, offer praise to the teacher; when the teacher has died, constantly keep him in mindful recollection. Here now is Biñja; a hero among men, delighting in the Dharma and pure in his precepts."

The Licchavī householders of Vaiśālī requested of the Buddha: "Would you be willing tomorrow morning to enter the city together with the monks to accept a meal?" The Buddha said: "The Nāgī woman has already come this morning to invite the Buddha and the community of monks." The householders all departed together.

The next morning the Nāgī woman came to where the Buddha was and said: "The seats are already prepared and the food is fully ready. May the Honored One graciously extend his spiritual power." The Buddha said: "Go on ahead; I will follow shortly." The Buddha rose, put on his robes, took up his bowl, and entered the city together with the monks. The onlookers in the city numbered many hundreds of thousands. Among them, the upright and good upāsakas all said: "The Buddha is like the bright moon, and his disciples are like the bright stars, following in the moon's train." Such indeed was the Buddha's beauty at that time.

The Buddha arrived at the Nāgī woman's home, took his seat, and water for washing was brought around. When the Buddha and the entire community of monks had finished eating and had finished washing their hands, the Nāgī woman took a small stool and sat before the Buddha. The Buddha told the Nāgī woman: "Sages and those who are honored and wealthy throughout the world hold above all else purity of precept and clear understanding of the Buddha's various sūtras. Their words in conversation are universally pleasant to hear; wherever they go they are universally revered and loved. Now abiding in this world as a human being, not coveting wealth or sensual pleasures, upholding and following the Buddha's transforming influence; there are none who at death will not be reborn in heaven." The Buddha told the Nāgī woman: "Take good care of yourself. Uphold the five precepts." The Buddha and the monks then departed together.

The Buddha set out from the country of Vaiśālī and told Ānanda: "Let us go together to Bamboo Fragrance Village." Ānanda said: "So be it." Word came that at Bamboo Fragrance Village the price of grain had risen greatly and the monks were finding it difficult to obtain alms. The Buddha sat in contemplation: the country of Vaiśālī was suffering famine, grain prices had soared, the villages were small and could not supply the monks with alms. The Buddha reflected and wished to send the monks to disperse to other lands where grain was cheap to seek alms. The Buddha told the community of monks: "At this Bamboo Fragrance Village, grain prices have risen greatly and alms are difficult to obtain. The Śālāṭavī country over there has had an abundant harvest. Throughout the four borders of Vaiśālī grain is expensive everywhere. I myself together with Ānanda will remain here at Bamboo Fragrance Village." The monks received the Buddha's instruction and all departed for Śālāṭavī country.

The Buddha and Ānanda went together to Bamboo Fragrance Village. The Buddha's entire body was in great pain, and he was on the verge of parinirvāṇa. The Buddha thought to himself: "All the monks have gone away. If I enter parinirvāṇa alone, would that not be without teaching or precept?" Ānanda rose from beneath a tree and came to where the Buddha was, and asked the Buddha: "Is the holy one's body ill at ease? Has it improved at all?" The Buddha said: "It has not improved; it is greatly worsened. I am on the verge of parinirvāṇa." Ānanda said: "Please do not yet enter parinirvāṇa. Wait until the community of monks has assembled." The Buddha told Ānanda: "I have already given the sūtras and precepts. You should simply practice and follow them in accordance with the sūtras and precepts. I am also present within the community of monks. The community of monks already knows all of the Buddha's instructions. The Dharma of serving a teacher has been entrusted entirely to the disciples. Disciples should simply uphold and practice it, studying it thoroughly. Now my body is in great pain, but I draw upon the Buddha's spiritual power to treat the illness, no longer using the mind to dwell on the illness. It is as though it has somewhat improved."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "The Buddha is now advanced in years, already eighty. Like an old carriage without strength, my body is also without strength. I have not said to you before that there is anyone who falls to the ground and does not die. Even the highest heaven, named the Realm of Neither Perception nor Non-Perception, with a lifespan of eighty billion four hundred million kalpas, must at last die again. It is for this reason that the sūtras arose in this world; to sever the root of birth and death. After my parinirvāṇa, do not abandon these sūtras and precepts. Pass them on and use them in turn. Inwardly and outwardly reflect for yourself; rectify the mind and correct conduct; uphold the precepts and teachings; let inner and outer remain as they always have been. Whatever fourfold disciples uphold the precepts and teachings are all disciples of the Buddha; whoever studies the Buddha's path through the sūtras is a disciple of the Buddha. The Buddha relinquishes the status of wheel-turning king out of concern for the people of heaven above and earth below; you may also be concerned for yourself about illness; cast off the defilements of lust, anger, and delusion."

The Buddha, departing from Bamboo Fragrance Village, called to Ānanda: "Let us return once more to the country of Vaiśālī." Ānanda said: "I receive your instruction." The Buddha returned to Vaiśālī, entered the city carrying his bowl to seek alms, and then withdrew to sit in the open beneath the Swift Spirit's sacred tree to contemplate the matter of birth and death. Ānanda, sitting beneath a distant tree, was contemplating the affairs of a shaded chamber, when he rose and came to where the Buddha was, made obeisance to the Buddha, and then stood to one side and said: "Why have you not yet entered parinirvāṇa?" The Buddha told Ānanda: "Vaiśālī is a great joy. The country of Vejja is a great joy. The land of the Swift Spirit Deity is a great joy. The city gate of Śādhāṭhī is a great joy. The streets within the city are a great joy. The shrine named Fubō is a great joy. The Jambudvīpa world is a great joy. Vajjī is a great joy. The country of Cāpa is a great joy. The city gate of Śā is a great joy. The country of Magadha is a great joy. Mañjubodhī is a great joy. Uttara is a great joy. The golden mountains from which the Hiraṇyavatī flows are a great joy. Within Jambudvīpa, what is produced in five colors is like a painting; those who are born and dwell therein experience great joy."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a monk or nun uphold these four methods, thoroughly contemplating and rectifying the mind without following the mind, outwardly also contemplating what is good, inwardly also contemplating what is good, the mind having nothing further it craves or delights in, the mind neither startled nor frightened, never turning away; for a monk or nun who upholds such an aspiration as these four methods, these are called the four bases of spiritual power. Wishing to not die for a full kalpa, this can be obtained." At that moment Māra entered Ānanda's belly. The Buddha again told Ānanda: "Such things as this are still possible."

Ānanda spoke again: "Why does the Buddha not enter parinirvāṇa? Now would be a fitting time to enter parinirvāṇa." The Buddha spoke again: "Jambudvīpa is a great joy. Those who know these four bases of spiritual power can remain in the world for a kalpa and more." The Buddha told Ānanda thus three times over, and Ānanda failed to respond to the matter of the four bases of spiritual power. The Buddha told Ānanda: "Go and sit in contemplation beneath your tree."

The Buddha rose and went to sit beneath a tree by the bank of the Hiraṇyavatī river. Māra came to the Buddha and said: "Why do you not enter parinirvāṇa?" The Buddha said: "Away with you, wicked Māra! The time has not yet come for parinirvāṇa. I must wait until my fourfold disciples of wisdom have attained the Way; I must wait until the gods of the heavens, the people of the world, and the spirits have attained the Way through wisdom; I must wait until my Dharma and teachings have spread throughout the world; only then may parinirvāṇa come." Māra, knowing the Buddha would enter parinirvāṇa, departed in joy.

The Buddha sat in contemplation: "Perhaps it is appropriate to release and relinquish the life force. The mind wishes to release and relinquish the life force." At that moment the great earth shook, and all the ghosts and spirits were startled. Ānanda, beneath his tree, was startled and rose and came to where the Buddha was, placed his forehead upon the Buddha's feet in prostration, then withdrew to stand to one side and said to the Buddha: "While I was sitting beneath my tree, the great earth shook. I was startled and the hairs of my body stood on end. In all my life I have not experienced such a great earthquake as this."

The Buddha explained to Ānanda: "There are eight occasions when the earth moves. What are the eight? The earth rests upon water, the water rests upon wind; the wind supports the water. It is as though looking up from the earth toward the sky; at times the wind moves the water, the water moves the earth, and the earth accordingly moves. This is the first movement. There is an arhat of noble eminence who, wishing to test his own spiritual power, directs his intention to cause the earth to move, and thereupon presses upon the earth with two fingers; the great earth moves because of this. This is the second movement. There is one whose heavenly spiritual power is great, who wills the earth to move, and the earth immediately moves greatly. This is the third movement. When the Bodhisattva descended from the Tuṣita Heaven, the fourth heaven, and entered his mother's womb, the great earth moved at that time. When the Bodhisattva was born from the right side, the great earth moved. When the Bodhisattva attained the Way and became the Buddha, the great earth moved. When the Buddha first arose to expound the sūtras, the great earth moved. When the Buddha releases and relinquishes his life force, the great earth moves." The Buddha told Ānanda: "Three months hence the Buddha will enter parinirvāṇa, and the earth will move greatly once more; this is the eighth movement."

When Ānanda heard the Buddha set a term of three months for himself, he wept and asked: "Has the life force then been released and relinquished?" The Buddha told Ānanda: "Yes, the life force has been released and relinquished."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "From the Buddha's own mouth I have received and accepted these words: if a monk possesses these four methods, called the four bases of spiritual power, wishing to not die for a full kalpa, this can be obtained. The Buddha's virtue surpasses the four bases of spiritual power; why then does he not remain for more than a full kalpa?" The Buddha told Ānanda: "This is your own doing, this is what you have made. I told you three times over that Jambudvīpa is a great joy; you simply remained silent without responding. I saw horns growing on your head; how did you come to let Māra enter your belly? I am now unable to remain longer. Three months hence I will enter parinirvāṇa." Ānanda thereupon rose and announced to the community of monks: "Three months hence the Buddha will enter parinirvāṇa." The Buddha told Ānanda: "Gather all the monks together in the great assembly hall." Ānanda said: "The community of monks is already assembled in the great assembly hall." The Buddha immediately rose and went to the great assembly hall. All the monks rose and made obeisance to the Buddha.

The Buddha told the monks: "In this world there is no one who is constant and firm. Those who delight in clinging to birth and death without seeking the path of transcendence are all foolish. Father and mother must ultimately part from us; there is the feeling of grief and weeping. People turn toward one another with love, longing, and sorrow. In all the world, there is nothing born that does not die. As I have said in the sūtras: those who are born must all die; those who have died are born again; people grieve and weep for one another without respite. Even Mount Sumeru will crumble and collapse; the gods of heaven above also die; those who become kings also die; from the wealthy and noble down to animals; there is nothing born that does not die. Do not wonder that three months hence the Buddha will enter parinirvāṇa. When the Buddha is gone, you must uphold the sūtras and precepts; those who remain must also uphold the sūtras and precepts; proceed toward the path of transcendence, no longer subject to birth and death, no longer troubled by grief and weeping. Make the Buddha's sūtras endure for a long time. After the Buddha is gone, the wise in this world should together uphold the sūtras and precepts. Those in this world who correct their own minds; all the gods of heaven above will gladly assist people in attaining blessings. The Buddha's sūtras may be read, recited, studied, upheld, contemplated, used to rectify the mind, used to correct the intention, and passed on to one another. There are four things: correct the body, correct the mind, correct the will, correct speech. There are also four things: those with thoughts of desire should practice patience; those with evil thoughts should abandon them; those with craving desires should abandon them; always be mindful of death. There are also four things: do not heed the mind when it wishes to go astray; do not heed the mind when it wishes toward sensual desire; do not heed the mind when it thinks of evil; do not heed the mind when it thinks of power and status. There are also four things: let the mind always be mindful of death; when the mind schemes toward evil, do not heed it; restrain the mind; the mind should follow the person, the person should not follow the mind. The mind misleads people; the mind kills the body; the mind attains arhatship; the mind attains heaven; the mind takes rebirth as a human; the mind takes rebirth as an animal, insect, bird, or beast; the mind takes rebirth in hell; the mind takes rebirth as a hungry ghost; all forms of existence are created by the mind. Life, the three together are companions: the mind is foremost as master, life follows the mind, lifespan follows life; the three follow one another in sequence. Now I have become a Buddha, revered by all of heaven above and earth below; all this is what the mind has made. You should contemplate the suffering of birth and death, the parting from family and household. Contemplate these eight things; reflect upon the Buddha's sūtras: First, abandon wife and children and seek the path of transcendence; do not contend with the world; have no greedy mind. Second, do not engage in divisive speech, harsh words, false speech, or idle talk, wailing, singing, or frivolous play. Third, do not take life, steal others' goods, or harbor thoughts of licentiousness. Fourth, do not harbor anger, delusion, or greed. Fifth, do not be envious of others or contemptuous of people. Sixth, do not scheme to inflict harm and suffering upon others. Seventh, do not act in a dissolute and self-indulgent manner; do not be negligent or given to lying about; do not be attached to flavors and food and drink. Eighth, be mindful of the birth, aging, illness, and death of your own body. By upholding these eight things and correcting the mind yourself, you may be without conflict with all in the world and should proceed toward the path of transcendence. Monks, contemplate these eight things; the original four sufferings; and the Buddha's sūtras may endure for a long time."

The Buddha, departing from Vaiśālī, called to Ānanda: "Let us go to Koṭigāma." Ānanda said: "So be it." The Buddha set out from Vaiśālī and turned to look back at the city. Ānanda at once stepped forward and asked the Buddha: "The Buddha does not turn to look back without cause." The Buddha told Ānanda: "When one becomes a Buddha, one does not casually turn to look backward." Ānanda said: "What is the meaning of the Buddha turning to look back?" The Buddha said: "My lifespan is ending today; I will not again enter this city, so I have turned to look once more." One of the monks following the Buddha stepped forward and asked the Buddha: "Will you truly not return and enter this city?" The Buddha said: "I am to enter parinirvāṇa and will not again behold the country of Vaiśālī. I will proceed to the land of Pāṭaliputta."

The Buddha arrived at Koṭigāma. Within the village there was a garden named Śiṃśapāvana. The Buddha called all the monks together: "Now sit with a purified and composed mind, in self-contemplation. Know that wisdom arises within birth; those whose wisdom causes the mind to be upright; for those whose mind is upright, the three defilements of lust, anger, and delusion are all dissolved. Such a monk, having declared for himself the severing of the root of birth and death and attainment of the path of arhatship, is of one mind with nothing further to worry about, no longer troubled by birth and death. Though encountering suffering again, one has attained the path that transcends birth and death."

The Buddha, departing from Koṭigāma, called to Ānanda: "Let us go to Nādikā." Ānanda said: "So be it." The Buddha and the community of monks went together to Nādikā. From Nādikā, the Buddha called to Ānanda: "Let us go together to Jambugāma." Together with the community of monks, the Buddha told the monks: "If a monk has a pure mind, a contemplative mind, a wise mind, and reflects upon himself; one who has wisdom and understands the sūtras, this is the foundation of the mind of wisdom. The mind of lust, the mind of anger, the mind of delusion; all three are extinguished and gone; the three minds are pure. Wishing to attain the path of transcendence is not difficult. Having attained the path of arhatship, all lust, anger, and delusion are consumed and dissolved away. You should declare for yourself that you have already abandoned these three things and will no longer practice the dharmas of birth and death."

The Buddha, departing from Jambugāma, called to Ānanda: "Let us once more go to Hatthi-gāma." Ānanda said: "So be it." They went together with all the monks to Hatthi-gāma. The Buddha told the monks: "With a pure mind, contemplative mind, wise mind; one who has a purified, mindful intention, the mind at once arises; the wise mind at once arises; as the wise mind arises, there comes liberation: no thought of lust, no thought of anger, no longer deluded; the mind thereby becomes liberated." The monks declared for themselves: "All that I sought I have obtained; thereby the path of arhatship has been seen."

The Buddha, departing from Hatthi-gāma, called to Ānanda: "Let us go to Āmba-gāma." Ānanda said: "So be it." They went together with all the monks to Āmba-gāma. The Buddha told the community of monks: "The method of the pure mind, the contemplative mind, the wise mind; reaching the state free from the defilements of lust and anger, one attains the path of the pure mind. The contemplative mind and wise mind at once give rise to the path of the contemplative mind. The pure mind and wise mind at once bring the liberating path of the wise mind. The pure mind and contemplative mind become illumined. When a person has cloth and uses it with dye to produce color, if the cloth is clean and pure, the colors produced are all beautiful; because the cloth is pure. When a monk has these three minds; the pure mind, the contemplative mind, the wise mind; the pure mind corresponds to śīla, the contemplative mind corresponds to samādhi, the wise mind corresponds to prajñā. The śīla mind means: no lust, no anger, no greed. Samādhi means: gathering the mind and preventing it from wandering. Prajñā means: the mind free of craving and desire, upholding the Buddha's sūtras and precepts. It is like a person who has cloth; if the cloth has stains, and the person wishes to dye it to produce a color and puts it into the dye, the color will not be bright. If a monk is not settled in the pure mind, the contemplative mind, and the wise mind, and wishes to attain the Way, it is difficult; because the mind is not liberated. When a monk's mind becomes liberated of itself, sitting in contemplation he can behold the heavens; he can fully know what is in others' minds; he can also behold the good and evil destinies of those in hell, hungry ghosts, and animals; just as in clear water below which there are sand and stones, blue and yellow and white and black, everything in the water is manifest; simply because the water is clear. Seeking the path of transcendence is like this: the mind is pure and clear. It is like murky water in a stream; below, the sand and stones cannot be seen, nor can one know the depth or shallowness of the water. If a monk's mind is not pure, he cannot attain the path of transcendence; because the mind is turbid."

The Buddha, departing from Āmba-gāma, called to Ānanda: "Let us go together to Jambupāli-gāma." Ānanda said: "So be it." They went together with all the monks to Jambupāli-gāma. The Buddha told the monks: "If one has the pure mind, the contemplative mind, and the wise mind; what the teacher teaches and transmits, the disciple should study; even reflecting on the teacher, one cannot enter the disciple's mind to rectify the disciple's mind. The monk should purify the mind himself, correct this mind; when the mind is corrected, one attains the path of transcendence, and should declare for oneself that the path of transcendence has been attained and the root of birth and death severed."

The Buddha called to Ānanda: "Let us go to Pāṭaligāma." Ānanda said: "So be it." They went together with all the monks to Pāṭaligāma. The Buddha told the monks: "The mind has three defilements: the defilement of lust, the defilement of anger, the defilement of delusion. Use the pure mind to repel the defilement of lust; use the contemplative mind to repel the defilement of anger; use the wisdom mind to repel the defilement of delusion. The monk should then declare for himself that the path of transcendence has been attained and the root of birth and death, grief and weeping, sorrow and longing has been severed."

The Buddha then departed from Pāṭaligāma, calling to Ānanda: "Let us go together to Pāvā city." Ānanda said: "So be it." They went together with all the monks to the north of Pāvā city and sat beneath a tree. Ānanda sat beneath a neighboring tree, deeply concentrating in inner observation, when the earth shook greatly. Ānanda rose and came to where the Buddha was, saying: "Why has the earth shaken so greatly?" The Buddha said: "There are four causes and conditions for the earth to move. First movement: the earth rests upon water, the water rests upon wind; when the wind below shakes the water, the water shakes the earth, and the earth accordingly moves; this is the first movement. When an arhat wishes to test himself in the Way, pressing upon the earth with two fingers, the earth moves; this is the second movement. When one whose heavenly spiritual power is great wills the earth to move, the earth accordingly moves; this is the third movement. When the Buddha is soon to enter parinirvāṇa, the earth will move greatly again; this is the fourth movement." Ānanda said: "The Buddha's spiritual power is indeed like this; when the Buddha enters parinirvāṇa, the earth moves greatly."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The Buddha's spiritual power is august and supremely venerable; his enlightening transformation is immeasurable. Do you wish to know the Buddha's spiritual power?" Ānanda said: "I wish to hear and know it."

The Buddha said: "I travel throughout all the lands below heaven. In whatever commandery or kingdom I arrive, those among its people who have understanding come to where the Buddha is. The Buddha himself transforms his body, taking on the clothing, language, and speech of that kingdom. I observe how the people practice the Dharma, and know what sūtras and precepts they have. The Buddha then augments their sūtras and precepts for them. The people do not know who I am, nor do they know whether I have come from heaven above or emerged from within the earth, yet they greatly revere me. I transform and go to the palaces of various kings. The king asks me: 'What manner of person are you?' I say: 'I am a man of the Way from this kingdom.' The king asks me: 'What sūtras do you practice?' I say: 'Whatever sūtra you wish to ask about, I can answer your question.' All that the king delights in, I expound in full for him. Then I transform and vanish, unseen by the king, and afterward none of them know who I was. I go to the various Licchavī kingdoms; I also transform myself into Licchavī clothing and speech. I ask: 'What sūtras and precepts do you practice?' I know the minds of you children, I know your speech. I explain the sūtras and offer instruction, then transform and vanish. You all afterward think of me, speaking among yourselves: 'What manner of person was that? A heavenly being, a ghost, or a spirit?' You do not know who I was; nor do I say that I am the Buddha. Having completed my traversal of one world dispensing the teaching of the Way, I ascend to the first heaven above, to the realm of the Four Heavenly Kings. I take on the clothing and speech of heaven, and ask the gods: 'What sūtras do you practice?' The gods say: 'We do not know the sūtras.' I then expound the sūtras for them in full, and transform and vanish; the gods also do not know who I was. I ascend again to the second heaven of Trāyastriṃśa, transforming into the clothing and speech of Trāyastriṃśa. I ask the Trāyastriṃśa gods: 'What sūtras do you practice?' The Trāyastriṃśa gods say: 'We do not know the sūtras.' I expound the sūtras for them in full, and transform and vanish; the gods also do not know who I was. I ascend again to the third heaven of Yāma, transforming into the clothing and speech of that heaven. I ask the Yāma gods: 'What sūtras do you practice?' The gods say: 'We do not know the sūtras.' I expound the sūtras for them. I ascend again to the fourth heaven of Tuṣita, transforming into the clothing and speech of that heaven. I ask the gods: 'What sūtras do you practice?' Those gods say: 'Maitreya expounds the sūtras for us.' I expound the sūtras for them once more. I ascend again to the fifth heaven of Nirmāṇarati, taking on the clothing and speech of that heaven. I ask the gods: 'Do you know the sūtras?' Those gods say: 'We do not know the sūtras.' I expound the sūtras for them, transform and vanish; the gods do not know who I was. I ascend again to the sixth heaven of Paranirmitavaśavartin, taking on the clothing and speech of that heaven. I ask the gods: 'What sūtras do you practice?' The gods say: 'We do not know the sūtras.' I expound the sūtras for them, and then transform and vanish once more. Afterward, the gods of the sixth heaven do not know who I was, nor do I say that I am the Buddha.

"I ascend again to the Brahmā Heaven, the Brahma-pāriṣadya Heaven, the Brahma-purohita Heaven, the Mahābrahmā Heaven, the Parittābha Heaven, the Apramāṇābha Heaven, the Ābhāsvara Heaven, the Parittaśubha Heaven, the Apramāṇaśubha Heaven, the Śubhakṛtsna Heaven, the Anabhrakha Heaven, the Puṇyaprasava Heaven, the Bṛhatphala Heaven, the Asañjñisattva Heaven, the Avṛha Heaven; all these gods come to see me, and I ask each of them: 'Do you know the sūtras?' Some know the sūtras and some do not. For all of them I expound the path of birth and death and the path that severs the root of birth and death. Whatever sūtras they delight in, I expound them all. I effect transformation into the clothing and speech of the heavens. As for the remaining four heavens, those gods cannot speak; they cannot respond to my questions. The twenty-fifth heaven is named the Heaven of Emptiness-Wisdom; the twenty-sixth is named the Heaven of Consciousness-Wisdom Entry; the twenty-seventh is named the Heaven of No-Thought-Wisdom Entry; the twenty-eighth is named the Heaven of Neither Perception nor Non-Perception."

The Buddha said: "There is nothing I do not see. Only nirvāṇa is the greatest joy."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The Buddha's spiritual power not only can move the earth; all twenty-eight heavens are moved greatly. The Buddha achieves this simply through the rectified mind."


Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "Not far from here are great kingdoms; Śrāvastī, Śāketa, Campā, Rājagṛha, Vārāṇasī, Vaiśālī; all these great states, accomplished in right teaching and understanding. When the Buddha is about to pass into extinction, why not do so in one of them? Instead, it is a small district, and moreover outside the city, a sparse and humble settlement; and here you enter parinirvāṇa?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Do not call it a small settlement. In the distant past, this place of Kuśinagara was called Kuśiāvatī. The kingdom was greatly joyful; at that time there was no pestilence or disease, rice and grain were abundant, and the populace thrived; in every household were filial children. The city extended four hundred and eighty li from east to west in length, and two hundred and eighty li from north to south in breadth. The city had seven walls, all built of fired bricks; gold, silver, lapis lazuli, and crystal were set into the city walls; the four jewels were likewise used as tiles to cover the city. The walls were sixty-four feet in height and twenty-four feet in breadth at the top. Within the city were jeweled trees with light and blossoms of five colors. The roads had three lanes, both sides covered above with the four jewels used as tiles; on both sides were dwellings and residences, carved and engraved, arrayed like the heavens above, with lutes, zithers, and diverse music. Men and women did not mingle; their songs were of virtue; the way of joy grew ever more luminous; the people had no sorrow or fear, their hearts ever in joy; the tops of their heads were unadorned, and fine fragrances reached far.

"The holy king was called Mahāsudassana, with the title of chakravartin emperor. His troops illuminated the world, without equal anywhere; all were guided by the Way, none transgressing the king's law; people who wished to fly through the air; with a single thought, their bodies would go. The king possessed seven treasures that arose of themselves: the golden flying wheel, the divine white elephant, the deep-blue divine horse, the pearl of the bright moon, the celestial jade woman consort, the jewel-mastering holy minister, and the army-commanding holy minister. The king had four virtues: during his childhood, during his time as crown prince, during his reign upon the imperial throne, and during the time he renounced the kingdom and severed desire to become a śramaṇa; each period lasting eighty-four thousand years; these constituted Mahāsudassana's first virtue, difficult to match. His food and drink transformed of themselves; his body was without lasting illness; the temperature was always moderate; his body and mind were ever at ease; these were the second virtue. His appearance was stately and imposing, his countenance unparalleled in the world, surpassing Indra himself; these were the third virtue. Under all of heaven, across all his territory, the people great and small loved the king with compassion, as a most filial child wishes his parents to be at peace; and the king also with a sincere heart compassionately loved all living beings as his own children: he gave wealth to the poor, food to the hungry, drink to the thirsty, and brought together the aged poor and orphaned young to live as family, as relatives and children, providing dwellings and carriages, swiftly giving them medicine in illness; these were called the fourth virtue.

"That country always heard twelve kinds of sound: the sound of elephants, the sound of horses, the sound of cattle, the sound of carriages, the sound of conch shells, the sound of drums, the sound of dancing, the sound of singing, the sounds of various stringed instruments, the sound of reciting benevolence and righteousness, and the sound of praising the Buddha's venerable conduct. The people's clothing and adornments were woven of many jewels, with bright-moon pearls intermingled; necklaces illuminated the roads. Their food, drink, and entertainments were like those of Trāyastriṃśa heaven; the residents rejoiced and delighted, not a day without happiness. When the king wished to go out in procession, he called his carriage minister, whose name was Sudhā, and commanded him: 'Have the carriage proceed slowly; I have not seen the devout householders for a long time and wish to see them now.' The devout householders, hearing that the king was about to go out, came bearing bright-moon pearls, white pearls, blue pearls, green pearls, coral, and sandalwood fine fragrances, and at once presented them to the holy king. The king did not wish to accept them, but they all bowed their heads and begged with entreaties, and so the king received them. He commanded the jewel-keeping minister to repay them double their value. The common people, great and small, also scattered jewels and flowers upon the ground and wished him boundless longevity. The kings of the various small states, eighty-four thousand in number, hearing that the chakravartin emperor was about to distribute gifts, all came as an attending retinue, arriving at the great hall. The emperor wished to go up to the main hall together with the various kings, but the various kings declined, saying: 'We ministers all have jeweled halls in our own kingdoms.' The emperor said: 'Your small halls are not worth speaking of; first observe the luminous hall.' And yet none dared to ascend. The various kings each had jeweled carriages ten zhàng in height, each with four wheels; from the bottom all the way to the top they were made entirely of the seven treasures, adorned above with banners and pennants, their colors brilliant and reflecting upon one another; the carriages were drawn by six horses, all flying through the air. At that time there was one carriage drawn by two camels and two elephants, called Kuñjara; the chariot of the holy emperor. Eighty-four thousand carriages led the way in the vanguard, arriving at the luminous hall.

"The hall was named Pārāsadana, extending forty li in all directions. Its walls were built of gold, silver, lapis lazuli, and crystal bricks; its columns were also of the four jewels; gold tiles, silver tiles, lapis lazuli tiles, crystal tiles; the steps were fifty-fold, all made of gold, silver, lapis lazuli, and crystal; gold beams, silver beams, lapis lazuli beams, crystal beams; gold rafters, silver rafters, lapis lazuli rafters, crystal rafters. Within the hall were eighty-four thousand beds: golden beds, silver beds, lapis lazuli beds, crystal beds; golden canopies, silver canopies, lapis lazuli canopies, crystal canopies; golden woven cloth, silver woven cloth, lapis lazuli woven cloth, crystal woven cloth, and red wool woven cloth; all spread upon the beds, with cloth woven in the heavens above as pillows.


"Ānanda! The palace walls are fourfold: a wall of gold, a wall of silver, a wall of lapis lazuli, and a wall of crystal. Four jeweled bathing pools are built, forty li in circumference; the golden pool has silver steps, the silver pool has golden steps, the lapis lazuli pool has crystal steps, the crystal pool has lapis lazuli steps. Within the pools, four-colored lotus flowers arise naturally: blue, red, purple, and white blossoms, growing in the pools through winter and summer alike. Outside grow fragrant flowering trees. Below the palace hall are four paths, enclosed with railings of the four jewels, each railing twenty li in length. Before the palace steps stand four jeweled trees, each forty li tall, casting shade of equal extent. The gold tree has silver leaves; the silver tree has golden leaves; the lapis lazuli tree has crystal leaves; the crystal tree has lapis lazuli leaves.

"The Emperor, standing below the hall, reflected that it was not fitting to ascend, and deferred to the assembled kings; yet none of the kings dared to ascend. Sudarśana commanded his close attendants to invite those śramaṇas who had stilled their minds, illumined the sūtras, and upheld the precepts to ascend first, and arranged sumptuous food, bestowing upon them precious gems. After the śramaṇas of stilled minds had departed, the Emperor fell into deep contemplation and reflected: 'Life is impermanent.' Taking a single attendant with him, he ascended the resplendent hall and said: 'I wish to dismiss the consorts and court ladies.' The attending ministers and assembled kings each sent word to have them depart. The Emperor sat upon the golden throne, his feet upon a silver footstool, and fell into deep reflection: 'What benefit to oneself is the conduct of licentiousness? The foolish are greatly covetous and know not its harm. Though my lifespan is now three hundred thirty-six thousand years, what flourishes must decay, what gathers must separate; the body is a vessel of decay and will ultimately turn to ash and dust. Who can long preserve this palace of the four jewels?' He said: 'I am but a single person; a small dwelling suffices. What use have I for a hall forty li across and eighty-four thousand beds?'

"He moved from the golden throne to the silver throne, his feet upon a golden footstool: 'If a person applies his mind, he must thoroughly purify the minds of craving, jealousy, hatred, delusion, and wrong lust, extinguishing the four marks of impermanence without remainder. Perceiving the impermanence of the world, how can I endure long?'

"From the silver throne he moved to the lapis lazuli throne, his feet upon a crystal footstool, saying: 'My inner palace has eighty-four thousand jade women; I will send each of them away. Of what use is it to be bound by them? A gathering of women multiplies evil; I must abandon impure thoughts.'

"From the lapis lazuli throne he moved to the crystal throne, his feet upon a lapis lazuli footstool: 'Reflecting deeply on the world, all affairs are evil; only non-action brings joy. I will remove my turbid aspirations and seek non-action. Even now, though I am a wheel-turning emperor of such wealth and nobility, of what benefit is it to my person?'

"An attendant came forward and said: 'The jade women inquire of the king: why does he tarry so long in the hall? All wish to come forward.' The Emperor told the attendant: 'Command the master of the treasury to dismiss the consorts, each to return to her home, taking with her all the jewels and precious gems adorning her person. The assembled kings, ministers, celestial horses, and precious elephants; send them all away.'

"King Sudarśana then ascended to a high tower. From afar he heard the sounds of the multitude crying out to heaven. The Emperor said: 'What sounds are these?' The attendant replied: 'The sounds of the celestial jade women.' The assembled kings and ministers prostrated themselves upon the ground, raising cries of grief to heaven. The precious elephants and celestial horses wept tears, full of longing for the Celestial King; none failed to prostrate themselves. The Emperor said: 'Bring a small stool and place it below the hall.' He invited the jade women, the assembled kings and ministers, and those accompanying the elephants, horses, and precious carriages forward. The first and principal consort took her seat at the Emperor's side. The Emperor, with a fondness as toward daughters and younger sisters, attended to the consorts. The principal consort raised her hand and pointed to the jade women, saying: 'The forms of the celestial women shine brilliantly throughout the world; dressed in celestial raiment rarely seen in the world, may the king retain even a small consideration and accord with their wishes. The precious elephants and celestial horses; a horse named Huanqing adorned with white pearl night-luminous jewels and precious garlands shines brilliantly throughout the realm. The four kings of the four directions all possess the sagacity of holy men; reverently they serve and call themselves subjects, filially obedient, compassionate, and loyal, full of longing for the Celestial King.'

"King Sudarśana said: 'In life after life I have cherished a heart of compassion. Among the women of this world, mutual jealousy generates disaster and evil that spreads even to the king's person. Considering these grave misfortunes, I wish to be far from them. From this day forward, all of you women are to me as daughters and younger sisters.' All the consorts raised cries of grief, saying: 'What shall become of us, parted from you while still alive?' They all removed their adornments from their persons and cast them to the ground. The principal consort beat her own breast, wept bitterly, and cried out: 'Celestial King! Upon whom shall I depend?' The Emperor said: 'Human life is extremely short; your sorrows will only grow longer. The body is a vessel of decay; death comes at no appointed time. From this day, fix your heart upon the virtues of the śramaṇas; keep women at a distance and befriend the worthy. Honor the Way above all else; cultivate yourself and attend to your own concerns; you cannot attend to the concerns of others.'

"He addressed the assembled kings: 'Life is short and anxieties are long. Care well for yourselves. There is none born who does not die. Rectify your hearts and conduct, with compassion, love, and filial obedience; honor is difficult to preserve long.'

"The assembled kings bowed their heads to the ground and took sincere leave, saying: 'The four celestial kingdoms all attend upon the Celestial King. We have ever heard the holy sages lament this land as having no equal. The Emperor and his assembled ministers; none are not divine sages. The precious treasures of the realm are like those of the heavens above. May the Celestial King take compassion and return to his sage reflections.'

"The Emperor addressed the assembled kings: 'Human life is extremely short; the afflictions of the mundane world are proportionately long. Care well for yourselves. Life endures no more than a single breath; there is none born who does not die. Abandon the conduct of covetous lust and defiling turbidity.'

"The Emperor arose and ascended the hall. Seated upon the golden throne, with a heart of great compassion he turned toward the consorts, the assembled ministers and kings, the common people, elephants and horses, and all those laboring in the ten directions; with a heart of grief and compassion, wishing them all to come to know the Buddha, he moved from the golden throne to the silver throne, contemplating the way of non-action. From the silver throne to the lapis lazuli throne, he contemplated the practice of compassion and pity, to deliver sentient beings. From the lapis lazuli throne to the crystal throne, he contemplated the conduct of great filial piety, wishing to liberate the parents of numberless kalpas. He reflected on himself, contemplating the impure outflows of the five organs and nine orifices. The Emperor said: 'In former times I once contracted a single illness that was as though bamboo ropes strangled my neck and wooden awls bored into my body. The body is a vessel of suffering; how can one rely upon it?'"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The wheel-turning emperor Sudarśana; that was my own person. After the king's life came to its end, he was reborn in the Brahma-heaven. Who could have known that the body of the Buddha would act as a wheel-turning emperor, practicing the true Dharma? Furthermore, there are four virtues and seven treasures that arise naturally within a territory four hundred eighty li long and two hundred eighty li wide from the region of Kuśinagara, all within the city. I have placed my body in this earth seven times before; now I have obtained the severance of craving, the concentration of emptiness and the signless, and have cut off the source of birth and death. From this day forward, I shall not take on a body again.

"Ānanda! Go into the city and announce to the people: 'At midnight tonight the Buddha will pass into parinirvāṇa. If any among you have doubts, hasten to come and resolve them. Be careful not to regret it later, carrying unresolved anguish for long ages.'" The Buddha was in a small settlement, contrary to what his endowment of precepts would suggest. Ānanda followed the instruction. The people all said: "For what reason does the Buddha, being in a small settlement, pass into nirvāṇa?" The people all prostrated themselves on the ground; some knocked their heads, others slapped their faces; some beat their chests, scratched their faces, and tore their hair; they rent their robes, stamped the ground, and wept, crying out: "What shall we do!" The king heard this and was startled, saying: "What grief is this?" The king sent a close minister to ask what the matter was outside. The people, sobbing and choking, said: "Ānanda has announced: 'The Buddha is about to pass into nirvāṇa. Whatever doubts are in your hearts, go and resolve them.' That is why we grieve." The minister returned and reported: "Ānanda has instructed the people that the Buddha is about to enter parinirvāṇa, and has told them to go and resolve their doubts. That is why they weep."

The king immediately summoned the crown prince Atiśa and commanded him: "Go to where the Buddha is, bow your head to the Buddha's feet, and respectfully inquire after his welfare. Humbly implore the World-Honored One to ascend the path of nirvāṇa in the great hall of the palace, and not to enter parinirvāṇa in a small settlement." The crown prince said: "If the World-Honored One does not grant this, what then?" The king said: "Receive his instruction and return quickly." The crown prince arrived where the Buddha was. Ānanda said: "The king of Kuśinagara has sent the crown prince. I have not yet dared to announce his arrival." The Buddha said: "Bid him enter." The crown prince prostrated with his five limbs upon the ground, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, then drew back and knelt upright, saying: "The king has sent Atiśa to bow his head to the Buddha's feet and respectfully inquire after his welfare. Sentient beings are submerged in the depths; only the Buddha saves and delivers them. Now that you are to pass into nirvāṇa, why so swiftly, and why here in a small settlement rather than in the palace?"

The Buddha told Atiśa: "Convey my regards to your father the king. I spoke of this previously: in former times I was a wheel-turning emperor, the last holy emperor, named Sudarśana. Seven times I gave up my body here; with this present death it is eight. Now that I have attained the Way, I shall no longer place my body in this earth. Convey my regards to your father the king, and console the crown prince."

The crown prince returned to the palace; evening had just fallen. The crown prince reported to the king everything from beginning to end. The king was startled and wept. He commanded the people of the realm throughout the land to go and receive the Buddha's illuminating Dharma. At the time of the second watch the king went to where the Buddha was, accompanied by a multitude of one hundred forty thousand people, all waiting outside. The king said to Ānanda: "I and one hundred forty thousand people wish to receive the Buddha's precepts." Ānanda conveyed the king's intention to the Buddha. The World-Honored One immediately said: "The king and the people have taken great pains." Ānanda asked: "Should the king be sent away?" The Buddha said: "No. I should meet with them." The king and the eminent and virtuous of the realm entered together; all bowed their heads and faces to the Buddha's feet, then drew back and stood with palms joined. At that time there were no lamps or fires before the Buddha; the Buddha emitted light from the crown of his head, illuminating two thousand li. The Buddha thanked the king and his ministers and people for their trouble in coming. The king bowed his head and said: "What precepts does the Buddha have for us to uphold and practice?"

The Buddha said: "I directed a messenger to say that the Buddha has expounded the sūtras for forty-nine years; the worthy men of your kingdom all practice them themselves. The king may now return to the palace; at midnight tonight I shall enter parinirvāṇa." The king and ministers and people all raised cries of grief. The Buddha told the king: "I have heard that of those who are born, none fail to decay and perish; what purpose is served by weeping? Subdue the fierce mind; ascend to the celestial gateway of the Dharma; distance yourself from evil and cherish yourself; diligently cultivate virtue and befriend the worthy. When affairs arise, reflect deeply; do not add hasty violence. Human life is difficult to obtain; take compassion on the ten thousand living beings. Those who are enlightened may be reproached; the ignorant should be pardoned and forgiven. The world is full of perversity; cherish and honor yourself." The king and the worthy ones all withdrew. The king halted and encamped five li from the Buddha.

In the realm there was an elder named Subhadra, one hundred twenty years old, at that time within the city. Waking in the night, he saw the Buddha's radiance illuminating the entire city. There was no one in his house; he immediately left the city and hastened to where the Buddha was. He said to Ānanda: "Please announce on my behalf; I have a doubt in my heart concerning the World-Honored One." Ānanda said: "The night is already past midnight; the Buddha is about to pass away. Please do not disturb him." Subhadra replied: "Can it truly not be announced? I have heard that only once in countless ages is there a Buddha. Now I have come to resolve a doubt, yet you will not announce it. The doubt I have, only the Buddha can resolve; no other can do so." Ānanda said: "Please stop! There is no need to inquire." The Buddha knew that Subhadra was outside wishing to resolve his doubt. He called Ānanda and asked: "Why have you not announced Subhadra's wish to have his doubt resolved?" Ānanda replied: "Seeing that the night is already past midnight and the Buddha is about to pass into nirvāṇa, I feared he would come in and trouble the Buddha with talk. The Buddha is now about to abandon the three realms of desire and attain the way of non-action." The Buddha said: "Bring Subhadra in; if he has a doubt, he should ask." Ānanda immediately brought Subhadra in. Upon hearing that he was to enter, Subhadra's heart leaped with joy and his whole body trembled. He came forward and bowed his head and face to the Buddha's feet. Seeing that Subhadra was elderly and his breath faint, the Buddha gave him a stool to sit upon. The Buddha asked Subhadra: "What is your doubt?" He replied: "The Buddha is the god above gods in the three realms, spiritually holy beyond measure, supremely venerable without equal. You have opened and transformed, guided and led beings for forty-nine years; the immortal sages, Brahmā, and Indra have all bowed their heads in submission. I have eight companions in aspiration: one called Kūrma, one called Nūpūrva, one called Zhīháng, one called Báilùzǐ, one called Yánshòu, one called Jìjīnfán, one called Duōjīyuàn, and one called Nirgrantha. The wisdom of these eight men shines no more than a firefly or a candle; their goodness lacks even a thread's worth of benefit. Within they harbor the three poisons; without they run after desires. Seated, they compose empty discourses; their writings are false, not true. They have never come to receive transformation; do they have karmic affinity?"

The Buddha told Subhadra: "The meaning of their teachings contradicts the Buddha's sūtras; it is a path of birth and death, pursuing wealth and honor, is it not? The aspiration of my Way is to sever craving and contemplate emptiness, without desiring worldly glory, tranquil and without action; this I take as my joy." Subhadra said: "What is the way of non-action?" The Buddha said: "To extinguish existence and return to the source, no longer being born or dying; this is called non-action. The aspirations of your companions all contain the eight evils. What are the eight? To sacrifice to ghosts and spirits, to divine and vainly kill; this is the first. To dwell at home in covetousness and gluttony, not to uphold the way of filial piety, to be covetously attached to ten thousand perversities with desires that know no cessation; this is the second. Double-tongued speech, malicious words, false speech, and duplicitous language, never presenting goodness or leading the ignorant away from evil; this is the third. Killing, stealing, and licentiousness; this is the fourth. Always harboring a mind of anger, being unfilial toward one's two parents, treating lightly and disrespecting brothers, wife, children, and the nine clans, the mind being deviant and conduct impure, with no virtuous guidance, always being arrogant and desiring others' fear and reverence; this is the fifth. Day and night harboring perversity, not fearing the law, treating the worthy with contempt, honoring the turbid and defiled, avoiding the true and correct, consorting with evil persons; this is the sixth. Upon hearing of worthy and wise persons, of śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas who have illumined the sūtras, harboring resentment and jealousy in advance and composing false slanders; this is the seventh. Not reverencing one's ancestors nor being filially devoted to one's parents, abandoning the worthy and bright while turning to wickedness, disparaging benevolence and righteousness, and not recognizing that the defilements and turbidity of worldly custom are shameful; this is the eighth evil. If it is claimed: 'The World-Honored One has expounded the sūtras for forty-nine years, yet these eight persons have never come to receive transformation'; your companions all harbor these eight evils. How could they rejoice in pure transformation? Even if they were to come, the Buddha would not accept them. Subhadra! If one's heart contains these eight evils, do not presume to ask the Buddha: 'By upholding these eight precepts, can one attain the stage of stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, or arahant?' By practicing these eight precepts, you must rectify your mind accordingly; then you are a disciple of the Buddha. If there are ordinary persons who presume on their own to serve as guides and teachers, yet violate these eight precepts, they are all malevolent deceivers; you should distance yourself from them and not listen to or receive their teachings."

The World-Honored One said: "Within the three realms, I speak and walk alone, with none my equal. Whatever doubt you have, ask freely without hesitation." Subhadra bowed his head and knelt upright, saying: "As the Buddha has said, I nearly lost my own person and nearly fell into foolishness." The World-Honored One asked again: "Have you understood the eight precepts?" He replied: "I have understood." He bowed his head again and said: "I wish to relinquish my inferior and base practices and undertake the pure conduct of a śramaṇa." The World-Honored One asked again: "Are you sincere?" He replied: "I implore the Buddha's compassion; receive me as a śramaṇa." Subhadra's hair fell to the ground of itself; the monastic robe appeared upon his body. With focused mind he contemplated the teaching; suddenly he was without conceptualization, his mind utterly pure, like a bright moonstone pearl. He immediately attained the path of arahantship. He then reflected: "I cannot allow my teacher to enter nirvāṇa before me." Immediately, before the Buddha, he entered the path of nirvāṇa.

The Buddha called the bhikṣus in and said: "After my passing, if there are people of the world who have abandoned their homes and left behind defilement, wishing to become śramaṇas and enter the community of bhikṣus, they should first be tested for three months, to ascertain the excellence or deficiency of their conduct. There are four types of people in the world: one type are the poor who cannot sustain themselves and wish to become bhikṣus; one type are those in debt who have no means to repay and wish to become bhikṣus; one type are those in official service who currently have no use and wish to become bhikṣus; one type are men of noble conduct, pure and undefiled, who have heard that only once in countless ages is there a Buddha, who have encountered the Buddha's scriptural teachings, whose hearts have awakened with joy, who have relinquished their homes and abandoned desire, who do not covet worldly glory, and who have come to become bhikṣus. After my nirvāṇa, for all those who come, observe their aspirations and tendencies, observe their sitting and rising, observe their speech and language, observe their gait and bearing, discern their conduct and behavior, noting where good and evil tend, whether they apply their minds to seeking the Way, whether they are diligent with delight. After three months of careful examination, if their aspirations are lofty and their conduct pure, the community may accept and ordain them as bhikṣus. Once one has become a bhikṣu, an elder who is well versed in the Dharma and Vinaya should be selected to serve as their teacher and confer upon them the ten precepts. After three years of upholding the precepts with trembling care and without transgression, if all the worthy of the community approve, the two hundred fifty precepts should be conferred; the ten precepts as the foundation, and the remaining two hundred forty precepts as matters of propriety and ritual. If in future generations you implement this Dharma, the gods of heaven and earth will all be filled with reverence and joy.

"The precepts and Dharma of the Buddha; all you bhikṣus, contemplate them carefully, and do not, because the Buddha has entered parinirvāṇa, become negligent and transgress the Dharma. What the Buddha practiced, what the disciples contemplate; let the old and young uphold one another in conduct, not acting with filial impiety. Those who do not joyfully seek the Way but covet venerable honor should read this sūtra. Those who seek longevity and wish to be reborn in the heavens should read this sūtra. The great essence of the Buddha is to tend toward the way of non-action. After my nirvāṇa, do not, because the Buddha has departed, say there is nothing left to rely upon; you should rely upon the sūtras and the precepts. After my nirvāṇa, transmit and receive the teaching in succession; take delight in the sūtras and uphold the precepts; hold the two hundred fifty precepts and honor one another in succession as you would in filial service to parents. Elder bhikṣus should instruct their successors just as when I was present. For later-born bhikṣus who fall ill, the elder bhikṣus should give heed and attend to their welfare with diligent inquiry. Illuminate the teaching, read the sūtras, admonish gently and with harmony, uphold the Buddha's precepts in accord; my Way can long endure. After my nirvāṇa, worthy sons and worthy daughters will in time reflect: 'In our world there was a Buddha, and there are wonderful scriptural teachings.' On the very day the Buddha first enters parinirvāṇa in the world, all of you demonstrate the utmost filial respect toward the Buddha and compassionate hearts toward the sūtras; when your own lives come to their end, you will all ascend to the heavens. Do not, because I have departed, fail to uphold the sūtras and precepts; be careful not to be negligent and remiss.

"All you bhikṣus! Look carefully at the Buddha's countenance; the Buddha cannot rise again. After fifteen billion, seven hundred sixty million years hence, there will again be a Buddha. A Buddha in the world is difficult to encounter; the Dharma is difficult to hear; the Sangha is difficult to encounter; only a Buddha is most difficult to see. Within Jambudvīpa there is a venerable tree called the Udumbara; it bears fruit but no flower. When the Udumbara tree bears a golden flower, then there is a Buddha in the world. Now, at this very moment, I am about to enter parinirvāṇa. For those among you who have unresolved doubts concerning the sūtras, while the Buddha is still present, resolve your doubts now. If you do not now dispel your entanglements, afterwards do not turn to disputing at length; while I am present, hasten to resolve your doubts."

At that time Ānanda was standing behind the Buddha. He bowed his head and said to the Buddha: "Since the Buddha began his transforming work, among the assembly of bhikṣus none have unresolved doubts." The disciples themselves declared: "We have no doubts, O God above gods!" The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The night is already past midnight; let there be no more sound."

The Buddha sat upright and entered deep contemplation of the source of the Way, relinquishing both good and evil, encompassing the three realms entire. His years had now reached seventy-nine. He contemplated severing the deep pool of the revolving stream of birth and death. Contemplating in deep meditation, he ascended from the Heaven of the Four Celestial Kings up to the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, then turned and descended back down through the body, reflecting upon the four great elements and the impure outflows within the body; not one thing worthy of cherishing. Facing north, he pillowed his hand, lay down upon his right side, bent his knees, and stacked his feet, and then entered parinirvāṇa.

Heaven and earth moved greatly. The gods scattered flowers and incense and wept in grief, crying out: "The Dharma King has passed away; upon whom shall we depend?" The king and his multitude of one hundred forty thousand people prostrated themselves, calling out to the Buddha: "Sentient beings are long in decline; what shall we do with this grief?" Some fainted and then revived. The second Indra addressed the gods: "The Buddha always said that of those who are born, none fail to die. You should all contemplate the truth of impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self, and weep no more." The king of the seventh heaven also rushed down, saying: "The Buddha's light has gone dark; even the Buddha has abandoned his body; what do you all hope for? Those who weep and prostrate themselves; can they recover what is lost?" Among the bhikṣus, some rolled upon the ground weeping and cried out: "The eye of the three realms has been extinguished; how swiftly! From this day forward the world is in long decline." Some stood weeping; some stopped breathing and stared fixedly at the body. Among them some reflected deeply: "The Buddha always said: 'None born fails to die.' To weep for the body; what benefit is that to the illuminating Dharma?"

There was one bhikṣu named Aniruddha who said to Ānanda: "Stop the bhikṣus from compounding their grief; stop the king and ministers and people; stop the gods above; let none grieve and lament further." Ānanda asked: "Looking up at the gods, how many are there?" He said: "For four hundred eighty li in all directions, heads joined to heads, all are venerable gods; if a small needle were cast from above, it would not fall to the ground." Aniruddha went up to stop the gods; the gods' grief and lamentation doubled in sorrow. Aniruddha said to Ānanda: "The Buddha did not instruct us to prepare the coffin and lay the body to rest; go quickly and inform the Licchavī household managers. We ourselves can prepare the burial. The World-Honored One has commanded that the Licchavī household managers prepare the coffin and burial, and there should be no resentment." Ānanda immediately went to the Licchavī household managers and told them the matter as it stood. The Licchavī household managers raised cries of grief: "The World-Honored One has passed away; we are left bereft. The wise cry out in sorrow, having relied only on the World-Honored One." Some five hundred persons went to petition the king, saying: "We beg to handle the burial alone." The king said: "The Buddha has departed; sentient beings are left bereft. With harmonious hearts and without contention, give your full filial care; the Buddha has compassion for all his children, and this will bring you great good fortune." When the king spoke these words, none were not choked with grief.

The household managers and the people lifted the Buddha's golden litter and returned into the city. The gods used covers of precious gems to spread over the Buddha's bed; pennants and canopies led the way; flowers, incense, and mixed jewels fell below like snow. Twelve kinds of music all played in accompaniment from behind; gods, humans, dragons, and spirits all raised cries of grief. The household managers asked: "When the World-Honored One was present, he commanded the method for preparing the coffin and burial; what is that Dharma?" Ānanda said: "While the Buddha was present he said: 'Follow the method of the wheel-turning emperor, and the Buddha exceeds even that.'" The household managers asked: "What is the burial method for a holy emperor?" He said: "Use new fine silk to wrap the body tightly; use new kappāsika cloth to wrap over it again. Place the body in an outer coffin and pour fragrant ointment throughout, letting it permeate the entire body; cover the top with a lid. With sandalwood fragrant firewood, fir fragrant firewood, catalpa firewood, and camphor firewood, arranged above and below the coffin, thirty zhàng high and wide on all four sides; cast fire and cremate. Twelve types of music play together simultaneously. Scatter all manner of fine flowers and incense on top. Gather the relics, separating out the ash and charcoal, thoroughly wash them clean with fine fragrant liquid, and place them in a golden vessel. Place the golden vessel on a golden litter and set it in the hall of fasting and precepts within the palace. After ninety days, at the four crossroads, erect a stūpa and raise a banner post, hang silk, distribute pennants, and make offerings of flowers, incense, and music. This is the burial method for a wheel-turning emperor; for the Buddha it shall exceed even this." The Licchavī household managers wiped away their tears and said: "Yes. We will certainly follow these clear instructions; we beg seven days." The household managers together informed the king: "We wish to prepare the coffin for the body of the Heavenly Honored One; we implore the king to be present." The king said: "I respectfully agree." The household managers together raised the Buddha's golden litter and, returning, entered from the west city gate, reaching the center of the city. After seven days, with a gathering of three hundred thousand people, they together prepared the coffin. All the assembled people held twelve types of music; day and night they burned lamps, and the lamplight extended twelve li outward from the city on every side, at every step.

The second Indra led a heavenly multitude of one hundred thousand to descend, bringing twelve types of celestial precious music, flowers, incense, and various jewels, hovering in the empty sky three li above the ground. Indra alone descended and asked Aniruddha: "What was the Buddha's command?" Aniruddha fully conveyed the Buddha's instructions to Indra. Indra said: "I wish to prepare the burial with all manner of precious jewels, musical instruments, flowers, and incense; is this appropriate?" He replied: "I should inquire about this." Aniruddha fully conveyed Indra's intention to Ānanda. Ānanda replied: "Did not the Buddha, while present, issue a command to the gods and the king; that they were not to prepare the coffin, and that he commanded the Licchavī household managers to carry out the burial? Convey my regards to Brahmā and Indra; I implore them to understand the Buddha's intention." He returned and conveyed Ānanda's words fully to the gods. The gods said: "Are our Emperor on High's burial preparations inferior to those of the common people?" He replied: "What manner of words are those? The World-Honored One's command was issued with weighty and precious import; be careful not to contradict one another." The Licchavī household managers then said: "Lift the Buddha's relic litter; we wish to bring it out the west city gate, but the litter will not move." The household managers all said: "The litter will not move; how can we get it out of the city?" Ānanda asked Aniruddha: "Why will the litter not move?" He replied: "The gods wish to prepare the coffin, and so they are causing the litter not to be lifted." Aniruddha said: "I shall go up and make this understood to Brahmā, Indra, and the gods." He immediately went up and told Brahmā and Indra: "Ānanda respectfully conveys his regards to the gods regarding the funeral arrangements; this is the Buddha's own intention." Brahmā, Indra, and the gods said: "We have brought our burial preparations and come here; may we not be permitted to walk on the right side of the litter, with the king and the common people on the left, sending off the World-Honored One with music, flowers, and incense?" He replied: "I shall return and report this." Aniruddha returned and fully reported the gods' intention to Ānanda. Ānanda said: "Those who wish to prepare the coffin; that violates the Buddha's instructions. But to serve as filial companions in the sending-off is permitted." He immediately reported this to Brahmā and Indra. Their request being granted, all the gods were filled with joy. They all descended to take their place on the right side of the Buddha's golden litter; the king and the people were on the left side. The household managers asked: "May we lift the Buddha's litter and carry it out the west city gate?" Ānanda said: "You may."

Indra went ahead and took the right front foot of the litter in his hand; the Brahmā King held the right rear foot of the litter; Ānanda held the left front foot of the litter; the king held the left rear foot of the litter. The Licchavī household managers bound the two front feet of the litter with silk. The gods and humans grieved and lamented together, drawing the golden litter forward. Gods, dragons, ghosts, and spirits scattered flowers, mixed jewels, fine incense, musical instruments, pennants, and flower canopies, each serving in the procession. The king and the common people likewise made their offerings. The gods above sang dirges praising the Buddha's virtues; the common people below followed with songs of grief. Gods, spirits, ghosts, and dragons, emperor, king, and common people; all lamented together, beating their chests and calling out to the Buddha who had passed away: upon whom could sentient beings now rely? The procession went out the west city gate and proceeded to the Pāvā-bandana hall, where there was a great lecture hall, and the Buddha was placed in the hall. The Licchavī household managers, following the Buddha's dying instructions, wrapped the body in fine silk, wound one thousand layers of kappāsika cloth crisscrossed over it, placed the body in an outer coffin, poured fragrant ointment to permeate the entire body, and covered it above with a celestial canopy. The household managers together lifted the coffin down from the hall, and in the courtyard they arranged sandalwood fragrant firewood, fir fragrant firewood, catalpa and camphor firewood, and nanmu firewood, thirty zhàng high and wide. Gods, spirits, ghosts, and dragons, assembled kings, and common people all scattered flowers and incense upon the firewood. The household managers lit the firewood, but the fire would not burn. They asked Aniruddha: "Why, when we light it, does the fire not burn?" He replied: "The Buddha has an elder disciple named Mahākāśyapa, who has been traveling throughout the land teaching and transforming beings, and is now returning, bringing two thousand disciples with him, together with an immeasurable host of gods, all wishing to see the Buddha whole one last time; therefore the fire does not burn." The household managers said: "Very well." They instructed everyone to wait.


Kāśyapa came together with four groups of disciples, five hundred in each group, and rested along the road. There was a wandering ascetic named Upavāna, who came from the place of the Buddha carrying heavenly flowers called māndārava. Upon seeing Mahākāśyapa accompanied by two thousand disciples, Upavāna approached and, having bowed and exchanged courtesies, Kāśyapa asked: "From where do you come?" He replied: "I come from the kingdom of Nāgara." Kāśyapa asked: "Do you know my great teacher, the Buddha?" "Yes, I know him. It has now been seven days since his passing into nirvāṇa. It is from there that I received these divine flowers."

At that moment, among the disciples, those who had not yet attained the vision of truth, upon hearing of the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, were without exception stricken with shock. They beat their breasts and rolled about in anguish, crying out: "What shall become of us! In what shall sentient beings take refuge?" Those who had attained the vision of truth, holding deeply to the Buddha's admonition that all the world is impermanent and that love and attachment are like illusions, asked themselves: who can abide forever?

Among the assembly of bhikṣus, there was one bhikṣu, aged and benighted, who did not comprehend the sage's intent. Seeing the bhikṣus overcome with intense grief, he stood and restrained them, saying: "Please do not grieve. While the Blessed One was present, the Dharma and precepts were many and weighty: 'This is not the Dharma, that is not proper conduct'; and to hold to this was without transgression or violation. Now that the Blessed One has departed, we are free. Is that not wonderful?" All the bhikṣus condemned him, and together they reported this to the devas. The devas seized the old bhikṣu and cast him out from the assembly.

Mahākāśyapa commanded the bhikṣus: "Hasten on the road." The four groups of disciples, along with countless devas and humans, wept as they walked, all arriving together at the place of the Buddha. They circumambulated the hall three times, placed their heads and faces to the ground, and some fell prone in the earth, vomiting blood and losing consciousness.

Kāśyapa gazed intently at the Buddha's golden coffin and thought to himself: "I have come too late, and have not been in time to see my teacher. I do not know where the Blessed One's head and feet lie." At the sound of this, the Buddha's two feet extended forth from the coffin. Kāśyapa immediately placed his head and face at the Buddha's feet, recounted the Buddha's virtues, and spoke this verse:

> He is free from birth and aging, likewise free from death;
> He will no more encounter hatred, no meeting with the loathed.
> No more will he cause loved ones to be parted by craving;
> Let us therefore seek skillful means to attain that place.
>
> For him the five aggregates are done, no longer to be received;
> No more will there be action that leads to receiving these five aggregates.
> Suffering is utterly exhausted; the root of existence also removed;
> Let us therefore seek skillful means to attain that place.
>
> The Buddha has severed all ties to the world; craving and desire are concluded;
> This is called forbearance, and the afflictions of the world are abandoned.
> The Buddha attained peace himself, and also brought peace and safety to the world;
> We can only join our palms and humbly pay reverence to the Buddha.
>
> The Dharma the Buddha taught is the brightest light in the world;
> The Buddha most clearly perceived the path, at peace and unobstructed.
> He also brought life to all under heaven, freeing them from aging and death;
> How could anyone in the world not receive the Buddha's grace?
>
> The moon rises to dispel the darkness of night;
> The sun rises to illuminate the day;
> Lightning flashes forth to illuminate the clouds;
> The Buddha's light has arisen to illuminate the three realms.
>
> Among all rivers, none surpasses the great Kunlun river;
> Among all great waters, none surpasses the ocean.
> Of all the brilliance of stars and constellations, the moon shines brightest;
> The Buddha in the world is the most venerable, above heaven and below.
>
> The Buddha has transcended the world, bestowing blessings even unto this day;
> The Buddha's teaching of precepts and conduct remains clear even unto this day.
> Even unto this day the Buddha's disciples receive and practice it;
> All devas and humans reverently join their palms in homage.

When Kāśyapa had finished his praise, the divine spirits, ghosts and nāgas, kings and common people, all paid reverence at the Buddha's feet. When the homage was complete, the feet withdrew back into the coffin. The devas, humans, ghosts, and nāgas, seeing the feet withdraw, were all the more choked with grief and wept together simultaneously.

When the weeping ceased, Kāśyapa and the bhikṣus exchanged words of condolence. The householders entrusted with the funeral rites kindled the fire for the cremation. The devas scattered flowers and incense, all saying: "How destitute will sentient beings now be!" The Buddha's light shone through to the seventh brahma heaven. In the dark, hidden, and shadowy places of the ten directions, beings who had never seen one another, receiving the radiance of the Buddha's light, were all at once brilliantly illuminated and said with joyful faces: "What is this light?"

The funeral householders deliberated. When the Buddha's flesh was consumed, they poured fragrant milk on the fire to extinguish it, then carefully washed the relics and placed them in a golden urn. The Buddha's inner and outer robes remained intact as before; the cotton burial cloth that had been wrapped around the body was burned to ash. They took the urn of relics, placed it upon a golden bier, and brought it back into the palace, where it rested in the main hall. The devas and humans scattered flowers and performed music; step by step around the city, lamps were lit, filling twelve li of ground.

Ānanda said to Aniruddha: "Bid farewell to the devas and nāgas and let each return to their dwelling." The devas, nāgas, and spirits, each shedding tears, said: "The sun and moon of the three realms are gone; the world will be in lasting decline. When facing such a great loss, how long can one stay? Why do you hasten us away?" He replied: "The people are in commotion, wishing to offer flowers and incense. It would be proper for you to withdraw temporarily so that the hearts of the people may be expressed."

Śakra asked Aniruddha: "On what day will the Blessed One's stūpa be erected?" Aniruddha asked Ānanda. Ānanda replied: "Ninety days from now, a caitya and stūpa shall be established at the crossroads." All the devas said: "Waiting ninety days; will there be occasion for that?" Aniruddha said: "Among the four groups of disciples, those in distant places will surely hasten here to fulfill their aspirations." All the devas simultaneously placed their heads and faces to the ground; with Śakra at the fore and the devas following in attendance, they circumambulated the hall three times and departed in tears.

Twenty thousand bhikṣus remained to guard the relics. The king was also bid farewell and returned to his palace; along with his ministers prostrating to the ground in tears, they circumambulated the hall three times and returned to the palace. The king commanded all the royal consorts and court ladies to observe the precepts for the full ninety days. Those householders in charge of the funeral rites were likewise solemn and reverent.

From all four directions, word spread that the Buddha had passed into parinirvāṇa. The four groups of disciples from the kingdom of Kuśinagara brought flowers and incense, weeping until the roads were blocked. They circumambulated the hall three times, prostrated to the ground, and in grief and anguish cried out: "What is to be done?" Kings within a thousand li all sent their crown princes; those beyond a thousand li sent their crown princes to lead their ministers and subjects, all coming to the place of the Buddha. They circumambulated the hall in grief and made offerings of flowers and incense; those who arrived first departed first, those who arrived later departed later.

All the bhikṣus asked Ānanda: "What is the proper form of the funeral?" He replied: "Go east, thirty li from the city. In that land there is a village called Vaidhita. At the four crossroads, establish a caitya and stūpa. Make bricks of jade, each brick three chi square; the stūpa to be fifteen chi square. Place the golden urn of relics precisely in the center. Erect the stūpa and set up the banner pole, hanging silk streamers high. Burn incense, light lamps, sweep the ground clean and scatter flowers. Perform twelve kinds of music morning and evening as offerings. Those householders entrusted with the funeral should together complete the stūpa. Śakra, Brahmā, ghosts and nāgas, kings and subjects, shall all escort the Buddha's relics." The householders respectfully agreed and followed Ānanda's instructions.

Mahākāśyapa and the arhants deliberated together with Aniruddha: "These three hundred thousand people, together with kings, ministers, and subjects, will eventually all be reborn in the Tuṣita heaven, where Maitreya dwells. When Maitreya becomes a Buddha, at his first teaching of the Dharma, ninety-six koṭis of bhikṣus will attain arhatship. Maitreya will then teach the Dharma to sentient beings, saying: 'All these miraculous powers belong to those who, in the time of Śākyamuni Buddha, built stūpas, hung silk banners, burned incense, lit lamps, and upheld the Buddha's precepts. They are all upāsakas and upāsikās.'"

Mahākāśyapa, Ānanda, and all the arhants deliberated: "When the king of Kuśinagara passes away, what path will he go toward?" Mahākāśyapa said: "When this king passes away, he will be reborn in the heaven of the Twelve Subtle Waters. Later, when Maitreya descends to become a Buddha, he will be named Sudatta. He will build palaces for Maitreya, construct halls for receiving the teaching of the path, hermitages, parks for the solitary and poor, and provide food, clothing, and medicine for the saṃgha of bhikṣus."

Ānanda asked Mahākāśyapa: "Why will the king of Kuśinagara not attain the path of arhatship in the presence of Maitreya Buddha?" Mahākāśyapa replied: "This king's mind of desire has not yet grown weary of the suffering of birth, aging, sickness, death, worry, and grief. Therefore he will not attain the path of arhatship." Kāśyapa said to Ānanda: "Those who are not weary of the afflictions of birth and death will ultimately not attain the path." Ānanda replied: "I have long been weary of them. Why then have I not attained the path?" Mahākāśyapa said: "You merely uphold the precepts, but do not contemplate the inner and outer evils of the body. You continue flowing through birth and death merely on account of sustenance."

Eight border kingdoms, upon hearing of the Buddha's parinirvāṇa and learning that the relics were in the kingdom of Kuśinagara, all sent armies to demand a share of the relics. The king of Kuśinagara said: "The Buddha dwelt in our kingdom and has now passed into parinirvāṇa. It is we who should make the offerings. You have come from afar in vain; the relics cannot be obtained." The eight kings replied: "We join our palms and request a share of the relics. If you absolutely refuse to give us any, we will take them by force of arms."

Śakra, Lord of Devas, saw the eight kings quarreling, each wishing to obtain relics to bring back to their kingdoms for worship. He transformed himself into a brahmin, calling himself Droṇa, joined his palms, and came forward to enlighten the eight kings, saying: "Hear my one word. When the Buddha was present, all kings reverently received his teaching of compassion and benevolence. As rulers of the people, it is not right to quarrel. You should practice the four immeasurables and divide the Buddha's relics, so that every kingdom may have a stūpa, opening the blind ignorance of the people so they may know there is a Buddha, making this their ancestral heritage, and thus attaining great blessings." The divine spirits, ghosts and nāgas, kings and common people, all said: "Well said, Droṇa! You universally bestow a field of merit upon sentient beings." They all requested Droṇa to make an equal division into eight parts. Droṇa himself used a heavenly golden urn coated inside with rock honey, measured out the relics evenly, and gave each king one urn. The kings, upon receiving them, were overcome with mingled sorrow and joy, and all made offerings of fragrant flowers, hanging silk banners, variegated silks, incense, lamps, and morning and evening music.

Droṇa knelt and begged for the remaining relics adhering to the honey inside the urn, saying: "I wish to erect a stūpa." The kings graciously granted this to him, and he retrieved what remained within the urn. A renunciant named Huanwei requested relics from the king. The king said: "The distribution is done and cannot be undone. There are only charred coals. Go and take those yourself." The renunciant took the coals and made offerings of flowers and incense.

There were also people from Cakagara who came requesting relics. It was said: "The distribution is done. There is only the remaining ash. Go take that yourselves." They then took the ash and made offerings for ninety days.

Mahākāśyapa, Aniruddha, and Mahākātyāyana deliberated together: "Ānanda followed the Buddha for the longest time and was uniquely intimate with him. The Buddha's teaching and its broad scope; Ānanda penetrated it with his mind, illuminating even the most subtle details. It would be proper to receive from Ānanda the Dharma and Vinaya and record them in full on bamboo and silk."

The assembly of bhikṣus deliberated: "Ānanda, being still without full realization, may perhaps have a covetous mind and conceal fine teachings, refusing to reveal them all." They said: "We should draw them out by a stratagem. Set up a high seat in a special place, have all the noble ones assemble, and with kindly questioning three times back and forth from the assembly of bhikṣus, then inquire about the essentials of the sūtras. In this way the truth may be obtained."

The king of Kuśinagara erected a stūpa hall for the Buddha with meditation halls and meditation chambers totaling three thousand. The bhikṣus dwelt within, reciting sūtras and sitting in meditation. The king sent a minister named Māna with three thousand troops to guard the Buddha's shrine.

Mahākāśyapa and Aniruddha together announced to the assembly of bhikṣus: the recension of the Buddha's sūtras and precepts is named the Four Āgamas. Ānanda had followed the Buddha and been uniquely intimate with him. Because sentient beings are boundlessly given over to licentiousness, the Buddha composed one Āgama; because of vicious anger and perversity, one Āgama; because of foolish ignorance and distance from righteousness, one Āgama; and because of failure to honor one's parents, distance from the wise, failure to revere the Buddha's grace, and failure to contemplate the repayment of kindness, one Āgama.

The assembly of śramaṇas said: "Only Ānanda knows these. The Four Āgamas should come forth from Ānanda." Mahākāśyapa said: "Ānanda is still without full realization; we fear he may have a covetous mind and not fully bring forth the sūtras." The assembly of bhikṣus said: "We can reprove Ānanda regarding past matters. Let Ānanda be placed on the high seat, with the assembly of noble ones below questioning him about the sūtras." All said: "Excellent! This is truly most fitting."

The officiating śramaṇa immediately convened the noble assembly and had Ānanda withdraw outside. When the noble assembly was all seated, they summoned Ānanda and bade him quickly come forward. He came forward and prostrated in homage to the noble assembly. Those who had attained arhatship all remained seated as before; those who had not yet attained it all rose. The officiating śramaṇa bade Ānanda ascend and sit on the central high seat. Ānanda declined, saying: "This is not my seat." The noble assembly all said: "For the sake of the Buddha's sūtras, we honor you there; from you we receive the supreme Dharma of the Buddha." Ānanda then took his seat.

The assembly of worthy ones questioned him: "You have seven faults. Do you know them? When the Blessed One was present, he said: 'Within Jambudvīpa there is great joy.' Did you remain silent about this?" The officiating śramaṇa called out to Ānanda. Ānanda immediately replied: "The Buddha is the supreme, truly enlightened, and holy Venerable One. Would he not have been at liberty to act as he chose? Was it necessary for him to have my words? Even had the Buddha remained in the world for an entire kalpa, the Most Venerable Maitreya would still become a Buddha in his own time." The noble assembly was silent. Ānanda was without fear. The noble assembly all said: "Return and take your seat. We know that your proclamation of the Dharma is the same as the right Dharma heard by the assembly." Having failed to elicit a response by this procedure three times above, Ānanda in turn replied three times from below.

Ānanda then ascended and said: "*Evaṃ mayā śrutam, evaṃ mayā śrutam*; thus have I heard from the Buddha." The assembly of bhikṣus heard Ānanda's words of Dharma: "*Evaṃ mayā śrutam*; thus have I heard from the Buddha." All were choked with grief, saying: "What is to be done! The Buddha has just been in the world, and yet one says again: 'Thus have I heard from the Buddha, who spoke thus.'" Divine spirits, ghosts and nāgas, kings and subjects, the four groups of disciples; none failed to raise their lamentations.

Mahākāśyapa and the noble assembly selected forty who had attained arhatship, and from Ānanda received the Four Āgamas, each Āgama filling sixty bolts of silk. Before the writing of the sūtras was complete, four auspicious trees spontaneously grew within the Buddha's stūpa hall: one tree named Kacana, one named Kapiyana, one named Avara, and one named Nyagrodha. The assembly of bhikṣus said: "With compassionate hearts we have copied the Four Āgamas, and spontaneously four wondrous and sacred trees have grown. The Four Āgamas are the trees of the Buddha's path."

They then made a compact with one another, receiving from the assembly of bhikṣus the two hundred and fifty pure and luminous precepts for monks, the five hundred precepts for bhikṣuṇīs, the five precepts for upāsakas, and the ten precepts for upāsikās.

When the copying of the sūtras was complete, all the bhikṣus practiced the sūtras and precepts and transmitted the teaching to one another for a thousand years. Among those within those thousand years who uphold the precepts, they will be present at the assembly of the fourth Buddha, Maitreya. The Blessed One Maitreya will teach the Dharma to the devas, saying: "Those gathered here today are all those who upheld the precepts in the time of Śākyamuni Buddha and have now come to assemble here." Maitreya Buddha will say: "Be diligent and energetic in your practice. The path is difficult and comprehensive; uphold it as much as you are able."

After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, eight stūpas were built; the ninth was the Vessel Stūpa, the tenth was the Charcoal Stūpa, and the eleventh was the Ash Stūpa.

The sūtra states: The Buddha was born on the eighth day of the fourth month; on the eighth day he renounced the kingdom; on the eighth day he attained enlightenment; on the eighth day he passed into nirvāṇa. At the time of the Puṣya star, he left home to pursue the path; at the time of the Puṣya star he attained the path; and at the time of the Puṣya star he passed into parinirvāṇa. The grasses and trees blossomed and leafed out anew; throughout the kingdom all the trees renewed their flourishing. With the Buddha's entry into parinirvāṇa, the light of the Heaven above heavens of the three realms was extinguished; yet all beings throughout the ten directions spontaneously take refuge in the Buddha.

## Fascicle 2

From the Buddha's parinirvāṇa to the eleventh day of the second month of the seventh year of the Yongxing era, it had been more than eight hundred and eighty-seven years, seven months, and eleven days. To the present year of bingxu, the total is nine hundred and fifteen years. This is recorded by the bhikṣu Kāngri. Furthermore, to the sixth year of the Qingli era, the year of bingxu, the total amounts to one thousand nine hundred and ninety-four years.
