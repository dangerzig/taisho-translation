# 一切流攝守因經
## 一切流攝守因經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0031

Translated from the Chinese. 後漢 安世高譯

## Fascicle 1

*Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on All Āsavas: Their Restraint, Guarding, and Causes*

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Kuru, at an assembly gathered for discussion and contemplation of the Dhamma. At that time, the Buddha addressed the bhikkhus: "Bhikkhus!" They replied: "Even so, Venerable One." The bhikkhus received the instruction from the Buddha. The Buddha then taught thus:

"For the wise and seeing, bhikkhus, the exhaustion of the āsavas is attained; for the unwise and unseeing, the āsavas cannot be exhausted. What, bhikkhus, enables the wise and seeing to exhaust the āsavas, while the unwise and unseeing cannot exhaust them? It is whether one engages in proper attention or improper attention. For one who attends improperly: desire that has not yet arisen arises; desire that has already arisen increases without being checked; the āsavas of existence and ignorance that have not yet arisen arise; those already arisen increase greatly without being checked.

"The ignorant, bhikkhus—the uninstructed worldling who does not see the noble ones, who has not learned the Dhamma from the noble ones, who has not received instruction from the noble ones, who has not received their analytic explanations—comes to engage in improper attention. This causes the āsavas that have not yet arisen to arise, and those already arisen to increase abundantly without being checked; the āsavas of existence and ignorance that have not yet arisen arise; those already arisen increase abundantly without being checked. Not knowing, not understanding, one attends to what should not be attended to and does not attend to what should be attended to. By attending to what should not be attended to and not attending to what should be attended to, the āsava of craving arises; and having arisen, it increases greatly without being checked. The āsava of craving together with the āsava of ignorance that have not yet arisen now arise; those already arisen increase greatly without being checked.

"For the instructed, bhikkhus—the noble disciple who sees with wisdom, who has received instruction from the wise, who has received their analytic explanations—when one thus knows: through improper attention, āsavas of craving not yet arisen will arise, and those already arisen will increase without being checked; āsavas of craving together with ignorance not yet arisen will arise, and those already arisen will increase without being checked. Through proper attention, āsavas of craving not yet arisen will not arise, and those already arisen will be relinquished; āsavas of craving together with ignorance not yet arisen will not arise, and those already arisen will be relinquished. When one thus knows, one attends to what is proper and does not attend to what is improper; what should be attended to, one attends to. By not attending to what should not be attended to and attending to what should be attended to, āsavas of craving not yet arisen do not arise; āsavas of craving already arisen are relinquished and released; āsavas of craving together with ignorance not yet arisen do not arise; āsavas of craving together with ignorance already arisen are relinquished and released.

"There are also, for the bhikkhu, seven āsavas from which affliction, fever, and distress arise. What are the seven? There are, bhikkhus, āsavas abandoned by seeing; āsavas abandoned by restraint; āsavas abandoned by avoidance; āsavas abandoned by using; āsavas abandoned by enduring; āsavas abandoned by dispelling; and āsavas abandoned by cultivation.

"What, bhikkhus, are the āsavas abandoned by seeing? The ignorant, bhikkhus—the uninstructed worldling who does not see the noble ones, who has not received understanding from the noble ones, who has not received their instruction and admonition—engages in improper attention thus: 'Did I exist in past lives? Did I not exist in past lives? What was I in past lives? How was I in past lives? Will I exist in future lives? Will I not exist in future lives? What will I become in future lives? How will I be in future lives?' One quarrels with oneself in doubt about the present: 'From where has this person come? Where will he go next? How must all this be?' Through such improper attention, doubt arises in regard to six positions and various fetters arise. Whether one takes this body to truly exist or takes this body not to truly exist—from this, doubt arises, giving birth to the view of a personal entity. From such doubt arises the view: 'This body is my self.' From such doubt arises the view of a body, a view born from the body itself. From such doubt arises the view that what is not the body is the body. From such doubt arises the reckoning of this as one's very self. Whatever is felt, spoken, done, experienced, whatever is aroused and originated, in whatever place and circumstance, whatever good and evil is enacted—receiving therein the fruit of fault—whether arising or not arising, it is reckoned as just so.

"These fetters, bhikkhus, cause bondage; these doubts cause further doubt. Evil doubt and wrong views, wavering doubt, the fetter of doubt clinging to yet more doubt—the uninstructed worldling, bhikkhus, from this there arises the origin of suffering; from this come birth and arrival at [further suffering]. The instructed, bhikkhus—the noble disciple—one who knows suffering as it truly is, who knows the origin of suffering as it truly is, who knows the cessation of suffering as it truly is, who knows the way leading to the cessation of suffering as it truly is: having thus known and thus seen, three fetters are completely exhausted. The first is the fetter of the view of the body; the second is the fetter of doubt; the third is the fetter of adherence to rules and vows. With these three fetters exhausted, one attains stream-entry, no longer falls into a bad destination, and is certain to be liberated. Moving between the human realm and the heavenly realms, one will not pass through more than seven rebirths; having passed through seven rebirths, one will have made a complete end of suffering. If, bhikkhus, the āsavas that arise in one who does not know and does not see—producing affliction, fever, and distress—are abandoned through seeing, then the affliction, fever, and distress of those āsavas no longer occur. This is called, bhikkhus, the abandonment of āsavas by seeing.

"What, bhikkhus, are the āsavas abandoned by restraint? Here, bhikkhus, the practising bhikkhu, upon seeing forms with the eye, restrains the eye-faculty, practices the contemplation of impurity, and attends mindfully to the original source from which such perceptions arise. When the eye-faculty is not guarded and restrained, when the contemplation of impurity is not practiced, when mindfulness of the original source is not cultivated, āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress arise. When the eye-faculty is guarded and restrained, and through the contemplation of impurity one views the original source, then the āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress are no longer present. This is, bhikkhus, the abandonment of āsavas by restraint. The same applies to the ear, the nose, the mouth, the body, and the mind.

"What, bhikkhus, are the āsavas abandoned by avoidance? Here, bhikkhus, the practising bhikkhu, guarding himself as appropriate, avoids a vicious elephant, avoids a vicious horse, avoids a vicious bull, avoids a vicious dog, avoids a venomous snake, avoids deep pits, avoids thorny ground, avoids ravines, avoids precipices, avoids ponds and lakes, avoids mountains, avoids unsafe places, avoids rivers, avoids deep gorges, avoids evil friends, avoids evil companions, avoids evil seeking, avoids evil acquisitions, avoids evil haunts, avoids evil resting places and bedding. When doubt arises, the wise one sees that one should avoid [these things] as described above, bhikkhus. Without separating from what has been said, the āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress that had been generated are avoided, so that the āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress no longer occur. This is called, bhikkhus, the abandonment of āsavas by avoidance.

"What, bhikkhus, are the āsavas abandoned by using? Here, bhikkhus, the practising bhikkhu uses robes not for elegance, not for pleasure, not for greed, not for adornment, but only to shield this body from mosquitoes, gnats, wind, sun, and contact with harmful creatures, and to guard against distraction of mind. The food consumed is not for pleasure, not for greed, not for strength, not for fine appearance, but only to allow this body to be sustained and to practice the path—to bring an end to [old] pain and not allow new pain to arise, to avoid falling into fault, and to gain the strength and ease needed to practice. The bedding and seats used are not for elegance, not for pleasure, not for greed, not for adornment, but only to let this body rest from the great exertion and fatigue of practice. The medicinal requisites used are also not for elegance, not for pleasure, not for greed, not for adornment, but only to cut off the severe afflictions and unbearable pains that have arisen in this body—to rescue, release, and stop them. If, bhikkhus, āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress arise through improper use of the requisites, they are brought to a stop through proper using; those āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress no longer occur. This is called, bhikkhus, the abandonment of āsavas by using.

"What, bhikkhus, are the āsavas abandoned by enduring? Here, bhikkhus, the practising bhikkhu rouses energy and strives to abandon evil states and to receive pure states; rousing vigor and determination, firm in energy and diligence, not abandoning pure states. Through diligent striving, even should the flesh, sinew, and bone dry up and be destroyed along with marrow, fat, and skin—as long as what one has set oneself to accomplish through energetic striving is attained—for one who has vigor, firmness, and diligent energy, what is sought has not yet been gained; one cannot stop partway. The practising bhikkhu endures cold and heat, hunger and thirst, stopping to endure the affliction of mosquitoes, gnats, wind, and scorching sun, and endures contact with venomous creatures. Enduring harsh and offensive speech that comes accompanied by arrogance, one is able to be patient and bear with it. The painful afflictions that have arisen—the disagreeable and severe pains—one passes through and stops. If, bhikkhus, the āsavas of fever, affliction, and distress arising from lack of endurance are passed through and departed from in this manner of practice, then the āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress are brought to a stop. This is called, bhikkhus, the abandonment of āsavas by enduring.

"What, bhikkhus, are the āsavas abandoned by dispelling? Here, bhikkhus, when desire has already arisen in the bhikkhu, one does not allow it or let it pass on; one relinquishes it, dispels it, repels it, and removes it. When anger has already arisen, one does not allow it or let it pass on; one relinquishes it, dispels it, repels it, and removes it. When thoughts of killing, deception, and stealing have already arisen, one does not allow them or let them pass on; one relinquishes, dispels, repels, and removes them. If, bhikkhus, the āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress that arose through failure to dispel are now dispelled, those āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress no longer occur. This is called, bhikkhus, the abandonment of āsavas by dispelling.

"What, bhikkhus, are the āsavas abandoned by cultivation? Here, bhikkhus, the bhikkhu's mind cultivates the awakening factor of mindfulness, dwelling alone and still, withdrawing, leaning away from evil and transforming [unwholesome] states. One cultivates the awakening factor of investigation of phenomena in the same way; the awakening factor of energy in the same way; the awakening factor of rapture in the same way; the awakening factor of tranquility in the same way; the awakening factor of concentration in the same way; and one cultivates the awakening factor of equanimity, through withdrawal and repelling, in the same way—not allowing, not passing beyond; one relinquishes, dispels, and removes. If, bhikkhus, the āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress arising from not practicing and not cultivating are brought to cessation once practice and cultivation are accomplished, those āsavas of affliction, fever, and distress no longer occur. This is called, bhikkhus, the abandonment of āsavas by cultivation.

"If, bhikkhus, the āsavas that should be abandoned by seeing have been abandoned by seeing; if the āsavas that should be abandoned by restraint have been abandoned by restraint; if the āsavas that should be abandoned by avoidance have been abandoned by avoidance; if the āsavas that should be abandoned by using have been abandoned by using; if the āsavas that should be abandoned by endurance have been abandoned by endurance; if the āsavas that should be abandoned by dispelling have been abandoned by dispelling; if the āsavas that should be abandoned by cultivation have been abandoned by cultivation—then, bhikkhus, this is called: all āsavas restrained and guarded through their causes. Evil craving has been destroyed; one has crossed over from the world to liberation from the world; bonds and fetters have been cast off; the essential goal has been attained; one is released from suffering."

The Buddha taught thus. The bhikkhus who heard this received it with joy of mind and practiced accordingly, completely attaining liberation from the world and gaining the path.
