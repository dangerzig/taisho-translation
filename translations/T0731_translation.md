# 佛說十八泥犁經
## Sūtra of the Eighteen Hells, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0731

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

The Buddha said: "In a human life, the days one sees the light are few, and the days one does not are many. The transformations of good and evil are not of the same kind. Those who insult their parents and transgress against the emperor, at death enter the hells. Among these there are depths and shallows. There are eight hot hells and ten cold hells. Below half the depth of the earth are the hot hells; at the boundary between heaven and earth are the cold hells. If one has done evil before but later becomes good, one does not enter the hells. Killing people, robbing people, cheating and violating others' wives, wishing people dead so as to obtain their property, being filthy and thievish, delighting in unwholesome deeds, cursing and reviling others in anger, flogging and chaining people, delighting in reporting and publicizing others' faults, harboring envy and speaking in anger, letting anger flare at the slightest provocation, defying heaven and earth and the spirits: such people lose their lifespan and die, and descend into the evil hells. If they later change and become good, there are some who do not enter the hells. If one knows the Buddha's path and changes accordingly, even if one enters the hells, one will certainly ascend to the heavens. The originally good ascend to nirvāṇa. Therefore it is said that the Buddha's path must not remain unknown. The petty person who does not change to goodness enters the hells with no joy; once in the hells, if still not good, one descends ever deeper. Among their kind, some may again become human. Though strong and fierce, if they do not kill and love life, they quickly become human again. Those who do not eat meat become human even more quickly. Those with hidden merit gain increased lifespan and become human quickly.

"The first hell is called Sañjīva, which people call 'Reviving.' Those dwelling in this hell, upon seeing one another, immediately wish to fight. Though there are no weapons at hand, weapons spontaneously appear. They wound and kill each other for years without number, yet do not die. Someone comes and says, 'Arise, do not die,' and a wind blows upon them and they are healed. This continues for years without number. Then again they wound and kill each other with hot iron swords, and again with iron hammers, and wound each other with fists as sharp as blades, for a long, long time without number. Such is their kind. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: three thousand seven hundred and fifty human years equal one day; thirty days equal one month; twelve months equal one year; and they live for ten thousand years, which equals 13.5 billion human years.

"The second hell is called Kālasūtra. The suffering of one moment in Kālasūtra equals twenty in Sañjīva. As people say, they are 'bound with ropes and made to fight.' Those dwelling in this hell are placed in a great fire; when red-hot they are brought out and made to fight. Then they are put back in the fire; when red-hot they are brought out again and repeatedly made to fight, for a long, long time without number. They are burned again, brought out, and made to fight. Made round and square, they somehow cannot die, and are revived again for years without number. Then again they run into the fire for years without number. Such is their kind. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: seven thousand five hundred human years equal one day; thirty days equal one month; twelve months equal one year; and they live for twenty thousand years, which equals 2.07 trillion human years.

"The third hell is called Saṃghāta. The suffering of one moment in Saṃghāta equals twenty in Kālasūtra. As people say, they are 'crushed to death.' Those dwelling in this hell are in the fire, with heat beyond words. Looking left and right they see mountains, and between the mountains it seems like a pleasant place. They run to enter between them, but the mountains come together and crush them, yet they do not die, for years without number. They are placed back in the fire, turn red-hot, and are brought out again, for years without number. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: fifteen thousand human years equal one day; thirty days equal one month; twelve months equal one year; and they live for forty thousand years, which equals 54 billion human years.

"The fourth hell is called Raurava. The suffering of one moment in Raurava equals twenty in Saṃghāta. As people say, they 'cry out in agony.' Those dwelling in this hell: the city is extremely vast. Within it is a smaller city. From outside, people see that inside there seem to be layers of heavens. People all enter therein, and it is red-hot like burning iron. Once inside the city, the heat is beyond words. Their skin and flesh all rot away for years without number; they cannot rest, cannot lie down. Skin and bone are all scorched, and they revive again for years without number. Such is their kind. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: thirty thousand human years equal one day; thirty days equal one month; twelve months equal one year; and they live for eighty thousand years, which equals 180 billion human years.

"The fifth hell is called Mahāraurava. The suffering of one moment in Mahāraurava equals twenty in Raurava. As people say, they are 'boiled until they dissolve.' Those dwelling in this hell: the pits are vast and deep, filled with fire. The hell-wardens beat them with iron rods and throw them in; their bodies are burned and scorched, completely incinerated for years without number, yet they do not die. They are accumulated and burned yet do not die. Fire embraces their bodies. They die and emerge from one pit only to fall into another, and so on without number. Such is their kind. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: sixty thousand human years equal one day; thirty days equal one month; twelve months equal one year; and they live for one hundred sixty thousand years, which equals 216 billion human years.

"The sixth hell is called Tapana. The suffering of one moment in Tapana equals twenty in Mahāraurava. As people say, they are 'scalded with heat.' Those dwelling in this hell: the city walls are two thousand leagues high and four thousand leagues wide. Fire fills it completely. People are placed inside. Then iron covers them for years without number. Thus they cannot rest; it is said they cannot lie down for years without number. Scorched and scorched again, such is their kind. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: one hundred twenty thousand human years equal one day; thirty days equal one month; twelve months equal one year; and they live for three hundred twenty thousand years, which equals 432 billion human years.

"The seventh hell is called Pratāpana. The suffering of one moment in Pratāpana equals twenty in Tapana. As people say, they are 'burned, roasted, and given to worms.' Those dwelling in this hell: great heaps of fire, like vast iron spits that skewer people, for years without number. From time to time a gate opens. All the people rush toward it wishing to escape; but the gate closes again, and they fall back into the fire for years without number. Then they see another gate open. All rush trying to escape; having gotten out the gate, they fall into a foul mire. In the mire are worms that bite them, and they cannot escape for years without number. Such is their kind. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: two hundred forty thousand human years equal one day; thirty days equal one month; twelve months equal one year; and they live for six hundred forty thousand years, which equals 864 billion human years.

"The eighth hell is called Avīci. The suffering of one moment in Avīci equals twenty in Pratāpana. As people say, they 'suffer greatly and are cooked thoroughly.' Those dwelling in this hell: the ground is entirely fire. The hell-wardens stand in the fire, roasting and skewering, making them stand on beds or lie upon them. They cannot leave, cannot rest. They dissolve and are scorched. They revive again for years without number. The suffering is ten-thousandfold that of the other hells, pain beyond words. Such is their kind. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: four hundred eighty thousand human years equal one day; thirty days equal one month; twelve months equal one year; and they live for one million two hundred eighty thousand years, which equals 1.728 trillion human years."

The Buddha said: "The eight hot hells: those with much evil go deep and remain long; those with little evil go shallow and emerge quickly. The hells are like a person confined in prison as a convict, whose sentence of labor is far away, who dies in the wilderness while the family is still halfway. It is as if one falls into water at this very birth, unable to find the path before death. Upon entering the hells there is immediate suffering, suffering beyond words, and it is long before one emerges. The cold hells are at the boundary of heaven. There is a great mountain two thousand leagues high that blocks the wind, called Mount Cakravāḍa. It is dark there, where the sun and moon cannot reach. Because the great mountain blocks them it is dark. Beyond it there are vastly many suns and moons, innumerable, within the cold hells.

"The ninth hell is called Arbuda. The suffering of one moment in Arbuda equals twenty in Avīci. As people say, one is 'struck violently and then revived.' Those dwelling in this hell: the cold is beyond words. For years without number their entire bodies freeze. Again and again they are violently struck, twenty times over. In the fire there is a sound; then it is done to them again. Their bones break in half like a crossbow bolt snapping, then are joined together again. Like great stones striking a great infant, the pain is beyond words. Yet they do not die. They are ground as by an iron millstone. First their feet are ground, then the entire body, until it stops. This continues for years without number. The suffering and pain are beyond words. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take one hundred twenty-eight bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended. Even this cannot account for one ten-thousandth. Such is their kind. Those who know the Buddha's path escape quickly. If a person does evil by day and commits no offense by night, that person enters the hells but suffers only by day. If one does evil by night and commits no offense by day, one suffers only by night, in misery beyond words. Therefore the Buddha's path must not remain unknown.

"The tenth hell is called Nirarbuda. The suffering of one moment in Nirarbuda equals twenty in Arbuda. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take two hundred fifty-six bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended.

"The eleventh hell is called Aṭaṭa. The suffering of one moment in Aṭaṭa equals twenty in Nirarbuda. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take five hundred twelve bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended.

"The twelfth hell is called Hahava. The suffering of one moment in Hahava equals twenty in Aṭaṭa. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take one thousand twenty-four bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended.

"The thirteenth hell is called Huhuva. The suffering of one moment in Huhuva equals twenty in Hahava. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take two thousand forty-eight bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended.

"The fourteenth hell is called Utpala. The suffering of one moment in Utpala equals twenty in Huhuva. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take four thousand ninety-six bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended.

"The fifteenth hell is called Sugandha. The suffering of one moment in Sugandha equals twenty in Utpala. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take eight thousand one hundred ninety-two bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended.

"The sixteenth hell is called Kumuda. The suffering of one moment in Kumuda equals twenty in Sugandha. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take twelve thousand three hundred eighty-four bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended.

"The seventeenth hell is called Puṇḍarīka. The suffering of one moment in Puṇḍarīka equals twenty in Kumuda. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take thirty-two thousand seven hundred sixty-eight bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended.

"The eighteenth hell is called Padma. The suffering of one moment in Padma equals twenty in Puṇḍarīka. The people there are tall and large. Their lifespan: take sixty-five thousand five hundred thirty-six bushels of mustard seeds; every hundred years remove one seed; when the mustard seeds are exhausted the lifespan is still not ended. The great cold and the suffering are beyond words, beyond description. All is ten-thousandfold the suffering of the other hells; the pain is without limit."

The Buddha said: "The eighteen hells: what people commit, according to the weight of their good and evil deeds, determines the hell they enter."

The Buddha said: "The eighteen hells: from phoenixes and dragons down to the smallest insects, all are subject to the eighteen hells. Those who do much good and little evil escape the hells quickly. Those who do much evil and little good escape the hells slowly."

The Buddha said: "How is it that there are ghosts guarding the hells? The dwelling places of the eighteen hells are dark. When the Buddha was first born, above and below the heavens and earth, up to the thirty-two heavens, all the eighteen hells were illuminated. When the Buddha first attained the path, there was another illumination. When the Buddha turned to the Brahmā heaven, there was another illumination. When the Buddha first set out to teach and instruct the world, there was another illumination. On the day of the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, there was another illumination, and up to the thirty-two heavens all was bright. Therefore the Buddha's path must not remain unknown. Those who do much good ascend to heaven; those who do much evil enter the hells. Some become animals. Those who know the Buddha's path do not die; the petty person will certainly have long life, but only by frequently hearing the Buddha's path. Those who do not study life, do not draw near to good people, and do not hear of good things should depart from grief and affliction. Those who mock the Buddha's path descend deep into the hells; the Buddha therefore reproves them. Those who do evil, who delight in cursing and speaking with foul mouths, continuing until old age without stopping: the heavenly spirits despise them and cause them to become birds, beasts, and animals. Their offspring use blood and flesh to make offerings on the family altar."

The Buddha said: "When a person does not do good and leaves the human kind to become an insect or animal, what is born in their family to receive offerings from ghosts: how can ghosts eat from this?"

The Buddha said: "Those who do good ascend to heaven and afterward are born again in human form."
