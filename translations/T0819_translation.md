# 佛說法常住經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Eternal Abiding of the Dharma

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0819

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was wandering in the Jetavana Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada in the kingdom of Śrāvastī. The Buddha addressed all the bhikṣus:

"The Dharma eternally abides. Whether a Buddha appears in the world or not, the Dharma remains as it is. The Tathāgata, the Perfectly True One, appears in the world and thereby proclaims and elucidates it, distinguishing its profound meanings and expounding the supreme wisdom.

"For those whose aspirations lie in the Lesser Vehicle, he teaches the Four Noble Truths: suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. The suffering of birth, the suffering of aging, the suffering of illness, the suffering of death, the suffering of not obtaining what one seeks—to speak to the essentials: there is the suffering of the skandha-body. If there were no body, what affliction would there be? This is called the truth of suffering.

"The eye craves form; the ear, nose, body, tongue, and mind are likewise. Through these conditions, the six corrupting sense-objects come into being. From time immemorial, over vast kalpas, this has been so. Having clearly seen this, one recognizes it as a harmful habituation, altogether without benefit to oneself. One therefore guards one's bodily conduct, protects against the ten evils of mind, speech, and body, and thereby uproots the twelve-linked chain of dependent origination, extinguishing it at the root. Having eliminated the three poisons and realized emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, one thereby attains the stage of Arhat.

"Contemplating the twelve links of dependent origination, understanding their mutual entanglement, and cutting off their root source, one forthwith becomes a Pratyekabuddha.

"Understanding that the body is fundamentally empty, one upholds the six pāramitās without limit, the four immeasurables, and the four great beneficences. The dharmas of the path number thirty-seven. Without emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness—without these three things—one does not attain realization. Exercising skillful means with the six supernatural powers, ever rescuing those in danger and distress, maintaining an equal mind toward all without partiality or preference—such a one is therefore called a Bodhisattva.

"Having attained non-retrogression, one will accomplish the unsurpassed, perfectly true way, become the supreme right enlightenment, and liberate beings throughout the ten directions—and is therefore named Buddha.

"The Dharma-path eternally abides. The practitioner merges with it, with no separation between self and other—just as the many rivers, before reaching the sea, each have their own names, but upon merging with the sea, there is no longer any distinct designation. The Dharma-virtue being so, the Buddhas of past, present, and future all merge into one Dharmakāya, their practice united with the Dharma. For the sake of sentient beings, they distinguish and expound the teachings.

"The aggregates, the corrupting sense-objects, the various āyatanas, and the twelve links of dependent origination—all of these are diseases. The pāramitās without limit, the four immeasurables, and the four great beneficences are the great medicine of the Dharma, curing the illnesses of sentient beings. Pure as empty space, brilliant beyond the light of the sun, virtue surpassing Mount Sumeru—divine, holy, majestic, and lofty—none can slander or defame. All sins are extinguished; there is no more suffering of any kind. With great compassion, one liberates beings throughout the ten directions."

When the Buddha spoke thus, all the bhikṣus had their outflows exhausted and their minds liberated. Countless bodhisattvas immediately attained the anutpattika-dharma-kṣānti, the acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas.

The Buddha having spoken thus, the bhikṣus, the bodhisattvas, and all the gods, dragons, and spirits, having heard what the Buddha said, rejoiced and reverently undertook to practice it.
