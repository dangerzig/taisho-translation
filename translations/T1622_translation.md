# Treatise on Nominal Designation through Apprehension of Causes

## *Qǔyīn Jiǎshè Lùn* (取因假設論)

Taishō 1622

Composed by the Bodhisattva Dignāga (*Chénnà*, 陳那). Translated by Yìjìng (*Yìjìng*, 義淨) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a treatise by the great Buddhist logician Dignāga (c. 480-540 CE) on the nature of conventional designation (prajñaptivāda). It argues that composite wholes (such as "body" or "forest"), temporal continuities (such as "person" throughout stages of life), and aspectual distinctions (such as "impermanent" predicated of "form") are all merely nominal designations (prajñapti), not real entities with intrinsic natures (svabhāva). The treatise systematically refutes the Vaiśeṣika doctrine that wholes (avayavin) are real entities distinct from their parts (avayava), using rigorous logical arguments about identity, difference, and the problems of infinite regress and absurd consequences. It concludes with a verse affirming the Yogācāra position that apparent objects arise from consciousness alone. This is one of Dignāga's important philosophical works bridging his Abhidharma background and his mature epistemological system.*

---

The treatise states: In order to exclude the positions of identity, difference, and non-existence, the Great Teacher [the Buddha] taught the essential Dharma solely in dependence upon nominally designated things (*prajñaptivastu*), wishing to enable sentient beings to enter by skillful means, to think in accordance with principle (*yoniśo manaskāra*), to depart from heterodox schools, and to permanently sever the afflictions. Since all three of these extreme positions have faults, I shall now explain this.

## The Three Kinds of Nominal Designation

Herein, "nominal designation through apprehension of causes" is of three kinds: first, aggregate (*samudāya*); second, continuum (*saṃtāna*); third, aspectual distinction (*avasthāviśeṣa*).

**Aggregate** means: at one time there are many dharmas gathered together, and in accordance with worldly convention one speaks of them as a unity, as in [the words] "body," "forest," and so on.

**Continuum** means: at different times there is an unbroken [succession of] cause and effect, and one speaks of it as a unity, as when the embryonic stage (*kalala*) and subsequent stages are called "a person," or when sprout and subsequent transformations are called "grain."

**Aspectual distinction** means: in a single thing there are many characteristics, established at the same time or at different times, as when at the arising of form one [distinguishes] the nature of impermanence, visibility, resistance, and being an object of action.

Through these three meanings, one designates with hidden intent the existence of a *pudgala* (person) and the attainment of nirvāṇa. However, these three meanings are merely nominal designations. They cannot be said to be of one nature, of different nature, or of no nature at all, for all [three positions] have faults.

---

## Part One: The Aggregate

### Against Identity

Let us first discuss the aggregate. There is [the thesis of] "no-difference of nature" between the aggregate and its members. Are there only these two natures? Or is the denial of difference in nature [between] the members-with-aggregate and the aggregate the meaning of "no difference"? Or does "no difference" mean simply the absence of anything further? What fault does this entail?

The verse states:

> If, by the identity of nature, there were no distinction of essence,
> Then the members of the aggregate would be mutually without difference.
> Or else, the distinct components being different from the aggregate,
> This would result in multiple essences.

**Commentary**: If one holds that the natures of hand and such are identical with the body, called "no difference," then because there is no distinct nature within the body, the hand and foot would be mutually indistinguishable; the hand would become the foot, contradicting the world. Alternatively, if one says that the individual limbs are not the nature of the body, and that only at the time of collection are they called "body," then there is absolutely no essence of the aggregate that can be apprehended even in part. [The word] "or" is placed to distinguish from the previous meaning. If the individual things are not the nature of the aggregate, this is a difference of nature. Being of different nature, how can "identity of nature with the aggregate at the time of collection" be reasonably established? It is not the case that the aggregate abandons its own nature. "This would result in multiple essences": because the aggregate has "no difference of nature" with the many, if things are individually and collectively of no-different nature, since neither aggregate nor individual abandons its own nature, whether simultaneously or successively, all become multiple natures, because they are of "no-different nature" with respect to those things.

Thus the faults of both positions have been explained.

[The treatise continues with verse and commentary refuting each position in detail through rigorous logical analysis, showing that:]

- If "no difference" means merely denying any further entity, then the same argument applies to both sides symmetrically.
- If one entity is denied, then by the same "no difference" logic, the other is also denied.
- Just as there is no body apart from hands and feet, hands and feet themselves await fingers and so on to constitute them as aggregates. This regress continues down to atoms, which await sound and other qualities, which await the *guṇas* (sattva, rajas, tamas), and so on, until the opponent's entire ontology collapses.

### Against Difference

> Difference is the same [in having two faults]. By one portion,
> Turning at each limb separately,
> The other portion has the fault of infinite regress;
> Or it is not one, and completely non-existent.

**Commentary**: If the whole (*avayavin*) is different from its parts, when one portion turns at the hand and other portions, that portion again should have yet another portion arising alongside it, leading to infinite regress. Alternatively: if one says the body-part's single portion does not further turn, then there is no single-whole meaning; the whole would consist of only one portion. If so, one should simply accept that only hands and such exist. Why insist that a whole turns in its own parts?

> If pervading, it becomes multiple in nature,
> And hands and feet become mutually indistinguishable.
> Or else, "this is not that," and so it follows
> That all things become the nature of a single atom.

**Commentary**: If one says the whole pervades [all parts], then, because the whole is commensurate with the parts and pervades each locus, it becomes multiple in nature. Furthermore, since it is everywhere present in one location (and one does not accept it has different natures at different locations), the remaining limbs become mutually indistinguishable. If, to avoid this, one says that "the whole, when turning at the foot, does not turn at the hand," then all wholes reduce to a single real thing, and therefore all things become a single atom, since the whole, when turning successively in its own parts, ultimately arrives at a single real atom as the only locus. This single part would then be identical with the whole at a single atom. The whole-thesis becomes useless and contradicts one's own school.

### On Atoms

If one claims that, apart from color and such, there is a separate atom, and therefore the above faults do not apply (because it does not turn at the locus of color and such), this too is unreasonable, for two positions again have faults. One should accept that [atoms] either have directional parts or are without directional parts.

> Having directional parts, they are not atoms. To exclude identity and difference:
> Or without directional parts, many cannot aggregate;
> Or else, being together, all are the same as one atom.

**Commentary**: Atoms so conceived cannot be established as real, since they have directional parts, and these parts become yet finer. Furthermore, to exclude identity and difference: in relation to their own parts, [the same faults arise]. If without directional parts, even when gathered together they do not truly combine. If one grants that they share the same locus, then all are identical with a single atom. Moreover, such atoms cannot aggregate, because one [atom] obstructs all others. All atomic bodies thus merge into one, since they share the same locus.

## Part Two: The Continuum

> If the continuum is one, abandoning the infant,
> Gradually proceeding to the childhood stage,
> One should lose one's self. It is not "non-different."
> If one says it is not lost, there would be admixture.

**Commentary**: If one holds that the continuum is "of non-different nature" with the thing that continues, then at the infant stage, "non-different" means identity; when one shifts to the childhood stage, the self should be lost, since at this stage there is no "non-different" nature. But it is not [truly] non-different; the stages shift, and safety and danger differ.

If not lost, the characteristics become admixed: not abandoning the infant while entering the childhood stage, the essences of infant and child are mixed together and should become indistinguishable. Yet we never see such a thing.

> If of different nature, the body is not at ease.
> The effort to seek ease would be a futile contrivance.
> Transformation would not correspond, and there would be [no] arising and ceasing.
> If one says the essences are distinct, they become real things.

**Commentary**: If the continuum's entity at different times were truly different, then for one who is first ill and later seeks to be cured, all effort would be a futile contrivance, since the principle of continuity would not correspond. One never sees a thing of separate essence, like a cow and a horse, forming a continuum. Furthermore, if of different nature, the principle of arising and ceasing also would not correspond. Whatever has arising and ceasing in continuous succession should turn at the present moment's locus. How could past, future, and present turn? As before, both "partial" and "fully pervading" are refuted.

---

## Part Three: Aspectual Distinction

> At the locus of aspectual distinction such as fruit-nature,
> Having apprehended that thing, one makes a verbal designation.
> If it is different from that thing, it has no essence.
> The two faults of "non-different" are as explained before.

**Commentary**: At the locus of aspectual distinctions such as fruit-nature, one apprehends that thing and then makes a verbal designation. "Different from that" would be without essence. That is to say, with regard to form and such, one designates cause, effect, identity, difference, self, other, visibility, resistance, and so on. If one says that thing is different from this [aspect], it would not be cause or effect. It would also have no self-nature, since there is no single thing that, without depending on attributes, can at its own locus constitute a verbal designation. The "non-difference" position has two kinds of faults, as explained above for the aggregate.

---

## Against Total Denial

> If one does not accept that the body is real,
> Teachings to counteract [wrong views] would be useless.
> Furthermore, there would be no persons of wrong views,
> Nor any differentiated functional activity.

**Commentary**: As when, regarding the body, one erroneously designates it as permanent, blissful, self, and pure, and as a corrective one teaches the four foundations of mindfulness: if there were no body, this would be useless. Likewise, if one does not accept that there is a continuum, both the affliction to be treated and the treatment itself become incoherent. If there is no aspectual distinction, teachings about impermanence and so forth would also be useless. Furthermore, one who holds wrong views by denying giving, receiving, and so on, and all the meritorious activities of aggregates, should not count as holding wrong views. The property given in charity, clothing, food, and such, is all aggregates. If there is no continuum, there is no such wrong view either. If there is no aspectual distinction, the differentiation of wrong views regarding suffering and origination with respect to a single form would not exist.

---

## Conclusion

If there are such manifold faults [in denial], why not accept these as real entities? This is unreasonable, for there are faults [in that too]. What are they?

> Because identity and difference are excluded,
> They are not things established through mutual dependence on causes.
> Their own essence is also expressible; therefore,
> Apparent objects arise solely from consciousness.

**Commentary**: Because, with respect to form and such, which are real, the nature of identity and difference between them can be expressed, aggregates and so on are inexpressible [in terms of identity or difference]. If so, how is the body said to differ from another body? Because it is not established through mutual causation. Whatever things, when one sets aside one and the mind apprehends another, are not called "nominal designation through apprehension of causes," since one does not apprehend the other as a cause. But for body and such, when one sets aside the other, the mind does not apprehend this: this is precisely what is called "nominal designation through apprehension of causes."

As for aspectual distinction: "different" from the other aspects is what is called "nominal" (*prajñapti*). If so, because one does not apprehend it at the locus of form and such, one calls it "different nature," and it should not be real. This is unreasonable, because its own essence is expressible and also apprehensible, its characteristics being distinct. However, these apparent objects also arise from consciousness. Whatever objective characteristics there may be, if separated from consciousness, they cannot be known. All self-natures are also nominal designations; therefore there is no fault.

As there is a verse:

> "Ghosts, animals, humans, and gods,
> Each according to their respective [karma]:
> At the same place, minds differ.
> Therefore objects are accepted as not truly real."

Furthermore, aggregates and so on are not real entities, being neither conditioned nor unconditioned in nature. All dharmas are encompassed by the conditioned and the unconditioned; aggregates are not conditioned entities.

[Further arguments show that aggregates cannot be unconditioned either, for if unconditioned, they would be indestructible and permanent.]

The Buddha did not destroy the world's [conventions]. As things are, apart from what is inconceivable, for the various sentient beings, according to their different dispositions and natures, in their condition of being entangled and oppressed by latent tendencies, in order to sever their afflictions, he taught the essential Dharma.

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Do not, like worldly people, engage in fruitless deliberation. I say: one who knows and sees exhausts all the outflows. It is not the case that one who does not know and does not see [achieves this]. One should think in accordance with principle, not in ways contrary to principle."

Thus one should know: thinking in accordance with principle is the true cause of severing the afflictions. Thinking contrary to principle gives rise to manifold suffering. One should dismiss wrong thinking and follow right mindfulness.

> All aims are accomplished through this as their foundation.
> Easy as a skillful means, yet extremely difficult to encounter.
> Form and life do not pause for even an instant.
> The wise should swiftly cultivate and practice.
