# 相續解脫如來所作隨順處了義經
## Definitive-Meaning Sūtra on the Conforming Abode of the Tathāgata's Deeds, from the Continuous Liberation

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0679

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

As the Continuous Liberation Sūtra says:

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The World-Honored One has spoken of the Tathāgata's Dharma-body. What is the characteristic of the Tathāgata's Dharma-body?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Through the well-cultivated bhūmi-pāramitā of the vehicle, the body is transformed and accumulated to completion. Mañjuśrī! This is the characteristic of the Tathāgata's Dharma-body. You should know there is also an inconceivable characteristic, arising from two causes: it is free from falseness and without conditioned action, whereas sentient beings cling to falseness and conditioned action."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Is the body-transformation of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas also the Dharma-body?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "It is not called the Dharma-body."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What body is it called?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "It is called the liberation-body. Mañjuśrī! The liberation-body belongs to śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. All the Tathāgatas are distinguished by having the Dharma-body alone. The distinction of the Dharma-body lies in its immeasurable, excellent, and extraordinary merit, beyond all comparison."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is the characteristic of the Tathāgata's arising?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The characteristic of the transformation-body is like the arising of the world, producing all kinds of Tathāgata-merit and the characteristic of abiding in pure adornment. You should know that the transformation-body has the characteristic of arising, while the Dharma-body has no arising."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! By what skillful means is the transformation-body manifested?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Throughout all the three-thousand great-thousand buddha-worlds: being born in the families of supreme rulers, being born in families that are fields of merit, entering the womb and leaving the womb, growing and enjoying the five desires, leaving home to practice austerities, going to the place of awakening, conquering Māra beneath the bodhi tree and attaining buddhahood, turning the wheel of the Dharma, and manifesting parinirvāṇa. You should know that these are the skillful means by which the transformation-body is manifested."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many kinds of speech does the Tathāgata's transformation-body use to teach the Dharma to sentient beings, so that those not yet ripened are tamed and ripened, and those already ripened are enabled to attain liberation through conditions?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The Tathāgata's speech consists of three kinds: sūtra-speech, vinaya-speech, and mātṛkā-speech."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is sūtra? What is vinaya? What is mātṛkā?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "When I teach the classification of all dharmas and the demarcation of matters, it is called sūtra. This is by means of four matters, nine matters, and twenty-nine matters, making forty-two matters in all.

"What are the four matters? They are the matter of hearing, the matter of taking refuge, the matter of precepts, and the matter of the bodhisattva.

"What are the nine matters? They are the matter of establishing sentient beings, the matter of enjoyment, the matter of causal arising, the matter of abiding after arising, the matter of cessation, the matter of varieties, the matter of teaching, the matter of what is taught, and the matter of the assembly.

"What are the twenty-nine matters? They are: the matter of conduct pertaining to the defiled portion; the matter of the master's gradual and sequential arising; the matter of causes of future birth through conceiving of persons; the matter of causes of future birth through conceiving of dharmas; the matter of mindfulness fixed on conditions in the pure portion; the matter of determination regarding these; the matter of the abiding of mind; the matter of pleasant abiding in present dharmas; the matter of the skillful means for the conditions that give rise to all suffering; the matter of abandonment-knowledge, which is also of three kinds (abandonment-knowledge at the place of inversion, abandonment-knowledge at the place where beings go astray through external conceptions of sentient beings, and abandonment-knowledge at the inner place of freedom from the conceit of superiority); the matter of the place of cultivation; the matter of direct realization; the matter of cultivation; the matter of making firm what has been accomplished; the matter of those practices; the matter of those conditions; the matter of skillful investigation of what is and is not abandoned; the matter of distraction; the matter of non-distraction; the matter of the place of non-distraction; the matter of tireless cultivation and not abandoning what has been accomplished; the matter of cultivating benefit; the matter of firm cultivation; the matter of truly knowing and realizing; the matter of arriving at nirvāṇa; the matter of attaining the summit of all inner and outer right views through the worldly right views of the well-proclaimed Dharma-Vinaya; and the matter of deterioration of cultivation. Mañjuśrī! In the well-proclaimed Dharma-Vinaya, the deterioration of cultivation is not a fault of view.

"Mañjuśrī! When I teach the prātimokṣa for my disciple śrāvakas and bodhisattvas, and the training corresponding to the prātimokṣa, this is called the matter of vinaya."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "How many kinds of prātimokṣa do bodhisattvas have?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "There are seven kinds: first, explaining the reception of deportment; second, explaining the matter of pārājika offenses; third, explaining the intrinsic nature of transgressions; fourth, explaining the intrinsic nature of non-transgression; fifth, explaining emergence from transgression; sixth, explaining the reception of discipline; seventh, explaining the relinquishment of discipline.

"Mañjuśrī! There are eleven kinds of characteristics that are elucidated, analyzed, extensively taught, and displayed. These are called mātṛkā. What are the eleven characteristics? First, the characteristic of equality; second, the characteristic of ultimate truth; third, the characteristic of the objects of the factors of awakening; fourth, the characteristic of practice; fifth, the characteristic of fruition; sixth, the characteristic of the display of spiritual powers; seventh, the characteristic of intrinsic nature; eighth, the characteristic of its support; ninth, the characteristic of dharmas conforming to it; tenth, the characteristic of its faults; eleventh, the characteristic of its benefits.

"Mañjuśrī! The characteristic of equality means teaching about persons, teaching about the imagined intrinsic nature, and teaching about the activity and karma of all dharmas. The characteristic of ultimate truth means teaching seven kinds. The characteristic of objects means teaching all kinds of ālambana matters. The characteristic of practice means teaching eight kinds of investigation.

"What are the eight kinds of investigation? First, truth; second, establishment; third, faults; fourth, virtues; fifth, penetration; sixth, arising; seventh, accomplishment; eighth, the concise and the extensive.

"'Truth' means suchness. 'Establishment' means establishing sentient beings and the like, whether establishing the imagined intrinsic nature, whether establishing one-sided analysis, inquiry, setting aside, or answer, whether establishing the hidden or the revealed record of teaching. 'Faults' means I teach with innumerable causes and conditions the faults and dangers of afflicted dharmas. 'Virtues' means I teach with innumerable causes and conditions the merit and benefit of pure dharmas.

"'Penetration' is of six kinds: first, the penetration of true meaning; second, the penetration of attainment; third, the penetration of teaching; fourth, the penetration of freedom from both extremes; fifth, the penetration of the inconceivable; sixth, the penetration of intention.

"'Arising' means the three meditative attainments, the three characteristics of the conditioned, and the four conditions.

"'Accomplishment' is of four kinds: first, accomplishment through existence; second, accomplishment of what is to be done; third, supporting accomplishment; fourth, dharma-accomplishment. When causes and conditions give rise to conditioned formations and the teaching that follows them, this is called accomplishment through existence. When causes and conditions bring about the attainment or achievement of dharmas and the accomplishment of what is to be done regarding what has already arisen, this is called accomplishment of what is to be done. When causes and conditions, whether of a thesis, a teaching, a transmission, or a meaning, accomplish and bring it to realization: this may be further summarized in two kinds, first pure and second impure. The pure has five characteristics; the impure has seven characteristics.

"What are the five pure characteristics? First, the characteristic of direct perception; second, the characteristic dependent upon direct perception; third, the characteristic of inference from one's own kind; fourth, the characteristic of establishment; fifth, the characteristic of authoritative pure speech.

"'All conditioned things are impermanent; all conditioned things involve suffering; all dharmas are without self': if such things in the world are directly perceived, this is called the characteristic of direct perception. 'All conditioned things are momentary; there is another world; and the karma of purity and impurity is not destroyed': if this is derived by dependence upon directly perceiving coarse impermanence, or by dependence upon directly perceiving the various kinds of sentient beings and the various kinds of karma, or by dependence upon directly perceiving the happiness and suffering of sentient beings and their pure and impure karma, and through such inference one obtains what is not directly present, this is called the characteristic dependent upon direct perception. Furthermore, when from inner and outer conditioned things, from worldly dependent arising of arising and ceasing, one infers suffering and the like, or infers the absence of self-mastery, or from external worldly dependent arising one infers formation and destruction, and by such inferences: this is called the characteristic of inference from one's own kind. When the characteristic of direct perception, the characteristic dependent upon direct perception, and the characteristic of inference from one's own kind together form a conclusive establishment, you should know that if this is extensively expounded, it is taught by the omniscient one: namely, 'nirvāṇa is quiescent,' and such things. This is called the characteristic of authoritative pure speech. Mañjuśrī! Therefore these five characteristics constitute pure contemplation. Being pure, one should cultivate and practice them."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many kinds of characteristics does omniscience have?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "There are five kinds: first, the renown of omniscience is heard throughout the world; second, one is endowed with the thirty-two marks of a great person; third, by the ten powers one decisively resolves the net of doubt of all sentient beings; fourth, with the four fearlessnesses one teaches the Dharma such that no external doctrine can challenge or overcome it; fifth, within that Dharma-Vinaya one knows the noble eightfold path and the four fruits of the śramaṇa. You should know that these five practices are the characteristics of omniscience. If these are supported by such direct perception, inference, and authoritative testimony, this constitutes the five kinds of pure characteristics.

"What are the seven impure characteristics? First, the characteristic of perception partially similar to the other; second, the characteristic of perception partially dissimilar to the other; third, the characteristic of perception entirely similar; fourth, the characteristic of perception entirely dissimilar; fifth, inference from a different kind; sixth, the characteristic of non-establishment; seventh, the characteristic of impure testimony.

"If all dharmas are cognized by mental consciousness alone, this is called the characteristic of perception entirely similar. If there are differences in form, intrinsic nature, function, causation, and fruition, and these are determined to be individually distinct, this is called the characteristic of perception entirely dissimilar. Mañjuśrī! When that inference is different from the characteristic of perception entirely similar, having the characteristic of perception entirely dissimilar, because it is not conclusively established, this is called the characteristic of non-establishment. And when that inference is different from the characteristic of perception entirely dissimilar, having the characteristic of perception entirely similar, because it is not conclusively established, this too is called the characteristic of non-establishment. Because of non-establishment, the contemplation is not purely established. Being impure, one should not pursue it. The characteristic of impure testimony: you should know this is intrinsically impure.

"'Whether Tathāgatas appear in the world or do not appear, the dharmas abide as they are and abide in the dharma-realm': this is called dharma-accomplishment.

"As for the concise and the extensive: this means summarizing in a single statement of dharma, then gradually expanding and broadening, analyzing and expounding until it is fully exhausted.

"If any practice has objects and is a determinate factor of awakening, I teach the foundations of mindfulness and the rest. This is called the characteristic of intrinsic nature. The worldly and supramundane paths together cut off all afflictions and attract the fruits of worldly and supramundane merit. This is called the characteristic of the attainment of fruition. If, through the wisdom-knowledge of that liberation, one extensively teaches and displays it for sentient beings, this is called the characteristic of the display of spiritual powers.

"When one cultivates the factors of awakening in this way, those defiled dharmas that oppose the path are called obstructive dharmas; those that bring great benefit are called conforming dharmas. The faults of obstruction are called the characteristic of dangers. The merits of conformity are called the characteristic of benefits."

At that time Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I humbly wish you would further teach the assembly of bodhisattvas the summarized meaning of upholding sūtra, vinaya, and mātṛkā, which is not shared with any non-Buddhist path, so that the bodhisattvas may progressively and in due order enter into the Tathāgata's profoundly deep teaching."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Listen carefully now! I shall teach you the summarized meaning of upholding, so that the bodhisattvas may skillfully enter the Tathāgata's teaching of non-definitive meaning. Mañjuśrī! Whether defiled dharmas or pure dharmas, I teach that all dharmas have no maker and no person, and nothing whatsoever to be grasped. It is not that defiled dharmas are first defiled and later pure, nor that pure dharmas are later pure and first defiled. The foolish and ignorant ordinary person, regarding the body full of faults, clings to the conception of dharma and person, grasps at a prior intrinsic nature, falsely sees causes and conditions, and says 'This is I' and 'This is mine.' Through the characteristics of seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, touching, and cognizing, one falsely constructs purity and impurity and engages in wrong conduct. If there is one who truly knows, that one relinquishes the body of faults and is not defiled by any affliction. One attains ultimate blissful purity, free from all falseness, and obtains the unconditioned body, forever free from all conditioned action."

Mañjuśrī! You should know that this is the summarized meaning of upholding."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke in verse:

> Defiled and pure dharmas
> Have no maker and no person.
> I teach there is nothing to grasp;
> Purity and defilement have no before or after.
> The fool, regarding the body of faults,
> Conceives of self and what belongs to self,
> And based on this gives rise to views and clinging:
> "I consume; I act."
> "This is my affliction;
> This is my purity."
> One who truly knows in this way
> Forever casts off the body of evil faults.
> Afflictions do not defile one;
> One attains ultimate blissful purity,
> Forever free from all falseness:
> The unconditioned, ever-abiding body.

At that time Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is the characteristic of the arising of the Tathāgata's mind?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The Tathāgata has no arising of mind, mental consciousness, or cognition. Yet the Tathāgata's mind arises without conditioned action; you should know it is like an illusion. If the Tathāgata's Dharma-body is free from all conditioned action, how does the mind arise amid the unconditioned? Mañjuśrī! It arises through the wisdom originally cultivated. It is like one who is not without mind when asleep and then acts upon waking: there is the power of the original act that brings about the waking. It is like one who enters the cessation of perception and feeling and is not performing the act of waking: there is the power of the original act that brings about emergence. As the mind arises from sleep and from the cessation of perception and feeling, so too does the Tathāgata's mind arise, through the wisdom originally cultivated."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Does the Tathāgata's body have mind or no mind?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "It is without mind, for the mind is not autonomous; yet there is great sovereignty."

Mañjuśrī further said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is the difference between the Tathāgata's range and the Tathāgata's realm?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The Tathāgata's range extends to all that the Tathāgatas share, namely the inconceivable, immeasurable merit of adorning and purifying buddha-lands. Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata's realm has five aspects: the realm of all kinds of sentient beings, the realm of worlds, the dharma-realm, the realm of those to be tamed, and the realm of the means of taming. This is the distinction."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The Tathāgata's attainment of true awakening, turning of the Dharma-wheel, and great parinirvāṇa: what characteristics do these have?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "They have no dual characteristics. Neither attaining true awakening nor not attaining true awakening; neither turning the Dharma-wheel nor not turning the Dharma-wheel; neither entering great parinirvāṇa nor not entering great parinirvāṇa. This is because the Dharma-body is ultimately pure, and because the transformation-body is manifested."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If the transformation-body is what sentient beings see, hear, and make offerings to, why does this generate merit?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Because of the pure mind directed toward the Tathāgata, because the transformation-body is the Tathāgata's transformation, and because it accords with the practice of parinirvāṇa."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Both being equally without conditioned action, why does the Tathāgata's Dharma-body emit great light of wisdom and produce innumerable transformation-images for sentient beings, while the liberation-body of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas does not?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "It is like the sun, moon, water, fire, and crystal: they equally have no conditioned action, yet they emit light that universally illuminates sentient beings. The crystal of wholesome dharmas, through the great authority of sentient beings and their accumulated karma, becomes a pure crystal reflecting all kinds of images. Just so, by relying upon the immeasurable dharma-realm and cultivating wisdom through skillful means, the Tathāgata's Dharma-body is well-refined. Therefore it emits the light of wisdom and transformation-images, while the mere liberation-body does not."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! It is said that the Tathāgata's spiritual power establishes the beings of the desire realm, whether in kṣatriya and brahmin families of great lineage, or the devas of the desire realm with all their personal endowments, or the devas of the form realm with all their personal endowments, or the devas of the formless realm with all their personal endowments. What is the meaning of this?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The Tathāgata's spiritual power establishes the path and the way, enabling beings in all places to obtain all personal endowments: wherever they practice the path and way as taught, in all those places they obtain all personal endowments. But if they turn against the path and way, reviling and disparaging it, and further give rise to ill will and harmful intent toward me, then when their lives end, in all those places they obtain inferior bodies. Therefore, Mañjuśrī! You should know that it is not merely through the Tathāgata's spiritual power that personal endowments are established. It is also through the ill will that beings direct toward the Tathāgata that they obtain inferior bodies."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! In an impure buddha-land, what is hard to obtain and what is easy to obtain? In a pure buddha-land, what is hard to obtain and what is easy to obtain?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "In an impure buddha-land, eight things are easily obtained and two things are hard to obtain. The eight that are easily obtained are: non-Buddhist paths, impoverished beings, birth in families of low station, being endowed or not endowed with faculties, ruinous and evil conduct, violation of the precepts, the evil destinies, and bodhisattvas who practice with inferior aspirations and means. The two that are hard to obtain are: bodhisattvas who practice with superior aspirations and means, and the appearance of a Tathāgata in the world. Mañjuśrī! In a pure buddha-land it is the reverse: eight things are hard to obtain and two things are easily obtained."

At that time Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Within the Continuous Liberation Sūtra, what is this sūtra called? How should it be upheld?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "This sūtra is called 'The Definitive Teaching on the Conforming Abode of the Tathāgata's Deeds.' Uphold it in this way."

When the Definitive Teaching on the Conforming Abode of the Tathāgata's Deeds was spoken, seventy-five thousand bodhisattvas attained the samādhi of fully discriminating the Dharma-body.
