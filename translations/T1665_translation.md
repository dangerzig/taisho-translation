# Treatise on Generating the Mind of Anuttarā Samyak Saṃbodhi within the Vajraśekhara Yoga

## *Jīngāngjǐng Yújiā Zhōng Fā Ānòuduōluó Sānmiǎo Sānpútí Xīn Lùn* (金剛頂瑜伽中發阿耨多羅三藐三菩提心論)

Taishō 1665

Translated by Amoghavajra (*Bùkōng*, 不空) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a key esoteric Buddhist treatise on the generation of bodhicitta (the aspiration to supreme awakening) from the perspective of the Vajraśekhara (Diamond Crown) yoga tradition. Attributed to Amoghavajra (705-774), the great translator of esoteric texts, it presents bodhicitta through three gates: (1) the vow-and-practice gate (xíngyuàn, 行願), emphasizing compassion and benefiting beings; (2) the ultimate-truth gate (shèngyì, 勝義), realizing the emptiness of all dharmas; and (3) the samādhi gate (sānmódì, 三摩地), the distinctive esoteric practice of visualizing a white moon-disk in the heart, representing the inherently pure bodhicitta. The treatise draws on the Avataṃsaka, Vimalakīrti, Nirvāṇa, and Mahāvairocana sūtras, and explains the thirty-seven deities of the Vajradhātu maṇḍala, the five wisdoms of the five buddhas, the significance of the syllable "a" (non-arising), and the five stages of becoming the deity (wǔxiàng chéngshēn, 五相成身). It is widely studied in Shingon Buddhism.*

---

The Great Extensively Wise Ācārya (*Dàguǎngzhì Āshélí*, 大廣智阿闍梨) says: If there is a person of superior faculties and superior wisdom who takes no pleasure in the dharmas of non-Buddhists or the two vehicles, who has great capacity and courageous resolve without confusion, such a person should cultivate the Buddha-vehicle. One should generate this mind: "I now aspire to anuttarā samyak saṃbodhi and do not seek any other fruit." Because this aspiration is firmly determined, the palace of Māra trembles, and all the buddhas of the ten directions fully bear witness to it. One will constantly receive supreme happiness among humans and devas. In every place of birth, one will remember and never forget.

If one wishes to accomplish the bodies of the various bodhisattvas within the yoga, this is also called generating the bodhicitta. Why? Because these honored ones are all identical with the body of the great Vairocana Buddha (*Dà Pílúzhēnà*, 大毘盧遮那). It is like a person who craves an official title: he generates the mind seeking an official title and cultivates the conduct of seeking that title. Or like one who craves wealth: he generates the mind seeking wealth and engages in the conduct of managing goods. Whenever ordinary people wish to seek something, whether good or evil, they first set their mind upon it and then accomplish their aspiration. Therefore, one who seeks bodhi generates the bodhicitta and cultivates the bodhisattva practice.

Having generated this mind, one must understand the characteristics of bodhicitta's practice. Its practice-characteristics are distinguished through three gates. When all buddhas and bodhisattvas in the past generated this mind during the causal stage, they took the ultimate truth, vow-and-practice, and samādhi as their precepts, never forgetting them even for a moment until they attained buddhahood. Only in the mantra (*zhēnyán*, 真言) dharma does one attain buddhahood in this very body. Therefore the samādhi [gate] is taught; in other teachings it is omitted and not discussed. The three are: (1) vow-and-practice, (2) ultimate truth, and (3) samādhi.

---

## The First Gate: Vow and Practice

The first, vow-and-practice, means that the practitioner constantly cherishes this mind: "I shall benefit and bring happiness to all sentient beings without remainder." One regards all sentient beings in the ten directions as one's own body.

What is meant by "benefit"? It means exhorting and inspiring all sentient beings to abide in unsurpassed bodhi; one will never use the dharma of the two vehicles to ferry them across. The mantra practitioner knows that all sentient beings contain the tathāgatagarbha nature and are all capable of abiding in unsurpassed bodhi. Therefore one does not use the dharma of the two vehicles to ferry them across.

As the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (*Huáyán Jīng*, 華嚴經) says: "There is not a single sentient being who does not possess the wisdom of the Tathāgata. It is only because of deluded thinking, inverted views, and attachment that they fail to realize it. If they were to abandon deluded thinking, then omniscience, spontaneous wisdom, and unobstructed wisdom would become manifest."

What is meant by "happiness"? The practitioner knows that all sentient beings will ultimately attain buddhahood, and therefore does not dare to slight them. Furthermore, within the gate of great compassion one should especially rescue and save them. Whatever sentient beings seek, one should give and provide it, even to the point of one's own life without reluctance, so that their lives are preserved in peace and they are made joyful. Having drawn near to them, they trust the teacher's words, and through this closeness one can also instruct and guide them. Sentient beings are ignorant and cannot be forced; the mantra practitioner introduces them through skillful means.

---

## The Second Gate: Ultimate Truth

The second, ultimate truth, is to contemplate that all dharmas are without self-nature (*asvabhāva*). How are they without self-nature? Ordinary people cling to fame, profit, and the necessities of livelihood in order to secure the body, and indulge in the three poisons and five desires. The mantra practitioner should truly find these wearisome and truly worthy of abandonment.

Furthermore, the various non-Buddhist teachers cling to their lives, using medicines and alchemical arts to attain celestial palaces and extended lifespans, or else taking rebirth in heaven as the ultimate goal. The mantra practitioner should contemplate that when the karmic force of such beings is exhausted, they have not escaped the three realms; their afflictions still remain and the consequences of past misdeeds have not been eliminated. Evil thoughts quickly arise again. In the time that follows, they sink into the ocean of suffering and can hardly escape. One should know that the dharmas of non-Buddhists are likewise illusions, dreams, and mirages.

Furthermore, among the practitioners of the two vehicles, the śrāvakas cling to the Four Noble Truths, and the pratyekabuddhas cling to the twelve links of dependent origination. They know that the four great elements and five aggregates will ultimately be destroyed. They develop deep revulsion, break through the attachment to beings, diligently cultivate their own dharma, personally realize its fruit, and regard great nirvāṇa as the ultimate. The mantra practitioner should contemplate that although those of the two vehicles have broken the attachment to self (*ātmagrāha*), they still have the attachment to dharmas (*dharmagrāha*). They only still the sense-consciousness and do not know anything else. Furthermore, having attained the stage of fruition, they have reduced the body to ashes and extinguished wisdom, entering nirvāṇa. Like the great void, they abide in constant quiescence. Those of fixed nature find it difficult to reawaken; they must wait until the limit of their kalpa-period is fulfilled before they can reawaken. Those of unfixed nature, regardless of the kalpa-limit, when encountering the right conditions can turn their minds toward the great [vehicle] and proceed from the Conjured City onward. Having already transcended the three realms, because they believed in the Buddha in former lives, they receive the skillful means of all buddhas and bodhisattvas and thus generate the great mind. They then begin from the first of the ten stages of faith and pass through all the stages, undergoing three incalculable kalpas of difficult and painful practice before they can attain buddhahood.

Knowing that the wisdom of the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas is narrow and inferior, one should not delight in it either. Furthermore, there are sentient beings who generate the Mahāyāna mind and practice the bodhisattva path, pervading all dharma-gates without exception, and who also undergo three incalculable kalpas cultivating the six pāramitās and ten thousand practices until all are complete. Yet the realization of buddhahood is still distant, because the dharmas they studied are scattered, leading to gradual stages.

Now, the mantra practitioner, having contemplated as above and again generating the mind to benefit and bring happiness to all sentient beings without remainder, through the determination of great compassion eternally transcends the realms of non-Buddhists and the two vehicles. By further cultivating the supreme yoga dharma, one who can enter the buddha-stage from the ordinary stage also transcends the realm of the ten-stage bodhisattvas. Furthermore, one deeply knows that all dharmas are without self-nature. How are they without self-nature? This has already been indicated above. The dharmas of the deluded path arise from deluded thinking, and by further proliferation become innumerable, boundless afflictions, causing beings to revolve through the six destinies. If one awakens, deluded thinking ceases and the various dharmas are extinguished; therefore they have no self-nature.

Moreover, the compassion of the buddhas, bringing function from the true, rescues and gathers in sentient beings. According to their illness, [the buddhas] prescribe medicine and bestow various dharma-gates. According to each being's afflictions, they provide the antidote. When the lost traveler encounters the raft and reaches the other shore, the dharma too should be relinquished, being without self-nature.

As the *Mahāvairocana Sūtra* (*Dà Pílúzhēnà Chéngfó Jīng*, 大毘盧遮那成佛經) says: "All dharmas are signless, meaning they have the characteristic of space." Having made this contemplation, it is called the bodhicitta of ultimate truth.

One should know that all dharmas are empty. Having awakened to the fact that dharmas are originally unborn, the mind-essence is itself suchness (*tathatā*). One does not perceive body or mind and abides in quiescent, equal, ultimate, true, and real wisdom, without allowing it to regress. When deluded thoughts arise, one knows them and does not follow them. When delusion subsides, the mind-source is empty and quiescent, the ten thousand virtues are fully present, and wondrous function is inexhaustible.

Therefore all the buddhas of the ten directions take the ultimate truth and vow-and-practice as their precepts. One who possesses this mind can turn the dharma-wheel, benefiting both self and others.

As the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* says:

> "Compassion, light, and wisdom as the chief,
> Skillful means all corresponding,
> Faith, understanding, and the purified mind:
> The immeasurable power of the Tathāgata.
> Unobstructed wisdom manifests before one,
> Self-awakened, not through another.
> Endowed equally with the Tathāgata,
> Generate this most supreme mind.
> When a child of the Buddha first gives rise
> To this wondrous jewel-mind,
> One transcends the stage of the ordinary
> And enters the domain of the Buddha's practice.
> Born into the family of the Tathāgata,
> The lineage is without blemish.
> Equal with the Buddha,
> One will certainly attain the supreme awakening.
> Having just generated such a mind,
> One immediately enters the first stage.
> The mind's joy cannot be shaken,
> Like the great king of mountains."

Furthermore, the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* says: From the first stage through the tenth, at every stage great compassion is the chief. As the *Contemplation of Immeasurable Life Sūtra* (*Wúliàngshòu Guān Jīng*, 無量壽觀經) says: "The mind of the Buddha is great compassion." And the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* (*Nièpán Jīng*, 涅槃經) says: "Homage to Cunda! Though his body is a human body, his mind is the same as the Buddha's mind." It also says:

> "Compassionate healer of the world, great king of medicine,
> Body and wisdom both quiescent.
> Within the dharma of no-self there is a true self:
> Therefore I bow to the supremely honored one.
> The initial aspiration and the final attainment are without distinction;
> Yet of these two minds, the initial mind is more difficult.
> Not yet having crossed over oneself, one first crosses others:
> Therefore I bow to the initial aspiration.
> Having generated this mind, one is already a teacher of humans and devas,
> Surpassing the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas.
> Such a mind-generation transcends the three realms:
> Therefore it is called the most unsurpassed."

As the *Mahāvairocana Sūtra* says: "Bodhi is the cause, great compassion is the root, and skillful means is the ultimate fulfillment."

## The Third Gate: Samādhi

The third, samādhi: the mantra practitioner, having contemplated as above, asks how one can realize unsurpassed bodhi. One should know that according to the natural order of dharma, one should abide in the great bodhicitta of Samantabhadra. All sentient beings inherently possess the sattva (being-nature), but because they are bound by the afflictions of greed, hatred, and delusion, the buddhas in their great compassion, through skillful and wise means, teach this extremely profound and secret yoga, directing practitioners to contemplate a white moon-disk within the mind.

By making this contemplation, one illuminates and sees the original mind, limpid and pure, like the full moon whose light pervades space without discrimination. It is also called "awakening," also called "the pure dharma-realm," also called "the ocean of prajñāpāramitā of true reality" that can contain various innumerable jewel-treasures of samādhi, like the full moon, pure white and clearly bright.

Why? Because all sentient beings completely contain the mind of Samantabhadra. I see my own mind in the form of a moon-disk. Why is the moon-disk used as a metaphor? Because the body of the full moon, round and bright, corresponds to the bodhicitta. The moon has sixteen phases, which symbolize the sixteen great bodhisattvas from Vajrasattva to Vajramuṣṭi in the yoga.

Among the thirty-seven deities, the five directional buddhas each represent one wisdom. The eastern buddha Akṣobhya (*Āchù*, 阿閦), through the cause of the great perfect mirror wisdom (*ādarśajñāna*), is also called vajra-wisdom. The southern buddha Ratnasaṃbhava (*Bǎoshēng*, 寶生), through the cause of the equality wisdom (*samatājñāna*), is also called consecration-wisdom. The western buddha Amitābha (*Āmítuó*, 阿彌陀), through the cause of the discriminating wisdom (*pratyavekṣaṇājñāna*), is also called lotus-wisdom and dharma-wheel-turning wisdom. The northern buddha Amoghasiddhi (*Bùkōng Chéngjiù*, 不空成就), through the cause of the all-accomplishing wisdom (*kṛtyānuṣṭhānajñāna*), is also called karma-wisdom. The central buddha Vairocana (*Pílúzhēnà*, 毘盧遮那), through the cause of the dharmadhātu wisdom, is the foundation. From these four buddha-wisdoms arise the four pāramitā-bodhisattvas. The four bodhisattvas are Vajra, Ratna, Dharma, and Karma: the mother who gives birth to, nurtures, and raises all the holy sages of the three times. From within the dharmadhātu-essence-nature, the four buddhas flow forth through mudrā-accomplishment.

Each of the four directional tathāgatas subsumes four bodhisattvas. Eastern Akṣobhya subsumes: Vajrasattva, Vajrarāja, Vajrarāga, and Sādhu. Southern Ratnasaṃbhava subsumes: Vajraratna, Vajratejas, Vajraketu, and Vajrahāsa. Western Amitābha subsumes: Vajradharma, Vajratīkṣṇa, Vajrahetu, and Vajrabhāṣa. Northern Amoghasiddhi subsumes: Vajrakarma, Vajrarakṣa, Vajrayakṣa, and Vajramuṣṭi. Four bodhisattvas for each of the four directional buddhas makes sixteen great bodhisattvas. Among the thirty-seven deities, excluding the five buddhas, the four pāramitā[-bodhisattvas], the four gathering[-bodhisattvas], and the eight offering[-bodhisattvas], only the sixteen great bodhisattvas are subsumed under the four directional buddhas.

Furthermore, in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra*, from inner emptiness through to the emptiness of the non-existence of self-nature, there are also sixteen kinds of meaning. All sentient beings have within the substance of their mind one portion of pure nature, with all practices fully equipped. Its essence is extremely subtle, bright, and pure white. Even through revolving in the six destinies, it does not change, like one-sixteenth of the moon. When the moon is in its one-part brightness, during the period of conjunction, it is merely overpowered by the sun's light, so its own brightness does not appear. Afterward, from the first of the month, it increases day by day until the fifteenth, when it is full and unobstructed.

Therefore the contemplative practitioner initially uses the syllable *a* (*ā*, 阿) to activate the brightness within the original mind, gradually making it pure white and clearly bright, and realizes the wisdom of non-arising. The syllable *a* means "the non-arising of all dharmas."

According to the *Mahāvairocana Sūtra* commentary's explanation, the syllable *a* has five meanings: (1) *a* is the bodhicitta; (2) *a* is the bodhisattva-practice; (3) *āṃ* is the meaning of realizing bodhi; (4) *aḥ* is the meaning of parinirvāṇa; (5) *aḥ* is the meaning of complete skillful-means wisdom. Furthermore, the syllable *a* can be correlated with the four characters "open, show, awaken, enter" (*kāi shì wù rù*, 開示悟入) in the *Lotus Sūtra*. "Opening the Buddha's knowledge and vision" opens both bodhi and nirvāṇa simultaneously, corresponding to the first *a*, the meaning of bodhicitta. "Showing" shows the Buddha's knowledge and vision, corresponding to the second *a*, the meaning of bodhisattva-practice. "Awakening" awakens the Buddha's knowledge and vision, corresponding to the third *āṃ*, the meaning of realizing bodhi. "Entering" enters the Buddha's knowledge and vision, corresponding to the fourth *aḥ*, the meaning of parinirvāṇa. Speaking comprehensively of complete accomplishment, the fifth *aḥ* is the meaning of the fulfillment of skillful-means wisdom.

A gāthā praising the syllable *a* as the meaning of bodhicitta:

> "An eight-petaled white lotus, one cubit as its gate,
> Brightly manifesting the syllable *a* in luminous white hue.
> Meditation and wisdom together enter the vajra-clasp,
> Summoning entry into the Tathāgata's quiescent wisdom."

To "meet and support" (*fúhuì*, 扶會) the syllable *a* means to contemplate it precisely with firm determination, observing the round, bright, pure consciousness. If one just sees it, that is called seeing the true ultimate truth. If one constantly sees it, one enters the first bodhisattva stage. If it gradually increases and expands, it fills the entire dharmadhātu, vast as space, contracting and expanding freely. One then possesses omniscience.

All who cultivate the yoga contemplative practice must fully practice the three mysteries (*sānmì*, 三密) and realize the meaning of the five stages of becoming the deity-body (*wǔxiàng chéngshēn*, 五相成身). The three mysteries are: (1) The body-mystery: forming mudrās and invoking the sacred assembly. (2) The speech-mystery: secretly reciting the mantra syllables, each one clear and without error. (3) The mind-mystery: abiding in the yoga-correspondence of the pure white full-moon contemplation of bodhicitta.

Next, the five stages of becoming the deity-body: (1) the penetrating mind, (2) the bodhicitta, (3) the vajra-mind, (4) the vajra-body, and (5) the realization of unsurpassed bodhi and attainment of the adamantine indestructible body. When these five stages are fully equipped, one becomes the deity-body. That round brightness is the body of Samantabhadra and is also the mind of Samantabhadra, identical with all the buddhas of the ten directions. Although the practice and realization of the three times have a before and after, in the attainment of awakening there is no past, present, or future.

The mind of an ordinary person is like a closed lotus. The mind of the Buddha is like a full moon. If this contemplation succeeds, then all the lands of the ten directions, whether pure or impure, the sentient beings of the six destinies, the stages of the three-vehicle practice, and the lands of the three times in their formation and destruction, the karmic differences of sentient beings, the practice-characteristics of bodhisattvas in the causal stage, and the buddhas of the three times: all appear within it. One realizes the deity-body and fulfills all the vows and practices of Samantabhadra.

Therefore the *Mahāvairocana Sūtra* says: "Such a true mind is what the Buddha has proclaimed."

Question: You previously said that practitioners of the two vehicles, because they have the attachment to dharmas, cannot attain buddhahood. Now you direct us to cultivate the samādhi of bodhicitta. How do these differ?

Answer: Because practitioners of the two vehicles have the attachment to dharmas, after a very long time of realizing principle, they sink into emptiness and stagnate in quiescence, limited by the number of kalpas before they generate the great mind. Furthermore, within the gate of scattered wholesome practices, they pass through countless kalpas. Therefore one should truly find them wearisome and not rely upon them. Now, the mantra practitioner, having already broken through both the attachment to self and the attachment to dharmas, is able to correctly see the wisdom of true reality. Yet because of beginningless obstructions, one has not yet realized the all-knowing wisdom of the Tathāgata. Wishing to seek the wondrous Way and cultivate the stages of practice from the ordinary to the buddha-stage, it is precisely this samādhi that can penetrate the self-nature of all buddhas, awaken to the dharma-body of all buddhas, realize the wisdom of the dharmadhātu-essence-nature, and accomplish the self-nature body, enjoyment body, transformation body, and outflow-equal body of the great Vairocana Buddha. The outflow-equal body is taught because practitioners have not yet realized it, and therefore it is proper to cultivate it.

Therefore the *Mahāvairocana Sūtra* says: "Accomplishment (*siddhi*) arises from the mind." As the *Vajraśekhara Yoga Sūtra* says: The Bodhisattva All-Accomplishment (*Sarvārthasiddhi*), first sitting upon the vajra-seat to take up the realization of the supreme Way, received this mind-ground teaching from all the buddhas and was thereby able to realize the fruit. People of the present day, if their minds are firmly determined and they cultivate as taught, can, without rising from the seat, bring the samādhi to manifest presence. This should be the accomplishment of the deity-body.

Therefore the *Mahāvairocana Sūtra*, in the section on the sequential method of offerings, says: "If one lacks the power for extensive augmentation practices, one should simply contemplate the bodhicitta. The Buddha says that within this is contained the ten thousand practices, fulfilling the pure and stainless dharma."

This bodhicitta can contain and store all the merit-dharmas of all buddhas. If one cultivates and realizes it and it becomes manifest, one becomes a guide for all beings. If one returns to the source, it is the land of Akanisṭha (*Mìyán*, 密嚴, the Closely-Adorned Pure Land). Without rising from the seat, one can accomplish all things.

A gāthā praising the bodhicitta:

> If a person seeks the Buddha's wisdom,
> Penetrating the bodhicitta,
> In this very body born of father and mother,
> One swiftly realizes the stage of great awakening.
