# 佛說護淨經
## Sūtra of Guarding Purity, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0748

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

In the past, the Buddha was walking together with Ānanda when they encountered a pool forty leagues across from east to west, forty leagues from north to south, and forty leagues deep. In the pool were creatures that looked like tadpoles, black as ink.

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Do you know the creatures in this pool?"

He replied: "I do not."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "The creatures in this pool are members of the great assembly of the Saṃgha from the ten directions who ate impure food. They fell into this foul and filthy pool of excrement and constantly eat impurity. For five million lifetimes they undergo this suffering. After that, for five hundred more lifetimes they fall among starving dogs, constantly eating impurity. Then they fall among pigs for five hundred lifetimes, constantly eating impurity. Then they fall among dung beetles for five hundred lifetimes, constantly eating impurity. After that they are born as humans, always in poor and destitute families, without clothing to cover their bodies or food to fill their mouths, always eating chaff and bran, constantly hungry and never satisfied."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Those who commit such offenses suffer punishments that are excruciating, boundless and without limit. I admonish you who live in the final age of the semblance-dharma: bhikṣus must not be careless. Wherever the assembly of monks has a place of residence, if impure food is being prepared, one should not go to eat there. If one wishes to eat pure food, one must report everything to the assembly. Lay supporters should prepare food according to the Dharma. Wearing the robe and carrying the bowl, observing the four kinds of deportment, one should go begging in accord with the Dharma. This is a true bhikṣu, who eliminates wrong livelihood and sustains life in accord with the Dharma.

"The Buddha does not speak falsely: the reward of merit follows like a shadow or an echo. In the past there was a bhikṣu who had just attained arhatship. Though he still had the body produced by former karmic bonds, he needed to relieve himself. Going to the privy in the dark of night, he saw a bhikṣu at the edge of the privy, moaning. The arhat said to this bhikṣu: 'You were formerly a person of good intentions. How have you fallen among hungry ghosts, moaning like this?' The hungry ghost bhikṣu replied: 'I have been hungry and thirsty for a long time, more than five hundred years, without even seeing water. I was just trying to go to the privy to eat impurity, but the guardian spirit of the privy struck me with an iron staff and would not let me near. I recall that in my former life I was a bhikṣu in charge of the Saṃgha's affairs. I handled the Saṃgha's pure food with impure contact and fed impure food to the assembly of monks. That is why I suffer this calamity.' Having encountered the arhat as a good friend, the arhat burned incense in the midst of the bhikṣu-Saṃgha and made prayers and dedications. Thereupon the ghost was freed from being a hungry ghost and returned to the human path."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "You must not be careless. Under no circumstances may you contaminate the Saṃgha's pure food. The Buddha does not speak falsely: the reward of merit follows like a shadow or an echo. In the Sūtra of the Eighteen Hells, the consequences of sin and merit are set forth. This hungry ghost originally came from the human path, because with impure hands he touched the Saṃgha's clean vessels, and with impure hands he touched the śramaṇas' pure food, and placed impure food among the śramaṇas' pure food. Because he fed impure food to the assembly of monks, for five hundred lifetimes he fell among hungry ghosts, constantly eating impurity. Wanting to go to the privy to eat excrement, the guardian spirit of the privy held an iron staff and struck him, not letting him near. This ghost ate people's pus, blood, mucus, and spittle, as well as foul wash-water. He constantly watched for women giving birth, feeding on the impure blood. For a further five hundred lifetimes he fell among pigs, dogs, and dung beetles, constantly eating foul excrement and impurity. Suffering these extreme afflictions, lifetime after lifetime, for hundreds of thousands of kalpas, there is no prospect of escape and liberation is hard to attain. The pain is beyond words.

"Because with impure hands one touches the male organ or the female organ, then touches the śramaṇas' clean vessels or the śramaṇas' pure food, or places impure food among the śramaṇas' pure food, or feeds impure food to the assembly of monks: because of this, one suffers this calamity. Ordinary people with their fleshly eyes do not know the consequences of sin and merit. From now on, those who wish to obtain blessings and protection, who assist the Saṃgha in preparing food: if with clean hands they handle the Saṃgha's clean vessels, and with clean hands wash the rice, and place clean rice among the Saṃgha's pure food, the merit obtained is immeasurable. From now on, let this be the constant rule. Let all people be made to hear and know this. Whenever any patron prepares a Dharma feast and offers provisions, those who are fasting may eat, but those who are not fasting may not eat. The merit of fasting for one day provides surplus provisions for six hundred thousand lifetimes. Those who do not fast fall among hungry ghosts for six hundred thousand lifetimes. Why? Because faithful offerings are hard to repay. Better to swallow a red-hot iron pellet than to eat this food. Swallowing a hot iron pellet lasts only an instant, but eating faithful offerings without merit brings prolonged great suffering, with five million lifetimes of hungry ghost suffering.

"Wherever food is prepared for a feast, all must be done according to the Dharma. One must not conceal leftover food and carry it home to give to wife and children. Those who eat this food, or carry it under the arm, will for five hundred lifetimes constantly carry a hot iron wheel, entering from under the left arm and exiting from under the right. All food prepared for fasting must be treated with extreme care, for a single grain of rice transforms into a hot pellet.

"If any worthy person, in preparing a merit feast, first tastes and samples the food, it all becomes leftover food, and the feast is in vain. It would be better not to hold it at all. Why? Because the devas are not pleased and the ghosts and spirits are not happy. The person who first tastes will also suffer five hundred lifetimes among hungry ghosts. From now on, those who wish to obtain merit should prepare the fasting food according to the Dharma. Then merit and virtue can be obtained, the devas will rejoice, all the spirits will celebrate with delight, and the heavenly spirits will give their protection. The sūtras do not speak falsely: the reward of merit follows like a shadow or an echo."
