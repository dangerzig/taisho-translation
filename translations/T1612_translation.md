# Mahāyāna Treatise on the Five Aggregates

## *Dàshèng Wǔyùn Lùn* (大乘五蘊論)

Taishō 1612

Composed by Bodhisattva Vasubandhu (*Shìqīn*, 世親). Translated by Xuanzang (*Xuánzàng*, 玄奘) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is Vasubandhu's concise Yogācāra analysis of the five aggregates (pañcaskandha), the twelve sense-bases (āyatana), and the eighteen elements (dhātu). It systematically defines each category of dharma in the Yogācāra framework: the four great elements and derived form, the three types of feeling, perception, the fifty-one mental factors (organized as five omnipresent, five object-determining, eleven wholesome, six root afflictions, twenty secondary afflictions, and four indeterminate), the fourteen dharmas not associated with mind, the consciousness aggregate (including the foundational ālayavijñāna and the afflicted manas), four types of unconditioned dharmas, and concluding questions about the purpose and characteristics of each classificatory scheme. This text is a key Abhidharma primer of the Yogācāra school and was widely studied in East Asian Buddhism.*

As the Blessed One (*Bhagavān*) has briefly taught, there are five aggregates (*skandha*): (1) the form aggregate (*rūpaskandha*), (2) the feeling aggregate (*vedanāskandha*), (3) the perception aggregate (*saṃjñāskandha*), (4) the formations aggregate (*saṃskāraskandha*), and (5) the consciousness aggregate (*vijñānaskandha*).

---

## The Form Aggregate

What is the form aggregate? It is the four great elements (*mahābhūta*) and the forms derived from the four great elements.

What are the four great elements? They are the earth element (*pṛthivīdhātu*), water element (*abdhātu*), fire element (*tejodhātu*), and wind element (*vāyudhātu*). What is the earth element? It is the nature of solidity and resistance. What is the water element? It is the nature of fluidity and moisture. What is the fire element? It is the nature of warmth and dryness. What is the wind element? It is the nature of lightness and motion.

What are the forms derived from the four great elements? They are the eye faculty, ear faculty, nose faculty, tongue faculty, and body faculty; form, sound, smell, taste, a portion of the tangible, and non-manifest form (*avijñaptirūpa*), among others.

What is the eye faculty? It is the subtle material (*prasādarūpa*) that takes visible form as its object. What is the ear faculty? It is the subtle material that takes sound as its object. What is the nose faculty? It is the subtle material that takes smell as its object. What is the tongue faculty? It is the subtle material that takes taste as its object. What is the body faculty? It is the subtle material that takes the tangible as its object.

What is visible form? It is the object of the eye: display-color (*varṇa*), shape-color (*saṃsthāna*), and gesture-color (*vijñapti*), among others. What is sound? It is the object of the ear: sounds caused by the great elements that are appropriated, sounds caused by the great elements that are not appropriated, and sounds caused by both together. What is smell? It is the object of the nose: pleasant smell, unpleasant smell, and other smells. What is taste? It is the object of the tongue: sweet, sour, salty, pungent, bitter, and bland. What is the tangible in part? It is the object of the body: apart from the four great elements themselves, the remaining derived tangibles, namely smoothness, roughness, heaviness, lightness, cold, hunger, thirst, and so on. What is non-manifest form? It is the form produced by manifest physical actions (*vijñaptikarman*) and by meditative concentration (*samādhi*), which is invisible and non-resistant.

---

## The Feeling Aggregate

What is the feeling aggregate? It is threefold experience (*vedanā*): (1) painful, (2) pleasant, (3) neither-painful-nor-pleasant. "Pleasant" is defined as that which, when it ceases, gives rise to a desire for reunion. "Painful" is defined as that which, when it arises, gives rise to a desire for separation. "Neither-painful-nor-pleasant" is defined as that in which neither desire is present.

---

## The Perception Aggregate

What is the perception aggregate? It is the apprehension of various characteristics with respect to objects.

---

## The Formations Aggregate

What is the formations aggregate? It is all mental factors (*caitta*) apart from feeling and perception, together with the dharmas not associated with mind (*cittaviprayuktasaṃskāra*).

### Mental Factors

What are the remaining mental factors? They are those dharmas associated with mind. What are they? Contact (*sparśa*), attention (*manaskāra*), feeling (*vedanā*), perception (*saṃjñā*), volition (*cetanā*); desire (*chanda*), resolve (*adhimokṣa*), mindfulness (*smṛti*), concentration (*samādhi*), wisdom (*prajñā*); faith (*śraddhā*), shame (*hrī*), embarrassment (*apatrāpya*), the wholesome root of non-attachment (*alobha*), the wholesome root of non-hatred (*adveṣa*), the wholesome root of non-delusion (*amoha*), diligence (*vīrya*), pliancy (*praśrabdhi*), non-negligence (*apramāda*), equanimity (*upekṣā*), non-harming (*ahiṃsā*); attachment (*rāga*), hatred (*pratigha*), pride (*māna*), ignorance (*avidyā*), views (*dṛṣṭi*), doubt (*vicikitsā*); anger (*krodha*), resentment (*upanāha*), concealment (*mrakṣa*), affliction (*pradāśa*), jealousy (*īrṣyā*), miserliness (*mātsarya*), deceit (*māyā*), flattery (*śāṭhya*), arrogance (*mada*), harmfulness (*vihiṃsā*), shamelessness (*āhrīkya*), lack of embarrassment (*anapatrāpya*), torpor (*styāna*), restlessness (*auddhatya*), lack of faith (*āśraddhya*), laziness (*kausīdya*), negligence (*pramāda*), forgetfulness (*muṣitasmṛtitā*), distraction (*vikṣepa*), non-discernment (*asaṃprajanya*); regret (*kaukṛtya*), drowsiness (*middha*), initial thought (*vitarka*), and sustained thought (*vicāra*).

Among these mental factors: five are omnipresent (*sarvatraga*), five are object-determining (*viniyata*), eleven are wholesome (*kuśala*), six are root afflictions (*kleśa*), the rest are secondary afflictions (*upakleśa*), and four are indeterminate (*aniyata*).

**Contact**: Its nature is discrimination arising from the threefold conjunction [of faculty, object, and consciousness].

**Attention**: Its nature is causing the mind to become alert.

**Volition**: Its nature is causing the mind to act with respect to merit, demerit, and what is neither, constituting mental karma.

**Desire**: Its nature is aspiration toward what is agreeable.

**Resolve**: Its nature is affirmation with regard to what has been determined, exactly as it has been understood.

**Mindfulness**: Its nature is not forgetting what has been repeatedly practiced, keeping it clearly in mind.

**Concentration**: Its nature is one-pointedness of mind, non-distraction with regard to what is being contemplated.

**Wisdom**: Its nature is the discrimination of dharmas with regard to that [object]; it may be drawn from correct reasoning, incorrect reasoning, or neither.

**Faith**: Its nature is profound accordance with and purity of mind regarding karma and its fruits, the truths, and the [Three] Jewels.

**Shame**: Its nature is a sense of disgrace about wrongs committed, based on oneself and on the dharma.

**Embarrassment**: Its nature is a sense of disgrace about wrongs committed, based on the world.

**Non-attachment**: Its nature is the antidote to attachment, producing deep revulsion and non-clinging.

**Non-hatred**: Its nature is the antidote to hatred, having compassion (*maitrī*) as its nature.

**Non-delusion**: Its nature is the antidote to delusion, having correct practice in accordance with reality as its nature.

**Diligence**: Its nature is the antidote to laziness, being courageous and vigorous with regard to wholesome dharmas.

**Pliancy**: Its nature is the antidote to heaviness, making body and mind light, supple, and capable.

**Non-negligence**: Its nature is the antidote to negligence; it is non-attachment through diligence. Based on this, one abandons unwholesome dharmas and cultivates their wholesome antidotes.

**Equanimity**: Its nature is non-attachment through diligence. Based on this, one attains evenness of mind, straightness of mind, and non-excitability of mind. Furthermore, through this, one abides without affliction among the defiled dharmas that have already been eliminated.

**Non-harming**: Its nature is the antidote to harmfulness, having compassion (*karuṇā*) as its nature.

**Attachment**: Its nature is defiled love and clinging with regard to the five appropriated aggregates.

**Hatred**: Its nature is the desire to cause harm to sentient beings.

**Pride**: There are seven kinds of pride. (1) Pride (*māna*): thinking oneself superior to the inferior, or equal to the equal; its nature is mental exaltation. (2) Excessive pride (*atimāna*): thinking oneself superior to the equal, or equal to the superior; its nature is mental exaltation. (3) Pride upon pride (*mānātimāna*): thinking oneself superior to the superior; its nature is mental exaltation. (4) Self-pride (*asmimāna*): regarding the five appropriated aggregates as "I" or "mine"; its nature is mental exaltation. (5) Presumptuous pride (*adhimāna*): regarding what one has not yet attained among superior and extraordinary realizations as having been attained; its nature is mental exaltation. (6) Deferential pride (*ūnamāna*): regarding oneself as only slightly inferior when in fact one is greatly inferior; its nature is mental exaltation. (7) Perverted pride (*mithyāmāna*): regarding oneself as having virtues when one actually has none; its nature is mental exaltation.

**Ignorance**: Its nature is lack of knowledge regarding karma and its fruits, the truths, and the Jewels. It is of two kinds: innate (*sahaja*) and arising from discrimination (*parikalpita*). Furthermore, desire-realm attachment, hatred, and desire-realm ignorance are called the three unwholesome roots: the unwholesome root of attachment, the unwholesome root of hatred, and the unwholesome root of delusion.

**Views**: There are five kinds of views. (1) View of the perishable collection (*satkāyadṛṣṭi*): regarding the five appropriated aggregates as "I" or "mine"; its nature is defiled wisdom. (2) Extreme view (*antagrāhadṛṣṭi*): through the force of that [view], regarding [what is perceived as "I"] as either permanent or annihilated; its nature is defiled wisdom. (3) Wrong view (*mithyādṛṣṭi*): denying causes, denying fruits, denying function, or destroying wholesome matters; its nature is defiled wisdom. (4) Clinging to views (*dṛṣṭiparāmarśa*): regarding the first three views and the aggregates on which they depend as supreme, highest, superior, and ultimate; its nature is defiled wisdom. (5) Clinging to rules and rituals (*śīlavrataparāmarśa*): regarding rules and rituals and the aggregates on which they depend as purifying, liberating, or leading to emancipation; its nature is defiled wisdom.

**Doubt**: Its nature is vacillation regarding the truths and similar matters. Among the afflictions, the last three views and doubt arise only from discrimination; the rest arise both innately and from discrimination.

**Anger**: Its nature is mental harm when encountering something disagreeable in the present.

**Resentment**: Its nature is harboring grudges without letting go.

**Concealment**: Its nature is hiding one's own faults.

**Affliction** (*pradāśa*): Its nature is uttering harsh and abusive words with vexation.

**Jealousy**: Its nature is envy regarding others' prosperity.

**Miserliness**: Its nature is stinginess, contrary to generosity.

**Deceit**: Its nature is deceptively presenting false appearances to deceive others.

**Flattery**: Its nature is crookedness of mind through skillful means of hiding one's own faults.

**Arrogance**: Its nature is being intoxicated and haughty, relying on one's own prosperity.

**Harmfulness**: Its nature is causing injury to sentient beings.

**Shamelessness**: Its nature is not feeling disgrace within oneself for wrongs committed.

**Lack of embarrassment**: Its nature is not feeling disgrace before others for wrongs committed.

**Torpor**: Its nature is sluggishness and incapacity of mind, being clouded and dim.

**Restlessness**: Its nature is non-tranquility of mind.

**Lack of faith**: Its nature is the antidote to faith; it is incorrect trust regarding karma, fruits, and other matters, and impurity of mind.

**Laziness**: Its nature is the antidote to diligence; it is lack of courageous vigor regarding wholesome dharmas.

**Negligence**: Its nature is, through attachment, hatred, delusion, and laziness, not guarding the mind against afflictions and not being able to cultivate wholesome dharmas.

**Forgetfulness**: Its nature is defiled mindfulness, being unable to clearly remember wholesome dharmas.

**Distraction**: Its nature is the mind flowing and wandering due to attachment, hatred, and delusion.

**Non-discernment**: Its nature is not correctly abiding in present actions of body, speech, and mind.

**Regret**: Its nature is mental remorse.

**Drowsiness**: Its nature is involuntary mental dullness and withdrawal.

**Initial thought** (*vitarka*): Its nature is seeking through coarse mental discourse, a differentiation of volition and wisdom that makes the mind coarse. **Sustained thought** (*vicāra*): Its nature is examination through subtle mental discourse, a differentiation of volition and wisdom that makes the mind subtle.

### Dharmas Not Associated with Mind

What are the dharmas not associated with mind? They are provisionally established based on the aspects of form, mind, and mental factors; they cannot be defined as either identical or different in nature from those [dharmas]. What are they? Acquisition (*prāpti*), the attainment of non-perception (*asaṃjñisamāpatti*), the attainment of cessation (*nirodhasamāpatti*), the state of non-perception (*asaṃjñika*), the life faculty (*jīvitendriya*), homogeneity of beings (*nikāyasabhāga*), birth (*jāti*), aging (*jarā*), duration (*sthiti*), impermanence (*anityatā*), collections of names (*nāmakāya*), collections of phrases (*padakāya*), collections of syllables (*vyañjanakāya*), the state of being an ordinary person (*pṛthagjanatvam*), and others of this kind.

**Acquisition**: It is obtaining and possessing. It is of three kinds: as seed, as mastery, and as presently manifest, according to circumstances.

**Attainment of non-perception**: Having been freed from attachment to the third meditation (*śubhakṛtsna*) but not yet freed from attachment to the level above, preceded by an attention directed toward renunciation, it is the cessation of non-permanent mental and mental factors.

**Attainment of cessation**: Having been freed from attachment to the sphere of nothingness, seeking further progress from the peak of existence (*bhavāgra*), preceded by an attention directed toward quiescence, it is the cessation of non-permanent and a portion of permanent mental and mental factors.

**State of non-perception**: It is the fruit of the attainment of non-perception. Having been born in the heaven of non-perceptive beings, it is the cessation of non-permanent mental and mental factors.

**Life faculty**: It is defined as the determination of the duration of abiding within one's class of beings, drawn by prior karma.

**Homogeneity of beings**: Its nature is the similarity among sentient beings of the same kind.

**Birth**: Its nature is the arising of formations that previously did not exist within one's class of beings.

**Aging**: Its nature is the transformation of the continuity of those very formations.

**Duration**: Its nature is the continuing flow of those very formations.

**Impermanence**: Its nature is the perishing of the continuity of those very formations.

**Collections of names**: Their nature is the expressions designating the inherent natures of dharmas.

**Collections of phrases**: Their nature is the expressions designating the distinctions among dharmas.

**Collections of syllables**: Their nature is syllables (*akṣara*), because they can express the preceding two kinds. They are also called "displays" (*vyañjana*), because they serve as the basis for names and phrases and thereby make meaning clear. They are also called "syllables" (*akṣara*), because they are not changed by different contexts.

**State of being an ordinary person**: Its nature is the non-attainment of the noble dharmas. The above and similar constitute the formations aggregate as described.

## The Consciousness Aggregate

What is the consciousness aggregate? Its nature is discernment (*vijñapti*) with respect to objects. It is also called "mind" (*citta*) and "mentation" (*manas*), because it gathers and because it is subsumed under mentation.

The supreme mind is the ālayavijñāna (*ālàiyē shí*, 阿賴耶識, 'storehouse consciousness'). Why? Because within this consciousness, the seeds of all formations are gathered. Furthermore, the conditions for these formations are indiscernible; the continuity flows uniformly, before and after. Moreover, because of this [consciousness], when one emerges from the attainment of cessation, the attainment of non-perception, or the state of non-perception, the discerning cognitions called "evolving consciousnesses" (*pravṛttivijñāna*) arise again, because they depend on the differentiation of object-conditions, because they are repeatedly interrupted yet arise again, and because they cause the cycle of birth-and-death to revolve.

The ālayavijñāna is so called because it can store all seeds, because it can store the appearance of self-conceit (*asmimāna*), and because it takes the body as its object-domain. It is also called the ādānavijñāna (*ētuónà shí*, 阿陀那識, 'appropriating consciousness'), because it upholds and maintains the body.

The supreme mentation (*manas*) takes the ālayavijñāna as its object and is the consciousness constantly associated with self-delusion, self-view, self-pride, and self-love. Its continuity flows uniformly, before and after, except in the state of arhatship, the noble path, and when the attainment of cessation is presently manifest.

## Questions and Answers on the Aggregates

Question: "For what reason are they called 'aggregates' (*skandha*)?"

Answer: "They are called 'aggregates' in the sense of accumulation. That is, forms and the rest are summarily gathered according to their worldly continuity, their types, their destinies, and their distinctions."

---

## The Twelve Sense-Bases

Furthermore, there are twelve sense-bases (*āyatana*): the eye base and form base, the ear base and sound base, the nose base and smell base, the tongue base and taste base, the body base and tangible base, the mind base and dharma base. The five bases of eye and so on, as well as the bases of form, sound, smell, and taste, have been explained above. The tangible base comprises the four great elements together with the aforementioned tangibles in part. The mind base is the consciousness aggregate. The dharma base comprises the feeling, perception, and formations aggregates, non-manifest form, and the unconditioned.

What is the unconditioned (*asaṃskṛta*)? It is the unconditioned of space (*ākāśa*), the unconditioned of non-analytical cessation (*apratisaṃkhyānirodha*), the unconditioned of analytical cessation (*pratisaṃkhyānirodha*), and suchness (*tathatā*), among others. What is space? It is what allows room for material forms. What is non-analytical cessation? It is cessation that is not through separation. What does this mean? It is when, apart from the antidote to afflictions, the aggregates ultimately do not arise. What is analytical cessation? It is cessation through separation. What does this mean? It is when, through the antidote to afflictions, the aggregates ultimately do not arise. What is suchness? It is the dharmatā of all dharmas, the selflessness of dharmas (*dharmanairātmya*).

Question: "For what reason are they called 'sense-bases' (*āyatana*)?"

Answer: "The meaning of 'sense-base' is that they are the gates for the arising and development of the consciousnesses."

---

## The Eighteen Elements

Furthermore, there are eighteen elements (*dhātu*): the eye element, form element, and eye-consciousness element; the ear element, sound element, and ear-consciousness element; the nose element, smell element, and nose-consciousness element; the tongue element, taste element, and tongue-consciousness element; the body element, tangible element, and body-consciousness element; the mind element, dharma element, and mind-consciousness element.

The elements of eye and so on, and the elements of form and so on, are as explained in the sense-bases. The six consciousness-elements are, depending on the eye faculty and so on, and conditioning on form and so on as objects, discernment in nature. The mind element is the immediately preceding cessation of those [consciousnesses] and so on, established in order to indicate the sixth, mind-consciousness, and to broadly establish the eighteen elements. Thus the form aggregate is the ten sense-bases, ten elements, and a portion of the dharma base and dharma element. The consciousness aggregate is the mind base and the seven mind-elements. The remaining three aggregates, together with a portion of the form aggregate and the unconditioned, are the dharma base and dharma element.

Question: "For what reason are they called 'elements' (*dhātu*)?"

Answer: "They are called 'elements' in the sense of upholding their own characteristics of functionlessness."

---

## Purpose of the Threefold Classification

Question: "For what reason are the aggregates and the rest taught?"

Answer: "In order to counteract three kinds of self-grasping, respectively. The three kinds of self-grasping are: (1) the grasping of a unitary self, (2) the grasping of an experiencing self, and (3) the grasping of an acting self."

---

## Analysis of the Eighteen Elements

Furthermore, among these eighteen elements:

How many are material (*rūpin*)? Ten elements and a portion [of the dharma element]: these are the inherent nature of the form aggregate.

How many are immaterial? The remaining elements.

How many are visible (*sanidarśana*)? One: the form element.

How many are invisible? The remaining elements.

How many are resistant (*sapratigha*)? The ten material elements. If something is obstructed in a given place, that is the meaning of resistance.

How many are non-resistant? The remaining elements.

How many are contaminated (*sāsrava*)? Fifteen elements and portions of the last three, because afflictions arise in them and because they are the domain of present activity.

How many are uncontaminated? Portions of the last three.

How many are bound to the desire realm? All of them.

How many are bound to the form realm? Fourteen, excluding smell, taste, nose-consciousness, and tongue-consciousness.

How many are bound to the formless realm? The last three.

How many are unbound? Those very uncontaminated elements.

How many are subsumed under the aggregates? All except the unconditioned.

How many are subsumed under the appropriated aggregates? The contaminated ones.

How many are wholesome? How many unwholesome? How many indeterminate? Ten are of all three kinds: the seven mind-elements together with the form, sound, and dharma elements. Eight are indeterminate [only].

How many are internal? Twelve, excluding the form, sound, smell, taste, tangible, and dharma elements. How many are external? Six: those that were excluded.

How many have objects (*sārambaṇa*)? The seven mind-elements and a portion of the dharma element, namely the mental factors.

How many are without objects? The remaining ten and a portion of the dharma element.

How many have discrimination (*savikalpa*)? The mind element, mind-consciousness element, and a portion of the dharma element.

How many are appropriated (*upātta*)? The five internal material elements and portions of four elements: form, smell, taste, and tangible.

How many are non-appropriated? The remaining nine and portions of the four.

How many are of the same class (*sabhāga*)? The five internal material elements, when they are in the domain of their corresponding consciousness.

How many are of a corresponding class (*tatsabhāga*)? Those very same [elements] when they are empty of their corresponding consciousness, being similar to their own kind.
