# 佛說法華三昧經
## Sūtra on the Lotus-Flower Samādhi, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0269

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Zhìyán (智嚴)

---

The Buddha was in Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with his venerable disciple-bhikṣus, twelve hundred and fifty in number, and seventy-three thousand bodhisattvas. The Śākra, Brahmā, and others beyond counting, and from all ten directions, beings who had flown there in immeasurable numbers, all possessed of marvelous spiritual penetration, as well as from other realms, beings as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, including devas, humans, and bodhisattvas, hundreds of billions times the sands of the Ganges, all came to the assembly and sat before the Buddha.

At that time, Śāriputra, Subhūti, and the other venerable bodhisattvas all had doubts, thinking: "What cause and condition has brought all these eminent ones to assemble here? What extraordinary and auspicious sign is this?" Then the disciples, having given rise to these doubts, paid homage to the Buddha and knelt, wishing to question the Buddha.

At that time, the Buddha had already emitted immeasurable light from his mouth, in the hundreds of billions, gradually expanding greatly to pervade the sky with radiance, illuminating far off the lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. The earth also shook. When the light was complete, the Buddha's bodily form could no longer be seen. The great assembly was startled and conferred together: "Into what realm has the Buddha's samādhi gone?" Each pondered this, then sat down in samādhi to seek where the Buddha had gone. Before the assembly, a bodhisattva named Wisdom-Aspect said: "Excellent, excellent! You worthy ones have been contemplating, yet cannot know where he has gone."

After a moment, the king of Rājagṛha, together with his inner palace, the crown prince, princesses, and attendant women, thirty-two thousand in all, came together to the mountain and arrived but did not see the Buddha. Then the bodhisattva No-Thought asked the king: "How many are in your retinue?" The king, named Eloquent Penetration, answered: "We came because we saw the Buddha's light."

The king's daughter, named Lìxíng, then asked the bodhisattva: "Where is the Buddha now? To which direction has he gone?"

He answered: "Just now we already sought the Buddha, but could not find where he is."

The girl answered: "You are the foremost among those with the Buddha's spiritual powers; you should know where he is."

The bodhisattva answered: "Please sit down." After a moment, the earth shook. From within the earth, the Buddha emerged and sat upon a spontaneously appearing great jeweled lotus. The eminent ones in the assembly were startled.

The king's daughter Lìxíng rose, paid homage to the Buddha, and then stood before the Buddha and spoke a verse to ask:

> "Just now we saw a great light,
> And suspected the Buddha had an extraordinary purpose.
> Therefore I brought the great assembly,
> Wishing to ask about the doubts in our hearts.
> But instead we did not see the Buddha,
> And our minds were greatly alarmed.
> We pray you will explain for us in full,
> So the great assembly may understand.
> Each entered a samādhi,
> Searching for the Buddha's bodily form,
> Yet all said they did not know where he was,
> And conferred together:
> 'What further samādhi should we enter
> To seek the Buddha's intent?'
> The girl came and asked the assembly:
> 'Where is the Buddha now?'
> With sincere mind, Lìxíng
> Truly wishes to ask something.
> May we see the World-Honored One's instruction,
> To resolve the doubts in our minds.
> Please manifest before the assembly
> And explain it in detail,
> The samādhi you entered just now:
> What is its name, and where does it abide?"

The Buddha said to the girl Lìxíng: "Your question is very profound. I shall explain it for you in detail. The samādhi I entered just now is called the Lotus-Flower Samādhi. It is like a great tree in a great kingdom with a single flower that covers the great trichiliocosm. Its fragrance perfumes buddha-lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. If any person merely hears its name, or understands it, they will naturally and swiftly attain this samādhi. If any person suffering from illness hears of this samādhi, they will instantly be freed, and the diseases of their faculties will be entirely eliminated."

The girl Lìxíng asked the Buddha: "How is it that this flower has such awesome power and such wisdom?"

The Buddha said to the girl Lìxíng: "The flower is the beauty of a single tree; all who see it cannot help but love and delight in it. Those who wish to obtain it: the Lotus-Flower Samādhi is this. It is the beauty amid birth and death, a great light that takes form. Those who do not know, do not hear, and do not believe in this samādhi, and do not practice it, who have not yet become bodhisattvas, do not perceive wisdom. They lose the root of their true nature and instead follow the frothy current, never seeing the light."

The girl Lìxíng again asked the Buddha: "I now wish to attain this samādhi. What practices should I cultivate? How many things must one practice to enter it? I pray the Buddha will extend his great compassion and broadly open the discussion of practice, so that all who hear may understand and enter this samādhi."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! You will liberate many, delivering them to the infinite shore. There are two things. What are the two? First, knowing that the dharma-body is like an illusion, like a transformation. Second, knowing that lust, anger, and delusion have no root and no form."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The dharma-body contains all things,
> Transforming and appearing amid sinking and floating.
> Lust, anger, and delusion have no form,
> Like bubbles and foam appearing on water.
> Observe the human body and its objects:
> Like something extinguished, with no form remaining.
> Dispersing and combining, it forms of itself;
> Analyze and reckon, and all is empty."

The Buddha said to the girl Lìxíng: "There are four more things that distinguish the practice of this samādhi. What are the four? First, practicing the precepts without the perception of form. Second, practicing generosity without a recipient. Third, freedom from weariness without confusion. Fourth, practicing wisdom without delusion. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Not transgressing precepts, there is nothing to damage;
> Practicing generosity does not enter into wisdom;
> Without weariness, there is no deluded confusion;
> Without delusion, there is no wisdom.
> Not speaking of one without practice,
> Having practice, one does not speak of direction.
> This samādhi can be entered;
> It has no place, no center, no boundary."

The Buddha said to the girl Lìxíng: "There are further thirty-six things that are what is seen in this samādhi. What are the thirty-six? Not seeing birth, not seeing death, no decrease, no increase, no going out, no entering, not outside, not inside, no abiding, no stopping, no water-color, no fire-color, no wind-color, no earth-color, no pain, no itching, no thought, no perception, no birth, no death, no consciousness, no craving, no lust, no anger, no hatred, no foolishness, no delusion, no stinginess, no generosity, no evil, no good, no mind, no thought, no action of consciousness, not producing the above manifold things, not extinguishing the above manifold things, like a single formless image. These are the thirty-six things, what is seen in the Lotus-Flower Samādhi."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "No conceptual thought, no thought at all;
> Not practicing the essentials of the perception of form.
> Without practice, the Lotus-Flower is pure;
> Empty and quiescent, without self.
> Not abiding in any place or entry;
> Sinking and ceasing, without form or image.
> Not observing good or evil;
> Both are empty and naturally so."

The Buddha told the girl Lìxíng: "What is seen in the Lotus-Flower Samādhi is also like this."

When the Buddha taught these thirty-six things, innumerable devas and humans and the worldly great assembly, together with the king's retinue great and small, altogether four hundred billion, all generated the aspiration for unsurpassed, true enlightenment. The girl Lìxíng and the inner palace women, thirty-two thousand attendants and consorts, all attained the patient joy of the birthlessness of dharmas.

Among them, a girl standing in the assembly saw that all had generated the aspiration for enlightenment. Her heart was greatly joyful, and she rose to pay homage to the Buddha. She circumambulated the Buddha three times, stood before the Buddha, and spoke in verse:

> "The World-Honored One is truly marvelous and divine,
> Expounding knowledge of the three ages.
> Cutting off the world's stains of lust and anger,
> Opening and transforming those caught in the frothy current,
> All arriving at the city of the unconditioned,
> In happiness and peace.
> Thus among devas and humans,
> The earth greatly quaked.
> Today the great assembly gathers,
> Hundreds of billions upon billions.
> Those of the future, the past, and the present
> All attain the natural state.
> May the great universal grace be obtained,
> And the awesome spiritual power of the Lotus-Flower.
> May all beings in the three realms
> All attain this samādhi.
> May I, abiding in this world,
> Always practice the Lotus-Flower way,
> So the world may have no aging or death,
> In happiness without sorrow.
> The three sufferings naturally removed,
> All as though through this samādhi.
> Empty perception in the realm of aspiration and consciousness,
> Naturally the excellent marks appear.
> Teaching those in the frothy current,
> Who attain the intent of compassionate universal practice.
> Light upon light, with fine deportment,
> Equal to becoming a body like the Tathāgata.
> Gathering together those born in the froth,
> The samādhi gives prior birth to practice,
> Enabling one in empty space
> To attain wisdom like the foremost."

After the girl Lìxíng spoke the verse, she thought: "Now I wish to teach people, but I do not see any rule of Dharma. By what means can I open understanding for people?"

The Buddha immediately knew the thought in the girl's mind and said to her: "What Dharma do you wish to hold to teach what person? Where, moreover, is the Dharma? And where, moreover, do people abide?"

The girl addressed the Buddha: "As you have spoken, there is no Dharma, no teaching, no person."

The Buddha said to the girl: "Without Dharma there are eight practices; without teaching there are six things to eliminate; without persons there are seven things to disperse."

The girl asked the Buddha: "What are the eight practices, the six things to eliminate, and the seven things to disperse?"

The Buddha said: "First, right view without crookedness. Second, right hearing without listening. Third, right governance without distortion. Fourth, right speech without verbosity. Fifth, right conduct without delusion. Sixth, right mindfulness without discursive thought. Seventh, right intention without wavering. Eighth, right receptivity without seeking. These are the eight practices of being without Dharma.

"What are the six things to eliminate of being without teaching? First, not thinking of having sight or being without sight. Second, not thinking of having sound or being without sound. Third, not thinking of having taste or being without taste. Fourth, not thinking of having smell or being without smell. Fifth, not thinking of having touch or being without touch. Sixth, not thinking of having thought or being without thought. These are the six things to eliminate.

"What are the seven things to disperse of being without persons? No water-color, no wind-color, no fire-color, no earth-color, no mind-color, no consciousness-color, no action-color. These are the seven things to disperse. There is no person to teach. One should understand it in this way."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "If one understands the Lotus-Flower,
> The essential verses of the samādhi,
> One should be diligent and energetic,
> Understanding clearly and attaining right speech.
> The seven, eight, and six being complete,
> Reckon the origin: no form, no trace.
> Not receiving, naturally desireless;
> Abandoning thought, peaceful and still.
> Teaching the Dharma without verbal instruction,
> Not seeing any life-span.
> The human root is empty and quiescent;
> Not understanding, the frothy say it exists.
> Not removing, not cutting off desire,
> Coming and going, with no place to abide.
> No pain, no conceptual thought,
> Not born, not dying or ceasing.
> Having thought is toil and suffering;
> No longer attached to causes and conditions.
> Manifesting the appearance of sensual desire,
> One has already returned to love of dust.
> Observing and seeing illness and pain,
> One's mind always merges with the origin.
> Wisdom sees without empty thoughts;
> Quietly peaceful, emptiness upon emptiness.
> The Lotus-Flower Samādhi appears,
> Neither going out nor entering, just abiding.
> Not seeing, not seeing emptiness:
> This is the swift attainment of suchness.
> Then one can practice the giving of Dharma,
> With wisdom as generosity.
> Speak of wisdom equally like this,
> And all Buddhas will praise it together."

When the girl heard this teaching, she was doubly overjoyed and ecstatic. She rose to pay homage to the Buddha, leaped into the air to a height of seven feet, then returned and sat on a vajra lotus.

At that time, among the assembly there were bhikṣus who thought: "Is this truly a girl? Or is she an illusory being?" They rose and paid homage to the Buddha, knelt, joined their palms, and spoke a verse to ask the Buddha:

> "Originally born in ignorance,
> Not knowing the meaning of the Way and wisdom,
> I do not know whether Lìxíng
> Is truly female or male.
> If truly a settled person,
> Why ask further about the Dharma?
> If truly a girl's body,
> Why are her questions so profound?
> Since birth I have attended the Buddha's Dharma,
> Yet never seen such a person.
> What she perceives is beyond the ordinary;
> How can her wisdom be so?
> From what land did she originally come,
> To be born in the royal house?
> In past lives, what Dharma did she practice?
> How many Buddhas has she attended?
> Her diligence is so heroic;
> What she asks, the Tathāgata answers.
> Her precepts are complete, her practice genuine;
> She can ask as though teaching.
> She can endure with a gentle mind,
> Yet she only speaks of practice.
> How is her resting mind?
> I truly wish to go and test her.
> In how many dharmas should she abide?
> Can she face a challenge without arising thought?
> If she truly has wisdom,
> I wish to follow her to understand the essentials.
> Examining what she teaches,
> Chanting broadly, entering both Way and world.
> What extraordinary mind does she have
> To alone possess such wisdom?"

The Buddha then said to the bhikṣu: "It is due to manifold causes and conditions." He then spoke a verse in reply:

> "The girl Lìxíng's original mind
> Established virtue and knows the origin.
> She has been in this world for a thousand lifetimes,
> Always practicing this samādhi.
> Her mind understands the essentials of all forms;
> Quiescent and one with dhyāna.
> She is truly a girl's body,
> Not a transformation come to appear.
> Originally from the formless realm,
> She has now come into this world.
> Continuing as before in her original practice,
> Having practiced, she establishes the right.
> Without body, she manifests the teaching mind,
> Universally mindful in compassionate practice.
> Contemplating that Dharma is empty at its root,
> Not giving rise to the marks of causation.
> Bhikṣu, you do not understand yourself;
> How can you wonder at this girl's body?
> Not seeing that there is no origin,
> You instead bind yourself in attachment.
> In meditation you wish to leave form,
> But you are further confused by form.
> Fleeing suffering, avoiding the three poisons,
> You have already entered the suffering of the three poisons.
> You do not understand your own body,
> And claim to have attained constant peace.
> All things are like illusion and transformation,
> Entering and leaving without form or abiding.
> The four elements are originally empty space;
> Naturally one receives attachment to form.
> Clinging to habit and self-limitation,
> Destroying the root, one gives rise to derivative desire."

At that time, eighty-four thousand bhikṣus, hearing the verse, understood its meaning and generated the aspiration for unsurpassed, true enlightenment. Innumerable devas and humans scattered flowers and famous incenses, all coming to make offerings to the Buddha.

Then Śāriputra thought: "This girl has such eloquence; why does she not leave her female body and become a man?"

The Buddha immediately knew what Śāriputra was thinking and said to him: "Ask the girl yourself."

Śāriputra then asked: "Pray, girl Lìxíng! What you have spoken is no ordinary matter; it is as if you were conversing face to face with the Tathāgata. Why do you not leave your female body and become a man?"

The girl Lìxíng answered: "Pray, Śāriputra! The essential of the Way's virtue is to see well with wisdom. Do not look at the four elements, which are earth, water, fire, and wind, the conjunction of the five faculties, and the six entries as decay. Mind, thought, and consciousness are like illusion and transformation, entering and leaving without form. Because the deluded mind is not exhausted, one confronts the three currents, rising and falling. What is there to treasure? Though your outflows are exhausted and your fetters resolved, you have the perception of impurity. Without form, as if it were evil; abiding in suffering, taking it for happiness."

Śāriputra asked: "In studying the Buddha's Dharma, should there be words of slander?"

The girl asked: "Worthy Śāriputra! What do you mean by slander?"

He answered: "One thing good, one thing bad: that is slander."

The girl replied: "You have not yet understood or comprehended. When a great person speaks, they do not take the small as small or the great as great. Good and evil are non-dual; they are equal and without difference. Slander the body, and the body is form. Slander the mind, and the mind has no form. The four elements and dharmas are empty, without form and without fabrication. What is there that receives slander?"

Śāriputra answered: "What you speak of is the practice of a great being, a bodhisattva. You have not yet become a bodhisattva; how can you speak of such matters?"

The girl replied: "By what does a great person establish themselves?"

He answered: "By words of universal equality. What does 'universal' mean? Teaching and guiding the people of the ten directions, leading them far from suffering and to attainment of the Way: this is 'universal.' But what you speak of, worthy one, is not universal; you only speak of the toil and suffering of birth and death."

The girl said to Śāriputra: "What is called 'universal' means not seeing that there are persons or no persons, teaching or no teaching, Dharma or no Dharma to contemplate. This is called 'universal.' It is not that one sees the suffering of birth and death and wishes to teach them, enabling them to attain the Way. That is 'universal.'"

Śāriputra had no eloquence to rebut these words. The girl then spoke a verse in praise:

> "Because people are confused by the three dusts,
> They are immediately bound by the six decays.
> The five evils confront the ten bandits,
> And the three straits plunge them into destitution.
> The twelve links continue in succession;
> The four elements bind and drown in the three.
> Not understanding, this is called inversion;
> Sitting, one receives the storehouse of an empty village.
> Without reason, sinking in the three lusts,
> Enmeshing oneself, plunging into the abyss.
> Clinging firmly, fearing two and three,
> Self-extinguishing, one receives rebirth again.
> Bent and bound by emptiness,
> Always harboring the perception of impurity,
> Calling this constant peace,
> Claiming to have attained the truly natural.
> Such people fill Jambudvīpa,
> Hundreds of billions upon billions.
> Their practices pervade the ten directions
> But do not benefit a single being.
> Those who follow and receive their practice
> All fall together into the ocean current.
> One can undertake the great Dharma-practice,
> Entering the ocean to pull up the root of desire.
> Breaking through the ocean to make it cease entirely,
> Leveling it so there is no return-current.
> Reversing the source, exhausting the chamber of desire,
> Enabling people to return to their homeland.
> That homeland is called the Unconditioned,
> Named the Hall of Purity.
> Full of happiness and purple gold,
> Entering and leaving, radiating light.
> The Buddhas of kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges
> All praise and expound it.
> Through the Dharma, emptiness, and non-contrivance,
> Without marks and without aspiration or consciousness,
> Even the deep currents and the ocean's waters
> Are all insufficient to describe it.
> With a broad mind, opening and transforming people,
> Naturally always at peace.
> I think of you, worthy ones,
> Who instead do not understand yourselves.
> Without reason you bind yourselves,
> And through illusion and transformation receive retribution."

At that time, forty-two thousand arhats all rectified their minds at the seventh stage. Eighty-five thousand Śākra and Brahmā devas all attained the birthless state.

Sixty-four thousand virtuous women rose and paid homage to the Buddha, then stood before the Buddha and spoke in verse:

> "Today the girl Lìxíng
> Has spoken for us, the assembly.
> Hearing the Buddha's profound Dharma,
> Our minds are opened and we understand.
> We truly wish to know the Way's virtue;
> From where does it all come?
> Gathering the assembly together,
> What marvelous and divine virtue is this,
> That it brings the great assembly
> To be startled and awed in mind,
> Subduing their thoughts, gladly following,
> Practicing according to the teaching?
> We wish to beseech the Honored One among the Heavens
> To compassionately arouse and wash the assembly.
> With sincere minds we receive the great wisdom,
> The Tathāgata's divine manifestation.
> At this moment, may our women's bodies
> Be turned, through our vow, away from sensual desire.
> We wish to become śramaṇas;
> The Buddha knows our hearts.
> Having completely awakened to all truths,
> May our bodies become like bodhisattvas',
> Proclaiming the Buddha's divine Way and teaching,
> And in turn transforming others.
> All the worthy women of the great assembly
> Rise together before the Buddha,
> Prostrating with their heads at his feet,
> Wishing to become like the Buddha's image."

The Buddha said to the women: "Your words are truly sincere. Now, wishing to seek what you desire, you should first inform your parents, and next obtain the king's permission. Only with his consent may you go forth."

Thereupon the women spoke a verse in reply:

> "Wishing to become śramaṇas,
> First we should inform our parents,
> And next obtain the king's permission;
> With his consent, we may enter the Way.
> Entering the Way is never too late;
> One should simply be diligent and open the mind.
> Understanding the root of mind, thought, and consciousness,
> All is equal with everything.
> Then comes the great resolve;
> When the mind understands, one reaches the Way.
> The Way arises from understanding the mind,
> Not abiding in attachment.
> Transforming and penetrating, observing all views,
> As though the mind were being born again.
> From the origin, knowing the origin is empty,
> Knowing all is impermanent and suffering.
> When the mind is confused, one follows the current;
> What is seen must have its counterpart.
> Not giving rise to thoughts of good or evil:
> Only then does one become a śramaṇa."

The virtuous women rose and went to their parents. They knelt and addressed their parents and the great king: "Today we received the king's grace and came to the Buddha's place. For the sake of the honored girl Lìxíng, we asked the Buddha about the profound and wondrous Dharma, wishing to seek the unconditioned Way, for ourselves and all beings. The Buddha taught the girl about origin and end, birth and death, and suffering, all due to attachment to sensual desire, not knowing the root of the Way's virtue. Because of this, impermanence arrives, and one must undergo the three kinds of suffering. We wish to become śramaṇas. We pray our parents will permit us to go forth. Having attained the Way, we shall return and deliver our parents."

The parents said to the women: "To seek the natural Way, each of you should see your own benefit and follow the practice as your heart desires. You may go; we too shall follow you. Address the king yourselves. With his consent, go freely. Why ask us?"

The women prostrated before the great king and said with tears: "People in the three realms suffer because of attachment to the perception of form. They have no freedom. Impermanence arrives suddenly, and there is no one to take their place. We truly wish to become śramaṇas, together with all the women. Those among us who, through the unconditioned, attain the Way shall return and deliver our parents. We pray the king will follow our original intent."

The king said to the girl Lìxíng and the others: "I have long wished to let you go and follow the practice of śramaṇas. But because you had not yet fulfilled three things, I did not send you away. First, you had not yet finished learning the various rites and teachings. Second, you had always known pleasure but had not experienced suffering. Third, your mouths had eaten whatever flavors they wished and were not yet sated. For these reasons, I did not wish to send you away. If you have sincere resolve, then go without hesitation. I too wish to become a śramaṇa."

The king then rose and went to the Buddha, paid homage, and addressed the Buddha: "Hearing the teaching of wisdom, my heart deeply rejoices. I entrust the kingdom to the crown prince. I take refuge in the Three Treasures and wish to attend at the Buddha's side, receiving the Dharma teaching. I wish to become a śramaṇa and seek the Way, as the Buddha has."

The Buddha then emitted light of manifold colors, whose radiance illuminated the ten directions, and the earth quaked in six ways. The devas made music filling the sky and scattered flowers and precious jewels covering the great trichiliocosm. All the hills and ruins of the world became level. The great mountains transformed into gold. Withered trees grew again. Those who were not beautiful all had their wishes fulfilled. Withered trees all grew branches and leaves. A natural wind blew, and all sang in praise of the Buddha's merit. Trees in bloom spontaneously dropped their blossoms, and the fragrance of each reached the Buddha's place. They hovered in the sky and each spoke verses praising the Buddha's merit:

> "Now, by the Sage's divine power,
> We are rescued from death and live again.
> Our light and color return to their natural state,
> And the substance of the Way is truly restored.
> Birth, death, and the confused colors of desire
> Are like a withered tree.
> Encountering the Buddha's spoken sound,
> We return and merge with the origin.
> The six colors sink in the darkness of attachment;
> The four elements combine with the five desires.
> Distinguish and analyze: the Dharma is empty and so.
> Understanding the Way, one accomplishes the Dharma-practice.
> Attaining the truly settled wisdom,
> Happy, peaceful, and serene.
> All rejoice,
> And prostrate at the Buddha's feet."

At that time, all the six-power sages and arhats saw the flowers speak thus. The girl Lìxíng then transformed into a man, and further into a bodhisattva. A single girl made these two transformations, and all in the assembly rejoiced. At that time, in the entire buddha-field there were no women. Among the six-power practitioners, one hundred and five thousand, and thirty thousand stream-enterers, all attained the stage of non-retrogression. Eighty-six thousand humans and non-returners all attained the patient endurance of the compliant Dharma. Incalculable arhats further generated the aspiration for unsurpassed, true enlightenment, and those who had been practicing as pratyekabuddhas became like Maitreya and his kind.

The king then entrusted the kingdom to the crown prince. The prince, named Eloquent Accumulation, bowed and knelt and addressed the great king: "Does the king wish to give to his descendants the calamity of form-bodies, or the blessings of Dharma-bodies? Will you pass on to your descendants the punishment of enemies and the great crime of killing? The governance of a great kingdom, generation after generation, sinks and is lost. People's faculties do not know great wisdom; the path that extinguishes goodness is given to the descendants. What shall we do? The father-king has given his command, and I cannot but obey." He then bowed, took leave of the king, and departed. He returned to the palace, sat and governed the kingdom, and proclaimed: "Anyone who does not go to the Buddha's place to seek the unconditioned Way and perform good deeds of filial piety shall be deemed guilty as one who commits the three treasons."

On the palace hall, the crown prince was diligent, courageous, and wise, broadly opening the great aspiration for the Way. His mind far surpassed the immeasurable as before, and he immediately extinguished the evil of the entire city. The earth then quaked in six ways. The people all said: "May the crown prince soon become a Buddha, and may we, the great assembly, all become his disciples and also become Buddhas."

The Buddha then smiled, and five-colored light emerged, illuminating the buddha-lands of the ten directions. The people all made vows: "May I attain the Way like the Buddha. May food and drink naturally appear before us, as in Trāyastriṃśa Heaven. May the bodhisattvas of this land all be as in Amitābha's land."

The crown prince Eloquent Accumulation gained merit and stayed in the palace for seven days, then went to the Buddha's place, accompanied by the great assembly of ministers and people great and small, in immeasurable numbers. Arriving at the Buddha's place, he paid homage, stood before the Buddha, and spoke billions of verses of praise:

> "The Buddha's samādhi resolution,
> With merit lofty and imposing,
> Light, marks, and awesome divine illumination,
> Moves the great trichiliocosm.
> Bestowing upon people the supreme wisdom,
> Virtue universally entering the minds of all,
> Inspiring and opening the innocent,
> None fail to receive the fulfillment of blessings.
> Chanting and expounding the Lotus-Flower way,
> Transforming the world and cleansing the multitude,
> Subduing the fierce and obstinate,
> Who are changed and receive gentle compliance.
> Equally tracing the bodhimaṇḍa's light,
> Worldly people receive the enterprise for long.
> Leaving aging, reaching emptiness and quiescence,
> Not dying yet born again.
> Removing illness, far from filth and shame,
> The toil of sinking in the perception of form.
> Hindrances and coverings are thereby removed;
> Pure and clean, without the dust of desire.
> Contemplation and thought, receiving the samādhi of desire,
> Spreading and returning to the unconditioned.
> Emptiness and samādhi, always quiescent,
> Neither abiding nor active.
> Without practice, no discriminating consciousness;
> Manifesting light and marks, accomplished.
> The original Dharma neither increases nor decreases;
> Constantly peaceful, quiescent, empty upon empty.
> The Tathāgata manifests divine transformations,
> And all attain the true Way.
> Ignorance is quiescently settled;
> The mind becomes one with the unborn.
> Today I hear the Dharma taught,
> Drawing forth the Lotus-Flower wisdom.
> My mind understands and makes a vow;
> May all attain what the Buddha has attained.
> Now I wish for city and land alike;
> I pray, please teach the Lotus-Flower.
> By what practice is it attained?
> How many things must be understood?
> Swiftly attaining this Lotus-Flower,
> Relying on its meaning, teach its wisdom.
> May all be caused to know with discrimination,
> And all minds attain understanding.
> Thoroughly comprehending all Dharma matters,
> One should attain them from within and without.
> Is the attainment near or far?
> Can one immediately attain Buddhahood?
> Or must one further pass through kalpas,
> Long receiving it from how many Buddhas?
> Understanding wisdom and its essentials,
> In a moment, transformation is accomplished.
> I pray, explain it all in full,
> And let the great assembly know."
> The crown prince and the assembly
> Paid homage and returned to their seats.

The Buddha said to the girl Lìxíng: "If you wish to know the opening and understanding revealed by the Lotus-Flower, then know that the Buddhas of the past, future, and present all attained the Way and knew the truth through this very chapter on dispersing the body through analogies." At that time, there were hundreds of billions of devas, Brahmā kings, innumerable humans, and four hundred and eighty thousand bodhisattvas who all attained the birthless state. Those who were śrāvakas all further generated the aspiration.

Then the bodhisattva No-Thought addressed the Buddha: "Today the great assembly has heard the explanation of the essentials of the Lotus-Flower Samādhi, but none have received a prediction."

The Buddha told the bodhisattva: "All who have been taught the Lotus-Flower Samādhi today have already been given a prediction. They each have their kalpas and each has their own land and place; therefore I need not speak of it further. If you do not understand, go and ask them yourselves."

Then the crown prince and the girl Lìxíng addressed the bodhisattva about what he wished to ask, and each spoke a verse in reply:

> "O worthy one of supreme wisdom,
> Why do you not understand the essentials?
> Those who seek a thought-based reward
> Are all those who do not receive wisdom.
> If one speaks of having a place,
> Then it is not the Lotus-Flower's way.
> If it requires words to speak,
> And you wish to hear, say so loudly.
> Not understanding origin and end,
> Speech also is equally unknown.
> Pointing out the path and method of the Way
> Is still like being blind.
> The lion's great roar
> Is still called a small sound.
> Having received one's prediction and attained Buddhahood,
> One does not know to revere the true Way.
> Receiving a prediction with a land
> Is also like an illusion or transformation.
> When a counterpart appears, a response emerges;
> There is no further premeditated thought.
> Seeing, following, abiding, and teaching,
> Pervading past, future, and present.
> Spiritual powers know everything;
> There is no need for words.
> Receiving a prediction abides in emptiness,
> Quiescent and peaceful in the unconditioned.
> Constantly settled, without movement or turning;
> Subtle, and people do not know.
> Opening and expounding the not-yet-manifest,
> Quiescent and still, without any action:
> This is the joyful land,
> Pure, as witness and verification.
> Not thinking of having conceptual thought:
> This is the happiness and peace of the Dharma.
> The excellent marks manifesting light:
> This is the appearance of diligent effort.
> Having disciples and people:
> This is entering the turbidity of desire.
> Without suffering, without diligent thought,
> All is entirely exhausted, without arising or ceasing."

Thus they spoke one hundred and eighty billion verses in reply. The bodhisattva then rejoiced and spoke a verse of praise:

> "I was born in ignorance,
> Not understanding the words of the eminent ones.
> Not knowing their Dharma-sound,
> I wrongly said they had not received a prediction.
> What has now been spoken in verse
> Is profoundly deep and hard to fully express.
> I wish to arouse the original mind
> And together with the divine participate in discussion."

Then the crown prince addressed the Buddha: "Now all these worthy ones still do not understand the essentials. Please use skillful means to open their understanding."

The Buddha then smiled, and five-colored light issued from his heart and mouth, illuminating the ten directions. Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "The Buddha does not smile without reason. His smile must have a purpose. I wish to hear the explanation."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Do you see the great assembly?"

Ānanda answered: "I see them."

"Now all the Tathāgata's questions have been fully answered. Each of them, in the ten directions, teaches, transforms, and delivers devas and humans as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, causing all to become Buddhas." At that time, fragrant flowers and seven treasures spontaneously rained down, covering the great trichiliocosm, filling every place without remainder.

The Buddha said to the crown prince: "This is what the Lotus-Flower reveals about the nature of the human body." At that time, innumerable hundreds of thousands of humans as numerous as the sands of the Ganges all generated the aspiration for unsurpassed, true enlightenment and attained the birthless mind. Innumerable thousands of śrāvakas all attained the stage of non-retrogression.

Ānanda knelt and addressed the Buddha: "What is the name of this sūtra? How should it be practiced?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "It is called the 'Essential Collection of the Lotus-Flower Samādhi, the Questions of the Girl Lìxíng, Explaining the Human Body and Dispersing the Emotions.' If any man or woman copies, chants, and recites it, this surpasses practicing generosity for eighty kalpas. If one makes offerings and pays reverence to it, this surpasses a bodhisattva's practice of compassion for three thousand billion kalpas. If one clearly understands it and transmits the teaching to others, this surpasses making offerings to Buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. If anyone hears this sūtra even once, they shall never again undergo the toil of birth and death. Those who do not believe and slander it have followed the frothy current and have not yet returned to the origin."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "I entrust to you the matters of the Lotus-Flower Samādhi. Even in a thousand kalpas, they could not be exhausted. I have roughly spoken the essential truths. Receive, copy, uphold, and practice them. Do not omit even a single character; copy the sentences and punctuation correctly."

The great assembly that had accompanied the crown prince all attained understanding, each obtaining the Way's wisdom like the foremost. They rose, paid homage to the Buddha, and departed.
