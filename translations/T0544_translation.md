# 辯意長者子經
## Sūtra of the Elder's Son Biànyì

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0544

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Fǎchǎng (法場)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the state of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove at Anāthapiṇḍada's Park, together with 1,250 śramaṇas and ten thousand bodhisattvas. At that time, the World-Honored One was teaching the Dharma, surrounded by a vast, boundless assembly.

In the city of Śrāvastī there was a great elder's son named Biànyì. Together with five hundred other elders' sons, each accompanied by five hundred attendants, he came to the Buddha's abode. Coming forward, he touched his head to the ground and paid homage to the Buddha, then sat to one side. At that time, the elder's son Biànyì observed that the assembly was settled. Empowered by the Buddha's majesty, he rose from his seat, arranged his robes, and came solemnly before the Buddha. He paid homage, knelt with palms joined, and addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask a question. May the World-Honored One, out of compassion, explain and expound it. The World-Honored One, the True One of unsurpassed virtue in the three realms, with divine spiritual transformations rescues and ferries across all living beings, universally setting forth skillful teachings so that all may find their place. In ages to come, the five defilements will boil over and the three poisons will blaze fiercely. Beings will burn each other with these, regardless of rank or station, directing poisonous thoughts at one another. When ministers covet the king's throne and raise armies to wage war, they die and their names are obliterated. At such times, disaster befalls the common people. Even among the fourfold assembly of the Buddha's disciples, those who have received the grace of the Buddha's legacy and obtained the name of practitioners of the Way, outwardly wearing dharma robes but inwardly harboring jealousy, showing no reverence or compliance, slandering one another, proclaiming others' faults while concealing their virtues, arrogant and critical: all these people are destined for hell, the realm of hungry ghosts, and the animal realm. They enjoy a moment's glory without knowing the calamity of future kalpas. By what teaching can they be guided and transformed? May the World-Honored One fully demonstrate these teachings, so that people of the future may receive this blessing and be freed from the three evil destinies, dwelling forever in the hall of blessings."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Elder's son Biànyì, you make the lion's roar before the Buddha, raising these matters to enlighten and transform all beings. How wonderful that the foolish and dark, the fierce and evil people of the future shall receive the benefit of this teaching! Ask whatever you wish without hesitation, and the Tathāgata shall explain it in detail."

The elder's son Biànyì addressed the Buddha: "By what causes and conditions are people born in the heavens? By what causes and conditions are they born among humans? By what causes and conditions are they born in hell? By what causes and conditions are they always born among hungry ghosts? By what causes and conditions are they born among animals? By what causes and conditions are they born to nobility and respected by all? By what causes and conditions are they born as servants, used by others? By what causes and conditions are they born among common people yet with sweet-scented breath and a body and mind always at ease, praised by others and never slandered? By what causes and conditions are they born always slandered, hated, ugly in form, with mind and body uneasy, always dwelling in fear? By what causes and conditions do they always encounter the Buddha wherever they are born, hearing and upholding the Dharma without ever failing, meeting good teachers and attaining a wholesome mind, and if they become śramaṇas, always achieving their aspirations? These are my questions. May the World-Honored One explain them in detail, so that this assembly may hear the correct teaching, and may all beings find crossing to safety."

The Buddha told the elder's son Biànyì: "Listen well! Listen well! Consider this carefully! I shall explain the essential principles for you. There are five practices by which one is born in the heavens. What are the five? First, with a compassionate mind, not killing any living being, nurturing the lives of all creatures, and letting all find peace. Second, being virtuous and good, not stealing others' property, practicing generosity without greed, and relieving the destitute. Third, being chaste and pure, not engaging in sexual misconduct with others, men and women keeping the precepts, fasting and practicing diligently. Fourth, being sincere and truthful, not deceiving others, guarding against the four faults of speech, without greed or deceit. Fifth, not drinking liquor and not letting it pass one's lips. By these five practices one is born in the heavens."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Not killing brings long life,
> Freedom from illness, always healthy and vigorous.
> One receives a heavenly station,
> With a body at peace and radiant.
> Not stealing brings great wealth,
> With money and treasures arising naturally.
> Seven-jeweled palaces
> And a mind always joyful.
> Men and women who refrain from sexual misconduct
> Have bodies fragrant and pure.
> Wherever born they are always handsome,
> Their virtue and conduct naturally bright.
> Not deceiving brings sweet-scented breath,
> Speech always clear and eloquent.
> In discourse there is no stammering;
> Whatever is said, all follow.
> Not letting liquor or meat pass one's lips,
> No confusion or disorder of the mind.
> Wherever one is born,
> Humans and devas always attend.
> When at last one's life ends,
> Twenty-five guardian spirits come to escort,
> And the five blessings arise naturally,
> With radiance glorious and brilliant."

The Buddha told Biànyì: "There are also five practices by which one is born among humans. What are the five? First, giving generously and bringing benefit to the poor. Second, keeping the precepts and not committing the ten evils. Third, patience, not disturbing the minds of others. Fourth, diligence, encouraging the lazy. Fifth, single-mindedness, practicing filial piety and maintaining loyalty. By these five practices one is born among humans, with great wealth, long life, handsome appearance, and authority, becoming a leader whom all respect and serve."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Giving brings great wealth,
> With money arising naturally.
> Wherever born one is always noble,
> Readily receiving the father's inheritance.
> Keeping precepts completely,
> Receiving and practicing the teaching of the Three Honored Ones,
> Wholeheartedly committing no evil,
> One obtains long life.
> Patience, not disturbing others,
> Anger not directed at people,
> Blows and abuse not repaid in kind:
> Wherever born one is always handsome.
> Diligence, not being lazy,
> Always mindful of upholding the practice:
> Wherever born one is powerful,
> Becoming a general to all.
> Single-mindedness without backsliding,
> Loyal and faithful, mindful and reliable,
> Serving one's elders:
> Wherever born there is no hardship.
> If one practices these five things,
> One progressively becomes a human leader,
> With wealth, strength, handsome appearance,
> And a naturally brave and valiant bearing."

The Buddha further told Biànyì: "There are five practices by which one dies and enters hell, remaining there for hundreds of millions of kalpas before emerging. What are the five? First, not believing in the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha, while slandering and disparaging the holy path. Second, destroying Buddhist monasteries and ancestral temples. Third, the fourfold assembly slandering one another, not believing in the consequences of wrongdoing, and showing no respect or compliance. Fourth, rebellion, with no observance of hierarchy, rulers and ministers, parents and children not obeying one another. Fifth, in the future, those who wish to practice the Way, having already obtained ordination, then disobey the teacher's instructions, becoming arrogant, scorning and slandering the teacher. By these five practices one dies and enters hell, passing from hell to hell with no prospect of emergence."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "The foolish and ignorant of this world
> Do not believe in Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha.
> In their foolishness they seek to destroy them,
> Saying the Buddha has no spiritual power.
> Though they see good and evil with their own eyes,
> They deliberately commit various offenses.
> They destroy shrines and temples:
> Small is the gain, but great the guilt.
> The fourfold assembly of the latter age,
> Harboring venom and cherishing jealousy,
> Slander each other for fame and profit,
> Not knowing the gravity of future retribution.
> Ministers and common people of this world,
> Parents and children do evil to each other,
> For wealth, possessions, and fame,
> Showing no reverence or compliance.
> Evil people of the future,
> Having obtained ordination as śramaṇas,
> Do not accept the teacher's instruction:
> The retribution at death is not light.
> Those who practice these five things,
> Their offenses are beyond description.
> For hundreds of millions of kalpas in hell,
> Even the buddhas cannot rescue them.

"Furthermore, elder's son, there are five practices by which one falls into the realm of hungry ghosts. What are the five? First, miserliness and greed, with no desire to give. Second, theft and failure to honor one's parents. Third, foolish ignorance, with no compassionate mind. Fourth, hoarding wealth and refusing to use it for food or clothing. Fifth, not providing for parents, siblings, wife, children, or servants. By these five practices one falls into the realm of hungry ghosts."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Miserly and greedy, refusing to give,
> Stealing and not supporting one's parents,
> Amassing wealth in fear of losing it,
> Showing no compassion to the elderly,
> To wife, children, and servants
> Refusing to provide anything at all:
> One dies clinging to one's possessions
> And the suffering of a hungry ghost is severe.
> The body knows no clothing,
> The belly is huge but the throat thin as a needle.
> Searching east and west for food,
> Molten copper is poured into one's mouth.
> Not wanting to drink it,
> They force the mouth open and compel one to swallow.
> Each mouthful entering the belly
> Rots the liver, lungs, intestines, and stomach.
> Such bitter suffering
> Continues for tens of thousands of years.
> When the offense is exhausted, one finally emerges,
> Born as a poor and lowly person.

"Furthermore, elder's son, there are five practices that constitute animal behavior, leading to birth among animals. What are the five? First, violating the precepts by stealing. Second, borrowing debts and refusing to repay them. Third, killing living beings, to repay the debt with one's own body. Fourth, not being willing to listen to the sūtras and the Dharma. Fifth, always using hardship as an excuse to avoid fasting, giving, and dharma-assemblies, taking worldly concerns as one's pretext. By these five practices one is born among animals."

Thereupon the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Constantly stealing others' possessions,
> Borrowing money and refusing to repay,
> Delighting in killing, hunting, and fishing with nets,
> Making worldly pretexts to avoid dharma-assemblies,
> Without sincerity, knowing nothing of the Way,
> Past, present, and future.
> Committing various offenses unawares,
> Gradually, gradually falling among animals:
> Oxen, horses, elephants, donkeys, camels,
> Pigs, sheep, dogs beyond counting,
> Always bearing heavy burdens, flayed after death.
> Such suffering is difficult to endure.

"Furthermore, elder's son, there are five practices by which one becomes noble and respected by all. What are the five? First, giving generously with broad and universal benefit. Second, revering the Buddha, Dharma, and the Three Treasures, as well as all elders. Third, patience, without any anger. Fourth, gentleness and humility. Fifth, broad learning and recitation of the sūtras and precepts. By these five practices one becomes noble and is respected by all."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Giving always with an impartial mind,
> Bringing universal relief and peace to all,
> Blessed with beauty, strength, long life, and health:
> All one's kindred receive this grace.
> Revering the Three Treasures of the Buddha,
> Serving all elders with respect:
> Wherever born one is noble,
> Always receiving the homage of all.
> Patience, free from anger:
> Wherever born one is always handsome.
> All people, seeing one, rejoice
> And gaze without wearying.
> With a mind well-tempered, gentle and yielding,
> Humble and respectful,
> Studying broadly and reciting the sūtras:
> Such a one is honored among all people.

"Furthermore, elder's son, there are also five practices by which one is always born in lowly station as a servant to others. What are the five? First, arrogance, showing no respect to one's parents. Second, stubbornness, with no deferential mind. Third, heedlessness, not venerating the Three Honored Ones. Fourth, theft as one's livelihood. Fifth, borrowing debts and fleeing without repayment. By these five practices one is always born in lowly station among servants."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "If there are foolish people
> Who are arrogant toward their parents,
> Without a deferential mind,
> They are born in lowly station.
> Not venerating the Three Treasures,
> Stubborn before their elders,
> Without compassion or filial devotion to others,
> They are born as servants.
> Giving free rein to the mind's desires,
> Stealing others' property,
> Borrowing debts and refusing to repay:
> They are born among servants,
> Depending on others for food and clothing,
> Sent running errands with no freedom,
> Repaying their master through labor,
> And when the debt is exhausted, finally emerging.

"Furthermore, elder's son, there are five practices by which one is born among humans with sweet-scented breath and body and mind always at ease, praised by others and never slandered. What are the five? First, sincerity, not deceiving others. Second, reciting the sūtras impartially, without discrimination. Third, guarding one's speech and not slandering the holy path. Fourth, teaching people to turn from evil and pursue the good. Fifth, not seeking out the faults and failings of others. By these five practices one is born among humans with sweet-scented breath and body and mind always at ease, praised by others and never slandered."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Revering the Three Treasures,
> Not being arrogant toward one's parents,
> Sincere, not deceptive:
> Such conduct is respected by all.
> Guarding one's speech, not slandering,
> Treating all with an impartial mind,
> Encouraging people to abandon wrongdoing,
> Reciting and studying the correct Dharma.
> When people of the world are not arrogant,
> Respecting one another as parents,
> Suppressing evil and praising good:
> Thus one quickly attains buddhahood.

"Furthermore, elder's son, there are five practices by which one, living among humans, is always slandered, hated by others, ugly in appearance, with mind and body uneasy, always dwelling in fear. What are the five? First, being habitually insincere and deceiving others. Second, slandering dharma-assemblies and places where the Dharma is taught. Third, looking down on and testing one's fellow students. Fourth, falsely accusing others of things one has not actually seen them do. Fifth, engaging in double-tongued speech to stir up discord. By these five practices, if born among humans, one is always slandered, hated, ugly in appearance, with mind and body uneasy, always dwelling in fear."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Deceiving and deluding the assembly,
> Never being sincere,
> What the mind conceives and the mouth speaks,
> The body receives as heavy retribution.
> If born in hell,
> Iron hooks tear out the tongue,
> Molten copper is poured into the mouth,
> Day and night without rest.
> If born as a human,
> The breath is always rank and foul.
> People, seeing one, feel no pleasure,
> Showing no warmth or cheer.
> One always encounters legal troubles,
> Criticized and discussed by all,
> Beset by every kind of calamity,
> The mind never at peace.
> Dying, one returns to hell,
> Emerging as an animal,
> Passing through the five destinies,
> Never freed from all these sufferings.

"Furthermore, elder's son, there are five practices by which, wherever one is born, one always encounters the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha, never failing in this, and upon seeing the Buddha and hearing the Dharma attains a wholesome mind, and if one becomes a śramaṇa, always achieves one's aspirations. What are the five? First, personally serving the Three Treasures and encouraging others to do likewise. Second, making images of the Buddha, keeping them fresh and clean. Third, always following the teacher's instructions and never violating what one has received. Fourth, universal compassion toward all beings, regarding them as equal to oneself, cherishing them as one cherishes an infant. Fifth, reciting the sūtras and teachings one has received, day and night. By these five practices, wherever one is born, one always encounters the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha, never failing in this, and upon seeing the Buddha and hearing the Dharma attains a wholesome mind, and if one becomes a śramaṇa, always achieves one's aspirations."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Serving and revering the Three Treasures,
> Teaching and encouraging others to do the same,
> Making beautiful images of the Buddha,
> Following all one's teachers' instructions:
> One should regard all people
> As no different from oneself,
> Self and other equally balanced.
> Through such practice one meets the Buddha.
> Day and night always studying and learning;
> Wisdom is the great treasure.
> It opens the eyes of the blind and dark,
> Making all know the truth of the Way."

Thereupon the elder's son Biànyì, having heard the Buddha explain the essential teaching of these fifty matters, was delighted and joyful and attained patient acceptance of the Dharma. The five hundred elders' sons all attained the purity of the dharma-eye. All others in the assembly each attained their respective goals. Thereupon Biànyì rose from his seat, paid homage to the Buddha, knelt with palms joined, and addressed the Buddha: "Excellent, World-Honored One! How wonderfully you have taught this Dharma, allowing the assembly to hear what was needed and ensuring that people of the future will be ferried across their afflictions. May the World-Honored One, together with the entire assembly, condescend to come to my humble home tomorrow at midday and accept a meal." At that time, the World-Honored One silently consented. The elders' sons paid homage to the Buddha and departed with joy.

When Biànyì arrived home, he told his parents: "The one I have invited is rare among all people. His name is the Tathāgata, the unsurpassed teacher of the Dharma, without equal in the three realms." He then informed his wife, who prepared the feast, and provisions were soon made ready.

The next day, the World-Honored One came with the great assembly to the household and took his seat with dignity. The elder's son Biànyì, together with his parents and relatives, bowed at the Buddha's feet and each attended to the service. Biànyì rose to offer water for hand-washing and reverently served the food. Before the meal was finished, a beggar came and went from seat to seat asking for food. Since the Buddha had not yet given the blessing, no one dared give anything. Having received nothing at all, the beggar left in anger and gave rise to evil thoughts: "These śramaṇas are heedless and foolish. What sort of 'Way' do they have? A poor person begs from them and they have no mind to give. The elder is deluded, wasting food on these pitiless ones. If I were king, I would crush their heads with iron-rimmed carts!" Having said this, he departed.

After the Buddha had given the blessing, another beggar came in to beg. Everyone in the assembly gave to him, and he received a great deal of food. He departed happily, thinking: "These śramaṇas all have compassionate hearts. They took pity on my poverty and cold and gave me food to fill my belly, enough to sustain me for several days. How excellent that this elder is able to serve such great worthies! His merit is immeasurable. If I were king, I would make offerings to the Buddha and the entire assembly of disciples for a full seven days, to repay today's kindness of relieving my hunger and thirst." Having said this, he departed. When the Buddha had finished eating, he taught the Dharma and then returned to the monastery. The Buddha told Ānanda: "From this day forward, the blessing should always be given before the food is served; let this be the rule."

The two beggars wandered from place to place, begging their way to another kingdom. They lay down to sleep in deep grass by the roadside. At that time, the king of that country suddenly died without an heir. The royal minister and court astrologer, skilled in physiognomy, consulted the prophetic records, which said: "A person of humble station shall become king." The ministers and officials, with a thousand chariots and ten thousand riders, toured the borders of the kingdom to find who should be king. They noticed that above the deep grass by the road there was a cloud canopy. The astrologer pointed and said: "There is a person of divine destiny within!" They found the beggar, whose physiognomy marked him as one who should be king. The ministers bowed in homage, each declaring allegiance.

The beggar was startled and amazed, declaring himself lowborn and not of royal lineage. They all said his destiny matched the signs; this was not by force. He was bathed in fragrant water and dressed in royal garments. His radiant bearing was praised by all as excellent beyond measure. Escorted before and behind, the chariot turned to enter the capital. The beggar with the evil thoughts, asleep in the deep grass and unaware, was crushed beneath the chariot wheels and his head was severed. When the king arrived in the capital, the weather was harmonious and all four seasons flourished. The people lived in peace and praised the king's virtue.

At that time the new king reflected: "I was once a beggar. By what causes and conditions have I become king? When I was begging, I received the grace of the Buddha's assembly and was given a great quantity of food. I gave rise to the wholesome thought: 'If I become king, I shall make offerings for seven days.' The Buddha's grace has now borne fruit." He summoned his ministers, and facing in the direction of the state of Śrāvastī, burned incense and paid homage from afar. He dispatched an envoy to invite the Buddha, saying: "By the grace of the World-Honored One, I have become king. I beg the Honored One to condescend to come and transform the foolish and dark people of this kingdom, that they may receive your teaching." The Buddha then told his disciples: "We should accept this invitation."

The Buddha, together with his disciples and a boundless assembly, traveled to that kingdom. The king came out to greet them, and together with his ministers he touched his head to the Buddha's feet, burning incense, scattering flowers, and making offerings of music. The Buddha entered the palace and took his seat. The king rose to offer water and prepared the meal, which was quickly finished. The king then paid homage to the Buddha and addressed him: "I was originally a person of low station. By what meritorious practice did I come to enjoy this position? I beg the Buddha to explain, so that the people of this kingdom may have their eyes opened."

The Buddha told the king: "In the past, in the city of Śrāvastī, there was an elder's son named Biànyì who prepared a great feast and invited the Buddha and the Sangha. When the Buddha was seated and the food had been served but the blessing not yet given, a beggar came in wanting to beg, but received nothing. He left in anger and gave rise to an evil thought: 'If I were king, I would crush the heads of these monks with iron-rimmed carts!' Another person came later and begged, receiving a great quantity of food. As he left he thought: 'If I were king, I would make offerings to this assembly of holy monks for seven days.' The one who had the wholesome thought: that is you, the present king. The one who had the evil thought: lying in the deep grass as the king received his rightful position and the chariot turned to enter the capital, the chariot and its escorts crushed his head. After death he entered hell, where fire-carts crush him for hundreds of millions of kalpas before he will emerge. Your Majesty, by inviting the Buddha you have repaid your vow with generous abundance. Life after life you will receive blessings without end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "The human mind is a poisonous root,
> The mouth is a gate of calamity.
> What the mind conceives and the mouth speaks,
> The body receives as retribution.
> The one who harbored evil thoughts toward others
> Brought suffering upon himself.
> He wished to harm another,
> Yet unawares the cart crushed his own head.
> The mind is the nectar of the Dharma,
> Enabling one to be born in heaven.
> What the mind conceives and the mouth speaks,
> The body receives the resulting blessings.
> The one who harbored wholesome thoughts toward others
> Made his own body secure.
> With the mind set on all that is good,
> Like the king, he attained the heavenly throne."

At that time the king, hearing the sūtra, was filled with joy. The entire kingdom, ministers and people alike, attained the fruit of stream-entry. After making offerings to the Buddha for seven days, as the Buddha was about to depart, the king and his ministers and people paid homage to the Buddha and took their leave. The World-Honored One then returned to the Jeta Grove in Śrāvastī. The worthy Ānanda arranged his robes, rose from his seat, paid homage to the Buddha, knelt, and addressed the Buddha: "By what name should this sūtra be known, and how should it be practiced?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Questions of the Elder's Son Biànyì,' and should be upheld accordingly. It is also called 'The Essential Meaning of All Dharmas.'"

The Buddha further told Ānanda: "If there are good men and good women who practice this sūtra, who uphold, recite, and propagate it to later generations, causing others to receive and uphold it, such people are as if attending upon the Buddha himself, and their merit is no different. Those who recite this sūtra shall receive a prediction from Maitreya Buddha. What the Tathāgata speaks with his broad, long tongue is without any discrepancy."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, the devas, nāgas, spirits, and the fourfold assembly of disciples, hearing the sūtra, were filled with joy and paid homage to the Buddha.
