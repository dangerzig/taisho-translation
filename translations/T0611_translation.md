# 法觀經
## Sūtra of Contemplation of the Dharma

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0611

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

The Buddha said: "First, why does one count the breaths? Because the breath is light and easy to know. Since worldly people are all attached to the body, they are unable to relinquish the body and guard the mind. Moreover, the affairs within the body are difficult to distinguish, and all who do not trust the original emptiness cannot stop the mind. Why, when there is existence, does one speak of emptying the mind? Because through inverted habitual patterns of breathing one sees existence and non-existence; therefore one first teaches about the breath. Gradually one opens a person's understanding; at the higher stage one counts, and having attained practice, this constitutes the first dhyāna."

The Buddha said: "When sitting in meditation, one should establish three stabilities. What are the three stabilities? The first is stability of the body; the second is stability of the mouth; the third is stability of the mind. When sensations cease, this is stability of the body; when speech ceases, this is stability of the mouth; when mental activity ceases, this is stability of the mind. Cessation of mental activity means receiving the practice and constantly being mindful of the Way. Cessation of speech means severing the four evils. Cessation of sensation means not falling into craving. When the mind has reached cessation, and body, mouth, and mind are stable, one should establish and maintain the precepts with body and mind. To maintain means to be without any transgression whatsoever; further, maintaining body and mind is called cultivation. Cultivation means maintaining the mind and practicing the thirty-seven factors of the path." The sūtra says: "What one does not recognize and what one cannot stop refers to not recognizing within the breath that the mind has gone, and being unable to stop the mind from going. In such a case, one should be diligent, exert oneself, and guard carefully: sit upright, fold the hands, lower the head, hold the mind, and place it within the heart, letting it settle into arising and ceasing of itself. One should recognize when the mind goes; having recognized this, one will be able to stop it, and then one will not fall into the hindrances. The hindrances lie in distraction, doubt, and listening to the six sense-faculties; to be like this is not acceptable." The Buddha said: "When counting the breaths, the mind and the present breath do not count each other alternately; rather, the conceptual mind constitutes the alternating practice."

The Buddha said: "Having established the three stabilities, keeping the precepts in accord with the Vinaya, this is the method of the Way, and one delights in practicing the Way." Therefore the sūtra says: "One who delights in the Way's dharma practices the Way, having sat down to practice the Way. In the first watch and the last watch of the night, one rouses the mind and guards eating; the time from mid-morning through the afternoon to evening constitutes the four guards. One should be diligent and not depart from them; this is called vigorous effort. Midnight, sunrise, midday, and the late afternoon constitute the four rectifications. One reads the sūtras, practices walking meditation, and circumambulates the stūpa both inside and out. Observe your own body: look inward at the five cessations, and from without, from head to foot and from foot to head, examine each part one by one. What is there? All will rot and decay, joint by joint coming apart. Originally there was nothing that came and created this; when it departs, it too is extinguished, with nothing remaining. Turn the mind back and reflect again, using the counting of the mind. If the mind still does not understand, then look at a dead person and contemplate carefully from head to foot. Whether sitting, standing, or eating, constantly keep this in mind, using it to strengthen the mind; this is called the counting of recollection. When the breath goes out and comes in, recollect the moment of cessation. Having already awakened to the moment when the breath ceases entirely and there is nothing at all, compare and contemplate, and know that all people and things will likewise come to an end. When the mind has stopped and become settled, one then knows emptiness." Therefore the sūtra says: "The first is vigorous effort; the second is the counting of recollection; the third is contemplation."

The Buddha said: "In observing the body, sometimes one should observe another person's body. One should observe one's own body for the purpose of investigation. One should observe another's body because when observing one's own body the mind becomes attached; then one should observe another person's body in death and decay. Sometimes it is permissible to observe one's own body, and also permissible to observe another's body. It is permissible to observe one's own body when observing it without attachment. It is permissible to observe another's body when observing it also without attachment. Sometimes it is not permissible to observe one's own body, and also not permissible to observe another's body. It is not permissible to observe one's own body when one sees one's own body as plump, fair, and beautiful. It is not permissible to observe another's body when one likewise sees another's body as plump, fair, beautiful, elegant, with glossy eyebrows and rosy lips. When seeing plumpness, one should contemplate bloating. When seeing fairness, one should contemplate the bones of a dead person. When seeing glossy eyebrows, one should contemplate the color of a dead person about to decay, turning blue-black. When seeing rosy lips, one should contemplate blood: all will decay and rot. What is there to crave?

"In thus observing the body, the thirty-two things within it are to be reckoned: hair of the head, hair of the body, nails, teeth, bones, skin, flesh, and the five organs, making eleven things belonging to earth; tears, nasal mucus, saliva, pus, blood, fat, marrow, sweat, and urine, making seven things belonging to water; warmth, heat, and the power that digests food, making two things belonging to fire; and twelve things belonging to wind. These thirty-two things all arise from earth, water, fire, and wind. What is earth? A person is born from the vital essence of grain; grain is the earth, the mind is the seed, and vital essence is the rain-water; together they combine to produce the body. One seeks clothing and food to nourish the vital force and protect the master. The human body is fundamentally without substance; therefore it is extinguished, impermanent, and finished. When one attains the Way, one then knows that the body is not truly a body. Recollecting that the body will not last long and will certainly die and decay, and that the mind is the seed of human existence, one therefore guards the mind and keeps it singleminded. The fool does not guard the mind, does not protect the spirit, but merely nourishes the four limbs and is deceived by form and flavor. Thinking the body is the self, one does not know that all evil arises from the body. Eating greedily for the taste, one falls into suffering, going back and forth in birth and death without escape, suddenly encountering evil retribution, the spirit departing empty-handed to the paths of good or evil, while the body falls dead to the ground, rotting day and night. Originally there was nothing at all; it was only the action of the mind that transformed into a body. At death, all returns to earth; the ten thousand things are likewise. All will pass away; this is impermanence.

"People do not reckon with themselves. Their myriad thoughts never converge on the one essential point. For this reason there is suffering, and at death the body is cast aside and abandoned. The ten thousand things are likewise. When this cessation has already given way to another birth, and another birth again to suffering, one creates wholesome and evil actions as seeds, not knowing where one is headed. This is the absence of a true self. A practitioner of the Way should sever this. One who does not know the four truths of impermanence will never attain the Way. Having reckoned with one's own body, and having observed all things in their death and decay, knowing that people and things are all without substance, the mind then settles in stillness, and one attains practice and joy. Having attained practice, the mind is at peace. If one does not depart from the five, the mind becomes unified; this is the Way."

The Buddha said: "In being mindful of the body, contemplate the hair of the head and the brain. Reflect that the hair originally came from nothing; it came into being through transformation and will all rot and fall away. The brain is like congealed rice gruel and will all become putrid. The eyes are merely pits of fluid, and all will drain out into empty hollows. The ears are merely flesh and filth, all perforated and leaking. The nasal mucus and saliva of the nose and mouth all flow out and are discarded, dissolving and decaying. The tongue, throat, lungs, liver, and heart: the heart contains foul blood. The liver, gallbladder, diaphragm, and spleen are attached to the stomach. The kidneys are attached to the spine. In the stomach is undigested food. In the large intestine are feces. In the small intestine and the bladder is urine. The bowels expand and contract with increase and decrease. When the body dies and the breath is exhausted, all will bloat, swell, rot, and decompose. The intestines, stomach, feces, and urine splash and flow upon each other, a stinking and loathsome place. Below are the flesh of the buttocks, the two shins, and the two feet; gradually the muscle and flesh are consumed, the sinews and veins decay, and the bones come apart joint by joint. The shin-bones stand stiffly straight; the white thigh-bones are like the spokes of a wheel. The hip-bone is connected to the spine; the spine is connected to the ribs; the shoulder-bones are connected to the elbows, arms, and hands. The skin and hide decompose, and the joints come apart one by one. The neck-bone is connected to the skull. The flesh and blood are consumed, and gradually there are only bones. When the breath goes out and does not return, this is a dead person. The body lies stiff and straight and no longer moves. The wind departs and the body grows cold. The fire departs and yellow fluid flows from the nine openings. The water departs and the dead person no longer eats. The earth departs and after three or four days the color turns blue-green. Pus and blood flow from the mouth, nose, ears, and eyes, bright red. The flesh and muscle decay and the bones turn bright white. In time they turn dark and become ash and earth. Look at the foul dead people outside the city walls. The dead person's bones are like this, and your own body is likewise; all will be extinguished and finished. This is emptiness. At the moment when the outgoing breath is exhausted, one then knows emptiness; knowing emptiness, one then knows that the body is empty. Why? Because one knows that life is as close as the emptiness between breaths."

The Buddha said: "With this understanding, one should first observe and contemplate, extinguish mental activity, and await one's own mind, then guard the mind. When the mind does not leave the body, one is a practitioner of the Way; when it dwells on external things, one is involved with the ten thousand things. When mindfulness is directed inward, this is contemplation. When one recognizes that desire is about to extinguish mental activity, one should contemplate that all things are impermanent and decaying, that none of them are mine, and that I too am not the master of things. When the mind contemplates death, what does one take along? One takes along what is wholesome. One takes along the one essential thing. One must take along the sūtras, because of the merit of repeated practice and delight." The Buddha said: "These are your possessions to take with you; all the rest is not mine. The mind should recognize and recollect: that all whom one loves must eventually be separated, each rotting and decaying of their own accord; that thinking of them only confuses the mind and plunges one into wrongdoing; that one must return to the body and guard purity, and proceed toward the path of nirvāṇa.

"The Buddha proceeded from the one mind through to the nine contemplations, contemplating that the four colors all decay and are destroyed. When a person has been dead four or five days and is about to rot and decay, the color turns blue-green. After five or six days, pus and blood flow from the mouth, nose, ears, and eyes, bright red. Afterward the flesh and muscle decay, the intestines and stomach breed worms that feed upon the body in turn, the skin and hide decompose, and the bone-color turns bright white; in time it turns dark and becomes ash and earth. Understand clearly that earth, water, fire, and wind are empty, and none of them are mine. From beginningless time you have also been the wife, child, servant, or slave of others, and have also been an animal: a cow, horse, worm, or elephant, enduring toil and heavy burdens, and also being slaughtered, skinned, minced, and roasted by others. Now, as a human, you again serve as another's spouse, child, servant, or slave; you also take animals and slaughter, skin, mince, stab, and chop them. After your own body dies, you will certainly receive all of this in return. Practitioner of the Way! Have you ever seen a dead person? When the breath ceases, there is no more awareness. The body lies stiff and straight, then rots and becomes loathsome. Contemplate carefully and you will feel fear and not wish to look. Why then do you not fear the singleminded practice? The singleminded practice causes a person to ascend to heaven and attain the path of nirvāṇa. The Buddha knew that the nine contemplations are all empty and without substance; therefore he returned to the one mind. Practitioner of the Way! Swiftly extinguish mental activity. When you await without any other awareness, you then arrive at the land of the ultimate. Those who dwell in the ultimate are everywhere; they see by means of non-attachment, and therefore they are unseen."

The Buddha said: "When the mind is about to crave, contemplate impermanence and decay. When lust arises, contemplate the opposite. When anger arises, contemplate equanimity. When delusion arises, contemplate the root. All conditioned activities are impermanent; the unconditioned is peace and safety. One who does not know impermanence will never let go of craving, nor will one leave the path of the pretas. All that exists in the world is like a dream. In a dream one eats food and sees fine things, but upon waking they are nowhere to be seen. All that exists in the world is just like this: what is born dies, what is completed decays; all must ultimately return to emptiness. What then is there to crave? A person who has a spouse, children, and property is the same. Why? When a person makes a living, earns money, and profits, and the family gathers together in joy and happiness, it is like a flock of birds congregating: all is impermanent. One day they separate and are seen no more. Even if one has worry and the fear of ten thousand sufferings, while the mind remains in birth and death one accumulates wrongdoing daily. The wise person restrains himself and has few desires, seeking only clothing and food sufficient for the purpose, acting from settled intention, not seeking a fixed abode, always returning to the body to guard purity, and severing seeking by contemplating emptiness."

It was asked: "What is the root from which guarding the mind in the practice of the Way arises?"

The Buddha said: "After heaven and earth were formed, human beings descended from the fifteenth heaven. Their lifespans had no untimely end. Birth, death, and the five destinies arise from the six deteriorations. When people are born, their minds are originally wholesome, without craving, love, sensation, perception, birth, death, or consciousness. But they are deceived by the ears, eyes, nose, and mouth. The eyes' light looks at forms; the ears hear sounds; the nose knows scents; the mouth knows flavors; the body knows cold, warmth, coarseness, and fineness; and the mind creates the ten activities, forming the five aggregates. The mind, being consciousness combined, constitutes the six deteriorations. From these one creates wholesome and evil actions as seeds, and from this come old age, illness, and death, and birth in the five destinies. One who seeks the Way wishes to sever birth and death; therefore one guards the mind and stops the eyes from pursuing form, stops the ears from pursuing sound, stops the nose from pursuing scent, stops the mouth from pursuing flavor, stops the body from pursuing contact. Severing the six deteriorations, one practices contemplation, cherishes the mind in recollection, and sits in meditation to extinguish consciousness. For one who has attained the Way, the five aggregates are all extinguished. Knowing the original emptiness, one then contemplates the conception of emptiness and the path of emptiness leading toward nirvāṇa. The reason one guards the mind is that the mind is the master of consciousness. Because of wrongful actions, the six deteriorations become the calamitous practice, planting the roots of the five destinies. The practitioner concentrates carefully and of one's own accord guards the four aspects of the mind, wishing to stop wrong and evil, recognizing that consciousness, perception, and conceptual thought run about. What the practitioner wishes is to extinguish conceptual activity, consciousness, perception, and thought, by means of 'all conditioned activities are impermanent,' and to sever the ten activities of the body. Body, speech, and mind, these three, arise from the five aggregates and the six deteriorations and then stop. The three stabilities are: when the mouth is without any awareness, this is stability of the mouth; when the body is without any awareness, this is stability of the body; when the mind has nothing further to think about, this is stability of the mind."

The Buddha said: "The Way has four essentials; only by passing through these gateways can one gain release. What are the four? The first is to recognize suffering: no longer turning toward the ten thousand things, this is gaining release from the gateway of the three evil destinies. The second is to know that the body is not a true body, thereby dismantling the body and no longer loving it; this is the gateway of release from the human realm up to the sixth heaven. The third is to know impermanence and no longer turn the mind toward it; this is gaining release from the gateway of the eighteen heavens. The fourth is to know emptiness and extinguish emptiness; this is gaining release from the gateway of the twenty-eight heavens. Only when emptiness is extinguished does one truly arrive at the Way." The sūtra says: "The practitioner of the Way who awakens gains release; this means awakening to suffering, emptiness, the absence of self, and impermanence. To gain release means to pass through the four essential gateways and attain the first dhyāna. In the seven heavens above, there are no bodily forms. Why? Because the practitioner of the Way has dismantled the body."
