# Two Gates of Resolving Doubts and Practicing Vows for Rebirth in the Pure Land

## *Wǎngshēng Jìngtǔ Juéyí Xíngyuàn Èr Mén* (往生淨土決疑行願二門)

Taishō 1968

Composed by Zūnshì (*Zūnshì*, 遵式) of the Song dynasty.

*Note: This is a Pure Land devotional manual by the Tiāntái master Zūnshì (964-1032 CE), also known as Cíyún (*Cíyún*, 慈雲), one of the most important figures in the Song revival of Tiāntái Buddhism and a major proponent of combining Tiāntái doctrinal study with Pure Land devotion. The text is divided into two gates: (1) the "Gate of Resolving Doubts" (juéyí mén), which addresses three types of doubt that obstruct faith in Pure Land rebirth (doubt about the teacher, doubt about the teaching, and doubt about oneself), drawing on the Tiāntái classification of Buddhist teachings to establish that Pure Land doctrine belongs to the most profound level, the "perfect teaching" (yuánjiào); and (2) the "Gate of Practicing Vows" (xíngyuàn mén), which provides four concrete methods of practice: a prostration and repentance liturgy, the ten-recitation method of calling Amitābha's name, the practice of constant mindfulness of Buddha in daily life, and the accumulation of miscellaneous merits. The text is an important document in the synthesis of Tiāntái philosophy and Pure Land practice that characterized Song-dynasty Chinese Buddhism.*

## Preface

The jeweled realm of Sukhāvatī (*Ānyang*, 安養), praised by the Great Awakened One: those of the three grades ascend to its heights. In the evening one conceives within the golden lotus; the constellations of heaven pale beside a drop in its ocean. At dawn one wanders through its jade pools; the world's lamps can scarcely be compared to the sands of its rivers. Truly, beings come from all ten directions, and the four kinds of birth all benefit from going there. Although its radiance illuminates universally, in the end one must bow one's head and earnestly seek it.

In ancient India and the imperial realm of China, from the present looking back to antiquity, the great worthies and supreme sages of that land all composed writings as their pledge; the awakened masters and vast scholars of this land all wielded their brushes to compose vows. The dedications of merit have been continuous, echoing one after another without end. This is truly the universal thoroughfare of the Way and virtue, the direct crossing to eternal bliss.

Yet many in the world, newly encountering [this teaching], do not yet know its proper boundaries. When suddenly asked about the ford, they feel no shame at their ignorance. Some thrust out their arms and dismiss it as a "lesser teaching"; others laugh loudly and denounce it as a "provisional vehicle." Because such words contradict the sūtras, people become confused about the established canon. ... Therefore I have composed the *Two Gates of Resolving Doubts and Practicing Vows for Rebirth in the Pure Land*.

## Gate One: Resolving Doubts

Doubt is the obstacle to faith. Even small worldly good deeds cannot be accomplished [with doubt]; how much less the great Way of bodhi?

[Someone says:] "The Tiāntái Master Zhìzhě already wrote the *Treatise Resolving Ten Doubts*. Why is this text needed?" There are briefly three reasons: (1) The former work was composed for kings, ministers, and officials who have faith and delight but are occupied with government and have little leisure for lengthy texts. Here I present the main outline and briefly indicate the forms of practice, for easy reading and easy practice. (2) The former treatise mostly resolved difficulties according to specific matters; only the second and third doubts briefly drew on principle. Since matters are endless and doubts inexhaustible, I now directly illuminate a single principle as the source of all dharmas. Point to the source, and the streams can be recognized. (3) This text directly addresses those who contradict the sūtras and oppose principle, harming both themselves and others.

### Three Types of Doubt

These three doubts are drawn from the Tiāntái *Mohe Zhiguan* (Great Calming and Contemplation): (1) doubt about the teacher, (2) doubt about the teaching, (3) doubt about oneself.

**First, doubt about the teacher**: There are two kinds of teachers. Heterodox and non-Buddhist teachers who delude people through inverted teachings are not to be followed. Teachers of the correct Dharma include both ordinary and holy, causal-stage and fruition-stage [teachers]. Ordinary and causal-stage teachers may have things not yet fully understood. ... But those who now reveal the Western [Pure Land] and urge dedication to it are exclusively fruition-stage buddha-teachers: Śākyamuni Tathāgata and all the buddhas of the ten directions, who extend their broad and long tongues to speak sincere and truthful words, praising and encouraging rebirth. What further doubt could there be?

**Second, doubt about the teaching**: The Buddha-dharma has two levels: (1) the Hīnayāna, non-definitive teachings, and (2) the Mahāyāna, definitive teachings. Within the Mahāyāna, there are further distinctions of definitive and non-definitive. The Pure Land [doctrine] we now discuss belongs exclusively to the definitive within the definitive of the Mahāyāna.

The Hīnayāna sūtra collections, encompassing all the palm-leaf scriptures, contain not a single word praising or encouraging rebirth in another buddha's Pure Land. ... Within the Mahāyāna, there are three further kinds: (1) The teaching common to the three vehicles: although the gate is shared with the Mahāyāna, it is closely associated with the two vehicles. The bodhisattvas of this teaching, though they purify buddha-lands for others' sake, ultimately return like the two vehicles to permanent extinction. The profound principle of the Pure Land is not what they know. This is not the definitive. (2) The separate Mahāyāna teaching exclusively for bodhisattvas: although it discusses real principle, realization comes only after the path-stage. Cause and fruit are not perfectly integrated; the Pure Land then becomes a skillful means outside one's essential nature. This too is not yet definitive. (3) The perfect teaching of the Buddha-vehicle: this teaching's purport is perfectly integrated; cause and fruit are simultaneously complete in the subtlety of the Buddha-dharma. Beyond this, there is nothing more.

The sūtra says: "Seeking throughout the ten directions, there is no further vehicle; there is only the one Buddha-vehicle." This, then, is the Mahāyāna within the Mahāyāna, the definitive within the definitive. All the pure and defiled lands of the ten directions are rolled up and held together within a single instant. In a single thought-moment, form and mind are arrayed and comprehensively gathered throughout the dharma-realm. All is innately and naturally complete, not newly produced through causes and conditions. One thought-moment being thus, a single mote of dust is also thus. Therefore, in each and every mote of dust there are all the worlds; in each and every mind there are all minds; each mind and each dust-mote further interpenetrate; layer upon layer, inexhaustible and unobstructed.

If one trusts this perfect teaching, then nothing in the phenomenal world is beyond reach. If one is blind to this ultimate principle, then every category becomes a source of confusion. As the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* says: "The mind is like a master painter, creating all the five aggregates. Everything in the world is nothing but what the mind creates. As it is with the mind, so it is with the Buddha. As it is with the Buddha, so it is with sentient beings. Mind, Buddha, and sentient beings: these three are without difference."

**Third, doubt about oneself**: "I am a worldly being just beginning on the path, entangled in worldly conditions. How can this very body be born in the Pure Land?" If one understands the dharma-nature as described above, thoroughly open and unobstructed, and if one trusts Amitābha's original vow to embrace and receive, then one need only diligently cultivate merit. ... The ten-recitation practitioner attains rebirth. Even the five heinous offenses and slander of the correct Dharma are excluded only from the ordinary recitation; with concentrated mind and ten recitations, even these can be reborn.

## Gate Two: Practicing Vows

This is briefly opened into four gates: (1) the gate of prostration and repentance, (2) the gate of ten recitations, (3) the gate of constant mindfulness, (4) the gate of miscellaneous merits.

Why only these four? Complete practice requires exactly these. First, bow to the Buddha and repent, purifying karmic obstructions so that body and mind are clean: the first gate is like purifying good soil. Next, cultivate the ten recitations, concentrating the mind to establish practice and set up vows, planting the direct cause of rebirth: the second gate is like sowing the seed. Next, maintain constant mindfulness to protect and nurture, developing the sprout and stem: the third gate is like pouring down the sweet rain. Next, rely on miscellaneous merits to help bring lush growth and swiftly produce flowers and fruit: the fourth gate is like irrigating with rich fertilizer.

### First Gate: Prostration and Repentance

One should daily, in the early morning, in one's regular offering hall, wearing proper attire with composure and reverence, before the buddha-image personally burn incense, join palms, concentrate the mind, and chant:

"With all reverence, with one mind I bow to the permanently abiding Three Treasures."

[There follow verses of offering incense and praise:]

> "The Tathāgata's wondrous form-body is unequaled in the world,
> Incomparable, inconceivable: therefore I now bow.
> The Tathāgata's form is inexhaustible, and his wisdom likewise:
> All dharmas permanently abide; therefore I take refuge."

[Prostrations to Amitābha in his four buddha-lands: the Land of Eternally Quiescent Light, the Land of Real Reward and Adornment, the Land of skillful means and Holy Residence, and the Western Land of Peaceful Bliss. Prostrations to Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Mahāsthāmaprāpta Bodhisattva, and the assembly of the Pure Ocean.]

[A long repentance prayer follows, confessing all sins from beginningless time through the six sense-faculties and three types of karma, and dedicating all to rebirth in the Pure Land.]

### Second Gate: Ten Recitations

Each day at dawn, after dressing, face west, stand upright, join the palms, and in continuous voice recite "Amitābha Buddha" (*Āmítuó Fó*) for the duration of one breath; this counts as one recitation. Do this for ten breaths: this is called the ten recitations. Follow only the length of each breath, without counting the number of buddha-names. Only the long and sustained, with each breath carried to its full extent. The buddha-name should be neither loud nor soft, neither slow nor fast, evenly regulated. These ten breaths should be continuous and unbroken. The intent is to prevent the mind from scattering; concentrated effort is the accomplishment. Therefore this is called the "ten recitations": it clearly means using the breath to bind the mind.

After completing the recitations, make the dedication vow:

"I, your disciple, with one mind take refuge in Amitābha Buddha of the Land of Ultimate Bliss. May your pure light illuminate me; may your compassionate vow embrace me. I now, with correct mindfulness, recite the Tathāgata's name for the duration of ten recitations, seeking the Way of bodhi, aspiring to be born in the Pure Land. The Buddha formerly made this vow: 'If any sentient being desires to be born in my land, with sincere faith and joy, for even ten recitations, and is not born there, I shall not attain perfect enlightenment; excluded only are the five heinous offenses and slander of the correct Dharma.' ... May at the moment of my death, I know the time has come, my body be free from illness and pain, my mind free from attachment, not confused or scattered, as if entering meditation. May the Buddha and the holy assembly, holding the golden platform, come to welcome and receive me. In the space of a single thought, may I be born in the Land of Ultimate Bliss."

### Third Gate: Constant Mindfulness

In all activities, whether public or private, amid the affairs of daily life, though one may be in a hurry, the inner mind should never forget the Buddha or cease to be mindful of the Pure Land. It is like a person with urgent business on the mind: though going through speech, coming and going, sitting and lying, and various tasks, it does not prevent the secret remembrance of that matter, which remains vivid. Mindfulness of the Buddha should be just like this.

As the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* says: "Suppose there are two persons, one who solely remembers the other, and the other who solely thinks [of the first]. If the two remember and think of each other deeply, then from life to life they will be like form and shadow, never separated."

### Fourth Gate: Miscellaneous Merits

As the *Samantabhadra Contemplation Sūtra* says, if kings, great ministers, and officials wish to repent of grave sins, they should cultivate five things: (1) maintain a correct mind and not slander the Three Treasures; (2) be filial to parents and serve teachers; (3) govern the country with correct Dharma and not wrong the people; (4) on the six poṣadha days, decree throughout the realm that no killing shall occur; (5) deeply believe in cause and effect, believe in the one true Way, and know that the Buddha does not perish.

One who practices the samādhi of mindfulness of Buddha: in this present body one will see Amitābha Buddha and the two bodhisattvas. ... If a person merely hears the name of the Buddha and the two bodhisattvas, that person will permanently eliminate the sins of immeasurable kalpas of birth-and-death. How much more for one who is mindful! One who is mindful of the Buddha: know that this person is the puṇḍarīka (white lotus) among human beings. Avalokiteśvara and Mahāsthāmaprāpta shall be their supreme friends.
