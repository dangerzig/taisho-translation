# 七知經
## Sūtra on the Seven Knowledges

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0027

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhī Qiān (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

The Buddha was dwelling at Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park. The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Bhikṣus, receive the teaching; listen to the Buddha."

The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "There are seven qualities by which a disciple of the Path finds peace, harmony, and ease in this present life, abounds in practice, and through diligent contemplation of the Dharma brings one's training to completion. What are these seven? First, knowing the Dharma; second, knowing the meaning; third, knowing the proper time; fourth, knowing moderation; fifth, knowing oneself; sixth, knowing the assembly; seventh, knowing persons.

"Bhikṣus, what is it to know the Dharma? It means being able to understand the twelve divisions of scripture: first, prose (sūtra); second, mixed verse (geya); third, exposition (vyākaraṇa); fourth, stanzas (gāthā); fifth, parables (avadāna); sixth, origin accounts (nidāna); seventh, explanations of events (itivṛttaka); eighth, birth narratives (jātaka); ninth, extended discourses (vaipulya); tenth, spontaneous utterances (udāna); eleventh, marvelous dharmas (adbhutadharma); twelfth, instruction verses (upadeśa). This is called knowing the Dharma. Not understanding the twelve divisions of scripture is called not knowing the Dharma.

"What is it to know the meaning? It means fully comprehending the meaning of whatever scriptural teachings are expounded. This is called knowing the meaning. Not comprehending and not understanding what is expounded is called not knowing the meaning.

"What is it to know the proper time? It means knowing: now is the time to contemplate the perception of quiescence and cessation; now is not the time to contemplate the perception of active engagement; now is the time to contemplate the perception of careful guarding. This is called knowing the proper time. Not discerning the proper occasion for each practice is called not knowing the proper time.

"What is it to know moderation? It means being able to eat and drink sparingly, to properly regulate elimination, and to digest with ease; being able to regulate one's comings and goings, sitting and rising, walking and moving; and in sleeping, waking, speaking, and keeping silence, conducting all affairs with restraint and economy. This is called knowing moderation. Not restraining and economizing oneself is called not knowing moderation.

"What is it to know oneself? It means knowing one's own mind, whether it is mature or immature, and to what degree: one's faith, one's moral conduct, one's learning, one's generosity, one's wisdom, one's understanding, one's attainments, and one's realizations, the depth and shallowness, the thickness and thinness of each and every one of these, knowing them all for oneself. This is called knowing oneself. Not knowing the degree of one's own mind's realizations is called not knowing oneself.

"What is it to know the assembly? It means being able to discern any given assembly: whether it is an assembly of noble persons or an assembly of householders, an assembly of brahmins or an assembly of śramaṇas. On arriving at such an assembly, knowing whether it is fitting to sit or to stand, to speak or to remain silent, and knowing what is appropriate to the occasion. This is called knowing the assembly. Not knowing how to read an assembly and act in accordance with the occasion is called not knowing the assembly.

"What is it to know persons? Suppose there are two people: one has faith in the Path, and one does not. The one with faith is worthy of praise; the one without faith is not worthy of praise. Among those with faith in the Path there are two kinds: one frequently visits the place of practice and delights in the company of śramaṇas, and one does not frequently visit and has little regard for śramaṇas. The one who visits frequently is worthy of praise; the one who does not is not worthy of praise. Among those who frequently visit the place of practice there are two kinds: one reveres and honors the śramaṇas, and one does not revere and honor them. The one who reveres and honors is worthy of praise; the one who does not is not worthy of praise. Among those who revere and honor there are two kinds: one draws near to and associates intimately with the śramaṇas, and one does not draw near or associate intimately. The one who draws near and associates intimately is worthy of praise; the one who does not is not worthy of praise. Among those who associate intimately there are two kinds: one delights in questioning about the scriptures and the Dharma, and one does not delight in questioning. The one who delights in questioning is worthy of praise; the one who does not is not worthy of praise. Among those who delight in questioning there are two kinds: one lends an attentive ear, and one does not. The one who listens attentively is worthy of praise; the one who does not is not worthy of praise. Among those who listen attentively there are two kinds: one who hears the Dharma receives and retains it, and one who hears does not receive and retain it. The one who receives and retains is worthy of praise; the one who does not is not worthy of praise. Among those who receive and retain there are two kinds: one who hears reflects on the meaning, and one who hears does not reflect on the meaning. The one who reflects on the meaning is worthy of praise; the one who does not is not worthy of praise. Among those who hear the Dharma and reflect on its meaning there are two kinds: one understands in accordance with the meaning of the scripture, receives the Dharma, and establishes oneself in accord with the Dharma; and one does not understand in accordance with the meaning, does not receive the Dharma, and does not establish oneself in accord with the Dharma. The one who understands in accordance with the meaning is worthy of praise; the one who does not is not worthy of praise. Among those who understand in accordance with the meaning there are two kinds: one only brings peace to oneself and does not bring peace to others, does not bring peace to many, has no compassion for the world, and brings no benefit to all under heaven; and one who can bring peace to oneself also brings peace to others, brings peace to the many throughout the world, has compassion for the world, and brings benefit and tranquility to humans and devas alike. Bhikṣus, you should clearly distinguish: the one who brings peace to oneself, can bring peace to others, brings peace to the many throughout the world, has compassion for the world, and brings benefit and tranquility to all under heaven: that person is the highest, the greatest, the most honored, supremely venerable. It is like milk from a cow: from milk comes curds, from curds comes butter, from butter comes ghee, and ghee is the highest of all. In just the same way, such a person is a person among persons, one who walks the highest path, the path of honor, the supremely venerable path, the most excellent, the highest aspiration, unsurpassed. Bhikṣus, being able to see both kinds of persons with wisdom and discernment, to distinguish 'this person is good, this person is superior': this is called knowing persons."

When the Buddha had finished speaking, all rejoiced and received his words.
