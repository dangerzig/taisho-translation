# 諸經要略文
## Essential Excerpts from Various Sūtras

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2821

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

The *Abhidharma-hṛdaya* (*Zunpo xumi jing*) says: When a vegetarian feast is set for the deceased and the food has been served all around to the saṃgha, one should then set out food for the deceased. If this is not done, the deceased does not get to eat.

The *Sūtra on the Treasure of the Hells* (*Diyu bao ji jing*) says: If the host of the feast sends food out for living beings before the distribution to the assembly is complete, one falls into the tongue-cutting hell. The *Sūtra on the Definitive Determination of Sin and Merit* says: The *Kāśyapa* treatise says: If two vow-holders share a single seat, after death they become a two-headed snake that guides and instructs each other. The *Mahāyānasūtrālaṃkāra* says: From the tray set for the Buddha at a vegetarian feast, not a single grain or morsel may be taken by a layperson. One who eats such food receives the retribution of a hungry ghost. It also says: It may, however, be given to insects and birds. The *Sūtra on Entering the Buddha's Domain* says: If a person eats the food on the holy saṃgha's tray, one incurs immeasurable transgression. It also says: It may be given to the monastery guardians and to insects and birds. The *Vinayaviniścaya-sūtra* says: For any feast, regardless of size, the food on the Buddha's tray must not be eaten. The senior monk should dispose of it properly. If he does not, immeasurable transgression is incurred. Question: May one redeem it by purchase? According to the *Sūtra on the Definitive Determination of Sin and Merit*, the Buddha neither seeks to be assessed nor has anyone worthy of doing so. The senior monk's instruction is: Offer it to the Buddha and to the saṃgha, and then it may be distributed without offense. Otherwise, for a thousand kalpas, ten thousand kalpas, one falls into the great hell. If the teacher does not instruct in this, he also incurs the foregoing calamity. Even if one obtains a human body, for nine million years one is in the lowest station. It is said in the Tripiṭaka: "It would be better to burn nine hundred ox-heads in a single day than to speak of the faults of the Three Jewels or the teachers and saṃgha." The transgression is extreme. In the *Ratnakūṭa Sūtra*, the "not passing through in vain" section, seven are briefly stated. The factors of awakening are also called the seven jeweled springs, also called the seven holy treasures.

**A frog that heard the Dharma and was reborn in heaven** (from the *Sudarśana-vinaya-vibhāṣā*, essential excerpts):

Long ago, when the Buddha was in the world, he went to the Kāla Pond in the kingdom of Campā and taught the Dharma to the assembly. In that pond was a frog. Hearing the sound of the Buddha teaching the Dharma beside the pond, it came out of the water and went under a grass root to listen to the Buddha's teaching. At that time, a man holding a staff was herding cattle. Seeing the Buddha seated teaching the Dharma to the assembly, he went toward the Buddha, wishing to listen. He thrust his staff into the ground and accidentally struck the frog's head, killing it instantly. It was reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven. Because of this meritorious reward, its palace was twelve yojanas in breadth. It enjoyed pleasures with the heavenly maidens. After being reborn in heaven, because of having listened to the Dharma, it attained the fruit of stream-entry.

**A bird that heard a bhikṣu reciting sūtras and was reborn in heaven** (from the *Damamūka-nidāna-sūtra*):

Long ago, when the Buddha was in the world, there was a bhikṣu reciting sūtras in a forest. His voice was elegant and fine. A bird heard the Dharma and listened from a tree. At that time, a hunter shot it and it died, being reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven. Its face was handsome beyond compare. It recognized its past life and knew that because it had encountered the bhikṣu and heard the sūtra, it had been reborn there. It immediately took heavenly flowers and went to the bhikṣu's place, bowed in reverence, made greeting, and offered the heavenly flowers and fragrance to the bhikṣu. The bhikṣu asked and learned the full circumstances, then invited it to sit and taught the Dharma. It attained the fruit of stream-entry. Having attained the fruit, it returned to heaven. Even birds and beasts that hear the Dharma obtain immeasurable and boundless karmic reward from past lives. How much more so for a human being who listens to the Dharma with faith; surely wholesome roots will not be lacking.

**Two parrots that heard the teaching of the Four Noble Truths and were reborn in heaven** (from the *Damamūka-nidāna-sūtra*):

Long ago, when the Buddha was in the world, the Elder Sudatta of the kingdom of Śrāvastī had faith in and revered the Buddha-Dharma. As a patron of the saṃgha, he supplied whatever the saṃgha needed. In Sudatta's household were two parrots. The first was named Lüzhuo, and the second Shelüti. They were clever by nature and understood human speech. When a bhikṣu was seen coming, they would first announce it within the household so that people could come out to welcome him. Later, Ānanda went to the elder's house. Seeing the intelligence of the birds, he taught them the Four Noble Truths of suffering, origination, cessation, and the path. There was a tree at the gate. The two birds, having heard the Dharma, flew up into the tree. Joyfully they recited and upheld what they had heard. That night, while roosting in the tree, they were killed by a wildcat. Because of this wholesome root, they were reborn in the Heaven of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, and so on. Thus they passed through seven rebirths in heaven. When their heavenly lives were exhausted, they were reborn in the human world, went forth from the household, cultivated the path, and attained the fruit of a pratyekabuddha. One was named Dharma, the other Sudharma.

**Carefully examining the sūtras of both the greater and lesser vehicles: those who eat garlic-chives receive the retribution of evil destinies.**

The *Dashun Sūtra* says: Those who eat this vegetable will not be able to hear the sūtras or the Buddha-Dharma wherever they are born. The *Huabao Sūtra* says: Those who eat this vegetable will not see the buddhas wherever they are born, and will be afflicted with eight kinds of increasing illness. The *Tianpan Sūtra* says: If one mistakenly eats this vegetable and does not give rise to regret, one will remain in the Avīci Hell for a long kalpa with no prospect of escape. If one deliberately eats it, there is no possibility of confession and repentance. The *Mahāsaṃnipāta Sūtra* says: This vegetable was produced when the celestial Māra Pāpīyān transformed himself into a thousand-year-old seductive fox, and from its urine this vegetable grew. Eating it can destroy the path. Even if one does not have various sūtras in this life, in the next life one will certainly be able to loathe the holy teachings and become a non-Buddhist or an icchantika. Among all transgressions in the world, none are worse than the five heinous crimes and stealing the permanent property of the saṃgha. These do not admit of confession and repentance. Yet compared to a person who eats garlic-chives, the person guilty of the five heinous crimes or stealing the permanent property of the saṃgha: I can save them. But the person who eats garlic-chives has karmic obstructions so deep and heavy that I cannot save them. In the *Bodhisattva Precept Sūtra*, "orchid-scallion" is this very thing. Those who eat it bind themselves to karmic transgression. Nāgārjuna's treatise says: If a person who has eaten garlic-chives enters a buddha's stūpa, that person falls into the great hell and for a full ten kalpas suffers extreme torment as a preliminary retribution, being reborn in a cesspit of filth for an immeasurable number of kalpas. The *Pañcavidyā* treatise says: Those who eat this vegetable become scattered and confused, losing right concentration. Ghosts and spirits take advantage of this, and many die untimely deaths, falling into the Avīci Hell. The Venerable Vasumitra admonished his disciple Vāmita, saying: "You should protect the true Dharma and guide sentient beings along the path, so that the Buddha-seed is not cut off. This is true going forth. You should practice with diligent effort. In the future, do not eat garlic-chives, the five pungent vegetables, alcohol, or meat. Then the ten major precepts will naturally be pure, and in this present life you will realize the fruit. If a person eats garlic-chives, even though their fasting and precepts are complete, after death they fall among the latrine-guarding spirits. The spirits in charge command that it be severed."

> I exhort the saṃgha: with the laity guarding the platform,
> Do not eat garlic-chives and offend the Buddha's countenance.
> The bodhisattva who threw himself from the cliff could discern for himself;
> What difficulty is there in selecting among the vegetables?

**Cultivating the four right exertions:** First, evil dharmas that have not yet arisen: through effort, cause them not to arise. Second, evil dharmas that have already arisen: through effort, cause them to be severed. Third, wholesome dharmas that have not yet arisen: through effort, cause them to arise. Fourth, wholesome dharmas that have already arisen: through effort, cause them to increase. Evil comes forth from the mind and returns to the mind to be extinguished. Good arises from the mind and returns to the mind to grow. One should diligently contemplate the mind and observe these four kinds of dharma.

**Cultivating the four immeasurable minds:** First, cultivating the mind of loving-kindness: thinking of all sentient beings and wishing to give them happiness; reflecting and creating immeasurable provisions of clothing, food, and the like to give to sentient beings, equally to enemies and intimates, all with the happiness of this ground. This is called cultivating loving-kindness. Second, cultivating the mind of compassion: thinking of all sentient beings and wishing to remove their suffering. This is called cultivating the mind of compassion. Third, cultivating the mind of sympathetic joy: thinking of all sentient beings. If they cultivate what is wholesome, one praises them all. If they create what is unwholesome, one urges them to sever it. One should conceal the evil and proclaim the good. Fourth, cultivating the mind of equanimity: regarding all sentient beings equally, not seeing enemies or intimates, equal to the dharma of emptiness.

**Cultivating the six wondrous practices:** First, the consciousness does not engage with the contemplation of form. Second, the ears do not attach to sounds. Third, the nose does not become stained by fragrances. Fourth, the tongue does not dwell on flavors. Fifth, the body does not attach to the smooth and fine. Sixth, the mind does not reflect on unwholesome dharmas.

**The four kindnesses:** First, the kindness of parents: they gave birth to one's body and brought one to the present day. Second, the kindness of teachers and saṃgha: they give birth to one's Dharma-body and bestow the Dharma upon one. Third, the kindness of the king: he saves one from being submerged and provides water, land, grass, and wood, constantly giving them to one. Fourth, the kindness of patrons: they supply clothing and food and sustain one's bodily life.
