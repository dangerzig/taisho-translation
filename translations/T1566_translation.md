# Prajñāpradīpa (Lamp of Wisdom Commentary)
## 般若燈論釋

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1566, 15 fascicles

By Bhāviveka (清辯 / 分別明菩薩). Translated from the Sanskrit by Prabhākaramitra (波羅頗蜜多羅), Tang dynasty.

## Preface to the Prajñāpradīpa

The *Prajñāpradīpa*, also called the *Madhyamaka-śāstra*, is based on five hundred verses composed by the Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna. It borrows "lamp" for its name because non-discriminating wisdom possesses the function of quiescent illumination. It takes "middle" as its designation because the mirror-like contemplation, free from conditions, equally transcends both extremes. Thus the lamp is fundamentally without mind; wisdom too is without illumination. The nature of dharmas is equal, and the meaning of the middle lies herein. Therefore this treatise is composed to elucidate it.

As for those who cling to words and are obstructed by meaning, who grasp the conventional and are deluded about the true, who stumble between annihilationism and eternalism and rush amid existence and non-existence, who hold to names and lose what is real, who grasp at leaves and forget the root: is it their intention to be thus? Surely there is a cause! Let me attempt to explain.

When they construct the causes of discrimination and invite the fruits of delusion, when afflictions and karma perfume their inner consciousness and evil companions form their external conditions, then arrogance soars high as a lofty mountain, views run deep as the vast ocean, the fire of anger is hard to touch, and the blade of their words is difficult to withstand. Hearing talk of existence they are delighted; hearing discourse on emptiness they raise slander. Six types of biased attachment, each claiming to be unbiased; five hundred treatise-masters contending with rival theories. Some use the heterodox to disturb the orthodox; some equate the counterfeit with the genuine. Understanding seems like awakening yet turns to delusion; teaching though accessible becomes further obstructed. It may be called casting away pearls to play with stones, abandoning treasure to carry firewood, fearing painted dragons, shrinking from elephant tracks. When attachment is like this, it is truly lamentable.

The Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna, born to rescue the world, rebuked sensual craving and generated the aspiration for awakening. He studied the profound sūtras and was humbled. Having received the distant prediction of the Sole Honored One, he kindled the lamp of dharma in Jambudvīpa. His stage surpassed the initial grounds of reliance; his accomplishments transcended the positions of subduing. Having thoroughly penetrated the one reality and fully investigated the two capacities, he wore the two seals and settled the hundred schools, merging the three emptinesses to equalize the ten thousand things. Through dust-particle kalpas of trials, pitying the deluded masses, he composed this treatise. Its language is profound and its meaning subtle; its refutations are skillful and its expositions masterful. Yet when bestowed upon those of dull faculties, many became timid and retreated.

There was the Bodhisattva Bhāviveka (lit. "Clear Discrimination"), a great general of the Mahāyāna dharma, who embodied the Way within his heart. Having surveyed far and wide the true teachings, he composed this commentary, opening the treasury of secret meaning and bestowing the wish-fulfilling jewel. The concise and the extensive mutually support each other; the master and the disciple mutually illuminate each other. As for the differing interpretations arising in a thousand ways within his own vehicle, and the myriad threads of divergent theories among the non-Buddhist schools, whether donkey-carts competing to race against four-horse chariots or fireflies vying to outshine dragon-candles, he invariably classified their types and revealed their doctrinal lineages. Jade and stone were thus distinguished; black and white were thus determined. In the Western Regions, several scholars put pen to paper on this subject. In terms of verifying truth and analyzing subtleties, this work is the most refined. If the comprehensive version were included with the root text, it would amount to six thousand verses. Such is the Sanskrit; in translation it is reduced.

Our Emperor, whose divine way surpasses Fu Xi and Shen Nong, whose transformative influence equals that of creation itself, unifying the six directions and threading together the three powers, embracing the four kinds of birth and promoting the ten virtues, exalting the fundamental and quelling the peripheral, governing through non-action in great peace, guarding the mother and preserving the child, ruling without words: he was especially devoted to the Buddhist scriptures, with lofty aspirations for ultimate truth. He considered that since the holy teachings had flowed eastward over several hundred years, much of what the great elephants bore on their backs was still lacking. His desire to hear what had not yet been heard kept him occupied in both waking and sleeping.

The Tripiṭaka Master of Central India, Prabhākaramitra (Tang name: Mingyu, "Bright Friend"), whose learning encompassed both the Hīnayāna and Mahāyāna and who had comprehensively mastered the various treatises, who had dissolved the self and calmed the spirit, who probed the profound and nourished his nature, who wandered with mindfulness and benefited beings as his concern: he was thus able to cross oceans on logs, signal companions with smoke, brave ice and frost over the Pamir range, and endure wind and heat across the desert rivers. Over a period of five years, traversing a distance of forty thousand *li*, in the first year of the Zhenguan era of the Great Tang, on the twentieth day of the eleventh month, he arrived at the capital bearing Sanskrit texts upon his head.

In the past, when the Qin summoned Kumārajīva, they had to employ military force; when the Han invited Kāśyapa Mātaṅga, they had to send envoys afar. How can those compare to this auspicious response, where the Way accords with mysterious signs? Auspicious fortune for the nation and the dynasty: a person of virtue thus descended. When the relevant officials reported the audience, the Emperor was greatly pleased. That year, by imperial decree, he was installed at the Great Xingshan Monastery and requested to translate the *Ratnaketu-sūtra*.

In the fourth year, sixth month, he was moved to Shengguang Monastery. The scholarly monks Huicheng, Huilang, Fachang, Tanzang, Zhishou, Huiming, Daoyue, Sengbian, Sengzhen, Zhijie, Wenshun, Falin, Lingjia, Huize, Huijing and others were summoned as translators. The śramaṇa Xuanmo, Saṃgha, and the Tripiṭaka's fellow student, the Vinaya Master Gupta, together served as witnesses and jointly translated this treatise. The Left Vice-Director of the Department of State Affairs, Duke Fang Xuanling of Bin, the Grand Mentor of the Crown Prince Du Zhenglen, and the Minister of Rites, Prince Li Xiaogong of Zhao Commandery, were all worthy ministers assisting the sage, aiding the times in relief and governance. They exhausted their loyalty in serving their lord and transcended physical form in seeking the dharma. From the time the sage emperor conceived the plan until this dissemination was completed, the benefit was deep and the advantage substantial, truly owing to their opening and inspiration.

The Supervisory Imperial Commissioner, the Right Director of Glorious Emoluments and Grand Chamberlain, Xiao Jing of Lanling, whose roots of faith were earnest from the start and whose power of wisdom was essential in the end, who meditated in quiescence seeking truth and approached the Way with an empty heart: he praised and promoted the work tirelessly, encouraging and assisting without cease. All the venerable monks rose early and worked diligently, investigating the profound meaning, removing the ornate and preserving the substantial. When seeing directly, they rejoiced at meeting the principle; at arm's length, they examined right and wrong. Though the text was settled, they reviewed it again; though the meaning was clear, they re-examined it once more. On the seventeenth day of the tenth month in the year of Shouqing, the examination and verification were completed.

As for this treatise: contemplation illuminates the Middle Way, yet to preserve the middle is to lose contemplation. Emptiness reveals the ultimate, yet to attain the one is to deviate from emptiness. Thus the south-pointing chariot was originally shown to the lost; the gall-revealing mirror was made for examining the treacherous. Without treachery, the mirror has no use; without being lost, the chariot serves no purpose. The refutations and affirmations of this treatise are like this.

Although it censures from within and wards off from without, exhausting the false and penetrating the true, it preserves what is wonderfully preserved and refutes only what may be refuted. Vast! Expansive! Approaching it, one cannot fathom its source; following it, one cannot know its end. Truly it is the whetstone that polishes the spirit, the vessel that crosses dark and dangerous seas, the thunder that startles the benighted consciousness, the sun and moon that illuminate the shadowed path. In this land there previously existed the *Madhyamaka-śāstra* in four fascicles, whose root verses are largely the same, with Piṇḍola-bhāradvāja providing the commentary. But its sectarian affiliation was obscured, leaving students in the dark. Now that this treatise has appeared, it may serve as a bright mirror. May those gentlemen who awaken to the profound examine it carefully and savor its meaning.

## Fascicle 1

### Chapter 1: Examination of Conditions (Part 1)

> Universally severing all discriminations, extinguishing all conceptual proliferations,
> Able to uproot the roots of existence, skillfully teaching the true dharma.
> In the realm beyond speech, well establishing words,
> Destroying the deluded minds of evil understanding: therefore I bow in homage.

**Commentary:** What is the meaning of these verses? My master, the Holy One, as he himself realized, within the profound *prajñāpāramitā*, verified the true principle and revealed the actual meaning in order to sever all the nets of evil and wrong understanding. Although those of wrong views cultivate the pure conduct, because of their delusion it all becomes unwholesome. Now, wishing to cause them to awaken and understand the correct path, he composed this *Madhyamaka-śāstra* in accordance with the pure āgamas, to communicate the Buddha's words.

What is the purpose of the treatise? The Bhagavān saw those sentient beings shrouded in ignorance, ruined by the dense thicket of conceptual proliferations such as arising and ceasing, annihilation and permanence, identity and difference, coming and going. He aroused supreme compassion, generated courageous wisdom, and over immeasurable billions of koṭis of nayutas of kalpas, for the benefit of others, he sacrificed his body and life without weariness. He was able to shoulder the immeasurable burden of merit and wisdom, to plumb the ocean of the *prajñā* domain, to sever all nets of conceptual proliferation. Without relying on others, without discrimination, he attained the true nectar of all dharmas. Regarding their realms, lifespans, limits, natures, places, and times, he embraced and benefited them. Not shared with all śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, or non-Buddhist practitioners, it was exclusively for those who advance toward the foremost vehicle. Based on conventional truth and ultimate truth, he established names such as "non-arising" and similar terms. This teaching of dependent arising is supreme among all true teachings.

My ācārya likewise, in the passages on non-arising and similar topics, demonstrated the Tathāgata's true principle, attained a true understanding, and generated supreme courage. As he had penetrated it, he praised the Bhagavān and therefore composed this treatise. Moreover, moved by the waters of compassion, having verified his own understanding, he wished to cause the world to attain the same understanding, and therefore he spoke these words.

As the verse says:

> "Neither ceasing nor arising, neither annihilated nor permanent,
> Neither one nor many, neither coming nor going.
> Dependent arising, in which conceptual proliferations are stilled,
> Which the Teacher has well extinguished:
> I pay homage to that Bhagavān, the supreme among all who speak."

**Commentary:** Because the meaning of those phrases is explained in continuous sequence, to explain the meaning of this treatise, these phrases are spoken first. "Ceasing" means destruction; "arising" means production. "Annihilation" means the death of continuity; "permanence" means existing at all times. "One" means no distinction, the meaning of non-difference; "many" means distinction, the meaning of difference. "Coming" means approaching this; "going" means approaching that. "Neither ceasing" means there is no such ceasing, and so on up to "neither going" means there is no such going.

The arising and ceasing, identity and difference are negated in the ultimate sense. Annihilation and permanence are negated within the conventional. Coming and going are either both negated. Some say all of these are negated in the ultimate sense.

"That one" refers to the Buddha Bhagavān. "Dependent arising" means arising through the coming together of various causes and conditions, hence the name "dependent arising." Because the self-nature grasped through language permanently does not operate, it is called "conceptual proliferations stilled." Because all calamities and obstacles are absent, or because its own nature is empty, it is called "well extinguished." "Speaker" means one who expounds meaning, who is correct and non-inverted. Having penetrated the twofold selflessness of persons and dharmas, he is therefore called "Buddha Bhagavān." For this reason, I pay homage. "Supreme among all who speak" means: because he demonstrated the non-inverted dependent arising, the path to nirvāṇa for the faith and delight of gods and humans; because his instruction of śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and bodhisattvas is supreme; and because his teaching is correct, non-inverted, and surpasses other dependent arising teachings.

**Question:** You yourself have said you teach the dharma of dependent arising. If you say "dependent arising," how can you say "non-arising"? If you say "non-arising," how can you say "dependent arising"? These statements are self-contradictory. Moreover, since they destroy understanding and cause regression, both the words and meaning are ruined, like the statement "all speech is false."

**Answer:** If all dependent arising were non-arising, then you would understand it thus and I would have this fault. But I have never said that all dependent arising is non-arising, so the above fault does not apply. What is the meaning? Because in conventional truth there is dependent arising, it does not follow that in ultimate truth there is also dependent arising. As for your stating this reason, this meaning does not hold.

It is like *dāna* (generosity) and such: in ultimate truth they are not called "good," but because they lead beyond saṃsāra, they are called "good." Again, it is like saying consciousness is the self; in ultimate truth consciousness is truly not the self. Understand it in this way, and therefore there is no fault. Also, just as a conjured man arises yet the man's own nature truly does not arise, and just as illusions, mirages, and the arising of the sense-fields and such are spoken of conventionally but not in ultimate truth, therefore there is no fault.

**Question:** Arising should be negated after ceasing. That is the proper order of dharma-characteristics. Because ceasing comes first, as in the case of "not annihilated."

**Answer:** Because saṃsāra is beginningless, ceasing first and arising after are both equally negated. It is not a fault of one-sided reasoning. The sequence follows the meaning examined, not a different text. If ceasing were negated first, the fault would be the same as for arising.

Furthermore, a person of the Dharmaguptaka school says: "Your treatise begins by saying 'non-arising, non-ceasing,' and so on. These are unconditioned dharmas, distinct from dependent arising. This is not so. Why? Because they exist in our dharma. Your statement at the beginning of the treatise that this is not shared with śrāvakas and others does not correspond to the meaning."

The author says: Because we negate self-nature, we speak of non-arising and other distinct dependent arising dharmas, to help you understand. If you say there exists an unconditioned dependent arising that would make others believe, this is not so, because investigation shows it has no substance.

If you think that dependent arising is definitively called "unconditioned dependent arising," this understanding has a fault. Why? Because arising is negated. That arising has no substance, and therefore should not be called "shared." Because the unconditioned is without arising and has a cause, like abiding.

Furthermore, the Sautrāntika master says: The meaning of non-arising and so on is not unshared with śrāvakas. What is the meaning? That which is different from arising has no substance and is called "non-arising," like "non-sovereign." The non-Buddhist understanding of ceasing: this ceasing has no substance and is called "non-ceasing," like "non-self." Because it arises from causes and effects, it is "not annihilated." Because when the effect arises the cause perishes, it is "not permanent." Like a maṇi jewel, dry cow-dung powder, and sunlight combining to produce fire: it cannot be said to be that substance, so "not one"; it cannot be said to be a different substance, so "not many." Thus arising is the same as perishing, so "not coming, not going" have this same meaning.

Your statement at the beginning that this is not shared with śrāvakas and is a distinct dependent arising is not so.

The author says: Although you use such words, they violate correct reasoning. What is the meaning? Because that which arises does not arise. I wish to cause people to understand the meaning of non-arising as a distinct dependent arising. Because it is not shared, as a distinct dependent arising, at the beginning I praise the Buddha Bhagavān and then compose this treatise, first causing them to understand that "that which arises does not arise." The remaining terms such as "non-ceasing" can then easily be contemplated.

How does one cause understanding of non-arising and the rest? Through discriminations being directly known. All such statements, whether claiming self-arising, other-arising, joint arising, or causeless arising, are all not so. Based on the āgamas and correct reasoning, when one truthfully examines, arising has no meaning. Therefore the treatise's author, with sovereign certainty, speaks this verse:

> "At no time and in no place
> Is there any entity whatsoever
> That arises from itself, from another, from both,
> Or without a cause."

**Commentary:** "Not from itself" means that all those who establish the various arising dharmas ultimately have no substance. Each in sequence: "self" means "I" in meaning. Why is all substance negated? Because the negation is in the supreme sense, and because all remaining nets of discrimination are negated. "Without remaining nets of discrimination" means without remaining cognizable domains. "Without domains" means wishing to establish non-discriminating wisdom.

Furthermore, "negation" means negating the possessing of remainder. Through a different skillful means, one teaches that dharmas do not arise, that skillful means does not arise, causing others to understand. This is the Mahāyāna siddhānta. How do we know? As the āgama says: "Form does not arise or act; it does not act upon the *prajñāpāramitā*."

Furthermore, "not arising from itself" means not arising as such a substance. This is the correct understanding. If one understands it differently, saying "does not arise from its own substance," this meaning has a fault. What fault? The fault of other-arising. Furthermore, your saying "does not arise from its own substance" does not only have the fault of other-arising but also the fault of joint self-and-other arising. This is not my intention, because it contradicts the siddhānta. This is a skillful expression.

In ultimate truth, the internal sense-fields and such have no meaning of self-arising. This is not practiced in the world, because they exist, like thought. The different schools' circular arguments do not cause understanding. "Because they exist" as a reason, using the same non-reason, since the analogy has no substance. Thus their reasoning circulates and does not hold everywhere, because there is no fault of lacking an analogy.

Furthermore, the Sāṃkhya person says: What do you establish? Is the effect called "self"? Or is the cause called "self"? What fault is there? If you establish the effect-substance as "self," then my siddhānta is established. If you establish the cause-substance as "self," it contradicts your meaning, because the substance exists within the cause. Thus all that exists should be called "arising." Your saying "non-arising": how can that meaning be so?

The author says: This speech is meaningless. Do you not know? Because the portion of arising is negated: arising from the cause's own nature and arising from another's nature are all negated. Your incorrect thinking produces these words out of confusion, so there is no fault.

There is an alternative commentary: Dharmas do not arise from their own substance, because that arising has no meaning, and also because it would lead to infinite regress. That is not compatible. What is the meaning? Because no reason or analogy is stated, and because one cannot avoid the faults alleged by others. This refutation reveals the fault of establishing an inversion. What inversion? The fault of other-arising, the fault of the effect already existing, and the fault of production having an end, which contradict the siddhānta.

Furthermore, another Sāṃkhya person says: That entities do not arise from themselves is not so. Why? Because one's own desire produces arising, and one's own self again removes it. It is like saying "in the three realms there arises a hare's horn," and desiring to remove your meaning like this. What I establish is that cause and effect can be understood without a different substance. Just as from the cause-substance the effect-dharma arises from itself, therefore the meaning is established.

The author says: This is wrong discrimination and does not accord with reason. That meaning was already negated before, so there is no fault.

Thus dharmas do not arise from themselves. That they arise from another is also not so. Why? At no time and in no place does any entity arise from another. What is the meaning? "Other" means "different"; this is a skillful expression. In ultimate truth, the sense-fields do not arise from those conditions. Why? Because they are other, like a pot and so forth.

Furthermore, in ultimate truth, other-conditions cannot produce the eye and other sense-fields. Why? Because they are other, like a sūtra and so forth.

**Question:** Your word "other" does not establish the reason. Why? Because it is a part of the thesis. Like "sound is impermanent; because it is sound."

**Answer:** Your objection is not well stated. "Sound is impermanent" refers to Vedic sound. "Because it is sound" means "like the sound of a drum." Because one sees that a part of the thesis can be a valid reason, it is not one-sided.

Furthermore, the Vaiśeṣika person says: Atoms are the cause; they produce dharma-effects. Two atoms as the beginning, then progressively, earth, water, fire, and wind aggregate and substantially arise. Your word "other": is it discriminating our substances (*dravya*) and qualities (*guṇa*) as the cause-meaning? Or is it discriminating the meaning of difference? If you discriminate substances and qualities as the cause, the reason is not established. Why? Because apart from the substance there are no separate qualities. If you discriminate the meaning of difference, then it is refuted by worldly understanding.

The author says: That objection is not well stated, because the reason is stated in general terms. The aggregation of those dharmas is what produces cognition in others. Such a cognition-cause is generally called "other"; it is not a matter of separately considering substances and qualities. Worldly understanding is also not violated, because the thesis has a distinct meaning.

In ultimate truth, the initial arising of earth-atoms is not called "earth-substance." Why? Because they are atoms, like fire-atoms. Likewise, in ultimate truth, the initial arising of fire-atoms is not called "fire-substance." Why? Because they are atoms, like water-atoms. And so on in sequence.

Furthermore, the Abhidharma person says: Your word "other": is it said to be "other" because the capacity to produce effects is empty? Or is it said to be "other" because that capacity is not empty? Both have faults. Why? If it is said to be "other" because the capacity to produce effects is empty, then the reason is not established. If that capacity is not empty, then the established dharma is empty and the analogy is destroyed.

The author says: Because the reason is stated in general terms, examined from the perspective of things, and producing cognition in others, your claims that the reason is not established and that the established dharma is empty and the analogy destroyed do not constitute real faults; they are mere semblances of light and shadow.

Furthermore, someone from our own school says: If in ultimate truth neither the internal nor external sense-fields arise, then dharma-substances cannot be established and their supports are destroyed. You incur the fault of the reason not being established.

The author says: Because in conventional truth, speech is real, pots, eye-fields and other internal and external things can be found. Your alleged fault does not apply.

Furthermore, the ācārya Buddhapālita comments: "Other-production also is not so. Why? Because there would be the fault of everything arising everywhere."

The author says: If he states the fault thus, then the established and the establishing are inverted, because there is the fault of self-joint-cause arising. Or sometimes, in some place, some thing arises, so it contradicts what was previously stated. Moreover, if it were different from this, "the fault of everything arising everywhere" does not correspond to establishing the fault of other-arising. This is not appropriate. As the verse says:

> "Musta root and bitter sophora, āmalaka curing heat,
> A barren woman has no child, bamboo shoots have a bitter taste again,
> A rabbit's mark upon the moon, making music in the springtime sun."

Furthermore, another Sāṃkhya person says: Things that are both distinct and non-distinct, like earth-seeds producing sprouts, thus we speak of joint arising. That statement is not so. Why? Because "not shared" means neither self nor other; at no time and in no place is there any entity that arises from both. That statement has a fault.

Why is this so? If you claim joint arising to make others believe, investigation shows it has no substance; the meaning cannot be established.

Furthermore, the Nirgrantha (Jain) position of non-shared arising is also refuted here. What is the meaning? They claim that gold and non-gold, human effort, fire, and so on, through the power of self and other, produce rings, bracelets, and the like. To refute them, non-shared arising is taught.

Furthermore, "not without cause" means: at no time and in no place does any entity arise without a cause. Why is it without cause? Because investigation shows it has no substance. If one says there is evidence, then it would be refuted by worldly understanding, which is a fault. What is worldly understanding? Conventionally, one wishes the substance of the sense-fields to be born. Why? Because they are in general and particular. Like sprouts and so forth. Furthermore, the fault of worldly understanding is this: if in the world there is such a thing, one knows it is born from a cause, like silk becoming cloth, like bamboo becoming baskets, like clay becoming pots. For this fault.

Furthermore, wrong causes are also called "without cause," like "having no wife" and so forth. What are wrong causes? Self-nature (*prakṛti*), God (*Īśvara*), the Person (*Puruṣa*), Time, Nārāyaṇa, and so forth are not real, and therefore these "without cause" entities cannot produce substances.

If you claim that things arise from self-nature and the like to make others understand, investigation shows otherwise. If you say there is evidence, this too has a fault.

Furthermore, one who holds to self-nature (*prakṛti*) says: "I establish this: self-nature is what produces the sense-fields and such. Why? Because it adorns the self. Like water producing flower-roots, stems, and leaves with beautiful colors and forms, like great sapphires, indranīla gems, and abhinīla gems; and like the various eye-spot patterns shining beautifully on a peacock's neck, all are so by nature."

The author says: You establish that self-nature is the maker, but you do not observe that there is no maker without karmic causes. If so, then the determined worldly understanding of cause and condition for the arising of the sense-fields, along with conventional speech, becomes a further established fault. In ultimate truth, the analogy has no substance. Why? Because in ultimate truth, lotus-flowers, gems, and so forth were originally unproduced.

Furthermore, you wish to establish together with me the thesis of causelessness, which would establish all dharmas. I now show you that because of causelessness, nothing is established. Moreover, those who establish causelessness, if they state reasons, their prior position is destroyed.

Furthermore, another Sāṃkhya-Bhaddhila person says: That *kalala* (embryonic tissue) and sprouts and such arise without conditions. If pots, clothes, and such arise with conditions, not all substances arise from self-nature. This establishes what I maintain.

The author says: Because all substances at all times are negated, what you say does not apply. By this reasoning, there is no arising from self-nature.

Furthermore, an external person who holds God (*Īśvara*) as the cause says: "Sentient beings, lacking wisdom, have no sovereignty over suffering and pleasure. Good destinies and evil destinies are all directed by God."

The author says: You establish that God is the cause of the world's arising. Even conventionally, this is not so. Why? Because there are causes of sorrow and joy, like a cowherd. If you insist that God is the cause of everything in the world, this is not so. It should be known thus: because it is an object of measurement, like God himself. Therefore, know that even conventionally, God cannot produce dharmas.

If you definitively claim God as the cause producing dharmas, then is this cause and effect of the same nature? Of different nature? Of joint nature? These different discriminations have already been negated before. Arising with existence and arising without existence will be extensively refuted later. In ultimate truth, God cannot produce dharmas.

Some say: "Sentient beings and worlds, as well as material worlds, are all produced by various karmic causes. God is the basis for their abiding, arising, and destruction, for the increase and decrease of suffering and pleasure, serving as the universal support." Those who speak thus establish what I maintain as conventional speech, not ultimate truth. Because in ultimate truth, karma does not arise.

Furthermore, those who hold the Person (*Puruṣa*) as the cause of production say: "The Person is the cause of all worldly beings." The meaning is like threads woven into a net, like the moon-gem producing water, like a tree producing branches and leaves. All sentient beings have the Person as their cause, likewise. The past and future, moving and unmoving, far and near, internal and external, all have the Person as their cause.

The author says: The refutation of God as cause has already negated this view. Now I will state it again. The self of Devadatta does not create the bodily sense-aggregate of Devadatta. Why? Because of the self, like the self of Yajñadatta. Furthermore, the bodily sense-aggregates of Yajñadatta are not made by the self of Yajñadatta. Why? Because they are the arising-cause of pleasure, suffering, and cognition, like the bodily sense-aggregates of Devadatta.

If you claim the bound self is the cause of the three realms but not of everything, this is not so. Why? Because of the self, like the liberated self. Your position is not established, and there is a fault in the thesis.

**Question:** Your reason "because of the self" is not established, because it is part of the thesis. Your reasoning has the fault of being inconclusive.

**Answer:** The argument for "no fault" has already been stated. Why no fault? As stated above: "Sound is impermanent; because it is sound; like the sound of a drum." If someone says: "What I establish is that there is only one self, like one space with pot-discriminations being merely conventional, and because conventional, they are innumerable. For this reason, the analogy has no substance, the evidence is defeated, and the thesis stands without fault."

The author says: That is not well stated. What is the meaning? Because space is unproduced, like a flower in the sky, its substance cannot be found. Speaking of "one space" in this way, the meaning is not established; it is mere words. Within the conventional dharma, generally speaking of "self" is a provisional designation for identification. Your establishment of one self to make others believe: investigation shows it has no substance, so the meaning is not established.

**Question:** The bound self and the liberated self have no different substance. Why? Because of the self, like the liberated self.

**Answer:** In the realm of nirvāṇa without remainder, the one liberated self is not established. As previously stated, the fault cannot be avoided. As will be extensively explained in the chapter on the Self.

Furthermore, the Sāṃkhya person says: As I maintain, self-nature is the cause. From Brahmā at the beginning down to the sustaining limit, all dharma-effects are produced from self-nature. The sense-fields are the causes that definitely produce suffering, pleasure, and delusion, because they possess these. If in the world something possesses these, I know it to be the cause. Like sandalwood chips, like pottery shards, like gold ornaments. Because of the general and particular causes, the sense-fields possess pleasure, suffering, and delusion, and therefore the sense-fields are the cause of pleasure, suffering, and delusion.

Thus should it be known: form, perception, formations, and consciousness aggregates are all of the nature of pleasure, suffering, and delusion. Why? Because they are aggregates, like the feeling-aggregate. Therefore both reason and analogy are established.

The author says: In ultimate truth, the analogies of sandalwood and the rest are not established, because they have no substance. Conventionally, delusion belongs to the formations-aggregate, so the analogy is not established. Pleasure, suffering, and so on are distinct from other external dharmas and are not of the nature of pleasure and suffering. This should be known. Why? Because they are objects of measurement, like cognition. The evidence does not correspond.

**Question:** In your ultimate truth, there is no analogy.

**Answer:** Because of the general statement of cognition, worldly shared understanding is taken as analogy; the analogy is not without substance. Therefore, that store (*prakṛti*) is not the cause of the great (*mahat*) and other principles. Why? Because it is not understood, like the Person. If you wish to say self-nature is the cause, your own evidence is defeated.

The external person says: "I establish the Person. When combined with thought, it attains clarity. Your saying 'because it is not understood' does not establish the reason; moreover, the established dharma is not complete, and the analogy has a fault."

The author says: Those words are meaningless. What is this? Because the reason is stated generally. Establishing a particular meaning, it is not understood everywhere, and the general statement is not established.

Some say it is also not without cause that dharmas can arise, because nature, time, Nārāyaṇa, and so on are the causes. As stated in the refutation of God, one should know.

Furthermore, the Sāṃkhya person says: "You say not from self, not from other, not from both, not without cause. If sometimes somewhere some entity arises, then as you say, truly it does not arise. Although it truly does not arise, because of being made manifest..."

The author asks: "What kind of thing? How is it made manifest?"

The Sāṃkhya person says: "Like a lamp and a pot."

The author says: "The lamp and the pot are originally unproduced. How can an unproduced lamp make manifest unproduced pots? Like the non-existent horns of a horse, how can they illuminate anything? Because in ultimate truth dharmas are not born. Based on the conventional, I ask this question: what function does the lamp perform on the pot?"

The external person says: "It functions as reception."

The author says: "Reception is originally non-existent, and afterward it comes into being. Originally non-existent, afterward existing: reception is itself production. If you say that in darkness, eye-consciousness at that time has no reception, and because the presence of lamplight destroys the darkness-obstruction and so on, this has already been negated as before. Because it is a productive dharma. Moreover, the destruction of the darkness-obstruction: is that not production? If you insist that reception existed before, if it already existed, what use is the lamp?"

"Furthermore, what is called a pot? In our dharma, because the four great elements and what they produce combine together, it is called a pot. When the lamp is present, it arises together with light. Therefore, in the conventional dharma, there is a productive cause; each individual substance arises from its own cause in continuous succession. Because light and the substance arise together, this is the clarifying cause. In ultimate truth, all arising dharmas are without any clarification; it is not that the great (*mahat*) and other principles, being unclear things, can make them clear. Why? Because they are unclear, like flowers in the sky. Therefore your saying 'what is unclear becomes clear' is wrong."

Furthermore, Buddhapālita comments on this verse: "Also, it is not from no cause that those substances arise. Why? If there were no cause, everything would arise everywhere and at all times. This fault exists."

This meaning is not so. Why? The words' meaning inverts the established and the establishing. What inversion? Because substances arise from causes, sometimes there is a substance arising, sometimes there is a place where a thing arises, and there is an initial arising. This contradicts what was previously stated.

Furthermore, this passage also says "not arising from no cause." In all treatises, there is no such teaching. Sometimes somewhere, whether in one's own school or others', there is nothing, whether defiled or pure, that arises from no cause. Each case should be stated in this way.

Therefore, the distinct dependent arising of non-arising and such, not shared with non-Buddhists, is established.

Furthermore, the Abhidharma person says: "There are four kinds of conditions that produce all dharmas. How can you say dependent arising is non-arising?"

As the verse says:

> "Efficient cause, objective condition,
> Immediately antecedent condition, and dominant condition:
> These four conditions produce all dharmas;
> There is no fifth condition."

**Commentary:** The efficient cause (*hetu-pratyaya*) refers to the five causes: co-existent, self-class, associated, universal, and retributive conditions. The objective condition (*ālambana-pratyaya*) refers to all dharmas. The immediately antecedent condition (*samanantara-pratyaya*) excludes the final moment of mental and mental-associate dharmas of an arhat. The dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*) refers to the productive cause. "No fifth" means that in one's own school or others', in heaven or among humans, in the sūtras, in the abhidharma, or in other treatises, the Buddha has never taught a fifth condition.

Furthermore, the Mahāsāṃghika school also says: "Conditions such as 'previously arisen, not yet existing' are all included within the four conditions. Therefore, these four kinds of conditions produce all dharmas. Your saying that substances do not arise from other is not so."

The author's verse says:

> "All entities whatsoever,
> And also the external conditions,
> Words, speech-sounds, and the like:
> These all lack self-nature."

**Commentary:** "Entities" refers to the eye and the rest. "External conditions" refers to the *kalala* and the like. "Words and sounds" refers to the time of combination. "Lacking self-nature" negates self-substance. What is the meaning? Those entities and such all lack self-nature; nor are they found in another place or in God and so on. Therefore it is said: "the other has no substance."

Furthermore, what is self-substance such that you call the conditions "other-substance that exists"? As stated in the non-arising passage, the evidence has already been refuted. Therefore, in this regard, you cannot defeat me.

Furthermore, some who fabricate from their own minds' vain discrimination say: "If there is something that can produce dharma-substances, it is called 'other-arising,' not self-substance. Without other-conditions, it cannot be produced. Because other-conditions exist, dharmas can arise; the conditions are determinate. My understanding is thus."

This is not so. Why? If your statement negates self-arising, it supports my meaning. If entities have not yet arisen and the other can cause them to arise, this statement is not good, being the same as the previous negation.

Furthermore, if you say that entities do not arise from other, negating something different from the entity that causes arising, this supports my analogy. Therefore, in the red-white conditions (sperm and ovum), there are no eye-fields and so on, because within the conditions the eye-dharma is empty. Why? Because the conditions have no self-substance, since there is no other.

Furthermore, there are two kinds of statements here. In ultimate truth, the eye-fields and so on do not arise from the red-white conditions. Why? Because the eye and so on are non-existent, like a pot. In ultimate truth, the red-white conditions have no capacity to produce the eye-fields and so on. Why? Because the eye is empty, like a weaver's knife.

Therefore the Buddha says: In ultimate truth, causes and conditions cannot produce the eye. Thus should it be known. The Buddha, out of compassion for the world, to those who abide in confused understanding, adhering to wrong causes or no cause, quarreling: in conventional truth, he taught the efficient cause, immediately antecedent condition, objective condition, and dominant condition. Because of these conditions, my meaning is not defeated.

Some other discriminator says that substances arise from another. The author says: They should further contemplate. Among these four conditions, how can they produce eye-substances and other entities?

Other different names and distinctions, as the Mahāsāṃghika and Vaiśeṣika and others discriminate, are also subsumed here by their characteristics. Therefore it is determined: there is no fifth condition. Thus, in ultimate truth, the eye and the other are all not so.

How are they not so? As the verse says:

> "The self and other entities,
> The sense-fields and the conditions:
> Each and every one is non-existent,
> Because they lack self-nature."

**Commentary:** Among the conditions, whether general or particular, the substance of the eye and the rest cannot be found. "And the rest" means: not in the individual causes, not in the combination, not in anything different. Whether in conventional truth or ultimate truth, at no time has there been a substance lacking self-nature that first arose, nor has there ever been a thing lacking self-nature whose conditions, as other-substance, were about to arise in the future. Since the meaning of "other" is entirely absent, how can it be established? There is entirely no "other," because the other-cause has no substance.

Furthermore, if you fabricate with your own mind, placing the existence of dharma-substances that are about to arise in the future, and because of these substances the conditions serve as "other" through their power of mutual dependency, and thus you say the conditions are "other," this is mere words. Why? Because the conditions lack other-nature. Therefore one should not become attached to this.

In conventional truth, it is provisionally said that there is "other." In ultimate truth, that "other" does not arise, as previously explained.

The Sāṃkhya person says: "As I think, there is a subtle self-substance (*sūkṣma*), which at a later time is made manifest. The non-manifest effect-condition is thus the meaning of 'other,' and therefore it is established. How can you defeat this?"

The author says: Your words are wrong. Worldly foolish people do not have this understanding. The subtle self of pots and the like is difficult to establish. Your so-called "making manifest" has already been refuted.

The arguments follow the same formal pattern of thesis-reason-example throughout.)*

## Fascicle 2: Chapter 1, Part 2

The chapter continues with extensive debates about whether conditions produce effects, whether there is action (*kriyā*) within conditions, whether effects exist or do not exist in their causes, and whether conditions have real self-nature. The author consistently demonstrates through formal logical arguments that in ultimate truth, conditions lack self-nature, effects cannot be found within conditions, and the relationship between cause and effect cannot be established as either identical or different.

Key verses include:

> "There is no action in the conditions."

> "Whether effects exist or do not exist,
> Neither can be established from conditions."

The chapter concludes by establishing that the Buddha taught the four conditions conventionally, out of compassion, while in ultimate truth all conditions are empty of self-nature.

## Fascicle 3

### Chapter 2: Examination of Going and Coming

**Commentary:** The first chapter having explained that all dharma-substances lack arising in the ultimate antidote, now to further clarify the non-coming and non-going aspects of distinct dependent arising, so that beings may know, this second chapter arises to negate that meaning.

What is the meaning? In the worldly dharma, where speech is free, people develop deep attachment to actions and things. Now, wishing to remove the arrow of their attachment, by negating the single characteristic of action, all other activities become easy to refute.

The external person says: "There should be arising of the sense-field substances. Why? Because their domains can be distinctly spoken of. If there were no arising, those domains could not be distinctly spoken of. Like the son of a barren woman, of whom one cannot say there is coming or going. But Devadatta and Yajñadatta are not like this. Through this analogy, the arising of self and other dharmas is established."

The author says: If, because of extensive cultivation of giving, morality, meditation and such, arising from self-nature is established, or if going and abiding are established by worldly understanding, this would be an already-established fault. When one in meditative absorption observes with the eye of wisdom, giving, morality, and such activities and non-activities: in ultimate truth, their substance cannot be found. The reason that "domains can be distinctly spoken of" does not establish the meaning. As the negation of action-arising negates arising, action is also destroyed.

If in ultimate truth there is someone who goes, then there should be findable the three aspects of "already gone," "not yet gone," and "going." As the verse says:

> "The 'already gone to' should not be accepted."

**Commentary:** This means: because the going-dharma has already passed. This meaning is understood by both self and other; it does not require establishment.

The verse says:

> "The 'not yet gone to' also should not be accepted."

**Commentary:** Because of the goer. The meaning is the same as for "already gone." Furthermore, what is "not yet gone"? The goer has not yet initiated action; because that dharma has not yet gone, the established and establishing dharmas can both be established freely by the dharma-substance and dharma-characteristic of "wishing to go," as analogy and evidence show.

Furthermore, the disciple of Ulūka (founder of Vaiśeṣika) says: "What is 'not yet gone'? Is it like Devadatta not yet gone and then going, thus not accepted? Or is it that Devadatta's going-action is 'not going,' making others understand?"

The author says: "Why do you ask this question?"

The external person says: "If you intend to accept the first analysis, my position is established. If you intend to accept the second analysis, it contradicts your reason. Therefore, the first reason is not non-established."

"Furthermore, I establish that apart from substance (*dravya*), there is a separate going-dharma. Your saying it is not so is wrong. Apart from substance, there is going. How is it established? The inherence (*samavāya*) of a distinct category of sentence-meaning (*padārtha*) beyond one's own substance, with the domain of Devadatta, constitutes the going of Devadatta. My meaning is thus, because conditions follow, like the inherence of Devadatta."

The author says: "If conventionally there is a going-inherence with Devadatta, revealing a distinct category of inherence-domain beyond one's own substance, producing the cognition of going, and making others understand, then in conventional truth it is already established, which is a further-established fault. Why? There is only a momentary place-by-place difference, called 'inherence.' The name 'Devadatta' is merely the aggregate of actions; since the self has no substance, what separate going is there to combine with? As the wise understand, this is my intention."

"Furthermore, the going-padārtha combining with Devadatta: in ultimate truth, there is no analogy, so the substance cannot be found. Thus even conventionally it violates reasoning, let alone in ultimate truth! These faults you cannot escape."

The chapter continues with extensive debates about the impossibility of finding "going" in what has already been gone to, what has not yet been gone to, or what is presently being gone to. Various schools (Sautrāntika, Sāṃkhya, Vaiśeṣika, grammarians) propose different understandings of motion, all of which the author refutes through formal logical arguments.

Key verses include:

> "Apart from the 'already gone' and 'not yet gone,'
> The 'presently being gone to' also should not be accepted."

> "If in the 'presently being gone to' there is going,
> How can this meaning hold?"

> "The 'presently being gone to' is empty of going;
> Therefore going in the 'presently being gone to' is not so."

> "If going in the 'presently being gone to' is combined
> With the goer, this is mere discrimination."

> "If going in the 'presently being gone to'
> Plus further going there,
> Then there would be the fault of two goings:
> This meaning is not so."

> "The goer does not go."

> "The non-goer does not go."

> "Apart from the goer and the non-goer,
> There is no third goer."

The chapter concludes by establishing that going and coming, goer and non-goer, are all empty of self-nature in ultimate truth, though conventionally spoken of.

---

### Chapter 3: Examination of the Six Sense-Fields

This chapter establishes that the six internal sense-fields (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind) have no self-nature. The central argument is that the eye cannot see itself, and therefore cannot see others.

As the verse says:

> "The eye-faculty itself
> Cannot see its own substance.
> If it cannot see its own substance,
> How can it see another?"

The chapter debates with Abhidharma, Sautrāntika, Sāṃkhya, and Vaiśeṣika proponents about whether the eye sees form, whether "seeing" exists as an independent dharma, whether there is a "seer" apart from the eye, and whether the analogy of fire (which burns others but not itself) can establish that the eye sees others but not itself.

The author refutes the fire analogy:

> "The fire analogy cannot
> Establish the meaning of the eye seeing."

Because in ultimate truth, burning is not established; and conventionally, fire is not of the nature of seeing. The eye's emptiness extends by analogy to the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind:

> "The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind,
> The hearer, what is heard, and so on:
> Know that this meaning
> Is all the same as the negation of eye-seeing."

The chapter concludes that because the sense-fields are empty, arising and going are also not established.

---

### Chapter 4: Examination of the Five Aggregates

This chapter negates the self-nature of the five aggregates (*skandha*). The central argument is that form cannot exist apart from its cause, and that the cause of form cannot exist apart from form.

> "Apart from the cause, form is not found;
> Apart from form, the cause also is not found."

The chapter applies the same reasoning to feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, establishing that none of the five aggregates have self-nature.

---

### Chapter 5: Examination of the Six Elements

This chapter examines the six elements (earth, water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness) and demonstrates that none have self-nature.

> "Before space, there is no characteristic of space."

Space cannot exist before its characteristic; if the characteristic existed first, there would be characteristicless space. Space and its characteristic cannot arise simultaneously either. By analogy, the other five elements are equally without self-nature.

---

### Chapter 6: Examination of Desire and the Desirer

This chapter examines the relationship between desire (passion/defilement) and the one who desires (the desirer). It argues that desire and desirer cannot be identical, nor different, nor can they arise simultaneously.

> "Apart from desire, there is no desirer;
> Apart from the desirer, there is no desire."

If they were identical, agent and action would be one. If different, there could be a desirer without desire. Since both options fail, and simultaneous arising also fails, desire and the desirer are empty.

---

## Fascicles 4-5

### Chapter 7: Examination of Conditioned Characteristics

This chapter examines the three characteristics of conditioned dharmas: arising (*utpāda*), abiding (*sthiti*), and ceasing (*vyaya*). The external person claims these three characteristics establish conditioned existence. The author refutes this systematically.

If arising is conditioned, it too must have three characteristics, leading to infinite regress:

> "If arising is conditioned,
> It too should have three characteristics."

If arising is unconditioned, how can an unconditioned dharma be a characteristic of the conditioned?

> "If arising is unconditioned,
> How can it be a conditioned characteristic?"

The three characteristics cannot operate in sequence (because if the dharma hasn't arisen, abiding and ceasing have nothing to operate on), nor simultaneously (because arising, abiding, and ceasing at the same moment is contradictory):

> "How can one thing
> Simultaneously have three characteristics?"

The Pudgalavāda (Vātsīputrīya) school proposes a system of fifteen dharmas arising together to avoid infinite regress, with "arising of arising" (*jāti-jāti*) arising the root arising. The author refutes this with the famous mutual-dependence argument:

> "If you say that the arising of arising
> Can produce the root arising,
> Since it is born from root arising,
> How can it produce root arising?"

> "If you say root arising
> Can produce the arising of arising,
> Since it is born from the arising of arising,
> How can it produce the arising of arising?"

Another opponent proposes the lamp analogy: just as a lamp illuminates both itself and others, arising can produce both itself and others. The author refutes this:

> "In the lamp there is no darkness;
> Where the lamp abides there is also no darkness.
> What does the lamp illuminate,
> That you say it illuminates both self and other?"

> "How can the lamp, at the time of arising,
> Destroy darkness?"

> "If the lamp, not reaching darkness,
> Could destroy that darkness,
> Then a lamp stationed here
> Should destroy all darkness everywhere."

---

### Chapter 8: Examination of Agent and Action

This chapter examines whether there is an agent (*kartṛ*) that performs action (*karma*). The analysis follows the same pattern: if the agent exists, it is either identical with or different from the action. Both options fail.

---

### Chapter 9: Examination of the Appropriator

This chapter examines the "appropriator" (*upādātṛ*), the one who grasps or takes up the aggregates. It demonstrates that the appropriator cannot exist before appropriation, nor can appropriation exist before the appropriator. The relationship cannot be established as simultaneous, sequential, or otherwise.

---

### Chapter 10: Examination of Fire and Fuel

This chapter uses the analogy of fire and fuel to examine the relationship between the self and the aggregates.

> "If fire is identical with fuel,
> Agent and action would be one.
> If fire is different from fuel,
> Fire could exist without fuel."

Neither identity nor difference can be established. This applies by analogy to the self and the aggregates.

---

## Fascicles 6-7

### Chapter 11: Examination of Saṃsāra

This chapter examines whether saṃsāra has a beginning, end, or middle.

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Saṃsāra is long and distant, with coming but no boundary." The external person takes this to prove the existence of the five aggregates. The author responds that the Buddha spoke thus to counter those who claim there is no cause, to show that there is a cause at the beginning that produces dharmas. This was spoken for a certain portion of sentient beings.

> "Saṃsāra has a boundary or not? The Buddha said: ultimately none.
> This saṃsāra is without boundary; neither beginning nor end can be found."

The chapter then examines whether birth, old age, and death can be established in sequence:

> "If birth is first and old age and death are after,
> Then birth would exist without old age and death:
> One would be born without dying."

> "If first there is old age and death, and then birth,
> Then without being born, how would there be old age and death?"

> "Birth and old age and death simultaneously is also not so;
> Dying at the time of birth, both would lack a cause."

Therefore:

> "Since first, after, and together
> In sequence are all not so,
> Why engage in the conceptual proliferation
> Of asserting that there is birth, old age, and death?"

The same analysis applies to all cause-effect, substance-characteristic, receiver and received, and so on:

> "Not only in saṃsāra
> Is the beginning boundary not found;
> All dharmas likewise
> Are entirely without a beginning boundary."

---

### Chapter 12: Examination of Suffering

This chapter examines the nature of suffering (*duḥkha*), demonstrating that suffering is neither self-made, other-made, both, nor without cause. Whether suffering is identical with or different from the sufferer, neither can be established.

---

### Chapter 13: Examination of Saṃskāras (Formations)

This chapter examines conditioned formations, establishing that they are empty because they are deceptive (*mṛṣā*), like illusions. The Buddha said all formations are deceptive and empty. If deceptive, they have no self-nature; if empty, they have no self-nature.

---

### Chapter 14: Examination of Contact

This chapter examines whether the seer, the seen, and seeing can combine (*saṃyoga*). It demonstrates that identical things cannot combine, different things cannot combine, and the combination itself cannot be found.

---

## Fascicles 8-9

### Chapter 15: Examination of Being and Non-being

This chapter examines whether dharmas possess self-nature (*svabhāva*) or other-nature (*parabhāva*), being (*bhāva*) or non-being (*abhāva*).

> "The arising of self-nature from causes and conditions
> Is not reasonable.
> Self-nature arising from causes and conditions
> Would be fabricated."

If self-nature depends on causes and conditions, it is fabricated and therefore not self-nature, which by definition is independent and unchanging. Other-nature is the self-nature of another, so it too cannot be established. Without self-nature and other-nature, neither being nor non-being can be found.

---

### Chapter 16: Examination of Bondage and Liberation

This chapter examines whether saṃsāric existence (*saṃsāra*) involves bondage (*bandha*) or liberation (*mokṣa*). It demonstrates that the five aggregates are neither bound nor liberated, and that there is no entity apart from the aggregates that is bound or liberated.

> "If the aggregates transmigrate, they do not transmigrate with self-nature."

---

### Chapter 17: Examination of Karma

This chapter is an extensive examination of karma and its fruits. It addresses how karmic action, which is momentary, can produce results in the distant future.

The chapter examines various theories: the Sarvāstivāda theory of "possession" (*prāpti*), the Sautrāntika theory of "seeds" (*bīja*), the Vātsīputrīya theory of "inexhaustible" (*avipraṇāśa*), and others. All are shown to be untenable in ultimate truth.

The chapter establishes that karma is like an illusion, like a dream, like a city of gandharvas. Though empty, it functions conventionally to produce results:

> "The Buddha, great sage, said
> Karma is like an illusion, like a dream."

---

## Fascicles 9-10

### Chapter 18: Examination of the Self (Dharma)

This is one of the most important chapters. It examines the self (*ātman*) and demonstrates its non-existence through formal logical arguments.

> "If the self is the aggregates,
> Then it is subject to arising and ceasing.
> If the self is different from the aggregates,
> Then it lacks the characteristics of the aggregates."

The author debates extensively with Sāṃkhya and Vaiśeṣika proponents who claim the self is permanent, all-pervading, and a enjoyer or agent.

The author establishes in ultimate truth:

1. The four great elements, derived form, the sense-faculties, and consciousness are not the self, because they are subject to arising and ceasing, like external four great elements.
2. Apart from the five aggregates, there is no separate self, because it would lack aggregate-characteristics, like the child of a barren woman.
3. The Vaiśeṣika self is neither all-pervading (because it is a substance, like a pot), nor permanent (because it is a substance, like a pot), nor an agent (because it is non-material, like mental action).

The chapter then turns to the fruits of realizing selflessness:

> "When I and mine are entirely exhausted,
> And the internal and external sense-fields are exhausted,
> And all appropriation is exhausted,
> When appropriation is exhausted, then birth is exhausted."

> "Liberation is the exhaustion of karma and afflictions;
> Those sufferings are exhausted through liberation.
> Afflictions and karma arise from discrimination;
> Seeing emptiness extinguishes discrimination."

The chapter addresses the important distinction between the Middle Way teaching of emptiness and the Lokāyata (materialist) denial of everything:

"If someone says: 'The Middle Way says all sentence-meanings are non-existent; this is no different from the Lokāyata saying "non-existent,"' one should answer: 'My saying all sentence-meanings are non-existent also has a distinction. You do not understand, and therefore speak thus.'"

The distinction is explained: The Lokāyata who denies cause and effect uproots the wholesome roots and performs all unwholesome actions. The Middle Way teaches that cause and effect continue like illusions and mirages; one practices the wholesome path because of the continuation of the aggregates with outflows. The Lokāyata's consciousness of negation grasps the non-existent as its object and is wrong knowledge; the Middle Way practitioner, upon seeing the truth and attaining the understanding of emptiness, no longer gives rise to the grasping-consciousness of form and other objects.

The true nature of reality:

> "Quiescent, free from other-conditioning,
> Inexpressible by conceptual proliferation,
> Without difference, without multiplicity:
> This is called the true characteristic."

> "From conditions what arises,
> This thing is not the condition's substance,
> Nor is it apart from that condition:
> Neither annihilated nor permanent."

> "Not one, not different,
> Not annihilated, not permanent:
> This is what all the World-Honored Ones call
> The supreme nectar-dharma."

---

### Chapter 19: Examination of Time

This chapter examines whether time (*kāla*) has self-nature. If the present and future depend on the past, they must exist in the past. If they don't exist in the past, how can they depend on it? The same reasoning applies to past depending on future.

> "If the present and the future
> Depend upon the past,
> Then the present and the future
> Should exist in the past."

Time cannot be established as existing or non-existing, as depending or not depending.

---

### Chapter 20: Examination of Cause and Effect in Combination

This chapter examines whether cause and effect can be found in combination. It demonstrates that an effect is neither produced from a cause that has already ceased, from a cause that has not yet ceased, nor from a cause that is simultaneously ceasing. Since all three temporal possibilities fail, the combination of cause and effect cannot be established.

---

### Chapter 21: Examination of Arising and Perishing

This chapter examines whether arising and perishing (*saṃbhava* and *vibhava*) can be found. Arising and perishing cannot exist together (they are contradictory), nor separately (each presupposes the other), nor in sequence (the same temporal problems arise).

> "Arising and perishing cannot exist simultaneously;
> Arising and perishing cannot exist separately."

---

## Fascicles 11-12

### Chapter 22: Examination of the Tathāgata

This chapter examines whether the Tathāgata can be found. The Tathāgata is neither the aggregates, nor apart from the aggregates, nor possessing the aggregates, nor in the aggregates, nor having the aggregates in him.

> "The Tathāgata is not the aggregates,
> Nor is he apart from the aggregates."

After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, the fourfold negation applies: it cannot be said that the Tathāgata exists, does not exist, both exists and does not exist, or neither exists nor does not exist.

---

### Chapter 23: Examination of Error

This chapter examines wrong views (*viparyāsa*), particularly the four inversions: seeing permanence in the impermanent, pleasure in suffering, self in the selfless, and purity in the impure. It demonstrates that errors cannot be found in ultimate truth, because the basis of error (the aggregates) is itself empty.

---

### Chapter 24: Examination of the Noble Truths

This is one of the longest and most important chapters. An opponent from within the Buddhist tradition objects:

> "If all dharmas are empty, without arising and without ceasing,
> You say the Noble Truths have no substance;
> You incur this fault."

Because if everything is empty, there would be no suffering to know, no accumulation to abandon, no cessation to realize, and no path to cultivate. Furthermore:

> "Because the Noble Truths have no substance,
> The four fruits also do not exist.
> Because the fruits have no substance,
> Those who abide in the fruits also do not exist."

> "If there is no saṃgha-jewel,
> Then there are no eight persons.
> If the Noble Truths have no substance,
> There is also no dharma-jewel."

> "If there is no dharma and saṃgha,
> How can there be a buddha-jewel?
> If the Three Jewels are all empty,
> Then all existence is destroyed."

The author responds:

> "You now do not understand
> Emptiness and the meaning of emptiness,
> Which can extinguish all conceptual proliferations,
> And yet you wish to destroy emptiness?"

The crucial teaching on the two truths:

> "The buddhas, relying on two truths,
> Teach the dharma for sentient beings:
> One is conventional truth,
> The second is ultimate truth."

**Commentary:** Conventional truth refers to worldly speech, such as speaking of the arising, abiding, and ceasing of form and such; speaking of Devadatta going and coming, Viṣṇumitra eating food, Sumadatta practicing meditation, Brahmadatta achieving liberation. Such worldly speech is called conventional truth; these are not spoken of as ultimate truth.

What is ultimate truth? It is the foremost and has meaning, hence "ultimate meaning." It is the truth of the supreme non-discriminating wisdom, hence "ultimate truth." Truth means: free from other-conditioning, and so on, as its characteristic. If one abides in the domain of the true, with non-discriminating wisdom, this is called ultimate truth.

> "If a person cannot understand
> The distinction between the two truths,
> Then they do not understand the true,
> Profoundly deep buddha-dharma."

> "If one does not rely on conventional truth,
> One cannot attain ultimate truth.
> If one does not rely on ultimate truth,
> One can never attain nirvāṇa."

The opponent's fault is turned back upon them:

> "If you are correct about emptiness,
> Then everything is correct.
> If you are incorrect about emptiness,
> Then everything is incorrect."

> "You now carry your own fault
> And wish to give it to me,
> Like one riding a horse
> Who forgets the horse he is riding."

The chapter establishes that emptiness is not nothingness but is the very condition for the Noble Truths, the Three Jewels, and the entire Buddhist path to function:

"In ultimate truth, seeing non-arising and such is called seeing the Noble Truths." As the *Mañjuśrīvihāra-sūtra* says: "The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: 'If one sees all dharmas as non-arising, one understands the truth of suffering. If one sees all dharmas as non-abiding, one can abandon accumulation. If one sees all dharmas as ultimately nirvāṇa, one can realize cessation. Mañjuśrī! If one sees all dharmas as lacking self-nature, this is cultivating the path.'"

The chapter also includes the famous warning about misunderstanding emptiness:

> "Those of little wisdom and foolishness,
> With wrong views they destroy emptiness,
> Like one who does not skillfully grasp a snake,
> Or one who does not properly hold a spell."

---

## Fascicles 13-14

### Chapter 25: Examination of Nirvāṇa

This chapter examines the nature of nirvāṇa. Nirvāṇa is neither an existent thing (*bhāva*) nor a non-existent thing (*abhāva*), neither both nor neither.

If nirvāṇa is an existent thing, it would be conditioned and subject to arising and ceasing. If it is a non-existent thing, it would depend on existence and could not be liberation. If both, it would involve the faults of both.

> "Neither abandoned nor attained,
> Neither annihilated nor permanent,
> Neither ceasing nor arising:
> This is what is called nirvāṇa."

Saṃsāra and nirvāṇa are ultimately without distinction:

> "The limit of saṃsāra
> Is the limit of nirvāṇa.
> Between the two,
> There is not the slightest difference."

The fourteen unanswerable questions (whether the world is eternal or not, whether the Tathāgata exists after death or not, etc.) are shown to be based on grasping at self-nature, which does not exist in ultimate truth.

---

### Chapter 26: Examination of the Twelve Links of Dependent Arising in Conventional Truth

This chapter expounds the twelve links of dependent origination (*pratītyasamutpāda*) from the perspective of conventional truth: ignorance conditions formations, formations condition consciousness, and so on through old age and death.

> "With ignorance obscuring, the three kinds of actions are created;
> By these actions, one enters the various destinies."

> "With the cessation of ignorance, formations do not arise;
> The cessation of ignorance comes from practicing wisdom."

The chapter affirms that while the twelve links are taught conventionally, in ultimate truth all links are empty of self-nature. The cessation of ignorance through wisdom leads to the cessation of all suffering.

---

### Chapter 27: Examination of Wrong Views

This chapter examines the various wrong views about the self and the world: views about the past (whether the self existed or not), views about the future (whether the self will exist or not), and the fourteen unanswerable questions.

The four propositions about the past are examined: "I existed in the past," "I did not exist in the past," "I both existed and did not exist," and "I neither existed nor did not exist." All are shown to be untenable.

The four propositions about the future are similarly refuted. The four propositions about whether the world has an end (finite, infinite, both, neither) are also refuted by analyzing whether the previous aggregates continue into the next life:

> "If the previous aggregates perish
> And do not become the continuing cause of the later aggregates,
> This would be having an end."

> "If the previous aggregates do not perish
> And the later aggregates do not arise,
> Not depending on the previous aggregates,
> This would be having no end."

> "One portion having an end,
> One portion having no end,
> Apart from having and not having an end,
> There is no further 'both' or such."

In ultimate truth, all views are empty:

> "In this ultimate truth,
> Because all dharmas are empty,
> In what place, by what cause,
> What person gives rise to what views?"

---

## Closing Verses

**Commentary:** The Buddha Bhagavān, as the kinsman of the world, saw all sentient beings arising various seeds of suffering through vain discriminations and their various views, and aroused compassion. As the verse says:

> "The Buddha, to sever all sufferings,
> Proclaimed the subtle and wondrous dharma.
> With compassion as the cause,
> I now pay homage to Gautama."

**Commentary:** "Severing suffering" means severing the suffering of birth and death and all sufferings of all sentient beings. "Wondrous dharma" means pure, and therefore called wondrous. It can extinguish the fire of affliction-habituations, hence called "pure." Furthermore, because all causes of merit increase and are fully perfected, it is also called "pure." The wondrous dharma is the Mahāyāna. As the *Śrīmālā-sūtra* says: "World-Honored One! Embracing the wondrous dharma means protecting the Mahāyāna. Why? World-Honored One! Because all śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha vehicles are born from the Mahāyāna. Up to all worldly and supramundane wholesome dharmas are also born from the Mahāyāna. World-Honored One! Just as from Lake Anavatapta flow the four great rivers, so too does the Mahāyāna give birth to the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha vehicles. This vehicle has compassion, joy, equanimity, and loving-kindness as its cause, not worldly fame and profit."

"I now pay homage to Gautama" means: because he opens and reveals the unsurpassed jewel of the wondrous dharma, he is called Gautama. Furthermore, because his family name is Gautama, he is called Gautama. What is "homage"? There are two kinds: first, verbal praise; second, bowing with the body and placing the head upon the ground.

As the *Brahma-paripṛcchā-sūtra* verse says:

> "Deeply understanding the dharma of dependent arising,
> There are no wrong views.
> All dharmas belong to causes and conditions,
> With no self-determined root.
> The dharma of dependent arising does not arise,
> The dharma of dependent arising does not cease.
> If one can understand thus,
> All buddhas are always present before one."

The purpose of this chapter at the beginning was the opponent from our own school establishing a proof with faults; and because all views are empty, causing understanding. This is the chapter's meaning.

As the *Prajñāpāramitā* says, the Buddha told the Bodhisattva Extremely Courageous: "Know that form is neither a place for arising views nor a place for severing views. So too feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are neither places for arising views nor places for severing views. If form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are neither places for arising views nor places for severing views, this is called *prajñāpāramitā*."

Now, causing understanding through the distinct dependent arising of non-arising and such: the quelling of all conceptual proliferations, and the complete quiescence of all views of oneness, difference, and so on. This is the self-realized dharma, the dharma like space, the dharma without discrimination, the dharma of the domain of ultimate truth. Through such true nectar, causing understanding: this is the intent of the entire treatise.

**Question:** What the buddhas teach is true in the beginning, middle, and end. Why does this treatise need to extensively establish proofs?

**Answer:** There are sentient beings who are foolish and dull, who cannot have correct faith in the Buddha's āgamas. In order to attract those sentient beings, proofs are extensively established.

I now pay homage to the Ācārya Nāgārjuna, and therefore compose these verses:

> The son of the Dharma King, the Muni,
> The great-wisdom ācārya,
> With the wondrous principle of prajñā,
> Expounded this Madhyamaka-śāstra.
>
> Skillfully understanding the practice of benefiting others,
> As sun and moon illuminating the world,
> He revealed the most profound dharma,
> Teaching the cause of attaining buddhahood.
>
> What the ācārya has composed,
> I have now fully commented upon.
> To quell all wrong views,
> I composed the Prajñāpradīpa.
>
> This Prajñāpradīpa
> Is a profound and incomparable dharma.
> Yet what I have now composed,
> If it has the slightest merit,
>
> May this Lamp of Wisdom
> Embrace all classes of sentient beings,
> That they may see the Dharmakāya Tathāgata
> Pervading the buddha-fields of the ten directions,
>
> Attaining the self-realized dharma,
> Quelling the conceptual proliferations of all views,
> Quiescent, without discrimination,
> Incomparable, like space.
>
> And may the Lamp of Wisdom
> Universally illuminate the world,
> So that those covered by darkness
> May be established in nirvāṇa.

*End of the twenty-seventh chapter of the Prajñāpradīpa.*

---

## Colophon

The one who has reached the other shore of all treatises, of profound great wisdom, who rides the Mahāyāna, the great bodhisattva Bhāviveka (lit. "Discriminating Illumination"), having completed this commentary in long prose on the *Madhyamaka-śāstra*, made this aspiration:

> May by the merit of a single thought,
> Dedication and sympathetic joy and such,
> Together with all sentient beings,
> At life's end behold Maitreya.

*End of the Prajñāpradīpa, Fascicle 15.*
