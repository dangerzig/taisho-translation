# 天尊說阿育王譬喻經
## Sūtra of Parables Told by the Heavenly Honored One Concerning King Aśoka

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2044

Translator unknown.

---

Long ago there was a great king named Aśoka who ruled over many states, and none did not submit to him. The great king was brilliant and possessed immeasurable wisdom. He inquired of his ministers: "Is there anyone in the realm who does not belong to me?" The ministers replied: "The entire realm belongs to Your Majesty. There are none who have not submitted." Among them, one wise minister replied: "Within Your Majesty's borders, there is one who does not belong to the king. In the sea there is a nāga king who does not belong to Your Majesty. He has never sent a messenger nor offered tribute. By this I know he does not belong to Your Majesty. The king may test this." With a thousand chariots and ten thousand horsemen, beating drums and sounding bells, with banners and standards, he proceeded to the seashore. The nāga king remained still and did not come out. The king then called out: "You dwell within my domain. Why do you not come out?" There was silence, and no reply. The king then asked the wise minister: "How can I get the nāga king to come out?" The wise minister replied: "It can be done. The nāga king's merit and virtue are exceedingly great. That is why he is unwilling to submit. If Your Majesty does not believe my words, take a set of balanced scales and cast two gold images, each weighing two catties: one in the king's likeness, one in the nāga king's likeness. Then weigh them again. The nāga king's image will be heavier; Your Majesty's image will be lighter. By this you will know that the nāga king has more merit, and Your Majesty has less." The king's mind was greatly illuminated, and he rejoiced boundlessly. He proclaimed throughout the realm that the elderly and orphaned should be cared for, that the destitute and needy should be relieved, and that in all commanderies and counties, shrines to the Heavenly Honored One should be erected and establishments for the Heavenly Honored One's relics and offerings to the sangha should be built. After three years, the nāga image and the king's image were weighed again. The nāga image was now lighter, and the king's image was now heavier. The wise minister told the king: "The nāga can now be subdued." The royal procession was arranged as before, and they went again to the seashore. The nāga king transformed himself into a young brahmin, knelt before the king, and greeted him with inquiries about his health. He presented in tribute all the rare and precious jewels of the sea, declared himself a subject, and submitted. All the people of the realm rejoiced. This is recorded separately in the sūtras to show to future generations. In all the world, no power surpasses merit and virtue. One who guards the sūtras and Dharma is like a mother guarding her child. Is this not worth reflecting upon?

Long ago, when the Heavenly Honored One was in the world, he led his disciples, teaching and transforming sentient beings. He saw a piece of paper on the ground. The disciple Ānanda immediately picked it up. The Heavenly Honored One told Ānanda to put it back on the ground. Ānanda put it back on the ground, and his hand became very fragrant. Walking a little farther, they saw dry grass blown by the wind lying on the ground. Ānanda again picked it up. The Heavenly Honored One told Ānanda to put it back on the ground. His hand became foul-smelling. Ānanda did not understand. Shortly afterward, they arrived at the monastery, where the meaning should be asked about. Ānanda addressed the World-Honored One: "Why did touching the paper make my hand fragrant, and touching the grass make my hand smell foul?" The Heavenly Honored One told Ānanda: "The paper originally came from a place of incense. The fragrance adhered to the paper, and so it made your hand fragrant. The grass came from a place of foul odor, and so it made your hand smell foul. Associating with the worthy is like fragrance adhering to paper. Associating with evil people is like foul odor adhering to grass." Therefore the sūtras say: "Draw near to the worthy and become wise; draw near to the foolish and become more confused. Three things harm me; three things benefit me." This is what is meant. One cannot be anything but careful.

Long ago, the Heavenly Honored One led his disciples to a riverside. The Heavenly Honored One told his disciples: "Take a stone about the size of a fist and throw it into the water. Does it float or sink?" The disciples replied: "The stone sinks to the bottom of the water." The Heavenly Honored One said: "Without the right conditions, that is so." Then there was another stone, three feet square, placed upon the water. The sūtra then crossed the river, and the stone was not even wet. How could this be? The disciples did not understand and were all astonished. The disciples knelt and addressed the Buddha: "Why is this so?" The Heavenly Honored One said: "It is because of good conditions. What are the conditions? A boat." The Heavenly Honored One used this as a parable. One who encounters a good teacher is freed from all sufferings. One who encounters a bad teacher will practice evil deeds and not be separated from all misfortunes. This is told to future generations. One cannot be anything but careful.

Long ago, there was a poor, destitute, elderly man with no means of livelihood. He happened upon an axe at the market. It was the quintessence of precious substances, but he did not recognize it. He carried it about, chopping firewood to sell, sustaining his meager existence. The axe was nearly worn out. Then he met a foreign merchant, a great trader named Sārthavāha. Seeing the axe, the merchant recognized it and asked the old man: "Will you sell this axe?" The old man said: "I depend on this axe for chopping firewood to earn my living. I will not sell it." The merchant said: "I will give you a hundred bolts of silk. Why will you not sell it?" The old man, thinking himself mocked, did not respond. The merchant said: "Why do you not respond? I will give you two hundred bolts." The old man then became dejected and unhappy. The merchant said: "Do you think it is too little? I will add more. Why are you unhappy? I will give you five hundred bolts." The old man then began to weep loudly, saying: "I do not regret the small amount of silk. In my foolishness, this axe was originally a foot and a half long, and I have chopped the ground until it is nearly used up. Only five inches remain, and it is still worth five hundred bolts. This is what I regret!" The merchant said: "Do not harbor any more regret. I will now give you a thousand bolts." They immediately drew up a contract, and the merchant took it away. This axe was the quintessence of all precious substances. The merchant, regardless of the amount, placed the axe on top of firewood and burned it; everything transformed into precious gems. The Heavenly Honored One used this as a parable. Those who have received a human body with the six faculties complete, with intelligence and keen discernment, should seek out a wise teacher to find the path of liberation from the world. Supernatural powers can be attained. But those worldly people who are attached and cannot distinguish the true from the false, who are driven by the plowing seasons and the demands of the times, who reach old age and death and must again receive punishment: they are like the old man who used up the precious axe. Is this not a mistake?

Long ago, in the west there was a bird called a parrot. It was transported far and eventually came to be on Mount Tai in the east. The birds and beasts all treated it with respect and affection, because it had come from far away. They treated it as a close friend and cherished it greatly. In a spring month, a wildfire swept through the area. The parrot went into the water, flew over the fire, and shook the water from its feathers over the flames. It went back and forth many times, not just once, crying out loudly in distress. The water that a parrot could carry could hardly put out the fire. But its utmost sincerity moved heaven, which sent down rain on its behalf. The fire was immediately extinguished. The Heavenly Honored One's sūtras use this as a parable. The worthy one, like a practitioner who has come from afar, refining his practice with diligence, reduces his own food and drink, even depriving his wife and children's share, to make offerings to the sangha. Although he has no supernatural powers or special response, his utmost sincerity in burning incense and seeking deliverance is rewarded with immeasurable merit, like heavenly rain extinguishing all calamities.

Long ago, there was a great elder of immeasurable wealth who stored a thousand measures of grain in his cellar. When he later went to take it out, the grain was gone. In its place he found a small child, perhaps three years old, who could not yet speak. The elder did not know what it was called. He carried it and set it down beside the main road. Among the passersby, some might recognize it. From the east came a cart drawn by a yellow ox, with the driver wearing yellow garments and all his attendants also in yellow. Passing by and seeing the small child, they said: "Grain-thief! Why are you sitting here?" This child was the spirit of the five grains. They told the elder: "Bring a shovel and axe. I will tell you where to find a basin of gold." The one who had just passed was the gold spirit. "Follow the road west, then when it turns south, walk two hundred paces. West of the road there should be a rotten old tree. Beneath it is a jar containing ten measures of gold. Go and dig it up; it is enough to repay you for your grain." The elder followed the child's instructions and obtained it. He managed his household and became exceedingly wealthy. The Buddhist sūtras use this as a parable. Making offerings to the sangha and widely establishing the dāna assembly involve expenses, like losing grain. When a practitioner lectures on the sūtras, explains the meaning, and teaches people to abandon evil and pursue good, afterward one obtains immeasurable merit and can even attain the Way, like obtaining the jar of gold. This is told to future generations. Merit and virtue should not be left undone. Later regret will be too late.

Long ago, there was a prosperous and peaceful country that lacked nothing. The king told his great minister: "Send a capable envoy to other countries to purchase for me whatever we do not have." He dispatched a man who went to another country, amply furnished with precious goods. He surveyed every marketplace but found nothing unusual; everything was the same as in his own country. Finally, he saw a worthy man sitting alone in an empty shop. He asked: "I see that you have nothing for sale. Why are you sitting here alone?" The man replied: "I am here selling wisdom." He asked: "What does your wisdom look like, and how much do you ask?" The man replied: "My wisdom is worth five hundred taels of gold. Weigh out that amount of gold, and I shall tell it to you." The foreigner thought to himself: "In our country, no one sells wisdom to others." He then weighed out five hundred taels of gold and gave it to the man, who thereupon spoke words of wisdom, twenty characters in all: "Think carefully and deliberate at length. Do not act hastily in anger. Though today you may not use it, a time will come when you will." Thereupon he received and memorized these words until they were fluent, and each returned to his own home. The minister who had purchased the words of wisdom returned to his country and went by the path past his house. Entering at night under the bright moon, he saw two pairs of shoes before his wife's bed. Suspecting a stranger, he gave rise to evil thoughts. But his wife had suddenly fallen ill, and her mother had come to stay the night. He then spoke the words of wisdom and did not stop. His mother, startled awake, said: "My son! Have you returned?" He then ran out of the room and called out: "Worthless, worthless!" His mother asked what he meant. "How is it that you say you bought something? What did you get? Why do you say 'worthless'?" He said: "My mother would not give my wife for even ten thousand taels of gold to another. Yet she is priced at a mere five hundred taels: is that not worthless?" The Heavenly Honored One's sūtras use this as a parable. As the proverb says: "A single word of assistance surpasses the benefit of a thousand pieces of gold." This is what is meant.

Long ago, the Heavenly Honored One was at the bank of the Ganges River, broadly teaching the sūtra-dharma. At that time, devas, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, kings, people, flying birds, and running beasts all came to hear the Dharma. There was an old cowherd leaning on his staff and listening. Without realizing it, a toad was beneath his staff. The toad, listening to the sūtra, was delighted in mind and also did not notice the staff pressing on its back. After a long while, the toad died. Its spirit was immediately reborn in the heavens. Using the deva-eye, it observed its past: from what path had it come? It saw its former body in the form of a toad. The deva scattered heavenly flowers upon its former body. This is told to future generations. A toad, hearing the sūtra with a delighted mind, obtained rebirth in heaven. How much more so for a worthy person who listens to the sūtra with utmost sincerity. Is this not lofty indeed?

Long ago, there were two brothers. The younger pursued a wise teacher and became a śramaṇa. He attained the path of the arhat. He came repeatedly to tell his elder brother: "You should diligently create merit and virtue." The brother said: "I am busy right now; it will have to wait until later." This happened not just once or twice. The elder brother later died. The younger, with the Way-eye, observed in which path his brother's spirit had been reborn. He looked in the deva-path and the human path but did not see his spirit. He then looked in the hell-path and the hungry-ghost path but still did not see him. He was in the animal path. He saw that his brother's spirit had become a great ox. At that time, a merchant had yoked the ox and was trading on the road. The road was bad, and the ox slipped and fell in the mud, unable to get up. The merchant beat it with a stick, but the ox just stretched out its neck and would not rise. The younger brother, seeing his brother in this state, went directly to the front of the ox and said: "How is being busy now compared to how it was before?" The ox, ashamed and overcome with feeling, died on the spot. The practitioner then left. The merchants discussed among themselves, saying: "Why did the practitioner come and curse my ox to death?" They chased after the practitioner, brought him back, and asked him to explain. The practitioner told them the truth: "The ox was my elder brother. He did not follow my advice, and so he received this punishment." The practitioner then tossed his bowl into the air. It flew, and he followed after it. The merchants knew he was a sage. They then reproached themselves. They burned incense for the ox and made merit on its behalf. By this merit, the ox's spirit was reborn in the heavens.

Long ago, there was a worthy man who lived thirty li southeast of the city of Śrāvastī. His household revered the Dharma and made offerings to practitioners. The head of the household was fond of slaughtering pigs to sell their meat. The practitioner gradually became aware of this but had not yet had the chance to admonish him. The old man then died. He was reborn in the Ganges River as a ghost-spirit. An iron wheel with blades sharp as frost and snow followed the current and slashed him. The suffering was beyond words. One day later, the practitioner was crossing the Ganges and happened to meet the ghost-spirit. The ghost emerged halfway from the water and grabbed the boat, saying: "Seize the practitioner and throw him into the water! If not, I will kill everyone on the boat!" At that time, a worthy man asked the ghost-spirit: "Why do you seek this practitioner?" The ghost-spirit said: "When I was in the world, I made offerings to this practitioner. The practitioner knew in his heart that I was slaughtering pigs and selling meat, yet he did not admonish me. That is why I want to kill him." The worthy man then said: "You incurred this punishment because of your own pig-slaughter. Now you wish to kill the practitioner as well. Will your punishment not be even greater?" The ghost-spirit reflected and recognized the truth of the worthy man's words. He released the practitioner. The practitioner, once freed, returned and told the family's children and grandchildren, who performed merit-making on his behalf. The ghost's spirit was then freed from its suffering. This is told to future generations. A practitioner who accepts offerings must not fail to give admonishment.

Long ago, a man was walking on the road. He saw a dead person on the road, and a ghost-spirit was whipping it with a staff. The traveler asked: "This person is already dead. Why do you whip him?" The ghost-spirit said: "This is my former body. When I was alive, I was not filial to my parents, not loyal to my sovereign, did not respect the Three Honored Ones, and did not follow the teachings of my master and father. This caused me to fall into punishment with suffering beyond words. This is all because of my former body, and so I have come to whip it." Walking a little farther, he saw another dead person. A heavenly spirit descended and scattered flowers upon the corpse, gently stroking it with his hands. The traveler asked: "You appear to be a heavenly being. Why do you stroke this dead body?" The spirit replied: "This is my former body. When I was alive, I was filial to my parents, loyal and trustworthy in serving my sovereign, reverently devoted to the Three Honored Ones, and obedient to the teachings of my master and father. This caused my spirit to be reborn in heaven. This is all due to the kindness of my former body, and so I have come to repay it." The traveler, having seen these two scenes in one day, returned home and observed the five precepts and practiced the ten wholesome deeds. He was filial to his parents and loyal and trustworthy in serving his sovereign. This is told to future generations. Sin and merit follow a person, enduring and never ceasing. One cannot be anything but careful.

Long ago, there was a practitioner studying the Way in the mountains. He sent a novice to Śrāvastī to collect one measure of rice daily, along with a daily assignment of memorizing one verse. In the marketplace there was a worthy man who kept a shop. Seeing the novice mumbling as he walked, he asked: "You rush about seeking alms everywhere. Why do you mutter as you walk?" The novice replied: "My teacher is studying the Way in the mountains. He requires me to bring one measure of rice daily and assigns me one verse daily. That is why I walk along reciting one verse." The worthy man then asked: "If you did not have to deliver rice daily, how many verses could you memorize?" He said: "I could memorize ten verses." The worthy man then said: "Do not divide your efforts anymore. I will deliver the rice on the novice's behalf." The novice, overjoyed, was then able to sit quietly and study. The worthy man delivered rice for the novice for nine measures of rice. Afterward, he tested the novice on the sūtras, and the novice was fluent in all of them. The worthy man was later reborn in the world as a disciple of the Heavenly Honored One, named Ānanda. The Heavenly Honored One had twelve divisions of sūtras. The merit-reward of his intelligence is beyond question: hearing one, he understood ten. This is told to future generations. Merit follows the person like a shadow follows a form. Whatever one plants, each reaps the corresponding merit. It should not be left undone.

---

Long ago, there was a butcher who had a thousand head of cattle, which he fattened well. Each day he slaughtered one ox and sold the meat. After slaughtering five hundred, five hundred remained. The remaining cattle were leaping, frolicking, and butting one another. The Heavenly Honored One was entering the city at that time. Seeing the cattle behaving this way, he felt compassion and passed them by. He told his disciples: "These cattle are foolish. Their companions are nearly all gone, and yet they frolic together. People are the same. Day by day passes, and human life diminishes. One cannot fail to reflect and diligently seek the path of liberation from the world."
