# 菩薩生地經
## Sūtra of the Bodhisattva's Birthplace

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0533

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the land of Kapilavastu, at the Śākya clan's vihāra, beneath a nyagrodha tree, together with five hundred bhikṣus.

At that time, in the city there was a young man of the Śākya clan named Kṣemakara, who went to the Buddha's dwelling. After bowing his head in reverence, he sat to one side. With palms joined, he addressed the Buddha: "What practice enables a bodhisattva to swiftly attain the unsurpassed right and true path, to be fully endowed with the thirty-two marks, to travel from one buddha-land to another, to maintain an unconfused mind at the end of life, to never be reborn in the eight difficult circumstances, to always know the affairs of past and future, to perfect all dharmas and accomplish all matters, to know all dharmas without obstruction, to trust in and understand the practice of emptiness, to attain the patience of non-arising dharmas, to always wholeheartedly wish to become a śramaṇa, to never violate the precepts, and to find no delight in worldly attachments?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Kṣemakara, you ask about the practice of the bodhisattva. Patience is the foundation. By establishing the power of patience, one swiftly attains buddhahood. Patience has four aspects. What are the four? First, when reviled, to remain silent and not retaliate. Second, when struck and beaten, to endure and not strike back. Third, when faced with anger, to turn a compassionate mind toward it. Fourth, when disparaged, to not dwell on the other's ill will."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "When struck or reviled, not responding with anger;
> When disparaged, harboring no resentment.
> A bodhisattva who is patient in this way
> May attain all that was asked.
> Careful in speech, without deceit or arrogance,
> Never giving rise to turbulent thoughts,
> Not transgressing, not harboring evil:
> This practice attains buddhahood swiftly.
> The practitioner who always abides in patience,
> With sincere earnestness and great compassion,
> By this attains complete buddhahood,
> Radiant with the thirty-two marks.
> Those who pursue evil,
> Who are always fond of beating and punishment,
> Who inflict harm upon others:
> These do not leave the evil destinies.
> Wrong views and conceit,
> Impatience and fondness for anger:
> These lead one to cast oneself into darkness
> And never come near the bodhisattva.
> The fool who is greedy and overbearing,
> Self-willed and without propriety,
> Who does not know filial devotion to parents:
> For this there is the suffering of prison.
> To swiftly attain buddhahood,
> Always cultivate the foundation of precepts and virtue,
> Follow and accept the good teacher's instruction,
> And give equally to all.

"There are four more practices by which one may swiftly attain buddhahood. What are the four? First, to love and delight in the profound sūtras, to cherish the bodhisattva path, to wholeheartedly guard the Dharma, and to teach and guide others. Second, to keep far away from women and not associate with them. Third, to always delight in giving to śramaṇas and brahmins. Fourth, to refrain from sleep and drowsiness, and to train the mind in the practice of emptiness."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "If one delights in the śramaṇas
> And diligently guards the path of the sūtras,
> Loves the Dharma and does not stray from the teacher:
> Such a person is rare to find.
> To study deeply and seek the Buddha's intent,
> To be widely learned and broadly teach others,
> To delight in giving without a stingy mind:
> This practice attains buddhahood swiftly.
> Women are not to be approached;
> They corrupt virtue and disorder the world.
> One who pursues desire
> Has never drawn near the bodhisattva.
> Therefore the noble practitioner
> Always guards against and keeps far from feminine allure,
> Purely cultivating the bodhisattva path,
> With great compassion delivering the world."

Thereupon Kṣemakara removed the pearl and jewel ornaments from his body and scattered them above the Buddha. By the Buddha's supernatural power, what he had scattered remained suspended in midair and transformed into a jeweled canopy. Within it appeared five hundred transformation-beings, who also removed their pearl and jewel ornaments and scattered them above the Buddha, all proclaiming in unison: "May we give rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed right and true enlightenment!"

Kṣemakara, seeing the transformation-beings, was elated with joy and asked the Buddha: "Where do these transformations come from? Have they come from heaven? From the four directions and four intermediate directions? Have they arisen from the earth?"

The Buddha replied: "These transformations have not come from the ten directions. They are neither devas, nor nāgas, nor spirits, nor humans. They are neither earth, water, fire, wind, nor space. They are neither form, sensation, perception, volitional formations, nor consciousness. They are neither intention, nor mind, nor action, nor going, nor coming. They are neither of this life nor of the next life, neither of birth nor of death. They are called 'transformation,' with the name 'arisen from nothing,' and the designation 'emptiness.' They are like reflections appearing in a mirror: without grasping, without releasing, without origin, without attainment, without self, without person, without life, without consciousness. If any man or woman perceives and knows that all dharmas are like transformations, without consciousness, and hears this, trusts it, and practices accordingly, such a person is a child of the Buddha. They have left behind darkness and brought forth the light of the world. They are able to subdue Māra and accomplish the greatest virtue. They are a śramaṇa, a brahmin, a pure bodhisattva, a great person. They are one arisen from nothing, who has already received the prediction. They are an irreversible person of the unsurpassed path. To trust in and delight in this Dharma is to be like this."

The Buddha told Kṣemakara: "If anyone hears this sūtra and is startled, alarmed, or slanderous, mocking and ridiculing it, know that such people are not śramaṇas or brahmins. They are heterodox people of negligence, without sincerity or integrity, followers of evil teachers, people darkened and blinded without eyes, who falsely claim to be bodhisattvas. They are deceivers and aggressors."

At that time, the evil Māra came and asked the Buddha: "How many people are there who trust in this Dharma?"

The Buddha replied to Māra: "There are four hundred billion devas of the desire realm and humans who have all attained the bliss of non-arising dharmas and are established in this."

At that time, Kṣemakara attained the patience of non-arising dharmas. Five hundred bhikṣus, five hundred laymen, and twenty-five laywomen all attained establishment in the stage of non-retrogression. When their lives end, they shall all be reborn in the pure land of Amitāyus Buddha in the west. They shall always guard and maintain the Dharma of countless Buddhas, teach and bring to fulfillment all people, causing them to never retrogress. For immeasurable kalpas, numberless as the sands of the Ganges, they shall in turn become Buddhas in this land.

Māra, hearing the Buddha's words, slowly backed away, and said to the Buddha: "Hereafter, may you not need to teach this Dharma again!"

The venerable Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What should this sūtra be called? How should it be upheld?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "This sūtra is called the 'Sūtra of the Bodhisattva's Birthplace, as Asked by Kṣemakara.' It should be upheld accordingly. Even if one were to practice the five pāramitās for a hundred kalpas, without great wisdom and without the bodhisattva way, it would not compare to reciting this sūtra and its meaning and distributing and teaching it to others."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "If one trusts in and studies the Sūtra of the Birthplace,
> Its merit is beyond measure.
> Such a one has already crossed beyond the three evil destinies;
> Future receivers shall have this same fortune."

When the Buddha had finished speaking, Kṣemakara, the fourfold assembly of disciples, and all the devas, nāgas, and spirits were all filled with joy and received the teaching.
