# 大乘法界無差別論
## Treatise on the Non-Differentiation of the Mahāyāna Dharma-Realm

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1627

Composed by Bodhisattva Sthiramati (堅慧菩薩). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Devaprajñā (提雲般若).

---

> The dharma-realm is neither born nor destroyed,
> Without old age, illness, death, or the faults of the aggregates.
> Because from it the supreme mind of bodhi is aroused,
> I now bow my head in homage.

> The mind of bodhi in sentient beings is fully present,
> Able to produce the noble ones and the self-arisen.
> The ground upon which all wholesome dharmas depend
> Is like the earth, the ocean, and seeds.

> Those seeds are contained within the mother's womb,
> And like a wet-nurse, they nurture and develop.
> Faith and supreme wisdom are the root of bodhi;
> Great samādhi and great compassion nurture it, one should know.

> The naturally pure awakened mind is ever without stain,
> Like a great jewel or the vast sky,
> Like Mount Sumeru towering above all peaks:
> The birthplace of all white dharmas' treasures.

> Greed, hatred, delusion, and all the rest are completely severed;
> One is not dragged by the faults of afflictions.
> Surpassing the sands of the Ganges in capacities,
> The ten thousand dharmas are complete and radiant in their splendor.

> Purity, self, bliss, and permanence as pāramitās:
> One becomes worthy of offerings, the honored one of the ten directions.
> At the stage of cause, this is the mind of bodhi;
> When the fruit is full and virtue complete, it is called perfect enlightenment.

> That body can contain the marks of the dharma-realm;
> The light of wisdom is bright and clear, pure and without flaw.
> The dharmas of the mind of bodhi are inconceivable;
> All buddhas and tathāgatas praise and extol them.

> Without beginning, not fabricated,
> Without obstruction, also without end.
> Through the wisdom of emptiness and signlessness one understands:
> This is the realm of all buddhas and tathāgatas.

> That nature is the basis of all dharmas,
> Far removed from the two views of annihilationism and eternalism.
> The Dharma-body and the realm of sentient beings:
> Therefore the Buddha says they are originally without difference.

> Impure, pure-and-impure, and supremely pure:
> These should be known as sequential stages.
> The first is sentient beings, the next is bodhisattvas,
> The last is the tathāgatas, supremely pure.

> Polluted by dust and defilement, the nature is not clear,
> Like dense clouds obscuring the beautiful sun.
> When the net of clouds of afflictions is entirely liberated,
> The sun's orb shines and illuminates the whole of space.

> The kalpa-fire blazes fiercely in space,
> Yet the great void originally has no mark of being burned.
> Just so, the dharma-nature is not burned:
> The fire of old age, illness, and death cannot destroy it.

> All worldly dharmas of arising and ceasing
> Are entirely inseparable from empty space.
> Just so, within the unconditioned dharma-realm,
> The faculties depend on this to arise and cease.

> Like a lamp whose light, warmth, and color are combined:
> Apart from these three dharmas there is no lamp.
> Just so, the Buddha-dharmas and the body are together:
> Apart from these dharmas there is no separate body.

> Adventitious afflictions are without inherent nature
> And are primordially separate from the pure body.
> The not-empty, undefiled dharmas are concordant;
> Without severance or detachment, they constantly revolve.

> Like a lotus, after opening, covered by its leaves;
> Like gold, pure in nature, sunk in filth;
> Like the full moon swallowed by Rāhu:
> Unable to illumine the world, obscured by afflictions.

> Like pure water in a pond with wondrous flowers blooming;
> Like a golden mountain that mud and grime cannot pollute;
> Like the pure sky filled with stars and moon:
> When the wisdom of understanding shines in fullness, defilements are removed.

> Like the rising sun illumining the world,
> A thousand rays blazing and reaching everywhere;
> Like the earth, like the ocean, full of grain and jewels:
> Gaining liberation from birth-and-death, nourishing living beings.

> Ever abiding in birth-and-death, arousing wisdom and compassion,
> Without attachment to permanence, impermanence, and the rest.
> Dhyāna, dhāraṇī, and waters of purity:
> The Muni-King cloud, the cause of wholesome grain.

> This is the Dharma-body, this is the Tathāgata,
> Also called the Sacred Sovereign, true and perfect nirvāṇa.
> As water and coldness cannot be separated,
> The fruit of buddhahood and nirvāṇa are likewise so.

The treatise's summary verse says:

> Fruit, cause, and self-nature,
> Variant names and distinctions,
> Variant marks and the nature of non-defilement,
> Also called "constantly in union,"
> Existence, non-existence of meaning, and single nature:
> Briefly stated, there are twelve.
> Under the name "mind of bodhi,"
> One should know their sequence.

The commentary says: This is the body of the treatise.

Among these, first the fruit of the mind of bodhi is shown, then the capacity arising from that cause is explained. Once the cause has arisen, the self-nature, established characteristics, variant names, and distinctions are set forth. Following wherever one takes birth, since one is not defiled, it is said to be "constantly in union." Without any separate wholesome dharma being conjoined, abiding within afflictions is called "without meaning." When freed from the wrappings and purified, it is called "with meaning." It is also called "single nature," because both are nirvāṇa. One should know the twelve kinds of meaning in sequence.

Among these, what is the fruit of the mind of bodhi? It is the tranquil nirvāṇa of all buddhas, and nothing else. Why? Because the subtlest habitual tendencies have all been severed. "Without birth" means that the aggregates of the mentally-produced body are originally unborn. "Without old age" means that the capacity of tranquil function has grown supreme and reached its ultimate limit. "Without illness" means that all afflictive obstructions and cognitive obstructions have been permanently severed. "Without death" means that inconceivable transformation is without end. "Without aggregates" means that beginningless ignorance as the ground of abiding has been completely severed. "Without fault" means that all karma of body, speech, and mind is without fault, and also because one surpasses all capacities. From what is it obtained? From the mind of bodhi's supreme skillful means, the non-retrogressing cause, one can realize the fruit of nirvāṇa. What is called the "realm of nirvāṇa"? It is the inconceivable dharma-realm nature of all buddhas, the turning of the basis and the Dharma-body: this is the realm of nirvāṇa. Therefore, I now bow to that inconceivable mind of bodhi, whose cause and fruit grow and gradually become bright and full, like the first-quarter moon.

Furthermore, as for the seed of the mind of bodhi: it is the ground upon which all worldly wholesome dharmas and grain are produced and which serves as their abode, like the great earth. It is the place from which the Dharma-jewel of all noble ones emerges, like the great ocean. It is the sequential cause from which the bodhi-tree of all buddhas grows, like a seed. This is the fruit of the mind of bodhi.

Furthermore, how does one know the correspondence with its cause? It is like the son of a wheel-turning king. "Pure faith" is the seed of the mind of bodhi. "Supreme wisdom" is the supreme prajñā that can comprehend everything and is called the mother. "Samādhi" means that concentration serves as the womb, in which all wholesome dharmas abide peacefully, with well-being as its essence. "Great compassion" means that because one arouses great compassion toward all living beings, one does not weary of birth-and-death. It also can bring to fulfillment the all-knowing wisdom, nurturing the mind of bodhi as a wet-nurse does. Furthermore, the mind of bodhi arising from the union of those causes should be known as twofold. What are the two? First, the aspect of being defiled by afflictions; second, the aspect of the self-nature of white dharmas.

Among these, as for the defiled: that naturally pure and constant mind is not defiled, yet it is covered and obstructed by adventitious afflictions, just as fire and other things are naturally pure but obstructed and concealed by ashes, mud, clouds, dust, and the like. Just as fire, precious jewels, empty space, and water are by nature undefiled, when separated from ashes and the rest, the self-natures of fire and the rest are all purified. All sentient beings are likewise: the self-nature of their minds is equally pure, yet it is defiled by afflictions such as greed. If separated from greed and the rest, the mind becomes pure. Furthermore, how does one know that the aspect of white dharmas is also entirely pure? The naturally pure self-nature is the basis of all white dharmas, and all white dharmas also arise from it, just as Mount Sumeru produces jewels. The mind of bodhi is likewise: all skills and arts are brought to complete fulfillment, and one obtains the four great pāramitās. Therefore it is called the Tathāgata's Dharma-body.

As the sūtra says: "World-Honored One! The Dharma-body of the Tathāgata is the pāramitā of permanence, the pāramitā of bliss, the pāramitā of self, the pāramitā of purity. That Dharma-body of the Tathāgata is defiled by afflictions and secondary afflictions." The naturally pure mind is a variant name for this, as the sūtra says: "Śāriputra! This dharma of true suchness, of the dharma-realm, of the naturally pure mind, the dharma-body that corresponds to it: I teach this naturally pure mind for the sake of sentient beings as inconceivable."

Furthermore, that mind performs ten kinds of activities for sentient beings. Its mark of non-differentiation is as follows: it is unconditioned. Without beginning, because it is unborn. Without end, because it is unceasing. Without obstruction, because its self-nature is luminous. Through the wisdom of emptiness, one knows that all dharmas have a single-taste mark, because they are without nature. Without nature means without marks, because it transcends the domain of the sense-faculties. It is the practice of the noble ones, because it is the realm of the buddhas. It is the basis of all dharmas, because it is the ground of defiled and pure dharmas. Far from permanence, because defiled dharmas are impermanent. Far from annihilation, because white dharmas are not cut off.

This has three further distinctions in brief. "Impure" is the first designation, called the "realm of sentient beings." "Pure-and-impure" is the next designation, called "bodhisattvas." "Supremely pure" is spoken of as the Tathāgata. As the sūtra says: "Śāriputra! This very dharma-realm, wrapped in an eggshell of boundless afflictions exceeding the sands of the Ganges, tossed about by the waves of birth-and-death since beginningless time, coming and going, arising and ceasing, ever dwelling in the middle stream, is called 'sentient being.' Śāriputra! This very dharma-realm, boundlessly wearied of birth-and-death, not abiding in nirvāṇa, dwelling in all desire-realm existences, practicing the ten pāramitās, gathering the eighty-four thousand Dharma-gates, practicing the practice of bodhi: at this time it is called 'bodhisattva.' Śāriputra! This very dharma-realm, liberated from koṭis of afflictions, having crossed beyond all suffering, far removed from all afflictive latent tendencies, wrappings, and defilements, having realized the pure and supremely pure dharma-nature, dwelling therein, gazed upon by all sentient beings, abiding in all the grounds of experience, having obtained great power, without obstruction, without attachment, having obtained sovereign mastery over all dharmas, is called 'Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One.' Therefore, Śāriputra, there is no separate realm of sentient beings, no separate Dharma-body. The realm of sentient beings is the Dharma-body; the Dharma-body is the realm of sentient beings. This is non-dual in meaning, different only in words."

How is this further explained? In the impure state, one is defiled by afflictions, like dense clouds obscuring the beautiful sun. The naturally pure mind is without defilement; once the adventitious afflictions have been removed, the sun's orb shines brightly and fills all of space. Since there are birth, old age, illness, and death, how can one further say this nature is permanent? Just as the kalpa-fire burns empty space, the realm of space, being unconditioned, originally has no mark of being burned. The dharma-realm, being unconditioned, is likewise: the fire of old age, illness, and death cannot burn or destroy it.

Therefore the sūtra says: "World-Honored One! Worldly conventional speech speaks of death and birth, but the tathāgatagarbha does not have birth and death. World-Honored One! 'Death' means the former sense-faculties are destroyed; 'birth' means new sense-faculties arise. World-Honored One! The tathāgatagarbha is separate from the marks of the conditioned realm, constantly abiding and tranquil."

Since this treasury-nature is constantly abiding and unchanging, if it has not yet been activated, how can it be in accord with the Buddha-dharmas? Just as a lamp's light, warmth, and color have no separate marks, the Dharma and the Dharma-body are likewise. As the Buddha said: "Śāriputra! Just as a lamp's two dharma-functions are not different, namely its light and warmth-color, which are inseparable, or like the light, form, and color of a precious jewel: just so, Śāriputra, the Dharma-body as taught by the Tathāgata and its inseparable dharmas, the function of wisdom and its accomplishments, surpass the sands of the Ganges as tathāgata-dharmas."

As it is said: "World-Honored One! There are two kinds of tathāgatagarbha: empty and not-empty wisdom. What are the two? World-Honored One! The empty tathāgatagarbha is combined with the affliction-shell, without differentiation, not understanding liberation. The not-empty exceeds the sands of the Ganges: inseparable, undetached, undifferentiated, inconceivable Buddha-dharmas are accomplished and are called the Tathāgata's Dharma-body."

Why is it that the Dharma-body, replete with ten thousand virtues and complete in meritorious qualities, yet sentient beings cannot obtain liberation? It is like the lotus: covered by the leaves of the net of wrong views. Like true gold: fallen into the impurity and filth of doubt and confusion. Like the full moon: swallowed by the Rāhu of conceit. Like pure water in a pond: muddied by the dust and grime of desire. Like a golden mountain: smeared with the mud of anger. Like the great void: entirely covered by the clouds of delusion. Like the sun not yet risen: obstructed by the ground of beginningless ignorance, dwelling within the womb of the great aggregate of the six sense-bases. Like the container-world whose marks have not yet formed. Like the rain that has not yet fallen because conditions have not yet come together.

> Lotus, gold, full moon,
> Pond-water, golden mountain, space,
> Like the sun, the great earth, and clouds:
> The Buddha-nature is stained by adventitious defilements.
> When afflictions cover its capacities,
> Buddha-activity cannot be performed.
> In brief, nine similes are stated;
> One should know the reverse as the defiled and purified.

By this, the Tathāgata's Dharma-body is likewise: because all afflictive adventitious defilements have been separated and exhausted, and because the self-nature's capacities are complete, one becomes worthy of offerings. All sentient beings share in this equally, realizing the permanent, tranquil, cool, and inconceivable realm of nirvāṇa. This is called Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One. It is not the case that apart from the Tathāgata's Dharma-body there is a separate nirvāṇa.

As it is also said: "When the realm of sentient beings attains purity, one should know that this is the Dharma-body." The Dharma-body is the realm of nirvāṇa; nirvāṇa is the Tathāgata. As the sūtra says: "World-Honored One! Unsurpassed perfect enlightenment is nirvāṇa. World-Honored One! The realm of nirvāṇa is the Tathāgata's Dharma-body. World-Honored One! Apart from the Dharma-body there is no separate Tathāgata. World-Honored One! The Tathāgata is the Dharma-body; these are not different." This is the cessation of suffering and the rest. Therefore the sūtra says: "World-Honored One! It is not because dharmas of destruction are destroyed that it is called 'cessation of suffering.' Rather, the cessation of suffering is without beginning, without fabrication, without birth, without arising, without cessation, without exhaustion, free from exhaustion, constantly abiding, unmoving, tranquil. It is the naturally pure mind that has destroyed all affliction-shells. Surpassing the Ganges River's sands, inseparable, undetached, inconceivable Buddha-dharmas are complete and accomplished: this is called the Tathāgata's Dharma-body. World-Honored One! This is the tathāgatagarbha. While not yet freed from the affliction-shell, it is called the tathāgatagarbha. World-Honored One! The tathāgatagarbha is the empty wisdom of all tathāgatas. World-Honored One! The tathāgatagarbha has never been seen, never been obtained by any śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha. Only the Tathāgata has realized and verified it, and has destroyed all affliction-shells and cultivated the path that is the cessation of all suffering. Therefore, as water and coldness, the nature of enlightenment and nirvāṇa are non-dual and without difference."

Some say there is a single-vehicle nature and aspiration, or else no nirvāṇa. Others say that within the single dharma-realm there is a lesser nirvāṇa, a middling nirvāṇa, or a great nirvāṇa. This is not so. If the lower, middling, and upper causes were transformed into the fruit, it should be one. Since the causes are differentiated, the fruits are also differentiated. Therefore it says: "World-Honored One! There is no lower, middling, or upper that obtains nirvāṇa. World-Honored One! Equal dharma, equal wisdom, equal liberation, equal knowledge-and-vision of liberation: these realize nirvāṇa. Therefore, World-Honored One, the realm of nirvāṇa is of one taste and equal taste, namely the taste of understanding and liberation."

---

*Note: This is what the Song and National editions call the "Treatise on the Non-Differentiation of the Dharma-Realm," translated by Devaprajñā. Upon examining the Kaiyuan Catalog and Fazang's commentary, the cinnabar edition with twenty-four verses in five-character lines is truly the one translated by Devaprajñā and commented upon by Fazang. Moreover, this treatise is listed in the catalog and commentary as a single translation; yet the National and Song editions, though differing in wording from the cinnabar edition, are not different in meaning, and must be a retranslation by a later generation after the Kaiyuan era. However, it is unknown which era or whose translation it is, and this awaits further investigation. The attribution in the two editions directly to Devaprajñā as translator is erroneous.*
