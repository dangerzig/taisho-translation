# 五陰譬喻經
## Sūtra of the Similes for the Five Aggregates

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0105

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the country of Mṛgadāva, and while crossing a river ford he saw a great mass of foam floating along with the current. He then addressed the bhikṣus: "Bhikṣus! It is like this great mass of foam floating along with the current. A person of discernment, seeing it, observes and examines it, and then knows it to be insubstantial, empty, unreal, quick to dissolve, and coming to an end. Why? Because foam has no solidity. Just so, bhikṣus! All form, past, future, and present, internal and external, coarse and fine, beautiful and ugly, far and near: a bhikṣu seeing this should carefully observe and examine it, seeing that it is insubstantial, empty, unreal. It is only disease, only bondage, only a wound, only falsity; not true, not lasting; suffering, empty, without self, destined for dissolution. Why? Because the nature of form has no solidity.

"It is like, bhikṣus, when rain falls from the sky and water bubbles appear: one bubble arises and another immediately bursts. A person of discernment, seeing them, observes and examines them, and then knows them to be insubstantial, empty, unreal, quick to dissolve, and coming to an end. Why? Because bubbles have no solidity. Just so, bhikṣus! All feeling, past, future, and present, internal and external, coarse and fine, beautiful and ugly, far and near: a bhikṣu seeing and knowing this should carefully observe and examine it, seeing that it is insubstantial, empty, unreal. It is only disease, only bondage, only falsity, only a wound; not true, not lasting; suffering, empty, without self, destined for dissolution. Why? Because the nature of feeling has no solidity.

"It is like, bhikṣus, a mirage shimmering at midday in the late summer heat. A person of discernment, seeing it, observes and examines it, and then knows it to be insubstantial, empty, unreal, quick to dissolve, and coming to an end. Why? Because a mirage has no solidity. Just so, bhikṣus! All perception, past, future, and present, internal and external, coarse and fine, beautiful and ugly, far and near: a bhikṣu seeing this should carefully observe and examine it, seeing that it is insubstantial, empty, unreal. It is only craving, only bondage, only a wound, only falsity; not true, not lasting; suffering, empty, without self, destined for dissolution. Why? Because the nature of perception has no solidity.

"It is like, bhikṣus, a person seeking good timber who takes an axe and enters the forest. Seeing a great plantain tree, tall, straight, and unbent, he cuts its base, lops its top, and strips its leaves. Splitting it layer by layer he finds no core inside; how could there be anything solid? A person of discernment, seeing it, observes and examines it, and then knows it to be insubstantial, empty, unreal, quick to dissolve, and coming to an end. Why? Because the plantain tree has no solidity. Just so, bhikṣus! All formations, past, future, and present, internal and external, coarse and fine, beautiful and ugly, far and near: a bhikṣu seeing this should carefully observe and examine it, knowing that it is insubstantial, empty, unreal. It is only craving, only bondage, only a wound, only falsity; not true, not lasting; suffering, empty, without self, destined for dissolution. Why? Because the nature of formations has no solidity.

"It is like, bhikṣus, a skilled conjurer and his apprentice who, at a great crossroads before an assembly of people, display various magical illusions, conjuring herds of elephants, herds of horses, chariots, and foot soldiers. A person of discernment, seeing them, observes and examines them, and then knows them to be insubstantial, empty, unreal, formless, illusory, and coming to an end. Why? Because illusions have no solidity. Just so, bhikṣus! All consciousness, past, future, and present, internal and external, coarse and fine, beautiful and ugly, far and near: a bhikṣu seeing this should carefully observe and examine it, seeing that it is insubstantial, empty, unreal. It is only craving, only bondage, only a wound, only falsity; not true, not lasting; suffering, empty, without self, destined for dissolution. Why? Because the nature of consciousness has no solidity."

Thereupon the Buddha spoke in verse:

> Foam is the simile for form; feeling is like a water bubble;
> Perception is likened to a summer mirage; formations are like a plantain tree;
> Illusion is the simile for consciousness: thus have the Buddhas taught.
> One should observe these essentials, carefully examining and reflecting.
> That emptiness is the truth one sees; nowhere does one find permanence.
> To see the aggregates, one should do likewise: so the true wisdom teaches.
> When the three things are cut off, one knows the body has no worth.
> When life-breath, warmth, and consciousness leave the body and pass on,
> Lying dead upon the ground, like grass, knowing nothing:
> Observing this, see how it is, mere illusion, yet fools are greedy.
> The mind, the mind has no rest, nor has it any solidity.
> Knowing the five aggregates are thus, bhikṣus should be diligent.
> Therefore, day and night, with self-awareness and right knowledge,
> Practice the path of quiescence: this is the supreme and most peaceful happiness.

The Buddha spoke thus. The bhikṣus, having heard, all rejoiced.
