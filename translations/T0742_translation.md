# 佛說自愛經
## Sūtra of Self-Love, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0742

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time the king of the country went to the Buddha's dwelling. Seeing the monastery from afar, he descended from his carriage, removed his canopy, took off his sword and shoes, and with palms joined went straight ahead. He prostrated his entire body upon the ground, bowed at the Buddha's feet, then knelt and addressed him: "I wish tomorrow, at the four crossroads, to invite the Buddha and the Saṃgha to a modest meal, so that the ignorant people may know the Buddha's supreme honor. Seeing the Buddha's dignified manner, they will transmit it for generations as a model. I wish that all sentient beings may be kept far from ghosts, demons, and sorcery, and that all may uphold the five precepts, thereby eliminating the calamities of the nation!"

The World-Honored One said: "Excellent, excellent! As a king, it is fitting to have a wise guide, to lead the people by the path, and to seek future merit. When I was formerly a king, I too made offerings to the Buddhas, śramaṇas, and brahmins, constantly practicing the four immeasurables and the six perfections, diligently cultivating until I attained Buddhahood, majestic and supreme."

The king said: "Truly! It is just as the Buddha teaches. If one does not plant the seed, there is no way to obtain the fruit. I have received the Buddha's grace and was born in this world as a human being, having left the female body and obtained the male, with all six sense-faculties complete. By fortunate circumstance I encounter a Buddha dwelling in the world, whose radiant Dharma-transformation is in my country. The accumulated good is beyond measure. I beg leave to withdraw and make preparations."

The World-Honored One said: "Excellent, excellent!"

The king returned to his palace. He leveled and prepared the great road, making the high and low places even and smooth. He broadly set up curtains and canopies and erected banners and flags. From the four crossroads to the monastery gate, along both sides of the road he placed railings, hung lamps like stars, and at every step placed incense burners. Heavenly music and various entertainments sang of the Buddha's supreme spiritual power and praised the virtue of the śramaṇas' pure integrity. Flowers and miscellaneous treasures were scattered like rain, the ground was sprinkled with fragrant water, and fine carpets were spread. The king personally stayed up all night, preparing the feast with his own hands. He went in person to invite the Buddha, bowed his head to the ground, and knelt: "May the World-Honored One, with great compassion, grace us with his presence and thereby deliver sentient beings!"

The Buddha arose and put on his Dharma-robe. Together with all the śramaṇas he went to the four crossroads. The king and his ministers flanked them on left and right. When the Buddha arrived and took his seat, the queen, the crown prince, and all bowed to the ground, rolling up their sleeves and removing their stockings. After water for washing was passed, the king served the food with his own hands. When the Buddha had finished eating, the king bowed his head and said: "Today I have offered this modest meal. May devas, humans, ghosts, dragons, and all flying and crawling creatures, in life after life, encounter the Buddha, encounter the Dharma, and encounter the Saṃgha assembly. May they leave behind the filth and stench of the world and embrace the right truth of the Buddha."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! As the king is the father and mother of the people, nourishing them with kindness and guiding them with great illumination, whatever you wish will be attained!"

The king said: "When the people of the land part from one another, they all say 'Love yourself.' What is the essential meaning of self-love?"

The World-Honored One exclaimed: "An excellent question! When a person dwells in the world harboring poisonous thoughts in the mind, speaking poisonous words with the mouth, and performing poisonous deeds with the body: these three things issue from the mind, the body, and the mouth. They bring about evil and inflict it upon sentient beings. Sentient beings, suffering from the poison, form resentment and hatred and vow in their hearts to seek revenge. Sometimes retribution is received in the present life; sometimes, after death, the spirit ascends to heaven and then descends to take revenge. Among humans, animals, ghosts, spirits, and the hells, beings harm one another in turn, all due to past karma; this does not arise from nothing. The three of the body, four of the mouth, and three of the mind are all evil. The foolish indulge in them, being unfilial to their parents, worshipping ghosts and demons, engaging in lust, drunkenness, and rebellion, descending into low and base impurity, thereby bringing about the catastrophe of endangering the body and destroying the clan. At death they enter the cruelty of boiling water and fire in the hells, and for a long time they do not obtain a human body. Far from the Buddha and from what is right, they take no delight in the pure precepts of the śramaṇas and always associate with the foolish. These are people who delight in the catastrophe of ruin and do not love themselves!"

The king said: "Excellent, excellent! I wish only for the Buddha's instruction! May I hear the principle of self-love?"

The Buddha said: "The method of self-love is: first, take the three refuges. Nourish one's parents with the Dharma. Show kindness and love toward all beings. Have compassion and pity for the foolish and deluded. Seeing what is right, rejoice and advance. Be impartial and universally protective. Bring peace and deliverance to sentient beings. Bestow these four kinds of grace. Practice generosity to the poor and destitute. When sentient beings bear no resentment, all the devas will nurture and protect one. No calamity will strike. Prisons, sharp swords, and all poisons will be dispelled. One's family will be at peace and one's clan will flourish. In life there will be no disaster or affliction, and at death one will ascend to the heavens, always meeting with the illuminated. This is what it means to love oneself!"

The king said: "Excellent! I wish only for the Buddha's instruction!"

"Those who are truly lofty in conduct and worthy, who are pure and upright, guarding the truth: impure gain and deviant pleasure do not stain their minds. The four evils of the mouth they do not speak. The three evils are kept far from the body. They risk their lives to perfect their conduct. All the Buddhas treasure them. Their families are at peace and their clans flourish. In the end they ascend to the heavens and always meet with good fortune. This is what it means to love oneself!"

The king said: "Excellent! I wish only for the Buddha's true instruction!"

"When all manner of poisonous wrongs are inflicted, one endures in silence without complaint. With kindness and compassion one pities the perpetrators and forever seeks to deliver them. One is diligent without slacking and plants the mind in the Three Honored Ones. Outwardly calm and inwardly still, one cultivates mindfulness at the root of the path. Deeply contemplating the noble aim, one illuminates and transforms with true words. Being filial to parents and having delivered oneself, one guides the multitude to do likewise. One always meets with good fortune. This is what it means to love oneself!"

The king said: "Excellent! I wish only for the Buddha's true instruction!"

Among the countless onlookers, there were two merchants. One thought: "The Buddha's body is sixteen feet tall, of golden hue like purple gold, with a fleshy protuberance on the crown and sunlight at the nape, majestic beyond words. The Buddha is like an emperor, and the śramaṇas are like loyal ministers. The Buddha sets forth the luminous Dharma, and the śramaṇas recite and transmit it. This king is truly wise to know the Buddha's worthiness!" The Buddha knew his thought and gazed at him intently. The man's heart was filled with joy, like one who has found a treasure. The other merchant thought: "This king is a fool, deluded! Being a ruler of the country, what more could he want? The Buddha is like an ox, and the disciples are like a cart. The ox pulls the cart east, west, south, and north; the Buddha is just like that. What path do you have that you lower yourself to serve him?" The Buddha knew his evil thought and knew he would surely suffer its consequences, and with sorrow felt pity for him. The man's heart became fearful, as though something were about to befall him.

The two departed together and stopped to rest thirty leagues away. They bought wine and drank, and together settled their business accounts, arguing noisily. For the one with good thoughts, the Four Heavenly Kings dispatched good spirits to protect him. For the one with poisonous thoughts who slandered the Buddha, the ghosts of the hells caused the wine to enter his bowels like fire burning the body. He left the resting place and lay down on the road, and soon rolled into a cart rut. At dawn a caravan of five hundred carts came and crushed him to death. His companion searched for him and, seeing what had happened, said: "My fortune is ruined! If I return to our country, I will be suspected of stealing his goods. To leave would be dishonorable." So he abandoned his wealth and left with only his person, journeying far away.

Many tens of thousands of leagues from Śrāvastī there was a country whose king had died without a crown prince. A book of prophecy stated: "In the central lands there is a man of humble station who will become king of this country." The ministers deliberated: "A country without a ruler is like a body without a head; it cannot stand for long! The late king had a horse that always performed the ritual of bowing before the king. If there is anyone fit to be king, the horse will surely kneel!" All said: "An excellent plan!" They immediately prepared the royal carriage, placed the king's seal and sash upon it, and the people and horses filled the road. All the onlookers wept without exception.

The merchant also came out to watch. The Grand Historian of the country said: "There is a yellow cloud canopy above that man; it is an aura of royalty!" The divine horse went straight forward, knelt, and licked the merchant's feet. The ministers rejoiced. They bathed him in fragrant water and enthroned him as king, and all declared themselves his subjects. The king said: "I was originally a merchant, with no virtue toward the people. I am not fit for the throne!" The ministers said: "Heaven bestows it upon the virtuous, and the divine horse has knelt!" Thereupon he took up residence in the royal palace and attended to the governance of the country. He reflected deeply: "I have no slight merit; how have I obtained this? It must be the Buddha's grace that has made it so!" Early in the morning, upon the throne, he praised the Buddha's supreme holiness. He led all his ministers and, facing toward Śrāvastī, bowed his head: "This humble person, having received the World-Honored One's blessings, has become a king of men. This country has been passed down for generations without knowledge of the Buddha; even in the secular books there is no record. I pray that with the great illumination, you will open the deafness and blindness of this country's people! Tomorrow I wish the Buddha and the assembly of realized ones to kindly grace us with your presence for three months!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Instruct all the bhikṣus! Tomorrow that king has extended an invitation. All should proceed slowly, manifesting transformations and displaying the spiritual power of honored virtue, so that the people of that country may all behold it!" When the devas heard that the Buddha was going there to teach, they all accompanied him in procession, playing music and singing of his virtue. Jeweled canopies, banners, and flags were displayed, and flowers fell in profusion. The radiant light dazzled the eyes. The Buddha and the realized ones all took their seats in the main hall. The king, following the model of the king of Śrāvastī's offering and the luminous Dharma, personally served the food. When the meal was finished, he sat on a small bench before the Buddha. The Buddha broadly taught the Dharma.

The king said: "I was originally a man of humble station, with no fine virtue at all. How have I obtained this?"

The Buddha told the king: "Formerly, when that king offered food to the Buddha, a man thought: 'The Buddha is like a king, and the śramaṇas are like ministers.' The king planted that seed and has now reaped its fruit. The other man said: 'The Buddha is like an ox, and the disciples are like a cart.' He himself planted the seed of being crushed by a cart, and now in the hells he is being crushed by a wheel of fire, reaping his own fruit. This is not something that courage or strength can bring about! When one does good, merit follows. When one walks in evil, calamity pursues. Like an echo answering a sound, good and evil are like their tones. This is not done by devas, dragons, ghosts, or spirits, nor by the spirits of ancestors. The mind creates it; the body and mouth accomplish it!"

The Buddha spoke these verses:

> "The mind is the source of all dharmas.
> The mind is honored, the mind directs.
> If within the mind one creates evil,
> Then speaks it and acts upon it,
> Sinful suffering pursues of itself,
> Like a cart wheel following the rut.
> The mind is the source of all dharmas.
> The mind is honored, the mind directs.
> If within the mind one thinks of good,
> Then speaks it and acts upon it,
> Fortunate happiness pursues of itself,
> Like a shadow following form."

The World-Honored One further told the king: "Among all evil sins, the five most grave are these. Being unfilial and disloyal, killing one's parents, killing one's ruler: the household is destroyed and the nation is thrown into chaos. This is the first grave sin. Arhats, whose practice has attained the concentrations of emptiness, wishlessness, and signlessness, whose minds are equal to the Buddha's, who rescue and deliver sentient beings: if a fool harms such a person, this is the second grave sin. The Buddha is one whose sins are all exhausted and whose radiant merit is fully accomplished, endowed with the marks and the ten powers, guiding sentient beings with the Dharma, with kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and protection, whose heart surpasses a loving mother's: if a fool speaks evil slander against him, this is the third grave sin. Pure śramaṇas whose resolve is pure and whose conduct is lofty, who embrace the sūtras and Dharma and assist the Buddha in transforming the deluded: all the Buddhas succeed each other in succession, and sentient beings attain liberation, all because of the Saṃgha. If flattering and slanderous people create discord and cause the Saṃgha to be disrupted, when the Saṃgha is disrupted, the governance and the Dharma are ruined, and the people run wild. When governance and the Dharma are ruined and the people run wild, the three evil paths flourish. To trouble the bhikṣu-Saṃgha is the fourth grave sin. The honored temples of the Buddha, their precious goods, water, and land: sentient beings with sincere hearts offer these to the Three Honored Ones. If a fool destroys or steals them, this is the fifth grave sin. Those who commit these five: their sins admit of no reprieve. They kill themselves, destroy their own clans, and throw themselves into the fire of the hells! The weight of these five sins is heavier than Mount Sumeru. Be extremely careful never to commit them!"

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, the king and all the ministers attained the fruit of srotāpanna. They received the five precepts and became upāsakas. Among the country's people, some became śramaṇas and some upheld the precepts as upāsakas. They adopted the five precepts and the ten wholesome deeds as the law of the land, and the devas gave their protection, so the country flourished. All the devas, dragons, spirits, the king, ministers, and common people rejoiced without exception.
