# 普法義經
## Sūtra on the Universal Meaning of the Dharma

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0098

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra invited the bhikṣus to listen as he taught the Dharma, good in the beginning, good in the middle, good at the end, well expounded and distinguished, complete, pure, and clear. He invited them to hear of the practice of a worthy one, called the Complete Dharma Practice. "You should listen with good minds and careful attention." The bhikṣus assented to the worthy one's words and listened to the Venerable Śāriputra.

The Venerable Śāriputra then taught: "There are twelve opportune gatherings that can bring one to the path of a worthy one. What are the twelve? First, being able to teach oneself. Second, being able to teach others. Third, being born among humans. Fourth, being born among worthy ones. Fifth, having complete faculties. Sixth, not following worldly pursuits. Seventh, rejoicing upon seeing worthy ones. Eighth, having a Buddha present. Ninth, having the Dharma taught. Tenth, being able to receive the taught Dharma. Eleventh, being able to listen and receive from outside. Twelfth, being able to practice in accordance with what one has obtained. These are the twelve opportune gatherings of a worthy one, the path attained through this practice.

"If one wishes to teach the Dharma to another, one should teach with twenty qualities. What are the twenty? First, teaching well. Second, teaching abundantly. Third, teaching with beginning and end. Fourth, teaching in proper sequence. Fifth, teaching with joy. Sixth, teaching what is appropriate. Seventh, teaching to resolve doubts. Eighth, teaching without shame. Ninth, teaching without finding fault. Tenth, teaching with moderation. Eleventh, teaching what is fitting. Twelfth, teaching without scattering. Thirteenth, teaching the Dharma. Fourteenth, teaching in accordance with the audience. Fifteenth, teaching with equanimity of mind. Sixteenth, teaching with a protective mind. Seventeenth, not teaching for the sake of fame. Eighteenth, not teaching for the sake of profit. Nineteenth, not using the teaching to display oneself. Twentieth, not using the teaching to disparage others. If a worthy bhikṣu wishes to teach the Dharma to others, one should teach with these twenty qualities."

Śāriputra further told the bhikṣus: "One who wishes to hear the Dharma should have sixteen practices. What are the sixteen? First, listening at the proper time. Second, listening abundantly. Third, listening with attentive ears. Fourth, listening with purpose. Fifth, not criticizing. Sixth, not finding fault. Seventh, not seeking to find flaws. Eighth, respecting the Dharma. Ninth, respecting the Dharma speaker. Tenth, not disdaining the Dharma. Eleventh, not disdaining the Dharma speaker. Twelfth, not disdaining oneself. Thirteenth, having a focused mind. Fourteenth, not having a distracted mind. Fifteenth, holding the mind rightly. Sixteenth, being aware of every thought while hearing the Dharma.

"If a worthy one wishes to hear the Dharma, one should listen according to these sixteen practices. What are the sixteen? First, listening at the proper time. Second, listening abundantly. Third, listening with attentive ears. Fourth, listening with purpose. Fifth, not criticizing while listening. Sixth, not seeking faults while listening. Seventh, respecting the Dharma while listening. Eighth, respecting the sūtra teacher while listening. Ninth, not disdaining the Dharma while listening. Tenth, not disdaining the Dharma speaker while listening. Eleventh, not disdaining oneself while listening. Twelfth, listening with a focused mind. Thirteenth, listening without a distracted mind. Fourteenth, listening with right and balanced attention. Fifteenth, listening with fully unified mind. Sixteenth, listening with settled mindfulness.

"If a worthy one wishes to hear the Dharma, one should listen according to these sixteen comparisons. Having heard the Dharma as described above, faith and a pleasing mind arise; from this one arrives at the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, the worthy one's love arises, free from desire; from this one reaches the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, joyful love of mind arises; from this one reaches the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, one abandons unwholesome attachment and the mind becomes settled; from this one reaches the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, one abandons doubt, wrong views, and regains clarity; from this one reaches the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, one sees the aggregates as insubstantial, sees the aggregates as empty, sees the aggregates as light; having seen, the mind is liberated, the mind is purified, the mind is stilled, the mind is freed; thus from this one reaches the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, one sees all worldly formations as empty, no longer dwells in them; craving exhausted, dispassion attained, cessation; one can attain the unconditioned, the mind turns and is stilled, liberation is attained; from this one reaches the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, the mind aspires to practice in accordance, sitting alone and cutting off delusion, attaining the foremost aspiration; from this one reaches the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, the pure eye sees the four truths; from this one reaches the unconditioned. Having heard such Dharma, one's practice is complete; from this one reaches the unconditioned.

"Having heard such Dharma, the noble disciple of a worthy one does not distress the speaker, and from hearing obtains happiness, does not transgress the teaching, follows in peace, and the fulfillment of one's own aspiration comes about through these ten dharmas, arising from wise practice. What are the ten? First, having good friends. Second, having good moral conduct. Third, having good fellow practitioners. Fourth, knowing how to receive and understand. Fifth, receiving instruction. Sixth, asking questions. Seventh, hearing the sūtras. Eighth, gathering and teaching the sūtras. Ninth, being frightened by what is truly frightening. Tenth, having been shamed and frightened, contemplating this as the root. Through root contemplation, one severs all unwholesome dharmas; being able to sever through root contemplation and being able to reject these dharmas, the mind becomes settled and attains mastery. All from root contemplation; thus in truth, having abandoned, the noble disciple of the path proceeds.

"One can then practice the ten contemplations. What are the ten? First, contemplation of impurity. Second, contemplation of impermanence. Third, from impermanence, contemplation of suffering. Fourth, from suffering, contemplation of non-self. Fifth, contemplation of repulsiveness of food. Sixth, contemplation that in all the world there is no pleasure. Seventh, contemplation of death. Eighth, contemplation of light. Ninth, contemplation of turning away the mind. Tenth, contemplation of cessation.

"The contemplation of impurity, worthy ones, follows fourteen wrong dharmas. What are the fourteen? First, dwelling with one's former household and losing mindfulness. Second, seeing one's former household and craving. Third, hasty desire. Fourth, not understanding the meaning of the contemplation of impurity. Fifth, not being able to contemplate impurity. Sixth, associating and working together with people of wrong conduct. Seventh, not knowing what is right. Eighth, not serving. Ninth, not questioning. Tenth, not guarding the senses. Eleventh, not knowing moderation in food. Twelfth, not practicing in the first and last watches of the night. Thirteenth, not being able to sit alone in contemplation. Fourteenth, not being able to observe as it truly is. The contemplation of impermanence and suffering, worthy ones, follows six wrong dharmas. What are the six? First, discontent. Second, lack of effort. Third, lack of faith. Fourth, desire. Fifth, not wishing to sit in a quiet place. Sixth, not being able to observe as it truly is. The contemplation of suffering and non-self: the view of the body as mixed is its hindrance. The contemplation of repulsiveness of food: attachment to the taste of craving and not practicing is its hindrance. The contemplation that in all the world there is no pleasure: craving and desire for worldly things is what makes the practice wrong. The contemplation of death: following the attachment to life and practicing is its hindrance. The contemplation of light, worthy ones, follows eleven wrong dharmas. What are the eleven? First, doubt. Second, non-reflection. Third, coarseness of body. Fourth, drowsiness. Fifth, excessive effort. Sixth, abandoning effort. Seventh, deluded joy. Eighth, fear. Ninth, non-unified contemplation. Tenth, being without discernment. Eleventh, fixation on observing forms. The contemplation of turning away: desire causes deviation from the path. The contemplation of cessation: if the mind dwells on dharmas, it deviates from the path. These, worthy ones, cause deviation from the path. For what has not yet been overcome, three dharmas are most beneficial. What are the three? First, desire. Second, cutting off. Third, sitting practice.

"The contemplation of impurity, worthy ones, is accomplished through practicing fourteen dharmas abundantly. What are the fourteen? First, not dwelling with one's former household. Second, stilling the mind. Third, not seeing one's former household. Fourth, guarding oneself. Fifth, not having hasty desire. Sixth, not following the perception of purity. Seventh, not contemplating the perception of purity. Eighth, not wishing to associate with worldly practitioners. Ninth, not wishing to follow worldly practices. Tenth, guarding one's own senses. Eleventh, knowing moderation in food. Twelfth, practicing in the first and last watches of the night without drowsiness. Thirteenth, delighting in sitting alone in contemplation. Fourteenth, observing as it truly is, practicing the contemplation of impurity abundantly.

"Worthy ones! Through this practice, one severs craving and desire. The contemplation of impermanence, practiced and cultivated abundantly, through this severs craving and desire. The contemplation of suffering from impermanence, having been cultivated and practiced abundantly, through this severs confusion. The contemplation of non-self from suffering, having been cultivated and practiced abundantly, through this severs the view of the body. The contemplation of repulsiveness of food, having been cultivated and practiced abundantly, through this severs craving. The contemplation that in the world there is no pleasure, having been cultivated and practiced abundantly, through this severs worldly attachment. The contemplation of death, properly cultivated and practiced abundantly, through this severs attachment to life. The contemplation of light, having been cultivated and practiced abundantly, through this brings about wisdom and vision. The contemplation of turning away, having been cultivated and practiced abundantly, through this severs craving. The contemplation of cessation, having been cultivated and practiced abundantly, through this frees one from wrong. Thus received in truth, noble disciples of the path proceed.

"Noble disciples of the path have twenty dharmas that prevent them from following the path. What are the twenty? First, dwelling with people who do not practice the path. Second, not questioning. Third, not knowing what one should practice. Fourth, confusion. Fifth, wrong conduct. Sixth, a greedy mind. Seventh, having many affairs. Eighth, scant effort. Ninth, undermining one's own refuge. Tenth, vanity. Eleventh, seeking praise. Twelfth, being upside-down. Thirteenth, losing mindfulness. Fourteenth, greed. Fifteenth, dwelling with unwholesome companions. Sixteenth, not guarding the sense doors. Seventeenth, not knowing moderation in food. Eighteenth, not practicing in the first and last watches of the night. Nineteenth, not delighting in sitting alone in contemplation. Twentieth, not observing as it truly is. These twenty things, worthy ones, cause deviation from the path. For what has not yet been severed, when one wishes to sever it, eleven dharmas are most beneficial. What are the eleven? First, desire. Second, attainment. Third, seeing convenience. Fourth, having concern for others. Fifth, being without waste. Sixth, victory. Seventh, attaining the characteristic of dharmas. Eighth, following. Ninth, questioning. Tenth, guarding oneself in solitude. Eleventh, observing as it truly is. Thus rightly assembled:

"Noble disciples of the path, the twenty dharmas are most beneficial. What are the twenty? First, dwelling with people who practice the path. Second, questioning. Third, knowing what one should practice. Fourth, not being confused. Fifth, proper conduct. Sixth, not being greedy. Seventh, having few affairs. Eighth, not abandoning effort. Ninth, being without error. Tenth, not following vanity. Eleventh, not seeking praise. Twelfth, not being upside-down. Thirteenth, guarding mindfulness. Fourteenth, not being greedy. Fifteenth, dwelling with wholesome companions. Sixteenth, guarding the sense doors. Seventeenth, knowing moderation in food. Eighteenth, being able to practice in the first and last watches of the night. Nineteenth, delighting in sitting alone in contemplation. Twentieth, observing as it truly is.

"Having accomplished this, worthy ones, one should further contemplate twenty-two occasions. One who has already become a śramaṇa and practices the path should quickly contemplate these matters. What are the twenty-two? First, having accepted an unattractive appearance. Second, having adopted a different livelihood. Third, my life depends on others. Fourth, for the rest of my life I must seek robes, food and drink, medicine for illness, and bedding. Fifth, for the rest of my life, desires are covered over. Sixth, for the rest of my life, bodily pleasures of the human realm are to be given up: one who has become a śramaṇa should quickly contemplate this. Seventh, may my body not be harmed. Eighth, may I be able to dwell happily alone in emptiness. Ninth, may I not receive food contrary to the precepts. Tenth, may my body not violate the precepts. Eleventh, may the wise and my fellow practitioners not argue about me. Twelfth, may I, among the four fruits of merit of the path, attain one so that at the end of my life, if fellow practitioners question me, I can speak without sudden hesitation: one who wishes to practice the path should contemplate this. Thirteenth, may I be able to contemplate the aggregates as impermanent. Fourteenth, may I be able to contemplate the aggregates as insubstantial. Fifteenth, may I be able to contemplate the aggregates as not worth clinging to, with the mind returning to reliance on liberation: one who practices the path should urgently contemplate this. Sixteenth, may I see worldly formations as empty, without attachment, craving exhausted, dispassionate, ceased, unconditioned; may the mind contemplate, joyfully receive, be stilled, and attain liberation, having left behind form; one should quickly contemplate this matter at the proper occasion. Seventeenth, one who is born has not yet crossed over from birth. Eighteenth, one who is old has not yet crossed over from old age. Nineteenth, one who is ill has not yet crossed over from illness. Twentieth, the dharma of death, from the dharma of death, one has not yet crossed over; one should quickly flee the form and contemplate this. Twenty-first, all whom I love must part and separate, or be lost, or taken by others, or die; one cannot remain together long. Having fled the form, one should urgently contemplate this matter at the proper occasion. Twenty-second, each person receives according to their own actions; each has their own actions as root; each receives suffering from their own actions; each person creates good and evil, and from what is done, one receives. Having fled the form, one should urgently contemplate this matter at the proper occasion. Through these twenty-two practices, having cultivated and practiced them abundantly, the name of śramaṇa is fulfilled, and one's intended practice is complete."

"When the intended practice of the śramaṇa is complete, one can then fulfill seven contemplations. What are the seven? First, constant unceasing practice that enters. Second, non-regression. Third, non-contention. Fourth, right mindfulness. Fifth, not giving rise to arrogant pride. Sixth, in the world, seeking only robes and food. Seventh, stilling the mind and attaining mastery. These, worthy ones, are what the mind, consciousness, and awareness, over a long time without number of days and nights, has done among form, sound, smell, taste, and touch. Being in the world and unable to control it, having been able to control it and bring it to rest, one enters the seed of the deathless.

"Furthermore, worthy ones, through these twenty kinds of practice, one who has not yet attained the path should frighten the mind. What are the twenty kinds? First, having nothing, entering emptiness. Second, not having studied death. Third, throwing oneself into a sandbar. Fourth, trusting a place of fear. Fifth, not knowing the place without fear. Sixth, not knowing the path. Seventh, not attaining a settled mind. Eighth, suffering in the next life. Ninth, the gathering of worthy ones is hard to encounter. Tenth, opening the doors of the world, worldly people have nothing extraordinary. Eleventh, not yet having built a bridge to escape from the hells. Twelfth, not yet having been freed from evil destinies. Thirteenth, universal doubt without number. Fourteenth, not yet having built the essential dam for the world. Fifteenth, being unwise and confused at the time of death. Sixteenth, no one can create actions on another's behalf. Seventeenth, what is not done should also not be done. Eighteenth, without doing, there is neither good fortune nor misfortune. Nineteenth, what has already been done cannot be forgotten. Twentieth, only one's own actions exist; only one's own actions follow; only one's own actions are the root; only one's own actions are the destination. Whatever actions a person does, whether good or evil, in whatever existence, one must accept them. Worldly people should from this frighten the mind.

"Having from these twenty causes and conditions become more and more disenchanted and frightened, turning away further and further, there are twenty kinds of practice by which the mind quickly becomes settled. What are the twenty? First, through the thought of mindful attention, the mind quickly becomes settled. Second, through knowing within the mind, the mind quickly becomes settled. Third, through the mind being unified, the mind quickly becomes settled. Fourth, through right contemplation and knowing, the mind quickly becomes settled. Fifth, through the contemplation of right cessation, the mind quickly becomes settled. Sixth, through the contemplation of right arising, the mind quickly becomes settled. Seventh, through the contemplation of gathering, the mind quickly becomes settled. Eighth, through the contemplation of supporting, the mind quickly becomes settled. Ninth, through the contemplation of guarding, the mind quickly becomes settled. Tenth, through practicing the four foundations of mindfulness, the mind quickly becomes settled. Eleventh, through the four right efforts, the mind quickly becomes settled. Twelfth, through the four bases of supernatural power, the mind quickly becomes settled. Thirteenth, through detachment from improper conduct, the mind quickly becomes settled. Fourteenth, through approaching the proper field of practice, the mind quickly becomes settled. Fifteenth, through having reliance and learning, the mind quickly becomes settled. Sixteenth, through recitation and understanding, the mind quickly becomes settled. Seventeenth, through having a mind of compassionate sorrow, the mind quickly becomes settled. Eighteenth, through having much joy in the path, the mind quickly becomes settled. Nineteenth, through knowing what is important, the mind quickly becomes settled. Twentieth, through practicing this, the mind quickly becomes settled.

"Thus used rightly, worthy ones, noble disciples of the path should have eleven obstacles and should know them. What are the eleven? First, assembling in gatherings. Second, excessive eating. Third, excessive business. Fourth, excessive talking. Fifth, excessive sleeping. Sixth, delighting in wandering about. Seventh, delighting in dwelling with others. Eighth, indulging the body. Ninth, frivolity. Tenth, craving and desire. Eleventh, dwelling in an unwholesome district. These, worthy ones, are the obstacles. What has not yet been severed should be severed.

"One should further study ten mutual skills. What are the ten? First, the mutual skill of settling the mind. Second, the mutual skill of stabilizing cessation. Third, the mutual skill of stabilizing arising. Fourth, the mutual skill of stopping. Fifth, the mutual skill of restraining. Sixth, the mutual skill of protecting. Seventh, the mutual skill of the root. Eighth, the mutual skill of guarding against obstacles. Ninth, the mutual skill of skillful means. Tenth, the mutual skill of entering. When one has thus entered the mutual skills, the noble disciple of the path has thirteen kinds of merit. What are the thirteen? First, when with a faithful mind one rightly reflects on the Tathāgata, joy arises from faith. Second, likewise with the Dharma. Third, likewise with those who study. Fourth, likewise with one's own moral conduct. Fifth, likewise with others' moral conduct. Sixth, likewise with one's own attainments. Seventh, likewise with others' attainments. Eighth, likewise with one's own giving. Ninth, likewise with others' giving. Tenth, the path greatly eliminates suffering. Eleventh, the world greatly teaches the sūtras and enables reflection. Twelfth, from innumerable wrong practices, one returns. Thirteenth, from innumerable wholesome dharma practices, one enters, and joy arises from the ability to have the seed of faith.

"Such joy: the noble disciple of the path should rely on four dharmas and fulfill five dharmas. What are the four dharmas of reliance? First, reliance on the Dharma. Second, reliance on aspiration. Third, reliance on further progress. Fourth, reliance on sitting alone, without remaining desire and attachment.

"What are the five dharmas fulfilled for use on the path? First, joy. Second, love. Third, reliance. Fourth, happiness. Fifth, concentration.

"The practitioner with such joy, a noble disciple of the path, is able to extinguish eight wounds. What are the eight? First, the wound of desire. Second, the wound of anger. Third, the wound of foolishness. Fourth, the wound of arrogant pride. Fifth, the wound of craving. Sixth, the wound of delusion. Seventh, the wound of gain, respect, and fame. Eighth, the wound of doubt and unclarity. When the practitioner has submerged and extinguished these eight wounds, one thereby attains crossing over the world."

"The ten dharmas of one beyond training: what are the ten? First, the right view of one beyond training. Second, right intention. Third, right speech. Fourth, right action. Fifth, right livelihood. Sixth, right effort. Seventh, right mindfulness. Eighth, right concentration. Ninth, right liberation. Tenth, right knowledge. Through these ten dharmas of one beyond training, from these causes and conditions, one attains direct encounter. One then abandons the five kinds, is direct in the six kinds, follows the one guardian, has the four supports, does not grasp partially at truth, having abandoned difficulty, without attachment to seeking, stilled bodily action, stilled vocal action, stilled mental action, mind's contemplation has crossed over, the most wise has crossed over, with nothing remaining, practice is complete: one is called the supreme.

"Worthy ones! This is what the worthy one's final consciousness is: having come from afar, it neither creates nor gathers nor again meets, and thus comes to an end. This is the essential cutting off of suffering.

"What was spoken above, worthy ones, is the Dharma that the worthy one teaches: good in the beginning, good in the middle, good at the end, beneficial, with entry, most complete, pure, and clear. The essential path of teaching is called the Complete Dharma Practice. What was spoken above was spoken for this reason."

The Venerable Śāriputra spoke thus. The bhikṣus received it wholeheartedly, remembering what was taught.
