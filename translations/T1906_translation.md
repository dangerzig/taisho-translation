# Essentials of the Bodhisattva Precept Scripture

## *Púsà Jiè Běn Zōngyào*

Taishō 1906

Composed by Dàxián (*Dàxián*), Silla (Korea).

*Note: This is a scholastic treatise on the bodhisattva precepts (púsà jiè) from the Brahmajāla Sūtra (Fànwǎng Jīng), composed by the Korean (Silla) Yogācāra scholar-monk Dàxián. The work is preceded by a laudatory preface by an anonymous author praising Dàxián as a rare figure who arose "in accordance with the five-hundred[-year cycle]." The treatise itself is structured in three gates: (1) the meaning of the sūtra (explaining the title "Brahmā's Net" as purifying and gathering sentient beings); (2) what constitutes the precepts, subdivided into the conditions for receiving and the nature of what is received (with detailed comparison to śrāvaka precepts regarding reception, violation, and relinquishment, drawing on the Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra, the Mahāyānasaṃgraha, and the Brahmajāla Sūtra); and (3) the differences in practice, presented through four sub-gates: (a) associating with good teachers, (b) hearing the true Dharma, (c) proper mental engagement (contemplation of impermanence, suffering, impurity, and non-self), and (d) practicing in accordance with the teaching (the pāramitās, the distinction between light and heavy transgressions, and the four aspects of observance and violation). The treatise culminates in a discussion of the "ultimate" (jiūjìng), where the three-wheel emptiness (agent, act, and recipient) realizes the precepts as beyond grasping and abandoning, identical with the dharma-nature itself.*

## Preface by Dharma Master Dàxián's Annotator

I observe: the drifting multitudes of beings pursue the dust of karma, augmenting the roots of defilement. The anxious masses of people cling to tainted conditions, nourishing the tree of delusion. It is like empty flowers appearing and vanishing, like a fire-wheel spinning. Therefore the true Brahmā [the Buddha] had compassion for them, conveying them on the precious raft. The Hero of the World, governing transformation, bestowed the golden chapter [of precepts]. If one penetrates their subtlety and enters their hidden depths, one transcends the ocean of false consciousness, abandons the tumult and confusion, and dwells in the spiritual realm, delighting in tranquil repose.

Since the heavenly drum first sounded in the radiance [of the Buddha's teaching], the Way has been truly correct. Since the World-Eye first closed in the Sala grove [at the parinirvāṇa], the principle admits but one awakening. After a thousand years, two schools arose: those who grasp existence reject emptiness; those attached to emptiness reject existence. Each occupies an extreme shore, none having navigated the middle source.

Alas! The wind of virtue ceased to blow and the nectar of the ṛṣis lost its taste. As the great meaning was about to be lost, who would take up the guiding rope? Then the five suns [the five great teachers] transmitted their light to the five Indias, unfurling their splendid radiance. A thousand treatises echoed their fame through a thousand ages, re-establishing their vast achievement. Truly, transmitting the lamp from candle to candle, the spring orchid gives way to the autumn chrysanthemum.

Yet to arise as a hero in accordance with the five-hundred[-year cycle]: who might that be? None other than Dharma Master Dàxián of the Eastern Kingdom [Silla]. His profound style was clear and far-reaching, his spiritual energy solitary and heroic. With the bearing of a dragon-chapter and phoenix-chick, and the capacity of a Héguān peak. Before his twentieth year, he planted the tree of enlightenment on the green hills [of Korea]. In his years of maturity, he sailed the boat of compassion across the continental sea. Yet his traces were hidden and his aims far-reaching, so he concealed his use and dimmed his light.

Lamenting the decay of the garden of meaning and sighing over the collapse of the bridge of Dharma, he composed the *Yogācāra Compendium* (*Yújiā Zuǎnyào*) in three fascicles, the *Vijñaptimātratā Determinations* (*Wéishí Juézé*) in one fascicle, and the *Essentials of the Bodhisattva Precept Scripture* in one fascicle, along with one hundred lines of root-verse. All serve to transmit [the teaching] to future generations. His words are elegant and his principles profound; his text is concise and his meaning rich. The radiance of the wisdom-sun is increased; the beauty of the mountain of awakening is enhanced. May those lost at crossroads directly see the south-pointing chariot. May those unclear about their own features look up and perceive the mirror of the Western Qín.

## The Treatise

> The warrior in battle faces death without reluctance;
> The man of resolve, setting out on the Way, what excuse has he?
> At the start, it is always difficult; ease never comes forever.
> If, because of difficulty, one retreats, in what kalpa will one succeed?
> The man who would seize the kingship of the three realms
> Must wield the wisdom-sword and sever the host of māras.
> In the ocean of suffering, I vow to be without fear,
> Adorning the precept-raft to gather all directions.

### Gate One: The Meaning of the Sūtra

I shall now, according to this sūtra, explain the essentials of observance and violation. There are broadly three gates: (1) the gate of the sūtra's meaning, (2) the gate of what constitutes [the precepts], and (3) the gate of the differences in practice.

First, the sūtra's meaning. The sūtra says: "The Brahmā-Net Sūtra: the mind-ground chapter of the bodhisattva [precepts] spoken by Vairocana Buddha." "Brahmā" (*fàn*) means "that which purifies." "Net" (*wǎng*) means "that which gathers sentient beings." This sūtra, extending even to the summit of existence, gathers and holds sentient beings in the great ocean of birth and death, ultimately bringing them to the shore of supreme quiescence, endlessly benefiting all who hunger and thirst. It is like a worldly net. Because the World-Honored One, on account of Brahmā's Net, taught the central principle of this one division, [the teaching] generates and nurtures ten thousand virtues and is therefore called the "mind-ground."

### Gate Two: What Constitutes the Precepts

This has two sub-gates: (1) the conditions for receiving and (2) the nature of what is received.

#### Receiving the Precepts

As the sūtra says: "All who have minds should receive the Buddha's precepts." The supreme vehicle is both most profound and most vast. Because most profound, its extreme is truly difficult [to reach]. Because most vast, it becomes the cause for all. A single grain of dust or a single drop of water is the basis for a mountain or an ocean. Therefore the buddhas established the precepts without restriction. For anyone who understands language, the mind that is generated, through the power of the [buddha-]nature lineage, all becomes a cause [of awakening].

How so? Suppose a person definitively aspires to sever all evil, cultivate all good, and, to the extent of the realm of sentient beings, perform the conduct of a great person. At that time, in this mind that is part of the liberation portion, through the conditions of the karmic ceremony, the bodhisattva precepts are obtained.

"All evil" means all the defilements to be severed and abandoned, from the first generation of the mind up to bodhi. "All good" means all the purities to be generated and obtained, from the first generation of the mind up to bodhi. "The realm of sentient beings" means bearing the burden of all beings throughout the endless future. No aspiration to the Way is greater than this; hence "the conduct of a great person."

It is like the egg of a phoenix: though the shell has not yet broken, the potential for freely transcending realms is already present. The mind at its first generation, though still enmeshed, already possesses the power [of awakening]. By contrast, a mosquito that lifts its wings cannot shade the sky. The two vehicles, though they emerge from defilement, lack the function of sheltering [other] beings.

As the Bodhisattva Kāśyapa's verse says:

> The first generation of the mind and the ultimate: these two are not different.
> Of these two minds, the first is the more difficult.
> Not yet having saved oneself, one first saves others:
> Therefore I bow to the first generation of the mind.
> Having already generated this mind, one becomes the teacher of devas and humans,
> Surpassing the śrāvakas and the pratyekabuddhas.
> Such a generation of the mind surpasses the three realms;
> Therefore it is called the most supreme.

#### The Nature of What Is Received

As the sūtra says: "All bodhisattvas have trained, all bodhisattvas will train, all bodhisattvas now train. I have briefly described the characteristics of the prātimokṣa." The bodhisattva precepts are based on reality for both grasping and abandoning, unlike other precepts. The Buddha, knowing the appropriate time, declared the light to be heavy and the heavy to be light. Therefore, across the three times, the precept-characteristics are definitive.

Compared to the śrāvaka [precepts], there are roughly three kinds of differences:

**First, differences in reception.** The bodhisattva precepts, excluding the seven obstructions, can be received by all. As the *Sūtra of Original Acts* (*Běnyè Jīng*) says: "Beings of the six destinies can receive the precepts. If they understand language, they obtain the precepts and do not lose them." Therefore there is also the method of self-reception. Furthermore, even one who has committed a grave offense, if not one of the seven obstructions, may receive [the precepts] again in this very life. This differs from other precepts. As the *Sūtra of Original Acts* says: "For the ten grave [offenses], there is violation but no irrevocable [loss]; one may receive them anew." The *Yogācārabhūmi* says the same. However, in this sūtra, regarding those who have committed the seven obstructions, the general statement is given with a hidden intention: those who violate the ten grave precepts cannot receive the precepts in this present life.

**Second, differences in violation.** The *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* (*Shè Dàchéng*) says: "The bodhisattva does not actively commit natural transgressions (*xìngzuì*), which is similar to the śrāvaka. But regarding conventional prohibitions (*zhēzuì*), there are cases of active [commission], which differs [from the śrāvaka]. In this regard, some things that are violations for the śrāvaka are not for the bodhisattva; and some things that are violations for the bodhisattva are not for the śrāvaka."

The bodhisattva possesses body, speech, and mind precepts; the śrāvaka possesses only body and speech precepts. [The analysis continues with four permutations regarding persons and actions, explaining how the bodhisattva may, through skillful means and for the benefit of others, commit minor natural transgressions without fault, and indeed generate merit, as taught in the *Yogācārabhūmi*.]

As the sūtra says: "If the bodhisattva knows that through breaking the precepts, he can cause a person to receive, cherish, and delight in the Mahāyāna, then breaking the precepts is permitted. The bodhisattva should think: 'I would rather spend a kalpa, or less than a kalpa, in the Avīci hell and receive this punishment, if I can cause that person not to regress from bodhi.'" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "For one who violates the precepts for this purpose, to fall into the Avīci hell: there is no such possibility." The Buddha praised this, saying "Excellent!" The *Yogācārabhūmi* agrees.

**Third, differences in relinquishment.** A bhikṣu [relinquishes the precepts] through five conditions; a bodhisattva through four, as discussed extensively in the treatises. Furthermore, a bhikṣu who commits any of the three grades of grave offense relinquishes the pure precepts. A bodhisattva relinquishes only through an offense committed with the highest degree of affliction. As Maitreya says: "If a bodhisattva violates the four pārājika dharmas, repeatedly committing them with utter shamelessness, generating deep attachment and seeing this as meritorious, that is called violation with the highest degree of affliction. A bodhisattva does not relinquish the pure precept-discipline through a single, momentary commission of a pārājika dharma, as do the bhikṣus."

Furthermore, a bhikṣu's precepts are necessarily received in their entirety; therefore, violating one grave precept means losing all. The bodhisattva is different. As the *Yogācārabhūmi* says: "In the bodhisattva precepts, there is no 'total violation.'" That is, even if one grave precept is broken through the highest degree of affliction, the remaining precepts are not lost. It is like a lay devotee who receives the precepts as a whole: though called a "precept-violator," the essence [of the precepts] remains. As the sūtra says: "One who has [precepts] and violates them is superior to one who has none and does not violate them. One who violates is called a bodhisattva; one who does not violate is called a non-Buddhist."

Furthermore, a bhikṣu's precepts are relinquished at the end of life. The bodhisattva's are not: even upon transmigrating to another life, the precepts follow. As the sūtra says: "All bodhisattva precepts, both those of ordinary beings and of sages, take the mind as their substance. Therefore, when the mind is exhausted, the precepts too are exhausted. Since the mind is inexhaustible, the precepts too are inexhaustible."

### Gate Three: Differences in Practice

There are broadly four sub-gates: (1) associating with good teachers, (2) hearing the true Dharma, (3) proper mental engagement, and (4) practicing in accordance with the teaching.

#### Associating with Good Teachers

As the World-Honored One said: "For all sentient beings, there is no closer proximate cause of great bodhi than a good friend." There are four kinds of friends: (1) one who accords with the good and opposes evil, (2) one who accords with evil and opposes good, (3) one who accords with both good and evil, and (4) one who opposes both. Among these, only the first should be made one's companion. Even from the evil, one takes reform as the teacher, not making a like-minded, "gold-cutting friend" who pries open the jewel on one's forehead, leaving one unfit to be a vessel for bodhi. Who has ever established their achievement without relying on a superior companion? Therefore, associating with good teachers comes first.

#### Hearing the True Dharma

As the sūtra says: "Even if the entire world were filled with fire, one must pass through it to hear the Dharma." Through a single phrase, one can sever the wheel of suffering. For hearing half a verse, one's soul sinks into the Snowy Mountains [as in the jātaka of Śākyamuni sacrificing himself to a rākṣasa for half a verse]. One should stabilize the mind in correct mindfulness at four points, and then ask questions, as the treatise explains.

What is heard in the morning should be transmitted by evening. Take others' correct practice as one's own ten thousand practices. Take others' attainment of the Way as one's own bodhi. Even if one mistakenly loses correct mindfulness and falls into the Avīci hell, if it benefits even one sentient being, one gladly accepts the suffering. As the sūtra says: "For the sake of one sentient being, one practices for a hundred million kalpas, causing immeasurable beings to cross the ocean of suffering."

#### Proper Mental Engagement

This means contemplating four things, first treating the four inversions: (1) All conditioned things are impermanent, because what is born must perish. All prosperity returns to decline and is not ultimate. (2) Everything with outflows is suffering, because of the three kinds of suffering. Yet beings, entangled in the net of craving, turn the wheel of birth and death; the poisonous blade of meeting and parting cuts them. The arrows of anxiety and suffering pierce and burn them. (3) The three realms are impure, being defiled in nature. Yet they appear as if pure, like an enemy deceiving a friend. (4) All dharmas are without self, because the two kinds of self are empty. The dharmas of the aggregates, with arising and ceasing as their body: arising does not announce itself as arising; ceasing has no awareness of ceasing. All dharmas have one characteristic, namely no characteristic. Like an illusion, they arise from conditions and are without self-nature. The nature of being without nature is itself the dharma-nature.

#### Practicing in Accordance with the Teaching

Vowing to traverse the ten stages, with far-reaching aspiration toward bodhi, one leaves home, bidding farewell to loved ones, with patched robe and measured bowl received according to the Dharma. Establishing the hundred fields [of merit] as one's household provision, within precepts, concentration, and wisdom, one practices in accordance with the teaching.

This is accomplished through four gates of skillful means by which the bodhisattva can purify the precept-practice and perfectly equip the provisions [for awakening]: (1) the gate of guarding correct mindfulness, (2) the gate of the supremacy of the pāramitās, (3) the gate of the nature of light and heavy [offenses], and (4) the gate of the characteristics of observance and violation.

[The treatise continues with detailed analysis of each gate, including: how to guard the mind against attraction, aversion, and indifference; how each pāramitā contains all the others; the ten grave and forty-eight light precepts; and the four-proposition analysis of whether praise-and-blame constitute transgressions.]

### The Ultimate

The ultimate is realized through the emptiness of the two [selves], extinguishing the characteristics of the three wheels [agent, act, and recipient]. As the sūtra says: "One should perfect the pure precept-pāramitā through non-guarding. Because the characteristics of violation and non-violation cannot be apprehended." The characteristics of the three wheels, the precept, the transgression, and the person, are not identical with conditions; therefore, though characteristics are not non-existent, they are not separate from conditions; therefore, their self-nature is not existent. Neither identical, nor separate, nor in between can they be apprehended.

Because their nature is not existent, do not add the idea of "one who upholds." Because their characteristics are not non-existent, do not deny that there are transgressions. Empty yet not denied: the precept-characteristics are not lost. Existent yet not added to: there is no nature of transgression. Though one illuminates the characteristics of light and heavy, right and wrong, to see the three wheels [as real] is not the ultimate practice.

The suchness of all dharmas: this is called "Tathāgata." Obtained through cultivating non-self. This practice without characteristics eternally severs the two obscurations and fulfills the two benefits [self-benefit and other-benefit]. This is called the ultimate.

In a single thought, the three times are perfectly fused. One may then say that bodhi does not depart from the first generation of the mind. The intention of supreme aspiration arises from this. As the sūtra says: "The buddhas of the future will pat [the bodhisattva's] crown and teach the Dharma."

The emptiness of a single dharma: all are unborn. One may then say that one's own mind flows forth as the six destinies. The great compassion of shared substance arises from this. As the sūtra says: "This very dharma-body, tossed about by immeasurable afflictions, going and coming in birth and death, is called 'sentient being.'"

Guarding the precepts is thus; liberation is likewise. As the verse says:

> All the oceans of karmic obstruction
> Are entirely born from false thinking.
> If one wishes to repent,
> Sit upright and contemplate true characteristics.
> All transgressions, like frost and dew,
> Can be melted by the sun of wisdom.
> Therefore, with utmost sincerity,
> Diligently repent the transgressions of the six sense-faculties.

---

These three collections of precepts [the precepts of restraint, the precepts of gathering wholesome dharmas, and the precepts of benefiting sentient beings], avoiding the extremes of suffering and pleasure, are the basis for the three bodies of realization, severance, and wisdom-compassion. The precepts are like tuning a stringed instrument: one takes the wondrous middle.

As the sūtra says: "The bodhisattva, for the sake of the Way, accepts the four kinds of offering. If the body is not firm, one cannot endure suffering. If one cannot endure suffering, one cannot cultivate good. Toward suffering, one generates aversion; toward pleasure, one generates craving." And so on at length.

I have broadly surveyed all the treatise-schools, like one counting others' treasures, my youthful career now spent. Now, for the profit of half a coin, I have gathered these essentials as a gate, for the sake of future practitioners to collect these ancient traces. As in the *Yogācāra Records* and their verses, I express my own aspiration. As in the *Vijñaptimātratā Determinations* in one fascicle: may those of similar inclination examine and decide for themselves.

> The holy canon's subtle and essential secrets
> Are already opened, like a perfect mirror suspended in the empty sky.
> The human body and the holy teaching are difficult to encounter again;
> Those whose hearts aspire to transcendence should seize the time.
