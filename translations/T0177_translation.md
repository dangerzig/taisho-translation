# 大意經
## Sūtra of Mahāmati

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0177

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the Anāthapiṇḍada Monastery, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Long ago, there was a country named Joyful and Without Sorrow, whose king was called Broad Compassion. In the country there was a householder named Mahādāna. His wife was named Candā. She gave birth to a son of exquisite appearance, without peer in the world. As soon as he was born, he spoke, making this solemn vow: 'I shall give to all under heaven, rescuing and aiding the people. For those who are orphaned, alone, poor, or destitute, I shall provide and protect them, and bring them peace.' His parents therefore named him Mahāmati. Seeing that his appearance was extraordinary and unlike other people, they feared he might be a deva, nāga, ghost, or spirit, and wished to have his fortune read. Mahāmati, knowing their intention, spoke to them: 'I am truly a human, not a deva, nāga, ghost, or spirit. I merely think of those among the people who are poor and afflicted, and wish to care for them.' Having said this, he fell silent and did not speak again. When he was seventeen, he told his parents: 'I wish to practice generosity, bringing ease to those who toil and suffer.'

"His parents thought: 'When our son was first born, he already had this wish.' They told their son: 'Our wealth is beyond counting. Give freely as you desire; we shall not restrict you.' Mahāmati replied: 'Though my parents' wealth is great, it is still not enough for my purposes. I must go to sea and gather the seven treasures to provide for all the people.' He repeated this many times, and his parents finally permitted him to go.

"Mahāmati then bowed, took his leave, and set out for the sea. On the way, he passed through another country. A brāhmaṇa there, of immeasurable wealth, saw that Mahāmati's countenance was handsome and radiant, and was greatly delighted. He told him: 'I hold you in esteem. I have a young daughter whom I wish to offer to you. Please stay here.' Mahāmati replied: 'I have left home and am heading to sea to gather the seven treasures. I dare not yet accept. Please wait until I return.' He then continued on and gathered the seven treasures. He immediately sent people to carry the treasures back to his home country, and then traveled farther to the edge of the sea, seeking extraordinary things. Suddenly he saw a great tree, eighty yojanas high and equally broad. Mahāmati climbed the tree and from a distance saw a silver city. Its palaces, halls, and buildings were all of white silver. Celestial maidens attended on each side, and music played of its own accord. A poisonous serpent coiled around the city three times. When it saw Mahāmati, it raised its head and gazed at him. Mahāmati thought to himself: 'People who are harmed by poison are so because they lack a wholesome mind.' He sat down, focused his mind, and entered deep concentration. In a moment, the serpent lowered its head and fell asleep.

"Mahāmati wished to enter the city. The gatekeeper went in and reported to the king: 'Outside there is a worthy person who wishes to see the king.' The king himself came out to welcome him and joyfully said: 'I pray that the virtuous one will stay for one season of three months, so that I may make offerings.' He answered: 'I am on a journey to gather treasures and should not stay long.' The king replied: 'I shall set aside the affairs of state. I only wish you to stay.' Mahāmati then agreed to remain. The king provided clothing, food, drink, music, beds, and furnishings. After a full ninety days, Mahāmati took his leave of the king and prepared to depart. The king took rare and precious treasures of the seven kinds to send him off. Mahāmati said: 'I do not need so many of these seven treasures. I have heard that the king possesses a luminous pearl. That is what I wish to request.' The king said: 'I do not begrudge this pearl, but I fear the road ahead is steep and perilous, and it will be difficult to carry.' Mahāmati said: 'For one whom merit guides, there is nothing to fear from peril.' The king said: 'This pearl draws treasures from a radius of twenty li. I offer it to the virtuous one, for whatever you desire. If in the future you attain the path, I wish to be your disciple and make offerings that surpass those of today.' Mahāmati received the pearl and departed joyfully.

"Mahāmati then traveled further and saw a golden city. Its palaces, halls, and buildings were all of yellow gold. Trees of the seven treasures made music of their own accord. Celestial maidens and attendants were twice as many as before. There was also a poisonous serpent coiling around the city six times. When it saw Mahāmati, it raised its head and gazed at him. He again sat in concentration, and the serpent lowered its head and slept. Mahāmati wished to enter the city. The gatekeeper went in and reported to the king. The king came out to welcome him and invited him in, saying: 'I wish you to stay for one season of three months, so I may make offerings.' Mahāmati stayed. The king provided food, drink, clothing, celestial maidens, and music for his enjoyment. After sixty days, he took his leave of the king, who pressed him to stay, but his heart was set on departing. He bade farewell and left. The king again took rare treasures of the seven kinds to send him off, but Mahāmati declined, saying: 'I do not desire these many treasures. I have heard the king possesses a luminous pearl. I wish you would bestow it upon me.' The king said: 'I dare not cling to it, but I fear the road is far and dangerous, and it will be hard to carry.' He answered: 'For one whom merit guides, what difficulty is there?' The king replied: 'This pearl draws rare treasures from a radius of forty li. I offer it to the virtuous one. I wish that when you attain the path in the future, I may be your disciple, with supernatural powers beyond compare, and may make offerings that surpass those of today.' He joyfully received the pearl and departed.

"Mahāmati then traveled further and saw a crystal city. Its palaces, halls, and buildings were all of crystal. Trees of the seven treasures made music of their own accord. Celestial maidens and attendants were again twice as many as before. There was also a poisonous serpent coiling around the city nine times. When it saw Mahāmati, it raised its head and gazed at him. He again sat in deep concentration and entered samādhi. The serpent lowered its head and fell asleep. Mahāmati wished to proceed. The gatekeeper went in and reported to the king. The king came out to welcome him, and invited him in, saying: 'I wish you to stay for one season of three months.' Mahāmati stayed. The king devoted himself to making offerings of food, drink, clothing, and music. After forty days, Mahāmati took his leave. The king took rare treasures of the seven kinds to send him off, but Mahāmati declined, saying: 'I do not need these many treasures. I have heard the king possesses a luminous pearl. I wish you would bestow it upon me.' The king replied: 'This pearl draws treasures from a radius of sixty li. I offer it to the virtuous one. I wish that when you attain the path in the future, I may be your disciple, with wisdom beyond compare, and may make offerings that surpass those of today.' He received the pearl joyfully and departed.

"Mahāmati then traveled further and saw a lapis lazuli city. Its palaces, halls, and buildings were all of lapis lazuli. Trees of the seven treasures made music of their own accord. Celestial maidens and attendants were again twice as many as before. He also saw a poisonous serpent coiling around the city twelve times. When it saw Mahāmati, it raised its head and gazed at him. He again sat in deep concentration and entered samādhi. The serpent lowered its head and fell asleep. Mahāmati wished to proceed. The gatekeeper went in and reported to the king. The king came out to welcome him, invited him in, and asked him to stay one season of three months. Mahāmati stayed. The king personally provided food, drink, clothing, and music for his enjoyment. After twenty days, he took his leave and prepared to depart. The king took rare treasures of the seven kinds to send him off, but Mahāmati declined, saying: 'I have heard the king possesses a luminous pearl. I wish you would bestow it upon me.' The king replied: 'This pearl draws treasures from a radius of eighty li. I offer it to the virtuous one. I wish that when you attain the path in the future, I may be your disciple, making offerings with a pure mind that surpass those of today, and may I gain lasting wisdom.' Mahāmati received the pearl joyfully and departed.

"Mahāmati thought: 'I originally came to seek treasures. Now my wish has been fulfilled. I should return the way I came.' He retraced his path intending to return to his home country. As he crossed the great sea, the spirit kings of the sea conferred together: 'Although our sea has many precious and famous treasures, none compares to these pearls.' They ordered the sea spirit to intercept and seize the pearls. The spirit transformed into a person and appeared before Mahāmati, asking: 'I hear you have obtained rare and extraordinary things. May I borrow them for a look?' Mahāmati extended his hand and showed the four pearls. The sea spirit then shook his hand, causing the pearls to fall into the water. Mahāmati thought: 'The kings warned me that these pearls would be hard to keep. I was fortunate to have obtained them, yet now this fellow has stolen them. This is no trivial matter.' He said to the sea spirit: 'I labored and endured hardship, crossing treacherous terrain to obtain these pearls. You have taken them from me and will not return them. I shall drain the sea dry.' The sea spirit, knowing his resolve, asked: 'How can your aspiration be so lofty? The sea is three million three hundred and sixty thousand yojanas deep, and its breadth is without limit. How could you exhaust it? It is like saying the sun might fall to the earth, or that the great wind might be tied in a bundle. The sun may sooner fall to earth and the wind be tied in a bundle, but the waters of the great sea can never be bailed dry.' Mahāmati laughed and answered: 'I reflect that through all the lifetimes I have undergone in birth and death, the accumulated bones surpass Mount Sumeru, and the blood that has flowed would more than fill the five rivers and four seas. I still wish to sever the very root of birth and death. This small sea is nothing to bail dry.' He further said: 'I recall that in the past, when I made offerings to all the Buddhas, I made this solemn vow: \"May my aspiration be courageous on the path, and whatever I face, may no difficulty stand in my way.\" Even if I must move Mount Sumeru and dry the great sea, I shall never waver.' He then concentrated his mind and bailed the sea with a vessel. The sincerity of his effort reached the heavens, and the first of the Four Heavenly Kings descended to help Mahāmati bail the water. Two-thirds of the sea had already been bailed away. The spirit kings of the sea were all greatly terrified and conferred: 'If we do not return his pearls now, this is no small matter. When the water is gone and the mud exposed, he will destroy our palaces.' The sea spirit then brought out many treasures to offer Mahāmati, but Mahāmati would not take them, saying: 'I do not want these. I only want my pearls returned. Return them at once, for I shall never relent.' The sea spirit, knowing his resolve was unshakable, brought forth the pearls and returned them.

"Mahāmati, having recovered his pearls, went back for the brāhmaṇa's daughter, and returned to his home country, where he distributed gifts freely and lavishly. From then on, within his borders there were no longer any who suffered from hunger, cold, poverty, or want. People from all four directions left their former lands and came with their families to his realm of benevolence. He continued in this generosity for many years, and his kindness extended even to insects, creeping things, and tiny creatures; there was nothing that did not receive his blessings. Later, when his lifespan ended, he was reborn above as Śakra, or below as a wheel-turning emperor. He accumulated merit and virtue, and ultimately attained Buddhahood as the supreme honored one of the three realms. All of this was due to his past deeds; it did not arise of itself."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Mahāmati of that time is my present self. The householder Mahādāna of that time is now my father Śuddhodana. The mother Candā of that time is now my mother Māyā. The king of the country Joyful and Without Sorrow is now Mahākāśyapa. The brāhmaṇa's daughter is now Gopī. Her father is now Maitreya. The king of the silver city is now Ānanda. The king of the golden city is now Maudgalyāyana. The king of the crystal city is now Śāriputra. The king of the lapis lazuli city is now the bhikṣu Sudā. The Fourth Heavenly King who helped Mahāmati bail the sea is now Udāyin. The one who stole the pearls is now Devadatta. The four gatekeepers of the four cities are now Subhūti, Panthaka, Svāhā, and Kuru. The four poisonous serpents that encircled the four cities are now the four ministers who killed Sundarī."

Ānanda straightened his robes, bowed to the Buddha, and said: "At that time, by what merit did Mahāmati come to encounter these four treasure cities, receive offerings everywhere, and obtain the four luminous pearls, with treasures following after?"

The Buddha said: "Long ago, in the time of Vipaśyin Buddha, Mahāmati once built a stūpa of the four treasures for the Buddha, made offerings to the Three Treasures, and observed the fast for seven days. At that time, there were five hundred people who together built a monastery. Some hung silk banners and lit lamps; some burned incense and scattered flowers; some made offerings to the sangha of bhikṣus; some chanted sūtras and expounded the path. All of them have now come to this assembly."

Ānanda and the fourfold assembly of disciples, hearing the sūtra, were joyful, and came forward to bow to the Buddha.
