# 長壽王經
## Sūtra of King Dīrghāyus

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0161

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Long ago there was a bodhisattva who was a great king, named Dīrghāyus. The king had a crown prince named Dīrghajīvī. The king governed his country with righteousness. The affliction of weapons and punishment was not imposed upon the officials or the people. The rains came in season and the five grains ripened abundantly. There was a neighboring king who ruled with cruelty and violence, who did not cultivate righteous governance, and whose people were impoverished and distressed. He said to his ministers: 'I have heard that the kingdom of King Dīrghāyus is not far from here. It is prosperous, abundant, and joyful, and has no military defenses. I wish to go there and attack and seize his kingdom. Can it be done?' His ministers replied: 'Excellent!' He then raised an army and marched to the borders of King Dīrghāyus's kingdom.

"The officials and people at the border ran to report to the king: 'The greedy king has raised an army and is coming. He wishes to attack Your Majesty's kingdom. We beg you to make preparations.' King Dīrghāyus summoned his ministers and told them: 'The reason he comes is merely because he covets our people, granaries, and treasures. If I were to do battle with him, my people would surely be harmed. To fight over a kingdom and kill the people is something I will not do.' The ministers said: 'We, your ministers, are all skilled in the arts of war. We can certainly defeat him and prevent Your Majesty's soldiers from being harmed by the enemy.' The king said: 'Even if I defeat him, there will be death and injury. Both his soldiers and my people cherish their lives. To love my own and harm his: a worthy person would not do this.' The ministers would not listen. They kept the king in the palace and of their own accord raised troops outside, going out to the border to resist the invader.

"King Dīrghāyus then said to his crown prince: 'He covets our kingdom, and so comes to attack us. Now my ministers, for my sake, wish to resist him. When two enemies face each other, there will surely be casualties. I wish to go with you and abandon the kingdom and flee.' The prince said: 'I agree.' Father and son together climbed over the city wall and departed, hiding in the mountains.

"Thereupon, the greedy king entered the kingdom and offered a reward for King Dīrghāyus: one thousand catties of gold and ten million coins. One day afterward, King Dīrghāyus went out and sat under a tree by the roadside. A brāhmaṇa from a distant land also came to rest under the tree. He asked King Dīrghāyus: 'Where are you from, sir? What brings you here?' The king said: 'I am a person from this country. I happened to come here to amuse myself.' King Dīrghāyus asked the brāhmaṇa: 'Worthy sir, where have you come from? Where are you going?' The brāhmaṇa said: 'I am a poor and humble ascetic from a distant country. From afar I heard that King Dīrghāyus of this country delights in generosity and aids the poor and weak. So I have come from far away, wishing to beg from him for my livelihood. I do not know the king's inclination at present. You are a native of this country: have you heard his intention? Is he still willing to give?' The king silently thought: 'This man has come from afar because of me. Now that I have lost my kingdom, he will have nothing to obtain and will have to leave empty-handed. How pitiable!'

"Then the king wept and said to the brāhmaṇa: 'I am King Dīrghāyus. A king from another country came and attacked me. I abandoned my kingdom and fled, hiding here. Now I hear that you, worthy sir, have come specifically seeking me, but you find me destitute and I have nothing to give you. What shall I do?' The two men faced each other, choking with sobs and weeping. The king said: 'I have heard that the new king wants me very badly. If you take my head to him, you will receive a great reward.' The brāhmaṇa said: 'From afar I heard that the great king rescues all people. So I came to beg, hoping to receive even a little to sustain my remaining life. I find that the king has lost his kingdom; this is my own misfortune. You now tell me to cut off your head, but I dare not accept this command.' The king said: 'You came from afar wishing to receive something, but encountering my destitution, I have nothing to give you. All people born into this world will eventually die. Since we must die, I wish to give you my body. Why do you refuse? If you do not take it now, others will come later, and I will still give it to them. Better to take it now.' The brāhmaṇa said: 'I cannot bear to kill the great king. If the great king has such vast compassion and truly wishes to sacrifice his life to benefit me, then let me simply follow you with hands at my sides.' The king then went with him. When they arrived outside the city gate, the brāhmaṇa had the king bound and presented him to the greedy king. The greedy king paid the brāhmaṇa the reward of gold and coins and sent him away.

"The greedy king then ordered that King Dīrghāyus be burned to death at the crossroads of the four main streets. Because of their former king, the ministers said to the greedy king: 'This man was our former sovereign. Now that he is about to suffer his final punishment, we beg to be allowed to prepare a small offering to send him off.' The greedy king permitted it. The ministers prepared food and came to him, choking with sobs. The common people who watched all said: 'The king dies unjustly.' From the city walls to the open fields, all cried out to heaven.

"Prince Dīrghajīvī had come out and was by the roadside. Hearing people's words, he learned that his father had been captured by the greedy king. He disguised himself as a woodcutter, carrying firewood to sell at the market, mingling in the busy crowd. He stood before his father and watched as his father was about to die, his heart filled with grief. The father saw Dīrghajīvī and feared that in anger the son might seek revenge for his father. So the father looked up to heaven and sighed deeply: 'For a son who wishes to show the utmost filial devotion: let your father die without resentment. Do not seek revenge for your father! That would make your father die in joy and without sorrow. But if you disobey your father's words and kill another person, then your father will die with lingering regret.' Dīrghajīvī could not bear to see his father die and turned back into the mountains. King Dīrghāyus was then put to death by burning.

"Long afterward, Dīrghajīvī reflected: 'My father's benevolence and righteousness were profound and steadfast; even at death he did not waver. Yet this greedy king was without principle, unable to distinguish good from evil, and unjustly killed my father. Although my father had a compassionate, pure, and benevolent heart, and died without anger, I cannot endure it. If I do not go out and kill this greedy king, I will never live on in this world.' He then went out and hired himself for work. A great minister of the country was looking for a gardener at the market. He obtained Dīrghajīvī and set him to planting vegetables. The vegetables grew very well. One day, the great minister came to inspect the garden fields and saw the vegetables were excellent. He called and questioned the garden overseer. The overseer replied: 'Previously we hired a man to tend them. That is why they are so fine.' The minister then called Dīrghajīvī. Seeing him, the minister asked: 'Can you also prepare food?' He replied: 'I can.' He prepared food that was exceedingly sweet and delicious. The minister then invited the king to come. The king came and ate. The food was sweet and delicious. He asked: 'Who prepared this food?' The minister replied: 'I previously hired a man who can prepare this food.' The king then summoned him and brought him back to the palace to prepare food.

"One day, the king asked Dīrghajīvī: 'Do you happen to be skilled in the arts of war?' He replied: 'I am indeed skilled in them.' The king then took him and placed him at his side, telling him: 'I have an enemy: the son of King Dīrghāyus. I am always afraid he might come and suddenly encounter me. I now rely on you. Please help me guard against him.' Dīrghajīvī replied: 'So be it. I shall exert my strength and offer my life for the great king.'

"One day, the king asked Dīrghajīvī: 'Do you enjoy hunting?' He replied: 'I have enjoyed hunting since my youth.' The king then ordered the preparations for a hunt, and went out together with Dīrghajīvī. As soon as they entered the mountain forest, they spotted wild game. The king and Dīrghajīvī galloped in pursuit, and gradually went deeper into the mountains, losing their way and unable to get out. Lost and confused for three days, they became hungry and exhausted. The king then dismounted, unfastened his sword, and handed it to Dīrghajīvī, saying: 'I am extremely tired. Sit down. I wish to lie down with my head on your lap.' Dīrghajīvī said: 'Very well.' The king then lay down. Dīrghajīvī thought to himself: 'All this time I have been seeking an opportunity. Today I have obtained what I wished.' He drew the sword and was about to kill the greedy king. Then he reflected: 'My father, at the time of his death, earnestly entrusted me with his dying wish. How can I indulge my foolish desire and disobey my compassionate father's final instruction?' He sheathed the sword and stopped. The king suddenly awoke and asked Dīrghajīvī: 'I dreamed that the son of King Dīrghāyus was about to come and kill me. I was greatly frightened. Why is this?' Dīrghajīvī said: 'There is a powerful ghost-spirit in this mountain. Seeing the great king here, it has come to frighten the great king. I am guarding you. The king should just sleep peacefully and have no fear.'

"The king fell asleep again. Dīrghajīvī again drew the sword to kill him, but again he recalled his father's words and stopped. The king again awoke with a start and told Dīrghajīvī: 'I again dreamed that the son of King Dīrghāyus was about to come and kill me. I am greatly afraid. Why is this?' Dīrghajīvī said: 'It is again the doing of the mountain spirit. The king has nothing to fear.' The king fell asleep again. Dīrghajīvī again drew the sword to kill him. He reflected on his father's words and again stopped. He then cast the sword upon the ground, no longer having any intention of killing the king. The king again awoke and told Dīrghajīvī: 'I again dreamed of the son of King Dīrghāyus, who said: "I beg your pardon." He will not kill me.' Thereupon, Dīrghajīvī said: 'I am Prince Dīrghajīvī, the crown prince of King Dīrghāyus. I truly came out intending to kill the great king to avenge my father. But I recalled my father's earnest entreaty at the time of his death: he did not wish me to seek revenge. In my foolishness, I wished to disobey my father's words, but when I carefully considered my father's earnest and compassionate instruction, I dared not disobey it. Therefore I have now cast the sword upon the ground, in obedience to my father's words. Even so, I fear that in the future I may become confused and make a terrible mistake, disobeying my late father's teaching. So I now confess to you of my own accord. I beg the great king to execute me and end this evil intention, so that it may be cut off once and for all.' The king then felt remorse and said: 'I have been violent and cruel, unable to distinguish good from evil. This worthy father and son practiced benevolence with unwavering steadfastness even unto death, and I, greedy and harsh, was utterly unaware. Today it has come to this: my life is in your hands. Yet you cherish benevolence, remembering your father's words and not harming me. I am truly moved by your great generosity. Now I wish to return to the capital. By which road should we go?' Dīrghajīvī said: 'I know the paths. When I first came, I deliberately led the great king astray, wishing to avenge my father.'

"Dīrghajīvī then accompanied the king out of the forest. They soon encountered the ministers, scattered throughout the woods. The king then stopped and sat down, and food was prepared. The king asked his ministers: 'Do any of you recognize Prince Dīrghajīvī, the son of King Dīrghāyus?' Among them, those who did not recognize him replied: 'We do not.' Among those who did recognize him, some had received Dīrghajīvī's kindness in the past and feared the king would kill him, so they also said: 'We do not.' The king then pointed and said: 'This is Dīrghajīvī.' The king said: 'From this day forward, I shall return to my own former country. I wish to restore this kingdom to the prince. From this day forward, you shall be my younger brother. If any other country comes to invade or seize your kingdom, I shall come to your aid.' The king then led his ministers and soldiers back to his own country. Thereafter, whenever either country had rare treasures, they exchanged gifts."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The King Dīrghāyus of that time is now my present self. Prince Dīrghajīvī is Ānanda. The greedy king is Devadatta. Devadatta has borne enmity toward me life after life. Although I have always had good intentions toward him, he has still wished to harm me. Ānanda has always had no ill will toward him, and so whenever they meet, there arises a spirit of reconciliation. The bodhisattva's search for the path was arduous like this: even when encountering a murderous enemy, he had no mind of anger or resentment. Therefore he was able to attain Buddhahood and become the Honored One of the three realms."

All the bhikṣus were joyful and bowed to the Buddha.
