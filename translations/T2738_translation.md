# T2738 金剛經疏

# Commentary on the Diamond Sūtra

*Note: This is an anonymous commentary (shū 疏) on the Diamond Sūtra (Jīngāng jīng 金剛經, T235). The surviving text is fragmentary, beginning mid-discussion with the patience (kṣānti) section concerning King Kalirāja (Gēlì wáng 歌利王), then proceeding through the "no-abiding mind" (wú suǒ zhù xīn 無所住心) teaching, the five kinds of speech (true, real, as-it-is, non-deceiving, non-contradictory), and classification of pure lands. The commentator draws extensively on Vasubandhu's (Tiānqīn 天親) *Vajracchedikā-ṭīkā*, Paramārtha's (Zhēndì 真諦) interpretations, and Asaṅga's (Wúzhuó 無著) explanations, comparing their different readings. The commentary also references the *Buddhabhūmi-sūtra* discussion of pure lands and Bodhiruci's (Liúzhī 流支) views. The author and date are unknown.*

---

Therefore a woman's power lies in anger, for when she desires something she must first become angry. A king's power lies in pride and dominance. An arhat's power lies in vigorous effort. All buddhas' power lies in great compassion. A monk's power lies in patience. Thus the *Sūtra of Bequeathed Teachings* (Yíjiào jīng 遺教經) says: "One who can practice patience may be called a person of great power." The verse of the Seven Buddhas says: "Patience is the foremost path; the Buddha declares the unconditioned supreme. A renunciant who troubles others is not called a śramaṇa."

Although patience is of three kinds, what is meant here is the forbearance of the non-arising of dharmas (wúshēng fǎ rěn 無生法忍). Why? Because in practicing according to the sūtra, there is no grasping of either person or dharma. The text has three parts: first, the ability to be patient; second, the absence of suffering; third, the presence of joy. This section demonstrates the ability to be patient.

The forbearance of the non-arising of dharmas is what only the buddhas fully fathom; therefore it is called "the Tathāgata's patience pāramitā." It is not what others can thoroughly investigate; therefore it is said to be "not the pāramitā." Here "patience pāramitā" refers to the Tathāgata's pure wholesome root. "Not the pāramitā" means it is not the wholesome root of those of the two vehicles. If so, then when one practices according to the sūtra and gives rise to the supreme patience of non-arising, how could one be wearied by the experience of suffering? Hence the verse says:

> One who can endure austerities,
> Through austerities there is good:
> That merit is immeasurable;
> This is the supreme meaning.

"One who can endure austerities" means practicing austerities according to the sūtra with the ability to endure. "Through austerities there is good" means that these austerities have the pure patience wholesome root as their essence, and therefore there is good. "That merit is immeasurable" means that this patience wholesome root cannot be fathomed by others. "This is the supreme meaning" means only the Tathāgata can measure it. Therefore it says "patience pāramitā" and "said to be not the patience pāramitā."

## The Absence of Suffering

The sūtra passage "Why? ... no mark of a life-span" and what follows demonstrates the absence of suffering. This section first establishes and then proves the absence of suffering.

The previous section showed that there is suffering but one can endure it. This section shows that because there is patience, there is no suffering. How so? If one perceives a distinction between one's own self and another's self, then one perceives a distinction between the one who inflicts harm and the one who is harmed. At that time, when I practiced according to the sūtra, since I had no grasping of the two selves, I did not see King Kalirāja as the one who inflicts harm nor my body as the one being harmed. Since there is no substance of either form of harm, from whom could the experience of suffering arise?

The commentary on the sūtra says: "No mark of self, no mark of person, no mark of sentient being, no mark of life-span: marklessness and also not non-markedness." Paramārtha (Zhēndì 真諦) says: "No mark of self and the rest means no self-characteristic. 'No mark' means no other. 'Also not non-marked' means it is not without the characteristics of compassion and wisdom."

Asaṅga (Wúzhuó 無著) says: "The 'one who is able to be patient' refers to comprehending the selflessness of dharmas. As the sūtra says: 'The Tathāgata speaks of the kṣānti-pāramitā.'" This refers to the forbearance of the non-arising of dharmas. He further says: "How does one know the characteristic of patience? When others perform evil and the like against oneself, one is without the mark of self and the rest, does not give rise to thoughts of anger, and also does not generate the notion of existence regarding the kṣānti-pāramitā, nor the notion of nonexistence regarding what is not the pāramitā." This explanation differs somewhat. The present interpretation is: regarding the perfection of patience, one does not make a dharma-notion; regarding what is not patience, one does not make a non-dharma notion. Therefore it says "marklessness and also not non-markedness." Though there are acts that bring no benefit, they are encompassed by patience through correct contemplation of dharmas. The story of King Kalirāja should be expounded in detail here.

## Proof of the Absence of Suffering

The sūtra passage "Why? In the past I ... should have given rise to anger and hatred" is the proof, further clarifying the meaning of the absence of suffering. The limb-by-limb dismemberment is the extremity of harm. Not giving rise to anger and hatred is the might of patience. Anger and hatred arise from self-grasping. Since self-grasping is absent, from whom would anger and hatred arise? Anger is a major delusion; hatred is a minor entanglement. A major delusion may provoke a response of murderous intent. A minor entanglement merely causes affliction through torment. Anger is called "major" because it possesses five characteristics; hatred is called "minor" because it lacks them, as explained in the *Treatise on the Mind*.

## The Presence of Joy

The sūtra passage "Subhūti! Moreover, recalling the past ... no mark of a life-span" demonstrates the presence of joy. The previous section showed that because there is patience, there is no suffering. This section shows that because there is no suffering, there is joy. How so? Acting as the sage of patience (rěn xiānrén 忍仙人) reveals the presence of kindness and compassion. The absence of marks of self and the rest reveals the absence of anger and hatred. Because anger and hatred are absent, there is no suffering. Because kindness and compassion are present, there is joy. The verse says:

> Free from the marks of self and anger,
> Truly there is no suffering;
> In suffering and joy there is kindness and compassion:
> This is the fruit of austerities.

The upper half demonstrates the absence of suffering, because there are no marks of self and other. The lower half demonstrates the presence of joy, because there are the characteristics of compassion and wisdom. Through compassion there is joy; through wisdom there is no suffering. The harmony of compassion and wisdom is therefore called "shared."

## The Non-Abiding Mind

The sūtra passage "Therefore, Subhūti ... the mind of samyak-saṃbodhi" introduces the second section, which further prevents faults. One who perceives suffering while practicing austerities becomes mentally fatigued and regresses. One who forgets suffering while practicing austerities becomes mentally light and advances. To prevent the conduct of regression, one is taught to generate the mind of non-abiding. The text has four parts: first, demonstrating the non-abiding mind; second, demonstrating the non-abiding object; third, demonstrating the non-abiding obstacle; fourth, demonstrating non-abiding practice.

This section demonstrates the non-abiding mind. Bodhi has non-abiding as its essence. If one can forget characteristics and generate the mind, the mind itself merges with non-abiding. Therefore, generating the mind free of characteristics is the essence of non-abiding.

Asaṅga says: "To reveal the antidote to the suffering of transmigration, one says 'one should be free of all notions' and the rest." Transmigration means the cycle of birth and death. If one practices giving while attached to characteristics, then within transmigration there is the suffering of weariness. Now, to counteract this, one says "free of all characteristics."

The verse says:

> For not abandoning the mind, giving rise to practice and firmness:
> For the patience pāramitā, cultivate the mind that is able to learn.

"For not abandoning the mind" has two meanings: first, not abandoning the bodhi-mind; second, not abandoning the signless mind. Therefore it says "one should be free of all marks and generate the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi." This is not abandoning the mind. "Practice" means not abiding in form, sound, scent, taste, touch, and practicing. "Firmness" means that if one is free of characteristics, practice is firm.

The commentary explains: generating the mind for practice, what is it for? It is for the patience pāramitā. Cultivate the mind that is able to learn. The patience pāramitā here is the Tathāgata's perfection of patience. The mind that is able to learn is the first-bhūmi mind. Now, to seek the Tathāgata's perfection of patience, one generates the mind and practices, seeking the mind that is able to learn at the first bhūmi. Alternatively, the patience pāramitā is the first-bhūmi mind, and the mind that is able to learn is the mind before the bhūmis. Therefore one cultivates the non-abiding mind before the bhūmis. Hence it says "cultivate the mind that is able to learn." The commentary says: "Entering the first bhūmi, the kṣānti-pāramitā, this is called the non-abiding mind." Non-abiding means not abiding in dharma nor non-dharma. Asaṅga says: "In non-dharma and in dharma-selflessness, one does not abide."

## The Non-Abiding Object

The sūtra passage "One should not abide in form and generate the mind ... one should generate the mind of non-abiding" demonstrates the non-abiding object. "Not abiding in form and generating the mind" causes one to be free of the obstacle of repaying kindness. "Not abiding in sound, scent, taste, touch, or dharma and generating the mind" causes one to be free of the obstacle of karmic retribution. "One should generate the mind of non-abiding" causes one to be free of the obstacle of grasping at characteristics and doubt.

Paramārtha says: "Here one should completely abandon three obstacles: first, one's own body; second, repaying kindness; third, karmic retribution. Regarding one's own body: if one is attached to one's own body, the two views of eternalism and annihilationism arise. Through eternalism, one does not seek the future. Through annihilationism, one does not see the future. Thus practice is obstructed." The other two can be understood similarly.

## The Non-Abiding Obstacle

The sūtra passage "If the mind has an abiding place, that is not an abiding" demonstrates the non-abiding obstacle. If the mind has an abiding place, abiding in the two previous objects, then it is "not abiding," meaning it does not abide in bodhi. If one has this mind, it becomes an obstacle.

Paramārtha says: "'If the mind has an abiding place' means abiding in the three graspings. The three graspings are: first, the view of eternalism; second, the view of annihilationism; third, the view of both existence and nonexistence. To free one from the view of eternalism, one is told not to abide in dharma. To free one from the view of annihilationism, one is told not to abide in non-dharma. To free one from the view of both existence and nonexistence, one is told to abide in the middle way. The first destroys ordinary beings' grasping of the discriminated nature as existent. The second destroys ordinary beings' grasping of the dependent nature as nonexistent. The third destroys the two-vehicle practitioners' grasping of the perfected nature as both existent and nonexistent. If one does not abandon these three graspings, one is not in accord with bodhi, and therefore it says 'not an abiding.'"

## The Five Kinds of Speech

The sūtra passage "Subhūti! The Tathāgata is one who speaks truly ... not one who speaks differently" is the section that urges faith. The five kinds of speech display four meanings:

- "True speech" (zhēnyǔ 真語) refers to true wisdom.
- "As-it-is speech" (rúyǔ 如語) refers to true suchness.
- "Real speech" (shíyǔ 實語) refers to the principles of the four noble truths.
- "Non-deceiving speech" (bùkuángyǔ 不誑語) refers to the recording of events across three time periods.
- "Non-contradictory speech" (bùyìyǔ 不異語) is a single term that comprehensively establishes the previous four as irreversible.

Hence the verse says:

> Though the fruit does not abide in the path,
> The path can serve as a cause.
> Through the buddhas' true speech,
> That wisdom is of four kinds.

The first two lines show that both cause and fruit are non-abiding. The fruit, being free of characteristics, does not abide in the cause. The cause, being free of characteristics, does not abide in the fruit. Though each does not abide, cause and fruit succeed one another. This is abiding within non-abiding.

Question: Is not the "abiding within non-abiding" still an abiding?

Answer: From the conventional standpoint, there are cause and effect, and one can speak of abiding and non-abiding. From the ultimate standpoint, there are no cause and effect, and one cannot speak of abiding or non-abiding.

Asaṅga says: "Conventionally, there are cause, effect, and bodhi." He further explains: "True speech is to reveal the characteristic of conventional truth. Real speech is to reveal conventional practice, with affliction and purity. As-it-is speech is for the characteristic of ultimate truth. Non-contradictory speech is for ultimate practice, with affliction and purity."

The first two kinds of speech establish that speaking the conventional is real. The latter two establish that speaking the ultimate is not false.

Paramārtha says: "True speech refers to the Tathāgata's self-proclamation, which has two aspects. First, the Tathāgata's turning of the Dharma wheel: all worldly brāhmaṇas, heavenly māras, and the rest cannot overturn what the Tathāgata has spoken. Second, the Tathāgata's establishment of the ten epithets: all worldly brāhmaṇas, heavenly māras, and the rest cannot destroy these names. Because the speech is true, it is called 'true speech.' Real speech refers to teaching the four noble truths and three gates of liberation to those of the lesser vehicle: all dharmas are empty of self-nature; being empty, they are signless; being signless, they are wishless. This is spoken only from the perspective of the emptiness of persons. Although there are three names, they share a single essence. Because this teaching is real, it is called 'real speech.' As-it-is speech refers to teaching the three natures and three naturelessnesses to those of the Mahāyāna, namely the emptiness of dharmas and true suchness. The *Treatise on Buddha-Nature* (Fóxìng lùn 佛性論) says: 'Knowing the discriminated nature, one penetrates the emptiness gate of liberation, removing the affliction of the flesh. Knowing the dependent nature, one penetrates the wishlessness gate of liberation, removing the affliction of the skin. Knowing the perfected nature, one penetrates the signlessness gate of liberation, removing the affliction of the heart.' The first removes the obstacle of liberation; the second, the obstacle of dhyāna; the third, the obstacle of omniscience. Having departed from these three obstacles and awakened to suchness, one speaks of suchness; therefore it is called 'as-it-is speech.' Non-deceiving speech refers to recording events of the three times without any error, unlike others who may or may not be accurate. These four kinds of speech are all named after their respective objects."

## The Dharma as Neither Real Nor Empty

The sūtra passage "Subhūti! The Dharma obtained by the Tathāgata: this Dharma is neither real nor empty" prevents faults. Having previously adduced speech to urge faith, there is a risk of guarding words and losing the path. To prevent this, the text offers a corrective.

"This Dharma" refers to the Tathāgata's verbal teaching. This Dharma is what the Buddha's Dharma-discernment penetrates and what the Buddha's verbal discernment articulates. Being in accord with the Buddha, it is the Dharma obtained by the Tathāgata.

The path dwells in the realm beyond words. Guarding words, one loses the path; therefore this Dharma is "not real." Yet words can indicate the path, and apart from words one does not see the path; therefore this Dharma is "not empty." Words can indicate the path; hence the simile of the finger pointing at the moon. The path dwells in the realm beyond words; hence the simile of abandoning the raft.

The verse says:

> In accordance with that true wisdom,
> One speaks of it as neither real nor empty.
> Like hearing a sound and obtaining realization:
> As an antidote, one speaks in this way.

The upper half establishes the thesis. The lower half explains it. Because the Tathāgata's speech accords with true wisdom, it is not empty. Because the principle is realized beyond words, it is not real. "Like hearing a sound and obtaining realization as an antidote" means: when hearing the Dharma, one is free of the two graspings. To break the attachment that says "speech is the path" or "speech is not the path," one says "as an antidote, one speaks in this way."

Asaṅga says: "The Tathāgata's perfect awakening Dharma and his speech: therein is neither reality nor falsity. 'Not real' is because the nature of verbal expression does not exist." That is, the substance of verbal expression is empty, its self-nature being void. "'Not false' is because the self-nature that is expressed through verbal expression does exist." That is, although verbal expression is empty, the principle it reveals has real self-nature.

Paramārtha says: "'Neither real nor empty' has three meanings. First, the understood and the not-understood. Second, the reached and the not-reached. Third, revealing actual reality. For the first meaning: relying on the explicit text to extract the principle, it is not empty. Relying on the implicit text to extract the principle, it is not real. For the second meaning: following words that can reach the path, it is not empty. The path dwells beyond words, so it is not real. For the third meaning: 'not real' means 'truly not real'; 'not empty' means 'truly not empty.' Since both empty and real are actual, this reveals true speech."

The commentary then discusses the Tathāgata's speech as having both the real and the empty through the four intentions (four kinds of implicit meaning) and the four reliances, as taught in the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha*. The four intentions are: first, the intention of equality (as when the Buddha says "I was formerly the Buddha Vipaśyin," spoken from the perspective of the equality of the dharma-body across cause and fruit); second, the intention of distinct meaning (teaching the three naturelessnesses to Mahāyāna practitioners, though previously teaching the selflessness of persons to śrāvakas); third, the intention of distinct time (as when the Buddha says that whoever vows to see Amitābha will be reborn there, understood as rebirth at a distinct future time); fourth, the intention of distinct inclination (praising or reproving according to each person's particular wholesome roots).

These four intentions serve to break eight obstacles: the intention of equality breaks the obstacle of belittling the Buddha; the intention of distinct meaning breaks the obstacle of belittling the Dharma; the intention of distinct time breaks the obstacle of laziness; the intention of distinct inclination breaks five further obstacles (meager aspiration, craving-conduct, pride-conduct, regret-conduct, and indeterminacy).

The commentary then discusses the four reliances (four nodes), corresponding to Paramārtha's and others' analyses. The text continues with the section on practice, discussing the merit of receiving, upholding, reciting, and explaining this sūtra, including the ten kinds of correct practice: copying, making offerings, disseminating, listening, reading, memorizing, self-recitation, broadly explaining, contemplating, and practicing.
