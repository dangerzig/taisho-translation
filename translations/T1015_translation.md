# 佛說阿難陀目佉尼呵離陀隣尼經
## Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1015

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Buddhaśānta (佛馱扇多).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling in Vaiśālī, in the great grove, in a monastery called Decorated Crossbeam Hall, together with a great assembly of three hundred thousand monks.

At that time the Buddha was about to relinquish his life, having set a limit of three months before his parinirvāṇa. Then the Buddha told the Worthy Mahāmaudgalyāyana to go throughout the three-thousandfold great-thousandfold world system and summon all the śrāvakas, those of the pratyekabuddha lineage, and those who had set out on the Mahāyāna, causing them all to come and assemble. Maudgalyāyana immediately received the instruction, bowed at the Buddha's feet, paid homage, and departed. By his own merit, in a single step he reached the summit of Mount Sumeru. Receiving the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, he sat in contemplation, thinking: "What samādhi should I enter so that the three-thousandfold world system will hear my summoning voice?" Instantly the samādhi arose as he wished, and in all ten directions everyone heard his voice. Then a million monks came to assemble at the monastery.

The Worthy Śāriputra also gave rise to this thought: "I too shall enter samādhi, wishing to know the locations of all the monks in Jambudvīpa, near and far, and cause them to gather." Then as he wished, they all assembled and he knew their locations. Then four hundred thousand monks came to assemble at the monastery.

The Buddha further told Adarśanamukhā Bodhisattva, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Duḥkha-prahāṇa Bodhisattva, Sarva-daurmanasya-tamomardana Bodhisattva, Nīvaraṇa-viṣkambhin Bodhisattva, Sarveśvara Bodhisattva, Sarva-lokānukampaka Bodhisattva, Gandhahastin Bodhisattva, Pratibhāna-koṭi Bodhisattva, and Maitreya Bodhisattva: "Good sons! Go to the buddha-fields of the ten directions, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and summon all those who have generated the bodhisattva aspiration: those who have attained non-retrogression and those who have not yet attained it, those who have attained the patience of non-arising dharmas and those who have not yet attained it. Cause them all to come and assemble at Decorated Crossbeam Hall."

The ten bodhisattvas each received the Buddha's instruction, bowed, and departed, going throughout the buddha-fields of the ten directions, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. Then eighty billion hundred-thousand one-lifetime successor bodhisattvas came to assemble; billion hundred-thousand non-retrogression bodhisattvas came to assemble; thirty billion bodhisattvas who had attained the patience of non-arising dharmas came to assemble; sixty billion bodhisattvas of purified mind and liberation came to assemble. Those who had first generated the great aspiration and those who were practicing in accordance with the dharma were beyond count. All alike, sustained by the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, flew in from the ten directions, paid homage to the Buddha, and sat together.

Then Śāriputra, seeing the bodhisattva assembly with their varying ranks and differing abodes, yet all alike flying together to assemble, was startled and awestruck, saying: "This must be accomplished by the Buddha's merit and spiritual power. For what purpose have they gathered like this?"

Thereupon Śāriputra rose from his seat, paid homage to the Buddha, knelt with palms joined, and addressed the Buddha: "I am filled with wonder, and now wish to ask the Buddha, so that the assembled bodhisattvas may attain direct faith. Having heard the Buddha's teaching, may each attain their proper station. May the sūtras and dharma taught by buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges all be heard equally by everyone and lead to purity. May they be able to answer the various questions of countless people. What practice should one cultivate to never lose one's intent and swiftly attain the supreme, equal, most perfect enlightenment?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Śāriputra! For the sake of the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas you wish to ask this meaning. How wonderful! Listen attentively, remember well, and contemplate it. I shall deeply liberate you and cause all who hear to swiftly attain the supreme, equal, most perfect enlightenment."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "These bodhisattvas have already attained and responded to this dhāraṇī scroll. They have already illuminated all dharmas and serve as guides for all dharmas. Why? For the sake of the people of the world."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva, through four practices, attains this dharma. What are the four? First, the body's conduct is always careful and disciplined. Second, the mouth's speech is always sincere. Third, the mind's thoughts are always gentle and yielding. Fourth, through skillful means one rescues and protects all beings. These are the four practices by which one attains this dharma."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva, through four further practices, attains the inexhaustible wisdom of the empty body. What are the four? First, by pure abiding, burn away all afflictions and dissolve the stains of doubt, liberating all the people of the world. Second, by pure abiding, uphold the sūtras and dharma to guide and benefit all. Third, by pure abiding, perform meritorious deeds to enrich and benefit all. Fourth, purify the buddha-fields, ferrying all across, enabling them to attain the buddha-dharma. These are the four practices. The wisdom of the empty body in pure abiding is inexhaustible."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva, through four further practices, holds the dhāraṇī gateways and uses them to enter birth and death, enabling swift attainment. What are the four? First, the dhāraṇī gateway uses worldly name-and-form to enable swift attainment of the pure dharma. Second, the dhāraṇī gateway enters patience. Third, the dhāraṇī gateway enters all the roots of thought. Fourth, the dhāraṇī gateway enters all the dharmas of good and evil deeds, enabling swift attainment. These are the four practices for swiftly attaining the dhāraṇī gateways."

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva who wishes to attain the Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī should first know and awaken to its forty-eight names. What are the forty-eight?

*Akimakkhi samantamukhi siddhini luti parāpai ile kapai kapaṃdali sali salārabhali hila hilī hiklī candi cala-nai phacane alanai niḥvadi niśedhini arāpai māli śuthane parāhadabhane bhaupipai jihī asaṅghidāmi viphalā-khaiṣai saṅkasthidhili mahāsthidhili yajapidhī aścarī māścarī samāścarī dīlā saṇdhī śauthidhī asaṅkaṣai hali samantamukhi nihāla-sudhī sumidaṃpi-ṣabhadi dīlāmi mabhadi mahā-khaiṣaṣai phulā-laimī saharū ṇukatī anajithi dhāraṇī-mukha dvinadhi.*

"The present buddhas have spoken these divine mantras of forty-eight names."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva should be mindful of the dhāraṇī in this way: whether liberated dharma or non-liberated dharma, not performing this or that action, neither knowing nor understanding; within it, neither increasing nor decreasing; neither seeing liberation nor seeing non-liberation; neither seeing arising nor seeing ceasing; neither seeing future, past, nor present; neither seeing coming nor seeing going; neither seeking the Buddha nor turning the mind, neither grasping marks of excellence nor the detailed marks, nor retinue; neither having precepts, nor samādhi, nor wisdom, nor liberation, nor the knowledge-and-vision of liberation; neither sitting practice nor non-attainment, nor removal of defilements; neither wisdom nor non-wisdom; neither teaching nor purifying all; neither having a self nor having a person; neither acting for the dharma nor being diligent; neither practicing nor being pure; neither body nor mind nor mouth; neither future nor past nor present; neither for one's own body nor for another's body.

"Thus, Śāriputra! This dharma is named the supreme dharma among all dharmas. It also gathers all dharmas. It also enters all dharmas. This is called mindfulness of the buddhas' dharma. This dhāraṇī is named the lion's roar of dharma, which briefly gathers all dharmas. This is named the chapter of clear understanding. It purifies all people, fulfills all vows, and enables attainment of the renowned awakening-mind samādhi. This is named the self-accomplishment of fundamental merit. This is named the dharma-seed treasury. It arouses all the faculties, making them deeply honored, adorned with marks of excellence, immovable. Even if heavenly māras come, they cannot destroy it. None can seize it; none can approach it.

"Thus, Śāriputra! If a bodhisattva hears this Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī, they have already attained non-retrogression and the supreme, equal path. Why? Because the practices of all buddhas are already fully accomplished herein. It performs the practice for all, shatters the five paths of birth and death and all doubtful fetters, and enables swift attainment of the dhāraṇī."

Then the Buddha spoke these verses:

> Do not think of dharmas as empty;
> Do not lightly speak of attainment or non-attainment.
> Have direct faith in the dharma without harboring doubt,
> And you will swiftly attain the dhāraṇī.
>
> Fear being bound by emptiness itself;
> Discerning emptiness, attain buddhahood swiftly.
> This essential wisdom has no shore or bottom;
> Swiftly draw near the dhāraṇī.
>
> The bodhisattva who holds the dhāraṇī
> Thereby gathers all the dharma principles.
> They hear the teachings of buddhas in the ten directions
> And obtain all wisdom.
>
> Comprehending the nirhāra-dhāraṇī
> Is like the great radiance of the sun.
> Receiving the named and prophesied dharma of all buddhas,
> Purely upholding it, one attains dharma understanding.
>
> This mukhā-dhāraṇī,
> In which all that exists manifests before one,
> Is supreme among all dharmas,
> Equally protecting the people of the world.
>
> If all humans and non-humans of the ten directions
> Were to question its wisdom-meaning for an entire kalpa,
> One could resolve all their doubts,
> And when the kalpa ends, the wisdom would still not be exhausted.
>
> If one faithfully receives and follows the sūtra path,
> Diligently upholding the correct dharma therein,
> One draws near the station of one-lifetime successor
> And becomes a dharma prince.
>
> If one holds this dhāraṇī,
> One thereby has compassion for all people.
> Their fame spreads throughout Jambudvīpa,
> And the World-Honored One praises them.
>
> Those who hold this sūtra
> Will, at the end of their lives, see
> Eighty billion koṭi buddhas
> Extending their hands to guide them to an honored rebirth.
>
> Those who study this dhāraṇī
> Will have all the sins and evil darkness
> Committed through thousands of billions of kalpas
> Entirely extinguished within a single month.
>
> If a bodhisattva practices merit-giving
> For ten thousand billion kalpas without wearying,
> It does not compare to studying the dhāraṇī
> For a single month, whose merit is equal.
>
> Completely receiving and teaching the dhāraṇī,
> One attains all samādhi and wisdom.
> The mind, once attained, will never regress,
> And one will become a buddha with a buddha-field.
>
> If one makes offerings to this sūtra,
> Though the three realms send māras to act,
> Wishing to disturb and destroy one's intent,
> They can never shake this person.
>
> All emptiness becomes clearly understood;
> Through this, countless buddhas are born.
> These words are without error:
> The dhāraṇī is inexhaustible.
>
> In this place, hearing this matter,
> Dīpaṃkara bestowed my prophecy.
> Buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges
> Were all visible at that time.
>
> Faithfully upholding this sūtra scroll,
> All wishes will be swiftly attained.
> Entering the dharma of all buddha-fields,
> One transforms and liberates as appropriate.
>
> Always purifying the buddha-fields,
> Within the saṅgha, without flaw or stain,
> And the profoundly subtle dharma of the named depths,
> This sūtra scroll can know it all.
>
> All licentiousness has ceased;
> In contemplation there are the seven factors of awakening.
> Eighty billion buddhas
> Bestow and entrust the dhāraṇī entirely.
>
> Always calm the mind and do not think wrongly;
> What should not be thought, carefully do not think.
> By thinking thus, without not-thinking,
> The merit attained hereafter is immeasurable.
>
> Always understand the matters of this sūtra;
> Carefully do not allow any doubt.
> Like a person who enters the great ocean,
> One never says that treasures are hard to find.
>
> Receiving the blessings and joys of devas and humans,
> Such matters are never far away.
> One will also swiftly attain buddhahood;
> Therefore, never abandon this mindfulness.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva, through four practices, swiftly attains the dhāraṇī dharma. What are the four? First, distance oneself from worldly desire. Second, do not speak of others' faults so that others cannot find fault with you. Third, when someone comes to ask for something, do not go against their wishes; be without attachment, and after giving, have no regret. Fourth, day and night practice diligently, always aspiring to seek all dharmas. These are the four. Śāriputra! If a bodhisattva practices these, they will swiftly attain the dhāraṇī."

Then the Buddha spoke these verses:

> Distance yourself from all evil and depart from sensual desire;
> Committing sins leads to falling into the hells.
> Without the three poisons, calamity does not arise;
> Abandon what you cherish and attain this sūtra.
>
> Do not be jealous of others;
> Do not praise yourself or your own kin.
> Always maintain an equal mind toward all,
> And your body will be greatly upright and proper.
>
> Always abandon the body's pleasures;
> In the world, be without anger or contention.
> Do not engage in disputes with others,
> And you will draw near the dhāraṇī.
>
> Morning and night, always study diligently;
> From non-Buddhist paths, hope for nothing.
> Seek the supreme dharma in this way,
> And the dhāraṇī will naturally appear.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva, through four practices, swiftly attains this dhāraṇī. What are the four? First, dwell alone in a secluded place. Second, abide in profound dharma patience. Third, if one receives gifts, do not be overjoyed. Fourth, do not cherish body or life, let alone material treasures. These are the four. Śāriputra! If a bodhisattva practices these, they will swiftly attain the dhāraṇī."

Then the Buddha spoke these verses:

> In seclusion, contemplate the true dharma;
> Do not be contemptuous of others.
> The realm of birth and death is like carrying fire on your head;
> Having obtained a human body, one should practice.
>
> Always maintain the mind in profound patience;
> Regarding material treasures, be content with enough.
> From the ancestral home, generate an equal mind;
> Do not show favoritism to your own lineage.
>
> Only make offerings to the Buddha and Dharma;
> Abide in good power to transcend worldly difficulties.
> Then shave the head and lower the hair,
> And you will attain merit and enter the subtle and wondrous.
>
> Know emptiness and worldly affairs;
> For this reason, abandon all possessions.
> Foolish practitioners crave wealth and gain
> And lack precepts, patience, and wisdom.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva, through four further practices, swiftly attains this dhāraṇī. What are the four? First, there are eight syllables that open entry into the midst of wisdom and discernment. What are the eight?

"The first is named *pa*: it causes all dharmas of attachment to enter the dharma of emptiness.

"The second is named *ra*: whether the tathāgata manifests the marks of excellence or does not manifest them, the dharmakāya enters all dharmas.

"The third is named *bha*: whether the dharma of the foolish or the dharma of the wise, it causes entry into the dharma of understanding and wisdom.

"The fourth is named *ka*: knowing where sins and misfortunes lead, it causes entry into merit and blessings.

"The fifth is named *ja*: knowing birth, aging, sickness, and death, one thereby enters formlessness.

"The sixth is named *tha*: seeing that all grounds and worlds are empty without alarm, one enters wishlessness.

"The seventh is named *śa*: it brings all dharmas to rest and causes entry into the dharma of coolness.

"The eighth is named *kṣa*: knowing that all dharmas are empty.

"These are the eight. Furthermore, there are four practices: First, always contemplate and be mindful of these eight syllables. Second, if one writes this sūtra, write it with careful attention. Third, if one upholds this sūtra, uphold it with careful attention and recite it through the fifteenth day. Fourth, practice in accordance with the dharma and be mindful of these eight syllables. These are the four practices. Śāriputra! Through these practices, the bodhisattva swiftly attains the dhāraṇī."

Then the Buddha spoke these verses:

> Always be mindful of the eight syllables;
> Write, uphold the scroll, and faithfully practice.
> Through the fifteenth day, recite without ceasing,
> And exhort all to seek the buddha path.
>
> You must learn to teach and transform others;
> Attaining wisdom, swiftly draw near the Buddha.
> You will see all the buddhas of the ten directions,
> All appearing and standing before you.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva, through four further practices, obtains the dharma-benefit of the dhāraṇī. What are the four? First, one is always remembered by the buddhas and world-honored ones of the ten directions. Second, no māra can disrupt one's intent. Third, all sins from past lives are entirely extinguished. Fourth, one's sincere speech is never interrupted, and one can resolve all questions. These are the four. Śāriputra! Such is the dharma-benefit the bodhisattva attains from the dhāraṇī."

Then the Buddha spoke these verses:

> Always remembered by all buddhas,
> No māra can find an opening.
> All sins committed are instantly extinguished,
> And one resolves all doubts for all.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Long ago in the past, the kalpas that have elapsed are countless, and redoubled, and immeasurably long, and incalculably distant. At that time there was a buddha in the world named Ratnasaṃpanna-guṇacāri-rāja Tathāgata, who had crossed beyond the four paths and attained the equal, perfect enlightenment, at peace among the devas, called Devatideva. That buddha's teaching of the dharma to all was unsurpassed.

"When his parinirvāṇa was imminent, there was a golden-wheel king named Dhāru, who ruled the four continents. His son was named Asmṛti-yaśas-saṃpanna. He was sixteen years old. At that time the prince went to the buddha's presence and heard that buddha teach the dhāraṇī sūtra. Having heard it, he rejoiced and immediately attained this sūtra. Having attained it, he chanted, studied, constantly recited, and upheld it. He practiced diligently without sleeping, his sides never touching the mat, for seven thousand years. He did not think of desire for seven thousand years. He did not think of material treasures for seven thousand years. He did not inquire about others' affairs for seven thousand years. He always dwelt alone, his mind unmoved, for seven thousand years. At that time he heard ninety-nine billion incalculable buddhas teach the dharma of wisdom. Having heard, he then abandoned his kingdom, relinquished his throne, and became a śramaṇa. For ninety-nine thousand years he practiced the Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī and further taught it to all. In one lifetime he taught and transformed the people of the world, causing eighty billion nayutas of people to all generate the aspiration for the supreme, equal path and attain non-retrogression.

"At that time there was also an elder's son named Candravīrya, who in the great assembly heard this dhāraṇī. Having heard it, he encouraged and assisted in its upholding. By the merit of this encouraging rejoicing, he went to ninety billion buddha-fields, made offerings to the buddhas, established merit, and in all those buddha-lands attained various dhāraṇīs. He then attained the station of one-lifetime successor and became a flying bodhisattva.

"The Buddha told the bodhisattvas: 'Through three kalpas he will make offerings to that many buddhas. After three kalpas have passed, he will attain the supreme, equal, most perfect enlightenment.'

"The elder's son Candravīrya of that time is none other than Dīpaṃkara Buddha. King Dhāru's son Asmṛti-yaśas-saṃpanna is none other than Amitābha Buddha."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "In the Bhadrakalpa, I and the bodhisattvas taught the dhāraṇī. Those who heard this sūtra and rejoiced on behalf of others, those who encouraged all, by the merit of their encouragement and assistance, transcended four hundred thousand kalpas of suffering, and will later attain the path and fulfill their wishes. Furthermore, having made offerings to ninety thousand billion buddhas, they will attain the supreme, equal, most perfect enlightenment."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "If in the final age a bodhisattva hears this dhāraṇī and upholds it, encourages and assists, and rejoices on behalf of others, their merit is as described. If a bodhisattva, through the merit of rejoicing, with a compassionate mind sheds tears for the sūtra and their hair stands on end, they will all attain the supreme, equal path and be established in non-retrogression. How much more so for those who make the effort to write, uphold, study, chant, recite, or read it! Śāriputra! The blessings of such people are immeasurable, incalculable, inexpressible. No one among all people can reckon them or reach their limit."

Then the Buddha spoke these verses:

> Those who hear and encourage and assist,
> Who write, uphold, chant, recite, and read,
> All the devas and humans who reckon their merit
> Cannot know the limit of their blessings.
>
> Wherever they are born, they always see the Buddha;
> In the profound sūtras, they attain the proof of wisdom.
> Firmly holding the dharma, immovable,
> They swiftly attain clear, perfect enlightenment.
>
> With mind always settled, neither increasing nor decreasing,
> Faithfully never losing the dhāraṇī,
> Without form-perception yet establishing the marks of excellence,
> They become the World-Hero, unsurpassed.
>
> The elder's son who hears this sūtra
> Will in every birth remember past lives,
> Always see countless honored ones,
> And as he wishes, attain buddhahood.
>
> If you wish to swiftly attain buddhahood
> And quickly be free from all māras,
> And wish to attain the hundred marks of merit,
> Always be mindful of this, and attainment will not be long.
>
> If worlds as numerous as the sands of the Ganges
> Were filled with precious treasures and given in charity,
> It would not compare to writing and upholding this;
> Even a simile could only be a rough approximation.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "If a bodhisattva, with concentrated mind, studies the dhāraṇī, there is a mountain called Himavat where eight spirit-deities dwell, who will always guard and protect such a person and open their aspirations. What are the eight? First, the spirit-deity named Heroic Strength. Second, the spirit-deity named Illuminating the Ten Directions. Third, the spirit-deity named Greatly Beneficial. Fourth, the spirit-deity named Nāga King Great Power. Fifth, the spirit-deity named Sincere Practice. Sixth, the spirit-deity named Tamer of the Untamed. Seventh, the spirit-deity named Youthful One. Eighth, the spirit-deity named Swift Arms. These are the names of the eight spirits.

"If one who studies this sūtra wishes the spirits to come, one should bathe in purity and put on fresh garments. When practicing walking meditation, one should always maintain a compassionate mind toward all. Then, with a composed heart, recite the dhāraṇī. One should firmly uphold this sūtra. Then one will swiftly gain open understanding and attain wisdom, and at that time the spirit-deities will stand before one."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Furthermore, there are eight bodhisattvas dwelling in the desire heavens who always equally protect people and bestow the sūtra path. What are the eight? First, the deva named Illuminating the Ten Directions. Second, the deva named World Radiance. Third, the deva named Wisdom Light. Fourth, the deva named Sunlight. Fifth, the deva named Supreme Discernment. Sixth, the deva named Fulfiller of Wishes. Seventh, the deva named Star King. Eighth, the deva named Practice Intent. These are the names of the eight devas. They always equally protect people and encourage those who are mindful of and uphold this sūtra, enabling them to quickly settle the mind and attain the dhāraṇī.

"Those who seek this sūtra should always maintain earnest aspiration and be mindful of the dhāraṇī teaching, practicing the one dharma without violating the sūtra precepts. Their speech should be sincere, and their conduct likewise. They should always be filial and respectful to their elders, regarding all people as no different from the Buddha. Whatever they cherish, they should not treat lightly. Always be mindful of reciprocity, and one will immediately attain a good reward, and eventually realize profound patience and the vision of the dharma of non-arising."

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, thirty-two times the sands of the Ganges of bodhisattvas all attained the dhāraṇī and were established in the stage of non-retrogression. Sixty billion deva-sons attained the patience of non-arising dharmas. Thirty-two thousand devas and humans generated the aspiration for the supreme, equal path. At that time, the three-thousandfold great-thousandfold world system shook in six ways, heavenly flowers rained down, and the harps and musical instruments sounded without being struck.

Then Śāriputra approached and asked the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is this sūtra called? How should it be practiced?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "This sūtra is called the Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī. It swiftly enables people to attain omniscience."

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, Śāriputra and the countless bodhisattvas of the ten directions, devas and humans, gandharvas, asuras, and world-protectors all rejoiced, approached, bowed at the Buddha's feet, paid homage, and departed.
