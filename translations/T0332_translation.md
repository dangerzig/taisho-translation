# 佛說優填王經
## Sūtra of King Udayana Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0332

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Fajù (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the country of Kauśāmbī. The king of that country was named Udayana. In Kauśāmbī there was a householder named Mahāmoha, whose daughter was exceptionally beautiful, a rare beauty in the world. Her father, seeing her appearance as unique in all the land, named her Anupamā. Kings of neighboring countries, ministers, and men of noble families all sought her hand in marriage. Her father answered: "If there is a gentleman whose appearance equals my daughter's, I shall give her to him."

At that time the Buddha was traveling in that country. The householder saw the Buddha's thirty-two marks and eighty excellent characteristics, his body the color of purple-gold, majestic and imposing, his radiant deportment unsurpassed, and was delighted, thinking: "My daughter has found her match." He returned home and told his wife: "I have found a husband for Anupamā." They hurried to adorn and dress the daughter and set out. Husband and wife together adorned the daughter, who walked like a jade flower, radiant with pearls and gem necklaces, her splendor illuminating the land. Together they brought the daughter to the Buddha.

On the road, the wife saw the Buddha's footprints, their marks of excellent characteristics and radiant color such as do not exist in the world. Knowing this was the World-Honored One, she told her husband: "The prints and markings of this person's feet are such as are never heard of in the world. This is surely no ordinary man; he must be self-purified, with no more desire for sensuality. He will not take our daughter. Do not bring shame upon yourself." The husband said: "How do you know this?" The wife then spoke a verse:

> "A lustful man drags his heels when he walks;
> An angry man walks stomping his toes;
> A foolish man stumbles on the ground.
> These footprints belong to the World-Honored of devas and humans."

The householder said: "This is not something you as a woman would understand. If you don't wish to come, then go back home." So he himself brought the daughter to the Buddha, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, and addressed the Buddha: "Great One, you labor in teaching and instruction, yet have no one to attend upon you. I have this simple daughter. May she serve to sweep and keep your quarters."

The Buddha said: "Do you consider your daughter beautiful?"

He answered: "Having been given this daughter, whose countenance is truly lovely and unmatched in the world, though kings and nobles of many countries sought her, I did not give her to them. Seeing the Great One's radiance and majesty, such as are never seen in the world, and desiring to make an offering, it is proper that I present her."

The Buddha said: "Wherein lies this daughter's beauty?"

The householder said: "Examining her from head to toe, there is nothing that is not beautiful."

The Buddha said: "How deluded are eyes of flesh! When I examine her from head to toe, there is nothing beautiful at all. The hair upon the head is merely animal hair; the tails of elephants and horses are no different. Below the hair is a skull, which is merely bone; the skull of a pig at the butcher's stall is the same. Within the head is brain, which is like mud, rank and foul; if the nose dripped it upon the ground, none could step on it. The eyes are fragile sacs of fluid. The nose contains mucus. The mouth has only saliva. The belly contains the liver and lungs, all of which are rank and foul. The intestines, stomach, and bladder produce only feces and urine, putrid and foul beyond description. The belly is a skin-sack wrapping all manner of impurities. The four limbs and hands and feet are bone upon bone, held together by sinews and encased in skin, depending upon breath to move. It is like a wooden puppet worked by mechanisms: once disassembled, its joints and limbs scatter in disarray. A person is the same. What beauty is there, that you call her 'unmatched'? Long ago, when I was beneath the bodhi-tree, the king of the sixth desire-heaven adorned three daughters, with beauty and color unmatched in all the heavens, not merely of this human sort, and tried to use them to ruin my resolve for the path. I described to them the impurities within the body, and they were immediately transformed into the appearance of old women, broken and ruined. Ashamed, they departed. This sack of excrement: what is there to cling to? Take her back at once. I will not have her."

When the householder heard what the Buddha had said, he was suddenly overcome with shame and had nothing more to say. He then addressed the Buddha: "If the Benevolent One will not accept her, may I instead give her in marriage to King Udayana?" The Buddha did not reply. The householder then sent his daughter to King Udayana. The king, delighted to receive the woman, appointed her father as Grand Tutor, and built a palace for the woman, with a thousand musicians and attendants to serve her.

The king's chief queen was a disciple of the Buddha and had attained the fruit of stream-entry. The new woman slandered her to the king. The king, beguiled by her words, shot a hundred arrows at the queen. The queen saw the arrows but showed no fear and had no anger at all. With single-minded focus she thought of the Buddha with a compassionate mind, kneeling and facing the king. The arrows all circled around the queen three times and came to rest before the king. All one hundred arrows did so. The king was amazed and struck with awe.

He immediately mounted a white elephant with a golden chariot and raced to the Buddha. Before arriving, he descended from his chariot, dismissed his retinue, and proceeded on foot. He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, knelt, and confessed: "I have committed a grave offense before the Three Treasures. Through that lustful temptress, following desire and breeding wickedness, harboring evil thoughts toward the Buddha and the holy saṅgha, I shot a hundred arrows at a disciple of the Buddha. I confess this as it happened. Seeing this, my heart is filled with dread. Only the Buddha, the true and perfectly awakened one, possesses immeasurable compassion. A lay disciple's power of compassion is already such; how much more so the unsurpassed, perfectly awakened Buddha? I now confess my wrongdoing and take refuge in the Three Treasures. May the Buddha, in his vast compassion, pardon my offense."

The Buddha smiled and said: "Excellent, O King! To recognize one's evil and repent is the conduct of a wise person. I accept the king's good intention."

The king bowed his head, and did so three times; the Buddha also accepted three times. The king then placed his forehead on the ground, withdrew to his seat, and said: "I am endowed with a violent and headstrong temperament, willful and without a mind of patient endurance. The three poisons are not eliminated; evil conduct brings pleasure. I followed the seductive temptress and did not know her wickedness. Reflecting on myself, when my life ends I shall surely enter hell. I beseech the Buddha to extend compassion and broadly teach me of the evils of women, their beguiling and bewitching ways. Those who fall into their net can rarely free themselves. If I hear of their calamities, I shall surely use them to admonish myself, and the people of my kingdom, great and small, may also reform their conduct."

The Buddha said: "Why ask about this? Let me rather speak of other principles."

The king said: "Other matters can be discussed another day. The confusion and calamity wrought by women is immense. Without hearing of their dangers, how can one distance oneself from them? I beseech the Buddha to explain for me in full the transformations of hell and the impurities of women."

The Buddha said: "Listen well! It is the evil of men's lust that turns back to see the beguiling of women."

The king said: "Excellent! I wish to receive the clear teaching."

The Buddha said: "Listen well! Men have four evils of which they should be urgently aware. There are lustful men in the world who constantly imagine and gaze upon women, thinking to hear seductive voices, who abandon the true Dharma from afar, doubt truth and believe in falsity, are ensnared in the net of lust and sink into blind darkness, driven by desire like slaves fearing their master. They delight in women's beauty, unaware of the foul discharge from the nine openings, wallowing in lust like pigs in a sty, not perceiving the stench but finding it comfortable. They do not reckon that hereafter they shall be in waterless hells, suffering pain without limit. Their minds are fixed on lust: they swallow saliva and mucus, play with pus and blood, treasuring it like jade and savoring it like honey. Therefore they are called men of lustful behavior. This is the first evil.

"Furthermore, parents raise their children, enduring pregnancy and birth, nurturing them from infancy to adulthood with toil and hardship beyond description. When the child comes of age, they exhaust the household's wealth, going on bended knee, employing matchmakers to express their feelings and procure a wife for their son. Even if she is in a foreign land, they pursue her, regardless of distance or hardship. Their minds fixed on lust, they neglect and forget their aging parents. Once they have obtained a wife, they treasure her like a jewel, wishing only for private pleasure, and resenting the sight of their parents. Believing her seductive words, they may even start quarrels and litigation, forgetting the body from which they came and the immeasurable grace of their forlorn parents. This is the second evil.

"Furthermore, in this world one toils with exhausting labor to earn one's own wealth, originally possessing sincere faith and a mind that reveres the path, honoring śramaṇas and brahmins, perceiving the impermanence of the world, and making offerings as merit. But after taking a wife, one is confused and blinded by lustful desire, turning away from truth toward falsehood, devoted entirely to female beauty. Even if the thought of generosity arises, though one wishes to speak of it, the sight of the woman's face cuts off all pure conduct and one becomes a petty person, ignorant of the precepts of the Buddha's sūtras and the consequences of fortune and misfortune. Foolishly pursuing lustful beauty, one casts oneself into the net, surely falling into the evil paths, and to the end does not reform. This is the third evil.

"Furthermore, as a son one does not consider the grace of one's upbringing; in acquiring wealth one does not use it to support one's parents but only seeks lustful paths far and wide, carrying precious things to seduce other men's wives, or slaughtering animals in lustful sacrifices to ghosts and spirits. After drinking, singing, dancing, and meeting together, they contrive opportunities to call upon each other to consummate their illicit passion. Having obtained their partner, their joy knows no bounds, bound in the fetters of lust and unable to recognize anything else. At such a time, they think only of this pleasure, unaware of the foul discharge or the suffering and pain of hell. It is laughable on one hand and terrifying on the other, like a mad dog that does not know its own wrongdoing. This is the fourth evil."

The Buddha said: "Men have these four evils by which they fall into the three paths of suffering. One should carefully distance oneself from these behaviors to avoid suffering." He continued: "Now listen to the evils of women." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Already driven by desire,
> The mind let loose cannot find peace.
> Habituated to unlawful acts,
> How can one be called virtuous?

> "Desire is the conduct of animals;
> Through desire one brings calamity on oneself.
> Worms in filth,
> Not knowing their situation is dire.

> "Like worms in darkness,
> Not knowing east from west:
> Bound and attached to lustful desire,
> Such a one is of the same sort as worms.

> "Lust prevents seeing the path;
> Day and night it plants roots of sin.
> In the present world, ruler and minister are disordered,
> High and low are confused and bewildered.

> "The king's law becomes disordered,
> The true Dharma becomes a tangled fence.
> Farmers abandon their regular work,
> Merchants become entangled in their wares.

> "In this life one enters prison;
> After death one enters the mountain of hell.
> One must endure a hundred kinds of poison;
> The pain is difficult to bear.

> "Molten copper is poured into the mouth,
> Mountains press down upon the body.
> These punishments number in the hundreds;
> They are difficult to describe one by one.

> "Always in the three evil paths,
> Turning like a wheel.
> Even if a Buddha appears in the world,
> One cannot hear it.

> "Women are the worst of evils,
> Difficult to make connection with.
> Once bound by love and affection,
> They pull one through the door of sin.

> "What is beautiful about a woman?
> She is but a sack of feces and urine.
> Why not look carefully and see this,
> Rather than going mad and reckless?

> "Within she is exceedingly foul;
> Without she is adorned with fine appearance.
> In the house there is a poison-scorpion:
> More deadly than a snake or dragon.

> "Like a brocade sheath covering a lance,
> Gauze and silk wrapping the blade's edge:
> Fools see only the outside
> And play with it to their own calamity.

> "The wise perceive and renounce it;
> The deluded bring upon themselves mortal injury.
> Lustful desire is just like this:
> Embracing a blade to one's own destruction.

> "Seeing the new, one wearies of the old;
> What one delights in is likewise impermanent.
> Words are like axes that cut;
> Smiles are like thorns and briars.

> "Within she harbors foul poison;
> Without she adorns herself with flowers and fragrance.
> The fool craves the taste,
> Not considering the calamity that follows.

> "Like poisoned wine mixed with sweet dew,
> It destroys whatever it touches;
> All who drink it collapse.

> "Like firewood that catches fire,
> Like grass and trees struck by heavy frost:
> Seeing the surface without considering within,
> This is the most inauspicious of all.

> "The poison of women is worse than this,
> For grass only harms the visible form.
> To sever desire and seek the path,
> Yet still to harbor lustful feelings:

> "The form is easy enough to see,
> But the deluded do not sever their feelings.
> The net is spread on all four sides;
> From the path it is as thin as a thread.

> "Humans are originally of a pure nature,
> Like fish dwelling in a deep pool.
> The wise alone awaken to this;
> Caught in the net, one cannot return.

> "The net of desire is worse than this;
> Its bonds are firmer than any fetter.
> Casting one's body into thorns,
> One may yet free the body.

> "Like a hungry monkey
> That sees ripe sweet fruit,
> Single-mindedly rushing toward lustful beauty:
> Such persons fall a hundred ways.

> "Like a fish swallowing a hook,
> Like a moth entering a lamp's flame:
> The fool sees it and rejoices,
> Not considering the calamity to follow."

When the Buddha had taught this, King Udayana was filled with joy. He placed his forehead on the ground and addressed the Buddha: "Truly, from birth until now I had never heard that the evils of women were so great, or that men's disordered follies in pursuing them lead to such grave sins. But only because I did not know, I did not control my mind and thoughts. From this day forward, I shall repent for the rest of my life. I take refuge in the Three Treasures and dare not transgress again." He bowed to the Buddha and departed with joy.
