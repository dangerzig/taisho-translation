# 佛說滿願子經
## Sūtra of Pūrṇa Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0108

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the country of Mṛgaśīrṣa, in the Trackless Mountains, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus. At that time, the Venerable Pūrṇa, rising from quiet sitting in the late afternoon, went to where the Buddha was. Baring his right shoulder and placing his right knee on the ground, he bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "Excellent, World-Honored One! Please teach me the essential Dharma in brief. I shall practice it, that my body may enjoy lasting peace and happiness without end."

The Buddha said: "Listen carefully and reflect well upon it."

Pūrṇa replied: "Yes, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha told Pūrṇa: "When the eye sees a beautiful form, a pleasing object, one that is lovable and delightful and stirs craving; when the ear craves pleasant sounds, the nose delights in fine scents, the tongue savors delicious tastes, the body clings to soft and pleasurable touch; when one loves what is desired and yearns for what is craved: suppose a bhikṣu delights in it, approves of it, and lets his mind dwell in it. Having dwelt in craving, there is delight and confusion, and from this come affliction, sorrow, and grief. But suppose, Pūrṇa, a bhikṣu sees form with his eyes, a pleasing object, yet does not take delight in it, and his mind does not dwell in it: then affliction and grief are eliminated. It is the same for the ear, nose, mouth, body, and mind. This is the essential Dharma taught in brief. The Buddha's instruction and admonition are given to you. Now where do you wish to travel?"

Pūrṇa addressed the Buddha: "Yes, World-Honored One. There is a country called Śrōṇāparānta. I wish to travel to that country."

The Buddha said: "The people of that country are fierce and evil, rough in temperament, unkind, and fond of quarrels and strife. Suppose the strange and evil people of that country revile and insult you. What will you do?"

"Suppose the strange evil people of that country revile and insult me. I shall think in my mind: 'They still love me and respect me; they are forgiving toward me, and their hands do not strike me.'"

The Buddha said: "Suppose they push and shove you. What will you do then?"

Pūrṇa said: "I shall think in my mind: 'They still love me and respect me; they are kind and gentle, and they do not throw stones and tiles at me.'"

The Buddha said: "Suppose they throw stones and tiles at you. What will you do then?"

Pūrṇa said: "The people of that country are good, humane, and gentle. They do not wound my body with swords and clubs."

The Buddha said: "Suppose they wound your body with swords and clubs. What will you do then?"

Pūrṇa said: "I shall think: 'The people of that country are good, gentle, and mild. They do not take my life with a sharp blade.'"

The Buddha said: "Suppose they take your life with a sharp blade. What will you do then?"

Pūrṇa said: "I shall think in my mind: 'This body has six sense faculties, and because of them there is affliction. I am weary of the body and its many torments and impurities. I seek the blade as sustenance; my aspiration is only for the flavor of entering into quiescence, taking the blade as sustenance.'"

The Buddha said: "Excellent, Pūrṇa! You are able to endure this. With such patient forbearance, gentle quiescence, endurance, and benevolence, you may dwell in that country as you wish."

Thereupon Pūrṇa rose from his seat, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated him three times, went to his quarters, and that night put away his belongings, arranged his bed, and rested. At dawn he donned his robes, took his bowl, and set out for that country. Straightaway in that country, during a single summer retreat, he taught, converted, and established five hundred male lay followers, five hundred female lay followers, and built five hundred monasteries, five hundred caves, five hundred beds and couches, and five hundred sets of Dharma robes and bedding, each numbering five hundred. He converted five hundred people, all of whom became śramaṇas. Within that year he realized the three knowledges and then entered nirvāṇa.

Not long after his nirvāṇa, an assembly of innumerable thousands of bhikṣus went to where the Buddha was, bowed their heads at his feet, sat to one side, and all together addressed the Buddha: "There was a bhikṣu named Pūrṇa, for whom the Buddha taught the essential Dharma in brief. He has now entered nirvāṇa not long ago. What did he attain? What realization did he achieve?"

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! That son of good family had aroused the three knowledges, realized the six supernatural powers, contemplated the Dharma in accordance with truth, and had no equal. Without engaging in any other matter, he only expounded the Dharma canon. All his outflows were exhausted, with no further defilements remaining. He had crossed beyond thought and perception, and was liberated through wisdom. In the present Dharma he had fully penetrated all supernatural powers, realized complete attainment, cut off birth and death, fulfilled the brahma conduct, accomplished what was to be done, and was clearly renowned in his true nature. All forms of wisdom were without arising. His sacred knowledge was complete and he had attained arhatship." At that time, the World-Honored One ceaselessly praised and extolled Pūrṇa Maitrāyaṇīputra.

The Buddha spoke thus. The bhikṣus all rejoiced.
