# 佛為勝光天子說王法經
## Sūtra of the Dharma of Kingship Spoken by the Buddha for King Prasenajit

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0593

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, at the Jeta Grove of the Anāthapiṇḍada Garden, together with a great assembly of hundreds of thousands of bhikṣus, all of whom were great arhats whose outflows were exhausted. He was also together with immeasurable bodhisattva mahāsattvas, great dragons among humans, destined for buddhahood in one more life. At that time the World-Honored One, beneath a tree upon a supremely fine seat, sitting cross-legged, was teaching the assembly of humans and devas the wondrous Dharma realized by himself, which is good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, with skillful language and meaning, pure and complete, clean and bright, bearing the marks of the holy life.

At that time, Prasenajit, the king of Kosala, with his grand retinue, departed the city of Śrāvastī and went to the Jeta Grove, wishing to pay respects to the World-Honored One, to revere and make offerings, to serve and draw near to him. Having arrived at the grove, he descended from his chariot, straightened his garments, and went to the Great Teacher's place. From a distance he saw the Tathāgata seated beneath a tree, teaching the Dharma for the assembly. His countenance was upright and beautiful, his faculties tamed and his mind's delight tranquil, abiding in supreme concentration: a dragon-elephant among humans, like the lion-king, like the bull-king, like a well-trained horse, the foremost among humans, like a white lotus, like a serene pool, like Mount Sumeru resting in the great ocean, endowed with the thirty-two marks and the eighty minor excellences, like a wondrous golden banner, his form and color fully radiant, like the bright sun with a thousand rays blazing, like a full moon encircled by the stars.

When the king saw this, great joy arose, the hairs of his body stood on end, and he exclaimed that it was unprecedented. The anointed great king possessed five adornments: the wish-fulfilling crest-jewel, the white parasol, the white whisk, the jeweled shoes, and the jeweled sword. He set them all aside and dressed in common garments. With his great ministers, in a calm and mindful manner, his faculties tranquil, he bared his right shoulder, adjusted his clothing, bowed with palms joined, came before the World-Honored One, bowed at the Buddha's feet, spread fine flowers, and burned various renowned incenses. Having made offerings, he circumambulated three times on the right and sat to one side.

Then King Prasenajit rose from his seat and, in his accustomed dignified manner, joined his palms toward the Buddha and spoke thus: "May the Great Teacher! Awaken me and instruct me well in the dharma of being a sovereign, so that in this present life I may always receive peace and happiness, and after my life ends I may be born in heaven, and up to the attainment of awakening my wholesome mind may continue."

The Buddha said to the great king: "Excellent! Excellent! Listen singlemindedly. This is most rare! Who could ask such a question, seeking the supreme provision? You should follow the Dharma in your conduct and eliminate evil matters. Why? Great King! If a king or great minister abandons wholesome dharmas and practices evil dharmas, in this present life he will be scorned by the people; no one will dare draw near. All will be suspicious and doubtful. He will always have bad dreams and many enemies. Afterward he will be filled with remorse, and after his life ends he will fall into Naraka. Great King! If a king or great minister is far from evil dharmas and cultivates wholesome dharmas, in this present life he will be admired by the people, who will all come to draw near without suspicion or doubt. He will always have good dreams and be able to overcome enemies. He will have no further regret, and after his life ends he will be born in heaven, and up to the attainment of awakening he will realize the true, eternal happiness.

"Great King! Just as parents love their children with compassion, always wishing for their safety and peace, shielding them from harm, blocking their evil conduct, and encouraging them to cultivate good deeds, so too, Great King, should one who is a sovereign be toward all ministers, subjects, and the people down to servants and attendants: nourishing them all with kindness through the four methods of attraction: generosity, kind speech, beneficial action, and working together. When the king can broadly act thus for the benefit of his realm, he accomplishes two kinds of benefit. What are these two? The king is like a parent, loving without partiality; the people are like children, all loyal and filial.

"Furthermore, Great King! One who is a sovereign should have a heart of grace and forgiveness, keep taxation light, reduce corvée labor, establish offices and assign duties without making them overly numerous, dismiss and punish the evil, and promote and reward the virtuous. Those who are not loyal and good should be swiftly removed. Follow the ancient sage-kings and do not carry out executions. Why? Those born in the human realm have been brought there by superior conditions. If one cuts off their lives, one will certainly receive evil retribution. Great King! You should always singlemindedly revere the Three Treasures and not give rise to wrong views. After my nirvāṇa, I entrust the Dharma to the kings, great ministers, and counselors. You should protect it and not let it decline. Light the torch of the true Dharma, turn the wheel of the true Dharma, and to the end of future ages cause it never to be extinguished. If you can practice in accordance with this teaching, the nāga kings of your realm will be pleased, the winds will be timely and the rains will fall in season, the devas will rejoice, there will be abundance and happiness, peace and ease, all disasters will be eliminated, and the land will be at peace. The king's body will be in comfort, his supreme position will be eternally preserved, the power of his blessings will be extended, he will have no further worry or vexation, and his lifespan will increase. His fame in this present life will fill the ten directions. The kings of other lands will all praise him together: 'The sovereign of that land is benevolent, yielding, loyal, and filial. He teaches and transforms with the Dharma and relieves and cares for the common people. Among all the lands, he is foremost. We should now all submit to this great Dharma-king. After we cast off our bodies, we shall be born in heaven and receive supreme, wondrous happiness, up to the attainment of awakening!'

"Furthermore, Great King! The essential nature of all dharmas is empty. They are impermanent and subject to destruction. Suppose a person in a night dream sees a beautiful garden, mountains and rivers and people, flourishing forests and clear springs, halls and pavilions all delightful. But upon waking, nothing is seen. Great King, you should know! The royal position you have inherited, together with your lifespan, all your supreme pleasures, your freedom and honor, the enjoyments of the five desires, your elephants, horses, chariots, and foot-soldiers, your parents and brothers, your sons and daughters, your queens and consorts, all your subjects and servants, your gold, silver, and precious treasures, your clothing, food and drink, and all your storehouses: at the time of death, all will be abandoned. All these things are dharmas of impermanence and destruction. They are hard to hold and protect; their nature is to be in motion and will ultimately disperse. They are places of fear and produce suffering. There is no self, nothing belonging to a self, and no master. You should constantly observe this and not be heedless.

"Furthermore, Great King! Suppose a great tree first produces leaves and flowers, then bears fruit. When the fruit is ripe, it gradually falls. The green leaves turn yellow and afterward all drop away, until at last there is only the bare tree. The tree dries out, and when a great fire comes, the fierce blaze soon burns it to nothing.

"Furthermore, Great King! Suppose the sun and the moon have great power and great radiance, able to eliminate all darkness completely. Yet they too will eventually be worn away and extinguished. Great King! Thus you should observe the impermanent, selfless nature of destruction and give rise to fear. As a king, you should govern with the Dharma and not practice what is wrong. Always cultivate goodness and do not follow evil conduct.

"Furthermore, Great King! Suppose from four directions great mountains advance, solid and without gaps, pressing upward against the sky and grinding the earth below. Among them, all the grasses, trees, and forests, and all living beings, not one flying or running creature can escape. No strong man can resist them; no spells, medicines, or wealth can turn them back. Great King! The four mountains of the human world are just the same: old age, illness, death, and loss of power. Great King! When old age comes, it makes a person withered and frail. When illness arrives, it produces suffering and vexation. When the time of death appears, life must surely be cut short. When power is lost, one's might is extinguished.

"Furthermore, Great King! Like the lion-king, swift and powerful, with sharp claws and teeth, entering a herd of deer and feeding at will with nothing to obstruct him; these creatures, beset by his terror, have no freedom. Great King, you should know! All sentient beings are struck by the arrow of death. There is no power, no refuge, no protection. When life is about to end, the joints separate, the flesh and blood dry out, the mouth cannot speak, the hands and feet flail in confusion, all strength is gone. Saliva, feces, and urine soil the body. The six sense-faculties of the eyes and the rest are all blocked. The breath in the throat reverses, and food and drink cannot pass. Moment by moment, the final consciousness is about to end. From beginningless time, through birth, old age, illness, and death, one flows and transmigrates in the ocean of suffering according to one's karma. At this very time, the life-faculty is about to be severed, and all the karma one has created appears before one. The messengers of Yama are terrifying. In the long dark night there is nothing one can defy. The breath in and out ceases in an instant. One goes alone without a companion, bewildered in every direction. Leaving this human world, one proceeds to the next life, about to fall into a great pit, entering a place of deep darkness. One travels only a dangerous road, without provisions for the journey. Blown by the wind of karma, one does not know the road ahead. At that time of extremity, there is no other refuge. In this moment, one receives retribution according to one's karma.

"Great King! There is a dhāraṇī called Jayapatākā. If a person has previously received and upheld it, it can serve as a good companion in birth and death, mutually rescuing and protecting. Great King, listen well! I shall now teach it to you. The dhāraṇī is:

Namo Śākyamunaye Tathāgatāya Arhate Samyaksaṃbuddhāya. Tadyathā: Oṃ śame śame sarva-pāpa-praśamanaye svāhā.

The Buddha said to the great king: "This dhāraṇī was spoken by all the Buddhas. Each day, having purified oneself with bathing and rinsing, one should constantly recite it seven times. It has great spiritual power and can provide rescue, like encountering a blazing fire in extreme cold; like obtaining cool water in great heat; like finding a good tree's shade while traveling in midsummer; like finding a clear spring when thirsty; like obtaining fine food when hungry; like receiving a healing spell when ill, and also meeting a good physician; like a frightened person finding a strong companion. Great King! When a person of such merit is about to die, auspicious signs appear to guide them. Great King! At this time, only wholesome dharmas can protect and be mindful of one, serving as a refuge. Only this dhāraṇī can truly rescue and save. Therefore, Great King! You should always recite this sacred spell each day. It can eliminate all karmic obstructions and further generate immeasurable causes of merit. You should carefully observe: impermanence, destruction, and ultimate emptiness. At the gate of death, give rise to great fear. Transform the world with goodness and do not practice evil dharmas. Always cultivate meritorious deeds and arouse great compassion. Why? For this body that one constantly loves and protects, supplied with fine kitchens and supreme food and drink, resting at ease in due season, without worry or care; though one receives such supreme pleasure, in the end one cannot escape, at the time of death, being pressed by the fire of hunger and dying without food.

"Furthermore, Great King! The garments you wear are all exquisite: Kāśī cloth, white cotton, brocade, damask, silk, and gauze, suited to the season's warmth or cool, receiving pleasure as you wish. Yet in the end you cannot escape: at the time of death, lying helplessly on the bedding, turning only with another's help, filth clinging to the body, soiled sheets covering you, causing all who see to feel aversion.

"Furthermore, Great King! In your daily life you bathe and adorn your body, applying paste-incense and powdered incense, with all manner of ornaments, suffused with fragrant incense, with flower garlands bound upon your head. Even if you receive such supreme and delightful adornments, in the end you cannot escape the gradual decay, returning to your original form, with foulness appearing before you. Bathing and applying incense are all in vain.

"Furthermore, Great King! You dwell much in the inner palace, surrounded by beautiful women, with pipes and strings playing in turn, and singing and dancing as you please. You pass the time in pleasure without hearing of sorrow. Yet in the end you cannot escape when the suffering of death arrives; you die in fear.

"Furthermore, Great King! The palace where you dwell is adorned in every way: the windows are open and airy, the temperature is pleasant and comfortable. You enjoy yourself all day and take pleasure all night. Chamber lamps burn brightly; many beautiful women attend you; thin curtains diffuse fragrance; famous flowers are spread everywhere. Upon a bed adorned with the seven treasures, with blankets and cushions layered deep, with pillows placed for comfort, you sleep as you please, without worry or toil. Yet when your karma is exhausted, in the end you cannot escape: after your death, your body is sent to the cold forest and placed in the empty wilderness. The corpse decays and rots, pus and blood flow freely, bones and flesh come apart. All people despise it. Foxes, wolves, owls, eagles, and vultures compete to feed upon it. Alas, this body! It comes to this at last.

"Furthermore, Great King! On fine mornings and festive days, you prepare your grand chariot and leave the city, going to fragrant groves to enjoy yourself freely. Elephants, horses, chariots, and foot-soldiers follow before and behind. You ride at your pleasure with every wish fulfilled. Ministers and attendants carry pheasant-plume fans in solemn array. Parasols are raised high and golden canopies held aloft. Drums and music play together, and bells and chimes ring in harmony. All the people revere you like Śakra, lord of the heavens. Yet when your merit and life are exhausted, Yama's messengers come to seize your spirit and bring it before their king, who judges and sentences according to one's portion, and none can escape. Only the remains are placed upon the ground. Parents, wives, children, and the people all weep together, beating their chests in grief. The spirit is carried in a funeral carriage to the charnel ground, where the body is burned, or buried, or sunk in water. Flying birds, running beasts, fish, turtles, and crocodiles, smelling the flesh, compete to feed upon it. The bones turn to dust, no different from the earth. Great King, you should know! All sentient beings, all that have consciousness, are just like this: all are ultimately destroyed by impermanence. The body is hard to trust and cannot be preserved. Moment by moment it changes and moves. This body of afflictions has nothing lovable about it. What wise person would not feel revulsion?

"Therefore, Great King! You should observe that this body is the root of affliction, constantly followed by impermanence, forever driven by the king of death. Knowing this, you should be a Dharma-king and should not indulge your feelings in greed, anger, and delusion or practice evil deeds. Why? Great King! I do not say that foolish ordinary people, who in the five sense-realms of form, sound, scent, taste, and touch constantly amass and always delight in, can generate weariness and contentment. Great King! Who can give rise to weariness regarding the objects of desire? It is the wise and noble ones who, when their superior wisdom appears before them, generate weariness and contentment, gradually depart, and realize the wondrous nirvāṇa."

At that time King Prasenajit, having heard the Buddha teach him to bring peace to his own person, to preserve his royal position forever, to benefit all sentient beings, to have his fame fill the ten directions, to be born in heaven and receive supreme and wondrous happiness, was deeply joyful in his heart, experiencing what he had never experienced before. With palms joined in reverence, gazing upon the Buddha singlemindedly, he said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The Tathāgata's great compassion has taught me such wondrous and subtle Dharma. I now receive it upon my head and shall always wish to propagate it. All the people of my kingdom shall be caused to recite and learn it."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Great King! You cultivated causes in former lives and now receive this supreme reward, becoming a sovereign with every wish fulfilled. Practice in accordance with what has been taught, and do not be heedless!"

Then King Prasenajit and the great assembly were all filled with great joy, faithfully received and practiced it, bowed to the Buddha, and departed.
