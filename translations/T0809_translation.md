# 佛說乳光佛經
## Sūtra of the Milk-Light Buddha, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0809

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in Vaiśālī, beneath the Music Tree of the brahmin Māyāri, together with eight hundred bhikṣus and a thousand bodhisattvas. Kings, great ministers, people, devas, nāgas, spirits, and other beings had all assembled for the teaching of the sūtra.

At that time, the Buddha, the World-Honored One, happened to have a slight wind-ailment and needed cow's milk. Now, in the country of Vaiśālī there was a brahmin named Māyāri. He was teacher to five hundred disciples and was also honored by the king, great ministers, and people. He was powerful, wealthy, jealous, and miserly; he had no faith in the Buddha-Dharma, took no delight in generosity, and followed only heterodox paths. He always kept nets covering his rooftop and courtyard to prevent birds from stealing the grain in his household. His dwelling was neither near to nor far from the Music Garden. Thereupon the Buddha said to the worthy Ānanda: "In the Tathāgata's name, go to the house of the brahmin Māyāri and ask him for some cow's milk."

Ānanda received the instruction, put on his robe, took up his bowl, and went to the brahmin's gate. The brahmin Māyāri had just been about to set out with his five hundred senior disciples to visit the king at the palace. He was just then leaving his house when he encountered Ānanda and asked him: "Why have you come so early in the morning? What do you seek?"

Ānanda replied: "The Buddha, the World-Honored One, is slightly unwell and has sent me this morning to ask for cow's milk."

The brahmin Māyāri fell silent and thought to himself: "If I do not give milk to Ānanda, people will say I am stingy. But if I give him the milk, the other brahmins will say I follow the Way of Gautama. I am caught in a dilemma either way. Nevertheless, I shall direct him to the vicious cow and let Ānanda milk it himself. Moreover, this Gautama is always competing with us over merit and always wants to come out on top. I shall have this vicious cow gore his disciple to death; that will humiliate his Way, and he will be abandoned, and I can regain the people's respect. Whether Ānanda gets the milk or not, it will at least show everyone that I am not stingy. If the cow kills him and he cannot get the milk, my purpose will be achieved, and no fault will be mine."

After the brahmin Māyāri had schemed in this way, he told Ānanda: "The cow was let out to graze this morning in the ditch over there. Go and milk it yourself."

Māyāri then ordered his son and servants: "Take Ānanda and show him where the cow is. Take care that you do not milk the cow for him. Let us see whether Ānanda can get the milk."

When the five hundred disciples heard their teacher say this, they were all greatly delighted. They were also suspicious of what Ānanda had said earlier, and they said to one another: "The śramaṇa Gautama always praises himself, saying: 'In all the heavens above and below, I am the most honored. I deliver all beings in the ten directions from old age, sickness, and death.' How is it that the Buddha himself is now ill?"

After the five hundred brahmins had spoken thus, at that time Vimalakīrti was coming to visit the Buddha, and his path happened to pass before the gate of the brahmin Māyāri. Seeing Ānanda, he said to him: "Why are you standing here with your bowl so early in the morning? What do you seek?"

Ānanda replied: "The Tathāgata is slightly afflicted with a wind-ailment and needs cow's milk. He therefore sent me here."

Vimalakīrti then told Ānanda: "Do not say such things! The Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One: his body is like vajra. All evils have already been severed; only wholesome merit has gathered together. What illness could there be? Be silent and go. Do not say such things again, lest the devas, nāgas, and spirits hear this sound, and lest the bodhisattvas and arhats of the ten directions all hear these words. The wheel-turning sage-king, with the wheel before him, through his innumerable merits always attains sovereignty. How much more the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One, who through incalculable kalpas has given to all beings, and whose immeasurable merit has gathered to form the Tathāgata's body? Ānanda! Do not let the non-Buddhists, heterodox practitioners, and brahmins hear these improper words! How could the World-Honored One himself be ill, unable to cure himself, yet able to save others from old age, sickness, and death? The Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One has a Dharma-body, not a body still bound by suffering. The Buddha is the most honored in all the heavens above and below; he has no illness. The Buddha's illness has been entirely extinguished. The Tathāgata's body possesses innumerable merits and all afflictions have been removed. His illness has a cause and purpose; it does not arise without reason. Ānanda! Do not be ashamed to seek the milk. Go quickly, and take care not to say too much!"

When Ānanda heard this, he was greatly ashamed and fearful. Then he heard a voice from the sky saying: "Ānanda! What the elder Vimalakīrti says is true. But the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One has appeared in this world, in a defiled and evil age of the five corruptions. It is for this reason that he manifests this, to liberate all beings in the ten directions from the conduct of desire, anger, and delusion. Go now and get the milk. Although Vimalakīrti has spoken these words, do not be ashamed."

At that time, Ānanda was greatly startled and amazed, wondering if he had heard incorrectly. He thought to himself: "Could this be the awesome spiritual power of the Tathāgata at work?"

Thereupon the five hundred brahmins, hearing what the voice from the sky had spoken, were freed from all doubt and suspicion. Filled with joy, they all generated the aspiration for supreme, true enlightenment.

At that time, the brahmin Māyāri's household members, relatives, and the people of his village, together numbering several thousand, all followed Ānanda to see the cow. When Ānanda arrived, he stood beside the cow and thought to himself: "According to the rules of a śramaṇa, I should not milk the cow myself."

No sooner had he finished this thought than the throne of Śakra, Lord of the second Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, began to tremble. Śakra descended from heaven and transformed himself into a young brahmin in brahmin's garments, and stood beside the cow.

When Ānanda saw him, his heart was filled with joy. He said: "Young brahmin! Please milk the cow."

The young man replied: "I am not a brahmin. I am Śakra, Lord of the second Trāyastriṃśa Heaven. I heard that the Tathāgata wished to have cow's milk, so I left my abode and came here, wishing to establish the foundation of merit."

Ānanda said: "Lord of the Heavens, your rank is exalted. How can you come near this foul-smelling cow?"

Śakra replied: "Though I am powerful, how can I compare to the Tathāgata? The Honored One does not weary of building merit; how much less should a minor deva like myself? My station is impermanent, and all shall pass away. If I do not build merit now, my store of blessings will be exhausted, and I shall have nothing to rely on hereafter."

Ānanda said to Śakra: "If you would milk the cow for me, please do so now."

Śakra replied: "Very well." He immediately took a vessel and approached the cow. At that moment, the cow stood calm and still, not daring to move.

The onlookers were all astonished and amazed: "What urgent cause brings this young brahmin to milk the cow for Gautama's disciple? What if this vicious cow gores him to death? Why does the śramaṇa not come and milk the cow himself?"

At that time, Śakra milked the cow for Ānanda and spoke in verse:

> "The Buddha now has a slight wind-ailment; give to him your milk.
> If the Buddha drinks it and recovers, the merit gained is immeasurable.
> The Buddha, honored teacher of devas and humans, with a constant mind of compassion,
> Cares for all beings, even those that wriggle and crawl, wishing to liberate them all."

At that time, the calf's mother spoke in verse to Śakra:

> "How wonderful is the touch of this hand that strokes me!
> Take the milk from two of my teats and leave the rest.
> Keep what remains for my calf, who has not yet drunk this morning.
> Although I know there is great merit, one should be fair in one's intentions."

Thereupon the calf spoke in verse to its mother:

> "Through innumerable kalpas, I have now heard the Buddha's name.
> I say: take my share as well and offer it all to the Buddha.
> The World-Honored One, teacher of all, is so rarely encountered again.
> I shall eat grass and drink water; that will suffice for me today.
> Since I was human, I have drunk milk for ages uncountable,
> And in my lives as livestock, likewise beyond reckoning.
> The foolish of this world are many indeed,
> Not knowing that giving to the Buddha brings benefit; later, remorse is of no use.
> In my former lives, my stinginess led me to quarrel and push,
> And I followed evil companions, with no faith in the Buddha's precepts,
> Causing me to become an ox or horse for fully sixteen kalpas.
> Now at last I have encountered a Buddha, like a sick person finding medicine.
> Take all the milk I would have drunk and fill the bowl.
> May I later gain wisdom and attain the Way, as the Buddha has done."

Śakra then milked the cow for Ānanda and obtained a full bowl. Ānanda received the milk with great joy.

At that time, the brahmins and villagers who had come to watch all heard what the cow and her calf had said. They were all astonished, and they said to one another: "This cow is crude and has always been vicious; no one can come near her. Why is she so gentle and docile today? It must be because Ānanda has transformed her. The disciple of Gautama can do even this; how much more the merit and awesome spiritual transformations of the Buddha himself! And yet we have not believed his teaching." Thereupon they were filled with joy and came to understand the Buddha-Dharma. The entire household of the brahmin Māyāri, the men and women of the village, together numbering more than ten thousand, were all filled with joy. Freed from dust and defilement, they attained the purity of the Dharma-eye.

Ānanda carried the milk back to the Buddha. At that time, the World-Honored One was teaching the Dharma for innumerable thousands of people. Ānanda came forward, straightened his robes, knelt on both knees with palms joined, and said to the Buddha: "Earlier, when I was sent to the house of the brahmin Māyāri to seek milk, the cow and her calf spoke in human words. When I heard their words, I was greatly astonished."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "What did the cow and her calf say, and why are you perplexed?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "This Māyāri has a cow that is extremely vicious and likes to gore and trample people. No one in the household dares come near her. The master himself cannot milk her, but she produces milk on her own. This cow gave birth to a calf, large and fine, a hundred, a thousand times superior to other calves. The brahmin secretly instructed his son and servants not to milk the cow for me. I thought to myself: 'According to the rules of a śramaṇa, I should not milk the cow myself.' No sooner had I finished this thought than Śakra, Lord of the second Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, descended and transformed himself into a young brahmin in brahmin's garments, standing beside the cow. I said: 'Would you please milk the cow?' Śakra said: 'Very well.' He went forward and milked the cow, telling her: 'The World-Honored One now has a slight wind-ailment and needs your milk. If you give milk to the Tathāgata, the merit will be immeasurable.' Then the cow spoke to Śakra: 'Take milk from two of my teats, but leave the milk from the other two for my calf.' The calf was beside its mother. Hearing the World-Honored One's name, its heart was filled with joy, and it said to its mother: 'Offer my share of milk entirely to the Buddha. The Tathāgata, World-Honored One, teacher of devas and humans, is so rarely encountered. Since I was human, I have drunk milk for a very long time, and as an animal, likewise. The foolish of this world are many; most do not know that giving will bring them reward in future lives. In my past lives, because I followed evil companions and did not believe in the sūtras, I liked to quarrel and fight. For this reason, I have fallen among oxen and horses for sixteen kalpas. Only now have I heard the Buddha's name. Give all the remaining milk to the Tathāgata. May I later gain wisdom and attain the Way, as the Buddha has done.' This is what the cow-mother and her calf said."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "It is truly as the cow and her calf have spoken."

The Buddha said: "Listen carefully to what I shall say. In former lives, this cow and her calf were once elders, very wealthy and possessed of great riches, yet they were stingy and miserly, unwilling to give. They had no faith in the Buddha's sūtras and precepts and did not understand the root of birth-and-death. They constantly enjoyed lending money and charging interest to those who came to borrow. When the day of repayment arrived, they delighted in demanding excessive interest. Without any reason or justification, even after debts were repaid, they would falsely claim they had not been fully repaid. Solely because of this, they fell into the realm of livestock for sixteen kalpas. That they now rejoice upon hearing my name is because their punishment as livestock is nearly complete. Therefore, upon hearing the Buddha's name, the mind of compassion arose, and they offered their milk to the Buddha. Through this cause and condition, they shall attain liberation." At that moment, the Buddha smiled. Five-colored light issued from his mouth, and the heavens and earth were greatly shaken. The light illuminated the ten directions, then circled his body three times and separated into two parts: one part entered his navel, and one part entered through the crown of his head and was no more visible.

Thereupon Ānanda came forward, knelt on both knees with palms joined, and said to the Buddha: "The Buddha does not smile without reason; there must be a cause."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Your question is excellent! Why? After the lives of this cow-mother and her calf come to an end, they will be reborn seven times in Tuṣita Heaven and Brahmā Heaven, and seven times among humans, where they will always be children of powerful and wealthy families. They will never again be born in the three evil destinies. Wherever they are, they will always recall their former lives. They will make offerings to all the Buddhas with banners, pennants, canopies, scattered flowers, and burnt incense, and they will receive and uphold the sūtras. Through this cause and condition, the cow-mother will finally encounter Maitreya Buddha, become a śramaṇa, practice vigor, and before long attain the way of the arhat. The calf will likewise do so. After twenty kalpas have passed, the calf will become a Buddha named Milk-Light, and his land will be called Banner-Light. When Milk-Light Tathāgata becomes a Buddha, he will liberate devas and humans above and below, as well as all beings that wriggle and crawl, in numbers like the sands of the Ganges. At that time, the people of his land will all live for seven thousand years. Their clothing and food will be like those of the beings in the northern continent of Uttarakuru. That Buddha will teach in the world for forty thousand years. After he enters parinirvāṇa, his sūtras and Dharma will endure for ten thousand years before coming to an end."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "The cow and her calf, with good hearts and wholesome intentions, offered their milk to the Tathāgata, and both shall attain liberation. Even livestock have wholesome hearts; how much more should human beings? With their six sense-faculties complete, able to distinguish good from bad, yet they do not believe in the brightness of where life comes from and where death leads, and they do not trust the Buddha's sūtras and precepts, and they do not believe that giving will bring reward in future lives. People fall solely because of stinginess and greed, thereby deceiving themselves. Their minds conceive evil, their mouths speak evil, and their bodies commit evil. Such is the foolishness of people, and all because of this they cannot attain liberation."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, five hundred sons of elders in the assembly all generated the aspiration for supreme, true enlightenment. Three thousand eight hundred brahmins who had previously not believed in the Buddha-Dharma, upon hearing the sūtra, were filled with leaping joy and at that moment attained the way of stream-entry. Five hundred people who had previously not believed in the retribution of birth-and-death, upon seeing the Buddha's transformation, all took the five precepts and became lay disciples. When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the bhikṣus, elders, brahmins, and all the people were greatly delighted. They bowed their heads at the Buddha's feet and withdrew.
