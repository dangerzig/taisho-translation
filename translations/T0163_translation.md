# 妙色王因緣經
## Sūtra on the Causes and Conditions of King Suvarṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0163

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada.

At that time, the World-Honored One arose from meditation and taught the supreme sweet-dew Dharma for the four assemblies. There were innumerable hundreds of thousands of people assembled around him, their faculties undisturbed, listening to the essential Dharma.

The bhikṣus, seeing that the great assembly was tranquil in body and mind, diligently listening to the Dharma, all had doubts and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! We beseech your compassion to cut through our net of doubt. O Tathāgata, Great Teacher, Unsurpassed Dharma King: why do the people in this assembly listen to the Dharma so diligently, with body and mind unmoving, hearing the wondrous Dharma as if drinking sweet dew?"

The World-Honored One said: "Bhikṣus! In the past, when I sought the Dharma, my reverent heart was deeply earnest. Listen carefully! Reflect well upon it! I shall explain the causes and conditions for you.

"Long ago in the past, in the great city of Vārāṇasī, there was a king named Suvarṇa. He governed the world with righteousness. The land was abundant and joyful, the people flourishing and thriving. There were no battles, no deceit, no enemies. There was no sickness, no calamity. Rice, sugarcane, cattle, and sheep filled every place. There were no stones, no wasteland, no thorns. He cherished the people as he would gaze upon his own child. The king was faithful and reverent, his intentions benevolent and good. He benefited himself and others, made firm vows, had a compassionate heart, aspired to the great Dharma, had pity on the people, loved his officials, and cast off miserliness, always practicing great generosity. The king's queen was named Suvarṇāṅgī. She was beautiful and dignified, her deportment composed, her virtues complete, and she was loved by all. The king had only one son, named Rūpavat. Though young in years, he was loyal, filial, benevolent, and compassionate. The king loved and doted on him and never let him leave his side.

"At a later time, King Suvarṇa longed for the supreme Dharma. He summoned all his ministers and told them: 'I thirst for the wondrous Dharma. You should seek it out for me.' The ministers came before the king and said: 'Great king! You should know that only when a fully enlightened World-Honored One appears in the world is there wondrous Dharma.' The king replied to his ministers: 'Although there is no Buddha now, try to seek it for me.' The king then placed a box filled with exquisite golden treasures atop a banner, sounded drums, and proclaimed throughout the four directions: 'If anyone can teach me the supreme Dharma, I shall repay his kindness with this golden box and celebrate with abundant music.' Though this proclamation was made over a long time, not a single person came who could teach the Dharma. The king waited in longing, dwelling in anxiety.

"At that time, Śakra surveyed the world below: who was good and who was evil? Who was tireless in pursuing the supreme cause? He saw that this king was troubled by his longing for the Dharma, and thought: 'This King Suvarṇa has long desired the supreme Dharma. I shall test him, to see if his resolve is genuine or not.' He then transformed himself into a great yakṣa, with strange limbs and a terrifying face. He came into the midst of the assembly and said to the king: 'You seek the supreme Dharma, and I can teach it.' The king, hearing this word of Dharma, was overjoyed and said to the yakṣa: 'Lord of the Secret Ones! If you possess the wondrous Dharma, I beg you to teach it. I shall listen with full attention!' The yakṣa said: 'The great king now takes the Dharma lightly, thinking it easy to obtain and commanding me to teach it. This should not be so. My body is famished and weak. How can I teach?' When the king heard these words, he immediately ordered the kitchen steward to quickly bring the finest food. The yakṣa said: 'The food from the king's kitchen is not what I eat. Only the hot blood and flesh of humans is my constant food.' The king said: 'How can human blood and flesh be quickly obtained?' The yakṣa said: 'The king's beloved son should be given to me.' Hearing these words, the king thought: 'I have long toiled in seeking the supreme Dharma. Now that I hear a word of Dharma, it is beyond all price.' At that time, Prince Rūpavat was standing beside his father. Hearing these words, he knelt and said to the king: 'I beseech my royal father not to be troubled! What my father wishes, I shall fulfill. You may offer my body to the Lord of the Secret Ones to serve as his food.' The king said: 'You see the value of seeking the Dharma and are willing to give up your beloved body. Excellent, noble one! Follow your own wish.' The prince then offered his body to the yakṣa. The yakṣa received it and, before the king and the great assembly, tore apart his body and devoured his flesh and drank his blood. Though the king saw this, his longing for the Dharma was so deep that he had no alarm or fear.

"The Lord of the Secret Ones then told the king: 'I am still not sated. Give me your wife as well.' At that time, Queen Suvarṇāṅgī was also beside the king. Hearing these words, she likewise offered her body to the yakṣa. The yakṣa received it and devoured her blood and flesh. He then told the king: 'My hunger is still not satisfied.' The king said: 'Lord of the Secret Ones! I have already given my only son, and you have also consumed my wife, and still you say you are hungry. Take whatever you wish. I will provide it, without any thought of turning back.' The yakṣa said: 'The king's own body should be given to me to eat.' The king said: 'Excellent! I truly do not begrudge it. But if my body dies, how shall I hear the Dharma? Let me first listen to the wondrous Dharma. Having received and upheld it, I shall immediately offer my body.'

"Thereupon the yakṣa made a pact with the king, and in the midst of innumerable hundreds of thousands of millions of the great assembly, he spoke this supreme and wondrous gāthā:

> "From love arises sorrow,
> From love arises fear.
> If one is free from love,
> There is no sorrow and no fear."

"When the king heard this supreme and wondrous Dharma, his heart was filled with joy, and his delight was boundless. He told the Lord of the Secret Ones: 'I have now heard the Dharma. I shall receive and uphold it as taught. Now this body of mine: eat it as you wish.'

"At that time, the god Śakra saw that the king, for the sake of the Dharma, was immovable in body and mind, like Mount Sumeru, and knew that he would surely attain the supreme enlightenment. He cast off the form of the yakṣa and resumed his form as the god Śakra. Faith and joy filled him within, and with a cheerful countenance he came forward, one hand holding the son and the other holding the wife. He told the king: 'Excellent! Excellent! Noble man! You have put on the armor of resolve and defeated the army of afflictions. You will save the deluded and ferry them across the ocean of birth and death. Seeing such courage, you shall surely before long accomplish the supreme, perfect enlightenment. Your wife and son I now return to you.' The king then said to the god Śakra: 'Excellent! Excellent! O Lord of the Devas, Kauśika! You have descended in great compassion as a good spiritual friend and have already fulfilled my heart's desire for the Dharma.' At that time, the god Śakra suddenly vanished from the midst of the great assembly."

Then the World-Honored One told the bhikṣus: "What do you think? Do not have any other thought. The King Suvarṇa of that time is my present self. Prince Rūpavat is Rāhula. The queen Suvarṇāṅgī is Yaśodharā. You should know that in the past, for the sake of seeking the Dharma, I gave up my beloved wife and son, and even my own body, without any reluctance, to say nothing of other things. Because of these causes and conditions, at the present time, all the great assembly who hear the Dharma from me listen with concentrated attention and without fatigue. And because in the past I sought the Dharma without weariness, now through the long night I teach the Dharma for the assembly, also without weariness. Bhikṣus! You should learn from me. With reverence and earnestness, diligently seek the supreme Dharma. Having heard the Dharma, practice it as taught, and do not be heedless."

At that time, all the bhikṣus and the assembly of devas and humans, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully practiced in accordance.
