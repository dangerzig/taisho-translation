# 佛說長者音悅經
## Sūtra of the Elder Yinyue, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0531

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Rājagṛha, on Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain, together with his honored disciples, twelve hundred and fifty in number, as well as bodhisattvas and laymen and laywomen, all gathered in a great assembly surrounding him as he taught the Dharma.

The Buddha addressed the great assembly: "There was an elder named Yinyue, whose wealth was beyond counting. He was old and had no son, which caused him great sorrow. Nevertheless, his accumulated merit from past lives caught up with him, and its reward came in four forms: first, his wife gave birth to a boy of incomparable beauty; second, five hundred white mares all foaled at the same time; third, the king sent an envoy to confer upon him a golden seal of office; fourth, five hundred treasure-laden ships all arrived at the same time."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What treasure ships arrived together?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The elder Yinyue had a great many clansmen. Previously he had sent five hundred men by ship to the sea, and they had obtained many treasures and returned home safely. Therefore the Tathāgata has described these four blessings as arriving together at the same time."

The elder was overjoyed and thought: "Heaven has showered blessings upon my household. I should prepare a fine banquet and celebrate with the whole clan." He did as he had planned and arranged a great feast. They enjoyed themselves to the fullest, with drums, music, and singing so loud it was heard in the heavens. At that time, the Four Heavenly Kings, Lord Śakra, King Brahmā, the nāgas, ghost-kings, asura kings, and all the spirit-kings, each with their retinues, thronged the sky to behold this elder's boundless merit.

The Tathāgata, with his divine knowledge, perceived the elder's joy and elation. Taking advantage of his happiness, the Tathāgata wished to go and praise him, for if his mind could be opened, the seeds of merit could be planted. The Tathāgata then chanted a song of the eight kinds of auspicious sounds and went to the elder's gate, where he spoke in verse:

> "Today, O Elder,
> Auspicious fortune gathers upon you.
> All blessings respond in kind;
> Your household and clan are blessed.
> The merit you planted of old
> Has now borne its fourfold reward.
> Great and small rejoice together;
> In all the world there is none to compare.
> The devas, nāgas, and spirits
> All bow in submission.
> How wonderful, O Elder!
> You have been graced with auspicious fortune.
> Like sowing grain in spring
> That ripens in autumn,
> What was done before is received after;
> The shadow of reward follows without cease."

At that time, the elder heard the Buddha's virtuous voice. His five senses were transported with delight, and he came out joyfully. Seeing the Buddha, he was filled with awe and reverence, and said: "Gautama the śramaṇa is truly divine and marvelous! He knew that my household's auspicious fortune is beyond measure, and has graciously come in person to praise me." He then offered the Tathāgata a fine white cloth worth ten million gold coins. The Buddha accepted it and gave blessings in return.

The Buddha told the elder: "Wealth has five dangers, of which people of the world are unaware. Stingy and greedy, attached and reluctant, they cannot bring themselves to share and give to relieve the destitute. When their life ends and the spirit departs, their wealth is left behind in the world. That you are now able to give will surely bring its reward of merit. Wherever you are reborn, blessings will follow."

The elder asked the Buddha: "What are the five dangers?"

The Buddha replied: "First, a great fire may burn your wealth without warning. Second, a great flood may sweep it away without warning. Third, corrupt officials may confiscate it unjustly. Fourth, a wayward son may spend it without limit. Fifth, thieves and bandits may seize and plunder it. When even one of these five occurs, it cannot be resisted. It is like a person who has violated the king's law and is locked in prison, about to be executed, with all property confiscated by the government. Can he possibly recover it? Furthermore, it is like Anāthapiṇḍada, whose treasures were beyond counting, yet the king seized them, and the owner could not prevent it, nor could spirits or nāgas stop it. Why? Because in a past life, his generosity was tainted by seven regrets. Therefore, in this life, he has been seven times wealthy and seven times impoverished." The elder, hearing this teaching, was even more elated. Thereupon the Tathāgata suddenly returned to Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain.

At that time, there was in the country a nirgrantha heterodox practitioner named Pūraṇa Kāśyapa. He had heard that the Tathāgata went to the elder's house, chanted a single verse, and received from the elder gold worth ten million. His heart was filled with envy, and he thought: "If Gautama the śramaṇa can obtain gold, surely if I go and ask, I shall receive some too." He also thought: "I shall go and obtain the very verse that Gautama the śramaṇa spoke, and then go to recite it. I shall surely receive precious treasures. My words of praise should surpass Gautama's."

Pūraṇa Kāśyapa, harboring this foolish and envious intention, went and bowed before the Tathāgata. He knelt and said to the Buddha: "I am of little merit and poor fortune; my clothing and food are insufficient. I have heard that Gautama went to the elder's house, chanted a single verse, and received great treasures. Might you have compassion, bestow the verse upon me, and allow me to recite it? I shall go and chant praises, hoping to receive treasures."

The Tathāgata, with his perfect knowledge of past, present, and future, knew that this elder's wealth would later be scattered. Pūraṇa Kāśyapa, not understanding the proper timing, would chant an auspicious verse at a household struck by calamity and would certainly receive a severe beating from the elder. The Tathāgata said: "I do not begrudge this verse. But you do not understand the proper timing. If you recite this verse, you will surely suffer a painful beating. Therefore the Tathāgata declines your request. If you wish instead to have a timely and fitting verse, a supremely excellent passage, I shall give you one. It will allow the elder to hear the truth and will also spare you from the pain of a beating."

Pūraṇa Kāśyapa thought: "Gautama the śramaṇa does not want me to go and beg for treasures. That is why he begrudges and withholds the verse from me." He then insisted again: "Just give it to me! How could you know what else will happen?"

The Tathāgata, out of compassion, admonished him three times, but he would not listen or understand. The Buddha also foreknew that Pūraṇa Kāśyapa's past karma required him to undergo this suffering. The Tathāgata further said: "The debt of sin cannot be transferred." The Buddha then recited the auspicious verse for him. The nirgrantha memorized it, and it took him an entire year to learn it by heart.

Afterward, the elder's house caught fire and was burned down, with all his precious and rare possessions destroyed. The five hundred foals all burned to death at the same time. His wonderful son died suddenly one day. The king sent an envoy to revoke and confiscate his golden seal. Later, the elder sent ships to sea again to gather treasures. They returned safely and moored for some days, but then the five hundred treasure ships were all swept away and sunk in a single day. The entire household, great and small, was overcome with anguish, like a person about to be executed, whose terror in the moments before death is beyond words.

On that very day, Pūraṇa Kāśyapa went to the elder's gate and chanted the Tathāgata's auspicious verse:

> "Today, O Elder,
> Auspicious fortune gathers upon you.
> All blessings respond in kind;
> Your household and clan are blessed.
> The merit you planted of old
> Has now borne its fourfold reward.
> Great and small rejoice together;
> In all the world there is none to compare.
> The devas, nāgas, and spirits
> All bow in submission.
> How wonderful, O Elder!
> You have been graced with auspicious fortune.
> Like sowing grain in spring
> That ripens in autumn,
> What was done before is received after;
> The shadow of reward follows without cease."

When Pūraṇa Kāśyapa recited this verse, the elder heard it and the entire household flew into a rage: "No one under heaven has suffered worse calamity than I! How is it that this naked and shameless person comes here to work his sorcery, declaring my fortune auspicious and adding to my grief?" They rushed out and beat him from head to foot, leaving no part of his body unharmed. In great pain throughout his body, he crawled back home. The followers of the six teachers met him and asked what had happened. He answered: "This disaster was entirely because of Gautama." Rather than blaming himself, he resented the World-Honored One.

At that time, the World-Honored One was in Rājagṛha, in the Bamboo Grove, surrounded by a great assembly as he taught the Dharma. The Buddha told the assembly: "Pūraṇa Kāśyapa previously asked the Tathāgata for a single verse, intending to go to the elder's house and chant praises in hopes of receiving treasures. The Tathāgata admonished him, but he would not listen. Now he has gone there and suffered painful torment."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What connection do Pūraṇa Kāśyapa and this elder have from past lives, that he should suffer such a beating?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Long ago, in the distant past, incalculable asaṃkhyeya kalpas ago, there was a king also named Yinyue. There was also a bird called a parrot, perched upon the king's palace, whose call was beautiful and melodious. Once, while the king was napping, he was awakened by the bird's call and asked his attendants: 'What bird is this, with such a wonderful song?' The attendants replied: 'There is a remarkable bird, resplendent in five colors, that was just on the palace roof. It sang and then flew away.' The king immediately dispatched a great host, cavalry and infantry in succession, to pursue and capture it. After a long search, they caught it and brought it to the king. The king was delighted and loved the bird insatiably. He adorned its body with pearls, crystal, lapis lazuli, true pearls, and coral ornaments, covering its head, neck, and feathers completely. He kept it always at his side, gazing at it day and night, never parting from it for a moment.

"Later, another bird called the horned owl came to the palace. Seeing that the parrot alone received such special favor, the owl asked the parrot: 'How did you come by this?' The parrot replied: 'I came to the palace and sang beautifully, and the king loved and honored me, took me to always be at his side, and adorned my body with ornaments of five-colored pearls.' The horned owl, hearing this, was filled with jealousy, and thought: 'I too shall sing, and my singing shall surpass yours. The king will surely love and favor me as well.' When the king came out to rest, the horned owl immediately began to call. The king was startled awake, and his hair stood on end as if with fright. The king asked his attendants: 'What was that sound that startled and frightened me?' The attendants replied: 'It was a foul-voiced bird called the horned owl.' The king said angrily: 'Quickly dispatch men in all directions to find it!' They caught it and brought it to the king. The king ordered his attendants to pluck its feathers alive. In great pain throughout its body, it walked away. When it reached the open fields, the other birds asked: 'How did this happen to you?' The horned owl, in its anger, did not blame itself, but answered the birds: 'This is entirely the parrot's fault that I have suffered this.'"

The Buddha said: "A pleasant voice brings fortune; a foul voice brings disaster. The retribution of sin is one's own doing, yet the owl blamed the parrot."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The king of old is the elder Yinyue of today. The parrot was myself. The horned owl is Pūraṇa Kāśyapa of today. In the past he was jealous of the parrot and suffered painful torment. Now he is jealous of the Tathāgata and suffers unbearable pain. Envy and jealousy burn oneself; how much more so the suffering they bring! Why is this? Pūraṇa Kāśyapa has slandered the Tathāgata on six occasions, past and present. What are the six? First, in the land of Nán, he harbored envy and slandered the Tathāgata. Second, in Rājagṛha, on account of the Bamboo Grove, he slandered the Tathāgata. Third, in Rājagṛha, at the elder Yinyue's house, out of greed for gold and treasures, he slandered the Tathāgata. Fourth, in the territory of Magadha, out of greed for offerings, he slandered the Tathāgata. Fifth, in the land of Vaiśālī, out of greed for fame and offerings, he slandered the Tathāgata. Sixth, in the land of Śrāvastī, out of greed for offerings and jealousy of renown, he slandered the Tathāgata. At that time, the king banished him from the country. Pūraṇa Kāśyapa and the followers of the six teachers said in concert: 'Gautama is truly divine; all revere and honor him. Our arts are shallow, and our fame has crumbled. Everywhere we are slighted. What shall we do to survive?' Thereupon they placed sand in jars, weighted themselves, and drowned in the water. At that moment their lives ended, and they fell into the hells, where they were punished with all manner of immeasurable suffering."

The Buddha further said: "In future ages, there will be many envious and depraved people who slander others, are conceited, and seek out each other's faults. They will only burn themselves. How painful, Ānanda! Therefore you should widely propagate this sūtra, to protect people of future generations who fall into such ways."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What merit did the elder Yinyue plant in a past life to receive these four blessings? And what sins did he commit that caused him to lose them?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "In a past life, when Yinyue was a young man, he admired the Buddha-Dharma and the sangha, and made offerings with wholehearted devotion, wishing for wealth and prosperity. But after he married, he became solely infatuated with sensual pleasures, turned his back on the Three Treasures, and lacked a compassionate mind of benevolence toward old and young. Therefore, when his reward was fulfilled, his fortune was scattered. You should open people's minds and proclaim this, so that they may understand the essential meaning."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the fourfold assembly of disciples, devas, nāgas, ghostly spirits, the king, his ministers, the people, and the entire assembly, having heard the sūtra, were filled with joy, and all made obeisance.
