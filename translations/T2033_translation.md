# Treatise on the Differences of the Schools

## *Bùzhí Yì Lùn* (部執異論)

Taishō 2033

Composed by the Bodhisattva Vasumitra (*Tiānyǒu*, 天友). Translated by Paramārtha (*Zhēndì*, 真諦) of the Chen dynasty.

*Note: This is Paramārtha's Chinese translation of Vasumitra's Samayabhedoparacanacakra ("Wheel of the Formation of the Differences of the Schools"), one of the most important sources for understanding the schisms and doctrinal differences among the early Buddhist schools (nikāya). The text describes how, roughly one hundred years after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, the original saṃgha split into the Mahāsāṃghika and Sthavira (Theravāda) schools, and how subsequent splits produced eighteen (or more) distinct schools. For each school, the text lists its distinctive doctrinal positions (the "tenets of the school," zhíyì). Three Chinese translations of this text exist in the Taishō: T2031 by Xuánzàng (the Yìbù Zōnglún Lùn), T2032 by an unknown translator (the Shíbā Bù Lùn), and the present T2033 by Paramārtha. Paramārtha's translation is notable for preserving the original Sanskrit school-names in transliteration alongside the Chinese equivalents, and for including the opening verses attributed to Vasumitra. The text is an invaluable document for the study of early Buddhist sectarianism.*

---

[Opening verses:]

> After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, within one hundred years,
> His disciples' schools diverged in their tenets,
> Damaging the Tathāgata's correct teaching
> And the benefit of sentient beings.
> Through grasping the words of sūtras whose meaning was not fully elucidated,
> They lost [the true intent]:
> This aroused weariness and fear in sentient beings.
> Now, relying on principle and teaching, I shall explain.
> The great Bodhisattva Vasumitra,
> Contemplating suffering, aroused his vast vow.
> With supreme wisdom, concentration, and compassion,
> He investigated and discerned this meaning.
> I see all sentient beings
> Flowing along with various views.
> Therefore I speak the true meaning,
> As revealed by the Buddha's words.
> If one knows the Buddha's correct teaching,
> With the noble truths as its foundation,
> One should take the true [teaching],
> Just as one pans for gold amid the sand.

## The Initial Schism

Thus have I heard: after the World-Honored One's parinirvāṇa, one hundred years had fully passed. Just as a bright sun hides behind Mount Asta, after one hundred years and a further sixteen years, there was a great kingdom called Pāṭaliputra (*Bōchálìfúduōluó*). Its king was named Aśoka (*Āshūkē*), who ruled over Jambudvīpa, with a great white parasol covering the entire world.

At that time, the great assembly was divided and scattered. The scattered great assembly comprised four groups: (1) the assembly of the great kingdom, (2) the assembly of the border regions, (3) the assembly of the learned, and (4) the assembly of the greatly virtuous.

These four assemblies jointly discussed five propositions established by non-Buddhist [or dissenting] teachers. The five propositions are as stated in this verse:

> "Others can defile one's robe; there is ignorance;
> There is doubt; there is being guided by others;
> The noble path is also revealed by words:
> These are the correct teaching of all buddhas."

Through deliberation on these five points, [the saṃgha] divided into two schools: (1) the Mahāsāṃghika (*Móhēsēngqí kē*) school, and (2) the Sthavira (*Tābìlì yǔ*) school.

## Divisions from the Mahāsāṃghika

In the second century, from the Mahāsāṃghika there emerged three further schools: (1) the Ekavyavahārika (*Yīkēbìyǔ pósēlìkē*), (2) the Lokottaravāda (*Lújùduōluó pósētuó*), and (3) the Gokulika (*Gāojùlìkē*).

Also in the second century, from the Mahāsāṃghika there emerged one more school, called the Bahuśrutīya (*Póhōushūdǐkē*). Again in the second century, from the Mahāsāṃghika there emerged one more school, called the Prajñaptivāda (*Bōluòruòdǐ pótuó*).

At the end of the second century, there was a non-Buddhist teacher named Mahādeva (*Móhētípó*), who had gone forth within the Mahāsāṃghika. Dwelling alone in the mountains, he proclaimed the five divergent tenets of the Mahāsāṃghika. His followers divided into two schools: (1) the Caitīyaśaila (*Zhīdǐyǔ shìluó*), and (2) the Uttaraśaila (*Yùduōluó shìluó*).

Thus the Mahāsāṃghika, through four divisions and five divisions, produced in all seven schools.

## Divisions from the Sthavira

The Sthavira school existed for some centuries. In the third century, over a minor cause, it divided into two schools: (1) the Sarvāstivāda (*Sàpó āsǐdǐ pótuó*), also called the Hetuvāda, and (2) the Haimavata (*Xīmóbáduō*), also called the Sthaviravāda.

Also in the third century, from the Sarvāstivāda there emerged: the Vātsīputrīya (*Básīfúdǐlì yǔ*). From the Vātsīputrīya, four further schools emerged: (1) Dharmottarīya (*Dámóduōlìyǔ*), (2) Bhadrayānīya (*Bátuóyǔ nìyǔ*), (3) Saṃmitīya (*Sānméidǐyǔ*), and (4) Ṣaṇṇāgarika (*Shāntúojiālìkē*). Also from the Sarvāstivāda: the Mahīśāsaka (*Míxīshě pókē*). From the Mahīśāsaka: the Dharmaguptaka (*Dámójíduō*), whose school says "Moggallāna is our great teacher." Also from the Sarvāstivāda: the Kāśyapīya (*Sūbálìshākē*), also called the Dīrghabhāṇaka.

In the fourth century, from the Sarvāstivāda there emerged the Sautrāntika (*Sēngqiānlándǐ pótuó*), also called the Sūtravāda.

Thus the Sthavira school divided into eleven schools in total.

## Tenets of the Schools

### Mahāsāṃghika Group (Four Schools)

The Mahāsāṃghika, Ekavyavahārika, Lokottaravāda, and Gokulika: the doctrinal tenets these four schools share as their common basis are as follows:

All buddha world-honored ones who appear in the world have not a single dharma that is conditioned (*sāsrava*). All speech uttered by the Tathāgata is for turning the Dharma-wheel. The Tathāgata, with a single sound, can teach all dharmas. The Tathāgata's speech is never not in accord with meaning. The Tathāgata's form-body is boundless. The Tathāgata's majestic power knows no diminution. The Tathāgata's lifespan is immeasurable. The Tathāgata, in teaching sentient beings, causes them to give rise to faith and delight without satiation. The Tathāgata is forever without sleep. The Tathāgata answers questions without deliberation. All speech uttered by the Tathāgata causes sentient beings to give rise to love and delight. The Tathāgata's mind is always contemplating quiescence and immovability. The Tathāgata, with a single mind, can penetrate all realms. The Tathāgata, in a single kṣaṇa of cognition conjoined with prajñā, can comprehend all dharmas. The Tathāgata's "knowledge of exhaustion" (*kṣayajñāna*) and "knowledge of non-arising" (*anutpādajñāna*) constantly and equally follow the mind in functioning, even up to the remainderless nirvāṇa.

All bodhisattvas, when entering the womb, are without the [embryonic stages of] kalala, arbuda, peśī, ghana, and praśākhā. When bodhisattvas wish to enter the womb, they all assume the form of a white elephant. All bodhisattvas are born from the mother's right side. All bodhisattvas have no thought of lustful desire, no thought of anger, and no thought of harming others. If a bodhisattva has a vow to be born in an evil destiny, by the power of the vow they attain birth there. Bodhisattvas enter the evil destinies to teach and transform sentient beings, not because they are bound by afflictions and karma.

Through a single mind, directly contemplating the four noble truths, a single wisdom penetrates all four noble truths and their characteristics. Among the five consciousnesses, there is both defilement and purity. The formless realm also has the six groups of consciousness. The five faculties are the flesh itself. The eye does not see form, up to the body not feeling touch. Even when the mind is in concentration, speech can occur. The "subduing mind" (*zhéfú xīn*) is always present. The "deteriorating mind" (*xiànghuài xīn*) is always present. Therefore ordinary beings have [the potential for] ascent and descent.

There is no [permanent] location for already-accomplished dharmas. The srotāpanna (*xūlěiduō ābànnà*): mind and mental factors have their own self-nature. There are arhats who can have their robes defiled by others [through emissions]. Arhats have ignorance, have doubt, and can be guided by others. The noble path is also revealed by words. Speaking of suffering is also the path; speaking of suffering is also its cause. Prajñā conjoined with cessation eliminates suffering. Suffering-sensation is also food. The eighth [consciousness] also long abides, up to: those in the nature of dharma also regress. Srotāpannas can regress. Arhats cannot regress.

There is no right view in the world. There is no faculty of faith in the world. There are no morally indeterminate (*avyākṛta*) dharmas. When a person enters right concentration, all fetters are extinguished. A srotāpanna can commit all evils, except the five heinous offenses. All sūtras are of definitive meaning. Unconditioned dharmas are of nine kinds: (1) cessation through deliberation (*pratisaṃkhyānirodha*), (2) cessation not through deliberation (*apratisaṃkhyānirodha*), (3) space, (4) the base of infinite space, (5) the base of infinite consciousness, (6) the base of nothingness, (7) the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, (8) the twelve links of dependent arising, and (9) the eight components of the noble path.

The mind is by its own nature pure; it is defiled by adventitious dust. There are two types of affliction: (1) latent afflictions (*anuśaya*) and (2) actively manifesting afflictions. Latent afflictions are neither mind nor mental factors and have no object. Latent afflictions differ from actively manifesting afflictions. Latent afflictions are dissociated from mind; actively manifesting afflictions are associated with mind. The past and future are non-existent; the present is existent. Dharmas in the dharma-base (*dharmāyatana*) are neither objects of knowledge nor objects of cognition. The intermediate existence (*antarābhava*) is non-existent. The srotāpanna attains concentration.

### Points Where the Mahāsāṃghika Differs

The four noble truths are all real. There is "suchness" (*tathatā*) corresponding to praiseworthy practice; there is suffering. Some suffering is self-made; some is made by others; some is made by both; some is made by neither, arising through dependent origination. There are [dharmas] not arising through dependent origination. In a single moment, many minds can harmonize and co-exist. The path and afflictions can arise simultaneously. Karma and fruit can arise simultaneously. The seed is the sprout. The six faculties and four great elements undergo transformation; mind and mental factors do not undergo transformation. Mind pervades the entire body. Mind grows and increases. One should know that such doctrinal positions exist; the schools' preferences differ. Each has its own tenets: this is called "difference in tenets."

### Bahuśrutīya

The Tathāgata's five calls are what should be taught. The world-transcending five calls are: impermanence, suffering, emptiness, non-self, and the quiescence of nirvāṇa. These five are the correct world-transcending path. The Tathāgata's remaining calls are the worldly path. Remaining tenets are similar to the Sarvāstivāda.

### Prajñaptivāda

Suffering is not the aggregates. Nothing in any sense-base is truly possessed. All conditioned dharmas are provisionally named "suffering" because they are established through mutual dependence. There is no personal effort. There is no untimely death. All that one obtains was created by prior karma. The cause-and-effect of growth can produce karma. All suffering arises from karma. The noble path is obtained through merit. The noble path is not obtained through cultivation. Remaining tenets are similar to the Mahāsāṃghika.

### Sarvāstivāda

All that exists, exists as it is. These two dharmas encompass everything: past, present, and future all exist. [There are four bases:] (1) based on correct speech, (2) based on two dharmas, (3) based on having objects, (4) based on having fruits. Dharmas in the dharma-base are of three kinds: known, cognized, and penetrated. Birth, aging, abiding, and impermanence are formations dissociated from mind, subsumed under the formation-aggregate. Conditioned dharmas are of three types; unconditioned dharmas are of three types. Conditioned dharmas have three characteristics; unconditioned dharmas have three characteristics. Of the four truths, three are conditioned; one is unconditioned. The four truths are contemplated sequentially.

If a person wishes to enter right concentration, they must enter through the gate of the emptiness liberation or the signlessness liberation. If contemplating the formations associated with the desire realm, one enters right concentration. A person who has entered right concentration, while in the fifteenth mind-moment, is called "one oriented toward stream-entry." At the sixteenth mind-moment, they are called a srotāpanna.

The "peak of the world" (*laukikāgradharma*) lasts a single kṣaṇa-moment. The three preparatory stages can regress; the peak of the world cannot regress. The srotāpanna cannot regress; the arhat can regress. Not all arhats obtain the knowledge of non-arising. Ordinary beings can also abandon desire and anger. Non-Buddhists can attain the five supernatural powers. Devas also experience the night-watch (*pūrvayāma*). In the seven concentrations there are factors of awakening; in the remaining concentrations there are not.

All concentrations are subsumed under the four foundations of mindfulness. One can enter right concentration without relying on concentration. One can also attain arhatship. Depending on the mind of the form and formless realms, one attains arhatship; one need not enter right concentration. In the desire realm, one enters right concentration and also attains arhatship. In Uttarakuru, there are no persons who have attained detachment. Noble persons are not born there, nor in the heaven of non-perception.

One need not necessarily attain the four fruits of the noble path in sequence. A person who has entered right concentration can, relying on the worldly path, reach the stage of... The four foundations of mindfulness can be said to [subsume] all dharmas. Latent afflictions are mental factors, not associated with mind. All latent afflictions can be designated as "actively manifesting." All actively manifesting afflictions can be designated as such, but cannot be designated as "latent." The twelve links of dependent arising are conditioned.

Arhats also have merit that increases. In the desire and form realms, there is intermediate existence. At the moment when the five consciousnesses arise, desire can be generated but detachment cannot be attained. The five consciousnesses apprehend particular characteristics without discrimination. Mind and mental factors definitely have objects. A self-nature is not associated with itself. Mind is not associated with mind.

There is right view in the world. There is a faculty of faith in the world. There are morally indeterminate dharmas. Arhats have no "learner" dharmas. All arhats attain concentration; not all arhats realize [specific] concentrations. Arhats can still receive retribution from past karma. All ordinary beings can also die while in a wholesome state of mind. A person who is in concentration definitely cannot die. The Tathāgata and his disciples are without difference in the extinction of delusions. The Tathāgata's compassion does not take sentient beings as its object. If a person grasps the characteristic of sentient beings, the intention of liberation cannot be accomplished. All bodhisattvas are definitely ordinary beings, possessing the nine fetters. If a bodhisattva has entered right concentration but has not yet passed beyond the stage of an ordinary being: this is a provisional designation, a continuity called "sentient being."

All formations are momentary (*kṣaṇika*). There is no dharma that passes from this world to the next world. Depending on worldly conventional designation, one speaks of a "person" (*pudgala*) who transmigrates. The correct Dharma, when its time of practice-cessation comes, is extinguished without remainder. The aggregates have no transformation. There is a supramundane concentration. There are aspects of cognition that are uncontaminated. There are wholesome [dharmas] that have causes. A person who is in concentration cannot speak. The eight components of the noble path are called the Dharma-wheel. Not all of the World-Honored One's speech is turning of the Dharma-wheel. A single sound does not fully teach all dharmas. Not all speech is in accord with meaning. Not all sūtras are definitive; there are sūtras that are not definitive.

### Haimavata (Sthavira)

Bodhisattvas are ordinary beings. There is no craving that draws one to rebirth. [Bodhisattvas] are not enveloped by the fetal [stages]. Non-Buddhists do not attain the five supernatural powers. Devas have no night-watch. There are arhats who can have their robes defiled by others. Arhats have ignorance, have doubt, and can be guided by others. The noble path is also revealed by words. Remaining tenets are similar to the Sarvāstivāda.

### Vātsīputrīya

It is neither the case that the five aggregates are the person, nor that the person is something other than the five aggregates. Because [the person] subsumes the aggregates, elements, and sense-bases, provisional designations such as "person" are established. There are three kinds of provisional designation: (1) designation by subsuming everything, (2) designation by subsuming a portion, and (3) designation by subsuming cessation.

All conditioned dharmas perish moment by moment. Apart from form, there is no single dharma that passes from this world to the next. One can speak of a person who "migrates." Non-Buddhists have the five supernatural powers. The root of affliction is of two kinds, always accompanying all sentient beings: ignorance and craving for existence. There are seven stages of purification.

From one original verse, this school divided into four: the Dharmottarīya, Bhadrayānīya, Saṃmitīya, and Ṣaṇṇāgarika. The verse says:

> "Having attained liberation, one again falls back;
> Having fallen through attachment, one returns again.
> Having reached the place of safety, one wanders in delight;
> Following what one enjoys, one arrives at the place of bliss."

### Mahīśāsaka

The past and future are non-existent; the present and the unconditioned are existent. The four noble truths are contemplated simultaneously. If one sees the truth of suffering, one sees all the truths. Latent afflictions are neither mind nor mental factors and have no object. Ordinary beings cannot abandon desire-realm desire and anger. Non-Buddhists do not have the five supernatural powers. Devas have no night-watch. There is no intermediate existence. Arhats' merit does not increase. There is no supramundane concentration. Cognition conjoined with discursive and sustained thought [is a feature of this school]. The srotāpanna can regress; the arhat cannot regress.

### Dharmaguptaka

The Buddha is within the saṃgha. Respectful service to a stūpa yields superior reward. Respectful service to the great assembly is not as [meritorious]. The Buddha's path differs from the śrāvaka's path. Non-Buddhists do not have the five supernatural powers. Arhats' bodies are uncontaminated. Remaining tenets are similar to the Mahāsāṃghika.

### Kāśyapīya

Dharmas that have already been extinguished and already abandoned are non-existent. Those not yet extinguished and not yet abandoned are existent. If the fruit of karma has already ripened, it is non-existent; if not yet ripened, it is existent. Conditioned dharmas do not take past dharmas as causes, but take present and future dharmas as causes. All formations are momentary. Learner-dharmas have fruition-retribution. Remaining tenets are similar to the Dharmaguptaka.

### Sautrāntika

The aggregates pass from the previous world to the next. If separated from the noble path, the aggregates are not extinguished. The aggregates have a beginning and an end. In the ordinary-being stage, there are noble dharmas. There is a real person (*pudgala*). Remaining tenets are similar to the Sarvāstivāda.
