# 雜譬喻經
## Sūtra of Miscellaneous Parables

### (1)

Long ago, at the Sparrow Monastery, there was an elder bhikṣu who had attained the arhat's path. He was leading a novice and had come down into the city for a tour. His robe and bowl were very heavy, so he had the novice carry them and follow behind. Along the way, the novice thought: "People born into this world all undergo suffering. If I wish to be free from this suffering, what path should I pursue?" Reflecting in this way, he thought: "The Buddha always praises the bodhisattva path as supreme. I should now generate the bodhisattva aspiration." The moment he had this thought, his teacher, using the supernatural power of knowing others' minds, perceived what he was thinking. He said to the novice: "Hand me the robe and bowl." The novice handed the robe and bowl to his teacher. The teacher said: "Walk ahead of me." The novice walked ahead, and then had another thought: "The bodhisattva path involves extremely hard and toilsome effort. If someone asks for your head, you give your head; if someone asks for your eyes, you give your eyes. This is extremely difficult and beyond my ability. It would be better to quickly attain arhatship and swiftly be free from suffering." The teacher again perceived his thoughts and said to the novice: "Carry the robe and bowl and follow behind me again." This happened three times. The novice was puzzled and did not understand why. When they arrived at their lodging, he joined his palms and respectfully asked his teacher to explain. His teacher answered: "Three times you advanced toward the bodhisattva path, so three times I let you walk ahead of me. Three times your mind retreated, so I put you behind me. The reason is that generating the bodhisattva aspiration, its merit surpasses that of all the arhats who fill the three-thousand great-thousand worlds."

### (2)

Long ago there was a cakravartin, a wheel-turning sage king, who first fathered nine hundred and ninety-nine sons. They all grew up to be handsome, distinguished, intelligent, clever, and physically strong. Some possessed twenty-eight marks, some thirty marks, and some thirty-one marks. The very last son had just entered his mother's womb, dwelling amid the impurities of the afterbirth. At that time, the eight classes of powerful ghosts and spirits played drums, strings, and songs to attend the mother. The king also issued orders left and right to provide offerings, with various ornaments and provisions three times more lavish than usual. The people addressed the king: "Your Majesty, your earlier sons have all grown up. They are wise, brilliant, and possess extraordinary physical marks. Yet your heart remained calm, never showing special joy. Now this one son has only just entered the womb. What is so extraordinary that the provisions are so exceptional?" The king answered: "Though my elder sons surpass others in talent and beauty, none is fit to ascend the great throne. My last son, when he is born and grown, will certainly be fit to inherit the great position." The sage king represents the Buddha. The elder sons represent the lesser two vehicles. The last son in the womb represents the bodhisattva. This means that although a bodhisattva is mixed with dust and defilements, so long as one can generate the great aspiration, one will certainly be remembered by all the buddhas, and gods, dragons, ghosts, and spirits will all give rise to respect and love.

### (3)

In the time of Kāśyapa Buddha, there were two brothers who left home and both became śramaṇas. The elder brother delighted in keeping the precepts, sitting in meditation, and single-mindedly seeking the Way, but he did not delight in giving. The younger brother delighted in giving and cultivating merit, but he was fond of breaking the precepts. When Śākyamuni appeared in the world, the elder brother encountered the Buddha, left home and practiced the Way, and immediately attained arhatship. But his merit was meager, and he always lacked sufficient clothing and food. When he went on alms rounds with his companions, he alone always returned hungry. His younger brother was reborn as an elephant, and because the elephant was powerful and could repel enemies, it was beloved by the king. The king adorned it with fine gold, silver, and precious jeweled necklaces, and allocated several hundred villages to provide for the elephant's every need. The elder brother, the bhikṣu, was living in a time of great famine. He went on alms rounds for seven days without obtaining food. At last he obtained a little coarse food and barely survived. He already knew that this elephant was his brother from a past life. He went before the elephant, took hold of its ear, and said: "You and I both have our faults." The elephant reflected on the bhikṣu's words and immediately recalled its past life and saw the past karmic connections. The elephant then became depressed and would no longer eat or drink. The elephant keeper was alarmed and reported to the king: "The elephant will no longer eat or drink. I do not know why." The king asked the keeper: "Was there anyone who previously offended this elephant?" The keeper answered: "There was no one unusual. I only saw a śramaṇa come to the elephant's side and leave shortly after." The king then sent people in all four directions to search for this śramaṇa. Someone found him in a forest among the trees and brought the śramaṇa before the king. The king asked: "What did you say to my elephant?" The śramaṇa answered: "I did not say much. I simply told the elephant: 'You and I both have our faults.'" The śramaṇa then explained in detail the past-life karmic connections to the king. The king's mind was enlightened, and he released the śramaṇa to return to his dwelling.

### (4)

Long ago there was a performer who practiced various kinds of entertainment. He asked a wealthy elder for a cow. The elder had no intention of giving one, so he deliberately said: "If you can diligently perform your entertainment day and night without rest for a full year, I shall give you a cow." The performer answered: "I can." He further asked the patron: "Can you listen for that long?" The elder also said: "I can." The performer was delighted upon hearing this and single-mindedly performed his music. After three days and three nights without ever stopping, the elder grew weary of listening and immediately ordered his sons and nephews to lead a cow over and give it to him. This is a parable that those who practice the Way and cultivate merit should not consider the distance of kalpas. The more earnest one's diligence, the sooner the reward arrives. It is not necessary to go through all those kalpas.

### (5)

Long ago there was a bhikṣu who had been expelled. Distressed and grief-stricken, he wept as he walked along the road. On the way he met a ghost who had also committed an offense and been banished by the heavenly king Vaiśravaṇa. The ghost asked the bhikṣu: "What has happened to you that you weep as you walk?" The bhikṣu answered: "I committed an offense against the saṃgha, and the saṃgha expelled me. All the offerings from the faithful have been lost, and my bad reputation has spread far and near. That is why I am grieving and weeping." The ghost said to the bhikṣu: "I can make your bad reputation disappear and cause you to receive great offerings. Just stand on my left shoulder, and I will carry you through the sky. People will see you but not see me. When you receive great offerings, give me some first." The ghost then immediately carried the bhikṣu and flew through the sky above the very village where he had been expelled. When the villagers saw him, they were all amazed, thinking he had attained the Way. They said to each other: "The saṃgha has unjustly expelled a person who has attained the Way." The villagers all went to the monastery and scolded the monks. They then welcomed the bhikṣu and lodged him in the monastery, and he received great offerings. Whatever robes, food, and goods the bhikṣu received, he always gave the ghost his share first, not breaking his original agreement. On another day, the ghost again carried the bhikṣu through the sky. They happened to encounter the officials of the heavenly king Vaiśravaṇa. When the ghost saw the officers, he was terrified. He dropped the bhikṣu and fled with all his might. The bhikṣu then fell to the ground and died, his body and head shattered. This is a parable that practitioners should cultivate their own practice and should not rely on the power and influence of others. Once that support collapses, one is no different from this.

### (6)

Long ago, Maudgalyāyana was going with his disciples down from Vulture Peak Mountain to the city of Rājagṛha to beg for food. On the road, Maudgalyāyana looked up into the sky and burst out laughing. His disciples asked: "What is the reason for this laughter?" Maudgalyāyana answered: "If you wish to know, wait until we return to the Buddha's presence, and you may ask again." After they had finished begging for food and returned to the Buddha, his disciples again asked Maudgalyāyana about his earlier laughter. Maudgalyāyana answered: "I saw a hungry ghost in the sky above. Its body was extremely long and large, and its appearance was hideous. Seven red-hot iron balls entered through its mouth, passed straight down through its body, and after passing through, re-entered through its mouth. Its entire body was ablaze with fire; it writhed in agony, collapsed and rose, rose and collapsed again. That is why I laughed. It is not only I who saw it; the Buddha also saw it." The disciples asked: "What karmic cause produced such suffering?" Maudgalyāyana answered: "Ask the World-Honored Buddha about this." His disciples then immediately asked the Buddha and inquired about the cause. The Buddha answered: "In a past life, this hungry ghost was once a novice. At that time the world was in great famine, and they subsisted on beans. The novice was serving food to the saṃgha; when he came to his own teacher, he secretly gave him seven extra beans. Because of this offense, he received the body of a hungry ghost and suffers this bitter torment." The Buddha said: "I too have always seen this, but the reason I did not speak of it is that I feared people would not believe it and would incur extremely grave offenses." This is a parable: if someone does not believe and slanders the prajñāpāramitā taught by the Buddha, the offense is heavier than the five heinous acts, and one will receive the utmost suffering in hell.

### (7)

In a past age, innumerable dust-mote kalpas ago, there was a bodhisattva named Pramuditamūla (Joy-Root). Before a great assembly, he was teaching the Mahāyāna. Mañjuśrī was at that time an ordinary person who had left home and was practicing the Way with devoted austerity, practicing the twelve dhūtaguṇas and liberating all through merit. He happened upon the Dharma teaching and stopped to listen to Bodhisattva Pramuditamūla expounding the Dharma of true reality, saying that lust, anger, and delusion are not different from the Way; indeed, they are the Way and they are nirvāṇa. At that time Mañjuśrī heard this but did not believe it, and he departed. He went to the home of Pramuditamūla's disciple and taught the Dharma of the impure and repulsive aspects of the body. Pramuditamūla's disciple immediately challenged him: "The truth of all dharmas is that there is nothing. All dharmas are empty. How could there be purity or impurity?" The ascetic bhikṣu was silent and could not reply. He harbored anger inside, which then developed into resentment. Pramuditamūla's disciple then recited seventy verses praising the Dharma of true reality. With each verse the ascetic bhikṣu heard, his anger increased by one degree. By the end of the seventy verses, his anger had increased seventy-fold. As soon as the verses were finished, the earth split open, and the Avīci hell appeared in its entirety. The ascetic bhikṣu immediately fell into it. After immeasurable kalpas, when his offenses were exhausted, he emerged. Only then did he understand how grave the offense of not believing in the wondrous Dharma is. Later he became a bhikṣu, devoted himself to study and inquiry, attained great wisdom, and became foremost in understanding emptiness. This parable illustrates that if one does not believe and slanders the prajñāpāramitā taught by the Buddha, although there may be loss at present, the benefit later is great.

### (8)

Long ago, in northern India, there was a master wood-carver of great skill. He made a wooden woman of unequaled beauty. She was dressed and adorned no differently from a real woman; she could come and go, serve wine, and attend to guests. The only thing she could not do was speak. At that time, in southern India, there was a painter who was also skilled. The wood-carver heard of him and prepared fine food and drink and invited the painter. When the painter arrived, the wood-carver had the wooden woman serve wine and carry food from morning to evening. The painter did not know she was not real and thought she was a living woman. His desire became intense and he could not forget her. As evening fell, the wood-carver retired to his quarters and also had the painter stay the night. He stood the wooden woman at the painter's side and said: "I leave this woman here; you may share the night with her." After the master retired, the wooden woman stood by the lamp. The guest called to her, but she did not come. He thought she was too shy to come, so he went and took her by the hand, and only then realized she was made of wood. He was deeply embarrassed, and thought: "The master has tricked me; I shall repay him." The painter then devised a stratagem. On the wall he painted his own likeness, wearing the same clothes as himself, with a rope around his neck, appearing as though he had hanged himself. He even painted flies and birds pecking at his mouth. Having finished, he closed the door and hid under the bed. At dawn the master came out and saw the door still closed. He peered inside and saw only the image on the wall of his guest, apparently hanged. Greatly alarmed, he believed the guest had truly died. He broke open the door and cut the rope with a knife. The painter then emerged from under the bed. The wood-carver was deeply ashamed. The painter said: "You could trick me, and I could trick you. Host and guest are now even; neither owes the other." The two men said to each other: "People in the world deceive and delude one another; how is it different from this?" At that time, the two of them truly understood deceit and delusion. Each left behind what he cherished and left home to practice the Way.

### (9) The Story of Kāśyapa's Wife

Kāśyapa's father was named Nyagrodha, a man of Magadha, from the brāhmaṇa caste. Through meritorious karma from past lives, he was born into the world exceedingly wealthy. His rare treasures and precious goods were the finest in the land, and his wealth was only one thousandth less than the king's. But the couple had no children. Near their dwelling there was a great tree deity. The husband and wife prayed to this tree deity for a child, offering sacrifices three times a year without ceasing, but their prayers were never answered. The man then grew angry and issued an ultimatum to the tree deity: "I will worship you with all my heart for seven more days. If there is still no result, I shall cut you down, drag you to the crossroads, and burn you." The tree deity, hearing these words, was terrified and did not know how to make a child appear. He then went up and reported to the heavenly king Dhṛtarāṣṭra, explaining the situation in full. Dhṛtarāṣṭra then brought the tree deity before the heavenly lord Śakra and reported everything. Śakra then surveyed the desire realm with his divine eye, but there was no one fit to be born as their son. Śakra then informed Brahmā and explained the entire situation. Brahmā surveyed his realm with his divine eye and saw a certain Brahmā deity whose lifespan was about to end. He told him: "You should descend and be born in Jambudvīpa as a son to the brāhmaṇa Nyagrodha of Magadha." The Brahmā deity replied: "Brāhmaṇas hold many wrong views. If I descend and am born there, I cannot be his son." Brahmā answered: "This brāhmaṇa has great merit from past lives. No being in the desire realm is fit to be his son. If you go and are born there, I will order the heavenly lord Śakra to protect you, so that you do not fall into wrong views along the way." The Brahmā deity said: "Very well, I shall not disobey the holy command." The heavenly lord Śakra then returned to the desire realm, communicated this fully to the tree deity, and the tree deity joyfully told the elder: "Do not worry, do not fear, do not be angry. Within seven days you shall certainly have a son." As he had said, when the seven days were complete, the wife became aware she was with child. After ten months, the son was born. His body was golden in color and radiated light. The physiognomists predicted: "This child possesses great merit and awesome virtue from past lives. His aspiration is pure and lofty; he will not covet worldly affairs. If he later leaves home, he will certainly attain the holy path." When his parents heard this, they were again filled with sorrow and fear, worried that when the child grew up he would abandon them and leave home. They wondered what stratagem might restrain him. They reflected: "What the desire realm values most is beauty. We should find a supremely beautiful woman to bind him." When he was fifteen, they wished to arrange his marriage. When Kāśyapa heard this, he was greatly distressed and said to his parents: "My aspiration is for purity. I do not need a wife." Kāśyapa pleaded three times, but his parents' answer remained the same. Kāśyapa then said to his parents: "I will not have an ordinary woman as my wife. Only if you can find a woman whose body is of purple-gold color, beautiful beyond compare, will I agree to marry." His reason for saying this was to make the task impossible to fulfill.

His parents then summoned brāhmaṇas and ordered them to search throughout the kingdom. If they found a woman whose body was golden in color, who possessed all the marks of womanhood and was extraordinarily beautiful, they should bring her. The brāhmaṇas then devised a clever scheme: they cast a golden statue of a goddess, beautiful in features and wondrously radiant, and carried the divine image from country to country, loudly proclaiming: "Any woman who sees this golden goddess, bows, and makes offerings will, when she is married, obtain a fine husband whose body is of golden color, wonderfully beautiful and incomparably wise." In every village and city, every woman who heard this proclamation emptied her heart and came out to welcome, bow, and make offerings. Only one woman, whose body was of golden color and who was extraordinarily beautiful, remained in her secluded chamber and would not come out. The other women urged her: "All who see the golden goddess have their wishes fulfilled. Why are you the only one who will not come out?" She answered: "My aspiration is for solitude and purity; I desire nothing else." The other women said: "Even if you have no wishes, what harm is there in coming to look?" The women then brought her out to the golden goddess. When this woman arrived, her radiant purity outshone the golden goddess so completely that the golden statue's light was no longer visible. The brāhmaṇas, seeing this, returned and reported everything in detail to the elder. The elder then sent a matchmaker to the woman's home to convey his intentions. The woman's parents had already heard Kāśyapa's name, and respectfully assenting, they agreed. The woman, hearing this, was deeply distressed. But compelled by her parents, she could not refuse, and so she went to the elder's household. After arriving and meeting Kāśyapa, the two faced each other, each with a pure and unwavering spirit. Though they were husband and wife, there was no bond of affection between them. The wife then made a pact with Kāśyapa: "You and I shall each stay in a separate room and never touch one another." The husband and wife then each occupied a separate room. When Kāśyapa went out, his father secretly sent someone to demolish one room, leaving husband and wife in a single chamber. Though they shared one room, they slept in separate beds. His father then sent someone to remove one bed. Although the couple then shared one bed, the wife made another pact with her husband: "When I sleep, you shall do walking meditation; when you sleep, I shall do walking meditation." Once, while the wife was sleeping, one of her arms hung over the side of the bed. A large venomous snake was about to bite it. When Kāśyapa saw this, out of compassion, he wrapped his hand in his robe and lifted her arm back onto the bed. She woke with a start and was furious, saying: "We had an agreement. How could you violate it?" Kāśyapa answered: "Your arm fell off the bed, and a venomous snake was about to bite it. I was saving you, not deliberately touching you." The venomous snake was still nearby; he pointed and showed it to her. Only then did the wife understand. The husband and wife then discussed with each other: "Why do we not leave home and practice the Way?" So the two of them took leave of their parents and together left home, practicing in the mountains and marshes.

At that time, a brāhmaṇa with five hundred disciples also lived on this mountain. Seeing Kāśyapa and his wife, he slandered them: "Those who have left home should be chaste and pure. How can a husband and wife follow each other around?" Kāśyapa then parted from his wife. With five hundred pieces of gold he purchased a paṃśukūla robe and went to live alone in another forest. His wife then attached herself to the brāhmaṇa, seeking to become his disciple. The brāhmaṇa's five hundred disciples, seeing this woman's beauty, day after day engaged in misconduct with her. The woman, unable to resist, could not bear it. She reported it to their teacher, and the teacher then imposed discipline on his disciples, ordering them to restrain their desires.

Later Kāśyapa encountered the Buddha when he appeared in the world. Hearing the Dharma, he was converted and immediately attained arhatship. Hearing that his former wife was with the brāhmaṇa, he brought her before the Buddha. The Buddha taught her the Dharma, and she attained arhatship. Her hair fell of its own accord, and Dharma robes appeared on her body. She became a bhikṣuṇī and traveled about teaching. She happened to arrive during a great assembly of King Prasenajit. The bhikṣuṇīs were allowed to enter the royal palace, where she taught all the consorts and had them observe a one-day fast. When the king returned to the palace that evening and summoned his consorts, they all said they were fasting and no one would come. The king was furious and asked: "Who taught my consorts to fast?" An attendant answered: "Such-and-such bhikṣuṇī." The king then summoned her and ordered her to take the consorts' place in serving the king's desires for ninety days. This was all the result of the karmic vows and connections of the past coming back to her. Even though she had attained arhatship, she could not escape.

### (10)

Long ago there were two brothers who left home to study the Way. The elder was always devoted to meditation, diligent and focused in cultivating the Way, and he attained the arhat's fruit with the six supernatural powers and complete clarity. His younger brother was always devoted to broad learning and much study, fond of selling his reputation and wishing to glorify himself. The elder always admonished his brother: "A human body is hard to obtain, and a buddha's appearance in the world is hard to encounter. Having obtained a human body, you should make good use of your time." The younger brother said: "I must first complete my studies and master the Tripiṭaka, becoming worthy to teach others. Only then will I practice meditation." The elder further expounded for his brother the meaning of impermanence: the out-breath that does not return belongs already to the next life. But the younger brother stubbornly clung to his original intention and would not follow the teaching. Before long, the younger brother fell seriously ill. Scores of physicians could not save him. When the physicians knew he would certainly die, they gradually withdrew. The younger brother was filled with dread, knowing he would die. He said to his elder brother: "In my foolishness I did not follow your teaching. Now as my life is ending, I do not know which destiny awaits me." With tears streaming down his face, he repented before his brother. Before long, he died. The elder brother then entered samādhi and observed where he had been reborn. He saw him in the womb of an elder's household. That elder's house was near a monastery. The elder brother then frequently visited the house, seeking to become a spiritual friend, wishing to save his brother. When the elder's son was three years old, the elder brother gave him to the monastery as a disciple. At age four, a nurse carried the child to the teacher's monastery on the mountain. The path up the mountain was built of stacked stones. The nurse was carrying the child but did not hold him firmly; he slipped and fell, striking his head on a stone, his brains spilling out, and he died. At the moment of death, the child gave rise to an evil thought, resenting the nurse for not holding him securely and causing this calamity. Because of this anger at the moment of death, he fell directly into the great hell. The elder brother again entered samādhi and observed, seeing he had been born in hell. He then sighed with deep regret: "This is surely the end. The suffering of hell is so fierce it is impossible to save him from. Even all the buddhas cannot do anything about it, let alone me." This parable illustrates that one who pursues fame and cannot practice meditation will later fall into evil destinies, and even the closest parent or sibling cannot save him.

### (11)

Rāhula-Pearl was a disciple of Śāriputra. In a past life, he had once stolen the food of a pratyekabuddha. Because of this offense, he was born in the realm of hungry ghosts, suffering for immeasurable kalpas. After finishing his existence as a hungry ghost and being reborn among humans, for five hundred lives he suffered the punishment of hunger. In his final body, he encountered the Buddha in the world, left home and studied the Way, and wore the three Dharma robes. When he went on alms rounds to beg for food, no one would give to him. Sometimes five days, sometimes seven days would pass without obtaining food. Maudgalyāyana, out of compassion, begged food and gave it to him, but as soon as it entered his bowl, a great bird snatched it away. Śāriputra begged food and gave it to him, but as soon as it entered his bowl, it turned to mud. Mahākāśyapa begged food and gave it to him, but as he brought it to his mouth, his mouth closed shut and there was no opening. The Buddha gave him food, and through the power of his great compassion, it was able to enter his mouth; the flavor was extraordinary. The Buddha also employed various skillful means and taught him the Dharma. When Rāhula-Pearl heard this supreme Dharma, sorrow and joy mingled together, and with single-minded contemplation he attained the arhat's path.

### (12)

A dragon ascended to heaven and caused a great rain to fall. Where the rain fell on the heavenly palace, it became the seven precious substances. Where it fell among humans, it became nourishing moisture. Where it fell on the bodies of hungry ghosts, it transformed into a great fire that burned their entire bodies. It was the same rain, yet where it fell, it changed differently. These two things illustrate that the forms of all beings have no fixed substance; they follow what is induced by offenses and merit.

### (13)

In a foreign land, there was a dwelling place. Among the community, there was one practitioner who defecated on the saṃgha's clean ground. There was another practitioner of an angry temperament who then licked it with his tongue to show the community. Though he wished to expose the other's offense, he did not realize he was defiling his own mouth. This illustrates that those who enjoy speaking of others' faults are just like this: they only know how to expose others' offenses but do not realize they are ruining their own practice.

### (14)

In a foreign land, a servant, wishing to please a noble man, would compete to step on the noble man's spit whenever he spat on the ground. One man, not being very agile, always wanted to step on it but never could. Later, seeing the noble man about to spit, while the spittle was still gathering in his mouth, the servant stepped on his mouth. The noble man asked: "Are you rebelling? Why did you step on my mouth?" The servant answered: "I mean well; I am not rebelling." The noble man asked: "If you are not rebelling, how did it come to this?" The servant answered: "Whenever the noble man spits, I always want to step on the spit, but as soon as it leaves your mouth, others always get to it first. I could never get there in time. That is why I stepped on it while it was still in your mouth." This is a parable about debating: one must wait until the meaning has left someone's mouth before raising objections. If the meaning is still in the mouth and the reasoning has not yet been fully expressed, and one rushes to challenge it, it is like stepping on someone's mouth.

### (15)

Long ago, the Buddha and his disciples entered the city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. Along the road they saw a pit that collected all the city's filth and impurities. They saw an old sow leading her piglets, lying together in the filthy pit. The Buddha then smiled slightly, revealing his forty teeth and his four canine teeth. From the four teeth he emitted a great radiance that illuminated the three-thousand worlds and extended throughout the ten directions. The light then returned, circled the Buddha's body three times, and entered through his chest. The Dharma of the buddhas is as follows: when speaking of matters concerning the hells, the light enters from below the feet. When about to speak of matters concerning animals, the light enters from the shoulder. When about to speak of matters concerning hungry ghosts, the light enters from the thigh. When about to speak of human matters, the light enters from the navel. When about to speak of matters concerning the gods, the light enters from the chest. When about to speak of matters concerning śrāvakas, the light enters from the mouth. When about to speak of matters concerning pratyekabuddhas, the light enters from the ūrṇā between the brows. When about to speak of matters concerning buddhas and bodhisattvas, the light enters from the crown.

Ānanda, seeing the light enter through the chest, knew the Buddha was about to speak of matters concerning the gods. He immediately knelt and asked the Buddha to explain. The Buddha said to Ānanda: "In a past age, innumerable kalpas ago, there was an elder who had no children, only one daughter. She was extraordinarily beautiful, intelligent, and eloquent. Her parents cherished her greatly. When the girl had grown up, she spoke a verse to ask her parents:

> 'All swift waters flow; the joys and sorrows of the world:
> From where do they originally come, and when will they cease?'

"Her parents were delighted by her extraordinary refinement, but did not know how to answer this verse. The girl wished to understand its meaning, but having received no answer, she became deeply sorrowful and would no longer eat or drink. When her parents saw their daughter's distress, they became very alarmed and immediately arranged a great assembly, inviting brāhmaṇas and wise elders. A multitude gathered. When the feast was complete, a small seat was placed in the midst of the assembly. The girl sat upon it and recited the same verse to ask the assembly. The assembly was silent; none could answer. The elder then filled a dish with the seven precious substances and made a proclamation: 'Whoever can answer shall receive this.' At that time there was a brāhmaṇa, handsome in form but deficient in wisdom, who, coveting the treasures, said: 'I can answer.' The girl then recited the verse to question the brāhmaṇa. He also did not understand the meaning of the verse, and simply said: 'This matter is entirely nothing at all.' The girl then reflected, and immediately attained the concentration of nothingness. She exclaimed: 'This is truly a great teacher who has benefited me greatly.' After the girl died, she was reborn in the Realm of Nothingness. After forty kalpas, when her heavenly lifespan was exhausted, she was reborn here."

The Buddha said: "The elder's daughter at that time is now this old sow. Her heavenly merit is exhausted and her past karmic offenses have come due. In this life she has received the form of a pig. When this girl originally recited the verse to ask, if she had encountered a wise teacher, she could have attained the Way. Although this girl practiced meditation, she lacked wisdom. When the reward of her concentration was exhausted, she fell back into evil destinies."

### (16)

Long ago there was a great king who had been seriously ill for twelve years without recovering. No physician could cure him. In a small neighboring kingdom under his rule, there was a physician skilled in treating illnesses. The king summoned him and had him treat his illness. Before long, the king recovered. The king then wished to repay this physician's kindness. He repeatedly sent messengers bearing orders to the small kingdom: "This physician has cured the king's illness and deserves a great reward. He should be given elephants, horses, carriages, cattle, sheep, fields, dwellings, servants, and all ornamental goods." The small kingdom's king obeyed the orders and provided the physician's wife with a dwelling with lofty halls and multi-storied pavilions, clothing, food, drink, pearl ornaments, as well as elephants, horses, cattle, and sheep, everything in abundance. But the physician, staying by the king's side, heard nothing of this. He thought: "I have exerted great effort in curing the king's illness. I wonder whether the king will ever repay me." After several more days, the king recovered further. The physician then asked leave to return to his homeland. The king permitted him and provided a single emaciated horse with worn-out trappings. The physician sighed greatly in resentment: "I exerted such great effort to cure the king's illness, yet the king does not recognize the debt of gratitude and has made no provision for me, letting me go empty-handed." He sighed with sorrow all the way home, considering it an eternal regret.

Just as he reached his homeland, he saw a herd of elephants. He asked the elephant keeper: "Whose elephants are these?" The keeper answered: "These belong to such-and-such physician." He further asked: "How did such-and-such physician come by these elephants?" The keeper answered: "He cured the great king's illness, and these are his reward." Going a little further, he saw a herd of horses. He asked the groom: "Whose horses are these?" The groom answered: "Such-and-such physician's horses." Going further, he saw herds of cattle and sheep. He asked: "Whose cattle and sheep are these?" The herder answered: "Such-and-such physician's cattle and sheep." Going further, he saw his own dwelling, now a lofty hall with multi-storied pavilions, far grander than his original home. He asked the doorkeeper: "Whose house is this?" The doorkeeper answered: "This is such-and-such physician's house." Entering the inner chambers, he saw his wife, whose appearance was radiant and who wore precious garments. Puzzled, he asked: "Whose wife is this?" An attendant answered: "This is such-and-such physician's wife." From the moment he saw the elephants and horses to his entry into the house, he realized it was all the reward for curing the king's illness. He then bitterly regretted that his effort in treating the king had been too little.

This is a parable for meritorious virtue. The obstacles of meritorious virtue are like the king's illness. The physician represents one who cultivates merit. Treating the king's illness is like a practitioner who performs meritorious deeds. The king's recovery is like the completion of merit. The king's orders granting elephants, horses, and a dwelling mean that merit accumulated here bears fruit elsewhere. Those who eagerly hope for speed always fear delay. It is like people of little faith who sometimes perform meritorious deeds and then expect a reward morning and night. When old age, sickness, and death arrive, they say it is just natural law and there is no reward for goodness. But when one obtains the intermediate state of the heavenly realm, all the good rewards come together, like the physician seeing the elephants and horses. Riding this intermediate state and arriving at the heavenly palace, one receives that new existence. Seeing the celestial halls adorned in every way, one then deeply regrets not having done more in the past, just as the physician, seeing all the rewards, regretted that his efforts in treating the illness had been too small.

### (17)

In a foreign land, there was once an evil rain. If it fell into rivers, lakes, wells, moats, or city pools, and people drank this water, it would drive them mad and drunk for seven days before they recovered. At that time, the king of the land was a man of great wisdom and good judgment. When the evil rain clouds arose, the king foresaw what would happen and covered one well to keep the rain out. At that time, all the officials and ministers drank the evil rain water. The entire court went mad, stripped off their clothes, sat naked, and smeared mud on their heads. They sat in the king's hall. The king alone was not mad. He wore his usual garments, his heavenly crown and jeweled necklaces, and sat upon his usual throne. All the ministers, not knowing they were mad, instead declared the king to be greatly mad: "Why does he alone dress like that?" They all said to one another: "This is no small matter; we should deliberate together." The king, fearing his ministers were about to rebel, became frightened and said to them: "I have a fine medicine that can cure this illness. Everyone please wait a moment. Let me take my medicine, and I shall come out shortly." The king then went into the palace, removed his garments, smeared mud on his face, and shortly came back out. All the ministers, seeing this, were greatly pleased and thought this was how things should be, not knowing they themselves were mad. After seven days, the ministers regained their senses and were deeply ashamed. They each put on their robes and hats and came to the morning assembly. But the king was still sitting naked as before. The ministers were all shocked and asked: "Your Majesty has always been wise. Why are you like this?" The king answered: "My mind has always been settled, without change. Because you were mad, you declared me mad instead. That is why I am like this; it is not my true mind."

The Tathāgata is just like this. Because sentient beings drink the water of ignorance and are always mad, if they hear the Great Sage constantly teach that all dharmas are unborn and unperishing, of one characteristic and no characteristic, they will certainly declare the Great Sage to be speaking madly. Therefore the Tathāgata follows the ways of sentient beings and provisionally teaches that dharmas are good and evil, conditioned and unconditioned.

### (18)

In a past life, an asura was a poor man who lived near a river and always crossed it to carry firewood. The river was deep and the current swift. This man was repeatedly swept away by the water; he lost what he carried and his body was submerged. Tossed and turned by the current, he barely managed to escape. At that time a pratyekabuddha, in the form of a śramaṇa, came to his dwelling to beg for food. The poor man joyfully gave to him. After the meal and the ablution water, the pratyekabuddha placed his bowl in the air and flew away. The poor man, seeing this, made a vow: "May I, in a future life, have a body so large that all deep waters will not come above my knees." Through this karmic connection, he obtained an extremely large body. The water of the four great oceans could not rise above his knees. Standing in the great ocean, his body surpassed Mount Sumeru. With his hands on the mountaintop, he looked down upon the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. How much more so the Buddha, who over innumerable kalpas accumulated great vows: his Dharma-body fills all of space. Is that anything to wonder at?

### (19)

Long ago there was a prince of a certain kingdom who at the age of seven entered the deep mountains to study the way of the sages. He had never learned about the court, the hundred officials, or their duties. Later, the king's life ended, and there was no one fit to be king. The ministers deliberated: "The sage in the mountains was originally the prince, and he has also cultivated virtue and morality. If we make him king, the myriad states will benefit." All the subjects then went to the mountain, bowed to this sage, and made him king. They placed him in the royal carriage and welcomed him back to the kingdom. They ordered the royal cook to prepare exquisite dishes of the finest flavors to feast the great king. Because the king found the food delicious, he asked the kitchen official for everything else he needed. All the ministers laughed and said to the king: "Each of the hundred officials has his own charge. The kitchen official manages food, the wardrobe official manages clothing, and military affairs and the treasury each have their own superintendents. You cannot, just because the food is good, demand everything from one person." This parable illustrates that each sūtra illuminates its own subject. One cannot demand that a single sūtra cover everything. Just as one text clarifies the true nature of all dharmas, and the Abhidharma clarifies the existence of all dharmas, each is different in nature. They are to be explained in terms of both characteristics and the absence of characteristics.

### (20) The Deer Park

Long ago there were five hundred deer living in a certain forest. In this forest there were two deer kings: one was a bodhisattva, and the other was a true deer king. At that time, a king of that country went out of the city to hunt and saw the herd of deer. He led his troops to surround them. The two deer kings devised a plan. They both went before the human king, knelt, and addressed him: "We are now within your kingdom's territory and must submit to slaughter. If the king kills all the deer at once, the meat cannot be consumed in time and will rot and stink. We propose to send two deer each day to supply the king's table. The rest, in their turn, will be sent day by day without fail. We beg the king to consent, so that we may gain a brief reprieve. Would this not be a grace from the great king?" The human king agreed and opened the encirclement, releasing them. From then on, the two deer kings selected and arranged the order, sending two deer each day to the royal kitchen.

After several days, a pregnant doe whose turn had come to die went before her deer king and asked to wait until she had given birth. Her king replied: "The other deer's turns have not yet come. Who will take your place?" The doe then went before the bodhisattva deer king and addressed him: "My king is not benevolent and will not grant me reprieve. I have come to take refuge. I beg you to resolve this." The bodhisattva deer king, pitying her, went himself to the royal kitchen. The kitchen official reported to the king: "The deer king has come to the kitchen himself, asking to take the place of the pregnant doe." The king was amazed and said to the kitchen official: "Bring the deer king here." The deer king then went before the human king and explained the entire situation. The human king's faith was awakened: "Even among beasts there is such virtue. How much more should there be among humans?" He ordered that throughout the entire kingdom, hunting should be forever prohibited, and he gave this forest and wilderness as a permanent sanctuary for the deer. From that time on, the forest was named the Deer Park.

### (21) Śrīgupta

Long ago there was a householder whose wife was pregnant. He invited the Buddha to his home and made offerings. He wished the Tathāgata to predict whether his wife's future child would be a boy or a girl. The Buddha said: "She will bear a boy, handsome and fine. When he grows up, among humans he will enjoy heavenly pleasures, and later he will attain the arhat's path." The householder heard this but had doubts and did not believe it. He then invited the six heretical teachers, made offerings, and had them predict as well. He told the six teachers: "I previously had the śramaṇa Gautama predict, and he said it would be a boy. I do not know if it will truly be a boy." The six teachers said: "It will be a girl." The six teachers, who despised the Buddha's Dharma and wanted to contradict him at every turn, then reflected: "If a boy is born, the householder will abandon us and follow Gautama." So they lied: "Householder, your wife will bear a boy, but after his birth, there will be great calamity. Your family and kinfolk for seven generations will be utterly destroyed. Because it is inauspicious, I previously said falsely that it was a girl." When the householder heard this, he was terrified and did not know what to do. The six teachers then said: "If you wish for good fortune, you must simply get rid of it." The six teachers then pressed on the householder's wife's belly, trying to make her miscarry. The pressing did not stop, and the householder's wife died, but the child did not die, because of his past-life meritorious karma. The householder then placed his wife's body in the charnel ground, piled high with firewood, and cremated her. When the flames were blazing, the Buddha brought his disciples to observe. When the wife's body had broken apart, they saw the child sitting on a lotus flower, handsome and fine, his complexion like snow. The Buddha ordered Jīvaka: "Take this child." Jīvaka took the child and returned him to the original householder, who then raised him. At the age of sixteen, his talent and beauty surpassed all others. He then prepared an elaborate feast and invited the six teachers. After the six teachers were seated, before long, they burst out laughing. The young man asked: "Why do you laugh?" The six teachers answered: "We see a mountain fifty thousand leagues away. Below the mountain there is water, and a monkey has fallen in. That is why we laugh." The young man, knowing their words were false, filled their bowls with various fine soups and covered them with rice on top. He had someone carry the bowls and serve them. The other guests' bowls had rice on the bottom and soup on top. Everyone ate, but only the six teachers sat angrily and would not eat. The host asked: "Why will you not eat?" The six teachers answered: "There is no soup. How can we eat?" The host said: "Your eyes can see a monkey falling into water fifty thousand leagues away, yet you cannot see the soup under the rice?" The six teachers were furious and left without eating. They went straight to Śrīgupta and complained in detail. This man's sister was Śrīgupta's wife. When Śrīgupta heard, he too was angry and told the six teachers: "Gautama is his teacher. I am a great teacher. Let me invite him and humiliate him." And so he prepared a fire pit and poisoned food. This story is very extensive and cannot be fully recounted here, so only its essential points are given.

### (22)

Long ago there was a practitioner who went to a brāhmaṇa's house to beg for food. The brāhmaṇa had his wife carry the food and serve it. The wife stood before him. She was beautiful. The practitioner gazed at her and his mind changed. He said to the brāhmaṇa: "The taste of desire: the fault emerges." The brāhmaṇa did not understand and asked: "What do you mean by 'the taste of desire; the fault emerges'?" The practitioner then embraced the brāhmaṇa's wife and kissed her, then said to the brāhmaṇa: "This is the taste of desire." The brāhmaṇa was furious and struck the practitioner once with a stick. The practitioner then said: "This fault is the suffering." When the brāhmaṇa was about to strike again, the practitioner fled to the doorway. He turned back and said to the brāhmaṇa: "This is the emergence." This is a parable that people cannot understand the taste of meaning in the abstract. One must point to concrete events before one can awaken to it.

### (23)

Long ago a country bumpkin went briefly to the capital. He saw someone being whipped and having hot horse dung applied to his back. He asked: "Why is this being done?" The man answered: "It makes the wound heal quickly without leaving a scar." The country bumpkin secretly stored this in his mind. Later, when he returned home, he said to his family: "I have gained great wisdom from going to the capital." Later his family asked: "What wisdom did you gain?" He then called to his servant: "Bring a whip here and give me two hundred lashes hard." The servant, fearing his master, dared not disobey. He gave him two hundred hard lashes until blood ran down his back. Then the bumpkin said to the servant: "Bring hot horse dung and apply it to my back. It will make it heal quickly without leaving a scar." He said to his family: "Do you see? This is wisdom." This is a parable about a practitioner of the lesser precepts. He originally received precepts from a wise teacher and was fine. Then, seeing others receive precepts, he discards his original precepts and reverts to being a layperson, thereby ruining his Dharma-body. This is like receiving two hundred lashes until blood runs down his back. Then seeking to receive precepts again is like applying horse dung.

### (24)

In a foreign land, there was a dragon-charmer. He took a waterpot filled with water and went to the side of the dragon's pool. Single-mindedly he chanted incantations over the dragon. Immediately a great fire arose from the bottom of the pool, and the entire pool was ablaze. When the dragon saw the fire, he lifted his head and looked toward the mountains, but he saw that a great fire was burning all the mountains and marshes. He looked up at the mountaintops, but there was no space above to dwell. Everything was hot, and there was no place to flee. The only place he saw where the water might provide refuge was the waterpot. He then extinguished his fire, made his body extremely small, and entered the waterpot. The dragon's pool represents the desire realm. The mountains and marshes he looked toward represent the form realm. The mountaintop represents the formless realm. The dragon-charmer represents the bodhisattva. The water in the waterpot represents nirvāṇa. The incantations represent skillful means. The great fire represents the manifestation of impermanence. The dragon's great body represents arrogance. Making his body small represents humility. This means that the bodhisattva manifests the conflagration at the end of the kalpa, burning through the desire and form realms entirely. The great fire of impermanence terrifies sentient beings, causing them to abandon arrogance and become humble and lowly. Only then do they all enter nirvāṇa.

### (25)

Long ago, in a foreign land, since time immemorial there was a stone by the side of a road. Over time, it was worn down slightly by the trampling of carts and horses. A person in that era, finding it an obstruction, wished to remove it and broke it apart. From the stone a venomous snake emerged. Exposed to the wind, it grew larger and larger. In an instant, its body filled all of Jambudvīpa. It devoured all the beings, people, and creatures in Jambudvīpa in a single day, and only then did it die. The retribution of evil is this swift. How much more so the bodhisattva: originally an ordinary person, accumulating merit over dust-mote kalpas, from the first moment of generating the aspiration, one achieves buddhahood, teaches the Dharma, liberates beings, and enters nirvāṇa. That such benefit comes so swiftly, is that anything to wonder at?

### (26)

Long ago there was a snake whose head and tail quarreled with each other. The head said to the tail: "I should be the leader." The tail said to the head: "I too should be the leader." The head said: "I have ears to hear, eyes to see, a mouth to eat, and when we move, I am always in front. Therefore I should be the leader. You have none of these abilities and should not be the leader." The tail said: "I am the one who makes you move. Without me you cannot go. Watch: I shall wrap myself around a tree trunk three times and not let go for three days." The head was then unable to move or find food and was near death from starvation. The head said to the tail: "Release me! I agree, you shall be the leader." The tail, hearing these words, immediately released. The head then said: "Since you are the leader, walk in front." The tail walked in front, but after only a few steps, it fell into a fire pit and died. This parable illustrates that within the saṃgha, there may be wise and virtuous senior monks who can decide matters of Dharma and discipline. But juniors below them refuse to obey. The senior monks, unable to control them, say: "Do as you wish." The result is failure, and both fall into wrong Dharma, just as the snake fell into the fire pit.

### (27)

Long ago there was a bird catcher who spread his net over a marsh and placed the birds' food within it. A flock of birds, calling to one another, came eagerly to eat. The bird catcher pulled the net, and all the birds fell into it. Among them was one bird, large and powerful. It lifted the entire net and flew away with all the birds. The bird catcher watched their shadow and followed on foot. Someone said to the bird catcher: "The birds are flying through the sky and you are chasing them on foot. How foolish!" The bird catcher answered: "It is not as you say. When evening comes, these birds will each want to roost, and their directions will not agree. Then they will fall." He continued to follow without stopping. As evening drew near, he looked up and saw the birds flapping and struggling: some wished to go east, some wished to go west, some aimed for a tall forest, some wished to dive into a pool. Going on like this without agreement, in a moment they fell. The bird catcher then caught and killed them one after another. The bird catcher is like Māra. Spreading the net is like the fetters. The bird carrying the net and flying is like a person who has not yet freed himself from the fetters but wishes to seek liberation. Stopping at evening is like a person who becomes lazy and no longer advances. Seeking different roosts is like giving rise to the sixty-two wrong views, which are always in conflict with one another. The birds falling to the ground is like a person who receives the fruit of wrong views and falls into hell. This illustrates that the dust and defilements of the fetters are Māra's net.

### (28)

Long ago, when the Buddha was in the world, there were five hundred strongmen who had all become śramaṇas. They were together in one place, sitting in meditation and reciting sūtras. Wicked thieves came and stole all the śramaṇas' robes and bowls, leaving them with nothing but their undergarments. After the thieves left, the śramaṇas quickly put on their undergarments and all went before the Buddha to report what had happened. The Buddha said to the śramaṇas: "Why did you not shout?" The śramaṇas answered: "The Buddha had not given permission, so we dared not shout." The Buddha said to the bhikṣus: "If you dare not shout, then the thieves will strip you bare by day. Who will be able to constantly supply you? From this day forward, I permit you, when you see thieves coming, to shout loudly, to take up sticks, bricks, and stones and frighten them away. Only do not truly injure them."

What people value most is their body, their life, and their possessions. These three things are both not worth cherishing and not to be taken lightly. They are not worth cherishing because they are impermanent, subject to decay, and have nothing solid about them. Fools cherish them, considering them "mine." Through craving and attachment, they create unwholesome karmic connections and later fall into evil destinies. That is why they are not worth cherishing. They are not to be taken lightly because, having a body, one can encounter sages and saints, kneel and bow in reverence. Later one obtains the indestructible vajra body. That is why the body is not to be taken lightly.

Life is not worth cherishing because, for the sake of life, people kill, steal, commit adultery, commit the four offenses of speech, and generate greed, anger, and wrong views in their minds. Later they fall into hell. That is why life is not worth cherishing. Yet it is also not to be taken lightly because, having life, one can encounter sages and saints, hear the Dharma and its profound meaning, practice throughout one's entire lifespan, and later obtain a precious life that is immeasurable and inexhaustible. That is why life is not to be taken lightly.

Possessions are not worth cherishing because wealth belongs to the five families: thieves, water, fire, officials, and wayward sons. When the five families arrive suddenly, everything is gone in an instant. That is why possessions are not worth cherishing. They are not to be taken lightly because, encountering a good field of merit, one can use them for giving and making various offerings without holding anything back. Later one obtains precious wealth from the four great treasuries, sufficient to relieve all poverty and never exhausted. That is why possessions are not to be taken lightly.

Those who cultivate merit should all set their minds on attaining buddhahood. They should not merely seek the fruits of heavenly or human reward. Why? It is like planting grain: one seeks only the harvest. Although the grain has not yet ripened, the stalks, joints, branches, and leaves are naturally obtained. Giving and cultivating merit are just the same. If one sets one's intention solely on attaining buddhahood and the path of nirvāṇa, though the Way has not yet been achieved, the joys of the human and heavenly realms, the position of a golden-wheel sage king, Śakra, and Brahmā, all come naturally. It is like planting grain: one does not expect the stalks, joints, branches, and leaves, yet they come naturally. That is why one should not merely seek the pleasure of heavenly and human rewards.

### (29)

Long ago in India, there was a dwelling place with one hundred thousand śramaṇas. More than fifty thousand had attained arhatship, with the six supernatural powers and complete clarity, all outflows exhausted. The remaining fifty thousand had either attained the lower three paths or had not yet attained anything. An elder wished to seek the pleasures of heavenly and human realms for himself. He came to a stūpa and monastery and prepared food as an offering to the saṃgha. At that time there was a senior monk who had attained the six supernatural powers as a great arhat. This man was extremely old; his beard was white, his teeth had fallen out, and his body was withered. Among the hundred thousand, he was the most senior. When he had finished pronouncing blessings for the elder and the meal and ablution water were complete, he said to the elder: "Benefactor, through this offering you will actually incur a great offense." At that time, those in the assembly who had not yet attained the Way all thought: "The senior monk is old, and that is why he speaks such deranged words." The senior monk replied: "It is truly so; these are not deranged words." The assembly asked: "This man has planted merit. How can he incur an offense?" The senior monk answered: "You know one thing but not the other. This man plants merit and will receive heavenly and human pleasures. But amid those pleasures he will become greatly arrogant, thinking himself satisfied, and will not seek liberation. Seeing the Buddha, he will not pay respect; seeing the sūtras, he will not read them; seeing a śramaṇa, he will have no heart of reverence. He will indulge freely, and when his stored merit is exhausted, he will fall into evil destinies for innumerable asaṃkhyeya-kalpas. Only when his offenses are finished will he emerge. The reason he will incur a great offense is that it comes from receiving a great worldly reward. If he had set his mind on the holy path and performed this merit, when he later received the reward, this consequence would never occur."

### (30)

Long ago in India, there were two poor men who made their living by selling yogurt. Each carried a pot of yogurt on his head and went to market to sell it. It happened to rain, and the road became muddy and slippery. One of them, being wise, reflected: "Today the rain has made the road difficult. I may slip and fall, breaking the pot and losing everything. I should churn out the butter now. If I fall, the loss will be small." The other, less wise, kept his yogurt whole and went to market. On the muddy road, both slipped and fell. One was sorrowful, weeping and rolling on the ground. The other showed no sign of sorrow or regret. A passerby asked: "Both of your yogurt pots broke. Your losses are equal, no different from each other. Why does one of you alone weep in sorrow while the other is calm and shows no sign of regret?" One answered: "The yogurt I carried had not yet been churned into butter. Today my pot broke and everything is lost. That is why I grieve beyond measure." The other answered: "The yogurt I carried had already been churned into butter. Though my pot broke, the loss is small. That is why I am at ease with no regret."

The pot represents the body. The butter represents possessions. Some people are miserly and hoard their possessions, greedily pursuing present gain without reflecting on impermanence. When the pot of the body suddenly breaks, their possessions are entirely lost, like the man who lost his yogurt and butter and regretted it bitterly, to no avail. Others deeply believe in the karmic rewards of the next life and use all their possessions for generosity. When the pot of the body breaks, the loss is negligible, just like the man whose yogurt pot broke but whose loss was very small. His heart was at ease, with no regret.

### (31)

Long ago, five hundred merchants boarded a ship and sailed into the sea seeking precious treasures. They encountered a makara fish that raised its head and opened its mouth, wanting to devour the living beings. The sun was dim, the wind was slight, and yet the ship moved as fast as an arrow. The chief merchant said to the others: "The ship is going far too fast. Let us lower the sail and drop anchor." They did as he said, lowering the sail and anchor, but the ship went even faster and could not be stopped. The chief merchant asked the man on the lookout platform: "What do you see?" "I see two suns above, a white mountain below, and a black mountain in between." The chief merchant was alarmed: "This is a great fish! What shall we do? You and I have now encountered this disaster. If we enter this fish's belly, there is no hope of survival. Let each of you single-mindedly call upon whatever deity you worship." Everyone then called upon their respective deities with single-minded devotion, but the more fervently they prayed, the faster the ship moved. In a moment it would enter the fish's mouth.

The chief merchant then announced: "I know of a great deity whose name is Buddha. Let all of you abandon what you have been worshiping and single-mindedly call upon him." The five hundred men then all raised their voices and cried: "Namo Buddha!" When the fish heard the name of the Buddha, it reflected: "In this world there is indeed a buddha. How could I bear to harm living beings?" The moment it had this thought, it closed its mouth. The waters all reversed their flow, carrying the ship far from the fish's mouth. The five hundred merchants were all saved at once.

This fish had been a practitioner of the Way in a past life. Because of offenses, it received this fish's body. Having heard the name of the Buddha, it immediately recalled its past life, and thus a wholesome thought arose. This illustrates that the five hundred merchants, simply by single-mindedly recalling the Buddha and briefly calling his name, were immediately delivered from this overwhelming danger. How much more so for those who undertake and maintain the samādhi of recollecting the Buddha: heavy offenses become light, and light ones are extinguished. That such a response occurs should not be thought remarkable.

### (32)

When the kalpa ends in fire and all becomes empty, through the power of sentient beings' meritorious karma, winds come from the ten directions. The winds, one upon another, are able to support a great body of water. Upon the water there is a person with a thousand heads, two thousand hands, and two thousand feet, named Viṣṇu. From this person's navel grows a thousand-petaled golden lotus. Its radiance is as bright as ten thousand suns shining together. Within the lotus is a person sitting in full lotus posture. This person has immeasurable radiance; he is called Brahmā. From his mind he generates eight sons, and the eight sons generate heaven, earth, and all people. This Brahmā has completely exhausted all lust and anger, with nothing remaining. For this reason it is said: if a person practices meditation and pure conduct, severing all sensual desire, this is called practicing the Way of Brahmā. When the Buddha turns the Dharma-wheel, it is sometimes called the Brahmā-wheel. This Brahmā sits upon a lotus. Therefore all the buddhas, following worldly convention, sit in full lotus posture upon a precious lotus and teach the six pāramitās. Those who hear this Dharma will certainly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

### (33)

Long ago there was a noble woman of fine and distinguished appearance who had left home to study the Way and attained the arhat's path. She was walking alone among the trees in a forest outside the city when she encountered a man on the road who, seeing the bhikṣuṇī's beautiful countenance, became greatly infatuated. He stood before her and blocked her way, swearing: "If you do not yield to me, I will not let you pass." The bhikṣuṇī then taught him the Dharma of the impure and repulsive aspects of the body: "The head, eyes, hands, and feet: what is there to covet?" The man then said to the bhikṣuṇī: "I love your beautiful eyes." The bhikṣuṇī then, with her right hand, gouged out one of her eyes and showed it to the man. Blood streamed down her face. When the man saw this, his desire immediately subsided. The bhikṣuṇī, holding the eye in her hand, returned to the Buddha's presence. She restored the eye to its original place and reported everything to the Buddha. Because of this, a precept was established. From that time on, bhikṣuṇīs were not permitted to dwell outside a city or to walk alone outside a village.

### (34)

All the grasses and trees in the world can serve as medicine. It is only that those who do not know how to distinguish them do not recognize this. Long ago there was a saintly physician king named Jīvaka who could blend medicinal herbs into the form of a child. Those who saw it rejoiced, and all illnesses were cured. Sometimes he used a single herb to cure many illnesses; sometimes he used many herbs to cure a single illness. There was no grass in the world that he could not use, and no illness in the world that he could not cure.

When Jīvaka died, all the medicinal herbs in the world wept at once, all raising their voices: "We can all be used to cure illnesses, but only Jīvaka could understand us. After Jīvaka's death, there is no one who can understand us. In future ages, people may use us incorrectly, adding too much or too little, causing illnesses not to be cured. They will make everyone in the world think we are ineffective. Reflecting on this, that is why we weep."

Only one myrobalan fruit stood apart and did not weep. It said: "I can cure all illnesses. Those who take me will all be cured. Those who do not take me simply will not be cured. I do not need someone to explain me. That is why I do not weep."

Jīvaka represents the Buddha. The various medicinal herbs represent the various dharmas. The myrobalan fruit represents the contemplation of impermanence. When the Buddha was in the world, he skillfully used dharmas and could employ even lust, anger, and delusion as medicine to cure people's illnesses, along with all other wholesome dharmas, using each as appropriate. The path of impermanence is like a good physician for the sick. After the Buddha's passing, few can skillfully use the various dharmas and adapt them to circumstances. The contemplation of impermanence cures many things: it can cure lust, it can cure anger, it can cure delusion. Used skillfully, it removes illness. Used without skill, it causes no harm. That is why it is compared to the myrobalan fruit. The other dharmas are not so easy to use. Those who use them must have a good teacher. Used skillfully, they diminish illness; used without skill, they increase it.

### (35)

Long ago there was a butcher who went before King Ajātaśatru and asked for one favor. The king said: "What favor do you ask?" He answered: "At the king's festivals, when slaughter is needed, I beg the king to grant that I may do all the slaughtering." The king said: "Slaughtering is something no one enjoys. Why do you wish to do it?" He answered: "In a past life I was a poor man. I made my living by slaughtering sheep. Because of this, I was reborn in the First Heaven. When that heavenly lifespan ended, I returned to the human world and again slaughtered sheep. After death, I was reborn in the Second Heaven. In this way, six times I slaughtered sheep, and because of this, I was reborn through all six heavens, receiving immeasurable blessings. That is why I now make this request of the king." The king said: "Even if what you say is true, how do you know this?" He answered: "I can recall my past lives." The king heard this but did not believe it, thinking it a lie: "How could such a lowly person recall past lives?" He then went and asked the Buddha. The Buddha answered: "What he says is true; it is not a lie. In a past life, this man once encountered a pratyekabuddha. Seeing the Buddha, he was delighted and carefully observed him, looking up at his head and down at his feet, and a wholesome thought arose. Through this merit, he was reborn, one life at a time, in all six heavens. When he descended to the human world, he could recall his past lives. His meritorious karma had already ripened, so six times he was born in heaven among humans. His sinful karma had not yet ripened, so he had not yet received it. After this present body, he will fall into hell and receive the retribution for slaughtering sheep. When his time in hell is finished, he will be reborn as a sheep, repaying each one." This man's ability to recall past lives was shallow; he could only see the events of six heavenly lives and could not reach back to the seventh life. Therefore he thought that slaughtering sheep was the cause of being reborn in heaven. This is merely recollection of past lives, not the supernatural power of penetrating knowledge.

### (36)

Long ago there was a king of a certain country who deeply understood the karmic consequences of merit and offense, and who believed in retribution. He always delighted in giving and never refused anyone. His fame spread far and wide, and all had heard of him. A neighboring country raised an army to attack his kingdom. The king reflected: "If I go out to battle, there will certainly be harm and killing. I would rather lose my own life than unjustly sacrifice my people." When the enemy army arrived and entered through the east gate of the city, the king departed through the west gate, alone, fleeing into the wilderness. At that time, a brāhmaṇa coming from a distant land traveled through the forest and happened to meet the king. The two greeted each other. The king asked the brāhmaṇa: "Where have you come from, and where are you going?" The brāhmaṇa said: "I have heard that a certain king delights in giving and never refuses anyone. I have come from far away hoping to receive something." The king immediately answered: "The person you speak of is myself." The brāhmaṇa, hearing this, was shocked. He asked the king: "Why is Your Majesty in such a state?" The king then explained the entire situation. The brāhmaṇa, hearing this, collapsed on the ground and died. The king lifted him up and sprinkled water on him, and after a long while he revived. The king asked: "Why did this happen?" The brāhmaṇa said: "I have always been poor and destitute. I came from far away hoping to receive treasure. How is it that today I find the king in such a state? I was overcome with anguish." The king then consoled the brāhmaṇa: "Do not be sorrowful. I shall see to it that you obtain great treasure. The rival king, though he has taken my kingdom, has not captured my person. There is a great reward proclaimed throughout the land for my capture. You may bind me and deliver me to the king's gate. The king will be delighted and will certainly reward you greatly."

The brāhmaṇa then did as the king said: he bound the king's two hands with grass rope and delivered him to the gate of the palace. The guards, seeing this, quickly reported to the king inside. The king, hearing this, was surprised and delighted and ordered them brought forward. The soldiers then brought the captive king and the brāhmaṇa before the throne. The king asked the brāhmaṇa: "What skill do you have that you were able to capture this man?" The brāhmaṇa answered: "I have no skill. This man, when he was king, always delighted in giving. I came from far away hoping to receive something. In the forest, we happened to meet. He asked me: 'Where are you going?' I answered: 'To the kingdom of such-and-such king.' He said: 'That king is myself.' When I heard these words, I immediately died and knew nothing. He lifted me up and sprinkled water on me, then asked me: 'Why have you come here?' I answered: 'In past lives I did not give, so in this life I am born poor. I came from far away hoping to beg for treasure. My original wish was not fulfilled, so I was overcome with anguish.' He consoled me: 'Do not be troubled. I shall provide for you with my own person.' He then said: 'You may bind my two arms with rope and deliver me to the king's gate. The king will certainly reward you.'"

When the king heard the brāhmaṇa's account, tears fell from his eyes. He rose from his seat and came down, saying to the former king: "You are a true king. I am merely a brigand." He then withdrew his forces and returned to his own country. The former king was restored to his throne, and all was as before. This illustrates that even as an ordinary person, the bodhisattva's practice of supreme virtue is like this. If a person copies and upholds a sūtra with a sincere heart, neither heaven nor wicked people can prevail against him.

### (37)

There are two kinds of thieves: one, the thief of physical force; two, the thief of stratagem. The thief of physical force digs through walls with his own hands, making openings in the shape of a lion's head or a lotus flower. When he enters a house and takes goods, he does not take everything but always leaves some, so that the owners can still make a living. He wishes people to say: "This is a good thief." He then changes his clothes and goes with others to the house that was robbed to look. When the crowd sees where the thief dug through the wall, they all say: "This is a clever thief." At that time, a thief of stratagem, dressed in a brāhmaṇa's robes, was also in the crowd. He said: "This is not a clever thief. He expends much effort and gains little. How is that clever? A truly clever thief expends no effort and gains much." The thief of physical force secretly memorized this. When the crowd left, he followed and asked: "What is a thief of stratagem?" The other answered: "If you wish to know, just follow me. Within a month or so, I shall show you."

The thief of stratagem then disguised himself in brāhmaṇa's robes and visited the home of a wealthy elder. He told the elder: "I am in need of a small sum. Could you give it to me?" The elder, thinking he meant a garment's worth, readily agreed: "I shall provide it." Before he received anything, the stratagem thief returned again and said: "What you previously promised me, will I definitely receive it?" The elder answered: "You shall certainly receive it." This went on three times. The stratagem thief then forged a document and went to the authorities, claiming: "Such-and-such elder owes me one hundred thousand pieces of gold and will not repay me." He then recruited the elder's enemies as witnesses. The official interrogated the witnesses: "Is this true?" The witnesses answered: "It is true." The official then ordered the elder to pay the gold to the brāhmaṇa. The thief of stratagem, without lifting a hand, obtained a great haul. Sympathetic rejoicing in others' merit works in just the same way.

### (38)

A dragon is able to take a single drop of water and rain it over one country, or two, or three, and even rain it over all of Jambudvīpa. The dragon thought: "I wish to store this single drop of water so that it is always present and never dries up. Where can I do this?" Reflecting, he concluded: "Nowhere else will work. Only if I place it in the great ocean will it never dry up." This is a parable: a small act of giving that yields a great and inexhaustible reward should be directed toward the path of buddhahood. This means that the water drop, combined with the dragon's wisdom, finds a place where it does not dry up. Giving, combined with prajñā, finds a place where it is never exhausted.

### (39)

The reason a cakravartin, a wheel-turning sage king, obtains the golden wheel is as follows. Śakra always orders the Four Heavenly Kings to inspect the world on the six days of each month and observe people's good and evil deeds. When the Four Heavenly Kings, their princes, and their emissaries see a great king governing the realm with the ten wholesome deeds and the four immeasurables, concerned for the welfare of all beings with a mind like a compassionate father, they report this to the heavenly lord Śakra. When Śakra hears this and rejoices in his ability, he orders Viśvakarman to bestow the golden wheel. Viśvakarman then produces the golden wheel and delivers it to the heavenly king Vaiśravaṇa. Vaiśravaṇa delivers it to a flying yakṣa. The flying yakṣa brings it to the great king. Vaiśravaṇa orders this yakṣa: "You shall always hold this golden wheel for the king. You shall keep it above the king's head for his entire lifespan and must not abandon it midway." This yakṣa always holds it. In all the king's comings and goings, the wheel follows the sage king's wishes. When the king's lifespan ends, it is returned to Vaiśravaṇa. Vaiśravaṇa returns it to Viśvakarman. Viśvakarman returns it to the treasure vault.

### (40)

Long ago there was a great Brahmā named Baka. Because of karmic causes and conditions from past lives that planted the seeds of longevity, his lifespan extended through seventy-two Brahmā lifespans. Throughout all these, his lifespan never ended. Because of this longevity, he developed the wrong view that he was eternal. He further thought: "I am sovereign and free. From now on, no one can presume to see me unless I permit it. If I permit it, they may see me; if not, they may not."

The Buddha, with his divine mind and Dharma-eye, perceived his thoughts. Together with Śāriputra, Maudgalyāyana, and the other four great disciples, he soared through the sky and sat upon Brahmā's head, with Śāriputra on the right, Maudgalyāyana on the left, Mahākāśyapa in front, and Mahākātyāyana behind. He told the Brahmā king: "You consider yourself eternal and sovereign. Yet how is it that I am now sitting on top of your head?" He further asked: "What things have you seen that make you consider yourself eternal and sovereign?" The Brahmā king answered: "Among those in my Brahmā heaven, seventy-two beings have, one after another, come to the end of their lifespan, yet mine has not ended. Furthermore, three beings of great meritorious virtue have completed their lifespans, yet mine has not ended. For this reason, I consider myself eternal." The Buddha said to the Brahmā king: "I am the omniscient one. I saw the time of your birth and I see the time of your death, as well as all dharmas without error. Do not be deluded, thinking yourself eternal."

This Brahmā king could also recall his past lives. Wishing to test whether the Buddha was about to become a buddha and truly knew, he said to the Buddha: "Does the Buddha know by what karmic causes and conditions I obtained this lifespan?" The Buddha said to the Brahmā king: "You were once a sage with the five supernatural powers. You saw a group of people who had boarded a ship to cross the sea. A violent storm arose, and waves reached the sky. Using the power of your supernatural abilities, you rescued the people and placed them on the shore, delivering them from mortal danger. This was the first cause. Furthermore, you were once a minister of a great country. A certain village had violated the king's law. The king was furious and wished to execute the entire village. You, in compassion, exhausted your household wealth and property and arranged for their deliverance, enabling them to be fully saved. This was the second cause. Through these two causes and conditions, you obtained this longevity. After another thirty-six kalpas, your lifespan will end."

When the Brahmā king heard the Buddha's words, faith immediately arose. With single-minded contemplation, he attained the fruit of the anāgāmin. Through these karmic causes and conditions, even the Brahmā king obtained such a lifespan. How much more so the Buddha, who over innumerable asaṃkhyeya-kalpas accumulated great vows and had compassion for sentient beings: when someone asked for his head, he gave his head; when someone asked for his eyes, he gave his eyes. Whatever was asked of him, he was able to provide in full. His body fills all of space: is that anything great? His lifespan of dust-mote kalpas: is that too many?
