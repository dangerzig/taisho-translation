# 鞞摩肅經
## Sūtra of Vīmaṃsaka

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0090

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka (鞞摩肅), after the midday meal, went wandering and came to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he addressed the World-Honored One: "Indeed, Gautama! Your physical form and appearance are supremely wonderful."

"Kātyāyana! What do you mean by 'wonderful form'?"

"Gautama! By wonderful form I mean supremely wonderful, with nothing more wonderful, most excellent, most supreme, most fine. That form is the most wonderful, that form is the most supreme and fine."

"It is as if, Kātyāyana, someone were to say: 'There is a wonderful person among people, and I desire to be intimate with her.' And suppose someone asks: 'This wonderful person you speak of among people: what is her name, what is her family, what is her appearance? Is she tall, short, or of medium height? Is she beautiful or not? Is she fair or dark? Is she a kṣatriya woman, a brāhmaṇa woman, a merchant's daughter, or an artisan's daughter? Is she from the east, the south, the west, or the north?' When asked, that person cannot answer."

"You neither know nor see this wonderful person among people, even to the north, and yet you say you desire to be intimate with her."

"Just so, Kātyāyana! You say that this form is the most wonderful, this form is the most supreme, this form is unsurpassed, yet when questioned about form you cannot explain it."

"It is like, Gautama, Jambudvīpa gold that a master craftsman has carefully polished and purified, placed in a white vessel: the form and appearance are supremely wonderful, and the color has radiance. Therefore, Gautama, I speak thus: form is the most wonderful, form is the most excellent; that form is the most excellent, that form is unsurpassed, that form is the most supreme."

"Therefore, Kātyāyana, I shall now question you in return; answer as you think fit. Kātyāyana, what do you think? Take this Jambudvīpa gold, carefully polished and purified by a master craftsman, placed in a white vessel with radiant form. Now compare this with a firefly in the darkness, whose form is wonderful and has radiance. Which light is the most excellent, most supreme, most wonderful, most fine?"

"Gautama! The firefly surpasses the Jambudvīpa gold in brilliance, being the most excellent, most supreme, most fine, most wonderful."

"Kātyāyana, what do you think? The firefly in the darkness has radiance. Now compare an oil lamp in the darkness with radiance. Which light is the most excellent, most wonderful, most fine, most supreme?"

"Indeed, Gautama! The oil lamp's light surpasses the firefly, being the most excellent, most supreme, most wonderful, most fine."

"Kātyāyana, what do you think? The oil lamp's light in the night darkness has radiance. Now compare a great bonfire in the night darkness with radiance. Which light is the most excellent, most supreme, wonderful, and fine?"

"Indeed, Gautama! The bonfire's light surpasses the oil lamp, being the most excellent, supreme, wonderful, and fine."

"Kātyāyana, what do you think? The bonfire in the darkness has radiance. Now compare the stars past midnight when the sky is clear and cloudless with radiance. Which light is the most excellent, supreme, wonderful, and fine?"

"Indeed, Gautama! The starlight surpasses the bonfire, being the most excellent, supreme, wonderful, and fine."

"Kātyāyana, what do you think? The stars past midnight when the sky is clear and cloudless have radiance. Now compare the moon approaching fullness when the sky is clear and cloudless with radiance. Which light is the most excellent, supreme, wonderful, and fine?"

"Indeed, Gautama! The moonlight surpasses the starlight, being the most excellent, supreme, wonderful, and fine."

"Kātyāyana, what do you think? The moon approaching fullness when the sky is clear and cloudless has radiance. Now compare the sun at midday in summer with radiance. Which light is the most excellent, supreme, wonderful, and fine?"

"Indeed, Gautama! The sunlight surpasses the moonlight, being the most excellent, supreme, wonderful, and fine."

"Now, Kātyāyana! There are many celestial beings with such divine power and great capability, whose radiance these cannot reach. I have formerly sat among them, I have formerly spoken among them, and yet I do not declare that their light is the most excellent, most supreme, most wonderful. You, Kātyāyana, declare that the firefly's light, which is the lowest and most inferior, is the most supreme, most excellent, most wonderful! When questioned about radiance, you cannot understand it." Thereupon the non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka, rebuked to his face by the World-Honored One, stood silent with nothing to say, his face stained with shame, turning away without words.

Then the World-Honored One, having rebuked Vīmaṃsaka to his face and wishing him to speak again, addressed the non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka: "Furthermore, Kātyāyana! There are five kinds of sense desire, loved, cherished, delightful, tainted by passion and attachment: the eye knows form, the ear knows sound, the nose knows scent, the tongue knows taste, the body knows touch. Kātyāyana! Some forms are loved and some are not loved. There are those who find complete satisfaction and delight in a form, whose joy and thoughts are entirely fulfilled, and who regarding that form desire no other form, do not wish for it, do not seek or aspire to it: that form is for them the most wonderful and most supreme. Kātyāyana! For some it is sounds, scents, tastes, or touches that are loved, while for some they are not. There are those who find complete satisfaction in a touch, whose joy and thoughts are entirely fulfilled, and who regarding that touch desire no other touch, do not wish for it, do not seek or aspire to it: that touch is for them the most supreme and most wonderful."

Thereupon the non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka, joining his palms toward the World-Honored One, addressed the World-Honored One: "How extraordinary, Gautama! The ascetic Gautama, with immeasurable skillful means, has explained sensual pleasure and the pursuit of sensual pleasure to me. It is like, Gautama, grass and wood that burn because of fire, and fire that burns because of grass and wood. Just so, the ascetic Gautama, with immeasurable skillful means, has explained sensual desire, sensual pleasure, and the pursuit of sensual pleasure, and brought it to rest."

"Kātyāyana! You have not done well. Throughout this long night, you have held different views, different convictions, different desires, different pursuits. What I have taught here, I have taught with equal understanding of its meaning. Kātyāyana! My disciples, the bhikṣus, rising at dawn and dusk, never sleeping idly, always engaged in discussion and teaching, inevitably attain the true path, with complete discernment and understanding. They know as it truly is that birth is ended, the brahma conduct is established, what was to be done has been done, and name-and-form is understood. What I have taught here, I have taught with equal understanding of its meaning."

Thereupon the non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka became extremely angry at the World-Honored One, harboring resentment and displeasure, and reviled the World-Honored One, abused the World-Honored One, and cursed the World-Honored One: "This ascetic Gautama is wrong!" Having cursed and reviled, he addressed the World-Honored One: "Gautama! There are some ascetics and brāhmaṇas who do not know the past world, do not know the future world, and do not know the countless lives of worldly existence, yet they declare themselves sages who know as it truly is that birth is ended, the brahma conduct is established, what was to be done has been done, and name-and-form is understood. Therefore, Gautama, I think: 'How can some ascetics and brāhmaṇas, not knowing the past world, not knowing the future world, not knowing the countless lives of worldly existence, declare themselves sages who know as it truly is?'"

Then the World-Honored One thought: "This non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka is extremely angry at me, harbors resentment and displeasure, reviles me, abuses me, and curses me. And he asks me about those who do not know past and future worlds yet claim to know as it truly is." The World-Honored One, knowing this, addressed the non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka: "Kātyāyana! If some ascetic or brāhmaṇa does not know the past world, does not know the future world, and does not know the countless lives of worldly existence, yet knows as it truly is, then at that time one should say: 'Set aside the past world, set aside the future world; one need not think about even a single lifetime.'

"Furthermore, Kātyāyana, I say this: 'Set aside the past world, set aside the future world; one need not think about even a single lifetime. My disciples, the bhikṣus, are neither flattering nor deceitful; they are straightforward in their conduct. I instruct them, I teach them the Dharma, and as instructed they are able to train, drawing near to the Dharma and knowing what is wholesome.'

"It is like, Kātyāyana, a young boy whose parents have bound his hands and feet. At that time, wisdom arises, and his parents unbind his hands and feet. He remembers only the unbinding, not the binding. Just so, Kātyāyana, I say: 'Set aside the past world, set aside the future world; one need not think about even a single lifetime. They draw near to the Dharma and know what is wholesome.'

"It is like, Kātyāyana, an oil lamp that burns because of oil and a wick. If someone does not add more oil or replace the wick, the previous supply is all exhausted and nothing more is added, and the lamp soon goes out. Just so, Kātyāyana, I say: 'Set aside the past world, set aside the future world; they draw near to the Dharma and know what is wholesome.'

"It is like, Kātyāyana, having ten woodpiles, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty, sixty woodpiles, all burning with fire. If someone does not add more firewood, does not add grass, does not add cattle dung, does not add husks, does not add sweepings, before long all is consumed and nothing more added, and the fire soon goes out. Just so, Kātyāyana, I say: 'Set aside the past world, set aside the future world; they draw near to the Dharma and know what is wholesome.'"

When this was spoken, the non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka was freed from dust and defilement, and the Dharma eye arose in him. Thereupon the non-Buddhist ascetic Vīmaṃsaka saw the Dharma, attained the Dharma, comprehended the Dharma, was purified in the Dharma, departed from wrong views and doubt, no longer revered other gods, no longer relied on others, departed from all hesitation, and was established in the fruit, attaining fearlessness in the realm of the World-Honored One. Rising from his seat, he prostrated at the feet of the World-Honored One: "World-Honored One! May I be permitted to study the path under the World-Honored One, to receive full ordination as a bhikṣu, and to practice the brahma conduct in the World-Honored One's presence?" "This bhikṣu shall practice the brahma conduct!" Thus Vīmaṃsaka studied the path under the World-Honored One and received full ordination as a bhikṣu. The Venerable Vīmaṃsaka, having studied the path and received full ordination, comprehended the Dharma and attained arhatship.

The Buddha spoke thus. Vīmaṃsaka, having heard what the World-Honored One taught, was delighted and joyful.
