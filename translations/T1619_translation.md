# 無相思塵論
## Treatise on Examining Sense-Objects Without Form

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1619

Composed by Dignāga (陳那). Translated from the Chinese. Chen dynasty, translated by Paramārtha (真諦).

---

> If one says paramāṇus are the fundamental cause,
> Since consciousness does not arise in their likeness, they are not the object,
> Like the faculties. Consciousness arises resembling aggregates,
> But does not arise from them. Aggregates have no substance,
> Like the second moon. By these two reasons,
> External objects are not sense-objects.
> Some say that paramāṇus aggregate to form all things,
> And consciousness arises resembling them, hence they are established as sense-objects.
> If the substance-characteristics of paramāṇus are truly existent,
> Since consciousness does not resemble them, they are not the object, like the sense-objects.
> If paramāṇus were sense-objects, consciousness would be indistinguishable;
> If one says their characteristics differ, consciousness would differ too.
> Different characteristics exist only in the conventional,
> So their substance is not real. The size of paramāṇus
> Has no difference across various things. If one removes paramāṇus,
> The myriad consciousnesses do not arise; therefore all things
> Are merely conventional designations. Internal sense-object forms,
> Appearing as if external, are established as consciousness-objects,
> Since consciousness appears to resemble them. This is the object-condition
> Of consciousness. Following arising, it is determined.
> Establishing capacity together causes successive arising.
> The two faculties arise together; the predominant capacity is the faculty.
> Without obstruction in consciousness, they are mutual causes.
> The predominant capacity is the sense-object; their mutual arising is beginningless.

The treatise states: If someone maintains that the six consciousnesses of the eye and so forth arise conditioned by external objects, such a person either discriminates paramāṇus as the object, because they are the cause of consciousness, or discriminates aggregates of paramāṇus as the object, because consciousness arises resembling aggregates. What is a sense-object? If consciousness can discern its substance-characteristics and arises according to those characteristics, then it is called a sense-object. Paramāṇus do not have this quality. If paramāṇus are truly the cause of consciousness, they are like the five faculties; therefore paramāṇus are not sense-objects. If so, then aggregates of paramāṇus should be the object, since consciousness arises resembling aggregates. Although this is so, and consciousness arises according to their characteristics, consciousness does not arise from them; therefore aggregates too are not sense-objects. Why? If a sense-object can generate consciousness arising in the likeness of its substance-characteristics, it can be accepted as a sense-object. Why? Because one can say that this sense-object serves as the productive condition for consciousness. But aggregates are not like this, since they are not truly existent. Just as when the eye-faculty is disturbed and consciousness arises resembling a second moon, the second moon is not the object of consciousness, since it has no real existence. Aggregates are also like this: apart from paramāṇus there is no real substance, so aggregates are not the object of consciousness. Therefore, by these two reasons, external sense-objects are not the objects of consciousness, since each individually lacks one of the two requirements.

Some teachers say: It is paramāṇus that aggregate to form all things, having many kinds of characteristics complete in them, and this is established as the object. Why? Because there is a distinct characteristic that can generate direct cognition, not merely the paramāṇu or the aggregate of paramāṇus. Therefore, within paramāṇus and their aggregates there is a characteristic that serves as the object for the six consciousnesses. The characteristic of the paramāṇu is not the sense-object, like solidity and such properties. Among paramāṇus there are the tangible qualities of solidity, moisture, heat, and motion. These things are truly existent, but they are not the objects of eye-consciousness, since eye-consciousness does not arise in their likeness. The characteristics of all things within paramāṇus are also like this. Why? If paramāṇus within all things were to generate consciousness, that consciousness would be indistinguishable, since paramāṇus within all things have no difference. If you say that consciousness differs because of different characteristics, and that pots, bowls, and other objects have different appearances, and that consciousness differs because it is conditioned by these characteristics, this reasoning is untenable. Why? Such different appearances exist only in conventionally designated objects like pots and bowls, not in the truly real paramāṇus. The size of the paramāṇu, namely its round and minute form, has no difference. Therefore the appearances of all things are not truly real but are merely conventional designations. What are conventional designations? They are objects like pots and bowls: if one removes the paramāṇus, consciousness resembling pots and the like does not arise. As for real objects, if one analyzes the constituent dharmas, the consciousness resembling real objects is not extinguished. As before the analysis, among pots the five sense-consciousnesses arise; after analysis, the five sense-consciousnesses are also not extinguished. Therefore the five sense-objects and the like are truly real. For this reason, paramāṇus, aggregates, and all things cannot generate consciousness, and therefore external sense-objects are not the objects of consciousness.

If so, what dharma is called the sense-object? Internal sense-object forms that appear as if external are called the sense-objects of consciousness. External sense-objects truly have no existence. Within internal consciousness, because sentient beings' confused minds discriminate, the six kinds of sense-object discrimination arise. This discrimination appears as if external, manifesting in this way. Among the four conditions, this is called the object-condition of consciousness, because it is the substance-characteristic of consciousness, and consciousness arises from it. Why is this? This consciousness creates internal sense-object forms and arises from internal sense-objects, fulfilling both requirements. Therefore internal sense-objects are called the object.

Question: It is also possible that sense-objects give rise to consciousness. But the internal sense-object is a part of consciousness, arising simultaneously with it. How can it serve as the object-condition?

Answer: In establishing the object-condition, consciousness arises conditioned by it, and there is no duality. As for conditions: sometimes things arise simultaneously and can establish one another. One thing arises from another, determinately following it, whether it arises or does not arise.

Question: If they arise successively, what are the characteristics of the perceived and the perceiver?

Answer: If the cause precedes and the effect follows, the effect follows the cause but the cause does not follow the effect. If the cause exists, the effect must exist; if the cause does not exist, the effect must not exist. The effect follows the cause, sometimes existing and sometimes not. This is called the characteristic of cause and effect. Furthermore, in order to establish the capacity for successive arising, the perceived and the perceiver are posited. The consciousness resembling sense-objects arises successively in order to produce the capacity for generating effects resembling sense-objects, continuing the stream of consciousness.

Question: If internal sense-objects are consciousness and the object-condition arises from conditions, how should the sūtra be explained? The sūtra states: "Depending on the faculty and conditioned by form, eye-consciousness arises," and so on as the sūtra extensively explains.

Answer: The capacity whose substance-characteristic can co-produce an effect is called a faculty.

Question: What is the substance and function of the faculty?

Answer: The predominant capacity is its substance. By what is this substance to be known inferentially? It is because it produces its own effect that this predominant capacity can be discerned. It is not the form of the four great elements. This capacity, within consciousness, has no obstruction. This capacity is within consciousness, yet apart from consciousness its substance cannot be indicated. As I have established the faculty and as you have established the faculty, both having capacity as substance, what difference is there? Such capacity and the appearance resembling sense-objects are mutual causes. Such capacity and the arising of sense-object appearances have been so since beginningless time. Based on capacity, the name "faculty" is established; conditioned by internal sense-object appearances, the name "object" is established. This confused consciousness cannot be described in its characteristics. The dharma of mutual causation in generating this also has no beginning. Why? Either because the capacity reaches maturation and consciousness resembling sense-objects arises, or because consciousness resembling sense-objects causes the capacity to mature. As for consciousness, it is either different from both, or not different from both, or cannot be stated. Since internal sense-objects fulfill both requirements, they can serve as the objects of consciousness.
