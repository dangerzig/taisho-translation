# Text for Stamping Sand Buddhas

## *Yìnshā Fó Wén* (印沙佛文)

Taishō 2842

Author unknown. A Dunhuang ritual text.

*Note: "Stamping sand buddhas" (*yìnshā fó*, 印沙佛) refers to the practice of pressing buddha-images into sand as a meritorious offering, popular in Dunhuang and throughout Tang-dynasty China.*

### Invocation for the Merit Assembly

I reverently invoke the pure dharma-body of the Lotus-Treasury World (*Liánhuā Zàng Jiè*, 蓮花藏界), the hundred billion tathāgatas, the buddhas of transformation numerous as the sands of the Ganges: the Great Saint Mañjuśrī (*Wénshū*, 文殊) atop Mount Qīngliáng (*Qīngliáng*, 清涼), the arhat who attained the way at Kukkuṭapāda (*Jīzú Yánshān*, 雞足嵓山, "Cock's Foot Mountain"), the esoteric scriptures of the nāga palace, and the subtle words of Vulture Peak (*Jiùlǐng*, 鷲嶺). With the divine eye, knowledge of others' minds, and all the worthy sages, I pray that you will exercise your spiritual powers, put your compassionate hearts into motion, descend to this sacred site, and bear witness to this merit.

Now a pure altar has been established in the eight directions, buddhas have been displayed at the four gates, a place for Avalokiteśvara (*Guānyīn*, 觀音) has been set up in the center, and the sangha chants the golden words. Fragrant food is offered to the buddhas of the three times. Pure food is scattered to the living creatures of water and land. The entire territory is reverently devoted; the whole city bows with earnest foreheads. For whom is this done? It is for the merit assembly to bring auspicious fortune and avert calamity on behalf of our Prefecture's Grand Protector.

I humbly submit that our Grand Protector has transmitted through generations a family of merit and distinction, corresponding to an age of five hundred years of worthies. Born of clear mountain and river spirits, he is a scholar for this half-millennium. In literary accomplishment surpassing the refined works of the Duke of Zhou and Confucius, his carving enters the wood to a depth of seven parts. Drawing a bow to pierce the willow, when his arrow flies, the apes cry and circle the trees. Governing the multitudes with generosity and breadth, ten thousand households marvel at his auspicious ways. Establishing order and bringing peace to the people, a thousand families celebrate as in the age of Yáo (*Yáo*, 堯).

Moreover, his jewel of faith is bright and pure, his mirror of the mind clearly illuminated. He relies on the Buddhist teaching to settle the eight directions and serves the sages to bring peace to the altars of state. He first displayed his earnest aspiration, inclining his heart toward Vulture Peak. Inviting buddhas and welcoming monks, he established a pure altar on the ninth ground. Six times daily, the sound of sūtra recitation moves the dawn throughout the whole city. Commoners and officials invoke heaven; the ringing of bells and chanting of Sanskrit fills the town.

At this time, the great sun shines northward and the frozen cold yields. The monks have completed their recitations in their circuits; the consecration ceremonies of devout sincerity have lasted five days. I bring together all these manifold wholesome deeds and immeasurable clouded causes, first dedicating them to adorn Brahmā (*Fàn*, 梵), Śakra (*Shì*, 釋), the Four Heavenly Kings, the nāga-devas, and the eightfold assembly.

I humbly pray: may your awesome radiance grow ever more brilliant, may your powers of merit increase, and may compassion be aroused to save people and protect the nation. May the present Sage Ruler forever sit upon the imperial throne. May the ten provinces compete in rushing their loyal hearts toward the nation. Furthermore, may this special merit be dedicated to adorn the honored position of our Grand Protector. I humbly pray: may your blessings be like the seas and mountains, forever welcoming auspicious fortune with the tiger tally. May your emoluments extend to the Yangtze and Huai, governing Hexi (*Héxī*, 河西) for generations. May the nation's great good fortune be like the sun and moon, growing ever more full. May the princess and consort forever maintain their radiant beauty in purity. May the military governors, presidents of ministries, and others firmly receive vows in unbroken succession. May all the young daughters receive longevity, chaste and luminous.

Then may the Yellow River run clear and the seas be calm, with no sounds of sorrow heard. May the four border regions submit, and may the fires of beacon and dust of war be forever extinguished. May the three calamities be fully quelled, and may the nine untimely deaths be exhausted at the ocean's corner. May plague be dispelled and disasters be eliminated. May famine be sent to the gates of the earth. May the dharma be scattered and bestowed upon the dharma-realm, universally reaching all sentient beings. By this supreme cause, may all ascend to the way of awakening. Mahāprajñāpāramitā.

### Text for the Sand-Stamping Ceremony

In the vast expanse of the great kalpa of worthies, there was a holy person. He emerged from the palace of the Śākya clan and was named Bhagavān (*Bóqiéfàn*, 薄伽梵). His mind crystallized in great quiescence; his body and mind were without limit. Through compassionate transformation of sentient beings, he was called "Buddha."

Now, before this seated assembly, in the early spring at the beginning of the fourth month, we stamp stūpas in the sand and initiate this prayer assembly. This is offered on behalf of our lord protector, as a merit assembly.

The lord's golden voice was manifest early on, and his jade reputation was heard long ago. He ranked among the distinguished positions and rose to the glory of the official cap. Through wondrous turning of wholesome sprouts cultivated in past lives, the fruits of karma are pure and the mind of the way is firm. On this day the four great elements are without obscurity, and the five aggregates are all emptily liberated. The true countenance of a thousand miles stamps the sand in countless impressions. Furthermore, he burns incense, assembles all the monks before this meritorious event, and scatters food throughout the realms of water and land.

With this merit of stamping buddhas, I transfer and dedicate it to the causes of merit. First it is dedicated to adorn Brahmā, Śakra, the Four Heavenly Kings, the nāga-devas, and the eightfold assembly. I humbly pray: may the awesome radiance grow ever more brilliant, may the powers of merit increase, to save people and protect the nation. May the sage's body enjoy long life, may the five grains ripen abundantly, may the people within the territory be at peace, and may songs of joy fill the city.

Furthermore, may this supreme merit be dedicated to adorn the donor's own person. I humbly pray: may your body be like a jade tree, eternally pure and bright. May your constitution be like vajra, constantly firm and solid. In this life and future lives, may wholesome affinities never be severed. In this world and the next, may the sprouts of goodness ever increase. Then may this be scattered and bestowed upon the dharma-realm, universally reaching all sentient beings. By this supreme cause, may all equally accomplish the fruit of buddhahood. Mahāprajñāpāramitā.
