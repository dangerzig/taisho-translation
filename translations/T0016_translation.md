Translated from the Chinese. 後漢 安世高譯

## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra on Śīgāla's Salutation of the Six Directions, Spoken by the Buddha

The Buddha was dwelling at Cock Mountain in Rājagṛha. At that time, there was a householder's son named Śīgāla who rose early, adorned his head, bathed, and put on patterned robes. He bowed four times toward the east, four times toward the south, four times toward the west, four times toward the north, four times toward heaven, and four times toward the earth. The Buddha, entering the city on his alms round, saw this from afar. He went to the young man's home and asked: "Why do you make these six-direction salutations? What principle does this accord with?"

Śīgāla said: "When my father was alive, he taught me to bow in the six directions. I do not know what it accords with. Now that my father has passed away, I dare not disregard his instructions."

The Buddha said: "Your father taught you to make the six-direction salutations, but not to bow merely with the body."

Śīgāla then knelt with one knee to the ground and said: "May the Buddha please explain to me the meaning of these six-direction salutations."

The Buddha said: "Listen well and take this to heart. A wise householder who can uphold the four precepts without transgression will be respected by others in this life and will be reborn in the heavens in the next. First, do not kill any living beings; second, do not steal; third, do not covet the wives and daughters of others; fourth, do not speak falsely or with a double tongue. As for the mind's desires of greed, lust, anger, and delusion — restrain yourself and do not give in to them. Those who cannot restrain these four mental states will have their ill repute spread day by day, like the light gradually dimming at the waning of the moon. Those who can restrain their evil mental states will be like the new moon gradually brightening in its light, until the fullness of the fifteenth day."

The Buddha said: "Furthermore, there are six things that cause one's wealth to dwindle daily. First, being fond of drinking wine; second, being fond of gambling; third, being fond of going to bed early and rising late; fourth, being fond of hosting guests and wanting others to host one in return; fifth, being fond of associating with bad companions; sixth, being arrogant and contemptuous of others. If one transgresses the four evils above and also practices these six things, they obstruct one's virtuous conduct, and one will be unable to attend even to making a living. With wealth dwindling daily, what benefit can the six-direction salutations bring?"

The Buddha said: "There are four kinds of bad companions. The first harbors enmity inwardly while outwardly feigning friendship. The second speaks pleasant words to one's face while speaking ill behind one's back. The third, in times of urgency, shows distress in one's presence while rejoicing behind one's back. The fourth appears intimate outwardly while inwardly harboring hostile schemes. There are also four kinds of good companions. The first appears like an enemy outwardly while harboring genuine goodwill inwardly. The second remonstrates directly to one's face and speaks well of the person to others. The third, when one is ill or in trouble with officials, exerts effort to resolve the difficulty. The fourth does not abandon the poor and lowly, but thinks of means to bring them to prosperity.

"There are again four kinds of bad companions. The first, when advised and instructed toward goodness, deliberately associates with evil people instead. The second, when instructed not to keep company with those who love wine, deliberately keeps company with drunkards. The third, when instructed to be self-restrained, becomes involved in still more affairs. The fourth, when instructed to befriend the virtuous, instead becomes intimate with gamblers.

"There are also four kinds of good companions. The first, seeing someone in poverty and sudden want, helps them establish a livelihood. The second does not quarrel or contend with others. The third goes daily to inquire after one's welfare. The fourth thinks of one whether sitting or rising.

"There are again four kinds of good companions. The first, when one is arrested by officials, takes one in and conceals one, then resolves the matter afterward. The second, when one is ill, takes one home to care for one. The third, when a friend dies, attends to the laying in of the coffin. The fourth, after a friend has died, continues to look after the household.

"There are yet four more kinds of good companions. The first, when one is about to fight, stops one. The second, when one is about to follow bad companions, remonstrates and stops one. The third, when one does not wish to tend to one's livelihood, encourages one to do so. The fourth, when one does not rejoice in the Dharma, teaches one to have faith and joy in it.

"There are again four kinds of bad companions. The first becomes greatly angered at small offenses. The second, when urgently needed and called upon, refuses to go. The third, upon seeing someone in urgent need, flees from them. The fourth, upon seeing someone die, abandons them without attending to them."

The Buddha said: "Choose the good and follow them; keep far from the evil. By associating with good companions, I myself attained Buddhahood."

The Buddha said: "As for bowing toward the east, this refers to children serving their parents; there are five things to be observed. First, one should think of providing for them. Second, one should rise early and give instructions to the servants to prepare food at the proper time. Third, one should not increase the worries of one's parents. Fourth, one should think of the kindness of one's parents. Fifth, when one's parents are ill, one should be alarmed and seek a physician to treat them. Parents, in looking after their children, also have five things to do. First, they should think of leading them away from evil and toward goodness. Second, they should teach them to calculate, write, and read. Third, they should teach them to uphold the precepts of the sūtras. Fourth, they should arrange their marriage in good time. Fifth, whatever is in the household, they should provide to them.

"As for bowing toward the south, this refers to disciples serving their teacher; there are five things to be observed. First, one should show them respectful deference. Second, one should think of their kindness. Third, one should follow whatever they teach. Fourth, one should think of them without weariness. Fifth, one should praise them in their absence. A teacher, in instructing disciples, also has five things to do. First, one should cause them to learn quickly. Second, one should cause them to surpass other disciples. Third, one should cause them to know without forgetting. Fourth, one should explain all doubts and difficulties to them. Fifth, one should desire that the disciple's wisdom surpass the teacher's.

"As for bowing toward the west, this refers to a wife serving her husband; there are five things to be observed. First, when the husband returns from outside, one should rise to welcome him. Second, when the husband is away, one should cook, sweep, and wait for him. Third, one should not harbor lustful thoughts toward other men; when cursed or scolded, one should not curse back or show anger. Fourth, one should heed the husband's instructions and guidance and not conceal any belongings. Fifth, one should wait until the husband has rested and covered himself before lying down. A husband, in looking after his wife, also has five things to do. First, whether going out or coming in, one should show respect to the wife. Second, one should provide food and drink and give clothing and bedding at proper times. Third, one should provide gold, silver, and jewels. Fourth, whatever is in the household, large or small, one should entirely entrust to her. Fifth, one should not illicitly keep concubines outside.

"As for bowing toward the north, this refers to how one regards one's relatives and friends; there are five things to be observed. First, when one sees them committing wrong or evil, one should privately go to a secluded place to remonstrate and admonish them. Second, when there is a small emergency, one should hasten to rescue and protect them. Third, when there is a private matter, one should not speak of it to others. Fourth, one should show mutual respect and deference. Fifth, whatever fine things one has, one should share them, however little.

"As for bowing toward the earth, this refers to how a master regards servants and workers; there are also five things to be observed. First, one should provide food and clothing at proper times. Second, when they are ill, one should call a physician to treat them. Third, one should not beat them arbitrarily. Fourth, one should not seize their private property. Fifth, whatever is distributed to them should be equitable. Servants and workers, in serving the master, also have five things to do. First, one should rise early and not wait for the master to call. Second, whatever tasks are to be done, one should attend to them with wholehearted care. Third, one should cherish the master's property and not waste it or give it away as alms. Fourth, when the master goes out or returns, one should see him off and welcome him. Fifth, one should praise the master's virtues and not speak of his faults.

"As for bowing toward heaven, this refers to how a person serves śramaṇas and men of the Way; there are five things to be observed. First, approach them with a wholesome mind. Second, choose good words when speaking with them. Third, show them bodily reverence. Fourth, cherish and long for them. Fifth, śramaṇas and men of the Way are heroes among humankind; one should respectfully attend upon them and ask about matters pertaining to transcendence of the world. Śramaṇas and men of the Way, in looking after laypeople, have six things to do. First, teach them to give generously and not be stingy or greedy themselves. Second, teach them to uphold the precepts and not transgress them. Third, teach them to practice forbearance and not give way to anger. Fourth, teach them to be diligent and not be lazy or negligent. Fifth, teach the people to unify the mind and not let the will run loose. Sixth, teach the people wisdom and not remain in their own ignorance. Śramaṇas and men of the Way teach people to abandon evil and do good, and reveal the right path to them; their kindness is greater than that of parents. To practice in this way is to understand the teaching of the six-direction salutations that your father gave during his lifetime. Why would you have cause to worry about not becoming prosperous?"

Śīgāla then received the five precepts, made obeisance, and departed.

The Buddha chanted a hymn:

> When the cock crows, rise early,
> Don your robes and descend from the bed,
> Rinse the mouth and wash until the mind is pure,
> Raise both hands in offering of flowers and incense.
>
> The Buddha is honored beyond all the gods,
> Ghosts and spirits cannot compare;
> Bow the head, circumambulate the stūpa and monastery,
> Join the palms in homage to the ten directions.
>
> The wise one who lacks diligence
> Is like a tree without roots;
> When the roots are cut the branches and leaves fall,
> When shall they ever be joined again?
>
> Plucked flowers left out in the sun,
> How many hours can they remain fresh?
> Letting the mind run loose and following its whims,
> When life has passed, what words remain?
>
> One should contemplate impermanence,
> For death may come with no fixed time;
> Transgressing without self-awareness,
> When life has passed, one has deceived oneself.
>
> Now one may enter niraya,
> When will there be a time of release?
> The wise one heeds the Buddha's words,
> Uphold the precepts with care and without doubt.
>
> The Buddha is like a beautiful flowering tree,
> There is none who does not love and delight in it;
> In every place people hear of him,
> All are filled with joy.
>
> When I attain Buddhahood,
> May I be as the Dharma King,
> Ferrying all beings beyond birth and death,
> None left without liberation.
>
> The virtue of the precepts is a worthy refuge,
> The fruits of merit always follow oneself;
> In the present Dharma one becomes a leader among people,
> And in the end is far from the three evil destinies.
>
> Vigilance in the precepts dispels fear,
> Merit and virtue are honored throughout the three realms;
> Harmful spirits, evil poisons, and injuries
> Do not befall those who uphold the precepts.
>
> Fallen into the mundane world, born into suffering,
> Life is swift as a flash of lightning;
> When old age, illness, and death arrive in their time,
> None can face them with might or strength.
>
> No kinsmen can serve as a refuge,
> No place where one may hide;
> Even the blessings of the gods eventually come to an end,
> How long can human life last?
>
> Parents and household dwell together
> Like guests lodging at an inn;
> When the lifespan allotted by past karma is exhausted,
> One abandons the old and must take on the new.
>
> Each pursues the karma of their own deeds,
> Boundless as the turning of a cartwheel;
> Arising and ceasing according to sin and merit,
> Birth and death through the twelve nidānas.
>
> In this present body, wander free from confusion,
> Bringing salvation and nurturing all people;
> With compassion for those fallen into manifold errors,
> Sinking and lost in the deep abyss.
>
> Strive onward with the six pāramitās,
> Through cultivation and practice, naturalness is attained;
> Therefore I bow my head in salutation,
> Taking refuge in the Deva-atideva.
>
> The human body is already hard to obtain,
> Having obtained it, one is again ensnared by desire;
> Lust and craving permeate consciousness and perception,
> Suffering and sensation are without end or satisfaction.
>
> In advance one plants seeds for future lives,
> Joyfully proceeding toward the hells;
> The six sense faculties are fortunately complete,
> Why bring shame and distress upon oneself?
>
> When all can correct their minds,
> The spirit of the three times will be auspicious;
> Not coveting the eight difficult states,
> Following one's deeds, born in the ten directions.
>
> In every birth, strive toward diligence,
> The six pāramitās serve as a bridge;
> Broadly encouraging limitless wisdom,
> All beings receive the divine light.
