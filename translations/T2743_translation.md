# Record of Miraculous Verifications and Merits from Reciting the Diamond Sūtra

## *Chísòng Jīngāng Jīng Língyàn Gōngdé Jì* (持誦金剛經靈驗功德記)

Taishō 2743

Author unknown. Colophon dated 908 CE (Tang Tianfu 8th year).

*Note: This is a collection of miracle tales (língyàn jì) attesting to the spiritual power of reciting the Diamond Sūtra (Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra, T235). The text contains approximately fifteen anecdotes, followed by a laudatory poem attributed to the Kaiyuan Emperor (Xuánzōng, r. 712-756), invocations of the Eight Diamond [Vajra] Protectors, and several dhāraṇīs. The miracle stories follow the standard pattern of Chinese Buddhist miracle-tale collections: a devout person recites the Diamond Sūtra and is saved from death, illness, demons, execution, or the punishments of the underworld. Several stories involve the protagonist dying, meeting King Yama (the lord of the dead), being asked what merit they have cultivated, declaring their recitation of the Diamond Sūtra, and being released back to life. The text reflects popular devotion to the Diamond Sūtra in Tang-dynasty China and the widespread belief that reciting this sūtra conferred protection, longevity, and karmic merit. The colophon identifies the copyist as Zhái Fèngdá, a commoner, who added the laudatory verses and merit records for distribution.*

---

## Miracle Tales

### 1. The Monk Yǎn of Zhāotí Monastery

In the Liáng dynasty, the monk Yǎn of Zhāotí Monastery first became a novice (*śrāmaṇera*). A physiognomist told Yǎn: "Master, though you are greatly intelligent and wise, your physiognomy indicates a short life. What can be done?" When Yǎn heard this, he consulted senior monks to determine what merit he should cultivate to extend his years. The senior monks said: "According to the Buddha's holy teaching, if one upholds and recites the Diamond Prajñā, the merit is the greatest, and one will certainly gain extended years." Yǎn obeyed and entered the mountains to uphold the Prajñā sūtra. After six years, he came out and met the physiognomist again, who said: "Dharma Master, what merit have you been cultivating? The marks of longevity are now remarkably present!" Yǎn explained his earlier prognosis of a short life, and how he had entered the mountains to uphold the Diamond Prajñā, practicing nothing else. The physiognomist exclaimed: "Inconceivable!" Through this merit, Yǎn became a great elder of the Dharma, living past one hundred years before passing away.

### 2. The Monk Zàng of Kāishàn Monastery

In the Liáng dynasty, the monk Zàng of Kāishàn Monastery was known for his Dharma lectures. A physiognomist named Yìn, son of Hé Yìn, who was skilled in prognostication, saw him and said: "Master Zàng, you need intelligence for lecturing. You should go to the sūtra treasury and randomly select one volume to ensure longevity." Zàng drew the Diamond Prajñā. He stayed in his room reciting it for three years without going out. When he later met Yìn, who said joyfully: "Master, what practice have you done to change your appearance? My prognostication has proven wrong!" Zàng replied: "Your divination was in fact correct. At that time, I narrowly escaped calamity precisely because of the power of upholding the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra." Yìn exclaimed: "Truly inconceivable are its merit and fruition!" He then prognosticated that Zàng would live over one hundred years, and this proved correct.

### 3. The Monk Who Subdued the Barbarian God

In the Suí dynasty, there was a monk named Zàng who could recite the Diamond Sūtra and use dhāraṇīs to eliminate all evils. A junior monk studied his mantra methods for several years, then went to the riverbank where there was a temple of a barbarian god (*húshén*). He sat in the temple overnight, reciting mantras, but was killed by the spirit. When Zàng heard that his disciple had died, he went in anger to the god's temple, sat, and recited mantras, but he too was killed. At that time, another monk from the same monastery, who constantly upheld the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra and practiced nothing else, heard that Zàng and his disciple had both been killed by the spirit. He went to the god's temple, sat, and recited the Prajñā sūtra. In the night, he heard a great rushing wind. Suddenly he saw a being of extraordinary and terrifying appearance, with eyes like lightning. The monk sat in right mindfulness, reciting the Diamond Sūtra without ceasing and without fear. The god came before him, withdrew all his fierce power, knelt on his right knee, and joined his palms in reverence, listening until the recitation was finished. The monk asked: "What deity are you? You came fiercely at first but then became still and unmoving." The god answered: "Your disciple is the barbarian god of this temple. My nature is fierce and violent. When I saw the master reciting a Mahāyāna sūtra of inconceivable [power], I prostrated and listened." The monk asked: "Since you are so respectful, why did you kill the two monks who recited mantras?" The god replied: "Those two monks did not recite Mahāyāna sūtras. When they saw me, they cursed and reviled me with evil mantra-words. I did not submit to them. They died of their own terror upon seeing my form; it was not that I intentionally killed them." The local people, learning the monk had survived the night in the spirit's temple, came at dawn and found him safe. When they heard the whole story, many of them took up the practice of reciting the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra.

### 4. Zhào Wénchāng Visits King Yama

In the eleventh year of the Kāihuáng era [591 CE], Zhào Wénchāng, Assistant Director of the Court of Imperial Supplies, died. His body remained warm, so his family did not immediately prepare the coffin. Wénchāng traveled to King Yama, who asked: "Since birth, what merit have you cultivated?" Wénchāng replied: "I have no other merit; I have only constantly recited the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra." The King joined his palms in reverence and praised him: "Excellent! The merit of upholding the Diamond Prajñā is the greatest and most inconceivable." He told his officers: "Investigate the records again. Do not wrongly summon this man." It was found that he had indeed been summoned by mistake. The King told Wénchāng: "Go to the sūtra treasury and bring back the Diamond Prajñā." An attendant led Wénchāng southwest to the treasury, where he saw dozens of great halls, supremely magnificent, filled with sūtras on golden rollers with jeweled covers, adorned and beautiful beyond description. Wénchāng closed his eyes in single-minded devotion, and a great elder handed him a sūtra. He feared it might not be the Prajñā, but when he opened it, it was indeed the Diamond Prajñā. He brought it to the King, and standing in the east while the officer stood in the west, he recited the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra once through without error. The King released him, admonishing him: "You must continue to uphold this sūtra and never neglect it."

As he was being escorted out, Wénchāng saw Emperor Wǔ of the Zhōu dynasty [r. 560-578] confined in a room east of the gate. Emperor Wǔ called out: "You are a person of my kingdom. Come here briefly; I wish to speak with you." Wénchāng bowed twice. Emperor Wǔ asked: "Do you know me?" Wénchāng replied: "Your subject once served Your Majesty as a palace guard." Emperor Wǔ said happily: "Then you are an old acquaintance. When you return home, please tell the present Emperor on my behalf that all my other offenses have been settled, but the matter of destroying the Buddha-Dharma remains unresolved. At that time, I was misled by the slander of Wèi Yuánsōng and have been imprisoned here for a long time, with no end in sight." Wénchāng asked about Wèi Yuánsōng, and Emperor Wǔ replied: "He is a being beyond the Three Realms, outside King Yama's jurisdiction, so they cannot apprehend him. When you return, please beg the present Emperor on my behalf to grant even a small amount of merit to rescue me from this suffering."

Wénchāng revived after five days of illness. He presented the full account to Emperor Wén of the Suí [r. 581-604], who thereupon issued an edict for monks throughout the realm to observe three days of fasting and practice on behalf of Emperor Wǔ of Zhōu, reciting the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra. This was also recorded in the official histories.

### 5. The Man from Suìzhōu

In the first year of the Zhēnguān era [627 CE], a man from Suìzhōu died and was revived after three days. He reported that when he first died, he was driven along with several companions to King Yama's court. Among them was a monk. The King summoned the monk first and asked: "What merit have you cultivated in your life?" The monk answered: "I only recited the Diamond Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra." The King immediately rose, joined his palms, and praised him: "Excellent! Since you have upheld the Diamond Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra, you should ascend to heaven. Why were you wrongly brought here?" Before the King had finished speaking, heavenly garments descended and guided the monk up to heaven.

The King then sat again and asked the man from Suìzhōu: "What merit have you cultivated since birth?" The man replied: "In my life, as for sūtras, I was fond of studying the literary works of Yǔ Xìn and various collected writings. Only recently have I begun to learn the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra, but I have not yet mastered it." The King said: "Great sinner!" He then showed the man a vision of suffering: a great tortoise with one body and many heads. Someone said: "This is Yǔ Xìn." After the tortoise departed, the King said: "Since this man has been studying the Diamond Prajñā, release him for now." Then the man saw a person who said: "I am Yǔ Xìn. During my life, I was fond of taking from various sūtras and using [their phrases] in my literary compositions. At times I slandered and defamed sūtra texts. Now I suffer this great retribution in the tortoise-form you just saw."

After reviving, the man told this story. All who heard were deeply grieved and recognized its truth. The people of Suìzhōu, who were largely transplanted settlers who made their living by hunting and taking life, upon learning of this, abandoned killing and together took up the practice of upholding the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra with faith and reverence.

### 6. Qí Yàntōng of Wèizhōu

Qí Yàntōng of Wèizhōu had recited the Diamond Prajñā his entire life. He had previously served under Lǐ Mì as magistrate of Wǔláo County. When bandits breached the city walls and sought to kill him, Yàntōng fled in terror and leaped from the east wall into a ravine, expecting to die. The ravine was over a hundred feet deep, yet as he fell through the air, it felt as though someone were supporting him, and he reached the bottom without realizing it, landing safely and sitting upright on a flat rock. After a long while, he stood up entirely uninjured. Yàntōng was astonished and filled with mixed joy and awe. From that time on, knowing the great power and inconceivable merit of the Diamond Prajñā, he redoubled his faith and diligent practice, never daring to be negligent, and always received boundless blessings.

### 7. The Monk Fǎzàng of Bǎoshì Monastery

The monk Fǎzàng of Bǎoshì Monastery in Fūzhōu was pure in his observance of the precepts and straightforward by nature. In the thirteenth year of the Kāihuáng era [593 CE], in Luòjiāo County, he built over twenty monks' quarters, three buddha-halls with brick and tile, a sixteen-foot unadorned image [of the Buddha], and many other works. He also had over eight hundred fascicles of the complete canon copied, using sandalwood rollers and fine decoration. In the second year of the Wǔdé era [619 CE], in the fourth month, Fǎzàng fell ill. After twenty days, he saw a person in green robes upon a high pavilion, holding a single fascicle and declaring: "Dharma Master Zàng, the merits you have cultivated in your life are all excellent. But there is a minor offense of misusing the Three Treasures' property that has not yet been expiated. What I hold in my hand is the Diamond Prajñā, the foremost great Mahāyāna sūtra. If you yourself copy one fascicle, all your offenses will be completely eradicated." Fǎzàng responded: "If my illness is cured, I resolve with firm determination to copy one hundred copies of the Prajñā." He reflected that although he had cultivated many merits in his life, he had never copied the Diamond Prajñā. Having now received this awakening from the buddhas and bodhisattvas, he would not be negligent. He entrusted all his possessions to his attendant with instructions to use them for copying the Diamond Prajñā. Within a few days, he recovered and fulfilled his vow, beginning with the first copy and distributing them to others for recitation.

### 8. The Monk Língjì and His Treacherous Disciples

In the Suí dynasty, there was a monk named Língjì who had two disciples. One day, the master called his disciples and said: "I have heard that on Mount Wǔtái, the great Mañjuśrī manifests his form to guide sentient beings. Let us three go there to pay our respects. Would that not be good?" They took two donkeys, one carrying two hundred bolts of raw silk, with the disciples driving one donkey and the monk riding the other.

This monk had practiced nothing in his life but reciting the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra. In his chest, he carried only this sūtra scroll. After traveling for several months, they stopped to rest in a marshy area. While the donkeys grazed, the monk sat under a tree, spread a felt mat, and fell asleep.

The two disciples conspired: "Let us kill the master. Each of us will take one hundred bolts of silk and one donkey, and go to the capital to enjoy ourselves. Would that not be a lifetime of pleasure?" Having resolved upon this, one disciple took a knife and slashed the master three times across the waist. Blood flowed. But at the fourth stroke, the knife stuck fast in the air and could not be pulled free, neither upward nor downward. It remained fixed for about the time it takes to eat two meals.

When the master awoke, he saw his disciple kneeling with both hands holding the knife, speechless. The monk asked: "Why have you formed such evil intentions?" The disciple knelt and confessed the entire plot. The master forgave him, then took out his sūtra scroll to read. At his waist were three knife-wounds, and the scroll was cut through to the roller. But when others examined the master's waist, they found only three red marks. Despite the cuts through several layers of clothing, the knife had not penetrated his flesh. Was this not the divine power of the Diamond Prajñā Sūtra?

### 9. The Monk Língyōu and the Missing Verse

Long ago, the monk Língyōu of Wēnguó Monastery in Cháng'ān suddenly died. After seven days, he met King Píngděng [King Yama]. The King asked: "What sūtra practice have you cultivated in your life?" Língyōu answered: "I recite the Diamond Sūtra." The King joined his palms and asked him to recite it. When he finished, the King asked: "Although you can recite this sūtra, there is one verse missing. Why?" Língyōu replied: "Your disciple has only recited according to the text he has; I do not know which verse is missing." The King said: "Your lifespan is already exhausted, but I will grant you ten more years of life. The complete true text of this sūtra is on a stone stele west of Háozhōu city. Have it transmitted throughout the realm." The monk revived and told the whole story.

### 10. Chén Zhāo of Hànzhōu

Chén Zhāo, a records clerk of Hànzhōu, died and was revived after two days. He reported that when he first arrived in the underworld, the judge asked: "In the matter of Magistrate Liú, you slaughtered forty-seven head of cattle. Why did you take one of his cattle?" Zhāo denied taking it. Shortly, a person appeared as a witness, and Zhāo knew he could not deny it. He asked the judge: "How can I be freed from this offense?" The judge asked: "What merit have you cultivated in your life?" Zhāo replied: "I have constantly recited the Diamond Sūtra." Before he finished speaking, the sūtra descended from the sky. He was immediately released and returned to life for eighteen more years.

### 11. The Scholar Gǒu and the Invisible Sūtra Altar

The layman Gǒu, devoted to good works, recited the Diamond Sūtra exclusively. He resolved to write the Diamond Sūtra in the air with his brush for the benefit of heaven, at a spot in a village northwest of Xīnfán County. Thereafter, whenever rain fell, that spot remained dry. At first, no one understood why. Villagers who grazed cattle there sheltered from the rain at that spot. Later, a foreign monk passing through told the villagers: "This is a sūtra platform. There is a sūtra in the sky above it. On fast-days, a jeweled canopy sometimes appears." This platform remains visible to this day, thirty miles northwest of the county seat.

### 12. Wáng Tuó

Long ago, Wáng Tuó had committed countless acts of killing throughout his life. He resolved to recite the Diamond Sūtra ten thousand times and had completed five thousand recitations. One day at noon, he suddenly saw several dozen ghosts approaching, saying: "King Yama has a summons for you. Stop reciting the sūtra!" Tuó continued to recite mindfully. The messenger-ghosts came from behind, and then another ghost-messenger arrived on horseback, announcing: "By the King's decree, release the sūtra-reciter!" Wáng Tuó was thereupon spared and lived to the age of ninety-eight.

### 13. Wáng Chuò

Long ago, Wáng Chuò served as a judicial officer in Tiānshuǐ Commandery. When Xuē Jǔ raised a rebellion, soldiers were being executed. When the executioner's blade reached Wáng Chuò, the sword broke into pieces. Another strong man, Sòng Luó, was ordered to behead him, but his blade also shattered. Xuē Jǔ was astonished and asked: "What art do you possess?" Wáng Chuò replied: "From youth I have recited the Diamond Sūtra. It must be the power of the sūtra." Xuē Jǔ released him. Chuò fled in terror and hid in a latrine, reciting the sūtra. Light appeared in the latrine. The rebels followed the light to the latrine and asked: "Who is there?" The person inside answered: "The one whom the blade could not kill: Wáng Chuò." The rebels said: "You need not hide. All the troops know of you. Go home at once." He was thus freed from this calamity.

### 14. Zhū Shìhéng

Long ago, Zhū Shìhéng was coarse and violent by nature and did not respect the Three Treasures. He served as Vice President of the Left in the Liáng state. His wife was devoted to good works and constantly recited the Diamond Sūtra. One day, when her husband was away, she was reciting the sūtra. Her husband returned unexpectedly, snatched the sūtra scroll from her hands, and threw it into the fire. When the fire died down, the sūtra was completely undamaged. Husband and wife together retrieved the scroll and repented before the Buddha.

### 15. Cuī Shànchōng

Long ago, Cuī Shànchōng served as a judicial officer in Xiézhōu. During a rebellion, the prefect was killed. Shànchōng led more than twenty men, fully armed, fleeing toward Kūnmíng city. At night, they lost their way. Shànchōng told his soldiers: "Recite the buddha-name with single-minded devotion." He himself recited the Diamond Sūtra. Trusting his horse to lead the way, a fire-torch suddenly appeared before them, guiding their path. After several miles, the flame went out and there was no more light, but they had already reached Kūnmíng city. This was entirely the power of the sūtra.

### 16. Táng Yàn

Long ago, Táng Yàn was a clerk from Qī County in Zǐzhōu. He constantly recited the Diamond Sūtra. On account of a transgression, he was pursued by county officers on the road to Suìzhōu. He heard a voice in the sky saying: "Táng Yàn, flee at once!" Looking up, he saw a foreign monk. Yàn hid behind a tree and recited the sūtra with utmost devotion. Dozens of pursuers passed directly in front of him without seeing him. He was thus freed from this calamity.

### 17. Wèi Xù

Wèi Xù was a man of Qín. He constantly recited the Diamond Sūtra. The underworld messengers came for him three times but could not take him. The King issued a decree through the messengers: "Release him for now."

### 18. Lǐ Yán

Lǐ Yán was a man of Nányáng Commandery, serving as county lieutenant in Dézhōu. He recited the Diamond Sūtra throughout his life. Whenever he recited the sūtra, a divine light appeared.

### 19. Lady Dòu

Long ago, Lady Dòu suffered a sudden headache in the night. She ordered her maid to fetch fire from the kitchen. The maid said there was none. Suddenly a torch appeared before the steps, ascended the stairway, and became bright as day. The lady's headache was immediately cured. All this was because she constantly recited the Diamond Sūtra. There are still more verifications from reciting the Diamond Sūtra, too numerous to record fully.

## Concluding Exhortation

In light of all these verifications: suppose a person were to fill the great trichiliocosm with the seven treasures and offer them in charity; this would not compare with the merit of transmitting this sūtra. If a person copies, upholds, reads, and recites the Diamond Sūtra, and also causes others to copy and distribute it, it is like one lamp lighting a hundred thousand lamps: all darkness is illuminated and the light never ceases. If one can copy this text and post it on a monastery wall, the merit is boundless and inconceivable.

## Praise of the Diamond Sūtra's Merit by the Kaiyuan Emperor

> The single volume of the Diamond [Sūtra] outweighs Mount Sumeru;
> Therefore our Emperor especially upholds it.
> The eighty-four thousand dharma-gates are all penetrated;
> With the wisdom-eye, other minds are known beyond measure.
> The songs of recent days are old tunes;
> Listen instead to the words of the Diamond Prajñā.
> The Kaiyuan Emperor himself annotated it;
> With utmost devotion, prostrate, and harbor no doubt.
>
> This sūtra can remove all suffering;
> With an aspiring mind and the divine eye, one foreknows all.
> Do not be led by the six thieves of ignorance,
> Day after day in darkness clinging to delusion.
> The World-Honored One entered nirvāṇa immeasurable kalpas ago,
> Past a hundred billion *asaṃkhyeya* kalpas.
> Kings and great ministers transmitted the holy teaching;
> Our Emperor's reverent faith is rare in the world.
>
> On the ten monthly fast-days, he forbids slaughter;
> He broadly cultivates wholesome karma and ordains monks and nuns.
> Those born from wombs and eggs should diligently recite the buddha-name;
> With courageous diligence and great compassion.
> He is weary of seeing the five desires of the palace;
> He knows that bodily form is perilous and unreal.
> A sovereign of the realm is thus awakened;
> How much more should common folk reflect!
>
> In the past, King Dīpaṃkara renounced his consorts
> For the sake of the wondrous Dharma.
> Through arduous practice and the Mahāyāna teaching,
> He served as a slave. What manner of service was this!
> Now our wise Emperor surpasses ten thousand kingdoms;
> His every thought is foreknown through insight.
> His civil and martial sagely authority pervades all under heaven;
> The myriad peoples are at peace and the four frontiers are secure.
>
> He personally annotated the Diamond's profound wondrous meaning;
> All creatures with sentience uphold it.
> Dharma-protecting spirits faithfully respond;
> The devas praise it without measure.
> White elephants hasten to Jìngshòu Monastery;
> Jeweled chariots and banners form layered circles.
> Illustrious monks, bearing incense and flowers, lead the way;
> Immortals ride cranes, filling the sky.
>
> Those in the eight difficulties are reborn in the Land of Ultimate Bliss;
> The five turbidities are transformed into pools of the seven treasures.
> The Kaiyuan reign will endure for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges;
> Māra-kings and heretics all surrender and take refuge.
> Through ten thousand years and a thousand autumns, the holy teaching is transmitted,
> Like a kalpa that never frays the celestial robe.
> Simply because sentient beings have great merit,
> They encounter all buddhas in this age of supreme opportunity.
>
> The wondrous principle of the Diamond is truly hard to expound;
> Among all sūtras, I rank it supreme.
> The Buddha spread gold to cover the entire ground,
> To purchase Prince Jeta's garden.
> The eight classes of spirits attended the Buddha;
> The Goose Pagoda and Dragon Palaces spread the teaching through heaven.
> The Jeta trees extend their branches for bird-song;
> Below, golden sands wash the feet at springs.
>
> After meals, he took his bowl in the city of Śrāvastī,
> Broadly guiding sentient beings to create fields of merit.
> At that time, the World-Honored One was without self-marks;
> Subhūti looked up and received his words.
> The four fruits and six supernatural powers are the highest grade;
> The Dragon Palace's delights belong to the heavenly rebirth.
> The wheel-turning sage-king dwells in the immortal station;
> Our divine-martial Emperor is likewise.
>
> He also told of King Kaliṅga in past lives,
> Whose body was cut limb by limb, and who attained heaven.
> King Śibi sacrificed his body to save a dove;
> The arhat's body surpassed the trichiliocosm.
> Sentient beings of Jambudvīpa cling to the burning house;
> Our Emperor leads them out and sends them to heaven.
> All beings with feeling are like his own children;
> Whatever the hundred clans' sorrows, he pities them all.
>
> Having attained the fruit of *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi*,
> He has formed karmic connections with all buddhas.
> Through a hundred kalpas and a thousand lives, never retrogressing:
> Merit without limit and without boundary.
> Not only in temples near and far,
> But in all worlds of the ten directions, it is the same.
> All this is the profound wondrous meaning of the Diamond;
> Your disciple would never dare transmit falsehood.

## Invocation of the Eight Vajra Protectors

Before reciting the Diamond Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra, one should first invoke the names of the Eight Great Vajra [Protectors], arouse the utmost sincere mind, and then recite the sūtra. These Eight Vajra naturally come to constantly protect those who uphold the sūtra.

1. **Blue Disaster-Removing Vajra** (*Qīng Chúzāi Jīngāng*): Can eliminate all sentient beings' accumulated calamities and misfortunes, causing them to be completely eradicated.

2. **Poison-Averting Vajra** (*Pì Dú Jīngāng*): Can remove all sentient beings' febrile and toxic illnesses and suffering.

3. **Yellow Wish-Fulfilling Vajra** (*Huáng Suíqiú Jīngāng*): Can cause all sentient beings' wishes and desires to be fulfilled.

4. **White Pure-Water Vajra** (*Bái Jìngshuǐ Jīngāng*): Can remove all sentient beings' feverish afflictions and suffering, causing them to be completely eradicated.

5. **Red Sound Vajra** (*Chì Shēng Jīngāng*): Can illuminate all sentient beings with light, enabling them to see the Buddha.

6. **Disaster-Quelling Vajra** (*Dìng Zāichú Jīngāng*): Can remove all sentient beings' suffering of the three calamities and eight difficulties.

7. **Purple Worthy Vajra** (*Zǐ Xián Jīngāng*): Can cause all sentient beings' minds to open in awakened understanding and to arouse the aspiration for bodhi.

8. **Great Divine Vajra** (*Dà Shén Jīngāng*): Can cause all sentient beings' wisdom-teeth to mature and their powers of discernment to be fully furnished.

## Dhāraṇīs

**Great Body Dhāraṇī** (*Dàshēn Zhēnyán*):

> Namo bhagavate prajñāpāramitāye. Oṃ īriṭi īśrī śurodhā viśaya viśaya svāhā.

**Heart-Following Dhāraṇī** (*Suíxīn Zhēnyán*):

> Namo bhagavate prajñāpāramitāye. Tadyathā: Oṃ hū vajra vāri svāhā.

**Heart-Within-Heart Dhāraṇī** (*Xīn Zhōng Xīn Zhēnyán*):

> Oṃ ūrṇi śa svāhā.

**Vajra Child Dhāraṇī** (*Jīngāng Ér Zhòu*):

> Namo vajrakumāraka nidū aṃ svāhā.

**Buddha-Mother Dhāraṇī** (*Fómǔ Zhòu*):

> Namo daśa, namo daku. Oṃ huru huru siddhalocanī sārasāra thasādhanī svāhā.

**Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva's Heart Dhāraṇī** (*Wénshū Púsà Xīn Zhōng Zhēnyán*):

> Arapacana.

If a person recites this dhāraṇī with utmost devotion, it is as though one has recited the entire canon once.

## Colophon

In the eighth year of Tiānfù of the Táng [908 CE], the year *wùchén*, fourth month, ninth day, the commoner Zhái Fèngdá copied this text. He added this record of sūtra praise, verifications, and merit for distribution, for the benefit of the faithful, the spirits of the departed, living parents, and all in the community. May their merits grow like spring grass, their offenses wither like autumn seedlings, and in the future may all together arrive at the assembly of the Buddha.
