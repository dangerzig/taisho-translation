# 佛治身經
## Sūtra on the Buddha's Governance of the Body

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 795

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

The Buddha said: "One should study. Are you skilled in speech? The śramaṇa's practice according to the Dharma is to dwell alone in one place, maintaining only right thought. One place, one resting place, one mode of conduct; is this not so? Being centered and correct, one purpose is to illuminate the body. Receiving the teachings also requires dwelling alone.

Even so, one person can overcome a thousand; in an army of a thousand one can be victorious. One person who can overcome the self, this is true victory. Victory in battle, conquering the conqueror, this is not equal to the remaining, higher victory. Conquering one's own body is always the true victory.

Without strength one also cannot conquer. Even Māra and Brahmā cannot overcome the self of such a person. They cannot turn such a victory into defeat. The bhikṣu's true victory is then no ordinary conquest.

First, establish the correct teaching for one's own person; afterward, teach others, being toward all as I am. If, from the outset, one's own person follows the Dharma in practice, then teaching others is easy in this way. Being thus in one's own person and teaching others accordingly, having already attained the meaning of bodily instruction, it is no longer difficult to teach others.

If you wish to teach others, first teach yourself. Turn this so that self-teaching becomes your own standard; from bodily teaching one gains wisdom.

For the sake of oneself and others, many people will also not transgress. When there is nothing extreme about oneself, one's self-consideration is also without excess.

Taking refuge in oneself: there is nothing that surpasses bodily self-refuge; yet self-refuge is not equal to taking refuge in truth.

When bodily instruction achieves wisdom, when taking refuge achieves wisdom, when the Dharma achieves wisdom, when the precepts achieve wisdom; among one's brothers in the Dharma, one no longer worries, wisdom having been achieved. Attaining happiness, one achieves wisdom; no longer worrying about oneself, taking refuge in one's own body also attains refuge for others. When there is nothing extreme about one's own person, all are taught to be liberated from suffering."

A question was asked: "What is skilled speech?"

The teacher said: "Expounding the sūtras of the thirty-seven [factors of awakening] is skilled speech; whatever is spoken without confusing people's minds is also skilled speech."

"What is practice according to the Dharma?"

"Not departing from the two hundred fifty precepts; this is practice according to the Dharma."

"What is 'one purpose for illuminating the body'?"

"Knowing and discerning the affairs within the body."

"What is 'one person in an army being able to be victorious'?"

"It means a person cannot overcome evil [through military conquest alone]."

"What is 'one person being able to overcome the body'?"

"When the mind conceives of evil thoughts, being able to restrain and cut them off by oneself; this is overcoming the body."

"What is 'teaching of body and mind'?"

"The body receives the seven precepts; the mind receives the three precepts."

"What is 'many people also not transgressing'?"

"With an equanimous mind, causing people not to fall into the fetters."

---

**Translator's note:** The passage on overcoming a thousand in battle closely parallels Dhammapada 103–105, where it is likewise said that neither a god, gandharva, nor Māra together with Brahmā can turn the victory of one who has conquered the self into defeat. The name 摩汎藍 is a phonetic transcription, here reconstructed as "Māra and Brahmā" on the basis of this parallel. The "thirty-seven factors of awakening" (三十七品) refers to the bodhipakṣa-dharma: the four foundations of mindfulness, four right efforts, four bases of spiritual power, five spiritual faculties, five spiritual powers, seven factors of awakening, and the noble eightfold path. The "seven precepts" received by the body and "three precepts" received by the mind correspond to the seven wholesome actions of body and speech and the three wholesome actions of mind within the standard ten wholesome deeds (daśa-kuśala-karmapatha).
