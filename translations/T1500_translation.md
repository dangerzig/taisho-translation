# T1500 菩薩戒本

# The Bodhisattva Precept Text

*Note: This text, attributed to Maitreya Bodhisattva (Císhì 慈氏) and translated by Dharmakṣema (Tánwúchèn 曇無讖) during the Northern Liang dynasty, presents the bodhisattva precepts in the format of a prātimokṣa recitation ceremony. It enumerates four pārājika (grave defeats) and numerous duṣkṛta (minor offenses) governing the conduct of bodhisattvas. The four grave offenses are: (1) praising oneself and disparaging others for gain, (2) miserliness with wealth and dharma, (3) striking others in anger and refusing to accept their repentance, and (4) slandering the Bodhisattva-piṭaka and teaching semblance-dharma. The duṣkṛta offenses cover a wide range of conduct: daily worship, contentment, respect for elders, accepting invitations and gifts, teaching the dharma, disciplining wrongdoers, the relationship between bodhisattva and śrāvaka precepts, right livelihood, proper attitude toward nirvāṇa, guarding one's reputation, appropriate severity, retaliation, apology and forgiveness, resentment, attendants, sleep, idle talk, accepting instruction, the five hindrances, attachment to meditation-bliss, the relationship between Mahāyāna and śrāvaka teachings, slander of the profound dharma, flattery, attending dharma-lectures, assisting others, caring for the sick, admonishing wrongdoers, repaying kindness, relieving distress, giving food and clothing, teaching disciples, compliance with others, praising merit, and disciplinary measures. The text represents an early Chinese version of the Yogācārabhūmi bodhisattva-śīla tradition.*

---

> Homage to Vairocana,
> The vajra buddhas of the ten directions;
> Homage also to the former treatise-master,
> The awakened lord Maitreya.
> Now I shall teach the threefold aggregate of precepts;
> Let all bodhisattvas listen together.
> Precepts are like a great bright lamp
> That can dispel the darkness of the long night.
> Precepts are like a true jeweled mirror,
> Reflecting all dharmas without omission.
> Precepts are like a wish-fulfilling gem (maṇi),
> Raining down provisions to relieve the destitute.
> To leave the world and swiftly attain buddhahood,
> Only this dharma is supreme!
> Therefore all bodhisattvas
> Should diligently guard and uphold them.

## The Four Pārājika Offenses

"Great beings! These four pārājika offenses are declared in accordance with the bodhisattva mātṛkā.

"If a bodhisattva, out of greed for gain, praises his own virtues and disparages others: this is the first pārājika offense.

"If a bodhisattva, though personally possessing wealth, out of an innately miserly nature, when destitute beings with no one to rely on come seeking help, does not arouse a compassionate mind and give what is sought; or when someone wishes to hear the Dharma, grudgingly withholds the teaching: this is the second pārājika offense.

"If a bodhisattva, in anger, utters harsh and abusive words, and still not at peace in mind, strikes with the hand, or adds staves and stones, causing harm and terror, with anger and hatred increasing; and when the offender seeks to repent, does not accept the confession, holding a grudge and refusing to let go: this is the third pārājika offense.

"If a bodhisattva slanders the Bodhisattva-piṭaka, teaches semblance-dharma, vigorously establishes semblance-dharma, and either understands it in his own mind or receives it from another: this is the fourth pārājika offense.

"Great beings! The four pārājika offenses have been declared. If a bodhisattva, through intensified afflictions, commits even one of these offenses, the bodhisattva precepts are lost and should be received again.

"I now ask the great beings: Are you pure in this matter?

"Great beings! You are pure in this matter, as shown by your silence. Thus should it be upheld!

## The Duṣkṛta Offenses

"Great beings! These numerous duṣkṛta offenses of the bodhisattva are declared in accordance with the bodhisattva mātṛkā.

### 1. Daily Worship

"If a bodhisattva dwelling in the discipline-precepts, throughout a single day and night, fails to make at least some small offering to the Buddha (if present), or to a buddha's stūpa or shrine, or to the Dharma, or to sūtra-scrolls, or to the Bodhisattva-sūtra-piṭaka, or to the Bodhisattva-mātṛkā-piṭaka, or to the bhikṣu-saṃgha, or to the great bodhisattva-assembly of the ten directions; if one does not make even the least offering, not even a single prostration; if one does not praise the merit of the Three Jewels with even a single verse; if one cannot produce even a single moment of pure mind: this is an offense, a multiple offense. If through disrespect, laziness, or negligence, it is a defiled offense. If through forgetfulness or error, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: for a bodhisattva who has entered the pure-mind stage, like a bhikṣu who has attained the unshakeable purity, who constantly makes dharma-offerings to the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṃgha jewels.

### 2. Contentment

"If a bodhisattva has many desires, does not know contentment, and is attached to material wealth: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one arouses the wish and makes effort to cut off such desires, applying counter-measures, but due to the force of strong afflictions, they repeatedly arise.

### 3. Respect for Elders

"If a bodhisattva, seeing an elder of virtue and a fellow practitioner worthy of respect, out of pride or resentment does not arise in respect, does not yield the seat, does not respond to greetings or requests for teaching: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness, negligence, inattentiveness, or forgetfulness, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if seriously ill, mentally disturbed, or asleep thinking one is awake. Not responding to greetings or requests for teaching is not an offense if: the elder is teaching the Dharma or conducting a definitive discussion; or one is teaching the Dharma oneself; or one is listening to the Dharma; or one is conducting a definitive discussion; or one is in a dharma-lecture assembly or definitive-discussion assembly, where not bowing is permissible; or to protect the speaker's mind; or as a skillful means to help the other correct unwholesome conduct and cultivate wholesome dharmas; or to uphold the saṃgha regulations; or to protect the minds of many people.

### 4. Accepting Invitations

"If a bodhisattva, when a patron comes to invite one, whether to one's own dwelling, to a monastery, or to another household, offering robes, food, and various requisites, out of anger or pride does not accept or does not go: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if ill, without strength, or mad; if the place is distant or the road is frightening and dangerous; if one knows that not accepting will help the other correct their conduct, abandoning evil and abiding in good; if one has already accepted another invitation; if one is cultivating wholesome dharmas and does not wish to interrupt even briefly, for the sake of hearing a previously unheard dharma, a beneficial teaching, or a definitive discussion; if one knows the inviter intends to deceive or trouble one; if to protect many people from resentment; or to uphold the saṃgha regulations.

### 5. Accepting Offerings

"If a bodhisattva has a patron who offers gold, silver, pearls, maṇi-gems, lapis lazuli, and various precious objects, and the bodhisattva out of anger or pride refuses and does not accept: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense, because it abandons sentient beings. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if mad; if one knows that accepting will produce attachment; if one knows the donor will feel regret; if one knows the donor will become confused; if one knows the donor will become impoverished and distressed; if one knows the objects belong to the Three Jewels; if one knows the objects were obtained through theft; if one knows that accepting will bring much suffering, such as killing, imprisonment, punishment, fines, confiscation of wealth, or reprimand.

### 6. Teaching the Dharma

"If a bodhisattva, when sentient beings come to hear the Dharma, out of anger, resentment, or jealousy does not teach: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if non-Buddhists are seeking faults; if seriously ill or mad; if one knows that not teaching will help the other reform; if one has not yet mastered the dharma being studied; if one knows the questioner will be disrespectful, the deportment being improper; if the person is of dull faculties and, hearing the profound dharma, will give rise to fear; if one knows that after hearing, wrong views will increase; if one knows that after hearing, the person will slander and regress; if one knows that after hearing, the person will relay it to evil people.

### 7. Not Abandoning Wrongdoers

"If a bodhisattva, toward fierce and evil beings who violate the precepts, out of resentment, either personally abandons them or prevents others from helping them, not teaching or transforming them: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness, negligence, or forgetting to prevent others, it is a non-defiled offense. Why? Because a bodhisattva's compassion toward evil people should be deeper than toward good people. Not an offense: if mad; if one knows that not speaking will help the other reform, as previously stated; if to protect others' minds; or to uphold the saṃgha regulations.

### 8. Bodhisattva and Śrāvaka Precepts

"If a bodhisattva, regarding the Tathāgata's prātimokṣa and the vinaya's establishment of prohibitive offenses to protect sentient beings, to cause the faithless to have faith and those with faith to increase: one should study these in common with the śrāvakas. Why? If even śrāvakas, who seek only their own liberation, do not neglect protecting others, causing the faithless to have faith and the faithful to increase through studying the precepts, how much more so the bodhisattva, who aims at the supreme liberation?

"Furthermore, regarding prohibitive offenses where the stipulations involve little gain, little action, and little effort, and which the World-Honored One established for śrāvakas: the bodhisattva does not study these in common. Why? Śrāvakas liberate only themselves and abandon others; they should abide in little gain, little action, and little effort. But the bodhisattva, liberating both self and others, should not abide in little gain, little action, and little effort.

"Therefore, for the sake of sentient beings, a bodhisattva may seek hundreds and thousands of robes from unrelated brāhmaṇas and householders, and may accept whatever is freely given, observing whether the donor can afford it and accepting accordingly. As with robes, so with almsbowls. As with robes and bowls, so with personally soliciting thread and having unrelated weavers weave. For the sake of sentient beings, one should accumulate kauśeya bedding and sitting-mats, even hundreds and thousands. Even gold and silver in hundreds and thousands should be accepted.

"In all such matters, the prohibitive offenses of the śrāvakas involving little gain, little action, and little effort are not studied in common by the bodhisattva. In the bodhisattva's discipline-precepts, if one abides in little gain, little action, and little effort out of resentment toward sentient beings: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence one abides in little gain, little action, and little effort, it is a non-defiled offense.

### 9. Wrong Livelihood

"If a bodhisattva engages in deception of body and speech, whether through display, through disparagement, or through using gain to seek gain, dwelling in wrong livelihood without shame, unable to abandon it: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one arouses the wish and makes effort to cut it off, but due to the force of strong afflictions, it repeatedly arises.

### 10. Restlessness

"If a bodhisattva is restless, does not delight in stillness, making noise and joking loudly to please others, acting in this way: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through forgetfulness or error, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if one arouses the wish and makes effort to cut it off, as previously stated. Also not an offense: if another is resentful and one wishes to make them stop; if another is sorrowful and one wishes to bring them relief; if the other's nature delights in play and one wishes to attract and then transform them, wishing to cut off their attachment, or to look after them; if the other suspects the bodhisattva of resentment and is turning away, and one shows a cheerful and playful manner to reveal one's pure mind.

### 11. Attitude Toward Nirvāṇa

"If a bodhisattva holds such a view and speaks thus: 'Bodhisattvas should not delight in nirvāṇa, should turn their backs on nirvāṇa, should not fear afflictions, should not be averse to them in any way. Why? Because bodhisattvas should endure birth and death for three incalculable kalpas, seeking great bodhi.' One who speaks thus commits an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Why? The śrāvakas' deep delight in nirvāṇa and their fear and aversion toward afflictions does not equal one hundred-thousandth of the bodhisattva's deep delight in nirvāṇa and fear and aversion toward afflictions. Śrāvakas seek only their own benefit, but the bodhisattva is not so: the bodhisattva acts universally for all sentient beings. The bodhisattva cultivates the non-defiled mind, surpassing the arhat, and achieves the conditioned state free from all afflictions.

### 12. Guarding One's Reputation

"If a bodhisattva does not guard against words of disbelief, does not guard against reproach, and does not remove these: if one actually has faults and does not remove them, this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If one actually has no faults but does not remove the accusation, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if non-Buddhists are slandering, or other evil people; if one is a renunciant begging for alms and cultivating wholesome causes that produce others' reproach; if the accuser is angry or mad and therefore reproaches.

### 13. Appropriate Severity

"If a bodhisattva observes that a sentient being should be benefited through harsh and stern words as a skillful means, but out of fear of causing distress does not do so: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if one observes that in the present there would be little benefit but much distress.

### 14. No Retaliation

"If a bodhisattva responds to abuse with abuse, to anger with anger, to blows with blows, to disparagement with disparagement: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense.

### 15. Apologizing

"If a bodhisattva has wronged another person, or even without having wronged them, causes the other to have doubts, and should immediately apologize, but out of resentment or contempt does not apologize properly: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if as a skillful means to help the other reform; if the other wishes the bodhisattva to perform an impure act before accepting the apology; if one knows the other's nature is quarrelsome and that apologizing would increase their anger; if one knows the other is forgiving and tolerant, without resentment, and fears causing them embarrassment.

### 16. Accepting Repentance

"If a bodhisattva, when another comes to confess properly, out of resentment and wishing to trouble them, does not accept the confession: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If without resentment one is simply by nature unwilling to accept confessions, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if as a skillful means to help the other reform, as previously stated; if the other does not confess properly and the mind is not at ease.

### 17. Holding Grudges

"If a bodhisattva gives rise to resentment toward another and clings to it without letting go: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one arouses the wish and makes effort to cut it off, as previously stated.

### 18. Keeping Attendants for Gain

"If a bodhisattva, out of craving for service, maintains attendants and followers: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one maintains them without a mind of craving.

### 19. Excessive Sleep

"If a bodhisattva is lazy and negligent, indulging in sleep at improper times or without knowing proper measure: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if ill, without strength, or fatigued from long travel; or if one arouses the wish and makes effort to cut it off, as previously stated.

### 20. Idle Talk

"If a bodhisattva, with a defiled mind, discusses worldly affairs at length: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through forgetfulness one talks at length, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if one sees others gathered in conversation and briefly listens to protect their feelings; or if one briefly answers another's question about something previously unheard.

### 21. Refusing Instruction

"If a bodhisattva, seeking concentration, out of resentment or pride does not accept the teacher's instruction: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if ill or without strength; if one knows the person gives distorted instruction; if one is well-learned and capable; if one has already received the teaching.

### 22. The Five Hindrances

"If a bodhisattva gives rise to the mind of the five hindrances and does not dispel it: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one arouses the wish and makes effort to cut it off, as previously stated.

### 23. Attachment to Meditation-Bliss

"If a bodhisattva regards the taste of meditation as a virtue: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one arouses the wish and makes effort to cut it off, as previously stated.

### 24. Disdaining Śrāvaka Teachings

"If a bodhisattva holds such a view and speaks thus: 'Bodhisattvas should not listen to the sūtras and dharmas of the śrāvakas, should not receive them, should not study them. What use are the śrāvaka dharmas to a bodhisattva?' This is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Why? Because bodhisattvas even listen to the treatises of non-Buddhists, how much more the words of the Buddha! Not an offense: if one specializes in studying the Bodhisattva-piṭaka and has not yet been able to encompass everything.

### 25. Neglecting the Bodhisattva-piṭaka

"If a bodhisattva, regarding the Bodhisattva-piṭaka, does not make effort but abandons and neglects it, studying only the śrāvaka sūtras: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a non-defiled offense.

### 26. Studying Non-Buddhist Texts

"If a bodhisattva abandons what the Buddha has taught and instead studies non-Buddhist treatises and worldly scriptures: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one is of superior intelligence and can learn quickly; if through long study one does not forget; if one understands the meaning through reflection; if one has thoroughly examined the Buddha-Dharma and obtained unshakeable wisdom; if daily one devotes two parts to studying the Buddha's scriptures and one part to outside texts. Such a bodhisattva, skilled in worldly scriptures and non-Buddhist treatises but attached to them and not treating them as poison: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense.

### 27. Slandering the Profound Dharma

"If a bodhisattva, hearing the profound meaning and true meaning of the Bodhisattva Dharma treasury, and the immeasurable spiritual powers of all buddhas and bodhisattvas, slanders and does not accept them, saying: 'These are not beneficial, these are not taught by the Tathāgata, and they cannot bring peace and happiness to sentient beings': this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Whether one slanders through one's own distorted thinking or by following others. Such a bodhisattva, hearing the most profound meaning, does not give rise to the mind of understanding. Such a bodhisattva should arouse the mind of faith, free from deception, and think: 'I am greatly at fault, blind and without the eye of wisdom. The Tathāgata's wisdom-eye teaches accordingly. The Tathāgata has further things to say. How can I give rise to slander?' Such a bodhisattva places himself in the position of not-knowing. Thus the Tathāgata's directly known and seen dharma, rightly contemplated and rightly directed, is not violated; it is not slander from lack of understanding.

### 28. Self-Praise and Disparagement

"If a bodhisattva, out of craving or anger, praises his own virtues and disparages others: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one disparages non-Buddhists and praises the Buddha-Dharma; if as a skillful means to help the other reform, as previously stated. Also not an offense: if to cause the faithless to have faith and the faithful to increase.

### 29. Not Attending Dharma Lectures

"If a bodhisattva, when there is a dharma-lecture or definitive-discussion, out of pride or resentment does not go to listen: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if one does not understand; if ill or without strength; if the person gives distorted teaching; if to protect the speaker's mind; if one has heard it repeatedly, already received and upheld it, and already knows the meaning; if one is well-learned, retains what is heard, and practices what one has heard; if one is cultivating meditation and does not wish to interrupt even briefly; if one is of dull faculties, slow to comprehend, receive, and retain: in all these cases, not going is not an offense.

### 30. Disrespecting Dharma Teachers

"If a bodhisattva disparages a dharma-teacher, does not show respect, laughs at and ridicules them, attending only to the words and not relying on the true meaning: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense.

### 31. Not Assisting Others

"If a bodhisattva, dwelling in the discipline-precepts, sees sentient beings engaged in activities and, out of resentment, does not cooperate, whether in deliberating matters, traveling, lawful enterprise, farming, herding cattle, settling disputes, auspicious gatherings, or meritorious deeds: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if ill or without strength; if the other can manage alone; if the other already has many companions; if the activity is unlawful or unrighteous; if as a skillful means to help the other reform; if one has already promised another; if the other has enemies; if one is cultivating wholesome deeds and does not wish to interrupt; if one is of dull nature; if to protect many people's feelings; if to uphold the saṃgha regulations.

### 32. Not Visiting the Sick

"If a bodhisattva sees a person weak and ill and, out of resentment, does not go to care for them: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if oneself ill or without strength; if one instructs a capable person to tend to the patient; if one knows the person has family members or is capable of managing on their own; if the illness recurs frequently or is chronic; if one is cultivating a superior practice and does not wish to interrupt; if one is of dull nature, slow to comprehend, receive, and retain, and hard to sustain in such circumstances; if one is already caring for another sick person. As with illness, so with poverty and hardship.

### 33. Admonishing Wrongdoers

"If a bodhisattva sees sentient beings creating evil karma for this life and future lives and, out of resentment, does not speak rightly to them: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. Not an offense: if one lacks wisdom or strength; if one sends a capable person to speak; if the other is capable and has good spiritual friends; if as a skillful means to help the other reform; if speaking rightly would cause the other to become hateful, utter evil words, receive the teaching in a distorted way, show no reverence, or if the person's nature is wicked and obstinate.

### 34. Repaying Kindness

"If a bodhisattva has received another's kindness and, out of resentment, does not reciprocate equally or more: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if one makes effort but lacks the means; if as a skillful means to help the other reform; if one wishes to repay but the other does not accept.

### 35. Relieving Distress

"If a bodhisattva sees sentient beings suffering from the loss of kin or possessions and, out of resentment, does not help resolve their distress and alleviate their sorrow: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: as stated previously in the section on not assisting others.

### 36. Giving Food and Clothing

"If a bodhisattva, when someone seeks food or clothing, out of resentment does not give: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if one has nothing; if what is sought is unlawful; if the item would not benefit the person; if as a skillful means to help the other reform; if the person has violated the king's law and one wishes to protect the king's intentions; if to uphold the saṃgha regulations.

### 37. Teaching Disciples

"If a bodhisattva takes in disciples and, out of resentment, does not teach and instruct them properly, and does not at the proper time seek robes, food, bedding, medicine, and lodging from brāhmaṇas and householders to provide for them: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness, negligence, or heedlessness, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if as a skillful means to help them reform; if to uphold the saṃgha regulations; if ill or without strength; if one sends a capable person; if the disciple is capable and well-known with much virtue and can seek requisites on their own; if the disciple has already received instruction and knows the dharma; if a non-Buddhist has stolen the teaching and cannot be disciplined.

### 38. Compliance with Others

"If a bodhisattva, out of resentment, does not comply with another: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness or negligence, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if the other wishes to do something unlawful; if ill or without strength; if to uphold the saṃgha regulations; if the other's action, though lawful, could cause many to commit unlawful acts; if to subdue non-Buddhists; if as a skillful means to help the other reform.

### 39. Praising Merit

"If a bodhisattva knows another sentient being has real merit and, out of resentment, does not speak of it to others or praise it; and when others praise the person, does not say 'Well done!': this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness, negligence, or heedlessness, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if one knows the other is of few desires and wishes to protect their feelings; if ill or without strength; if as a skillful means to help the other reform; if to uphold the saṃgha regulations; if telling would cause the person to develop afflictions, excessive joy, pride, or unrighteous conduct, and one wishes to prevent these problems; if the real virtue appears to be non-virtue; if what was well-spoken appears not to be well-spoken; if to crush the wrong views of non-Buddhists; if waiting for the person to finish speaking.

### 40. Disciplinary Measures

"If a bodhisattva sees sentient beings who should be reprimanded, rebuked, or punished, and out of a defiled mind does not reprimand; or reprimands but does not rebuke; or rebukes but does not punish: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a defiled offense. If through laziness, negligence, or heedlessness, it is a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if the person is incorrigible, cannot be spoken to, or is difficult to instruct, and much resentment would arise; if one observes the timing; if one fears that the action would cause disputes, antagonism, or litigation; if the saṃgha would quarrel or split; if the person is not deceitful, has a sense of shame, and will gradually reform on their own.

### 41. Using Spiritual Powers

"If a bodhisattva has achieved various spiritual powers and should use them to terrify those who need terrifying, and to attract those who need attracting, wishing to cause sentient beings to make good use of their faith-offerings; but does not use spiritual powers to terrify or attract: this is an offense, a multiple offense, a non-defiled offense. Not an offense: if the sentient beings would develop further attachment; if non-Buddhists would slander the sages; if they hold accomplished wrong views. If they would become mad or suffer increased pain, these are all not an offense.

---

"Great beings! The numerous duṣkṛta offenses have been declared. If a bodhisattva commits any one of these offenses, one should make a duṣkṛta confession. If one does not confess, it obstructs the bodhisattva precepts.

"I now ask the great beings: Are you pure in this matter?

"Great beings! You are pure in this matter, as shown by your silence. Thus should it be upheld!

## Conclusion

"Great beings! I have declared the bodhisattva's four pārājika offenses and the numerous duṣkṛta offenses. These are declared by the World-Honored Maitreya in accordance with the mātṛkā: the discipline-precepts, the precepts for gathering wholesome dharmas, and the precepts for gathering sentient beings. All these precept-dharmas can give rise to the bodhisattva's practice and can perfect the bodhisattva's path.

"Great beings! Those who wish to arouse the mind seeking anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi should skillfully guard and uphold these precepts. If one guards and upholds them, one will not give rise to the thought that the Semblance Dharma and the Dharma's extinction have come. One can cause the Semblance Dharma's true meaning to blaze forth. One can cause the True Dharma to never be extinguished. The mind attains right abiding. One perfects the Buddha-Dharma in oneself. One teaches and transforms sentient beings without ever growing weary. The wholesome karma is brought to completion, and one swiftly accomplishes the Buddha's path!"

This completes the Bodhisattva Precept Text, spoken by Maitreya Bodhisattva, translated by Dharmakṣema of the Northern Liang.
