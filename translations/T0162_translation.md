# 金色王經
## Sūtra of King Suvarṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0162

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Wei dynasty, translated by Gautama Prajñāruci (瞿曇般若流支)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. At that time, the World-Honored One had many bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, kings, ministers and officials, various non-Buddhist practitioners, śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, parivrājakas, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas, who attended, made offerings, revered, honored, and provided for his needs. The World-Honored One thus received abundant pure offerings of clothing, food, bedding, medicine for the sick, and all manner of things used by devas and humans. Yet the Buddha, the World-Honored One, was neither attached nor stained, just as a lotus flower dwells in water. His supreme and excellent reputation was heard throughout the world, and all praised him.

At that time, the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Perfectly Enlightened One, the one endowed with knowledge and conduct, the Sugata, the knower of the world, the unsurpassed one, the trainer of people, the teacher of devas and humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One, among all the worlds of devas, humans, Māra, Brahmā, śramaṇas, and brāhmaṇas, knowing what was timely and appropriate, taught the Dharma accordingly. The Dharma he taught was good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, good in meaning and good in words, wholly complete, pure, and bright, the practice of the holy life.

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus: "Bhikṣus! If sentient beings could know the fruit and reward of generosity as I know the fruit and reward of generosity, then at mealtimes, whether with the first morsel or the last morsel, they would not eat without first setting aside a portion to give. Free from the stain of jealousy, they would be able to give. Bhikṣus! If sentient beings do not know the fruit and reward of generosity as I know the fruit and reward of generosity, then such beings, whether with the first morsel or the last morsel, will not set aside a portion to give to others, but will eat it themselves. Having the stain of jealousy, they are unable to give. Why is this? Bhikṣus! In the past there was a king named Suvarṇa. He was exceptionally handsome, endowed with all the marks, and possessed the most supreme and wonderful body. King Suvarṇa was exceedingly wealthy and prosperous, possessing great treasure: manifold goods, things for use, coins, grain, pearls and genuine pearls, conch shells, agate, mother-of-pearl, and coral. He had abundant gold and silver, rich raw gold, many elephants and horses, many herds of cattle, many herds of mares, filling every stable. King Suvarṇa's capital was named the City Rich in Gold. The king dwelt therein. The city was twelve yojanas from east to west and seven yojanas from north to south. It was filled to overflowing with people, without any empty space, and was peaceful, prosperous, and joyful. Fifty-seven hundred million villages and settlements were filled with people, peaceful, prosperous, and joyful. Sixty thousand mountains and rivers all had great cities, each with its own lord, filled with people, peaceful, prosperous, and joyful. King Suvarṇa had a great retinue of ministers, eighteen thousand in number. The women of his inner palace numbered twenty thousand. King Suvarṇa was well versed in the laws of kingship and governed in accordance with the Dharma. In that land, he was a righteous king. King Suvarṇa was able to give away everything he possessed, withholding nothing, even down to his own flesh. The lifespan of the people at that time was eighty-four thousand years.

"At another time, King Suvarṇa was in a secluded place, silently reflecting. This thought arose in his mind: 'I shall impose no duties on any merchants. I shall levy no taxes on any of the people.' When King Suvarṇa had finished this reflection, he summoned his ministers, his inner and outer officials, and all the hundred officers, and issued this command: 'From this day forward, all the people and all the merchants shall be exempt from taxes and levies. Throughout all of Jambudvīpa, all the people shall be released from their taxes and duties.' King Suvarṇa, by this expedient, governed the country in accordance with the Dharma for many years.

"At another time, an evil star appeared, portending that for twelve years heaven would not send rain. There was a brāhmaṇa skilled in the knowledge of signs and well versed in the texts of mantras, who understood the courses of Venus and the other stars. Having seen the evil star and read the signs, he went to King Suvarṇa. When he arrived, he fully reported to the king, saying: 'May the king know that an evil star has appeared. It is an ill omen for the king. For twelve years heaven will not send rain.'

"When King Suvarṇa heard these words, he wept and cried out in lamentation: 'Alas for the people of Jambudvīpa! Alas for the people of Jambudvīpa! How is it that this land of Jambudvīpa, once peaceful, prosperous, and teeming with people, shall in not long a time become empty and desolate, without any people?'

"Then King Suvarṇa, after weeping for a short while, reflected thus: 'The wealthy, who have ample provisions and abundant grain, can survive twelve years without dying. But those who are poor, with very little wealth and insufficient grain, how can they stay alive? How can they survive twelve years?' After this reflection, King Suvarṇa thought further and gave rise to this intention: 'I shall now collect all the grain in Jambudvīpa and store it in one place: from all the outlying dwellings, all the villages, all the cities, all the inhabited places, and all the royal territories, all grain shall be brought here. I shall measure and know the total amount and build a single granary. Then I shall count the number of all the people of Jambudvīpa and, calculated over twelve years, distribute the food equally.'

"King Suvarṇa, having formed this intention, summoned his ministers, inner and outer officials, all the hundred officers, and all the guardians of the borders and checkpoints. When all had assembled, he issued this command: 'Go throughout Jambudvīpa and collect all the grain. Measure and know the total amount.' From all the outlying dwellings, all the villages, all the cities, all the inhabited places, and all the royal territories of Jambudvīpa, the ministers heard King Suvarṇa's command and collected all the grain throughout Jambudvīpa, measuring and knowing the total amount. All the grain from every outlying dwelling, village, city, inhabited place, and royal territory in Jambudvīpa was brought together and stored in a single granary. Then they went to King Suvarṇa and said: 'May the king know that all the grain in Jambudvīpa, from all the outlying dwellings, villages, cities, inhabited places, and royal territories, has been collected and measured. All the grain from every outlying dwelling, village, city, inhabited place, and royal territory has been brought together and stored in a single granary. May the king decide as he sees fit.'

"King Suvarṇa then summoned the accountants and scribes of Jambudvīpa and issued this command: 'Go quickly and count all the people of Jambudvīpa. Know the total number. Beginning with myself, distribute the food equally to all the people of Jambudvīpa. Give everyone a reduced ration.'

"The accountants and scribes, having heard King Suvarṇa's command, went out at once. They counted all the people throughout Jambudvīpa and knew the total number. The scribes prepared the written records and respectfully submitted them to the king.

"King Suvarṇa, beginning with himself, distributed the food equally to all the people of Jambudvīpa. Everyone received a reduced ration. In this way, they survived through eleven years without dying.

"At the end of eleven years, after one month had passed, here and there many men and women, both male and female, gradually began to suffer from hunger. Why was this? Because the grain was nearly exhausted. There were still eleven months remaining, and here and there many men and women, both male and female, were dying of hunger and thirst.

"At that time, all the grain in Jambudvīpa was completely exhausted. Every granary was empty. There remained only five measures of cooked rice, enough for one person's food for one day, for King Suvarṇa's single meal.

"At that time, there was a man who for the past forty kalpas had practiced the bodhisattva path. Having arrived in this Sahā world, he saw in a certain forest two beings, a mother and son, copulating together. When the bodhisattva saw this, he sighed: 'Such beings! Extremely vile afflictions dwell in their hearts. Having drunk her milk, they do such things. Where else could there be such evil behavior? I now have no use for such beings. I have no use for such lawless beings, defiled with unlawful desires, covered by wrong views and evil craving, who do not recognize father and mother, do not know śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas, do not protect their lineage, do not respect their elders, and do not care for their kin. I now have no use for benefiting such extremely vile beings. I shall now abandon the practice of enlightenment and seek only my own benefit.'

"When the bodhisattva had given rise to this thought, he went to another place, to the base of a different tree. Having arrived, he sat down at the base of that tree, crossed his legs, straightened his body, and settled his mindfulness. The bodhisattva then, with regard to the five aggregates of clinging, whether arising or ceasing, observed them in sequence: 'This is the arising of form; this is the ceasing of form. Likewise, this feeling, this perception, these formations, this consciousness arises; this consciousness ceases.' As the bodhisattva thus observed the five aggregates of clinging in sequence, having perceived their cessation, before long all that was subject to arising was completely extinguished. Having thus understood, by this cause and condition, he immediately attained the enlightenment of a pratyekabuddha. Having attained enlightenment, he spoke this verse:

> "From love arises suffering;
> Therefore one should abandon love.
> One should delight in solitude,
> Like the single horn of a rhinoceros."

"The pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones, then reflected: 'For the sake of benefiting sentient beings, I practiced many austerities, yet not a single being received my benefit. Today, out of compassion for sentient beings and wishing to benefit them, from whom shall I accept food?' The pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones, having attained the pure divine eye surpassing that of humans, surveyed all of Jambudvīpa. He saw that all the food in Jambudvīpa had been exhausted. Only King Suvarṇa's single meal of five measures of rice remained. Having observed this, he gave rise to this thought: 'Out of compassion for King Suvarṇa and wishing to benefit him, I shall now go and receive King Suvarṇa's one meal of food.' The pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones, then flew through the sky by his supernatural powers, making his body visible for all to see. Like a śakunī bird in flight, he headed toward King Suvarṇa's City Rich in Gold.

"At that time, King Suvarṇa was standing on a high tower. The five thousand ministers all saw the pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones. Among the ministers, one saw from afar the pratyekabuddha approaching from the distance. Seeing this, he said to the other ministers: 'All of you look! Look! In the distance, there is a red-winged śakunī bird coming this way.' A second minister looked and said: 'Look carefully! That is not a red-winged śakunī bird. That is a rākṣasa, a power-eating evil ghost, coming here to eat our strength. It comes now to devour us.' The ministers then pointed this out to King Suvarṇa. King Suvarṇa wiped his face with both hands and then looked carefully. Having looked carefully, he said to the ministers: 'Ministers, you should know that it is not a red-winged śakunī bird, nor is it a rākṣasa or a power-eating evil ghost. It is a sage who, out of compassion for us, comes here.'

"The pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones, arrived in an instant at the tower where King Suvarṇa was staying. King Suvarṇa saw the pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones, and immediately rose to welcome him, bowed his head to his feet, and then prepared a fine seat and invited him to sit. When the pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones, had taken his seat, King Suvarṇa said to him: 'May I ask, O sage, why have you come here?' He replied: 'Great king! I have come here for food.' When King Suvarṇa heard these words, he immediately wept and through his tears said: 'How is it that I am now so destitute? I had obtained the wealth and mastery of all Jambudvīpa, and yet today I cannot provide a single sage with a single good meal.' At that time, in that place, there was a celestial maiden who dwelt in the City Rich in Gold. She spoke this verse to King Suvarṇa:

> "What is suffering? It is poverty.
> What suffering is heaviest? The suffering of poverty.
> The suffering of death and the suffering of poverty,
> These two sufferings are equal, with no difference.
> Better to endure the suffering of death
> Than to live in poverty."

"When King Suvarṇa heard this, he summoned his kitchen steward and asked: 'Is there any rice? I wish to make an offering to this great sage.' The steward replied: 'May the king know that all the grain in Jambudvīpa has been completely exhausted. Only the king's one remaining meal is left.' King Suvarṇa then reflected: 'If I eat it myself, my life will be preserved for a short time. If I do not eat, my life will quickly end.' After this reflection, he thought again: 'Even if I eat it myself, I cannot escape death. If I do not eat, death will come just the same. I shall not choose this brief survival. This great sage is pure in his precepts and practices the good Dharma. Now that he has come to my home, how can I let him depart without food, with his bowl empty?' King Suvarṇa, having reflected thus, addressed his ministers, inner and outer officials, all the hundred officers, and his retinue: 'All of you should rejoice! This is the final act of generosity of King Suvarṇa. By this root of merit, may all the people of Jambudvīpa, from this day forward and in all future lives, be forever free from poverty.' When King Suvarṇa had made this vow, he took the single meal of rice and placed it in the bowl held by the pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones. Having placed it, he set it in the right palm of the pratyekabuddha.

"The pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones, as is the way of all such beings, teaches the Dharma by bodily demonstration, not by spoken words. Having received the food offered by King Suvarṇa, the pratyekabuddha flew away through the sky by his supernatural powers.

"King Suvarṇa and all the great assembly joined their palms and gazed intently without blinking, until he passed beyond the range of their sight. King Suvarṇa then addressed his ministers, inner and outer officials, all the hundred officers, the gatekeepers, and his retinue: 'All of you, go. Return each to your own home, and there await death from hunger and thirst.' The ministers and retinue all said: 'In times of royal joy, we all followed the king, enjoying amusements and pleasures together. How can we now abandon the king and depart?' When King Suvarṇa heard these words, he wept and wiped his tears, and said to his ministers, inner and outer officials, all the hundred officers, and retinue: 'All of you, go. Return each to your own home. Do not let anyone die of hunger and thirst within my palace.' At that time, the ministers, inner and outer officials, all the hundred officers, and the retinue all wept and wiped their tears. They came forward together toward King Suvarṇa. Having reached the king, they bowed their heads at the feet of King Suvarṇa. They all joined their palms and said to King Suvarṇa: 'Whatever offenses, great or small, we have committed, may the great king forgive us. We behold the king this morning for the last time.'

"During this time, the pratyekabuddha, the World-Honored One of the Solitary Awakened Ones, having received the offered food, went to another place to eat it. As he ate, from all four directions, four great masses of clouds arose. A cool wind blew, purifying the entire land of Jambudvīpa. When the cool wind had blown and the land of Jambudvīpa was purified, in the latter half of the day, heaven rained down various kinds of food, both hard food and soft food, of this sort: rice, parched grain, and cooked beans. Such soft foods rained down, and such hard foods as cakes, roots, stems, leaves, flowers, fruits, and sesame. Such hard food. And also oil, ghee, and fried confections. Such hard food. And rice-flour cakes. Such hard food. Various kinds of food rained down thus. When King Suvarṇa saw this, his heart was filled with great joy, boundless delight, and good thoughts arose. He said to his ministers, inner and outer officials, all the hundred officers, and retinue: 'All of you, look! Look! Such was the reward from a single gift of food this morning. Beyond this, there are immeasurable further rewards that will surely come in the future.' Thus it continued through that day. From the second day through the seventh day, there rained down various kinds of grain: sesame, soybeans, small beans, barley, wheat, cowpeas, garden peas, rice, millet, and other grains. After the seven days of grain, for seven days it rained ghee, for seven days it rained oil, for seven days it rained coins, for seven days it rained fine cloth, and then there were various kinds of mixed rain. For a further seven days, it rained only the seven treasures: gold, silver, lapis lazuli, crystal, red pearls, agate, and musāragalva.

"Bhikṣus! You should know that because of King Suvarṇa's gift of food, poverty was forever eradicated for all the people of Jambudvīpa.

"Bhikṣus, what do you think? The King Suvarṇa of the past: could he have been someone else? Do not think so. Why? Bhikṣus! That King Suvarṇa of the past is my present self. Bhikṣus! This teaching is thus: you should understand it well. Sentient beings who know the fruit of generosity and the reward of giving, as I know the fruit and reward of giving, whether with the first morsel or the last morsel, will not eat without first setting aside a portion to give. Free from the stain of jealousy, they are able to give. Sentient beings who do not know the fruit of generosity and the reward of giving as I know the fruit and reward of giving, whether with the first morsel or the last morsel, will not set aside a portion to give to others, but will eat it themselves. Having the stain of jealousy, they are unable to give." At that time, the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Good and evil done in the past:
> The karma of sin and merit is not lost.
> Those who draw near to the wise:
> The karma of coming and going is not lost.
> Good words spoken among the noble assembly:
> The karma of speech is not lost.
> Those who know gratitude and repay it:
> The karma of their deeds is not lost.
> Good deeds make one beautiful;
> Evil deeds make one base.
> Both kinds of karma bear their reward:
> This is certain, and the fruit is truly obtained."

When the World-Honored One had spoken these words, the bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, and all the great assembly, having heard what the Buddha taught, were all greatly joyful.

## Translator's Colophon for the Sūtra of King Suvarṇa

The past lives of Śākyamuni Tathāgata were innumerable. In one lifetime, he was King Suvarṇa. Through the cause and condition of the practice of giving, he attained Buddhahood. In teaching the gate of generosity, he cited this as evidence, and thus this sūtra is named "King Suvarṇa." The Wei Minister of Works and Yitong, Lord Gao, whose mind of generosity rivals that of King Suvarṇa of old, opened this gate to reveal it universally to all. In the upper chambers of his magnificent residence, he brought forth this wondrous text. The śramaṇa Tanlin and Gautama Prajñāruci translated it in the fourth year of Xinghe (542 CE), in the year renxu, in the ninth month, on the first day yiwei, beginning on the guichou day and completing it on the yimao day. Three thousand five hundred and fourteen characters.
