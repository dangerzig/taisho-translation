# Record of the Tiāntái Transmission of the Buddha's Mind-Seal

## *Tiāntái Chuán Fó Xīnyìn Jì* (天台傳佛心印記)

Taishō 1938

Composed by Huáizé (*Huáizé*, 懷則) of the Yuan dynasty.

*Note: This is a doctrinal treatise by the Yuan-dynasty Tiāntái monk Huáizé (dates unknown), arguing that the distinctive teaching of the Tiāntái school, the doctrine of "inherent evil" (xìng'è), constitutes the authentic "mind-seal" (xīnyìn) transmitted from the Buddha through Mahākāśyapa and down to the Tiāntái patriarchs. The text makes a bold claim: what truly distinguishes Tiāntái from all other Buddhist schools, including Chan, is the teaching that both good and evil are inherently complete in the nature of mind. Huáizé argues that other schools, including those that claim "direct pointing at the mind" and "seeing the nature to become a buddha," ultimately fall into the "separate teaching" (biéjiào) because they merely point to the pure true mind, requiring the elimination of the nine dharma-realms of deluded beings to reveal the buddha-realm of pure goodness. By contrast, Tiāntái's "perfect teaching" (yuánjiào) holds that the evil of the nine realms is itself the nature of evil, identical with the buddha-realm, requiring no destruction whatsoever. The text traces the Tiāntái lineage from Nāgārjuna through Huìwén, Nányuè Huìsī, Zhìyǐ (Tiāntái), Zhāng'ān, Zuǒxī, Jīngxī Zhànrán, and Sìmíng Zhīlǐ. It is one of the most forceful statements of Tiāntái polemics against Chan Buddhism.*

---

The single word "inherent" (*jù*) fully reveals the purport of our school. That the nature inherently possesses good, other masters also know. But that it inherently possesses evil in its conditions and subsidiary [causes], none of them can fathom. Thus, the distinctive contribution of our school's teaching of inherent nature lies precisely in "inherent evil" (*xìng'è*). Were there no inherent evil, one would necessarily have to destroy the cultivated evil of the nine [dharma-]realms to reveal the inherent good of the buddha-realm. This would be "severing the nine by contemplating principle," which is not what we discuss here.

Therefore, the ten vehicles of wondrous contemplation (*shíchéng miàoguān*) elucidated in the *Mohe Zhǐguān* (Great Calming and Contemplation) contemplate the ten objects such as the aggregates, the three obstructions, and the four māras, and each and every one of them is completed as the perfectly wondrous three truths. This is the essential framework for arousing the aspiration and establishing practice. How could there be anything in the perfect and sudden [teaching] that surpasses this?

When one first begins contemplation, one must start with the inner mind. Therefore, among the three categories [aggregates, sense-bases, elements], one sets aside the elements and sense-bases. Among the five aggregates, one further excludes the first four and specifically takes the consciousness-aggregate as the object of contemplation. Like going from a ten-foot measure to a one-foot measure, and from a foot to an inch: this is the "totality of ignorance mind." If specified within this totality, it is the sixth consciousness. Like felling a tree by getting at the root, or cauterizing an illness by finding the vital point: a thousand branches and a hundred diseases will naturally perish.

If this does not succeed, one then passes through the remaining [objects]. One mind serves as the model for the remaining aggregates, sense-bases, and elements, and so on through the nine objects, which are contemplated only when they arise; if they do not arise, they are not contemplated. All without exception are like this. This is precisely how the cultivated evil of the three paths of the nine realms is itself the dharma-gate of inherent evil. Inherent evil being perfectly interpenetrating, there is no dharma it does not tend toward. It naturally encompasses the inherent good of the buddha-realm.

Since cultivated evil is itself inherent evil, there is no cultivated evil to be destroyed, and no inherent evil to be revealed. The full identity of evil with evil is precisely the meaning of "identity" (*jí*). Thus, our school's understanding of "identity" permanently differs from that of all other masters. It is not two things being combined, nor opposites being reversed. One must directly recognize the complete identity of the thing itself: only then is it called "identity." Why would one need to sever afflictions and birth-and-death in order to reveal the buddha-realm of bodhi and nirvāṇa?

### Inherent Good and Evil in the Great Vehicle Scriptures

One should further understand that this inherent good and evil is named differently in various Mahāyāna [texts], with broader or narrower formulations.

As for different names: The *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (*Huáyán Jīng*) says: "Being able to follow defiled and pure conditions, the ten dharma-realms are thereby distinguished." In delusion, all ten realms are defiled; in awakening, all ten realms are pure. Reading "ten dharma-realms" in three different combinations, the three causes are fully present...

The *Lotus Sūtra* (*Fǎhuá Jīng*) says: "The true characteristic of all dharmas" does not go beyond the provisional and the real. "All dharmas" is the good and evil of conditions and subsidiary [causes] within the co-substantial provisional. "True characteristic" is the good and evil of the direct cause within the co-substantial real...

The *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* says: The icchantika and the good person both possess inherent good and inherent evil, which are called the good and evil of conditions and causes.

### The Three Thousand [Dharmas] in a Single Thought-Moment

In brief, the ten realms; in detail, the three thousand. Thus, good and evil do not go beyond the ten realms. The ten realms, being perfectly fused in their nature, mutually possess one another, making one hundred realms. Each realm having ten suchnesses makes one thousand suchnesses. One thousand for provisional names, one thousand for the five aggregates, one thousand for lands: these three thousand are fully present in the single present thought-moment of cultivated evil. They are originally complete, not produced through fabrication, not arising through mutual generation, not contained through mutual inclusion. A single thought-moment does not precede them; the three thousand do not follow. A single thought-moment is not less; the three thousand are not more.

One must know that feelings may be destroyed, but dharmas cannot be destroyed. Yet if one clings to dharmas and this becomes an illness, that too must be destroyed. Thus, good and evil, pure and defiled, are the principle-body of the dharma-gate. The body is originally bright and pure; not a fiber is severed. Therefore, severance and realization, delusion and awakening, are discussed only in terms of defilement and purity.

### Distinguishing Tiāntái from Other Schools

Since cultivated evil is itself inherent evil, the three contemplations and ten vehicles have no delusion to destroy and no principle to reveal. Only then is this called "actionless wondrous practice" (*wúzuò miàoxíng*). As for the fruition-stage, universally manifesting form-bodies, descending into the nine realms, and roaming through the six destinies: being entirely raised from inherent evil, this is called "responding without deliberation." If, however, one were to reverse evil into good and sever evil to realize good, then the practice accomplished in the causal stage would be a "contrived action," and the supernatural powers at the fruition stage would be intentional: how would this differ from non-Buddhist paths?

To call this "suddenly identical with the buddha-body" is to mistake a fish-eye for a bright pearl, or point to a mountain pheasant and call it a phoenix. Even a three-foot child would know the error.

### The Tiāntái Lineage

The gold-mouth ancestral succession: all were those who "saw and knew." This comes from the *Fùfǎ Zhuàn* (Transmission of the Dharma). Some add six buddhas before and four patriarchs after, speaking of verse-transmission and dharma-entrustment, or "holding up a flower with a subtle smile," proclaiming it an "extra-canonical separate transmission." But these have no basis in the sūtras and treatises, and people universally reject them.

When the light of the Buddha's teaching reached the East with Emperor Ming of the Han, and by the time of the Northern Qí, there was Master Huìwén, who, through exploring the *Treatise on the Great Perfection of Wisdom* (*Shìlùn*), awakened to the three wisdoms in a single mind. Tracing laterally to Nāgārjuna, who is the thirteenth patriarch among the twenty-four: Master Wén was one who "heard and knew." He transmitted this to Nányuè [Huìsī]. Nányuè fully realized the Lotus Samādhi and attained the six purified faculties. He transmitted this to Tiāntái [Zhìyǐ]. Tiāntái, on the sacred peak, personally received [the teaching] at Dàsū, marvelously awakened through the power of concentration, and realized without relying on others. Therefore, using the wondrous purport of the *Lotus Sūtra*, he consolidated the three thousand as the peerless wondrous contemplation. He transmitted this to Zhāng'ān. Zhāng'ān compiled the dharma-treasury and transmitted it to Èr Wēi. Wēi transmitted it to Zuǒxī. Zuǒxī transmitted it to Jīngxī [Zhànrán]. Jīngxī extensively composed commentaries and records, supporting and amplifying the great meaning, shining like the sun and stars.

Passing further down, all were those who "saw and knew." The teaching and contemplation of our school illuminated the four seas. At first, the An-Shǐ Rebellion caused disruption; then the Huìchāng suppression destroyed [the texts]. Later, through the Five Dynasties' military fires, the doctrinal corpus was nearly obliterated, almost lost entirely. Luóxī searched for the lost [texts], casting a net throughout the realm. The King of Wúyuè dispatched envoys to Goryeo and Japan, and the teachings and contemplation were restored. Passed down to Sìmíng [Zhīlǐ], who recorded what Jīngxī had left unrecorded, and fully practiced the four samādhis that had been difficult to practice: he revived this Way like a great light in the sky that cannot be concealed.

### Critique of Other Schools

Since all other schools do not know "inherent evil," when they discuss the nine realms they only speak of "arising from the nature" (*xìngqǐ*). Even those who say "the perfect school takes inherent nature as its principle" know only the inherent possession of good, not the inherent possession of evil. Therefore, although they say "afflictions are bodhi" and "birth-and-death is nirvāṇa," this is like a rat's squeak or a bird's empty call: there are words but no meaning. They must reverse the cultivated evil of the nine realms to realize the inherent good of the buddha-realm.

As for "directly pointing at the human mind, seeing the nature to become a buddha" and "this very mind is the buddha," these point to the true mind as becoming buddha, not the deluded mind. Someone asked: "'This very mind is the buddha': is it the true mind or the deluded mind?" The answer was: "The true mind." Another said: "Cultivation and realization are not absent, but defilement does not apply." This merely singles out the pure dharma-body as the essence of the "extra-canonical separate transmission."

They select: "The reward and transformation [bodies] are not the true buddha, nor the ones who teach the Dharma." Yet the great function and great activity are not without the reward and transformation [bodies]. If one's understanding encompasses the reward and transformation bodies, one would be mired in defilement. If one does not have the protection of mindfulness, one cannot suddenly see the dharma-body. All of this does not go beyond the meaning of the "exclusive middle" (*dànzhōng*).

They have not yet been able to know even the "exclusive middle" of the buddha-realm's inherent possession of the three bodies. How could they know the three bodies of the nine realms? In terms of good and evil, they are biased toward inherent good. In terms of the ten realms, biased toward the buddha-realm. In terms of true and false, biased toward the true. In terms of the nine consciousnesses, biased toward the true, permanent, pure consciousness. In terms of the four teachings, biased toward the separate teaching. In terms of the ten objects such as the aggregates, it belongs to the bodhisattva object. They have not left behind the three obstructions and four māras. How can this be called the "mind-seal of the perfect and sudden [teaching]"?

### Conclusion

Alas! This is the unique, peerless proclamation of our school, ancient and modern: the authentic transmission of the buddhas and patriarchs. But "White Snow" and "Spring in the Sunlight" are songs so lofty that few can harmonize with them. I am fortunate to have encountered this auspicious age and, not declining my own crudeness, have ventured to rely on paper and ink to broaden what is seen and heard. Emulating the *Lotus Sūtra*'s [teaching] "whether in fields or villages," and the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra*'s "whether by trees or stones," even if this provokes slander and denunciation, one hopes that even strong poison, like a beast crossing a river, might [be carried across]. How could I concern myself with those who merely get their tails wet?
