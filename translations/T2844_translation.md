# Ten Signless Prostrations of Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva

## *Wénshūshīlì Púsà Wúxiàng Shí Lǐ* (文殊師利菩薩無相十禮)

Taishō 2844

Author unknown. A Dunhuang liturgical text.

The Great Saint Mañjuśrī (*Wénshūshīlì*, 文殊師利) Bodhisattva on Mount Qīngliáng (*Qīngliáng Shān*, 清涼山, "Cool and Clear Mountain"):

Sitting upright, directly observe the mind. The mind too cannot be found.

With wholehearted sincerity I take refuge and bow in reverence.

The dharma-body buddha (*fǎshēn fó*, 法身佛) of true suchness (*zhēnrú*, 真如) is without color, without image, without root, without dwelling place, because it neither arises nor ceases. I bow with immeasurable contemplation. It neither comes nor goes, neither grasps nor relinquishes, because it has departed from the six sense-gates. It transcends the three realms and is equal to empty space, because desires do not defile it. Within all deportments, going and coming, sleeping and waking, it is always in samādhi. Going and coming are completely equal; one already abides in equality, because one does not destroy equality. Entering the samādhi of all signlessness (*wúxiàng dìng*, 無相定), one sees all dharmas as quiescent and pure, because one has departed from all quiescence and purity. All buddhas have the characteristic of empty space. Empty space too is without characteristics, because it has departed from all causes and effects. Empty space is without center or periphery. The body of all buddhas is likewise, because the mind is the same as empty space. The Buddha is always in the world yet is not defiled by worldly dharmas, because he makes no distinctions about the world. All dharmas are like an illusion. Illusions cannot be found, because one has departed from all illusory dharmas.

For all things, I make a prostration of equality. There is no prostration and no non-prostration. A single prostration pervades all sentient beings, equally returning to the substance of true characteristics (*shíxiàng tǐ*, 實相體).

Universally, for the sake of the four benefactors, the three realms of existence, and all sentient beings of the dharma-realm, may all equally abide in suchness (*rúrú*, 如如).

**Taking Refuge and Repenting.** With wholehearted sincerity I repent: in the three times I seek the nature of sin. Within, without, and between, the mind truly has nothing. Since the mind has nothing, all dharmas are quiescent. The three poisons and four inversions are all just suchness.

Having repented, I take refuge and bow to the dharma-body Tathāgata.

**Exhortation.** With wholehearted sincerity I exhort and request: all dharmas are fundamentally without arising. Since they are without arising, how could there be cessation? Neither arising nor ceasing, the nature eternally abides. I only wish that all buddhas will not enter nirvāṇa, so that all sentient beings may illuminate their original nature and naturally roam in the city of nirvāṇa. The practitioner need only illuminate the five aggregates: no self, no person, both extremes empty.

Having exhorted, I take refuge and bow to the dharma-body Tathāgata.

**Sympathetic Joy.** With wholehearted sincerity I rejoice: dharmas are fundamentally without craving and without fear. Self and other are neither one nor different. Together contemplating the one reality, realizing non-arising, with impartial contemplation, I completely rejoice. Contemplating emptiness, illuminating existence, both of the nature of suchness. I only wish that sentient beings will diligently illuminate the mind. The substance of the mind has always been pure in nature. Illusory form and empty space alike are the truth of wisdom.

Having rejoiced, I take refuge and bow to the dharma-body Tathāgata.

**Transfer of Merit.** With wholehearted sincerity I transfer: lost within a single room, one follows the thought of brightness. By grasping the thought of brightness, the dust of self arises. Now illuminating the self-dust as being without inherent nature, I transfer toward the city of nirvāṇa that has no abiding. The five dharmas contain right knowledge. The eight consciousnesses purify the mind-king. I transfer this body of the aggregates to accomplish the buddha-way. In all four deportments, I constantly practice what is directly present.

Having transferred, I take refuge and bow to the dharma-body Tathāgata.

**Vows.** With wholehearted sincerity I make vows: may all sentient beings subdue the six thieves. May the twin illuminations of compassion and wisdom be constantly present in practice. Neither annihilated nor eternal, free from immeasurable faults. Neither empty nor existent, practicing with clear comprehension. May the four wisdoms and three bodies correspond to that substance. May the five eyes always illuminate, radiating the triple knowledge. May the three kinds of mind-born bodies be without obstruction. May all beings be delivered beneath the bodhi tree.

Having made vows, I take refuge and bow to the dharma-body Tathāgata.

**Universal Reverence.**

> Taking refuge in the buddha's merit of bodhi:
> May the aspiration for the way never retrogress.
> May I, together with all sentient beings,
> Enter together into the substance of true suchness.
>
> Taking refuge in the dharma of sarvajña (*sàpóruò*, 薩般若):
> Attaining the gate of great total retention (*dhāraṇī*).
> May I, together with all sentient beings,
> Enter together into the ocean of true suchness.
>
> Taking refuge in the saṃgha that has ceased all contention:
> Entering together into the ocean of harmony.
> May all sentient beings
> All give rise to the bodhi-mind.
> May the three modes of activity be eternally pure.
> Homage to all the dharma-body buddhas.

### Verses for the Assembly

Let the assembly hear the verse of purity for the early morning: If you wish to seek the bliss of quiescence, you should study the way of the śramaṇa. Clothing and food sustain the body's life; fine or coarse, share equally with the assembly.

All of you, today at the early morning hour of purity, whether in the upper, middle, or lower seats, each keep the six recollections.

Let the assembly hear the verse of impermanence for midday:

> A human life without diligent effort
> Is like a tree without roots.
> Leaves and flowers, by day's end,
> Can they be new again?
> Flowers too do not long stay fresh;
> Beauty too does not long endure.
> A human life is but a kṣaṇa;
> In an instant it is hard to repay.
> I now urge the assembly:
> Diligently cultivate the unsurpassed way.

Let the assembly hear the verse of impermanence for dusk:

> This day has passed;
> Life accordingly diminishes, like a little...
