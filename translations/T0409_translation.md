# 觀虛空藏菩薩經
## Sūtra on the Contemplation of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0409

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Dharmamitra (曇摩蜜多)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling on Mount Khadira, at the place where the Sage of True Awakening had resided, together with twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus, and the thousand bodhisattvas of the Fortunate Kalpa with Maitreya at their head.

At that time the elder Upāli rose from his seat, straightened his robes, bowed to the Buddha, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, previously in the Sūtra of Merits you spoke the name of the Bodhisattva Mahāsattva Ākāśagarbha, who is able to remove all evil and unwholesome karma, curing the evil conduct of royal caṇḍālas through śramaṇa caṇḍālas and the various unwholesome observances. If one wishes to cure such evil matters, how should one contemplate the Bodhisattva Ākāśagarbha? If one succeeds in seeing him, how should one abide together, in poṣadha and saṃgha affairs? If an upāsaka violates the five precepts or the eight precept-fast, if a renunciant bhikṣu, bhikṣuṇī, śrāmaṇera, śrāmaṇerikā, or śikṣamāṇā violates the four grave prohibitions, if a lay bodhisattva transgresses the six grave dharmas, or if a renunciant bodhisattva violates the eight grave prohibitions: such offenders, World-Honored One, you have previously said in the Vinaya should be definitively expelled, like a great stone broken apart. But now in this sūtra you speak of the great compassionate Ākāśagarbha who can rescue all sufferings, and you teach spells to remove transgressions. If such a person exists, how should we recognize them? What shall serve as verification? I pray the World-Honored One explain in detail."

The Buddha told Upāli: "You and all those in the future age who are good upholders of the Vinaya should instruct those beings who have committed offenses, calming their minds. The World-Honored One's great compassion and vast vows are immeasurable. He does not abandon anyone. In the profound Sūtra of Merits he taught the method for curing transgressions, called the Definitive Vinaya. There are thirty-five buddhas, great compassionate saviors of the world. You should pay reverence to them. When you pay reverence, you should wear the robe of shame and remorse. Like one with a sore in the eye, you should feel deep shame. Like a leper following a good physician's instructions, you too should give rise to shame and remorse. Having felt shame, for one day and up to seven days, bow to the buddhas of the ten directions, calling the names of the thirty-five buddhas and separately calling the name of the great compassionate Bodhisattva Ākāśagarbha. Bathe and cleanse the body, burn various fine incenses including dark, firm agarwood. When the morning star appears, kneel with joined palms, weep tears of sorrow, call upon Ākāśagarbha, and say: 'O greatly virtuous, greatly compassionate bodhisattva! Out of compassion for me, please manifest your body before me.'

"At that time, one should give rise to this visualization: Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva has a wish-fulfilling jewel atop his head, and that wish-fulfilling jewel is the color of purple gold. If one sees the wish-fulfilling jewel, one then sees the celestial crown. Within this celestial crown, images of the thirty-five buddhas appear. Within the wish-fulfilling jewel, the images of the buddhas of the ten directions appear. Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva's body is twenty yojanas in height. If he manifests his great body, it is equal to that of Avalokiteśvara. This bodhisattva sits in the cross-legged posture, holding the wish-fulfilling jewel king in his hand. That wish-fulfilling jewel proclaims the sounds of the various Dharma in accord with the Vinaya.

"If this bodhisattva, out of compassion for sentient beings, manifests the form of a bhikṣu or any other form, whether in a dream or during seated meditation, he stamps that person's arm with the maṇi-jewel seal, and the seal inscription bears the characters 'Transgression Removed.' Having obtained this inscription, one may return to the saṃgha and participate in poṣadha as before. If an upāsaka obtains this inscription, it does not obstruct going forth into homelessness. If one does not obtain this inscription, then there will be a voice in empty space proclaiming: 'Transgression extinguished! Transgression extinguished!' If there is no voice in space, then one who knows the Vinaya will, in a dream, see the Bodhisattva Ākāśagarbha, who will say: 'Vināya-sa! Vināya-sa! Such-and-such bhikṣu! Such-and-such upāsaka!' and will further instruct the person to repent for one day up to forty-nine days, bowing to the thirty-five buddhas. 'By the power of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva, your transgressions have become light.' One who knows the Dharma should further instruct the person to plaster and clean the latrines for eight hundred days. Each day one should say: 'You have done an impure deed. Now with a single mind, plaster all the latrines. Do not let anyone know. After plastering, bathe and cleanse yourself. Bow to the thirty-five buddhas. Call upon Ākāśagarbha. Before the twelve divisions of the canon, prostrate with all five limbs on the ground and declare your offenses.' After repenting in this manner for a further twenty-one days, at that time the wise one should gather close companions, and before a buddha image, call the names of the thirty-five buddhas, call upon Mañjuśrī, call upon the bodhisattvas of the Fortunate Kalpa to serve as witnesses, and perform the white karman anew, following the previous precept-conferral procedure. This person, through the power of austere practice, will have their karmic transgressions permanently removed, and the three kinds of bodhi-practice will not be obstructed."

The Buddha told Upāli: "Uphold this contemplation of Ākāśagarbha as the Dharma, and for the sake of shameless beings of the future age, for those who have committed many offenses, explain it widely and in detail."

When these words were spoken, the Bodhisattva Ākāśagarbha, sitting in the cross-legged posture, emitted golden light. Within the wish-fulfilling jewel the thirty-five buddhas appeared, and he said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, this wish-fulfilling jewel treasure of mine emerged from the Śūraṅgama Samādhi. Therefore, sentient beings who see the jewel will attain wish-fulfillment and mastery."

At that time the World-Honored One instructed Upāli: "Uphold this sūtra and do not expound it widely for many people. Only for one person, an upholder of the Vinaya, for the sake of future sentient beings who lack the Dharma-eye, so that you may serve as their eyes, take care not to forget or lose it."

At that time Upāli, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully put it into practice.

---

*According to the Kaiyuan Catalog, this Sūtra on the Contemplation of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva is a unique translation, comprising only two sheets. After the sūtra, there were formerly eight additional sheets of text. According to the collation notes of the Zhutang Lecture Master of the Great Canon, these later sheets were compiled by later people, collecting Ākāśagarbha sūtra spells along with buddha-names and spells from various sūtras as an exhortation for practice, and should not be appended to this sūtra. The Zhuke and Fuke editions both lack these additions.*

*Furthermore, according to this sūtra, the World-Honored One previously taught the method for curing transgressions in the profound Sūtra of Merits, called the Definitive Vinaya, which mentions the thirty-five buddhas. This is the same text as the Upāli Assembly in fascicle ninety of the Ratnakūṭa Sūtra and the Sūtra of the Definitive Vinaya. The remaining text likewise follows this precedent. On detailed examination, the editorial notes are reasonable and can be understood from context. What follows is preserved tentatively.*

### Dhāraṇī of Gathering the Dharma, Joy, and Abandoning Suffering

[Dhāraṇī] Namo buddhāya, namo dharmāya, namo saṃghāya. Namo viśuddha-cāryāya. Namo agga-kabhu-cāryāya. Namo mahā-sarva-bhāga-liryā. Tadyathā atimiribha-bha-bhā miri-bhaspa-bhā mirucāha tanma-da-nā-jā-na hīhī-jī-tsī-di-bhā kubhā-cāryā-nā-ya-bhā-rā sarva mahā hī-jī-di-ri-yaṅ kujī-ri mēku-jī-ri vibhā-sarva yā-nā birim-bhā jhāha da-śā-ya śu-sarva-sāra samam-bha-tti-bhā-bhā bhari mahā ānā svāhā.

At that time the Buddha told the great assembly: "In the past, through countless kalpas, when I was an ordinary person, my name was Cāthadā, and I was in the land of Kaluṇalā, working as a merchant, buying and selling for a living. I was dishonest and untruthful, committing all manner of evil deeds beyond reckoning. I was wanton and without principle, beyond description. At that time, in my foolishness, I killed my father and loved my mother. After several years, all the people of the land knew about it. They raised their voices and proclaimed: 'This Cāthadā killed his father and loves his mother; it has been years now.' At that time I reflected that I was no different from an animal, with nothing human about me. Then in the land of Kaluṇalā, I leaped over the city wall and fled to a deep marsh.

"At that time the king of this land, named Vijaya, issued a decree throughout the land: 'This Cāthadā is wanton and unprincipled, and has done such deeds. Whoever can capture this person shall receive a great reward of treasures.'

"At that time, the people of this land each accepted the reward and sought to capture me. In my terror, I immediately left the country and became a śramaṇa. In another land I cultivated the ten wholesome deeds, practiced seated meditation and studied the path, weeping day and night. After thirty-seven years, because of the obstruction of the five heinous transgressions, my mind could not settle. I was distressed and miserable, unable to find peace. For thirty-seven years in a mountain cave, I constantly cried out: 'How bitter! How bitter! With what mind can I escape this suffering?' Weeping, I went down from the cave to beg for food.

"At that time, on the road, on the ground I found a large bowl. Inside was a small box containing a text. There was no other scripture, only the Dhāraṇī of Gathering the Dharma, Joy, and Abandoning Suffering. It said that all past buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Gaṅgā, when they were about to enter nirvāṇa, would always, in the land of Viyela, teach this dhāraṇī and entrust it to the great bodhisattvas. If in the future a person hears this dhāraṇī, it is because in past lives that person cultivated the five precepts and the ten wholesome deeds, which enabled them to hear it. If a person hears it but does not take it to heart and does not cultivate it, that person is called one without affinity. This dhāraṇī can remove the great transgressions of birth-and-death and the five heinous crimes accumulated over a hundred billion kalpas. If there is a person who receives, upholds, reads, and recites it, they will never fall into the three paths of hell, hungry ghosts, or animals. Why? Because all past buddhas, when they were about to enter nirvāṇa, would always teach it, honoring and praising its merits beyond measure, and entrusting it to the bodhisattvas. If in the future sentient beings hear this dhāraṇī and cultivate it, taking it to heart, their merit will be incalculable, like the jeweled ocean of Mount Sumeru, which ordinary beings cannot measure. If a person who has committed various evil deeds secretly hears even the name of this dhāraṇī, and without having time to cultivate it, holds it in mind even once, and then falls into hell, throughout all the hells, by the grace of this person's merit, suffering will not be inflicted. If a person can practice it and, in this present body, diligently cultivates and attains it, they will behold hundreds of thousands of millions of buddha-lands, and will attain immeasurable merit beyond description. Only the buddhas and bodhisattvas can fully comprehend it. Śrāvakas and those of the two vehicles cannot know it. Why? Because this dhāraṇī was taught not by one or two buddhas, but by all past buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Gaṅgā.

"At that time, having obtained this text, I did not go begging for food but joyfully returned to my cave. In the cave I burned incense, bowed in reverence, and wept tears of praise. In the cave I cultivated, reading and reciting it. After one year I finally succeeded. Because of the obstruction of my karmic transgressions, I could not take it to heart. Then, on an autumn moonlit night, I bathed and cultivated for seven days, like a child first learning, fumbling and confused. I practiced another seven days, and it was the same, fumbling and without change. In my mind I was worried and distressed, not knowing what to do. In my mind I contemplated the syllables of this dhāraṇī. After repeating it several times, my mind suddenly became settled. At that time my delight was like a person who finds hundreds of thousands of pounds of gold on the ground, with no one knowing; the inner joy was unceasing. My feeling at that time was the same. After cultivating for several years, I could fly without obstruction and beheld the buddhas of the ten directions and the three times. Those who practice in the future should practice according to this Dharma."

### Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva Dhāraṇī Spell

[Dhāraṇī] Namo buddhā-tu-buddhā mahā-maudgalyāyana-sā. Tadyathā iri-giri miri tikhi sarva-ga iri miri sarva-ga miri svāhā.

This dhāraṇī should be recited on the fourteenth and fifteenth days of the month, when the morning star appears, eight hundred times, burning fine agarwood incense with the smoke unbroken. Use eight hundred yellow flowers. This will cause a person to attain merit. If a good man, in this present body, seeks what the mind desires, there is nothing that will not be obtained. If a woman, she will be transformed into a man. If one can sincerely practice for one day and one night in the six periods, reciting and upholding it, the transgressions of one or two kalpas will be permanently removed and one will never enter the evil destinies. It should be practiced during the cool months of spring and autumn: the third, fourth, eighth, and ninth months.
