# Treatise on the Pure Land: Birth and Non-Birth

## *Jìngtǔ Shēng Wúshēng Lùn* (淨土生無生論)

Taishō 1975

Composed by Chuándēng (*Chuándēng*, 傳燈) of the Ming dynasty.

*Note: This is a doctrinal treatise by the Ming-dynasty Tiāntái monk Chuándēng (1554-1628 CE), one of the most important Tiāntái masters of the late Ming period. The text synthesizes Tiāntái philosophy with Pure Land devotion, arguing through ten "gates" (mén) that rebirth in the Pure Land (shēng) and the principle of non-arising (wúshēng) are ultimately non-dual. Drawing on the Tiāntái concepts of "inherent possession" (xìngjù), the three truths (sāndì), and the "six identities" (liùjí), Chuándēng argues that the Pure Land of Amitābha is originally inherent in one's own mind-nature, that Amitābha and the practitioner are non-dual, and that the act of reciting the buddha-name, when understood through Tiāntái contemplation, is itself the dharma-realm. The ten gates are: (1) the one true dharma-realm, (2) the dependent origination of body and land, (3) the mutual identity of mind and land, (4) the non-duality of sentient being and buddha, (5) the dharma-realm as念佛 (mindfulness of buddha), (6) the mutual encompassing of object and contemplation, (7) the three contemplations as naturally so, (8) the spontaneous response between stimulus and response, (9) the permanent oneness of "here" and "there," and (10) the mutual presence of present and future. The text was highly influential in late-imperial Chinese Buddhism and remains widely studied in Tiāntái and Pure Land circles.*

### Homage

> I bow to the perfectly complete wisdom of the Able Sage [Śākyamuni],
> To the Great Guide, the Awakened One of Immeasurable Life [Amitābha],
> To the Mahāyāna sūtras spoken [by them] on the Land of Peace and Nourishment [Sukhāvatī]:
> Definitive within the definitive, most perfectly sudden.
> To Mañjuśrī, Samantabhadra, Avalokiteśvara,
> Mahāsthāmaprāpta, and the pure ocean-wide assembly;
> To Aśvaghoṣa, Nāgārjuna, and Vasubandhu;
> To the patriarch of the Lotus Society of Mount Lú in this land [Huìyuǎn];
> To the Tiāntái Master of Wisdom [Zhìyǐ] and the Dharma-Wisdom [Zhīlǐ];
> To all Dharma-masters past and present who have spread the teaching:
> I now take refuge and bow to the Three Treasures.
> I beg that the unseen blessing may awaken my understanding.
> Reverently gathering the secret purport of the sūtras and treatises,
> I elucidate the Pure Land's "birth and non-birth."
> May all in the future awaken to this gate,
> Sever doubt, give rise to faith, and ascend to non-retrogression.

---

In composing this treatise, I establish ten gates: (1) the gate of the one true dharma-realm, (2) the gate of the dependent origination of body and land, (3) the gate of the mutual identity of mind and land, (4) the gate of the non-duality of sentient being and buddha, (5) the gate of the dharma-realm as mindfulness, (6) the gate of the mutual encompassing of object and contemplation, (7) the gate of the three contemplations as naturally so, (8) the gate of spontaneous stimulus and response, (9) the gate of the permanent oneness of here and there, and (10) the gate of the mutual presence of present and future.

---

### Gate One: The One True Dharma-Realm

> In the one true dharma-nature
> The ten dharma-realms are fully present.
> Dependent and direct [rewards] are originally interpenetrating;
> Sentient beings and buddhas are not fundamentally different.

**Treatise:** The one true dharma-realm is the mind-nature originally possessed by all sentient beings. This mind-nature possesses immeasurable virtues and receives immeasurable names.

How does it possess immeasurable virtues? In brief, there are three: the nature-essence (*xìngtǐ*), the nature-extent (*xìngliàng*), and the nature-possession (*xìngjù*). The nature-essence means that this mind-nature transcends the four propositions, is beyond the hundred negations, firm and stable, pure and undefiled, unborn and undying, permanently abiding and indestructible. The nature-extent means that this mind-nature vertically exhausts the three times and horizontally pervades the ten directions: worlds have limits but empty space has no limit; empty space has limits but mind-nature has no limit. The nature-possession means that this mind-nature possesses the ten dharma-realms (buddha, bodhisattva, pratyekabuddha, śrāvaka, deva, asura, human, animal, hungry ghost, and hell realms) as provisional names, with the five aggregates of each as substantial dharmas, and the lands of each as dependent rewards.

These three are also the three truths: the nature-essence is the middle truth; the nature-extent is the true truth (*zhēndì*); the nature-possession is the conventional truth.

---

### Gate Two: The Dependent Origination of Body and Land

> The one true dharma-realm nature,
> Unchanging yet able to follow conditions:
> The three bodies and four lands
> Are all created by the mind's transformation.

**Treatise:** Because the nature-essence and nature-extent are identical with the nature-possession, the true suchness is "unchanging yet following conditions, following conditions yet unchanging." As the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* (Jin translation) says: "Being able to follow defiled and pure conditions, it thereby creates the ten dharma-realms." The ten dharma-realms of phenomenal reality, the three bodies [of the buddha], and the four lands are all created through the true suchness following conditions.

If this is so, then Sahā and Sukhāvatī, the sentient beings of this world and the nine grades of those to be reborn, the already-accomplished Amitābha as the fruit of our own minds: all are created by the transformation of mind-nature. Mind possesses [all dharmas] and creates them: how can one divide subject from object? "This very mind is the buddha, this very buddha is the mind; this very mind is the land, this very land is the mind." Understanding this principle and seeking rebirth in the Land of Bliss: this is the ultimate Way in which both birth and non-birth are merged.

---

### Gate Three: The Mutual Identity of Mind and Land

> The Western Land of Peace and Bliss
> Is ten trillion [buddha-lands] from here.
> Yet with my momentary mind,
> There is originally no difference of "here" and "there."

**Treatise:** The *Amitābha Sūtra* says: "Passing westward beyond ten trillion buddha-lands, there is a world called 'Ultimate Bliss.'" Yet an ordinary being who recites the buddha-name and seeks rebirth arrives there in a finger-snap. This is because one is born into the buddha-land possessed by one's own mind. "Momentary mind" means the mind of an ordinary being reciting the buddha-name: a kṣaṇa-moment of mind, the most subtle and feeble. Yet this feeble momentary mind arises entirely from the nature, and the nature is boundless, so the mind is also boundless. The nature is like a great ocean; the mind is like a bubble. The entire ocean becomes the bubble; the bubble returns to pervade the ocean.

---

### Gate Four: The Non-Duality of Sentient Being and Buddha

> Amitābha and the ordinary being:
> Though delusion and awakening differ,
> The buddha's mind and the sentient being's mind
> Are ultimately without duality.

**Treatise:** The buddhas have awakened to the buddhas within the minds of sentient beings. Sentient beings are the sentient beings within the minds of the buddhas, deluded. That which is awakened is the nature-essence, nature-extent, and nature-possession originally possessed by sentient beings. That which is deluded is the nature-essence, nature-extent, and nature-possession realized by all buddhas. Therefore, Amitābha is my own mind; my own mind is Amitābha. Before a single thought of recitation is raised, [the connection] is already accomplished. The moment one raises the thought of recitation, it is immediately complete. Stimulus and response interact for exactly this reason; therefore the merit of one who recites the buddha-name is never wasted.

---

### Gate Five: The Dharma-Realm as Mindfulness

> The dharma-realm's perfectly interpenetrating body
> Becomes my single thought of mind.
> Therefore my mind of mindfulness of buddha
> Is entirely the dharma-realm.

**Treatise:** When a practitioner recites the buddha-name, contemplates the buddha, contemplates the principal and attendant buddhas with their dependent and direct [rewards], cultivates the three kinds of pure karma, or attains one-pointed concentration, or even recites with a scattered mind: since all these arise from the true suchness following conditions, they are entirely the dharma-realm. If this is so, then other mental states are also thus; how much more the mind of mindfulness of buddha! Therefore, at the moment of reciting the buddha-name, this mind is the perfectly interpenetrating, pure, jeweled enlightenment. Reciting Amitābha with this wondrous mind: which of the three bodies would not be recited? Seeking which of the four lands would one not be born?

---

### Gate Six: The Mutual Encompassing of Object and Contemplation

The sixteen [contemplation] objects and the two kinds of contemplation (of principle and of phenomena) mutually encompass each other like Indra's net, because all are entirely the dharma-realm.

### Gate Seven: The Three Contemplations as Naturally So

The three truths (true, conventional, middle) and the three contemplations (emptiness, provisional, middle) are naturally present in the mind: "Emptiness is the mind's natural emptiness; things come and are responded to, the mind's natural existence; emptiness and existence are mutually identical, the mind's natural middle."

### Gate Eight: Spontaneous Stimulus and Response

Because buddha and sentient being share the same awakened source, when the sentient being's mind is mindful of the buddha, Amitābha spontaneously responds, like a magnet attracting a needle.

### Gate Nine: Permanent Oneness of Here and There

At the moment of death, when one does not lose right mindfulness, the Pure Land lotus appears before one, a mind-only realm without the characteristic of going or coming.

### Gate Ten: The Mutual Presence of Present and Future

When a practitioner recites the buddha-name in the present, the cause already contains the fruit, like a lotus flower that bears fruit within its blossom. In the lotus pools of the Pure Land, a lotus seed sprouts at the moment of one's first aspiration. If one is diligent and does not regress, the flower grows daily. If one becomes slack, the flower withers. If one renews one's effort, the flower becomes splendid again. If not, the sprout scorches and the seed perishes.
