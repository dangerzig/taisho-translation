# 師子素馱娑王斷肉經
## Sūtra on King Siṃha-Sudāsa Renouncing Meat

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0164

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Zhiyan (智嚴)

---

> I recall in the past, through immeasurable kalpas,
> There was a king named Sudāsa.
> One time the king went out to hunt in the mountains,
> With ministers and attendants hunting birds and beasts.
> Suddenly thunder, hail, and fierce winds arose,
> All the men scattered in terror and alarm.
> The king alone ran deep into the mountain forest,
> By a river he rested, with no companions.
> A female lion in her mountain lair
> Saw the king sitting alone and drew near to him.
> Because of the evil karma and past causes of sentient beings,
> They cycle through the hells with immeasurable suffering.
> The king and the lioness, by past-life causes and conditions,
> Both gave rise to desire and copulated together.
> Through many kalpas of eating meat and killing,
> By force of past habit he entered the lioness's womb,
> And was born with a human body but a lion's head,
> A spotted and powerful man, like a king of beasts.
> When he grew, he was swift and exceedingly fierce.
> He asked his mother: "Whose child am I?"
> His mother the lioness answered her son:
> "Your father is King Sudāsa of the Magadha country."
> Hearing this, the son swiftly went to seek
> His royal father in the country of Magadha.
> He presented himself and explained the past causes and conditions.
> The king heard, understood, and took him in as his son.
> But the royal father was now old and advanced in years.
> He ascended a tower, installed his son as king,
> With the title King Siṃha-Sudāsa,
> To hold court and govern the ministers and people.
> The lion, by the turning force of evil habits,
> Through many kalpas had eaten meat and harmed living beings.
> Though dwelling as a human king, he would not eat grain,
> Consuming only birds, beasts, and creatures of water and land.
> When the time came to present the varied meats,
> A dog snatched away the animal flesh.
> The cook, fearing the king would have him beheaded,
> Ran out and seized a small infant.
> Secretly he cut off its head, hands, and feet,
> Boiled it whole in a cauldron, and presented it to the king.
> The king ate its flesh and found it most delicious.
> His craving for the taste of meat grew like a burning fire.
> The king asked the food steward: "What meat is this?"
> The steward, terrified, reported everything to the king.
> The king pardoned him: "Do not worry.
> Each day, bring me this kind of meat."
> The cook, having received the great king's command,
> Changed his clothes each day and stole other people's children.
> Year after year he stole boys and girls.
> He was like a rākṣasa, or like a hawk.
> The people throughout the land all wore mourning clothes,
> Having lost their children, each in anguish and confusion.
> Two by two they clasped hands and questioned one another,
> Choking with grief, with no way to appeal to heaven.
> The townspeople watched and caught the thief.
> The kitchen thief pleaded that he was not acting freely.
> The people reported and remonstrated with the king.
> The king heard them and in fury greatly rebuked them:
> "Until now you secretly brought me children's flesh.
> From this day, I shall personally select one person each day."
> Relatives, ministers, and people were eaten in order,
> Like sheep dragged from a pen.
> The whole nation despaired, with no way to protest.
> They gathered at the king's court, planning to depose their ruler.
> The king climbed a high terrace and prayed to the spirits:
> "Grant me wings to fly and escape this calamity!
> If I gain the power of flight, I shall seize kings from all lands,
> And offer the heads of a hundred kings to the mountain spirit."
> By the force of the lion's fierce and evil habits,
> He immediately gained wings and flew to seize the kings,
> Chaining them high on steep, precipitous cliffs.
> He had already captured ninety-nine kings.
> Needing only one more king for the sacrifice,
> The lion descended to search once more.
> At that time, the Bodhisattva King of Rājagṛha,
> Named Śrutacandra, was bathing in his garden.
> The lion saw the king sitting on a jade stone,
> Descended, seized his right arm, and was about to carry him off.
> King Śrutacandra then wept.
> The lion asked the king: "Why do you cry?"
> "I have heard the great king is brave and wise.
> A bodhisattva does not cling to body, life, or wealth."
> "If that is so, you should endure it.
> Why such bitter grief and loss of freedom?"
> Śrutacandra answered King Siṃha:
> "Of all suffering, none surpasses compassion.
> I cultivate the bodhisattva's great compassion.
> My grief now is for those hundred kings:
> Born to a life of nobility, rulers of the world,
> Today chained and imprisoned, their lives about to end.
> Moreover, I sought the Buddha's Dharma across a hundred kingdoms,
> And invited a Dharma teacher to come from afar.
> Before I could hear the teaching and personally receive instruction,
> My people thirst for it and have not yet heard.
> Grant me a reprieve of seven days,
> To make offerings to the Three Treasures and hear the sound of Dharma,
> To assemble my ministers and entrust the Dharma to them.
> On the eighth day, I shall come of my own accord to meet the great king."
> Because the bodhisattva was without deceit or falsehood,
> The lion granted the king a seven-day period.
> On the eighth day, Śrutacandra went out from the city to meet him,
> Offering his body in fulfillment of his promise to the lion king.
> Then the lion appeared like a cloud,
> Seized Śrutacandra and carried him before the assembled kings.
> The lion asked the king: "Are you not afraid?
> You dare to come before me like a king of beasts?"
> Śrutacandra answered King Siṃha:
> "This body is a false thing, which I offer to the great king.
> I would rather give up a hundred thousand bodies and lives
> Than break my former word and fail to keep my promise to you."
> With a calm and joyful expression, using skillful speech:
> "Listen while I briefly explain the causes and conditions.
> You wish to sacrifice to evil spirits and ghosts,
> But the good spirits of all quarters will bring you misfortune.
> In the Buddha-lands of the ten directions, the sages and worthies:
> For many kalpas, you will never again hear their names.
> This body is a false thing, a combination of causes and conditions.
> Life is like a flash of lightning, with no fixed abode.
> The five faculties and six consciousnesses have no self or person.
> Eyes, ears, nose, tongue, and touch are the causes.
> Like magical illusions that display various forms,
> Sentient beings, through false thinking, grasp them as real.
> Examine this body from head to foot:
> There is not a single thing that is permanent.
> Like a bubble on water, destroyed in an instant,
> Old age, sickness, death, and suffering are also impermanent.
> Though you now nourish your body with flesh,
> In the end there is no refuge and no good path.
> Having killed beyond measure and devoured flesh,
> You will cycle through suffering in the evil paths."
> At that time, Śrutacandra spoke innumerable verses,
> Exhorting and converting King Siṃha-Sudāsa.
> The lion, hearing them, gradually turned his mind.
> He listened to the teaching on no-self and the true nature of reality.
> The lion asked the king: "How should I arrange
> A sacrifice without sin that will please the spirits?"
> Śrutacandra answered: "Prepare vegetarian offerings.
> Innocent, pure food for sacrificing to heaven."
> The lion followed the command and sacrificed to the mountain spirit.
> He offered his body and gave himself up to King Śrutacandra.
> The captive kings imprisoned in the mountains
> Were all entrusted to Śrutacandra to take with him.
> Śrutacandra escorted each back to his own country,
> Restoring them as before to govern their peoples.
> He also brought King Siṃha-Sudāsa
> To the country of Magadha, to sit in his original palace.
> He reconciled all the ministers and the people,
> And the entire nation renounced meat and ceased killing.
> At that time, Śrutacandra made a great vow:
> "May I, when I attain supreme, perfect enlightenment,
> Liberate all sentient beings without exception.
> May these kings together attain Buddhahood.
> The wondrous Dharma I have taught to King Siṃha:
> May his heavy transgressions be dispersed like clouds."

The Buddha further reflected: "In the past, immeasurable kalpas ago, Śakra Devānām Indra dwelt in the Trāyastriṃśa palace. Because of residual habits from eating meat in the past, he transformed his body into a hawk and chased a dove. At that time, I was a king named Śibi. Out of compassion for the dove, I placed myself on the scale and cut my own flesh to substitute for the dove and spare its life. The King Śibi was my present self. In the future he would become the king named Śrutacandra. The god Śakra who then transformed into a hawk would in the future become King Siṃha-Sudāsa. Even Śakra, by testing me, still fell into the evil paths. How much more so for other sentient beings who, shameless and intent on killing, devour blood and flesh without ever being satisfied?"

All sentient beings, from beginningless time, have without exception been one another's parents and relatives. They change form and are reborn as birds and beasts. How can one bear to eat them? Those who eat meat will, through kalpas upon kalpas, be reborn as birds and beasts, eating others' blood and flesh, and repaying with their own lives in an endless cycle. If born among humans, they will be devoted to killing and to the taste of flesh. At death, they will fall into the Avīci hell, with not a moment of rest. If a person can renounce the eating of meat for one lifetime, up until the attainment of Buddhahood, they will never eat it again.
