# 佛說月燈三昧經
## Sūtra of the Moon-Lamp Samādhi

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0640

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Xiāngōng (先公)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, staying at the Jeta Grove in the park of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus and sixty thousand bodhisattvas, as well as countless devas and humans. At that time the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī was seated in the assembly.

The Buddha addressed Mañjuśrī: "Child! A bodhisattva who practices giving has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, one firmly eliminates jealous thoughts. Second, one always gives with a pure mind. Third, countless hundreds of thousands of people cannot take one's wealth. Fourth, one passes away possessing the most excellent things. Fifth, one is born into a great and noble family. Sixth, in every place of rebirth one delights in giving. Seventh, one is cherished by the four assemblies. Eighth, one is fearless, enters any assembly without hindrance, and one's reputation is heard in all ten directions. Ninth, one's youth is marked by soft and supple hands and feet. Tenth, one always delights in good teachers, even until sitting beneath the bodhi tree. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who practices giving." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> Having cast far off all jealousy,
> with a mind that always delights in giving,
> one passes away possessing the finest things
> and is born at once in a wealthy, noble house.
>
> In every place of rebirth, the mind always delights
> in taking joy in the practice of giving.
> One is cherished by all beings,
> both those who dwell at home and those who have renounced.
>
> In any assembly one is free from fear;
> wherever one goes, there is no difficulty.
> One's reputation is heard afar
> in all counties, districts, and towns.
>
> One's hands and feet are always soft and supple;
> whatever one wishes for is no longer hard to obtain.
> Thereupon one gains good teachers:
> the buddhas and all their disciples.
>
> One never again gives rise to jealous thoughts;
> the mind always delights and takes joy in giving.
> Having passed away possessing the finest things,
> in such practice there is no jealousy.
>
> One is born at once in a great and noble house;
> the mind always delights and takes joy in giving.
> One is cherished by myriad millions of people:
> such is the practice of one who gives.
>
> Gaining good teachers is no longer difficult;
> one always sees the buddhas and their disciples.
> Having seen them, one delights in making offerings to them:
> such is the practice of one who gives.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who upholds the precepts with purity has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, one's vows are fulfilled. Second, one studies the buddha-path. Third, one always honors and delights in wisdom. Fourth, one would die rather than speak falsely. Fifth, one's mind is not swayed by worldly things. Sixth, one abandons saṃsāra. Seventh, one seeks nirvāṇa. Eighth, one practices in solitude. Ninth, one attains samādhi. Tenth, one is free from impoverished practice. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva's pure precept-keeping." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> One's vows are at once fulfilled;
> one studies the path and practice of all buddhas.
> One always delights in honoring wisdom
> and is also free from moments of fear.
>
> One's vows are no longer diverted,
> nor are one's careful practices overturned.
> One always casts far off saṃsāra
> and seeks the path of nirvāṇa.
>
> One always practices in solitary places
> and at once attains samādhi.
> There is never a time of impoverishment:
> thus one is established in the stage of precept-keeping.
>
> Such a person fulfills their every vow;
> this bodhisattva studies the path of all buddhas.
> The wise one does not boast among others;
> such a person's precepts are pure.
>
> Holding firm vows, nothing is difficult;
> one's undertakings are never again shaken.
> Having seen the countless evils of saṃsāra,
> one casts them aside and seeks the path of nirvāṇa.
>
> The mind no longer clings to what it thinks upon;
> such a person's establishment in the precepts is strong.
> Attaining the right buddha-land is never difficult:
> such is the practice of one whose precepts are pure.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who dwells in patience has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, fire cannot burn one. Second, blades cannot harm one. Third, poison cannot take effect. Fourth, water cannot drown one. Fifth, non-human beings protect one. Sixth, one's body is adorned with the marks. Seventh, the gates to all evil paths are closed. Eighth, being reborn in the Brahmā heaven is not difficult. Ninth, one obtains peace and safety day and night. Tenth, one is settled in happiness, never displaced. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who abides in patience." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> Fire cannot burn such a person;
> blades too cannot cause them harm.
> Poison cannot take effect;
> water too cannot sweep them away.
>
> All non-human beings protect them;
> at once they attain the thirty-two marks.
> The gates to the evil paths are then closed:
> such is the virtue of one who is patient.
>
> To seek the stations of Brahmā or Śakra,
> even those are not difficult to obtain.
> One always gains the practice of peace and safety
> and fully awakens to the truth of impermanence.
>
> Blades and fire cannot cause harm;
> walking amid poison, one cannot be injured.
> Devas, humans, ghosts, and spirits all give protection:
> such is the practice of one who is patient.
>
> One's body at once attains the thirty-two marks;
> such a person no longer fears the evil paths.
> Upon dying, one is at once born in the Brahmā heaven:
> such is the path of one who practices loving-kindness.
>
> Day and night one at once gains the practice of peace and safety;
> one always delights in the body's attainment of stability.
> Toward all beings one holds a pure mind
> and never harbors the intention of anger.

The Buddha addressed the child: "A bodhisattva who is diligent has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, one possesses awesome spiritual power. Second, one is protected by all buddhas. Third, Non-human beings all protect one as well. Fourth, having heard the dharma, one never forgets it. Fifth, one hears dharma one has not heard before. Sixth, one attains lofty and brilliant wisdom. Seventh, one attains various kinds of samādhi. Eighth, one is never afflicted by illness. Ninth, one receives food and is at peace. Tenth, one becomes soft and supple like an utpala lotus, not rigid. Child! These are the ten benefits of a diligent bodhisattva's practice." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> One always possesses awesome spiritual power
> and never turns to commit evil deeds.
> All non-human beings protect one,
> and one swiftly attains the buddha-path.
>
> Having heard the sūtras, one never forgets;
> what one has not heard, one seeks and obtains.
> Such a person at once gains lofty brilliance:
> such is the virtue of the diligent.
>
> One attains the same practices of the various samādhis;
> there is never a time of illness.
> Wisdom and diligence go together:
> such a person attains the buddha-path.
>
> The food one receives brings peace,
> and at once one carries out diligent practice.
> Just as an utpala lotus in water
> gradually grows and reaches full bloom,
>
> so too does the pure white dharma,
> causing the bodhisattva to gradually mature.
> None can ever stand against such a person,
> who gains a place of peace and happiness in the heavens.
>
> The Tathāgata's diligent practice
> has surpassed countless kalpas through effort.
> All the bodhisattvas' strenuous practices
> and the cultivation they undertake: he has fully expounded them.
>
> The diligent person possesses awesome spiritual power
> and is always protected by all buddhas.
> All receive and practice this path:
> such a person will attain the buddha-path before long.
>
> What one hears one never again forgets,
> and moreover one gains the various dharma practices.
> Such a person's wisdom gradually increases:
> these are the benefits of diligent practice.
>
> Various samādhis constantly increase of themselves;
> such a person is never afflicted by illness.
> All the food they receive to eat
> everywhere brings peace and safety.
>
> Day and night one accomplishes the pure white practice;
> the diligent one never rests.
> Such a person will swiftly attain buddhahood before long:
> so honored is the practice of diligence.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who practices seated meditation has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, single-minded stability of practice. Second, performing the work of the Way. Third, freedom from fear and difficulty. Fourth, rightly settling the sense faculties. Fifth, being cherished by others. Sixth, distancing oneself from desire. Seventh, single-mindedness that does not waver. Eighth, liberation from Māra's domain. Ninth, dwelling in the Buddha's domain. Tenth, attaining liberation. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who practices seated meditation." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> Such a person's practice is unwavering;
> one at once dwells in the practice of stability.
> One devotes oneself to the work of the Way;
> such a person casts off incorrect practices.
>
> What one cultivates is free from attachment;
> the sense faculties are already settled in stillness.
> One at once attains the joy of peace and safety
> by sitting in contemplation on the Way's practice.
>
> Such a person has already left behind desire
> and sits at peace in single-minded concentration.
> Having distanced oneself from Māra's domain,
> one at once dwells in the Buddha's domain.
>
> The devoted practitioner has such steadfastness;
> one who delights in solitude among the trees
> at once attains the practice of liberation
> and accomplishes these ten qualities.
>
> The bodhisattva abides, no longer turning to wrong practice;
> all incorrect practices are cast aside.
> Casting off wrong practice, one delights in right practice:
> such is the practitioner of samādhi.
>
> Such a person is never attached or craving;
> the practitioner of peace and safety is wise and free from greed.
> Body and mind are well-aware, and one keeps the precepts:
> such is the practitioner of samādhi.
>
> Among trees in open spaces one is without fear;
> such a person is never attached or craving.
> All non-human beings cherish and protect one:
> thus one distances oneself from desire, practicing alone.
>
> Never attached to desire, never craving,
> one is thus liberated from Māra's domain.
> One then dwells in the Tathāgata's domain;
> such a person's liberation from evil is complete.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who practices prajñāpāramitā has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, one gives away all one's possessions without expecting anything in return. Second, one no longer violates the precepts and does not adorn oneself with precept-keeping. Third, one abides in the power of patience without the notion of a person. Fourth, one practices diligence without craving for body or life. Fifth, one practices meditation without abiding in meditation. Sixth, one conquers the wicked Māra. Seventh, the ninety-six kinds of outside paths cannot shake one. Eighth, one comes to know birth and death for oneself. Ninth, one has compassion toward sentient beings. Tenth, one does not seek the stages of the śrāvaka or the pratyekabuddha. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who practices prajñāpāramitā." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> Such a person gives equally to all
> and does not think of hoping for any reward.
> One guards the sūtra-precepts and dares not transgress,
> and one has no thought of grasping or seeking.
>
> Practicing patience together with wisdom,
> one never harbors the notion of a person.
> Seeing others, one is at once diligent;
> body, will, and mind are without attachment.
>
> Practicing single-mindedness together with wisdom,
> there is no abiding and no thought.
> Having conquered the host of Māras:
> such is the virtue of the wise.
>
> The ninety-six kinds of paths
> are all unable to shake or move one.
> One comes to understand the matter of birth and death:
> such are the benefits of wisdom.
>
> Toward all sentient beings everywhere
> one has the practice of great compassion.
> Regarding the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha,
> one never thinks of seeking their practice.
>
> Giving all one has without hoping for reward,
> not violating the precepts and never thinking of evil,
> practicing patience without the notion of a person:
> such are the benefits of cultivating wisdom.
>
> Being diligent in empty and solitary places,
> in meditation without thought and without abiding,
> such a person's wisdom conquers Māra:
> such are the benefits of practicing wisdom.
>
> The outside paths cannot shake one;
> such a person comes to understand birth and death.
> Toward the people of the world one has sorrow and compassion:
> such are the benefits of practicing wisdom.
>
> Regarding the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas,
> one never thinks of seeking their attainment.
> Such a person dwells in the buddha-path in this way:
> such are the benefits of practicing wisdom.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva of much wisdom has ten benefits of practice. What are the ten? First, one knows the evil paths. Second, one knows the good paths. Third, one resolves doubts. Fourth, one reveals the straight path to others. Fifth, one casts aside the evil paths. Sixth, one dwells on the right path. Seventh, one stands at the gate of amṛta. Eighth, one attains a seat beneath the bodhi tree. Ninth, one reveals the bright path to the people. Tenth, one does not fear the evil paths. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva of much wisdom." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> One comes to know all the afflictions
> and fully understands both kinds.
> Such a person then casts off the afflictions
> and at once follows the buddha-path's practice.
>
> The wise one resolves all doubts
> and reveals what is straight and right.
> One then casts aside the practices of the evil paths
> and at once arrives on the right path.
>
> One is seen standing at the gate of amṛta
> and then attains a seat beneath the bodhi tree.
> One explains and reveals the right path for all people,
> causing them not to fear the evil paths.
>
> Understanding countless dharmas of affliction,
> knowing and understanding both kinds in this way,
> such a person then casts off affliction
> and studies the highest and most excellent dharma.
>
> Resolving all people's doubts,
> one then gains sight of the straight and right.
> At once casting aside the evil paths,
> the one of much wisdom always dwells on the path.
>
> Always standing at the gate of amṛta,
> one gains a seat beneath the immeasurable bodhi tree.
> One reveals the bright path for immeasurable millions of people;
> such a person never fears the evil paths.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who honors dharma-giving and shares the dharma with others has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, one casts off evil. Second, one carries out good. Third, one cultivates what is upright. Fourth, one purifies one's buddha-land. Fifth, one sits beneath the bodhi tree. Sixth, one gives generously all that is needed. Seventh, one conquers all afflictions. Eighth, one gives wisdom to all people. Ninth, one practices loving-kindness. Tenth, one attains a mind of peace and safety in the present life. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who honors dharma-giving and shares the dharma with others." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> All that is unwholesome one casts aside;
> all that is wholesome one carries out.
> One dwells and is established in the wisdom of the dharma;
> the mind always delights in giving.
>
> One then purifies one's buddha-land
> and at once attains the highest realm.
> One then sits beneath the bodhi tree:
> dharma-giving is like a treasure.
>
> Giving all that one has,
> one studies from the dharma kings.
> One then casts far off all afflictions;
> for such a person, attaining buddhahood is not difficult.
>
> Giving to all people,
> one always has the practice of loving-kindness.
> One has no practice of jealousy
> and brings peace and safety to all non-human beings.
>
> The one of much wisdom casts aside all that is unwholesome;
> such a person always dwells in what is wholesome.
> One is firm and unshakable in the honored dharma;
> the one of much wisdom always gives the dharma.
>
> One always attains a pure land;
> one always carries out the work of the buddha-path.
> One always gains a seat beneath the bodhi tree:
> such are the benefits of giving the dharma.
>
> Free from affliction, one gives to the multitude;
> one then knows and understands one's own affairs.
> All are liberated from worldly matters;
> such a person is never hindered.
>
> Such a person, knowing it, arouses the aspiration;
> for all people one gives in this way.
> Having loving-kindness, one is without jealousy;
> seeing all dharmas as peaceful, without a self.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who practices emptiness has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, one practices the buddha-path. Second, one's practice is without attachment. Third, one does not wish for any particular rebirth. Fourth, one does not violate the precepts and dharma. Fifth, one does not slander the worthy. Sixth, one does not engage in contention. Seventh, one has nothing that is obtained. Eighth, one practices the Way alone. Ninth, one does not contend with the Buddha. Tenth, one receives the dharma and practices it. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who practices emptiness." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> The practice of all the highest beings
> attains the highest and most honored world.
> The courageous ones practice there;
> no being with a life-span can be found.
>
> All are unattached to any world;
> in meditation and contemplation one sits in peace.
> One does not wish for any particular rebirth;
> one then comes to understand the dharmas of emptiness.
>
> One never again violates the precepts and dharma;
> one holds the precepts without blame.
> For one's entire life one does not speak evil
> or slander other worthy ones.
>
> One's practice of the Way is free from contention;
> there are never any disputes.
> One then comes to know all matters;
> one practices in accordance with the dharma.
>
> Even at the cost of one's life,
> one never slanders the World-Honored One.
> One accumulates all dharmas
> and guards one's mind without fear.
>
> In all the worlds,
> the buddha-path is inconceivable.
> One receives and upholds all the buddhas' dharmas
> and no longer doubts the dharma of emptiness.
>
> Such a person's practice is supreme and marvelous;
> one does not dwell among the outside paths.
> Practicing meditation in peace and without attachment,
> there is no being, no life-span, and no person.
>
> Such a person is never attached or craving;
> the one of single-minded practice has no thoughts.
> Having understood the dharma of no person and no self,
> one never again has any wishes.
>
> One fully understands the dharmas of emptiness;
> toward all things one is never attached.
> Such a person is never attached in thought;
> toward the Buddha one always has a pure and trusting mind.
>
> Such a person never engages in contention;
> the solitary practitioner empties all things.
> Such a person dwells on the buddha-path
> and upholds all the Tathāgata's dharmas.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who practices in solitude has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, one has a pure mind. Second, one is free from desire. Third, one is mindful of all buddhas. Fourth, one has faith in one's practice. Fifth, one does not doubt wisdom. Sixth, one has gratitude toward all buddhas. Seventh, one does not slander the dharma. Eighth, one's practice is tranquil and still. Ninth, one attains settled composure. Tenth, one understands the four kinds of unhindered knowledge. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who practices in solitude." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> One never has the practice of desire;
> one always has a pure mind.
> One then devotes oneself to desirelessness:
> in solitude one practices single-mindedly.
>
> Being mindful of the light of the universal world,
> such a person's faith deepens and matures.
> Toward wisdom one has not a single doubt:
> the Buddha's wisdom is inconceivable.
>
> Toward all buddhas one has gratitude;
> the Buddha never casts aside the dharma.
> One then undertakes the practice of stillness
> and dwells in the dharma of tranquility.
>
> Such a person at once attains unhindered knowledge;
> alone one delights among the trees.
> One then casts aside wealth and gain
> and practices the Way in one place.
>
> Such a person at once has a pure mind;
> one always casts aside all evil deeds.
> Such a person's practice of stillness is unsurpassed;
> one never again doubts the buddhas' wisdom.
>
> Such a person is mindful of the unsurpassed Buddha;
> one has faith in the practices of the gods among gods.
> One no longer doubts the buddhas' wisdom:
> such are the benefits of the practitioner of tranquility.
>
> Toward all the highest beings one has gratitude;
> one never again casts aside the dharma practices.
> The solitary practitioner is tranquil and still:
> such are the benefits of the practitioner of solitude.
>
> One then attains the excellent ground of tranquility and stillness;
> one swiftly understands and realizes all matters.
> One always expounds countless sūtras;
> such a person is never hindered.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who practices in the wilderness has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, the practice of stillness. Second, distancing oneself from the crowd. Third, freedom from contention. Fourth, freedom from anger. Fifth, not entering into various pursuits. Sixth, not entering into others' faults. Seventh, mindfulness of liberation. Eighth, peaceful and stable single-minded practice. Ninth, swiftly attaining the realization of liberation. Tenth, through non-attachment, attaining samādhi. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who practices the honored practice of the wilderness." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> One always has the quality of stillness;
> one distances oneself from the crowd.
> There is never a time of contention;
> alone one carries out one's practice.
>
> One's mind is always free from anger;
> one never turns to attach to any realm.
> One no longer engages in dispute:
> such is the virtue of dwelling in the wilderness.
>
> One then takes up the practice of stillness;
> one is always in solitary practice.
> At once there is the matter of liberation;
> one swiftly crosses over.
>
> Alone one sits in the highest wilderness;
> one always casts aside evil company.
> Such a person never enters into others' faults:
> such are the benefits of sitting among the trees.
>
> One then grows weary of all saṃsāric birth and death;
> such a person has no craving for worldly things.
> There is no fearful matter from the multitude:
> such are the benefits of sitting beneath the trees.
>
> One never engages in contention with others;
> the solitary practitioner always delights in stillness.
> One always guards the body, speech, and mind:
> immeasurable is the virtue of dwelling in the wilderness.
>
> One then attains the highest and most excellent liberation;
> one delights in sitting in the samādhi of tranquility.
> Among the trees one practices the conduct of stillness:
> such is the virtue of the one who dwells in the wilderness.

The Buddha said to the child: "A bodhisattva who goes on alms-round has ten benefits. What are the ten? First, one does not wish to have others know of one's practice. Second, one does not make others aware of one's merits. Third, one does not desire material gain. Fourth, one does not boast, nor does one flatter. Fifth, one dwells on the path of the noble ones. Sixth, one does not speak of one's own merits. Seventh, one does not take one's fill from others. Eighth, arriving at another's home, one is neither delighted nor distressed. Ninth, apart from giving clothing and food, one gives the dharma to people. Tenth, one dwells in virtue without flattery, taking only the gift of dharma. Child! These are the ten benefits of a bodhisattva who practices the alms-round, dwelling in virtue without flattery." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> One does not wish to have others know of one's practice,
> nor is one attached to what one does.
> Whether there is gain or not, the mind is equal;
> such a person dwells in the teaching.
>
> One does not violate the ways of the noble ones
> and has no boasting or flattery.
> One does not speak of one's own goodness
> and also does not speak of others' evil.
>
> One is neither worried nor glad;
> one teaches the dharma, set apart from clothing and food.
> All that one teaches brings joy:
> such is the virtue of one who goes on alms-round.
>
> Not wishing for praise, not seeking fame,
> one always dwells in the four practices of the noble ones.
> Without flattery, without seeking material gain:
> such are the benefits of following the teaching.
>
> Not praising oneself and not speaking evil,
> never from the first speaking evil or using divisive speech,
> hearing of others' merits one always rejoices:
> such is the one on alms-round who knows contentment.
>
> Apart from clothing and food, one skillfully gives the dharma;
> one also does not seek material gain.
> The wholesome teachings make all people rejoice:
> such are the benefits of following the honored teaching.

When the Buddha spoke this sūtra, seventy-two thousand people aroused the aspiration for supreme, perfect enlightenment, and ten thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas.

The Buddha spoke thus. The child Mañjuśrī, and the entire assembly of devas, nāgas, and humans of the world all rejoiced. They came forward and paid respect to the Buddha before departing.
