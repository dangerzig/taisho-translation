# 大方廣華嚴十惡品經
## Vaipulya Avataṃsaka Sūtra on the Chapter of the Ten Evils

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2875

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

"Good men, consider a king who rules over the affairs of his kingdom and possesses abundant maṇi-gems, conch shells, agate, coral, amber, precious jewels, and necklaces: at death he enters the hells. Consider likewise an ordinary person in this world who possesses abundant horses, cattle, sheep, silk, cotton cloth, and great and small grains and beans, all in full measure: at death he enters the hells. Therefore there is suffering."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, it is truly as the sage teaches."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what constitutes goodness?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "If all sentient beings cultivate roots of goodness, there are five: first, not harming sentient beings; second, not engaging in heedlessness; third, not drinking alcohol; fourth, not eating meat; fifth, constantly practicing great compassion. Those who are thus do not sever their roots of goodness."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, as the Buddha has taught, those who accept the Buddha's teaching are not permitted to drink alcohol."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Excellent, excellent! You understand my meaning. All sentient beings who do not drink alcohol are my true children and are not ordinary persons.

"Good men, those who drink alcohol: a ruler may not recognize his ministers, or ministers may not recognize their ruler. A father may not recognize his children, or children may not recognize their father. An elder brother may not recognize his younger brother, or a younger brother may not recognize his elder brother. An elder sister may not recognize her younger sister, or a younger sister may not recognize her elder sister. A husband may not recognize his wife, or a wife may not recognize her husband. A teacher may not recognize his disciples, or disciples may not recognize their teacher. One may not recognize inner and outer family members.

"Good men, such confusion occurs in the present. How much worse in the future!

"Good men, all sentient beings who do not consume alcohol and meat can give rise to the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the great assembly: "Good men, listen well, listen well!

"In the kingdom of Śrāvastī there was a woman named Jīpantā who sold alcohol for a living. Her hairpin fell into the well. Eight myriad arhats surrounded the well and drew up the water to drink. They immediately became confused and intoxicated and did not recognize the Tathāgata.

"At that time, the Tathāgata expounded the essentials of the Dharma for the arhats. Having heard the Dharma, the arhats' intoxication cleared. They came before the Buddha and addressed him: 'World-Honored One, we have cultivated the Way and obtained the four fruits of the path. Why is it that today we did not recognize the Tathāgata?'

"The Buddha told the good men: 'In the kingdom of Śrāvastī there is a woman named Jīpantā who sells alcohol for a living. Her hairpin fell into the well. When you drank the water, you became confused and intoxicated. I have now removed your delusion for you.

"'I now see that you have given rise to minds of fear. The woman Jīpantā, because she sold alcohol for a living, suffers and has fallen into the Saw-Bed Hell. Her body is sawn into a hundred pieces. She is blown into fine dust, and then reassembles and receives her form again. Her body is three feet tall, her complexion dark blue-black. She has no hair on her head, both ears are blocked shut. She has no eyes and no nostrils. Her lower lip droops. Her hands lack ten fingers and her feet lack both soles. All this is because she sold alcohol. How much worse for those who drink it!

"'Although she previously drank alcohol, she also confessed openly and gave rise to deep remorse in her heart, feeling shame and contrition. Moreover, she drank no more. It is like a person whose body encounters a severe illness: encountering a good physician who applies medicine, through the medicine the illness is cured. One who repents is also like this. The heart gives rise to deep remorse, and moreover one transgresses no more. One then attains the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.'"

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the great assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās: "Those who have received the five precepts, those who have received the two hundred fifty precepts, those who have received the complete precepts of deportment, and all who have received precepts are not permitted to drink alcohol. Those who drink alcohol commit a pārājika offense. Bhikṣus and bhikṣuṇīs who commit this offense immediately enter the hells. Ordinary persons who commit this incur a duṣkṛta offense and for eighty thousand kalpas enter the hells."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, alcohol itself has no life. Why does the Tathāgata admonish that alcohol leads to suffering?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "In the kingdom of Śrāvastī there was Aṅgulimāla. Drinking alcohol in a state of intoxication, he committed sexual misconduct with his mother and slew his father. His mother then had illicit relations with another man, and he took up a knife and killed her. Therefore I now admonish that alcohol leads to suffering."

At that time, the World-Honored One told Kāśyapa Bodhisattva: "Sons of the Buddha, I admonish all laypeople and monastics. Those who receive my precepts are not permitted to pour alcohol for others. They are not permitted to visit drinking establishments. They are not permitted to forcibly urge others to drink. They are not permitted to jointly brew alcohol. Śrāvaka disciples are likewise. Bodhisattva-mahāsattvas restrain the mind. Śrāvakas restrain the body.

"Good men, one is not permitted to pour alcohol for a bhikṣu. If one does so, for five hundred lifetimes one will be without arms. If one jointly brews alcohol with a bhikṣu, for five hundred lifetimes one will be deaf, the ears sealed shut, constantly unable to hear any voice or speech.

"If a bhikṣu visits a drinking establishment, for five hundred lifetimes he will be mute, the mouth unable to speak.

"If one forcibly urges a bhikṣu to drink, one falls into the Knee-Severing Hell. Its dimensions extend eighty thousand yojanas in all directions, perfectly equal. Within it are strongmen numbering five hundred, who wield swords and blades to sever one's two knees. Those who forcibly urge a bhikṣu to drink receive the same treatment."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what retribution does one who eats meat receive?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Excellent, excellent! All sentient beings who do not eat meat are my posthumous children and are not ordinary persons.

"Good men, all sentient beings who accept the Great Vehicle Mahāparinirvāṇa: good men, all sentient beings, even for the duration of a single kalpa, are not permitted to eat meat.

"World-Honored One, into which hell do those who eat meat fall?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Those who eat meat fall into the Avīci Hell. Its dimensions extend eighty thousand yojanas in all directions, perfectly equal. On all four sides are gates. Outside each gate blaze fierce fires. East, west, south, and north, fire penetrates throughout. It is surrounded by iron walls, covered with iron nets. Its ground is red-hot iron. Fire from above reaches below; fire from below reaches above. Iron shackles, iron fetters, iron bits, and iron pokers are held in the fire and applied to them. Those who eat meat are driven into this hell to suffer great torment.

"If the heart gives rise to deep remorse and one feels shame and contrition, and moreover one eats no more: it is like turbid water into which a bright pearl is placed; by the pearl's power, the water immediately becomes clear. Like clouds and mist dispersing, the moon becomes clear and bright. One who commits evil yet is able to repent is also like this."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "All sentient beings who eat meat sever the seeds of great compassion. Those who do not eat meat have great merit, a hundred thousand ten-thousandfold beyond comparison."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how many places are distinguished for those who boil meat, roast meat, chop meat, and kill living beings?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Those who boil meat fall into the Cauldron Hell. Its dimensions extend five hundred yojanas. Within it is water; beneath it is fire. The fire is maintained and burns until the water seethes and boils. Those who boiled meat are driven into this hell to suffer great torment.

"Those who roasted meat fall into the Roasting-Bed Hell. Its dimensions extend eighty thousand yojanas. Above is an iron bed; beneath is fire. The fire is maintained and burns. Those who roasted meat are made to lie upon it. Their flesh dries and chars as they suffer great torment.

"Those who chopped meat fall into the Pounding-Mortar Hell. Within it are strongmen numbering five hundred. They chop them into ten thousand pieces, blow them into fine dust, and they once again receive great torment."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, those who eat meat are not disciples of the Tathāgata. They are members of the retinue of non-Buddhists."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Those who eat meat do not perceive, do not know, do not hear, and do not see what meat they are eating. A ruler may eat his minister's flesh, or a minister may eat his ruler's flesh. A child may eat his father's flesh, or a father may eat his child's flesh. A younger brother may eat his elder brother's flesh, or an elder brother may eat his younger brother's flesh. A younger sister may eat her elder sister's flesh, or an elder sister may eat her younger sister's flesh. A wife may eat her husband's flesh, or a husband may eat his wife's flesh."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Those who eat meat are in fact eating the flesh of their parents and family."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, into which hell do those who eat meat fall?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Those who eat meat fall into the Excrement Hell. Its dimensions extend eighty thousand yojanas in all directions, perfectly equal. Within it is excrement ten thousand fathoms deep. Those who eat meat are driven into this hell to suffer great torment. For five million lifetimes there is no prospect of release.

"Good men, those who eat meat are like a pack of dogs fighting over a bone, each greedy for more. Those who eat meat are also like this.

"Good men, to chop meat is to chop one's own father. To slice meat is to slice one's father's flesh, to slaughter one's own mother. Consider: when a father dies, he will certainly become a cow or sheep. One takes up a knife and slaughters it, and it is one's own father. All sentient beings' minds are confused. Those who eat meat are eating their father's flesh. Those who gnaw bones are gnawing their father's bones. Those who drink meat broth are drinking their father's blood.

"Good men, all sentient beings who have any sense of shame should not eat meat. Although one may have previously eaten meat, if one can openly confess and give rise to deep remorse, one also attains the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, we humbly pray the Tathāgata will explain for us: into which hell do those who break the fast fall?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Those who break the fast fall into the Hungry Ghost Hell. The hungry ghosts within are five hundred yojanas tall. Their throats are as thin as needles. Their heads are as large as Mount Tai. Their hands are like dragon claws. In the morning they consume three thousand; in the evening they consume eight hundred. With a single cry, thirty thousand are driven forth. Those who broke the fast are driven into this hell to suffer great torment.

"Departing from this hell, they are driven around a great mountain. The mountain is the color of scarlet. Those who broke the fast are driven to press their backs against it. Their flesh dries and chars as they suffer great torment. Departing from this suffering, they are reborn in another form to receive yet more.

"Good men, if one has finished keeping the fast but wraps up the fasting food and gives it to parents, brothers, rulers, ministers, teachers, elders, friends, or acquaintances, in a future life one falls into the Iron-Wheel Hell. It enters through the left armpit and exits through the right side, while molten copper is poured into the mouth. Those who receive such fasting food meet the same fate."

The Buddha said: "Those who eat chicken meat fall into the hells. Three persons share the retribution, meeting at one-and-a-half times the original amount, and together enter the hells."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, it is truly as the sage teaches! It is truly as the sage teaches!"

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, we humbly pray the Tathāgata will explain for us: what merit does one who abstains from alcohol and meat obtain?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Suppose there were a person who, with elephants, horses, cattle, sheep, lapis lazuli, precious jewels, necklaces, kingdoms, wives, and children, used all of these to give in charity. This still would not equal a person who can abstain from alcohol and meat. A hundred thousand ten-thousandfold, it would not equal one part.

"Setting that aside: suppose there were a person who, with a hundred thousand taels of gold filling the entire great trichiliocosm, used all of it to give in charity. This still would not equal a person who can abstain from alcohol and meat. A hundred thousand ten-thousandfold, it would not equal one part.

"Setting that aside: suppose there were a person who could cast gold into human figures, numbering in the hundreds, and used all of them to give in charity. This still would not equal a person who can abstain from alcohol and meat. A hundred thousand ten-thousandfold, it would not equal one part.

"Setting that aside: suppose there were a person who fashioned banners, flowers, and jeweled canopies filling the entire great trichiliocosm. This still would not equal a person who can abstain from alcohol and meat. A hundred thousand ten-thousandfold, it would not equal one part.

"Setting that aside: suppose there were a person who constructed great stūpas and jeweled pagodas, their eaves touching one upon another like stalks of rice, hemp, bamboo, and reeds, reaching up to the Brahmā heavens. This would not equal a person who can abstain from alcohol and meat. A hundred thousand ten-thousandfold, it would not equal one part.

"Good men, those who do not eat meat are bodhisattvas in the present life and are not ordinary persons. Good men, those who do not eat meat constantly have no thought of meat. Those who eat meat, seeing living beings, give rise to the thought of harm and immediately think of meat. For this reason..."

Kāśyapa Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, it is truly as the sage..."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "All sentient beings are like a destitute, solitary old person who late in life begets a single, beloved child. Holding two balls of rice, he gives them both to the child, as if exchanging the rice for gold. All sentient beings are also like this: like a kind father, yet sentient beings do not entrust themselves to me."
