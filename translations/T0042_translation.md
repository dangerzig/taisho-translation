# 鐵城泥犁經
## The Iron City Niraya Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0042

Translated by Zhu Tanwulan of the Eastern Jin dynasty

---

## Fascicle 1

### Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Iron City Niraya

When the Buddha was dwelling at the Jetavana-ārāma of Anāthapiṇḍada in Śrāvastī, he admonished the various śramaṇas, saying: "With my divine eye I observe all people under heaven: their deaths and births, the beautiful and ugly, the noble and the lowly, those who after death attain a good path and those who attain an evil path. Those who in the world commit evil with their bodies, speak evil with their mouths, and contemplate evil in their minds, who delight in slaughtering animals for sacrificial offerings to ghosts and spirits; when they die, they shall enter the niraya. Those whose bodies constantly practice good, whose mouths constantly speak good, and whose minds constantly contemplate good; when they die, they ascend to heaven."

The Buddha said: "People are like bubbles formed by rain falling from above; as one bubble breaks, another forms. People living in the world: those who are born and those who die are like bubbles arising in an instant. With his divine eye the Buddha observes all people under heaven: among the dead, those who ascend to heaven and those who enter the niraya, the poor and the wealthy, the noble and the lowly, and all the good and evil that people do."

The Buddha said: "I know all of this. It is like torches burning on both sides of a city gate on a dark night: among the tens of thousands of people passing in and out, all those entering and exiting through the firelight are visible to one looking on from the darkness."

The Buddha said: "With my divine eye I observe all people under heaven; those who after death ascend to heaven and those who enter the niraya; as one standing in the darkness sees those passing through the firelight, or as one standing atop a high tower, gazing down at the thousands of households below, can see all the homes from above."

The Buddha said: "I see all people under heaven; those who after death ascend to heaven and those who enter the niraya; as one looking down from a high tower upon all the households."

The Buddha said: "It is like a person sailing a boat through clear water, able to see all the fish and stones within. With his divine eye the Buddha observes all people under heaven; those who after death ascend to heaven and those who enter the niraya; as one sees the fish and stones in clear water. If one strings a bright moon-pearl on five-colored thread, whoever looks upon the pearl can see all five colors and discern how each passes through and is strung together with the pearl."

The Buddha said: "I see the origins and all the transformations of good and evil of all people under heaven, as one sees the colors in a pearl."

The Buddha said: "I see those people under heaven who do not show filial piety to their parents, do not attend upon śramaṇas and religious practitioners, do not show reverence to elders, do not fear the prohibitions and precepts of officials, and do not fear this world or the world to come; who feel neither dread nor terror. When such people die, they immediately enter the niraya and come face to face with King Yāma, having abandoned the good and returned to evil. The niraya wardens are called Pāṅ. The Pāṅ immediately conduct the person forward to Yāma's presence. The niraya warden declares: 'This person, while living as a human being in the world, did not show filial piety to his parents, did not attend upon śramaṇas and religious practitioners, did not show reverence to elders, did not practice generosity, did not fear this world or the world to come, and did not fear the prohibitions of officials. May Yāma pronounce judgment upon the transgressions of this person.'

"Yāma then summons the person forward to stand before him and says: 'When you were a human being, you did not think upon how your parents raised you; placing themselves upon the wet ground so that you might lie on the dry, nursing and nourishing you until you were fully grown. Why did you not show filial piety to your parents?' The person replies to Yāma: 'I was truly foolish and arrogant.' Yāma says: 'The one who has placed you in this punishment is not your parents, not heaven, not the emperor, not śramaṇas or religious practitioners; it is the fault of your own deeds. You must receive the consequences yourself. This is the first question.'

"The second question: 'Have you not seen people in the extremity of severe illness, utterly wasted and enfeebled, their hands and feet incapable of movement?' The person says: 'I have indeed seen this.' Yāma says: 'Why then did you not reform yourself and practice good?' The person says: 'I was truly foolish and arrogant.' Yāma says: 'This fault is not heaven's, not your parents', not the emperor's, not śramaṇas' or religious practitioners'; it is the fault of your own deeds. You must receive the consequences yourself.'

"Yāma's third question: 'Have you not seen men and women in the world in their old age; their eyes unable to see, their ears unable to hear, walking with the aid of a staff, their black hair turned white, no longer as they were in youth?' The person says: 'I have indeed seen old people walking with a staff.' Yāma says: 'At such a time, why did you not reform yourself and practice good?' The person says: 'I was truly foolish and arrogant.' Yāma says: 'This fault is not your parents', not heaven's, not the emperor's, not śramaṇas' or religious practitioners'; it is the fault of your own deeds. You must receive the consequences yourself.'

"Yāma's fourth question: 'When you were in the world, did you not see men and women who had died; after one day, two days, up to seven days, their bodies rotting and decomposing, their form and appearance ruined and decayed, eaten by worms and ants, repulsive to all who beheld them? Having seen this, why did you not reform yourself and practice good?' The person says: 'I have seen this. I was truly foolish and arrogant.' Yāma says: 'In all your conduct, why did you not rectify your mind, rectify your speech, and rectify your actions? This fault too is not your parents', not heaven's, not the emperor's, not śramaṇas' or religious practitioners'; it is the fault of your own deeds. You must receive the consequences yourself.'

"Yāma's fifth question: 'When you were a human, did you not see in the world officials arresting bandits, murderers, and thieves; binding their hands behind their backs and sending them to prison, where they were flogged, tortured, and interrogated, some being led outside the city and beaten to death on the road, some being dismembered while still alive? Did you not see this?' The person says: 'I did indeed see this.' Yāma says: 'Why then did you not practice generosity and do good? When you were a human, why did you not rectify your conduct, rectify your speech, and rectify your mind?' The person says: 'I was truly foolish and arrogant.' Yāma says: 'This fault too is not your parents', not heaven's, not the emperor's, not śramaṇas', not religious practitioners'; it is the fault of your own deeds. You must receive the consequences yourself.'

"When the questioning is complete, the niraya warden seizes the person and drags them away, conducting them to an iron city; this is the first niraya. The city of the Avīci niraya has four gates and measures four thousand li in circumference. Within it are great caldrons forty li in length. The niraya wardens pierce people with forks and thrust them inside. Everywhere within, beyond all count, there is fire; all who behold it from afar tremble with terror. Tens of thousands upon thousands of people are thrust inside in this manner by the niraya wardens; day and night they cannot leave. All the gates are shut and cannot be opened; no one can get out. People remain there for tens of thousands upon thousands of years. The fire never goes out and the people never die.

"After a very long time, people see the east gate open by itself from afar and all rush toward it hoping to get out. Just as they reach the gate, the gate closes again, and all those trying to escape fight and struggle with one another at the gateway. After a long time they see the west gate open from afar and all rush toward it, but the gate closes again and people fight with one another at the gate. After a long time they see the south gate open and all rush toward it, but the gate closes again and people fight with one another at the gate. After a long time all four gates open once more; people manage to get out, thinking they have escaped; but they enter the second niraya, the Kūyāna.

"Wherever their feet touch the ground they are scorched; when they lift their feet, the flesh grows back. Some run east, some west, some south, some north; the ground all around blazes with intense heat. After tens of thousands upon thousands of years this comes to an end, and they think they have escaped.

"They enter the third niraya, the Mīlimadata. Within it are insects named Kūṭacañcu, iron-colored, with black heads and feet. Seeing people from afar, the insects all swarm toward them and peck away until all flesh and bone is consumed. This continues for tens of thousands upon thousands of years before it ends.

"Thinking they have escaped, they enter the fourth niraya, the Śūlata. Within it are mountains whose stones are sharp as knives. People scramble up to the peaks and scramble back down, all seeking escape, not knowing which way to turn; their feet are all sliced and flayed. The stones on the ground are all like sharp blades. This again continues for tens of thousands upon thousands of years before it ends.

"Thinking they have escaped, they enter the fifth niraya, the Āyapotahuan. Within it there are hot winds; they encounter them and cannot escape. Those people seek death but cannot die, seek life but cannot live. Thus after a long time; tens of thousands upon thousands of years; they finally emerge.

"Thinking they have escaped, they enter the sixth niraya, the Āyucāoponīhuan. It is filled with many trees all covered with thorns; between the trees dwell demons. Those who enter find demons whose heads blaze with fire and whose mouths blaze with fire, whose bodies bristle with sixteen spikes. Seeing people coming from afar, the demons fly into great rage. The fire and all sixteen spikes are plain to see; the spikes pierce through people's bodies and tear them apart as if consuming them. People run in all directions trying to escape but constantly encounter these demons. This continues for tens of thousands upon thousands of years before they finally emerge.

"Thinking they have escaped, they enter the seventh niraya, the Pāṃśikava. Within it are insects named Chún. When people enter, these insects fly in through their mouths and consume their bodies from within. People run about in utter exhaustion, but the insects do not cease eating; people flee in all four directions seeking escape but cannot escape. This continues for tens of thousands upon thousands of years before they finally emerge.

"Thinking they have escaped, they enter the eighth niraya, the Vaitaraṇī. Within it flows a river, and people all fall into the water. Along the banks there are thorns and brambles. The water is hotter than any boiling cauldron in the world; boiling and surging, it scalds and cooks all people until they are utterly done. Trying to climb out onto the bank, they are met by demons wielding spears who stab them through the abdomen and thrust them back in so they cannot emerge. All are swept downstream by the current.

"Then there are demons with hooks who snag people out, questioning them: 'Where have you come from? This is what I ask you.' The person says: 'I do not know where I came from, nor do I know where I should go. I am only hungry and thirsty and seeking food and drink.' The demon says: 'I will give you food.' It then takes a hook, hooks open both the upper and lower jaw, forcing the mouth wide apart, and pours molten copper into the person's mouth, burning and scorching everything within. Thus they seek death but cannot die, seek life but cannot live. Because during their lifetimes, when they were human beings, they had committed the gravest of evils, they seek release but cannot find release.

"All the people in the various nirayās finally get out, thinking they have escaped, but they re-enter the seventh niraya. The demons question them as they pass back through: 'You have gone; why have you returned?' They re-enter the fifth, then return to the fourth, from the fourth return to the third, return to the second, and return to the first, the Avīci niraya.

"Before they arrive, people see the iron city from afar and all rejoice, crying out in great jubilation, proclaiming 'Ten thousand years!' Yāma hears this and asks the niraya wardens: 'What is that sound?' The wardens reply: 'Those who are crying out are the ones who have previously passed through the nirayās.' Yāma says: 'These are all people who did not show filial piety to their parents, did not fear heaven, did not fear the emperor, did not attend upon śramaṇas and religious practitioners, and did not fear the precepts and prohibitions.'

"Yāma then summons the people forward again and addresses them: 'Since you show no fear of what Yāma says; you are now released and may go. If you are to be reborn as a human child, you must practice filial piety and serve your elders; you must fear the emperor's prohibitions and precepts; you must attend upon śramaṇas and religious practitioners; and you must rectify your mind, your speech, and your body. When people live in the world, their transgressions are small and light; but when they die and descend below the earth, the niraya punishment is great and heavy. If you encounter śramaṇas and religious practitioners, you should attend upon them and follow their teachings. If one attains arhatship, all the paths leading to the nirayās are closed and sealed.'

"When all the questioning is complete, all the people in the nirayās are able to leave. Outside the city they all die once more. Those who die; because in their former lives and past existences, even though they committed evil when human, they retained some small measure of good; upon emerging from the nirayās, are all reborn in a good path. People who emerge from the nirayās, having each rectified their minds, speech, and conduct, do not return to the nirayās. The nirayās no longer summon them. Having experienced the ugliness, poison, and suffering of the niraya, each one reflects and resolves that it is right to do good."

The Buddha said: "Among people who die and enter the niraya, only lords, kings, śramaṇas, and religious practitioners are granted audience with Yāma; all other ordinary people simply enter with the multitude. Yāma is the name of the king of the hells."
