# 佛說沙曷比丘功德經
## Sūtra on the Merits of the Bhikṣu Svāgata, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0501

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Faju (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas. At that time, in the land of Sūryaka, there was a poor man who made his living by going from place to place cutting children's hair. All those whose hair he cut agreed: "When the wheat ripens, we shall each pay you one bushel of wheat." Shortly after he had departed and not yet gone far, he met an old friend along the road and brought him back. He sought wheat so that he could buy wine to drink with his friend. But he went everywhere, and no one returned his wheat. Thereupon he gave rise to an angry thought, saying: "When my life ends, I wish to become a great and powerful nāga dragon, and I shall destroy this country." After his death, his soul indeed returned as a nāga, and the country thereafter suffered from storms and untimely rains year after year, and the five grains were ruined.

The Buddha, thinking of the people of this land suffering from famine, dispatched the bhikṣu Svāgata to go and subdue the dragon. When the nāga saw the bhikṣu coming, it gave rise to an evil intention, wishing to destroy the country and kill the bhikṣu Svāgata. Svāgata then transformed his alms-bowl by expanding it to cover the entire country. The nāga rained down upon it, thinking the country was submerged. But the bhikṣu, by the Buddha's spiritual power, caused the nāga to see that the people were safe and sound as before. The nāga again gave rise to anger and sent down snow. The bhikṣu received it in his bowl. When the snow grew extreme, the bhikṣu swept it aside with his hand, piling it in one place like a mountain. The bhikṣu then entered the nāga's lair, and the nāga came out. The bhikṣu came out, and the nāga went in. The bhikṣu went in again. This went back and forth many times until the nāga was exhausted and finally stopped. It knelt and asked: "What manner of deity are you, that you trouble me like this?" The bhikṣu said: "I am a disciple of the Buddha." The nāga said: "I wish to take refuge in you." The bhikṣu replied: "I have a great teacher: the Buddha, most honored in the three realms. You should take refuge in him." The nāga asked: "Where is the Buddha?" He answered: "The Buddha is in the land of Śrāvastī." The nāga said: "Allow me to follow you, O practitioner of the path." The bhikṣu said: "If you wish to come, that is good!" He then placed the nāga inside a basket.

When the people saw the bhikṣu capture the nāga in this way, they all rejoiced and asked: "O practitioner of the path, what manner of great deity are you, that you have subdued the affliction of our country?" He replied: "I am a disciple of the Buddha." The people asked: "Can the Buddha be seen?" He answered: "If you wish to see the Buddha, please wait for my return." At that time it was approaching midday, and while on the road he received alms. Among the people, some gave him rice and some gave him wine. The bhikṣu accepted and consumed both, and becoming drunk from the wine, he lay down to sleep under a tree, with the nāga's bowl and kāṣāya robe each in a separate place.

The Buddha then smiled, and light of five colors issued forth. Ānanda straightened his robes, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "The Buddha does not smile without reason; a smile must have a purpose!" The Buddha told Ānanda: "Do you see the bhikṣu Svāgata?" Ānanda said: "I do not see him." The Buddha said: "He is now lying drunk under that tree."

At that time, the one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas spoke to one another, saying: "The bhikṣu Svāgata has already attained arhatship. Why is he lying drunk?" The Buddha, knowing that all had doubts in their minds, then explained four things: "First, an arhat cannot know without entering samādhi. Second, an arhat cannot manifest supernatural powers at will. Third, an arhat cannot forcefully refuse alms-food offered to him. Fourth, within his body there are still worms. By these four things, an arhat falls short of a Buddha." At that time, the ten thousand bodhisattvas all shifted their aspiration, wishing to become arhats instead.

The Buddha dispatched Mahāmaudgalyāyana to go to the bhikṣu Svāgata and instructed him to bring the nāga back. The nāga prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and the Buddha then taught it about the causes and conditions of its past lives from beginning to end. The nāga's mind was immediately awakened. It received the five precepts, practiced the ten wholesome actions, and immediately attained the fruit of stream-entry. It made obeisance to the Buddha and departed.

The Buddha then spoke of the bhikṣu Svāgata's subtle and wondrous merits. Ānanda joined his palms and addressed the Buddha: "The bhikṣu Svāgata drank wine and lay drunk, yet the Buddha speaks of his merits as being so subtle and wondrous?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "An arhat does not experience hunger or thirst. For three reasons, he appeared to be lying drunk: first, because the Buddha wished to open and instruct the minds of the bodhisattvas; second, because he did not wish to reject the generous intention of those who offered alms; third, because he feared that disciples who had not yet attained the path might drink wine and thereby commit many transgressions. For this reason, the strictest precept was established. Though the bhikṣu Svāgata drank wine, in truth he was not intoxicated." The bodhisattvas and the fourfold assembly of disciples, hearing the Buddha's teaching, all rose, straightened their robes, and made obeisance to the Buddha.

The bhikṣu Svāgata then came forward, knelt, and addressed the Buddha: "The king and people of the land of Sūryaka wish to see the Buddha." The Buddha silently consented. The bhikṣu Svāgata then, receiving the Buddha's instruction, in the span of a finger-snap returned to the land of Sūryaka. The king and people, seeing the bhikṣu, all rejoiced. Some prostrated, some knelt, and some merely joined their palms. The bhikṣu Svāgata announced: "The Buddha will come here tomorrow." When the king heard that the Buddha was coming, he was overjoyed and made preparations, sweeping and sprinkling the four main roads and broadly setting up curtains and canopies.

The next day, the Buddha came together with the one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, traveling through the sky. Beneath the Buddha's feet there appeared natural lotus flowers. The king, ministers, and people all took flowers and incense and went outside the city to welcome the Buddha, prostrating with their five limbs on the ground, making obeisance with their foreheads to the earth. When the Buddha and the bhikṣus arrived at the palace, there naturally appeared a lion throne, spread with fine silk, adorned with canopies of the seven treasures, draped in garlands of five colors. The king set out offerings and personally served the food. After the meal, washing water was given and blessings were chanted. The Buddha then taught the king and people about the causes and conditions of the nāga from beginning to end. The king and people's minds were opened. They immediately received the five precepts and practiced the ten wholesome actions. Some attained the fruit of stream-entry, some the fruit of once-returning, some the fruit of non-returning, and some attained arhatship; the number was beyond reckoning.

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, the fourfold assembly of disciples, devas, nāgas, and spirits all joyfully practiced it.
