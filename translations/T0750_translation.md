# 沙彌羅經
## Sūtra of Śāmaṇera

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0750

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

In the past there was a young boy named Śāmaṇera. At just seven years of age, his mind was drawn to the path and its virtue, so he followed a śramaṇa and became his disciple. He dwelt in the mountains, serving his teacher in all that was needed, reciting and studying the sūtras and Dharma, his mind never slackening. At the age of eight he attained arhatship. With the eye of the path he could see through all things without limit. His ears could hear all that happened in the heavens above and the earth below, all the good and evil that was done. His body could fly through the air, and wherever he wished to go he could arrive. He could divide his single body and transform it into ten thousand bodies. He manifested transformations freely, with nothing he could not do. He knew his own past lives, where he had been born, and likewise knew the past lives of all people, creatures, insects, and crawling things. Sitting in meditation and seeing his past lives, he found that he had been a son to five mothers, and at this he laughed.

His teacher turned to him and asked: "Śāmaṇera, what are you laughing at? Here in these mountains there is no singing or dancing. Are you laughing at me?"

Śāmaṇera said: "I would not dare laugh at my teacher. I was laughing at myself! A single spirit took on bodies and became a son to five mothers. Each of these five mothers grieves for me day and night, overcome with sorrow and anguish, unable to stop. They constantly speak of longing for their child and have never once forgotten. I reflected that a single self caused grief in five households. This is why I laughed; I would not dare laugh at my teacher.

"When I was a son to my first mother, there was a neighbor who also bore a son on the same day as me. After I died, the boy born on the same day went out walking. My mother, seeing him, said: 'How I grieve for my son! If he were at home, he too would be walking about like this!' Overcome with sorrow, her tears fell like rain.

"When I was a son to my second mother, I died young. When my mother saw another woman nursing her child, she thought of nursing me, and was overcome with grief and sorrow.

"When I was a son to my third mother, I was just ten years old when I died again. At mealtimes my mother would weep: 'If my son were here, we would eat together. He left me and died! Why must I eat alone?' Choking with sobs, she cried out to heaven, uttering words of longing for her child.

"When I was a son to my fourth mother, I died young. My mother saw other boys my age, at the time when matchmakers were arranging marriages, and in grief said: 'If my son were alive, he too would be taking a wife! What wrong have I committed, that my son was taken from me?'

"When I was a son to my fifth mother, at just seven years old, I loved the path and left home. I left my mother and followed my teacher into the mountains to seek the path. With single-minded concentration and dhyāna meditation, I attained the path of the arhat. Day after day my mother weeps and longs for me: 'I bore a single son, and he went with his teacher to study the path. I do not know where he is. Is he hungry or thirsty? Cold or hot? Is he now dead or alive?'

"And so these five mothers all came together in one place, each grieving and each speaking of longing for her child. Facing one another, they wept without being able to stop. My single spirit passed through the wombs of five mothers to become their son. Depending on both parents, I took form and became a person, yet I caused five mothers to weep until they nearly lost their minds. Each longed for my self to the point of wishing to kill herself. This is why I laughed!

"I reflect upon the world: the net of desire, the causes and conditions, the sin and merit of birth and death, the root of action from which evil enters the hells and good conduct leads to rebirth in the heavens. I feared the suffering of the world, so I left home and entered the mountains. Through diligent dhyāna concentration, I attained the path and ascended to the state of a transcendent being. Seeing the hungry ghosts, the hells, and the places of suffering among animals, I felt terror on their behalf. I grieve for my five mothers, who cannot free themselves, and who also worry about me. What I seek and wish for, may my vow be fulfilled as I have spoken: to be forever separated from birth and death, to sever the root of embodiment. Like a person who does not plant a seed, I shall enter nirvāṇa."

When the teacher had finished speaking, he rose into the sky.
