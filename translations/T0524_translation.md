# 佛為優填王說王法政論經
## Sūtra on the Treatise of Royal Governance Spoken by the Buddha for King Udayana

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0524

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Amoghavajra (不空)

---

At that time, King Udayana was alone in an empty, quiet room, sitting in seclusion, when this thought arose: "How can I know the true faults and true virtues of rulers? If I can know them, I should abandon the faults and cultivate the virtues. Is there a śramaṇa or a practitioner of pure conduct who understands these things and can explain them to me at length?" After thinking for some time, he reflected: "Only my World-Honored One, the Great Teacher of the Three Realms, possessed of omniscience, surely knows the true faults and true virtues of all rulers. I should now go to the World-Honored One's presence and ask about this matter."

"Therefore I have now come to the Buddha's presence. May the Tathāgata please explain to me: What are the true faults of rulers? What are the true virtues of rulers?" Having made this request,

the World-Honored One then told King Udayana: "Great king, you should now understand the faults of a king, the virtues of a king, the gates of a king's decline, the lovable qualities of a king, and what gives rise to the lovable qualities of a king.

"What are the faults of a king? Great king, you should know that the faults of a king are briefly of ten kinds. If a king possesses these faults, then even if he has a great treasury, great ministers, and a great army, he cannot be relied upon. What are the ten? First, his lineage is not exalted. Second, he lacks autonomy. Third, his nature is violent and wicked. Fourth, he is fiercely wrathful. Fifth, his generosity is meager. Sixth, he accepts flattering and deceitful words. Seventh, his actions do not accord with the institutions of the ancient kings. Eighth, he has no regard for wholesome dharmas. Ninth, he does not discern right from wrong, the superior from the inferior. Tenth, he is given wholly to self-indulgence and devotes himself to heedlessness.

"What does it mean that a king's lineage is not exalted? It means that there is a king born from a lesser minister or a commoner, not from an established noble house, who has inherited the throne. This is called a lineage that is not exalted.

"What does it mean that a king lacks autonomy? It means that there is a king who is controlled by his great ministers, prime ministers, and officials, and cannot act as he wishes. His actions are always checked and restrained, and he cannot enjoy the five exquisite pleasures or amuse himself as he desires. This is called a king who lacks autonomy.

"What does it mean that a king's nature is violent and wicked? It means that there is a king who, when he sees his ministers or other persons commit even the slightest offense, immediately utters harsh and coarse words to their faces, flaring with anger, frowning and demoting them. Or if not to their face, then behind their back and in the presence of others, he carries out the same sort of denouncing and reviling. Sometimes his anger does not last long, but sometimes it persists for a great while without abating. Such violence to the face and violence behind the back is called a king whose nature is violent and wicked.

"What does it mean that a king is fiercely wrathful? It means that there is a king who, when he sees his ministers commit even a minor offense or slight transgression, immediately strips them of their fiefs and emoluments, takes away their wives and concubines, and inflicts severe punishments upon them. This is called a king who is fiercely wrathful.

"What does it mean that a king's generosity is meager? It means that there is a king whose ministers are close attendants and guards, and though they serve with the utmost purity and devotion, the king only consoles them with feeble and soft words. When it comes to bestowing rank, salary, rewards, and honors, he cannot fulfill them properly or follow established norms. He either reduces what is owed, or delays it, only granting it afterwards. This is called a king whose generosity is meager.

"What does it mean that a king accepts flattering and deceitful words? It means that there is a king who, among his ministers, sees those who are in truth not loyal in governance, not well-versed in laws and statutes, who secretly plot among the inner circle, who flatter and take sides, who do not implement good governance, and who are jealous of the worthy and able. The king trusts and follows the counsel of such persons, and because of this, royal affairs, treasury, reputation, and good governance all decline. This is called a king who accepts flattering and deceitful words.

"What does it mean that a king does not accord with the institutions of the ancient kings? It means that there is a king who cannot carefully investigate and properly evaluate his ministers regarding the various affairs and matters of state. He entrusts responsibilities to those who are not capable and does not entrust them to those who are capable. He rewards those who should be punished and punishes those who should be rewarded. Furthermore, when his ministers are assembled at great court audiences, before another discussion has concluded, they interrupt with their own words, showing no respect or restraint, and raise objections and remonstrances. They cannot follow the law and properly carry it out, and they cannot rightly abide by the commands of the former kings. This is called not according with the institutions established by the ancient kings.

"What does it mean that a king has no regard for wholesome dharmas? It means that there is a king who does not believe in cause and effect, does not realize the future rewards of good and evil karma among humans and devas, and acts as he pleases in creating evil deeds of body, speech, and mind. He cannot, at the proper time, give generously, cultivate merit, observe fasting, study the precepts, receive dhāraṇī and the Dharma-gate of abhiṣeka. He does not cultivate the four immeasurables for the broad benefit of all. This is called a king who has no regard for wholesome dharmas.

"What does it mean that a king does not discern right from wrong, the superior from the inferior? It means that there is a king whose mind is inverted regarding his great ministers, prime ministers, and officials. He does not properly know the distinctions among loyalty and trustworthiness, skills and abilities, and wisdom. Because of his ignorance, where there is no loyalty, he imagines loyalty. Where there is no skill, he imagines skill. Where there is poor judgment, he imagines good judgment. Where there is good judgment, he imagines poor judgment. Furthermore, when his ministers have grown old and frail, having served in close attendance for a long time, knowing them to be powerless, he fails to respect or care for them, does not bestow rank or salary, does not reward their service, allows others to humiliate them, and abandons them without inquiry. This is called a king who does not discern right from wrong, the superior from the inferior.

"What does it mean that a king is wholly given to self-indulgence and devoted to heedlessness? It means that there is a king who is utterly absorbed in and attached to the five exquisite pleasures, addicted to amusement and play. He cannot from time to time exercise vigilance and skillful effort, do what should be done, and console his ministers. This is called being wholly given to self-indulgence and devoted to heedlessness.

"If a king possesses these ten faults, then even if he has a great treasury, great ministers, and a great army, before long his kingdom will naturally fall into disaster and disorder, and he cannot be relied upon. Great king, you should know that of these ten faults, the first pertains to the fault of the king's lineage, and the remaining nine pertain to the faults of the king's own character.

"What are the virtues of a king? Great king, the virtues of a king are briefly of ten kinds: First, his lineage is exalted. Second, he has great autonomy. Third, his nature is not violent. Fourth, his anger is mild. Fifth, his generosity is strong. Sixth, he accepts upright words. Seventh, his actions are carefully considered and accord with the former teachings. Eighth, he cherishes wholesome dharmas. Ninth, he is skilled in discernment. Tenth, he does not indulge himself and does not practice heedlessness.

"What does it mean that a king's lineage is exalted? It means that there is a king who, through planting good roots in the past and by the power of great vows, was born into a royal family, inherited the throne, and nurtures the myriad people with benevolence, having pure faith in the Three Treasures. This is called a king whose lineage is exalted.

"What does it mean that a king has great autonomy? It means that there is a king who freely acts as he wishes, enjoying the five exquisite pleasures in amusement and play. Whatever rewards and grants he issues are made according to his will, and the edicts he promulgates to all officials are carried out without obstruction. This is called a king who has great autonomy.

"What does it mean that a king's nature is not violent? It means that there is a king who, even when he sees his ministers commit offenses or minor transgressions, can tolerate and forbear, not immediately demoting them, not uttering harsh words, not flaring with anger to their faces, and not harboring resentment secretly. This is called a king whose nature is not violent.

"What does it mean that a king's anger is mild? It means that there is a king whose ministers, even when they commit major offenses or great transgressions, does not strip them entirely of their fiefs and emoluments, does not take away their wives and concubines, and does not inflict severe punishments upon them. Instead, he measures the severity of the offense and extends clemency accordingly. This is called a king whose anger is mild.

"What does it mean that a king's generosity is strong? It means that there is a king whose ministers are close attendants and guards, and when they serve with pure and devoted hearts, the king promptly and fully consoles them with proper and kind words, bestows honors and rewards without causing them loss or delay, hardship or resentment. He is easy to approach and not difficult to serve. This is called a king whose generosity is strong.

"What does it mean that a king accepts upright words? It means that there is a king whose ministers are truly loyal in governance, without turbidity or partiality, well-versed in laws and statutes, without treason in their hearts. The king trusts and follows the counsel of such persons, and because of this, state affairs and the treasury are all accomplished, his reputation spreads far, and the people are content. This is called a king who accepts upright words.

"What does it mean that a king's actions are carefully considered and accord with the former teachings? It means that there is a king who, by nature, can carefully investigate and properly evaluate his ministers regarding the various affairs and matters of state. He does not entrust responsibilities to those who are not capable, and he entrusts them to those who are capable. He properly rewards those who should be rewarded and properly punishes those who should be punished. In all that he does, he deliberates carefully before acting and is never hasty or violent. When his ministers are assembled at court audiences, they never interrupt another discussion. They wait until the speaker has finished before raising their counsel and objections. They properly carry out the king's commands. This is called according with the former teachings.

"What does it mean that a king cherishes wholesome dharmas? It means that there is a king who believes in cause and effect, in good and evil karma, and in the rewards of humans and devas. He is endowed with moral shame and conscience. He does not act freely in creating evil deeds of body, speech, and mind. From time to time he gives generously, cultivates merit, observes fasting, establishes maṇḍalas, receives the Dharma of abhiṣeka, performs homa ceremonies and offerings to the sacred assembly, and always bears the four immeasurables in mind for the broad benefit of all. This is called a king who cherishes wholesome dharmas.

"What does it mean that a king is skilled in discernment of right and wrong, the superior and the inferior? It means that there is a king whose mind is not inverted regarding his great ministers, prime ministers, and officials. He properly knows the distinctions among loyalty and trustworthiness, skills and abilities, and wisdom. Whether they possess these qualities or not, he knows all of them as they truly are. Those who lack them he regards lightly and keeps at a distance; those who possess them he respects and cares for. Furthermore, when his ministers have grown old and frail, having served in close attendance for a long time, even knowing them to be powerless, he remembers their past service. His respect and care for them only deepens, and he does not slight them. He continues bestowing rank, salary, and rewards without fail. This is called a king skilled in discerning right from wrong, the superior from the inferior.

"What does it mean that a king does not indulge himself and does not practice heedlessness? It means that there is a king who does not become absorbed in the five exquisite pleasures, is not addicted to arrogant amusement, and can from time to time exercise vigilance and skillful effort, do what should be done, and console his ministers. This is called a king who does not indulge himself and does not practice heedlessness.

"If a king possesses these virtues, then even without a great treasury, great ministers, or a great army, before long his kingdom will naturally prosper and he can be relied upon. Great king, you should know that of these ten virtues of a king, the first is the virtue of the king's lineage, and the remaining nine are virtues of the king's own character.

"What are the gates of a king's decline? Great king, you should know that the gates of a king's decline are briefly of five kinds: First, not carefully evaluating ministers before employing them. Second, evaluating carefully but lacking generosity, or if generous, not in a timely manner. Third, being devoted to heedlessness and not attending to affairs of state. Fourth, being devoted to heedlessness and not guarding the treasury. Fifth, being devoted to heedlessness and not cultivating wholesome dharmas. These five are all called the gates of a king's decline.

"What does it mean that a king does not carefully evaluate ministers before employing them? It means that there is a king who, without carefully investigating or properly evaluating his ministers' loyalty, skills, and wisdom, takes them as close attendants and favorites, bestows generous rank and salary upon them, and entrusts them with important responsibilities, frequently consoling them with soft words. Yet these ministers squander the wealth entrusted to them, and when they encounter enemies, hostile forces, or battle, they are the first to flee and scatter. Out of fear and cowardice, they betray their lord without loyalty. This is called a king who does not carefully evaluate ministers before employing them.

"What does it mean that a king evaluates carefully but lacks generosity, or if generous, not in a timely manner? It means that there is a king who by nature can carefully investigate and properly evaluate ministers, knowing their loyalty, skills, and wisdom, and takes them as close attendants, yet does not show them favor. He does not consider their abilities, does not bestow rank or salary, and does not entrust them with important responsibilities. Then suddenly one day, the king encounters enemies, hostile forces, or some great and fearful event. Only in the dire emergency does he begin to bestow rank and console them with soft words. At that time the ministers say to one another: 'The king, under the pressure of present circumstances, shows us temporary generosity. This is not a lasting commitment!' Knowing this, even though they possess loyalty, skills, and wisdom, they hide these and do not display them. This is called a king who evaluates carefully but lacks generosity, or if generous, not in a timely manner.

"What does it mean that a king is devoted to heedlessness and does not attend to affairs of state? It means that there is a king who, regarding matters of diplomacy, strategy, and all that should be accomplished in affairs of state, does not from time to time retire to a quiet place, or together with wise counselors, properly think through strategies of alliance and rupture, and matters of reward and military action. He is not diligent in attending to essential affairs. This is called a king devoted to heedlessness who does not attend to affairs of state.

"What does it mean that a king is devoted to heedlessness and does not guard the treasury? It means that there is a king who neglects management of affairs, does not oversee the various departments, does not secure the palace gates, courts, and storehouses, tells state secrets to women, and wastes wealth and treasure on hunting and gambling without guarding them carefully. This is called a king devoted to heedlessness who does not guard the treasury.

"What does it mean that a king is devoted to heedlessness and does not cultivate wholesome dharmas? It means that there is a king who does not frequently draw near to, pay respects to, and consult with the well-known, gentle, honest, wise, eloquent, principled, and liberated śramaṇas and brahmins of the world, asking: 'What is wholesome? What is unwholesome? What is blameworthy? What is blameless? What are the auspicious Dharma-gates of merit that keep one far from evil?' Even if he does hear such teachings, he does not practice them accordingly. This is called a king devoted to heedlessness who does not cultivate wholesome dharmas.

"If a king possesses these five gates of decline, know that he will lose the rewards of the present life and, in lives to come, lose the blessings of humans and devas. The first four gates cause the loss of rewards received in the present; the last gate causes the loss of rewards in lives to come.

"What are the lovable qualities of a king? Great king, you should know that there are briefly five kinds of lovable, delightful, pleasing, and agreeable qualities of a king. What are the five? First, being respected and loved by the people. Second, increasing in sovereignty. Third, being able to subdue enemies. Fourth, properly nourishing the body. Fifth, being able to cultivate wholesome deeds. These five are the lovable, delightful, pleasing, and agreeable qualities of a king. What skillfully gives rise to these lovable qualities of a king? Great king, you should know that there are briefly five things that skillfully give rise to the lovable qualities of a king. What are the five? First, nurturing the people with benevolence. Second, being endowed with heroic valor. Third, skillful use of strategy. Fourth, properly attending to one's domain. Fifth, diligently cultivating wholesome dharmas.

"What does it mean that a king nurtures the people with benevolence? It means that there is a king who is by nature content, able to be cautious, accomplished in non-greed and pure dharmas. From his treasury he distributes as he is able to the poor, the destitute, and the orphaned. He is gentle and patient, and often consoles the people of his kingdom with kind words. When ministers have committed offenses that cannot be pardoned, he measures the crime and extends clemency, administering punishments with truth, timeliness, and reason. This is called a king who, by the Dharma of just governance, nurtures the people with benevolence, and thus inspires the world's respect and love.

"What does it mean that a king is endowed with heroic valor? It means that there is a king whose divine strategy never falters, whose military skill is perfected, who subdues those not yet subdued, and protects those already subdued. This is called a king endowed with heroic valor.

"What does it mean that a king is skilled in strategy? It means that there is a king who clearly understands all advantageous matters, is able to reconcile through skillful means, and by drawing strong allies to himself, is able to subdue all enemies.

"What does it mean that a king properly attends to his domain? It means that there is a king who skillfully measures the increase and decrease of his treasury, who is neither extravagant nor stingy, and who shares resources equitably. According to what is appropriate for the season, he provides for all his ministers, relatives, fellow rulers, and entertainers. Furthermore, when ill, he eats what is suitable and avoids what is not, following medical advice on the nature of foods before eating. If food is not yet digested, or if food causes diarrhea, he should not eat it. What should be shared should not be eaten alone; fine flavors should be distributed so that all rejoice. This is called a king who properly attends to his domain, and thereby skillfully nourishes his own body.

"What does it mean that a king diligently cultivates wholesome dharmas? It means that there is a king who is fully endowed with pure faith, precepts, learning, generosity, and wisdom. Regarding pure faith, he clearly believes in the other world and in the future rewards of good and evil karma among humans and devas. This is called being endowed with pure faith. He receives and upholds the pure precepts, observing the three annual long-fast periods and the six monthly fast days, keeping far from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, false speech, and drinking alcohol and all heedless activities. This is called being endowed with pure precepts. Regarding pure learning, he is diligent in hearing the wonderful Dharma-gates of prajñā concerning present-life karma and future rewards, concentrating with a devoted mind until he thoroughly comprehends them. This is called being endowed with pure learning. Regarding the pure mind of generosity, he stays far from stinginess and greed, extends his hand in giving, always cultivates merit, and is complete in equanimity. This is called being endowed with pure generosity. Regarding the place of pure wisdom, he truly understands what is blameworthy and blameless, what to cultivate and what not to cultivate, and the superior and inferior means. He draws near to śramaṇas of much learning and pure conduct, stays far from all wicked and heretical teachers, and well understands three kinds of completeness: the completeness of reward, the completeness of human endeavor, and the completeness of merit. That is: the king inherits and continues the royal lineage, and the family into which he is born is wise and astute, with a treasury of wealth adequate for all needs. This is called the completeness of reward. If a king is skilled in strategy, ever accomplished in heroic valor, advancing and retreating with skill, and is adept in all arts, this is called the completeness of human endeavor. If a king upholds the true Dharma, and together with the inner palace, the princes, and the great ministers cultivates generosity, performs wholesome deeds, observes fasting, receives the precepts, practices the samādhi of loving-kindness, cultivates the sublime brahma-conduct, frequently performs homa for appeasement and increase, establishes maṇḍalas, and fully receives abhiṣeka, this is the completeness of merit. If one can practice in this way, it is called being endowed with pure wisdom.

"Furthermore, great king, you should know that I have briefly taught the faults of a king, the virtues of a king, the gates of a king's decline, the lovable qualities of a king, and what gives rise to the lovable qualities of a king. Therefore, great king, if you read or recite this secret royal teaching each morning and practice in accordance with it, you will be called a sacred king, a Dharma king. All buddhas, bodhisattvas, and the eight classes of devas and nāgas will protect and sustain you day and night, constantly guarding you with mindfulness. You will cause the winds and rains of the world to come in their proper seasons, the weapons of war to be at rest, tribute to flow from all nations, boundless blessings and prosperity, and peace throughout the kingdom, with a long life and far-reaching destiny. Therefore you shall gain every benefit and peace and joy in the present life."

At that time King Udayana, having heard what the Buddha taught, was exultant with joy, accepted with faith, and put it into practice.
