# Biography of Bodhisattva Aśvaghoṣa

## *Mǎmíng Púsà Zhuàn* (馬鳴菩薩傳)

Taishō 2046

Translated by Kumārajīva (*Jiūmóluóshí*, 鳩摩羅什) of the Later Qín. A hagiographic biography.

*Note: This is a hagiographic account of the celebrated Indian Buddhist philosopher and poet Aśvaghoṣa (Mǎmíng, "Horse-Neighing"), traditionally dated to the first or second century CE. The biography recounts his conversion from an external-path debater to a Buddhist monk under the elder Pārśva (Xié, "Ribs"), his fame as a master debater and dharma teacher, and the famous episode in which he caused horses to weep by the power of his preaching, earning him the name Aśvaghoṣa. It also describes how he was given as tribute to the Kuṣāṇa king of the Little Yuèzhī in exchange for peace.*

There was a great master named Bodhisattva Aśvaghoṣa (*Mǎmíng Púsà*, 馬鳴菩薩). He was a disciple of the Elder Pārśva (*Xié*, 脇).

At that time the Elder Pārśva, anxiously concerned for the buddha-dharma, entered samādhi to observe who was capable of leaving home, broadly proclaiming the Way and its transformations, and awakening sentient beings. He saw that in Central India there was a renunciant of the external paths, worldly-wise and eloquent, skilled in debate. This man had proclaimed: "If any bhikṣu is able to debate with me, you may strike the *gandhī* (wooden signal board). If none can, you are not worthy to publicly sound the *gandhī* and receive the offerings of people."

At that time the Elder Pārśva set out from North India heading for the central kingdom. In a city named Śākya (*Shìjiā*, 釋迦), on the road he encountered young novices who all teased him, saying: "Elder, give me your *pravaraṇā* offering!" Some of them actually took things away, mocking him in various ways without any propriety. The Elder Pārśva's countenance did not change; he was tranquil and unperturbed.

Among the novices, the more broadly learned ones perceived his profundity and suspected he was no ordinary person. They tested him, questioning and observing his conduct. He answered every question without slowing his pace. His manner was deeply far-reaching, unconcerned with trifles. The novices, upon fully observing the elder's virtue, deep and unfathomable, redoubled their reverence. All together they escorted him.

Thereupon the Elder Pārśva, by his supernatural power, traveled through the sky and arrived in Central India. He stayed at a certain monastery and asked the bhikṣus: "Why do you not sound the *gandhī* according to the dharma?"

The bhikṣus said: "Venerable elder, there is a reason we do not strike it."

He asked: "Why?"

They answered: "There is a renunciant of the external paths who is skilled in debate. He has proclaimed throughout the land that if the śramaṇa disciples of the Śākya clan cannot debate with him, they may not publicly sound the *gandhī* or receive the offerings of people. Because of his words, we do not strike it."

The Elder Pārśva said: "Just sound the *gandhī*. If he comes, I shall deal with him myself."

The senior bhikṣus were greatly impressed by his words, but doubted whether he could succeed. They gathered and deliberated: "Let us sound the *gandhī*. If the external-path man comes, we shall let the elder manage it as he sees fit." They then sounded the *gandhī*.

The external-path man immediately asked: "Why have you struck that wood today?"

They answered: "An elder śramaṇa has come from the north. It is he who sounds the *gandhī*, not us."

The external-path man said: "Bring him out." They went out and met face to face.

The external-path man asked: "Do you wish to debate?"

He answered: "Yes."

The external-path man smiled and said: "This elder bhikṣu, given his appearance," and continued, "does not exceed an ordinary person. How could he wish to debate with me?" They then made an agreement: after seven days, the king, great ministers, śramaṇas, external-path practitioners, and all the great dharma teachers would be gathered for the debate.

On the night of the sixth day, the Elder Pārśva entered samādhi to contemplate the appropriate response. On the morning of the seventh day, the great assembly gathered like clouds. The Elder Pārśva arrived first and ascended the high seat. His countenance was joyful and serene, surpassing his usual appearance. The external-path man arrived afterward and sat before him. Observing the śramaṇa's appearance, harmonious and delighted, his resolve peaceful and at ease, and further noting that his entire person displayed the marks of a debater, he thought: "Could this be a holy bhikṣu? His resolve is peaceful and delighted, and he possesses the marks of debate. Today will be a fine debate."

They then established their terms: "If the loser fails, what penalty shall he bear?"

The external-path man said: "If the loser fails, his tongue shall be cut out."

The Elder Pārśva said: "That will not do. Simply becoming a disciple is sufficient to fulfill the agreement."

He answered: "Agreed."

He then asked: "Who should speak first?"

The Elder Pārśva said: "I am already old and have come from afar. Moreover, I was seated here first. By right I should speak first."

The external-path man said: "Very well then. Present your statement, and I shall refute everything."

The Elder Pārśva then said: "May there be great peace under heaven, may the great king enjoy long life, may the kingdom be prosperous and happy, and may it be free of all calamities."

The external-path man was speechless, not knowing what to say. In the rules of debate, having no response constitutes defeat. He submitted and became a disciple. His beard and hair were shaved, he was ordained as a novice, and he received the full precepts.

Sitting alone in his quarters, he thought to himself: "My talent and brilliance are far-reaching; my reputation shakes the world. How is it that a single statement has brought me to defeat and made me another's disciple?" Thinking this, he was displeased.

His master, knowing his mind, summoned him to his room and manifested various supernatural transformations. Knowing his master was no ordinary person, his mind became delighted and submissive. He thought: "It is truly fitting that I should be his disciple."

The master said to him: "Your talent and brilliance are not easily matched; you simply have not yet completed your development. If you study the dharma I have attained, the faculties, powers, factors of awakening, the path, and the depths of eloquent penetration, and clearly comprehend their meaning, then no one under heaven will be your match."

The master returned to his own country, and the disciple remained in Central India. He broadly penetrated all the sūtras and was profoundly versed in both internal and external teachings. His talent and eloquence overshadowed the age, and the fourfold assembly revered him. The king of India treasured and honored him greatly.

Afterward, the king of the Little Yuèzhī (*Xiǎo Yuèzhī*, 小月氏) kingdom of North India attacked the central kingdom and besieged it for a long time. The king of Central India sent a message asking: "If there is something you want, I will provide it. Why must you inflict hardship on the people by staying so long?"

The answer came: "If you submit, send three hundred million in gold and I will pardon you."

The king said: "In this entire kingdom there is not even one hundred million in gold. How can three hundred million be obtained?"

The answer came: "In your kingdom there are two great treasures: the Buddha's alms-bowl and the eloquent bhikṣu. Give me these, and they will count for two hundred million in gold."

The king said: "These two treasures I deeply cherish and cannot part with."

Thereupon the bhikṣu preached the dharma for the king. His words were: "All who possess feeling and receive transformation are the same throughout the world. The buddha-way is vast and profound; its meaning lies in universal salvation. A great person's virtue likewise takes the relief of beings as supreme. Worldly teaching is beset with many difficulties; therefore a king's transformation extends only to one nation. Now, broadly proclaiming the buddha-way, you can become the dharma-king of the four seas. A bhikṣu delivers people; the meaning does not allow for distinctions. Merit resides in the mind; in principle there is no near or far. It is best to take the broad and far-reaching view. Why limit yourself to what is before your eyes?"

The king had always revered and respected him, and followed his counsel. He gave the two treasures. The Yuèzhī king then returned to his own country.

His ministers deliberated: "That the king should reverence the Buddha's alms-bowl is fitting. But as for a bhikṣu, there are bhikṣus everywhere under heaven. To value one at one hundred million in gold is surely excessive."

The king, knowing well that the bhikṣu was loftily brilliant and deeply accomplished, and that his guidance and eloquent preaching of the dharma could even move non-human beings, wished to awaken the multitudes from their confusion. He starved seven horses for six days. On the morning of the seventh, he broadly gathered both internal and external śramaṇas and different-school practitioners and invited the bhikṣu to preach the dharma. All who listened were without exception awakened. The king tethered the horses before the assembly and offered them grass. The horses shed tears, listening to the dharma, with no thought of eating.

At this, the entire world knew he was no ordinary person. Because the horses understood his voice, he was thereupon called Bodhisattva Aśvaghoṣa (*Mǎmíng*, 馬鳴, "Horse-Neighing").

In North India he broadly proclaimed the buddha-dharma, guiding and benefiting all beings. Skillful in skillful means, he accomplished others' merit. The fourfold assembly revered him. He was also universally called "the Sun of Merit" (*Gōngdé Rì*, 功德日).
