# 佛說施燈功德經
## Sūtra on the Merit of Offering Lamps

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0702

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Qi dynasty, translated by Narendrayaśas (那連提耶舍).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, Anāthapiṇḍada's park. At that time, the World-Honored One said to Śāriputra: "Śāriputra! The Buddha possesses four kinds of surpassing and wondrous wholesome qualities that enable sentient beings to obtain immeasurable fruition, immeasurable radiance, immeasurable wondrous form, immeasurable treasuries of merit, immeasurable treasuries of happiness, and the treasuries of immeasurable morality, concentration, wisdom, liberation, knowledge-and-vision of liberation, and eloquence: all the unattached and undefiled dharmas.

"Śāriputra! What are the four? First, the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One has attained the perfection of moral discipline (śīla-pāramitā), possessing immeasurable morality. Second, he has attained the perfection of meditation (dhyāna-pāramitā), possessing immeasurable concentration. Third, he has attained the perfection of wisdom (prajñā-pāramitā), possessing immeasurable wisdom, including comprehensive wisdom, penetrating wisdom, wisdom of true nature, innumerable wisdom, decisive wisdom, and certain knowledge-and-vision. Fourth, he has attained an unturbid mind, a mind of excellent and surpassing action, possessing wondrous liberation and supreme liberation. These are the four kinds of surpassing and wondrous wholesome qualities.

"Śāriputra! This Buddha, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One has completely distanced himself from all evil and has perfectly accomplished all wholesome dharmas. His practices are fully complete. He possesses the vision of reality. He has left behind darkness and is able to serve as illumination. He is endowed with immeasurable provisions of merit and wisdom. He surpasses the world and cannot be outshone by the world. He has attained morality, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge-and-vision of liberation. He possesses the ten powers and four fearlessnesses. He has attained the powers of all buddha-dharmas, is fully endowed with the powers of all buddha-dharmas, possesses the powers of the great compassion of all buddhas, and the power of eloquence. His original vows and skillful means are all completely fulfilled. He has well cultivated his fundamental practices, possesses the jewel of wisdom, and his diligence is immeasurable and never ceases. He is free from all anxiety and sorrow, without oppression or affliction, without grasping or attachment. He is well able to tame and subdue, like a great dragon king. He has no residual tendencies. He is the supreme field of merit for all sentient beings.

"Śāriputra! If monks, nuns, novice monks, novice nuns, laymen, or laywomen generate a mind of pure faith, seeking merit, delighting in merit, contemplating the Tathāgata's supreme skillful means and the perfection of his fundamental practices, contemplating that throughout all future lifetimes and all births and deaths, and in the present world, the Buddha has accomplished immeasurable unattached morality, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge-and-vision of liberation; if they contemplate even one kind of the Buddha's merits; and having contemplated these merits, contemplate the wholesome roots cultivated throughout immeasurable billions of nayutas of hundreds of thousands of kalpas in the field of the three clear knowledges, in the field of pure morality, in the field of unequaled morality, in the field of immeasurable true virtues; and if, before stūpas and temples and before images, they make offerings by presenting lamp-light, even with a small wick dipped in ghee or oil, offering a light that illuminates only a single step of the path:

"Śāriputra! Such merit is not something that any śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha can fully comprehend. Only the Buddha, the Tathāgata, can know it! Śāriputra! If even for those who seek worldly rewards the merit is already this great, how much more so for good men and good women who, with a pure and deeply joyful mind, not seeking any reward, abiding in reverence, continuously and without interruption contemplating the Buddha's merits? Śāriputra! The merit of illuminating a single step of the path is already this great; how much more so for fully illuminating a single step, or two steps, or three steps, or four steps, or one level of the stūpa body, two levels, and so on up to many levels; one face, two faces, and so on up to four faces, and the Buddha's image?

"Śāriputra! That lamp which was lit may quickly go out, or be blown out by wind, or go out when the oil is exhausted, or when the wick is exhausted, or when both are exhausted. Just as the nāgas, out of anger, produce clouds that spread out, and within them lightning flashes, which flash and immediately disappear.

"Śāriputra! Even offering such a brief lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple: if those monks, nuns, novice monks, novice nuns, laymen, laywomen, or others who have not taken precepts, out of a love of goodness, for the protection of their own person, with faith in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha, make such a small lamp offering to the field of merit, the fruits and accumulation of merit they obtain can only be known by the Buddha. All the devas, humans, māras, brahmās, śramaṇas, brahmins, and even śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas of the world cannot know it. The reward received from lighting even such a small lamp cannot be expressed.

"Śāriputra! The domain of all buddhas is inconceivable. Only the Tathāgata can know this meaning. Śāriputra! The accumulation of merit obtained by one who offers lamps is immeasurable, boundless, and incalculable. Only the Tathāgata can fully know it. Śāriputra! The merit of even a small lamp-light is already so incalculable; how much more so after my nirvāṇa, at buddha stūpas and temples, if one personally makes or instructs others to make one lamp, two lamps, or even many lamps, along with incense, flowers, garlands, jeweled standards, banners, canopies, and all other kinds of surpassing and wondrous offerings?

"Śāriputra! There are four kinds of dharmas that should be received with faith. What are the four? First, the buddha-dharma is immeasurable; this should be received with faith. Second, even a small cultivation of wholesome roots can yield immeasurable reward; this should be received with faith. Third, the reward obtained by those who deeply generate reverent faith in the Three Jewels and well cultivate their practices: even you śrāvakas, who can now see me directly, still cannot fully comprehend or even conceive of it; how much less after my nirvāṇa could śrāvaka disciples, far removed from me, be able to presently know or conceive of it? If anyone claims to know or conceive of it, that is impossible; this should be received with faith. Fourth, these śrāvakas cannot know or measure the karma and karmic fruits of all sentient beings. Śāriputra! You śrāvakas need not try to measure these matters. Why? Śāriputra! The Tathāgata has always said that the karmic actions and fruits of all sentient beings are inconceivable. The buddhas of the past, worthy ones, perfectly enlightened ones, have also said that the karmic retributions of sentient beings are inconceivable. The buddhas of the future, worthy ones, perfectly enlightened ones, will also say that the karmic retributions of sentient beings are inconceivable. The faith of sentient beings' minds and the intrinsic nature of their minds are also unknowable and inconceivable. This meaning should be received with faith!

"Śāriputra! You śrāvakas who dwell in the noble lineage, regarding the karmic retributions of all sentient beings, do not possess the real eye or skillful means; how much less the frivolous and feeble-minded, those who have lost morality, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge-and-vision of liberation, those who have lost right mindfulness, those whose eyes are obscured by the darkness and cataracts of ignorance? Regarding their own bodies and all internal and external dharmas, they cannot even know: 'Who exactly am I? To whom do I belong? Where do I dwell? Are my merits great or small? How should I conduct myself? Am I in accord with morality or not? Am I of right mindfulness or have I lost mindfulness? Are the deeds I perform the deeds of a wise person or a fool? Where did I come from? Where will I go?' Śāriputra! Ordinary people with inverted views cannot even know such matters about their own persons; how could they possibly know the various karmic retributions of all sentient beings? If anyone could, that would be impossible.

"Śāriputra! The Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One: his morality is without diminishment, his concentration is without diminishment, his wisdom is without diminishment, his liberation is without diminishment, his knowledge-and-vision of liberation is without diminishment, his marks are without diminishment. Śāriputra! The Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One possesses immeasurable morality, unobstructed morality, inconceivable morality, unequaled morality, ultimate morality, and pure morality. That Tathāgata truly knows the karma and karmic retributions of all sentient beings.

"Śāriputra! How does the Tathāgata truly know the karmic retributions of all sentient beings? Śāriputra! The Buddha knows thus: there are beings whose wholesome karma is exhausted while unwholesome karma increases; there are beings whose unwholesome karma is exhausted while wholesome karma increases; there are beings whose wholesome karma is about to arise while unwholesome karma is about to cease; there are beings whose unwholesome karma is about to arise while wholesome karma is about to cease. Śāriputra! The Tathāgata thus penetrates the various differences in the karma and karmic retributions of all sentient beings, truly knowing each sentient being, whether ignorant or deluded, whether good or unwholesome. Śāriputra! I possess such wisdom and such skill. Regarding the inconceivable and various karmic retributions of sentient beings, I am able to explain them all.

"Śāriputra! If there are sentient beings who have accomplished faith, they can believe in me. If there are sentient beings without faith, who are far removed from my Dharma, who do not believe my words and slander me, they will experience meaninglessness, lack of benefit, and will fall into suffering and affliction throughout the long night. Śāriputra! If those sentient beings offer lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple, the wholesome karma produced by this offering will enable them to attain happiness and the fruit of delight. When they perform the wholesome act of offering lamps, rejoicing arises in them, born from a mind of faith, and in the present life they attain three kinds of mental purity. What are the three? Those good men and good women think: 'I have already made offerings to the Tathāgata. I know that this body is not solid, and I establish the thought of a solid body. I know the faults of wealth, and I establish the thought of solid wealth.' Śāriputra! This is the first purified mind from offering at a buddha stūpa. Furthermore, Śāriputra! Those good men and good women give rise to this thought: 'I have already made offerings at the Tathāgata's supreme field of merit, the most excellent field of merit, the one who can receive the most excellent offerings. I now need not fear falling into the hells, the animal realm, or the realm of hungry ghosts. This wholesome root of mine has already become a cause for the good paths of humans and devas. I shall obtain wondrous form, material provisions, wisdom, safety, and happiness, all the way to attaining the fruit of bodhi.' Śāriputra! This is the second purified mind from offering at a buddha stūpa. Furthermore, Śāriputra! Those good men and good women think: 'I have already made gifts to the buddhas. I have already performed meritorious deeds. I have already abandoned miserliness and greed. I have already removed the fault of stinginess.' Having thought this, their mind of giving is free from miserliness, and their mind of giving grows. Śāriputra! This is the third purified mind from offering at a buddha stūpa.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! If good men or good women offer lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple, at the time of their death they obtain three kinds of clarity. What are the three? First, at the time of death, all the meritorious deeds previously performed appear before them, and they remember wholesome dharmas without forgetting. Śāriputra! This is the first clarity. Because of this, they can recollect the wholesome karma they previously planted before the Buddha. Furthermore, Śāriputra! At the time of death, those good men or good women have this thought: 'I once made offerings before buddha images, stūpas, and temples.' Having this thought, their hearts are filled with elation. Śāriputra! This is the second clarity. Because of this, they can give rise to the recollection of the Buddha. Furthermore, Śāriputra! At the time of death, those good men or good women see other beings practicing generosity. Seeing others practice, they think: 'I too once offered lamp-light at a buddha caitya. I should now practice generosity again!' Contemplating generosity, they attain a joyful mind. Having attained a joyful mind, they are free from the suffering of death. Śāriputra! This is the third clarity. Because of this, they attain a mind that recollects the Dharma.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! Having offered lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple, those good men or good women, at the time of death, further see four kinds of radiance. What are the four? First, at the time of death, they see the disk of the sun, full and rising. Second, they see the disk of the pure moon, full and rising. Third, they see a host of devas gathered in one place. Fourth, they see the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One seated beneath the bodhi tree, about to attain bodhi, and they see themselves reverently facing the Tathāgata with palms joined in respect. Śāriputra! These are the four kinds of radiance seen at the time of death by one who has offered lamps at a buddha stūpa or temple."

At that time, having explained this meaning, the World-Honored One spoke these verses:

> "The supreme Dharma King, the great sage:
> If someone offers lamps at his stūpa or temple,
> That wise one, having performed this deed,
> Obtains boundless and most surpassing happiness.
> At the time of death, mindfulness is not lost;
> One sees the lamps one offered long ago.
> Obtaining four kinds of joy, free from all faults,
> At the moment of death, one is not confused.
> At death, one sees radiance in all ten directions,
> Witnessing the sun and moon rise from the earth.
> One sees thousands, myriads, nayutas of devas,
> And teaches the buddha-dharma to the celestial host.
> Parents, wife, children, and relatives
> All gather round, crying in great grief,
> But the dying one neither thinks of them nor looks,
> For that person's right mindfulness is always undisturbed.
> Before their eyes appear celestial palaces,
> And facing the celestial maidens, the mind is at peace.
> They also see splendidly adorned parks and groves,
> Fully endowed with the five surpassing pleasures.
> They also see the Buddha seated beneath the bodhi tree,
> Surrounded by devas, humans, and asuras.
> They see themselves with palms joined, standing before the Buddha,
> Making offerings to the victorious Sage.
> Having seen the Guide with deep reverence,
> Their hearts are filled with joy and they invite the Tathāgata.
> The World-Honored One, seeing their joyful hearts,
> Does not refuse but accepts their invitation.
> That person's wishes are fulfilled and joy pervades them;
> At the time of relinquishing life, there is no suffering.
> Having felt joy before the Buddha,
> There is no great fear at the time of death.
> At the time of death, mindfulness is not lost;
> They see great radiance in all ten directions.
> They see unprecedented, surpassing, and wondrous forms:
> This is the fruit of offering lamps.
> After death, they are certainly reborn in heaven,
> And see themselves seated on a celestial couch,
> Surrounded by celestial maidens.
> Through offering to the Buddha, they obtain this fruit."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! Having offered lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple, after death one is reborn in the Heaven of the Thirty-Three. Having been born in that heaven, one attains purity in five respects. Śāriputra! How does that deva attain purity in five respects? First, one obtains a pure body. Second, among the devas one obtains surpassing majesty and power. Third, one constantly obtains pure mindfulness and wisdom. Fourth, one constantly hears only pleasing sounds. Fifth, one's retinue is always pleasing to one's mind, and one's heart is filled with joy. Śāriputra! These are the five respects in which that deva attains purity."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke these verses:

> "That deva obtains a body of radiance,
> Endowed with merits, honored by all,
> Becoming the chief among a thousand devas,
> Through offering lamps at a buddha caitya.
> The celestial sounds they hear are always pleasing,
> Mournful and beautiful, surpassing other devas.
> They possess the foremost superior mindfulness and wisdom,
> And obtain the most supreme retinue.
> Wherever that deva goes,
> All the devas revere them.
> 'What kind of karma was cultivated in the past,
> To now possess such a blazing body?'
> There are trees all named 'Supreme Joy,'
> Their light illuminating all around like the moon.
> That deva has earned these wondrous trees,
> Which adorn the celestial palace gardens.
> Countless devas are all amazed:
> 'What are these tree-blossoms called?
> They shine like the light of lamps,
> Emitting a fragrance of wish-fulfilling incense everywhere.'
> That deva's entire retinue
> Adorns themselves with those tree-blossoms.
> Among countless billions of devas,
> Their radiance shines like the sun."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! Having offered lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple and been reborn in the Heaven of the Thirty-Three, that deva knows: 'At such-and-such a time, I shall dwell here; at such-and-such a time, my life will end.' When that surpassing deva's life is about to end, they teach the Dharma to their retinue and the other devas, exhorting and converting them to their delight. Having relinquished life in that heavenly palace, they do not fall into evil destinies but are reborn among humans in the most noble lineage, in a family that has faith in the Buddha's Dharma. If at that time there is no buddha in the world, they are also never born into a family given to superstitious divination or wrong views."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke these verses:

> "That deva is born with such wisdom,
> Knowing how long they will dwell in heaven.
> That deva can also know of themselves:
> 'Before long, my life will come to an end.'
> When the five signs of death appear,
> And that deva's life is about to expire,
> They teach the Dharma to billions of devas,
> Free from delusion, their mind without sorrow.
> Among the devas they speak these words:
> 'All that exists is impermanent and without happiness;
> Some are born and some die.'
> Without thinking of their own death, they teach this Dharma.
> Their retinue all grieve and sorrow,
> And countless devas likewise.
> Yet, though they see their own five signs,
> They recall their merits and do not grieve.
> Having died in that heavenly palace,
> They at once descend and are born among humans.
> Dwelling in the womb and being born, their mindfulness is undisturbed;
> They always experience happiness without suffering.
> Once born, they obtain the knowledge of past lives,
> Able to recall their former abode.
> Mindful of human suffering, they do not crave pleasures:
> 'In a moment, death comes and presses upon us.'
> Having recalled the heavenly rewards,
> They find no pleasure in this human world:
> 'Even in heaven there is suffering; how much more among humans?
> All existences are not solid and are always in flux.'
> That person, once established,
> Will certainly leave home and go forth.
> Their mind never entertains unwholesome thoughts,
> And they shall obtain these fruits:
> Life after life, they always obtain knowledge of past lives,
> And they never perform unwholesome deeds.
> They will certainly go forth and uphold the pure precepts:
> This is the fruit of offering lamps.
> They are never blind or crippled,
> Their eyes are never dimmed,
> Their body is free from illness and unpleasant sounds,
> Their mind is always sharp and never confused.
> They are never afflicted with eye disease,
> And wherever they are reborn, their eyes are not impaired.
> They are never one-eyed or blind;
> Their eyes are always clear and undisturbed.
> Their eyes are long and well-defined in black and white,
> Like the leaves of a pure and wondrous blue lotus.
> Their pure eyes can see the most minute objects,
> Like a clear and limpid maṇi jewel.
> For immeasurable asaṃkhyeya kalpas,
> They obtain pure fleshly eyes that never deteriorate.
> They are also always free from eye diseases:
> This is the fruit of offering lamps.
> Skilled in the seals of wisdom, in wholesome roots, and in treatises,
> They thoroughly understand all crafts and skills.
> Those wise ones observe well:
> Their marvelous wisdom can see the ultimate truth.
> They observe well the lack of sovereignty in all existences,
> And in the buddha-dharma they obtain illumination.
> They see all buddhas, world-honored ones,
> And having seen them, practice reverence and make offerings.
> Life after life, they obtain surpassing beauty,
> And their relatives and retinue all love and respect them.
> They obtain great wealth, power, and sovereignty,
> And an indestructible retinue.
> Just as that lamp-light can dispel darkness,
> Blazing and illuminating all directions,
> That person's radiance is likewise:
> It is not hidden by any darkness.
> If one raises a mind of faith toward a buddha stūpa,
> And offers a surpassing garland of lamps and ornaments,
> When the mind is pure at the time of offering lamps,
> One attains the most surpassing honor among humans.
> Beautiful, wonderful, and most lovable,
> Delighting the entire world.
> Their mind does not frivolously engage in divination,
> Nor do they delight in the wrong paths of the world.
> All the wrong views that exist in the world,
> And heretical paths, they do not accept.
> If they become a king, they are always content,
> Not coveting others' lands or waging wars.
> They are always free from suffering, sorrow, and anxiety,
> And free from all afflictive heat.
> They suffer no decline or loss,
> No ill repute, no decline or distress.
> If they serve as a royal minister, whatever they say
> Is trusted by the king and the people of the country.
> Their body is never afflicted with wasting illness;
> They are never made a eunuch or deformed.
> Their physical marks are complete and they dwell in happiness;
> Illness and suffering cannot touch their body.
> They never see evil dreams,
> And whether sleeping or waking, they are always at peace.
> Life after life, they can obtain hidden treasures,
> And they make offerings at all buddha caityas.
> The merits of the buddhas are without limit;
> What that person obtains is likewise."

"Śāriputra! If sentient beings offer lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple, they obtain four kinds of delightful dharmas. What are the four? First, physical form. Second, material resources. Third, great goodness. Fourth, wisdom. Śāriputra! If sentient beings offer lamp-light at a buddha caitya, they obtain such delightful dharmas."

At that time, the Tathāgata, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke these verses:

> "With a well-proportioned and robust body, endowed with great strength,
> They do not contend or fight with others.
> They travel everywhere without anyone troubling them,
> Through offering lamps at a buddha caitya.
> Born into a great, wealthy, and noble family,
> Endowed with merits, respected by all,
> Life after life they always obtain knowledge of past lives,
> Through offering lamps at a buddha caitya.
> They are always compassionate toward all beings,
> Their words are received with respect by their retinue.
> Their minds are free from harm, always tame and gentle;
> They never create karma leading to evil destinies."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! If sentient beings make offerings at a buddha stūpa, they obtain four kinds of purity. What are the four? First, purity of bodily action. Second, purity of verbal action. Third, purity of mental action. Fourth, purity of good companions.

"Śāriputra! How does one obtain purity of bodily action? Those good men or good women, in whatever place they are born, distance themselves from taking life, have no intention to kill, and also always distance themselves from stealing and sexual misconduct. Regarding even their own spouses, they do not engage in improper conduct; how much less with others' spouses? They also do not drink alcohol or indulge in heedlessness. They do not use swords, clubs, or other instruments of suffering to oppress sentient beings. They leave behind unwholesome dharmas and all evil deeds. Śāriputra! Distancing oneself from all these is called purity of bodily action.

"Śāriputra! How does one obtain purity of verbal action? That person, life after life, never speaks falsely. If they have not seen or heard something, they never claim otherwise. If they have seen or heard, they wait for the appropriate time to be asked, and only then speak, for the benefit of self and others, without making divergent claims. Even if someone urges them to lie, to protect truthful speech they will never speak falsely. They do not carry words from one person to another, nor report another's affairs back to the first. When two sides have already quarreled, they do not make it worse. Whatever they say can skillfully reconcile disputes. They distance themselves from painful words, coarse words, bitter and evil words, unpleasant words, disagreeable words, unloving words, words that do not enter the heart, words that trouble others, and words that create enmity. Whatever they say is gentle, soft, pleasing, not coarse, delightful to the ear, beautiful and wondrous, entering the heart, loved by many, enjoyed by many, lovable, delightful, and able to remove enmity. They always speak such various beautiful and wondrous words. They also abstain from frivolous speech. They do not entertain one thought while speaking another, nor make one gesture while intending another, concealing the reality of things. They do not speak unnecessarily at length, nor speak at the wrong time; they always speak to the point. Śāriputra! Distancing oneself from impure verbal action in this way and accomplishing pure verbal action is called purity of verbal action.

"Śāriputra! How does one obtain purity of mental action? Regarding others' precious treasures and material goods, one does not give rise to attachment. One does not give rise to anger, distances oneself from harmful intent, and also distances oneself from wrong views and all evil views. Śāriputra! Distancing oneself from all these is called purity of mental action.

"Śāriputra! How does one obtain purity of good companions? One's good companions distance themselves from false speech, do not drink alcohol, are free from coarseness and fierceness, are tame and hold right views. One goes to them, draws near, and receives their counsel. One also goes to buddhas, bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, and śrāvakas, draws near, makes offerings, and seeks out teachings not yet heard. Śāriputra! This is the fourth purity: purity of good companions.

"Śāriputra! If good men or good women offer lamp-light at a buddha caitya, they obtain these four kinds of purity."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke these verses:

> "Lighting lamps to illuminate the stūpa,
> Bodily, verbal, and mental actions well subdued,
> Distanced from wrong views and endowed with pure precepts,
> Through this one obtains the eye of wish-fulfillment.
> Like the pure sun illuminating the ten directions,
> One swiftly attains the exhaustion of all defilements.
> That great wise one, endowed with majestic power,
> Obtains the pure divine eye, free from dust and defilements.
> The wise one can comprehend the minds of beings,
> And also obtains supernatural powers and eloquence.
> Attaining the path of the two vehicles is not difficult:
> This reward comes from offering lamps to the Buddha.
> If one seeks unsurpassed bodhi,
> The divine eye, wisdom, and material resources:
> In these three things there is never any diminishment,
> Through offering lamps at a buddha caitya."

"Śāriputra! If good men or good women who abide in the Great Vehicle offer lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple, they obtain eight kinds of surpassing and delightful dharmas life after life. What are the eight? First, they obtain surpassing fleshly eyes. Second, they obtain surpassing mindfulness, beyond anyone's ability to measure. Third, they obtain the surpassing and supreme divine eye of penetrating insight. Fourth, in order to fully cultivate and complete the path, they obtain precepts without deficiency. Fifth, they obtain the fullness of wisdom and realize nirvāṇa. Sixth, regarding their previously performed good deeds, they meet with no obstacles. Seventh, their wholesome karma enables them to encounter buddhas and to serve as the eyes of all beings. Eighth, those good men or good women, by those wholesome roots, become wheel-turning kings whose wheel-treasure is unobstructed by others, whose bodies are upright and beautiful; or they become Śakra, endowed with great majestic power and possessing a thousand eyes; or they become Brahmā, well-versed in Brahmā matters and attaining great meditative absorption. Śāriputra! By dedicating those wholesome roots toward bodhi, they obtain these eight kinds of surpassing and delightful dharmas.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! Good men and good women who abide in the Great Vehicle further obtain eight kinds of immeasurable and surpassing dharmas. First, they obtain the immeasurable buddha-eye. Second, they obtain the immeasurable supernatural powers of the Tathāgata. Third, they obtain the immeasurable morality of the Buddha. Fourth, they obtain the immeasurable samādhi of the Tathāgata. Fifth, they obtain the immeasurable wisdom of the Tathāgata. Sixth, they obtain the immeasurable liberation of the Tathāgata. Seventh, they obtain the immeasurable knowledge-and-vision of liberation of the Buddha. Eighth, they are able to enter into the thoughts and desires of all sentient beings. Śāriputra! Good men and good women who offer lamp-light at a buddha stūpa or temple can accumulate such immeasurable and surpassing rewards.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! If sentient beings see someone teaching the Dharma and think: 'How can I ensure that this person can always proclaim and reveal the buddha-dharma? I shall offer a lamp to them. By offering an oil lamp, I will help the Dharma teacher obtain the lamp of the Dharma.' Having this thought, they take a lamp and offer it. By the wholesome root of this offering of lamp-light, they obtain eight kinds of immeasurable provisions. What are the eight? First, they obtain immeasurable provisions of right mindfulness. Second, they obtain immeasurable provisions of great wisdom. Third, they obtain immeasurable provisions of faith. Fourth, they obtain immeasurable provisions of diligence. Fifth, they obtain immeasurable provisions of great insight. Sixth, they obtain immeasurable provisions of samādhi. Seventh, they obtain immeasurable provisions of eloquence. Eighth, they obtain immeasurable provisions of merit. Śāriputra! These are the eight kinds of provisions from offering lamps. They also obtain the four unhindered knowledges, and progressively attain the knowledge of all aspects (sarvākāra-jñāna).

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! If good men or good women see someone else offering lamps before the Tathāgata and, with a mind of pure faith, join their palms and give rise to a mind of sympathetic joy, by this wholesome root they obtain eight kinds of superior dharmas. What are the eight? First, they obtain superior physical form. Second, they obtain a superior retinue. Third, they obtain superior morality. Fourth, among humans and devas, they obtain a superior birth. Fifth, they obtain superior faith. Sixth, they obtain superior eloquence. Seventh, they obtain the superior noble path. Eighth, they attain supreme, perfect enlightenment (anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi). Śāriputra! These are the eight kinds of superior dharmas. Śāriputra! Why can one obtain these eight kinds of superior and surpassing dharmas? Śāriputra! Because the Buddha possesses immeasurable morality, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge-and-vision of liberation, those who make offerings to the Buddha obtain immeasurable fruits and benefits."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke these verses:

> "Practice the conduct that leads to liberation,
> Diligently cultivate the buddha-dharma,
> Cast off the army of death,
> Like an elephant crushing a flower grove."

At that time, the Buddha said to the Elder Śāriputra: "There are five dharmas that are most difficult to obtain. First, it is difficult to obtain a human body. Second, it is difficult to attain faith and delight in the Buddha's true Dharma. Third, having delighted in the Buddha's Dharma, it is difficult to go forth from home. Fourth, it is difficult to be fully endowed with the pure precepts. Fifth, it is difficult to attain the exhaustion of all defilements. Śāriputra! These five dharmas are the most difficult for all sentient beings to obtain, and you have already obtained them all."

At that time, the World-Honored One, wishing to restate the foregoing meaning and exhort Śāriputra and the others, spoke these verses:

> "Making offerings at the Tathāgata's caitya,
> Seeking bodhi for the benefit of sentient beings,
> The wise ones who create this surpassing cause
> Life after life always obtain the most surpassing reward.
> Born in surpassing births among devas and humans,
> Serving as objects of offering for devas and humans.
> Like Mount Sumeru, stable and unmoving,
> Their radiance pervades and illuminates the ten directions.
> The celestial hosts who see them are all reverent,
> And also feel love and give rise to faith.
> They make offerings to them and praise them;
> All are delighted, wishing to see them again and again.
> 'How marvelous is this deva's mark of merit,
> Like Brahmā's light illuminating Brahmā's palace!
> What kind of karma did this deva once perform
> To have such blazing bodily radiance?'
> Who, seeing this, would not cultivate goodness?
> Who would not study the precepts of the noble lineage?
> Who, seeing the Sage, would feel aversion?
> Who, hearing the wondrous Dharma, would be heedless?
> That one, in a former human life,
> Constantly offered lamps at the Tathāgata's stūpa,
> And once made offerings in the buddha-dharma.
> Through these good merits, they were born in heaven.
> 'May I always obtain a human body,
> May I always generate pure faith in the buddha-dharma,
> May I always be diligent and dwell in the path of the Buddha.
> I would rather lose my life than abandon the Dharma.'
> Obtaining a human body is the most difficult;
> Why do the foolish not create merit?
> They waste their resources without practicing the Dharma,
> And after death, they fall into a great abyss.
> The devas, having seen this one's stainless majesty and power,
> Reproach themselves in their hearts and make this vow:
> 'May I always be born in the human world,
> Diligently cultivating the holy life.
> May I, at the very end, at the time of my death,
> Attain pure faith in the buddha-dharma.
> May I obtain right mindfulness without losing it,
> And may I see the immeasurable tathāgatas.'
> Served by thousands of billions of devas,
> Enjoying themselves with celestial maidens,
> All the celestial maidens love and respect them;
> The celestial maidens adorn themselves and play in the palace gardens.
> Celestial fragrances from all directions come to perfume them;
> Their ears hear all manner of wondrous sounds.
> Wherever that deva travels,
> They always behold the most surpassing and wondrous forms.
> All the forms they see are lovable;
> They never behold any evil forms.
> They also always experience the most surpassing touch:
> All this is from offering lamps at a caitya.
> Having departed from there, they are reborn in the human realm,
> With right mindfulness while dwelling in the parents' womb.
> Once born, they remember the celestial affairs;
> The power of their wisdom does not regress.
> That person, having performed such karma,
> Becomes a great and powerful wheel-turning king.
> That king's appearance is most dignified and beautiful:
> This reward comes from offering lamps.
> Through that karma, they obtain long life,
> Always a vessel of purity, peace, and happiness.
> Their body is free from all illness and pain:
> Such is the fruit obtained from lighting lamps.
> There is no danger from kings or enemies or thieves;
> No one dares encroach upon their spouse.
> They are not troubled by evil people,
> Through offering lamps to the Buddha.
> Safe, prosperous, and without fear,
> Powerful, wealthy, and abundant in treasures,
> They obtain surpassing ornaments and parks:
> All this is from lighting lamps and offering to the Buddha.
> They shall behold the buddhas, world-honored ones,
> And having seen them, give rise to reverent faith.
> With a joyful mind, they make offerings to the Buddha,
> And abandon the throne to go forth from home.
> The Buddha of immeasurable wisdom, ultimate wisdom,
> Endowed with praiseworthy virtues, able to convert others:
> Having offered lamps at this buddha stūpa,
> That person's bodily radiance shines like a lamp.
> The Sage, the Bull among Men, with his pure eye:
> Having illuminated that stūpa with fine lamp-light,
> One attains the undefiled, unsurpassed path,
> And one's bodily radiance illuminates the ten directions.
> Seeing the four noble truths, possessing the ten powers,
> The unique dharmas are also fully realized.
> Obtaining the all-seeing eye, becoming the Sugata:
> This fruit comes entirely from offering lamps.
> Even if all sentient beings
> Had in the past made offerings to immeasurable buddhas,
> Endowed with great majestic power and seeing the true meaning,
> And through billions of kalpas attained the pratyekabuddha path;
> And in all the worlds of the ten directions,
> Garlands of lamps were laid out without remainder,
> And with the lamp-garlands of all these worlds,
> Someone with a faithful mind made offerings to them;
> And that person made such offerings
> Continuously for immeasurable kalpas,
> Still, if another offers a single lamp to the Buddha,
> The merit surpasses all the former beyond measure.
> If the lamp oil were like the water of the great ocean,
> And the wick were like Mount Sumeru,
> And someone could light such a lamp
> That illuminated all the worlds everywhere;
> And that person, with a deeply faithful and reverent mind,
> Aspiring only to the pratyekabuddha path,
> Placed such lamps everywhere in the ten directions,
> Making offerings with a single mind and reverence:
> Still, if another generates the mind of bodhi
> And takes a grass torch to briefly offer to the Buddha,
> That person's merit surpasses all the former.
> Seeing the true meaning, I declare this.
> Even if all sentient beings of the ten directions,
> Each with offerings as described above,
> Continued for as many kalpas as there are sands in the Ganges,
> Their minds aspiring only to the pratyekabuddha path:
> If someone at a buddha stūpa or temple
> Lights a single lamp or makes a single prostration,
> Seeking the unsurpassed path for the sake of sentient beings,
> This merit surpasses all the former beyond measure.
> Those without faith hear this and are not pleased;
> Such fools, afflicted by Māra, destroy the true Dharma.
> Realizing the pure dharma-realm is most difficult;
> The Sugata alone is supreme in all the worlds.
> Therefore you all should rejoice
> And aspire to the merits of the Buddha."

When the World-Honored One had finished teaching this Dharma, the Elder Śāriputra and the others, along with immeasurable devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, and non-humans, having heard what the Buddha taught, all generated the mind of unsurpassed bodhi. Filled with immeasurable joy, they prostrated and departed.
