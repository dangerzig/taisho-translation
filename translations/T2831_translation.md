# 無心論
## Treatise on No-Mind

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2831

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

The ultimate principle is without words, yet one must borrow words to display the principle. The great path is without characteristics, yet to accommodate the coarse one sees its form. I now provisionally establish two persons to jointly discuss this treatise on no-mind.

The disciple asks the Master: "Is there mind or no mind?"

The Master answers: "No mind."

The disciple asks: "If you say there is no mind, then who is able to see, hear, feel, and know? Who knows that there is no mind?"

The Master answers: "It is still no-mind that sees, hears, feels, and knows. It is still no-mind that can know no-mind."

The disciple asks: "If there were truly no mind, then there should be no seeing, hearing, feeling, or knowing. How can there be seeing, hearing, feeling, and knowing?"

The Master answers: "Although I have no mind, I am able to see, able to hear, able to feel, and able to know."

The disciple asks: "Since you are able to see, hear, feel, and know, then this is having a mind. How can you call it no mind?"

The Master answers: "Seeing, hearing, feeling, and knowing is itself no-mind. Where else, apart from seeing, hearing, feeling, and knowing, could there be a separate no-mind? I am now afraid you do not understand, so I shall explain each one for you, so that you may awaken to the true principle. For instance, seeing: all day long seeing, yet it is as if not seeing; seeing too is no-mind. Hearing: all day long hearing, yet it is as if not hearing; hearing too is no-mind. Feeling: all day long feeling, yet it is as if not feeling; feeling too is no-mind. Knowing: all day long knowing, yet it is as if not knowing; knowing too is no-mind. Therefore it is said: seeing, hearing, feeling, and knowing are all no-mind."

The disciple asks: "How can one know this is no-mind?"

The Master answers: "Just carefully investigate and examine: what appearance or form does the mind have? Can the mind actually be obtained? Is it mind or not mind? Is it inside, outside, or in between? If you search for the mind in these three places, it is utterly unobtainable. And if you search for it in every other place as well, it is also unobtainable. This is how you should know it is no-mind."

The disciple asks: "Since the Master says that all places are entirely no-mind, then there should be no sin or merit. Why then do sentient beings transmigrate through the six destinies, with birth and death never ceasing?"

The Master answers: "Sentient beings are confused and deluded. Within no-mind, they erroneously generate mind and create all kinds of karma, erroneously clinging to it as real. This is enough to cause transmigration through the six destinies, with birth and death never ceasing. It is like a person in the dark who mistakes a tree stump for a ghost and a rope for a snake, and gives rise to fear. The erroneous clinging of sentient beings is just like this. Within no-mind, they erroneously cling to a mind and create all kinds of karma, when in reality there is no transmigration through the six destinies. If such sentient beings encounter a great and good spiritual friend who teaches them to sit in meditation and awaken to no-mind, all karmic obstructions are completely destroyed and birth and death are instantly severed. It is like the sun shining in the dark: all darkness is exhausted. If one awakens to no-mind, all transgressions are likewise extinguished."

The disciple asks: "The disciple is foolish and dull, and my mind is not yet clear. Are you certain that all places, all that the six sense-faculties employ, all that one responds to: speech, all kinds of actions, afflictions, bodhi, birth and death, and nirvāṇa, are definitely no-mind?"

The Master answers: "Definitely no-mind. Only because sentient beings erroneously cling to having a mind do afflictions, birth and death, bodhi, and nirvāṇa come to exist. If one awakens to no-mind, then there are no afflictions, no birth and death, and no nirvāṇa. Therefore the Tathāgata, for those who have mind, speaks of the existence of birth and death. Bodhi is named in contrast to afflictions; nirvāṇa is named in contrast to birth and death. These are all teachings of the antidote. If no mind can be obtained, then afflictions and bodhi are also unobtainable, and even birth and death and nirvāṇa are unobtainable."

The disciple asks: "If bodhi and nirvāṇa are unobtainable, then the buddhas of the past all attained bodhi. How can this be so?"

The Master answers: "It is only in terms of conventional truth and literary words that one speaks of 'attaining.' In terms of ultimate truth, there is truly nothing to be attained. Therefore the *Vimalakīrti Sūtra* says: 'Bodhi cannot be attained through the body, nor can it be attained through the mind.' And the *Diamond Sūtra* says: 'There is not the slightest dharma that can be attained. The tathāgatas, the buddhas, attain only through non-attainment.' You should know: with mind, there is everything; with no-mind, there is nothing at all."

The disciple asks: "Since the Master says that everywhere is entirely no-mind, wood and stone also have no mind. Are you not the same as wood and stone?"

The Master answers: "My no-mind-mind is not the same as wood and stone. Why? Consider the heavenly drum: though it has no mind, it naturally produces all kinds of wondrous Dharma that teaches and transforms sentient beings. Consider also the wish-fulfilling jewel: though it has no mind, it naturally produces all kinds of transformations. My no-mind is likewise. Though I have no mind, I am fully able to perceive the true characteristics of all dharmas. I am endowed with true prajñā and the three bodies, with sovereign mastery of responsive function, without obstruction. Therefore the *Ratnakūṭa Sūtra* says: 'With no mind-intention, yet manifesting practice.' How could this be the same as wood and stone? No-mind is the true mind. The true mind is no-mind."

The disciple asks: "Now, within the mind, how should one cultivate practice?"

The Master answers: "Just, in all affairs, be aware that there is no mind. This itself is cultivation. There is no other, separate cultivation. Therefore, know that no-mind is everything. Quiescent extinction is no-mind."

The disciple thereupon suddenly had a great enlightenment, and for the first time knew that beyond mind there are no things, and beyond things there is no mind. Every gesture, every movement and action was entirely free. The net of doubt was severed, and there were no further entanglements. He rose, made a bow, and inscribed a verse on no-mind:

> The spirit of the mind tends toward quiescence, without color, without form.
> Looking at it, one does not see it; listening for it, there is no sound.
> It seems dark yet is not dark; it seems bright yet is not bright.
> Abandon it and it is not destroyed; grasp at it and it is not born.
>
> When great, it fills and pervades the dharma-realm.
> When small, it does not cease even at a hair-tip.
> Afflictions, mixed with it, do not make it turbid.
> Nirvāṇa, clarified in it, does not make it pure.
> True suchness is originally without discrimination,
> Yet it can discern sentient and non-sentient.
> Gather it, and nothing whatsoever is established.
> Scatter it, and it pervades all beings universally.
> Its wondrous spirit is not what thought can fathom;
> Right seeking transcends all cultivation.
> Extinguished, one does not see its loss;
> Born, one does not see its accomplishment.
> The great path is quiescent, its title "signlessness";
> The myriad images are deep and dark, their title "namelessness."
> Such operation, such sovereign freedom:
> All of this is the essence of no-mind.

The Master further said: "Among all prajñās, the prajñā of no-mind is the most supreme. Therefore the *Vimalakīrti Sūtra* says: 'With no mind-intention and no action of reception, completely vanquishing the non-Buddhists.' And the *Dharmadundubhi Sūtra* says: 'If one knows that no mind can be obtained, then dharmas too are unobtainable, sin and merit are also unobtainable, and birth and death and nirvāṇa are also unobtainable. Indeed, everything is entirely unobtainable. "Unobtainable" too is unobtainable.'" He then composed a verse:

> In former times, when confused, there was a mind.
> At the moment of awakening, I understood there is no mind.
> Though there is no mind, it can illumine and function.
> Illumining and functioning in constant quiescence: this is suchness as it is.

He repeated:

> No mind, no illumination, and no function.
> No illumination, no function: this is non-action.
> This is the Tathāgata's true dharma-realm,
> Not the same as the bodhisattva's or the pratyekabuddha's.

"'No-mind' means there is no mind of false conceptions."

He was further asked: "What is called 'the Supreme'?"

The Master answered: "'Supreme' (*tài*) means 'great'; 'above' (*shàng*) means 'lofty.' It refers to the ultimate principle of lofty subtlety; hence it is called 'the Supreme.' Furthermore, 'supreme' refers to the position of universal penetration. The heavens of the three realms, though they have the longevity of extended tranquility, eventually exhaust their merit. Therefore they still transmigrate through the six destinies. This is not sufficient to be 'supreme.' The bodhisattvas of the ten stages, though they have departed from birth and death, have not yet exhausted the subtle principle. This too is not yet 'supreme.' The bodhisattvas of the ten stages cultivate the mind: the false enters the non-existent, and then there is no non-existent. Both are relinquished, and one does not falsely cling to the middle way. This too is not yet 'supreme.' Furthermore, forgetting the middle way, all three places are completely exhausted. The position is entirely that of wondrous awakening. The bodhisattva, though he has relinquished the three places, cannot be without that which he considers wondrous. This too is not yet 'supreme.' Furthermore, forgetting that wondrousness, the Buddha's path reaches the ultimate. There is nothing preserved. Without preserved thoughts, there are no thoughts or deliberations. All false mind-wisdom is forever stilled. Illumination and awareness are both exhausted. Quiescent and without action: this is called 'supreme.' 'Supreme' has the meaning of the ultimate of principle. 'Above' means without equal. Therefore it is called 'the Supreme.' This is another name for the Buddha-Tathāgata."
