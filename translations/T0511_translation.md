# 佛說蓱沙王五願經
## Sūtra of King Bimbisāra's Five Wishes, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0511

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak Mountain, together with five hundred bhikṣus. At that time, the king of Rājagṛha, named Bimbisāra, from his youth as a crown prince, had always harbored five wishes: first, "May I become king while still young"; second, "May there be a Buddha in my country"; third, "May I always be able to go back and forth to the Buddha's dwelling"; fourth, "May I always hear the Buddha teach the sūtras"; fifth, "May my mind quickly open and understand upon hearing the sūtras, and may I attain the fruit of stream-entry." These five wishes were all fulfilled for King Bimbisāra.

At that time, to the north of the land of Rājagṛha, there was another country called Dekhairā. Its king, named Pukkusāti, was greatly exalted and wondrous. In a past life he had encountered a Buddha, received the Buddha's sūtra teaching, and studied the sūtra of the six constituents within the body. What are the six constituents? First, there is earth within the body. Second, there is water within the body. Third, there is fire within the body. Fourth, there is wind within the body. Fifth, there is space within the body. Sixth, there is mind within the body. Within the body there are these six constituents in total.

King Bimbisāra and King Pukkusāti had never met in their lives, but from afar they loved and respected each other like brothers, constantly exchanging letters and gifts without cease. King Bimbisāra always thought: "If only I could obtain something supremely rare and wonderful to send to Pukkusāti." King Pukkusāti also always thought: "If only I could obtain something supremely rare and wonderful to send to King Bimbisāra."

In King Pukkusāti's country, a lotus flower suddenly appeared. A single blossom had a thousand petals, all of golden color. He sent an envoy to present it to King Bimbisāra. When King Bimbisāra saw the flower, he was overjoyed and said: "King Pukkusāti has sent me something supremely rare and extraordinary."

King Bimbisāra wrote a letter to King Pukkusāti saying: "In my country there is an abundance of gold, silver, and precious treasures, but I do not regard them as treasures. In my country, a human flower has appeared. This human flower is called the Buddha, whose body is the color of refined gold and who possesses the thirty-two marks." When King Pukkusāti read the letter and heard the word "Buddha," he was filled with great joy and excitement, and his hair stood on end, because in a past life he had already seen a Buddha, and so his hair stood on end.

King Pukkusāti wrote a letter to King Bimbisāra: "I wish to hear in full the practices and teachings of the divine Buddha, and what I should practice and follow. Please inform me of everything."

Several days later, King Pukkusāti thought to himself: "A human life is uncertain; it hangs by a breath. I cannot wait for Bimbisāra's reply. It would be better to go myself and see the Buddha."

King Pukkusāti was sovereign over ninety-nine minor kingdoms. When the minor kings came to court, Pukkusāti commanded the minor kings, his ministers, officials, and all his troops to prepare their carriages and set out for the land of Rājagṛha, to the Buddha's dwelling. On the road he met a letter from King Bimbisāra. The letter said: "The Buddha teaches people to leave the household, abandon wife and children, sever desire, shave the head and beard, don the Dharma robes, and become a śramaṇa. Why? Because people are deluded. To do what should not be done is delusion. From delusion arise formations; from formations arises consciousness; from consciousness arise name-and-form; from name-and-form arise the six sense-bases. What are the six sense-bases? First, the eye; second, the ear; third, the nose; fourth, the mouth; fifth, the body; sixth, the mind. These are the six. These six things all face outward: the eye faces form; the ear faces sound; the nose faces scent; the mouth faces taste; the body faces the soft and smooth; the mind faces desire. These are the six facings. From the six facings arises contact; from contact arise pleasure and pain; from pleasure and pain arises craving; from craving arises grasping; from grasping arises becoming; from becoming arises birth; from birth arise old age, death, sorrow, grief, suffering, discontent, and affliction. Thus arises the great mass of suffering, following its habits. The aggregate of all this toil and suffering is what is called a person. The wise one eliminates delusion. When delusion is exhausted, all evils are eradicated. When evils are eradicated, formations are exhausted. When formations are exhausted, consciousness is exhausted. When consciousness is exhausted, name-and-form is exhausted. When name-and-form is exhausted, the six sense-bases are exhausted. When the six sense-bases are exhausted, contact is exhausted. When contact is exhausted, pleasure and pain are exhausted. When pleasure and pain are exhausted, craving is exhausted. When craving is exhausted, grasping is exhausted. When grasping is exhausted, becoming is exhausted. When becoming is exhausted, birth is exhausted. When birth is exhausted, old age and death are exhausted. When old age and death are exhausted, sorrow, grief, suffering, discontent, and affliction, and thus the great mass of suffering following its habits, are all exhausted. Then one is no longer born; not being born, one attains the path of nirvāṇa, the unconditioned."

When King Pukkusāti had finished reading the letter, he reflected deeply. Late at night, after all the ministers, officials, and soldiers were asleep, in utter silence, he secretly rose and slipped away. He went among the tombs, shaved his own head, put on the Dharma robes, and became a śramaṇa. Having no alms bowl, he took the skull of a person long dead in the charnel ground, scraped and washed it clean, and used it as his bowl. Carrying this skull-bowl, he traveled onward until he arrived in the territory of King Bimbisāra's Rājagṛha. He stopped outside the city, looked up at the sun, and thought: "It is too late to go to the Buddha today; I will go tomorrow." Pukkusāti then approached a potter's house: "I ask to stay for one night."

The potter said: "Very good! My dwelling is fortunately spacious; there is a place to stay."

King Pukkusāti took a small grass mat from outside and entered. In a secluded spot he spread the seat, sat upon it, and contemplated his inner organs.

The Buddha, with his divine eye, from Vulture Peak Mountain saw King Pukkusāti from afar. He had arrived in Rājagṛha and was staying outside the city at the potter's house. The Buddha reflected that King Pukkusāti's life would end the next day, and he feared they would never meet.

The Buddha then flew and arrived at the potter's door. The Buddha said to the potter: "I ask to stay for one night."

The potter replied: "My dwelling is fortunately large, and can accommodate you. A śramaṇa just came to stay for the night. Please speak to him; if it is agreeable to both, you may stay for the night."

The Buddha then went to where King Pukkusāti was and said: "I asked the host to stay one night. He said I should speak with you. Will you allow me to stay one night?"

Pukkusāti said: "I have only a small grass mat, just enough to sit on. This dwelling is fortunately spacious; you may freely stay wherever you wish."

The Buddha then found a small grass mat nearby and sat down in one spot. The Buddha sat upright through three watches of the night, and Pukkusāti also sat upright. The Buddha thought to himself: "This Pukkusāti sits with such composure, so still and tranquil, without moving or swaying." The Buddha wished to test him, and decided to go forward and ask why he had become a śramaṇa. The Buddha rose and went to Pukkusāti, asking: "From what teacher did you receive the path? Why did you become a śramaṇa?"

Pukkusāti replied: "I heard there is a Buddha, of the Gautama clan, whose father is named Śuddhodana, the Pure-Rice King. His son shaved his head and beard and became a śramaṇa, attaining the Buddha's path. I take him as my teacher. It is because of the Buddha that I became a śramaṇa. The Buddha's sūtra teaching has entered my heart, and I greatly rejoice in it."

The Buddha asked: "Have you ever seen the Buddha?"

Pukkusāti said: "I have never seen him."

"If you were to see him, would you be able to recognize him as the Buddha?"

Pukkusāti said: "If I saw him, I would not be able to recognize him."

The Buddha thought: "This worthy man has become a śramaṇa for my sake. I should now teach him the sūtra of what he knew in past lives; only then will he quickly understand." The Buddha said to Pukkusāti: "I will teach you a sūtra. Its words are good at the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end. I will teach you the matter of the six constituents within the body. Listen well!"

Pukkusāti said: "Excellent!"

The Buddha said: "When these six things come together, they can form a human body. A human body is composed of six things, by which there is perception and awareness. A person's inclinations are turned by eighteen things. There are four things that a practitioner should follow. When these are followed, the inclinations no longer turn. When the inclinations no longer turn, one attains the path. Having attained the path, there is no more birth, no more old age, no more sickness, no more death in this life or in future lives, no more grief, no more worry, no more anger, no more longing, no more craving. This is the path that transcends the world.

"I will now explain how the six things together form what is called a person. Listen carefully! First, earth; second, water; third, fire; fourth, wind; fifth, space; sixth, mind. What is earth? Earth has two aspects: the earth of the body and external earth. What is the earth of the body? It is the hair of the head, body hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, spleen, kidneys, liver, lungs, intestines, and stomach: all that is solid within the body is earth. The earth of the body and external earth are both earth together. The earth of the body and external earth are not my earth; there is nothing further to crave, desire, or know. One should contemplate this deeply and thereby gain understanding.

"What is water? Water has two aspects: the water of the body and external water. What is the water of the body? It is tears, mucus, saliva, pus, blood, sweat, fat, marrow, brain, and urine: all that is liquid within the body is water. The water of the body and external water are both water together. The water of the body and external water are not my water; there is nothing further to crave, desire, or know. One should contemplate this deeply and thereby gain understanding.

"What is fire? Fire has two aspects: the fire of the body and external fire. What is the fire of the body? It is the warmth and heat within the body and the digestive heat in the abdomen: all that is warm within the body is fire. The fire of the body and external fire are both fire together. The fire of the body and external fire are not my fire; there is nothing further to crave, desire, or know. One should contemplate this deeply and thereby gain understanding.

"What is wind? Wind has two aspects: the wind of the body and external wind. What is the wind of the body? It is the upward-moving wind, the downward-moving wind, the wind in the bones, the wind in the abdomen, the wind in the four limbs, and the breath: all that moves within the body is wind. The wind of the body and external wind are both wind together. The wind of the body and external wind are not my wind; there is nothing further to crave, desire, or know. One should contemplate this deeply and thereby gain understanding.

"What is space? Space has two aspects: the space of the body and external space. What is the space of the body? It is the space of the eyes, the space of the ears, the space of the nose, the space of the mouth, the space of the throat, the space of the abdomen, the space of the stomach, and the space through which food enters and exits. This is the space of the body. The space of the body and external space are both space together. The space of the body and external space are not my space; there is nothing further to crave, desire, or know. One should contemplate this deeply and thereby gain understanding.

"The wise one who studies the path can distinguish and know these five constituents within the body. The remaining one is mind. When the mind is pure and without desire, one thinks: 'I am as pure as this.' If one should wish to ascend to the twenty-fifth heaven of space-wisdom, one might fear that the lifespan of thousands of kalpas in that heaven would leave one unable to gain liberation. If one should wish to ascend to the twenty-sixth heaven of consciousness-wisdom, with a lifespan double that of the twenty-fifth heaven, one might fear again of not gaining liberation. If one should wish to ascend to the twenty-seventh heaven of non-ideation-wisdom, with a lifespan double that of the twenty-sixth heaven, one might fear again of not gaining liberation. If one should wish to ascend to the twenty-eighth heaven of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, with a lifespan of eighty-four million kalpas, one might fear again of not gaining liberation. The mind then grows weary of suffering. When the lifespan is long and liberation is not attained, one takes the path of nirvāṇa.

"What are the six contacts? The eye contacts form; the ear contacts sound; the nose contacts scent; the tongue contacts taste; the body contacts the smooth and soft; the mind contacts knowing. These are the six contacts.

"What are the eighteen turnings of inclination? The eye turns toward beautiful form, turns toward ugly form, and turns toward neutral form. The ear turns toward pleasant sound, toward sorrowful sound, and toward harsh sound. The nose turns toward pleasant scent, toward unpleasant scent, and toward foul scent. The tongue turns toward delicious taste, toward unpleasant taste, and toward tasteless things. The body turns toward the soft and smooth, toward the coarse and hard, and toward the cold and warm. The mind turns toward wholesome things, toward unwholesome things, and toward worldly things. These are the eighteen turnings of inclination.

"What are the four things that firmly control a person? First, sincerity. Second, equanimity. Third, wisdom. Fourth, the destruction of all evils. These are the four firm resolves. The eye craves and desires; when one obtains what it desires, there is pleasure. When pleasure departs from a person, one perceives that it has passed. From suffering comes suffering. To be able to know it as suffering, and when suffering has passed, to know that one is freed from suffering: a person walks through suffering and finds happiness difficult to attain. One should contemplate and cut off all unwholesome things. Through this one attains neither suffering nor pleasure, and knows that one is far removed from all suffering. It is like two sticks rubbed together producing fire; when the two sticks are separated and placed apart, the fire is extinguished and the wood grows cold. When love and attachment come together, suffering results; when love and attachment are abandoned, one knows that one is freed. It is like a goldsmith who obtains fine gold and can freely make whatever marvelous things he wishes: bracelets, earrings, anklets, flowers of light, and a hundred other things, all of which he can make. A practitioner should hold the mind just like this goldsmith, free to choose, not wishing to be born in the twenty-fifth, twenty-sixth, twenty-seventh, or twenty-eighth heavens. For though all of these truly exist, however long they last they will decay; all will pass away; nothing is permanent. Knowing that all must pass, the mind no longer turns toward it, no longer thinks of it, no longer longs for it, no longer craves it. This is called the unconditioned. The wise one contemplates thus; this alone is what is lofty. When a person is far removed from all evils, then truly this is wisdom. Everything the eye sees will pass away; nothing is permanent. The unconditioned neither comes nor goes. A practitioner who knows this trusts in the unconditioned path; among all things, it is the most sincere. Before attaining the path, whatever one took pleasure in and loved arose from body and mind. Having attained the path, all is abandoned. When one abandons the love and attachment of whatever one is immersed in, this is called the unconditioned.

"If the mind clings to lust, liberation is not attained. If the mind clings to anger, liberation is not attained. If the mind clings to delusion, liberation is not attained. A practitioner who knows this abandons the mind of lust, abandons the mind of anger, abandons the mind of delusion, pulls up the root of love and attachment, cuts off its branches, severs its stem, and it no longer grows. This is called the unconditioned.

"If one thinks 'I have a self,' the mind is again agitated. If one thinks 'I have no self,' the mind is again agitated. If one thinks 'I am handsome,' the mind is again agitated. If one thinks 'I am not handsome,' the mind is again agitated. One should reflect in advance: this is illness, this is suffering, this is pain, this is the failure to gain liberation. Therefore, one should not engage in excessive thinking. This is the essential point concerning all suffering."

Pukkusāti had not known that this was the Buddha, but having attained the third fruit of non-returning, he was then able to recognize that it was the Buddha. He immediately rose and placed his head at the Buddha's feet, saying: "I was truly foolish and lacking in propriety; I failed to show proper reverence." The Buddha then manifested his radiance and supernatural power. Pukkusāti then repented and said: "I am a foolish person."

The Buddha said: "If you can repent, that is good. Your fault is thereby removed."

Pukkusāti said: "I wish to be ordained as a śramaṇa under you."

The Buddha asked: "To become a śramaṇa, do you have the robes and bowl ready?"

Pukkusāti said: "I do not have them yet."

The Buddha said: "Without the robes and bowl of a śramaṇa, one cannot be ordained."

Pukkusāti said: "Very well. I shall go and obtain them."

The Buddha said: "Very good!"

Pukkusāti rose, made obeisance to the Buddha, and circumambulated him three times.

The next day, Pukkusāti entered the city. He had not gone far into the city when a young cow with few teeth charged wildly and gored him with its horns. When the bhikṣus heard of this, they reported to the Buddha: "Yesterday you taught a sūtra to a śramaṇa at the potter's house. He left to obtain the robes and bowl and was gored to death by a charging cow. What destiny will he attain?"

The Buddha said: "This great elder received the sūtra I taught and practiced it with his whole heart. He immediately attained the third fruit: stream-entry, once-returning, and non-returning. He thereby abandoned the five hindrances: first, lust; second, anger; third, sloth and torpor; fourth, frivolous amusement; fifth, remorseful doubt and wrong-mindedness. He is now reborn in the sixteenth heaven above, in the realm of the non-returners, and from there in the heavens he will attain arhatship and pass beyond the world. Now, bhikṣus, you should together take Pukkusāti's body and properly bury it, and erect a stūpa over the site." The bhikṣus received the Buddha's instruction and built a stūpa for him.

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, all the bhikṣus joined their palms and made obeisance to the Buddha.
