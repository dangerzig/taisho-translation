# 阿闍世王授決經
## Sūtra of King Ajātaśatru Receiving the Prediction

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0509

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Faju (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak Mountain.

At that time, King Ajātaśatru had invited the Buddha for a meal. When the meal was finished, the Buddha returned to the Jetavana Monastery. The king consulted with Jīvaka, saying: "Today I invited the Buddha, and the Buddha has finished his meal. What else should be done?" Jīvaka said: "It would be best to light many lamps." Thereupon the king ordered a hundred measures of hemp oil to be prepared, and lamps were set from the palace gate all the way to the Jetavana Monastery.

At that time, there was a destitute old woman who had always had a sincere wish to make offerings to the Buddha but had no means. Seeing the king perform this merit, she was deeply moved. She went begging and obtained two coins, and with them went to the oil merchant's shop to buy oil. The oil merchant said: "Old woman, you are so terribly poor. You begged two coins; why not buy food to sustain yourself? What will you do with this oil?"

The old woman said: "I have heard that a Buddha is born only rarely, encountered once in a hundred kalpas. I am fortunate to have been born in the Buddha's time, yet I have made no offering. Today I saw the king perform this great, magnificent, and immeasurable merit, and it stirred my heart. Though I am truly destitute, I still wish to light a single lamp, to serve as the root for future lives." Thereupon the oil merchant, recognizing her sincerity, gave her the oil that two coins would normally buy, which was two measures, and further added three measures extra, making five measures in all. The old woman then went and lit her lamp before the Buddha. She calculated that this oil would not last even half the night. She then made a vow: "If in a future life I attain the path as the Buddha has, may this oil last through the entire night, with its light undiminished." She made obeisance and departed.

Of the lamps the king had lit, some went out and some ran dry. Though there were attendants, they could not keep them all lit. But the single lamp of the old woman burned with a particularly bright radiance surpassing all the other lamps. Through the entire night it did not go out, and the oil was not exhausted. When the next morning came, the old woman returned and bowed her head to the ground before the Buddha, then stood to one side with palms joined.

The Buddha told Maudgalyāyana: "It is now daylight. You may extinguish the lamps." Maudgalyāyana received the instruction and extinguished the lamps one by one. All the lamps were put out, but the single lamp of the old woman could not be extinguished despite three attempts. He then lifted his robe and fanned it. The lamp's light grew even brighter. He then used his supernatural power to summon a wind to blow along the lamps in sequence, but the old woman's lamp blazed still more fiercely, its light rising to illuminate the Brahmā heaven and spreading to illuminate the three-thousand worlds, all of which could see its light.

The Buddha told Maudgalyāyana: "Stop! Stop! This is the radiant merit of a future Buddha. It is not something your supernatural power can extinguish. In her past lives, this old woman has made offerings to one hundred and eighty billion Buddhas. From those former Buddhas she received a prediction, and her task has been to teach, guide, and transform the people through the sūtras and Dharma. She has not yet had the opportunity to practice generosity, and therefore in this life she is destitute and without possessions. After thirty kalpas from now, when her merit is complete, she will become a Buddha named Sumerupradīpa Tathāgata, Arhat. In her world there will be no sun or moon; the bodies of all people will possess a great radiance, and the palaces and treasures will illuminate one another, just as in the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven above."

The old woman, hearing the prediction, was overjoyed. Instantly she became light, her body rising into the sky to a height of one hundred and eighty fathoms. She descended, bowed her head to the ground in obeisance, and departed.

The king, hearing of this, asked Jīvaka: "I performed merit of such magnificent scale, and yet the Buddha did not give me a prediction. This old woman lit a single lamp and received a prediction. Why is this?"

Jīvaka said: "Though the king's deeds are many, his mind was not single-pointed. It does not compare to this old woman's focused devotion to the Buddha." The king then went again to invite the Buddha. He ordered all the garden overseers to gather fine flowers each morning and deliver them to the palace by midday.

The Buddha went out from the Jetavana Monastery early in the morning, walking slowly and at ease, teaching the Dharma to the people along the road. He arrived at the palace around midday. There was a garden overseer who had just taken flowers out of the garden. He happened to meet the Buddha at the crossroads of the great avenue. Hearing the Buddha teach the sūtra, his heart was filled with single-minded joy. He took all the flowers he was carrying and scattered them upon the Buddha. The flowers remained suspended in the air above the Buddha's head. The Buddha then gave him a prediction: "You have already made offerings to ninety billion Buddhas. After one hundred and forty kalpas from now, you will become a Buddha named Buddhapuṣpa Tathāgata." The man was overjoyed. Instantly he became light, his body rising into the sky. He descended, made obeisance, and then thought to himself: "The king is by nature strict and severe. He previously ordered me to observe purificatory practice, to bring these flowers to offer to the Buddha. But I have given them all to the Buddha myself. I return empty-handed. He will certainly have me killed." He then went straight home, set the empty flower basket outside the door, went in, and told his wife: "I have not yet eaten this morning. The king will now have me killed. Quickly prepare food for me."

His wife, hearing this, was greatly alarmed. "Why would the king kill you?" He then told his wife the whole story from beginning to end. His wife went out to the kitchen to prepare food. Śakra, Lord of the Devas, then filled the empty basket with heavenly flowers. When the wife brought the food back, she saw the basket by the door full of flowers, as before, but their radiance and color were extraordinary. She went in and told her husband. He went out to look, recognized that they were heavenly flowers, and was greatly overjoyed. He decided not to eat and instead carried the flowers out.

The king had just come out to welcome the Buddha and met the overseer on the road. The king saw how exceptionally beautiful the flowers were, unlike anything in the world. He asked the overseer: "My garden has such fine flowers as these? Yet all this time you have never delivered them to me. Your crime deserves death. Do you know that?"

The overseer said: "Great King! The garden does not have such flowers. This morning, while I was on the road carrying the garden flowers, I met the Buddha. My joy was beyond measure, and I scattered them all upon the Buddha. He then gave me a prediction. I knew I would be put to death, so I stopped at home to ask for food. In the time it took, I went back out and saw in the empty basket these flowers. They must be heavenly flowers, not from the garden. Now, my birth is lowly; I serve the king as a garden keeper, bound and restrained by the court, unable to practice the path freely. Having already received the prediction, even if I die, I will surely be reborn in the heavens, before the Buddhas of the ten directions, with no restraints, able to practice the path as I please. If the king puts me to death, it matters nothing to me."

The king, hearing about the prediction, was filled with shame and awe. His hair stood on end. He immediately rose, made obeisance, and knelt to confess and repent. When the Buddha arrived at the palace, the meal was served. When the meal was finished, the Buddha spoke a blessing and departed.

The king then asked Jīvaka: "The previous time I invited the Buddha, the old woman received a prediction. Today, when I established merit, the garden overseer received a prediction. Why have I alone obtained nothing at all? My heart is deeply grieved. What other kind of merit should I perform?"

Jīvaka said: "Though the king has set up merit on consecutive days, it was done with the treasury of the state, using the labor of the people. Perhaps his mind was proud, perhaps his thoughts were angry. Therefore he has not received a prediction. Now you should cut back your personal provisions, remove your necklaces and jeweled ornaments of the seven treasures, and fashion jeweled flowers from them. Together with the queen and the crown prince, you should join palms with united effort, and with your own hands, with single-minded diligence, offer them to the Buddha. The Buddha, seeing the king's utter sincerity, will surely give the prediction."

Thereupon the king reduced his kitchen provisions and observed purificatory precepts day and night. He removed the treasures from his own body, gathered them all together, and before the assembled artisans, fashioned flowers from them. The king, the queen, and the crown prince all worked with their own hands. After ninety days, everything was completed. He ordered his attendants to prepare the carriage, for he would go and offer them to the Buddha. A minister reported: "We have heard that the Buddha has previously gone to the land of Kuśinagara and has already entered parinirvāṇa."

The king, hearing this, was stricken with grief. His tears streamed and his voice choked: "I labored with utter sincerity, making these flowers with my own hands. Though the Buddha has entered parinirvāṇa, I shall still bring them to Vulture Peak Mountain and offer them at the place where the Buddha sat, to express my heart's devotion."

Jīvaka said: "The Buddha is without body, and there is also no nirvāṇa. He neither abides permanently nor ceases nor has location. Only one who has utter sincerity can see the Buddha. Even when the Buddha was in the world, those without sincerity did not see the Buddha. Since the great king's sincerity is so deep, even though the Buddha has entered parinirvāṇa, if you go you will surely see the Buddha."

He then went to Vulture Peak Mountain. There he saw the Buddha, and with both grief and joy, his tears streaming, he advanced, bowed his head to the ground in obeisance, and scattered the jeweled flowers upon the Buddha. The flowers remained suspended in the air, transforming into a jeweled canopy directly above the Buddha. The Buddha then gave the king a prediction: "After eighty thousand kalpas from now, in a kalpa named Joyous Observation, you will become a Buddha. Your Buddha-name will be Viśuddhadṛṣṭi Tathāgata. Your buddha-land will be named Flower King. At that time, the people's lifespan will be forty minor kalpas."

King Ajātaśatru's crown prince, named Candravāḍi, who was then eight years old, seeing his father receive the prediction, was greatly overjoyed. He removed the treasures from his own body and scattered them upon the Buddha, saying: "When Viśuddhadṛṣṭi becomes a Buddha, may I be a golden-wheel-turning king and make offerings to the Buddha. After the Buddha enters parinirvāṇa, may I succeed him and become a Buddha." The scattered treasures transformed into an open-lattice canopy, covering the Buddha directly above.

The Buddha said: "It shall be as you wish. When the king becomes a Buddha, you will certainly be a golden-wheel-turning king. When your life ends, you will be reborn in the Tuṣita Heaven. When your heavenly lifespan is exhausted, you will descend and become a Buddha in the Bhaiṣajyarāja buddha-land, teaching and guiding. Your Buddha-name will be Candana. The people's lifespan and the qualities of that land will all be the same as those of Viśuddhadṛṣṭi."

When the Buddha had just finished giving the prediction, the king and Candravāḍi came forward to make obeisance to the Buddha. Suddenly they could no longer see the Buddha's form.
