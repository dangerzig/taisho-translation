# 皇帝降誕日於麟德殿講大方廣佛華嚴經玄義一部
## Lecture on the Profound Meaning of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra Delivered at Linde Hall on the Emperor's Birthday

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1743

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, composed by Jingjū (靜居).

---

This sūtra has seven locations, nine assemblies, thirty-nine chapters, and a total of eighty fascicles. The first assembly, at the site of enlightenment, comprises six chapters: (1) *Wondrous Adornments of the World-Lords*, five fascicles; (2) *Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, one fascicle; (3) *Samādhi of Samantabhadra* and (4) *Formation of the Worlds*, two chapters sharing one fascicle; (5) *The Flower-Treasury World*, three fascicles; (6) *Vairocana*, one fascicle. The second assembly, at the Universally Radiant Dharma Hall, comprises six chapters: (7) *Names of the Tathāgata* and (8) *The Four Noble Truths*, two chapters sharing one fascicle; (9) *Radiant Awakening* and (10) *Bodhisattvas Ask for Clarification*, two chapters sharing one fascicle; (11) *Purifying Practices* and (12) *Worthy Leader*, two chapters sharing two fascicles. The third assembly, at the summit of Mount Sumeru, comprises six chapters: (13) *Ascending Mount Sumeru*, (14) *Verse-Praises at the Summit of Sumeru*, and (15) *The Ten Abodes*, three chapters sharing one fascicle; (16) *Brahma-Conduct* and (17) *Merit of the Initial Resolve*, two chapters sharing one fascicle; (18) *Clarifying the Dharma*, one fascicle. The fourth assembly, at the Yāma Heaven palace, comprises four chapters: (19) *Ascending the Yāma Heaven Palace*, (20) *Verse-Praises in the Yāma Palace*, and (21) *The Ten Practices*, three chapters sharing two fascicles; (22) *The Ten Inexhaustible Treasuries*, one fascicle. The fifth assembly, at the Tuṣita Heaven palace, comprises three chapters: (23) *Ascending the Tuṣita Heaven Palace*, one fascicle; (24) *Verse-Praises in the Tuṣita Palace* and (25) *The Ten Dedications*, two chapters sharing eleven fascicles. The sixth assembly, at the Paranirmita-vaśavartin Heaven, comprises one chapter: (26) *The Ten Grounds*, six fascicles. The seventh assembly, returning to the Universally Radiant Dharma Hall, comprises eleven chapters: (27) *The Ten Samādhis*, four fascicles; (28) *The Ten Superknowledges* and (29) *The Ten Patiences*, two chapters sharing one fascicle; (30) *Incalculable Numbers*, (31) *The Tathāgata's Lifespan*, and (32) *Dwelling Places of the Bodhisattvas*, three chapters sharing one fascicle; (33) *Inconceivable Dharmas of the Buddha*, two fascicles; (34) *Ocean of Marks of the Tathāgata's Ten Bodies* and (35) *Merit of the Tathāgata's Minor Marks and Radiance*, two chapters sharing one fascicle; (36) *Practices of Samantabhadra*, one fascicle; (37) *Manifestation of the Tathāgata*, three fascicles. The eighth assembly, again at the Universally Radiant Dharma Hall, comprises one chapter: (38) *Transcending the World*, seven fascicles. The ninth assembly, at the Jeta Grove, comprises one chapter: (39) *Entering the Dharma-Realm*, twenty-one fascicles.

---

The title reads *Buddhāvataṃsaka Mahāvaipulya Sūtra*. "Great, square, and broad" refers to the Dharma; "buddha flower-adornment" refers to the person. Furthermore, "great" has the meaning of the essence being all-pervading; "square and broad" is named for the function being universally complete. Arising of function based on essence is called "great and broad." "Buddha" is the person who realizes and expounds it. "Flower-adornment" means adorning the fruit through the cultivation of causes. The sūtra expresses that meaning; hence it is called the *Buddhāvataṃsaka Mahāvaipulya Sūtra*. This sūtra has a total of seven locations, nine assemblies, thirty-nine chapters, and eighty fascicles.

The first assembly is at the site of enlightenment, the araṇya dharma. The sūtra has six chapters, elucidating the Buddha's fruit-virtues. Vairocana had just achieved right awakening. The world-lords of the ten directions, such as the Great Sovereign Deva King, together with the bodhisattvas, a total of forty assemblies of the same class of retinue, each as numerous as the atoms in a buddha-land, all came to the assembly. Hence there is the chapter *Wondrous Adornments of the World-Lords*. Once the ocean-assembly had gathered, the Buddha manifested his true form; hence there is the chapter *Manifestation of the Tathāgata*. Since the true marks transcend marks and are not what lesser bodhisattvas can contemplate, the great being Samantabhadra entered the samādhi of sovereign mastery and realized the buddha-realm; hence there is the chapter *Samādhi of Samantabhadra*. The Buddha has a retribution-environment; within this retribution-environment, the enabling causes and conditions are first explained; hence there is the chapter *Formation of the Worlds*. Next, the dependent buddha-land ocean is elucidated; hence there is the chapter *Flower-Treasury World*. Once the retribution-environment is made manifest, the grand and magnificent direct reward belongs to the august one; hence there is the chapter *Vairocana*. Furthermore, this first assembly constitutes the object of faith and aspiration.

The second assembly, without departing from the site of enlightenment, responds to the invitation of the nāga palace and is called the Universally Radiant Dharma Hall assembly. The sūtra has six chapters, elucidating the mind of the one who has faith. The Buddha responds to sentient beings, first manifesting the three kinds of karma: names are established in terms of bodily karma, hence *Names of the Tathāgata*; teachings are proclaimed in terms of verbal karma, hence *The Four Noble Truths*; the assembly is alerted in terms of mental karma, hence *Radiant Awakening*. To give rise to right faith, one must first have right understanding; hence *Bodhisattvas Ask for Clarification*. Understanding without practice is like talking about food to seek satiation. Practice is established through understanding and understanding through understanding; hence *Purifying Practices*. Through practice, virtue is accomplished as the source of all wholesome deeds; hence *Worthy Leader*. Although this second assembly elucidates right faith, the root of wisdom has not yet been established, like a light feather following the wind with no fixed direction.

The third assembly, without departing from the site of enlightenment, responds to the invitation of the deva king and ascends to the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven palace. The sūtra has six chapters, giving rise to the root of right wisdom. *Ascending the Trāyastriṃśa Palace*: the teaching host goes to the assembly. *Verse-Praises at the Trāyastriṃśa*: the assisting teachers praise his virtues. These two chapters are the regular feature of this assembly. *The Ten Abodes* elucidates the understanding within the abodes; *Brahma-Conduct* clarifies the practice within the abodes; *Merit of the Initial Resolve* reveals the virtue within the abodes. These three chapters are the principal teaching of this assembly. The single chapter *Clarifying the Dharma* represents the advancing stage of this assembly. In this assembly, right wisdom has been clarified.

The fourth assembly, without arising from the site of enlightenment, ascends to the Yāma Heaven palace. The sūtra has four chapters: relying on the root of wisdom, it gives rise to right practice. *Ascending the Yāma Palace*: responding to beings and harmonizing with suchness. *Verse-Praises in the Yāma Palace*: virtue is fully equipped. These two chapters give rise to what pertains to this assembly. *The Ten Practices* properly elucidates the accomplishment of practice. *The Ten Inexhaustible Treasuries* gathers practice for advancement. Although this assembly establishes the root and stem, the branches and leaves have not yet spread.

The fifth assembly, without arising from the site of enlightenment, ascends to the Tuṣita Heaven palace. The sūtra has three chapters, elucidating the dedication-vows that pervade the realm of sentient beings, providing universal shade without omission. *Ascending the Tuṣita Palace* shows that in relation to the Buddha, when there is a stimulus there is surely a response. *Verse-Praises in the Tuṣita Palace* indicates that there is no virtue of the assembly that goes unpraised. *The Ten Dedications* is the principal teaching of this assembly. "Dedication" means: turning from self toward others, turning from the small toward the great, turning from phenomena toward principle. Through the three kinds of dedication, one realizes the three bodies. The first five assemblies constitute the three stages of worthy ones up to and including the abodes.

The sixth assembly, without moving from the site of enlightenment, responds to the Paranirmita-vaśavartin Heaven palace. The sūtra has one chapter: ascending to the ten stages of holiness, elucidating the abiding in the ten grounds. The worthy ones are those who are good; the holy ones are those who are right. The reason they are called "three" is: the ten abodes are the lower worthy stage, named for wisdom and the severing of confusion; the ten practices are the middle worthy stage, named for cultivating and advancing in goodness; the ten dedications are the upper worthy stage, named for the superior vows of benefiting both self and others. Hence there are three stages of worthy ones. As for the grounds, "ground" has the meaning of producing and accomplishing, indicating that the uncontaminated holy wisdom can produce the bodhisattva's fruit-virtue of buddhahood, just as the ground produces. Turning from the ordinary and ascending to holiness, severing obstructions and realizing truth, one is newly born into the Buddha's family; hence the name "joy." The second, the ground of freedom from defilement: one purely cultivates the three kinds of karma, like fire refining gold, freeing oneself from the defilement of inadvertent precept-violations; hence the name "freedom from defilement." The third, the ground of radiance: one cultivates and realizes dhyāna-concentration, is able to manifest supernatural powers, and emits the light of wondrous wisdom; hence the name "radiance." The fourth, blazing wisdom: concentration gives rise to wisdom, and the fire of uncontaminated wisdom through the thirty-seven aids to awakening burns the fuel of afflictions; hence the name "blazing wisdom." The fifth, the ground of difficulty to conquer: the two wisdoms of ultimate and conventional truth are contrary in their operation; to harmonize them and make them compatible is therefore called "difficulty to conquer." The sixth, the ground of direct presence: the right wisdom of prajñā comprehends the twelve links of dependent origination, uproots the root of ignorance, enters the three liberations, and the truth of the emptiness of dharmas clearly manifests before one; hence the name "direct presence." The seventh, the ground of far-going: one perpetually abides in samādhi, which does not obstruct the arising of function. One transcends existence and non-existence, and those of lower stages cannot fathom it. In the first six grounds, humans, devas, and the two vehicles may still share in the practices; but here one travels alone, and hence it is called "far-going." The eighth, the ground of immovability: one realizes the patience of the unborn and is not moved by marks, function, effort, or practice. Receiving the Buddha's seven exhortations, one thereby gives rise to the ten-body mutual function and the ten kinds of sovereign mastery, and within the swift current of the Dharma one turns effortlessly without deliberate intention; hence the name "immovability." The ninth, the ground of excellent wisdom: having previously realized the Dharma-essence, one now gives rise to function. One specially studies the repose, speech, and silence of the Dharma-king; one possesses the four kinds of unimpeded eloquence adapted to the characteristics of sentient beings, and serves as a great Dharma-teacher. There is no request that goes unanswered; hence the name "excellent wisdom." The tenth, the ground of Dharma-cloud: the great Dharma-wisdom cloud can block the dust of confusion; it can receive all the rain of the buddhas' Dharma; and it further rains the Dharma universally upon all beings; hence the name "Dharma-cloud." Furthermore, the first, second, and third grounds correspond to the practices of humans and devas; the fourth, fifth, and sixth grounds correspond to the practices of the two vehicles; the seventh ground corresponds to the practice of the Great Vehicle; and the eighth, ninth, and tenth grounds enter the practice of the One Vehicle. Hence there are ten stages of holiness.

The seventh assembly, without moving from the site of enlightenment, returns to the Universally Radiant Dharma Hall. The sūtra has eleven chapters. Previously the three stages of worthy ones and the ten stages of holiness were elucidated; the positions of practice are about to be completed. Now the cause is perfected and the fruit fulfilled. *The Ten Samādhis*: one cultivates the ten samādhis and realizes that what exists is precisely non-existent. *The Ten Superknowledges*: the ten wisdoms illuminate, and one knows that emptiness is precisely not empty. *The Ten Patiences*: concentration and wisdom are in equilibrium, and emptiness and existence are unified. *Incalculable Numbers*: the numbers are so many that calculation cannot reach them, indicating the vastness of this virtue. *The Tathāgata's Lifespan* extends vertically to the future limit. *Dwelling Places of the Bodhisattvas* extends horizontally throughout the ten directions. These six chapters above all pertain to the merits of the bodhisattva-grounds; they represent the cause of virtual awakening. The five chapters below bring together both text and principle in perfect interfusion: the fruit of wondrous awakening is fulfilled. To achieve wondrous awakening, one first realizes the wondrous Dharma; hence *Inconceivable Dharmas of the Buddha*. Once the wondrous Dharma is established, it must accomplish the wondrous body; hence *Ocean of Marks of the Ten Bodies*. Once the great marks are accomplished, the virtue of the minor marks is then elucidated; hence *Merit of the Minor Marks and Radiance*. Once the bridge is complete, the function is to ferry across: benefiting beings without partiality; hence *Practices of Samantabhadra*. Samantabhadra is within the mind, yet the fruit-marks must be displayed; hence *Manifestation of the Tathāgata*.

The eighth assembly, without leaving the previous place, meets again at the Universally Radiant Dharma Hall. The sūtra has one chapter, called *Transcending the World*. The previous seven assemblies used differentiated dharmas to generate understanding. This assembly, relying on that understanding, accomplishes the interfused practice. Therefore the Bodhisattva Samantajñāna raises two hundred questions, and the Bodhisattva Samantabhadra responds with two thousand practices. Although one cultivates various dharmas of practice, their nature is without marks; though one constantly moves within the world, it is called "transcending the world."

The ninth assembly, without moving from the site of quiescent extinction, manifests at the Jeta Grove. The sūtra has one chapter, called *Entering the Dharma-Realm*. The previous assembly transcended the world; now one enters the dharma-realm, elucidating realization. The Buddha enters the Siṃhavijṛmbhita Samādhi, wishing to cause the great assembly to suddenly realize the universal gate. Therefore, the great being Mañjuśrī departs from the Excellent Abode Pavilion and travels south to teach. At the eastern side of the city of Dhanyākara, at the stūpa of an ancient buddha, he gathers a great assembly. He arouses the aspiration for enlightenment. Only the youth Sudhana, traveling through one hundred and ten cities, encounters good spiritual friends, and through each obtains a samādhi-liberation gate. At last he reaches the Bodhisattva Samantabhadra, and upon each and every hair-tip he suddenly realizes liberation, merging into the ocean of the fruit.

---

Furthermore, in these nine assemblies: the first assembly presents the Buddha as the object of faith; the second, the mind that is capable of faith; the third, giving rise to understanding based on faith; the fourth, arising of practice based on understanding; the fifth, arising of vows based on practice; the sixth, the culmination of the three worthy stages, about to ascend to the holy grounds; the seventh, gathering cause to accomplish fruit; the eighth, the mutual extinction of existence and non-existence, where true and false cannot be distinguished; the ninth, where object and wisdom are one suchness, beyond the reach of the fish-trap and the rabbit-snare. Furthermore, speaking in summary: the first assembly presents the two retributions, direct and environmental, as the Buddha who is the object of faith. The ninth assembly shows that the one who has faith can accomplish buddhahood. Thus the first and last assemblies together constitute a single meaning: the word "buddha" in the title. From the second assembly through the eighth, these are the causes of that buddha: the word "flower" in the title. Because causes have gradations, there are seven assemblies in the middle to mark them. Because the fruit has no limits, only one buddha can realize it. "Adornment" means using the flowers of vows and practices as causes to adorn the buddha-fruit of the dharma-body. Furthermore, "Buddha flower-adornment" is the person who transmits the Dharma; "great, square, and broad" is the Dharma that is transmitted. Because the Dharma constitutes the person, "great, square, and broad" is stated first. Because the person transmits the Dharma, "buddha flower-adornment" is stated after. Within the person, gathering cause to accomplish fruit: this is solely the buddha. Within the Dharma, gathering function and returning to essence: this is solely the great. "Great" is the dharma-realm that is realized; "buddha" is the great wisdom that realizes. Buddha and the great are like light and space: outside space there is no light, outside the great there is no buddha. Space and light are one body: buddha and the great are in silent accord and cannot be divided. The mind's conditions cannot reach it; words and speech do not attain it. That which can express this meaning is therefore called a sūtra. It is because of the principle that the teaching is formed; because of the teaching that the principle is seen. The sūtra says: "Just as by the light of the sun one sees the sun itself, so by the light of the Buddha's wisdom one sees the path the Buddha has traveled." Because the Buddha is permanent, the sūtra-dharma is also permanent. The reason is that never has there been a sun that abandoned its light. The sūtra says: "The people of Jambudvīpa, separated by Mount Sumeru, see the sun rise and set. Those who dwell within the sun always follow the sun: there is no rising or setting." Ordinary beings, separated by ignorance, see the Buddha appearing and disappearing. The great bodhisattvas always follow the Buddha: there is no appearing or disappearing. The solar disc is the essence; the light is the function. Never has there been an essence without function. Because there is the function of light, the practitioner sees the path and accomplishes what is to be done. The buddha-dharma is likewise: essence is there, and teaching the Dharma is the function. Never has there been a buddha who did not teach a sūtra. Through hearing and reflecting on the Dharma, one knows the path of liberation; cultivating morality, concentration, and wisdom, all is accomplished. The sūtra says: "Suppose there were a great sūtra-roll, its volume equal to a great thousandfold world, contained within a single mote of dust, and every mote of dust likewise. If there were a person of keen intelligence, with pure eyes seeing all clearly, who broke open the mote and released the sūtra-roll, it would broadly benefit sentient beings." Hence it is called the *Buddhāvataṃsaka Mahāvaipulya Sūtra*. However, the text and meaning are profound and difficult to fully present. According to my partial understanding from what I have seen and heard, I have presumptuously offered this praise. If even a single phrase or single verse accords with the principle, I humbly pray that its merit and blessings be boundless, offered in support of the sagely lifespan.

Twenty-first day of the fourth month, eighth year of Zhenyuan. Submitted by the śramaṇa Jingjū of Anguo Monastery.

Copied on the third day of the seventh month, autumn, in a xinyou year, based on the old text transmitted in the treasury of Kōzanji. Collation completed on the sixth day.

Written by Dōtō of Zenmyōji, a hermitage at Umemura on the Western Mountain.
