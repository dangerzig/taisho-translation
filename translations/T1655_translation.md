# 止觀門論頌
## Verses on the Treatise of the Gate of Śamatha and Vipaśyanā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1655

Composed by Vasubandhu (世親). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨).

---

> If one sees a woman's form and figure,
> Her alluring, captivating beauty,
> The fool, not understanding,
> Wrongly gives rise to lustful thoughts.
> The Buddha taught bloating and the rest
> To remove the thought of sensual desire.
> One who follows the World-Honored One's teaching
> Attains the supreme fruit; nothing else avails.
> With precepts pure, having heard and reflected,
> With diligent effort, constantly cultivate.
> Fix the mind and contemplate the various objects:
> This is the cause of liberation.
> If a person has anger and lust,
> As well as torpor and drowsiness,
> Restlessness, remorse, and doubt:
> These five obstruct the cultivation of concentration.
> Little learning, dwelling in crowds,
> Delighting in base affairs,
> Love of one's body and its pleasures:
> These too obstruct the concentrated mind.
> A distracted mind has five causes:
> The feelings chase after various objects,
> Relishing, sinking, and restlessness,
> Conceit, and craving for renown.
> A bhikṣu, relying on the sacred teaching,
> Should confess and remove whatever faults arise.
> Rightly taking up the object on which to fix the mind:
> This is the very first practice.
> Next, rely on a place of solitude
> Where hindrances are entirely absent.
> Then put an end to wrong thoughts:
> This is the bhikṣu's preliminary task.
> One should dwell in a charnel ground,
> Wearing robes of discarded rags,
> Always seeking the matter of quiescence,
> Severing the mind of lustful attachment.
> When begging for food and seeing a woman,
> One should contemplate her as impure.
> Restrain the eyes and remove wrong thoughts;
> With a correct mind, take the food.
> Much talking and many affairs:
> All these must be kept far away.
> When oppressive, disturbing conditions press in,
> One should endure them through the power of wisdom.
> Beneath a tree, in a pile of straw,
> Or dwelling within a cliff-cave,
> When it is time for contemplation, one should stay here
> In solitude to cultivate the mind.
> When practicing concentration on an object,
> Do not hold it too high or too low.
> It should be neither too near nor too far;
> Let the object be in accord with the mind.
> Rightly take up the object of contemplation
> And carefully examine it in detail.
> When the eyes are closed and the mind is settled,
> It is as if one sees with open eyes.
> Restrain all the sense-gates,
> Abide in mindfulness, concentrating the inner mind.
> Contemplate the object as present before one;
> Thought after thought, make it continuous.
> Upon the previously taken sign,
> Contemplate the form and appearance with focused mind.
> Around the genitals of the bloated female corpse:
> Fearsome and fit for revulsion.
> Just as when muddy pool-water
> Is blown and churned by the wind,
> When one contemplates the reflections of various trees,
> They cannot abide clearly and distinctly.
> The water of the mind's defilements and afflictions,
> Blown by the wind of confused emotions,
> When one tries to contemplate with settled mindfulness,
> Remains murky and cannot settle.
> When the mind sinks, one should rouse it
> By contemplating something excellent and sublime,
> Like a leech exhausted from drinking blood
> Revived by a splash of water.
> Then one should quickly gather it in again
> And settle the mind on the former object,
> Making the will capable and fit,
> Well-tamed and following every mindful thought.
> If the mind is restless and agitated,
> One should reflect on what is loathsome,
> Making the mind dwell in stillness,
> Like a hook striking an elephant's head.
> Having departed from both sinking and restlessness,
> One should direct the mind with equanimity,
> Letting the attention rest in balanced repose:
> At this time there is no fault.
> From this, one gradually attains stability.
> Taking up the sign, the mental image settles.
> In the clarity of the middle path's practice,
> Right mindfulness illuminates like a lamp.
> At this time, use inquiry and investigation
> To examine in proper sequence.
> The concentrated image then arises,
> Clearly and distinctly present before one.
> Unwavering, bright, and pure,
> Like the form of a great man:
> This image, compared to the earlier contemplation,
> Constitutes the distinguishing sign.
> Once this sign has arisen,
> Desire, craving, and the rest are removed.
> This is, for one who fixes the mind,
> The preliminary sign of the first concentration.
> Next, know that the substance of anger
> Is originally produced from attachment and lust.
> Since desire and craving have already been removed,
> One is freed from anger as well.
> Next, with diligent, exhortative mindfulness,
> Remove the mind of torpor and drowsiness.
> Having contemplated the distinguishing sign,
> Doubt immediately subsides.
> Next one should eliminate remorse
> And practice well the path of peace and security.
> In quiescence, free from obstruction,
> One can prevent restlessness.
> One should know that through coarse inquiry
> One contemplates the sign that was taken up.
> Then, within the mental image itself,
> Through investigation one makes subtle examination.
> Seeing the distinguishing sign, the mind rejoices.
> From this one attains ease and pliancy.
> Next one realizes the bliss of concentration.
> The factors of concentration arise in this order.
> This is the root concentration,
> With the mind well settled in mindfulness,
> Like one who intends to reach a village
> And then arrives within the village.
> Having attained the root concentration,
> One further undertakes additional cultivation,
> Attaining knowledge of others' minds, recollection of past lives,
> Supernatural powers, the divine eye, and divine ear.
> At this stage, while investigation remains,
> The mind cannot yet dwell in stillness.
> Just as a river still has waves,
> This is not yet the higher stages, one should know.
> Having attained the first concentration,
> One continues to abide on the object.
> Next, relying on the second dhyāna,
> Both inquiry and investigation are stilled.
> Although one abides at this stage,
> One is still tossed by the flood of joy.
> Upon entering the third dhyāna,
> The mind then dwells in stillness.
> Because the mind still has bliss,
> One cannot yet cause mindfulness to fully settle.
> Having realized the fourth dhyāna,
> All faults are entirely removed.
> Declining, superior, advancing,
> Abiding, and analytical:
> There are four kinds of dhyāna.
> The practitioner of concentration should first know these.
> If the concentration follows the afflictions,
> This is called the declining factor.
> What surpasses it is the superior factor;
> Abiding in itself is abiding, one should know.
> Through prior skillful discernment
> Comes the cause of the analytical path.
> This concentration can lead to that:
> It is called the analytical factor.
> Regarding the marks of impermanence and so forth,
> Contemplating them as suffering and the rest,
> If one attains this concentration,
> It belongs to the stages of warmth and the rest.
> Regarding the signs of the blue-bloated corpse and the rest,
> There are many avenues of contemplating objects.
> Practice as taught in the sacred instructions;
> One should recognize the distinctions.
>
> A dead body, bloated by the wind,
> With openings where pus flows out,
> The thighs swollen and all parts coarsened:
> This is called the sign of bloating.
> Those who do not love the coarse parts of the body
> But crave only the smooth and delicate:
> For such persons attached to desire,
> This contemplation is prescribed.
> If upon a dead body's parts
> A little white pus flows out,
> While the remaining flesh is mostly blue:
> This is described as the blue-bloated sign.
> Those who are smitten by color and generate desire:
> This is called a person attached to color.
> The antidote of the blue-bloated corpse
> Was taught by the Kinsman of the Sun.
> When pus flows everywhere from the dead body,
> This is called the sign of pus-flowing.
> For those attached to fragrance,
> Contemplate this and the lustful mind subsides.
> When the dead body's waist rots and breaks apart,
> This is called the sign of disintegration.
> For those attached to the whole body:
> The Great Sage taught this for them.
> The corpse gnawed upon by foxes and jackals,
> Pecked by birds, with sinews remaining:
> As antidote for those who crave flesh and skin,
> The sign of the partially devoured is taught.
> The bones of hands, feet, and all the limbs
> Are scattered in every direction:
> For those who crave particular body parts,
> The sign of scattered bones is taught.
> Upon the sign of scattered bones,
> Sword and staff hack them apart:
> This too is for those attached to the whole body,
> To contemplate the sign of battered disarray.
> The corpse is struck by swords
> Or wounded by spears and arrows,
> Blood flowing over the broken body:
> This is called the sign of blood-smeared.
> A clean body anointed with fragrant unguents,
> Freshly adorned in colorful garments:
> For those who generate craving for this,
> Use this as the excellent medicine.
> For one who desires the object in solitary attachment,
> Not permitting others to see:
> Many worms gnawing the dead body
> Should be contemplated to remove the evil thought.
> When flesh and frame have both decayed,
> Only the teeth and bones remain:
> For those who generate craving for the teeth,
> Contemplate the sign of teeth and bones.
> If one sees a freshly dead person,
> The consciousness departed yet the form remaining:
> For those attached to craving for living beings,
> This removes the desire for charm and allure.
> Feces, urine, mucus, and saliva
> Are gathered together to form this body:
> Thirty-two kinds of substances,
> A skin-bag called a person.
> Hair of the head and body, nails and teeth,
> Liver and stomach linked together,
> Internally supported by three hundred bones,
> Horizontally bound by nine hundred sinews,
> Nine openings flowing with impurity,
> Filth and defilement beyond description.
> If examined carefully, it is truly revolting;
> The wise should not grow intimate with it.
> Having contemplated another woman's body,
> One should likewise examine one's own form.
> Regarding these causes of lustful attachment,
> One should in principle always fix the mind upon them.
> Not escaping the prison of the three realms
> Is entirely due to the mind of lustful desire.
> Therefore the person of clear wisdom
> Should most carefully reflect upon this matter.
> After enduring the many sufferings of the hells,
> One has fortunately obtained a human body.
> How can one give free rein to a wild mind
> And not cultivate the supreme practice?
> Lustful craving has many kinds,
> Arising from different forms of attachment in each life.
> One contemplation can remove them all:
> This is the white-bone contemplation.
> Color, touch, form, charm, and allure,
> Clothing and ornaments that arouse attachment:
> What medicine can remove all these?
> None surpasses the white-bone contemplation.
> First, upon the great toe of the foot,
> Fix the mind and visualize a sore.
> It breaks open, pus flows out,
> And the flesh all falls away.
> Then contemplate the form of the toe-bone,
> White like the color of a dove.
> As the sore gradually spreads,
> Skin and flesh are entirely removed.
> Proceeding in this sequence,
> The flesh of the entire body is stripped away.
> When right mindfulness and resolute conviction are achieved,
> One contemplates only the skeletal chain.
> If any scrap of flesh remains,
> This is called a distracted mind,
> Let alone giving free rein to the monkey of the mind
> To race about pursuing various objects.
> Over a long period, fix the thoughts;
> Know for oneself that one can dwell steadily.
> Then gradually extend to other persons,
> Contemplating all as skeletal chains.
> Gradually widen to the ocean's edge,
> Filled entirely with white bones.
> When this concentration is achieved,
> Set aside the vast and return to the narrow.
> When narrowing, set aside from the outside inward,
> Until only one's own skeletal frame remains.
> Then return to contemplating the tip of the toe:
> This is the sequence of the concentrated mind.
> Or set aside piece by piece,
> Until reaching the crown of the skull.
> One should know that the final thought
> Is gathered and settled at the point between the brows.
> If one cultivates this practice,
> One is constantly reborn in the excellent Brahmā heavens,
> Does not fall into the three evil destinies,
> And is born in the five pure abodes.
> Scattered wholesome deeds in the human world
> Still flow back into the river of birth and death.
> The wise cultivate the concentrated mind
> As urgently as putting out a fire on one's clothing.
> One should forthwith set aside all entanglements
> And dwell in a quiet forest.
> Do not let impermanence press upon you,
> Dying in vain amid a scattered mind.
> With prajñā, purify the mind
> And ultimately obtain the beloved fruit.
> If one does not wish for future existence,
> One should cultivate the supreme path.
> Having shaved the head and donned the kāṣāya,
> One should cultivate the holy path.
> All other noisy, confused affairs
> Are entirely causes of birth and death.
