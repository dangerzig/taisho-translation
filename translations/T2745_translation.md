# 仁王經疏
## Commentary on the Renwang Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2745

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

...the time of difficult hearing. There is no before or after. Setting aside transmitted hearing establishes the above "thus have I heard." Therefore it says "at one time." The variant explanations are as follows.

Next, we distinguish the errors. First by verifying the facts, then by citing textual evidence. How do we verify by facts? As the sūtras state, Ānanda was born on the night the Buddha attained the Way. According to the Hīnayāna, after the Buddha attained the Way, six or seven days passed before he began to preach the sūtras. At that time, Ānanda was still an infant in arms and was physically not present at the assembly. After the Buddha attained the Way, twenty years passed before Ānanda renounced the household life, and it was not until thirty years later that he became the attendant. Before that, the many sūtras that were preached, he mostly did not hear in person. Although he did not hear them in person, the sūtras he compiled still say "at one time." Clearly, "at one time" does not mean distinguishing between personal and transmitted hearing.

Moreover, within the Hīnayāna, one cannot say that Ānanda was an expedient manifestation. How then can one say the preaching and listening were "at one time"?

Someone may object and ask: How do you know that Ānanda was born on the night the Buddha attained the Way? Will you use this to refute me? To establish this point, one must know the circumstances of how Ānanda received his name.

"Ānanda" is a foreign word. In Chinese it means "Joy." The name was established for three reasons.

**First, the past-life connection.** As the sūtras say, when Śākyamuni was practicing the bodhisattva path, he was a potter named Great Light. He encountered a past Buddha named Śākyamuni, whose father was named Śuddhodana, whose mother was named Māyā, and whose attendant-disciple was named Ānanda. The karmic connections and retinue were no different from the present. He thereupon made the vow: "May I in the future attain buddhahood, just like this buddha." Because of that original vow, he has now attained buddhahood, and therefore his attendant is again named Ānanda.

**Second, the present-life connection.** The bhikṣu Ānanda was handsome in appearance; all who saw him could not help but be delighted. Therefore he was named "Joy." Thus the sūtras praise Ānanda: "His face is like the moon, his eyes like pure lotuses. The ocean of the buddha-dharma is Ānanda's mind."

**Third, the name given by his parents.** Why did his parents give him the name "Joy"? Because he was born at a joyful time. What joyful time? The night the Tathāgata attained the Way. The Tathāgata at age nineteen had crossed over the city walls and left home. After leaving home, he spent five years studying meditation and six years in self-mortification, his body becoming extremely emaciated. His father the king sent people to constantly attend and watch over him. Knowing he was extremely frail, they constantly feared the worst. Later, the bodhisattva realized that fasting was not the Way and accepted an offering of milk-porridge, intending to take up the quest for perfect awakening.

Māra thought: "If the Buddha attains the Way, my realm will be emptied." Hearing the Way was not yet attained, he resolved to defeat the bodhisattva's resolve. Leading his forces of eighteen hundred million, bearing various weapons, he attacked the bodhisattva. The bodhisattva at that time entered the samādhi of supreme-intention loving-kindness, causing Māra's retinue to tumble and fall in confusion. Māra, having been subdued, then thought: "The bodhisattva's power is great. Alas, I cannot overcome him. I shall trouble his father instead."

He then went to King Śuddhodana's palace and cried out: "Prince Siddhārtha died last night!" When the king heard this, he fell from his couch and was unconscious for a long time before reviving. He lamented and grieved, thinking: "Had my son stayed home, he would have become a wheel-turning king. Who could have expected that he would leave home only to die having gained nothing?"

Before long, the spirit of the bodhi tree, because the Buddha had attained the Way, brought celestial flowers to congratulate the king. At the time of his attaining buddhahood, celestial flowers rained down as offerings, and so the spirit brought congratulations to the king. He too went to the palace of King Śuddhodana and proclaimed: "Great King, you should know that the Prince, your son, last night when the morning star appeared, subdued the hosts of Māra and attained buddhahood."

The king then grew doubtful: "A moment ago, a celestial being said my son had died. Now another says he has attained the Way. Which should I believe?" The bodhi-tree spirit said: "My words are trustworthy. The former was Māra, who came to trouble you. I am the spirit of the bodhi tree. Because the Buddha attained the Way beneath my tree, I have come to congratulate you."

The king then thought: "Had my son stayed home, he would have become a wheel-turning king. Now, having left home, he has become a great king of the Dharma-wheel. Either way, nothing is lost. A moment ago I heard of his death, and now suddenly I hear he lives: the first great joy. Then I hear he has attained the Way: a second great joy."

Before long, the king's younger brother Droṇodana, who that night had a son born, Ānanda, came carrying the infant to present to the king, saying: "Last night this son was born to me." The king then replied: "My son has attained the Way, and you have also had a son born. All the auspicious occasions have come together!" And so the child was named "Joy."

By this evidence we can determine that Ānanda was born on the night the Buddha attained the Way. Since Ānanda was born on the night of the Buddha's attainment, how can one say the preaching and listening were "at one time"?

The factual verification is thus. Next, the textual evidence. When the Buddha first attained the Way, at the Deer Park of the Ṛṣipatana in Vārāṇasī, he turned the Dharma-wheel of the four truths for the five bhikṣus. This is called the *Sūtra of Turning the Dharma-Wheel*. After the Buddha's passing, the bhikṣu Ānanda first compiled this sūtra. Before compiling it, he ascended the high seat and spoke a verse of his own:

> When the Buddha first taught the Dharma,
> At that time I did not see it.
> As at that time, it was heard through transmission.
> The Buddha traveled to Vārāṇasī,
> And for the five bhikṣus,
> Turned the Dharma-wheel of the four truths.

In that sūtra, the Way was already through transmission, and yet it still says "at one time." Clearly, "at one time" does not mean distinguishing between transmitted and personal hearing.

The refutation of errors is thus. Next, we explain the correct meaning. "At one time" is explained from the perspective of the Buddha: the occasion of his transformative teaching is called "time." Within the Buddha's entire career, the occasions of teaching were many. To distinguish from other times, therefore it says "one." The expression "at one time" occurs frequently in the sūtras. As the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* says: "At one time, I was in the kingdom of Kāśi. At one time, I was on the banks of the Gaṅgā. At one time, I was in the Śītavana. At one time, I was in Rājagṛha." Such instances are numerous. The present "at one time" is similar to those. How do we know that "one time" belongs to what follows and does not refer to what precedes? We know from the *Daśabhūmika Sūtra*. At the beginning of the Daśabhūmika section of the great Avataṃsaka, it says "thus have I heard, at one time."

---

The holy teachings, though many, are in summary only two: the Śrāvakayāna-piṭaka and the Bodhisattvayāna-piṭaka. Teachings for śrāvakas are called the Śrāvakayāna-piṭaka; teachings for bodhisattvas are called the Bodhisattvayāna-piṭaka.

Within the Śrāvakayāna-piṭaka, what is taught has two categories. The first is the "śrāvaka-śrāvaka." The second is the "pratyekabuddha-śrāvaka."

"Śrāvaka-śrāvaka" refers to persons who from the beginning sought the śrāvaka path, who delighted in contemplating the four truths, and who perfected the śrāvaka disposition. In their final body, encountering the Buddha and desiring the lesser vehicle, the Tathāgata taught them the Dharma of the four noble truths, and they attained awakening. Originally of the śrāvaka disposition, they again heard the teaching and attained the Way. Therefore they are called "śrāvaka-śrāvakas." The sūtra says: "For those who seek the śrāvaka path, he teaches the four noble truths." This is the basis for this category.

"Pratyekabuddha-śrāvaka" refers to persons who from the beginning sought the pratyekabuddha path, who always delighted in contemplating the twelve links of dependent arising, and who perfected the pratyekabuddha disposition. In their final body, the Buddha taught them the twelve links, and they attained awakening. Originally of the pratyekabuddha disposition, in their final body they heard the teaching and attained the Way. Therefore they are called "pratyekabuddha-śrāvakas." The sūtra says: "For those who seek the pratyekabuddha path, he teaches the twelve links." This is the basis for this category.

Though these two differ, they both aspire to the lesser fruit and depend on the teaching equally. For this reason, both are called "śrāvaka." The Dharma taught for these two kinds of persons is called the Śrāvakayāna-piṭaka.

Within the Bodhisattvayāna-piṭaka, what is taught also has two categories. The first is the "gradual enterers." The second is the "sudden awakeners."

"Gradual enterers" are those who in past lives cultivated the great Dharma but in an intermediate life heard about and inclined toward the lesser. Later they returned to the great. Since the great comes by way of the lesser, it is called "gradual." Thus the sūtra says: "Except for those who previously cultivated and studied the lesser vehicle, I now also cause them to enter this Dharma." These are the gradual-entry bodhisattvas.

"Sudden awakeners" are sentient beings who have long cultivated wholesome roots corresponding to the Mahāyāna. When they now first see the Buddha, they are immediately able to enter the great. Since the great does not come by way of the lesser, it is called "sudden." Thus the sūtra says: "There are sentient beings who, life after life, have always received my transformative teaching. When they first see my body and hear my preaching, they immediately have faith and acceptance, entering the Tathāgata's wisdom." These are the sudden awakeners.

Gradual-entry bodhisattvas advance from the shallow through distant stages. Sudden-awakening bodhisattvas leap to understanding of the great in a single bound. Though gradual and sudden differ, since at that time they both receive the great in one place, the teaching given to these two kinds of persons is called the Bodhisattvayāna-piṭaka.

Though the holy teachings are many, essentially they do not go beyond these two. Therefore Nāgārjuna says: "After the Buddha's passing, Kāśyapa and Ānanda, at Rājagṛha, compiled the three piṭakas. This is the Śrāvakayāna-piṭaka. Mañjuśrī and Ānanda, at the Iron Encircling Mountains, compiled the Mahāyāna. This is the Bodhisattvayāna-piṭaka."

The *Bodhisattvabhūmi* also says: "The Buddha taught śrāvakas and bodhisattvas the path leading out of suffering and preached the sūtras. Those who compiled the sūtras gathered them into two piṭakas: what teaches the practices of śrāvakas is the Śrāvakayāna-piṭaka; what teaches the practices of bodhisattvas is the Bodhisattvayāna-piṭaka."

The *Bodhisattvabhūmi* further says: "Of the twelve divisions of the sūtras, only the vaipulya division belongs to the Bodhisattvayāna-piṭaka. The remaining eleven divisions belong to the Śrāvakayāna-piṭaka." Thus we know that the holy teachings do not go beyond these two. These two are also called the Mahāyāna and the Hīnayāna, or the half and the full teachings. Though the names change, the meaning does not differ.

This present sūtra, among the two piṭakas, belongs to the Bodhisattvayāna-piṭaka. It is the sudden-teaching Dharma-wheel for those whose spiritual capacities are mature.

Having established the classification of the teaching, we next explain its title. The present title, "Sūtra of the Benevolent King on the Prajñāpāramitā for the Protection of the Nation, Spoken by the Buddha," is the specific title of the sūtra-division. The reason all sūtras have their names inscribed at the beginning is to indicate what Dharma they elucidate.

This sūtra takes the true nature as its essential doctrine, and therefore sets it forth at the beginning. However, the methods of establishing sūtra titles are not uniform and come in many varieties. Some are named after the Dharma, such as the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* and the *Prajñā Sūtra*. Some are named after persons, such as the *Sadāprarudita Sūtra* and the *Sudāna Sūtra*. Some are named after events, such as the *Withered Tree Sūtra*. Some are named after analogies, such as the *Great Cloud Sūtra* and the *Jeweled Casket Sūtra*. Some combine person and Dharma, such as the *Śrīmālā Sūtra*. Some combine event and Dharma, such as the *Vaipulya Mahāsaṃnipāta Sūtra*. Some use Dharma and analogy, such as the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*. Some combine person and event, such as the *Sūtra of Śāriputra's Inquiry about Illness*. Such examples are numerous.

The present sūtra is named after person and Dharma. "Spoken by the Tathāgata" is its person-name. "Prajñāpāramitā" is its Dharma-name. The Dharma is communicated through the person, hence both must be set forth. This is the Dharma that must be established. However, among the persons placed at the head of various sūtras, there are four kinds. First, naming the speaker, as in the *Śrīmālā Sūtra*. Second, naming the questioner, as in the *Sūtra of Maitreya's Questions*. Third, naming the subject of the account, as in the *Śyāma Sūtra* and the *Sudāna Sūtra*. Fourth, naming the person being taught, as in the *Sūtra of the Jade Necklace* and the *Sūtra of Sumatī*.

This sūtra names the speaker. The speakers are of five kinds: first, what the Buddha spoke; second, what the holy disciples spoke; third, what the devas spoke; fourth, what the divine ṛṣis spoke; fifth, what was spoken through magical transformations. This sūtra is spoken by the Tathāgata and the holy disciples.
