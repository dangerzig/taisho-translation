# 百千頌大集經地藏菩薩請問法身讚
## Praise of the Dharma-Body from the Hundred-Thousand-Verse Great Collection Sūtra, at Kṣitigarbha Bodhisattva's Inquiry

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0413

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Amoghavajra (不空)

---

> I bow in homage to the dharma-body
> That dwells within all sentient beings.
> Because they do not fully comprehend it,
> They wander in cyclic existence through the three realms.

> Its nature is birth and death itself,
> And when purified, it is the same.
> What is pure is nirvāṇa,
> And that is also the dharma-body.

> Just as when milk is mixed together,
> Ghee cannot be perceived,
> So too when mixed with afflictions,
> The dharma-realm cannot be seen.

> Just as when milk is purified,
> The butter-essence is fine and stainless,
> So too when afflictions are purified,
> The dharma-realm is utterly pure.

> Like a lamp inside a pot,
> Its radiance cannot shine forth;
> Like being inside the pot of afflictions,
> The dharma-realm does not illuminate.

> But if on one side or another
> A hole is made in the pot,
> Then from one side or another
> The light will shine outward.

> With the pestle of samādhi,
> Smash the pot of afflictions,
> And it will pervade all of empty space,
> Illuminating everywhere.

> The dharma-realm is also unborn
> And has never been destroyed.
> At all times it is unstained,
> From first to last forever pure.

> Like vaiḍūrya
> That is always supremely radiant:
> When covered by stone in a mine,
> Its light does not shine forth.

> So too, covered by afflictions,
> The dharma-realm is wondrously pure,
> But does not illuminate in birth and death;
> In nirvāṇa, its light shines.

> If there is gold-bearing ore
> And one applies effort, one sees the true gold.
> If the ore lacks gold
> And one applies effort, one labors in vain.

> Just as rice covered by its husk
> Is not called polished grain,
> So too, covered by afflictions,
> One is not called a Buddha.

> If one removes the husk,
> The polished grain is revealed.
> Far removed from afflictions,
> The dharma-body becomes manifest.

> In this world, by analogy,
> The plantain tree has no solid core,
> Yet it produces a genuine fruit
> Whose flavor is like sweet dew.

> So too, in birth and death, which has no substance,
> Wandering in the ocean of afflictions,
> The fruit is the Buddha-body itself,
> Bestowing sweet dew upon beings.

> Just as with various seeds,
> Similar fruits arise from them:
> Without a seed, there is no fruit;
> The wise would never believe otherwise.

> The seed is the very nature itself,
> The basis of all dharmas.
> If gradually it can be purified,
> One attains the stage of Buddhahood.

> The sun and moon are always stainless,
> Yet are obscured by five things:
> Clouds, fog, smoke,
> Rāhu's hand, and dust.

> So too the mind's radiance
> Is obscured by five defilements:
> Craving, anger, torpor,
> Agitation, and doubt.

> Just as fire washes a garment
> Stained with various impurities:
> If one casts it into the flames,
> The dirt burns, not the cloth.

> So too the sūtras on emptiness
> Taught by all the Tathāgatas
> Burn away all afflictions
> But never harm the nature itself.

> Just as water beneath the earth
> Abides always, pure and clear,
> So too wisdom hidden beneath afflictions
> Is likewise pure and clear.

> The dharma-realm is neither self,
> Nor woman, nor man.
> Far removed from all grasping,
> How could one discriminate a self?

> In all dharmas there is no attachment;
> Woman and man cannot be found.
> To tame the blind delusion of craving,
> The appearance of male and female is displayed.

> Impermanence, suffering, and emptiness:
> The mind's pure contemplation has three kinds.
> The most supreme contemplation of the pure mind
> Is that all dharmas have no self-nature.

> Just as a child in the womb
> Exists yet is not visible,
> So too, covered by afflictions,
> The true Dharma cannot be seen.

> Discrimination has four kinds,
> Which give rise to the great elements:
> Discrimination of self, of mine,
> Of name-concepts, and of sense objects.

> All the great vows of the Buddhas
> Are without existence and without marks.
> Because they correspond to self-realization,
> The Buddhas are eternally the dharma-nature.

> Just as one says a hare has horns,
> Which are discriminated but do not exist,
> So too all dharmas
> Cannot be found through discrimination.

> Analyzed down to atoms,
> Discrimination cannot find them.
> As with the beginning, so with the end:
> How can wisdom discriminate them?

> Thus what arises through combination
> Also perishes through combination.
> Not a single dharma arises by itself:
> Why do the deluded discriminate?

> The analogy of hare's horns and ox's horns:
> This describes the nature of universal conceptualization.
> Abiding in the Middle Way
> Is the dharma-nature of the Well-Gone One.

> Like the moon and stars
> Appearing in a vessel of clear water,
> Reflections manifesting:
> Such is the nature of perfect accomplishment.

> In the beginning, the middle, and always, it is good,
> Constant, eternal, and never deceiving.
> Since the five kinds of self do not exist there,
> How can one discriminate a self?

> Just as when water is heated,
> It is then called hot water,
> And when it cools,
> It is called cold water:

> Covered by the net of afflictions,
> It is called the mind.
> If separated from afflictions,
> It is called perfect enlightenment.

> The eye-consciousness conditions forms,
> And reflections are utterly pure.
> Neither arising nor perishing,
> The dharma-realm has no form or marks.

> The ear-consciousness conditions sounds;
> Pure consciousness has three kinds.
> Through one's own discrimination one hears,
> But the dharma-realm has no form or marks.

> The nose conditions fragrance and smells;
> Without color and without form.
> Nose-consciousness is suchness itself:
> The dharma-realm should be understood thus.

> The tongue-realm is empty by nature;
> The taste-realm's nature is far removed.
> Without basis and without consciousness,
> This is due to the dharma-realm's own nature.

> The pure body's own nature
> And the tactile objects it encounters in combination
> Are far removed from objects of perception:
> I call this the dharma-realm.

> All dharmas have mind as their foremost;
> Separated from the discrimination of subject and object,
> The dharma-realm has no self-nature:
> This is how the dharma-realm should be understood.

> Seeing, hearing, and smelling,
> Tasting and touching:
> The yogin knows these thus.
> Such is the nature of perfect accomplishment.

> Eye, ear, and nose,
> Tongue, body, and manas:
> The six bases are all pure.
> Such is their nature.

> The mind's seeing has two kinds:
> Worldly and beyond the world.
> Grasping at self leads to transmigration;
> Self-realization is suchness.

> The inexhaustible is nirvāṇa,
> When craving and delusion are exhausted.
> Awakening to this is the Buddha-body,
> The refuge of sentient beings.

> Within this very body, all beings
> Have both wisdom and ignorance.
> Bondage comes from one's own discrimination;
> Through awakening, one attains liberation.

> Bodhi is neither far nor near,
> Neither coming nor going.
> It is the destruction and manifestation
> Within this net of afflictions.

> Through the teachings of the sūtras,
> Abiding in one's own reflection,
> Illuminated by the lamp of wisdom,
> One attains the supreme peace.

> Regarding bodhi, let go of the thought of "far"
> And also the thought of "near."
> It is the reflections of the six sense-objects:
> All are known through this understanding.

> Like water and milk combined,
> Together in a single vessel:
> The goose drinks only the milk,
> And the water remains as before.

> So too, afflictions mixed together
> With wisdom in a single vessel:
> The yogin drinks the wisdom
> And discards the afflictions.

> Thus the self, the grasping at self,
> And even the grasping at objects:
> When one sees the two kinds of non-self,
> The seeds are destroyed.

> This is the Buddha's parinirvāṇa,
> Constant, eternal, pure, and stainless.
> The deluded discriminate duality,
> But duality is not the sentence of yoga.

> Various difficult practices of giving,
> Using precepts to gather sentient beings,
> Patience with all harm:
> These are three kinds of increase in the realm.

> Diligence toward all dharmas,
> Meditation's mental application,
> Constantly practicing wisdom:
> One further increases toward bodhi.

> Skillful means combined with wisdom,
> With vows all purified,
> With powers and sublime, firm knowledge:
> These are four kinds of increase in the realm.

> "One should not bow to a bodhisattva":
> This is a most evil teaching.
> Without closeness to bodhisattvas,
> The dharma-body cannot be born.

> Increasing the sugarcane crop,
> One wishes to eat rock sugar;
> But if the sugarcane crop is destroyed,
> Rock sugar cannot be produced.

> If one protects the sugarcane crop,
> Three things can be obtained:
> Sugar, half-sugar, and rock sugar
> Will necessarily be produced from it.

> If one protects the bodhi-mind,
> Three things can be obtained:
> Arhat, pratyekabuddha, and Buddha
> Will necessarily be produced from it.

> Just as one protects a rice seedling,
> The farmer must surely guard it;
> So too with the initial stage of devoted practice,
> The Tathāgata must surely protect it.

> Like the fifteenth day of the bright fortnight,
> When the full moon's disc is seen:
> So too in the stage of devoted practice,
> The Buddha's form is reflected.

> Like the new moon's disc
> That increases moment by moment:
> So too one who has entered the stages
> Sees increase with every thought.

> Like the fifteenth day of the bright fortnight,
> When the moon's disc becomes full:
> So too at the ultimate stage,
> The dharma-body is born.

> Devoted understanding, firmly established,
> Should always be directed toward the Buddha-Dharma.
> One who can give rise to such a mind
> Becomes non-retrogressing.

> The defiled basis obtains a transformed basis,
> Receiving it as a purified basis.
> Through partial awakening,
> This is called the Stage of Supreme Joy.

> At all times from defilements,
> From desires and various stains,
> Becoming stainless and attaining purity:
> This is called the Stage of Freedom from Defilement.

> Destroying the net of afflictions,
> Shining forth, freed from stains,
> In immeasurable darkness:
> Removing it, this is called the Stage of Radiance.

> Pure, with constant radiance,
> Far removed from worldly fortune,
> Surrounded by the flames of wisdom:
> This is called the Stage of Blazing Wisdom.

> All brilliant arts and skills,
> Adorned with various meditations,
> Difficult to surpass regarding afflictions,
> Victorious: this is the Stage of Difficult to Surpass.

> Regarding the three kinds of bodhi,
> Embracing and bringing them to perfection,
> With arising and ceasing profoundly deep:
> This is called the Stage of Direct Presence.

> Sporting in the net of light,
> Adorned everywhere with Indra's splendor,
> Transcending the torrent of desire:
> This is called the Stage of Far-Reaching.

> Empowered by all Buddhas,
> Entering the ocean of wisdom,
> Sovereign and effortless:
> Unmoved by the māra hosts.

> In all the unobstructed understandings,
> Having reached the far shore of yoga,
> In teaching and discoursing on the Dharma:
> This is called the Stage of Excellent Wisdom.

> The body made of wisdom,
> Like empty space, stainless;
> Upheld by all the Buddhas,
> Pervading everywhere like a Dharma-cloud.

> The basis of the Buddha-Dharma,
> Upholding the fruits of practice:
> When the basis is fully transformed,
> It is therefore called the dharma-body.

> Separated from inconceivable perfuming
> And separated from the habits of transmigration:
> How can one who thinks as you think
> Come to know it?

> Transcending the realm of all speech,
> Not an object of any sense faculty;
> What is grasped by mental consciousness:
> Such is the nature of what I am.

> Gradually and progressively accumulated,
> The Buddha's sons of great renown,
> All with the wisdom of the Dharma-cloud,
> Subtly perceive the dharma-nature.

> Then the mind is cleansed and purified,
> Crossing over the ocean of birth and death.
> Upon a great lotus flower
> They are established on a great seat.

> With immeasurable jeweled petals of light,
> A jeweled radiance forming the platform,
> With immeasurable billions of lotus flowers
> Pervading everywhere as their retinue.

> First with the ten powers,
> Then with the four fearlessnesses,
> And the remaining dharmas exclusive to Buddhas,
> They sit in great sovereignty.

> All goodness is gathered together,
> With the provisions of merit and wisdom.
> Like the full moon amid the stars,
> Pervading and surrounded on all sides.

> Then with the sun-hand of the Buddha,
> With jeweled light, stainless,
> They anoint the eldest sons,
> Pouring the consecration everywhere.

> Those who abide in the great yoga,
> All seeing with the divine eye,
> Perceive the world stirred up by ignorance,
> With evil habits, suffering, and fear.

> Resembling golden-colored light,
> From their yogic radiance,
> They open the door of ignorance
> For those covered by unknowing.

> Through the merit and wisdom they have gathered,
> They obtain the samādhi of non-grasping.
> Following conditions they enter perfect quiescence,
> While their minds produce all transformations.

> All dharmas have no self-nature;
> Self-nature is within the realm of objects.
> The bodhisattva-king's wondrous vision:
> The dharma-body, wondrous and stainless.

> All with stainless bodies,
> Abiding in the ocean of wisdom,
> They act for the benefit of sentient beings,
> Like a skillful wish-fulfilling jewel.

> All the yogins,
> Sovereigns of the great yoga,
> As Buddha-reflections and transformations,
> Stream forth pervading everywhere.

> Some have eight arms,
> Three eyes, bodies blazing with power.
> These are all from the yoga-king,
> Streaming forth pervading everywhere.

> All with hands of compassion and loving-kindness,
> With supreme joy holding the bow,
> Shooting the arrow of prajñā,
> They sever the subtle ignorance entirely.

> With great power they advance,
> Wielding the staff of wisdom.
> All the shells of ignorance
> Are shattered and destroyed everywhere.

> Strong and powerful beings,
> With blazing vajra bodies,
> To tame sentient beings,
> They become Vajrapāṇi.

> For those who create their own karma,
> They display the various fruits.
> Teaching and admonishing according to principle,
> They become the King of Equanimity.

> For those with bodies blazing with hunger and thirst,
> They provide food and drink.
> For those constantly afflicted with illness,
> They become the excellent Medicine King.

> For the māra king with his retinue
> And the māra women with their adornments,
> The bodhisattva acts as a dear friend,
> Able to lead them to the place of bodhi.

> Like the forms of the sun and moon,
> Their light is pleasing to all.
> Streaming forth like lightning flashes,
> They illuminate koṭis of lands.

> From a single lamp,
> All may be lit everywhere.
> If one lamp is extinguished,
> All the rest follow.

> So too the ripened Buddhas
> Display various kinds of light.
> When one manifests nirvāṇa,
> The other Buddhas show their passing into quiescence.

> Yet not a single one truly passes away:
> Could the sun's light become darkness?
> Constantly appearing and disappearing,
> They manifest throughout the ocean of lands.

> In the world of ignorance and darkness,
> They purify the eye of wisdom,
> Traveling to koṭis of lands,
> Compassionately transforming sentient beings.

> None of them ever grow weary,
> For they wear the armor of great compassion.
> In all the bases of supernatural power,
> Their yoga has reached the far shore.

> They observe what is timely and what is not,
> Causing beings to continue in their course.
> The stubborn and the deceitful
> They set aside for the time being.

> Immeasurable beings they tame,
> All at once causing them to be purified.
> Immeasurable Buddha-transformations
> All at once briefly appear.

> Into the ocean of the three realms
> They cast the net of taming.
> Spreading the wondrous Dharma-net,
> They pervade everywhere, bringing beings to maturity.

> With the net of taming,
> They pervade everywhere, bringing beings to maturity,
> And pervading everywhere they lift out
> Those who are adrift within.

> Just as with a thousand sentient beings,
> Having pervaded everywhere to ferry them across
> And having crossed them, causing them to awaken,
> In the wondrous Dharma they have no doubt.

> The World-Honored One's wondrous Dharma-bell
> Pervades everywhere, causing all to hear.
> Through the sound of its ringing,
> The dust of afflictions falls away.

> Those with deep ignorance
> Are all purified at once,
> As the sun's brilliant power
> Destroys the film upon the eye.

> Those who follow dark afflictions
> And those with other sinful bodies:
> For their benefit,
> They are gradually purified.

> Here and there they manifest transformations,
> Abiding like the moon reflected in water.
> Minds stirred by afflictions
> Cannot see the Tathāgata.

> Just as hungry ghosts see the ocean
> As entirely dried up,
> So too those of scant merit
> See no Buddha and make such distinctions.

> For sentient beings of scant merit,
> What can the Tathāgata do?
> It is like placing the most supreme jewel
> In the hand of one blind from birth.

> How could such a one see
> The unsurpassed dharma-body,
> A body of koṭis of suns' radiance,
> Surrounded by a net of light?

> Devas with their small goodness
> Cannot see it.
> Those next below the great devas:
> How could the rest, with lesser wisdom, see?

> Those forms that cannot be seen,
> Even by the sages free from afflictions:
> Devas, asuras, Brahmā, and the rest,
> How much less the others of lesser wisdom?

> Yet through the Buddha's awesome power
> And the purity of one's own mind,
> One can see such beings
> And obtain all prosperity.

> Beings adorned with merit,
> Before whom the Buddha abides,
> His body radiant with light,
> Bearing the thirty-two supreme marks:

> Such a person, like the great ocean,
> Should behold the Tathāgata.
> Before long has passed,
> They will obtain wisdom like the ocean.

> The World-Honored One's form-body
> Abides for many kalpas,
> Able to tame and benefit those who can be tamed,
> In accord with the varieties of their dispositions.

> Though the great yogin has a vast lifespan,
> Why do those of shorter lives exist?
> Among the many, koṭis and more,
> He displays increasing and decreasing lifespans.

> For immeasurable koṭis of kalpas,
> Through life after life, life increases.
> Because causes and conditions are inexhaustible,
> One obtains the inexhaustible fruit.

> If there is one who resonates with and reveals this truth,
> Analyzing it with wisdom applied to the body,
> That person will be born upon a pure lotus flower
> And hear the teaching of immeasurable life.
