# 國王不梨先泥十夢經
## Sūtra on the Ten Dreams of King Prasenajit

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0148

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, King Prasenajit dreamed of ten things during the night. What were the ten things? First, he dreamed of three vessels side by side: the two on either side were full, with steam passing back and forth between them, yet it did not enter the empty middle vessel. Second, he dreamed of a horse that was eating from its mouth and also eating from its rump. Third, he dreamed of small trees bearing flowers. Fourth, he dreamed of small trees bearing fruit. Fifth, he dreamed of a person twisting a rope, with a goat behind the person eating the rope. Sixth, he dreamed of a fox sitting on a golden bed, eating from golden vessels. Seventh, he dreamed of a large cow nursing from a small calf. Eighth, he dreamed of four bulls coming from the four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight; just as they were about to clash, the bulls were nowhere to be found. Ninth, he dreamed of a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges. Tenth, he dreamed of a great stream of water flowing bright red. When the king had dreamed these things and awoke, he was greatly frightened, fearing for the loss of his kingdom, his life, and his wife and children. The next morning, the king summoned his ministers and officials, as well as practitioners who were skilled in interpreting dreams. He asked: "Last night I dreamed of ten things as described. When I awoke from the dream, I was frightened and my mind was troubled. Who can interpret these dreams?" Among the practitioners, one brāhmaṇa said: "I can interpret them for the king, but I fear the king will be filled with sorrow and displeasure upon hearing it." The king said: "Speak according to what you perceive, and hold nothing back." The brāhmaṇa said: "What the king dreamed are all evil omens, not auspicious. You should take the honored queen, the crown prince, your close attendants, servants, and slaves, and kill them all as a sacrifice to heaven, and the king may then be spared. The bedding, precious treasures worn on the body, and fine things should all be burned as a sacrifice to heaven. Only then may the king's person be spared."

When the king heard the brāhmaṇa's interpretation, he was immediately filled with great sorrow and displeasure. He withdrew into his purification chamber to reflect on the matter. The king had a chief queen named Maṇi. She came to the king and asked: "Why have you entered the purification chamber in sorrow and displeasure? Have I committed some fault against the king?" The king said: "You have committed no fault against me. I am merely sorrowful on my own account." The queen asked again: "What is the cause of the king's sorrow?" The king said: "Do not ask me. To hear would only cause you displeasure." The queen said again: "I am half the king's own body. Whether good or bad, the king should tell me. Why will you not speak?" The king then told his queen: "Last night I dreamed of ten things. When I awoke, I was filled with great sorrow and fear, fearing for the loss of my kingdom, my life, and my wife and children. I summoned my ministers and officials and the various practitioners, and told them of my ten dreams. A brāhmaṇa interpreted my dreams, saying: 'You should take the honored queen, the crown prince, your close attendants, servants, and slaves, and the white elephants and prized horses, and kill them all as a sacrifice to heaven. The bedding, precious treasures, and fine things worn on the body should all be burned as a sacrifice to heaven. Only then may the king's person be spared.' This is why I am in sorrow and displeasure." The queen said: "Do not worry, great king! It is like a person buying gold who rubs it on a touchstone: its quality, whether good or bad, is seen upon the stone. The Buddha is now nearby at the monastery, not far from the capital. Why not go and ask the Buddha about the meaning of your dreams? Whatever the Buddha interprets, you should follow."

The king then ordered his attendants and ministers to prepare the carriage and horses. He departed and arrived at where the Buddha was. He walked slowly on the path, dismounted the carriage, and went forward to the Buddha's presence. He placed his head at the Buddha's feet, then sat to one side and said to the Buddha: "Last night I dreamed of ten things. First, I dreamed of three vessels side by side: the two on either side were full, with steam passing back and forth, yet it did not enter the empty middle vessel. Second, I dreamed of a horse eating from its mouth and also from its rump. Third, I dreamed of small trees bearing flowers. Fourth, I dreamed of small trees bearing fruit. Fifth, I dreamed of a person twisting a rope, with a goat behind eating the rope. Sixth, I dreamed of a fox sitting on a golden bed, eating from golden vessels. Seventh, I dreamed of a large cow nursing from a small calf. Eighth, I dreamed of four bulls coming from the four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight; just as they were about to clash, the bulls were nowhere to be found. Ninth, I dreamed of a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges. Tenth, I dreamed of a stream of water flowing bright red. My dreams were as described. When I awoke, I was frightened, fearing for the loss of my kingdom, my life, and my wife and children. I beseech the Buddha to interpret these ten dreams for me. I wish to hear the teaching."

The Buddha said: "Do not worry, great king! What the king dreamed is nothing harmful. What the king dreamed pertains only to events of the future age to come, not to the present age." The Buddha said: "In the future age, people will not fear the Dharma and its prohibitions. They will be licentious, greedy, and jealous, not knowing contentment, lacking in righteousness, without compassion, quick to anger, and without any sense of shame."

The Buddha said: "First, the dream of three vessels side by side, the two on either side full, with steam passing back and forth yet not entering the empty middle vessel: in the future age, the wealthy and powerful will associate only among themselves and will not look upon the poor. The king's dream of the three vessels pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Second, the king's dream of a horse eating from its mouth and also from its rump: in the future age, those who are emperors and high ministers will draw provisions and salaries from the government and also exploit the common people, not knowing contentment. The king's dream of the horse eating from both mouth and rump pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Third, the dream of small trees bearing flowers: in the future age, people not yet thirty years old will have white hair growing on their heads. Greedy and lustful with many desires, the young will be prematurely old. The king's dream of small trees bearing flowers pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Fourth, the king's dream of small trees bearing fruit: in the future age, women not yet fifteen years old will marry and carry children, without any sense of shame. The king's dream of small trees bearing fruit pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Fifth, the dream of a person twisting a rope with a goat behind eating the rope: in the future age, when a husband goes out on business or trade, his wife will have affairs with another man and consume the husband's wealth. The king's dream of the person twisting a rope pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Sixth, the king's dream of a fox sitting on a golden bed, eating from golden vessels: in the future age, the lowly will become noble and wealthy. People will revere and fear them. The descendants of lords and princes will become poor and base, seated in the lowest positions, served food last. The king's dream of the fox sitting on a golden bed and eating from golden vessels pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Seventh, the king's dream of a large cow nursing from a small calf: in the future age, people will be without propriety or righteousness. A mother will act as a matchmaker for her daughter, enticing another man to have relations with the daughter. She will sell her daughter to obtain wealth for her own sustenance, without any sense of shame. The king's dream of the large cow nursing from a small calf pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Eighth, the dream of four bulls coming from the four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight, but just as they were about to clash the bulls were nowhere to be found: in the future age, emperors, officials, and the common people will all lack sincerity. They will be deceitful, foolish, and angry, without reverence for heaven and earth. Because of this, the rains will not come at the proper season. Officials and the people will pray and supplicate for rain. Heaven will cause clouds to arise from the four directions, with thunder and lightning and great sound. The officials and people will all say: 'It is about to rain.' But in a moment, the clouds will scatter and the rain will depart, not falling. Why is this? Because emperors, officials, and the people lack loyalty, uprightness, compassion, and benevolence. The king's dream of the four bulls bellowing and rushing toward each other, about to clash but then nowhere to be found, pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Ninth, the king's dream of a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges: in the future age, the people of the central lands will be in turmoil, their governance and conduct will be unjust. The people will be unfilial to their parents and disrespectful to their elders. The people of the border countries will be peaceful and orderly, harmonious, and filial to both parents. The king's dream of the great pond, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges, pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "Tenth, the king's dream of a great stream of water flowing bright red: in the future age, various kingdoms will quarrel in anger. They will raise armies and gather troops to attack one another. They will make war with chariots, infantry, and cavalry, fighting and slaughtering one another in numbers beyond reckoning. The dead will line the roads, and blood will flow bright red. The king's dream of a great stream of water flowing bright red pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! For the king's country, for the crown prince, for the queen, there is no harm."

The Buddha said: "What the king dreamed all pertains to events of the future age to come, not to events of the present age. Do not be afraid or sorrowful, great king!"

The king then knelt and said: "Having received the Buddha's teaching, my heart is immediately filled with joy. It is like a person holding a small vessel to receive oil: when the oil is too much and the vessel too small, one seeks a larger vessel to pour it into, and one is then at ease and no longer afraid. Now I have received the Buddha's grace and attained ease." The king then bowed to the Buddha and returned to his palace. He bestowed additional rewards upon his chief queen and confiscated the stipends of all the ministers and officials. The king said: "From this day forward, I will never again trust the words of non-Buddhist practitioners and brāhmaṇas."
