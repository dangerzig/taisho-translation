# Mahāyāna Repentance of the Six Sense-Faculties

## *Dàchéng Liùqíng Chànhuǐ* (大乘六情懺悔)

Taishō 1908

Composed by Wŏnhyo (*Yuánxiǎo*, 元曉) of Silla (*Xīnluó*, 新羅). A repentance liturgy.

*Note: This text is attributed to the great Korean Buddhist scholar-monk Wŏnhyo (617–686), one of the most influential thinkers in East Asian Buddhism. It presents a Mahāyāna repentance practice that contemplates the emptiness of both the sins being confessed and the act of confession itself, drawing on the Avataṃsaka framework (Vairocana's Lotus Treasury World) and Mādhyamika logic. It culminates in a "dream contemplation" (mèngguān) samādhi, comparing all of saṃsāric experience to a long dream from which one gradually awakens.*

If one practices according to the dharma-realm and begins to journey, then in the four postures there is not a single idle step. Contemplating the inconceivable virtue of all buddhas, always reflecting on the true characteristics, one wears away karmic obstacles. Universally, for the sake of the sentient beings of the six destinies without limit, one takes refuge in the immeasurable buddhas of the ten directions.

All buddhas are not different, yet also not one. One is precisely all; all is precisely one. Although there is no abiding, there is nowhere that is not abided in. Although there is no action, there is nothing that is not accomplished. Each and every mark, each and every pore pervades boundless realms to the end of the future, without obstruction, without hindrance, without differentiation, teaching and transforming sentient beings without rest.

Why is this so? In the ten directions and three times, in a single mote of dust and a single thought-moment, birth-and-death and nirvāṇa are without duality and without distinction. Great compassion and prajñā neither grasp nor reject, because they correspond with the uncommon dharmas.

Now in this place, the Lotus Treasury World (*Liánhuā Zàng Jiè*, 蓮花藏界), the Buddha Vairocana (*Lúshěnà Fó*, 盧舍那佛) sits upon the lotus platform, releasing boundless light, gathering immeasurable sentient beings, turning the great-vehicle dharma-wheel that turns without anything being turned. The great assembly of bodhisattvas fills space, receiving the great-vehicle dharma-joy that is received without anything being received.

Yet now we are together present in this place of the One Reality and the Three Jewels, a place without error, and we neither see nor hear, as if deaf and blind, without any buddha-nature. Why is this so? Ignorance and inversion deludedly create external objects. Clinging to self and what belongs to self, we create all kinds of karma. We cover and obstruct ourselves and cannot see or hear. It is like hungry ghosts who, approaching a river, see fire.

Therefore, now before the buddhas, we deeply give rise to shame and remorse. We arouse the bodhicitta and with sincere minds repent.

Since beginningless time, I and all sentient beings, intoxicated by ignorance, have created immeasurable sins. The five heinous offenses and the ten evil deeds: there is nothing we have not committed. Whether done by ourselves, caused others to do, or seen done and rejoiced: such sins are beyond reckoning. All buddhas, worthy ones, and sages witness and know them. For sins already committed, we deeply give rise to shame. For sins not yet committed, we dare not commit them again.

These sins are truly without any real existence. Through the combination of conditions they are provisionally named "karma." Apart from conditions there is no karma; even apart from conditions there is nothing. Neither inside nor outside, not located in between. The past has already perished. The future has not yet arisen. The present has no abiding. Therefore, because what was done has no abiding, it also has no arising.

If something existed before, it is not born. If something did not exist before, who is born? If one says that "formerly nonexistent" combined with "presently existent" is called "arising," then at the time of "formerly nonexistent" there is no "presently existent," and at the time of "presently existent" there is no "formerly nonexistent." Before and after do not connect; existent and nonexistent do not combine. Since the two meanings do not combine, where is arising? Since the meaning of combination is already destroyed, dispersal also does not hold. Without combination and without dispersal, neither existent nor nonexistent. When there is no time, there is nothing for "nonexistent" to contrast with. When there is existence, there is nothing for "existent" to await. Before and after, existent and nonexistent: none can be established.

One should know that the nature of karma, from the very origin, is unborn. Since from the beginning it cannot have arising, at what point could it have "non-arising"? Arising and non-arising are both unobtainable. To say "unobtainable" is also unobtainable.

The nature of karma is like this; all buddhas are also thus. As the sūtra says: "It is like sentient beings creating all kinds of karma, whether good or evil: neither inside nor outside." The nature of such karma is thus: neither existent nor nonexistent. "Originally nonexistent, now existent" is not birth from no cause. Without a doer, without a receiver: through the combination of time and season, the fruit of retribution is obtained.

If a practitioner can repeatedly reflect on this true characteristic and repent in this way, then the four grave offenses and five heinous sins can do nothing to them. It is like space, which is not burned by fire. But if one is heedless, without shame and without remorse, unable to reflect on the true characteristics of karma, then even though sins have no inherent nature, one will enter the hells. It is like an illusory tiger that devours the illusionist himself.

Therefore, before the buddhas of the ten directions, one should deeply give rise to shame and remorse and perform repentance. When performing this repentance, do not take it as something "done." One should immediately reflect on the true characteristics of repentance. Since the sins being repented are without any real existence, how can there be one who repents? The one who repents and what is repented are both unobtainable. At what point can there be any method of repentance?

Having thus repented of all karmic obstacles, one should also repent of the heedlessness of the six sense-faculties.

Since beginningless time, I and all sentient beings have not understood that all dharmas are originally unborn. Through deluded thinking and inversion, we impute self and what belongs to self. Inwardly we establish the six sense-faculties; depending on them, consciousness arises. Outwardly we create the six sense-objects, clinging to them as truly existent. We do not know that all of this is the creation of our own mind, like an illusion, like a dream, forever without any real existence. Within this we erroneously impute characteristics of male and female, give rise to afflictions, and bind ourselves. We sink forever in the sea of suffering without seeking a way out.

When one quietly reflects upon this, how extraordinary it is! It is like being asleep: the cover of drowsiness covers the mind. One falsely sees one's own body being swept away by a great flood. Not knowing that this is merely the creation of the dreaming mind, one takes it for a real drowning and gives rise to great terror.

Before one awakens, one has another dream within the dream, thinking: "What I saw earlier was a dream, not real." Because the mind-nature is acute, within the dream one recognizes the dream. Regarding that drowning, one no longer gives rise to terror. But one does not yet know that one's body lies upon the bed. Moving the head, waving the hands, one diligently seeks to fully awaken.

At the moment of full awakening, recalling the earlier dream, the water and the body being swept away are both without any real existence. One sees only the original, always lying still upon the bed.

The long dream is also like this. Ignorance covers the mind and deludedly creates the six destinies. Revolving through the eight sufferings, inwardly through the inconceivable perfuming of all buddhas and outwardly through the power of all buddhas' great compassionate vows, one dimly comes to faith and understanding.

I and all sentient beings are merely sleeping in a long dream, deludedly taking it for real. Favorable and adverse sense-objects, the two characteristics of male and female: all of these are my dream. Forever without any real substance. What is there to worry about or rejoice in? What is there to crave or be angry about?

Repeatedly reflecting in this way on this dream-contemplation, gradually one cultivates and attains the dream-like samādhi (*rúmèng sānmèi*, 如夢三昧). Through this samādhi one attains the patience of the unborn. From the long dream one suddenly and completely awakens. Then one knows that from the very beginning there was never any revolving. It was only this one mind, lying upon the bed of one-suchness (*yīrú*, 一如).

If one can in this way repeatedly reflect, then even while encountering the six sense-objects, one does not take them as real. Afflictions are shamed and cannot run free.

This is called the Mahāyāna Repentance of the Six Sense-Faculties.
