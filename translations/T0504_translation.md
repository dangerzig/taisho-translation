# 比丘聽施經
## Sūtra of the Bhikṣu Śroṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0504

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmapriya (曇無蘭)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada.

At that time, a certain bhikṣu came to the lecture hall and said to the bhikṣus there: "Worthy ones! I cannot comprehend the sūtras and Dharma. I am greatly given to drowsiness. I take no delight in the practices of the path, and I have doubts about the sūtras and Dharma."

Among those seated, one bhikṣu went and informed the Buddha: "There is a bhikṣu named Śroṇa who came to the lecture hall and told the bhikṣus: 'I cannot comprehend the sūtras and Dharma. I am greatly given to drowsiness. I take no delight in the practices of the path, and I have doubts about the sūtras and Dharma.'"

The Buddha then replied: "This bhikṣu is foolish. He does not guard the doors of his sense faculties, does not eat sparingly, does not maintain wakefulness during the first and last watches of the night with vigilant practice, and does not contemplate wholesome dharmas. How then can he comprehend the sūtras and Dharma, be free from drowsiness, delight in the practices of the path, and not doubt the sūtras and Dharma? Śroṇa will never attain these by such means."

The Buddha said to the bhikṣu: "If one does not guard the doors of the sense faculties, does not eat sparingly, does not maintain wakefulness during the first and last watches with vigilant practice, and does not contemplate wholesome dharmas, then how can one comprehend the sūtras and Dharma, be free from drowsiness, delight in the practices of the path, and not doubt the sūtras and Dharma? One will never attain these by such means. But if a bhikṣu guards the doors of the sense faculties, eats sparingly, maintains wakefulness during the first and last watches with vigilant practice, and contemplates wholesome dharmas, then he can comprehend the sūtras and Dharma, be free from drowsiness, delight in the practices of the path, and not doubt the sūtras and Dharma. From such practice these may be obtained."

The Buddha told the bhikṣu: "Go and summon Śroṇa."

The bhikṣu rose, prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and went to summon Śroṇa. Śroṇa came to the Buddha, prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and took his seat. The Buddha then said: "Śroṇa! Whatever you wish to say, speak."

Śroṇa said: "My body cannot comprehend the sūtras and Dharma. I am greatly given to drowsiness. I take no delight in the practices of the path, and I have doubts about the sūtras and Dharma."

The Buddha said to Śroṇa: "I wish to ask you about something. Answer according to what you know."

The Buddha said: "Do you know this: if one craves form and is not free from it, if desire is not abandoned, longing is not abandoned, yearning is not abandoned, and love is not abandoned, then when that form is separated from one, there arises in another a change, and grief, sorrow, lamentation, pain, and distress of mind. Is this so or not?"

Śroṇa said: "Indeed, various instances of this arise from it."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Worthy one, it should be so. Śroṇa!"

The Buddha said: "Just as with being not free from form, do you know that regarding feelings, perceptions, formations, and consciousness: if one craves consciousness and is not free from it, if desire is not abandoned, longing is not abandoned, yearning is not abandoned, and love is not abandoned, then when that consciousness is separated from one, there arises in another a change, and grief, sorrow, lamentation, pain, and distress of mind. Is this so or not?"

Śroṇa said: "It is so."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Worthy one, it should be so. Śroṇa!"

The Buddha said: "Regarding being free from form: do you know that if one is free from craving for form, free from desire, free from longing, free from yearning, free from love, then when that form is separated from one, no change arises in another, and there is no grief, sorrow, lamentation, pain, or distress of mind? Is this so or not?"

Śroṇa said: "It is so."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Worthy one, it should be so. Śroṇa!"

The Buddha said: "Just as with being free from form, regarding feelings, perceptions, formations of birth and death, and consciousness: if one does not crave consciousness, is without desire, without longing, without yearning, without love, then when that consciousness is separated from one, no change arises in another, and there is no grief, sorrow, lamentation, pain, or distress of mind. Is this so or not?"

Śroṇa said: "It is so."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! It should be so. Śroṇa!"

The Buddha then told Śroṇa: "I wish to teach you the sūtra Dharma: good at the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end. I shall fully reveal for you the practices of the path to their conclusion, all wholesome and excellent. You should listen and hold it in your mind."

Śroṇa said: "Yes, please do."

The Buddha said: "Once there were two men who went out together on a road. One of them knew the way, and the other did not know the way. The one who did not know the way went and asked the one who knew: 'I wish to reach a certain village, district, or country. Please tell me which way to go.' The one who knew the way said: 'Follow this road, go straight to the right, and ahead you will come to a fork with two roads. Leave the left road and take the right road. Go straight to the right, and shortly ahead you will see a ravine. Above the ravine there will again be two roads. Leave the left road and take the right road. Go straight to the right, and shortly you will see a thicket of trees. Above the thicket there will again be two roads. Leave the left road and take the right road. Go straight to the right, and gradually you will reach the village, district, or country you wish to arrive at.'"

The Buddha said: "The parable I have given above: you should know that all that I have taught should be carefully contemplated in this way. The one above who did not know the way: this refers to the wrong path of the world, and also to all who accept what those of wrong views teach. The one who knew the way: this refers to the Tathāgata, the Unattached, the Perfectly Awakened One, and also to all who have received the correct awakening.

"The left road spoken of: this refers to the three evil thoughts of all wicked persons. The first is the thought of desire; the second is the thought of ill-will; the third is the thought of cruelty. It also refers to wrong view, wrong intention, wrong speech, wrong thought, wrong action, wrong effort, wrong mindfulness, and wrong concentration.

"The right road spoken of: this refers to the three wholesome thoughts. The first is the thought of renunciation; the second is the thought of non-ill-will; the third is the thought of non-cruelty. It also refers to right view, right intention, right speech, right thought, right action, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration.

"The fork in the road: this refers to a person's doubt. The ravine spoken of: this refers to anger. The thicket of trees spoken of: this refers to the five pleasures. The first is the eye's delight in form, craving for and attached to desirable forms. The second is the ear's delight in sound. The third is the nose's delight in scent. The fourth is the tongue's delight in taste. The fifth is the body's delight in the soft and smooth, craving for and attached to desirable forms. The village, district, and country spoken of: this refers to the unconditioned virtue of nirvāṇa."

The Buddha told Śroṇa: "This is the task of all the Buddhas. I have taught this out of a compassionate mind. For those who wish to attain liberation, I have already shown them compassion. Now this is your task: you should go to a quiet place under a tree, or to a secluded spot, and with single-minded effort, practice bodily. Whether in the mountains, marshes, or among the tombs, you should subsist on fruits and berries. Bhikṣu, do not crave desire! In the world, one only repents afterward. This is the practice of all the Buddhas, and also the teaching of all the Buddhas."

Now the Buddha had spoken. The worthy Śroṇa rejoiced and contemplated the Buddha's teaching.
