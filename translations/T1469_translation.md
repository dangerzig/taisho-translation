# 佛說迦葉禁戒經
## Sūtra on Kāśyapa and the Precepts Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1469

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Juqu Jingsheng (沮渠京聲).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the kingdom of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, Anāthapiṇḍada's park, with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus and twelve thousand bodhisattvas.

At that time, the Buddha addressed the bhikṣu Mahākāśyapa: "A bhikṣu has two faults by which his body falls into hell: first, claiming things as his own; second, seeking to receive offerings from others. A bhikṣu further has two faults: first, turning to listen to the teachings of non-Buddhist paths; second, being greedy and accumulating robes, kaṣāyas, and bowls. A bhikṣu further has two faults: first, being overly familiar with laypeople; second, becoming jealous when seeing a good śramaṇa who keeps the precepts. A bhikṣu further has two faults by which he falls into a cauldron of boiling water: first, constantly thinking of sensual desire; second, delighting in associating with friends. A bhikṣu further has two faults: first, having transgressions but refusing to repent; second, dwelling on the faults of others instead. A bhikṣu further has two faults by which he falls into hell: first, slandering the sūtras and the path; second, damaging and injuring the sūtras and the precepts. A bhikṣu further has two faults: first, openly violating the precepts; second, gaining nothing from the Dharma. A bhikṣu further has two things to regret: first, forcibly donning the Dharma robes and kaṣāya; second, not keeping the precepts himself while failing to serve śramaṇas who do keep the precepts. A bhikṣu further has two faults that are truly hard to cure: first, having a mind that is perverse and confused; second, obstructing others from practicing the bodhisattva path."

The Buddha addressed Kāśyapa: "Why is a śramaṇa properly called a śramaṇa? There are four kinds of śramaṇa. First, one whose appearance and garments resemble those of a śramaṇa. Second, one who outwardly appears to be a śramaṇa but inwardly harbors flattery and deceit. Third, one who only seeks to gain service, renown, and praise, and is filled with arrogance. Fourth, one who practices the precepts without transgression: this is a true śramaṇa. What is meant by one whose appearance and garments resemble a śramaṇa? He shaves his head, wears the Dharma robes, and carries the proper bowl, but his mind does not govern itself. He only wishes to do evil and delights in studying heterodox paths. This is one who outwardly dresses in the semblance of a śramaṇa. What is meant by one who inwardly harbors flattery and deceit? He walks slowly, goes out slowly and enters slowly. Outwardly his food and clothing are coarse and poor, but inwardly he craves sweets and fine things. Outwardly he dwells in mountain retreats in grass huts, but inwardly he has no faith. He is outwardly lenient with himself and deceptive, but inwardly jealous of the honest and upright. Through connections he seeks much wealth, building up his reputation. This is flattery and deceit, not keeping the precepts. As for one who does not keep the precepts: he only wishes to be praised by others. In flattery he secretly desires others' praise, not examining himself with urgency to seek liberation. He has only the manner of flattery and deceit. This is not keeping the precepts. What is a true śramaṇa? One who keeps the precepts and practices the Way, not sparing his own life and relinquishing the body, not seeking material goods and not seeking offerings. If a bhikṣu maintains the practice of emptiness, constantly contemplating the pure Dharma as originally without flaw, practicing wisdom through his own effort and not receiving it from others, and attaining nirvāṇa within the Buddha-Dharma: this is a true śramaṇa."

The Buddha addressed Kāśyapa: "If you wish to seek the Way, you should emulate the true śramaṇa. Do not imitate the reputation-seeking śramaṇa or the flattering and deceitful śramaṇa. It is like a poor man who claims to be enormously wealthy: he has only the name of wealth but possesses nothing within." The Buddha asked Kāśyapa: "Should such a person exist?" Kāśyapa said: "He should not." The Buddha said: "Just so. Although one has the name of śramaṇa, if one does not practice the śramaṇa's dharma, it is like a poor man claiming to be enormously wealthy. It is like a person drowning in water who, though submerged, dies of thirst. Although a śramaṇa recites much, is greatly talented and wise, if he does not abandon sensual desires, he will die of hunger and thirst from those very desires, and because of this he enters hell, the animal realm, or the realm of hungry ghosts. It is like an excellent physician who has a full vessel of medicine but cannot cure his own illness: so too is one who recites much but does not keep the precepts. It is like a maṇi-jewel that has fallen into filth: so too is one who recites much but does not keep the precepts. It is like a dead person adorned with gold, silver, and jewels: so too is one who does not keep the precepts yet wears the kaṣāya in the semblance of a śramaṇa. It is like the son of an elder, adorned and wearing fine new garments, pure within and without: reciting much but not keeping the precepts is like this."

The Buddha addressed Kāśyapa: "There are four things that resemble keeping the precepts. What are these four? First, there is a bhikṣu who, with regard to the precepts that govern speech, says, 'I do not transgress.' Though he says this, he has attachment, and claims for himself that he has virtue. Second, there is a bhikṣu who fully understands the profound sūtras and is attached to practice, saying, 'This is what I practice.' Third, there is a bhikṣu who is greatly attached, saying, 'This is what I am attached to.' Fourth, there is one who claims, 'I always practice equanimity,' yet is attached to fear of birth and death. This is a śramaṇa who praises himself for keeping the precepts."

The Buddha addressed Kāśyapa: "The precepts are formless and are not attached to the three realms. They are impermanent, without self, without person, without life, without intention, without name, without designation, without transformation. They neither cause action nor are caused by it. They come from nowhere and go to nowhere. They are without form and without cessation. There is no body that transgresses, no mouth that transgresses, no mind that transgresses. They are without worldly reckoning, without worldly affairs, without abiding. There are also no precepts and no object of mindfulness. They are also without deterioration. This is called the precepts."

At that time, the Buddha said: "The precepts are without flaw and without attachment. The precepts are without anger or resentment, settled and secure, following the path of transcending the world. This is keeping the precepts. Not loving the body's form, not loving the life-span, and not delighting in the five destinies, fully understanding and entering the Buddha's path: this is keeping the precepts. Neither in the center nor at the edge, not attached and not turning, like wind in empty space: this is keeping the precepts. This is called a person without fixed nature who has a settled mind, without attachment and without self, as the characteristic of devas and humans. One who understands this is one who purely keeps the precepts, does not deviate from the precepts, is not arrogant, and always wishes to maintain the Way and keep the precepts. Who could surpass this? There is no person who trusts in emptiness, follows what the Buddha practiced, and remains undefiled, who enters from the darkness of the world into illumination and light, whose standing is without any fixed abode, and who is also not within the three realms. This is the Dharma of the precepts."

When the Buddha proclaimed this Dharma of the precepts, thirty-three thousand devas and human beings all attained the fruit of stream-entry, and eight hundred śramaṇas, through this causal condition, had their minds liberated and attained deliverance. Such is the practice of wisdom.
