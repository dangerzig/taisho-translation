# 佛說般舟三昧經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Pratyutpanna Samādhi

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0417

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by Lokakṣema (支婁迦讖)

---

### Chapter One: On Asking About the Matter

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was at Rājagṛha in the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove, together with great bodhisattvas, monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen, as well as devas, nāgas, asuras, yakṣas, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas, an immeasurable multitude, all gathered in the great assembly. At that time the Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla rose from his seat, arranged his robes, knelt on both knees, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask something. If permitted, I shall now ask."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Ask as you wish. I shall now explain it for you."

Bhadrapāla asked the Buddha: "What practice should a bodhisattva undertake to attain wisdom like the great ocean embracing ten thousand streams? What practice leads to broad mastery of all kinds of knowledge, so that whatever one hears one completely understands without doubt? What practice leads to recognizing one's own past lives and the places from which one has been reborn? What practice leads to long life? What practice ensures one is always born into a great and noble family, with parents, brothers, relatives, and acquaintances who are without exception loving and respectful? What practice leads to a handsome and beautiful appearance? What practice leads to great talent that surpasses all others, with wisdom that penetrates and encompasses everything? What practice leads to the establishment of marks and fulfillment of characteristics, the self-attainment of buddhahood with immeasurable spiritual power, the accomplishment of a buddha's realm and the adornment of a pure land? What practice leads to vanquishing Māra's forces? What practice leads to self-mastery so that one's wishes are never thwarted? What practice leads to entering the gate of dhāraṇī? What practice leads to spiritual powers that reach all buddha-lands? What practice leads to being courageous like a lion, fearless, so that all māras cannot shake one? What practice leads to obtaining the Buddha's holy nature, receiving and retaining all sūtras and dharmas, completely understanding them without forgetting? What practice leads to self-sufficiency, free from flattery, unattached to the three realms? What practice leads to being without obstruction, upholding the teaching of omniscience (sarvajñā) without losing the Buddha's intent? What practice leads to being trusted by people? What practice leads to the eight kinds of voice that enter ten thousand billion tones? What practice leads to the complete marks and excellent characteristics? What practice leads to the penetrating ear? What practice leads to the dharma-eye that sees what has not yet come to pass? What practice leads to the ten powers and right true wisdom? What practice, with the mind in equanimity, leads to mindfulness of the buddhas of the ten directions, so that they all appear before one? What practice leads to knowing the fundamental non-existence of the four things? What practice, right here in this world, leads to seeing the innumerable buddha-lands of the ten directions, together with the people, devas, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, and crawling creatures within them, and to completely understanding the destinies of the good and evil among them? Such are my questions. What practice should one undertake? May the Buddha explain and resolve all doubts."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "Excellent! Your questions bring immeasurable benefit to others, beyond all reckoning. The reason you are able to ask such questions is that in past lives, in the time of former buddhas, you performed meritorious deeds, made offerings to the buddhas, delighted in sūtras and dharma, observed the precepts with pure conduct, always went on alms-rounds without accepting invitations, frequently gathered bodhisattvas together and taught them to abandon all evils, regarded all beings equally, and always practiced great loving-kindness and great compassion. Your merit is beyond all reckoning."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "There is a samādhi called 'All Buddhas of the Ten Directions Standing Before One.' One who can practice this dharma can attain everything you have asked about."

Bhadrapāla addressed the Buddha: "May you explain it. What the Buddha teaches today brings immeasurable benefit and peace to the ten directions, manifesting a great sign of illumination for all bodhisattvas."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "There is a samādhi called 'Settled Concentration.' A bodhisattva should constantly guard, practice, and maintain it, and must not follow any other dharma. Among all merits, it is foremost."

### Chapter Two: On Practice

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "A bodhisattva who wishes quickly to attain this concentration should establish great faith and practice it according to the Dharma; then it can be attained. Do not entertain doubt even as much as a hair's breadth. This method of settled concentration is called the Bodhisattva's Transcendent Practice.

> "Establish single-mindedness, trust in this Dharma,
> and following what you have heard, direct your mind to that direction.
> With single-minded attention, cut off all conceptualization.
> Establish settled faith; do not entertain doubt.
> Practice with diligence; do not be lax.
> Do not give rise to thoughts of existence or nonexistence.
> Do not think of advancing; do not think of retreating.
> Do not think of what is before; do not think of what is behind.
> Do not think of left; do not think of right.
> Do not think of nonexistence; do not think of existence.
> Do not think of far; do not think of near.
> Do not think of pain; do not think of itching.
> Do not think of hunger; do not think of thirst.
> Do not think of cold; do not think of heat.
> Do not think of suffering; do not think of pleasure.
> Do not think of birth; do not think of old age;
> do not think of sickness; do not think of death.
> Do not think of the body; do not think of life;
> do not think of lifespan.
> Do not think of poverty; do not think of wealth.
> Do not think of nobility; do not think of lowliness.
> Do not think of form; do not think of desire.
> Do not think of small; do not think of large.
> Do not think of long; do not think of short.
> Do not think of beauty; do not think of ugliness.
> Do not think of evil; do not think of good.
> Do not think of anger; do not think of joy.
> Do not think of sitting; do not think of rising.
> Do not think of walking; do not think of standing.
> Do not think of sūtras; do not think of dharmas.
> Do not think of right; do not think of wrong.
> Do not think of abandoning; do not think of grasping.
> Do not think of conception; do not think of consciousness.
> Do not think of severing; do not think of attachment.
> Do not think of emptiness; do not think of reality.
> Do not think of light; do not think of heavy.
> Do not think of difficult; do not think of easy.
> Do not think of deep; do not think of shallow.
> Do not think of broad; do not think of narrow.
> Do not think of father; do not think of mother.
> Do not think of wife; do not think of child.
> Do not think of intimates; do not think of strangers.
> Do not think of hatred; do not think of love.
> Do not think of gain; do not think of loss.
> Do not think of success; do not think of failure.
> Do not think of purity; do not think of turbidity.
> Cut off all thoughts. Concentrate in single-pointed mindfulness.
> Let the mind not be disturbed. Always be diligent.
> Do not be lax. Do not count the years. Do not weary of the days.
> Establish single-mindedness. Do not falter midway.
> Eliminate drowsiness. Refine your attention.
> Always dwell alone. Do not gather in crowds.
> Avoid evil people. Draw near to good friends.
> Be close to a wise teacher. Regard them as a buddha.
> Hold firm to your resolve. Always be gentle.
> Regard all with equanimity.
> Avoid your home village. Distance yourself from relatives.
> Abandon desire. Tread the path of purity.
> Practice the unconditioned. Sever all desires.
> Abandon scattered thoughts. Cultivate settled practice.
> Study the wisdom of learning. Be sure to practice meditation.
> Eliminate the three defilements. Remove the six sense-gates.
> Cut off lust. Leave behind all sensation.
> Do not covet wealth or hoard possessions.
> Eat with moderation. Do not crave flavors.
> Do not eat the flesh of sentient beings.
> Wear robes properly. Do not adorn yourself.
> Do not engage in jest. Do not be proud.
> Do not be conceited. Do not be arrogant.
> When teaching the sūtras, speak according to the Dharma.
> Understand the body's nature as being like an illusion.
> Do not cling to the aggregates. Do not enter the sense-realms.
> The aggregates are like thieves. The four elements are like snakes.
> All is impermanent, all is evanescent.
> There is no permanent master. Understand fundamental non-existence.
> Causes and conditions come together; causes and conditions disperse.
> Completely understand this: know fundamental non-existence.
> Extend loving-kindness to all beings.
> Give to the poor; aid without expecting return.
> This is the settled practice, the bodhisattva's conduct:
> the essential wisdom that gives rise to all knowledge."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "By maintaining this practice, one attains the samādhi in which all the buddhas of the present appear before one. Any monk, nun, layman, or laywoman who practices according to the Dharma with the precepts fully maintained, dwelling alone in one place and mindful of the Buddha Amitābha now present in the west, following what they have heard, should be mindful: beyond a thousand billion myriads of buddha-lands from here, there is a land called Sukhāvatī. With single-pointed mindfulness for one day and one night, or seven days and seven nights, after the seventh day one will see him. Just as a person sees things in a dream, not knowing day or night, not knowing inside or outside, and not because one is in darkness or physically obstructed. Bhadrapāla! The bodhisattva should contemplate in this way. At that time, in the realms of all the buddha-lands, all the great mountains and Mount Sumerus, and every dark place, will be opened wide without any obstruction. This bodhisattva does not use the divine eye to see through, does not use the divine ear to hear through, does not use supernatural powers to travel to that buddha-land, and does not die here to be reborn there. Rather, sitting right here, one sees him.

"It is like a person who hears that in the country of Vaiśālī there is a courtesan named Sumanaḥ, another person hears of a courtesan named Apalālā, and yet another hears that Upāhvānā has also become a courtesan. Though these men have never seen these three women, hearing of them their lustful desires are immediately aroused. These three men are all in the city of Rājagṛha, and each thinking simultaneously, in their dreams each goes to the women and lies with them, and upon waking each reflects on this."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "I use these three women as a parable. You should use this matter to explain the sūtra to people, enabling them to comprehend this wisdom and reach the stage of non-retrogression on the path of supreme, right, and true enlightenment. When in the future you attain buddhahood, your name shall be Well-Awakened."

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva in this land who is mindful of the Buddha Amitābha, through focused mindfulness comes to see him. One then asks: 'By practicing what dharma does one come to be born in this land?' The Buddha Amitābha replies: 'Those who wish to come and be born here should be mindful of my name without ceasing, and then they will come to be born.'"

The Buddha said: "Through focused mindfulness one attains birth there. One should always be mindful of the Buddha's body with its thirty-two marks, eighty excellent characteristics, and vast brilliant radiance shining everywhere, handsome beyond compare, teaching the Dharma amidst the assembly of bodhisattvas, his form indestructible. Why is his form indestructible? Form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness, soul, spirit, earth, water, fire, wind, the worldly realms up to Brahmā and Mahābrahmā: none of these can destroy form. Through mindfulness of the Buddha, one attains this samādhi."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "Who can attest to this bodhisattva samādhi? My disciples Mahākāśyapa and Indradatta, the deva-son Sucinta, and those who know the proper time, who have practiced and attained it: they are the witnesses. Thus, Bhadrapāla, whoever wishes to see all the buddhas presently in the ten directions should direct single-pointed mindfulness toward that direction, without any other thought, and then one will be able to see them.

"It is like a person who travels far to another country and thinks of the family and relatives of their homeland; in a dream they return home, see family and relatives, happily converse with them, and upon waking tell their acquaintances about it."

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva who hears a buddha's name and wishes to see him should constantly direct mindfulness toward that direction, and then will see him. It is like a monk contemplating the bones of a dead person: placing them before himself and observing, seeing blue at times, white at times, red at times, black at times. These colors are not brought by anyone; they are what the mind conceives. A bodhisattva likewise, through the Buddha's spiritual power, standing in samādhi of their own accord, can see whichever buddha in whatever direction they wish to see. Why? Through the power of the Buddha, the power of samādhi, and the power of one's own past merit: through these three things one comes to see them.

"It is like a young person, handsome and well-dressed, who wishes to see their own reflection. If they take a mirror, sesame oil, clean water, or crystal, they will see themselves reflected therein. Consider: does the reflection enter the mirror, oil, water, or crystal from outside?"

Bhadrapāla said: "No, Heavenly among the Heavenly! Because the mirror, oil, water, and crystal are pure, one sees one's own reflection. The reflection does not come out from within, nor does it enter from without."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, Bhadrapāla! Because form is pure, all that exists is pure. When one wishes to see the Buddha, one immediately sees. Seeing, one asks; asking, the Buddha replies. Hearing the sūtras, one is filled with great joy and thinks: 'Where does the Buddha come from? Where have I gone?' One reflects: the Buddha has not come from anywhere, and I have not gone anywhere. One further reflects: the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm, these three realms are created by the mind alone. Whatever I am mindful of, that I see. The mind creates the Buddha. The mind itself sees. The mind is the Buddha-mind. The Buddha-mind is my own body. The mind sees the Buddha, yet the mind does not itself know the mind. The mind does not itself see the mind. When the mind gives rise to conception, that is delusion. When the mind is without conception, that is nirvāṇa. These dharmas hold nothing to delight in. Even should one be mindful, it is merely empty, having nothing at all. A bodhisattva standing in this samādhi sees in this way."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "The mind does not itself know:
> having mind, one does not see mind.
> When the mind gives rise to conception, that is delusion;
> without mind, that is nirvāṇa.
> These dharmas have no solidity
> but constantly abide in mindfulness.
> Through understanding, one sees emptiness:
> all is without conception or aspiration."

### Chapter Three: On Four Things

"A bodhisattva has four sets of dharmas for quickly attaining this samādhi. First, faith that cannot be destroyed by anyone. Second, diligence that cannot be turned back by anyone. Third, wisdom that none can match. Fourth, constantly associating with a good teacher. These are the four.

"There are four more for quickly attaining this samādhi. First, for three months not giving rise to worldly thoughts even for the snap of a finger. Second, for three months not sleeping even for the snap of a finger. Third, for three months walking without rest, except for meals and the restroom. Fourth, teaching the sūtras to others without hoping for offerings in return. These are the four.

"There are another four for quickly attaining this samādhi. First, gathering people and bringing them to the Buddha. Second, gathering people and having them hear the sūtras. Third, being free from jealousy. Fourth, teaching people to study the Buddha's path. These are the four.

"There are another four for quickly attaining this samādhi. First, making Buddha images to accomplish this samādhi. Second, copying this samādhi on fine silk. Third, guiding arrogant people into the Buddha's path. Fourth, constantly protecting the Buddha's Dharma. These are the four."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always have faith and delight in the Buddha's Dharma,
> practice diligently, understand the profound wisdom,
> broadly distribute and teach it to others,
> but take care never to covet offerings.
> Let the mind skillfully understand and leave desire behind,
> always mindful of the Buddha's awesome virtue,
> fully seeing and knowing his immeasurable transformations.
> Buddhas of the past, of the future,
> and the presently honored among humans,
> with their various marks of many kinds,
> golden-colored and free from defilement.
> Firmly teach the boundless wisdom,
> listen to this Dharma without a scattered mind,
> always abandon the practice of laxity,
> and harbor no anger toward others.
> Revere your teacher as a buddha;
> take care never to doubt this sūtra,
> which all buddhas praise.
> Always make Buddha images.
> Always teach others to study this Dharma:
> practicing thus, one attains the samādhi."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "One who wishes to study this samādhi should revere their teacher, serving and making offerings, regarding them as a buddha. One who does not regard the good teacher as a buddha will find it difficult to attain this samādhi. When a bodhisattva reveres a good teacher and studies under them and attains this samādhi, then through the Buddha's spiritual power, standing within it, looking east one sees many hundreds, thousands, millions, and billions of buddhas, and equally sees them in all ten directions. It is like a person who rises at night and gazes up at the multitude of stars. A bodhisattva who wishes to see all the buddhas of the present standing before them should revere the good teacher, not focusing on the teacher's faults, and should be complete in generosity, moral discipline, patience, diligence, and concentration, and must not be lax."

### Chapter Four: On Parables

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "If a bodhisattva who has attained this samādhi does not practice it diligently, it is like a person who loads a ship full of precious treasures to cross the great sea, but the ship breaks apart midway. All the people of Jambudvīpa would grieve greatly, lamenting: 'We have lost so many treasures!' If a bodhisattva hears this samādhi but does not study it, all the devas and people would grieve and say: 'We have lost so many sūtra-treasures!'"

The Buddha said: "This samādhi-sūtra is what the Buddha has entrusted and what the Buddha has praised. If one hears this profound samādhi but does not copy, study, recite, uphold, or practice it according to the Dharma, one is truly foolish. It is like a foolish child to whom someone offers sandalwood incense, but who refuses to accept it, calling it impure. The owner of the incense says: 'This is sandalwood! Do not think it impure. Smell it and you will know its fragrance. Look at it and you will see its purity.' But the person closes their eyes, refusing to smell or look."

The Buddha said: "One who hears this samādhi but will not accept and uphold it is like that foolish child, truly ignorant. Such a person calls the worldly 'real,' does not enter emptiness, does not know non-existence, claims to follow the proper Dharma, and even ridicules it, saying: 'Does the Buddha really have such profound sūtras? Does the Buddha really have such spiritual power?' They mockingly say: 'Is there really a monk in the world like Ānanda?'"

The Buddha said: "Such people, departing from those who uphold this samādhi, go off in groups of twos and threes, saying to each other: 'What kind of teaching is this? Where did they get such words? They must have composed this sūtra themselves; it is not what the Buddha taught.'"

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "It is like a merchant who shows a maṇi pearl to a foolish farmer. The farmer asks: 'How much is this pearl worth?' The merchant says: 'If this pearl is placed in a dark room, its light illuminates everything, and the value of the treasures within its light would fill the entire room.'"

The Buddha said: "The farmer, not understanding the pearl, says: 'Can it match the price of one cow? If you will trade it for a cow, that would be fine; if not, forget it.' In this way, Bhadrapāla, a bodhisattva who hears this samādhi but does not believe and instead ridicules it is like that foolish farmer."

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva who hears this samādhi and believes, accepts, upholds, and practices it is protected on all four sides, has nothing to fear, is complete in moral discipline, and is truly of high understanding. With wisdom deep and penetrating, one should distribute and teach it to others, transmitting it from one to another, so that this samādhi endures long in the world."

The Buddha said: "Foolish people, having made no merit or performed no meritorious deeds in past lives, are instead arrogant, prone to slander and jealousy. Coveting wealth and seeking fame, they only wish to talk idly and do not believe in profound sūtras. Hearing this samādhi, they do not believe, do not delight in it, do not study it, and instead slander this sūtra, saying it is not what the Buddha taught."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "This is why I tell you: if a good man or good woman fills the three-thousand-fold world with precious treasures and gives them in charity, the merit of that, however great, does not compare to hearing this samādhi and believing in it with joy. That person's merit surpasses the former by a hundred thousand million billion times. Know that the merit of this joyful faith is supremely great."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "This is just as I have said, without any difference. I deliberately speak these words now. If anyone hearing me teach this samādhi has doubts, that person in the future will follow evil teachers. Even if they are near good teachers, their merit will be insufficient. Such people will increasingly associate with evil teachers, and hearing this samādhi will not believe, not delight, not study it. Why? Because they have encountered few buddhas and have little wisdom, and so they do not believe."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "If anyone hears this samādhi and does not ridicule it, does not slander it, does not doubt it, does not waver between believing and not believing, but rejoices, delights in copying, studying, reciting, and upholding it, I already know and see that person. Such a person has not merely cultivated merit with one or two buddhas; they have heard this samādhi in the presence of a hundred buddhas. In a future age, when they hear this samādhi and copy, study, recite, and uphold it for even one day and one night, their merit is incalculable. They will of themselves attain the stage of non-retrogression, and whatever they wish for, they will obtain."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "Listen to my parable. Suppose someone takes a single buddha-land and grinds it entirely to dust, then takes each mote of dust and grinds it again, as many as the dust-motes of a buddha-land. Would those dust-motes be numerous?"

Bhadrapāla said: "Extremely numerous, extremely numerous, Heavenly among the Heavenly!"

The Buddha said: "Suppose a bodhisattva takes each mote of dust and places it in a single buddha-land. As many buddha-lands as that, all filled with precious treasures, offered to the buddhas: that merit is still slight! It does not compare to hearing this samādhi, studying, copying, reciting, upholding, and teaching it to others. The merit of hearing it even for a brief moment is incalculable. How much more so for one who has attained this samādhi in its entirety!"

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "If there is a bodhisattva who seeks all virtues,
> they should teach, practice, and uphold this samādhi.
> For those who believe, delight in, chant, and do not doubt,
> their merit and blessings are without measure.
> Take a single buddha-land world
> and smash it all to dust;
> then all buddha-lands beyond that number,
> filled with precious treasures as offerings:
> this does not compare to hearing this samādhi,
> whose merit surpasses that giving.
> Even by parable, that merit cannot be expressed.
> I entrust this to you all: encourage and teach it;
> practice with vigor and without laxity.
> Whoever recites and upholds this samādhi
> has already beheld a hundred thousand buddhas face to face.
> Even in the final great terror,
> holding this samādhi, one has nothing to fear.
> A monk who practices this has already seen me
> and always follows the Buddha, never distant.
> As the Buddha has spoken, there is no difference:
> a bodhisattva should always follow this teaching
> and swiftly attain right awakening, the ocean of wisdom."

### Chapter Five: On the Four Groups

Bhadrapāla addressed the Buddha: "How rare this is, Heavenly among the Heavenly! If someone has abandoned worldly desires and become a monk, and hears this samādhi, how should they study it? How should they uphold it? How should they practice it?"

The Buddha said: "A monk who has abandoned desires and wishes to study this samādhi should maintain the precepts in purity without the slightest breach, not even as much as a hair's breadth. One should always fear the suffering of the hells and distance oneself from flattery. This is purity."

"What constitutes a breach of the precepts?"

The Buddha said: "Seeking form."

"What is seeking form?"

The Buddha said: "When a person thinks: 'By upholding the precepts and disciplining myself, I shall in a future life be reborn as a deva or as a wheel-turning king.' This is delighting in desire, and it constitutes a breach of the precepts."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "One who wishes to study this samādhi should keep oneself pure, uphold the precepts completely, and not be flattering, but be praised by the wise. In practicing the sūtras, one should be generous, diligent, firm in resolve, strong in faith, encouraging and joyful, and should serve the teacher, regarding them as a buddha. Then the samādhi will come quickly. If one is disrespectful, frivolous, or deceives the teacher, even if one studies this samādhi for a long time, one will quickly forget it."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "If this bodhisattva hears this samādhi from a monk, a nun, a layman, or a laywoman, they should regard that person as a buddha, always reverent. One must not approach with a flattering mind. A bodhisattva must not have a flattering mind but should always be sincere. One should always delight in dwelling alone. Without cherishing body or life, without expecting what others might give, one should always go on alms-rounds without accepting invitations, maintaining one's own discipline, content with whatever one has. Walking in meditation, one must not lie down in laziness. Such is the teaching of this sūtra. Those who study this samādhi should uphold it in this way."

Bhadrapāla addressed the Buddha: "How rare this is, Heavenly among the Heavenly! If in a future age there are lax bodhisattvas who hear this samādhi but are unwilling to study it diligently, what then? If there are bodhisattvas who diligently wish to study it, we shall teach them according to this sūtra."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, Bhadrapāla! I support your joy in this. The buddhas of the past, the future, and the present all support this joy."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Accept and uphold all that I have taught.
> Always dwell alone and practice merit.
> Maintain your discipline; do not gather in groups.
> Always go on alms-rounds; do not accept invitations.
> Revere the Dharma-teacher as a buddha.
> Eliminate drowsiness; let your resolve be open.
> Always be diligent and without laxity:
> practicing thus, one attains the samādhi."

Bhadrapāla addressed the Buddha: "If a nun seeks the bodhisattva path and wishes to study this samādhi, how should she practice?"

The Buddha said: "A nun who seeks this samādhi must not be self-important but always humble and deferential. She must not consider herself precious, must not be conceited, must not be jealous, must not be angry, must not covet wealth or beauty. She should always be pure, not cherishing her own body or life, always delighting in sūtras and Dharma, striving to study broadly, and abandoning lust, anger, and delusion. She should not covet fine clothing or pearl ornaments, and should be praised by the wise. She should revere a good teacher, regarding them as a buddha, and must not have a flattering mind."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "If a nun seeks the samādhi,
> she should always be diligent, never lax,
> and never listen to the covetous mind.
> Eliminate anger and self-importance.
> Do not be arrogant, deceptive, or playful.
> Always be sincere and establish single faith.
> Revere the good teacher as a buddha:
> practicing thus, one attains the samādhi."

Bhadrapāla addressed the Buddha: "If a householder cultivates the path and hears this samādhi and wishes to study it, how should they practice?"

The Buddha said: "A householder who wishes to study this samādhi should firmly uphold the five precepts, must not drink alcohol or give it to others, must not be intimate with women or encourage others to do so, must not feel attachment toward wife, children, sons, or daughters, must not covet wealth and property, should always think of leaving home to become a śramaṇa, should regularly observe the eight precepts at a Buddhist monastery, should always think of giving generously, and after giving should not think 'I shall receive the reward myself' but give for the sake of all. One should always practice great loving-kindness and revere the good teacher. Upon seeing a monk who keeps the precepts, one must not look down on them or speak ill of them. Having practiced in this way, one should study and uphold this samādhi."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "A householder who wishes to study this samādhi
> should uphold the five precepts without breach.
> Always think of becoming a śramaṇa;
> do not covet wife, children, or wealth.
> Regularly observe the eight precepts at a monastery;
> do not be arrogant or look down on others.
> Let the mind be free from longing and desire.
> Practice the sūtras sincerely, without flattery.
> Abandon miserliness and always give generously.
> Always make offerings and revere the sangha.
> Always be single-minded and without laxity:
> studying this samādhi, one should practice thus."

Bhadrapāla addressed the Buddha: "If a laywoman hears this samādhi and wishes to study it, how should she practice?"

The Buddha said: "A laywoman who wishes to study should uphold the five precepts and take the three refuges. What are the three? Taking refuge in the Buddha, taking refuge in the Dharma, taking refuge in the Sangha. She must not follow other paths, must not bow to devas, must not worship ghosts and spirits, must not consult auspicious days. She must not be playful, must not be self-indulgent with lustful thoughts, must not have a covetous mind. She should always think of giving generously, delight in hearing sūtras, strive to study broadly, and revere the good teacher. Her mind should always be earnest and never lax. If monks or nuns come by, she should offer them a seat and serve them food."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "A laywoman who wishes to study the samādhi
> should uphold the five precepts without breach,
> serve the good teacher, regarding them as a buddha.
> Do not bow to devas or worship spirits.
> Eliminate killing, stealing, and jealousy.
> Do not engage in divisive speech or stir up conflict.
> Do not be miserly; always think of giving.
> When seeing faults, conceal them; praise only the good.
> Do not be flattering or engage in sexual misconduct.
> Always be humble; do not be conceited.
> Respectfully serve monks and nuns:
> practicing thus, one attains the samādhi."

### Chapter Six: On Protection

The Bodhisattvas Bhadrapāla, Ratnākara, Gaurīdvīpa, Nārada, Sucinta, Mahāsusaṃvara, Indradatta, and Valunārjuna, these eight bodhisattvas, seeing what the Buddha had taught, were all filled with great joy. They offered five hundred kalpaka robes in charity, offered their own bodies in surrender, and made offerings of precious treasures. The Buddha told Ānanda: "Bhadrapāla and the others, five hundred people, are teachers among humans, constantly upholding the true Dharma and teaching in accordance with it. All who follow them are joyful, and those who attend them have minds that are pure and free from desire."

At that time the five hundred all stood with palms joined before the Buddha. Bhadrapāla addressed the Buddha: "By practicing how many dharmas does a bodhisattva quickly attain this samādhi?"

The Buddha said: "There are four things. First, not believing in other paths. Second, cutting off worldly desire. Third, maintaining pure conduct. Fourth, having no greed. These are the four. One who practices thus attains five hundred merits in this present life.

"It is like a monk of loving-kindness who is never poisoned, whom weapons cannot harm, whom fire cannot burn, and who enters water without drowning. Even when the kalpa-ending fire arises, if such a person falls into that fire, the fire will immediately be extinguished, just as a great flood extinguishes a small flame. A bodhisattva who upholds this samādhi is likewise: whether it be a king, a thief, flood, fire, nāga, yakṣa, python, lion, tiger, wolf, ape, piśāca, or kumbhāṇḍa, or any poisonous beast, ghost, or spirit that might wish to trouble, kill, or rob them of robes and bowl, or destroy their meditation and steal their mindfulness: none will ever be able to harm this bodhisattva, except as a consequence of past karma. What I say is without error."

The Buddha said: "One who upholds this samādhi will never suffer pain in the eyes, ears, nose, mouth, or body, and the mind will never be anxious, except as a consequence of past karma."

The Buddha said: "This bodhisattva is praised by all the devas, nāgas, spirits, asuras, yakṣa-spirits, garuḍa-spirits, kiṃnara-spirits, mahoraga-spirits, humans, and non-humans. They all protect, serve, and make offerings to this bodhisattva, gaze upon them with reverence, and long to see them. The blessed buddhas are likewise. Whatever sūtras this bodhisattva has not yet memorized, whatever teachings not previously heard, through the spiritual power of this samādhi they will all be spontaneously obtained. What is not attained during the day will be attained in dreams at night."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "The merit of one who upholds this samādhi, even if I were to describe it for one kalpa after another, could never be exhausted. I have merely outlined the essentials."

### Chapter Seven: On Encouraging Assistance

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "For a bodhisattva within this samādhi, there are four kinds of joyful assistance. The buddhas of the past upheld this samādhi and, with joyful assistance, attained supreme, right, and perfect enlightenment (anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi), and their wisdom was perfected. The immeasurable buddhas presently in the ten directions likewise attained it through the four kinds of joyful assistance within this samādhi. Those of the future will also attain it through these four kinds of joyful assistance. I too support them with joy."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "Regarding the four kinds of joyful assistance within this samādhi, I shall give a small parable. Suppose a person with a lifespan of a hundred years walked along the ground without ever resting, traveling faster than the swift wind. Could anyone calculate the distance they would traverse?"

Bhadrapāla said: "No one could calculate it. Only the Buddha's disciple Śāriputra or a bodhisattva at the stage of non-retrogression could calculate it."

The Buddha said: "I deliberately tell all bodhisattvas: if a good man or good woman were to fill that entire distance with precious treasures and give them as offerings, this would not compare to hearing this samādhi and giving the four kinds of joyful assistance. The merit of that surpasses the merit of that giving by a hundred thousand million billion times. Know that the merit of joyful assistance is supremely great."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "Long ago, in an immeasurable and incalculable aeon, there was a buddha named Siṃhamati, Perfectly Awakened One, Unsurpassed One, Master of the Dharma Path, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One, dwelling in a secluded place. At that time Jambudvīpa was one hundred and eighty thousand billion leagues in length and breadth, with a total of six million and four hundred thousand countries, all prosperous, with thriving populations. There was a great country called Bhadra, ruled by a wheel-turning king named Vaiśravaṇa. He went to that Buddha, paid his respects, and sat to one side. The Buddha, knowing his thoughts, taught him this samādhi. The king, hearing it, was filled with joyful support. He immediately scattered precious treasures over the Buddha and thought: 'May I use this merit to bring peace and security to the people of the ten directions.'

"After the parinirvāṇa of the Buddha Siṃhamati, King Vaiśravaṇa reached the end of his life and was reborn in his own family as a crown prince named Brahmadatta. At that time there was a monk named Jewel who taught this samādhi to the fourfold assembly. Brahmadatta heard it and was filled with joyful support, leaping with joy. He scattered treasures worth a hundred billion and offered fine robes in homage, generating the aspiration for buddhahood. Together with a thousand people, he became a śramaṇa under this monk, seeking to study this samādhi. Together with the thousand, he served the teacher for eight thousand years without rest or weariness. Having once heard the four kinds of joyful assistance of this samādhi, he entered exalted and illuminated wisdom. Afterward he encountered sixty-eight thousand more buddhas, and under each one he heard this samādhi, until he attained buddhahood under the name Tīrthaviddha, Perfectly Awakened One, Unsurpassed One, Master of the Dharma Path, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. At that time the thousand monks all attained perfect enlightenment, each bearing the name Tīrthottama. They taught immeasurable people, causing them all to seek the buddha-path."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "Who, hearing this samādhi, would not give joyful assistance, study, uphold, recite, and teach it to others?"

The Buddha said: "Whoever upholds this samādhi will swiftly attain buddhahood. Simply hearing of its merit is incalculable, so how much more for one who studies and upholds it! If one must travel a hundred or a thousand leagues to find this samādhi, one should seek it. How much more so when it is nearby and one does not seek to study it! If anyone who hears this samādhi wishes to study it, they should serve the teacher for ten years, making offerings and attending to them, without following their own will but following the teacher's instruction, always mindful of the teacher's kindness."

The Buddha said: "I deliberately speak thus for you. If one must travel four thousand leagues for this samādhi, one should go there. Even if one does not get to hear it, the merit is incalculable. Why? Because through dedicated effort, one will eventually attain it and of oneself become a buddha."

### Chapter Eight: On Sincerity

The Buddha said: "Long ago there was a buddha named Saddharmasmṛti, Perfectly Awakened One, Unsurpassed One, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. At that time there was a monk named Valunda. After that buddha's parinirvāṇa, this monk upheld this samādhi. I was then a king of the kṣatriya caste. In a dream I heard this samādhi, and upon waking I set out to seek this monk. I became a śramaṇa under him, wishing to hear this samādhi. I served that teacher for thirty-six thousand years, but Māra's interferences arose repeatedly and in the end I never got to hear it."

The Buddha told the monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen: "I deliberately tell you all: quickly take up this samādhi and do not let it be lost. Serve the good teacher well and uphold this samādhi. Even for a kalpa, or a hundred kalpas, or a hundred thousand kalpas, do not become lax or weary. Never leave the good teacher. Whether it be food, drink, provisions, clothing, bedding, or precious treasures, do not hold anything dear. If you have nothing to give, you should go on alms-rounds to supply the teacher. Persist in seeking this samādhi without weariness. You should even cut your own flesh to serve the good teacher; how much more so material things, which are not even worth mentioning! Serve the good teacher as a slave serves a master. One who seeks this samādhi should do so in this way. Having attained the samādhi, one should firmly uphold it and always be mindful of the teacher's kindness. This samādhi is rare and hard to encounter. Even seeking it for a hundred thousand kalpas, merely to hear its name, one may not succeed. How much less if, having obtained it, one does not practice diligently!

"For one who has obtained this samādhi, practices it diligently, and transmits the teaching to others: even if buddha-lands as numerous as the sands of the Ganges were all filled with precious treasures and given as charity, that offering would not compare to one who studies this samādhi."

The Buddha told Bhadrapāla: "If there are people who wish to study, support them with joy. Those who wish to study and do obtain it: it is through the Buddha's spiritual power that they study. One should carefully copy this samādhi on fine silk, seal it with the Buddha's seal, and make good offerings to it.

"What is the Buddha's seal? It is non-practice, non-greed, non-seeking, non-conception, non-attachment, non-aspiration, non-inclination toward rebirth, non-grasping, non-looking back, non-abiding, non-obstruction, non-binding, non-possession, the exhaustion of desire, non-arising, non-ceasing, non-destruction, non-decay. It is the essence of the path, the root of the path. This seal is beyond the reach of arhats and pratyekabuddhas, let alone the foolish. This seal is the Buddha's seal."

The Buddha said: "At the time I teach this samādhi, one thousand eight hundred billion devas, asuras, ghosts, spirits, nāgas, and humans attain the fruit of stream-entry. Eight hundred monks attain arhatship. Five hundred nuns attain arhatship. Ten thousand bodhisattvas attain this samādhi and establish themselves in the dharma of non-arising. Twelve thousand bodhisattvas reach the stage of non-retrogression."

The Buddha told Śāriputra, Maudgalyāyana, the monk Bhadrapāla, and the others: "From incalculable kalpas I have sought the path, and now I have attained buddhahood. I entrust this sūtra to all of you. Study, recite, uphold, and guard it; do not let it be lost. If there are those who wish to study it, instruct them fully and properly. If there are those who wish to hear it, teach it to them fully."

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the Bodhisattva Bhadrapāla and the others, Śāriputra, Maudgalyāyana, the monk Ānanda, and all the devas, asuras, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, and humans were all filled with great joy. They paid their respects to the Buddha and departed.
