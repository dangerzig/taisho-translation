# 大聖文殊師利菩薩讚佛法身禮
## Praise and Homage to the Buddha's Dharmakāya by the Great Holy Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1195

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Amoghavajra (不空).

---

This homage is drawn from the Sūtra of the Wisdom-Light Adornment of All Realms of the Mahāyāna.

The Emperor, governing the world with profound benevolence and illuminating all things with great brilliance, universally rains sweet dew and refreshes the multitudes. Humbly receiving the imperial compassion, and unworthily appointed to the task of translation, I was specially given the gracious command to gather sixteen scholar-monks of the capital, including Liangben and others, to translate the Prajñāpāramitā for Benevolent Kings Protecting the Nation and the Mahāyāna Ghanavyūha Sūtra at the inner chapel. Wishing to offer praises to supreme awakening and to bestow blessings upon the imperial person: I noted that the Scripture of the Great Holy Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva's Praise and Homage to the Buddha's Dharmakāya, according to the Sanskrit original, contains forty-one homages. The previously circulating version had only ten, the text being incomplete and the praises of virtue not exhaustive. Fearing this might depart from the holy one's earnest sincerity and deprive beings of their superior benefit, Amoghavajra, who already possessed a complete Sanskrit manuscript, has now translated and circulated it to be of broad benefit. As for the remaining repentance rituals and other procedures, all follow the old edition and are not repeated here. This was done in the fourth month of the first year of Yongtai of the Great Tang [765 CE]. The sūtra says:

Thus have I heard. At one time the Buddha was in Rājagṛha on Vulture Peak, together with a great assembly of twenty-five thousand bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats, and seventy-two nayuta-koṭis of bodhisattva mahāsattvas, with Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva as their chief. At that time Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva rose from his seat, arranged his robes, bared his right shoulder, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, joined his palms in reverence, and praised the Tathāgata, speaking gāthās:

> "Without color, without form or appearance,
> Without roots, without any abiding place,
> Neither arising nor ceasing:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Neither going nor staying,
> Neither grasping nor releasing,
> Far removed from the six sense-entries:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Not abiding in any dharma,
> Apart from existence and non-existence,
> Practicing in equality:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Transcending the three realms,
> Equal to empty space,
> Unstained by any desire:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "In all modes of deportment,
> Going, coming, sleeping, and waking,
> Ever abiding in quiescence:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Going and coming are perfectly equal,
> Already abiding in equality,
> Never destroying equality:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Entering all signless samādhis,
> Seeing all dharmas as quiescent,
> Ever abiding in samādhi:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Without abiding, without perception,
> Attaining sovereign mastery over all dharmas,
> Wisdom's function ever in concentration:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Not abiding in the six faculties,
> Not attached to the six objects,
> Ever abiding in the single characteristic:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Entering signlessness,
> Able to sever all defilements,
> Far removed from name-and-form:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Not abiding in the aspect of existence,
> Also apart from all aspects,
> Entering aspect-and-signlessness:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Without discriminative thought,
> The mind abides in non-abiding,
> No thought arising:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "The storehouse-consciousness like space:
> Without defilement, without idle discourse,
> Far removed from the three times:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Space has no center or edge;
> The mind of all buddhas is likewise.
> The mind being equal to space:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "All buddhas are the aspect of space;
> Space too is without aspect.
> Apart from all cause and effect:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Not attached to any dharma,
> Like the moon in water, ungraspable,
> Far removed from the aspect of self:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Not abiding in the aggregates,
> Not attached to the sense-fields and elements,
> Far removed from inversion:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Ever equal to the dharmadhātu,
> All views of self entirely severed,
> Far removed from the two extremes:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Not abiding in any form,
> Neither grasping nor releasing,
> Far removed from what is contrary to dharma:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Realizing the unobstructed dharma,
> Penetrating all dharmas,
> Far removed from the dharma of Māra:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Neither existent nor non-existent,
> Existence and non-existence both unobtainable,
> Apart from all verbal expression:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Crushing the banner of pride,
> Neither one nor two,
> Far removed from one and two:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Body, speech, and mind are without fault;
> The three karmas are ever quiescent.
> Far removed from all analogy:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Omniscience is ever-abiding,
> Manifesting without any effort,
> Far removed from all faults:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Subtle and wondrous, without outflows:
> Mindfulness unlimited and undiscriminating,
> Equal toward sentient and non-sentient:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Because the mind is unobstructed,
> It knows all minds.
> Not abiding in self or other:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Unobstructed and unperceivable,
> Ever abiding in the unobstructed dharma,
> Far removed from all minds:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "The mind is ever without an object;
> Its own nature cannot be grasped.
> Equal and beyond measure:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "With a mind that depends on nothing,
> It perceives all buddha-fields
> And knows all sentient beings:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "All dharmas and omniscience
> Are ultimately without any existence.
> The Buddha's mind is beyond fathoming:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "All dharmas are like an illusion;
> Like an illusion, they are unobtainable.
> Apart from all illusory dharmas:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "The Buddha is ever in the world,
> Yet is not stained by worldly dharmas.
> Not stained by the world:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Omniscience is ever-abiding:
> Emptiness-nature, emptiness-realm;
> Speech also being empty:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Realizing the non-discriminating samādhi,
> Attaining the illusion-like samādhi,
> Sporting in supernatural powers:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Neither one nor different,
> Neither near nor far,
> Unmoved with respect to all dharmas:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "In the single-thought vajra samādhi,
> In an instant attaining perfect awakening,
> Realizing the signless beyond reflection:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "With regard to all dharmas of the three times,
> Accomplishing all skillful means,
> Unmoved from nirvāṇa:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Nirvāṇa is ever unmoved,
> Without this shore or that shore.
> Penetrating skillful means:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "Without characteristics, without existence,
> Without affliction, without idle discourse,
> Not abiding in existence or non-existence:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "The field of wisdom is perfectly equal,
> Quiescent and without discrimination.
> Self and other being one characteristic:
> I reverently bow to the unperceivable.

> "With this all-equal homage,
> Without homage and without non-homage,
> A single bow pervading all sentient beings,
> All alike return to the body of true reality."

At that time the World-Honored One praised Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Excellent, excellent! You have now skillfully proclaimed the merits of the Tathāgata, that all dharmas are originally pure. Mañjuśrī! Even if someone were to convert all beings in the great trichiliocosm and bring them to the stage of pratyekabuddhahood, it would not compare to the merit of a person who, upon hearing these merits, generates a single thought of faith and understanding; this surpasses the former by a hundred thousand ten-thousand fold. Such is its expansion, beyond all praise, analogy, or comparison." The full account is as set forth in the original sūtra.
