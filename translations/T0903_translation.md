# 都部陀羅尼目
## Comprehensive Catalog of the Dhāraṇī Divisions

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0903

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Amoghavajra (不空)

---

The root sūtra of the Yoga totals one hundred thousand verses and consists of eighteen assemblies. The sūtra of the first assembly is titled "The True Gathering of All the Tathāgatas." That sūtra teaches five divisions: the Buddha Division, the Vajra Division, the Ratna Division, the Padma Division, and the Karma Division. The lord of each of the five divisions has four bodhisattvas as retinue, arranged before, to the right, to the left, and behind. The four inner offerings each belong to four divisions; one should know their sequence. The four outer offerings also belong to four divisions. The four gate deities (hook, lasso, chain, and bell) should also be known according to the sequence of the four divisions.

Additionally, there are the sixteen great bodhisattvas of the Bhadrakalpa in the four directions, representing all the bodhisattvas of the Bhadrakalpa.

Furthermore, on the outside there are five classes of devas, each class having four devas, totaling twenty devas together with their queens.

There are again five classes making twenty more. The five classes are the four devas of the upper realm:

> Four devas dwelling in the sky, four devas roaming the sky,
> Four devas dwelling on the earth, and four devas dwelling beneath the earth.

The Yoga Division has four maṇḍalas: first, the Vajradhātu; second, Trailokyavijaya; third, Universal Subjugation; fourth, Sarvārthasiddhi. These four maṇḍalas represent the four wisdom-bodhisattvas within Vairocana Buddha: Vajra, Abhiṣeka, Padma, and Karma constitute the four wisdoms.

The four wisdoms are also: the great perfect mirror wisdom, the wisdom of equality, the wisdom of wondrous observation, and the wisdom of accomplishing action.

Furthermore, within each individual maṇḍala, six maṇḍalas are established: the great maṇḍala, the samaya maṇḍala, the dharma maṇḍala, the karma maṇḍala, the four-seal maṇḍala, and the one-seal maṇḍala. Only the Trailokyavijaya maṇḍala has a full ten maṇḍalas; the others each have six. All the mudrā-contracts and all the essential methods are completely gathered under four wisdom-seals: the great wisdom-seal, whereby one accomplishes the principal deity's yoga through the five aspects of realization; the samaya seal; the dharma wisdom-seal; and the karma wisdom-seal.

Furthermore, in the yoga there are four kinds of gaze: the blazing gaze, the wrathful gaze, and the compassionate gaze.

Furthermore, in the collected yoga teachings of all the Tathāgatas, there are one hundred and twenty kinds of homa, employing twenty-five kinds of hearths. Within the homa hearths, the mudrā-contracts and emblems each differ, and the sought-for aims are swiftly accomplished. Whether for worldly or world-transcending accomplishment and their resultant rewards, the various assemblies are vast and the textual meaning is rather extensive. Fearing prolixity, I shall only briefly indicate the general directions.

According to the Mahāvairocana Abhisaṃbodhi Sūtra, the complete text totals one hundred thousand verses, roughly three hundred fascicles. The abbreviated translation made in China comprises seven fascicles. This sūtra teaches the one hundred and sixty minds, the ten conditions of arising, and the five wheels: the earth-wheel, water-wheel, fire-wheel, wind-wheel, and space-wheel.

In this sūtra there are two kinds of practice. The mind of enlightenment serves as the cause, great compassion serves as the root, and skillful means serve as the ultimate fulfillment. It is based on two truths, ultimate and conventional. If one practices according to ultimate truth, one establishes the dharma-body maṇḍala. Therefore, within this sūtra it first describes the maṇḍala in the midst of empty space. Thus one contemplates the principal deity's dharma-body, far removed from form and shape, like empty space, and abides in such samādhi. If one practices according to conventional truth, one relies upon the four wheels to form the maṇḍala: if the principal deity is yellow, one abides in the earth-wheel maṇḍala; if the deity is white, in the water-wheel maṇḍala; if the deity is red, in the fire-wheel maṇḍala; if the deity is blue or black, in the wind-wheel maṇḍala.

The great maṇḍala is placed upon an eight-petaled lotus platform: the five Buddhas and four bodhisattvas are placed upon the platform and its petals. Outside the central maṇḍala, there are three more maṇḍalas: first, the All-Tathāgata maṇḍala; second, the Śākyamuni maṇḍala; third, the Mañjuśrī maṇḍala. This maṇḍala is called the Mahākaruṇā-garbha maṇḍala, used when a disciple receives the abhiṣeka ritual. The small maṇḍala is exceedingly subtle and intricate; the other divisions cannot substitute for it. The practice and offering within it combine two kinds of Dharma: the phenomenal and the principled.

Within this sūtra, the fire-deva homa has forty types. Among them, twelve types of fire are supreme. The hearth shapes and woods include milkwood, fruit-trees, neem, and others; the materials used each differ. The petitions for the east, west, south, and north each differ. The inner and outer homa also follow the five wheels. The four kinds of aims are swiftly accomplished: pacification of disasters, increase, subjugation, and attraction. The fire-devas invited each differ: tranquil, pleased, wrathful, and joyful-wrathful. One should know the sequence.

As for the Susiddhikara Sūtra, the teaching follows three divisions: the Buddha Division, the Padma Division (whose varieties are very many), and the Vajra Division (Vajrasattva and others, whose transformations are immeasurable).

There are three kinds of samaya: Buddha Division, Padma Division, and Vajra Division.

There are three division heart-mantras:

*Jina-jika ārulika vajra-dītā*

The division lords are of three kinds: the Golden Wheel King Uṣṇīṣa is the lord of the Buddha Division; Hayagrīva Avalokiteśvara is the lord of the Padma Division; Trailokyavijaya Vajra is the lord of the Vajra Division.

The three kinds of division-mothers: Buddha-eye is the mother of the Buddha Division; White-Robed Avalokiteśvara is the mother of the Padma Division; Māmakī Bodhisattva is the mother of the Vajra Division.

The three kinds of vidyā-consorts: Aparājitā Bodhisattva is the vidyā-consort of the Buddha Division; Tārā Bodhisattva is the vidyā-consort of the Padma Division; Vajra-Sundarī Bodhisattva is the vidyā-consort of the Vajra Division.

The three kinds of wrathful ones: Acala is the wrathful one of the Buddha Division; Āṅkuśa is the wrathful one of the Padma Division; Kuṇḍalī is the wrathful one of the Vajra Division.

There are four kinds of boundaries: vajra pegs for the earth boundary; vajra walls for the eight-direction boundary; vajra nets for the upper boundary; and the sealed-seam asamaṅginī boundary.

There are again four kinds of boundaries for the protection of the maṇḍala: vajra ropes protecting the east; vajra banners and pennants protecting the west; vajra kālī protecting the south; and vajra peaks protecting the north. The great boundary is called śaṅkara. Even if a wheel-turning king protected by the Buddha's uṣṇīṣa is nearby, there is no obstruction.

In this sūtra, the mantra practitioner accomplishes worldly siddhi by relying on the proper time and place. "Time" means the three times of day; "place" means before the image of the principal deity. There are five kinds of offering (excluding the argha): first, unguent; second, flower-garlands; third, incense; fourth, food and drink; fifth, lamplight. White, yellow, black, and red correspond to pacification, increase, subjugation, and attraction respectively. One should know the appropriate aim. The accomplished practitioner carries eighteen objects at all times, as extensively described in the sūtra.

One bathes three times daily and changes clothes three times. One month is divided into four periods: from the first to the eighth day of the new moon, one should perform pacification; from the ninth to the fifteenth, increase; from the sixteenth to the twenty-third, subjugation; from the twenty-fourth to the end of the month, attraction.

The Dhyānottara-paṭala-krama is the same as the Susiddhikara, teaching the layout of the maṇḍala and the method of marking the ground. This sūtra is extremely detailed, and it cannot be fully recorded here.

Furthermore, regarding the eight directions and the methods of protection for the sovereign's military campaigns and entourage: the five kingdoms of India deeply revere the Buddha-Dharma, and these may be transmitted to their rulers. As for the Subāhu-paripṛcchā Sūtra, this sūtra teaches the homa for those who seek accomplishment. The vajra may be of gold, silver, copper, iron, stone, crystal, khadira wood, and immeasurable other kinds. The prongs may be five, three, or one. A length of sixteen finger-breadths is superior; twelve is middling; eight is inferior. Down to one finger-joint is the minimum. This sūtra teaches that one who recites mantras without holding the vajra cannot attain accomplishment. The vajra bell embodies the meaning of prajñā-pāramitā; the vajra pestle embodies the meaning of bodhicitta. It can destroy the two extremes of eternalism and nihilism and accords with the middle way. It has the sixteen bodhisattva positions, which also represent the sixteen emptinesses as the middle way. On each side there are five prongs, which embody the meaning of the five Buddhas and five wisdoms, and also represent the ten pāramitās. They can crush the ten kinds of affliction, accomplish the ten kinds of suchness, and thereby realize the ten bhūmis. Realizing the vajra samādhi, one obtains the vajra wisdom, sits upon the vajra seat, which is also the wisdom of all wisdoms, also called the Tathāgata's self-awakened noble wisdom. If one does not cultivate this samādhi-wisdom, it is impossible to attain Buddhahood. Fearing prolixity, I cannot elaborate further. If one were to expound it in full, an entire kalpa would not suffice.

The Trī-samaya Sūtra corresponds with the assembly of Vairocana. All the holy assembly's methods of practice and teaching are of self-nature accomplishment. In the practice of this teaching, one need only abide in the mind of enlightenment, the aspiration of great compassion, and never abandon the boundless realm of sentient beings. What should not be done must not be established or consumed; if one does so, one violates the samaya. This sūtra teaches that by reciting the Mahācakra-vajra mantra, one is not stained by any transgressions. Using this as a skillful means, in this very life all mantras are swiftly accomplished. In this sūtra, Acala and other deities, the forty-two servants and messengers of the Tathāgatas, declare: "If a bodhisattva who practices the mantra path firmly upholds the mind of enlightenment, we shall serve, make offerings to, and protect that practitioner, and eat the leavings of that practitioner's food. Such a one shall attain unsurpassed enlightenment. All obstructing Vināyaka demons shall find no opportunity, and the practitioner shall swiftly realize unsurpassed enlightenment."
