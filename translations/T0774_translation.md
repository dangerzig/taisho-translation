# 大乘四法經
## The Mahāyāna Sūtra of the Four Dharmas

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0774

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Śikṣānanda (實叉難陀).

---

> Homage to the great ocean of wisdom, the Buddha Vairocana.

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's park, together with a great assembly of five hundred monks, eight thousand bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who had all donned the firm and great armor of their vows, and the heavenly sons of the desire and form realms, numbering hundreds of thousands beyond count. All reverenced the Tathāgata and listened to the essentials of the Dharma.

At that time the youth Mañjuśrī held a jeweled canopy, one hundred leagues in extent, and covered the Tathāgata with it.

There was then a Tuṣita heaven god named Good Victory, who had attained irreversibility from supreme perfect enlightenment. Together with his retinue, being present in this assembly, he said to Mañjuśrī: "Venerable one! Has your offering to the Tathāgata still not reached its fullness?"

Mañjuśrī said: "What do you think? Does the ocean ever grow weary of receiving the many streams that flow into it?"

The heavenly being said: "No, it does not!"

Mañjuśrī said: "Heavenly son! The great ocean is deep and vast, without shore or limit. Ten thousand streams return to it, and yet it never overflows or ceases. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva seeking omniscience and making offerings to the Tathāgata is just the same: never reaching satiety."

The heavenly being further asked: "When making offerings to the Buddha, what should one do?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "One should practice four things. First, for the sake of omniscience. Second, for the sake of liberating all sentient beings. Third, for the sake of preserving the lineage of the Three Treasures without interruption. Fourth, for the sake of incorporating the merit and adornment of all buddha-fields. These are the four things by which a bodhisattva makes offerings to the Tathāgata."

The heavenly being further asked: "Excellent, venerable one! A bodhisattva should never be miserly with the Dharma. Just as you formerly taught the four Dharma gates of the bodhisattva's Way to the Brahmā king Śikhin and his retinue, please now proclaim this meaning again for us and for the entire assembly."

Mañjuśrī said: "Listen carefully, listen carefully! Reflect well on this, and I shall teach you.

"Heavenly son! A bodhisattva-mahāsattva should generate four kinds of superior aspiration. What are the four? The mind that gathers in all sentient beings; the mind that brings all sentient beings to maturity; the mind that accumulates all roots of merit; the mind that awakens to all the buddha-dharmas. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva should generate four kinds of mountain-like mind. What are the four? The mind free of anger or resentment toward those who beg; the mind of compassion toward those heading for evil destinies; the mind that never abandons the perfection of wisdom; the mind that brings all practices to completion. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva should generate four kinds of ever-surpassing mind. What are the four? Ever-surpassing in upholding precepts; ever-surpassing in learning; ever-surpassing in great loving-kindness; ever-surpassing in great compassion. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva should generate four kinds of adamantine, indestructible mind. What are the four? Indestructible faith and devotion; indestructible reliance on good spiritual friends; indestructible practice; indestructible pursuit of the Mahāyāna. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva should generate four kinds of mind that cannot be defiled. What are the four? Not defiled by afflictions; not defiled by fame and profit; not defiled by lesser vehicles; not defiled by evil beings. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva should generate four kinds of unsurpassed mind. What are the four? The mind that relinquishes all that one loves; the mind that feels no regret after giving; the mind that does not hope for reward; the mind that dedicates all to awakening. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dharma that reach the summit. What are the four? Wisdom; skillful means; upholding the true Dharma; transforming sentient beings. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of aid to the path of awakening. What are the four? Diligently cultivating the perfections; according with the four means of attraction; abiding in the four sublime states; sporting in supernatural powers. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of foremost and supreme dharma. What are the four? Having no mind to harm any sentient being; when harmed by others, harboring no resentment; in the midst of the five desires, not being heedless; in poverty, hardship, and distress, not abandoning the practice of the Dharma. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of peaceful and secure mind. What are the four? When living as a householder, being content with one's own wealth; not coveting others' wealth. When having left home, depending on the four noble lineages; practicing the ascetic austerities. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of giving. What are the four? Giving of material goods; giving of the Dharma; giving of paper and brushes; giving by sincerely praising the Dharma teacher. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of firm and excellent dharma. What are the four? Being able to practice what one has heard; being able to give when possessing wealth; being able to make offerings to the respected; being able to plant roots of merit during one's lifespan. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of non-abandonment. What are the four? Not abandoning the mind of enlightenment; not abandoning the true Dharma; not abandoning any sentient being; not abandoning the pursuit of all wholesome dharmas. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of garden. What are the four? Delighting in dwelling in forest solitude; delighting in sitting alone in the open; delighting in seeking the wholesome Dharma; delighting in liberating sentient beings through skillful means. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of palace. What are the four? Delighting in abiding in the sublime states; delighting in hearing the wholesome Dharma; delighting in contemplating the emptiness of all things; delighting in dwelling together with those of like practice. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of inexhaustible wealth. What are the four? The wealth of learning; the wealth of teaching the Dharma; the wealth of providing for the destitute; the wealth of dedicating to awakening. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of hidden treasury. What are the four? The treasury of dhāraṇī; the treasury of eloquence; the treasury of the Dharma; the treasury of inexhaustible wealth dedicated to awakening. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of renunciation. What are the four? Renouncing idle talk; renouncing the objects of the five desires; renouncing the ignoble mind; renouncing the three realms. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of happiness. What are the four? The happiness of being free from self and possessions, without attachment; the happiness of not looking back at anything, of far-reaching renunciation; the happiness of being free from all objects, of tranquility; the happiness of not abandoning any sentient being yet being free from affliction: the happiness of nirvāṇa. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of joy. What are the four? Joy upon seeing the Buddha; joy upon hearing the Dharma; joy in giving without regret; joy in bestowing peace and happiness on all sentient beings. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of authenticity. What are the four? Not abandoning the mind of enlightenment; not abandoning the great vow; not abandoning those who come seeking refuge; not abandoning any word of beneficial counsel. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of wholesome dharma. What are the four? Delighting in cultivating all wholesome dharmas; not looking down on beginners; regarding all sentient beings with equal mind toward friends and foes; being an unbidden friend who benefits sentient beings without thinking of reward. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of purity. What are the four? Purity of precepts, because there is no self; purity of concentration, because there are no sentient beings; purity of wisdom, because there is no soul; purity of liberation, because there is no birth in any destiny. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of foundation. What are the four? The foundation of meaning; the foundation of the Dharma; the foundation of ascetic virtues; the foundation of accumulating the provisions for awakening. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of hand. What are the four? The hand of faith; the hand of precepts; the hand of learning; the hand of wisdom. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of eye. What are the four? The physical eye, through creating wholesome karma; the divine eye, through supernatural powers not declining; the wisdom eye, through never wearying of what is heard; the Dharma eye, through contemplating all dharmas truly and attaining patience. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of tirelessness. What are the four? Tirelessness in giving; tirelessness in dwelling in forest solitude; tirelessness in listening to the Dharma; tirelessness in cultivating goodness. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of difficult practice. What are the four? Patiently enduring all degradation and insult; though oneself in poverty, giving all one has; when someone comes begging for head, eyes, or body, the mind not refusing but regarding them as a good friend; contemplating emptiness and non-self while manifesting birth. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of freedom from illness. What are the four? Freedom from the illness of imbalanced elements; freedom from the illness of the fever of afflictions; freedom from the illness of not benefiting sentient beings; freedom from the illness of doubt and confusion about dharmas. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of self-possessed dharma. What are the four? The perfections; the factors of awakening; true and good spiritual friends; not committing any evil action. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of immovability. What are the four? The mind of enlightenment; acting in accordance with one's vows; acting in accordance with one's words; diligently cultivating right practice. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of provisions. What are the four? Calm abiding; insight; hearing all teachings; roots of merit. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of paired practice. What are the four? Generating aspiration and undertaking practice; giving and dedication; great loving-kindness and great compassion; wisdom and skillful means. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate obstacles to the Dharma. What are the four? Dreaming that the moon falls onto flat ground into a well; dreaming that the moon appears in a turbid spring pool; dreaming that the moon is in the sky covered by great clouds; dreaming that the moon is in the sky obscured by smoke and dust. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate obstacles to karma. What are the four? Dreaming of falling into a place of great danger; dreaming of a road that goes up and down; dreaming of a winding and curved road; dreaming of losing one's way in fright. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate obstacles from afflictions. What are the four? Dreaming of being disturbed by poisonous snakes; dreaming of horrible sounds from a herd of beasts; dreaming of falling into a dangerous place infested with bandits; dreaming that one's body is covered in dust and grime. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate attaining dhāraṇī. What are the four? Dreaming of a great hidden treasury filled with various treasures; dreaming of a clear pool in which many flowers bloom simultaneously; dreaming of obtaining a pair of pure white cotton cloths; dreaming of heavenly beings holding canopies overhead. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate attaining samādhi. What are the four? Dreaming that a beautiful maiden adorned with precious jewels offers flowers; dreaming of white geese flying in formation, circling in the sky; dreaming that the Tathāgata places his hand upon one's head; dreaming that the Tathāgata sits on a lotus seat and enters samādhi. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate seeing the Tathāgata. What are the four? Dreaming that the moon appears; dreaming that the sun appears; dreaming that a lotus flower opens; dreaming that the Great Brahmā King appears in tranquil dignity. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate obtaining the marks of a great being. What are the four? Dreaming of a śāla tree full of wondrous flowers and fruit; dreaming of a great bronze vessel brimming with precious jewels; dreaming of the sky adorned with banners and canopies; dreaming of a wheel-turning king ruling the world through the Dharma. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate non-retrogression. What are the four? Dreaming that a white silk band is tied around one's head; dreaming that one has set up an assembly of unrestricted giving; dreaming that one's body is seated on a Dharma seat; dreaming that the Buddha sits at the seat of enlightenment, teaching the Dharma for the assembly. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate vanquishing the army of Māra. What are the four? Dreaming of a great strongman overpowering a lesser one and carrying the victory banner away; dreaming of a great and brave general who conquers in battle and departs; dreaming that one receives the consecrated kingship; dreaming that one sits beneath the Bodhi tree and vanquishes the hosts of Māra. These are the four.

"Furthermore, a bodhisattva possesses four kinds of dream that indicate sitting at the seat of enlightenment. What are the four? Dreaming that an auspicious vase is full; dreaming that a multitude circumambulates one's body to the right; dreaming that wherever one goes, the trees bow their branches; dreaming that golden light shines everywhere. These are the four."

When Mañjuśrī had taught this Dharma, the heavenly being Good Victory and his retinue leapt with joy and offered heavenly māndārava flowers, padma flowers, kumuda flowers, and puṇḍarīka flowers to Mañjuśrī, scattering them also upon the entire assembly. Through the Buddha's supernatural power, the scattered flowers rose into the sky and became great lotuses, each as large as a cart-wheel, exquisitely fragrant and pure, delighting the hearts of all. Upon the petal-thrones sat bodhisattvas, their bodies adorned with the thirty-two marks.

At that time the heavenly being Good Victory said to Mañjuśrī: "From where have these bodhisattvas come?"

Mañjuśrī answered: "They come from the same place as the flowers."

The heavenly being said: "These flowers are transformation-born and come from nowhere."

Mañjuśrī said again: "You should know that those bodhisattvas are likewise!"

At that time the World-Honored One smiled, and from his mouth radiated lights of various colors: blue, yellow, red, white, and crystal, illuminating the immeasurable lands of the ten directions, reaching up to the Brahmā world, outshining the light of sun and moon, then returning to enter through the crown of his head. The heavenly being Good Victory then rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and praised him in verse:

> "Pure golden light, the wondrous thirty-two marks,
> Endowed with billions of nayutas of incomparable, supreme merits.
> Savior of the world: why do you manifest this smile?
> The Brahmā voice, deep and far-reaching, the words pure, subtle, and wondrous.
> Ever treasuring the seven kinds of noble wealth, radiating the great light of wisdom.
> With a voice like the kalaviṅka bird: we pray you will explain the meaning of this smile.
> World-hero, great champion, vanquisher of Māra and heterodox paths,
> Always honored and served by heavenly beings, humans, asuras, and others.
> Possessor of the ten powers: why do you manifest this smile?
> All greed, anger, delusion, defilements, and obstructions to wisdom
> Are forever severed without residual tendencies. Your face is like the pure full moon.
> Bestower of universal peace and happiness: we pray you will explain the meaning of this smile.
> Well-Gone One, Teacher of gods and humans, aggregate of immeasurable merits,
> Whose mind is always equanimous, who has fully practiced all good deeds.
> Liberator of all: why do you manifest this smile?
> Ever holding the great lamp of wisdom, dispelling the dense forest of darkness,
> Walking with the gait of a bull king, fearless as a lion.
> Benefactor of all sentient beings: we pray you will explain the meaning of this smile.
> Most honored among humans, rarely seen and hard to fathom,
> Without self and without deception, transcending the ocean of all existences.
> Sovereign of wisdom and power: why do you manifest this smile?
> Thousand-spoked, web-toed feet, matchless in the three realms,
> Drying up the flood of birth and death, severing the net of ignorance.
> Excellent, O great one of wisdom: we pray you will explain the meaning of this smile."

At that time the World-Honored One addressed the heavenly being Good Victory: "Do you see the bodhisattvas upon the lotus seats in the sky?"

The heavenly being said: "Indeed, I have seen them!"

The Buddha said: "All these bodhisattvas were transformed and led by Mañjuśrī. Having come from the ten directions to hear these four Dharma gates, they all abide at the stage of one-birth supplement, and in the lands of the ten directions they shall each attain supreme perfect enlightenment, each under a different name."

The heavenly being said: "World-Honored One! Can anyone calculate and know their number?"

At that time the World-Honored One asked Śāriputra: "Can you know?"

Śāriputra said: "World-Honored One! I can, in the space of a single thought, know all the stars in the entire great thousandfold world-system. But even in a hundred years I could not calculate and know the number of these bodhisattvas!"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Even if atoms of dust filled all of Jambudvīpa, they could be counted and known. But the number of these bodhisattvas is beyond the limits of knowledge."

Śāriputra said: "Where could there be so many buddha-fields to accommodate these bodhisattvas attaining perfect enlightenment?"

The Buddha said: "Say no more! Do not speak thus! The worlds among all world-systems that are empty and without a buddha are immeasurable and boundless.

"Śāriputra! Suppose the Tathāgata were to abide in the world for as many kalpas as grains of sand in the Ganges, and each day were to teach as many dharmas as grains of sand in the Ganges, and in teaching each dharma were to bestow upon as many bodhisattvas as grains of sand in the Ganges the prediction of supreme perfect enlightenment; and suppose that for every such prediction, one would have to pass through as many buddha-fields to the east as grains of sand in the Ganges before a single bodhisattva attained buddhahood in one of them: even so, the buddha-less worlds to the east could not be exhausted. How much less the worlds in all ten directions that are empty and without a buddha?

"Śāriputra! All these immeasurable worlds are what the Tathāgata's physical eye can see. All the sentient beings within them are what the Tathāgata's mind can know."

At that time the great śrāvakas and the entire assembly felt wonder in their hearts and said: "Today we have all gained a great benefit, having met such a teacher endowed with vast merit and wisdom!" Then the bodhisattvas who had come through the sky all descended from the air, prostrated before the Tathāgata and Mañjuśrī, reverently circumambulated them to the right, and each returned to their own land.

At that time the heavenly being Good Victory said to Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, venerable one! You do the Buddha's work, having been able to transform these immeasurable bodhisattvas and establish them in great enlightenment. Please teach me the dharmas that accomplish their abiding in awakening."

Mañjuśrī said: "Heavenly son! There are thirty-five dharmas that accomplish a bodhisattva's abiding in awakening. They are: one should always act in a timely manner without missing the right occasion. One should rouse and discipline the sense faculties. One should gather the mind without wavering. One should cultivate the perfections. One should follow skillful means. One should generate a superior aspiration. One should establish great loving-kindness. One should give rise to great compassion. One should not abandon the Mahāyāna. One should keep far from the lesser vehicles. One should always be truthful. One should act in accordance with truth. One should protect the true Dharma. One should practice in accordance with what one has heard. One should understand that the nature of sentient beings is equal and non-dual. One should regard those who break precepts and those who keep precepts alike as fields of merit. One should be aware of all the workings of Māra. One should fulfill the great vow. One should not grow weary of birth and death. One should vanquish the hosts of Māra. One should know kindness and repay it. One should enter the dharmas that cause cessation. One should not be frightened by the gates of liberation. One should make offerings to all buddhas. One should do whatever sentient beings require. One should not be tainted by worldly dharmas. One should delight in forest solitude. One should practice having few desires. One should be mindful of contentment. One should cause those not yet liberated to be liberated. One should cause those not yet understanding to understand. One should cause those not yet at peace to be at peace. One should cause those not yet in nirvāṇa to enter nirvāṇa. One should preserve the lineage of the Three Treasures without interruption. One should incorporate the merit and adornment of all buddhas' pure lands. These are the thirty-five dharmas that accomplish a bodhisattva's abiding in awakening. One should study them thus.

"Furthermore, heavenly son! A bodhisattva should be free from ten kinds of pride. They are: pride in self; pride in learning; pride in eloquence; pride in gain, fame, and renown; pride in dwelling in forest solitude; pride in ascetic virtues; pride in wealth, status, and retinue; pride in being served by Śakra, Brahmā, and the world-protectors; pride in dhyāna and supernatural powers; pride arising from receiving the unshakable faith of the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha, together with the reverence and praise of devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas. If a bodhisattva can be free from these ten kinds of pride, then one will not retrogress from supreme perfect enlightenment and will not be damaged or defeated by any heterodox path or māra-enemy."

The heavenly being Good Victory said to Mañjuśrī: "Wherever the venerable one abides, one should know that this Dharma gate is present there. It is as if the Tathāgata had appeared in that place and turned the wheel of the true Dharma."

The Buddha said: "Just so, just so! It is as you have said. In whatever land Mañjuśrī teaches this Dharma gate, it is as if the Dharma King abides therein. If any sentient being practices this Dharma, that person is a true child of the Buddha. If anyone can believe, understand, receive, and uphold this Dharma, that person is called a true practitioner. Such a person has already been tamed by the Buddha and will not retrogress from supreme perfect enlightenment."

At that time the World-Honored One addressed the Bodhisattva Maitreya, Kāśyapa, and Ānanda: "Good men! I now entrust this Dharma gate to you. You should receive, uphold, read, recite, practice it as taught, and explain it widely for others. After my nirvāṇa, you should use this Dharma to do great buddha-work, enabling all sentient beings to attain peace and happiness."

Maitreya said: "So it shall be! We receive the teaching! World-Honored One, what should this sūtra be called? How should it be upheld?"

The Buddha told Maitreya: "This sūtra is called 'Teaching the Four Dharma Gates,' and is also called 'Accomplishing the Bodhisattva's Way.' Therefore you should all receive and uphold it together."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Maitreya, the Elder Mahākāśyapa, the Elder Ānanda, and all the worldly beings, heavenly beings, humans, and asuras, hearing what the Buddha had taught, joyfully received and practiced it.
