## Fascicle 1

*Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on Times Ancient and Future*

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at Ṛṣipatana, the Deer Park of the Sages, in Vārāṇasī. At that time, after the bhikṣus had finished their meal, they assembled in the lecture hall and discussed among themselves: "Suppose there is a householder of upright and equitable conduct, and a bhikṣu who upholds the pure precepts and practices the true precepts enters his home and receives alms from him. If one were instead to obtain a hundred catties of gold, which would be superior?" Some bhikṣus replied: "What benefit are a hundred or a thousand catties of gold? Think it through carefully. To have a bhikṣu who upholds the precepts and cultivates what is right and true receive one's alms—thinking of that householder, his merit is the highest."

At that time the Venerable Aniruddha was present in the assembly. Hearing these words of the Dharma, he replied: "How much more than hundreds or thousands! Even treasures without limit could not compare to a householder's offering of a meal to a bhikṣu of true precepts. Why is this? I recall that in a past life, in the land of Vārāṇasī, when grain was expensive and the people were suffering from famine, I carried grass to sell in order to support myself. There was a pratyekabuddha named Hali who had come to sojourn in that land. I left the city early, wishing to shoulder a load of grass. At that time the pratyekabuddha had put on his robe and taken up his bowl to enter the city for alms. On my way back, carrying my grass, I met him again at the city gate—and he was leaving with an empty bowl. The pratyekabuddha Hali saw me coming from afar and thought to himself: 'I entered the city early and this man was leaving; now he returns carrying grass. I imagine he has not yet eaten this morning—I should follow behind him to his home and beg, so that my hunger may be eased.' At that time I carried my grass back to my own dwelling, set it down on the ground, and looking back, I saw the pratyekabuddha following behind me like a shadow. I thought to myself: 'When I left the city this morning, I saw this pratyekabuddha entering the city to receive alms, yet he has returned with an empty bowl—he must have received no food. I should give up my own meal and offer it to him.' I immediately took out my food, knelt on both knees, and offered it to him, saying: 'May your body be at ease and well-provided. I pray that the venerable practitioner of the Way will take compassion and accept this.' The pratyekabuddha said: 'Grain is expensive and the people are weak with hunger—divide it into two portions: put one in the bowl and keep one for yourself to eat. That would accord with the Dharma.' I replied: 'Indeed, holy one! We who are white-robed and live as householders have cooking vessels and utensils—we can cook again at our leisure, whether early or late, and there is no concern. Venerable one, please accept all of it out of compassion for this household.' At that time the pratyekabuddha accepted the entire meal. Through this virtuous deed, I was reborn in the heavens seven times as king among the gods, and seven times in the human world I was honored among people. Through this single act of generosity, I have been reverenced and served by various kings, householders, the common people, ministers, and officials; the four assemblies of disciples—bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, laymen, and laywomen—have provided me with robes and garments, food and drink, bedding and furnishings, and medicine for illness, all coming to me of their own accord, while I sought nothing for myself. At birth I entered a household of the Śākya lineage, and treasure-stores sprang forth—gold, silver, and jewels beyond calculation, along with other wealth without measure. I renounced the household and worldly pursuits to go forth and practice as a śramaṇa. If at that time I had known that the mendicant was a pratyekabuddha who had completed the path, and had expanded my mind in accordance with that understanding, the merit would have been immeasurable."

Thereupon he spoke these verses:

> I once carried grass as a laborer,
> poor, hired out to live.
> I made an offering to the śramaṇa
> known as Hali the pratyekabuddha.
>
> Through this I was born of the Śākya lineage,
> named Aniruddha.
> I became accomplished in singing and dancing,
> in drum, lute, zither, and flute.
>
> Then I beheld the Teacher,
> the Perfectly Enlightened One, surpassing amṛta—
> my heart gave rise to joy,
> and I left home to become a śramaṇa.
>
> I came to know my former lives,
> the experiences traversed in past existences.
> In the Trāyastriṃśa heaven
> I enjoyed blessedness seven times.
>
> Seven times there and seven times here—
> fourteen in all, from first to last.
> In the heavens and in the human world,
> I never once descended to an evil path.
>
> I came to know the comings and goings of beings,
> the destinations of birth and death.
> Though dwelling in those pleasures,
> they are not as sweet as the noble path.
>
> Through the five grades of meditative concentration,
> serene and unified in one-pointedness,
> washing away the fetters and manifold defilements,
> the eye of the path beholds purity.
>
> This is why I left the home life—
> remaining a householder impairs one's practice.
> My vow has now been fulfilled,
> fully accomplished within the Buddha's teaching.
>
> I take no delight in birth,
> nor do I seek death;
> from the very first I have not chosen the time,
> but serenely settled my resolve.
>
> Among the bamboo trees at Vaiśālī
> my life will come to its end;
> beneath those bamboo trees
> I shall pass into nirvāṇa, free from all āsravas.

At that time the World-Honored One, through his divine hearing that penetrated afar, had been listening as the bhikṣu Aniruddha spoke to the assembled bhikṣus of his past lives, recounting the fruition of virtuous merit from the experiences of former existences. The Buddha emerged from his meditative chamber, went to the lecture hall, and sat before the bhikṣus. He addressed the bhikṣus: "What have you assembled here to discuss?" The bhikṣus said: "We have all gathered together, each speaking of the respective destinations of sin and merit, good and evil. The Venerable Aniruddha has been speaking of the original sources of virtue that arose from his past lives." The Buddha said to the bhikṣus: "You have spoken of the affairs of past ages—do you now wish to hear the Tathāgata speak of the basis of the ages to come?" The bhikṣus replied: "Yes, World-Honored One! Now is indeed the right time—please expound the Dharma of the coming age for the bhikṣus; having heard it, we shall uphold it." The Buddha said: "Listen well, reflect carefully, and bear it in mind." "Yes, World-Honored One! We joyfully wish to hear."

The Buddha said: "In the age to come, humans will have long lives, reaching eighty thousand years. The people of Jambudvīpa will thrive and flourish, the five grains will be plentiful and inexpensive, and the settlements will be so close that the crowing of roosters can be heard from one to the next. Women will not take husbands until they are five hundred years old. There will be only three ailments: old age, the need for bodily relief, and desire. At that time there will be a king named Śaṅkha, lord of the four continents, a wheel-turning sage-king ruling by the true Dharma. The seven treasures will arise of themselves—the golden wheel, the white elephant, the dark-blue horse, the luminous jewel, the jewel-woman consort, the treasurer-minister, and the military-minister—and the king will have a thousand sons, courageous and powerful, of surpassing physical excellence, who will subjugate opposing armies. Ruling the four continents, he will apply neither whip nor rod, and wield no blade, but govern by the true Dharma, and the people will dwell in peace. The king will have four carriages, all fashioned from the seven treasures; each wheel has a thousand spokes, and the carriages rise thirty-two zhang in height, magnificent and majestic in their radiance. From atop them, with banners raised aloft, he will practice universal generosity toward all beings—offering food, clothing, bedding, carriages, incense, flowers, and lamps to śramaṇas, practitioners of the Way, and the poor. When the distribution of gifts is complete, with the faith of a householder he will relinquish his kingdom and his throne, leave the household life, study the Way, and go forth as a śramaṇa. At that time, sons of good family, inspired to aspire to the Way, will shave their beards and hair, don the Dharma-robe, attain the unsurpassed, purely cultivate the brahmacarya, and bring the Buddha's teaching to its completion. In this very life they will spontaneously achieve the six supernatural powers, sever birth and death, complete all that is to be done, and comprehend the basis of nāmarūpa."

At that time a Venerable bhikṣu in the assembly rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, knelt on both knees, joined his palms, and addressed the World-Honored One: "In the coming age, will I become King Śaṅkha—lord of the four continents, with the seven treasures arising of themselves and a thousand sons, ruling by the true Dharma and giving generously to all, and then leaving the household to study the Way and attain the wisdom of non-attachment?" The World-Honored One sharply rebuked the bhikṣu: "Foolish one! You should complete the path in this very life, yet instead you seek to revolve further in birth and death, saying that in the coming age you will be a wheel-turning sage-king, coveting the seven treasures, with a thousand courageous sons—and only then enter the path?" Then the Buddha told the bhikṣu: "In the coming age you will indeed become King Śaṅkha, lord of the four continents; you will give generously to all, leave the household, and attain the path."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "In the coming age, when human life spans have increased to eighty thousand years, there will be a World-Honored One named Maitreya—Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly and Fully Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Persons to Be Tamed, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One—even as I am today. The gods and humans of heaven and earth, together with Brahmā and Śakra, śramaṇas and brahmins, will all submit, take refuge, and receive his teaching of the path. He will teach the Dharma universally to the highest, the middling, and the lowest; distinguishing its meaning with clarity, he will cultivate the pure brahmacarya and widely spread the transforming influence of the Way—just as I do today. His pure teaching will flow and spread throughout heaven and earth, and there will be none who do not receive and uphold it; his assembly of bhikṣus will number in incalculable thousands."

At that time the Venerable Maitreya was present in the assembly. He rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, knelt on both knees, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! When human life reaches eighty thousand years in the age to come, will I indeed become Maitreya Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly and Fully Enlightened, and transform the gods and humans of heaven and earth—as the Buddha does today?" The World-Honored One praised Maitreya, saying: "Excellent, excellent! You have given rise to a compassion that is gentle, vast, and all-encompassing, wishing to rescue beings beyond number and without limit; you have brought forth this aspiration, wishing to serve as guide and herald for all in the age to come—even as I do today. In the coming age you shall indeed attain buddhahood, bearing the name Maitreya—Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly and Fully Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Persons to Be Tamed, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One."

At that time the Venerable Ānanda was attending the Buddha, holding a fan. The Buddha told Ānanda: "Bring the golden-threaded woven robe; I wish to bestow it upon the bhikṣu Maitreya." Ānanda received the instruction, went and fetched it, and offered it to the World-Honored One. The World-Honored One received it and gave it to Maitreya, saying: "Take this Dharma-robe and present it to the saṃgha. Why? The Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly and Fully Enlightened One brings great benefit to beings in the world and saves them through the highest virtue." Maitreya thereupon presented it to the saṃgha.

At that time Māra Pāpīyas thought to himself: "The śramaṇa Gautama is speaking to the bhikṣus about the coming age—I now wish to go and disturb his teaching of the Dharma." Māra immediately went before the World-Honored One and recited these verses:

> I picture the people of that time—
> bodies supple, hair lustrous and fine,
> their persons adorned with garlands of precious jewels,
> heads crowned with ornaments of pearl and flower.

The World-Honored One said: "Māra Pāpīyas has come here deliberately to disturb the teaching of the path." The Buddha replied to Māra in verse:

> The people of that time
> will be free from attachment, their doubts utterly severed,
> having removed the net of birth and death,
> their work accomplished, free from all outflows—
> under the teaching of Buddha Maitreya,
> they will purely cultivate the brahmacarya.

Māra then replied to the World-Honored One in verse:

> I picture the people of that time—
> clothed in bright and ornate garments,
> their bodies anointed with candana,
> adorning both body and head,
> in the city of Ketumati,
> the domain governed by King Śaṅkha.

The World-Honored One replied to Māra in verse:

> The people of that time will be utterly sincere,
> without self and without clinging,
> with no use for precious or rare objects,
> their minds free from greed and attachment—
> in the age of Maitreya,
> they will purely cultivate the brahmacarya.

Māra then replied to the Buddha in verse:

> I picture the people of that time—
> greedy for treasures, fond of food and drink,
> skilled in singing and dancing,
> delighting only in drum, lute, and zither,
> in Ketumati,
> in the domain of King Śaṅkha.

The Buddha replied to Māra in verse:

> Those people will have crossed to the boundless shore,
> having broken the net, with nothing to bind them,
> practicing dhyāna with equanimity,
> joyful and free from all attachment.
> Māra Pāpīyas, know this well:
> you have been cast to the ground.

At that time Māra Pāpīyas thought to himself: "The Tathāgata is holy and divine; he has already perceived my abiding, arising, and ceasing." Grieved and joyless, he departed in shame.

When the Buddha had spoken thus, all present rejoiced without exception.
