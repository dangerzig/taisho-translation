# 佛入涅槃密迹金剛力士哀戀經
## Sūtra of the Secret-Trace Vajra Warrior's Grief and Longing at the Buddha's Entry into Nirvāṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0394

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

The World-Honored Sage was in the city of Kuśinagara, in the śāla grove, lying with his head to the north. He had just entered parinirvāṇa.

At that time, the Secret-Trace Vajra Warrior, seeing the Buddha's passing, was in anguish and grief. He spoke thus: "The World-Honored One was endowed with the supreme, unsurpassed ten powers. How is it that today he has been overcome by the frail, impermanent, enfeebled force of mortality? The Tathāgata has abandoned me and entered quiescence. From this day forward, I have no refuge, no support, no shelter, no protection. Decline, affliction, and calamity have all descended at once. The poisoned arrows of grief have pierced deep into my heart." Having spoken thus, the Secret-Trace Vajra Warrior, longing for the World-Honored One, was consumed by ever fiercer fires of sorrow. His five organs were torn apart, his heart and spine ground to pieces. He fell writhing to the ground in a faint, like a cliff collapsing, and lay there a long while before reviving. He sat up, weeping and choking with sobs, and said through his tears: "Alas! Alas! How monstrous is this Death-Māra! The accumulated ocean of immeasurable merit and pāramitās has been destroyed by this Death-Māra."

He spoke again: "I beg you, True Refuge, please arise for my sake! I am now wretched and bereft, with no place to depend upon. How can the World-Honored One abandon me and enter quiescence alone? From now on, I am forever separated from that compassionate countenance. The World-Honored One is at peace; the deeds of body, speech, and mind can no longer be beheld. Never again shall I see the Buddha Bhagavān enter the Buddha's abode.

"When the Tathāgata formerly entered the samādhi of the Buddha's abode, his awesome radiance and virtue exceeded their usual splendor. The Buddha's face was as fresh and radiant as a lotus newly opened, like the morning sun's first light upon the dawn. Such a peerless face can never again be seen. The Tathāgata, when he stood amid the great assembly and sent forth his great thunderous voice, his wondrous subtle sound, can never again be heard: his speech of sincerity without duplicity, speech free from error, speech without flattery or deception, speech easy to understand, speech beloved by all. In all the world, for extinguishing evils and arriving at the city of sweet dew, nothing surpasses the Buddha-Dharma. Alas! The True Refuge has entered nirvāṇa forever, leaving sentient beings without rescue or protection, wandering in the vast wilderness of birth and death; moreover, without eyes, without a guide, who will show them the way?

"The Tathāgata's dense clouds could rain sweet dew, but now they have been blown away by the wind of impermanence. All sentient beings are burned by the fire of craving, yet now the Buddha has entered nirvāṇa: who will rain the Dharma-rain to extinguish their fire of craving? The Tathāgata has now extinguished the conditioned and attained the unsurpassed path, becoming the great Medicine King for all sentient beings. All the worlds are afflicted with the illness of defilements. Now that he has entered nirvāṇa, who will have compassion and transform them with the correct path, curing the disease of sentient beings' bondage? The Tathāgata, the World-Honored One, is known as one who recognizes kindness and remembers kindness. From the time I was in the womb, I have followed the Tathāgata as a shadow follows a form, harmonious and devoted, never failing in my duty. How is it that he was not moved by my utmost sincerity, and instead abandoned me like one who repays kindness with ingratitude?

"Alas, how strange! How great is this suffering! This vajra-club: whom shall it now protect?" He then threw it away. "From now on, whom shall I attend? Who will kindly instruct and admonish me? When will I again behold that honored countenance? The King who guards the world dispatched me to come and protect the Buddha for the sake of revealing the sweet dew. How can it be that today he has so abruptly abandoned us and entered nirvāṇa? The life I have depends entirely upon the Buddha. Now that he has suddenly abandoned me, upon whom shall I depend to sustain this life? Alas, True Refuge! You had compassion for all, always teaching the wondrous Dharma to illuminate the deluded and ignorant. Why is it that today you are suddenly silent?

"The Tathāgata's all-knowing, all-seeing wisdom surpasses everything. He constantly wished to benefit all sentient beings with whom he has connections. Where has he gone today? Today he has closed himself in silence and no longer rescues and aids those he was transforming. The māras and evil people, seeing the Buddha's nirvāṇa, all greatly rejoice. The Tathāgata, the World-Honored One, was the great ship-master in the sea of birth and death, yet today he has forever abandoned the work of ferrying beings across. These sentient beings, through immeasurable kalpas, have flowed along with the current of birth and death. Only the Tathāgata could, through the correct path, cause all sentient beings to reverse the current. The Tathāgata, the World-Honored One, has forever severed afflictions. For the deluded and ignorant sentient beings he was the great illumination. Today, entering nirvāṇa, the beings of the world will see their darkness increase, forever covered by ignorance."

The Vajra Secret-Trace cried out in grief and spoke again: "The World-Honored One's thirty-two marks, the marks of a hundred-fold merit great person, were all fully complete. How can they now be destroyed and forever unseen?

"Alas, you who vanquished the māras! Alas, you who turned the Dharma-wheel! Alas, you who extinguished the firefly-light of all non-Buddhist teachers! Alas, you who could destroy the body of existence! Alas, city of all wisdoms! Alas, the Dharma-lamp has been blown out by the wind of impermanence! Alas, the Dharma-moon has been swallowed by the Rāhu of death!"

He spoke again: "Great Tranquil True Refuge! I beg you to speak to me. Where have you gone today? To which direction? To which country? Have you gone to Śrāvastī, or to Rājagṛha, or to Kapilavastu, or to Vārāṇasī? In which of these countries do you now dwell? In which grove are you now? In the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove, the Āmra Grove, the Jeta Grove? In which of these groves are you now? On which mountain? On Mount Svagata, Mount Vaidehī, or Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa? On which of these mountains are you now? I beg you to tell me where you truly are.

"The eight divisions of beings, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas, seeing that I always follow the Buddha, should they ask me: 'Where has the Buddha gone?' what answer can I give them?

"In the past, when the World-Honored One was teaching and transforming sentient beings, if he was slightly weary, when he briefly rested, his mind remained focused on radiance for the benefit of sentient beings. How can it be that today he has abandoned everything and forever entered nirvāṇa, never again to bring benefit?

"True Refuge! I beg you to arise for my sake! The fire of my grief blazes in my chest. My life will not long endure. I beg you to grant me a single word, like cool water to extinguish my blazing fire. I am now stung by the poisonous serpent of anguish and suffering. I beg you to grant me the agada medicine of the Dharma to remove my poison. The poisoned arrows of grief have pierced deep into my heart. I beg you to grant me the tongs of your words and pull them out for me.

"For all sentient beings suffering the pain of separation from loved ones, the Tathāgata constantly taught the Dharma in various ways to remove their suffering. Why alone do you not have compassion for me, and extinguish this manifold suffering for me?

"As I am now, grief has clouded and blocked me. I cannot employ reason to free and cure myself of this inner malady and all these disconsolate words. How is it that the World-Honored One does not console me? My devoted heart is never weary. I delight in seeing the compassionate countenance without satiation. I throw myself to the ground, begging for a single glance. How can the World-Honored One not have pity on me? O great Bull among those who have severed the bonds, you who always held me close: why do you not take me with you into nirvāṇa, leaving me alone and abandoned? Having lost the Tathāgata, the suffering that assails me is immeasurable and boundless, and moreover I have not yet realized the truth. Why do you alone abandon me and enter nirvāṇa?

"Alas! Alas! Has the Tathāgata fallen asleep, never to rise again? Has the Tathāgata departed, never to return? Like a lamp extinguished, never to shine again; like a jeweled tower collapsed, never to be rebuilt; like a jeweled treasury sunk, never to resurface."

He raised his hands and cried out to the sky, weeping aloud. Like Indra's banner when its supporting ropes break, he fell to the ground and could not rise, rolling and wailing. His heart, liver, throat, lips, and tongue all dried out. Dazed and distracted, he collapsed, and after a long time recovered. Lovingly gazing upon the Tathāgata's body of merit, he seized the Buddha's wheel-marked feet and held them tight, refusing to let go, saying: "The Tathāgata's feet are like utpala flowers, like the rising sun, pure and soft. Feet firmly planted, feet with thousand-spoked wheels: even the most skilled artisan could not paint them. Though a cakravartin holy king also possesses these marks, the marks are not clearly visible. The Tathāgata's wheel-marks, with hubs and spokes complete, are brilliantly manifest. His toes are long and slender, close together, not sparse, not crowded. His nails are reddish and lustrous like red copper. His hands and feet are webbed like the feet of a king of geese. His flesh is full without visible tendons, veins, or wrinkled skin. Heavenly kings, human kings, all ghost and spirit kings, and nāga kings all, with heavenly crowns, bowed at the Buddha's feet. For the sake of transforming all those with karmic connections, with his wheel-marked feet he traversed the entire world. Yet now these feet shall never be of use again.

"In days past, my heart was always filled with joy. Now, with this sudden nirvāṇa, I shall never again know joy. This impermanence is the greatest of evils, able to destroy an inconceivable, immeasurable body of merit.

"The Tathāgata's awesome presence could cause all who beheld him to feel body and mind rejoicing. Immeasurable power of merit sustained the Tathāgata's body. Yet the power of impermanence is truly the greatest of all, able to bring the Tathāgata to the place of death. The Tathāgata's power from his parents' nourishment, the power of dhyāna concentration, the power of wisdom, the power of supernatural abilities: all these powers could not rescue him from the power of impermanence.

"In the past, Ānanda urged the World-Honored One to remain in the world for a full kalpa. Why did the Tathāgata not accept that request? Had not the True Refuge, throughout three incalculable kalpas, performed a hundred thousand austerities, relinquishing whatever was difficult to relinquish, and through countless kalpas attended upon the Buddhas with offerings and service, seeking omniscience in order to deliver sentient beings? In but a short time, having delivered only a few, he enters nirvāṇa. When the Tathāgata was formerly a bodhisattva, teaching and transforming sentient beings, he was never weary. Can he be weary now?

"The sentient beings of the turbid Dharma are like newborn calves filling twelve yojanas. How can you cut off their milk and abandon them? I beg you to arise and give the calves of the turbid Dharma their fill of sweet milk.

"At this time, Śakra and his millions of devas have come to ask questions. Why do you not resolve their difficulties for them? The Lord of the Thousand Worlds, Brahmā, with palms joined, requests the Dharma. Why do you not today speak the essentials of the Dharma to satisfy his wish? Vaiśravaṇa with his millions of yakṣas surrounding him; Dhṛtarāṣṭra with his gandharva host surrounding him; Virūḍhaka with his kumbhāṇḍa host surrounding him; Virūpākṣa with his nāga host surrounding him: all such multitudes have come here to drink the sweet dew of the Dharma. Why does the Tathāgata not use good medicine to cure the ailing? The non-Buddhist multitudes slander and defame the Dharma: why do you not swiftly arise and destroy their wrong doctrines? The lord of the desire realm causes destruction everywhere: why do you not subdue him? Tathāgata! The śrāvakas are deficient in wisdom, not diligent in recitation, and weary of the vast texts: why do you not swiftly arise and teach them the essentials in brief so they may know the correct path? Moreover, Ānanda, who is the Buddha's own kinsman and has attended the World-Honored One, has not yet severed the bonds at their root. Why do you not instruct him so that he may exhaust all bonds?

"Alas, how strange! A person of such solid, great merit, destroyed in a single day! This impermanence is like a treasury-guarding elephant that destroys without count. This treasury-guarding elephant is as large as a mountain, and the Tathāgata in the past was able to tame such a great elephant. How is it that today he is conversely tamed by impermanence, even unto total destruction? Like the nāga Āpalāla, who could devastate Magadha, raising great clouds and thunder, blazing lightning, raining great hail, crushing and breaking the trees: the Tathāgata was able to subdue that great and powerful nāga. Yet today he is conversely mastered by impermanence. Like the wild-field ghost Aṅgulimāla, who slaughtered and murdered all, emptying the country: the Tathāgata was able to tame him and cause him to receive and uphold the precepts. Yet today the Tathāgata has fallen into the snare of impermanence, destroyed without remainder. Like Uruvilvā-Kāśyapa, who was attached to self-views, submerged in the tangled forest of wrong views and difficult to extricate: the Tathāgata, with compassion, manifested eighteen kinds of supernatural transformations and was able to tame him. Yet today he is toppled by impermanence.

"All sentient beings are scant in blessings. The great ocean of wisdom has been dried up by the sun of impermanence. The Mount Sumeru of right wisdom has been shattered by the vajra-club of impermanence. The tree of the Buddha's merit, with its wondrous flowers of the factors of enlightenment and its full fruits of the path, has been chopped down by the axe of impermanence. The vast and great light of wisdom, whose fame is heard throughout the world and which could burn the fuel of all existences, has been doused by the water of impermanence. The mighty power of impermanence has no Dharma teaching that can restrain it. It is not something that the wise, the diligent, the valiant, the powerful, the renowned, the gentle, or those with controlled faculties and settled minds can escape. Alas, impermanence! So cruel and violent, making no distinction between good and evil, destroying those with merit and those without alike."

When he spoke thus, the great earth shook, mountain peaks crumbled and collapsed, great meteors fell, fires arose in the four directions, and the sun, moon, and stars all lost their light. All devas and humans were without joy.

"My body can no longer support itself. I am about to sink into the earth. Dazed and faint, my complexion turns yellow and black. My mind is disordered, my thoughts confused. My lips are parched, my tongue dry. My words come out wrong, my voice cracked and broken. Death is not far off. My life will surely end now. The Buddha has abandoned me and departed."

With these and many other hundreds of thousands of words of grief, in various ways crying out, he longed for the Buddha.

Śakra then spoke to him: "Enough! Enough! Have you already forgotten the few words of the great sage? The Buddha told the bhikṣus: 'All conditioned things are impermanent. Nothing abides. They cannot be trusted. They are dharmas of change. All that is gathered together will eventually scatter and perish. What is high must fall. What meets must part. Where there is birth, there must be death. All conditioned things are like trees on a steep riverbank, like marks drawn on water that disappear as soon as they are made, like bubbles, like dew on a blade of grass that cannot long remain, like the city of the gandharvas that is but a momentary appearance before the eyes. Human life is swifter than an arrow, swifter than the sun and moon traversing the sky. Human life is swifter even than the heavens. The ruin and decay of impermanence should be understood. If there were any buddha-work remaining undone, I would not enter nirvāṇa. All buddha-work being accomplished, I enter nirvāṇa. I have entrusted this Buddha-Dharma to devas and humans, and this great responsibility to the śrāvaka disciples. I go now toward the place of fearlessness and tranquil extinction, where all suffering is exhausted and there is no further birth. You should not give rise to great sorrow.'"
