# 佛說菩薩本業經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Original Practices of the Bodhisattva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0281

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was at the site of the Way in Magadha, having just attained buddhahood. His radiance was extremely bright. He sat on a naturally appearing lotus and jeweled lion-throne, upon which all the Buddhas of old had likewise sat, their virtue, deportment, and excellent marks all alike, their bodies and minds pure, their merit-practices universally complete. Their illumination penetrated and shone upon all persons, lands, and dharma-realms. Past, future, and present were no longer obstructed, and having arisen in the world, everything was equal. The bodhisattvas gathered at that time were all at the stage of one remaining birth before succession. Their spiritual powers were wondrously accomplished, extending throughout the ten directions. They guided and benefited sentient beings, opened the Buddha's Dharma treasury, and revealed the essential path to nirvāṇa. They entered into the faculties of all people, possessed the wisdom and virtue of knowing past lives, used skillful means and expedients, trained and transformed beings gradually, understood both inner and outer dharmas, remained unshaken from beginning to end, regarded all buddha-lands equally without discrimination, and praised the names of the Buddhas to an extent beyond reckoning. The affairs of the three paths were all penetrated. Together they exclaimed in praise: "The Buddha, mindful of us, has established the great aspiration and is able to manifest for us all the good and evil that exists in the buddha-worlds of all the Buddhas, the extraordinary and supreme lands where Buddhas dwell, the flourishing of the Way and its transforming influence, their radiance and spiritual powers, their instruction of devas and humans, their revelation of the meaning of the Dharma, the original practices of the Buddha, the ten stages, ten wisdoms, ten practices, ten dedications, ten treasuries, ten vows, ten illuminations, ten samādhis, ten manifestations, and ten seals; cutting off our faults and all delusion; manifesting for us the Buddha's conduct, the Buddha's wisdom, the Buddha's spiritual powers, the Buddha's strength, the Buddha's samādhi, his immeasurable transformations responding to the times, the four fearless confidences, the four unguarded qualities, the eighteen unique dharmas, his supreme and complete wisdom, his unsurpassed virtue, and all things openly displayed."

These leaders such as Worthy-Leader were each foremost in their respective lands. Each came together with countless superior beings, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and sat on lotus seats to one side. At that time, the Bodhisattva Reverence-Leader, receiving the Buddha's holy intention, exclaimed in praise: "How wonderful! This assembly of superior beings is unprecedented! Observe the places where they dwell: their buddha-lands are pure. As for donning the Tathāgata's virtue-precepts, cultivating the subtle and marvelous, perfecting the faculties of enlightenment and the powers, expounding the sūtras and Dharma, obtaining the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, and according to whether the lands are pure or turbid, liberating people without limit, spreading the transforming influence of the Way everywhere without exception: in this buddha-land, the lands that are distinct and different number in the hundreds of thousands of koṭīs. Whether wise or foolish, beautiful or ugly, long-lived or short-lived, speaking various languages, all have heard the Buddha's virtue and each has their own name for it. Some call the Buddha a Great Sage; some call the Buddha a Great Śramaṇa; some call him World-Honored One; some call him Divine Being; some call him Valiant Wisdom; some call him World-Honored One; some call him Capable Scholar; some call him Ascended Immortal; some call him Teacher of Devas; some call him Supreme Victor. Thus in the ten directions, all the devas and people have names for the Buddha numbering in the hundreds of millions beyond count. All these are the disciples whom the Buddha has taught and transformed, proclaiming the Way's influence ever since he first aroused the aspiration."

At this time, the Buddha emitted light from the wheel-marks beneath his feet, illuminating all the small lands of the buddha-realm. One small land consists of one Mount Sumeru around which the sun and moon revolve, shining upon the four continents: Pūrvavideha to the east, Jambudvīpa to the south, Aparagodānīya to the west, and Uttarakuru to the north, surrounded by the great ocean with the Iron Ring Mountains as the outer border, above which are twenty-eight heavens. Such constitutes one small land, and in the ten directions all around there are a total of a hundred billion.

At this time, there appeared a hundred billion Mount Sumerus, a hundred billion suns and moons, and the Heavens of the Four Kings, the Heaven of the Thirty-Three, the Yāma Heaven, the Tuṣita Heaven, the Nirmāṇarati Heaven, the Paranirmita-vaśavartin Heaven, the Brahmā Heaven, the Brahmā Assembly Heaven, the Brahmā Auxiliary Heaven, the Great Brahmā Heaven, the Heaven of Clear Luminosity, the Heaven of Water Practice, the Heaven of Subtle Water, the Heaven of Limitless Water, the Heaven of Water Sound, the Heaven of Restrained Purity, the Heaven of Universal Purity, the Heaven of Pure Luminosity, the Heaven of Guarding the Marvelous, the Heaven of Subtle Wonder, the Heaven of Vast Wonder, the Heaven of Extreme Wonder, the Heaven of Merit and Love, the Heaven of Surpassing Love, the Heaven of Bordering the Limit, the Heaven of Good Observation, the Heaven of Delightful Vision, the Heaven of Freedom from Binding Attachment, the Heaven of Consciousness and Wisdom, and the Heaven of Wisdom Without Thought, up to the twenty-eight formless heavens, each numbering a hundred billion. This constitutes one buddha-land, called the World of Endurance. Śākyamuni Buddha's emanation bodies, numbering a hundred billion, pervaded it all. At that time, the devas and humans beheld all the small lands and the Buddhas and bodhisattvas therein, as though seeing them nearby.

## Chapter Two: Vow-Practices

The Bodhisattva Knowledge-Leader asked Reverence-Leader: "What practices does one originally cultivate to achieve the Buddha's holy Way? How does one make body, speech, and mind pure, never dwelling on others' faults, causing all under heaven likewise unable to find one's shortcomings? How does one achieve such great humaneness and compassion, such clarity of inner nature, surpassing the disciples and pratyekabuddhas, so that all heresies cannot sway one? How is one born with a handsome form, marks and characteristics incomparable, with a clan of supreme nobility, understanding the weight of the Buddha-Dharma? How does one maintain one's own resolute will, constantly practice the four immeasurables, possess high talent and keen insight, vigor and heroic effort, cultivate all roots of virtue and the perfections without limit? How is one always born in the fields of merit, with words trusted and heeded? How does one subdue the mind and rectify intention, focus thought and enter dhyāna, understand the dharmas of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, transcend the four elements, the five aggregates, the six sense-bases, and the twelve links of dependent arising? How do the seven factors of awakening remain unobstructed, the wisdom of the ten powers fully penetrated, and all paths broadly entered, so as to rescue the endangered and resolve difficulties, being honored by Śakra and Brahmā, dispelling darkness like a torch, illuminating the world like the sun and moon, ferrying beings across like a ship's captain, surpassing the three realms to become the foremost? To accomplish this Way, what practices must one cultivate?"

Reverence-Leader answered: "Excellent, child of the Buddha! Your aspiration reaches high and far, with the greatest compassion, feeling sorrow for beings in the ten directions. If sons or daughters of good family wish to accomplish the Buddha's Way, they should first rectify their bodies, making words and thoughts harmonize, have their mouths recite the sūtras, have their minds reflect on what is practicable, reform the past and cultivate the future, never releasing the aspiration for the Way, accumulate virtue and build up goodness, bestow kindness without weariness. If so, then all that you have asked can be attained. One who is a bodhisattva must truly receive and study according to the Buddha's Dharma-teaching, without adding or subtracting, binding oneself with vows, mindful of bringing peace to the world, upholding precepts and practicing vows, thereby establishing the root of virtue.

## Chapter Three: The Ten Stages

Thereupon, in the World of Endurance, the hundred billion lord Śakras each upon the purple-indigo halls of their respective Heaven of the Thirty-Three created lion-thrones of the seven treasures, hung jeweled canopies, and spread multicolored cushions, and each bowed their heads to invite the Buddha. The Buddha, knowing all their intentions, then divided his body and appeared in each of their halls. In each, the Buddha, together with the assembly of bodhisattvas and all the heavenly lords, brought nothing but delight. In the hundred billion small lands below, the beings still saw the Buddha as before, without diminishment. At that time, from the buddha-lands in the ten directions, there came another gathering like clouds: the Bodhisattvas Dharma-Intention, Chief-Intention, Worthy-Intention, Diligent-Intention, Thought-Intention, Knowing-Intention, Discerning-Intention, Focused-Intention, Solemn-Intention, and Exhaustive-Intention, each from the ten directions together with countless superior beings, bowed at the Buddha's feet and sat upon lotus seats to one side. The Bodhisattva Dharma-Intention then, in his proper form, sat upright and entered concentration, entering the Samādhi of Immeasurable Assembly-Seeing, beholding the countless Buddhas of the ten directions. Each extended their right hand and touched his head, all saying: "Excellent! Dharma-Intention! Heroic bodhisattva, you have attained this samādhi. The Tathāgatas of the ten directions, as well as Śākyamuni Buddha, have already congratulated you on establishing your merit, cultivating subtle and wondrous eloquence, understanding the practice of emptiness and non-attachment, fully penetrating the essentials of the Dharma, comprehending the speech of all Buddhas, and knowing the minds of all sentient beings. Your practices are already complete, and you will attain buddhahood before long. Now we commission you to explain the ten stages of the bodhisattva's abiding, so that all practitioners may universally know their course of practice."

Thereupon the Bodhisattva Dharma-Intention, having received the Buddhas' gift of eloquence, his insight bright and true, unforgetting and unhindered, arose from concentration and spoke: "Sons and daughters of good family! Those who seek buddhahood proceed through ten stages. From ancient times to the present, all have achieved it through these stages. They are praised by all the World-Honored Ones, and their exposition is immeasurable. I shall expound them in full, as the Buddha has said. What are the ten? First, arousing the aspiration; second, cultivating the ground; third, corresponding practice; fourth, noble birth; fifth, perfection through cultivation; sixth, ascending through practice; seventh, non-retrogression; eighth, the stage of the youth; ninth, understanding birth; tenth, succession.

"What is meant by 'arousing the aspiration'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things: at the initial meeting with a Buddha, ten kinds of merit arise. First, seeing the Buddha's noble appearance; second, seeing the completeness of the bodily marks; third, seeing the manifestation of spiritual powers; fourth, seeing the profundity of the Way's virtue; fifth, seeing the incomparable deportment; sixth, knowing others' minds; seventh, the clarity of the sūtras' teaching; eighth, that whatever he says is truthfully understood; ninth, seeing the suffering of birth and death; tenth, taking delight in the Buddha-Dharma. Gradually opening to understanding, one then arouses the aspiration for the Way. In order to comprehend the Buddha's wisdom of the ten powers, there are ten things to learn: one should understand how to revere and serve all Buddhas; one should understand how to speak of the bodhisattva's virtue; one should truly comprehend the root of birth and death; one should vow to cultivate what is noble and build merit; one should cause one's practice to surpass the three realms; one should study the meritorious deeds of all Buddhas; one should seek further opportunities to see the Buddhas; one should cultivate and practice all the profound samādhis; one should have compassion for all who suffer from wrongdoing; one should follow the turning wheel of birth and death. These are the first stage of the initial arousing of the aspiration.

"What is meant by 'cultivating the ground'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things to learn in sequence: first, being mindful of others' goodness; second, purifying the mind; third, softening the intention; fourth, calming the will; fifth, constant generosity; sixth, practicing loving-kindness; seventh, benefiting all under heaven; eighth, aiding equality; ninth, regarding others as oneself; tenth, respecting others as one's teacher. There are a further ten things to learn: one should recite the sūtras frequently; one should distance oneself from one's homeland; one should draw near to a wise teacher; one should study good speech; one should know the right time; one should be vigorous; one should enter the essentials; one should understand practice; one should not forget; one should steady the will. These are the practices of the second stage of cultivating the ground.

"What is meant by 'corresponding practice'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things, to be entered according to the sūtras: first, seeing impermanence; second, seeing the suffering of birth; third, seeing that actions are empty; fourth, seeing that there is no self; fifth, seeing that there is no master; sixth, non-craving; seventh, non-attachment; eighth, non-action; ninth, non-desire; tenth, non-seeking. There are a further ten things to learn: one should be mindful of persons; one should be mindful of lands; one should be mindful of the Dharma; one should be mindful of the earth element; one should be mindful of the water element; one should be mindful of the fire element; one should be mindful of the wind element; one should be mindful of the desire realm; one should be mindful of the form realm; one should be mindful of the formless realm, with no lingering attachment. These are the third stage of corresponding practice and its distinctions.

"What is meant by 'noble birth'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things, following the Buddha's practice: first, never returning to deviant paths; second, single-mindedly facing the Buddha; third, contemplating the meaning of the Dharma; fourth, observing meritorious conduct; fifth, seeing people as illusory; sixth, seeing lands as dreams; seventh, seeing fortune and misfortune as empty; eighth, seeing all dharmas as phantasms; ninth, seeing suffering and joy as without difference; tenth, understanding nirvāṇa as pure. There are a further ten things to learn: one should know that in thought, the past Buddha's intention is empty, the future Buddha's intention is empty, the present Buddha's intention is empty; the past Buddha's Dharma is pure, the future Buddha's Dharma is pure, the present Buddha's Dharma is pure; the past Buddha is naturally so, the future Buddha is naturally so, the present Buddha is naturally so. All Buddhas arise equally, without any difference. These are the fourth stage of noble birth in equality.

"What is meant by 'perfection through cultivation'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things, practicing the rescue of people: first, employing skillful means for people; second, causing people to be at peace; third, aiding all people under heaven; fourth, having compassion for all; fifth, feeling sorrow for sentient beings; sixth, causing people to rejoice; seventh, caring for and watching over all people and creatures; eighth, urging them to follow the Way; ninth, manifesting purity for them; tenth, causing them to attain nirvāṇa. There are a further ten things to learn: one should know that sentient beings have no fixed nature, no fixed type, no fixed number, no creator, no correctness, are inconceivable, immeasurable, incalculable, and inexpressible, being ultimately empty. These are the fifth stage of holy practice and perfection through cultivation.

"What is meant by 'ascending through practice'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things, through which one succeeds in crossing over: first, hearing praise of the Buddha or disparagement of the Buddha, the mind remains unchanged; second, hearing praise of the Dharma or vilification of the Dharma, the mind remains unchanged; third, hearing that bodhisattvas are good or that bodhisattvas are wicked, the mind remains unchanged; fourth, hearing people's evaluations and criticisms, the mind remains unchanged; fifth, hearing of many people or few people, the mind remains unchanged; sixth, hearing of many sūtras or few sūtras, the mind remains unchanged; seventh, hearing of the suffering of birth or the joy of birth, the mind remains unchanged; eighth, hearing that people are hard to save or easy to save, the mind remains unchanged; ninth, hearing of the flourishing of the Dharma or the decline of the Dharma, the mind remains unchanged; tenth, encountering the existence of the Way or encountering the absence of the Way, the mind remains unchanged. There are a further ten things to learn: the mind is without conceptualization; one does not accept conceptualization; one does not calculate the body; there is no sense of 'mine'; there is no view of self; there is no master; there is no receiver; all is like an illusion; all is without completion; there is nothing whatsoever. These are the sixth stage of ascending through practice with complete faith.

"What is meant by 'non-retrogression'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things, in which the will is firm and strong: first, whether one says there are Buddhas or no Buddhas, one does not retrogress; second, whether one says there is Dharma or no Dharma, one does not retrogress; third, whether one says there are bodhisattvas or no bodhisattvas, one does not retrogress; fourth, whether one says there are seekers of buddhahood or no seekers of buddhahood, one does not retrogress; fifth, whether there is attainment of buddhahood or no attainment of buddhahood, one does not retrogress; sixth, whether one says there was a holy Way in the past or no holy Way in the past, one does not retrogress; seventh, whether there is a holy Way in the present or no holy Way in the present, one does not retrogress; eighth, whether there will be a holy Way in the future or no holy Way in the future, one does not retrogress; ninth, whether one says the three paths are the same or the three paths are different, one does not retrogress; tenth, whether one says the Buddha's wisdom has an end or the Buddha's wisdom has no end, one does not retrogress. There are a further ten things to learn: opening subtle wisdom to enter great wisdom; opening great wisdom to enter subtle wisdom; manifesting one dharma to enter the many sūtras; manifesting the many sūtras to enter one dharma; understanding sentient beings to enter the essential of emptiness; understanding the essential of emptiness to enter sentient beings; releasing conceptual thought to enter quiescent samādhi; releasing quiescent samādhi to enter conceptual thought; explaining the few to enter the many; explaining the many to enter the few. These are the seventh stage of advancing without retrogression.

"What is meant by 'the youth'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things, entering wherever one goes: first, body, speech, and mind do not transgress; second, being entirely without faults; third, the will is single-pointed wherever one is born; fourth, seeing people and knowing their inner compassion; fifth, knowing what people's minds have faith in; sixth, knowing what people's intentions understand; seventh, not accepting others' confused thoughts; eighth, knowing the creation and destruction of all lands; ninth, spiritual powers swiftly pervading the ten directions; tenth, perfectly upholding all dharmas. There are a further ten things to learn: learning to know the worlds of the Buddhas; learning the wisdom and ability of the Buddhas; learning to manifest the practice of the Buddhas' spiritual powers; learning to adorn all buddha-lands; learning to travel throughout all lands; learning to answer all questions with the Dharma; learning to manifest transformations without limit; learning the Buddha's voice that expounds all dharmas; learning to turn and pervade the ten directions in an instant; learning to see countless Buddhas in a single thought. These are the eighth stage of pure youth.

"What is meant by 'understanding birth'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things, received through the vision of wisdom: first, knowing what destiny all beings are headed for; second, knowing the fetters by which beings are bound; third, knowing where people have come from in past lives; fourth, knowing the karmic results of people's wholesome and unwholesome deeds; fifth, knowing what dharma people practice and receive; sixth, knowing the good and evil that people's minds enter; seventh, knowing the myriad changes in people's thoughts; eighth, knowing the purity and defilement of all lands in the ten directions; ninth, knowing the immeasurable wisdom of the three paths; tenth, knowing the true essentials and being able to teach accordingly. There are a further ten things to learn: learning the righteous conduct of the Dharma King; learning the rites and deportment of the Dharma King; learning the establishment of the Dharma King; learning the Dharma King's going out and coming in; learning the Dharma King's movements and resting; learning the Dharma King's awesome dignity; learning the Dharma King's sitting and rising; learning the Dharma King's commands; learning the Dharma King's conferring of honors; learning the Dharma King's touring of the buddha-lands. These are the ninth stage of receiving prophecy and understanding birth.

"What is meant by 'succession'? In the bodhisattva's Dharma-abiding, there are ten things, in which wisdom is difficult to match: first, one is able to move countless lands through mindfulness; second, one is able to manifest radiance for countless lands; third, one is able to establish the Dharma for countless lands; fourth, one is able to liberate countless lands; fifth, one is able to benefit and bring peace to countless lands; sixth, one's voice is able to awaken countless people; seventh, one is able to observe and know the minds of sentient beings; eighth, one is able to know the infinite thoughts of sentient beings; ninth, one is able to cause countless people to enter the Dharma; tenth, one is able to reveal wisdom and understanding to people in proper sequence. There are ten things that those at the stage of understanding birth cannot equal that those at the stage of succession possess: they cannot know the succession-stage bodhisattva's bodily affairs, aspirations, practices, spiritual powers, samādhi, concentration, mindfulness, knowledge of the ancient and the present, vision of the future, understanding of place, cultivation of the land's Dharma, and the affairs of the sage's intention, and what the succession-stage bodhisattva desires. There are a further ten things to learn: learning the Buddha's immeasurable wisdom of the three paths; learning to fully possess all buddhadharmas; learning that dharma after dharma has no attachment; learning the bottomless treasury of all Buddhas; learning to complete one's land through spiritual wisdom; learning the radiance that illuminates the ten directions; learning the Buddha's samādhi that moves all lands; learning skillful and expedient transformation at will; learning to teach universally so that all achieve accomplishment; learning to gather all together and turn the Dharma wheel. The reason one studies is the desire to know all. Having come to know all and being swift in all things, with nothing more to learn: this is called the stage of succession. From ten sets of ten dharmas, one achieves realization, and in the present world one succeeds to the unsurpassed, true, and right Way, becoming the Most Perfectly Awakened One, liberating all under heaven."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! Bodhisattva Dharma-Intention, truly worthy of being called a child of the Buddha. All the Buddhas of the ten directions, past, present, and future, have all arisen through this. This Dharma is boundless, its illumination immeasurable, the people it liberates without limit, and its wisdom and intention without end."

When the Buddha had finished speaking, all rejoiced greatly and made obeisance to the Buddha.
