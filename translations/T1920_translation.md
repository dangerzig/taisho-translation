# 觀心論
## Treatise on Contemplating the Mind

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1920

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, composed by Zhiyi (智顗).

---

Question: The Buddha's sūtras are immeasurable, and the treatises are also very many. Those who propagate the Dharma fill the world in abundance, and the assembly that listens and receives is found everywhere. Thus, the Dharma-rain universally moistens the fourfold assembly, and its dew benefits without limit. What have you seen or heard, and what further benefit is there in composing this treatise?

Answer: As you say in your question, the Buddha's sūtras are immeasurable and the treatises are also very many. This is commonly known. But those who propagate the Dharma, for the sake of benefiting beings, mostly administer watered-down milk. This causes those who listen and receive to lose the true taste of the Way. The fourfold assembly increasingly drifts toward dilution, causing those of faith to gradually grow tepid in their discussion. I fear that the deep, vast, and great Dharma will not long remain, and the Dharma-eye of sentient beings will be extinguished, losing the benefit of the true Dharma. Therefore, in my leisure, grief and sorrow burn and press upon me unbearably. Not considering my own shallow ordinariness and scant hearing and limited vision, wishing to assist the parrot, I exert the strength of my wings and, relying upon the power of the Three Treasures, venture to compose this treatise.

The questioner should know that the author's main intention is twofold: first, as self-admonition for all students; second, if among the external fourfold assembly there are those who can believe and accept it, it may also be transmitted to them. I briefly answer with verses:

> The Great Teacher, about to enter nirvāṇa, the Compassionate Father left a bequest:
> "Cultivate the Way through the four foundations of mindfulness; dwell relying on the Prātimokṣa."
> We are not the Buddha's true children, not mindful of this bequest.
> The vehicle is lax, inwardly there is no Way; the precepts are lax, we fear the three evil paths.
> Because we do not inquire about contemplating the mind, others' faith gradually thins.
> If the crow does not give the Way, how can it repay the white crow's kindness?
> Not only is the field not good; there are no seeds of equality.
> If the Dharma-rain does not fall, the Dharma-seeds will surely wither and dry up.
> Each one lacking provisions for the future, losing the threefold benefit, incurring suffering.
> The great Dharma is about to crumble. Alas, to witness this!
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> The equal, true dharma-realm: no practice and no ability can reach it.
> But if one can inquire about contemplating the mind, one can both practice and arrive.
> This is the four foundations of mindfulness; one can rely on the Prātimokṣa precepts.
> The vehicle is urgent, inwardly there is the Way; the precepts are urgent, one is born among humans and devas.
> This is a true child of the Buddha, not departing from the Compassionate Father's bequest.
> Devas and nāgas all rejoice; would not all beings be delighted?
> One can repay the white crow's kindness, universally giving the black crow food.
> Having a good and fine field, having seeds of equality,
> The Dharma-rain falls in due season, and the Dharma-seeds all grow and flourish.
> Each one has provisions for the future; all obtain the threefold benefit.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma wish to hear the unsurpassed Way.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, the wisdom of hearing will never be activated.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma wish to reflect on the unsurpassed Way.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, the wisdom of reflection will never arise.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma wish to cultivate the unsurpassed Way.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, the wisdom of cultivation will never be accomplished.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma wish to cultivate the four kinds of samādhi.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, they will struggle in vain and gain nothing.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma, through much listening, obtain words and phrases.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, they will not attain true bliss.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma cultivate samādhi and attain concentration.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, their blind dhyāna will see nothing.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma wish to repent of all offenses.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, their offenses will ultimately be hard to escape.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma intend to be free of afflictions.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, afflictions will never be extinguished.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma originally intend to benefit others.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, they retrogress and cause others to slander.
> Those who come seeking the Dharma wish to promote and reveal the Buddha-Dharma.
> If they do not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, they retrogress and the great practice is damaged.
> Such various gains and losses cannot be fully stated in verse.
> Though such gains and losses exist, no one is aware.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> In the final age, those who contemplate the mind attain wrong concentration and develop wrong views.
> Their eloquence is inexhaustible; they consider themselves a treasure among people.
> The unknowing follow their scent; the wild fox's odor stings the eyes.
> Raising their tails, they walk with their legs; step by step they fall into a pit and perish.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Guarding the nose-tip with ānāpāna, and cultivating the contemplation of impurity:
> Through ānāpāna one attains the four dhyānas, but does not escape the karma of hell.
> Through impurity one claims to be an adept, but overturning the bowl, one accepts a woman's offering.
> Even if one attains birth through associated dhyāna, one falls into the difficulty of the long-life heavens.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Relying on ritualistic methods and applying the mind, without wisdom one develops ghost-concentration.
> Displaying anomalies and moving people's minds, when the matter erupts, it destroys the Buddha-Dharma.
> At death one is born in the ghost-realm, among the ninety-five types of followers.
> In the age of the semblance Dharma, the resolution of doubts is clear: three kinds of teachers destroy the Buddha-Dharma.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Inwardly the mind does not aim for the Way; with crookedness and flattery one thinks of fame and gain.
> One falsely displays the appearance of sitting in meditation and obtains fame, gain, and followers.
> When the matter erupts, it ruins others' minds and damages the Buddha's true Dharma.
> This is a caṇḍāla; at death one falls into the Avīci Hell.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Teaching the Dharma leads to liberation; the assembly that hears is likewise.
> If one does not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, it is like a pauper counting another's treasure.
> If the teacher inquires about contemplating the mind, without teaching there is no demonstration.
> If the listener inquires about contemplating the mind, without inquiry there is no attainment.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Precepts are for controlling the horse of the mind; though one upholds the five divisions of vinaya,
> If one does not know to inquire about contemplating the mind, the horse of the mind will never be tamed.
> The vinaya upholds and sustains the Buddha-Dharma, understanding the outer but not the inner.
> Vimalakīrti rebuked the chief disciples; only that is called truly upholding the vinaya.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Chanting sūtras leads to liberation, not for worldly wealth and gain.
> If one can inquire about contemplating the mind, breaking open a single mote of dust,
> One brings forth the sūtra-scrolls of the great-thousand world. To receive, uphold, read, and recite this:
> Hearing and retaining without forgetting, the mind opens and one attains liberation.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Observing others cultivating offerings, promoting and establishing those who practice,
> While secretly the mind seeks self-benefit, relying on and borrowing to nourish the body:
> This ruins others' joy and generosity. As a camel or donkey one will repay others.
> If one can inquire about contemplating the mind, one will be like a camel's swift steed.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> Each path has its own dharma, yet one does not investigate for oneself.
> Suddenly one peers through the hedge at the Buddha's teaching and spends over ten years thus.
> Not only is one's own dharma clumsy, one also harbors a scheming and destructive mind.
> This is like Kapila; would the sages and saints permit it?
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> The wealthy and noble who have no Way mostly increase in arrogance and indulgence.
> If one can inquire about contemplating the mind, one attains the true wealth and nobility of the Dharma.
> Though high, one is not in danger; though full, one does not overflow.
> Not attached to worldly wealth and honor, the mind constantly abides in the Way and the Dharma.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> The poor and lowly are mostly treacherous and deceitful, peering through hedges and creating many evils.
> In the present they are punished by the law of the king; at death they fall into the three evil paths.
> If one can inquire about contemplating the mind, one can be content in poverty and nurture the Way.
> Having the Way is true nobility; non-action is wealth and happiness.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> The fourfold assembly are all children of the Buddha; none is not a Dharma-relative.
> Due to clinging to wholesome dharmas, they quarrel and thereby form enmities for the future.
> If one can inquire about contemplating the mind, harmony is like water and milk.
> All are cubs of the lion; all are a forest of sandalwood.
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> As years decline and the body bears illness, eyes dim and ears grow gradually deaf,
> The mind is dull with much forgetfulness, each year worse than the last.
> The death-king, a garuḍa bird, will soon swallow the root of life.
> Once the rope of karma is suddenly cut, with breath gone, how could one speak?
> For these reasons, one must compose the Treatise on Contemplating the Mind.
> I bow my head to the buddhas of the ten directions. Deeply compassionate toward those who contemplate the mind,
> I urge them: carefully observe and contemplate, and activate the wondrous bliss of right awakening.
> I bow my head to the Dharma of the ten directions. Deeply compassionate toward those who contemplate the mind,
> I urge them: carefully observe and contemplate, and attain the true Dharma, freed from suffering.
> I bow my head to the Saṅgha of the ten directions. If one can skillfully observe and contemplate,
> One enters the great ocean of harmony, with a joyful mind beyond measure.
> I bow my head to the master Nāgārjuna. May he bless those who contemplate the mind,
> Causing them to swiftly attain enlightenment and also to bless the abandoning of the three minds.
> Now, relying on the power of the Three Treasures, I raise thirty-six questions.
> Their gates and the detailed gates within them, corresponding to each matter, are hard to count.
> If, in contemplating one thought-moment of mind, one can answer these questions,
> Know that the mind's eye has opened and one has entered the pool of coolness.
> If one cannot answer these questions, alas, how dark and blind!
> If one cannot see even a small meaning, how can one practice the great Way?
> Alas, in the final age of the Dharma, there are no more who attain the Way.
> Even if there are a few, can they discern these questions?
> Therefore, giving rise to a mind of compassion, I take refuge in and bow to the Three Treasures.
> I compose this treatise of questioning the mind, that those who contemplate may become clear and bright.
> May all who see and hear it not give rise to doubt or slander.
> Believe, accept, and diligently cultivate: you will certainly obtain the great benefit of the Dharma.

Question: Why compose this treatise on questioning and contemplating the mind?

Answer: In the final age, paupers counting treasure are originally not addressed by these words. But if practitioners of meditation who sit in dhyāna and cultivate contemplation merely settle the mind at the nose-tip with impurity and ānāpāna, this too is facing a wall. How can one discuss the Way? Those who contemplate the mind and enter the Way through attaining concentration, developing wrong understanding, with inexhaustible eloquence, consider themselves a treasure among people. How could they know that wrong pride blazes fiercely?

Now I pose these questions. If question by question one does not know the answer, how can one claim to have attained what has not been attained or realized what has not been realized? The "Exhortation to Uphold" chapter of the Lotus Sūtra says: "Such people are hard to transform and guide." Furthermore, the "Never Disparaging" chapter says: "After the passing of the Tathāgata Bhīṣmagarjitasvararāja, such types of people were exceedingly many, not just one."

Moreover, lamentably: the followers of our own school have accompanied me for accumulated years, watching the mind for some time. Yet they do not know how to investigate and question the mind in this way. Because of this, they are not imbued with the inner Dharma but attach to external written words. They furtively memorize notes and rush about, shouldering sūtras and treatises while wandering aimlessly. Why not set aside words and phrases and break open a single mote of dust to read the sūtra-scrolls of the great-thousand world? Thinking of these deluded followers who have accompanied me for accumulated years, empty and without attainment: if one day their eyes suddenly close, who will then show them the way? For these reasons, grief presses upon me increasingly. Therefore I compose this treatise.

The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* illuminates the gate of the forty-two letters. At the beginning it says: "If one hears the letter 'a,' one instantly comprehends all meanings. This means that all dharmas are originally unborn."

Nāgārjuna also composed the *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* and the commentary on the *Mahāyāna*. The main intention uses the eight negations beginning with "non-arising" to open the starting-point of the treatise. Using one verse, he explains "originally unborn": "Dharmas do not arise from themselves, nor do they arise from another. They do not arise from both, nor without cause. Therefore they are said to be without arising."

Now, taking only the contemplation of the single phrase "self-arising," I raise thirty-six questions. If, in contemplating the mind, one can answer these questions without obstruction, know that this practitioner, among the six kinds of identity, has entered the stage of contemplative practice, which is the initial stage of the five grades of discipleship, namely the stage of initial rejoicing. Therefore the sūtra says: "Even hearing a single verse, one penetrates immeasurable meanings, explaining in sequence as the Dharma dictates, from one month to four months to a full year." The treatise says:

> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how are the four inexpressibles?
> Departing from the clinging of discursive elaboration, the mind is pure like empty space.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how is Māra's conduct?
> Bound by karma and afflictions, burned by the fire of the three realms' blazing house.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how are non-Buddhist paths?
> Through the karma of afflictions of various views, one transmigrates through the six destinies.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how are the three vehicles?
> Through unskillful crossing, one severs views and thinking and escapes the blazing house of the three realms.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how is the skillful crossing?
> The three vehicles, without severing bonds, enter the two kinds of nirvāṇa.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how is the Separate Teaching?
> Seeking the fruit of the great-vehicle permanence, the bodhisattva severs the separate delusions.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how is the Perfect Teaching vehicle?
> Without destroying the dharma-realm, one abides in the nirvāṇa of the three virtues.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how is nirvāṇa?
> Cultivating the four kinds of samādhi, one attains the true forbearance of non-arising.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how is skillful accomplishment?
> The twenty-five preparatory methods: tuning the mind and entering the right path.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know causes and conditions?
> The ten kinds of objects arise and accomplish the three wisdoms in one mind.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know the ten objects?
> Each accomplishes the ten Dharma-vehicles and one roams in the four directions with happiness.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one not abide in dharmas?
> Entering the initial stage of arousing the mind and the forty-two stages.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one accomplish the six perfections?
> One obtains the various samādhis and the various dhāraṇīs.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one attain the six superknowledges?
> Using the four means of gathering to transform, the four kinds of unobstructed eloquence are without hindrance.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one attain the major and minor marks?
> Accomplishing both the true and response bodies of the Buddha, responding to conditions like a mirror-image.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one possess the ten powers?
> And the four kinds of fearlessness? Inner and outer illumination is perfectly complete.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does contemplation of the mind
> Enable one to attain the eighteen dharmas not shared with the world?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one attain great loving-kindness,
> Great compassion, and the three mindful states? Pitying beings without any differentiated mark.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how are skillful means
> That accomplish all sentient beings and purify all lands?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how, within one mind,
> Does one adorn the bodhi-tree and establish the pure site of the Way?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one sit at the site of the Way
> And manifest the four kinds of becoming a buddha, responding to capacities without error?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one turn the four teachings,
> The pure and wondrous Dharma-wheel, so that all obtain the sweet dew?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one manifest the four buddhas
> And the four kinds of nirvāṇa-marks? Ultimately knowing the remainderless.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know dependent and direct retribution,
> The four lands with identical heavenly vessels, yet with different colors of rice?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how, within this mind,
> Does one see all faculties and conditions, penetrating without hindrance?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know the siddhāntas,
> Formless and without objects, yet manifesting form and broadly teaching the Dharma?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know the gradual and sudden,
> The secret and indeterminate teachings? With one voice one teaches all four.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know the four teachings,
> Each opening four gates, and all the Dharma-gates?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how, within the four teachings,
> Do the four gates and sixteen gates compose treatises that penetrate the various sūtras?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one abide in wisdom and concentration
> And universally enter the ten dharma-realms, broadly benefiting all sentient beings?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know the four lands
> And use the teachings with increase and decrease, universally benefiting all beings?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know this mind
> Is complete with all buddha-dharmas, and not one dharma is outside the mind?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know this mind
> Is the equal dharma-realm? The Buddha does not deliver sentient beings.
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how does one know this mind:
> The dharma-realm is like empty space; ultimately there is nothing whatsoever to be thought?
> Questioning: contemplating the self-arising mind, how is there no written word?
> All words and speech are cut off; silently, there is no utterance.

Now, taking the contemplation of a single thought-moment of the self-arising mind, I briefly raise thirty-six questions to question those outside who contemplate the mind and the followers and attendants who have long accompanied me in practicing the four kinds of samādhi.

If the one who contemplates the mind can penetrate each and every one, one should give rise to the thought that this is a buddha, closely attend, receive, and practice as with the four reliances. If the followers and attendants are without obstruction in this, they are of the true causal practice. They are truly Dharma-king grandchildren and heirs, continuing the seed of the Three Treasures and causing it never to be cut off.

If one cannot, question by question, answer these questions while contemplating a single thought-moment of the self-arising mind, then one is a follower of heavenly demons and non-Buddhist paths, driven by them. One will remain in the prison of the three realms without any escape. If the mind does not strike the flint and one seeks to force an escape, one will certainly fall into the pit of the three vehicles and three evil paths. One will sever one's own Dharma-body and wisdom-life, slaughter the kinsmen of bodhi, and truly destroy the Buddha-Dharma nation. One's great-vehicle home is already ruined. Alas, alas! Knowing the great, what can be done?

If contemplating the self-arising mind has such gains and losses, then contemplating the other-arising mind, the jointly-arising mind, and the causeless-arising mind are likewise.

In the gate of conforming marks, one craves sound and form. In the gate of opposing marks, there are the countable and the uncountable. Unwilling to follow along, one enters the mud-hell. In the midst of the three realms, it is hard to cross. Whether crossing or not crossing is wondrously inconceivable. The deluded who follow conforming marks have much doubt. Not taking the priceless medicine of ghee, with a crazed mind, how can nirvāṇa be pursued?

Furthermore, in the gate of revealing marks, the four kinds of three and the three wheels are explained. The four teachings are interpreted according to the circumstances. Unwilling to follow along, one enters Avīci. This causes the five-fold analysis to trace the tracks. Neither track nor distance, neither five-fold. The three truths and threefold contemplation are within it. Ordinary people's feelings cannot skillfully discern it. The blind mind clings to views and pronounces all to be emptiness.

In the gate of hidden marks, form and shape are extinguished. The three, four, and their harmonious balance make twelve gates. Unwilling to follow circumstances, one gives rise to craving and attachment. How can skillful means then defeat the demon-enemies? Craving, attachment, and demon-enemies are the mother of the Buddha and also the profound original ground of the three bodies. Each, according to circumstances, establishes its name. For ordinary people, they are named the five hidden marks.

All day enduring hunger is not abstaining from food. All day enduring cold is not ascetic practice. All day having few desires is not apart from greed. All day practicing lovingkindness is seizing others' lives. All day fearing offense is increasingly committing wrongs. All day seeking a physician is increasingly aggravating the illness. All day practicing generosity is increasingly augmenting stinginess. All day severing anger, the three poisons flourish. Inconceivable! Inconceivable!

> The round and full bright moon has no increase or decrease;
> Ordinary feelings, in confusion, see it as waxing and waning.
> Even if its light were turned to shine upon the western lands,
> Its underlying principle has never been altered.
> Skillful application, skillful wonder, skillfully ferrying beings:
> Not making an enemy, not making a buddha.
> The Dharma-body's skillful application is wondrously inconceivable.
> May I ask: from what place does concentration emerge?
