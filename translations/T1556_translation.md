# Treatise on the Five Categories of the Sarvāstivāda School

## *Sàpóduō Zōng Wǔshì Lùn* (薩婆多宗五事論)

Taishō 1556

Translated by Fǎchéng (*Fǎchéng*, 法成) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a systematic Abhidharma treatise of the Sarvāstivāda school classifying all dharmas into five categories (pañcavastu): form (rūpa), mind (citta), mental factors (caitasika), formations not associated with mind (cittaviprayuktasaṃskāra), and the unconditioned (asaṃskṛta). It provides precise definitions for each dharma within these categories, covering the four great elements, sense faculties, sense objects, the six consciousnesses, fifty-some mental factors (including the roots of merit, unroots of merit, indeterminate roots, nine fetters, seven forms of conceit, five views, ten knowledges, and the latent afflictions classified by realm and path of abandonment), as well as the formations not associated with mind (acquisition, attainments, life-faculty, homogeneous class, etc.) and the three unconditioned dharmas. This text reflects the mature Sarvāstivāda Abhidharma taxonomy as transmitted in the Dunhuang manuscripts.*

---

Dharmas are of five kinds: first, form dharmas; second, mind dharmas; third, mental-factor dharmas; fourth, dharmas of formations not associated with mind; fifth, unconditioned dharmas.

### Form Dharmas

What are called form dharmas? All forms produced from the four great elements and generated by the four great elements. What are the four great elements? The earth element, water element, fire element, and wind element. What are called derived forms? The eye faculty, ear faculty, nose faculty, tongue faculty, and body faculty; forms, sounds, odors, tastes, a portion of tangibles, and unmanifest form.

### Mind Dharmas

What are called mind dharmas? Mind (*citta*), mentation (*manas*), and consciousness (*vijñāna*). What are these? The six consciousness-groups: eye consciousness, ear consciousness, nose consciousness, tongue consciousness, body consciousness, and mental consciousness.

### Mental-Factor Dharmas

What are called mental-factor dharmas? Dharmas associated with mind. What are these? Feeling (*vedanā*), perception (*saṃjñā*), volition (*cetanā*), contact (*sparśa*), attention (*manaskāra*), desire (*chanda*), resolve (*adhimokṣa*), faith (*śraddhā*), vigor (*vīrya*), mindfulness (*smṛti*), concentration (*samādhi*), wisdom (*prajñā*), investigation (*vitarka*), examination (*vicāra*), heedlessness (*pramāda*), non-heedlessness (*apramāda*), roots of merit, unroots of merit, indeterminate roots, the fetters, bonds, latent tendencies in their subtle and extensive forms, the secondary afflictions, entanglements and stable states, cognitions, views, and all direct realizations. There are further dharmas associated with mind; all such are called mental-factor dharmas.

### Formations Not Associated with Mind

What are called dharmas of formations not associated with mind? Dharmas that are not associated with mind. What are these? Acquisition (*prāpti*), the attainment of signlessness, the attainment of cessation, the state of signlessness, the life-faculty (*jīvitendriya*), homogeneous class (*nikāyasabhāga*), acquisition of place, acquisition of phenomena, acquisition of bases, birth (*jāti*), aging (*jarā*), abiding (*sthiti*), impermanence (*anityatā*), name-groups (*nāmakāya*), sentence-groups (*padakāya*), and syllable-groups (*vyañjanakāya*). There are further dharmas not associated with mind; all such are called dharmas of formations not associated with mind.

### Unconditioned Dharmas

What are called unconditioned dharmas? Three kinds of the unconditioned: space (*ākāśa*), non-analytical cessation (*apratisaṃkhyānirodha*), and analytical cessation (*pratisaṃkhyānirodha*). These are called unconditioned dharmas.

## Detailed Definitions

### The Four Great Elements

What is the earth element? The nature of solidity and hardness. What is the water element? The nature of fluidity and moisture. What is the fire element? The nature of heat and warmth. What is the wind element? The nature of lightness and motion.

### The Five Sense Faculties

What is the eye faculty? The pure form that serves as the support of eye consciousness. What is the ear faculty? The pure form that serves as the support of ear consciousness. What is the nose faculty? The pure form that serves as the support of nose consciousness. What is the tongue faculty? The pure form that serves as the support of tongue consciousness. What is the body faculty? The pure form that serves as the support of body consciousness.

### The Sense Objects

What are forms? Beautiful forms and ugly forms, forms abiding between those two, and manifest colors. Initially known by one consciousness, the eye consciousness, and received by eye consciousness; mental consciousness can also know them. Therefore forms are cognized by two consciousnesses: eye consciousness and mental consciousness.

What are sounds? Sounds are of two kinds: those produced from causes of appropriated great elements, and those produced from causes of unappropriated great elements. Initially known by one consciousness, the ear consciousness, and received by ear consciousness; mental consciousness can also know them. Therefore sounds are cognized by two consciousnesses: ear consciousness and mental consciousness.

What are odors? Pleasant odors, unpleasant odors, and neutral odors. Initially known by one consciousness, the nose consciousness, and received by nose consciousness; mental consciousness can also know them. Therefore odors are cognized by two consciousnesses: nose consciousness and mental consciousness.

What are tastes? Agreeable, disagreeable, and neutral tastes. Initially known by one consciousness, the tongue consciousness, and received by tongue consciousness; mental consciousness can also know them. Therefore tastes are cognized by two consciousnesses: tongue consciousness and mental consciousness.

What is a portion of tangibles? Smoothness, roughness, lightness, heaviness, cold, hunger, thirst, and so forth. Initially known by one consciousness, the body consciousness, and received by body consciousness; mental consciousness can also know them. Therefore a portion of tangibles is cognized by two consciousnesses: body consciousness and mental consciousness.

What are unmanifest forms (*avijñaptirūpa*)? Forms included in the dharma base (*dharmāyatana*). At all times they are cognized solely by mental consciousness.

### The Six Consciousnesses

What is eye consciousness? Depending on the eye faculty, it discriminately cognizes form. What is ear consciousness? Depending on the ear faculty, it discriminately cognizes sound. What is nose consciousness? Depending on the nose faculty, it discriminately cognizes odor. What is tongue consciousness? Depending on the tongue faculty, it discriminately cognizes taste. What is body consciousness? Depending on the body faculty, it discriminately cognizes tangibles. What is mental consciousness? Depending on the mental faculty, it discriminately cognizes dharmas.

### The Mental Factors in Detail

What is feeling? The nature of experiencing. It is of three kinds: painful, pleasant, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant.

What is perception? The nature of discernment. It is of three kinds: small, great, and immeasurable.

What is volition? The activity constructed by mind; the action performed by mentation. It is of three kinds: wholesome, unwholesome, and indeterminate.

What is contact? The coming together of three [faculty, object, and consciousness]. It is of three kinds: contact that produces pleasure, contact that produces suffering, and contact that produces neither suffering nor pleasure.

What is attention? The turning of mind. It is of three kinds: of a learner, of one beyond learning, and of neither a learner nor one beyond learning.

What is desire? The nature of wishing to act. What is resolve? That in which the mind delights, the nature of delight and wishing to act. What is faith? Supreme clarity of mind. What is vigor? Delight of mind. What is mindfulness? The nature of clear remembering of mind. What is concentration? One-pointedness of mind. What is wisdom? Discernment of dharmas. What is investigation? Coarseness of mind in nature. What is examination? Subtlety of mind in nature. What is heedlessness? Not cultivating wholesome dharmas. What is non-heedlessness? Cultivating wholesome dharmas.

**Roots of merit**: three roots of merit: the wholesome root of non-attachment (*alobha*), the wholesome root of non-aversion (*adveṣa*), and the wholesome root of non-delusion (*amoha*).

**Unroots of merit**: three unroots of merit: the unwholesome root of attachment (*lobha*), the unwholesome root of aversion (*dveṣa*), and the unwholesome root of delusion (*moha*).

**Indeterminate roots**: four indeterminate roots: indeterminate craving (*tṛṣṇā*), view (*dṛṣṭi*), conceit (*māna*), and ignorance (*avidyā*). What is indeterminate craving? That produced by the five kinds pertaining to the form and formless realms. What is indeterminate view? Identity-view (*satkāyadṛṣṭi*) and extreme view (*antagrāhadṛṣṭi*) operating in the desire realm, and the five views operating in the form and formless realms. What is indeterminate conceit? The five kinds operating in the form and formless realms. What is indeterminate ignorance? The ignorance associated with identity-view and extreme view operating in the desire realm, and the five kinds operating in the form and formless realms.

### The Nine Fetters

The fetters (*saṃyojana*) are nine: the fetter of attachment, the fetter of aversion, the fetter of conceit, the fetter of ignorance, the fetter of views, the fetter of adherence to the superior, the fetter of doubt, the fetter of envy, and the fetter of miserliness.

What is the fetter of attachment? Attachment pertaining to the three realms. What is the fetter of aversion? Resentment and hostility toward sentient beings. What is the fetter of conceit? Seven kinds of conceit constitute the fetter of conceit: conceit (*māna*), over-conceit (*atimāna*), conceit surpassing conceit (*mānātimāna*), self-conceit (*asmimāna*), presumptuous conceit (*adhimāna*), deprecating conceit (*ūnamāna*), and wrong conceit (*mithyāmāna*).

What is conceit? Regarding the inferior, one thinks oneself superior; regarding equals, one thinks oneself equal. Based on this, one harbors conceit, a self-reliant conceited nature: the mind's arrogance, the mind's pride, the mind's clinging. This is called conceit.

What is over-conceit? Regarding equals, one thinks oneself superior; regarding the superior, one thinks oneself equal. Based on this, one harbors conceit, a self-reliant conceited nature: the mind's arrogance, the mind's pride, the mind's clinging. This is called over-conceit.

What is conceit surpassing conceit? Regarding the superior, one thinks oneself superior. Based on this, one harbors conceit, a self-reliant conceited nature: the mind's arrogance, the mind's pride, the mind's clinging. This is called conceit surpassing conceit.

What is self-conceit? Regarding the five appropriated aggregates, one views them as self or as belonging to self. Based on this, one harbors conceit, a self-reliant conceited nature: the mind's arrogance, the mind's pride, the mind's clinging. This is called self-conceit.

What is presumptuous conceit? When one has not attained a superior and distinguished state, one claims to have attained it; when one has not realized, touched, or made manifest a superior and distinguished state, one claims to have realized it. Based on this, one harbors conceit, a self-reliant conceited nature: the mind's arrogance, the mind's pride, the mind's clinging. This is called presumptuous conceit.

What is deprecating conceit? When another is greatly superior, one considers oneself only slightly inferior. Based on this, one harbors conceit, a self-reliant conceited nature: the mind's arrogance, the mind's pride, the mind's clinging. This is called deprecating conceit.

What is wrong conceit? When one lacks virtue, one considers oneself virtuous. Based on this, one harbors conceit, a self-reliant conceited nature: the mind's arrogance, the mind's pride, the mind's clinging. This is called wrong conceit.

These seven kinds of conceit constitute the fetter of conceit.

What is the fetter of ignorance? Lack of knowledge pertaining to the three realms. This is the fetter of ignorance.

What is the fetter of views? Three kinds constitute the fetter of views. What are the three? First, identity-view (*satkāyadṛṣṭi*); second, extreme view (*antagrāhadṛṣṭi*); third, wrong view (*mithyādṛṣṭi*).

What is identity-view? Regarding the five appropriated aggregates, one views them as self or as belonging to self. Based on this: tolerance, delight, the wisdom of contemplation, and what is seen. This is called identity-view.

What is extreme view? Regarding the five appropriated aggregates, one views them as eternal or annihilated. Based on this: tolerance, delight, the wisdom of contemplation, and what is seen. This is called extreme view.

What is wrong view? Denying cause, denying effect, denying function, and destroying what exists. Based on this: tolerance, delight, the wisdom of contemplation, and what is seen. This is called wrong view.

These three kinds of views constitute the fetter of views.

What is the fetter of adherence to the superior? Two kinds of adherence constitute the fetter of adherence. What are the two? First, adherence to views (*dṛṣṭiparāmarśa*); second, adherence to rules and observances (*śīlavrataparāmarśa*).

What is adherence to views? Regarding the five appropriated aggregates, one views them as the best, the highest, the most excellent, the supreme. Based on this: tolerance, delight, the wisdom of contemplation, and what is seen. This is called adherence to views.

What is adherence to rules and observances? Regarding the five appropriated aggregates, one views them as pure, as liberating, as leading to escape. Based on this: tolerance, delight, the wisdom of contemplation, and what is seen. This is called adherence to rules and observances.

These two kinds of adherence constitute the fetter of adherence to the superior.

What is the fetter of doubt? The nature of uncertainty regarding the truths. What is the fetter of envy? The nature of mental agitation. What is the fetter of miserliness? The nature of mental clinging.

These are what are called the fetters.

### Bonds, Latent Tendencies, Secondary Afflictions, and Entanglements

**Bonds** (*bandhana*): What are called the fetters are also called bonds. Bonds are of three kinds: the bond of attachment, the bond of aversion, and the bond of delusion.

**Latent tendencies, subtle and extensive** (*anuśaya*): There are seven kinds. What are the seven? First, the latent tendency of attachment; second, the latent tendency of aversion; third, the latent tendency of desire for existence; fourth, the latent tendency of conceit; fifth, the latent tendency of ignorance; sixth, the latent tendency of views; seventh, the latent tendency of doubt.

[The text then enumerates each latent tendency by realm (desire, form, formless) and by path of abandonment (seeing suffering, seeing origination, seeing cessation, seeing the path, and meditation): attachment has 5 types in the desire realm; aversion has 5 types in the desire realm; desire for existence has 10 types (5 in the form realm, 5 in the formless realm); conceit has 15 types (5 per realm); ignorance has 15 types (5 per realm); views have 36 types (12 per realm, comprising identity-view, extreme view, wrong view, adherence to views, adherence to rules and observances distributed across the four paths of seeing and the path of meditation); doubt has 12 types (4 per realm, one for each of the four paths of seeing).]

**Secondary afflictions** (*upakleśa*): All the latent tendencies are also called secondary afflictions. There are secondary afflictions that are not latent tendencies, namely the defiled aggregates of formations produced in association with mind, excluding the latent tendencies.

**Entanglements** (*paryavasthāna*): There are eight entanglements. What are the eight? First, torpor (*styāna*); second, drowsiness (*middha*); third, agitation (*auddhatya*); fourth, regret (*kaukṛtya*); fifth, envy (*īrṣyā*); sixth, miserliness (*mātsarya*); seventh, shamelessness (*āhrīkya*); eighth, lack of moral dread (*anapatrāpya*).

### The Ten Knowledges

**Cognitions** (*jñāna*): There are ten kinds of knowledge. What are the ten? First, dharma-knowledge (*dharmajñāna*); second, subsequent knowledge (*anvayajñāna*); third, knowledge of others' minds (*paracittajñāna*); fourth, conventional knowledge (*saṃvṛtijñāna*); fifth, knowledge of suffering (*duḥkhajñāna*); sixth, knowledge of origination (*samudayajñāna*); seventh, knowledge of cessation (*nirodhajñāna*); eighth, knowledge of the path (*mārgajñāna*); ninth, knowledge of exhaustion (*kṣayajñāna*); tenth, knowledge of non-arising (*anutpādajñāna*).

What is dharma-knowledge? Uncontaminated knowledge regarding the conditioned dharmas pertaining to the desire realm, their causes, their cessation, and the path of their abandonment. Further, dharma-knowledge is the uncontaminated knowledge within the ground of dharma-knowledge.

What is subsequent knowledge? Uncontaminated knowledge regarding the conditioned dharmas pertaining to the form and formless realms, their causes, their cessation, and the path of their abandonment. Further, subsequent knowledge is the uncontaminated knowledge within the ground of subsequent knowledge.

What is knowledge of others' minds? Knowledge arising from cultivation, the fruit of practice, based on cultivation and attainment without regression. By this wisdom one knows the mind and mental factors of others operating in the desire realm and the form realm, as well as a portion of uncontaminated dharmas made manifest and present.

What is conventional knowledge? All contaminated knowledge.

What is knowledge of suffering? Uncontaminated knowledge produced by attending to the five appropriated aggregates as impermanent, suffering, empty, and without self.

What is knowledge of origination? Uncontaminated knowledge produced by attending to contaminated causes as cause, origination, arising, and condition.

What is knowledge of cessation? Uncontaminated knowledge produced by attending to cessation as cessation, peace, excellence, and escape.

What is knowledge of the path? Uncontaminated knowledge produced by attending to the path as path, suchness, practice, and deliverance.

What is knowledge of exhaustion? One has fully understood suffering, abandoned origination, realized cessation, and cultivated the path. Based on this: knowledge, vision, awareness, wisdom, understanding, clarity, and direct present realization.

What is knowledge of non-arising? Because one has fully understood suffering, there is nothing further to be understood; because one has abandoned origination, there is nothing further to be abandoned; because one has realized cessation, there is nothing further to be realized; because one has cultivated the path, there is nothing further to be cultivated. Based on this: knowledge, vision, awareness, wisdom, understanding, clarity, and direct present realization.

### Views and Direct Realization

**Views** (*darśana*): Although those cognitions are also views, there are views that are not cognitions. These are the eight patient acceptances (*kṣānti*) arising at the edge of direct realization: patient acceptance of the knowledge of suffering regarding dharmas, patient acceptance of subsequent knowledge of suffering, patient acceptance of the knowledge of origination regarding dharmas, patient acceptance of subsequent knowledge of origination, patient acceptance of the knowledge of cessation regarding dharmas, patient acceptance of subsequent knowledge of cessation, patient acceptance of the knowledge of the path regarding dharmas, and patient acceptance of subsequent knowledge of the path.

**Direct realization** (*abhisamaya*): Those cognitions and views are also direct realization.

### Formations Not Associated with Mind: Definitions

What is acquisition? The obtaining of dharmas. What is the attainment of signlessness? Having separated from attachment to the third dhyāna but not yet separated from attachment above, preceded by the intention of renunciation, with the nature of cessation of mind and mental factors. What is the attainment of cessation? Having separated from the attachment of the base of nothingness, preceded by the intention of stopping perception, with the nature of cessation of mind and mental factors. What is the state of signlessness? Being born among the beings of the realm of no-perception, with the nature of cessation of mind and mental factors. What is the life-faculty? The lifespan of the three realms. What is homogeneous class? The mutual similarity of sentient beings of the same type. What is acquisition of place? Obtaining domains. What is acquisition of phenomena? Obtaining the aggregates. What is acquisition of bases? Obtaining the internal and external bases. What is birth? The accomplishment of the aggregates. What is aging? The maturing of the aggregates. What is abiding? The non-destruction of formations. What is impermanence? The destruction of formations. What are name-groups? Designating terms. What are sentence-groups? Complete phrases. What are syllable-groups? The letters themselves.

### The Three Unconditioned Dharmas

What is space? The basis that allows movement; it is without obstruction, and the various kinds of form cannot completely cover it. This is called space.

What is non-analytical cessation? Cessation without separation. What is analytical cessation? Cessation through separation.

---

Therefore it is said that dharmas are of five kinds: first, form dharmas; second, mind dharmas; third, mental-factor dharmas; fourth, dharmas of formations not associated with mind; fifth, unconditioned dharmas.
