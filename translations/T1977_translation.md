# Resolving Doubts about the Pure Land

## *Jìngtǔ Yíbiàn* (淨土疑辨)

Taishō 1977

Composed by Zhūhóng (*Zhūhóng*, 袾宏, Liánchí, 1535–1615) of the Míng dynasty. A doctrinal treatise.

*Note: This is a concise but powerful defense of Pure Land practice by the great Ming-dynasty master Zhūhóng (Liánchí), one of the "Four Eminent Monks of the Late Ming." Written as a dialogue, it addresses the common Chan Buddhist objection that the Pure Land is merely a metaphor for the purified mind, not a real realm. Zhūhóng argues that Pure Land and Chan are complementary, not opposed, and sharply criticizes those who use empty talk of "no-characteristics" to disparage actual Pure Land practice.*

### Preface

Chan Master Liánchí (*Liánchí*, 蓮池) composed this Resolving Doubts about the Pure Land, which topples the banner of arrogance and shatters the net of delusion. It is like a lamp obtained in darkness, like a treasure found in poverty: truly a compassionate vessel for crossing the sea of suffering. Even the discussions of Tiāntái (*Tiāntái*, 天台) and Yǒngmíng (*Yǒngmíng*, 永明) have not been as precise, penetrating, and brilliantly clear as this. Those who cultivate the Pure Land should above all take it to heart.

Some may question: "At the end he warns against those who recklessly speak, inviting bitter retribution without end. Are his words not excessive?"

I say: "They are not excessive."

Consider the Tang dynasty case of Fáng Zhù (*Fáng Zhù*, 房翥), who suddenly died and reached the underworld. The King said: "According to the case records, you once encouraged an old man to recite the buddha's name, and he has already been born in the Pure Land. Thanks to this merit, you too are fit to be born in the Pure Land." He was then released and returned to life. Master Lǚ (*Lǚshī*, 呂師) said: "Those of the *icchantika* type who do not believe in the Western Land, who cannot cultivate themselves and further obstruct others from cultivating: they enter hell as fast as an arrow shot."

Now consider: it is the same oral karma. Encouraging someone to recite the buddha's name also causes birth in the Pure Land. Obstructing someone from reciting the buddha's name sends one to hell as fast as an arrow. Those who indulge their oral karma should truly be afraid! Therefore I say: "Chan Master Liánchí's words are not excessive."

End of preface.

### The Dialogue

Someone asked: "The teaching of the Pure Land is merely a representation of the dharma. The wise should directly awaken to the Chan school. Yet now you only praise and commend the Pure Land. Is this not clinging to phenomenal appearances and failing to understand the principle of the nature?"

Answer: "Returning to the source, the nature is without duality; in skillful means, there are many gates. If you grasp this meaning, Chan and Pure Land, though different paths, reach the same destination. Your doubts should instantly dissolve like ice. The ancients mutually promoted both, and their treatments are more than sufficient. As the great master Zhōngfēng (*Zhōngfēng*, 中峰) said: 'Chan is the Chan of the Pure Land; the Pure Land is the Pure Land of Chan. Yet those who cultivate must value deep entry through a single gate.' These few words are a settled pronouncement for ten thousand ages, never to be altered.

"Therefore the Bodhisattva Mahāsthāmaprāpta (*Dàshìzhì*, 大勢至) attained the buddha-recitation samādhi and said: 'Through the mind of buddha-recitation, one enters the patience of the unborn.' The Bodhisattva Samantabhadra (*Pǔxián*, 普賢) entered the inconceivable liberation of the Avataṃsaka and said: 'At the end of life, may I be born in the land of peace and bliss.' These two great bodhisattvas, one attending the teaching master of the Sahā world and the other attending the guide of the Land of Peaceful Nurture, should each have established separate approaches. Yet they harmonize and interpenetrate without mutual obstruction. Everyone has heard and knows this. How can you still cling to a one-sided view?

"Moreover, when you say 'the Pure Land is a representation of the dharma,' do you not mean that the purified mind is itself the Pure Land, with no additional world of the seven jewels? Then you might as well say that a wholesome mind is itself heaven, with no additional Yāma or Trāyastriṃśa? That an evil mind is itself hell, with no additional swords, cauldrons, or boiling water? That a deluded mind is itself the animal realm, with no additional fur-covered, horn-bearing beings?

"Furthermore, you like to talk about the principle of the nature and dislike discussion of phenomenal appearances. This is entirely because you want to show that you are of the lofty sort and fear being told that you do not understand the nature-principle. Alas! If you had truly penetrated the nature-principle, you would know that apart from phenomena there is no principle, and apart from characteristics there is no nature. They inherently interpenetrate. Why must you insist on abandoning phenomena to seek principle, or leaving characteristics to find the nature? Moreover, lands are of four kinds: do you say there is only the Land of Eternally Quiescent Light and no Land of Real Reward and Adornment?

"If you simply say 'no characteristics' as a way to seem lofty, then anyone of slightly keen ability who has read a couple of sūtras and memorized a few *gōng'àn* can do the same. What is so difficult about that? Since you claim to have thoroughly penetrated your own mind and that every place is a Pure Land, let me test you: Would you be willing to dwell in a latrine? Would you be willing to eat from the same trough as dogs, pigs, horses, and cattle? Would you be willing to enter a burial mound and sleep alongside rotting, stinking corpses? Would you be willing to wash, care for, and feed those afflicted with *gaṇḍamāla* disease, with pus, blood, feces, and urine, for months and years on end?

"If in these matters you are joyful and at ease, not bothered in the slightest, then I grant you the right to say that high mountains and level ground are all the Western Land. But if outwardly you force yourself to endure while inwardly doubt and aversion arise, then the boundary of pure and defiled has not yet been emptied, and the feelings of liking and loathing are still present. Yet you open your mouth and loftily discuss the realm beyond the measure of great sages, dismissing the buddha-land and looking down on rebirth in the Pure Land. This may be called deceiving heaven and deluding people, willingly blinding yourself. How bitter! How bitter!

"Again, if you have great power and great vows, willing to plunge headfirst into the sea of birth and death, practicing the bodhisattva path without any fear or hesitation, then I will not force the Pure Land birth upon you. But if you worry that in this land the winds of circumstances are overwhelming and you cannot be your own master; if you worry that meeting a buddha is rare and there is no means to study; if you worry that your power of patience is not yet firm and you cannot save sentient beings in the dangerous places of the three realms; if you worry that at the end of this present body you cannot permanently sever birth and death and avoid future existence; if you worry that with future existence still ahead, discarding one body and receiving another, the road ahead is vast and dark with no known destination: then to abandon the Pure Land and not seek birth there is no small loss.

"This Pure Land dharma gate seems shallow yet is deep, seems near yet is far, seems difficult yet is easy, seems easy yet is difficult. One day you will know this for yourself. Whether you practice Chan or recite the buddha's name, either way is fine; just enter deeply through a single gate. But beware of mistaking brass for gold and being satisfied with small attainment, as if peering through a tube with a narrow understanding, and then recklessly talking, slandering the Pure Land. It is like a deranged child cursing his parents: though the parents, out of compassionate love, may not punish him, what of the laws of heaven that do not permit it, and the laws of the king that must execute him? The retribution for those who speak recklessly is exactly like this. How frightening! How grievous!"

### Postscript

The retired layman Wēixiū (*Wēixiū*, 微休) proclaimed the Way of learning at Jìngjiāng (*Jìngjiāng*, 靖江), in the tradition of the stringed-instrument governance of Wǔchéng. After retiring from office, he devoted himself to the Avataṃsaka, carving the Sanskrit text into block-printed volumes for the convenience of reading and study. He further broadly promoted the Pure Land, having my Resolving Doubts engraved and widely circulated. To aspire to the gate of the ten mysteries and to lodge one's spirit in the realm of the nine lotuses: this may truly be called a profound accord with the vow-king Samantabhadra, a wondrous entry into the nature-ocean of Vairocana. It is a pity he passed away early, preventing Cáo Lǔchuān (*Cáo Lǔchuān*, 曹魯川) from seeing it.
