# 大唐青龍寺三朝供奉大德行狀
## Record of the Conduct of the Great Worthy Who Served Three Reigns at Qinglong Temple of the Great Tang

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2057

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

Our late master, whose taboo name was Huiguo, Preceptor, was of the surname Ma and a native of Guiming village, Wannian county, Jingzhao prefecture. At the young age of nine, he followed the Holy Buddha Cloister. The late National Teacher of Three Reigns, the Inner Chapel mantra-reciter, the imperially bestowed purple-robed śramaṇa whose taboo name was Preceptor Tanzhen, established his aspiration to study the sūtras.

At age seventeen, because his preceptor was constantly in the Inner Chapel reciting mantras and did not go out, he went to Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra at Xingshan Temple and sought instruction in the true words of the Great Buddha-Crown, the Wish-Fulfilling, and others.

At age nineteen, Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra conferred the abhiṣeka, and he scattered flowers. He obtained the Dharma-Wheel-Turning Bodhisattva. The master said: "In the kingdom of South India, I scattered flowers and obtained this very deity. Now it is no different. After me, you shall broadly transmit the comprehensive esoteric teaching, no differently from me."

In the third month of the eighth year of Dali (773 CE), by imperial decree, a Vaipulya practice-hall was established at Ci'en Temple. The Preceptor of the Holy Buddha Cloister memorialized: "By imperial decree, a Vaipulya practice-hall was established at Ci'en Temple. This humble monk has two novices who have reached the age of twenty and are fit to receive the full precepts. Not daring to presume, I dare not give them tonsure on my own authority. I humbly beg the imperial grace to permit me to return to the temple and confer the precepts, robes, and bowls upon the two novices."

By imperial decree, a razor was bestowed. Before the great Buddha-hall of Qinglong Temple, the decree was conferred upon the preceptor to tonsure the two novices. Imperially bestowed kāṣāya robes and bowls, two sets each, with ordination certificates for residence at the temple. An imperial messenger escorted the preceptor to Ci'en Temple. Upon completion, he reentered the palace to express gratitude: "This humble monk of one world-realm expresses gratitude for the imperial grace in bestowing upon the two novices their ordination. The robes, bowls, razor, ordination certificates, and the imperial messenger's escort to the Ci'en Temple practice-hall are completed. This humble monk is overwhelmed with gratitude."

After ordination was complete, at age twenty-two, he further sought instruction from Preceptor Xuanchao, a disciple of Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha, in the Great Compassion Garbhadhātu Vairocana Great Yoga teaching, the Susiddhikara Great Yoga method, and the yoga methods of the various deities. Each was personally and graciously transmitted by direct instruction.

He also sought instruction from the Tripiṭaka master at Daxingshan Temple in the Vajraśekhara Great Yoga Great Teaching King Sūtra Dharma, and the secret mudrās of the yoga of the various deities, receiving personal and direct instruction.

Everything the late master had received in imperial bestowals within the palace, he presented entirely to the Tripiṭaka master as recompense for the grace of receiving the Dharma. Each time, at the foot of the pavilion, he restricted his food and set a schedule, reciting mantras several times in each session.

At age twenty-five, by special imperial decree, he was summoned into the palace. At the Hall of Long Life, there was a decree summoning him. In the audience, he was asked: "Master, what efficacious accomplishments do you have?" He replied humbly: "This humble monk does not yet have efficacious accomplishments." By imperial decree, he was told to demonstrate immediately. Eight youths were summoned and subjected to invocations and empowerments, and all the matters the imperial command inquired about were accomplished successfully, including the turning-vase and joining-bamboo tests, all of which succeeded. The emperor was greatly pleased.

In the tenth year of Dali (775 CE), by separate imperial decree, the Eastern Pagoda Cloister within the temple was bestowed, and a Vairocana abhiṣeka practice-hall was established with seven monks for mantra-recitation.

In the eleventh year of Dali (776 CE), he performed empowerment for Emperor Daizong, who recovered immediately. He was bestowed an imperial gift of a pair of purple robes. He memorialized that he dared not accept, and immediately returned them. The decree stated: "We have already bestowed purple robes upon the master. Why will you not accept?" He memorialized: "A humble monk of one world-realm, receiving the purple, is overwhelmed with gratitude. However, as my preceptor previously received the purple by imperial bestowment, a disciple should not be made equal to his master." The emperor said: "The master is greatly filial. It was Our error." The decree stated: "That he, of a different lineage and surname, should maintain such reverence and filial devotion, hereafter he is worthy to be a National Teacher." He was then bestowed a pair of brown robes.

By imperial decree, he performed empowerments for Princess Huayang, who recovered within three days. Later, during the hours of shen and wei, the princess suddenly could not speak. The emperor and the palace women all came forward. The preceptor memorialized: "It is hot. I request that Your Majesty and the palace women temporarily step back from the steps, and this humble monk will perform the empowerment and she will immediately recover." The princess then recovered, speaking clearly and distinctly. The emperor was greatly pleased and bestowed a hundred bolts of silk and a set of garments. He expressed gratitude: "This humble monk of one world-realm thanks the imperial grace for the bestowed silk and garments. I am overwhelmed with gratitude."

During the Dali era, all imperial bestowals of money and goods, exceeding a thousand strings of cash, were entirely used to fund meritorious works at the pagoda. In the thirteenth year of Dali (778 CE), he memorialized requesting permission to make a pilgrimage to the Southern Terrace on two occasions, which was granted. At the Avalokiteśvara Terrace, the preceptor recited mantras. In the deep of the night, the Great Holy Avalokiteśvara appeared within a great moon-disc, manifesting a great body. The radiance was like broad daylight, with auspicious clouds pure and bright. At the same time, several hundred thousand people gazed upon it from afar and made reverent obeisance.

In the thirteenth year of Dali (778 CE), by decree, at the Inner Chapel of the Hall of Long Life, the Tripiṭaka master who had transmitted the Dharma and conferred abhiṣeka across three reigns passed away. The decree addressed Huiguo: "After the Tripiṭaka master's passing, We feared the buddha-dharma would have no one to carry it forward. Now We have heard that the master has learned the great Dharma, having received it entirely from the National Teacher's preceptor, having received the mantra-recitation Dharma-gates, though much may have been forgotten. When in the future We summon you to inquire about the Way, that will be the master's position."

On the fifteenth day of the tenth month of the thirteenth year of Dali (778 CE), the Imperial Commissioner Li Xiancheng made the proclamation. At the beginning of the Jianzhong era (780 CE), the monk Bianhong of the kingdom of Kalingga, coming from his homeland, brought a set of bronze cymbals to present to the Holy Buddha Cloister, two conches, and four bronze vases to present to the preceptor as offerings, and sought instruction in the Garbhadhātu Vairocana great Dharma.

In the second year of Jianzhong (781 CE), the Silla monk Hyeil brought articles of faith from his homeland and presented them to the preceptor, seeking instruction in the Garbhadhātu, Vajradhātu, Susiddhikara, and other teachings, along with the yoga of the various deities, thirty texts in all. After completing the transmission and achieving thorough mastery, he returned to his homeland, where he broadly propagated the great teaching. Through sincere fasting and mantra-recitation, accomplishments manifested before him, and he ascended bodily into the sky in broad daylight. In the palace of the king of India, gazing up in reverence, they sought to obtain his Dharma. A voice from the sky said: "In the great Tang kingdom of the West, there is a secret Dharma. The place of the Dharma is Qinglong Temple."

That same year, the Silla monk Wuzhen received the Garbhadhātu Vairocana and the yoga teaching methods of the various deities for mantra-recitation. In the fifth year of Zhenyuan (789 CE), he traveled to Central India. The Mahāvairocana Sūtra Sanskrit manuscripts and other sūtras were lost when he died in Tibet.

The disciples of this cloister, the monks Yiming, Yiman, and Yicheng, simultaneously sought from the preceptor instruction in the Vairocana Garbhadhātu, Susiddhikara, and other sūtras, thirty texts in all.

In the fifth year of Zhenyuan (789 CE), by imperial decree, seven monks were ordered to pray for rain at the great Buddha-hall of the temple. On the seventh day, at night, the rain was sufficient. Each was bestowed a bundle of silk and ten strings of tea. A memorial of gratitude was submitted. By imperial decree, the dragon was escorted by the Right Guard to welcome the True Body into the palace.

In the fourth month of the sixth year of Zhenyuan (790 CE), by imperial decree, the monk Huiguo was summoned into the palace and, at the Hall of Long Life, performed mantra-recitation for the nation. After over seventy days in the palace, he was released to return. Each person was bestowed thirty bolts of silk and twenty strings of tea. Afterward, they took turns in rotation. Seasonal clothing and provisions for the three festivals were bestowed.

That year, Chief Minister Du Huangchang and Chief Minister Wei personally came to receive the abhiṣeka and study mantra-recitation. He again memorialized requesting permission for a terrace pilgrimage, which was decreed permissible, and he was appointed Great Worthy of Guanyin Temple.

From the ninth year of Zhenyuan (793 CE) through the thirteenth year (797 CE), Yiheng, Yiyi, Yizheng, Yi, Yicao, Yiyun, Zhixing, Yimin, Xingjian, Yuantong, Yilun, Yibo, Yirun, and the lay disciples Wu Yin and Kaipi, approximately fifty persons, studied the Dharma.

In the fourteenth year of Zhenyuan (798 CE), in the fifth month, a great drought occurred. In the first ten days of the fifth month, by imperial decree, he prayed for rain for seven days within the Inner Chapel, concentrating on mantra-recitation. The rain fell sufficiently. He was bestowed a bundle of silk and ten strings of tea. He expressed gratitude: "We humble monks possess no merit or practice. Heaven has sent down sweet rain. It is the emperor's transformative influence. We monks express gratitude for the silk and tea. We are overwhelmed with gratitude."

In the fifteenth year of Zhenyuan (799 CE), in the last ten days of the eighth month, he performed empowerment for the Crown Prince, who recovered within three days. Each was bestowed twenty bolts of silk, fifty bolts of Wu damask, and twenty strings of tea. "We humble monks of one world-realm express gratitude for the imperial grace bestowing damask, silk, and tea. We are overwhelmed with gratitude."

On the sixteenth day of the eleventh month in the winter of the sixteenth year (800 CE), Jiao, the Military Commissioner of Shenwei Army, invited the preceptor to his military residence for offerings, and also had a portrait of the preceptor painted and decorated and sent to the cloister.

In the eighteenth year (802 CE), the preceptor fell ill and his condition gradually worsened. He submitted a memorial requesting retirement. By imperial command, he was released to return, with orders for the temple to care for him: "It is Our wish to be constant from beginning to end. You must not decline." That year, in the middle of the eighth month, he bequeathed his robes and bowls, entrusting them to Yiming and six others for their use.

In the nineteenth year of Zhenyuan (803 CE), the Japanese monk Kūkai, by imperial decree, brought a mantle of cloth and articles of faith from his nation, exceeding five hundred strings of cash, and presented them to the preceptor, who used them entirely to decorate and furnish the practice-hall for offerings. He sought instruction in the Great Compassion Garbhadhātu, the Vajradhātu, and the yoga teaching methods of the various deities, fifty texts of sūtras. Immediately he perceived the visionary realm of the Sanskrit letter "a" within the sun and moon discs, which appeared and entered his mouth.

On the fifth day of the eighth month of the twenty-first year of Zhenyuan (805 CE), the era name was changed to the first year of Yongzhen. On the fifteenth day of the twelfth month, facing north, he passed away.

In the first month of the first year of Yuanhe (806 CE), on the seventeenth day, his disciples, both monastic and lay, numbering approximately a thousand, conducted the funeral procession to a site beside the stūpa of the Longyuan Great Master in Meng Village. Later, on the twenty-first day of the eighth month of the second year of Baoli (826 CE), Yiyi, Shenda, and Yidan moved his burial to Biaolin Village beside the Chan River and built a stūpa for reburial.

In the fourth year of Kaicheng (839 CE), on the thirteenth day of the first month, the Japanese monk Engyō brought Dharma-robes and articles of faith.
