# 是法非法經
## 是法非法經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0048

Translated from the Chinese. 後漢 安世高譯

---

## Fascicle 1: Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Dharma and Non-Dharma

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the Jetavana of Anāthapiṇḍada's Garden in the land of Śrāvastī. At that time, the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus, and the bhikṣus responded: "So be it." The bhikṣus listened attentively to the Buddha. The Buddha said: "There is the dharma of the worthy one — bhikṣus, listen to it. There is also the dharma of the unworthy one — listen carefully, know it thoroughly, reflect upon it thoroughly, and it shall be spoken." The bhikṣus said "so be it" and received the teaching from the Buddha, and the Buddha then spoke thus.

"What, bhikṣus, is the dharma of the unworthy one? If a bhikṣu is of noble lineage and delights in the path, desiring to cultivate the path, yet there are other fellow-student bhikṣus not of noble lineage, and on account of his noble lineage this bhikṣu exalts himself and deceives the others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one.

"What is the dharma of the worthy one? The worthy one reflects in practice: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on noble lineage. Can I sever craving and desire? Can I sever anger and ill-will? Can I sever delusion?' Or perhaps there is a bhikṣu not of noble lineage who yet has the skillful means to receive the dharma, to speak in accordance with the dharma, to practice as required, to follow the dharma in practice — on account of this he gains good repute. He practices in accordance with the dharma and follows the truth of the dharma; he does not exalt himself, nor does he deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu has a highly handsome appearance, superior to other bhikṣus, and on account of his handsome appearance exalts himself and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one. The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one reflects: 'A handsome form — it is not necessary for me to rely on this. Can I sever craving and desire? Can I sever anger and ill-will? Can I sever delusion?' Or perhaps there is a bhikṣu who is not handsome, but who receives and practices the dharma to whatever extent he can, thereby gaining praise and good repute; he receives the truth of the dharma and practices following the dharma, does not exalt himself nor deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu speaks beautifully and preaches with skill, superior to other bhikṣus, and on account of his beautiful speech and skilled preaching exalts himself and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one. The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one reflects in practice: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on beautiful words, nor on knowing how to preach beautifully. Can I sever craving and desire? Can I sever anger and ill-will? Can I sever delusion?'

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu does not speak beautifully nor preach with skill, yet receives teaching in accordance with the dharma and follows it in practice to whatever extent he can, thereby gaining respect and good repute; he receives and practices the truth of the dharma, following the dharma, does not exalt himself nor deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or there is heard of a certain bhikṣu who is of advanced age, with many acquaintances, well-known and prosperous — superior to other bhikṣus — and on account of his advanced age and many acquaintances he exalts himself with pride and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply reflects in practice: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on advanced age, nor on many acquaintances, nor on receiving abundant benefit. Can I sever craving and desire? Can I sever anger and ill-will? Can I sever delusion?'

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu is not of advanced age, nor has many acquaintances, nor receives abundant benefit, but simply receives the dharma, wishes to follow the dharma, wishes to practice following the dharma to whatever extent he can, and thereby gains good repute. He follows the truth of the dharma, does not exalt himself, does not take pride, nor does he deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or there is heard of a certain bhikṣu who knows the sūtras and can expound them, who knows the Vinaya, who has mastered and penetrated throughout the scriptures — superior to other bhikṣus — and on account of this mastery and thorough penetration of the scriptures, exalts himself with pride and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply reflects in practice: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on mastery, nor on thorough penetration of the scriptures. Can I sever craving and desire? Can I sever anger and ill-will? Can I sever delusion?'

"Or perhaps there is a bhikṣu who has no mastery, nor thorough penetration of the scriptures, but simply receives and follows the dharma, rightly seeking to practice following the dharma, and thereby gains respect and good repute. He upholds the dharma, practices truthfully in accord with the dharma, does not exalt himself, does not take pride, nor does he deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu seeks alms for himself without relying on acquaintances, visits no more than seven households, sits in one place, eats at one time, and takes nothing afterward — superior to other bhikṣus — and on account of eating once and taking nothing afterward, exalts himself with pride and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one. The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply practices: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on eating once and taking nothing afterward. Through this, can I sever craving and desire? Can I sever anger and ill-will? Can I sever delusion?' Or perhaps a bhikṣu does not eat only once nor refrain from taking food afterward, but simply receives and follows the dharma, rightly receives and follows the dharma, and thereby gains respect and good repute; he follows the truth of the dharma, does not exalt himself, does not take pride, nor does he deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu dwells among burial mounds, and is known only for wearing the three robes — superior to other bhikṣus — and on account of this reputation, exalts himself with pride and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply reflects in practice: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on being known for wearing the three robes. Can I sever craving and desire? Can I sever anger and ill-will? Can I sever delusion?' Or perhaps a bhikṣu has no such reputation for wearing the three robes, but simply receives and follows the dharma, rightly receives and follows the dharma, and thereby gains respect and good repute; he follows the truth of the dharma, does not exalt himself, does not take pride, nor does he deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a certain bhikṣu dwells in the open air, or beneath trees, or in uninhabited wilderness and among burial mounds, sleeping wherever he finds himself — superior to other bhikṣus — and on account of this, exalts himself with pride and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply reflects in practice: 'It is not necessary for me to rely on dwelling in the open air, beneath trees, or in uninhabited wilderness. Can I sever craving and desire? Can I sever anger and ill-will? Can I sever delusion?'

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu has none of the qualities spoken of above, but simply receives and follows the dharma, rightly receives and follows the dharma, and thereby gains respect and good repute; he follows the truth of the dharma, does not exalt himself, does not take pride, nor does he deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu has already attained the first dhyāna, superior to other bhikṣus, and on account of the first dhyāna, exalts himself with pride and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one.

"The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply practices the first dhyāna; as the Buddha has said, one knows this for oneself. One receives the truth of this dharma, does not exalt oneself, does not take pride, nor does one deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu has attained the second dhyāna, or the third dhyāna, or the fourth dhyāna — as has been said of the first dhyāna — this is the dharma of the worthy one.

"Or perhaps a bhikṣu understands the cultivation of emptiness in mind, or understands the cultivation of consciousness in mind, or understands the cultivation of impermanence in mind, or understands the cultivation of neither-perception-nor-non-perception in mind — superior to other bhikṣus — and on account of attaining this practice of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, exalts himself with pride and deceives others — this is not the dharma of the worthy one. The worthy one is not like this. The worthy one simply reflects in practice upon neither-perception-nor-non-perception; as the Buddha has said, one reflects: 'I have attained this.' One receives and follows the truth of the dharma, does not exalt oneself, does not take pride, nor does one deceive others — this is the dharma of the worthy one."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! I have spoken the dharma of the worthy one, and I have also spoken the dharma of the unworthy one. Bhikṣus, you should reflect upon yourselves; you should also reflect upon the dharma of the worthy one. Having reflected on the dharma of the unworthy one, you should practice the dharma of the worthy one, abandon the dharma of the unworthy one, and receive the dharma of the worthy one. Bhikṣus who follow the dharma should practice accordingly."

The Buddha spoke thus. The bhikṣus received it, held it in their hearts, and put it into practice.
