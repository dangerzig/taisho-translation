# 般泥洹後灌臘經
## Sūtra on the Bathing and Pravaraṇā Ceremonies After the Parinirvāṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0391

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the Jetavana of Anāthapiṇḍada in the kingdom of Śrāvastī, seated in assembly together with a great Saṃgha of bhikṣus, devas, and lay people, expounding the Dharma.

Ānanda came forward, knelt on both knees with palms joined, and addressed the Buddha: "O Deva above all Devas! I wish to ask a question, and I pray that the Buddha will explain it. After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, what should the four assemblies of disciples, bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, do on the eighth day of the fourth month and the fifteenth day of the seventh month for the bathing of the image and the Pravaraṇā ceremonies?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The bathing of the Buddha image and the Pravaraṇā are for those who seek merit and aspire to liberation from the world. Each person should reduce their money and valuables, setting aside what is precious and dear, in order to seek the merit of liberation from the world. One should provide lamps for the monastery and burn incense, and use such offerings for scriptures and images. One may also make offerings to teachers and give to the poor. It is fitting to hold a vegetarian feast assembly. However, one must not make a vow to give and then fail to fulfill it. To do so is to be indebted to the Buddha in this present life. Since it is one's own heart and mouth that made the pledge, one will incur the transgression of false speech.

"Why is this? When one comes before the basin to pay reverence to the Buddha and with one's own hands bathes the Buddha image with the five kinds of fragrant water, and the teacher pronounces the dakṣiṇā blessing and vow, at that very moment, devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits all clearly witness and know the pledge. If this person has drawn upon the entire family's wealth, encroaching on the shares belonging to their spouse and children, in order to seek blessings and benefit, and then fails to give; they will incur five transgressions and fall into the three evil destinies.

"What are the five? First, one's wealth diminishes day by day. Second, one becomes prone to forgetfulness and the loss of things. Third, in one's livelihood, whatever one undertakes brings no profit. Fourth, one enters the hell of Mount Tai and undergoes punishment there; the suffering is beyond words. Fifth, in future lives one may be reborn as a slave, as an ox or horse, a mule or donkey, a camel, or as a pig or sheep. These are the five.

"Furthermore, the three evil destinies: first, one falls into the preta realm and becomes a hungry ghost; second, one is reborn among birds and beasts as an animal; third, in the naraka one passes through the eighteen hells; one's transgressions are beyond counting.

"The fifteenth day of the seventh month is a time when one turns toward one's parents of seven lifetimes, the five kinds of relatives, and those who have fallen into evil destinies and suffer greatly; dedicating the merit of reverencing the Buddha, wishing for their liberation from sorrow and suffering. This is called the Pravaraṇā ceremony.

"The Buddha is king of the three realms above and below the heavens; he does not consume the possessions of worldly people. Whatever is offered should be distributed among the Saṃgha; it is a great transgression to take it all for oneself. If there is no Saṃgha among whom to distribute it, give to the poor, the solitary, and the aged and infirm. This is planting roots of goodness."

All the disciples, having heard this sūtra, rejoiced, paid reverence to the Buddha, and departed.
