# 佛說水沫所漂經
## Sūtra on What Is Carried Away by Foam and Water Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0106

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was on the bank of the Ganges River in the country of Ayodhyā, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus. At that time there was a great mass of foam being carried along by the water. The World-Honored One, seeing it, addressed the bhikṣus: "Do you see this mass of foam being carried along by the Ganges?"

The bhikṣus replied: "Indeed so, World-Honored One!"

The World-Honored One said: "This mass of foam: if a person of discernment carefully observes it, examines it, and analyzes it, that person, having seen and carefully observed and examined and analyzed it, knows it to be empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and also without solidity. What solidity could this mass of water-foam have? Just so, all form, past, future, and present, whether coarse or fine, whether far or near: a person of discernment carefully observes it, examines it, and analyzes it. That person, having observed and examined and analyzed it, knows it to be empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and also without solidity. What solidity could this aggregate of form have?

"It is like rain in summer, when bubbles appear on the water: some arising, some bursting. A person of discernment carefully observes them, examines them, and analyzes them. That person, having seen and carefully observed and examined and analyzed them, knows them to be empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and also without solidity. What solidity could these water bubbles have? Just so, the feelings within this body, past, future, and present, whether coarse or fine, whether beautiful or ugly, whether far or near: a person of discernment carefully observes them, examines them, and analyzes them. Having observed and examined and analyzed them, one knows them to be empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and also without solidity. What solidity could this aggregate of feeling have?

"It is like a mirage shimmering brightly at midday after the summer rains, when there are no clouds. A person of discernment observes it, examines it, and analyzes it. Having observed and examined and analyzed it, one knows it to be empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and also without solidity. What solidity could this mirage have? Just so, all perception, past, future, and present, whether coarse or fine, whether beautiful or ugly, whether far or near: a person of discernment observes it, examines it, and analyzes it. Having observed and examined and analyzed it, it is empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and without solidity. What solidity could this aggregate of perception have?

"It is like a person who leaves a city or village seeking something solid, and taking an implement goes to a great grove of trees. If he sees a plantain tree, flourishing and fine, tall and straight, with no branches or leaves, and cuts its root, lops its top, and strips it section by section, peeling off layer after layer, he cannot find any pith inside, let alone anything solid. A person of discernment carefully observes it, examines it, and analyzes it. Having observed and examined and analyzed it, it is empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and without solidity. What solidity could this plantain tree have? Just so, all formations, past, future, and present, whether coarse or fine, whether beautiful or ugly, whether far or near: a person of discernment observes them, examines them, and analyzes them. Having observed and examined and analyzed them, one knows them to be empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and without solidity. What solidity could this aggregate of formations have?

"It is like a skilled conjurer and his apprentice who display magical arts before a crowd. A person of discernment carefully observes them, examines them, and analyzes them. Having observed and examined and analyzed them, they are empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and without solidity. What solidity could these magical arts have? Just so, all consciousness, past, future, and present, whether coarse or fine, whether beautiful or ugly, whether far or near: a person of discernment carefully observes it, examines it, and analyzes it. Having observed and examined and analyzed it, it is empty and without substance, without coming and without going, and without solidity. What solidity could this aggregate of consciousness have?"

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> Form is like a mass of foam; feeling is like a water bubble;
> Perception is like a summer mirage; formations are like a plantain tree;
> Consciousness is like a magical art: so the Most Honored One has taught.
> If one can carefully observe, examining and analyzing,
> It is empty and without substance. If one can see it thus,
> Examining within this body, as the Great Wise One has taught,
> One should extinguish these three dharmas, and be able to abandon and discard form.
> These formations are likewise, the conjurer's unreal arts.

At that time, the bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully practiced in accordance.
