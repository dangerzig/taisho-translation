# 佛說龍施菩薩本起經
## Sūtra of the Past Origins of the Bodhisattva Nāgadattā, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0558

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Vaiśālī, at the Āmrapālī garden, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, five thousand bodhisattvas, and countless devas and humans.

At that time the Buddha was teaching the sūtra, and the entire assembly was settled. The Bodhisattva Nāgadattā stood before the Buddha. He gave a lion's roar, extolling the Great Vehicle, and spoke of his past lives and accumulated merit and virtue: how he did not cling to body or life, did not reckon self or mine, and sought nothing for himself. He addressed the Buddha:

"In a past age, there was a Pañcaśikha who lived beneath a grove of trees, diligently practicing the Way, his mind free from attachment. He always had compassion for the people of the ten directions and for all crawling creatures, practicing the four immeasurable minds: kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity. He ate only fruits and melons and drank spring water. He did not desire worldly glory and was without greed or attachment. He had attained the five supernatural powers and delighted in them. What are the five? His eyes could see through all things. His ears could hear all sounds. His body could fly through the air. He understood the faculties of all people. And he knew the past and future of his own lives. In those mountains he recited and studied the meaning of the sūtras, day and night without ceasing.

"At that time there was a poisonous snake. Seeing Pañcaśikha reciting the sūtras day and night, it was filled with great joy. It went to Pañcaśikha's dwelling and bowed its head in reverence. It gathered grass to sweep the ground and carried water in its mouth to sprinkle the earth, serving the ascetic without daring to be negligent. It always stayed at his left side listening to the sūtras, never departing. Whatever sūtras Pañcaśikha recited, the poisonous snake would commit them all to memory.

"After several months, winter drew near and the trees, flowers, and fruits were about to be exhausted. Pañcaśikha thought: 'Winter has come, the flowers and fruits are gone, and I have nothing to rely upon. I should now return and stay among people.' He took up his robe and bowl and was about to depart.

"The poisonous snake, seeing this, wept with tears flowing and said to the ascetic: 'Where do you intend to go?' The ascetic replied: 'The cold is coming, there is no shelter, the flowers and fruits are exhausted, and I have no way to sustain myself. Therefore I must leave you and go to an inhabited land.'

"The snake, hearing this, grew even more sorrowful and said: 'With the ascetic here, it is like leaning against a great mountain. Day and night I delight in the Dharma, and my mind does not waver. Now you would leave me with nothing to rely upon. Please have pity on my pitiable self!' The ascetic replied: 'I have this body of the four elements, which requires clothing and food to sustain itself. In these mountains there are no more provisions. Though I have a compassionate heart, I am not free to remain.'

"The snake said: 'In these mountains the trees rise to the sky, the springs flow freely, and a hundred birds sing sweetly. It is a place of great delight. Why leave it? I only wish that the ascetic would not abandon me. If you must go, I wish to follow behind you and serve you. I would not dare stay behind, left here to grieve, with nothing but the sorrow of death.'

"The ascetic replied: 'You are a poisonous snake, hated by all people. Those who see you would harm you, for none delight in your presence. Along the road, tigers, wolves, venomous creatures, birds of prey, and wild beasts would all threaten your life. Though I am truly grieved beyond measure, and though my heart wishes it, I cannot act freely. I ask you to remain here, contemplating the Way and reflecting on virtue, practicing diligently and guarding yourself, enduring all hardships! If we are both well, we shall meet again next year.' The ascetic wept, dried his tears, and departed.

"The snake shed tears and could not stop. Longing to see the ascetic without end, it climbed a tree, gazing into the distance after the ascetic. It watched him walking, observed his path. When the ascetic disappeared from view, it climbed higher. When he again disappeared, it climbed to the very top of the tree, gazing far into the distance, but the ascetic was gone and could no longer be seen.

"The snake's grief deepened. It reproached itself with regret: 'My own sins have brought this about, causing me to lose the good ascetic. In past lives I was foolish and ignorant, committing many evils: lust, anger, darkness, recklessness, laziness, and ignorance. I did not practice diligently. My confusion did not cease, and my mind was not one-pointed. I did not encounter a Buddha's age. I was far removed from the true Dharma, losing great wisdom, separated from the highest light. From suffering I went to suffering, departing from the perfections, falling into the five destinies, becoming insects, moths, fleas, and lice. Now I bear the body of a snake, hated by all people. This is all the fault of my own body, not caused by others. The glory and honor of heaven and the human world are impermanent. How much more so this poison-bearing body of mine? Revolving through birth and death, it is like a turning wheel.'

"At that time the snake, having confessed its faults, grew calm in body and mind. It continued to reproach itself: 'This perishable body is not worth clinging to. I shall not cling to this bodily life, for there is nothing here to be attached to.' It then threw itself from the tree. Before reaching the ground, it fell between forking branches, its body was split in two, and its life came to an end. It was reborn in the Tuṣita heaven. Upon seeing the light, it reflected and recognized its former lives:

"'When I was in the world, my body was that of a poisonous snake. I served an ascetic, practiced what is right and shunned what is wrong, was diligent without ceasing, subdued the evil of Māra in my mind, and regarded my bodily life as no more than dust and sand. Knowing that life is impermanent, I threw myself from the tree. Having ended that life, I have been reborn here.'

"Then in that heaven, together with the celestial maidens and gods, each bearing incense and flowers, they descended and scattered them upon the snake's body. The reborn being said: 'Though this snake body was poisonous and vile, it was of great benefit to me, never failing me. Through diligent practice and a mind free of attachment, ending its life, I was able to ascend to heaven. Now I have come down to repay its kindness.'

"They then went to Pañcaśikha's dwelling, bowed their heads in reverence, offered flowers and incense, and praised his virtues. All together they extolled him: 'This ascetic is without equal. He practices great compassion without distinguishing between those close and far, teaching all beings and leading them away from the three evil paths. Though I was a poisonous snake, he regarded me as his own child, caring for all beings. His merit is great. How could I ever fully repay his kindness?' Having said this, they returned to the Tuṣita heaven."

> Together with the devas and humans,
> They went before Maitreya
> And all bowed their heads in reverence,
> Their minds all equally at peace.
> Seeing Maitreya, they rejoiced.
> After paying homage, they sat to one side.
> Maitreya taught them the Dharma,
> And all attained the unborn.

> After their heavenly lives ended,
> They were born in the human world,
> In the household of the elder Supuṇya.
> Born as a girl of brilliant mind,
> Beautiful, named Nāgadattā,
> She had cast off all lustful desires.

> When the Buddha came to the house,
> From between his brows he radiated light.
> The girl was in the bathing room,
> Her mind struck with amazement.
> She climbed the tower to look out
> And saw the Buddha, perfect in merit,
> His faculties all still and settled,
> His thirty-two marks shining bright.

> The girl's heart was filled with joy:
> "Now I have found true peace.
> I shall make offerings to the Buddha-Dharma."
> And she generated the mind of enlightenment.

> When Māra heard of this,
> In his heart he was filled with grief:
> "This girl has set her mind upon the Way;
> She will empty my realm of its people."

> He descended and took her father's form
> And spoke at length of trials and troubles:
> "The Buddha is present in the world today,
> His merit most honored and rare.
> The bodhisattva path is full of hardship;
> Arhatship is swiftly and easily won."

> The girl immediately replied:
> "Father's words have no true reason.
> The Buddha's wisdom is like empty space;
> The arhat's is like a mustard seed.
> Seeing it thus, the lesser path has no noble ones.
> The Buddha's merit is like a great ocean,
> Saving people without end."

> Then Māra said to the girl:
> "How foolish you are now!
> The bodhisattva path is full of toil;
> There is no telling when you would attain the Way.
> If you truly wish to become a Buddha,
> You must not cling to body or life.
> Throw yourself from this tower to the ground,
> And I shall know you are truly sublime,
> Diligent and free of attachment,
> Able to attain unsurpassed enlightenment."

> The girl stood at the railing
> And joined her palms toward the Buddha:
> "For the sake of all beings,
> May the Buddha know my sincerity."
> She threw herself from the tower,
> Attained the unborn,
> And transformed into a man.

> Ānanda was startled with fear.
> He joined his palms, straightened his robes,
> And addressed the Buddha, God of Gods:
> "My mind is greatly astonished.
> What manner of thing is this?
> All are bewildered.
> May the Buddha reveal the great light."

> The Buddha told Ānanda: "Did you see this girl?
> She threw herself into the air
> And transformed into a man.
> Not only today has she given up her body;
> In past lives it was the same.
> She has already served ten thousand Buddhas,
> Diligent without ceasing.
> In ages yet to come,
> She shall make offerings to Buddhas as countless as the Ganges sands
> And shall become a Buddha
> Named Nāgaśreṣṭha.

> "In the first great assembly,
> She shall liberate the devas and humans.
> Their numbers, too many to recount,
> Shall be like drifting clouds.
> When that Buddha rules the world,
> Happiness shall know no limit.
> Food and drink shall arise of themselves,
> As in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven."

> Then Nāgadattā, in his body,
> Stood before the Buddha
> And addressed his parents: "Allow me to become a śramaṇa."
> His parents immediately consented.
> Five hundred attendants
> And eight hundred devas
> All generated the unsurpassed mind.
> At that time Māra, grieved and bitter,
> Had nothing more to say in his remorse.

> Nāgadattā addressed the Buddha:
> "May you have compassion on all people,
> Sever the twelve seas,
> And remove all suffering.
> For the sake of the many who are foolish,
> Speak often of the great treasure."

> The Buddha then taught the Dharma.
> The five hundred attendants
> All attained the unborn.
> And the eight hundred devas
> Attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas.

> Then Nāgadattā
> Stood before the Buddha and spoke of his past lives:
> "Seeking the Way was bitter and arduous,
> Not for the sake of my own body,
> But for all people.
> The Tathāgata's merit
> Cannot be fully told.
> The Pañcaśikha of that time
> Is now the World-Honored One.
> The body of that poisonous snake
> Is now Nāgadattā.
> The five hundred celestial maidens of that time
> Are now the five hundred attendants.
> The eight hundred sons of heaven
> Share this resolve without equal.
> What the bodhisattva has revealed
> Is still grounded in cause and condition.
> To praise his merit fully
> Is something no one can ever accomplish."

> When the Bodhisattva Nāgadattā
> Gave this lion's roar,
> Countless devas and humans
> All generated the unsurpassed true resolve.
> All were filled with joy
> And paid homage before the Buddha.
