# 建立曼荼羅及揀擇地法

## Methods for Establishing a Maṇḍala and Selecting the Ground

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0911

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, compiled by Huìlín (慧琳).

---

Having respectfully examined the *Susiddhikara Sūtra*, *Subāhu Sūtra*, *Yuqixieye* (*Vairocanābhisaṃbodhi Tantra*), *Mahāvairocana Abhisaṃbodhi Sūtra*, and other scriptures, I have briefly compiled the methods for selecting the ground and establishing a maṇḍala. A practitioner of true words who, in accordance with the esoteric teachings, recites the true words of the various honored ones and seeks the two kinds of accomplishment (worldly and supramundane) should first arouse the irreversible mind of great bodhi, seek entry into the great maṇḍala of the ocean-assembly of all Buddhas, and receive the bodhicitta precepts from an ācārya. After receiving abhiṣeka, one receives and studies the ritual manual for practice in which the three mysteries are mutually responsive. Day and night, at the proper times, one recites according to the Dharma. The activities of body, speech, and mind are always accompanied by the Dharma. One abandons clinging, deluded emotions, and mental scattering. One constantly contemplates the true nature of all dharmas: pure, perfectly luminous. One deeply enters the wondrous samādhi of yoga. One skillfully penetrates both principle and phenomena, practicing in freedom from characteristics. One receives the essential teachings personally from an abhiṣeka ācārya, and clearly understands the skillful means of what is permitted and prohibited within the teaching.

Only such a person may establish the various maṇḍalas to benefit self and others, seeking all kinds of siddhi with certainty of accomplishment. If one lacks the above conditions, has not personally received the method from a wise teacher, and does not understand the intention of the teaching, one must not recklessly and hastily attempt to establish one. Not only will the spiritual beings be roughly disturbed and made uneasy, but one will also bring calamity upon oneself.

This teaching is the secret essential of the yoga of all Tathāgatas, the ritual of secret offerings through the gate of samādhi, the profound expedient practice of Samantabhadra's vows cultivated within one's own mind. Its intention is concise and wondrous, near yet difficult to know. Although it clarifies things through concrete procedures, it employs the wondrous contemplation of yoga: all activities, without exception, are Buddha-activities. The wise and discerning should carefully choose their teacher. Do not let those who are arrogant study without a teacher, interpreting texts by guesswork, clinging to words and names, utterly failing to understand the profound intention of yoga. Such confusion is extreme. Although these methods are fully explained in the great teachings, practitioners find them difficult to locate in a hurry, and the sequential order is also unclear. Therefore I have now compiled them for transmission to those not yet awakened. I only ask that the broadly learned and accomplished further verify and determine them.

---

The *Subāhu Sūtra* says: "If a practitioner wishes to recite true words and swiftly attain accomplishment, one should make a maṇḍala at places where Buddhas have formerly dwelt, or at places where bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, or śrāvakas have dwelt, and accomplishment will come swiftly."

The *Susiddhikara Sūtra* says: "If one seeks the upper grade of siddhi, one should go to the site of one of the Tathāgata's Eight Great Stūpas, or to the birthplace of a bodhisattva, or a place of ascetic practice, or a mountaintop, or an island in the sea, or a famous mountain, or a great seashore, or a deep mountain valley, or before a relic-stūpa, or a place of powerful features, or a place one loves, or a place with many flowers and fruits, or within a great forest, or beside a great dragon-pool, or above a deep river-pool, or at a pure spring-pool, or in a quiet place free of clamor, or at the edge of a great deep pool, or in a mountain hermitage with many fragrant trees, or beneath a single great tree standing alone whose shadow does not move, or at a place with many sacred traces. Such places are called the most supreme, having the power to accomplish the upper grade of siddhi.

"If at a great lotus pool, or on a sandbar in a great river, or within a deep rocky cave, or in a flower garden, or at a place with abundant milk-sap trees, or at a place where bodhisattvas of old once walked, or at a pure hermitage with many springs and pools, or a place without bitter cold, or without bitter heat, or on a solitary high terrace with no fierce beasts but many deer, or in a mountain hollow with water where no one goes, or at a place covered with soft auspicious grass, or on the bank of a great river, or within a pure monastery in a great Buddha-hall, or at a place where trees are lush with many flowers and fruits, or a place surrounded by water with extra soil, or in a land where people are numerous and compassionate, or in a city, town, or village where many people faithfully honor and practice the Buddha's teaching, or at a place where the Dharma-wheel was once turned, or in a clean and pure place within one's own dwelling. Such places are called exceptional, having the power to accomplish the middle grade of siddhi, including auspiciousness, merit-increase, disaster-pacification, and enchantment.

"If in the wilderness, or at places where spirits dwell, or in great burial grounds, or in charnel grounds, or on the highest peaks, or at the various great spirit-mountains, or before a vajra-holder, or beside a great dragon-pool, or in various deva-shrines, or in a great deity-temple at a place of great purification, or in an asura cave or a seer's grotto, or at a great pond or marsh, or at a great crossroads, or beside a great highway, or at a mountain-cliff dragon-cave, or upon a great flat stone, or at a Buddhist stūpa, or at a place where the earth has spiritual power and many sacred traces. Such places have the power to accomplish the methods of the Vajra Division: repelling ghosts and demons, destroying enemies, and subduing devas and nāgas. The various maṇḍalas will be swiftly accomplished."

The *Vairocanābhisaṃbodhi Tantra* says: "At rocky caves and mountaintops, or at previously purified ground, or above a cave, or upon a pillar, or upon a stone, or beside a *caitya*, or within a Buddhist stūpa, or above a river-pool, or near a river-sandbar: at such places, when making a maṇḍala, there is no need to dig the ground or beat it level. One need not be concerned about the fault of unevenness. Simply follow the contour of the ground, sweep it clean, sprinkle water, press the earth with the hand while reciting the mantra, and it becomes pure. Or if the ground at the maṇḍala site has faults that cannot be removed, one simply empowers it with the mantra to make it pure, and recitation may proceed. If one must make a maṇḍala urgently for the sake of expelling ghosts and demons that have possessed someone, or for one's own abhiṣeka, then one need not carefully select the ground but should make it as circumstances allow. Simply use fragrant water empowered with the Kīlikīla Wrathful Unrivaled Mantra, first sprinkle the ground, and also sprinkle the five pure products of the cow to purify the ground."

It also says: "If the site is low-lying and damp with water, then lay down clean boards closely over it. Empower according to the method, anoint and clean it, and making a maṇḍala upon it will also achieve accomplishment."

It also says: "The method for purifying the ground: seven days beforehand, go to the site. Protect yourself and your disciples according to the Dharma, make offerings to the earth-deity, and protect the ground. Only then begin to dig and remove the ground's impurities. If one performs the rites without removing the ground's impurities, accomplishment will certainly be difficult. Therefore one must remove from the ground all bones, stones, ash, charcoal, tree-roots, insect-nests, skulls, hair, broken tiles, and rubble, removing them completely to make it clean. One should finely pound the excavated soil, refill the pit, and pack it firmly. Then moisten it with cow-liquid and various fragrant waters. After moistening, pack it again until extremely level and smooth, like the palm of the hand. Then mix fragrant water with *gomaya* (cow-dung) and apply it starting from the northeast corner, moving rightward. Then, in the center of the maṇḍala, dig a small hole. Empower the five kinds of grain, five kinds of jewels, five kinds of incense, and five kinds of medicinal herbs, and place them in the hole. Pack it level and smooth. After thus placing the jewels and purifying the ground, next one should perform the method of claiming the ground. Three days beforehand, use the operational mantra of one's own division to empower fragrant water. At sunset, use it to sprinkle the ground. Press the ground with the right hand while reciting the mantra of the maṇḍala lord, to claim it. This is called the method of claiming the ground."

The above is from the *Susiddhikara Sūtra*.

The *Mahāvairocana Sūtra* says: "Lord of Secrets! In selecting the ground, remove gravel, broken tiles, shattered vessels, skulls, hair, chaff, dregs, ash, charcoal, thorns, bones, rotten wood, and the like, as well as insects, ants, dung-beetles, and venomous stinging creatures. Be free of all such faults. On an auspicious day, at dawn, when the fixed day, hour, constellation, and planetary alignments all correspond, at a time before the meal when auspicious signs appear, first make obeisance to all Tathāgatas. Then, to rouse the earth deity, recite this verse:

> "You, protector beloved of the devas,
> Who before the Buddha, the Guide,
> Practiced the supreme practice
> And perfected the pāramitā of purifying the ground:
> Just as the Lion of the Śākyas, Savior of the World,
> Crushed the hosts of Māra,
> I too shall vanquish Māra.
> I now draw the maṇḍala."

"The ācārya should recite the Sanskrit original. He should kneel with the right hand extended, pressing the ground, and recite this verse repeatedly. Then offer perfumes, flowers, and the like to all Buddhas, bodhisattvas, and the earth deity. After making such offerings, the practitioner should pay homage to all Tathāgatas and then prepare the ground, in proper sequence, endowed with all virtues."

It also says: "Lord of Secrets! At any of the places described above, prepare the chosen ground, making it firm. Take *gomaya* (cow-dung) and *gomutra* (cow-urine) that have not yet reached the ground, mix them, and apply them. Then sprinkle with fragrant water empowered by the mantra." Then the mantra is spoken:

This mantra for empowering fragrant water is used from beginning to end. The sequential procedures according to the various scriptures are as above. Now, in actual practice, oral transmission from teacher to teacher is not without its minute details.

Whenever one wishes to establish a maṇḍala, one first selects the ground. One considers one's own aspirations and wishes, selects a site that corresponds, and must first receive the method of empowerment from a teacher, with a mind free of doubt and obstruction. Afterward, one skillfully selects the time, choosing an auspicious day and hour before establishing. Seven days beforehand, sweep, sprinkle, and anoint. Use the above mantra to empower fragrant water and sprinkle it thoroughly until pure. At dusk, burn incense, scatter flowers, and spread new clean cloths, or lay lotus leaves, according to one's means. With whatever incense, flowers, food, lamps, *arghya*, and other offerings are available, make the requesting-the-ground rite for the noble ones and the earth deity. The practitioner puts on new clean garments, sends away all others, and enters the maṇḍala site alone. If it is open ground, enclose it with curtains, or screen it off as the occasion requires. Following the sequential mantra practices of one's own division, secretly empower one's own body. Face east, hold a censer in hand, and recite the invocation verse:

From the *Mahāvairocana Sūtra*:

> "All Buddhas, compassionate ones,
> Being mindful of us,
> Tomorrow I shall claim this ground:
> May the Buddha's sons also descend."

Although the scriptural verse is thus, the words are concise and the meaning is hidden, lacking a verse to request the earth deity. Now, the following verse has been orally transmitted; its meaning does not differ from the preceding, but the language is complete and the meaning is clear. Either verse may be used according to preference:

> "All Buddhas, compassionate ones who care for beings,
> We humbly ask that you be mindful of us.
> I now request and inform all noble ones,
> Firm-Earth Deva and retinue.
> All Tathāgatas and the Buddha's sons,
> Without abandoning your compassionate vows, please all descend.
> I claim this ground, seeking accomplishment:
> Be witnesses and grant me your protection."

Recite three times or seven times. If one can recite the Sanskrit original, that is best. [Sanskrit text omitted.]

After making such earnest invitations, visualize the holy assembly gathered like clouds. Then contemplate Mahāvairocana Tathāgata's own-nature pure body pervading the dharma-realm, and the Buddhas of the ten directions and three times likewise. Visualize one's own body as Vajrasattva Bodhisattva. Before all Tathāgatas, with purified three karmas, pay sincere reverence. After this obeisance, kneel with both knees. With the samādhi-hand (left) hold the vajra upright at the heart. With the prajñā-hand (right), spread the five fingers of the palm flat and press the ground. Recite the verse from the sūtra for rousing the earth deity seven times. With each recitation, press the ground once. Then recite the Earth Deva mantra 108 times:

Visualize the earth deity as resembling a celestial maiden, adorned with many jewels, together with an immeasurable retinue each holding jeweled vases and bearing incense and flowers. They emerge from the earth, remembering their original vow and not daring to disobey the Buddha's command, coming to serve as witnesses. Then recite the Tathāgata's Compassion-Protection Mantra 108 times to benefit the earth deity:

*Oṃ buddha maitri vajrarakṣa haṃ svāhā*

The practitioner, with utmost sincerity, burns incense and makes this announcement to the noble ones and earth deity: "I humbly announce to all Tathāgatas, all great bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, śrāvakas, the five classes of devas, the Firm-Earth Deity, and the spirits of this place: I, [name], in accordance with the Vidyādhara-piṭaka teaching, uphold the true words of [honored one] in order to seek unsurpassed bodhi. I wish to establish a maṇḍala on this ground to diligently practice recitation. May what I seek be swiftly accomplished. I humbly ask the Earth Deity to remember your original vow: permit me to establish the maṇḍala, protect and assist me, and do not allow Māra or evil spirits to create obstacles. Just as our original teacher, Śākyamuni Tathāgata, sat beneath the bodhi tree and vanquished all Māras, so may it be for me now. If there are obstacles, may all Buddhas and bodhisattvas show me signs beforehand, so that I may discern the beginning of good or evil. May the honored one and all vidyā-kings be mindful of me and protect me. Do not allow ghosts and spirits to manifest their forms to deceive and confuse me. If there are no obstacles, may I see auspicious and excellent visions."

After making this announcement, on the west side of the altar site, properly protect the body, sit quietly in peace, and recite the mantra one has been practicing 1,008 times. If the mantra text is lengthy, recite according to one's ability, either 108 times, or at minimum 21 or 35 times. Abandon all thoughts and settle into single-minded correct mindfulness. Then, at that very place, lie on the right side facing southeast with the head raised, the whole body at rest like a Lion King, peacefully falling asleep to obtain visionary signs. If one has a clean resting-place nearby, one may use it according to preference; it is not absolutely necessary to sleep at the site. Simply concentrate the mind single-pointedly, contemplating the bodhicitta moon-disk with perfect clarity. Within the bodhicitta, contemplate a wish-fulfilling jewel, internally and externally distinct, penetrating to the heart with no other thought, and then fall asleep. If one has previously studied meditative stillness, one need only calm the mind, then enter contemplation as before, contemplating the wish-fulfilling jewel. All signs of success or failure will appear in the mind's mirror. If the preliminary signs are inauspicious, one should not establish the maṇḍala. If one forces the matter, one risks bringing harm upon oneself. If the preliminary signs are auspicious, one may proceed to establish it.

The next day, at the center of the designated altar site, dig a square hole one cubit in each direction and one cubit deep. Remove the tiles and stones, refill the soil, pack it level, and test its fullness. If after refilling it is barely full, with no soil remaining, the ground is middling: accomplishment will be minor, without great power. If after refilling it is not full and the soil is insufficient, the ground is inferior: it is hollow and weak, and accomplishment will be difficult. If after filling there is soil remaining, the ground is auspicious: accomplishment will come swiftly and with great power.

One must also taste the soil to determine whether it is good or bad. If the soil tastes bitter, astringent, or foul, the ground is unsuitable. If the taste is sour, salty, or pungent, know that the ground is only suitable for the demolishing maṇḍalas of the Vajra Division, and not for accomplishing auspicious and other methods. If the taste is bland and free from all unpleasant flavors, the site is harmonious and gentle by nature, but slow to yield accomplishment. If the soil tastes sweet and fragrant, it is the most exceptional, capable of accomplishing all supreme siddhi and the great maṇḍala.

One must also observe the soil's color to determine whether it corresponds to what is sought, and only then establish the maṇḍala. White soil corresponds to pacification accomplishment. Yellow soil is suitable for enrichment. Blue-black soil is only suitable for subjugation. Red soil corresponds to summoning and enchantment. The rest is extensively explained in the *Susiddhikara Sūtra*.

After the practitioner has observed the visionary signs and tested the soil's fullness, color, and flavor, and found that all correspond as described above and the site is suitable for establishment, one empowers the ground with the Acala Mother-Mudrā Mantra 108 times for its protection. Then proceed with the full excavation. The Acala Mantra:

The purpose of digging is to guard against impurities in the ground, as described above: ash, charcoal, skulls, insect-nests, tree-roots, venomous stinging creatures, or ancient graves and buried corpses. If evil objects are excessively numerous, the site is unsuitable; abandon it and seek a better location. If there are no such faults, dig one cubit deep, or two cubits, or three cubits. Remove the soil and inspect it carefully. If one digs down to the natural undisturbed bed of earth, one knows the ground is certainly clean; with the mind free of doubt, there is no need to dig further regardless of depth. If the soil-color is mixed and uneven, that too must be removed and replaced with clean soil from a riverbank. Fill and prepare according to the method. With each layer of soil added, sprinkle once with empowered fragrant water, continuing until full. After filling, pack it firmly until it is smooth as a mirror.

If the building or room has been smoke-stained over many years and is unclean, one should plaster and wipe it until extremely clean, or apply clean fragrant-water soil throughout the building and on the walls until extremely pure. If the room is already new and clean, simply empower fragrant water with the mantra and sprinkle it throughout to achieve purity.

If one wishes to build up an altar, do not use packed earth, which will certainly crack when dry, an inauspicious sign. One or two months beforehand, in a clean place, take good clean soil, mix it with empowered fragrant water to make bricks (not sun-dried by exposure but air-dried), keeping chickens, dogs, people, and livestock from treading on them and causing contamination. When thoroughly dry, use these bricks to lay a flat altar. Measure using the practitioner's own cubit and finger-breadths, making a square altar of the size prescribed by the teaching. For six cubits and below, the altar is four finger-breadths high. For twelve cubits and below, the altar is eight finger-breadths high. For twelve cubits and above, increase proportionally but in a single tier, level and even without steps, with the northeast corner slightly lower. Apply plaster evenly on top and on all four sides. When dry, place the five jewels, five medicines, and the like in the center of the altar. The five jewels are: gold, silver, pearl, sapphire, and crystal.

Next, take the five kinds of medicine: *sahajira*, *sahanideva*, *kaṇṭakārī*, *gṛhīkaraṇa*, *vṛhatī*. These should be sought from foreign merchants. If these medicines are unavailable, use the medicinal herbs produced in the Tang lands as substitutes: *chìjiàn* (arrow-root), ginseng, *fúlíng* (poria), *shíchāngpú* (acorus), and *tiānméndōng* (asparagus root).

Next, take the five kinds of grain: rice, barley, wheat, mung beans, and sesame.

Next, take the five kinds of incense: sandalwood, aloeswood, clove, saffron, and camphor.

Take small amounts of the above jewels, grains, incenses, and medicines, place them together in a porcelain box, or in a porcelain jar, or in a gold or silver vessel. Empower them with the Earth Deva Mantra 108 times and bury them in the center of the altar, unknown to others.

Beneath the position of the maṇḍala lord, when the plaster has dried, apply a sand-and-clay coating. Apply it throughout as before, making it extremely smooth. When dry, filter and sprinkle *gomaya* (cow-dung) mixed with fragrant water to purify it. Empower the various powdered incenses with the Invincible Vidyā-King Mantra, or the Great Wheel Vajra Mantra, 108 times. The Invincible Mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ oṃ huru huru caṇḍāli mātaṅgi svāhā*

First anoint the top of the altar, then anoint the four sides, all starting from the northeast corner and moving rightward. Then recite the Ground-Anointing Mantra while applying. The Ground-Anointing Mantra:

*Oṃ kālari mahākālari svāhā*

Apply thus repeatedly, three to five coats. Then polish with lotus-seed grass, or take hollyhock leaves mixed with a little ink and pounded *kuśa* grass, combine them, and polish once or twice. While still damp, sweep until lustrous and clean. While sweeping, recite the Ground-Sweeping Mantra:

*Oṃ hara hara rāju graha raṇāya svāhā*

After the anointing is complete, burn incense and scatter flowers. Recite the Acala Vidyā-King Mantra one thousand times to repel obstructing beings and protect the site. Hold a censer with utmost sincerity and make an announcement. Then abide in the contemplation of Mahāvairocana Tathāgata, making the bodhicitta clearly manifest.

Within the moon-disk at the heart, contemplate the syllable *A*, white in color, emitting great radiance illuminating boundless realms. Then continuously recite the syllable-*A* mantra. With one breath, recite as long as possible, continuously like this, one breath or three breaths, until the mind attains correspondence, abandoning all discrimination, entering the pure dharma-realm. Then one may stop. Then form the Vajra-Wheel Mudrā and recite the mantra to empower one's own body and the altar. This mudrā establishes the great maṇḍala from the pure dharma-realm body. Then, according to the teaching, distribute the sacred positions, broadly or concisely as one wishes: placing one position, or five positions, or nine positions, or seventeen, up to twenty-nine, thirty-seven, and so forth, in boundless varieties. Each and every one should be established according to the original teaching. In distributing the sacred positions, always proceed from the center outward, each successive level decreasing by one-third. This is the general method for establishing a maṇḍala; the overall framework is thus, and the rest may be inferred.

Or there are those who secretly receive the esoteric method from an ācārya, establishing it from the mind: using the fire of wisdom to burn away all deluded discriminations, producing the pure dharma-realm, then within the dharma-realm, arranging each position in proper sequence, through to boundless varieties of supreme and vast Buddha-activities. This is the secret transmission of mind-to-mind: if the person is not the right vessel, the Way will not be transmitted in vain. How could it all be committed to writing?

Though Lín is without talent, I have briefly set forth a small portion. Of the great meaning, not one in ten thousand has been recorded. I only ask the broadly learned and accomplished to examine and discern these matters.
