# 讚法界頌
## Verses in Praise of the Dharmadhātu

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1675

Translated from the Chinese. Composed by Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna (龍樹菩薩). Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護).

---

> Homage to the buddhas of the ten directions, the dharmakāya, saṃbhogakāya, and nirmāṇakāya.
> May we, together with all sentient beings, swiftly accomplish the nature of the dharmadhātu.

> Through the three evil destinies of saṃsāra, the principle of the dharmadhātu remains utterly still.
> Originally ever pure, it cannot be altered by any characteristic.
> Silent as empty space, pervading everywhere,
> Its substance transcends "here" and "there," neither deep nor shallow.

> Before milk has been transformed, butter and ghee are not seen.
> Before afflictions are subdued and eliminated, the dharmadhātu has no way to manifest.
> Just as butter exists within milk, its original substance wondrously luminous,
> So the dharmadhātu, though covered by afflictions, is perfect and pure in substance.

> Just as a lamp obstructed by a barrier cannot illuminate other things,
> So when ignorance constantly covers the mind, the dharmadhātu is not clearly seen.
> Just as a lamp freed from obstruction can illuminate things everywhere,
> So when afflictions are destroyed, true suchness is always manifest.

> In the beginning, middle, and end, the two obstructions cannot disturb it,
> Like a pure vaiḍūrya jewel whose light always shines.
> When luminous things are obstructed, the brightness cannot be seen.
> When the dharmadhātu is covered by afflictions, the principle of true suchness is hard to reveal.

> The substance of perfect nirvāṇa is pure and bright; saṃsāra cannot stain it.
> If one diligently seeks the dharmadhātu, saṃsāra cannot conceal it.
> Just as rice is wrapped in husks, yet the rice within is not absent,
> So afflictions cover true suchness, yet true suchness exists within afflictions.
> Just as grain stripped of husks reveals the rice-body naturally,
> So when the husk of afflictions is removed, the principle of the dharmadhātu is revealed.

> With deluded attachment there is a worldly realm, but the banana plant is ultimately without substance.
> The dharmadhātu is not the worldly realm, nor is it a false and deluded perception.
> Just as one who drinks amṛta is freed of all heat and affliction,
> So if one realizes the nature of the dharmadhātu, all afflictive heat is abandoned.
> Extinguishing the flames of affliction, the sweet dew of the dharmadhātu appears.
> Among all sentient beings, high and low are all equal.

> The substance is real, yet fruit is not produced; clinging to seeds, fruit does not exist.
> When wisdom arises, the conditioned is not the dharmadhātu.
> The dharmadhātu originally has no fixed location; only at the ultimate can it be realized.
> Pure and always luminous, the sun and moon are both bright and clear.

> The dharmadhātu is without stain or defilement, like the dust of a dragon's night-rain.
> Moreover, like the face of Rāhu, its brightness is always radiant.
> Just as a fire-resistant cloth, placed in fire, can be freed from stain,
> When the dirt is gone the cloth remains, its brightness increasingly pure and clear.
> Craving and attachment defile the mind, and falsely there is saṃsāra.
> Just like the fire-resistant cloth: in true emptiness, the false does not exist.

> The three poisons are the root of birth and death; the fire of wisdom can burn them away.
> The substance of the dharmadhātu always exists, bright and always shining.
> Afflictions that defile are called "defilements," as the World-Honored One has always proclaimed.
> When defilements are extinguished, true suchness is revealed, like drawing spring-water from the earth.
> The substance of the dharmadhātu is without defilement; root and secondary afflictions can cover and conceal it.
> If afflictions are completely removed, its luminous purity is beyond measure.

> The dharmadhātu is originally without self, without the two forms of male and female.
> Its substance is without false clinging: where could further deliberation be?
> The dharmadhātu is free from aversion and attachment; sense-faculties and sense-objects are originally without substance.
> False clinging serves as the cause, and from this, differentiation arises.
> True emptiness is not suffering or affliction; craving and attachment are the cause of suffering.
> Intoxicated attachment arises from false thinking; thus one revolves through the three realms.

> As a fetus within the womb, the infant cannot yet be said to be seen,
> So the two obstructions cover true suchness, and the dharmadhātu cannot be realized.
> Various doubts and uncertainties arise: views, pride, anger, and delusion.
> What is falsely reckoned as real is not real; what is real is reckoned as non-existent.
> The horn of a hare has no substance, yet false clinging makes it seem real.
> The dharmadhātu transcends false clinging; through false clinging, what is real is not seen as such.

> Just as form necessarily breaks apart, and even atoms can still be known,
> The dharmadhātu does not break apart; in the three times it cannot be apprehended.
> What is born will also perish; glory and decline both follow.
> The dharmadhātu is neither born nor perished: how can it be said to be an object of knowledge?
> The horn of a hare is originally non-existent, yet through the three times it can still be pondered.
> True emptiness is not like the hare's horn: thought and deliberation cannot know it.

> True emptiness is called "Sugata"; all forms and characteristics are annihilated.
> Emanation-bodies arise according to conditions; through cultivating causes, clinging to non-existence is transcended.
> Perfectly penetrating like the sun and moon, reflections in water appear the same.
> When form and sound are both extinguished, how could differentiation exist?

> The three times can be investigated and pondered; birth, conditions, and time are determined.
> If one awakens to the dharma of one's own body, how could one's own body exist?
> Just as water in the hot season: in the heat, one realizes it is not real.
> In the cold season, the principle is likewise. Perfect penetration is just so.
> The mind is always covered by afflictions, deluded and unable to understand.
> If one departs from the bonds of affliction, there is awakening, yet it is not a thing that exists.

> Just as the eyes perceive various forms, free from obstruction they can illuminate.
> The principle of true emptiness is likewise: illumination free from birth and death.
> Ear-consciousness hears sounds, free from delusion and discrimination.
> The nature of the dharmadhātu is likewise: discrimination and delusion do not exist.
> The nose can smell various fragrances; the nature of false clinging does not exist.
> When both form and characteristics are annihilated, true emptiness is just so.

> The tongue-faculty is empty by its own nature, forever distant from the realm of taste.
> Consciousness is empty, and its substance is likewise. The principle of the dharmadhātu is just so.
> The body-faculty is pure by its own nature; cold, warmth, and touch do not exist.
> The principle of the dharmadhātu is likewise: the place of touch is always far removed.
> Mind conditions dharmas and is called supreme, yet its own nature is always far removed.
> The nature of all dharmas is originally empty; perfect penetration's principle is just so.

> Seeing, hearing, sensation, and cognition: their corresponding dharmas are also empty.
> When all false thinking is brought to an end, the principle of seeing and hearing is also not a thing.
> From sense-faculties and sense-objects, false clinging arises; the pure substance originally has no source.
> Deluded clinging posits sense-faculties and sense-objects, yet the principle of sense-faculties and sense-objects does not truly exist.

> The worldly and the world-transcending: empty nature is originally without difference.
> Self and dharmas arise from delusion; through pervasive conceptualization, one revolves of one's own accord.
> The principle of the dharmadhātu is pure; greed, hatred, and delusion are originally without substance.
> Delusion and awakening arise from the mind; the three poisons are merely conventional names.
> Deluded clinging binds one in self-fetters; understanding the conventional names is wisdom.
> Bodhi is neither near nor far; the principle of the three times does not exist.

> Afflictions cage and trap the deluded; the World-Honored One proclaimed this in the sūtras.
> When wisdom is born, defiled stains are extinguished; do not let false clinging entangle you.
> Past and future: clinging is supreme, yet its empty substance can still be pondered.
> Bodhi is not false clinging; right realization is also known to be not a thing.
> Water and milk in the same vessel: the goose drinks the milk without mixing.
> When emptiness of beings is realized and afflictions are left behind, the two obstructions are also not mixed.

> With false clinging, "I" is not non-existent; with understanding, it is originally not existent.
> In the pure principle of nirvāṇa, the two kinds of self are both not established.
> The three kinds of giving are cultivated equally; śīla departs from transgression.
> Patience yields the fruit of a dignified body; vigor relies on courageous diligence.
> Dhyāna causes the mind to cease; prajñā's function is without doubt.
> Vows joined with the power of skillful means: one abides securely in supreme bodhi.

> Bodhi transcends false clinging; in true emptiness, birth and death do not exist.
> Understanding the original nature of emptiness, the two characteristics are also without substance.
> Milk-sugar departs from sugarcane; apart from sugarcane, sugar does not exist.
> The three vehicles lead toward bodhi; apart from the seed, the substance does not exist.
> Guarding and protecting the seed of grain, sprouts and stalks will certainly be born.
> Guarding and protecting the seed of bodhi, bodhi arises from this.

> Just as during the dark half of the moon, the light cannot yet be seen,
> So sentient beings bound by afflictions have not yet made true suchness clearly manifest.
> The moon's initial light, though present, gradually increases.
> On the first ground, one realizes bodhi, yet bodhi is not yet perfected.
> On the fifteenth, the full moon shines pure and bright everywhere.
> Liberation reveals the dharmakāya; the principle of the dharmakāya is without deficiency.

> Defiled mentation's correspondences: bondage, co-arising, and cessation.
> When all obstructions are liberated, in the three times one awakens to non-existence.
> When the first great asaṃkhyeya-kalpa is complete, the three kinds of giving are universally practiced.
> The obstacle of discrimination is severed: the joy and wisdom of the first ground is unmatched.

> Regarding the three karmas, both errors and violations, guarding against the grave and the light:
> Śīla of the perfectly complete precepts. "Free from Defilement" alone bears this name.
> The two obstructions constantly stain, but the sword of wisdom, emptying both, removes them.
> "Radiant" can illuminate and shine, destroying and extinguishing them progressively without remainder.
> Far removed from root and secondary defilements, the blazing wisdom-fire progressively increases.
> Bodhi is called supreme; its burning illumination turns ever more brilliant.

> Conventional and ultimate, the two wisdoms, arising in correspondence, alternate.
> Uniting them without obstruction: "Hard to Conquer" is the constant task.
> The wisdom of the twelve links of dependent arising: the circling principle is complete.
> Profoundly deep, called supreme: prajñā manifests before one.
> The worldly practices of the two vehicles, long cultivated, the Way now clear.
> Signlessness and effortless function complete: the last is called "Far-Reaching."

> Wisdom's function is non-discriminating, always effortlessly accomplished.
> The hosts of māras are defeated and scattered: "Immovable" alone bears this name.
> "Good Wisdom" is called "unimpeded"; in the ten directions, the Dharma is taught wondrously.
> Clouds of emanation-bodies rain sweet dew, responding to beings as the supreme refuge.
> All virtues are like water; empty space is likened to the body.
> Heavy and coarse are all screened away: the great Dharma-wisdom is called "Cloud."

> Examine carefully the affairs of saṃsāra: who can escape the pull of karma?
> Know that there is no suffering or affliction; in the pure land, do not be entangled.
> Homage to the true children of the Buddha, who have ascended to the Wisdom-Cloud ground.
> The subtle and minute are all severed completely; they transcend suffering and depart from all dust.

> Abhiṣeka light shines everywhere; sense-faculties and sense-objects pervade the body.
> In the vajra-samādhi of great quiescence, all sufferings do not draw near.
> Upon the great jeweled lotus throne, fashioned of billions of wonders,
> Adorned everywhere, the merit and virtue are truly inconceivable.

> The ten powers together with the fearlessnesses; the three bodies and four wisdoms are perfected.
> The six supernatural powers are always sovereign; responding to beings, he transforms according to conditions.
> Illuminating like the full moon, the flames always blaze.
> Pervading everywhere in the ten directions, brilliant and increasingly bright.
> Forever severed from the stain of dependent arising, always abiding in nirvāṇa,
> Bodhi is called supreme; his transforming benefit delights all sentient beings.

> Wisdom's function is deep as the ocean; according to capacity, he manifests response-bodies.
> When the water is clear, the moon's reflection comes; everywhere he ferries the deluded across.
> Like a sphaṭika jewel, reflections appear according to conditions.
> Where beings' faculties are receptive, his universal response is without end.
> Hungry ghosts are always hungry and thirsty, unable to see springs of water.
> Sentient beings without even a little faith are entangled by their past karma.

> He manifests bodies with all manner of marks, their radiance brilliant.
> Though the Buddha is always in the world, those without past connection cannot see him.
> Comprehending the worlds as numerous as dust-motes, root and secondary defilements long absent,
> With the supreme wisdom of twofold emptiness, his wondrous function transforms the childish and foolish.
> The pure, stainless self-enjoyment body and other-enjoyment body
> Always dwell in Akaniṣṭha heaven, benefiting beings of the five vehicles.

> Rescuing sentient beings from suffering, his lifespan extends through countless koṭis.
> The twofold adornment is without end; merit and virtue are beyond measure.
> The Buddha expounds the One Vehicle Dharma; according to capacity, beings awaken to the shallow or deep.
> The lotus is without stain or defilement; the jewel is originally free from flaw.
> With but a small portion of this praise, the vast proclamation of the principle is profound.
> May the merit of this be universally bestowed upon gods and humans.
