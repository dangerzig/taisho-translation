# Verses of Exhortation by the Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna to the King

## *Lóngshù Púsà Quànjiè Wáng Sòng* (龍樹菩薩勸誡王頌)

Taishō 1674

Translated by Yìjìng (*Yìjìng*, 義淨) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is Yìjìng's translation of Nāgārjuna's Suhṛllekha (Letter to a Friend), the third Chinese version of this celebrated verse epistle addressed to a South Indian king. Yìjìng made his translation upon arriving at Tāmralipti (modern Tamluk) in eastern India. Unlike the earlier versions by Guṇavarman (T1672) and Sanghavarman (T1673), Yìjìng's rendering preserves the verse format throughout without prose interpolation and follows a more literal approach. The colophon preserves the Sanskrit title in transliteration: "Ārya Nāgārjuna Bodhisattva Suhṛllekha." The text covers the six recollections, the ten wholesome actions, the pāramitās, the eight worldly dharmas, the four applications of mindfulness, the sufferings of the six destinies (with vivid descriptions of hell, hungry-ghost, and animal realms), dependent origination, the eightfold noble path, and the aspiration for buddhahood.*

---

### Translator's Preface

This set of verses is a letter composed in verse by the Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna and sent to the king of the Śātavāhana country (*Qíngyǒu*, 親友乘土), his intimate friend in South India. This letter was previously translated and stored in Chinese repositories, but few people had seen it, so these wondrous words remained largely unknown. For this reason, I have newly established the base text, hoping to ensure its unimpeded circulation. Translated by the śramaṇa Yìjìng upon first arriving at the country of Tāmralipti (*Dānmólìdǐ*, 耽摩立底) in eastern India.

---

> Because the minds of sentient beings are covered by ignorance,
> Compassion therefore arises to open their understanding.
> The greatly virtuous Nāgārjuna, for the king,
> Sent this letter to instruct him in practice.

This opening verse was composed by a later person to indicate the letter's original purpose.

---

> O virtuous one, I expound the teaching of suchness (*tathatā*).
> To produce merit and love, I compose this text.
> The truly good should listen carefully:
> These verses are called the "Holy Suhṛllekha."

> Regardless of what material an image of the Buddha is carved from,
> All those with wisdom make offerings to it.
> Even if my verses are not skillfully wrought,
> Since they are based on the true dharma, do not treat them lightly.

> Although the king already understands the teaching of suchness,
> Hearing the Buddha's words further increases superior understanding.
> It is like a whitewashed wall in moonlight:
> Is it not even more vivid and beautiful?

> The Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha;
> Generosity, moral conduct, and the heavens:
> Each of these is a treasury of merit.
> The Buddha says you should constantly recollect them.

> The ten wholesome courses of action,
> By body, speech, and mind constantly cultivated,
> Shunning all intoxicants
> And also pursuing a pure livelihood.

> Knowing that wealth is not a lasting substance,
> Give according to the dharma to bhikṣus.
> The poor and lowly, and those twice-born:
> In future lives, they become your true friends.

> All virtues rest upon the precepts,
> As the earth supports all growth.
> Do not waste, emaciate, or dilute your hopes:
> The Buddha says you should constantly practice this.

> Generosity, morality, patience, vigor, concentration,
> And wisdom: these are beyond measure.
> These pāramitās should be cultivated;
> They cross the ocean of existence and lead to buddhahood.

> If one honors one's father and mother,
> In that household dwells Brahmā.
> In this life one receives a good reputation;
> In the next, one is born in heaven.

> Killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying,
> Addiction to food, love of high beds,
> Abandoning all intoxicants, song and dance,
> Garlands, adornments, and perfumes:
> If a woman or man can accomplish
> These eight branches of the sacred precepts,
> In the six heavens of the desire realm above,
> With lasting purity and goodness they shall be born.

> Miserliness, deceit, fraud, sloth, laziness,
> Arrogance, lust, anger, pride of birth,
> Pride of learning, pride of youth:
> Regard all of these as enemies.

> It is said that the deathless is due to diligence,
> And death comes from negligence.
> Diligence can increase wholesome dharmas:
> Therefore you should cultivate carefulness.

> If previously one was negligent
> But later reforms and cultivates diligently,
> It is like the clearing of clouds:
> On a fine night one beholds the bright moon.

> Sundarananda, Aṅgulimāla,
> Ajātaśatru, and Udayana:
> All reversed their evil and became good.

> Courageous effort is unmatched by patience;
> Do not let the force of anger proceed.
> Ultimately one attains the stage of non-return:
> The Buddha confirms that anger can be removed.

> Others may beat and curse me,
> Bully me and steal my possessions;
> Harboring resentment invites feuds and quarrels.
> Abandoning resentment, one sleeps in peace.

> As with water, earth, and stone,
> Human minds are entirely like these.
> Those who give rise to afflictions are like the first;
> Those who love the dharma are like the last.

> The Buddha speaks of three kinds of speech:
> Sweet, truthful, and false words
> Are like honey, flowers, and dung.
> Discard the last and practice the first.

> Now bright and later also bright;
> Now dark and later still dark;
> Or now bright but later dark;
> Or now dark but later bright:
> Among these four kinds of people,
> O king, follow the first.

> Some are raw though they appear ripe;
> Some are ripe though they appear raw;
> Some ripe are indeed ripe;
> Some raw are indeed raw.
> Among *āmra* fruits
> There are such differences.
> People too are like these four:
> Difficult to discern, O king, you should know.

> Do not gaze upon another's wife.
> If you look, regard her as mother, daughter,
> Or sister, according to her age.
> If desire arises, contemplate impurity.

> As one protects one's child, one's treasure, and one's life,
> So guard and hold the restless mind.
> Like beasts, poison, swords, enemies, and fire:
> Do not let the joy of desire encroach.

> Through desire, nothing beneficial is produced.
> It is like the *kimpāka* fruit.
> The Buddha says it should be removed:
> The shackle and chain of birth-and-death.

> Treacherous, deceitful, always shifting objects:
> One who can subdue these six consciousnesses,
> Taking up arms to sweep away all foes,
> Is acknowledged as supremely courageous.

> A foul-smelling dwelling of nine gates and myriad impurities,
> This body of action, never sated, wrapped in thin skin:
> Please look at a young woman stripped of her adornments.
> Examine and distinguish the body's parts: the ugliness is unspeakable.

> Like a leper whose worm-eaten [flesh] is painful,
> Seeking comfort by the fireside:
> The relief is never lasting.
> Addiction to desire is just the same.

> To know the true and supreme principle,
> Direct the attention to contemplate all things.
> Only this virtue should be cultivated;
> There is no other dharma worth pursuing.

> If a person has noble lineage,
> Is handsome, and widely learned,
> But lacks wisdom and violates moral conduct,
> How is such a person worthy of esteem?

> If a person has no noble lineage,
> Is ugly and of little learning,
> But has wisdom and upholds moral conduct,
> All people should make offerings to them.

> Gain and loss, suffering and pleasure,
> Praise and blame, fame and disgrace:
> Understand these eight worldly dharmas.
> With equanimity, detach from these conditions.

> Brahmins, devas, mendicants,
> Parents, wife, children:
> Do not commit evil on their account.
> The fruits of hell are not shared by others.

> When one performs evil deeds,
> It is not like the wound of a sword's cut:
> One must wait until the moment of death
> For the evil karma's fruit to fully appear.

> Faith, morality, generosity, purity, learning,
> Shame, self-reproach, and right wisdom:
> These seven treasures, the Sage has said,
> Are truly valuable; common goods are empty.

> Gambling, delight in spectacles and noisy distractions,
> Laziness, evil companions, attachment, and ambition,
> Drinking and going about at improper times: these six faults
> Destroy one's good name. You should abandon them in this life.

> In seeking wealth, contentment is the best.
> The teacher of humans and devas has fully proclaimed this.
> If one can cultivate contentment,
> Even in poverty, one is a wealthy person.

> If a person extensively seeks many things,
> Suffering returns in equal measure.
> The wise, if they do not cultivate contentment,
> Suffer torment like the many-headed serpent.

> One whose nature is resentful, like a murderer;
> One who bullies and despises her husband, like a man's equal;
> One who, even for trifles, will steal:
> Such three thieving wives should be abandoned.

> One who is agreeable like a sister, loving like a mother,
> Obedient like a servant, companionable like a friend:
> Such four wives should be honored.
> Know that such a household is called "heavenly."

> Eat meals as one takes medicine:
> Knowing the right measure, abandon greed and aversion.
> Not for the sake of fattening or pride,
> But only to sustain the body.

> Exert yourself through the long day;
> In the first and last watches of the night,
> Even while sleeping, maintain mindfulness.
> Do not let your life end in vain.

> Loving-kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity:
> Cultivate and practice these constantly.
> Even if one has not yet entered the higher stream,
> One can be born in the Brahmā heavens.

> Abandoning desire's suffering, seeking bliss through examination,
> According to one's karma, one shall be born in the four levels:
> Great Brahmā, Ābhāsvara, Śubhakṛtsna,
> And Bṛhatphala: birth in these heavens corresponds.

> If one constantly cultivates the antidotes,
> Has superior virtue, and pities sentient beings:
> These five practices constitute goodness.
> Not practicing them is a great evil.

> A pinch of salt in a small amount of water is salty;
> But what of casting it into the river?
> Even a minor misdeed:
> If wholesome deeds are great, it is annihilated, you should know.

> Anger, restlessness, regret,
> Torpor, craving, and doubt:
> These five hindrances are like thieves
> That constantly steal all wholesome benefits.

> There are five most supreme dharmas:
> Faith, vigor, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom.
> These should be diligently cultivated;
> They can bring the faculties, powers, and pinnacle.

> The suffering of illness, death, and separation from the beloved:
> All of these are produced by one's own karma.
> What has not been crossed, one should diligently cultivate.
> Counter each tendency; abandon vanity and indulgence.

> If you aspire to the heavens or liberation,
> You must cultivate right view.
> Even if a person practices goodness,
> With wrong views, the fruit is evil.

> No pleasure, constant [suffering], no self,
> And impurity: truly know this regarding the person.
> Deluded mindfulness and the four inverted views
> Are the root of suffering in this body.

> Form is said to be not the self;
> The self does not possess form.
> Form and self do not mutually abide.
> Know the remaining four aggregates are likewise empty.

> [The aggregates] are not born from time, nor spontaneously;
> Not from inherent nature, nor without cause,
> Nor from a creator god:
> They arise from ignorance, karma, and craving.

> [Attachment to] precepts and rituals, the view of the perishable collection,
> And doubt (*vicikitsā*):
> Know that these three fetters
> Bind the gate of liberation.

> Liberation ultimately depends on oneself;
> It is not accomplished through another's companionship.
> Diligently cultivating learning, morality, and concentration,
> The four noble truths will arise.

> The higher training in morality, mind, and wisdom:
> This threefold learning should be constantly cultivated.
> The one hundred and fifty-some precepts
> Are all subsumed under these three.

> On the body, establish mindfulness of the body.
> This path should be well cultivated at all times.
> If one neglects right mindfulness,
> All dharmas are lost and destroyed.

> Life is beset with many disasters,
> Like a water-bubble blown by the wind.
> If one can pause even for a breath's duration,
> To lie down and rise again is truly rare.

> In the end all returns to ash, parching, or rot:
> Impure filth cannot be maintained for long.
> Contemplate the body as not a real dharma:
> It decays, collapses, and falls apart.

> The great earth, Mount Sumeru, and the oceans
> Shall be burned when seven suns arise.
> How much more this infinitesimal body:
> How could it not become cinders?

> Thus impermanent and not enduring,
> Without refuge, without rescue, without home:
> Those of the highest capacity must turn away from birth-and-death,
> For, like the plantain tree, the body has no substance.

> A sea-turtle inserting its neck into the hole of a floating log:
> Such a meeting is extremely rare to encounter.
> To abandon an animal [body] and obtain a human one,
> Then commit evil deeds, only invites retribution again.

> A golden bowl used for scooping dung:
> This is the greatest folly.
> If one is born human and commits crimes,
> One becomes the most complete fool.

> In this life, rely on a good spiritual friend
> And generate the right aspiration.
> With merit-karma from former lives:
> These four great wheels are fully obtained.

> The Buddha says: Drawing near to a good spiritual friend
> Is the entirety of the holy life, one's closest ally.
> Good people, by relying on the Buddha,
> Have in great numbers realized nirvāṇa.

> Wrong views lead to birth as ghosts or animals;
> In the hells, the dharma is not heard.
> In borderlands, among barbarians,
> Born with innate delusion and muteness;
> Or born in the long-lived heavens:
> Eliminating these eight unfavorable conditions
> And having obtained leisure,
> You should strive for the sake of future lives.

> Separation from loved ones, old age, illness, and death:
> All these are places of manifold suffering.
> The wise should give rise to weariness.
> Listen to a brief account of their faults.

> A mother may become a wife;
> A father may be reborn as a son.
> An enemy may turn into a friend:
> The cycle of transmigration has no fixed rule.

> The mother's milk that each [being] has drunk one by one
> Exceeds the waters of the four seas.
> As one continues to take different bodies in birth,
> One drinks yet more than that.

> The bones of one's past bodies, each and every one,
> If piled up, would rival Mount Sumeru.
> If the earth were rolled into pellets the size of jujube pits,
> The number of one's past forms could still not be counted.

> Even Brahmā, whom all the world worships,
> By the force of karma finally falls to the earth.
> Even one who inherits the throne of a wheel-turning king
> May in the next life become a slave.

> In the heaven of the thirty-three, among celestial maidens' pleasures,
> After long enjoyment, one falls into the hells.
> With terrifying swiftness one passes through all sufferings,
> Body ground and crushed, constantly wailing.

> Atop Sumeru one enjoys bliss;
> The ground is soft beneath one's feet.
> Then one falls to suffer in the smoldering ash,
> Walking through the prison of excrement.

> In joyous, fragrant gardens,
> Celestial maidens accompany one's play.
> Then one falls into the sword-forest,
> Where hands, feet, ears, and nose are severed.

> Or one bathes in the wondrous pool of *mandārava* [flowers],
> Celestial maidens with golden blossoms of splendid beauty.
> Then one abandons that body and suffers in the hells:
> Amid blazing heat, in rivers of burning ash, unbearable.

> In the desire-heavens one enjoys the dharma's bliss;
> Free from desire, one reaches the Brahmā heavens.
> Then one falls again into Avīci,
> Where the fuel and flames of suffering never cease.

> Or one is born as the sun or moon,
> One's bodily radiance illumining the four continents.
> Then all at once one enters utter darkness,
> Where one cannot see one's outstretched hand.

> Three kinds of luminous merit
> Can be carried with one after death.
> Alone one enters the boundless darkness;
> The sun and moon shed no light there.

> The Living (*Saṃjīva*), the Black-Thread (*Kālasūtra*), the Hot (*Tapana*),
> The Crushing (*Saṃghāta*), the Screaming (*Raurava*), and the deepest Uninterrupted (*Avīci*):
> These are constantly wrapped in suffering,
> Burning all who practice evil.

> Some are pressed as on a bed of sesame [under a millstone];
> Some are ground to fine powder.
> Like a tree split with a sharp axe,
> Like a saw cutting and carving.

> In fierce fire one is constantly boiled.
> One is made to drink molten copper.
> One is driven onto the points of swords
> And impaled on a bed of burning iron.

> Sometimes with hands raised high,
> Iron-fanged ferocious dogs devour one.
> Eagles and vultures with sharp beaks and talons
> Tear freely at one's heart and liver.

> Gadflies, mosquitoes, and vermin
> In numbers exceeding a thousand million,
> With sharp mouths suck upon the body;
> Swarming down, all feed upon the flesh.

> If a person has committed many evil deeds
> And, hearing of such suffering, is not personally moved to fall prostrate,
> Such a dull person with a diamond-hard nature
> At the end of breath will meet the fierce fires of hell.

> Constantly contemplate transformation, listen and remember,
> Read and recite the sūtras, always searching and studying.
> Even hearing the echo of the hells makes one tremble with fear:
> How will one face such karmic fruition?

> Among all pleasures, which is the highest?
> The bliss of craving exhausted and non-arising is supreme.
> Among all sufferings, which is the worst?
> The suffering of Avīci hell is the most extreme.

> In the human world, in a single day,
> Three hundred spears are repeatedly thrust:
> Compared to the lightest suffering of the hells,
> It does not amount to even a hair's fraction.

> In that place one receives the ultimate suffering
> For a hundred *koṭi* autumns.
> As long as evil [karma] is not exhausted,
> There is no possibility of life ending.

> Such evil fruits as these
> Are planted by body, speech, and mind.
> You should diligently guard with all your strength:
> Let not even a dust-mote of evil encroach.

> Or one enters the animal realm,
> Where slaughter and bondage are constant companions.
> Far from all peaceful goodness,
> Beings mutually inflict hardship.

> Some are killed and bound in suffering,
> Sought for their pearls, tails, horns, or skins.
> Stabbed, whipped, hooked, and struck on the head,
> Trampled, beaten, ridden at another's will.

> Hungry ghosts suffer from hopes unfulfilled;
> Without respite, suffering constantly assails.
> Hunger, thirst, cold, and heat,
> Exhaustion and terror always encroach.

> Mouths small as the eye of a needle,
> Bellies as large as a hillock:
> Wracked by hunger, even their own filth,
> They cannot obtain the slightest bit.

> In form like withered stumps of trees,
> Their skin serves as clothing.
> Torches blaze from their mouths at night;
> Moths fall in to fill their food.

> Blood, pus, and every impurity:
> Those with little merit cannot obtain them.
> They press against each other, mouth to mouth,
> Feeding on ripe goiters and abscesses.

> Under the moon they feel scorching heat;
> Under the sun their bodies turn cold.
> They gaze at fruit trees, but only bare trunks remain;
> They look toward the river, but the water is already dry.

> Enduring such manifold suffering
> For fifteen thousand years,
> Long struck in body and life,
> All because their vessel of suffering is strong.

> If born among the hungry ghosts,
> Suffering this single flavor of torment,
> Not through goodness but through mean attachments:
> The Buddha says it is due to the stain of miserliness.

> Though born in heaven, one enjoys bliss,
> But when merit is exhausted, the suffering is inconceivable.
> In the end, one must fall again.
> Do not delight [in it]: this you should know.

> Weary of sitting, garments stained with grime,
> Bodily radiance showing decline,
> Sweat newly flowing from the armpits,
> Old flowers on the crown now wilted.

> When these five signs appear,
> Among the celestial hosts, death is certain.
> When a deva of the terrestrial heavens expires, like a human,
> Confusion and disorder alter their former bearing.

> If one falls from the heavenly realm,
> All wholesome [karma] is exhausted without remainder.
> At the mercy of fate, one falls among animals, ghosts,
> Or resides in one of the hells.

> The asuras by their very nature,
> Even if fully endowed with awakened wisdom,
> Through hatred toward the devas generate the mind of suffering;
> Their destinies obstruct the seeing of truth.

> Thus one drifts through the places of birth-and-death:
> Devas, humans, animals, and asuras,
> The lowly born by karma, vessels of manifold suffering,
> The realm of ghosts, and the plunge into hell.

> Even if fierce fire blazes atop your head
> And flames spread through all your garments,
> There is no leisure for this suffering to be brushed away:
> Dwell in the thought of non-arising, abiding in nirvāṇa.

> Seek morality, concentration, and wisdom:
> Peaceful, tamed, free from stain and harm.
> Nirvāṇa is without end, without old age and death;
> The four elements, sun, and moon all perish.

> Mindfulness, investigation, vigor,
> Concentration, wisdom, joy, and pliancy:
> These seven factors of awakening
> Can bring about the wondrous nirvāṇa.

> Without wisdom, concentration cannot exist;
> Without concentration, wisdom drowns.
> If both are yoked together,
> The ocean of existence [becomes] like a hoofprint of an ox.

> The fourteen undeclared matters
> Were so stated by the Kinsman of the Sun.
> On these one should not speculate:
> They cannot bring about awakening or cessation.

> From ignorance arises karma;
> From karma further arises consciousness.
> Consciousness conditions name-and-form;
> Name-and-form produces the six sense-bases.
> The six bases condition contact;
> Contact gives rise to feeling.
> Feeling then conditions craving;
> From craving comes grasping.
> Grasping further conditions becoming;
> Becoming further conditions birth.
> Birth conditions old age, death,
> Grief, illness, and unfulfilled seeking.

> The great mass of suffering in the cycle:
> This should be swiftly cut off.
> If birth is extinguished,
> All suffering ceases without remainder.

> The most supreme treasury of the teaching
> Is the profound and wondrous gate of dependent origination.
> If one can correctly see this,
> One contemplates the Supremely Honored One.

> Right view, right livelihood, right mindfulness,
> Right concentration, right speech, right action, right effort:
> This is called the eightfold noble path.
> For the sake of peace, cultivate and practice it.

> Where there is no gathering, craving does not arise;
> Entrusted to the body, manifold suffering is born.
> Eliminate these and realize liberation:
> The eightfold noble path should be practiced.

> Precisely this is the yoga-karma,
> The cause of the four noble truths.
> Though one dwells in an adorned chamber,
> Wisdom can bar the flood of afflictions.
> One does not fall from the realm of space;
> Like grain, [awakening] is cultivated upon the ground.

> All who previously realized the dharma
> Were ordinary beings fully endowed with afflictions.
> Why bother with much discourse?
> To remove afflictions, I offer these brief words.
> The matter rests on what the feelings can subdue;
> The sages have said that the mind is the source.

> The dharma I have set forth above
> Is difficult for even a bhikṣu to practice in full.
> According to one's ability, cultivate even one thing;
> Do not let this life pass in vain.

> Rejoice in all wholesome deeds;
> Cultivate excellent practice in the three [trainings] oneself.
> Dedicate [the merit] toward the attainment of buddhahood;
> Gather this treasury of merit constantly.

> In a future life, with immeasurable lifespan,
> Broadly delivering humans and devas,
> Like Avalokiteśvara,
> Transcending the difficulty of treating enemies and kin equally.

> Birth, old age, illness, death, and the three poisons removed;
> Born in a buddha-land, becoming a father of the world;
> Lifespan of immeasurable duration beyond reckoning:
> Equal to the Great Awakened One, the Lord Amitābha.

> Morality, generosity, and wisdom opened and made manifest;
> Fame pervading heaven, earth, and empty space.
> May all who dwell on the great earth, and the celestial hosts,
> Never be harmed by the seductive love of bewitching beauty.

> Sentient beings bound by the fetters of afflictions,
> Cut the stream of birth-and-death and ascend to right awakening.
> Transcend the world: these are but names.
> Having attained non-arising, one is free from the dust of defilement.

---

*Ārya Nāgārjuna Bodhisattva Suhṛllekha*. Completed.
