# 譬喻經
## The Sūtra of the Parable

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0217

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time the Bhagavān was dwelling in the Jetavana, Anāthapiṇḍada's garden, at Śrāvastī. At that time the World-Honored One, in the midst of a great assembly, addressed King Prasenajit:

"Great King! I will now set forth a brief parable for the King concerning the faults of attachment to the pleasures of saṃsāra. Let the King listen with care and reflect upon it well.

"In the distant past, countless kalpas ago, there was a man wandering in a vast wilderness who was pursued by a wild elephant. Fleeing in terror with no refuge, he came upon an empty well beside which grew tree roots. He climbed down the roots and concealed himself within the well. Two mice, one black and one white, were taking turns gnawing at the tree roots. On the four sides of the well were four venomous serpents seeking to bite him, and below was a venomous dragon. His heart trembled in fear of the dragon and the serpents, and he dreaded that the tree roots would be severed. From the tree roots, five drops of honey fell into his mouth. The tree then swayed, the bees scattered, and they stung the man. A wildfire came and set the tree ablaze."

The King said: "How can this person, suffering such measureless misery, still crave those few drops of sweetness?"

At that time the World-Honored One said: "Great King! The vast wilderness symbolizes the long night of ignorance — vast and far-reaching. The man symbolizes an ordinary being. The elephant symbolizes impermanence. The well symbolizes saṃsāra. The tree roots on the steep bank symbolize life. The two mice, black and white, symbolize day and night. Their gnawing at the roots symbolizes the cessation occurring moment by moment. The four venomous serpents symbolize the four great elements. The honey symbolizes the five desires. The bees symbolize wrong thoughts. The fire symbolizes old age and illness. The venomous dragon symbolizes death.

"Therefore, Great King, know that birth, old age, illness, and death are truly fearsome. One should constantly reflect upon them and not be consumed and oppressed by the five desires."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke verses in summary:

> The wilderness path of ignorance: a fleeing man symbolizes the ordinary being;
> The great elephant represents impermanence; the well symbolizes the shore of birth and death.
>
> The tree roots symbolize life; the two mice correspond equally to day and night;
> Gnawing at the root, declining moment by moment; the four serpents match the four great elements.
>
> The drops of honey symbolize the five desires; the bee-stings represent wrong thoughts;
> Fire corresponds to old age and illness; the venomous dragon stands for the suffering of death.
>
> The wise one who contemplates these things may arouse revulsion at the stream of rebirth;
> With mind unattached to the five desires — such a one is rightly called a person of liberation.
>
> Ever dwelling in the sea of ignorance, always driven by the King of Death —
> How is it they still cling to sounds and forms, and take no delight in departing from the ordinary state?

At that time King Prasenajit, having heard the Buddha set forth the faults and dangers of saṃsāra, experienced what he had never experienced before. A profound sense of revulsion arose within him. With palms joined in reverence, he gazed upon the Buddha with single-minded devotion and said to him: "World-Honored One! The great compassion of the Tathāgata has spoken this wondrous and subtle teaching. I now receive it upon the crown of my head."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! Great King, act in accordance with this teaching and do not be negligent."

King Prasenajit and the entire great assembly were all filled with joy, and with faith they received and practiced it.
