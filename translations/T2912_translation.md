# Sūtra of Impermanence with Three Invocations

## *Wúcháng Sānqǐ Jīng* (無常三啟經)

Taishō 2912

Translator unknown. An apocryphal liturgical text.

*Note: This text combines opening and closing verses with a short sūtra passage (drawn from the canonical Sūtra on Impermanence, T801) to form a complete liturgical unit used in funeral and memorial services. The "three invocations" (sānqǐ) refer to the three sections: opening verses of homage, the sūtra proper, and closing verses. This liturgical arrangement was widely used in Chinese Buddhist funerary practice.*

### Opening Verses

> Those who cultivate all reach the unconditioned shore.
> The cloud of dharma, the rain of dharma, nourishes all beings,
> Able to remove fevered affliction and cure the many ills.
> Those difficult to convert are made tame and compliant;
> Guiding according to capacity, not by force.
> I bow my head in homage to the true and holy saṃgha.
> The eight grades of noble ones are able to leave defilement.
> The vajra wisdom-pestle shatters the mountain of wrong views,
> Forever severing the bonds that have entangled since beginningless time.
> From the Deer Park to the twin śāla trees,
> Following the Buddha through one lifetime, they propagated the true teaching.
> Each, according to their original conditions, completed their transforming work,
> Then cremated their bodies, extinguished their knowledge, and realized the unborn.
> I bow my head and pay reverence to the Three Jewels together:
> This is the right cause that can universally save.
> Those lost in delusion through birth and death are forever sinking and drowning;
> May all be caused to leave and depart, and reach bodhi.

### Verses on Impermanence

> All who are born will return to death.
> Countenance and beauty all fade and decline.
> Strength and vigor are assailed by illness;
> No one can be exempt from this.
> Even if the wondrous Mount Sumeru,
> When the kalpa ends, is all scattered and destroyed,
> And the great ocean, deep and bottomless,
> Also completely dries up,
> The great earth and the sun and moon,
> When their time comes, all return to exhaustion:
> Never has there been a single thing
> Not swallowed by impermanence.
> From the upper births in the heavens above
> Down to the wheel-turning kings below,
> With the seven treasures always at their side
> And a thousand sons constantly surrounding them:
> When their life-span is exhausted,
> Not for a moment does it pause.
> They drift again in the sea of death,
> Following conditions, receiving manifold sufferings.
> Cycling within the three realms,
> Like a wheel drawing water from a well,
> Also like silkworms making cocoons,
> Spinning thread that binds themselves.
> The unsurpassed world-honored ones,
> The pratyekabuddhas and śrāvaka assemblies
> Still cast off their impermanent bodies:
> How much more so all ordinary beings?
> Fathers, mothers, wives, and children,
> Brothers and all relatives:
> They watch as birth and death divide them.
> How can they not grieve and lament?
> Therefore I urge all of you:
> Listen carefully to the true and real dharma.
> Together abandon the place of impermanence
> And walk through the gate of deathlessness.
> The Buddha's teaching is like sweet dew,
> Removing fever and obtaining cool clarity.
> With one mind you should listen well:
> It can extinguish all afflictions.

### The Sūtra

Thus have I heard. At one time the Bhagavān (*Bóqiéfàn*, 薄伽梵) was in the city of Śrāvastī (*Shìluófá*, 室羅伐), in the Jeta Grove (*Shìduō Lín*, 逝多林), the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada (*Jǐgūdú*, 給孤獨).

At that time the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "There are three kinds of dharmas in this world that are not to be loved, not radiant, not to be longed for, and not agreeable. What are these three? They are old age, illness, and death. Bhikṣus, these three, old age, illness, and death, in this world are truly not to be loved, truly not radiant, truly not to be longed for, and truly not agreeable. If old age, illness, and death did not exist in the world, the Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened One, would not appear in the world to teach sentient beings the dharma he has realized and the means of taming. Therefore you should know that these three, old age, illness, and death, in this world are not to be loved, not radiant, not to be longed for, and not agreeable. It is because of these three things that the Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened One, appears in the world to teach sentient beings the dharma he has realized and the means of taming."

At that time the World-Honored One repeated this meaning in verse:

> External adornments all return to ruin;
> The inner body's decline and change are also thus.
> Only the excellent dharma is not destroyed:
> Those with wisdom should well examine this.
> These three, old age, illness, and death, are universally loathed.
> Their appearance is ugly and extremely repulsive.
> The beauty of youth lingers but a moment;
> Before long, all becomes withered and wasted.
> Even if one's life-span reaches a hundred years,
> In the end one cannot escape the press of impermanence.
> Old age, illness, and the suffering of death always follow;
> They forever work against sentient beings.

At that time, when the World-Honored One had finished speaking this sūtra, the bhikṣu assembly, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, and all others greatly rejoiced, faithfully accepted it, and put it into practice.

### Closing Verses

> Always seeking the objects of desire,
> Not practicing wholesome deeds:
> How can you protect this body and life
> When you do not see death approaching?
> When the life-root and vital breath are about to end,
> The limbs and joints all separate.
> The many sufferings come together with death;
> At this time one laments in vain.
> Both eyes roll upward;
> Death's blade descends according to one's karma.
> Thoughts and imaginings are all in turmoil;
> No one can come to the rescue.
> Long gasps press against the chest;
> Choking breath catches in the throat.
> The King of Death dispatches his agents;
> Relatives stand by to no avail.
> All the consciousnesses become dark and confused;
> One enters into the perilous citadel.
> Kin and acquaintances all abandon one;
> Led away by the black rope,
> One is brought before King Yama (*Yánmó Wáng*, 琰摩王).
> According to one's karma, one receives retribution.
> Superior causes bring birth in good destinies;
> Evil karma casts one into the hells.
> No eye is brighter than wisdom;
> No darkness is greater than ignorance.
> No illness surpasses an enemy;
> No terror exceeds death.
> All who are born must surely die;
> Creating offenses brings suffering to the body.
> One should diligently exert the three kinds of karma,
> Always cultivating merit and wisdom.
> Relatives all depart and leave;
> Wealth and goods are taken by others.
> Hold only to your own roots of goodness
> As provisions for the perilous road.
> It is like a tree by the roadside:
> One rests briefly, not for long.
> Carriages, horses, wives, and children
> Are all like this before long.
> It is like a flock of birds roosting together:
> They gather at night and fly off at dawn.
> Death departs, parting from kin and acquaintances;
> The separation is also like this.
> Only the bodhi of the Buddha
> Is the true place of refuge.
> Based on the sūtras, I speak in brief;
> The wise should reflect well upon this.

> Devas, asuras, yakṣas, and the rest
> Who have come to hear the dharma should do so wholeheartedly.
> Protect and uphold the buddha-dharma so it endures long;
> Each one should diligently practice the World-Honored One's teaching.
> All those in the listening assembly who have come here,
> Whether on the ground or dwelling in space,
> Should always give rise to compassion toward the human world
> And day and night abide in the dharma themselves.
> May all the worlds be constantly at peace,
> And boundless merit and wisdom benefit all beings.
> May all offenses and karmic hindrances be entirely eliminated,
> And may all leave behind the many sufferings and return to perfect quiescence.
> Always use the fragrance of the precepts to anoint and polish the body;
> Always wear the garment of samādhi to nourish the self.
> May the wondrous flowers of bodhi adorn all places;
> Wherever one abides, may there always be peace and happiness.
