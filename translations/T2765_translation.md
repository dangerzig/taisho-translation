# 涅槃經疏
## Commentary on the Nirvāṇa Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2765

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

When one attains the principle, understanding becomes increasingly bright. Eliminating the ten evils of body, speech, and mind is called "contact." When the three karmas are pure, one attains "right livelihood," meaning that the continuous succession of the ten evils' life-force is eliminated; this is called attaining the contact of right livelihood.

"Sensation" is named "appropriation." When sentient beings experience sensation, they apprehend objects as suffering, existence, or non-existence; this is called "sensation." On the second ground, wisdom becomes increasingly bright and one comprehends the merit below the second ground; therefore sensation is called "appropriation." Because of sensation, afflictions arise. The thirty-seven factors of awakening can embrace the retribution of the three evil destinies. Therefore sensation is taken as appropriation. This is the name assigned in terms of what is eliminated.

"Thought" is the mind of the third ground. One has severed all coarse and subtle delusions of the path of seeing. When understanding of mind surpasses the previous two grounds, it is called "increase."

"Obtaining" pertains to the thirty-seven factors that destroy afflictions; this is the mind of the fourth ground. It can destroy the upper-grade afflictions in the path of cultivation. It necessarily relies on right mindfulness to govern merit; mindfulness is the "governor."

"As in the world" and what follows use analogies to explain.

"Having entered concentration" is the mind of the fifth ground. Cultivating the thirty-seven factors, one can skillfully discriminate dharma-characteristics and generate subsequent wisdom. Therefore the governor becomes the "guide."

"These thirty-seven factors" pertain to the sixth ground's prajñā conditioned by emptiness, and the seventh ground's prajñā of skillful means, which can discriminate dharma-characteristics. Wisdom and understanding are most excellent. Therefore wisdom is called "the supreme."

"If one destroys afflictions," eliminating three kinds of merit: the eighth, ninth, and tenth grounds. These three grounds' excellent understanding is then named "the real." These thirty-seven factors, though one attains the four fruits of the śramaṇa and liberation: from stream-enterer up to arhat. Yet these four fruits still cannot be called "ultimate." Severing the thirty-seven factors' domain of practice: when the mind's course of activity is extinguished, this is called "great nirvāṇa."

"Furthermore" begins the fourth section, explaining through the mutual arising of cause and fruit.

"The mind of loving thought is desire": the thought of wishing to attain bodhi and wishing to be born on the second ground is called "desire." Through lower-grade loving thought as a wholesome cause, the arising of the upper grade is "contact." This is called "cause."

The previous two mental states give rise to the second ground; this is named "sensation as appropriation." "Because of drawing near to good friends": because of the previous two grounds, one constantly understands the principle, and understanding becomes increasingly bright; this is called "increase."

"Because of these four dharmas": relying on the previous root-causes of appropriation, increase, and these four dharmas, one can generate and develop the fourth ground's unconditioned state. This is called "mindfulness." The fifth ground's concentration-mind. The sixth and seventh grounds' wisdom are then called "governor" and "supreme."

"Because of these three dharmas": because the governor and wisdom generate the latter three grounds, these are called "the two liberations." Delusion can obstruct wisdom; craving can bind the mind. By severing craving, the mind is liberated. By eliminating delusion, wisdom becomes bright. The two liberations of mind and wisdom are called "the real." These eight dharmas, with the empty understanding continuously maintained, ultimately attain the fruit called "great nirvāṇa."

"Desire is the initial mind of aspiration" begins the fifth section, explaining through the depth and shallowness of the Dharma of renunciation.

The lower-grade mind of the first ground is the first unconditioned state, called "initial aspiration to renounce the household." "Contact" is the white four-fold karman: contact is the upper-grade mind, like after renunciation receiving the white four-fold karman; this is the name of the second ground's mind. "Appropriation," like after the white four-fold karman, generates two kinds of precepts. "Increase" is surpassing, like from the two kinds of precepts, cultivating and attaining the four dhyāna concentration-minds. "Governor" becomes increasingly strong, like after concentration, generating wisdom that severs all fetters of the path of seeing. In the path of cultivation, the six grades of fetters are exhausted. This realizes the two fruits of stream-enterer and once-returner. "Guide" means severing the three subtle delusions completely, like a non-returner. "Supreme" means the two kinds of prajñā are supreme; this is named "arhat." "Real" means the latter three grounds' true mind, where the Dharma-stream illuminates both existence and non-existence with the sharpest insight; this is named "pratyekabuddha." "Ultimate" has the meaning of completing the cause; this is named "the unsurpassed great fruit."

"Desire is named consciousness": among the nine dharmas, desire is most fundamental. Just as in one lifetime, consciousness is most fundamental; therefore desire is called "consciousness." "Contact is named the six sense-bases": the fundamental contact engages the six sense-objects. Just as the upper-grade mind of the first ground contacts the second ground; also, one can contact objects and generate understanding. Therefore contact is called "the six sense-bases." "Appropriation is named sensation": sensation originally means receiving and comprehending suffering and happiness; appropriation can comprehend the merit below the second ground; therefore appropriation is called "sensation." "Increase is named ignorance": past ignorance generates delusion most powerfully. Just as the third ground's patient trust, in its upper grade, is stronger than what preceded it. "Governor is named name-and-form": name and form mutually depend on each other so they do not disintegrate or scatter, continuously receiving a hundred years of one segment of retribution. Just as the fourth ground's lower mind of the unconditioned is continuous; therefore the governor is called "name-and-form." "Guide is named craving": craving originally means lustfully attaching to sense-objects. Just as the fifth ground's mind can generate subsequent wisdom; therefore the guide is called "craving." "Supreme is named grasping": grasping means attachment to sense-objects. Just as the sixth ground's wisdom-prajñā and the seventh ground's skillful-means prajñā: these two wisdoms attain the principle with increasingly bright understanding; therefore it is called "supreme." "Real is named becoming": containing the fruit not yet manifested is called "becoming." Among what discerns birth, old age, and death, the strongest is called "becoming." This means the eighth, ninth, and tenth grounds: these three grounds' minds constitute the great patient acceptance of non-arising. Merit and understanding become increasingly bright, and the power to discern the great fruit's meaning is strong; therefore the real is called "becoming." "Ultimate is named old age and death": old age and death are originally the very end of one lifetime. Just as the nirvāṇa-fruit is the ultimate destination; therefore the ultimate is called "old age and death." Just as the preceding twelve links yield results, so nirvāṇa is what the preceding eight dharmas yield.

## The Kauṇḍinya Chapter

"Kāśyapa speaks" begins the fourth passage, which abbreviates the above nine dharmas into three.

Within this, there are also six turns of explanation. The first directly lists the names. The second, "root means 'having already made'" and what follows, converts names to explain meanings. The third, "in the future world" and what follows, directly explains through the dharmas of the present and future. The fourth, "root means 'seeking'" and what follows, formally establishes the three dharma-names. The fifth, "root means 'the path of seeing'" and what follows, explains through awakening and non-awakening. The sixth, "root means 'the correct cause'" and what follows, explains through the mutual arising of cause and fruit.

"How does root-cause differ from increase?" "Good man" begins the answer. From the first ground through the seventh ground, all are the initial arising. "Cause" means the latter three grounds share the same position within the Dharma-stream without being interrupted; therefore they are called "similar." "Increase" means that what the three similar ones, the practitioners who face extinction, attain in the fruit is the true similarity. Since the future retribution has not yet been manifested and received, it belongs to the practitioner and is called "cause." When the myriad practices are complete and omniscient wisdom manifests, it is called "increase." The text below can be understood accordingly.

"Kāśyapa says: 'How can such nirvāṇa be attained?'" The reason for this question is as follows. Above it was said that the thirty-seven factors in the mind of the nature-ground still cannot be taken as the brahma-conduct. One must be from the first ground upward, continuing to buddhahood, with the mind of emptiness continuously maintained, to accomplish the brahma-conduct. When people hear this, they may give rise to a mind of retreat: "The brahma-conduct only begins from the first ground upward. Below the grounds there is no entrance. We of the present assembly of devas do not yet have the cause below the grounds. The practices above the grounds: how can they be accessible? Since the causal practices are not accomplished, how can the great nirvāṇa-fruit be attained?"

"Good man" begins the answer. The four assemblies need only cultivate the ten marks as a cause. From this, one is born on the first ground, and continuously one ultimately attains great nirvāṇa. You cultivate the ten marks; therefore you can attain it. Why wait for many practices? This is the reach of that wisdom. From marks, names are born; therefore they are called "marks." The meaning of the ten marks can be easily understood from the text.

### The Kauṇḍinya Chapter

The sūtra-masters explain this chapter as follows. The previous two chapters urged people to cultivate the Way. Since there is cultivation of the Way, one must ultimately attain the fruit. Therefore, this chapter on Kauṇḍinya has the intent of transmission and circulation.

Why was Kauṇḍinya named first? Because Kauṇḍinya was the senior member of the assembly. After the Tathāgata's extinction, he was entrusted with circulating the teaching so that it would not be extinguished for a thousand generations. This is the first reason for the naming.

Second, the teaching of the sūtra being completed, ten ṛṣis awakened to the Way; their karmic connection lay with Kauṇḍinya. This is the second reason.

Third, at the very beginning when the Buddha first attained the Way, at the Deer Park he first taught the Four Noble Truths, originally for Kauṇḍinya. Now, at the final teaching at the end, at the twin trees, it is again for Kauṇḍinya. This demonstrates that the Tathāgata has both a beginning and an end. This is the third reason.

In this transmission and circulation, one transforms the impermanent form and attains the permanent form. Since it is called "transmission and circulation," how does its meaning relate to the chapter above? The Tathāgata himself says: "Above, I taught that form is impermanent. You should extinguish the impermanent form and attain the permanent form. In the future, circulate this Dharma of mine." Therefore this chapter is called "Transmission and Circulation."

"Form" is the substance of the permanent fruit. By severing and eliminating all the form of birth and death, the substance becomes bright, manifest, and pure: its meaning is the same as "form." The Tathāgata has severed the three lower sensations forever. The substance is the ultimate. Receiving and comprehending the true merit is called "sensation." The wisdom of true illumination reaches the end of all provisional names and characteristics; this is called "perception." "Formations": below, the accumulation of birth and death is called "formations." The Tathāgata has severed all evils and accumulated ten thousand virtues; this is called "formations." Below, "consciousness" means discriminating blue, yellow, and the like. True wisdom can reach the real dharmas; this is called "consciousness." Below, "the aggregates" means "covering." The practitioner is always within birth and death; this is called "the aggregate-fruit." The manifest aggregates can cover and shield the practitioner, causing them to be free and beyond; therefore they are called "the aggregates."

If anyone can understand the above meaning of the permanent and impermanent, they are called a "śramaṇa": one who truly ceases evil. They are called a "brāhmaṇa": one of truly pure conduct. Within my buddha-dharma, there are persons who are śramaṇas. If, apart from the buddha-dharma, non-Buddhists claim to have the śramaṇa-dharma or śramaṇa persons, this is untenable. Those who make such claims are entirely non-Buddhists.

Why was this passage composed? To reproach the non-Buddhists and cause each one who establishes a divine self to debate with the Buddha, distinguishing right from wrong. Through this discourse, they return to the correct teaching. Therefore this passage was composed.

The non-Buddhists say: "In former days, Gautama taught suffering, impermanence, emptiness, and non-self, and gathered my disciples. That was still tolerable. His understanding differed from ours. The unwise followed him; the wise returned to us. Therefore it was tolerable. But today, at the twin trees, he teaches the permanent, blissful, self, and pure. All our disciples have been converted and are gone. Our feelings cannot bear it. Therefore we seek debate and criticism."

Among the assembly, there were brāhmaṇas. Previously, the Tathāgata went up to the sixteen heavenly realms and debated with the non-Buddhists, and all were converted to the correct teaching. Among these, the ten ṛṣis are yet another group of non-Buddhists. Now they have just come to debate. Among these ten ṛṣis, those with the keenest faculties and swiftest capacity will awaken.
