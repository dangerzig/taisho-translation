# 梵網六十二見經
## Sūtra on the Brahmā Net of Sixty-Two Views

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0021

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhī Qiān (支謙)

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the land of Kuru, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, twelve hundred and fifty in all. At that time, the wanderer Subhadda and his disciple, the brahmin student Brahmadatta, were following along with the Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus. The wanderer Subhadda reviled the Buddha countless times, as well as the Dharma and the assembly of bhikṣus; his disciple, the brahmin student Brahmadatta, praised the Buddha, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus countless times. Teacher and disciple thus fell into dispute with each other, each contradicting the other's words, and they continually followed along after the Buddha and the bhikṣu sangha to receive invitations to meals. At that time, the Buddha traveled from the land of Kuru to the land of Śrāvastī, and took up residence in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park. The bhikṣus then assembled in the Kāḷī Lecture Hall and sat together in discussion, saying: "What is to be made of this matter? The wanderer Subhadda and his disciple, the brahmin student Brahmadatta, continually follow the Buddha and the bhikṣu sangha to receive invitations to meals. The wanderer Subhadda reviles the Buddha, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus countless times; his disciple Brahmadatta praises the Buddha, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus countless times."

The Buddha, with his penetrating hearing, heard from afar the bhikṣus discussing this matter, and arose and went to the lecture hall. The Buddha sat down, and asked the bhikṣus: "Just now you were assembled in the Kāḷī Lecture Hall; what were you discussing?"

The bhikṣus said to the Buddha: "Just now we were assembled and discussing: 'The wanderer Subhadda and his disciple, the brahmin student Brahmadatta, continually follow the Buddha and the bhikṣu sangha to receive invitations to meals. The wanderer Subhadda reviles the Buddha, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus countless times; his disciple Brahmadatta praises the Buddha, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus countless times.'"

The Buddha said: "Well done, bhikṣus! An assembly should always practice two things. What are the two? The first is discoursing on the Dharma; the second is meditation."

The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "If someone reviles me, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus, it is good for you not to give rise to anger, ill-will, or grief and sorrow. If someone reviles me, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus, and you give rise to anger, grief, and sorrow, that is a loss for you. Bhikṣus, if someone praises me, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus, you should not rejoice, nor should you feel grief or sorrow, nor should you be elated; for if you are elated, that too is a loss for you. If someone reviles me, the Dharma, and the bhikṣus, you should think in your mind: 'What that person says is not true; there is no such thing with regard to the Buddha.' Bhikṣus, if someone praises me, the Dharma, and the assembly of bhikṣus, you should think: 'These things are indeed so.' Why is this? Those of little knowledge who have only precepts and cannot hear much will praise the Buddha."

The bhikṣus asked the Buddha: "What is meant by those of little knowledge who have only precepts and cannot hear much praising the Buddha?"

The Buddha said: "Such a person might say: 'The Buddha does not kill living beings; he harbors no enmity or grudges; he does not carry knife or staff; he teaches people to do good; he has compassion for all beings, including crawling and flying creatures. He also does not take the property of others, but wishes only to give gifts, and his mind also intends to give; when he sees someone robbing and plundering, he has compassion for them. He himself practices purity and does not enter into the grounds of others' wrongdoing. He cultivates the pure brahmacarya life and delights in purity of conduct; he does not delight in evil, sensual desire, or the ways of passion. He also does not speak falsely. His words are true and sincere; he delights in reality without falsehood, and all people in the world trust his words without any doubt; he also does not think idle thoughts. He does not engage in divisive speech, carrying tales and provoking quarrels among people; if there is strife, he reconciles it and brings each party to peace and ease. He does not abuse others or speak harsh words. His speech brings joy to all, for he speaks only good and does not deceive. He knows the right time and is sincere; his actions have meaning and follow the Dharma; his words are gentle. He does not sit on high, ornate, fine beds; he does not use fragrant flowers; he does not listen to singing and dancing; he does not drink alcohol, nor does he wear gold, silver, or precious jewels. He always eats at the proper time, not missing the time for his meal; he does not accept male or female servants. He does not cut down living grain, nor does he accept chickens, sheep, or pigs. He has no house or dwelling, nor does he engage in buying and selling. He does not use deceitful weights and measures to cheat people. He is entirely free from knife, staff, beatings, and threats against people.

"'It is as if wanderers of other paths cling to food out of greed, and thus accumulate grain, wine, livestock, clothing, and medicines in their conduct; the śramaṇa Gautama has none of these.

"'It is as if wanderers of other paths, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, constantly perform foolish acts: moving about slowly in and out, slandering others, harboring jealousy, seeking only their own reverence; the Buddha is always free from such foolish conduct.

"'It is as if wanderers of other paths, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, accumulate livestock, villages, houses, grain, trees, fruits, and vegetable gardens, consuming these themselves; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who subsist on the faithful gifts of others, and sleep and rise on high, broad, ornate beds, spreading fine painted silk over gold and silver, along with hairs from all manner of elephants, horses, livestock, and flying birds as seat coverings; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'It is as if wanderers of other paths, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, engage together in conversation saying: "How are things with the king? How about bandits? How about troops? How about warfare? How about ministers? How about provinces, prefectures, and cities? How about women? How about those of licentious conduct?" They speak of worldly affairs, speak of the unfathomable matters of the sea; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform deceptive displays pretending reality, with their outward appearance and inner character inconsistent, showing bright and illuminating methods to seek wealth and profit, always greedy for their bowl; the Buddha is entirely free from such foolish views.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, engage in litigation with each other, saying: "I know the Dharma and the Vinaya; you do not know the Dharma and the Vinaya. You hold wrong views; how could you know the Dharma? I hold right views." Their words are contradictory and reversed: "My view is right, your view is wrong; you have lost, I have won; you are in the wrong, you are on the wrong side." Going so far as to no longer accept the other's words, saying: "You should study the practice; do you have a guarantor?" The Buddha is free from such animal-like conduct.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, are constantly engaged in dice games and gambling, saying: "I have obtained the stake, the game pieces, the dice, the royal game piece, the calf piece, the rook." The Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, bathe and anoint their bodies with mixed fragrances, adorning themselves, gazing at their reflection in mirrors, carrying tall canopied parasols, wearing shoes, dressing their hair, and adorning themselves with pearl necklaces and tassels; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, constantly engage in displays of harmful conduct: fighting with hands and feet, butting with heads and faces, inciting contests between elephants, horses, cattle, and sheep; provoking fights among men, women, and children; staging fights between chickens, pigs, and ducks; the Buddha is entirely free from such evil and wrong views.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform animal-like activities for their livelihood: distinguishing the qualities of knives, spears, bows, and arrows; reading the physiognomy of men and women, young and old; discerning the characteristics of elephants, horses, cattle, and sheep; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform animal-like activities for their livelihood: practicing medicine for men, women, and children; practicing veterinary medicine for elephants, horses, cattle, and sheep; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform animal-like activities for their livelihood: dealing with spirits and demons; making clothing; practicing medicine on themselves; practicing medicine for women; performing spells and incantations. When women come and go, they take grass and transform it into fine food for people to eat, and then deceptively demand fine goods in return; they transform plain garments and give them to people; they can enable people to fly through the air; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform animal-like activities for their livelihood, giving people medicines to cause vomiting; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform animal-like activities for their livelihood: summoning people and telling them to go east or west; casting spells to make people fight and quarrel with one another; beating people and knocking them to the ground; casting spells against women to harm their pregnancies; using reeds in spells attached to people's arms; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform animal-like activities for their livelihood: gathering firewood and lighting fires; making smoke from spells on chestnut bark, poisonous grapes, and seeds; casting spells on rats to injure and kill people; studying spells to know the times of people's birth and death; the Buddha is entirely free from such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform animal-like activities for their livelihood. One says there will be a great rain; another says there will be a light rain. One says grain will be plentiful and ripen well; another says it will not ripen. One says grain prices will be high; another says they will be low. One says there will be a great epidemic; another says there will not be. One says bandits will come and lay waste to this kingdom; another says there will be great mortality. One says there will be a king who falls and a king who rises; another says the earth will shake greatly; another says it will not. One says the moon will be eclipsed; another says the moon will not be eclipsed. One says the sun will be eclipsed; another says the sun will not be eclipsed. One says the sun moves from east to west; another says it moves from west to east. One says the moon and stars move from east to west; another says they move from west to east; because of this there is good fortune or ill fortune; another says because of this the sun, moon, and stars move from east to west; another says because of this the sun, moon, and stars rise; another says because of this the sun, moon, and stars set. One says clouds will cover the sun; another says it will emerge from the clouds; another says the sky will be clear without clouds. The Buddha is entirely free from all such things.

"'There are wanderers of other paths who, subsisting on the faithful gifts of others, perform animal-like activities for their livelihood. One says the king of this country will go to destroy that country; the king of that country will come to destroy this country; another says the king of this country has few chariots, horses, and animals. They interpret people's dreams; cast spells to make people unable to speak, causing people's mouths to clench shut; write for people for a fee; perform calculations for people for a fee; assess colors as good or bad for a fee. The Buddha is entirely free from all such things.'"

The Buddha said: "The śramaṇa eats one meal; in the evening he does not eat, eating at the proper time and refraining from eating at improper times. He knows how to stop and be content with robes and bowl; he takes food only in sufficient measure. Wherever he goes, he carries his robes and bowl with him on his person. It is like a bird in flight; wherever it goes, its two wings go with its body. So too with the bhikṣu. As for robes, coverings, food and drink, and the bowl; he takes only what suffices; wherever he goes, his robes and bowl go with him. So too with the bhikṣu. Receiving the admonitions of the worthy, he practices them; he observes his own body and does not engage in disputes; he contemplates the path; his actions are settled and composed. He does not form perceptions upon seeing forms, nor does he incite others to mutual conflict or agitation. He continues in stillness and is not foolishly confused; harmful, unwholesome mental states cannot disturb his resolve; he guards the eye faculty. This is the category of the bhikṣu's observance of the worthy one's admonitions. Thus the worthy one is composed with sense faculties at rest within, without thoughts of conflict or confusion; as for food and drink, he takes only what suffices. In eating, neither too much nor too little; finding just the right measure, he always eats thus, one meal, neither increasing nor decreasing, merely sustaining life without making strength the purpose, seeking only to keep the body at ease without suffering and pain, and to have the energy and strength for settled practice. For any bhikṣus who may come in the future, this is the excellent and wholesome conduct of precepts to be practiced; this is the excellent and wholesome. Taking food only in sufficient measure, he meditates on the path; in the first watch of the night and the last watch of the night he walks in practice, following the sublime way that does not waver. During the day he either sits or walks in kinhin, without entertaining evil thoughts. In the first watch of the night, he either walks in kinhin or sits. In the middle watch of the night, he reclines on his right side, stacking both feet, with his mind intent on arising, always wishing to see the light. In the last watch of the night he again sits, meditating on the path; walking in kinhin, he does not entertain evil thoughts.

"When entering provinces, prefectures, villages, and towns on alms rounds: arising in the morning, he puts on his robes, takes up his bowl, and enters the provinces, prefectures, villages, and towns on alms rounds, guarding all his bodily faculties and keeping his mind constantly focused and attentive. After completing the alms round, he finishes eating; washes his hands and feet, puts away the bowl, and then goes apart alone to sit for the night; whether beneath a solitary tree in an empty place, in an exposed mountain area between rocks, or in a grass hut in a place washed by water; sitting upright without looking left or right, separated from the foolish mental habits of the world, his intentions not performing evil, with a mind of compassion and sympathy for all people and for crawling, flying, and wriggling creatures. His mind does not think evil; he has departed from sensual desire; he has departed from and abandoned drowsiness; he always intends swiftly to attain settled concentration, practicing with mindfulness and not thinking of sleep. He has departed from and abandoned doubt and the multitude of thoughts; he does not speak of evil nor does he form thoughts of it; his inner mind is still and composed; he has departed from outward doubt; he has departed from the multitude of thoughts. In practicing, he does not practice evil mental states; his mind also does not think of the multitude of thoughts; he has cast aside the five hindrances and the dust and toil of mind. It is like a person who takes money on loan, conducts trade successfully, repays the principal and interest, and always has a surplus in profit; sufficient to support himself comfortably; that person, reflecting to himself, feels joy in his heart. It is like a person who has long been enslaved and then gains freedom from bondage, entering and departing at will; reflecting, he says: 'Formerly I was a slave; now I have been freed and become an ordinary person.' That person, reflecting to himself, also feels joy in his heart. It is like a person confined in prison who encounters an amnesty and is released; that person, reflecting to himself, also feels joy in his heart. It is like a person who has suffered a grave illness for years on end, who meets a skilled physician, is treated and healed, and has the energy to walk and move about and eat freely; that person thinks: 'Formerly I was ill for years; now the illness has been removed and I am healed, with the energy to eat and move about.' That person, reflecting to himself, also feels joy. It is like a person carrying heavy goods who must pass through a dangerous road, and whose goods and valuables pass safely through and reach a good road; that person, reflecting to himself, also feels joy. So too with the bhikṣu: departing from and abandoning the five hindrances is like repaying a debt, escaping confinement, recovering from long illness, freeing oneself from slavery, and passing safely through a dangerous road; the mind feels joy."

The Buddha said: "Those of little knowledge or those who have not heard much will revile the Tathāgata."

The Buddha said: "The Dharma I have understood is deep and wondrous; what I know and comprehend; when worthy disciples hear of it, they will praise the Tathāgata." The Buddha said: "What is this deep and wondrous Dharma that I have been able to comprehend and know? When worthy disciples hear of it, they praise the Tathāgata."

The Buddha said: "If there are wanderers of other paths who, within past eons, see past things; who in countless ways each study their respective matters and know the affairs therein; all of these fall within the eighteen views. If there are wanderers of other paths who, within future eons, see future things, study future things; who in countless ways each comprehend their respective matters; all of these fall within these forty-four views. Those wanderers of other paths who, within past eons, see past things; who in countless ways each comprehend their respective matters; all of this falls within the eighteen views. Those wanderers of other paths who each propound an eternalist view, who declare to worldly people that there is permanence, fall within these four views."

The Buddha said: "How do these wanderers of other paths fall within four views, each seeing permanence and declaring it, declaring to worldly people that there is permanence? If there is a wanderer of another path who cuts off sensual desire and practices meditation; entering properly into samādhi as befits; he is able to recollect the events of twenty eons past. That person says: 'I and the world are permanent. Why is this? I know the times of the formation and dissolution of past eons, but I do not know the times of the formation and dissolution of future eons.' That person then thinks: 'I know past things and relinquish future things.' This is the first view. Second: if there is a wanderer of another path who cuts off sensual desire and enters properly into samādhi as befits, he is able to recollect the events of forty eons to come. That person says: 'I and the world are permanent. Why is this? I do not know the times of the formation and dissolution of past eons, but I know only the times of the formation and dissolution of future eons.' That person then relinquishes the past as unknown and contemplates future things. This is the second view. Third: if a wanderer of another path cuts off sensual desire and practices strenuously in stillness; entering into settled samādhi as befits; he recollects events of eighty eons past and future. That person then says: 'I and the world are permanent. Why is this? I know the times of formation and dissolution of both past and future eons.' That person then contemplates knowledge of both past and future. This is the third view. Fourth: if there is a wanderer of another path who strenuously practices with single-pointed mind, practicing the cutting off of evil conduct; entering into settled samādhi as befits; he contemplates the resting place of the faculty of stillness and resides in foolish mindlessness. That person declares to worldly people: 'There is permanence. Why is this? I do not know the times of the formation and dissolution of past eons, nor do I know the times of the formation and dissolution of future eons.' This is the fourth view. Those wanderers of other paths who are said to propound an eternalist view, who declare to worldly people that there is permanence; all of them fall within these four views, and cannot go beyond them. The Tathāgata knows all of these, and moreover knows beyond them with sublime knowledge; therefore he does not admonish them about this, and transcending admonishment, he attains the unconditioned. The Tathāgata knows the arising and cessation of feeling and sensation, knows whence they originate; having seen with the Buddha-eye, he understands well without clinging to anything."

The Buddha said: "The Dharma I have understood is deep, profound, and illuminating; if worthy disciples hear it, they will praise the Tathāgata. Those wanderers of other paths who, within past eons, see past things, recollect deeds of former times; who on countless paths each delight in explaining and knowing the affairs therein; all fall within the eighteen views. Which of those wanderers of other paths? Those who, within past eons, see past things, recollect past deeds; who on countless paths each delight in explaining and knowing the affairs therein. Those wanderers of other paths who each propound an eternalist view and declare permanence, each declaring to worldly people that there is permanence; all fall within four views. Which wanderers of other paths? Those who say there is permanence, see permanence, and declare permanence to worldly people. When the eon is destroyed and collapses, the people below are born in ascent into the Ābhāsvara heaven, the twelfth heaven. When the eon is destroyed and collapses, those heavenly beings whose merit and virtue is thin; their lives exhaust and they gradually descend. A Brahmā comes to life in the empty space above, and there becomes the great honored one; Brahmā says to himself: 'I have done all things; I am the foremost honored one, the father of all, who resolves meaning above a thousand persons.' That Brahmā then thinks: 'From where might I obtain people to be born here?' Just as the thought arises, the remaining people from below immediately resolve to be born there. At that time, that Brahmā comes to hold the view: 'I have transformed and created all these people.' Those people also themselves give rise to the view: 'Brahmā has transformed and created all of us. Why is this? Brahmā was born first; we were born afterward; therefore he transformed and created us.' That Brahmā who was born first is the most excellent in form, pure and radiant, with awesome spiritual power. The remaining devas, following the pattern of thin merit and virtue, their lives exhausted, gradually descend in turn to be born among humans. Practicing strenuously, having departed from sensual desire, with single-pointed mind, entering settled samādhi as befits, they recollect the place of their former birth. Those persons say: 'The Brahmā who was born first and highest abides forever, without ever changing or dying. That foremost, honored Brahmā forever abides and transformed and created us; we who are impermanent undergo change and death.' This is said to be a declaration of permanence for some but not all; this is the first view. Second: if there is a wanderer of another path, and that Brahmā comes to hold a view thus: 'The form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness; these dharmas are permanent and do not change or die. The earth element, water element, fire element, wind element, and space element; these are impermanent and not firm.' Those beings of the Brahmā realm whose allotted merit and virtue are thin pass away and are born in human form. Those persons, practicing strenuously, having departed from sensual desire, with single-pointed mind, entering settled samādhi as befits, recollect the Brahmā realm of former times. Those persons say: 'The form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness; those dharmas are permanent and firm. The earth element, water element, fire element, wind element, and space element of the human realm; those dharmas are impermanent, without firmness, and have an end and perishing.' This is the second view. Third: if there is a wanderer of another path, what does he say? There is a heaven named Kṛtābhū; dwelling above in mutual enjoyment and bliss, thereafter they no longer think of bodily illness or affliction. Those beings whose allotted merit and virtue are thin pass away and are born in the human world. Those persons practice strenuously, having departed from sensual desire, with single-pointed mind, entering settled samādhi as befits, they recollect the place of their former birth; those persons then say: 'Those heavenly beings who are mutually enjoying and delighting; they abide forever, unmoved, without change or death. The mutual enjoyments here in the human world are impermanent, without firmness, and have an end and perishing. That heaven is permanent; this human world is impermanent.' This is the third view. Fourth: if there is a wanderer of another path, what does he say? There is a heaven named Saṃjñā; dwelling above there together, face to face; they give rise to anger and leave their original seats; those heavenly beings whose allotted merit and fortune are thin pass away and are born in the human world. Those persons practice strenuously, having departed from sensual desire, with single-pointed mind, entering settled samādhi as befits, they recollect the heaven above where they were formerly born; those persons say: 'Those devas who were dwelling together in mutual enjoyment; they abide permanently, firmly, without end or perishing. We who have been born in the human world are impermanent, without firmness, and have end and perishing. That heaven is permanent; I in the human world am impermanent.' This is the fourth view."

The Buddha said: "All those wanderers of other paths, each declaring in their own way that there is permanence, each declaring to worldly people that there is permanence; all fall within these four views, and cannot go beyond these four views. The Buddha knows all of these, and moreover knows beyond them with unsurpassed wondrous knowledge; therefore he neither ridicules nor disparages them, and attains the unconditioned. The Buddha knows feeling, sensation, and contact; knows the expedient means by which they arise and originate and are seen; the Buddha, understanding with a mind of non-grasping, understands well. The Dharma known to the Buddha is deep, profound, and illuminating; I comprehend all of it. If worthy disciples hear and know this, they will praise the Tathāgata. If there are wanderers of other paths who, within past eons, see past things, recollect deeds of former times; who on countless paths each delight in explaining and resolving these affairs; all fall within the eighteen views. Certain wanderers of other paths; one says that I see a boundary and an end; one says I see no boundary or end; one says I see both a boundary and no boundary; one says I see neither a boundary nor no boundary; all fall within these four views. What do those wanderers of other paths know? If there is a wanderer of another path who practices thus, who declares to worldly people that there is a limit, saying: 'What I say is true; the others are foolish and speak falsely.' Declaring to worldly people that there is no limit, he says: 'There is one who says, "I and the world have both a limit and no limit; I and the world have neither a limit nor no limit"; that too is false speech, a deceptive saying. Why is this? What I see is that the world has a limit.' This is the first view. Second: what does a certain wanderer of another path know? That is to say: 'In my practice and my seeing, I see no limit; I know that I and the world are without limit.' That wanderer of another path, holding this view and practicing thus, knowing that I and the world are without limit, says: 'I and the world are without limit; what I say is true; the others are foolish. In contradiction they say: "I and the world have a limit and no limit; I and the world have neither a limit nor no limit"; those who speak thus are speaking deceptively. Why is this? I and the world are without limit.' This is the second view. Third: what does a certain wanderer of another path know? That is to say, that wanderer of another path holds this view and practices thus: 'I know that I and the world have both a limit and no limit. What I say is true; the others are foolish and speak falsely. In contradiction they say: "I and the world have both a limit and no limit; I and the world have neither a limit nor no limit"; those who speak thus are speaking deceptively. Why is this? I and the world have both a limit and no limit.' This is the third view. Fourth: what does a certain wanderer of another path know? That person says: 'My intention is thus and my view is thus; I know that I and the world have neither a limit nor no limit. What I say is true; the others are foolish and speak falsely. In contradiction they say: "I and the world have a limit; I and the world are without limit; I and the world have both a limit and no limit"; those who speak thus are speaking deceptively. Why is this? I and the world have neither a limit nor no limit.' This is the fourth view."

The Buddha said: "All those wanderers of other paths who say 'there is a limit,' who say 'there is no limit,' who say 'there is both a limit and no limit,' who say 'there is neither a limit nor no limit' with regard to myself and the world; all fall within these four views, and cannot go beyond these four views. The Buddha knows all of these, and moreover knows beyond them with unsurpassed wondrous knowledge; therefore he neither ridicules nor disparages, and attains the unconditioned. The Buddha knows feeling, sensation, and contact; knows the expedient means by which they arise; without grasping or attachment, the Buddha understands well."

The Buddha said: "The Dharma I know is deep, profound, and illuminating. If worthy disciples hear and know it, they will praise the Buddha. If there are wanderers of other paths who, within past eons, see past things, recollect the deeds of former lives; who on countless paths each delight in explaining and resolving those affairs; all fall within the eighteen views. Various wanderers of other paths dispute and say things that each differ; if someone asks them something, they dispute and their statements each differ, saying: 'My teaching to such-and-such a person is thus; my teaching to that person is thus; my teaching to others is thus; my teaching to people is thus and not thus.' All of these fall within these four views. What are those wanderers of other paths who dispute, saying things that each differ? That is to say, there are wanderers of other paths who hold and practice such views as these, saying: 'I neither know nor see whether there is a future existence or no future existence; I do not know; there is no future existence at all; nor do I see it; what I cannot see, do not know, do not think upon; as I would say it, it is not as others say.' That person speaks only for himself with self-satisfaction: 'What I see is true; the others are foolish.'


The Buddha said: "Those who receive and hold foolish wrong views, at death they fall into the niraya realms of evil destinies. If there is a śramaṇa or brāhmaṇa of wide learning, clever in exposition, who carefully examines the meaning, whose words are consistent, whose fame has spread far, who has abandoned other views and comes to such a person, and sitting in peace inquires of them, yet cannot be answered, that person of heterodox ways, at death, falls into an evil destiny. This is the first view.

"As for the second: what does a person of heterodox ways know? That person of heterodox ways holds and speaks as follows: 'I do not know whether there is retribution of good and evil, nor do I know whether there is no retribution of good and evil; I neither know nor see.'
