# 不思議光菩薩所說經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0484

Translated from the Chinese. Later Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove at Anāthapiṇḍada's park, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and five hundred bodhisattva-mahāsattvas known to all. At that time the World-Honored One was residing near the great city of Śrāvastī, and the king, great ministers, brahmins, householders, and their retinues all served, revered, honored, and praised him, presenting many offerings of robes, food, bedding, and medicine. He was the Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, Possessed of Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Men, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. He was accomplished in birth, accomplished in lineage, fulfilled in samādhi, fulfilled in wisdom, fulfilled in liberation, fulfilled in the knowledge and vision of liberation. He was endowed with the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, and the eighteen distinctive qualities of a buddha. He taught the true Dharma, which is excellent in the beginning, middle, and end, beautiful in meaning and expression, complete, pure, and immaculate in the brahma-conduct. He was endowed with the five eyes, namely the physical eye, the divine eye, the wisdom eye, the buddha eye, and the Dharma eye, and knew well this world and the other world.

At that time the World-Honored One had fully subdued the heretics such as the Nirgrantha Jñātiputra. The Buddha-Dharma was flourishing, and devas and humans all paid homage. When the proper time arrived, the World-Honored One put on his robe and took his bowl. Surrounded by the assembly of bhikṣus, he set out to enter the great city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. His bearing in going and coming, in stepping and stopping, was composed and dignified. His gaze was serene, and his movements of bending and stretching, bowing and looking up, and holding his robe and bowl were all poised and stately. His golden body radiated brilliance like a blazing fire, a precious jewel, or the sun and moon illuminating a dark night. The thirty-two marks adorned his body. As his golden feet stepped upon the threshold of the gate, at that very moment, in the great city of Śrāvastī, various marvels and unprecedented events appeared, as the verse says:

> When the Sage among Men entered,
> as the Lion of the Śākyas stepped upon the threshold,
> many rare events appeared.
> With pure minds, hear what I shall say.
>
> The blind gained sight to see,
> the deaf gained hearing,
> the naked obtained clothing,
> the deranged recovered right thought.
> All joined palms in joy,
> gazing at the Buddha without satiation.
>
> All the drums sounded of themselves,
> flutes and pipes produced their sounds.
> Geese, mynas, doves, and mandarin ducks
> all emitted wondrous, soft tones.
>
> Those who had lost their wealth found treasures;
> from the various jewels arose sounds.
> The earth shook in six ways;
> the faithless gained pure minds.
>
> Where the Supremely Awakened One set down his feet,
> pure lotus flowers received them.
> Those he touched gained peace and joy,
> and at death were reborn in heaven.
>
> Women suffering the pains of pregnancy
> gave birth to beautiful children in comfort,
> free from greed, anger, and delusion,
> both parents and child alike.
>
> Staircases were wondrously adorned,
> and hundreds of millions of devas rained down flowers.
> Beings were free from illness,
> released from all suffering.
>
> When the Well-Gone entered the city,
> all received peace and joy.
> Each thought, "He accepts my offering,"
> each thought, "The Buddha looks at me."
>
> The power of merit of the Sage among Men
> is not something my words can exhaust.

At that time the World-Honored One begged for food in the great city of Śrāvastī from house to house. When he reached the middle of the road, there was an empty lot where an infant had been abandoned. The infant was of fine appearance, exceedingly fair, and was sucking its right thumb. In this empty lot there were many foxes, wolves, and dogs, but upon seeing the infant they only licked it and departed without being able to harm it. This infant was a person of merit who had long planted good roots. Many people stood watching with astonishment, going back and forth to see the infant abandoned in the empty lot, beautiful and lovely, delighting all who saw it.

At that time the World-Honored One, seeing the crowd of people going back and forth to the empty lot, already knowing the answer but purposely asking, said to Ānanda: "Go over there and see why those people are going back and forth to that empty lot. What is happening?"

Ānanda said: "Yes, World-Honored One!" The Elder Ānanda went to the empty lot and saw the infant, fair in appearance, sucking its right thumb, gazing at the crowd without blinking. Having seen this, Ānanda returned to the Buddha and reported what he had seen: "World-Honored One, in this empty lot there is an abandoned infant, fine in appearance and most worthy of affection, like a jeweled image. He gazes at the people without blinking."

At that time, the World-Honored One, moved by compassion for the infant, observed its original good roots and knew they had matured, and that it was capable of understanding the meaning of the Dharma he taught. Knowing also that the good roots of the assembly had ripened, he went to where the infant was. Arriving there and standing to one side, he addressed the infant in verse:

> "The evil karma you created in the past
> now manifests as this retribution.
> Abandoned in this empty lot,
> the infant's suffering is thus."

At that time the infant, by the Buddha's spiritual power and its own accumulated merit, replied to the Buddha in verse:

> "Does Gautama still possess
> the notion of 'abandoned in an empty lot'?
> When you were at the site of enlightenment,
> did you not know such notions?"

At that time the World-Honored One replied in verse:

> "I have understood notions,
> and I am forever without notions.
> It is out of compassion for you
> that I have come to this empty lot."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "If one cannot find sentient beings,
> as they are ultimately unfindable,
> whom does the Honored One pity?
> Toward whom does he turn his compassionate mind?"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "Sentient beings do not know
> selflessness, emptiness, and quiescence.
> To awaken them to this,
> I travel through villages and cities."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "Having thoroughly understood emptiness and quiescence,
> having awakened to emptiness and quiescence,
> the notion of 'sentient beings' still remains.
> Has the Tathāgata not severed it?"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "The power of the Buddha's compassion is such:
> having awakened to emptiness and quiescence,
> for the sake of teaching sentient beings,
> the Guide expounds the Dharma."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "Is there still inversion
> that the Tathāgata has not severed?
> Without a sentient being to give rise to the notion of beings,
> does compassion arise in this way?"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "The Buddha's protection and sustenance,
> the bodhisattva's diligence,
> for beings who do not understand:
> the Honored Among Men puts forth adornments."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "This is the adornment of delusion.
> If one cannot find any object,
> if dharmas are not objects,
> from what does adornment arise?"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "This is the spiritual power of great compassion.
> The Tamer of the World acts thus:
> still not attached to any object,
> he teaches the Dharma for sentient beings."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "Dharma has no words or letters.
> How can it be expounded?
> The world has a great failing:
> what is not Dharma is spoken of as Dharma."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "I do not corrupt the world,
> nor do I speak what is not Dharma.
> Sentient beings delude themselves through inversion.
> I liberate them from their bonds."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "Afflictions have no root;
> they have no location.
> They are not inside, outside, or in between.
> From what does one liberate their bonds?"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "They arise from false thinking
> and accompany inversion.
> To sever such false thinking,
> infant, I teach the Dharma."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "The nature of mind is inherently pure.
> Within it there are no defiled bonds.
> Even with abundant false thinking,
> the pure nature does not give rise to defilements."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "Indeed, as you say,
> the nature of mind is inherently pure.
> It is guest afflictions, dust, and bonds
> that make the unwise become defiled."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "Bonds have no location,
> nor are they found in any location.
> How then are they called 'arising'?
> Please explain this to me."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "Like clouds in empty space,
> visible yet without reality,
> bonds arise in just this way:
> though seen, they have no substance."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "All dharmas are equally thusness;
> their arising is by nature thusness.
> If dharmas are truly real,
> apart from thusness they cannot be found."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "All dharmas that are not thusness
> equally abide in thusness.
> Having awakened to this thusness,
> there are neither faults nor virtues."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "If one cannot find sentient beings,
> Gautama, with whom does one engage?
> First observe the root of dharmas:
> from whom do afflictions arise?"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "Past and future,
> together with the present:
> the Buddha knows and understands completely,
> and teaches the Dharma for sentient beings."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "To speak of knowing the three times,
> and to say 'I can understand':
> this is great pride,
> and amounts to self-praise."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "I do not praise myself,
> nor do I belittle others.
> Thusness is equally manifest:
> therefore the name Tathāgata."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "Thusness cannot be found;
> it does not correspond to words.
> To express the inexpressible through words:
> this is not thusness."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "Ordinary beings pursue notions
> and give rise to false thinking within thusness.
> To sever the notion of self,
> the Tathāgata appears in the world."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "True enlightenment does not appear in the world,
> for one has well cultivated the unborn.
> Within the dharma of non-arising,
> the Buddha's appearance is not fitting."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "Within the unborn there appears birth;
> the Buddha's appearance in the world is manifest.
> This is spoken in terms of conventional truth,
> not in terms of ultimate truth."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "There are still two notions:
> conventional truth and ultimate truth.
> Within the one vehicle path,
> Gautama speaks contradictions."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "I do not teach contradictions.
> I abide in non-contradiction,
> yet for beings who are in contradiction,
> infant, know it thus."

At that time the infant spoke again in verse:

> "I repent before the Perfectly Enlightened One
> for the words I spoke above.
> It is by the Buddha's sustaining power
> that I was able to speak thus."

At that time the World-Honored One extended his golden arm from within his robe and lifted the infant. The infant grasped the Buddha's finger and rose from the ground. The World-Honored One then took the infant from the empty lot and headed toward the main road. At that time the great assembly experienced something unprecedented, and their reverence for the World-Honored One redoubled. They exclaimed: "How rare! The Tathāgata, the World-Honored One, has achieved such supremely wondrous Dharma! He is able to cause one in extreme suffering and hardship to abide peacefully in this Dharma."

Then the World-Honored One said to the infant: "Your karmic deeds are exhausted. Now recall the good roots you planted in the past, and for the sake of inspiring wonder in this great assembly, display great supernatural power."

At that time the infant rose into the sky, passing beyond seven tāla trees in height. Light radiated from his body, and this light pervaded the entire great trichiliocosm, the buddha-world. Because of this light, Śakra, Brahmā, the world-protectors, and hundreds of thousands of other devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas, seeing this light, all came to the Buddha's presence. Arriving there, they prostrated at the Buddha's feet and scattered celestial flowers as offerings to the Buddha. Having made their offerings, they addressed the Buddha, the World-Honored One, saying: "The light of this bodhisattva is inconceivable! Light radiates from his body and pervades this entire buddha-world, bringing immeasurable inconceivable benefit to sentient beings. This child should be named Inconceivable Light."

At that time the World-Honored One Śākyamuni approved this name: "This child shall be named Inconceivable Light."

At that time the infant Inconceivable Light descended from the sky and stood upon the ground. By the Buddha's spiritual power and the power of his own good roots, his form became like that of a child of eight years. Then Śakra, Lord of the Devas, offered celestial garments to the infant and said: "Infant, out of compassion for us, please accept these celestial garments. Do not remain unclothed."

At that time the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant, addressed Śakra: "Kauśika, a bodhisattva is not adorned by garments; one should be clothed in Dharma garments as adornment. Kauśika, hear well what a bodhisattva's garments are. Kauśika, the mind of bodhi is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills all buddha-dharmas up through the site of enlightenment. Shame and moral conscience are a bodhisattva's garment, for they tame and bring all sentient beings to accomplishment, placing them beyond fault. Firm vows and adornments are a bodhisattva's garment, for they accomplish all tasks. Honesty without deception is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves the elimination of all illusion and deceit. Vigorous diligence is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills all good roots. Joyful aspiration is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills all buddha-dharmas. The abandoning of arrogance is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills all dhyāna concentrations. Desire for the Dharma and hearing the Dharma are a bodhisattva's garment, for they fulfill the prajñā pāramitā. Not giving rise to the pride of wisdom is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills unattached wisdom. Benefiting others is a bodhisattva's garment, for through compassion for sentient beings one is endowed with awareness. Relinquishing all possessions is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills all the excellent marks and minor characteristics. Maintaining pure precepts is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills all vows. Being harmonious and patient is a bodhisattva's garment, for it ultimately fulfills the brahma-voice. Staunch diligence without weariness or retreat is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills the surpassing of all other endeavors. Attaining dhyāna, liberation, and samādhi is a bodhisattva's garment, for it fulfills great supernatural wisdom. Indestructible wisdom is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves the severing of all bonds, views, and obstructions. Great skillful wisdom is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves the teaching and transformation of all sentient beings. Great loving-kindness is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves the rescue and deliverance of all sentient beings. Great compassion is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves tirelessness in the midst of birth and death. Great joy is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves the fulfillment of Dharma-joy. Great equanimity is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves the relinquishing of both love and hatred. A mind that does not harm any sentient being is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves not causing distress to oneself or others. Expounding and teaching the Dharma is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves not praising oneself or disparaging others. Practicing as one teaches is a bodhisattva's garment, for it achieves the severing of all bonds. Kauśika, one should know the Dharma garments of a bodhisattva in this way. Adorned by the Dharma, one is never born unclothed."

At that time Śakra, Lord of the Devas, felt even greater reverence and affection for the infant. He addressed the World-Honored One: "Out of compassion for us, please have this infant accept these garments."

Then the World-Honored One told the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant: "Accept Śakra's garments." At that time the World-Honored One took the garments in his right hand and gave them to the infant. The infant knelt on his right knee, received the garments with his right hand, and put them on.

Then the World-Honored One, together with the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant, and the great assembly, continued begging for food from house to house in the great city of Śrāvastī. At that time the assembly of men and women, old and young, elders and householders, kṣatriyas and brahmins, the king and his ministers, seeing the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant, were filled with wonder. They also came to see the Buddha, make offerings, and pay respects, and all gathered together.

Then the World-Honored One, having begged for food in due order, arrived at the home of the mother who had given birth to the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant. The Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light then entered the house, went before his mother, and addressed her in verse:

> "Mother, you are without fault.
> You should rejoice for yourself.
> It is my own evil karma of the past
> that caused me to be born in your womb.
>
> Mother, you are my field of merit,
> and I am grateful for the kindness of giving me birth.
> Mother, do not feel ashamed.
> Go quickly to the Tathāgata.
>
> Mother, you have gained great benefit
> by carrying me in your womb.
> The merit of this:
> go and ask the Guide."

Then the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light said to Śakra, Lord of the Devas: "Kauśika, give me fragrant flowers, clothing, and whatever is needed. I wish to present them to my mother, so that she may use them to make offerings to the Buddha and arouse the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi." Then Śakra gave the bodhisattva celestial māndārava flowers, celestial incense, and celestial garments.

Then the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light again addressed his mother in verse:

> "Accept these lovely flowers,
> celestial, wondrous māndārava,
> fine incense and garments,
> and offer them to the Sage of the Śākyas.
>
> Not through food, drink, or treasures
> can one repay the kindness of parents.
> By guiding them toward the true Dharma
> one truly serves both parents."

> Having offered to the Two-Footed Honored One,
> she aroused the pure mind of the supreme path.
> Through the long night I constantly urge:
> ever diligent, again and again.
>
> His mother gave rise to a joyful mind,
> ultimately feeling no shame.
> She went to the Sage among Men
> and, having bowed, stood before him.
>
> She scattered flowers upon the Tathāgata
> and offered flowers and garments.
> Having planted good roots with the Buddha,
> she then aroused the mind of bodhi.
>
> Firmly established in the mind of bodhi,
> she asked the Lion of the Śākyas:
> "For carrying this pure being in my womb,
> please tell me the reward of merit."
>
> "Through this good karma,
> you will not be born in the evil destinies.
> Having made offerings to many hundreds of millions of buddhas,
> you will attain buddhahood."

Then the World-Honored One, having begged for food in the great city of Śrāvastī in due order, together with the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light and the great assembly, left the great city and returned to the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's park. After the World-Honored One had eaten, he washed himself, rinsed his mouth, and took his seat to teach the true Dharma.

At that time, King Prasenajit of Kośala, hearing that the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant, possessed great and inconceivable supernatural powers, marshaled his fourfold army and proceeded to the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's park, to the World-Honored One's presence. Arriving there, he prostrated at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side. King Prasenajit addressed the World-Honored One: "Venerable World-Honored One, where is the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant? I have heard of his inconceivable supernatural powers." The Buddha then showed King Prasenajit the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant.

When the king saw the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant, with his surpassingly fine appearance exceeding even celestial beings, fearless and adorned with precepts, concentration, and wisdom, he thought: "What good roots were planted, what merit cultivated, to produce such a wondrous body?"

At that time the infant Inconceivable Light, sustained by the Buddha's spiritual power, knew the thoughts of the King of Kośala and addressed the king in verse:

> "Always cultivating loving-kindness to purify sentient beings,
> free from coarseness and evil, cultivating right mindfulness,
> guarding body, speech, and mind in pure brahma-conduct:
> such a one has a pure and wondrous body like this.
>
> "Staying far from the wicked and not creating evil oneself,
> increasing and cultivating immeasurable goodness,
> abandoning all evil disputes and quarrels:
> such a one has a pure and wondrous body like this.
>
> "Revering the Buddha, the Dharma, and the noble Saṃgha,
> constantly presenting wonderful offerings,
> not reviling or tormenting others:
> such a one has a pure and wondrous body like this.
>
> "Ridicule, abuse, slander, and defamation:
> toward others, one does not produce these.
> Praising goodness and not speaking of evil:
> such a one has a pure and wondrous body like this.
>
> "Miserliness, jealousy, and arrogance:
> truly observing one's own conduct, not disparaging others.
> Such a one has a pure and wondrous body like this."

Then King Prasenajit addressed the World-Honored One: "This Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the infant, has accomplished such supremely wondrous, great Dharma. What karmic obstruction caused him to be born from the womb of a courtesan and abandoned in an empty lot?"

The Buddha told the great king: "Long ago, ninety-one kalpas in the past, there was a buddha named Vipaśyin who appeared in the world as Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, Possessed of Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Men, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. Great king, you should know that at that time, in the Dharma of Vipaśyin Tathāgata, there were two bodhisattvas. The first was named Virtuous Deva. The second was named Abundant Wealth. Bodhisattva Virtuous Deva had attained non-retrogression on the path to supreme enlightenment, had attained dhāraṇī and unobstructed eloquence, had realized the patience of non-arising, had the power of merit and dignity, was of few desires and few occupations, always delighted in solitude, and had attained supernatural powers. At that time Bodhisattva Abundant Wealth practiced the austere dhūta observances and served as an attendant to Bodhisattva Virtuous Deva. This person was constantly going to villages and cities, involved in many affairs. Bodhisattva Virtuous Deva would reprove and instruct him: 'Why do you create so many affairs and not cut them off?' After being admonished repeatedly, Abundant Wealth became angry and resentful. Because of his anger, his body and mind deteriorated. Having deteriorated, he angrily cursed: 'You despicable son of a courtesan, born of illicit union, gotten from another man! You know not your father nor your mother. How could you possibly have precepts, learning, concentration, or wisdom?' Having cursed thus, he did not repent or desist. Bound by afflictions and constantly harboring anger toward Bodhisattva Virtuous Deva, he was at last abandoned by the bodhisattva. Once abandoned, his anger and abuse only increased. Because of this unwholesome karma, when his body perished and his life ended, he was born from the womb of a courtesan. Yet because Bodhisattva Virtuous Deva protected him, he did not fall into the hells. After being born from the courtesan, he was always abandoned, to be consumed by foxes, wolves, and dogs.

"Great king, because of this, for ninety-one kalpas he always died in this way, being abandoned in every birth. Crowds of people would always revile him: 'This is a courtesan's child.' Abandoned in empty lots, consumed by foxes, wolves, and dogs. Great king, do not doubt this. Why? The one who angrily cursed the bodhisattva at that time, Abundant Wealth, is none other than this Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light. His evil karma exhausted, through the power of his good karma, having purified his mind of bonds and delighted the Buddha, the paths of evil rebirth are completely and forever ended for this person. Great king, this Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light has previously encountered sixty-four hundred million buddhas, revering, serving, honoring, and praising them. In the presence of those buddhas, he constantly practiced the brahma-conduct and diligently sought the Dharma. It is through this power of original goodness that he has attained such accomplishments and supernatural power. Great king, dark and bright karma never perishes. Therefore, the wise guard their bodily, verbal, and mental karma. They would sooner relinquish their very lives than create evil karma."

Then King Prasenajit said: "World-Honored One, has Bodhisattva Virtuous Deva already attained omniscience, or is he still cultivating the bodhisattva practice?"

The Buddha said: "Great king, Bodhisattva Virtuous Deva is now in the land of Akṣobhya Buddha, cultivating the bodhisattva practice under the name Treasury of Virtue."

Then King Prasenajit said: "World-Honored One, good men and good women should always draw near to spiritual friends, be close to spiritual friends. Why? World-Honored One, by being close to spiritual friends, by revering and attending them, one hears the good Dharma. Having heard the good Dharma, one obtains a wholesome mind. Already having a wholesome mind, one cultivates wholesome conduct. Creating good karma, one tends toward wholesome destinations and finds spiritual friends. By gaining spiritual friends, one does not perform evil deeds but cultivates goodness. Having cultivated goodness, one is free from inner torment and does not torment others. If a bodhisattva protects both self and others, they can attain bodhi. If they are already established on the path, they possess great power to benefit beings."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, great king! You speak well these words. Great king, when a bodhisattva draws near to spiritual friends, all virtues and wholesome dharmas are fulfilled."

Then the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light said: "World-Honored One, by accomplishing how many dharmas does a bodhisattva swiftly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and the pure Dharma-patience?"

The Buddha said: "Infant, by accomplishing four dharmas, a bodhisattva swiftly attains the supreme true path and the pure Dharma-patience. What are the four? Understanding the patience of dependent arising, being free from the views of annihilation and permanence, understanding the absence of self, person, sentient being, and life span, and understanding empty quiescence while cultivating the practice of emptiness. These are the four.

"There are another four dharmas. What are the four? The past is quiescent. The future is known to be without coming. The present does not abide. The three times are equal. These are the four.

"There are another four dharmas, namely: purifying oneself, purifying sentient beings, purifying the Dharma, and purifying dhyāna concentration. These are the four.

"There are another four dharmas, namely: tranquility of body, tranquility of mind, tranquility of the path, and tranquility of dharmas. These are the four.

"There are another four dharmas, namely: contemplating the Buddha through the Dharma and not through form, contemplating the Dharma through non-attachment and not through self, contemplating the Saṃgha through the unconditioned and not through the multitude, and purifying the wisdom eye. These are the four.

"There are another four dharmas, namely: fulfilling all the pāramitās, not abandoning the four means of gathering, skillfully knowing expedient means, and teaching that there are no sentient beings while practicing great compassion. These are the four.

"Infant, these are the dharmas by which a bodhisattva, having accomplished them in sets of four, swiftly attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and the profound Dharma-patience."

When this Dharma was taught, the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light attained the patience of non-arising. In joy and exultation, he rose into the sky to a height of seven tāla trees. At that time the great trichiliocosm shook in six ways, a great light shone everywhere, and devas rained down flowers. Hundreds of thousands of musical instruments sounded of themselves without being struck.

At that time the World-Honored One, knowing the mind of the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, smiled. As is the constant way of buddhas, when they smile, lights of many hundreds of thousands of colors emerge from their faces: blue, yellow, red, white, and the color of red crystal. This light shone everywhere, pervading immeasurable, boundless worlds, eclipsing the palaces of Māra and the light of the sun and moon. After severing the sufferings of the hells and hungry ghosts, it rose to the Brahmā world, then circled the Buddha three times before entering through the uṣṇīṣa on his crown.

At that time the Elder Ānanda rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, and with palms joined addressed the Buddha in verse:

> "Your form and marks are supremely subtle,
> adorning your body with variegated beauty.
> With perfect radiance you answer all questions.
> For what cause and condition do you smile?
>
> "Supreme in precepts and fearless conduct,
> adorned with the finest concentration and wisdom,
> you reveal the firm fruit of liberation.
> For what cause and condition do you smile?
>
> "With the power of patience and the ten powers,
> patient, courageous, diligent, and immovable,
> joyfully revealing the four noble truths:
> for what cause and condition do you smile?
>
> "With a vajra body, firm and solid,
> and Nārāyaṇa strength at its utmost,
> with a brahma-voice pleasing to the mind,
> please explain the meaning of this smile.
>
> "The Brahmā Devas above
> cannot see the Tathāgata's crown.
> In succession they join palms in reverence.
> For what cause and condition do you smile?
>
> "Beneath the king of trees, subduing Māra,
> you attained the stainless, pure path.
> You know the conduct of all sentient beings.
> Please reveal the reason for this smile.
>
> "Turning the unsurpassed Dharma wheel,
> teaching impermanence while the earth shook,
> taming humans, devas, nāgas, and others:
> Venerable One, why do you smile?
>
> "Radiant, dispelling darkness,
> with stainless, all-pervading, pure eyes,
> with merit vast as space:
> for what cause and condition do you smile?"

At that time the World-Honored One said to Ānanda: "Do you see the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, suspended in the sky seven tāla trees above the ground?"

Ānanda said: "I see him, World-Honored One."

"Ānanda, this Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, after hundreds of thousands of asaṃkhyeya kalpas have passed, will become a buddha, also named Inconceivable Light, appearing in the world as Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, Possessed of Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Men, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. His land will be named Purified, and his kalpa will be named Without Fault. Ānanda, that purified buddha-land will be extremely clean and pure, like the celestial palaces of the Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven in this world. That buddha's lifespan will be twenty intermediate kalpas. His great assembly of śrāvakas will number eighty thousand, and his assembly of bodhisattvas will number thirty-two thousand. Ānanda, for what reason will the kalpa be named Without Fault? Ānanda, at that time, for hundreds of thousands of kalpas no buddha will have appeared in the world. The Buddha Inconceivable Light will be the first to attain buddhahood in that kalpa. The devas of the pure abodes, rejoicing and praising, will say: 'This kalpa is without fault! This kalpa is without fault! Because a Tathāgata has appeared in the world, this kalpa shall be named Without Fault.'"

When this Sūtra of the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light was taught, thirty-two thousand sentient beings aroused the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, sixty thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of non-arising, and five hundred bhikṣus, having severed all bonds and outflows, attained mastery of mind and became arhats.

Then the Buddha said to Ānanda: "Receive this sūtra. Uphold it, recite it, and expound it widely among the great assembly, so that my true Dharma may long abide in the world and greatly benefit bodhisattvas in the future."

The Elder Ānanda said: "World-Honored One, I have received and upheld it. World-Honored One, what is the name of this sūtra, and how should it be upheld?"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda, this sūtra is called 'The Purification of Karmic Obstructions.' It is also called 'Spoken by the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, Sustained by Spiritual Power.' Uphold it in this way. Ānanda, if a good man or good woman were to serve all the tathāgatas for the span of their entire lives, revering, honoring, and praising them, offering piles of multicolored flower garlands as high as Mount Sumeru, together with corresponding amounts of burned incense, powdered incense, anointing incense, banners, and jeweled canopies, and yet another good man or good woman were to receive and uphold this Sūtra Spoken by Inconceivable Light, recite it fluently, expound it widely among the great assembly, and practice as taught, the merit of the latter would exceed the former. Ānanda, whoever wishes to make offerings of Dharma to the Tathāgata, whoever wishes to become a great light of wisdom, should receive, uphold, and recite this sūtra."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the Bodhisattva Inconceivable Light, the Elder Ānanda, and the entire great assembly of humans, devas, nāgas, spirits, gandharvas, asuras, and others, having heard what the Buddha taught, all rejoiced greatly.
