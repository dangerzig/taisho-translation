# 佛說十二頭陀經
## Sūtra of the Twelve Dhūta Practices, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0783

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the fine garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with eight thousand bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas. All had put on their robes and taken up their bowls, and were going about for alms. Having eaten, they went to an araṇya place and sat with legs crossed.

At that time the World-Honored One smiled serenely. The Elder Mahākāśyapa rose from his seat, straightened his robes, knelt with palms joined, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! From the past until now, I have never seen the Buddha smile without a cause. I pray you will compassionately inform us."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "I see this araṇya place, praised by the Buddhas of the ten directions, where immeasurable merit all arises. Those who seek the śrāvaka vehicle attain the śrāvaka vehicle; those who seek the pratyekabuddha vehicle attain the pratyekabuddha vehicle; those who seek the Great Vehicle swiftly attain the unsurpassed, true, and right path. I now dwell here, and for this reason I am joyful!"

At that time Mahākāśyapa, hearing what the Buddha had said, leapt with joy and exclaimed that he had never heard such a thing. He again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The benefits of this araṇya place are vast and profound, able to cause sentient beings to rely on it for cultivation and accomplish the path of the three vehicles. I pray the World-Honored One will reveal to us the Dharma of the araṇya!"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Listen carefully and reflect well. I shall briefly explain its meaning for you."

Kāśyapa addressed the Buddha: "Yes, World-Honored One! I receive the teaching."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "The araṇya bhikṣu, having distanced himself from the two kinds of attachment, with body and mind pure, practices the dhūta dharmas. Those who practice these dharmas observe twelve things: first, dwelling in an araṇya place; second, always begging for food; third, begging in order of houses; fourth, receiving only one meal per day; fifth, eating with restraint; sixth, not drinking fruit-juice after midday; seventh, wearing patched robes of discarded rags; eighth, wearing only three robes; ninth, dwelling among tombs; tenth, resting beneath trees; eleventh, sitting in the open; and twelfth, sitting only, not lying down.

"First, when the araṇya bhikṣu practices the dhūta, he should think: 'I am now in this empty and secluded place. For the sake of the unsurpassed path I relinquish body, life, and possessions, cultivating the three firm dharmas. I shall die as a wild animal dies, without giving rise to attachment or longing.' If illness and suffering come and one needs others' help, one should think: 'I, a single person, left home for the Dharma. The Dharma is my companion. If I diligently practice the Dharma, the Dharma itself is my rescue and protection. This is the araṇya dharma.' The practitioner originally left home because the household life has many vexations. Leaving parents, wife, and children to practice the path, one then forms attachments again to teachers, fellow students, and companions, and the mind is again disturbed. Therefore one accepts the araṇya dharma, causing the body to be far from commotion and to dwell in empty seclusion. 'Far from' means far from the sounds of crowds; the nearest should be three leagues from any pasture, and the farther the better. Having achieved physical distance, one should also cause the mind to be far from the five desires and five hindrances. Such is the araṇya bhikṣu's dharma.

"Second, when one is about to enter a settlement to beg for food, one should restrain the six senses so they do not become attached to form, sound, scent, taste, touch, or dharmas. One should not discriminate between the marks of men and women. Whether one receives or does not receive, the mind should be equanimous. Whether the food is good or bad, one should give rise to neither increase nor decrease. When one does not obtain food, one should think: 'Śākyamuni Tathāgata renounced the position of a wheel-turning king, left home, and attained the path, yet when he entered the city to beg for food, there were still times when he did not receive any. How much more so for me, a person of little merit and meager virtue; how could I expect to receive?' This is the dharma of practicing alms-begging. If one accepts invitation meals or Saṃgha meals, one gives rise to causes of the outflows. Why? When one accepts invitation meals, if one receives food, one thinks: 'It is because I am a person of great merit and virtue that I have received this.' If one does not receive food, one resents the host: 'That person has no discernment; those who should not be invited are invited, while those who should be are not.' Or one belittles oneself, feeling frustrated and self-reproachful, giving rise to worry and suffering. This is the dharma of craving and attachment, which can obstruct the path. As for Saṃgha meals, when one enters the assembly one must follow the rules of the assembly: adjudicating disputes, administering expulsions, managing Saṃgha affairs, and giving assignments, all of which scatter and disturb the mind and hinder the practice of the path. Because of such disturbing affairs, one should accept the dharma of always begging for food.

"Third, the dhūta bhikṣu is not attached to appearances and does not slight any sentient being. With an equal mind of compassion, not choosing between rich and poor, one should accept the dharma of always begging in order of houses.

"Fourth, one should think: 'Seeking even a single meal already creates many hindrances. How much more so with breakfast, lunch, and an afternoon meal? If I do not limit myself, I lose half a day's work and cannot practice the path single-mindedly. For the sake of the Buddha-Dharma, for the sake of practicing the path, not for the sake of the body and life, as one would feed a horse or a pig.' Therefore, cutting off repeated meals, one should accept the dharma of one meal a day.

"Fifth, when one has obtained a single meal, one should think: 'If I see someone suffering from hunger and thirst, I shall give them a portion. I shall be the donor and they the recipient.' Having given, one makes this vow: 'May all sentient beings cultivate merit for their deliverance and not fall into stinginess and greed.' Taking the food to an empty and quiet place, one sets aside a portion on a clean stone as an offering to the birds and beasts, making the same vow. When about to eat, one should spread out one's sitting cloth, wash the hands, and think: 'Within this body there are eighty thousand families of worms. When the worms receive this food, they will all be at ease. I now give this food to these worms; later, when I attain the path, I shall give them the gift of Dharma.' This is not abandoning sentient beings. If one does not see anyone in want, one eats only two-thirds of the food to sustain body and life. Why? If the practitioner greedily eats to excess, the belly swells and the chest is blocked, hindering the practice of the path. If one leaves a third, the body is light and at ease, digestion is easy and without ailment. When the body suffers no harm, the practice of the path is not hindered. Therefore one should accept the dharma of eating with restraint.

"Sixth, after eating with restraint, if one drinks fruit-juice after midday, the mind gives rise to attachment and pleasure, seeking various juices: fruit juice, honey juice, and the like. Seeking without satiation, one cannot single-mindedly cultivate wholesome dharmas. It is like a horse without a bridle that wanders left and right eating grass, unwilling to proceed on the road. If the bridle is put on, the desire for grass is cut off and the horse follows the rider's will. Therefore one should accept the dharma of not drinking juice after midday.

"Seventh, one should enter a settlement and gather old, dust-covered, discarded materials, wash them clean, and sew them into patched rag-robes to cover the body against cold and dew. If one has fine robes, there arise the conditions of seeking from the four directions and falling into wrong livelihood. If one receives fine robes from someone, attachment and familiarity arise; if one is not familiar, the donor feels resentment. If one obtains robes from the Saṃgha, there are the faults of the Saṃgha assembly as described above. Fine robes are a source of attachment for those who have not yet attained the path. Fine robes invite the danger of thieves, even to the point of losing one's life. Because of such dangers, one should accept patched rag-robes.

"Eighth, one should have few desires and know contentment. Clothing should just suffice to cover the body, neither too much nor too little. Householders accumulate various garments out of fondness for fine things, while some non-Buddhist ascetics practice austerities and go naked without shame. Therefore the Buddha's disciples should abandon both extremes, abide in the middle way, and accept the dharma of wearing only three robes.

"Ninth, whether the Buddha is in the world or has entered parinirvāṇa, one should cultivate two dharmas: cessation and contemplation. The contemplation of impermanence and emptiness is the first gate of the Buddha-Dharma, able to cause weariness of and detachment from the three realms. In the charnel ground there are always the sounds of grief and weeping, and dead corpses lie scattered about, showing impermanence before one's very eyes. Furthermore, as fire burns and birds and beasts consume them, they are soon completely gone. Through this contemplation of corpses, among all dharmas one most easily attains the perception of impermanence. Furthermore, dwelling among tombs, when one sees dead corpses that are foul, rotting, and impure, one easily attains the nine-stage contemplation, which is the first gate of dispassion. Therefore one should accept the dharma of dwelling among tombs.

"Tenth, having already practiced the contemplations of impurity and impermanence, if the work of the path is accomplished, that is well. But if one has not yet attained the path, the mind becomes deeply weary, and therefore one should leave and go beneath a tree to meditate and seek the path. Furthermore, just as the Buddha was beneath trees at the time of his birth, at the time of attaining the path, at the time of turning the Dharma-wheel, and at the time of parinirvāṇa, the practitioner should follow the Dharma and always dwell beneath trees. For such reasons, one should accept the dharma of sitting beneath trees.

"Eleventh, dwelling beneath a tree is no different from half a house. The shade is cool and pleasant, and attachment arises, thinking 'my dwelling is good.' Beneath the tree, such outflows arise. Therefore one proceeds to dwell in the open, reflecting: 'Beneath a tree there are various faults: first, rain leaks through and brings cold dampness; second, bird droppings soil the body, and poisonous insects dwell there. There are such faults, but open ground is free of them.' In the open, one can dress and undress at will in comfort. Moonlight illuminates everywhere, making the mind bright and sharp, and one easily enters the samādhi of emptiness. Therefore one should accept the dharma of sitting in the open.

"Twelfth, among the four deportments of the body, sitting is foremost. Food is easily digested, the breath is harmonized, and for one seeking the path whose great work is not yet accomplished, the thieves of affliction are constantly watching for an opportunity. It is not fitting to lie down in comfort. Walking or standing, the mind is active and difficult to collect, nor can these be maintained for long. Therefore one should accept the dharma of always sitting. When one wishes to sleep, the ribs should not touch the mat. These are the twelve dhūta dharmas."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "You who are mindful should fix the mind on a single place and not let it be scattered or disordered. The merit of dhyāna concentration arises from this. All ordinary people, because of their inverted views, grasp at a self, a person, a being, and a life, following provisional names and giving rise to false views. From the very beginning the five aggregates have been pure, empty, and without any self or mine, neither arising nor ceasing, neither coming forth nor abiding, neither ordinary nor not-ordinary, neither noble nor not-noble, apart from all names and numbers, beyond the reach of speech. The Buddhas cannot practice there, cannot arrive there. All of you should now each still your conditions and carefully contemplate the marks of the body."

At that time the bhikṣus, hearing what the Buddha had taught, gave rise to joy in their minds and immediately contemplated this body: skin, blood, flesh, pus, rottenness, and filth; sinews, bones, veins, marrow, fat, and membranes; tears, snot, saliva, liver, gallbladder, spleen, and kidneys; heart, lungs, phlegm, the raw and cooked treasuries, small intestine, large intestine, urine and feces; hair of the head, body hair, nails, teeth, and the placenta and impurities. Thirty-six things, nine orifices of impurity. From the outside to the inside, from the inside to the outside, they searched for the mark of a self and could not find it at all. With unflagging diligence they came to see that body and mind arise and cease from moment to moment, like flowing water, like a lamp's flame: arising, there is no place from which they come; ceasing, there is no place to which they go; and in the present they do not abide. Knowing that these five aggregates have been from the very beginning empty and without any existence, they extinguished all marks and realized the wisdom of true suchness, becoming arhats. The bodhisattvas and others, having reflected on the Dharma, attained the patient acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas and fulfilled the ten grounds.

The Buddha addressed the great assembly: "Who is able, in the age of the semblance Dharma to come, to protect and uphold this sūtra, broadly proclaim and circulate it, and cause those who seek the Buddha's path to recognize its essential wonder?"

At that time Śakra and the nāgas, spirits, and the eightfold assembly, hearing the Buddha's proclamation, descended from the sky, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If in the age of the semblance Dharma there are practitioners of the three vehicles in secluded places seeking the Buddha's path, we shall serve as their guardians and protectors, not allowing evil ghosts and spirits to disturb them."

Mañjuśrī, Dharma-Prince, addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! By the spiritual authority of the Buddha, in the ages to come I shall protect and uphold this sūtra so that it is never cut off. For those who cultivate and study it, I shall serve as their guide."

At that time Ānanda came forward and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What should this sūtra be called? How should we receive and uphold it?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Dhūta Ascetic Practices.' It is also called 'Separating from Attachment and Gathering All the Roots of Goodness.' You should receive and uphold it."

At that time the devas, nāgas, the eightfold assembly, and the entire great assembly, hearing what the Buddha had taught, joyfully received and practiced it.
