# 般泥洹經
## Sūtra of Parinirvāṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0006

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was wandering on Vulture Peak near Rājagṛha, accompanied by a great assembly of bhikṣus, twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus in all. At that time, King Ajātaśatru of Magadha was at odds with the Vṛjjis. His ministers deliberated together, saying: "The Vṛjjis rely on the wealth of their state and the multitude of their people, their fertile land with its abundant fields and plentiful precious goods. They have not submitted to us; we should go and attack them." The king had a wise and virtuous minister named Varṣakāra, who was of brahmin lineage. The king commanded him to go, to bow his head to the Buddha's feet, and respectfully inquire about the Buddha's health and well-being, whether he moved about with ease and vigor, and whether his virtue and transformative influence were daily increasing, and to say: "Ajātaśatru has grievances against the Vṛjjis; the ministers advise going to attack them. We humbly wish to hear the Bhagavat's counsel on this matter." The minister received the command and immediately prepared five hundred chariots, two thousand cavalry, and two thousand infantry, and proceeded to Vulture Peak. Upon reaching the narrow path, he alighted from his chariot and continued on foot. Seeing the Buddha, he was filled with joy. His bearing was respectful and deferential; with solemn courtesy he completed the proper salutations, then kneeling at length said: "King Ajātaśatru of Magadha bows his head to the Buddha's feet and respectfully inquires about your health, whether you move about with ease and vigor, and whether your virtue and transformative influence are daily increasing." The Buddha said: "Very good. Are the king, the people of the realm, and you yourself all well?" Varṣakāra said: "The king has grievances against the Vṛjjis. The ministers advise that, since the Vṛjjis rely upon the wealth of their state and the multitude of their people, their fertile land and abundant produce and precious goods, and have not submitted to us, we should go and attack them. We humbly wish to hear the Buddha's teaching."

The Buddha replied to the minister: "Once, at a former time, I traveled to the Sārandada Shrine in the Vṛjji land, and I saw that the people of that land were all careful and diligent in their conduct. At that time I taught them seven principles for governing a state without danger. Those who are able to practice them shall prosper day by day and never decline." They clasped their hands and said: "We wish to hear the seven principles. How are they to be practiced?" The Buddha said: "Listen carefully." They replied: "We receive your teaching."

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda was standing behind the Buddha, fanning him. The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Have you not heard that the people of the Vṛjji state frequently assemble together to deliberate on affairs of governance and to maintain their defenses?" He replied: "I have heard that they frequently assemble together to deliberate on affairs of governance and to maintain their defenses." The Buddha said: "So it is; they will not decline. Have you heard that the ruler and ministers of the Vṛjjis are always harmonious, that those appointed to office are loyal and upright, and that responsibilities are passed on in proper succession?" He replied: "I have heard that their ruler and ministers are always harmonious, that those appointed are loyal and upright, and that responsibilities are passed on in proper succession." "Have you heard that the Vṛjjis uphold the law and lead one another in following it, that they are without covetousness or craving, and do not dare to transgress?" He replied: "I have heard that they uphold the law and lead one another in following it, are without covetousness or craving, and do not dare to transgress." "Have you heard that the Vṛjjis are careful and respectful in their culture of propriety, that men and women are distinguished, and that the elder and younger serve one another?" He replied: "I have heard that they are careful and respectful in their culture of propriety, that men and women are distinguished, and that the elder and younger serve one another." "Have you heard that the Vṛjjis are filial toward their parents, humble and respectful toward their teachers and elders, and receptive to instruction and guidance?" He replied: "I have heard that they are filial toward their parents, humble and respectful toward their teachers and elders, and receptive to instruction and guidance." "Have you heard that the Vṛjjis follow the pattern of heaven and earth, revere and hold in awe the spirits and deities, and honor the four seasons?" He replied: "I have heard that they follow the pattern of heaven and earth, revere and hold in awe the spirits and deities, and honor the four seasons." "Have you heard that the Vṛjjis revere virtue and the Dharma, that within their state they provide offerings of robes, food, lodging, and medicine to the śramaṇas and arahants, as well as to those who come from the four directions?" He replied: "I have heard that they revere virtue and the Dharma, and that within their state they provide offerings of robes, food, lodging, and medicine to the śramaṇas and arahants, as well as to those who come from the four directions." The Buddha said: "Those who govern a state and practice these seven principles will be difficult to endanger." Varṣakāra replied: "If the Vṛjji people hold to even one of these, they cannot be attacked; how much more so when they have all seven? Affairs of state are pressing; I must return." He took his leave. The Buddha said: "So be it; you know the time." He immediately rose from his seat, paid obeisance to the Buddha, and departed.

At this time, the Buddha instructed the Venerable Ānanda to invite all the wandering bhikṣus dwelling on Vulture Peak to assemble in the lecture hall. They were all invited and assembled. Having bowed their heads in obeisance, they sat to one side. The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Listen to what I say and practice it with mindful attention." They all said: "We receive your teaching."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus, there are seven teachings that, if followed, will keep the Dharma from declining. What are the seven teachings? First: they should frequently assemble to recite and discuss the scriptures and the path, without indolence. Second: they should be harmonious and compliant, teaching one another with loyalty and rectitude, and passing on responsibilities in proper succession. Third: they should be without covetousness or craving toward others, delighting only in mountain and forest retreats. Fourth: they should sever vain desires and serve one another according to seniority, elder and younger, with proper respect. Fifth: they should serve their teachers and elders with loving filial care, and be receptive to instruction and guidance. Sixth: they should uphold the Dharma, reverently observing the scriptures and precepts, and cultivate the brahmacarya. Seventh: they should follow the path, make offerings to the noble sangha, enlighten the ignorant, and provide robes, food, lodging, and medicine to those who come to study. Such are the seven teachings; through them one may long abide.

"Bhikṣus, there are also seven modes of guarding that, if practiced mindfully, will keep the Dharma from declining: First: guard purity; take no delight in conditioned activity. Second: guard non-desire; do not covet material support. Third: guard patience; engage in no quarrels or disputes. Fourth: guard the practice of emptiness; do not join in crowd gatherings. Fifth: guard the mind of Dharma; do not give rise to manifold thoughts. Sixth: guard one-pointedness of mind; sit in meditative concentration. Seventh: guard frugality and restraint; be coarse and simple in clothing and food, and use a straw mat as a bed. Such are the seven modes of guarding; through them one may long abide.

"Bhikṣus, there are also seven forms of reverence that, if practiced mindfully, will keep the Dharma from declining: First: reverence for the Buddha, with wholesome mind, pay homage and render service, without relying on other practices. Second: reverence for the Dharma, with will set on the path, without relying on other practices. Third: reverence for the Sangha, rely on and receive the teachings, without relying on other practices. Fourth: reverence for learning, serve those who uphold the precepts, without relying on other practices. Fifth: reverence for hearing, serve those who expound and teach, without relying on other practices. Sixth: reverence for purity and desirelessness, without relying on other practices. Seventh: reverence for concentration, serve those who sit in meditative stillness, without relying on other practices. Such are the seven forms of reverence; through them one may long abide.

"Bhikṣus, there are also seven treasures that, if practiced mindfully, will keep the Dharma from declining: First: have faith, upon seeing what is right, be joyful. Second: have precepts, guard them carefully and do not transgress. Third: have a sense of shame (hrī); correct one's faults and repent. Fourth: have modesty (apatrāpya); accord one's actions with one's words. Fifth: have much learning, recite and study without wearying. Sixth: have wisdom, practice deeply with subtlety and refinement. Seventh: give the gift of Dharma; without expectation of offerings in return. Such are the seven treasures; through them one may long abide.

"Bhikṣus, there are also seven factors of awakening (saptabodhyaṅga) that, if practiced mindfully, will keep the Dharma from declining: First: the awakening-factor of mindfulness (smṛti-saṃbodhyaṅga); pure and without defilement, abandoning scattered thoughts. Second: the awakening-factor of investigation of phenomena (dharmavicaya-saṃbodhyaṅga); pure and without defilement, abandoning scattered thoughts. Third: the awakening-factor of energy (vīrya-saṃbodhyaṅga); pure and without defilement, abandoning scattered thoughts. Fourth: the awakening-factor of joy (prīti-saṃbodhyaṅga); pure and without defilement, abandoning scattered thoughts. Fifth: the awakening-factor of tranquility (praśrabdhi-saṃbodhyaṅga); pure and without defilement, abandoning scattered thoughts. Sixth: the awakening-factor of concentration (samādhi-saṃbodhyaṅga); pure and without defilement, abandoning scattered thoughts. Seventh: the awakening-factor of equanimity (upekṣā-saṃbodhyaṅga); pure and without defilement, abandoning scattered thoughts. Such are the seven factors of awakening; through them one may long abide.

"Bhikṣus, there are also seven forms of knowing that, if practiced mindfully, will keep the Dharma from declining: First: know the Dharma, receive, recite, and discuss the twelve divisions of the Buddha's scriptures with care. Second: know the teaching, seek wisdom in the various dharmas and comprehend their essential meaning broadly. Third: know the time, when to recite, when to walk, when to meditate, when to rest, without neglecting what is appropriate to each moment. Fourth: know yourself, know your own progress in Dharma practice, whether much or little, deep or shallow, whether advanced or only beginning; let your determination improve day by day. Fifth: know moderation, do not covet fine and delightful things; eat moderately as suits the body; do not make yourself ill. Sixth: know the assembly, when entering the assembly of bhikṣus, brāhmaṇas, noble persons, gentlemen, and common people, know when to show reverence, when to stand, when to sit, when to be silent, and when to speak. Seventh: know persons; observe their inclinations, discern their aspiration and ability, guide and advise them accordingly, and lead them to know the noble transformation. Such are the seven forms of knowing; through them one may long abide.

"Bhikṣus, there are also seven contemplations that, if practiced mindfully, will keep the Dharma from declining: First: contemplate the path of scripture, as a person thinks of parents who give birth to a child and love them throughout a lifetime, so too the Dharma gives life to persons and ferries them across birth and death through countless ages. Second: contemplate human existence, there is no one without suffering; one is anxious about wife, family, and all one's possessions; at death all are separated and scattered, with no knowing where one will fall; if one's person has committed faults, even one's kin cannot effect release. Knowing this impermanence, one should be mindful and practice the path. Third: contemplate diligence, rectify body, speech, and mind; the attainment of the path is not difficult. Fourth: contemplate humility, have no arrogance or self-importance; serve the wise and learned; with compassion, teach and instruct those who have not yet heard. Fifth: contemplate restraint of mind, do not chase after the six senses; suppress the tendencies of lust, anger, and delusion; engage in no improper conduct. Sixth: contemplate the body, it contains nothing but foul impurity, wind, cold, heat, and blood; there is nothing in it worthy of craving. Seventh: contemplate yourself; the form is like dung and earth; daily one should contemplate death. Since heaven and earth were opened and the people came to be, there is none who has not died. The world is like a dream; what one sees with joy and love, one does not know is but transformation; awakened, one realizes emptiness. Know that this is illusion; do not deceive yourself. Such are the seven contemplations; through them one may long abide.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus, there are six weighty teachings; if practiced mindfully, one may long abide: First: cultivate the body, arouse a mind of loving-kindness; rely on the noble penetrating clarity; all those who are pure, practicing this weighty responsibility, be harmonious, loving, and respectful; give to fellow students; be without covetousness and without contention; strive together to uphold the practice. Second: cultivate wholesome conduct in speech, arouse a mind of loving-kindness. Third: cultivate wholesome conduct in mind, arouse a mind of loving-kindness. Fourth: whatever one encounters at the boundary of the Dharma, if one obtains robes, food, or any remaining articles in one's bowl, do not hoard them out of attachment. Fifth: uphold the precepts without transgressing; do not barter them away; use them to encourage others. Sixth: if, through right view, one has attained the essential truth, having received the path, ended suffering, and crossed over, with knowledge and vision perfected; then practicing this weighty responsibility, through the noble penetrating clarity and purity, be harmonious, loving, and respectful; give to fellow practitioners; be without covetousness and without contention; build one another up in turn; together uphold the practice of the path.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus, you should feel compassion for all living and moving beings, down to lice and nits, and show loving-kindness toward them all. When people die, you should grieve for them. Having obtained human birth, if they have not heard the Dharma, their families weep, and they themselves do not know where their spirits will go after death; only those who have attained the path can know this. It is for this reason that the Buddha expounds the scriptural Dharma. The scriptures must be studied; the path must be practiced. In the world there are many paths, and the royal path is great, but the Buddha's path is supreme above all of them. It is like tens of persons all shooting together at a target: some hit it first, some hit it later, but the shooting does not cease, and they must hit the target again. And it is also like the countless streams of the world, flowing without ceasing, all returning to the sea. So it is with bhikṣus: practice the path without ceasing and you will certainly attain liberation. In accordance with the Buddha's teaching, transmit it in succession; recite and chant the Buddha's words; give instruction at all times; let the four assemblies of disciples teach one another in turn; thus will the Buddha's scriptures long abide."

At that time, the Buddha asked the Venerable Ānanda to accompany him to the city of Pāṭaliputra. He immediately received the teaching and they set out. The Buddha gathered his robes and bowl, passed through Rājagṛha, and at the halfway point of the journey stopped to rest in the royal garden. The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Listen, all of you. Those who practice the path must know the Four Noble Truths (catvāri āryasatyāni). Ordinary people do not know them; therefore they run the long road, revolving in birth and death without rest. It is for this reason that I open your minds. What are the four? First: the truth of knowing suffering (duḥkha-satya); this is the noble truth. Second: suffering arises from accumulation (samudaya-satya); this is the noble truth. Third: the cessation of the accumulation of suffering (nirodha-satya); this is the noble truth. Fourth: the path to the cessation of the accumulation of suffering (mārga-satya); this is the noble truth. Not knowing suffering through wisdom, one runs the long road without ceasing in birth and death; one must know this truth of suffering. Suffering is: the suffering of birth, the suffering of aging, the suffering of illness, the suffering of death, the suffering of grief and lamentation, the suffering of separation from those one loves, the suffering of not obtaining what one seeks, in brief, the suffering of the five aggregates of clinging (pañcopādānaskandha). Having awakened to this suffering and being able to cut off the accumulation of craving, this is called attaining the eye. At the end of this birth there will be no further suffering. The accumulation arising from craving being completely exhausted, receiving the truth of the path, attaining the eye and directly witnessing, at the end of this birth there will be no further rebirth. Those who have seen the truth and attained the eye of the path will have no more birth and death; the long road is forever cut off.

"So it is, bhikṣus. You must also know the noble Eightfold Path (aṣṭāṅgika-mārga). What are the eight? First: with single-minded attention, receive the Buddha's scriptural Dharma. Second: abandon craving and desire; have no contention with the world. Third: never commit killing, stealing, or sexual misconduct. Fourth: do not deceive, slander, flatter, embellish evil, or revile others. Fifth: be without jealousy, covetousness, greed, or faithlessness. Sixth: be mindful of impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self. Seventh: contemplate the foulness and impurity within the body. Eighth: do not cling to the body; know that it will return to earth.

"All the Buddhas of the past have seen these Four Noble Truths; all the Buddhas of the future will also see these Four Noble Truths. Those who are attached to the affections of home and family, or who delight in worldly glory, fame, and longevity, will ultimately not attain this path of transcending the world. The path arises from the mind; only those with a pure mind can attain the path. Next: those with an upright mind who do not transgress the five precepts may attain the heavens. Next: those who have faith in the path and delight in studying the scriptural Dharma may later be reborn as humans. If one desires to completely sever the ways of hell, animals, and hungry ghosts, one must with single-minded heart uphold and practice the scriptural precepts. Now the Buddha, for the sake of all under heaven, liberates from birth and death and opens and reveals the right path. Those who wish to study should contemplate this carefully."

The Buddha and the Venerable Ānanda proceeded to Pāṭaliputra and stopped beneath the divine tree outside the city. The brāhmaṇas and householders, hearing that the Buddha had come with his disciples, all came out of the city wishing to pay respects and make offerings. Some brought cushions and mats, some brought carpets, some brought water and refreshments, and some brought lamps; they proceeded to the Buddha's place, bowed their heads in obeisance, and sat to one side. The Buddha addressed the brāhmaṇas and householders: "People in the world who are fond of greed and sensual indulgence will have five kinds of depletion: First, through self-indulgence, their wealth and property daily diminish. Second, through self-indulgence, they endanger themselves and lose the path. Third, through self-indulgence, the multitude does not respect them, and at death they have regrets. Fourth, through self-indulgence, their ugly name and ill repute spread throughout the world. Fifth, through self-indulgence, after death their spirit falls into the three evil paths. For those who can subdue the mind and are not self-indulgent, there are five virtuous blessings: First, through self-restraint, their wealth and property daily increase. Second, through self-restraint, they draw close to the mind of the path. Third, through self-restraint, the multitude respects them, and they have no regrets until death. Fourth, through self-restraint, their good name and wholesome reputation spread throughout the world. Fifth, through self-restraint, after death their spirit is born in the blessed realms of the heavens. Those who are not self-indulgent have these five goods; this should be contemplated." The Buddha taught the Dharma to the assembly, expounding the correct transformation in many important words. None were without joy; they all came forward, bowed their heads to the Buddha's feet, circumambulated three times, and departed.

Then the Buddha arose, went to Āvaddha, and sat beneath a tree. With his divine mind and the eye of the path, he saw the heavenly beings above and caused the virtuous deities to guard this land. The Venerable Ānanda rose from his meditation seat, bowed in obeisance, and stood to one side. The Buddha asked Ānanda: "Who planned this Pāṭaliputra and erected its ramparts?" He replied: "It was built by Varṣakāra, the great minister of Magadha; he intended it to check and cut off the Vṛjjis." The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! How wise of Varṣakāra to have planned this. I have seen the wondrous celestial deities of Trāyastriṃśa heaven jointly upholding this land. Wherever land is guarded by heavenly deities, it is certain to be peaceful and noble. Furthermore, this land is near the center of the heavens; the deity who presides over this land is named Manuṣyamati. Protected by Manuṣyamati, this realm will grow ever more excellent with the passage of time; it will certainly abound in sages, worthies, and men of benevolent wisdom and outstanding talent, unsurpassed by other realms, and none will be able to destroy it. But in the distant future, when this city is about to be destroyed, it will be through three causes: first, great fire; second, great flood; third, those within conspiring with those outside; only thus will this city be destroyed."

Varṣakāra heard that the Buddha had arrived with his disciples, and immediately rode out of the city with five hundred stately royal chariots, wishing to pay respects and make offerings. Upon arriving, he alighted from his chariot, walked through the garden gate on foot, and seeing the Buddha was filled with joy. His bearing was respectful and deferential; with solemn courtesy he completed the proper salutations and sat to one side. The Buddha taught him the Dharma, expounding the correct transformation in many important words. Varṣakāra was joyful, arose from his seat, and said: "I wish to offer a modest meal; I humbly beg that the holy assembly will deign to accept." The Buddha consented with silence. He immediately arose, bowed his head in obeisance, circumambulated the Buddha three times, and returned. The minister returned home and through the night prepared fine food and adorned the rooms, arranging beds and seats within. Early in the morning he informed the Buddha: "The meal is prepared; may the Holy One know the time." The Buddha then gathered his robes and took his bowl, went with his disciples to the minister's residence, and took the high seat, sitting before the assembly. Varṣakāra personally ladled out fine and excellent food and presented it in the bowl, and brought liquids; when the water for washing hands had been passed, he stood and said to the Buddha: "Having made these gifts of merit, I humbly beg the Buddha to chant words of aspiration for the people of this realm and all gods and humans, that they may long have peace." The Buddha chanted words of aspiration: "May the Buddha aid in your joy. As one who offers to gods and humans, who acts as guide to the people of this land, who has fed the Buddha and the bhikṣu sangha, who praises the right Dharma, who has received the wise words of the path, and who upholds and practices the scriptural precepts; for all of this I chant aspirations: may you know what is worthy of respect and know to respect it; may you know what is worthy of service and know to serve it; may you give widely and love universally, with a mind of compassion and mercy; may you all always obtain blessings and benefit, and attain the sight of the right path." The minister was joyful. The Buddha further said: "Though in this life you are engaged in official affairs, on account of this merit you will certainly attain liberation hereafter. For one who has had the fortune to feed the Buddha and those who are truly virtuous upholders of the precepts, the śramaṇas' aspirations will never be in vain. Furthermore, you should know: those who hold office and rank must not have a covetous mind, a dissolute mind, an arrogant mind, a cruel mind, or a pleasure-seeking mind. Rid yourself of these five and there will be no fault or regret hereafter; at death you will ascend to the heavens and be freed from the penalties of the evil paths." Having spoken, the Buddha rose from his seat and went out through the eastern city gate. Varṣakāra followed in attendance and said: "This gate shall be named the Gautama Gate." When the Buddha crossed the ford, Varṣakāra again followed and named it the Gautama Ford.

At that time, among the people, some crossed the river by large boats, some by small boats, and some by bamboo rafts or wooden floats; those who crossed were very many. The Buddha entered concentration and reflected: "In the past, before I had become a Buddha, passing through all the bodies I inhabited, I crossed on these rafts and boats more times than can be counted; now having attained liberation, I no longer cross in this way, and I shall also cause my disciples to be freed from this." The Buddha emerged from concentration and spoke these verses:

> The Buddha is the sea-crossing ship-master;
> the Dharma-bridge spans the river-ford.
> The canon of the Great Vehicle's path
> ferries all gods and humans across.
> And for one's own liberation...
> reaching the far shore, ascending to the immortal realm...
> all my disciples, every one,
> their bonds loosed, arrive at nirvāṇa.

At that time, the Buddha asked the Venerable Ānanda to accompany him to the village of Koṭigāma. They received the teaching and all set out; upon arriving they sat beneath a tree. The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Listen, all of you! You should uphold pure precepts, cultivate a composed mind, and practice liberating wisdom. Through these three, your meditative virtue will be richly fulfilled, and you will be freed from the defilements of lust, anger, and delusion, this is called rightly crossing beyond the disease of desire. In hope of this, strive with your own power to liberate yourself, so as to exhaust this birth; enter into pure conduct; diligently do what should be done; know single-pointedness of mind and thereby cultivate your nature; have no contention with the world; having come to know the affairs of the world, it is fitting to care for your own person; dwelling in stillness and reflecting inwardly, your will and intention will then be clear; the three defilements being removed, you will of yourself attain the path. The mind will no longer wander, and there will be nothing to which it clings, just as a king is lord over all his people, so the bhikṣu who cultivates self-reflection has ten thousand faculties, all with the mind as their master."

The Buddha and Ānanda together arrived at Nādikā and stopped beneath the great tree by the riverside. The disciples went into the city to seek alms; when finished they bathed, returned, paid obeisance to the Buddha, stood to one side, and said to the Buddha: "In this land there is much pestilence and many have died. This morning we all heard that ten upāsakas, Xuándǎn, Shíxiān, Chūdòng, Huòzhèn, Shūliáng, Kuàixián, Bózōng, Jiāndǔ, Déchēng, and Jìnggāo, have all died. Having lost their lives, where will these persons be directed?" The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "These ten persons have severed the natural course of their spirits and been reborn in the heavens of the Eighteen Levels, having reached the stage of non-return (anāgāmin); they will no longer come down to receive worldly phenomena. Moreover, those who have died in this land are not only these. With the Buddha's divine eye, I see five hundred upāsakas, all like Nandika, equally freed from the three defilements, having severed death across the five paths, all reborn in the stage of non-return, destined to attain nirvāṇa. There are also three hundred upāsakas who have severed the three fetters, who are without lust, anger, or delusion, who have ascended to the stage of once-return (sakṛdāgāmin); they will come again to be born below, and will reach the end of suffering. There are also five hundred upāsikās who have all attained the four joys, who have exhausted the three fetters, who have attained stream-entry (srotāpatti), who are free from the three evil paths, and who are reborn among gods and humans; in not more than seven lives, they will attain arahantship."

Then the Buddha said to the bhikṣus: "You speak of those who have died, is this to disturb the Buddha? Yet being the Buddha, I am no longer subject to such perturbation; what is there to fear? How subtle and wonderful it is. Birth and death have their time. As for the arising of Buddhas, though it is said they are born into the world, the nature of the Buddha is not of worldly attachment. Why? The Tathāgata's nature of Dharma has already ceased; there is nothing he does not perceive; having fully understood this birth, what is now proclaimed is clear and distinct. What is called wonderful is this: from the presence of this, one obtains this; from the absence of this, one does not obtain this; from this arising, this is born; when this ceases, all ceases. Why is this so? Because of seeking through desire there is ignorance (avidyā). Conditioned by ignorance, there are volitional formations (saṃskāra); conditioned by volitional formations, there is consciousness (vijñāna); conditioned by consciousness, there is name-and-form (nāmarūpa); conditioned by name-and-form, there are the six sense bases (ṣaḍāyatana); conditioned by the six sense bases, there is contact (sparśa); conditioned by contact, there is feeling (vedanā); conditioned by feeling, there is craving (tṛṣṇā); conditioned by craving, there is grasping (upādāna); conditioned by grasping, there is becoming (bhava); conditioned by becoming, there is birth (jāti); conditioned by birth, there are aging, death, grief, sorrow, suffering, vexation, and anguish, thus does this complete mass of suffering and its accumulation come about. This is the root of birth and death, revolving like a wheel, proceeding without rest. Because of delusion and ignorance there is birth and death. Suppose ignorance, with no remainder, ceases through the extinction of desire, then volitional formations cease; with volitional formations ceasing, consciousness ceases; with consciousness ceasing, name-and-form ceases; with name-and-form ceasing, the six sense bases cease; with the six sense bases ceasing, contact ceases; with contact ceasing, feeling ceases; with feeling ceasing, craving ceases; with craving ceasing, grasping ceases; with grasping ceasing, becoming ceases; with becoming ceasing, birth ceases; with birth ceasing, aging, death, grief, sorrow, suffering, vexation, and anguish; all this complete mass of suffering, accumulation, and conditioned existence ceases entirely.

"Therefore I have taught you before: the foolish have birth and death; the wise uphold the path and are no longer in birth and death. Contemplate this; subdue your mind and do not follow the path of birth and death again. Furthermore, to draw near to the path, you should have the four joys; practice them with mindful care: First, mindfulness of the Buddha (buddhānusmṛti); the mind joyful and inseparable. Second, mindfulness of the Dharma (dharmānusmṛti); the mind joyful and inseparable. Third, mindfulness of the Sangha (saṃghānusmṛti); the mind joyful and inseparable. Fourth, mindfulness of the precepts (śīlānusmṛti); the mind joyful and inseparable. Being mindful of these four joys and making them complete, seeing clearly for oneself, in hope of rightly crossing over, seeking the essential liberation, one can thereby sever and eliminate the ways of hell, animals, and hungry ghosts; attaining stream-entry (srotāpatti), one does not fall into evil states; though one continues to travel among the heavens and humankind, in not more than seven lives, one will of oneself reach the end of suffering."

At that time, the Buddha asked the Venerable Ānanda to accompany him to the land of Vaiśālī. They received the teaching and all set out. The Buddha joyfully passed through Koṭigāma and, having passed through the city, stopped outside the city at the garden of Āmrapālī. Āmrapālī heard that the Buddha had come with his disciples from the Vṛjji land, and immediately adorned her carriage and clothing; with five hundred female disciples, she went out of the city to the mango garden, wishing to kneel in reverence and attend upon him.

The Buddha, seeing from afar the five hundred women coming, instructed the bhikṣus: "Seeing this, all of you should bow your heads and look inward; rectify your minds. Those beautifully adorned women are like painted jars, though they appear colorful and bright on the outside, inside there is nothing but excrement and urine. You should know that all beautiful women are entirely of the kind of painted jars. Those who practice the path should not be deluded by them; therefore you should practice firm restraint, mindful contemplation, and discernment. This Āmrapālī comes also in accordance with my teaching. What is called firm restraint? When evil dharmas have already arisen, being able immediately to cut them off, cultivating one's nature with diligence and bringing one's mind to order: this is firm restraint. When evil dharmas have not yet arisen, being able to prevent them from arising, cultivating one's nature with diligence and bringing one's mind to order. When wholesome dharmas have not yet arisen, being able to cause the mind to generate them, cultivating one's nature with diligence and bringing one's mind to order. When wholesome dharmas have already arisen, establishing the will and not forgetting them, being able to increase and expand them, cultivating one's nature with diligence and bringing one's mind to order. Therefore one should: sooner break the sinews and bones, shatter the very body, than follow the mind into evil, this is firm restraint. What is called mindful contemplation? Contemplating the internal body, following the body in observation; contemplating the external body, following the body in observation, through inner and outer observation, with mindful discernment, cutting off and not allowing the mind to stray. Contemplating internal feeling, following feeling in observation; contemplating external feeling, following feeling in observation, through inner and outer observation, with mindful discernment, cutting off and not allowing the mind to stray. Contemplating the internal mind, following the mind in observation; contemplating the external mind, following the mind in observation, through inner and outer observation, with mindful discernment, cutting off and not allowing the mind to stray. Contemplating internal dharmas, following dharmas in observation; contemplating external dharmas, following dharmas in observation, through inner and outer observation, with mindful discernment, cutting off and not allowing the mind to stray. This is called mindful contemplation. What is called discernment of what may and may not be practiced? Following what is right, being able to distinguish one's conduct, this is called discernment. One who can practice firm restraint, mindful contemplation, and discernment possesses true strength, this is not the strength of a robust man with great physical power. Being able to depart from evil and approach goodness, this is called the greatest strength. Since I began seeking Buddhahood and contending with my mind, the kalpas have been without number; because I did not listen to the wayward mind, today I have become a Buddha in the world for you, and now this too may be brought to rest. Your minds have long been dwelling in impurity; you may lift yourselves free and escape these manifold sufferings. Seeing these women come, act as I have taught."

Then Āmrapālī arrived, bowed her head in obeisance, and sat to one side. The Buddha asked: "Now, how are the minds of you women?" She replied: "Having received the Buddha's great kindness, we have been able to hear the teaching of the Dharma; our ignorance has been awakened; day and night we discipline ourselves and dare not entertain wayward thoughts." The Buddha addressed Āmrapālī: "Those who are fond of sexual misconduct will have five self-harms: First, they will have an unsavory reputation. Second, they will be penalized by the laws of the king. Third, they will harbor estrangement and much suspicion. Fourth, after death they will enter hell. Fifth, when their hell-punishment is completed, they will receive the form of a beast. All of these consequences arise accordingly; those who can extinguish this desire in their minds will avoid them. Those who do not engage in sexual misconduct will have five increasing blessings: First, many people will praise them. Second, they will not fear the authorities. Third, their bodies will be at peace. Fourth, after death they will be born in the heavens. Fifth, they will establish themselves on the pure path of nirvāṇa. Therefore you should be weary and disgusted: women's bodies are afflicted with illness, the monthly period is impure, constrained and subject to ill-treatment, without freedom. If you receive and practice the scriptural precepts, you can attain a path of purity like the Buddha's." The Buddha taught Āmrapālī the Dharma, expounding the correct transformation in many important words. Āmrapālī was joyful; she arose from her seat, knelt at length, and said: "I wish to offer a modest meal; I humbly beg the Buddha and the holy assembly to deign to accept." The Buddha consented with silence, and she made obeisance and departed.

Not long after she left, the wealthy Licchavī nobles of Vaiśālī, hearing that the Buddha had come with his disciples, rode out from the city with stately four-colored chariots, wishing to see the Buddha. Among the Licchavis, some rode blue horses in blue chariots, with blue robes, blue canopies, blue banners, blue pennants, and all retinue in blue; some rode yellow horses in yellow chariots, with yellow robes, yellow canopies, yellow banners, yellow pennants, and all retinue in yellow; some rode red horses in red chariots, with robes, canopies, banners, pennants, and all retinue in red; some rode white horses in white chariots, with robes, canopies, banners, pennants, and all retinue in white. The Buddha saw hundreds of thousands of horses and riders filling the road as they came, and immediately said to the bhikṣus: "If you wished to see the retinue entering and departing the garden of Indra in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, it would be no different from this." The Licchavis arrived; they all alighted from their chariots, walked on foot into the mango garden, performed obeisance, and sat to one side. The Buddha taught the great assembly the Dharma, expounding the correct transformation.

There was a man named Bingxì who arose from his seat, straightened his clothing, and addressed the Buddha: "I have always heard that the Buddha's virtues are majestic and great; throughout the heavens and below, all are moved and drawn. I have constantly followed wherever you are, day and night reverently looking up to you, submitting to your pure transformation, daring to hold no afflictions." The Buddha said to Bingxì: "The wise and brilliant of the world know to revere the Buddha. Those who revere the Buddha will of themselves obtain blessings; after death they all ascend to the heavens and do not fall into evil paths." Then Bingxì spoke these verses of praise:

> Coming to pay reverent homage to the Dharma-King,
> the heart upright, the power of the path at peace.
> Most excellent, bearing the name of Buddha,
> his name shining forth like a snow mountain.
> Like a pure flower, doubtless and undefiled,
> one rejoices as if drawing near to fragrance.
> Gazing on his form, one never wearies;
> his radiance is bright as glittering dew.
> Only the Buddha's wisdom is lofty and wondrous,
> luminous and full, without impurity or dust.
> I vow to offer the precepts of pure faith,
> taking refuge in the Three Treasures.


At that time, the five hundred men of noble families seated in the assembly each removed the robes from their bodies and presented them to Bingxì. Bingxì received the robes and came forward to address the Buddha, saying: "These venerable ones, having heard these good words and rejoiced, together offer these five hundred fine robes to the World-Honored One. We pray that in your compassion you will accept them." After the Buddha received them, he said to them: "Bingxì! Know that the Buddha, the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Perfectly Enlightened One, endowed with wisdom and conduct, the Well-Gone, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Tamer of Persons, the Teacher of Gods and Men, called Buddha and Bhagavat, has appeared in the world, and there are five rare and wondrous natural qualities. What are the five? The Buddha appears in the world, teaching and transforming all under heaven; Śakra, Brahmā, śramaṇas, brāhmaṇas, dragons, spirits, emperors, and kings; and with natural wisdom manifests realization for the world, proclaims and expounds the true path: good in the beginning, good in the middle, good in the end, complete in essential meaning, pure and thorough, fully expounded in all aspects. This is the first rare and wondrous natural quality. The Buddha expounds the sūtras throughout the world, and all who hear them rejoice, take faith, study, recite, and rectify their body, mouth, and mind, departing from the wrong and entering the right. This is the second rare and wondrous natural quality. The peoples of the world, hearing the Buddha's sutric path, open their minds to understanding, contemplate it deeply, and all attain clarity and wisdom. This is the third rare and wondrous natural quality. The peoples of the world, hearing the Buddha's teachings and admonishments, many come to revere and respect them, emerge from the three evil paths, are born among gods and humans, and obtain great benefit. This is the fourth rare and wondrous natural quality. The peoples of the world, hearing the Buddha's profound and subtle Dharma teachings, understand the matter of the causes of birth and death from of old, sever attachments and cut off desires, and all obtain liberation. With first-level diligence one attains the path of the Arhat; with second-level diligence one attains the path of the Non-Returner; with third-level diligence one attains the path of the Once-Returner; with fourth-level diligence one attains the path of the Stream-Entrant. This is the fifth rare and wondrous natural quality. For any ordinary person who harbors a heart of gratitude toward the Buddha and makes even a small offering of goodness, all will obtain great merit; it shall not be wasted. Therefore, Bingxì, you should exert yourself diligently to study and attain this."

When the Buddha had finished speaking, the Licchavīs rose from their seats, straightened their robes, joined their palms, and said: "We had originally wished to invite the Buddha, but Āmrapālī preceded us. We hope to do so another day. We have many affairs and wish to take our leave." The Buddha said: "You may go. Know the right time." At that moment, they all prostrated at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated him three times, and departed.

Āmrapālī spent the entire night preparing rich and delicious food, adorned the interior of her rooms, and at dawn set out the beds and seats. She went before the Buddha and announced: "The meal is ready. May the Holy One know the time." The Buddha came together with his disciples to her dwelling and took the high seat, sitting before the assembly. Āmrapālī with her own hands served and poured, offered the almsbowl and presented beverages, and passed the water for hand-washing. Then she took a small seat and sat before the Buddha, wishing to inquire about the Dharma. The Buddha said: "I rejoice on your behalf, O lover of generosity. In future lives there shall be no resentment or fear; you shall receive great praise, your good name shall grow day by day, and people shall love and respect you. One who is without miserliness, benevolent and wise, lives thus without defilement and in peace, is born in the heavens above where the gods delight together." The Buddha expounded the Dharma for Āmrapālī, teaching the correct transformation with various essential words. All rejoiced.

The Buddha invited the Venerable Ānanda to accompany him to the city of Veṇugrāmaka, and they stopped beneath the grove of trees north of the city. That year there was famine in Veṇugrāmaka, and grain prices had risen steeply. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Here there is famine and it is difficult to beg for alms. You should divide into groups and go separately to Vaiśālī and the various towns and villages of Vajji, where you can avoid want." Having received the teaching, they were about to depart, when the Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Know how to practice self-restraint. When you obtain good, do not rejoice; when you obtain what is bad, do not be troubled. Take food only to sustain the body; do not greedily seek delicacies. It is precisely because of sitting and savoring tastes and craving them that birth and death does not cease. One who knows how to regulate the body and is able to practice self-restraint can attain stability of mind." The Buddha expounded the Dharma and rightly transformed them with various essential teachings. All rejoiced, paid homage to the Buddha, and departed, each group going to the various kingdoms and cities.

The Buddha remained alone with Ānanda and went to the village of Beluva. At this time the Buddha's body fell ill; his entire frame was in pain. The Buddha contemplated the severity of the pain, yet all his disciples were not present. He thought: "I must wait for the assembly to come before entering nirvāṇa. It is appropriate that through this illness I should marshal my strength and strive diligently, receiving the concentration that does not attend to the thought of the assembly." Accordingly, he entered and remained in samādhi, contemplating with right reception the concentration that does not attend to the thought of the assembly. Through this patient steadiness of mind, he was able to bear it himself.

The Venerable Ānanda rose from beneath a tree and came to the Buddha, prostrated at his feet, stood to one side, and inquired after the Buddha's condition: "Has the illness somewhat abated? Hearing that the holy body was ill, I was truly filled with anxiety and fear. World-Honored One, do you not wish to enter nirvāṇa? I pray you have some instruction for the assembly of disciples." The Buddha replied to Ānanda: "Would the Buddha go far from the assembly? I too am constantly within the community of bhikṣus. What should be carried out and taught has been fully set forth in what I have said before and after; it is all with the assembly. You should simply practice diligently in accordance with the sūtras. Earlier when this illness arose and my entire frame was in great pain, I contemplated the concentration that does not attend to the thought of the assembly. Because my mind was not attached to the illness, I maintained the essential in patient equanimity. Ānanda! The Dharma I have expounded is complete in inner and outer aspects. The Buddha being a teacher of the Dharma has left nothing out or forgotten; what should be practiced is sufficient and self-evident. I am now also aged; nearing eighty years; my body is like an old cart, without firmness or strength. I have originally said: birth and death have their time; nothing that is born does not end. At the highest level there is a heaven called the Sphere of Neither-Perception-nor-Non-Perception, whose inhabitants live for eighty-four thousand myriads of kalpas, yet even they die. Therefore the Buddha has established the sūtras throughout the world, showing to all the great path of nirvāṇa, in order to sever the root of birth and death. I now, for the sake of those with bodies, establish a lamp to guide the body to its return; for the sake of the Dharma teaching, I establish a lamp to guide the Dharma to its own return. What is meant by 'lamp'? What is meant by 'returning to oneself'? It means to concentrate the mind single-pointedly on the four foundations of mindfulness: first, contemplation of the body; second, contemplation of feeling; third, contemplation of mind; fourth, contemplation of phenomena. Vigorously restrain discursive thought; sever wandering notions. This is to create for all beings a lamp of the Dharma teaching; one should return to oneself. I have already spoken at length on this. If you wish to understand it, practice diligently in the inner and outer precepts, and ensure they are as they always should be. Those who take refuge in themselves and awaken to the Buddha's sutric path are all children and grandchildren of the Buddha. I have now relinquished the position of Wheel-Turning King to become the Buddha for all under heaven, crossing over and liberating the three realms in sorrow. You too should be concerned for your own bodies, and sever all suffering."

During that time for shelter from rain, when the mending of robes was finished, the Buddha invited the Venerable Ānanda and they went together to Vaiśālī. Having received the instruction, they set out at once. On arriving, they stopped at the Monkey Park. After going about for alms, washing the almsbowl, and bathing, the Buddha again went with Ānanda to the Cāpāla Shrine. The Buddha said: "Ānanda! Vaiśālī is a delight; Vajji too is a delight. Now throughout the world these sixteen great kingdoms; all their commanderies and cities are delightful. The Hiraññavatī River flows with abundant gold; the Jambudvīpa land is decorated with five colors; people born into this world take longevity as their joy. If a bhikṣu or bhikṣuṇī knows the four bases of spiritual power, this constitutes the pulling out of suffering. Through much practice and cultivation, keeping it constantly in mind without forgetting, according to what the mind desires, one can attain non-death for a full kalpa or more. So it is, Ānanda! The Buddha, through the four bases of spiritual power, having practiced much and cultivated extensively, keeping them single-mindedly in mind without forgetting, according to what the Tathāgata desires, can remain for a kalpa and more." The Buddha repeated these words two and then three times. But at that moment Ānanda's mind had sunk into peripheral thoughts and was obscured by Māra; he was dim and unawakened, and remained silent without responding. The Buddha said: "Ānanda, go to a tree and sit quietly, settling your mind in reflection." He received the instruction and sat alone in one place.

Then Māra Pāpīyas came and said: "The Buddha's mind has no desire and can enter parinirvāṇa. The teaching and instruction are now complete and finished; you may pass into extinction. Long ago, when the Buddha was traveling along the Uruvelā River and discoursing to various elders, he said: 'I, being the Buddha, though having attained freedom, do not covet a long sojourn; though not as of now. Those to be saved have likewise finished; I may enter parinirvāṇa.'" The Buddha replied to Pāpīyas: "The reason I have reached this point without yet entering extinction is that I await my community of bhikṣus and bhikṣuṇīs, causing them all to receive wisdom, to uphold and practice the sūtras and precepts, to exhort and guide those who have not yet entered, so that those who study may be perfected; and also that I await my upāsakas and upāsikās, causing them to attain wisdom, to uphold and practice the sūtras and precepts, so that those who have not yet entered may enter and those who have received the Dharma may be perfected. Thus, Pāpīyas, I have been waiting for these four assemblies of disciples, so that all might attain the meaning of the Dharma, and teach it to one another in succession, opening the minds of those still ignorant, and bringing those who study to accomplishment; this is why I have not yet entered extinction until now." Māra said: "The time is sufficient and it is complete." The Buddha said: "Be still. The Tathāgata will not be long; three months hence I shall enter parinirvāṇa." Māra's heart was then gladdened, and he departed in joy. The Buddha then sat upright, settled his mind in meditation, and contemplated: "In this samādhi I shall not sustain my vital breath; I relinquish the remaining formations of life." At this moment the earth shook greatly; the sky above became clear and bright; the radiance of the Buddha illuminated all without end; and spirits and devas came in multitudes, filling the empty sky. Emerging from meditation, the Buddha himself recited this verse:

> "The immeasurable deeds of accumulated virtue ...
> the conditioned; I now relinquish.
> Those nearby and afar who should be liberated
> have been liberated; those who should be liberated shall be."

The Venerable Ānanda's heart was startled and his hair stood on end. He went quickly to the Buddha, prostrated at his feet, stood to one side, and said to the Buddha: "How extraordinary, World-Honored One! The earth shook so greatly; what cause and condition is this?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "In general, when the earth moves, there are eight causes and conditions. What are the eight? The earth of this world rests upon water; the water rests upon wind; the wind rests upon space. The great wind in space sometimes rises of itself, then greatly disturbs the water; when the water is disturbed, the earth everywhere moves. This is the first. Sometimes a śramaṇa who has attained the path, or a spirit of wondrous power, whose virtue of precept-keeping is lofty and flourishing, wishes to test their power; placing their hand upon a small portion of earth, the entire earth moves. This is the second. When a bodhisattva is about to descend from the fourth heaven and enter the mother's womb, luminous with compassionate intent, wishing to manifest the transformation of the path and open the minds of the ignorant; he then releases divine radiance, shaking heaven and earth, causing Brahmā, Śakra, Māra, śramaṇas, and brāhmaṇas, all of them, to see the brightness. This is the third. When the bodhisattva is born and emerges from the mother's womb, his virtue moves the gods; the sky is clear and cloudless; divine radiance shines afar; and the earth everywhere moves. This is the fourth. When the bodhisattva attains the unsurpassed path and becomes the truly perfect Buddha, the earth moves greatly everywhere; divine spirits spread to the four directions, proclaiming and extolling the Buddha's name. This is the fifth. When one has become the Buddha, at the time of the first great assembly, the Wheel of Dharma turns three times, and gods and humans then understand; here and there bodhisattvas ascend and perfect the great path; radiance illuminates far and wide; and the earth everywhere moves. This is the sixth. When the Buddha's teaching is drawing to a close and he is about to abandon the vital formations, not sustaining his life, then he releases a great radiance, exhorting and inspiring gods and humans; the earth everywhere moves. This is the seventh. When the Buddha, the Bhagavat, is about to abandon his body and enter parinirvāṇa, his illumination reaches everywhere without exception, divine spirits and devas arrive in assembly; the earth everywhere moves. This is the eighth."

Ānanda said: "Has the Buddha now already relinquished his vital breath?"

The Buddha said: "It has been relinquished."

Ānanda said: "Long ago I heard the Buddha say: 'If there is a disciple who knows the four bases of spiritual power, who has practiced much and cultivated extensively, keeping them single-mindedly in mind without forgetting, according to what the mind desires, they can remain without dying for a full kalpa or more.' And the Buddha's virtue of the Way far surpasses this; can the Buddha not remain long as well?"

The Buddha replied to Ānanda: "Your words now; are they not too late? I spoke to you of the four bases of spiritual power, twice and then three times; yet you remained silent throughout, sunken in obscurity and dimness, not giving rise to clear thought, obscured by Māra. How then can you speak now of fully complying with the Buddha's words? Once a word leaves the mouth, can one rightly contradict it?"

He replied: "No."

"Just so, Ānanda! The unwise, having themselves uttered words, then proceed to contradict them. I have no such failing."

Ānanda wept and said: "How swift it is! The Buddha is entering parinirvāṇa; how hastily! The eye of the world is being extinguished."

At that time, the Buddha commanded the Venerable Ānanda to invite the wandering bhikṣus of the Vaiśālī kingdom. Having received the instruction, Ānanda invited them at once. They all assembled in the lecture hall, prostrated, and stood to one side. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The world is impermanent; nothing in it is solid and firm. All things must scatter and disperse; nothing that is impermanent endures. The activities of mind-consciousness are nothing but self-deception. Among those who have gathered through love and affection, who can long remain? Heaven, earth, and Mount Sumeru are subject to collapse and ruin; how much more so human beings, who would wish to endure forever? Birth, death, sorrow, and suffering are already cause enough for weariness and disgust! Three months hence the Buddha shall enter parinirvāṇa. Do not be alarmed; do not grieve. All the Buddhas who have come and gone and those present; all have attained their realization through the Dharma. While the Dharma of the sūtras yet remains, simply exert yourselves diligently, study with vigor, and practice with a pure heart, moving toward liberation. When mind-consciousness and emotion rest, then there is neither death nor rebirth; one no longer runs through the five paths, relinquishing one body to receive another. When the five aggregates have been fully severed, then there is no affliction of hunger, thirst, cold, heat, sorrow, grief, suffering, or distress. When a person knows how to rectify the mind, all the gods in heaven rejoice on that person's behalf. One should subdue the mind with gentleness and self-restraint, and not follow the mind's movements. The mind's activities are without limit; it is also by means of the mind that one attains the Way. The mind makes heaven; the mind makes humanity; the mind makes ghosts, spirits, animals, and hell; all are created by the mind. From the activities of mind all dharmas arise. The mind creates consciousness; consciousness creates volition; volition turns back into mind. Of these, mind is the most fundamental. The intent of mind is action; action creates life; wisdom and foolishness lie in action; longevity and brevity lie in life. These three; intention, action, and life; are mutually dependent. Whatever one does, whether good or evil, the body itself receives it. If a father does evil, the son does not receive it in his stead; if a son does evil, the father likewise does not receive it. Good deeds bring their own blessings; evil deeds bring their own calamities. The reason the Buddha is now revered by all under heaven above and below is entirely due to the work of intention. Therefore one should practice the Dharma with a rectified mind. It is only those who practice the Dharma who can attain peace in the present life and security in the present life. You should receive it well, keep it, recite it earnestly, settle the mind and contemplate it in stillness; for then my pure Dharma can abide long, and can compassionately deliver the multitude of sufferings of the world, peacefully benefiting the gods and people through the profit of the Way.

"Bhikṣus, know! What is called the Dharma? It is these: the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of spiritual power, the four meditations, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, and the Eightfold Path. Practicing and receiving them one can attain liberation and prevent the Dharma from declining.

"What are called the four foundations of mindfulness? Mindfully contemplating the body in the body from within; mindfully contemplating the body in the body from without; through inner and outer contemplation, distinguishing and reflecting, severing the mind of delusion and bewilderment. The contemplation of feeling, of mind, and of phenomena all follow as explained at the beginning.

"What are called the four right efforts? Unwholesome dharmas that have already arisen; one is able immediately to cut them off; practicing the cultivation of one's nature with diligence, one brings the mind to the right effort. Unwholesome dharmas that have not yet arisen; one restrains them so they do not arise; practicing the cultivation of one's nature with diligence, one brings the mind to the right effort. Wholesome dharmas that have not yet arisen; one is immediately able to bring them forth; practicing the cultivation of one's nature with diligence, one brings the mind to the right effort. Wholesome dharmas that have already arisen; one establishes the will so they are not forgotten, and is able to make them increase and grow; this is the right effort of the mind.

"What are called the four bases of spiritual power? Reflecting and desiring stable concentration, through which the multitude of activities is extinguished; with full mindfulness one possesses the spiritual basis, so that desire is not deviant, without grasping and without rejecting, constantly guarding pure conduct. Likewise the stable concentration of diligence, the stable concentration of the will, and the stable concentration of the precepts and practices; all are the same; the text is as stated at the beginning.

"What are the four meditations? Abandoning desire and unwholesome dharmas, practicing only mindfulness and activity, with the aspiration delighting in non-action; this is the accomplishment of the first meditation. When mindfulness and activity have been extinguished, guarding the mind inwardly as one, the aspiration dwelling in tranquility; this is the accomplishment of the second meditation. Contemplating without passion, the mind peaceful and the body upright, discerningly seeing the truth; this is the accomplishment of the third meditation. Having severed suffering and pleasure, with no thought of sorrow or joy, the mind now pure and clear; this is the accomplishment of the fourth meditation.

"What are the five faculties? First is the faculty of faith, the mind directed toward the four joys. Second is the faculty of diligence, which cultivates the four right efforts. Third is the faculty of mindfulness, keeping in mind the four foundations of mindfulness. Fourth is the faculty of concentration, practicing the four meditations. Fifth is the faculty of wisdom, seeing the Four Noble Truths.

"What are the five powers? First is the power of faith, the joyful mind unbroken. Second is the power of diligence, constantly able to vigorously restrain. Third is the power of mindfulness, attaining the contemplation of the foundations. Fourth is the power of concentration, the meditative mind undisturbed. Fifth is the power of wisdom, by which one verifies the path oneself.

"What are the seven factors of awakening? The awakening factor of mindful intention; the awakening factor of Dharma investigation; the awakening factor of diligence; the awakening factor of joyful delight; the awakening factor of single-pointedness; the awakening factor of concentrated stillness; the awakening factor of equanimous guarding.

"What is the Eightfold Path? Right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right intention, and right concentration; these are the pure dharmas for transcending the world."

At that time, the Buddha invited the Venerable Ānanda, and they went together to the town of Koṭigāma. Having received the instruction, they departed at once. As the Buddha delighted in Vaiśālī, passing out through the city gate, he turned and looked back at the gate, turning to the right, and smiled. The Venerable Ānanda straightened his robes, placed his right knee on the ground, knelt with joined palms, and asked: "Throughout the more than twenty years I have served you, I have never seen the Buddha turn back to look at something without cause. What is the cause and condition for now turning to look at the gate and smiling?"

The Buddha said: "Just so, just so. Ānanda! It is not the Buddha's custom to idly turn and smile without reason. This is my last sight of Vaiśālī; that is why I looked back and smiled." Thereupon the Buddha himself recited this verse:

> "This is my last time
> traveling through and beholding Vaiśālī.
> I shall journey to that nirvāṇa,
> and no longer receive another body."

A certain bhikṣu also recited a verse in praise:

> "The Buddha has declared this the last time;
> the movement of this body goes to its extreme here.
> If you proceed and sink into pure emptiness,
> where shall one look to see the Sage come forth?"

The Buddha and Ānanda went together to Koṭigāma and stopped beneath the grove of trees north of the city. He told the bhikṣus: "Protect the pure precepts; practice stability of mind; understand wisdom. For one who keeps the precepts and possesses concentration and wisdom, great virtue is accomplished and great repute is abundant. One forever departs from greed and lust, anger and hatred, and ignorance, and can attain Arhatship. If you wish to attain right liberation in this present life, you should add your own understanding, causing this present birth to be exhausted, and enter the pure path. Having practiced in accordance with what is proper, one then knows for oneself that this body will not again be received in a future life."

The Buddha again invited the Venerable Ānanda, and they went together to the town of Bhaṇḍagāma and stopped sitting beneath the trees north of the city. He told the bhikṣus: "Protect the pure precepts, practice stability of mind, seek the understanding of wisdom. One who protects the pure precepts does not follow the three flaws; one whose mind is stable does not scatter the mind; one who has already understood wisdom departs from the reception of desires and acts without obstruction. With precepts, concentration, and wisdom, one's virtue is great and one's repute is abundant; and being separated from the three defilements, in the end one attains Arhatship. If you wish to attain right liberation in this present body, strive diligently to seek understanding, causing this present birth to be exhausted and entering the pure path. Practicing in accordance with what is proper, one knows for oneself that extinction has come, and will not again be received in a future life."

The Buddha and Ānanda again went together, passing through the town of Yanmǎn and out past the towns of Jīn, Shǒushǒu, and Huāshì, to arrive at the town of Shānjìng. At each place along the way he expounded for his disciples these three essentials: "Protect the precepts; practice concentration; understand wisdom. Guarding these three, one's virtue is abundant and one's repute is great; lust, anger, and stupidity are dissolved; this is called right liberation. When one has a heart of precepts, the heart of concentration is accomplished; when the heart of concentration is accomplished, the heart of wisdom is bright. Just as pure clean fabric readily takes on dye bright and beautiful, having these three hearts the Way is easily attained. Simply apply yourself single-mindedly, striving diligently in the body to seek understanding, causing this present birth already to have entered purity. Practicing in accordance with what is proper, one knows that this is the uttermost and will not again receive birth. If one cannot fully accomplish the practice of precepts, concentration, and wisdom, it will be difficult to cross over the difficulties of the world. Having these three, the mind opens of itself to understanding; sitting in contemplation one can clearly see the five paths; the heavens above, humankind, hell, animals, and hungry ghosts; and know completely the intentions and aspirations of living beings. It is as when stream water is clear: the pebbles, sand, and gravel within it, blue, yellow, white, and black, all can be seen. The person of the Way, precisely because the mind is clear, sees all that is looked at. One who wishes to attain the Way should purify the inner mind. As when water is murky and turbid, nothing can be seen; one who keeps the mind impure cannot cross over the world. What the teacher sees and says, the disciple should practice. The teacher does not enter inside the disciple's mind to correct his thoughts directly. When thought and intention are upright, the Way is attained of itself."

The Buddha, having delighted in Shānjìng, again invited the Venerable Ānanda, and they went together to the town of Fūyán, stopping to sit beneath the trees north of the city. In the afternoon, Ānanda rose from his solitary sitting, came to the Buddha, prostrated, and with joined palms asked: "I suddenly wish to know; how many things cause the earth to move?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "There are three causes and conditions. First: the earth rests upon water, water rests upon wind, wind rests upon space; when the great wind rises, the water is disturbed, and when the water is disturbed the earth moves. Second: śramaṇas who have attained the path and spirits of wondrous power cause the earth to move in order to manifest their spiritual response. Third: by the power of the Buddha; from before and after my becoming Buddha, [the earth] has already moved; the three thousand suns and moons and twelve thousand heavens and earths, none have not been moved and stirred. Gods, humans, ghosts, and spirits have in great numbers heard and understood."

Ānanda exclaimed: "Wondrous indeed! The Buddha is without peer. Through the natural Dharma there is nothing that is not moved and stirred. Virtue so supreme, the Way so supreme; how lofty and magnificent it is!"

The Buddha said: "Just so, just so. Ānanda! The Buddha's virtue is not small. It is from the accumulation of merit through innumerable kalpas, reverently practicing all that is wholesome, that one has by oneself brought about becoming the Buddha; possessing these wondrous and natural qualities of transformation. Knowing all things, seeing all things, entering into everything, transforming everything. I recall that in the past, through compassion, among many hundreds and thousands of people, among the lords and noble assemblies of the kingdoms of the world, I manifested transformation, appearing before them; according to their various forms and appearances I spoke sūtras to comfort and exhort them in the way of the Dharma, circulating and teaching throughout, causing them to attain a wholesome mind. Such present transformations extended throughout the eight directions; adapting to the customs, dress, and languages of each country, reading each person's conduct, what Dharma they practiced and what sūtras they knew, I expounded for them and imparted to them the right path. For those who enjoyed words of meaning I provided the canonical teachings; for those who understood the principles of the Way I expounded the supreme essentials. I established their aspirations firmly, and then departed, having vanished. If kings, lords, and nobles did not know who I was, they would afterwards all deeply relish and reverently receive the transformation of the Dharma. This is the pure and wondrous natural Dharma of the Buddha.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! Obtaining the Buddha's power, I entered everywhere in manifest transformation with the Buddha's conduct, entering the community of śramaṇas as their guide and teacher; afterwards I entered the community of brāhmaṇas in transformation. Also among householders, Confucian scholars, and those of heterodox schools, adapting to their dress, voice, and language, I imparted to them the sūtras and the path, and all were accomplished. Having done this and departed, my followers all received my teaching without knowing who I was. This is the rare and wondrous natural Dharma of the Buddha. The Buddha also ascended above to the first heaven, the Heaven of the Four Great Kings, ascending further to the second, the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven; the third, the Yāma Heaven; the fourth, the Tuṣita Heaven; the fifth, the Nirmāṇarati Heaven; the sixth, the Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven; permeating everywhere, even to Māra. Then ascending to the seventh, the Brahma Heaven; the eighth, the Heaven of the Brahmā Assembly; the ninth, the Heaven of Brahmā's Ministers; the tenth, the Great Brahmā Heaven; the eleventh, the Heaven of Limited Radiance; the twelfth, the Heaven of Immeasurable Radiance; the thirteenth, the Heaven of Infinite Radiance; the fourteenth, the Heaven of the Sound of Water; the fifteenth, the Heaven of Contracted Purity; the sixteenth, the Heaven of Universal Purity; the seventeenth, the Heaven of Bright Clarity; the eighteenth, the Heaven of Guarded Wonder; the nineteenth, the Heaven of Profound Wonder; the twentieth, the Heaven of Merit and Virtue; the twenty-first, the Heaven of Sincere Simplicity; the twenty-second, the Heaven of Proximity; the twenty-third, the Heaven of Clear Sight; the twenty-fourth, the Heaven of Unbound Affection; throughout all of these I traveled among many hundreds and thousands of persons. For all these gods, appearing in whatever form was fitting, for those who delighted in purity I expounded purity; those who had penetrating understanding of the Way I exhorted to extend and spread the teaching; for those in purity I established in the great; those whose understanding of the Dharma was genuine I imparted the essentials to, guiding and encouraging them, benefiting and transforming them, causing them to attain the Way; and then I would depart, having vanished. Those gods did not know who I was. This is the rare and wondrous natural Dharma of the Buddha. The four heavens above are all without form or sound, so the Buddha does not go to them: the twenty-fifth, the Heaven of Infinite Space; the twenty-sixth, the Heaven of Infinite Consciousness; the twenty-seventh, the Heaven of Nothingness; the twenty-eighth, the Heaven of Neither-Perception-nor-Non-Perception.

"Thus it is, Ānanda! The Buddha's compassion is vast and great, accomplishing all things. He is pitiable and difficult to encounter; a Buddha appears in the world like an udumbara flower, only rarely. The Dharma the Buddha has taught is also difficult to hear. Having heard the Dharma of the sūtras, one should receive and protect it. How is it protected? After my passing into extinction, if a bhikṣu says: 'I personally received this from the Buddha's mouth; this is the Dharma, this is the Vinaya, this is the teaching'; yet his words do not accord with the sūtras and harm the Dharma, you should take the phrases of the Dharma, what the sūtras say and what the Vinaya shows, and expound them as elucidation. If these do not accord with the sūtras and conflict with the meaning of the Dharma, you should remonstrate, saying: 'Venerable one, please listen. The Buddha did not say this; you have received it wrongly. It contradicts the meaning of the Dharma; it is not Dharma, not Vinaya, not in accord with the Buddha's teaching. Know that this should be rejected.' If a bhikṣu says: 'I received this directly from a holy congregation that upholds the Dharma and precepts; this is the Dharma, this is the Vinaya, this is the teaching'; yet his words do not accord with the sūtras and harm the right Dharma, you should take the phrases of the Dharma, the meaning of the sūtras, and the words of the Vinaya, and expound them as elucidation. If these do not accord with the sūtras and conflict with the meaning of the Dharma, you should remonstrate, saying: 'Venerable one, please listen. The assembly of bhikṣus knows the Dharma and understands the Vinaya. This is not the Dharma and Vinaya; you have received it wrongly. It should not contradict the sūtras and the meaning of the Dharma; it is not in accord with the Buddha's teaching. Know that this should be rejected.' If a bhikṣu says: 'I received this directly from the mouth of an elder of experience and seniority; this is the Dharma, this is the Vinaya, this is the teaching'; yet his words and teachings do not accord with the sūtras and harm the right Dharma, you should take the phrases of the Dharma and the words of the sūtras, and expound them as elucidation. If these do not accord with the sūtras and conflict with the meaning of the Dharma, you should remonstrate, saying: 'Venerable one, please listen. The elder of experience and seniority knows the Dharma and understands the Vinaya. This is not the Dharma and Vinaya; you have received it wrongly. It should not contradict the sūtras and the meaning of the Dharma; it is not in accord with the teachings of the Vinaya. Know that this should be rejected.' If a bhikṣu says: 'I received this directly from a distinguished person of high wisdom, great merit, and great wisdom, revered by the many; this is the sūtric Dharma, Vinaya, and teaching'; yet his words do not accord with the sūtras and harm the right Dharma, you should take the phrases of the Dharma and expound them as elucidation. If these do not accord with the sūtras and conflict with the meaning of the Dharma, you should remonstrate, saying: 'Upright and venerable one, please listen. The distinguished and wise person who is bright and clear knows the Dharma and the Vinaya. This is not the Dharma and Vinaya; you have received it wrongly. It should not contradict the sūtras and the meaning of the Dharma; it is not in accord with the Buddha's teaching. Know that this should be rejected.'

"Furthermore, Ānanda! If someone says: 'I received this Dharma teaching directly from the Buddha'; yet his words are mistaken and do not accord with the sūtric Dharma. If someone says: 'I received this from one who reverently upholds the Dharma together with the holy congregation'; yet his words are mistaken and do not accord with the sūtric Dharma. If again someone says: 'I received this directly from the mouth of an elder of experience and seniority'; his words are in error and do not accord with the sūtric Dharma. If someone says: 'I received this teaching directly from a distinguished person of great wisdom, vast merit, and great wisdom'; yet his words are wrong and do not accord with the sūtric Dharma; you should raise up the Buddha's words to illuminate and clarify, simply causing that person to enter the sūtras and accord with the Vinaya, and as a detailed explanation of the Dharma teachings of the Buddha's sūtras, what the holy congregation upholds, what the elders have made clear, and what the distinguished ones have recognized; you should instruct that person to receive this carefully and without conflict, as the Vinaya teaches. Know to uphold these four. If, Ānanda, there are these four darknesses that harm the right Dharma, you should make distinctions for them, causing them to reject the deviant and flattering and to receive the four right intentions. This is called one who receives, upholds, and protects the Dharma. Those who do not uphold the sūtras and precepts; the community of bhikṣus should demote them. Tares and weeds that are not removed harm the good grain and sprouts; disciples who are not wholesome destroy my Dharma of the Way. You should check and examine one another, and not, because the Buddha has departed, fail to receive and practice the teachings. In the world, the śramaṇas who reverently practice the sūtras and precepts; because of them the world attains merit and the spirits and gods all rejoice. If you hear that in any place there is a bhikṣu learned in the sūtras, an elder bhikṣu, or a new person who is studying; you should go to them and inquire and receive. In this way the upāsakas and upāsikās will be glad to offer robes, food, beds and bedding, and medicine. Bhikṣus of the same path cannot but be harmonious; those who fall into hell and the three evil paths, all fall because of lack of harmony. Bhikṣus should not turn and mock one another, saying: 'I know many sūtras; you know few sūtras.' Whether knowing many or few, each should practice them. Words that accord with the sūtras should be used; those that do not accord should be rejected. This is what the Buddha has said and what the bhikṣus have received; hold it well and keep it well. If thereafter, whenever the sūtras are lectured and discussed, one should say: 'Thus have I heard. At one time the Buddha was in such-and-such a kingdom, in such-and-such a place, together with such-and-such a bhikṣu; he expounded this sūtra.' If that sūtra is genuine, one should not rashly say it is not. What the Buddha has said, received and transmitted and practiced in succession; those bhikṣus who do this can ensure the Dharma abides long."


## Fascicle 2

At that time, the Buddha invited the Venerable Ānanda to accompany him to the city of Kuśinagara. Having satisfied Māra Pāpīyas, they passed through the cities. Halfway along the journey, the Buddha's illness arose; his back was in pain, and he stopped to sit beneath a tree. He commanded the Venerable Ānanda to take the alms bowl and fetch water from the Kukusthā River. Ānanda received the instruction and set out. At that time, five hundred carts had just forded the river upstream, and the water was still turbid and unclear. Ānanda went to fetch water, returned, and addressed the Buddha: "Just now a group of carts passed through; the water is turbid and unclear, only fit for washing. The Hiraṇyavatī River is not far from here; I request to fetch drinking water from there." The Buddha took the bowl of water, washed his face and feet, and through patient endurance the illness again abated.

At that time, a great minister of the Mallas named Pukkusa, while traveling, saw the Buddha from afar: his faculties tranquil and silent, having attained the supreme calming and cessation of the mind's purity, his complexion bright and beautiful. His heart was filled with joy, and he came forward to pay respects to the Buddha. With solemn courtesy he completed the proper salutations and stood to one side. The Buddha asked Pukkusa: "From what did you obtain the joy of the Dharma?" He replied: "From the bhikṣu Āḷāra. Once, when I was traveling along the road, I saw Āḷāra sitting beneath a tree. At that time on the road, five hundred carts passed by. A person arriving afterward alighted from his cart and asked the bhikṣu: 'Did you see the group of carts that passed before?' He answered: 'I did not see them.' The person asked: 'Did you hear the sound of the carts?' He answered: 'I did not hear them.' The person asked: 'Were you sleeping?' He said: 'I was not sleeping; I was only contemplating the path.' The person exclaimed: 'The sound of carts was thundering, yet awake he did not hear them! How concentrated his mind must be! It is rare indeed! If he does not even hear five hundred carts, how could he hear anything else?' He immediately offered Āḷāra a dyed cloth robe. When I heard of this, it greatly strengthened my resolve, and I thereby obtained the joy of the Dharma, which endures to this day."

The Buddha asked Pukkusa: "Which do you think is greater: the sound of thunder and lightning, or the sound of five hundred carts?" He replied: "Even a thousand carts racing at full speed sounding together could not match it." The Buddha said: "Once long ago, I was traveling in Ātumā. In the afternoon of that day, a violent thunderstorm erupted; thunder and lightning struck and killed four oxen and two brothers who were plowing."

"Did the World-Honored One not hear it?"

The Buddha said: "I had been in settled concentration, walking in meditation. A person came, bowed his head in salutation, and walked along with me. I asked: 'Why so agitated?' The person said: 'Just now, thunder and lightning struck and killed four oxen and two brothers who were plowing. Did the World-Honored One not hear it?' I said: 'I did not hear it.' He asked: 'Were you sleeping?' I answered: 'I was not sleeping; I was in samādhi.' That person exclaimed: 'Rare indeed to hear of anyone attaining such concentration as the Buddha!' The sound of thunder fills heaven and earth, yet to be in such still concentration as not to hear it!" That person was gladdened in heart and also obtained the joy of the Dharma."

Pukkusa recited this verse of praise:

> How fortunate to behold the Buddha!
> What person would not obtain joy?
> Merit, vows, and occasion all converging,
> they bring me the benefit of the Dharma.

The Buddha replied with a verse:

> One who loves the Dharma sleeps in peace,
> obtaining joy, the mind's resolve grows pure.
> The Dharma spoken by the True One,
> the wise ever delight to practice.
> The Dharma protects those who practice the Dharma,
> as rain nourishes all that grows.

Thereupon the great minister commanded his servant to return and fetch a newly woven golden cloth. Offering it with his own hands, he said: "I know the Buddha does not use such things, but I humbly beg you to accept it with compassion." The Buddha received the cloth and taught him the Dharma, expounding the correct transformation with various essential words. Pukkusa rose from his seat and said: "From this day forward, I take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, and I take refuge in the Holy Sangha. I receive the pure faith precepts: I shall not kill, I shall not take what is not given, I shall not engage in sexual misconduct, I shall not speak falsely, I shall not drink wine, and I shall not eat meat. I shall not dare to transgress. Affairs of state are pressing; I must return and take my leave." He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated him three times, and departed.

The Buddha commanded the Venerable Ānanda to bring Pukkusa's golden cloth. Ānanda received the instruction and presented it. The Buddha took the cloth and draped it over his body. Ānanda saw the Buddha, his radiant countenance composed and serene, resplendent and bright, of an extraordinary purple-gold color. He knelt at length and said: "In the more than twenty years I have served you, I have never known the Buddha's face to be so lustrous and his color so brilliant. I wish to hear the reason."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! There are two causes and conditions for the Buddha's color to shine forth brilliantly. What are the two? First, on the night when one attains the unsurpassed, true, and perfect path of marvelous correct enlightenment. Second, on the final night when one relinquishes what has been received, with the unconditioned feeling of the remainderless, and enters final extinction. Tonight at midnight I shall enter parinirvāṇa; therefore my color shines forth."

Ānanda wept and said: "How swift! The Buddha is entering parinirvāṇa! How sudden! The eye of the world is being extinguished!"

Then the Buddha invited the Venerable Ānanda to proceed to the Hiraṇyavatī River. When the Buddha reached the riverbank, he put on his robe, entered the water, lifted his robe with both hands, and bathed his body. Having crossed to the far bank, he stood and adjusted his robes. He told Ānanda: "This morning I ate the meal offered by the disciple Cunda, and tonight I shall enter final extinction. You should explain to Cunda: 'The Buddha ate your offering and that very night entered final extinction.' In all the world there are two offerings that are difficult to encounter; if one is fortunate enough to encounter them and make offerings in person, one's doubts and fears are resolved, and there is moreover a right reward. What are the two? First, an offering of food whose nourishing power enables the recipient to attain the unsurpassed, true, and perfect path and become the supremely holy Buddha. Second, an offering of food whose nourishing power enables the recipient to relinquish what has been received, with the unconditioned feeling of the remainderless, and enter final extinction. Now Cunda, having fed the Buddha, shall obtain long life, shall be free from desire, shall obtain great wealth, shall obtain the highest honor, shall obtain retinue and kin, and shall ultimately be reborn in the heavens: these are the five blessings he shall receive. Tell Cunda not to grieve but to rejoice: by a single meal offered to the Buddha, he obtains manifold rewards. Know that the Buddha must be revered, the sūtras and Dharma must be studied, and the Holy Sangha must be served."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "As for bhikṣu Channa, whose nature is perverse, headstrong, and quick-tempered, who is fond of reviling and finding fault with others: after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, how should he be dealt with?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "After my parinirvāṇa, impose the brahma-punishment upon bhikṣu Channa. Let the sangha silently ostracize him and no longer speak to him. He shall then be ashamed and reform himself."

At that time, the Buddha commanded the Venerable Ānanda to prepare a bed and pillow, for his back was ailing. A bed and pillow were immediately prepared. The Buddha lay on his right side with knees bent and feet together, contemplating the path of perfect true wisdom. Then the Buddha invited the Venerable Ānanda to recite the seven factors of awakening. Ānanda said: "Yes! Formerly I heard from the Buddha: the first is the awakening factor of mindful intention; the Buddha used it to realize for himself the incomparable holy tranquility, the unconditioned, cessation, freedom from defilement, and relinquishment of scattered thoughts. The second is the awakening factor of Dharma investigation; the Buddha used it to realize for himself the incomparable holy tranquility, the unconditioned, cessation, freedom from defilement, and relinquishment of scattered thoughts. The third is the awakening factor of diligence; the fourth, the awakening factor of joyful delight; the fifth, the awakening factor of single-pointedness; the sixth, the awakening factor of concentrated stillness; the seventh, the awakening factor of equanimous guarding. Each the Buddha used to realize for himself the incomparable holy tranquility, the unconditioned, cessation, freedom from defilement, and relinquishment of scattered thoughts."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! Since you are able to recite these, you should certainly practice them diligently." He replied: "Yes." "One who can recite should practice diligently. Just so, Ānanda! Those who exert themselves attain the path swiftly."

The Buddha rose from his seat and contemplated the meaning of the Dharma. A bhikṣu recited this verse:

> The sweet dew of transformation, flowing forth from the Buddha;
> the disciples attentively listen and set it forth.
> Teaching with this, he exhorts those who study after;
> the seven awakening factors are wondrous: inquire of the wise.
> Through the arising of the Buddha, I have obtained
> the pure and immaculate practice, without flaw or deficiency.
> Those who study should know: right mindful intention,
> joyful delight in the Dharma, and diligent entrance.
> Single-pointed concentration, guarding the mind in stillness,
> with Dharma investigation: this is pure wisdom.
> Those who are ill should hear this;
> awakening to subtle thoughts, they dispel deviant reflections.
> This illness is the Dharma King;
> the treasure of the path flows from this source.
> If even he still requests to hear the Dharma,
> how much more should ordinary people not cease their listening!
> The foremost and supreme, the brilliant disciple,
> comes to ask after the illness, devoted to hearing the truth.
> Even the sage is never sated;
> how much less should others wish to abandon hearing!
> If the occasion passes and the Dharma teaching is complete,
> yet one raises other thoughts and the mind goes astray,
> that is not the joyful delight the Buddha taught,
> which is free from miscellaneous thoughts.
> Joyful delight is single-pointed Dharma:
> acting for the unconditioned, the mind's activities are stilled.
> Having rightly ceased with no thought of hearing,
> this is called the awakening factor of Dharma investigation.
> All formations have ceased, wisdom is complete;
> taking refuge in these three, the World-Honored Ones.
> May all people, gods, and spirits
> together study compassion, the great true path.
> Now that the Holy Teacher will enter final extinction,
> the assembly of the wise shall surely continue his bright teaching.
> At the proper times, recite the Dharma words;
> may the sacred relics aid the work of transformation.

At that time, the Buddha commanded the Venerable Ānanda: "Set up a rope-bed between the twin sāl trees with the head facing north. Tonight at midnight I shall enter final extinction." Ānanda received the instruction and prepared it at once, then returned to report that it was ready. The Buddha went to the twin sāl trees and lay down on the rope-bed on his right side. Ānanda was behind the bed, his head bowed, weeping bitterly, sighing and lamenting: "How swift! The Buddha is entering parinirvāṇa! How sudden! The eye of the world is being extinguished! Our fellow practitioners from the four directions who wish to see the Buddha, their hopes are now cut off. The Buddha will be difficult to behold again, difficult to attend upon again. Those who come and do not see him will all grieve and long for him. How can your heart bear this?"

The Buddha asked the bhikṣus: "What is Ānanda doing?" They replied: "He is behind you, weeping."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Do not weep. Why? Since you have attended the Buddha, your bodily conduct has always been compassionate, your speech has also been compassionate, and your mental conduct has also been compassionate. You have been considerate and provided comfort; your thoughts have been thorough and careful, with a devoted mind toward the Buddha. Although those who attended the Buddhas of the past performed the highest service, they could not surpass you. And those who will attend the Buddhas of the future, and those who attend the Buddhas of the present, though they serve with all their hearts, cannot surpass you. Why? You understand the Buddha and know what is fitting. Whenever bhikṣus wish to visit the Buddha, those who may be admitted always gain timely access. Whenever bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās wish to visit the Buddha, those who may be admitted always gain timely access. And whenever heterodox practitioners, brahmins, and householders come seeking an audience, those who may be admitted always gain timely access."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "In the world, the supremely noble Wheel-turning Sacred King has four rare and natural virtues. What are the four? When the kṣatriya kings of his vassal states come personally to pay court, the Sacred King is delighted and appears before them to teach the right Dharma; they all joyfully receive, honor, and practice it. This is the first virtue. When the brahmins who follow the path come personally to pay court, he joyfully appears before them and teaches them the Dharma; they all joyfully receive, honor, and practice it. This is the second virtue. When the householders come personally to pay court, he joyfully appears before them and teaches them the Dharma; they all joyfully receive, honor, and practice it. This is the third virtue. When the scholars of other schools and heterodox practitioners come personally to pay court, the Sacred King appears before them and teaches the Dharma; they all joyfully receive, honor, and practice it. This is the fourth virtue.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus, the Venerable Ānanda also has four beautiful and rare virtues. What are the four? Whenever bhikṣus come to Ānanda, he joyfully meets with them and teaches the sūtras and Dharma; there is none who does not gain understanding and joyfully receive and practice it. Whenever bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās come to Ānanda, he joyfully meets with them and teaches the sūtras and Dharma; there is none who does not gain understanding and joyfully receive and practice it. These are his first set of four virtues. There are four more: when the Venerable Ānanda teaches the sūtras and Dharma to bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, his mind is upright and his words are correct, without duplicity. Those who hear him are reverent and still, silently receiving. Because of this stillness, Ānanda's learning is vast and he forgets nothing. These are his second set of four virtues. There are four more: whenever bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, or upāsikās do not understand the sūtras, the Dharma, or the meaning of the Vinaya, when they ask Ānanda he immediately distinguishes and explains; all gain understanding and resolution. After they depart, there is none who does not praise Ānanda. These are his third set of four virtues. There are four more: among the twelve divisions of the Buddha's sūtras, the Venerable Ānanda has recited and memorized them all, transmitting them to the four assemblies of disciples, exactly as he heard them, without addition or omission, and without ever growing weary. These are Ānanda's fourth set of four virtues. They are rare and difficult to match; he has no equal in the world."

At that time there was a nirmāṇa monk standing before the Buddha. The Buddha said: "Monk, step aside; do not stand before me."

The Venerable Ānanda said to the Buddha: "In my twenty-five years of attendance upon you, I have never known a monk such as this one, who comes forward without any announcement or cause."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda, this is a nirmāṇa monk. Moreover, for many kalpas he has been a great honored deva of marvelous spiritual power and virtue; his anxieties and fears have been entirely eliminated. He knows that the Buddha's appointed hour is midnight, and the reason he has come is that from this time forward he will never see the Buddha again."

Ānanda said: "Is it only the devas who know that the Buddha is about to pass into nirvāṇa?"

The Buddha said: "From the city of Kusinārā, extending four hundred and eighty li in all directions east, west, south, and north, the devas are crowded together with no empty space; all are in sorrow, lamenting and unsettled, their minds thinking: the Buddha's parinirvāṇa is too swift."

The Venerable Ānanda asked the Buddha: "Near here there are great countries, the great country of Śrāvastī, the great country of Rājagṛha, the great country of Vaiśālī, the Buddha does not attain parinirvāṇa there, but precisely in this remote and humble little city?"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda, do not call this city remote and humble. Why? In ancient times this country was called the great capital of King Kusāvatī, with a city perimeter of four hundred and eighty li and a breadth of two hundred and eighty li. It was magnificent and splendid as a painting; the city walls were seven layers deep, with four tiers at the base, rising eight fathoms high and three fathoms wide at the top, all made of tiles of gold, silver, crystal, and lapis lazuli, four treasures. The battlements were carved and engraved with ornamentation; the ground was paved with precious gemstones, and the dwellings of the people were all fashioned from the four treasures. Along both sides of the roads there grew in abundance campaka trees, the trees also being of four treasures: the golden trees had silver leaves and fruit, the silver trees had golden leaves and fruit, and the crystal and lapis lazuli trees were likewise. When gentle breezes stirred the trees, they constantly gave forth five kinds of music, the sounds supple and moving, like the music of a five-stringed lute. Between the trees were bathing pools; at the edges of the pools were terraces and walkways succeeding one another; in the center were pavilions of four treasures, with steps, railings, walls, beds, and tables, all of four treasures. In the pools there were always lotus flowers of various kinds: blue lotuses (utpala), purple lotuses (kumuda), yellow lotuses (puṇḍarīka), red lotuses (padma); arrayed in rows in all four directions. Along the road on both embankments, there were also seven kinds of rare flowers, fragrant and sweet, blooming in both winter and summer, shining with five-colored radiance. The country constantly heard twelve kinds of sounds: the sound of elephants, the sound of horses, the sound of oxen, the sound of carts, the sound of conch shells, the sound of bells, the sound of small bells, the sound of drums, the sound of dance, the sound of song, the sounds of various stringed instruments, the sound of reciting virtue and righteousness, and the sound of praising the noble conduct of all Buddhas.

"At that time there was a Wheel-turning Sacred King named Mahāsudassana, who ruled over the four continents and governed with the right Dharma. He possessed the seven treasures that arise of themselves: first, the golden wheel treasure; second, the white elephant treasure; third, the dark horse treasure; fourth, the divine jewel treasure; fifth, the jade woman treasure; sixth, the householder treasurer; seventh, the holy counselor treasure. The king had four divine virtues: as a young prince he lived for eighty-four thousand years; as crown prince, for eighty-four thousand years; as Wheel-turning King, for eighty-four thousand years; after retiring and donning the Dharma robes, for eighty-four thousand years; a total lifespan of three hundred and thirty-six thousand years: this was his first divine virtue. The king could fly through the air, traveling the four continents, with the seven treasures preceding him; wherever he went, all submitted to his sovereignty: this was his second divine virtue. He was upright and beautiful in appearance, strong and healthy, with few ailments; his body was at ease, neither cold nor hot: this was his third divine virtue. His spiritual power was supreme; his mind was always harmonious and joyful; he clearly perceived the right path and transformed the people with the Dharma: this was his fourth divine virtue.

"Whenever the king went out in procession, he made gifts and accumulated merit, giving freely according to people's wishes: those who sought drink received drink, those who sought food received food; clothing, bedding, vehicles, horses, flowers, incense, money, and treasures were given without refusing anyone's wishes. He was as kind to the people as a father who loves his children, and the people adored the king as children who look up to their father. Whenever the king went out, he commanded his carriage to travel slowly, so that the people of the country might observe him at length. By nature he was pure and benevolent, and the four directions were at peace; this too was one of his supreme virtues.

"In the territories he governed, there were altogether eighty-four thousand countries. Whenever the petty kings came to pay court, King Mahāsudassana always invited them up to the hall, joyfully consoling them, teaching them the right Dharma, and asking what their countries lacked; the kings responded in deference, received the great bestowals, and were content. The king also commanded his envoys to each govern their territories strictly, to make them like his own hall, to transform the people with the right Dharma, and not to wrong the people under heaven. He then bestowed upon the kings crowns, hats, shoes, stockings, carriages, and treasures; receiving his commands, they took their leave, all in joy.

"At that time, the Dharma hall in which the great king ruled was forty li in length, with four tiers of steps, all of gold, silver, crystal, and lapis lazuli; the walls, railings, pillars, beams, rafters, ridgepoles, the upper roof and lower floors, beds, seats, tables, and mats, all were of the four treasures. Furthermore, in the Dharma hall there were eighty-four thousand jeweled canopied pavilions, all furnished with curtained enclosures: the golden pavilions had silver steps before them, the silver pavilions had golden steps, and the crystal and lapis lazuli pavilions were likewise. Between the canopied decorations hung flowers and suspended fruits, the four treasures interspersed; the coverings on top of the canopied curtains were gold and silver brocade, crimson felt embroidery, and brocade silk of mixed colors, with coral at the four corners; within each canopy was set a single seat of four treasures. The hall had bathing pools on all four sides, each one yojana in length and breadth; alongside the pools grew eighty-four thousand campaka trees, each one yojana tall, beside each canopied pavilion. Whenever the great king came out, he rode upon an elephant.

"At that time, Mahāsudassana made exceedingly widespread charitable offerings from his possessions; every morning he invited śramaṇas and brahmins up to the hall for meals. The king thought to himself: 'The days and months flow by, and I will grow old; I should use this palace with its five objects of desire as a foundation from which to restrain myself and cultivate pure conduct.' He then took only one attendant with him, ascended the Dharma hall, entered the golden canopied pavilion, and sat upon the silver throne, contemplating how all within the world is consumed by craving and lust without end, how those who are born must inevitably die, how the physical body returns to earth, and how all possessions and all things are impermanent. The king arose and entered the silver canopied pavilion, sat upon the golden throne, and reflected that all who come together must eventually part, that longing and attachment are of no benefit, and that he should abandon affection and purely cultivate brahmacarya. He arose again and entered the crystal canopied pavilion, sat upon the lapis lazuli throne, and contemplated that he must contend with old age, sickness, and death; he should reform his mind, change his conduct, eliminate desire, anger, and delusion, and contemplate the path of the unconditioned. He arose again and entered the lapis lazuli canopied pavilion, sat upon the crystal throne, and with focused one-pointedness thought to himself that he should abandon worldly craving and evil dharmas, contemplate the path of the unconditioned, guard purity of thought, and perfect the practice of the first dhyāna. In this manner, over a long period, he made a complete circuit of all the pavilions.

"Then the eighty-four thousand jade women together addressed the first jade woman treasure: 'As the heavenly queen knows, we have heard that we have not yet had occasion to attend personally upon her; maintaining feelings of devotion and respect, we wish to come and pay our respects.' She replied: 'Ladies, return and adorn yourselves; then we shall all go together to pay our respects.' She then told the holy counselor: 'We women have long been without personal attendance; with hearts of reverent longing, we all wish to come and pay our respects.' The counselor minister immediately harnessed eighty-four thousand elephants, armored in rhinoceros hide and decorated with gold, with trappings of precious jewels, the white elephant king with vermilion mane and tail being foremost; eighty-four thousand horses, armored in rhinoceros hide and decorated with gold, with trappings of precious jewels, the powerful horse king with a dark blue body and vermilion mane and tail being foremost; eighty-four thousand carriages, armored in rhinoceros hide, adorned with the four treasures, the holy counselor minister being foremost; eighty-four thousand women, one woman in each carriage, the jade woman treasure being foremost. The retinue of kings proceeded to the foot of the Dharma hall. The attendant reported: 'All the elephants, horses, carriages, ladies, and petty kings have come, wishing to pay their respects.' The king commanded the attendant to set out a seat below the hall; the king descended from the Dharma hall and saw the eighty-four thousand women in their resplendent adornments. At that time the people exclaimed: 'This is beyond words! The king's women are adorned to such a degree!' The jade woman replied: 'We have been long separated, unable to attend personally; therefore we have come in formal dress, wishing to pay our respects.'

"Then the king sat, and all the women came forward, bowed their heads in obeisance, and sat to one side. The jade woman treasure came forward and said: 'Now all these elephants, horses, carriages, jade women, and petty kings, all that heaven possesses, we pray the petty king will condescend to take notice, give his attention and find his delight. Furthermore, among the eighty-four thousand countries, the Heavenly King's capital is foremost; among the eighty-four thousand pavilions, the great right pavilion is foremost, we pray that heaven will give his attention and nourish his life.' The king replied: 'My dear, the reason I have been restraining myself throughout the night, reducing my indulgences, rectifying my mind, and practicing loving-kindness is precisely in order to distance myself from these desires. Why? The jealous nature of women brings calamity upon my body; therefore I have abandoned desire, wishing to be free from these faults.' The jade woman treasure wept and said: 'Why does the heavenly king alone sever his affections and desires, calling me his younger sister? Abandoning gracious feelings and severing the hopes of all the women, we wish to hear why the heavenly king disciplines himself and rectifies his mind to practice loving-kindness; what does this mean? We too wish to join together in cultivating it.' The king said: 'The right practice of the mind of loving-kindness does not fall into any outflows; abandoning craving and desire, cultivating virtue and guarding purity, reflecting that the days of life are few and the departure from life is swift, that people and things are all impermanent, and that only the Way is true, for this reason I have distanced myself from all elephants, horses, carriages, pavilions, countries, petty kings, women, and all desires, no longer binding my mind to them, wishing to attend to my own cultivation. Contemplating the interval between heaven and earth, there is no living being that does not come to an end. Ladies, each of you should rectify her mind and practice loving-kindness, and not give way to indulgence and fall into the outflows.' The jade woman treasure then dried her tears and said: 'Now the heavenly king restrains and diminishes himself, not wishing to fall into the outflows; reflecting that the days of life are few and the departure from life is swift, he withdraws to attend to his own cultivation and guards pure practice; reckoning that among all people and things there is no living being that does not come to an end, he distances himself from all possessions, not defiling his mind, we pray to receive these bright precepts and dare not forget them.' The king responded with a mind of loving-kindness, thanked all the women, and sent them all away. He returned and ascended the Dharma hall, entered the golden canopied pavilion; sitting in contemplation of the mind of loving-kindness, he completely forgot all enmity and hatred, without any jealousy or malice; advancing in contemplation of the great Way with immeasurable virtuous conduct, extending loving-kindness universally to the world, while restraining and examining himself. He arose again and entered the silver canopied pavilion; sitting in contemplation of the mind of compassion, he completely forgot all enmity and hatred, without any jealousy or malice; advancing in contemplation of the great Way with immeasurable virtuous conduct, extending compassion universally to the world, while restraining and examining himself. He arose again and entered the crystal canopied pavilion; sitting in contemplation of the mind of sympathetic joy, he completely forgot all enmity and hatred, without any jealousy or malice; advancing in contemplation of the great Way with immeasurable virtuous conduct, extending harmonious joy universally to the world, while restraining and examining himself. He arose again and entered the lapis lazuli canopied pavilion; sitting in contemplation of the mind of equanimity, he completely forgot all enmity and hatred, without any jealousy or malice; advancing in contemplation of the great Way with immeasurable virtuous conduct, wishing to guard everything, while restraining and examining himself. Through the practice of these four great brahmacaryas he withdrew from the mind of craving and desire, and greatly increased his pure conduct. Through practicing in this manner, the king attained freedom; at the time of death he was serene and at peace, his body without pain or affliction; and like a strong man savoring a fine meal in the space of a single swallow, his spirit departed and was reborn in the Seventh Brahma Heaven.

"The Wheel-turning King Mahāsudassana of that time was none other than my own former body. So it is, Ānanda, who can know that in my past lives I was a Wheel-turning King with seven self-arisen treasures, governing with the right Dharma and possessing four virtues, always able to be without greed? At that time, Kusinārā extended four hundred and eighty li in all directions, all within the heavenly king's city. I, at that former time, was also a kṣatriya king; I have already cast my bones into this earth six times, together with that occasion, seven. Now that I have become a Buddha, I have already severed birth and death; from this time forward I shall not again generate a body. I have also already completed a full circuit: teaching and transforming in the East, in the South, in the West, and in the North, moving every three months from direction to direction; yet in the end I shall lay my bones here."

The Venerable Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "After the Buddha passes into nirvāṇa, what manner of burial should be performed?"

The Buddha said: "Be still; the brahmins and householders will gladly attend to it themselves."

He asked again: "What should be the manner of burial for the brahmins and householders?"

The Buddha said: "It should be done according to the method for a Wheel-turning King: wrap the body with new kappāsikaṃ silk; then wrap it again with five hundred layers of cloth; place the body within a golden coffin, pour in sesame oil and rich unguent to fill it; then lift the golden coffin and place it within a great outer iron sarcophagus, pile an abundance of incense on top, and perform the cremation. When this is complete, collect the śarīra; at a major crossroads, erect a stūpa and build a shrine, raise a pillar and hang silk banners, make offerings of flowers and incense, and perform reverent ceremonies of worship; this is the method of burial befitting a Wheel-turning King."

The Buddha commanded Ānanda: "Go into the city and tell the Mallas: 'The Buddha will enter final extinction at midnight. Whatever you wish to do, do it in time, lest you have regrets afterward. Those who wish to come before the Buddha and receive his teaching should do so now.'" Ānanda received the instruction and went into the city of Kuśinagara. He found five hundred Malla nobles assembled in deliberation. Ānanda told the venerable ones: "The Buddha will enter final extinction at midnight. Whatever you wish to do, do it in time, lest you have regrets afterward. Those who wish to come before the Buddha and receive his teaching should come at once." All were startled and exclaimed in sorrow: "How swift! The Buddha is entering parinirvāṇa! How sudden! The eye of the world is being extinguished!" The sound of their grief and lamentation was heard within the palace.

The king dispatched Crown Prince Āchén together with the Mallas, each bringing their families, and all proceeded to the Twin Sāl Trees. Upon arriving, they reported to Ānanda: "We wish to come forward, pay respects, and inquire after the Buddha." Ānanda went in and announced: "Crown Prince Āchén, together with the noble families and their households, have all come to take the three refuges; the night grows late." The Buddha bade them enter. They all came forward, bowed their heads in obeisance, and sat to one side. The crown prince said: "The Buddha is entering final extinction; how very sudden it is!" The Buddha replied: "I have always said that the world is not real, and there is nothing in it to delight in. Ordinary people crave long life and yearn for the five desires, deluded and without benefit, only increasing birth and death and suffering without measure. Now I, being the Buddha, have attained the natural state of desirelessness. In this you too should exert yourselves. The wise of the world always wish to see the Buddha and delight in hearing the sūtras and the Dharma. Having this aspiration, you should strive to establish faith, establish precepts, practice generosity, acquire much learning, and attain wisdom. Build these five resolves to depart from defilement and miserliness, and then in life after life you will receive wealth and honor, your good reputation will be heard far and wide, you will be reborn in the heavens in peace and happiness, and you may attain nirvāṇa." When the Buddha had finished speaking, the crown prince and all the Mallas paid their respects and departed.

Then the king, with one hundred and forty thousand men, women, and children of the realm, went out at nightfall to the Twin Sāl Trees. Upon arriving, he reported to Ānanda, requesting an audience with the Buddha to receive his instruction. Ānanda went in and announced them; the Buddha bade them enter. The king, leading the worthy and virtuous of the realm, came forward, bowed his head in obeisance, and sat to one side. There were no lamps or torches before them, but the Buddha emitted a radiance from the crown of his head that illuminated two thousand li. The Buddha said: "You have wearied yourselves, great king, coming with your ministers." The king said: "The Buddha is about to enter final extinction. Have you any final instructions?" The Buddha replied to the king: "Since I attained Buddhahood, for forty-nine years all the sūtras and precepts I have taught are complete in every detail; the worthy talents of the kingdom have all been gathered." The king and his ministers were stricken with grief and sorrow. The Buddha told the king: "From ancient times to the present, among gods, spirits, human beings, and all creatures, none who are born fail to die; yet dying, they do not perish. Only nirvāṇa is bliss. Great king, why do you weep? You should simply think of what is good, reform past wrongs and cultivate future merit, govern the kingdom with just administration, not imposing sudden violence, treat the worthy and virtuous generously, pardon minor faults, and strive to practice the four kindnesses to bring peace to the hearts of the multitude. What are the four? First, practice generosity and provide for those in need. Second, be benevolent and loving, regarding the people as your own children. Third, benefit others by transforming them with goodness and rectitude. Fourth, share benefits and rejoice together with those below you. A king who acts in this way will always obtain blessings. In my former lives, I practiced these four kindnesses over countless ages, and therefore attained Buddhahood. When I first attained Buddhahood, I rejoiced at seeing nirvāṇa." He then recited this verse:

> Now I realize the Buddha is supremely honored,
> having abandoned desire and become pure, without outflows.
> His wisdom guides gods and humans;
> those who follow him attain joy.
> The reward of merit is the highest bliss;
> all wondrous vows and aspirations are fulfilled.
> Swiftly and courageously attaining the supreme liberation,
> I shall now pass into nirvāṇa.

The king and all who had come rose, paid homage to the Buddha, circumambulated him three times, and departed.

At that time in the city there was an aged wandering ascetic, one hundred and twenty years old, named Subhadra. Hearing that the Buddha would enter final extinction at midnight, he thought to himself: "I have a doubt concerning the Dharma that I have long wished the Gautama would resolve for me. I must go at once." He immediately arose and, exerting his own strength, walked to the Twin Sāl Trees. He said to Ānanda: "I have heard that the Gautama's appointed hour is at midnight. I request an audience to resolve my doubt." Ānanda said: "Desist! Desist, Subhadra! Do not disturb the Buddha." Subhadra earnestly requested twice and then a third time, saying: "I have heard that the Buddha is the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Truly Perfectly Enlightened One, Endowed with Wisdom and Conduct, the Well-Gone, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Guide and Tamer of the Dharma, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, called Buddha and Bhagavat, and that he is exceedingly rare to encounter, like an udumbara flower that appears but once in a hundred million ages. I beg only for a single audience to settle my doubt." Ānanda, thinking it would be a disturbance to the Tathāgata, did not wish to announce him. But the Buddha, whose divine mind hears all, surpassing ordinary humans in purity, perceived this from within. He immediately commanded Ānanda: "Do not prohibit him; let him enter. This is the last heterodox practitioner whom I shall convert: the wandering ascetic Subhadra." Subhadra, being admitted, was filled with elation and delight; a wholesome mind arose in him. Seeing the Buddha, he was filled with joy. His bearing was respectful and deferential; with solemn courtesy he completed the proper salutations, and stood to one side. He addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask something. Would you have a moment's leisure to resolve my doubt?" The Buddha said: "Ask freely, whatever you wish. Hearing it, you may obtain understanding."

Subhadra asked: "The scholars of the present age each proclaim themselves teachers. There is the master Gǔguī, the master Wúshī, the master Zhìxíng, the master Báilùzǐ, the master Yánshòu, the master Jìjīnfán, the master Duōjīyuàn, and Nirgrantha. Have these eight masters attained realization? Do they know of themselves?"

The Buddha told Subhadra: "They are different from the Buddha. Those disciples fashion for themselves thoughts of craving for life and reliance, following the deviant path. First is wrong view: not knowing this life or the next, not understanding that one reaps what one sows, delighting in divination and sacrificial rites to seek blessings. Second is wrong thought: the mind dwelling on sensual desire, harboring a contentious and angry heart. Third is wrong speech: falsehood, flattery, fawning, slander, and frivolous talk. Fourth is wrong action: killing living beings, taking by greed, harboring lustful and dissolute intentions. Fifth is wrong livelihood: seeking profit in clothing and food by means other than the right path. Sixth is wrong effort: unable to stop evil, unable to practice good. Seventh is wrong intention: clinging to craving and constant pleasure, regarding the painful body as pure. Eighth is wrong concentration: the mind fixed on what it desires, not seeing the way of escape. Thus, Subhadra! Long ago I left home, and after twelve years the path was completed and I attained Buddhahood. I have been expounding the sūtras and Dharma for fifty years. Since I left home, there have been precepts, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and the knowledge and vision of liberation. The one who teaches the right path is only the Buddha, the śramaṇa, and no ordinary person. The path I have trodden is the eightfold true path. The first fruit of the śramaṇa is attained through this; the second, third, and fourth are all accomplished through this. If one does not see this eightfold true path, one cannot attain the four fruits of the śramaṇa. What is called the eightfold true path? First is right view: seeing this life and the next, knowing that doing good brings blessings and doing evil brings calamity, knowing suffering and its accumulation, and through cessation and practice attaining the path. Second is right thought: delighting in leaving home, abandoning the contentious and angry mind. Third is right speech: words truthful and sincere, gentle, loyal, and trustworthy. Fourth is right action: not killing, not deviating, having no lustful mind. Fifth is right livelihood: seeking profit in clothing and food through the path, not by deviant means. Sixth is right effort: suppressing evil actions, giving rise to wholesome intentions. Seventh is right intention: mindfully contemplating the four objects, body, feeling, mind, and dharmas, understanding impermanence, suffering, non-self, and impurity. Eighth is right concentration: directed solely toward the unconditioned, accomplishing the four dhyānas. Śramaṇas and brahmacārins who tread this eightfold right path accomplish the four fruits. They are able to give the lion's roar. My worthy disciples, practicing without negligence, extinguish worldly intentions, and therefore attain arhatship."

Thereupon Subhadra said to Ānanda: "How wonderful, venerable one! This benefit is vast and beautiful, truly unprecedented. That a supreme disciple should be fortunate enough to encounter this, is that not marvelous? Now, having received the Holy One's grace and heard this Dharma, I wish to leave home and receive the precepts of perfection."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "The heterodox practitioner Subhadra wishes to receive the Bhagavat's natural Dharma and Vinaya, to leave home and take the precepts of the śramaṇa life."

The Buddha approved his resolve to take the precepts and said: "This is my final convert who shall attain the certification of seeing purity: the heterodox practitioner Subhadra." He then conferred the precepts and ordained him as a bhikṣu. With single-minded attention Subhadra received them without negligence, through firm restraint, through mindful contemplation, and through cutting off. As he wished, he shaved his beard and hair, donned the kāṣāya robe, and with the faith of one who has left home, departed from the household life. Practicing the path and obtaining the Dharma, his mind was furnished with pure conduct. He knew and testified for himself; his understanding was complete and penetrating; his practice was as it ought to be; his mind had fully realized. The Venerable Subhadra had crossed beyond the world and attained arhatship. Sitting, he thought to himself: "I cannot bear to wait for the Buddha's parinirvāṇa." He then entered final extinction before the Buddha.

At that time, the Buddha told the bhikṣus: "After my extinction, should there be such practitioners of heterodox arts and other disciplines among those of different birth who wish to abandon their topknots, come to tread the ford of the Dharma, bathe in the pure transformation, and leave home to take the precepts, you should permit them to become śramaṇas. Why? Because they possess great aspiration. You should first instruct them for three months, to know whether they are able to discipline themselves and apply their minds or not. If their words and actions are in accord, they are able to abandon their faults. First confer the ten precepts; after three years without transgression, then confer the two hundred and fifty precepts. The ten precepts are the foundation; the two hundred and forty precepts are the rules of propriety and deportment. Those who are able to practice these, all the devas rejoice on their behalf.

"Also, all those who aspire to receive the Vinaya, take precepts, and become śramaṇas are motivated by one of four conditions, all with an aspiration of delight and approach toward the path. After my nirvāṇa, some may leave government service and seek to become śramaṇas; some may be aged and elderly and seek to become śramaṇas; some may be poor and destitute and seek to become śramaṇas; some may have cultivated right conduct and seek to become śramaṇas. Those of talent who have cultivated right conduct, the aged and elderly, the poor and destitute, and those who have left government service who come to seek the path; for them in food and clothing, they should simply obtain what is adequate. Receiving and reciting the Dharma teachings, being like those of holy conduct, they may dwell for a long time, and will cause many people to be at peace and many people to attain liberation, giving the world something to rely upon and benefiting all gods and humans. Therefore, those who follow the Dharma will attain peace in the present life and attain liberation in the present life; you should truly and well receive this.

"What is the Dharma that enables one to attain peace and liberation in the present life? It is the twelve divisions of scripture taught by the Buddha: the first is prose discourse (sūtra); the second is mixed verse (geya); the third is prediction (vyākaraṇa); the fourth is verse (gāthā); the fifth is parable (avadāna); the sixth is historical narrative (itivṛttaka); the seventh is thematic analysis (nidāna); the eighth is birth stories (jātaka); the ninth is extensive discourses (vaipulya); the tenth is miraculous events (adbhuta-dharma); the eleventh is practice of the way (upadeśa); the twelfth is dual manifestation. These are called the Dharma. If one receives and upholds these, guarding them as Dharma, one will attain peace in the present life and be able to attain liberation. You should only truly receive them, guard, uphold, and recite them, reflect on them with a right mind, make the pure path clear, so that it may long abide.

"You, my disciples, should encourage and diligently exert yourselves. Do not grow slack, thinking: 'The Buddha has departed and there is nothing to take refuge in.' You must follow the Dharma teachings. Always, every half month on the full and new moon days, lecture on the precepts; on the six fast days, recite the sūtras from a high seat. Return your hearts to the sūtras so that it will be as though the Buddha is present.

"Moreover, sons and daughters of good family should contemplate and remember four matters: the first is when the Buddha as Bodhisattva first descended and was born; the second is when the Buddha first attained the path of wondrous and perfect enlightenment; the third is when he first taught the sūtras and turned the Dharma wheel; the fourth is when he relinquished the remaining conditioned feelings and attained parinirvāṇa. One should reflect and think upon these: contemplate how such was his merit and virtue at the time of the Buddha's birth; how such was his spiritual power at the time of the Buddha's attaining the path; how such were the people he liberated at the time of turning the Dharma wheel; and how such was the Dharma he bequeathed at the time of approaching nirvāṇa. Those in the intermediate and latter ages who contemplate these and rouse their minds to practice will all be born in the heavens. If anyone who receives these has doubts or heterodox views regarding the Buddha, the Dharma, the Holy Sangha, or the Four Noble Truths of suffering, arising, cessation, and the path; you monks should resolve their questions as though I were present. To respond with these very words to those who question directly is also truly to be a disciple; what they ask and are told, and what is explained through and derived from me, is this."

The Venerable Ānanda, standing behind fanning the Buddha, responded: "Yes, so it is. All have already taken delight in this; there is not one monk who has doubts or heterodox views regarding the Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha, or the Four Noble Truths."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Those who have already taken delight in the Tathāgata's right transformation, having no doubts regarding the Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha, suffering, arising, cessation, and the path; they should abandon covetous, arrogant, and contentious minds, follow the Buddha's teachings, receive them with diligent practice, silently contemplate the conduct of the path. This is the final testament of the Buddha, which you must together follow. You, my monks; gazing upon the Buddha's deportment and appearance, it will be difficult to behold again. After one hundred and forty thousand more years and beyond, there will again be a Buddha Maitreya, but he too is difficult to encounter at will. In the world there is the udumbara tree, which bears fruit without flowering; but when it does flower, there is a Buddha in the world. The Buddha is the sun of the world, ever solicitous in dispelling darkness from the multitude. Since I became a Holy Teacher, I have reached the age of seventy-nine. What needed to be accomplished has also been thoroughly completed. You should strive in this. The night is already half past."

Thereupon the Buddha entered the contemplation of the first dhyāna and penetrated it fully. Rising from this, he entered the contemplation of the second dhyāna and penetrated it fully. Rising from this, he entered the contemplation of the third dhyāna and penetrated it fully. Rising from this, he entered the contemplation of the fourth dhyāna and penetrated it fully. Rising from this, he entered the contemplation of the sphere of infinite space and penetrated it fully. Rising from this, he entered the contemplation of the sphere of infinite consciousness and penetrated it fully. Rising from this, he entered the contemplation of the sphere of nothingness and penetrated it fully. Rising from this, he entered the contemplation of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception and penetrated it fully. Rising from this, he entered the contemplation of the cessation of perception and sensation and penetrated it fully.

At this time Ānanda asked Anuruddha: "Has the Buddha already attained nirvāṇa?" Anuruddha answered: "Not yet. The Buddha is now in the contemplation of the cessation of perception and sensation." Ānanda said: "Formerly I heard the Buddha teach that from the fourth dhyāna, through non-knowing, relinquishing what has been received and the residual conditioned feelings, one attains parinirvāṇa."

At that time the Buddha left the cessation of perception and sensation and returned to the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception; leaving that, he contemplated the sphere of nothingness; leaving that, he contemplated the sphere of infinite consciousness; leaving that, he contemplated the sphere of infinite space; leaving that, he contemplated the fourth dhyāna; leaving the fourth dhyāna, he contemplated the third dhyāna; leaving the third, he contemplated the second dhyāna; leaving the second, he contemplated the first dhyāna. From the first dhyāna he again advanced to the third dhyāna, and then from the fourth dhyāna returned to the state of non-knowing, relinquishing what had been received and the residual nirvāṇic feelings, and attained parinirvāṇa.

At that very moment the earth trembled greatly. All the gods, dragons, and spirits thronged the sky and scattered flowers like rain; none failed to praise and admire, as they came to make offerings. At that time Śakra, Lord of the Second Heaven, descended and recited a verse:

> The aggregates move on; nothing is permanent ...
> This is simply the nature of arising and decline.
> Of those who are born, none fail to die;
> The Buddha's extinction is the highest bliss.

The Brahmā of the Seventh Heaven descended and recited a verse:

> Wondrous; the Buddha has now departed,
> Relinquishing all worldly supports.
> He broadly bequeathed the pure teaching,
> Unequalled throughout the three realms.
> The truth of spirit, power, and fearlessness ...
> Its radiant light is extinguished here.

The Venerable Anuruddha recited a verse:

> The Buddha now abides in the unconditioned,
> No longer using the breath of in and out.
> Originally descended bearing the luminous spirit,
> That radiance is now extinguished.
> The mind pure, with nothing to cling to,
> He accepted for humanity this swift passing.
> His bestowal of kindness and teaching now complete,
> He has retreated into quiescent cessation.
> All those who encountered the Buddha
> Were not without his beneficent grace.
> Now he has merged into the pure void;
> Seek understanding, and in time he shall again emerge.

At this time the monks were all in commotion, wandering about and calling out: "Swift! The Buddha has attained parinirvāṇa! How sudden! The eye of the world is extinguished!" Some were lamenting, grieving to themselves, thinking of the suffering of the world and that they had not attained this path. Some regarded the body, contemplating how the mind relies on existence and arises from conditions, how one acts and acts again, receiving extraordinary suffering; the living inevitably die, the dead are reborn, coming and going in birth and death, the spirit not extinguished, never arriving at this place.

The Venerable Anuruddha said: "Cease! Cease, Ānanda! Enlighten the assembled monks. The heavenly beings above see this and find it bewildering and confused. How can those who have left home and entered the natural Vinaya be unable to use the Dharma to benefit and liberate themselves?" Ānanda wiped his tears and asked: "How many heavenly beings are above?" Anuruddha answered: "From Vibhāvara to the Udumbara Temple and the Hiraṇyavatī River, four hundred and eighty li; gods and spirits fill it entirely, with no empty gaps. Moving about in commotion, all saying: 'Swift! The Buddha has attained parinirvāṇa! Also how sudden! The eye of the world is extinguished!' Some are lamenting, grieving to themselves, thinking of the suffering of the world, veiled by sensual craving, not seeing this path. Others are admonishing one another: 'The Buddha taught that the roots of birth and death arise from conditions; the mind acts and acts again, receiving extraordinary suffering; the living inevitably die, the dead are reborn; consciousness follows karma and none knows nirvāṇa. The Buddha has now crossed beyond the world; you should each be diligent. The night has passed halfway.'" Anuruddha ordered Ānanda to go into the city and announce: "The Buddha has already attained nirvāṇa. Whatever you wish to do, do it in time."

Ānanda went in and announced this to the city. The Mallas who heard it were all startled and astonished, leaping up and crying out in sorrow: "How swift! The Buddha has attained parinirvāṇa! How sudden! The eye of the world is extinguished!" The whole city gathered together, bearing flowers and incense, and proceeded to the Buddha's śarīra, bowing their heads in obeisance, attending and making offerings. They all asked Ānanda: "What is the funeral rite?" He answered as he had been instructed; the rites of a wheel-turning king, though the Buddha's should surpass even these. The noble families said: "Could we have a period of seven days, during which we wish to offer music, flowers, incense, lamps, and candles, to express our hearts?" Ānanda answered: "I freely permit whatever you wish to do." The Mallas then together made a golden urn, a golden palanquin and bier, a golden inner coffin and an iron outer coffin, and prepared five hundred lengths of new kalpavṛkṣa silk. At this time people from all four directions filled the area four hundred and eighty li in circumference, all bearing music, flowers, and incense; they came to the Twin Sāl Trees, together raised the Buddha's body, placed it upon the golden bier, and with music performed the rites of veneration and offering.

Thereupon the Mallas selected young men from among the assembly to carry the palanquin bier, intending to proceed to the Mukuṭabandhana sacred ground for the cremation. But the young men were unable to approach or move the Buddha's bier. They tried again, and yet again a third time, yet could not carry it at all.

The Venerable Anuruddha said to Ānanda: "The reason they cannot carry the Buddha's bier is the intention of the devas. The devas wish for the Malla youths to support the left side of the bier, the devas the right side, and the people of the realm to follow behind. Together they should raise the bier and enter through the east city gate, pass throughout the city, and having completed the celestial music offering, exit through the west city gate, proceed to the Mukuṭabandhana ground, pile up various incenses, and perform the cremation." Ānanda said: "So be it. I reverently follow the devas' intention." He informed the Mallas, and all said they would reverently comply. Then they had the young men attach various kinds of silk to the bier on the left side, fastening it to the left corner of the bier; the devas attached celestial silk on the right side, fastening it to the right corner of the bier and drawing it forward; while countless other devas in the void scattered various celestial flowers and let fall a rain of fragrant moisture.

At this time the great minister Bhaddiya and the minister of Kuśinagara deliberated, wishing to supplement the celestial music with human music to accompany the offering to the śarīra together. Proceeding as planned, they slowly entered through the east city gate, circled throughout the city, stopping at crossroads, main thoroughfares, and lanes, offering flowers, incense, and music. They exited through the west city gate, arrived at the Mukuṭabandhana ground, wrapped the Buddha's body in kalpavṛkṣa silk; five hundred lengths of cloth wrapped a thousand times; anointed with sesame oil and clarified butter, filled the golden inner coffin, placed the Buddha's body therein, raised the golden inner coffin and placed it in the iron outer coffin. Once the burial vessel was readied and the various incenses piled up, the great minister Ausu took fire and wished to ignite the cremation pyre; but when the fire was set, it immediately went out. He tried three times and could not ignite it.

The Venerable Anuruddha said to Ānanda: "The reason the fire cannot ignite is the intention of the devas. They see that Mahākāśyapa, leading five hundred disciples, is coming from Pāvā and is already halfway along the road, wishing to personally pay homage to the Buddha. It is for this reason that the fire cannot ignite." Ānanda said: "So be it. I reverently follow the devas' intention."

At this time there was a wandering ascetic named Ājīvika. Having been present at the Buddha's nirvāṇa, he had obtained celestial māndārava flowers and was traveling along the road. Kāśyapa saw him and questioned him from his carriage: "Do you know the Holy Teacher, the Buddha, whom I serve?" He answered: "I know of him fully. He has already attained parinirvāṇa seven days ago. Gods and humans gathered to make offerings to his body. I come from there and obtained these celestial flowers." Thereupon Kāśyapa was overcome with distress and displeasure. Among the five hundred monks, some wandered about in commotion, crying out to heaven in lamentation: "The Buddha has attained parinirvāṇa; how immensely sudden! The eye of the world is extinguished!" Some were lamenting with grief, grieving to themselves, thinking of worldly suffering, bound by love and attachment, not seeing this path.

Kāśyapa admonished them, saying: "O venerable ones, release your grief. You should know that all those with a body arise from conditions; the mind acts and acts again, incurring extraordinary suffering; the living inevitably die, and the dead are reborn; the five destinies know no peace; only nirvāṇa is bliss. Those who have not yet attained the path should seek the benefit of the Dharma; abandon conditioned existence and merge into non-arising, and you shall attain it. Gather your robes and walk swiftly; we may still see the Buddha's body."

Among their company there was one named Chanda, also a son of the Śākya clan who had left home together with the Buddha, who restrained the monks saying: "Why grieve further? From now on we ourselves have obtained freedom. That old one always said: 'You should practice this, you should not practice this.' Now that he has permanently departed, is that not greatly to our benefit?" Kāśyapa was displeased.

They walked on and arrived at the Twin Sāl Trees, approaching the place of the Buddha's cremation pyre. Kāśyapa said to Ānanda: "Before the cremation, I request to see the Buddha's body." Ānanda replied: "The Buddha's body has already been wrapped, treated with sesame oil, and placed in the golden inner coffin; various incenses have been piled all around and clarified butter poured over it. Although it has not yet been cremated, it is indeed difficult to see." Kāśyapa made his request three times; Ānanda's answer remained as before, for he felt it would be difficult to see the Buddha's body once more.

Thereupon from within the layered coffins the Buddha's two feet emerged together. All who saw this were filled with joy. Kāśyapa bowed his head in obeisance and paid homage. Seeing an unusual color on the Buddha's feet, he looked up and asked Ānanda: "The Buddha's body is golden in color; what is the cause of this unusual hue?" Ānanda answered: "A frail old woman bowed her head at the Buddha's feet, and her tears fell upon them; therefore they are of unusual color." Mahākāśyapa was again displeased; he then sighed deeply and recited a verse of praise:

> He has extinguished what is born, not to be born again,
> No longer subject to old age and death.
> Also no longer meeting again with what is hated,
> There is no longer any encounter with enmity.
> Originally having abandoned love and affection,
> Without sorrow at separation ...
> We should seek skillful means
> To cause us to attain this very place.
> The Buddha's five aggregates are pure,
> Having cut them off, there is no further arising;
> Nor does he further engage in action
> To receive these five aggregates.
> Suffering has entirely come to completion;
> The root of existence has also been eliminated.
> We should strive to seek skillful means
> To attain this peace as he has.
> The Buddha has severed the world,
> All love and desire entirely released;
> He was also fully able to endure,
> And attained liberation from all difficulties.
> He has already secured himself in peace
> And also brought peace to all under heaven.
> We should bow in homage to this one
> Who has permanently attained liberation from the three realms.
> The sūtras and precepts the Buddha taught
> Are the most illuminating in the world;
> He has broadly revealed the right path,
> True and certain, with nothing to doubt.
> He has also universally brought life to all under heaven,
> Enabling liberation from old age and death.
> Among all those who encountered the Buddha,
> Who was not the recipient of his vast grace?
> As the moon illuminates the night
> To dispel the darkness of shadows;
> As the sun illuminates the day,
> Making all under heaven bright ...
> Also as lightning appears
> To briefly illumine thick clouds,
> The Buddha's radiance arose for a time
> And has illuminated all three realms.
> Among all rivers bearing names,
> None surpasses the Kunlun River;
> Among all named great waters,
> None surpasses the ocean.
> Among all stars and constellations,
> The moon is the foremost in brightness.
> The Buddha is the guide of the world,
> Honored above the heavens and below.
> The reason the Buddha crossed beyond the world:
> His bestowal of merit has spread all around.
> The teachings, precepts, and practices he proclaimed
> Are everywhere and in all things clear.
> He has also spread the Dharma widely;
> His disciples joyfully receive and practice it,
> Causing gods, humans, ghosts, and spirits,
> And dragons to reverently receive, practice, and pay homage.

Having spoken these words, Kāśyapa bowed at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated the pyre three times, and stood to one side. The monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen, gods, dragons, spirit kings, gandharvas, yakṣas, garuḍas, deities of desire, and nāgas each came forward to bow at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated the pyre three times, and stood to one side when finished. Thereupon the Buddha's pyre spontaneously ignited without being set afire.

The Venerable Ānanda then recited a verse:

> The Buddha, pure within and without,
> With a body worthy of the Brahma-world,
> Originally descended bearing the luminous spirit ...
> And now is laid to rest in this place.
> Wrapped in silk a thousand times,
> Not requiring garments upon the body,
> Nor the need of washing and laundering ...
> As if clean, fresh, and brilliant.

By the end of that night the Buddha's pyre had burned to completion, and four trees naturally arose: the Sujñāna tree, the Kāviṭhu tree, the Aśvattha tree, and the Nyagrodha tree. The noble families of the realm together collected the Buddha's bones, filled the golden urn, placed it upon the palanquin bier, carried it into the city and set it in the great hall; together they performed music, scattered flowers, burned incense, and paid reverent homage.

At this time the Mallas of Pāvā, the Koliyas of Kaḷakagāma, the Mallas of Veṭhadīpa, the brahmins of Vethadīpa, and the Licchavis of Vaiśālī, having heard that the Bhagavān had stopped at the Twin Sāl Trees and attained parinirvāṇa, each mustered the four kinds of troops; elephant corps, cavalry, chariot corps, and infantry; arrived outside the city walls of Kuśinagara and sent a messenger saying: "We have heard that the Bhagavān, the World-Honored One, attained nirvāṇa here. He was also our teacher. With hearts of reverence and admiration, we have come together following your lord. We request a portion of the Buddha's bones to return to our homeland and erect stūpas and shrines." The king of Kuśinagara said: "The Buddha himself came here; I should be the one to make offerings. I regret to trouble all of you, but the relics cannot be divided."

The Śākyas of Kapilavastu also mustered the four armies and arrived to report: "We have heard that the Bhagavān, the World-Honored One, attained nirvāṇa here. This Śākyan holy champion emerged from our own kin; he is truly our forefather. With hearts of reverence and admiration, we have come to request a portion of the bones to return and erect stūpas and shrines." The king's answer was as before; he refused to divide them.

King Ajātaśatru of Magadha also mustered the four armies, crossed the river ford, and sent the brahmin Doṇa to enter and inquire about the situation, conveying earnest words: "From long ago we have been sincere and faithful friends, with no taking and no conflict. Now the Bhagavān, the World-Honored One, has attained nirvāṇa here; he is the Honored One of the three realms, truly my revered one. With a heart of reverence and admiration I come to request a portion of the bones. If you give them to me, I will give you all my precious valuables, and I hope we shall be companions to the end." The king answered: "The Buddha himself came here; I should be the one to make offerings. I thank Your Majesty the King, but the relics cannot be divided."

Thereupon Doṇa gathered the assembly and recited a verse to announce:

> Now each has marshaled himself
> And come from afar to bow in homage,
> Humbly and deferentially seeking a portion.
> If you do not give to us,
> We shall move to raise the multitudes;
> The four armies are here.
> If words of righteousness go unheeded,
> Lives will inevitably be at stake.

The people of Kuśinagara also responded with a verse:

> You have labored from afar, gentlemen,
> Condescending to bow in reverence.
> The Buddha came and left his form here;
> We dare not grant you this.
> If you wish to raise your forces,
> We have ours as well.
> If both our lives are at stake,
> That is no cause for fear.

The brahmin Doṇa admonished the assembly: "You gentlemen have all long received the Buddha's austere teachings; daily you recite the Dharma words, and your hearts are submissive to his benevolent transformation. All sentient beings still seek peace and happiness. Moreover, the Buddha in his great compassion, by cremating his form and leaving bones, wished to broadly benefit all under heaven. How should we destroy this fundamental intention of grace? The relics are here before us; we should simply divide them." The assembly all acknowledged this was good. They all went to the relics, bowed their heads in obeisance, and stood to one side. They then together commissioned Doṇa to divide them.

Thereupon Doṇa took one urn of about one shaku's capacity, anointed its interior with honey, divided the relics into eight portions, and announced to the assembly: "Having already shown reverence to the Buddha, and also rejoicing in the assembly's intention, I wish to receive the urn with relics to return and erect a stūpa and shrine." All said: "How wise! He knows the proper occasion." They immediately agreed and gave it to him.

There was also a brahmin named Wēnwéi who said to the assembly: "Cherishing this wholesome intention, I beg for the burned charcoal to return and erect a stūpa and shrine." All said: "Give it to him." Afterward an ascetic from Veṭhadīpa requested and received the ash.

At this time the eight kingdoms that received the eight portions of the Buddha's relics each returned and erected stūpas, all supremely beautiful. The brahmin Doṇa and the village mendicant Great Wēnwéi each returned to their respective towns; the ascetic from Veṭhadīpa obtained the ash and returned; all erected stūpas and shrines. There were eight portions of relics enshrined in eight stūpas, a ninth stūpa for the urn, a tenth stūpa for the charcoal, and an eleventh stūpa for the ash.

The Buddha was born on the eighth day of the fourth month; on the eighth day of the fourth month he left home; on the eighth day of the fourth month he attained the Buddha path; and on the eighth day of the fourth month he attained parinirvāṇa. All occurred when the Buddha's star arose; that season when all grasses and flowers bloom and trees and plants are lush and flourishing.

With the Buddha having attained parinirvāṇa, the brilliant light of all under heaven was extinguished. The gods and spirits of the ten directions all took refuge in and paid reverence to the Buddha.


After the distribution of the śarīra, and because the four assemblies of disciples from distant regions had not all received word of the Parinirvāṇa, they remained for ninety days before erecting the stūpa. The kings who had gathered, the noble families, the common people, their households and attendants, all observed the precepts and fasting for ninety days. The four assemblies of disciples from all distant regions gathered together at Kuśinagara and jointly asked Ānanda: "Where shall the stūpa be built?" Ānanda replied: "Going forty li outside the city, a stūpa should be erected at the crossroads of the Veda village." The noble families of Kuśinagara jointly made fired clay bricks, each three chi in length and breadth, gathered and assembled to build the stūpa. Its height, length, and breadth were all fifteen chi. A golden vessel was prepared with the śarīra placed within. A tall pillar bearing the dharmacakra was erected, with a canopy and hanging silks; lamps, flowers, incense, and music were offered in worship and veneration, and the people of the entire kingdom were able to accumulate merit together.

Mahākāśyapa, Aniruddha, and the assembly of monks met and deliberated: "Three hundred thousand people in a single day, together with nobles and ministers from various kingdoms, who encountered the Buddha, acted with reverence and performed meritorious deeds, will all ultimately be reborn in the fourth heaven, where they will meet Maitreya and attain liberation. The king of Kuśinagara will be reborn in the twelfth Heaven of Water Sounds, and when Maitreya becomes a Buddha, he will descend to build a vihāra for the Buddha, surpassing the present Garden of Anāthapiṇḍada." Ānanda asked Mahākāśyapa: "Why does the king of Kuśinagara not seek the path of arhatship under Maitreya Buddha?" He replied: "Because this king has not yet grown weary of the sufferings of saṃsāra; those who have not grown weary of suffering cannot attain arhatship." Ānanda said: "I have long been afflicted and weary of bodily suffering, yet I am unable to depart from the world. Why have I not attained the path?" Kāśyapa replied: "You merely observe the precepts without practicing contemplation of the body; you remain dependent on thoughts of sustenance within saṃsāra, and the activities of saṃsāra have not yet ceased in you."

When the ninety days had passed, Mahākāśyapa, Aniruddha, and the assembly of monks met and deliberated: "Among the twelve divisions of the Buddha's scriptures there are the four Āgamas. Only Ānanda attended the Buddha for long; what the Buddha taught, Ānanda has committed to memory. We should receive the teachings from him in writing. However, fearing that he has not yet attained the path and still harbors a mind of attachment, we wish to examine and reprove Ānanda through past events. By providing him a high seat, ascending three times and descending three times, we may in this way obtain truthful testimony." All agreed this was excellent.

When the assembly had taken their seats, the officiating monk expelled Ānanda. After a short while they summoned him again; Ānanda entered and paid respects to the assembled monks. Those who had not yet attained the path all rose in respect for him. The officiating monk directed him to the high seat at the center. Ānanda demurred, saying: "This is not Ānanda's seat." The assembled monks said: "On account of the Buddha's sūtras, you are placed upon the high seat; we wish to ask you something." Ānanda took the seat.

The assembled monks asked: "You have committed a grave transgression; are you aware of it? Formerly the Buddha said, 'Jambudvīpa is pleasant.' Why did you not respond?" The officiating monk ordered Ānanda to descend. He immediately descended and replied: "Was the Buddha incapable of speaking freely without requiring my words?" The assembled monks fell silent. The officiating monk again directed Ānanda to ascend. The assembly again asked: "The Buddha declared to you, 'One who has attained the four bases of supernormal power can remain for more than a kalpa.' Why did you remain silent?" Ānanda descended and said: "The Buddha declared that Maitreya will descend to become a Buddha; those who have newly entered the Dharma should attain liberation under him. If one were to remain, what would that mean for Maitreya?" The monks again fell silent.

Ānanda's heart was troubled. The monks said: "Good sir, you should set forth the Buddha's sūtras in full, in accordance with their meaning." He replied: "Yes, so I shall." After ascending thus three times, Ānanda finally ascended and spoke: "Thus have I heard: at one time..." Those in the assembly who had not yet attained the path all wept, saying: "The Buddha had only recently been teaching the sūtras; why is it already so long past?"

Mahākāśyapa then selected forty arhats from the assembly and received from Ānanda the four Āgamas: first, the Madhyama Āgama; second, the Dīrgha Āgama; third, the Ekottara Āgama; and fourth, the Saṃyukta Āgama. These four texts were compiled: the first for those afflicted by sensual desire, the second for those afflicted by elation and anger, the third for those afflicted by delusion, and the fourth for those lacking in filial piety and respect for teachers. Each of the four Āgama texts was sixty bolts of plain silk in length. The monks said: "Having transcribed these four texts, they should be propagated throughout the world." Therefore, at the place where the Buddha was cremated, four trees grew up of themselves. They thereupon gathered together and systematically transcribed the twelve divisions of the Buddha's sūtras. With the precepts and the Dharma now complete, within a thousand years those who uphold the Buddha's sūtras and precepts will all subsequently be reborn in the presence of Maitreya Buddha, and will from there be liberated and cross over from the passage through saṃsāra.
