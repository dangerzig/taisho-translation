# 灌頂王喻經
## Sūtra on the Simile of the Consecrated King

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0218

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護) et al.

---

At that time, the World-Honored One was dwelling at Śrāvastī. On account of a certain matter, he addressed the bhikṣus, saying: "You should know that there are three Kṣatriya kings who, at three different times, in various places receive royal consecration; and these three kings, until the very end of their lives, constantly keep this in mind. What are these three?

"The first king, in the full prime of his youth, undergoes royal consecration according to the rites of consecration, in a certain place; having received consecration, until the very end of his life, he constantly keeps this in mind.

"The second king, whose power has gradually grown great, undergoes royal consecration according to the rites of consecration, in a certain place; having received consecration, until the very end of his life, he constantly keeps this in mind.

"The third king, who possesses great might and is supreme in martial valor, before whom all adversaries have submitted in faith — on account of his supreme victory, dwelling in the security of that victory, he undergoes royal consecration according to the rites of consecration, in a certain place; having received consecration, until the very end of his life, he constantly keeps this in mind.

"You bhikṣus are likewise. At three different times, in various places, you engage in matters of cultivation and realization, and until the very end of your lives, you constantly keep this in mind. What are these three?

"There is a bhikṣu who, arousing sincere faith, leaves the household life and goes forth, shaves off his beard and hair, and dons the kaṣāya robe. This is the first — in a certain place, he engages in the practice of a bhikṣu and, until the very end of his life, constantly keeps this in mind.

"There is a bhikṣu who cultivates various noble practices and, in order to realize cessation, cuts off all factors of arising. Having known this thus, far removed from dust and separated from defilement, among all phenomena he attains purity of the Dharma-eye. This is the second — in a certain place, he engages in the practice of a bhikṣu and, until the very end of his life, constantly keeps this in mind.

"There is a bhikṣu whose āsravas are all exhausted, with no āsravas continuing to arise, whose mind is well liberated, whose wisdom is well liberated, who by his own direct knowledge has attained complete realization: 'My birth is destroyed, the holy life has been established, what had to be done has been done, there will be no more future existence.' This is the third — in a certain place, he has realized a noble fruit and, until the very end of his life, constantly keeps this in mind."
