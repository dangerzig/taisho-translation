# 異出菩薩本起經
## Alternative Account of the Bodhisattva's Origin

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0188

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Nie Daozhen (聶道真)

---

Śākyamuni Buddha, in a past life when he was still a human being, was in the country of Dvāravatī. Through countless lifetimes of doing good, after innumerable ages he became a Buddha. When the Buddha was still a bodhisattva, his name was Mānava. He dwelt in the mountains and wore a deerskin garment.

Once he entered a city called Padmaka, whose king was named Jaya. The Bodhisattva saw that everyone in the city was in a commotion, and he asked a passerby on the road: "Why is the city in such a commotion today?" The passerby answered: "The Buddha is coming today!" When the Bodhisattva heard that the Buddha was about to arrive, his heart was filled with private joy, and he said aloud: "If I see the Buddha come today, I wish to ask the Buddha to grant the desire of my heart."

After a moment, a woman named Gopī appeared, carrying a water pitcher, with seven flowers, a kind called utpala. The Bodhisattva followed her and called out: "Elder sister, please stop!" Gopī stopped and waited for him. The Bodhisattva said: "I request the utpala flowers in your hands." Gopī said: "The Buddha is coming today, and the great king is in the bathhouse. I must present these flowers to him. The flowers are not available." The Bodhisattva said: "I will buy the flowers for one hundred coins." Gopī said: "The flowers are not available." The Bodhisattva said: "You can go and get more." Gopī said: "That is not possible." The Bodhisattva then said: "I will buy the flowers for five hundred coins." Gopī thought to herself: "These flowers are barely worth two or three coins, yet he offers five hundred." She then gave him five of the utpala flowers and kept two for herself. The Bodhisattva reached into his robe and found exactly five hundred coins, which he gave to her in full, and they parted.

Gopī thought to herself: "This ascetic wears nothing but deerskin, yet he happened to have exactly five hundred silver coins, which he spent entirely on flowers. I suspect this is no ordinary person." She immediately followed and called out: "Sir! Sir! Please stop!" The Bodhisattva stopped and waited. Gopī said: "If you tell me the truth, I will give you the flowers. If not, I will take them back." The Bodhisattva said: "I bought the flowers, from one hundred coins up to five hundred. Why would you take them from me?" Gopī said: "These are the king's flowers. I have the power to take them from you."

The Bodhisattva then told her the truth: "I have heard that the Buddha is coming today. I wish to offer the flowers to him and ask the Buddha to grant the desire of my heart." Gopī said: "Excellent! I wish that in a future life I may become your wife. Whatever your birth, whether noble or lowly, I shall be your wife. Please set my wish before the Buddha and make it known." The Bodhisattva said: "Agreed." She then gave the Bodhisattva her remaining two flowers to offer to the Buddha. Gopī said: "As a woman, I cannot go forward myself. I entrust the flowers to you." The Bodhisattva accepted them, and they parted.

After a moment, the Buddha arrived. The king and all the people, down to the commoners, scattered hundreds of kinds of flowers upon the Buddha's head, but the flowers all fell to the ground. The Bodhisattva scattered his five flowers upon the Buddha's head, and the flowers all remained, facing upward in a row, as if rooted, and did not fall. The Bodhisattva then scattered Gopī's flowers upon the Buddha's head, and those flowers also remained, facing upward in a row, resting upon his two shoulders without falling. The Buddha knew the Bodhisattva's sincerity of heart.

The Buddha said: "I grant you the desire of your heart. After ninety kalpas, in the kalpa called Bhadra, you shall become Śākyamuni Buddha." When the Bodhisattva heard the Buddha's words, his heart was filled with great joy. He immediately spread his hair for the Buddha to tread upon, and standing before the Buddha, he leapt for joy. The Buddha, with his supernatural power, lifted him up, and he rose four zhang and nine chi from the ground, unsupported, then descended from above. The Buddha further said: "In a future life, when you attain the path and cross beyond the world, you too shall become a Buddha, just as I am."

The Buddha of that time was a Buddha of a former age, called Dīpaṃkara Buddha.

After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, the Bodhisattva returned to the mountains. When his life ended, he was reborn above in the second heaven, Trāyastriṃśa, where all the devas guarded and watched over him. When his heavenly lifespan was exhausted, he descended and was reborn in the country of Kuśinagara as a cakravartin emperor, ruling the four continents. When that lifespan ended, he again was reborn above in the second heaven, Trāyastriṃśa, as Śakra. Thus, ending and beginning again, he was thirty-six times Śakra, Lord of the Devas, and eighty-four thousand times a cakravartin emperor. When that lifespan ended, he was reborn above in the fourth heaven, Tuṣita. He then descended and was born in the country of Kapilavastu. Kapilavastu is the center of heaven and earth! When a Buddha is born, it cannot be in a border land or another country, for the earth would tilt to one side.

The king of Kapilavastu was a man of benevolence and virtue. The Bodhisattva descended and entered the queen's womb. Although there was impurity, he did not attach to it. The attendants, ministers, and kings of neighboring countries subject to Kapilavastu, hearing that the queen was with child, all came to congratulate the great king and made obeisance before the queen. The prince, from within the womb, could see the people outside, as if looking through a thin silk gauze. When the people outside made obeisance, the prince, from within the womb, waved them away with his hand. Why did he wave them away? Because he did not wish to trouble the people of the world.

When the queen was carrying the prince, the heavenly spirits daily brought celestial food and placed it before the queen. The queen did not know where this food came from, and she could no longer eat the food of the royal household, for it was bitter and pungent.

The prince was born at midnight on the eighth day of the fourth month, emerging from his mother's right side. Upon touching the ground, he walked seven steps, and within those seven steps his feet rose four cun high, not touching the ground. He then raised his right hand and said: "In heaven above and on earth below, there is none more honored than I!" The Four Heavenly Kings immediately descended, made obeisance, and held the prince, placing him upon a golden table. They prepared warm water and bathed his body. The king, the queen, and all the attendants were astonished.

At the time of the prince's birth, from the highest of the thirty-three heavens down to the sixteen hells, extending to the eight directions, throughout the twelve thousand heaven-and-earth realms, all was filled with great radiance, and heaven and earth shook. Thus he descended to become a child. His nurse wrapped him in fine cotton cloth. His mother also nursed him herself. He was named Siddhārtha. When Siddhārtha was born, his body bore the thirty-two marks.

The next day, the king and queen deliberated: "Our son is born unlike other people. In the kingdom there is a great sage, over one hundred years old, supremely skilled in reading physiognomy, whose name is Asita. We should go together and have the prince's marks read." The queen said: "Excellent!" The king and queen went together to the sage's dwelling. The king offered a bag of gold and a bag of silver to the sage, but the sage refused the gold and silver. He then opened the fine cloth and looked at the prince. The prince bore the thirty-two marks, and his divine radiance was manifest. The sage immediately wept with grief.

The king and queen asked: "Sage! Is there some misfortune for our son? The king has come today especially to have the prince's marks read, wishing to know whether they are auspicious or inauspicious. Why do you weep?"

The sage said: "Yesterday, when heaven and earth shook, it was precisely for the prince! I grieve because I am old. I am about to leave this world, and I regret that I will not live to see this person, that I will not hear his sūtras and precepts. This is why I weep."

When the king heard the sage's words, he immediately selected renowned courtesans and entertainers from throughout the kingdom, obtaining four thousand, and had one thousand in rotation perform music and singing day and night without rest. He also wished to guard the prince. The king knew well that the sage was supremely skilled in reading physiognomy, so he had the prince's palaces, gates, and walls all made complete and strong. If anyone tried to open the gates, the sound of the doors would be heard for forty li around.

When the prince was born, a servant boy in the palace was also born, and a white horse was also born. The servant was named Chandaka, and the horse was named Kaṇṭhaka. The king ordered the servant to attend the prince's horse, to care for and protect it, so that the prince could ride it.

Seven days after the prince's birth, his mother passed away.

When the prince was ten years old, he said to the great king: "As the king's crown prince, I have never gone out on an excursion." The king said: "Excellent!" He immediately ordered his attendants and officials to accompany the prince on his outing. The prince rode in a chariot out through the east gate of the city. Śakra, Lord of the second heaven, Trāyastriṃśa, then transformed himself into a sick person and appeared before them, with swollen belly and emaciated body, all flesh and muscle wasted away, leaning against a wall to rest.

The prince asked his charioteer: "What kind of person is that?" The charioteer answered: "That is a sick person." The prince asked: "What does it mean to be sick?" The charioteer answered: "This person committed evil in past lives. In this life as a human, he eats and drinks without restraint and sleeps and rises without regularity, and so he has contracted this illness." The prince asked: "I am the son of a king. If I eat and drink without restraint and sleep and rise without regularity, will I also get such an illness?" The charioteer said: "All people are subject to this." The prince immediately turned the chariot back, filled with sorrow and unhappiness, thinking: "All people in the world will become ill. I too will become ill. I shall not eat or drink."

The great king regretted having allowed the prince to go out. He again closed the palace gates and would not let him leave, and had the entertainers perform to cheer him. But the prince's sorrow only deepened, and he could not eat or drink. After a long while, he gradually recovered somewhat.

After several more years, the prince again reported to the great king: "I have been shut up in the palace for a long time. I wish to go out on another excursion."

The great king could not bear to refuse the prince, and again consented. He ordered throughout the kingdom in advance: "The prince is going out. Do not let any sick people or anything unclean be along the road." All was prepared, and the prince again rode his chariot out through the south gate of the city. Śakra, Lord of the Devas, again transformed himself into a person with a burning fever, hair unkempt and face unwashed, smeared with his own excrement and urine, lying in it, his life hanging by a breath. The prince asked the charioteer: "What kind of person is that?" The charioteer answered: "This person committed evil in past lives and would not restrain himself. He ate and drank without measure and slept and rose without regularity. He has contracted this illness, and his life hangs by a moment." The prince asked: "I too eat and drink without restraint and sleep and rise without regularity. Will I get this illness?" The charioteer said: "All people are subject to illness."

The prince immediately turned the chariot back. The prince was again filled with sorrow and would not eat or drink. The great king said: "Attendants! I gave orders in advance throughout the kingdom: do not let sick people or anything unclean be seen by the prince. How is it that a sick person was seen by the prince?" Afterward, the entertainers again performed to cheer the prince, but his sorrow would not lift, and he took no pleasure in the entertainment. After a long while, he gradually recovered somewhat.

After some time had passed, the prince again reported to the great king: "I am shut up in the palace and am unhappy. I wish to go out on another excursion." The king said: "Every time you go out, you return filled with sorrow and unhappiness and will not eat or drink. Why do you wish to go out again?" The prince said: "I will not be like that again."

The king again ordered throughout the kingdom: "The prince wishes to go out on an excursion. Do not let sick people or anything unclean be seen along the road." The prince rode his chariot out through the west gate of the city. Śakra, Lord of the Devas, again transformed himself into an old person, emaciated and hunchbacked, walking with a cane. The prince asked the charioteer: "What kind of person is that?" The charioteer said: "That is an old person." The prince asked: "What does it mean to be old?" The charioteer said: "When a person is born upon the earth, from the age of one to the very end, as their lifespan nears exhaustion, their strength wanes, and they can no longer eat or drink. Therefore they are called old." The prince asked: "Will I also grow old?" The charioteer said: "All who are born must grow old." The prince said: "Turn the chariot back. I too shall not long remain in this world." He was again filled with great sorrow: "All people must grow old and wane, their food and drink exhausted, and at last they must die. What use is it for me to remain long in this world?" He would not eat or drink, and hung his head in sorrow. The great king again coaxed and comforted him: "I have only you, my single son, and I intend to entrust the kingdom to you. Why is it that every time you go out, you return filled with sorrow and will not eat or drink?" The king had great entertainments performed to cheer him, and gradually his spirits lifted somewhat.

After a long time, the prince again reported to the king: "I wish to go out on an excursion." The king answered: "Every time you go out, you return filled with sorrow and unhappiness and will not eat or drink, growing thin nearly to the point of death. Why do you wish to go out again?" The prince said: "I am growing older."

The king again sent him out through the north gate of the city. Śakra, Lord of the Devas, again transformed himself into a funeral procession, with men and women inside and outside, carrying banners, weeping, and following the hearse. The prince asked the charioteer: "What is that sound?" The charioteer said: "It is the sound of weeping." The prince asked: "What does it mean, this sound of weeping?" The charioteer said: "Someone has died." The prince asked: "What does it mean to die?" The charioteer said: "A person is born upon the earth, their fate suspended from heaven. Lifespans are long or short; therefore there is death. The dead no longer know anything. The body is entirely consumed, and there is never a return. The household grieves and follows the hearse to send them off." The prince asked: "Will I also die?" The charioteer said: "All people must return to death." The prince said: "I cannot long remain between heaven and earth. I too must come to this death." He then turned the chariot back. The king asked the charioteer: "Why has the prince returned so quickly?" The attendants replied: "The prince went out on his excursion and saw a funeral procession on the road. His heart was unhappy, and so he returned quickly." The king said: "I truly did not wish to let the prince go out on excursions."

When the prince was twenty, the king wished to arrange a marriage for him. The prince said: "I do not wish to marry."

The king reviewed all the women of the kingdom for the prince, obtaining several hundred thousand women, and had the prince examine them with his own eyes. When the viewing was complete, none pleased the prince. At the very last, one woman named Gopī appeared. The prince said: "I wish to marry this woman." The king then arranged the marriage for the prince. The woman whom he married for the prince was the one who in a former life had sold flowers to the Bodhisattva. In that past life her name was Gopī, and in this life she was again named Gopī.

The prince said to his wife: "Let us share one bed, lying side by side. I wish to place fine flowers between us and gaze upon them together. Would that not be pleasant?" His wife said: "Flowers can be obtained." She immediately brought flowers and placed them in the center, and husband and wife lay on either side. The wife wished to draw close to the prince, but the prince firmly said to his wife: "If you come near me, you will surely crush the flowers, and their sap will stain the bedding." His wife then drew back.

After a long while, the prince again said to his wife: "Let us share one bed, lying side by side. I wish to place fine cotton cloth between us and gaze upon it together. Would that not be pleasant?" His wife said: "Cotton cloth can be obtained." She immediately brought cotton cloth and placed it in the center. The wife wished to draw close to the prince, but the prince said: "If you come near me, sweat and dirt will stain the cotton cloth." His wife then drew back, not daring to come close to the prince, though she harbored suspicions about him, and in sitting and rising she always followed him.

At midnight, the Four Heavenly Kings came through a skylight in the ceiling and called to the prince: "The time has come. You may go." The prince said: "I wish to go, but I cannot get out." The Four Heavenly Kings then caused the dancing women and musicians to fall into a deep sleep, knowing nothing.

His wife had rolled over in her sleep. The prince slowly rose from the bed and looked at his wife, fearing she might awaken. The prince then got down from the bed and left. He softly called the servant boy Chandaka, who had been born on the same day, and ordered him to saddle the white horse Kaṇṭhaka in the courtyard. Chandaka immediately saddled the horse. The prince mounted the horse to leave, but fearing the sound of the gate, he paced back and forth in the courtyard. The hoofbeats of the prince's horse were usually heard for twenty li, and the sound of the gate for forty li; therefore the prince dared not open the gate. The Four Heavenly Kings then ordered the ghosts and spirits to lift the horse by its hooves, and they passed over the roof and out of the city, arriving at the royal farmland, where they stopped beneath a tree.

The next day, the king did not know where the prince was, and the palace was in an uproar. The king said: "My son has never before gone on an excursion. He is surely at the farmhouse." The king then went to the farmhouse himself, and from afar he saw the prince sitting beneath a tree. The sun was about to shine upon the prince, but the tree bent its branches and leaves to shade him, not allowing the sunlight to reach him. The king's heart was filled with fear and apprehension. He dismounted and made obeisance to the prince. The prince also made obeisance to the king. The prince said: "As the king's son, I have never gone out before. Now that I have gone out once, the king has come after me. My horse and servant are still nearby. I beg the great king to return to the palace. In a few days I shall return on my own." The king then mounted his horse and went back. He said to Gopī: "The prince is now at the farmhouse. He will return in a few days."

The prince sat beneath the tree, concentrating his mind in prolonged contemplation on the matters of accumulated kalpas, surveying from the thirty-three heavens above down to the sixteen hells below, and finding nothing satisfactory. He saw in the fields the plowmen turning up worms from the soil: some were injured, some were killed, and crows followed behind to eat them. The prince sighed: "To be born upon the earth and after death to enter the hells: is that not suffering! I cannot long remain in this world." He then mounted his horse and departed.

After traveling more than ten li, he saw a man named Pañcika. Pañcika was a great spirit among the ghosts and spirits, fierce and violent. In his left hand he held a bow, in his right hand an arrow, and at his waist he wore a sharp sword. He stood blocking the road. Where Pañcika stood there were three paths: the first, the path of heaven; the second, the path of humans; the third, the path of the hells and evil beings. The prince saw him from afar and was displeased at heart, but rode his horse straight toward him. Pañcika was then seized with fear and trembling. He set down his sword, bow, and arrows, and stepped aside from the road. The prince asked: "Which path should I take?" Pañcika then showed him the path of heaven: "This path you may take."

After the prince had traveled several tens of li, he encountered a hunter on the road. The prince said: "I wish to borrow something from you. Would that be possible?" The hunter said: "What you seek can be had." The prince said: "I wish to have your deerskin." The hunter immediately gave the skin to the prince, and the prince gave him precious objects in return.

After the prince had traveled several more tens of li, he halted his horse and dismounted. He said to Chandaka: "You must return from here." Chandaka said: "I follow the great prince. I cannot go back." The prince said: "Go back and convey my farewell to the great king and to my wife. Tell them I wish to enter the mountains to practice the path, and I shall never return in this life."

The prince took the jeweled crown from his head and the precious garments from his body, and handed them to Chandaka. Chandaka received them, weeping. The white horse knelt down on its front knees, shedding tears, and licked the prince's feet. Chandaka led the horse on foot back the way they had come. Chandaka was weeping, and the white horse was also weeping. From behind they watched the prince, who took the deerskin and put it on, wishing to change his garments.

The wife had been watching daily for the prince to return. Instead she saw the horse return empty. She wept and threw herself from the palace, then rushed to embrace the horse's neck. She said to Chandaka: "Where is the prince?" Chandaka said: "The prince sends farewell to the great king and to his wife. He said: 'I am entering the mountains to practice the path, and I shall never return in this life.'" Gopī said: "How wretched my fate! To lose my husband! Where shall I seek him? My husband, my husband! Are you in heaven, on earth, or among humans? I shall go and search for him!" She said to the white horse: "The prince went out together with you. How could you return empty?" All the attendants were deeply moved.

The king, hearing that the prince had gone, wept with tears streaming down his face. He said to Gopī: "For those born upon the earth, all must return to death. My son studies the path to cross beyond the world. Is that not good?" He wished thereby to soothe Gopī's heart. But the king too thought of the prince without ceasing. The king then summoned the wise and learned men of the kingdom, obtaining several thousand. From these several thousand, the king selected several hundred. From the several hundred, he selected several tens. From the several tens, he selected five. The king called these five and asked them: "You spend your days and nights at home, holding your sons and embracing your grandchildren. Are you truly happy? I have but one son, who has never gone out on an excursion and knows nothing of the world. Suddenly he has left me and gone far away into the famous mountains, passing through deep forests, crossing valleys, enduring cold, heat, hunger, and thirst. Who will know of this? Should there be tigers, wolves, fierce beasts, or any danger, who will be there? Now, each of you five shall send one son to pursue my son. If they find him, they shall stay and attend him. If my son never returns in this life, and any of you five abandon my son and leave along the way, I shall destroy your entire families." The five men then each sent one son to pursue the prince, and they found him in the mountains and attended him.

After several years, the prince still did not ask the five men where they had come from. The prince always went to the most remote and hidden places in the forest. The five men found it burdensome and painful, and said to one another: "This crown prince is not practicing the path at all. He is mad and deluded. He does not choose where he walks. We five cannot follow him. If we return, the king will destroy our families. It is better to stay here." All five said: "Agreed." The place where the five men stopped was beside a great river, and above the river there were fruits and rare plants of various kinds, growing in all seasons, giving them food, so they did not go hungry. The five men stayed behind, and the prince did not ask about them either. The prince then went deep into the mountains, to a place where no one dwelt. He gathered tall grass from high ground and sat upright beneath a tree. With single-minded resolve he said to himself: "Today, even if my flesh, bones, sinews, and marrow all wither and rot, I shall not rise from this seat until I have attained Buddhahood."

The prince then attained the first dhyāna, then the second dhyāna, then the third dhyāna, then the fourth dhyāna. During the first watch of the night, he attained the first abhijñā, knowing by himself where he had been born in all his countless past lives. During the second watch, he attained the second abhijñā, the divine eye, seeing clearly and without limit, knowing the paths of good and evil that beings follow after birth and death. Toward dawn, he attained Buddhahood.

The Buddha thought to himself: "I have now attained Buddhahood! It is difficult to attain, difficult to know, and difficult to comprehend."

Having attained the Buddha path, he went to the nāga's waters. The nāga was named Mucalinda. Beside Mucalinda's waters there was a tree. The Buddha sat upright and thought to himself: "Long ago, in the time of countless kalpas, Dīpaṃkara Buddha said that I would become Śākyamuni Buddha. Today I have indeed attained Buddhahood! From the time of countless kalpas past, I have sought Buddhahood, and only now have I attained it! From the time of countless kalpas past, all that I have given, fulfilling the six pāramitās: my merit has not been forgotten, and today I have fully attained it." As the Buddha thought this, he entered the pāramitā of dhyāna.

As the Buddha sat in meditation beneath the tree by the water, his radiance entered the water and illuminated the nāga's dwelling place. The nāga, seeing the Buddha's light, was greatly startled, and his scales stood on end. Mucalinda had previously witnessed three Buddhas: the first named Krakucchanda Buddha, the second named Kanakamuni Buddha, and the third named Kāśyapa Buddha. All had sat beneath a tree, and their radiance had entered the water and illuminated the nāga's dwelling. Seeing the Buddha's radiance as before, the nāga thought: "After three Buddhas' radiance appeared in the world, there should be no further Buddha." The nāga was then greatly overjoyed. He emerged from the water, looked about to the left and right, and saw the Buddha sitting beneath the tree, bearing the thirty-two marks, of pure golden color, with a countenance as radiant as the sun and moon. The Buddha's thirty-two marks, seen from afar, were like a tree covered in flowers. Mucalinda then approached the Buddha and circumambulated him seven times. The nāga had seven heads, and he spread them to shelter the Buddha above. The nāga emerged from the water and attended the Buddha. Then wind and rain came for seven days. The Buddha sat in meditation for seven days without moving or stirring, without breathing in or out. The Buddha's mind was at ease beyond measure. After seven days, the wind and rain ceased.

Because the Buddha had just attained the path, he was so overjoyed that he did not eat for seven days. The nāga, seeing the Buddha's joy, attended the Buddha and also did not eat for seven days. After the seven days were complete, the Buddha became aware of himself. The nāga then transformed into a young brāhmaṇa, knelt with palms joined, and asked the Buddha: "Are you not cold? Are you not hot? Have you not been troubled by insects, moths, or mosquitoes?" The Buddha answered: "The sūtras say: 'To be in a secluded place is bliss.' What I heard of old, I have now all experienced for myself; this is bliss. To dwell in the world and not be troubled by others is also bliss. To not trouble the beings of the world, including even the smallest flying and crawling creatures, is also bliss. To have crossed over and no longer be a being of the world or a deva is also bliss. To be free of anger, resentment, lust, and excess is also bliss. To have attained the path of the Buddha's nirvāṇa in this world is also bliss."

The nāga said to the Buddha: "From this day forward, I take refuge in the Buddha and take refuge in the Dharma."

The Buddha said to the nāga: "In the future there will be many arhats and a sangha of bhikṣus. You should also take refuge in them."

Among the animals, Mucalinda was the first to take refuge in the Buddha. The Buddha's supernatural powers were penetrating and complete. The devas all assembled and came before him to pay homage.

The Buddha dwelt in a quiet and secluded place, contemplating deeply: "With compassion for all under heaven and pity for all beings, I wish to teach them. Whom should I teach first? The king sent five men to attend me, but the five men could not keep up with me and are now by the river. I should teach them first." The Buddha then returned along his former path. The five men, seeing the Buddha approach from afar and not knowing who he was, said to one another: "When this person arrives, we must not make obeisance to him and must not speak to him." All five said: "Agreed." The Buddha heard from afar what the five men had said.

When the Buddha arrived, all five were seized with awe and made obeisance before him. The Buddha said: "You five, why do you lack steadfast resolve? Just now you agreed among yourselves: 'When this person arrives, we must not make obeisance.' Why then do you make obeisance?" The five men dared not reply. The Buddha took the five men with him and they traveled for several days. The Buddha then touched the heads of the five men with his hand, and their hair and beards fell away, and all became śramaṇas.

There were three ascetics, each teaching his own disciples. One ascetic taught five hundred disciples; one taught three hundred disciples; and one taught two hundred disciples, making one thousand in all.

The Buddha, together with the five śramaṇas, went to the dwelling place of the three ascetics. All the disciples were greatly overjoyed and followed the Buddha. The Buddha led all the disciples and traveled through the various countries. When they arrived at the city gates, the bells and drums sounded of their own accord, and the zithers and harps played by themselves. The sick were healed, the old became young again, the blind could see, the deaf could hear, the hunchbacked could stand straight, and the lame could walk. All the animals cried out together in harmony. The devas flew down and scattered flowers, playing music above.

The Buddha's radiance illuminated countless heavens. Under his guidance were three thousand suns and moons and twelve thousand heaven-and-earth realms. From first to last, he taught and guided disciples, instructing countless billions of people, all of whom attained the path and crossed beyond the world.
