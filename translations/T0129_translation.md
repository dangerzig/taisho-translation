# 佛說三摩竭經
## Sūtra of Sumagadhā Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0129

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhu Lüyan (竺律炎)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, five hundred bodhisattvas, emperors, common people, devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits beyond number. At that time, there was a king of the land of Nandā named Fenpiṭan who did not believe in the Buddha-Dharma but was devoted only to heterodox ways. Each day in his palace he fed more than ten thousand nirgrantha ascetics. The king of Nandā was always arrogant and conceited, considering himself to have wisdom without equal. He bound his belly with iron plates, constantly fearing that his wisdom would burst forth sideways from his belly.

The king wished to find a wife for his crown prince. He asked his ministers on the left and right: "Is there anyone in the world whose wisdom equals mine? If there is, I wish to take his daughter as a wife for my son." The ministers received the king's command and went throughout the country to search, but found none to equal the king. Knowing that there was no one in their own country, they dispatched envoys to travel to other countries to seek the daughter of a wise man. The envoys received the king's command and traveled to the country of Śrāvastī. The envoys asked the people of the country: "Is there in this country a good and virtuous person who follows the path?"

The people answered: "There is."

The envoys said: "What is their name?"

The people replied: "His name is the Buddha."

The envoys asked: "Does the Buddha have a daughter?"

The people said: "The Buddha is a religious practitioner. He has no daughter."

The envoys asked: "Who else is there?"

They answered: "There is a person named Anāthapiṇḍada, who is greatly virtuous and follows the path. He has a fine daughter, the foremost in the country."

The envoys asked: "In what way is she the foremost?"

The people of the country said: "He once joined with Prince Jeta in purchasing a garden of eighty hectares to offer to the Buddha. He further had elephants carry and transport hundreds of millions of gold coins to pay for the garden. He does not covet precious treasures but only thinks of doing good."

The envoys, hearing the people's words, were greatly delighted, knowing they had found what they sought. They returned to their own country and reported to the king: "In the country of Śrāvastī there is a virtuous and good man who follows the path, named Anāthapiṇḍada." The king of Nandā, hearing the envoys' report, wrote a letter and immediately dispatched the crown prince together with ministers and officials, who set forth in a magnificent procession loaded with precious treasures, all traveling together to the country of Śrāvastī. The prince was lodged outside the city, and the envoys entered the city and went to Anāthapiṇḍada's residence. The gatekeeper went in and announced to Anāthapiṇḍada: "There are envoys outside."

Anāthapiṇḍada went out himself to meet them at the gate. Seeing that the envoys were dark and ugly, like ghosts, Anāthapiṇḍada exclaimed in great alarm: "What manner of people are you?" They answered: "We are envoys of the king of Nandā."

Anāthapiṇḍada said: "What do you seek in coming here?"

The envoys said: "We have come to convey our king's command. Though the king of Nandā has not met you in person, he admires and respects you from afar. People who travel back and forth constantly sing the praises of your boundless merit. Hearing that you are virtuous and good and follow the path, and that you have a daughter, he has come to seek your daughter's hand for the crown prince. The king has also sent a letter for your perusal."

Anāthapiṇḍada wanted to curse them but restrained himself and said nothing. He invited them to sit down, and they exchanged greetings and conversed. The envoys showed him the letter. Anāthapiṇḍada read the letter through, then replied to the envoys: "I have a great person to whom I must report this. If he gives permission, I shall return and give you word." He asked the envoys to remain seated, and Anāthapiṇḍada went to where the Buddha was. He bowed before the Buddha, then knelt with palms joined and said: "The king of Nandā has now sent envoys to my home. The envoys are dark in appearance, like ghosts. They say they wish to seek the hand of my daughter Sumagadhā for the king's crown prince. What should I do?"

The Buddha said: "Give her to them."

Anāthapiṇḍada said: "The envoys are as dark as ghosts. What must the crown prince look like? Moreover, I have heard from the Buddha that the king of Nandā serves only nirgrantha ascetics, who go naked without any clothing and are ugly and dark in appearance. Will they not terrify my daughter?"

The Buddha said: "No. Give her to them. You should know this karmic connection: Sumagadhā is destined to liberate eighty thousand people in the land of the naked ascetics."

Anāthapiṇḍada did not dare question the Buddha further, though his heart was filled with resentment. He returned home and said to the envoys: "You have come bearing the king's command from afar to seek my daughter. Very good, I shall give her to you."

The envoys, hearing Anāthapiṇḍada's words, returned outside the city to where the prince was. They accompanied the prince to Anāthapiṇḍada's residence. The prince descended from his carriage and presented gold, silver, and betrothal gifts to Anāthapiṇḍada. They invited guests, and for seven days there was feasting and celebration. When it was over, Anāthapiṇḍada sent off Sumagadhā, with servants, garments, and precious treasures beyond number. The prince then took Sumagadhā and returned to his own country.

At that time, the king of Nandā, seeing his son's wife arrive, was greatly delighted. He invited his teacher, the nirgrantha Jñātaputra, along with twelve thousand disciples, all into the palace for a meal. The king of Nandā, his queen, and the prince all served the food. They then called Sumagadhā to come out, intending to have her serve the food and pay respects to the teachers together with them. When Sumagadhā reached the third gate and saw from afar that all the nirgranthas were seated naked, without any clothing, she was greatly shocked, thinking they were no different from dogs or livestock. She covered her face with both hands and spat at them from afar, then returned to her chamber and refused to come out again.

All the nirgranthas were furious at Sumagadhā and told the king: "From where did you get this inauspicious and misleading person in the royal palace? Send her away at once! If you do not, she will bring ruin to your kingdom." The nirgranthas refused to continue eating and were about to leave. The king apologized to the teachers: "I shall send her away for the great teachers' sake. Tomorrow she will no longer appear in the palace." The nirgranthas then finished their meal and departed.

That very day, the prince went to Sumagadhā's quarters, but Sumagadhā, filled with anger, ordered her maid to close the door. This went on for four or five days, and the prince did not dare go again. The queen asked the prince: "Why do you not go?" The prince remained silent and said nothing. The queen understood, and went herself to Sumagadhā's quarters: "I had my son marry you, and you should now serve my son. Why do you humiliate him?"

Sumagadhā replied: "The teachers your son serves, as well as the people of this country, are all no different from dogs and livestock."

The queen, hearing this, was greatly ashamed. She returned and told the king: "Your Majesty! You consider your wisdom to be without equal in the land, and no one in the country can match your will. Yet you sent ministers eight thousand li to find a wife! Now this wife fears nothing and shows no deference. She humiliates my son and curses me to my face, comparing us to dogs and livestock!"

The king, hearing the queen's words, went himself to Sumagadhā's quarters. But Sumagadhā was greatly proud and disdainful and would not come out to pay respects to the king. The king called out to Sumagadhā from a distance: "I traveled eight thousand li to marry you, because of your virtue. The other day you insulted my teachers, and now you curse the queen and the prince to their faces. How can this be acceptable?"

Sumagadhā replied: "Indeed so! Great king, your teachers, as well as the queen, the prince, and all the people of this country, are no different from dogs and livestock."

The king exclaimed in shock: "This little girl! She insults me like this! I feared my wisdom would burst from my belly and so bound it with iron plates. I feed ten thousand religious practitioners each day. Who can equal me? And yet you curse me to my face?"

Sumagadhā said: "The teachers served by the king and the people of this country are always naked, viewing each other without clothing. What kind of path can they have? Even if they had a path, it would still not be worthy of honor, much less having no path at all! Great king! Though you feed more than ten thousand of this sort every day, they are all the kind of people I do not respect, whom I would constantly spit upon and despise."

At that time, the king of Nandā was at a complete loss. He thought to himself: "With whom can I discuss this matter?" He went to the nirgrantha Jñātaputra and bowed before his teacher, saying: "I took a wife from the country of Śrāvastī. She has been outrageous, first insulting the great teacher, and now proudly and disdainfully cursing me, the queen, and the prince, comparing us to dogs and livestock. Though she is my daughter-in-law, she does not treat me with the courtesy due to a parent-in-law. What should I do?"

The teacher told the king: "Go back and ask her: 'Those whom the people of your country serve, how are they superior to those whom the people of my country serve?' Say just those words. Go, but take care not to become angry. Question her gently, and she will surely have something to say."

The king received the instruction and, as the teacher had said, went to Sumagadhā and asked: "Those whom the people of your country serve, in what way are they superior to those whom the people of my country serve?"

Sumagadhā said: "Those whom the people of my country serve are the most honored. Men and women all wear clothing, with separate paths for the noble and the lowly, so that they do not see each other's bodies. There is one called the Buddha, the most exalted of all, who teaches and guides many thousands of millions of people, enabling them all to cross over the world and attain the path of nirvāṇa. He enters fire without being burned and enters water without drowning. He can govern the suns and moons of three thousand great thousand worlds, twelve thousand billion heavens and earths. He transforms and enters where there are no gaps, and exits where there are no openings. He knows past, future, and present affairs. His body has the thirty-two marks of a great man and the eighty minor characteristics. His path-virtue is fully penetrating. Devas, emperors, kings, common people, and all manner of beings come to bow their heads in audience with him."

The king, hearing Sumagadhā's words, leapt with great joy and delight.

At that time, the king of Nandā said to Sumagadhā: "This Buddha whom you serve, can he be seen?"

Sumagadhā said: "I am able to invite him from afar on the great king's behalf, and he can be brought here instantly."

The king said: "Excellent! But we are eight thousand li apart. How can the Buddha be invited?"

Sumagadhā said: "The king need not send anyone to invite him. You need only face in his direction with a sincere mind and burn incense to invite him. The Buddha's supernatural penetration illuminates the thoughts in people's minds. The king, the queen, and the prince should all follow behind me." Sumagadhā then went up to a high terrace, properly arranged her robes, and facing the country of Śrāvastī, knelt and burned incense. Placing her head upon the ground in homage, she said: "Now the king of Nandā does not know there is a Buddha in the world. For the sake of all the people, out of compassion for their toil and suffering, I beseech the Buddha tomorrow morning to condescend, together with the assembly of bhikṣus, to come to where the king of Nandā is for a meal." As soon as she finished speaking, the fragrant smoke reached the place where the Buddha was, circled the Buddha three times, and above the Buddha was transformed into a canopy of incense.

At that time, the Buddha was just then teaching the Dharma to numberless thousands of people. Ānanda came forward, knelt with palms joined, and said to the Buddha: "What manner of spiritual response is this? We beseech the Buddha to explain its meaning."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The king of Nandā and Sumagadhā wish to invite the Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus for a meal tomorrow morning. Sumagadhā has utmost sincerity. She wishes to cause all the people of the land of Nandā to abandon wrong views and turn toward the right path. The incense has come here to invite the Buddha." The Buddha then told Mahāmaudgalyāyana to instruct the assembly of bhikṣus that tomorrow morning they should go to where Sumagadhā is in the land of Nandā for the meal. Mahāmaudgalyāyana received the Buddha's instruction and announced to the bhikṣus: "Tomorrow you should accept the invitation. Do not go anywhere else."

Sumagadhā then had the king, the queen, the prince, and all the palace women observe a purificatory fast, burn incense, lay out seats, and prepare the meal in full. Knowing the Buddha would come, Sumagadhā, together with the king, the queen, the prince, the palace women, and all the nirgranthas, stood in the central courtyard. Sumagadhā told the king, the queen, and the prince: "Follow behind me. The arhats will arrive first, and the Buddha will come last. Do not be frightened. Do as I do." Sumagadhā then again knelt and burned incense, saying: "The meal is prepared. We beseech the Buddha to come at the proper time."

The Buddha knew what was in Sumagadhā's mind and told the bhikṣus: "Today we shall go to the land of Nandā for the meal. Each of you, through the supernatural transformations of the path, may do as you freely wish." The bhikṣus received the instruction. Some transformed into dragons, tigers, phoenixes, or peacocks. Others became cattle, pigeons, doves, or hundreds of different birds and beasts. They created jeweled canopies, and all the members of the Saṃgha sat within them, each one different from the others. At that time, the Buddha himself sat in a lion-canopy. Together with the twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus, five hundred bodhisattvas, devas, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas, they traveled through empty space by supernatural flight.

When they reached the land of Nandā, the Buddha emitted a radiant light and the heavens and earth trembled greatly. The arhats and bodhisattvas each performed transformations and descended first. The people of the land of Nandā, seeing these transformations, were all greatly alarmed. The king asked Sumagadhā: "Is that the Buddha?" She answered: "No." Sumagadhā told the king: "Do not be afraid. These are all disciples. The Buddha will come last. His body has the thirty-two marks and eighty minor characteristics."

In a moment, the Buddha descended from above together with all the others. Śakra, Brahmā, and the Four Heavenly Kings went before him as guides, while devas played zithers and performed music and dance to delight the Buddha. Sumagadhā, together with the king, the queen, and the prince, took flowers and incense and went to welcome the Buddha. They bowed before the Buddha and then escorted him into the palace, where he took his seat. The bodhisattvas and arhats all sat before him in proper order of rank. Sumagadhā then had the king, the queen, and the prince come forward to pour the water for washing hands, and then served the meal. The people of the country who came to see were extremely numerous. The king ordered a minister to close the palace gates. When the people saw the king closing the gates, they became furious and each took up axes, intending to break down the gates.

The Buddha, knowing this from afar, said: "In all my teaching and transforming, I wish to cause all beings to do good. Now the people of the land of Nandā have all come to observe, yet the king has closed the palace gates." The Buddha then, wishing to let all people see him, caused the palace gates and walls to transform entirely into crystal, so that those inside and outside could see each other. The people of the land of Nandā, seeing the Buddha and all the bodhisattvas and arhats, were all greatly delighted.

At that time, the Buddha had an arhat named Piṇḍola who was then sitting on a mountain and had forgotten about going to the land of Nandā. Piṇḍola had been sitting for a long time and was just about to sew his patchwork robe with a needle. He stuck the needle into the ground, and the thread connected to the robe. By this time the Buddha had already taken his seat in the palace of the king of Nandā. Piṇḍola then flew by supernatural power to the land of Nandā, and the mountain followed behind him. At that time, there was a pregnant woman in the country who saw the mountain approaching, entirely black, and feared it would fall upon her. She was so terrified that she miscarried.

The Buddha, knowing this from afar, immediately sent Mahāmaudgalyāyana to fly by supernatural power and meet him with the question: "Piṇḍola! What is that behind you?" Piṇḍola turned around and saw the mountain. With his hand he grasped the mountain and hurled it back to its original place, eight thousand li away.

Piṇḍola then arrived and bowed before the Buddha, then took his seat.

The Buddha told Piṇḍola: "I teach the people of the world, wishing to enable them all to cross over the world. Now you have not only missed the appointed time but have also caused the death of one person. A human life is of the utmost weight. This is not acceptable in my path. From today onward you may no longer dine with me or attend the assemblies. You shall remain behind: only when Maitreya Buddha appears in the world may you enter parinirvāṇa."

Piṇḍola, hearing the Buddha's words, was silent with sorrow and reproached himself. When the meal was finished, he rose and bowed before the Buddha, took his leave of the bodhisattvas and arhats, and entered the mountains.

At that time, the teacher of the king of Nandā, the nirgrantha Jñātaputra, said to the Buddha: "Would you be willing to have a contest of spiritual powers? Whoever loses shall be thrown into a well."

The Buddha said: "Very well! No need for many words. Whoever fails after three questions shall be thrown into a well."

Jñātaputra said: "Very well!"

The Buddha then asked: "When you chant your scriptures, in what posture do you do so?"

Jñātaputra said: "When I chant my scriptures, I crawl on all fours."

"Crawling on all fours is the way of a dog. Dogs crawl on all fours."

Jñātaputra was defeated by the Buddha. His disciples were all furious. Some said this was the proper verdict. They all turned their anger upon their teacher and seized him, intending to throw him into the well. The teacher was greatly terrified and braced himself against the ground with both hands, refusing to go in. The Buddha said: "Let him be."

At that time, a great fire spontaneously arose in the central courtyard, its flames reaching up to the seventh Brahmā heaven. Within the fire there spontaneously appeared a thousand-petaled lotus, and upon the lotus sat five hundred Brahmā devas, all with palms joined and kneeling, who asked the Buddha: "For feeding what kind of person does one gain the most merit? For feeding what kind of person does one gain the least?"

The Buddha answered the Brahmā devas: "It is like scattering five kinds of grain into a fire. Will they grow?" The Brahmā devas said: "They will not."

The Buddha said: "What the king of Nandā has fed in the past, all these nirgranthas, is like casting five kinds of grain into fire: they will never grow again. But by feeding the Buddha, the bodhisattvas, and the arhats today, the merit gained is immeasurable. It is like a person who has good land and good seeds, and the heavens send timely rain: why worry about not growing? The Buddha is the field of merit for all people. Whatever anyone sows, they will certainly obtain their wish. Foolish people delight in teaching others to follow heterodox paths. When such people die, they will all fall into the Mountain Hell, where the suffering is extreme and regret comes too late. Before the former ones have emerged, the latter ones are again taught to do the same. The people of the world are foolish and only deceive one another. For this reason they do not know the true path. If there is a wise person, one should study the right path. That path involves no birth, no aging, no illness, and no death. This is the great path of nirvāṇa. In the world there are ninety-six kinds of paths, but none can match the path of the Buddha."

The Buddha said: "If one were to use the branches of all the trees and the feathers of all the birds in a single world to make pens and write down the Buddha's sūtras, all the branches and feathers could be exhausted, but the Buddha's wisdom could not be exhausted. If one were to grind Mount Sumeru into ink and dip pens into the waters of the four seas, both the ink of Mount Sumeru and the waters of the four seas could be exhausted, but the Buddha's wisdom could never be exhausted."

The five hundred Brahmā devas, hearing the Buddha's words, cried out in unison: "Excellent! Truly it is as the Buddha says." Thereupon the five hundred Brahmā devas suddenly vanished.

At that time, the king of Nandā's retinue of three hundred, twelve hundred palace women, and five hundred ministers, seeing the Buddha's transformations, all leapt with joyful delight and generated the mind of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi. Two thousand brāhmaṇas then shaved off their hair and all became bhikṣus, immediately attaining arhatship. Twelve thousand nirgranthas were all immediately liberated: among them some attained the fruit of stream-entry, some attained the fruit of once-returning, and some attained the fruit of non-returning. Furthermore, sixty-four thousand common people of the country all developed faith in the Buddha-Dharma, received the five precepts, and all became upāsakas.

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, he and the bodhisattvas and arhats all manifested their supernatural powers and flew away. At that time, the king of Nandā, the queen, the prince, the ministers, and the people all rejoiced greatly. They all placed their heads upon the ground and from afar bowed in homage to the Buddha.
