# 大乘破有論
## Mahāyāna Treatise on Refuting Existence

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1574

Composed by Nāgārjuna (龍樹). Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護).

---

Homage to all buddhas! All who are wise should truly understand all dharmas as they really are.

What does this mean? It means that all natures arise from the absence of nature, and yet they do not arise from the absence of nature either. If all natures had arising, those natures would be permanent. But such natures have no reality, like flowers in the sky. One should know that all dharmas are equal to empty space. The arising of those dharmas is also equal to emptiness. All conditioned dharmas are entirely like empty space; being without reality, how could they exist? Dharmas are without cause and also without effect. There is no intrinsic nature of karma to be found. In all of this, nothing has reality. Because there is no worldly, there is no supramundane. All is unborn and without inherent nature. How then could any dharma have arising?

In the world, loved ones, fathers, sons, and kinfolk, though they appear to be born, have no reality, because they are not produced from a previous life, nor does the present life possess their true characteristics. In the world, no real meaning can operate. It is like seeing reflections within the moon. The world has no reality and arises from conceptual discrimination. From this discrimination, a discriminating mind arises. With this mind as cause, a body comes to be. Therefore a body acts within the world.

Being constituted by the aggregates, it is called a body. All aggregates are empty and without inherent nature. The aggregates having no inherent nature, there is also no mind. Because there is no mind, therefore there is no body. One should know that inherent nature is free from all discrimination. If there is no mind, there are also no dharmas. If there is no body, there are also no elements.

What is explained herein is the path of nonduality. What is explained here is truthful speech. Herein, all is free from objects of perception. What is explained herein is free from objects of perception. What is done herein is free from objects of perception. What is attained herein is free from objects of perception.

If one constantly practices all the dharmas of giving, morality, patience, vigor, meditation, and wisdom in this way, in a short time one will be able to attain unsurpassed bodhi. With wisdom and skillful means, one abides in the true nature of reality, gives rise to compassionate conduct, and broadly liberates sentient beings. Although there appears to be something attained, the nature of omniscience cannot be said to be attained.

All those dharmas are mere names. All merely abide within the realm of conceptual thought. Before us, no real distinctions are produced. Dharmas produced through distinction have no existence. Originally, all those dharmas have no names. They are merely indicated through conventional designations. One should know that all dharmas are without real substance, and all arise from conceptual discrimination.

If within this there is no discrimination, then it is the same as empty space, free from all discrimination. When it is said that the eye can see forms, this statement is true speech. Those in the world who have minds of wrong attachment grasp this teaching as if it operated in reality. All those dharmas appear through the gathering of their kinds. One should know that this teaching is the Buddha's teaching.

Therefore one should know: the meaning herein is that the eye does not see form, and so on up to the mind not knowing dharmas. If one knows thus, one is a person of wisdom and can penetrate the ultimate truth. This is called the supreme reality. I have now briefly explained this in accordance with the sūtras.
