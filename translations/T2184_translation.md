# Newly Compiled General Catalog of the Teachings of All Schools

## *Xīnbiān Zhūzōng Jiàozàng Zǒnglù* (新編諸宗教藏總錄)

Taishō 2184

Compiled by Ŭich'ŏn (Yìtiān, 義天) of Koryŏ. A catalog of commentarial literature.

*Note: This is the catalog compiled by the Korean monk-prince Ŭich'ŏn (1055-1101), fourth son of King Munjong of Koryŏ. Ŭich'ŏn traveled to Song China in 1085, where he studied Tiantai, Huayan, Vinaya, and Chan under leading masters. Modeled on Zhìshēng's Kaiyuan Catalog of 730 CE (which cataloged sūtras and śāstras), Ŭich'ŏn's work catalogs the commentarial and exegetical literature (zhāngshū, 章疏) of all Buddhist schools, a category that the canonical catalogs had neglected. Compiled over twenty years, the catalog was organized in three fascicles by school. Only the prefaces and section headers survive in the extracted text; the actual catalog entries (lists of titles, authors, and fascicle counts) were apparently not captured in the XML extraction. The work was reprinted in 1693 (Genroku 6) in Japan.*

## Preface to the Dissemination of the Newly Compiled General Catalog of the Teachings

The pearl of the bright moon, the jade disk of blazing light: if shut away in a case and never used, what benefit do they provide?

I humbly note that Ŭich'ŏn (*Yìtiān*, 義天), surnamed Wang, was the fourth son of King Munjong Inhyo of Koryŏ. He left home to become a monk and was enfeoffed as the Usi (*Yòushì*, 祐世) Saṃgha-Superintendent. At the beginning of the Yuanyou era (1086), he entered China and submitted a memorial requesting transmission of the Xianshou (Huayan) teaching. He was granted an imperial decree of permission. He first went to Siming (Mingzhou), where he served under Master Cíbiàn and received the Tiantai contemplative teaching. He then proceeded to Língzhī, where he paid respects to the great master Yuánzhào and studied the Vinaya. From Jìnshuǐ Jìngyuán he heard the doctrinal vehicle of the Xianshou school. He also met the Chan master Yuánzhào Zōngběn and inquired about the great purport of the school's meaning. The details are as recorded in the monks' biographies. His fame resounded like thunder throughout China and shook the three kingdoms of Korea.

Five thousand and forty-eight fascicles were insufficient to satisfy even a single draught. He further searched out commentaries and exegetical records, and over twenty years, as he obtained them, he arranged them in sequence, producing three fascicles entitled the *Newly Compiled General Catalog of the Teachings of All Schools*. This catalog gradually spread to our country, where for over six hundred years it was stored in vain among the monastic libraries, serving only as a dwelling for bookworms. Recently, Master Inoue took it upon himself to carve the printing blocks, wishing to disseminate it throughout the world. I am gratified by his aspiration and have lent the strength of my own arm. How can one say that urging on those who are lax is not an urgent task?

Written in the Genroku year *guǐyǒu* (1693), on the sixteenth day of the fifth month, by the Biśāna (Vairocana) Orthodox-Lineage monk Byakugen Tenryū (*Báixuán Tiānlóng*, 白玄天龍) of the West Lake, at the Sangaku-zan Grass Hall.

## Original Preface to the Newly Compiled General Catalog of the Teachings of All Schools

After Emperor Ming of Han had his vision in a dream, the writings on leaves [from India] continued to arrive. In every age, translators disseminated [the teachings]; there was no dynasty without them. By the Zhenguan era (627-649), the sūtras and śāstras were largely complete. From that time onward, the teachings of the Western Sage poured forth irresistibly.

From Niè Dàozhēn and Dào'ān onward to the Vinaya Master Míngquán, each compiled catalogs, called the Jin Catalog, the Wei Catalog, and so forth. Yet regarding identical works with different translations, old titles and new names, many were confused by the divergent paths, and the authentic and the spurious became entangled. Sometimes one sūtra was made into two versions; sometimes a chapter was treated as a separate translation. Over forty catalographers produced a welter of confusion that persisted for a long time.

In the Kaiyuan era (713-741), there finally appeared a great dharma-master whose name was Zhìshēng (*Zhìshēng*, 智昇). He struck out the errors, deleted the duplications, and compiled everything into a single work called the *Kaiyuan Catalog of the Buddhist Canon* (*Kāiyuán Shìjiào Lù*, 開元釋教錄), in twenty fascicles. This was the most essential and refined. Critics have said that for a genealogy of the scriptural dharma, none surpasses Zhìshēng. For upholding the transmitted teaching, nothing is greater.

I have privately considered that although the sūtras and śāstras are complete, the commentaries and exegetical works have sometimes been neglected, and thus there is no means of dissemination. I have therefore emulated Master Zhìshēng's aspiration to protect the dharma, and taken it as my own responsibility to search out and visit the records of the teachings. Diligently and without rest, I have pursued this for nearly twenty years up to the present. Now, with the newly and previously compiled doctrinal works of all schools that I have obtained, I dare not keep them private. I have arranged and published them. In the future, whatever more I obtain, I also intend to record in sequence. If perhaps in time to come they can be arranged in cases and boxes alongside the canonical texts of the Tripiṭaka and transmitted for eternity, then my wish will be fulfilled.

In the eighth year of the reign of the thirteenth sovereign of Later Koryŏ, the *gēngwǔ* year (1090), on the eighth day of the eighth month. Preface by the śramaṇa Ŭich'ŏn, Transmitter of the Huayan Great Teaching in the Eastern Sea.

## Catalog of Texts Extant and Current in the Eastern Sea [Korea], Part One

[Fascicle 1]

Ŭich'ŏn, surnamed Wang, was the fourth son of King Munjong Inhyo of Koryŏ. He left home to become a monk and was enfeoffed as the Usi Saṃgha-Superintendent. At the beginning of the Yuanyou era, he entered China to inquire about the Way. He submitted a memorial requesting transmission of the Xianshou teaching. An imperial decree directed the Two Streets [Buddhist administration] to designate a suitable teacher. The Chan Master Chéng of Juéyán Monastery in the Eastern Capital was nominated, but Chéng recommended Jìngyuán of Qiantang in his place. At each monastery, he was welcomed and escorted with the courtesies due a traveler. He first arrived at the prefecture of Siming, where he studied under Míngzhì. He also studied under Cíbiàn. Following Jiàn, he received the Tiantai contemplative teaching. Next he stayed at Língzhī, where he paid respects to the great master Yuánzhào and received the Vinaya. From Jìnshuǐ Jìngyuán he inquired about the doctrinal vehicle of the Xianshou school. The Chan master Yuánzhào Zōngběn showed him the essential purport of the school.

### Colophons

Copied on the fourth day of the sixth month of the second year of Angen (*Ānyuán*, 安元), *bǐngshēn* [1176], from the original held at the Kegon-in of Ninna-ji, by Myōkū.

In the twenty-first year of Kan'ei (1644), in the seventh month, copied from the original at the Dharma-Drum Terrace of Toganoo, at the Zendō-in. The old copy was worm-eaten and had lost many characters. Some were inferred and supplied; others were left as lacunae where doubtful. Moreover, this catalog was originally incomplete. The commentaries currently extant in our country [Japan] that were omitted are quite numerous. I hope to await a discerning and widely-read scholar to collate and expand it for broad circulation in the world. This is my humble aspiration to protect the dharma.

The śramaṇa Unchō (*Yùnchàng*, 運敞) of the Anraku-ju-in.

Printed in the sixth year of Genroku, the *guǐyǒu* year (1693), by Inoue Chūbei of the Rakka (Kyoto) book trade.
