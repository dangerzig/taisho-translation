# 佛說枯樹經
## Sūtra on the Withered Tree, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0806

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

North of the city of Saṃkāśya stood a stūpa from the time of Kāśyapa Buddha, which had sunk down to the vajra-boundary of the lowest realm. The Buddha, having ascended to the heavens to preach the Dharma for his mother, now descended again to this city.

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "When the Buddha descended from the heavens, what served as a sign?"

The Buddha replied: "I shall now give manifest proof." He then extended his hand downward and drew out the stūpa of Kāśyapa Buddha. This stūpa was fashioned from jade and gemstones of many colors. The stūpa is called Saṃkāśya. The name of Kāśyapa Buddha's father was Śulibāru, meaning "compassion," and it was he who caused it to be built. It stands three hundred cubits in height and three and a half li on each side, originally formed from a combination of the seven true treasures, and therefore displays seven kinds of colors. The present city takes its name from this stūpa.

The reason Mahākāśyapa, Ānanda, and others compiled the sūtras at this place, rather than at a site of nirvāṇa, involved two considerations: first, such a site is not situated among human settlements; second, when the four assemblies of gods and humans arrived, all would be overcome by grief and their minds unsettled, and thus they would be unable to listen with calm attention.

When the compilation was complete, the various kings built a monastery at that site, called the Monastery of the Various Kings, located north of the stūpa. Later, King Kuṇḍa resented that the stūpa stood blocking the city gate. The king wished to destroy it, and ordered the people within the city to prepare iron tools, intending to remove it the following morning. At midnight, the stūpa moved of its own accord, passing over the city wall; the sound of its passage shook the entire kingdom. It came to rest in the forest among the trees, twenty li south of the city. The official who had advised the king to destroy the monastery was immediately put to death, along with five clans. The king, witnessing this marvel, recognized his error and reformed, becoming a lay devotee.

It has been approximately forty years since the stūpa moved. The Monastery of the Various Kings had fallen into decay; later, King Héng arose and restored it, and it stands to this day.

---

A few translator's notes on difficult passages:

- **Śulibāru**: A phonetic transcription of an unidentified Sanskrit name, glossed in the text as meaning "compassion" (慈, *maitrī*).
- **King Kuṇḍa** (群茶王): Phonetic transcription; identity uncertain.
- **King Héng** (橫王): May be a Chinese rendering of a foreign name or a Chinese personal name; identity uncertain.
- The compiler of the sūtras here is **Mahākāśyapa** (not Kāśyapa Buddha), paired with Ānanda as at the traditional First Council — though the text locates this activity at Saṃkāśya rather than the canonical Rājagṛha.
