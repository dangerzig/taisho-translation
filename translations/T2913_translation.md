# Sūtra of the Seven Daughters' Contemplation

## *Qīnǚ Guān Jīng* (七女觀經)

Taishō 2913

Translator unknown. An apocryphal text.

Thus have I heard. At one time the Buddha spoke the Sūtra of the Seven Daughters' Contemplation.

Long ago, King Míngyuán (*Míngyuán Wáng*, 冥緣王) had seven daughters who awakened to the impermanence of the world. They did not wear silk garments or finery. They did not wear rings or bracelets on their fingers or arms. They did not anoint their bodies with fragrant oils. They observed the three long fasting months of the year and the six monthly fast days. The sisters went together to their father the king's palace and prostrated before him, saying: "We daughters, because of sins accumulated in past lives, lack merit and now find ourselves in female bodies, confined within the deep palace where our eyes can see nothing. We beg the king to release us daughters to go outside the city to contemplate the world."

The king said to his daughters: "I am sovereign of all under heaven, lord of ten thousand foundations. I have garden parks, bathing pools, flower terraces, and pavilions. These too can be contemplated. What need is there to go outside the city?"

The daughters replied to the king: "Although there are garden parks, bathing pools, flower terraces, and pavilions that can please the ear, these are not the places where your daughters wish to contemplate." The king then permitted them.

They went out through the eastern gate and came to the charnel ground. There they saw a dead body, bloated and putrid, trampled by foxes and wolves, its limbs scattered in different places. The sisters went together and circumambulated the corpse three times, weeping and sobbing: "Our female bodies will one day be just like this." The seven sisters each spoke a verse:

### The first daughter spoke this verse:

> One treasures the body as if it were a fine jewel,
> Walking with the elegant bearing of a goose.
> But cast dead in the charnel ground,
> Birds and beasts compete to gnaw upon it.
> All things are like this;
> What use is distinction in the world?

### The second daughter spoke this verse:

> Like a single dwelling from which the person departs,
> The house is left without light.
> When the body dies it is like a spirit departing;
> Only then does one know this is impermanent.
> Now gazing upon the dead corpse,
> Suddenly it is met by jackals and wolves.
> All things are like this;
> How could anything endure?

### The third daughter spoke this verse:

> Like a horse drawing a carriage:
> When the carriage breaks, the horse departs.
> When the four great elements disperse,
> Where does the spirit-soul then dwell?
> When the four elements first assembled,
> From where did they come?
> Only then does one know this is impermanent;
> Only merit can be relied upon.

### The fourth daughter spoke this verse:

> Like a fine city with walls,
> Where many people dwell in peace.
> Before their desires are satisfied,
> How suddenly it is all emptiness!
> Human life is like plants and trees:
> Flourishing in spring, withering in winter.
> Only the cultivation of merit
> And wisdom follow the body.

### The fifth daughter spoke this verse:

> One who does not cultivate merit
> Is like crossing a river by boat.
> When the four elements part company,
> It is like the boat leaving the shore.
> When earth is settled and wind does not stir,
> When fire is faint and water returns to its source,
> The body is not the spirit's lasting home;
> How could there be a lasting place?

### The sixth daughter spoke this verse:

> Like a person who dresses in fine array,
> Wearing various silks and satins,
> Freely enjoying glory for a time,
> So that others admire and attend.
> But lying stiff and unmoving,
> Where then is the spirit?
> Gazing upon the corpse in the charnel ground,
> One should change one's ways for the future.

### The seventh daughter spoke this verse:

> Like a sparrow in a jar,
> With gauze covering its mouth.
> When the gauze tears, the sparrow flies away;
> The spirit follows where its deeds lead.
> When the four elements are joined together,
> It is like the sparrow within the jar.
> Only the cultivation of good merit
> And wisdom follow the body.

Then Śakra (*Dìshì*, 帝釋), king of the fourth Trāyastriṃśa (*Dāolì*, 忉利) heaven, descended from heaven and came before the daughters. He asked: "What do you seek? I will grant it to you."

The daughters were silent for a long time and did not answer. He asked again: "What do you seek? I will grant it to you."

One daughter said: "I beg to be without root, without beard, without branch, and without leaf, yet to have life arising within. This would be bliss."

Another daughter wished: "May I be forever without the five desires, forever dwelling in stillness and tranquility, my ears feasting on the sound of the dharma, my eyes beholding the holy teachings. This would be bliss."

Another daughter wished: "In the deep darkness, like empty space, I aspire to profound awakening and to forever behold the holy countenance. This would be bliss."

The heavenly lord said to the daughters: "Cease, cease! What you wish for is the realm of no-thought. I cannot grant it. The king among the sun and moon would have you attain it."

Thereupon all seven daughters attained the fruit of the path.
