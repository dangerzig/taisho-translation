# 佛說正恭敬經
## Sūtra on Right Reverence, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1496

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Buddhaśānta (佛陀扇多).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the kingdom of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of the Garden of the Benefactor of Orphans, together with two hundred fifty śrāvaka-bhikṣus and five hundred bodhisattvas. All were seeds of the Tathāgata, who by expedient means traversed the six destinies to assist the Buddha in spreading his transformative teaching, who knew the faculties of sentient beings and possessed sovereign spiritual power, and who revealed the Tathāgata's skillful and secret teaching. Their names were: Bodhisattva Arising-Doubt, Bodhisattva Jewel-Virtue, Bodhisattva Radiance-King, Bodhisattva Wisdom-Ascent, Bodhisattva Virtue-Attainment, Bodhisattva Siddhartha, Bodhisattva Fearless, Bodhisattva Awakening-Chief, Bodhisattva Wealth-Chief, Bodhisattva Jewel-Chief, Bodhisattva Virtue-Chief, Bodhisattva Eye-Chief, Bodhisattva Vigor-Chief, Bodhisattva Dharma-Chief, Bodhisattva Wisdom-Chief, and Bodhisattva Worthy-Chief. Together with five hundred such bodhisattva-mahāsattvas.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how should good men and good women who love the Dharma show reverence to the Dharma and reverence to the Dharma-teacher?"

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Ānanda: "Enough, enough, Ānanda! The beings of the present age are unable to show reverence or to have reverence for the Dharma."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I now love the Dharma and revere the Dharma, and have revered and will continue to revere it. World-Honored One, since I have attended the Tathāgata, I have never heard such a Dharma-gate. Because I have not heard it, my reverence toward the Tathāgata must be lacking in proper form. If now I hear it, I can practice this matter in accord with the Dharma. World-Honored One, moreover, there are good men and good women who love the Dharma who, upon hearing this Dharma-gate, will be able to practice it. World-Honored One, furthermore, there are bhikṣus who have gone forth within the Tathāgata's Dharma, yet who are poor and lowly, seeking only clothing and food, and who do not delight in seeking the Dharma or reverencing the Dharma-teacher. Although they are close to the buddha-dharma, their base conduct cannot be hidden and will inevitably be revealed. World-Honored One, therefore I now, for myself in the present and for all bhikṣus of the future, inquire of the Tathāgata about this reverence for the Dharma. World-Honored One, how may we practice the right conduct? We humbly pray the Tathāgata will explain it for us."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Good men and good women who delight in reverencing the Dharma, when they wish to read, recite, or inquire, should go to the place of the upādhyāya or ācārya where the sūtras and Dharma are. Having arrived, they should ask the upādhyāya or ācārya about the Tathāgata's correct Dharma, according to whatever their heart desires. They should know what Dharma is in the upādhyāya's or ācārya's mind, and should first ask whether they have permission to listen and inquire. If the teacher grants permission, only then should they ask. Even if one has been ordained for ten or twenty years, for the sake of loving the Dharma, one should go and inquire and also receive dependence. Why? Though in the Tathāgata's Dharma it is permitted for a bhikṣu of five years to be free from dependence, that person should, out of initial reverence for the Dharma and love for the Dharma, still seek it. Why? Because that person wishes to accomplish his own virtuous conduct.

"Ānanda, the ācārya should grant dependence in this manner, saying either: 'Permitted,' or 'It is so,' or 'Indeed,' or 'Beneficial,' or 'I shall instruct,' or 'Be careful and do not be negligent; practice correctly and uprightly in accord with the Dharma.' In this way, dependence is granted. A bhikṣu who is accomplished in such dharmas may grant dependence, and the one who receives it is then called 'accomplished in dependence.' Even a bhikṣu of a hundred years, if unable to penetrate such phrases, should receive dependence. How much less could such a one serve as a dependence-teacher for others? Even a bhikṣu of no years who is accomplished in such dharmas, in the secret language of the śramaṇa, is immediately said to have received dependence. Even a centenarian who does not himself understand such phrases and rules of the Vinaya should receive dependence.

"In this, the sūtra-reciting bhikṣu should generate a mind of respect and esteem toward the ācārya, and show right reverence to the one who reads, recites, and receives the sūtras. Before the ācārya, one must not bare the teeth, must not look at the feet, must not shuffle the feet, must not stack the feet, must not tap the feet, must not fidget with the feet, and must not sit on a high seat. Unless the teacher asks, one must not speak. One must not contradict the teacher's words. One must not stare continually at the teacher's face.

"Standing before the teacher, one should stand at a distance of three cubits. When the teacher gives permission to sit, one should sit. Having sat, one should give rise to a mind of loving-kindness and compassion toward the teacher. The sūtra-reciter should first recite what has been thoroughly memorized. After reciting what is memorized, one should receive sūtras from the teacher, as much or as little as one wishes. Regarding any doubt among the Dharma-gates, one should first ask whether one has permission to inquire. If the teacher gives permission, only then should one ask.

"Having received the sūtra, one should kneel on the right knee and with both hands touch and bow to the teacher's feet. If the ground is uneven, one should step back along whatever path there is until reaching level ground. If the ground is already level, one should bow to the teacher's feet and then depart. Having walked ten cubits, one should bow once more, and then proceed along the path.

"One should think thus: 'The ācārya is always following behind me. I cannot be far from the ācārya.' One should know the proper times, and go to the ācārya's presence three times daily. If one fails to go, one should be dealt with according to the Dharma. If one goes but does not see the ācārya, one should leave a marker of grass, wood, a stick, a clod of earth, or a stone. If the ācārya is in his room in seated meditation, one should circumambulate the room with reverence and then depart.

"If there is something to be done, one must not do it without asking the teacher, except for going to the lavatory. One must not address the teacher with rough, harsh, or evil words. One must not repeat the teacher's words. Wherever the teacher sits, whether on a rope bed or a wooden bed, one must not sit there. If the bed is broken, one should repair it.

"One should go in the early morning, go at the proper time, and not go at improper times. Having gone, one should ask the ācārya: 'What is needed and what should be done? Will you enter the village?' If the teacher says he will, one should wash one's hands, dry them with one's own cloth, take the teacher's robes with both hands and place them in a clean spot. First give the teacher clean water to wash his hands, then hand the teacher his robes. Afterward, brush the dust from the antarvāsa and offer it, or the covering robe, or the rain robe, or whatever other robes and requisites are needed.

"One should show reverence in this way. Before the ācārya, one must not spit. Whether inside the monastery or to the east or west, one must not go about with robes tucked up to the left or right, and must not wrap the head. Wherever the teacher resides, if there is a walking-meditation path, one should sweep it clean. Three times daily, one should fan the teacher. Three times daily, one should prepare bathing water. Three times daily, one should inquire, fetch water, and beg food for the teacher. If the teacher has work to be done, one should exert oneself to do it.

"After the meal, one should ask the teacher for his bowl to wash it. If the teacher gives it, first wash the teacher's bowl, then wash one's own. If the teacher does not give it, one must not ask again. Why? Ānanda, in the future there will be bhikṣus who think: 'The bowl of the Tathāgata, the Perfectly Awakened One, has no one to wash it.' Learning from me, they will wish to wash it themselves. The Tathāgata permits such persons to take cool water in summer and warm water in winter, and to obtain whatever is needed.

"One must not chew a toothstick before the teacher. One must not speak of the teacher's merits or faults. If one sees the teacher from a distance, one should rise and go to meet him.

"Ānanda, if from a teacher one has received even a single four-line verse for reading, reciting, or inquiry, that teacher is one's ācārya. Therefore, one should revere the ācārya.

"Ānanda, if one does not revere the ācārya in this way, then because of that lack of reverence, one abides in wrong conduct. One who speaks of the teacher's faults and evils does not regard me as the World-Honored One. Why? Ānanda, such a person does not honor the Buddha, does not revere the Dharma, and is not counted among the Saṃgha. Why? Ānanda, such a foolish person cannot be called one who abides in right conduct. Ānanda, for those who abide in right conduct, I teach the buddha-dharma."

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, weeping with tears streaming, said: "World-Honored One, if in the future world there are sentient beings who can abide in and practice such conduct, that would be extremely rare. World-Honored One, I am able to practice and abide in such conduct. World-Honored One, if a bhikṣu is unable to revere the upādhyāya and ācārya and speaks of their faults, what retribution will that person receive?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a bhikṣu does not revere the upādhyāya and ācārya and speaks of their faults, I say that person is a foolish ordinary being. Why? Ānanda, one must not speak of the ācārya's real faults, how much less fabricated ones!

"In this regard, Ānanda, for one who does not revere the upādhyāya and ācārya, there is a pratyeka hell called 'Extinction.' That person, at the end of life, is born in that hell. Upon being born there, he immediately has four heads, and his body burns with fire like a hot iron ball. In that place, there are insects called 'iron dogs' that constantly devour that person's tongue-root. When life in that place ends, one is born among the animals, as a tiger, wolf, or jackal. All beings who see them cry out: 'That is a tiger, wolf, or jackal!' None who see them can feel joy. Because of the former transgression of speech, they constantly eat filth and excrement.

"When the retribution is exhausted and one is reborn among humans, it is always in the borderlands where there is no buddha-dharma. Though born as a human, one is replete with all evils and distant from all merit. In bodily form, complexion, and strength, one does not resemble a human. The body one receives does not resemble one's parents, and one is not loved by one's parents. One is constantly slandered, far from the buddhas, and in life after life, foolish, dull, and without wisdom, quickly falling into the hells. Why? Because of not reverencing the one who instructs, bestows the Dharma, and delivers one from difficulty.

"Ānanda, such a person repeatedly encounters suffering. Ānanda, even if one reads, recites, receives, and upholds a single four-line verse, or copies and makes offerings to the sūtra-scrolls, and for kalpas equal in number to those characters bears that teacher upon one's crown, shoulders, or back, and makes offerings with every kind of pleasure: Ānanda, even having made such offerings, one still cannot repay the ācārya's kindness."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "In the future world, there will be such evil bhikṣus who, having obtained this sūtra, do not revere the ācārya and upādhyāya, and because of their lack of practice, speak of the ācārya's and upādhyāya's faults. I now make this prediction for such foolish bhikṣus: they will fall into the various hells and suffer great torment. Ānanda, I now tell you and solemnly admonish you: the Tathāgata has explained the paths of good and evil conduct. According to the good and evil that sentient beings practice, they receive retribution accordingly. Therefore, Ānanda, you should now carefully show reverence and carefully reflect.

"Ānanda, good men and good women who show right reverence and obtain this Dharma-gate, who praise renunciation and censure the impurity of desire: when all their dust and defilements are exhausted, they attain the purity of the Dharma-eye."

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda adjusted his robes and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, by what name should this essential Dharma be called? How should bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās receive, uphold, and practice it, so that in the future world the seeds of the Three Jewels will not be severed?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'Right Reverence.' Receive and uphold it thus: namely, by loving the Buddha, loving the Dharma, and loving the Saṃgha."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the Venerable Ānanda, all the bhikṣus, and all the bodhisattvas, having heard the Buddha's teaching, all leaped with great joy and reverently put it into practice.
