# 梵志頞波羅延問種尊經
## Sūtra on the Brāhmaṇa Assalāyana's Question about Caste Supremacy

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0071

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Śrāvastī, in Jeta's Grove, the park of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with five hundred bhikṣus. In the city of Śrāvastī there were five hundred brāhmaṇas. The five hundred together went out of the city and proceeded to their farms. Sitting together, they discussed and said: "When people first arose on the earth, they were all of our brāhmaṇa caste. The second caste is the kṣatriya, the third the farmers, the fourth the artisans. Our caste is the most honored. When people first arose on the earth, they were all of our caste. At the original birth, we came forth from the mouth. Now worldly people are born from below. Of all under heaven, our caste is the most honored. Our caste consists entirely of the grandsons of the seventh-generation Brahmā. Yet the Buddha says that all under heaven are of one caste. The Buddha groups our caste together with kṣatriyas, farmers, and artisans as equals. Our caste upon death all ascend to the Brahmā heaven, yet the Buddha groups our caste with common people as equals." They discussed among themselves: "Who is able to debate with the Buddha and distinguish the matter of caste?"

At that time, among the brāhmaṇas there was one who had a son, aged fifteen or sixteen, named Assalāyana the Great Sage. He was brilliant in learning and writing, knew of things to come, and among the five hundred brāhmaṇas there was none who could equal him. They all regarded him as their teacher. He could expound the scriptures and understand the affairs of the world. Assalāyana the Great Sage had remarkable physical marks. The brāhmaṇas discussed among themselves: "Only Assalāyana is able to debate with the Buddha. None of us can debate with the Buddha." The five hundred together told Assalāyana: "The Buddha considers all people under heaven as one caste. Our caste differs from kṣatriyas, farmers, and artisans. Our caste descends from Brahmā; at birth we come forth from the mouth, while people of the present world are born from below. The Buddha says, 'Under heaven there are four castes, and all four are good.' We wish Assalāyana to trouble himself to go with us and debate with the Buddha."

Assalāyana said: "The Buddha holds to the true Way, and can answer those who come with the true Way. If I go presenting the brāhmaṇa caste position, can it hold up against the Way of truth?" The five hundred all said: "We regard Assalāyana as our teacher. Why would he not go to discuss this matter of the four castes?" After being asked three times, Assalāyana then arose and went with the five hundred to where the Buddha was, at Jeta's Grove.

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "There is a brāhmaṇa's son named Assalāyana, aged fifteen or sixteen, accompanied by five hundred elder brāhmaṇas, waiting outside." The Buddha said: "Invite them in." Ānanda went out and invited Assalāyana in. Assalāyana and the five hundred all stood without making obeisance to the Buddha. He simply said: "I have a small matter to ask the Buddha." The Buddha said: "Please sit." Assalāyana addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask something. Will you consent to answer?" The Buddha said: "If you have doubts, speak them."

Assalāyana said: "Our caste tradition teaches that we differ from the kṣatriya, farmer, and artisan castes. It says our caste consists of Brahmā's grandsons. Our ancestors at the original birth all came forth from the mouth, and upon death all ascend to heaven."

The Buddha replied: "My teaching does not speak of different castes. If a brāhmaṇa marries a kṣatriya woman, the kṣatriya woman bears children. If a kṣatriya marries a farmer's daughter, the farmer's daughter bears children. If a farmer marries an artisan's daughter, the artisan's daughter bears children. If an artisan marries a brāhmaṇa woman, the brāhmaṇa woman bears children." The Buddha said: "In my teaching, conduct is fundamental. One whose conduct is good belongs to the highest caste. Whatever is honored and noble in this world is attained through good conduct, not through caste. In my past lives through countless kalpas, I was born as a brāhmaṇa's son, as a kṣatriya's son, as a farmer's son, and as an artisan's son. I attained birth as a prince, and in this present body I am the Buddha."

The Buddha told Assalāyana: "I shall ask you one thing; answer as it truly is." The Buddha said: "Have you seen in the world a son of a good family who became another's slave, while a slave was freed and made another's son?"

Assalāyana addressed the Buddha: "I have heard that in the country of the Yuezhi there is such a thing."

The Buddha said: "Why is this so?"

"A son of a good family becomes a slave, while a slave becomes another's son, because the slave's intentions and conduct are good, so people make him a son. The son who becomes a slave: his intentions and conduct are evil, so he sells himself to become another's slave."

"If you say that people have castes as you describe, then where is this caste? If among brāhmaṇas, kṣatriyas, farmers, and artisans there are those of all four castes who delight in killing, delight in stealing, delight in sexual misconduct, delight in divisive speech, delight in harsh speech, delight in false speech, delight in slander, delight in keeping company with fools, delight in anger, and delight in ritual sacrifice, those who act thus: will they not fall into hell?"

Assalāyana said: "According to brāhmaṇa tradition, though there may be such evils, our caste is the most honored. We are Brahmā's grandsons, born from the mouth. Upon death we shall all ascend to heaven."

The Buddha said: "If among brāhmaṇas, kṣatriyas, farmers, and artisans there are those who have no inclination to kill, no inclination to steal, no inclination toward sexual misconduct, no inclination toward divisive speech, no inclination toward harsh speech, no inclination toward false speech, no inclination toward slander, no inclination to follow fools, no inclination toward anger, no inclination toward ritual sacrifice: when such people die, would they not be born in heaven?"

Assalāyana said: "Indeed, they would all be born in heaven."

The Buddha said: "Then where is this caste distinction of which you speak?" The Buddha said: "All human castes arise from the mind, intention, and consciousness. Those whose mind, intention, and consciousness lead to good conduct are born in the heavens or among humans. Those whose mind, intention, and consciousness are evil enter the paths of insects, beasts, animals, ghosts, spirits, or hell. If there are brāhmaṇas, kṣatriyas, farmers, or artisans whose conduct is evil, they all alike enter the three evil paths. If this is so, then where is caste? The conduct of brāhmaṇas may be good or evil. The conduct of kṣatriyas may be good or evil. The conduct of farmers may be good or evil. The conduct of artisans may be good or evil. You say there are castes; where then are these castes? If a brāhmaṇa directs his mind with enmity toward empty space, so too kṣatriyas, farmers, and artisans might direct enmity toward empty space, but they cannot harm it. The power of intention is equal. Brāhmaṇas merely use their minds to exalt themselves above the world. If brāhmaṇas, kṣatriyas, farmers, and artisans enter a great river and each bathe, their dirt falling into the water, can you distinguish which is brāhmaṇa dirt, which is kṣatriya dirt, which is farmer dirt, which is artisan dirt?"

Assalāyana said: "With the dirt in the great river, how could one know where each is?"

"If you cannot even distinguish people's dirt, how can you claim that your caste is in heaven, that you originally came from the mouth, that others are born from below, and that your caste is the most honored among people?" The Buddha told Assalāyana: "If a king hears that in a certain country, a certain district, a certain village, there is a brilliant brāhmaṇa's son, a brilliant kṣatriya's son, a brilliant farmer's son, and a brilliant artisan's son, the king summons them all to serve as his ministers. Would the king ask about their caste? Those of high talent and keen discernment, the king would first assign to good districts and territories. Why would the king not ask about their sons' castes? You say there are castes, but Assalāyana, where are these precepts of yours? Sitting among all these elders, seated in the senior position, these five hundred people: why do they not question your caste, when you serve as their teacher? If there are brāhmaṇas, kṣatriyas, farmers, artisans, and other castes, when it is cold they all gather around a great fire. The fire's warmth does not go to one caste alone; its warmth reaches all equally. If there is a great ship crossing the water, and brāhmaṇas, kṣatriyas, farmers, and artisans of all castes are together on one ship, the ship does not ferry only the brāhmaṇa caste, nor does it ferry only other castes."

The Buddha asked Assalāyana: "Whether brāhmaṇa, kṣatriya, farmer, artisan, or any other caste, when a child is in the mother's womb, the time is the same ten months. Is there any increase or decrease?"

Assalāyana said: "It is all ten months, without any increase or decrease."

"Then why do you claim that your caste consists of Brahmā's grandsons, born from the mouth? Whether brāhmaṇa, kṣatriya, farmer, artisan, or any other caste, the sun and moon do not shine only upon your one caste. Why do they shine equally upon all castes?"

Assalāyana said: "Our caste tradition simply says that it is superior to other castes."

The Buddha told Assalāyana: "If a donkey is the father and a mare is the mother, the mare bears a foal. What is it called?"

Assalāyana said: "It is called a mule. The father is not called a mule, nor is the mother called a mule."

"Then why is it called a mule?"

"My ancestors called it a mule, so I follow and call it a mule."

"If a stallion is the father and a female donkey is the mother, the donkey bears a foal. What do you call it? It is called a hinny. The father is not called a hinny, nor is the mother called a hinny. How do you know it is a hinny?"

"My ancestors called it a hinny, so I follow and call it a hinny."

"If a brāhmaṇa marries a kṣatriya woman and the kṣatriya woman bears a child, what should it be called? Should it be called brāhmaṇa caste, or kṣatriya caste?"

Assalāyana addressed the Buddha: "I do not know what caste to call it."

The Buddha said: "If you do not even know your own classification, how do you know to call a mule a mule or a hinny a hinny? If a kṣatriya woman bears a child for a brāhmaṇa, entering your household, following your rituals, following your caste customs, and this child later proves brilliant, would you serve him, or would you send him away? Would we serve him? If a brāhmaṇa marries a farmer's daughter and the farmer's daughter bears a child, and this child has inclinations toward killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, divisive speech, harsh speech, false speech, slander, keeping company with fools, anger, and ritual sacrifice, would you associate with one who acts thus?"

"We would not associate with him. Because his mind, intention, and consciousness lead to evil conduct, we would expel him and not include him in our group."

The Buddha said: "You claim to be Brahmā's grandsons, born from the mouth, who upon death will ascend to heaven. Yet the moment you see that someone's mind, intention, and consciousness lead to evil conduct, you expel each other. Why then do you speak of caste?"

The Buddha said: "If among the brāhmaṇa caste there are those who are unfilial to their parents, who delight in killing, stealing, and the ten evils, to what path will they go upon death?"

Assalāyana said: "Those whose mind, intention, and consciousness lead to such conduct, upon death will enter the hells."

The Buddha said: "You claim that your caste is originally from Brahmā, born from the mouth, the most honored among people. Then why would you enter hell?" The Buddha said: "Among all people under heaven, there are no castes, and none who are permanently exalted. Those whose mind, intention, and will are good, whose conduct is good, these are noble. Those whose mind, intention, and conduct are evil, these are lowly."

Assalāyana thought to himself: "I originally did not wish to come; the others compelled me." He reflected: "My original intention was not to contend with the Buddha. I should now affirm that the Buddha's Way is correct."

The Buddha said: "Earlier you said that people should be judged by caste. Now you come to agree with me that mind, intention, and will are what matter."

Assalāyana thought to himself: "The Buddha's words have blocked me coming and going."

The Buddha told Assalāyana: "Long ago in the past, there were seven brāhmaṇas, all possessing the Way, who daily performed fire sacrifices to heaven. The seven brāhmaṇas also proclaimed: 'We are Brahmā's grandsons. We at birth also came forth from the mouth. Common people are born from below. Our caste differs from common people. Our caste upon death will all ascend to the Brahmā heaven.'" The Buddha said: "At that time, I was also a practitioner, called Asita. People together called my Way the 'Heavenly Way.' At that time, I saw the seven brāhmaṇas performing their fire-sacrifice rituals. The brāhmaṇas all said: 'When we die, like this, the firelight will carry us up to heaven.'"

The Buddha said: "When I saw what you were doing, I transformed my body and appearance, holding a golden-handled pennant banner, dressed in white garments, and borrowing a carriage from someone, I went to where the seven brāhmaṇas were. The seven brāhmaṇas at that time were doing walking meditation. When you all saw me, you called out: 'Brāhmaṇa! Practitioner, from where do you come?' The seven brāhmaṇas said: 'You follow the Heavenly Way, which is similar to ours. Why do you wear fine clothes and ride in a carriage, holding a golden pennant? Where were you born? Where are you going? From where have you come? Why have you come here?' At that time I did not speak with you. When you saw I would not speak, you were all angered and cursed me, wishing to make me fall dead. But with your curses, my complexion became even more radiant. You cursed again, and my complexion became even more beautiful. The seven brāhmaṇas were greatly alarmed and said: 'All whom we have cursed have died. Now we curse him, yet his complexion becomes more beautiful.' They again called out to Asita. I reflected: 'What path do you wish to pursue?' Asita then replied: 'My Way and intention are superior. Why are you angry? Have you heard of Asita's Heavenly Way?' You said: 'We have not heard of it.' 'When worthy practitioners meet, they should question each other. Why be angry with each other?' Asita said: 'I heard your claim that brāhmaṇas under heaven are Brahmā's grandsons, born from the mouth, uniquely honored among people. For this reason I came to ask you: you kindle this fire and sacrifice to heaven, but by what teacher's instruction did you obtain this method of sacrificing to heaven? Did you not merely hear it from your ancestors?' The seven brāhmaṇas said: 'Our ancestors set the example for us.' The practitioner Asita said: 'Were the mothers of your ancestors brāhmaṇa women or other women?' They said: 'We do not know whether our ancestors' mothers were kṣatriya women, farmers' daughters, or artisans' daughters.' 'I do not know whether your ancestors' mothers bore brāhmaṇas or whether you cherish children born of other women as your own caste. Since you do not even know your ancestors, how do you know they came from Brahmā? A woman's heart cannot be guaranteed. Can you know how worldly people, when husband and wife unite, conceive a child?' They all said: 'I do not know.' 'You neither know your ancestors nor know how people are conceived. Why then were you angry with me? When people meet, they should first greet each other courteously, high and low. One should understand a person's intentions and will before becoming angry. Why be angry first? Do you know when a child first enters the womb? The father has a disposition of desire and the mother a heart of longing. For one who is to become their child, these three combine to form the child. For the one who is to become the child, who causes the warm intention to combine with father and mother to form the child? While the child is in the womb, perhaps due to evil deeds in past lives, the child in the womb may be born blind, deaf, mute, hunchbacked, lame, or without limbs. The parents have no knowledge of this. The closest people, father and mother, carrying the child in the womb, cannot even know whether their child will be beautiful or ugly. How then can you know that your ancestors were Brahmā's grandsons, born from the mouth, uniquely honored among people?'"

The Buddha told Assalāyana: "In that former time, those seven brāhmaṇas could even kill people with their curses. Yet at that time I did not heed what you said, nor did I record it. Now that I have become the Buddha, you come again proclaiming, 'We are Brahmā's grandsons, born from the mouth, uniquely honored among people.'" The Buddha told Assalāyana: "Reflect on what I have said. Under heaven, those who give birth are parents, and what completes a person is a teacher."

The brāhmaṇa Assalāyana, together with the five hundred brāhmaṇas and all the brāhmaṇas, reflected: "In a former time, I served as teacher even to the seven brāhmaṇas, and the seven brāhmaṇas were all men of virtue who could kill people with their curses. Now I shall discern and explain: 'People have castes: one becomes a hundred, a hundred becomes a thousand, a thousand becomes ten thousand, ten thousand becomes one.'" Assalāyana and all the brāhmaṇas came forward together and prostrated, pressing their heads to the Buddha's feet: "We do not understand 'one becomes a hundred, a hundred becomes a thousand, a thousand becomes ten thousand, ten thousand becomes one.' What does this mean?"

The Buddha said: "Among worldly people, those who crave and delight in birth and death are many. One person's descendants then divide into a hundred families. A hundred families seeking the Way find only one Way. What is this one Way? It is the unconditioned Way. In this manner, one becomes a thousand, a thousand becomes ten thousand."

Assalāyana came forward, knelt, and said: "Will you have compassion and accept us, just as you have compassion for your śramaṇas? Do not harbor resentment toward us. We request to take refuge and be gathered in." The Buddha said: "Good! You have not eaten since this morning." They all arose, pressed their heads to the Buddha's feet, and departed.
