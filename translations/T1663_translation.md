# 菩提心觀釋
## Commentary on the Contemplation of Bodhicitta

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1663

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmadeva (法天).

---

> Homage to the Original Teacher, the Great Awakened World-Honored One.
> I now briefly explain the contemplation of bodhicitta.

As the Buddha said, all dharmas arise from the mind. I shall now examine that mind of bodhi. What is its nature? It is free from all natures. What are "all natures"? They are the natures of the skandhas, āyatanas, dhātus, and the like. Because the mind of bodhi is free from grasping and rejecting, dharmas are selfless, inherently equal, originally unborn, and empty by nature. As for what is meant by "all natures," these are the natures of self and the like, namely the natures of self, person, sentient being, life-span, pudgala, mānavaka, and so forth. Those natures are not the mind of bodhi. Why is this? Because those such as "self," within an inherent nature free from all marks, give rise to the view of self, and from the view of self arise all afflictions. This mind does not give rise to that. As for the assertion that the skandhas, āyatanas, dhātus, and the like are also free from grasping and rejecting: this is because the true nature of the skandhas, āyatanas, dhātus, and the like is ultimately unobtainable.

Why is form and the like without reality? The form-skandha is composed of the four great elements. The four great elements are the earth, water, fire, and wind dhātus. They further produce the five sense-objects: form, sound, smell, taste, and touch. The earth element and the others, as well as the five sense-objects, are each individually unobtainable in their own nature. All dharmas are likewise. Therefore one knows that form is a mere designation, and by this one knows the form-skandha is empty. Just as a shadow exists because of a tree, and when the tree perishes the shadow vanishes, the form-skandha is like this, and the feeling-skandha is also so.

What is called feeling? Feeling is of three kinds: painful feeling, pleasant feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. These three feelings are mutually conditioned. There are further two kinds: bodily feeling and mental feeling. The body belongs to the form-skandha. Since the body is unobtainable, if there is no body, there is no feeling; it can neither be expressed nor described, is neither short nor long, neither form nor mark, without substance and without attachment, because it is unknowable. Bodily feeling is like this, and mental feeling is also so. The feeling-skandha is thus.

Seeing the feeling-skandha as empty, the perception-skandha is also a mere designation without reality. It belongs to deliberation, and since that deliberation is unobtainable, it is not deliberation. Not being deliberation, one sees the perception-skandha as empty.

The perception-skandha being thus, the formations-skandha is also so. Good mental actions, intentions, memories, and other formations have nothing that exists. The form-skandha and the rest that are produced by mental dharmas are each individually without arising. Therefore one knows that the karmic characteristics of the formations-skandha are without reality and without any ruler, and one sees the formations-skandha as empty.

The formations-skandha being thus, the consciousness-skandha is also so, up to and including eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. Eye-consciousness and the rest are each individually unobtainable in their own nature. The eye conditions visible form; from this condition, consciousness arises. Without the condition, consciousness does not arise. But this eye and form, along with the form-skandha and the rest, have no fixed boundaries. When one discriminates eye and form, there is no eye and form, and consciousness has nothing from which to arise. Eye-consciousness being thus, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind are also so. Thus one knows that this consciousness depends upon the manas-consciousness. Because it depends upon the manas-consciousness, it gives rise to dharmas of the past, future, and present. What are dharmas of the past, future, and present? The past has already ceased, the future has not yet arisen, and the present does not abide. From this one knows the consciousness-skandha is empty.

Thus, explaining the skandhas, āyatanas, and dhātus one by one, discriminating each, their inherent natures are all empty. That which is not without nature is the sentence of true reality. Just as without a seed, no sprout or stem is produced, therefore the skandhas, āyatanas, dhātus, and the like are also free from grasping and rejecting.

Why is the mind of bodhi without grasping and without rejecting? As the Buddha said, addressing the Lord of Secrets: "That mind of bodhi, as the Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly Awakened One has understood, is neither blue nor yellow, neither red nor white, neither crimson nor the color of crystal, neither short nor long, neither round nor square, neither bright nor dark, neither female nor male, nor of the indeterminate sex." Moreover: "Lord of Secrets! The mind of bodhi is not of the nature of the desire realm, not of the nature of the form realm, not of the nature of devas, not of yakṣas, not of gandharvas, not of asuras, not of humans, not of non-humans, and so forth. Even when sought by omniscient wisdom, it is unobtainable." If grasping this mind is nonexistent in this way, how can one speak of rejecting? Therefore, as the Buddha also said, addressing the Lord of Secrets: "The mind of bodhi is neither within, nor without, nor in between, because it is unobtainable." Why is this? Because its inherent nature is quiescent. Moreover: "Lord of Secrets! That mind of bodhi, when sought by omniscient wisdom, is unobtainable. How can there be grasping and rejecting?" Thus, with regard to dharmas, one attains freedom from grasping and rejecting, which is equality and selflessness. As all dharmas are, so too is selflessness.

As the Buddha said, the mind of bodhi is also thus. All dharmas are empty, without marks, without self; all dharmas are quiescent, without the mark of quiescence. The mind is originally equal, originally unborn and also not "not born." What then is its nature? It is emptiness. What is the nature of emptiness? It is like space. As the Buddha said, the nature of space is empty and without comparison. The mind of bodhi is also like this. The name "bodhi" is without nature and without marks, without arising and without ceasing, neither awakening nor non-awakening. If one understands in this way, this is called the mind of bodhi.

Further, as the Buddha said, addressing the Lord of Secrets: "When one truly knows one's own original mind, and even in what is nonexistent there is nothing obtainable, this is called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi." He further addressed the Lord of Secrets: "Having truly contemplated one's own mind, one should then give rise to skillful contemplation of sentient beings, knowing that all sentient beings do not truly know their own nature of self-awakening, and that from doubt, delusion, and inverted attachment they undergo the great suffering of various cycles of rebirth. For this reason, I give rise to the mind of great compassion, to cause all sentient beings to realize and awaken to the Dharma of their own mind as it truly is. This is precisely what is called the mind of bodhi. This is called the mind of benefit, the mind of happiness, the supreme mind, the mind of good awakening to the dharma-realm." Because one gathers in sentient beings with such wisdom, it is called the mind of bodhi. By generating this mind, the merit obtained is also like space, without limit or boundary, and its ocean of merit is also immeasurable. Even when the kalpa ends, this merit is inexhaustible. This is called generating the most supreme mind of bodhi, which is the root of omniscient wisdom.
