# 佛說阿難同學經
## Sūtra of Ānanda's Fellow Student Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0149

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, in the city of Śrāvastī there was a bhikṣu named Gupta, who was the Venerable Ānanda's childhood friend. Ānanda loved and respected him greatly, and they were close and intimate, never having been angry with each other. However, Gupta did not delight in cultivating the holy life and wished to abandon the precepts and return to lay life.

At that time, Ānanda went to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and stood to one side. Ānanda said to the World-Honored One: "In this city of Śrāvastī there is a bhikṣu named Gupta, who is my childhood friend. He is unable to bear the cultivation of the holy life and wishes to abandon the precepts and return to lay life. I beseech the World-Honored One to teach the Dharma to the bhikṣu Gupta, so that in this present life he may cultivate the holy life with purity."

The World-Honored One then addressed Ānanda: "Ānanda! Go yourself to where the bhikṣu Gupta is."

He replied: "So be it, World-Honored One."

Ānanda, having received the Buddha's instruction, went to where the bhikṣu Gupta was. "The World-Honored One is calling you."

He replied: "So be it."

The bhikṣu Gupta, following Ānanda's instruction, went to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side.

The World-Honored One then addressed the bhikṣu Gupta: "Is it true, bhikṣu, that you do not delight in cultivating the holy life and wish to abandon the precepts and return to lay life?"

The bhikṣu replied: "Truly so, World-Honored One. The reason is that my body burns with passion and my mind also burns. I am unable to cultivate the holy life with purity."

The World-Honored One said: "Bhikṣu! Women have five impure qualities. What are the five? Bhikṣu! Women are foul-smelling; their speech is coarse and rough; they have no sense of gratitude. They are like venomous snakes, always harboring poisonous filth. Women increase Māra's host and are difficult to liberate, like hooks and chains. Women cannot be drawn near, like mixed poisons that cannot be consumed. Women cannot be digested, like vajra that destroys the body. Bhikṣu! They are like flames, like the fire of the Avīci hell. Bhikṣu! Women are not to be gazed upon, like foul excrement. Bhikṣu! Women are not to be listened to, like the sound of a corpse. Bhikṣu! Women are like a prison, like the prison of Vemacitra. Bhikṣu! Women are enemies, like venomous snakes. Bhikṣu, you should stay far away, as you would from an evil companion. Bhikṣu! Women are a source of fear, like a village of bandits.

"Bhikṣu! A human body is difficult to obtain, like the udumbara flower. Bhikṣu! A human body is exceedingly difficult to obtain, like a plank with a single hole cast upon the water: in the course of tens of thousands of years, one may chance upon that hole. Bhikṣu! The right time is also difficult to encounter; aside from the eight inopportune moments, there is no other occasion.

"You, bhikṣu, have already obtained a human body. This is entirely due to actions performed in the past. Bhikṣu! The appearance of a Buddha-World-Honored-One in the world is exceedingly rare, like a barren woman who has no children. Bhikṣu! The appearance of a Tathāgata in the world is exceedingly rare, like the udumbara flower.

"Bhikṣu! You have already obtained a human body, you have already received the complete precepts, and you have also been admitted into the Saṃgha. Like a king honored and revered, you teach the Dharma for others, practicing calming and insight to reach the realm of nirvāṇa and arrive at that place. The Tathāgata has well taught this Dharma. You, bhikṣu, should purely cultivate the holy life and exhaust the source of suffering."

At that time, the bhikṣu, having received this instruction from the Buddha, right there upon his seat, became free from all dust and defilement and attained the purity of the Dharma-eye. The bhikṣu then rose from his seat, bowed his head at the World-Honored One's feet, and withdrew.

The bhikṣu, having heard the World-Honored One's instruction, dwelt in a secluded place and delighted in solitary practice. Having delighted in solitary practice in a secluded place, that son of good family shaved off his hair and beard, put on the kāṣāya robes, and before the Tathāgata cultivated the unsurpassed holy life. He exhausted the source of birth and death; the holy life was established, what was to be done had been done, and he would not again be received into a mother's womb. At that time, the bhikṣu attained arhatship.

The Venerable Gupta then went to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side. The Venerable Gupta said to the World-Honored One: "World-Honored One! The instruction you gave: I have now fully realized it. I beseech the World-Honored One to permit me to enter parinirvāṇa."

The World-Honored One was silent and did not reply. The Venerable Gupta the bhikṣu asked a second and a third time: "World-Honored One! The instruction you gave: I have now fully realized it. I beseech the World-Honored One to permit me to enter parinirvāṇa."

The World-Honored One said: "Bhikṣu! Now is the right time."

The bhikṣu then rose from his seat, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated the World-Honored One three times, and withdrew. He returned to his own quarters. Having arrived, he put away his sitting mat, laid out his sitting cloth in the open ground, and ascended into the sky, manifesting various supernatural transformations. He transformed one body into many bodies, and many bodies into one body. He became stone and iron, or vajra, or walls and city ramparts, or high mountains and cliff faces, all passing through without obstruction. He emerged from and sank into the earth as smoothly as flowing water without any hindrance. Sitting cross-legged, he filled the empty sky, like a great blazing fire, or like a flying bird, or like the sun and moon with great awesome spiritual power and great strength. With his hand he reached up, and his transformed body extended to the Brahmā heaven. In empty space he sat, reclined, and walked about. Sometimes he manifested smoke and flame: smoke from the lower body and fire from the upper body, smoke from the upper body and fire from the lower body, smoke from the left and fire from the right, smoke from the right and fire from the left, smoke from the front and flame from the back, smoke from the back and flame from the front. His entire body emitted smoke, his entire body emitted flame, his entire body emitted fire.

Then the bhikṣu withdrew his supernatural powers and, alone, sat cross-legged with body upright, mind correct, and mindfulness settled before him. He entered the first dhyāna. Arising from the first dhyāna, he entered the second dhyāna. Arising from the second dhyāna, he entered the third dhyāna. Arising from the third dhyāna, he entered the fourth dhyāna. Arising from the fourth dhyāna, he entered the sphere of infinite space. Arising from the sphere of infinite space, he entered the sphere of infinite consciousness. Arising from the sphere of infinite consciousness, he entered the sphere of nothingness. Arising from the sphere of nothingness, he entered the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception. Arising from the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception, he entered the samādhi of the cessation of perception and feeling.

Arising from the samādhi of the cessation of perception and feeling, he entered the sphere of neither perception nor non-perception, then the sphere of nothingness, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of infinite space, the fourth dhyāna, the third dhyāna, the second dhyāna, and the first dhyāna. Again from the first dhyāna, he entered the second dhyāna, then the third dhyāna. Arising from the fourth dhyāna, the Venerable Gupta then relinquished his body and lifespan, and in the realm of nirvāṇa without remainder, he entered parinirvāṇa.

Ānanda then made offerings to the Venerable Gupta's relics and went to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and stood to one side. Ānanda said to the World-Honored One: "The bhikṣu Gupta received the Tathāgata's instruction and dwelt in a secluded place, delighting in solitary practice. That son of good family shaved off his hair and beard, put on the three Dharma robes, and with firm faith went forth from the household life to study the path. He cultivated the unsurpassed holy life, exhausted the source of birth and death; the holy life was established, what was to be done had been done, and he would not again be received into a mother's womb. World-Honored One! The Venerable Gupta has entered parinirvāṇa."

The World-Honored One said: "How marvelous! How extraordinary! Ānanda! The Buddha-World-Honored-One, endowed with immeasurable wisdom, was able to cause the bhikṣu Gupta to cross the abyss of birth and death. This, Ānanda, is what the Tathāgata's practice has accomplished. How much more so countless hundreds of thousands of beings who will cross the abyss of birth and death, and others yet to be delivered! Therefore, Ānanda, you should generate this aspiration toward the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha. Thus, Ānanda, should you train."

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully practiced in accordance.
