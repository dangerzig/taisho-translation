# 廣義法門經
## Sūtra on the Extensive Meaning of the Dharma

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0097

Translated from the Chinese. Chen dynasty, translated by Paramārtha (真諦)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Venerable Śāriputra was dwelling in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus. At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra addressed the bhikṣus. The bhikṣus said: "Venerable Śāriputra!" Śāriputra said: "Elders! I shall now teach you the Dharma, good in the beginning, good in the middle, good at the end, good in meaning and good in expression, pure and unmixed, complete and pure. I shall now reveal to you the brahma conduct, namely the Extensive Meaning of the Dharma. Therefore, listen carefully now with focused and reverent minds, reflect well upon it.

"This Extensive Meaning of the Dharma, elders, has twelve kinds of occasions free from difficulty, favorable to the path, which can give rise to skillful means for realizing the sacred Dharma. What are the twelve? First, self-victory. Second, victory through others. Third, birth in the human realm. Fourth, birth in a sacred land. Fifth, naturally possessing keen faculties. Sixth, attaining right view. Seventh, having well-established livelihood. Eighth, establishing faith in a good place. Ninth, encountering the appearance of a Buddha in the world. Tenth, the Buddha correctly turning the Dharma wheel. Eleventh, the true Dharma being present in the world and not yet having perished. Twelfth, practicing in accordance with truth within the Buddha's teaching. Elders! These are the twelve kinds of occasions free from difficulty, favorable to the path, which can give rise to skillful means for realizing the sacred Dharma.

"Elders! When a capable teaching bhikṣu wishes to teach the true Dharma to others, his words should accord with both the Dharma and its meaning. Such speech should be spoken, namely: with respect, in proper sequence, connected and coherent, generating joy and aspiration in others, fulfilling right effort, not distressing others, in accordance with truth, coherent and unmixed, appropriate to the audience. Such words should be spoken. With a mind of loving-kindness, a mind of benefit, a mind of sympathy, without attachment to gain, respect, or praise. When teaching the Dharma correctly, one should not praise oneself or exalt oneself, nor should one disparage others.

"Elders! If a person wishes to hear the true Dharma, one should possess sixteen qualities before listening. What are the sixteen? First, listening at the proper time. Second, with respect. Third, with aspiration. Fourth, without attachment. Fifth, practicing in accordance with what is heard. Sixth, not for the purpose of fault-finding. Seventh, giving rise to a mind of reverence for the Dharma. Eighth, giving rise to a mind of reverence for the speaker. Ninth, not disdaining the true Dharma. Tenth, not disdaining the speaker. Eleventh, not disdaining oneself. Twelfth, with focused and undistracted mind. Thirteenth, with a mind seeking understanding. Fourteenth, listening with single-pointed attention. Fifteenth, reflecting in accordance with truth. Sixteenth, remembering what comes before and after while listening to the true Dharma.

"If the Buddha's noble disciples can listen respectfully and attentively in this manner, the faculty of faith grows. Within the true Dharma, the mind becomes clear and pure. With this as the forerunner, one gives rise to a mind of joy and a mind of aspiration toward nirvāṇa. With this as the forerunner, one gives rise to a mind of delight in nirvāṇa, free from attachment and clinging. With this as the forerunner, one eliminates the obstructions of delusion regarding nirvāṇa and attains a concentrated mind. With this as the forerunner, one abandons doubt and wrong views regarding nirvāṇa and gives rise to right and direct vision. With this as the forerunner, one gives rise to a mind of dedication toward nirvāṇa, for the cultivation of contemplative practice, for ardent cultivation, for following the dharmas of the path, for eliminating obstructions to the path, for attaining stable abiding of mind, and for attaining the ultimate meaning. With this as the forerunner, regarding the quiescence of all conditioned dharmas, one realizes true emptiness: craving extinguished, dispassion attained. Regarding the unconditioned nirvāṇa, one enters and establishes a mind of faith and delight. With this as the forerunner, regarding nirvāṇa and the impermanence of the aggregates, one enters and establishes a mind of faith and delight. With this as the forerunner, regarding nirvāṇa and the four noble truths, the Dharma eye becomes pure, and the wisdom eye arises. With this as the forerunner, one attains liberation regarding nirvāṇa. With this as the forerunner, regarding nirvāṇa, liberation, and the knowledge and vision of liberation, all become complete.

"Elders! By being able to listen to the true Dharma in this manner, with right attention and single-pointed mind, the noble disciples do not distress the teacher. Having already discerned the meaning of right speech, this itself is offering to the great teacher through the Dharma, realizing one's own benefit and nirvāṇa.

"For the person who hears the Dharma, ten qualities arise that ripen wisdom. What are the ten? First, associating with good friends. Second, maintaining pure moral conduct. Third, having a mind that seeks understanding. Fourth, delighting in receiving good instruction. Fifth, delighting in making offerings to the speaker. Sixth, asking difficult questions at the proper time. Seventh, listening carefully to the true Dharma. Eighth, constantly practicing the true Dharma. Ninth, giving rise to weariness toward what is to be despised. Tenth, having given rise to weariness, being able to properly arouse the four kinds of right effort. What is right effort in accordance with truth? Regarding wholesome dharmas, the mind is without slackness. One constantly refines the mind, purifying it of unwholesome dharmas. If the mind has not yet attained concentration, one causes it to attain mastery. If the mind has not yet penetrated, one causes it to attain clear comprehension. This is called right effort in accordance with truth.

"Elders! If a noble disciple thus relies on truth to arouse right effort, there are ten corresponding dharmas to cultivate. What are the ten? First, the perception of impurity. Second, the perception of impermanence. Third, from impermanence, contemplating the perception of suffering. Fourth, within suffering, contemplating the perception of non-self. Fifth, the perception of repulsiveness of food. Sixth, the perception that in all the world there is no pleasure. Seventh, generating the perception of light. Eighth, contemplating the perception of dispassion. Ninth, contemplating the perception of cessation. Tenth, contemplating the perception of death.

"Elders! There are fourteen dharmas that oppose and obstruct the perception of impurity. What are the fourteen? First, dwelling together with women. Second, gazing at women with unmindful attention. Third, constantly giving rise to heedlessness. Fourth, generating strong desire. Fifth, frequently cultivating the perception of purity. Sixth, not frequently cultivating the perception of impurity. Seventh, constantly dwelling together with people engaged in worldly affairs. Eighth, following their conduct. Ninth, not delighting in hearing the true Dharma. Tenth, not inquiring about the true Dharma. Eleventh, not being able to guard the six sense doors. Twelfth, not being moderate in eating. Thirteenth, not being able to settle the mind when dwelling alone in empty places. Fourteenth, not being able to observe as it truly is.

"Second, regarding the perception of impermanence: attachment to conditioned dharmas is its obstruction.

"Third, regarding the perception of suffering in what is impermanent: six dharmas are its obstruction. What are the six? First, sloth. Second, laziness. Third, constantly delighting in dwelling at rest. Fourth, heedlessness. Fifth, not being able to practice accordingly. Sixth, not being able to observe as it truly is.

"Fourth, regarding the perception of non-self in what is suffering: the view of self is its obstruction.

"Fifth, regarding the perception of repulsiveness of food: craving for flavors is its obstruction.

"Sixth, regarding the perception that in all the world there is no pleasure: desire and craving for worldly things is its obstruction.

"Seventh, regarding the perception of light: eleven dharmas are its obstruction. What are the eleven? First, doubt. Second, inability to reflect. Third, heaviness of body. Fourth, drowsiness. Fifth, excessive effort. Sixth, insufficient effort. Seventh, mental turbidity. Eighth, mental agitation. Ninth, generating various perceptions. Tenth, excessive talking. Eleventh, extreme fixation on visible forms.

"Eighth, regarding the perception of dispassion: having desire is its obstruction.

"Ninth, regarding the perception of cessation: grasping at characteristics in accordance with dharmas is its obstruction.

"Tenth, regarding the perception of death: attachment to life is its obstruction.

"When such obstructions have not yet been subdued and eliminated, for subduing and eliminating them there are three dharmas of the greatest benefit. What are the three? First, delighting in cultivation. Second, cessation and detachment. Third, frequently abiding in the preceding two.

"Elders! There are fourteen dharmas of the greatest benefit for the contemplation of impurity. First, not dwelling together with women. Second, not gazing at women with unmindful attention. Third, constantly being unheedless. Fourth, not generating strong desire. Fifth, frequently cultivating the perception of impurity. Sixth, not frequently cultivating the perception of purity. Seventh, not dwelling together with people engaged in worldly affairs. Eighth, not following their conduct. Ninth, delighting in hearing the true Dharma. Tenth, delighting in inquiring about the true Dharma. Eleventh, guarding the six sense doors. Twelfth, being moderate in eating. Thirteenth, settling the mind when dwelling alone. Fourteenth, being able to observe as it truly is.

"Elders! First, the perception of impurity: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate attachment to sensual desire. Second, the perception of impermanence: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate attachment to all conditioned dharmas. Third, contemplating the perception of suffering in what is impermanent: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate sloth and laziness. Fourth, contemplating the perception of non-self within suffering: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate the view of self. Fifth, the perception of repulsiveness of food: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate craving for flavors. Sixth, the perception that in all the world there is no pleasure: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate rare desire and craving for the world. Seventh, the perception of light: if actually cultivated, it can generate and increase wisdom and vision. Eighth, the perception of dispassion: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate desire for existence. Ninth, the perception of cessation: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate all grasping at conditioned dharmas. Tenth, the perception of death: if actually cultivated, it can eliminate craving and attachment to life.

"Elders! If a noble disciple thus properly cultivates right effort, there are twenty dharmas that obstruct diligent cultivation. What are the twenty? First, dwelling together with people who do not cultivate contemplation. Second, not delighting in listening. Third, not receiving proper instruction. Fourth, rendering oneself deaf and mute. Fifth, having many desires and cravings. Sixth, having many affairs. Seventh, not establishing livelihood in accordance with the Dharma. Eighth, abandoning the burden of wholesome dharmas. Ninth, encountering the eight kinds of difficulty. Tenth, following the stream of distraction. Eleventh, arrogance. Twelfth, not accepting good instruction. Thirteenth, loss of mindfulness. Fourteenth, heedlessness. Fifteenth, not dwelling in a proper land. Sixteenth, not guarding the sense doors. Seventeenth, not being moderate in eating. Eighteenth, not awakening to practice in the first and last watches of the night. Nineteenth, not being able to settle the mind when dwelling alone in empty places. Twentieth, not being able to observe as it truly is.

"Elders! When such twenty kinds of obstruction have not yet been subdued and eliminated, eleven dharmas are of great benefit for subduing and eliminating them. What are the eleven? First, faith and delight in practice. Second, contemplating the merits of cultivation. Third, being able to practice what is difficult to practice. Fourth, being able to subdue one's own mind. Fifth, the mind not falling back. Sixth, clearly comprehending the true characteristic of dharmas. Seventh, not disdaining oneself. Eighth, delighting in listening without weariness. Ninth, questioning to resolve doubts. Tenth, settling the mind when dwelling alone in quiet places. Eleventh, being able to observe as it truly is.

"Elders! If a noble disciple can cultivate right effort in this manner, twenty dharmas are of the greatest benefit. What are the twenty? First, dwelling together with people who cultivate contemplation. Second, delighting in hearing the true Dharma. Third, receiving proper instruction. Fourth, not being deaf or mute. Fifth, not having many desires and cravings. Sixth, not having many affairs. Seventh, establishing livelihood in accordance with the Dharma. Eighth, not abandoning the burden of wholesome dharmas. Ninth, not encountering the eight difficulties. Tenth, not following the stream of distraction. Eleventh, the mind being without arrogance. Twelfth, being able to accept good instruction. Thirteenth, not losing right mindfulness. Fourteenth, the mind being without heedlessness. Fifteenth, dwelling in a proper land. Sixteenth, being able to guard the sense doors. Seventeenth, being able to be moderate in eating. Eighteenth, constantly awakening to practice in the first and last watches of the night. Nineteenth, settling the mind when dwelling alone in quiet places. Twentieth, observing as it truly is.

"Elders! There are twenty-two matters that a person who has gone forth should repeatedly contemplate. What are the twenty-two? First, reflect: 'My appearance is now unattractive; I have abandoned the lovable characteristics of the household life.' This first matter a renunciant should repeatedly contemplate. Second, reflect: 'I now wear robes of ruined and muted color.' Third, reflect: 'My adornment differs from that of worldly people.' Fourth, reflect: 'My livelihood depends upon the four assemblies.' Fifth, reflect: 'I must now always seek my livelihood from others, namely robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine for illness.' Sixth, reflect: 'For the rest of my life, regarding sensual desire in the human realm, I have accepted restriction.' Seventh, reflect: 'For the rest of my life, regarding worldly amusements and pleasures, I have permanently accepted prohibition.' Eighth, reflect: 'According to my precepts, will I be admonished and reproached for my own conduct, or will I not?' Ninth, reflect: 'Will my good companions in practice refrain from reproaching me within the Dharma?' Tenth, reflect: 'Will I be able to keep this body from being harmed?' Eleventh, reflect: 'Am I partaking of the food and drink of this land without empty fruit?' Twelfth, reflect: 'Am I able to settle my mind when dwelling alone in an empty place?' Thirteenth, reflect: 'What have I attained? Can I pass these days and nights?' Fourteenth, reflect: 'Regarding the quiescence of all formations, do I enter and establish a mind of faith and delight in the attainment of emptiness, the extinction of craving, dispassion, cessation, and the unconditioned nirvāṇa?' Fifteenth, reflect: 'Regarding the characteristics of impermanence, insubstantiality, emptiness, unreality, and decay of the aggregates, do I enter and establish a mind of faith and delight?' Sixteenth, reflect: 'Among the four fruits of the śramaṇa, have I already realized any one fruit, so that when death approaches, if wise companions come to question me, I may answer without doubt or fear, with a mind of joy, and correctly declare what I have attained? And when I so declare, will it be in accordance with truth?' Seventeenth, reflect: 'I have not yet transcended the dharma of birth; in future lives I have not yet crossed over rebirth in whatever realm.' Eighteenth, reflect: 'I have not yet transcended the characteristic of old age.' Nineteenth, reflect: 'I have not yet transcended the calamity of illness.' Twentieth, reflect: 'I have not yet transcended the calamity of death; I have not crossed over the dharma of death.' Twenty-first, reflect: 'Separation from all beloved and cherished ones, each going to different places, not meeting, not gathering, not knowing one another, must certainly come to be.' Twenty-second, reflect: 'I belong to my actions, am controlled by my actions. Actions are the cause; actions are the basis. Whatever actions I do, whether good or evil, I shall certainly receive their result in accordance with my own actions.'

"Such matters a renunciant should repeatedly contemplate. If a renunciant repeatedly contemplates these twenty-two matters, the name of śramaṇa becomes complete. When the name of śramaṇa becomes complete, seven kinds of characteristics become complete. What are the seven? First, the characteristic of constant, unceasing practice. Second, the ability to constantly teach others without weariness. Third, attaining the characteristic of non-attachment. Fourth, attaining the characteristic of non-anger. Fifth, attaining the characteristic of right mindfulness. Sixth, attaining the characteristic of freedom from overestimation. Seventh, attaining all the requisites of livelihood. For the accomplishment of these, one attains concentration. Thus these seven characteristics all become complete.

"Elders! This mind, consciousness, and awareness has for a long time played among forms. Sounds, smells, tastes, and tangibles are likewise. Form, sound, smell, taste, and tangible objects are what nourish and give rise to such a mind. Due to improper attention, this mind cannot be established in the realm of the deathless.

"Elders! There are twenty characteristics by which an ordinary person should repeatedly frighten and disgust their own mind. What are the twenty? First, I am now empty and lacking in noble qualities. Second, I must now die, and I cannot conquer death. Third, I will now fall to the lowest depths. Fourth, I must now travel through places of fear and difficulty. Fifth, I do not now know the place without fear. Sixth, I do not now understand the straight path of light. Seventh, I have not now attained an undistracted and concentrated mind. Eighth, In future lives, the suffering of birth will be unbearable. Ninth, The gathering of favorable conditions cannot always be obtained. Tenth, the killer is always pursuing me. Eleventh, the six realms of existence have no shelter for me. Twelfth, I have not yet attained liberation from the four evil destinies. Thirteenth, I have not yet freed myself from immeasurable kinds of views. Fourteenth, I have not yet built a levee to prevent the future flood of actions leading to the uninterrupted hells. Fifteenth, I have not yet brought an end to the continuity of beginningless birth and death. Sixteenth, If one does not intentionally create wholesome actions, they will never be accomplished. Seventeenth, No one else can create actions on another's behalf. Eighteenth, Without creating them, there is no good fortune. Nineteenth, Actions already created cannot be forgotten; they will certainly bear fruit. Twentieth, I am now ignorant, covered by ignorance, and the calamity of death is certain. For these reasons, an ordinary person, with these characteristics, should repeatedly frighten and disgust their own mind.

"If an ordinary person, using these twenty characteristics, frightens and admonishes and restrains their own mind, then regarding twenty kinds of dharmas, one quickly attains stable abiding. What are the twenty? First, through the characteristic of correcting and cultivating the mind, the mind quickly becomes stable. Second, through knowing the characteristic of mental sequence. Third, through the characteristic of single-pointed abiding of mind. Fourth, through the characteristic of entering samādhi. Fifth, through the characteristic of emerging from samādhi. Sixth, through attaining the characteristic of suppressing the mind. Seventh, through attaining the characteristic of uplifting the mind. Eighth, through attaining the characteristic of equanimity of mind. Ninth, through attaining distance from improper objects. Tenth, through attaining the proper field of practice. Eleventh, through correctly receiving right instruction. Twelfth, through frequently cultivating the characteristic of disgust. Thirteenth, through frequently cultivating the characteristic of joy. Fourteenth, through being able to attain the superior knowledge of the Dharma gate. Fifteenth, through correctly relying on one's teacher. Sixteenth, through the mind quickly becoming stable in right cultivation of wholesome conduct. Seventeenth, through correctly cultivating mindfulness of breathing. Eighteenth, through correctly cultivating the contemplation of impurity. Nineteenth, through correctly cultivating the four foundations of mindfulness. Twentieth, through correctly cultivating the contemplation of the four noble truths, the mind quickly becomes stable in the true characteristic.

"Elders! If a noble disciple can properly cultivate right effort in this manner, one should further know that there are eleven kinds of obstructive dharmas that cause difficulty. What are the eleven? First, frequently gathering in assemblies. Second, attachment to food and drink. Third, constantly delighting in starting projects. Fourth, constantly delighting in talk. Fifth, constantly delighting in sleep. Sixth, constantly delighting in idle chatter. Seventh, constantly delighting in not being alone. Eighth, cherishing one's own body. Ninth, the mind being constantly distracted. Tenth, the mind being constantly heedless and not delighting in practice. Eleventh, dwelling in an improper place.

"Elders! These eleven kinds, called obstructive dharmas, when not yet subdued and eliminated, for their elimination there are ten superior knowledges that should certainly be cultivated. What are the ten? First, the superior knowledge of samādhi. Second, the superior knowledge of abiding in concentration. Third, the superior knowledge of emerging from concentration. Fourth, the superior knowledge of suppression. Fifth, the superior knowledge of uplifting. Sixth, the superior knowledge of equanimity. Seventh, the superior knowledge of skillful progress. Eighth, the superior knowledge of skillful withdrawal. Ninth, the superior knowledge of skillful means. Tenth, the superior knowledge of attraction and guidance.

"Elders! If a noble disciple can attain these ten kinds of superior knowledge, then there are thirteen kinds of joy that arise from within, based on reliance. What are the thirteen? First, when a person of faith correctly reflects on the Great Teacher, at that time joy arises from within. This is called the first joy based on reliance for a person of faith. Second, correctly reflecting on the true Dharma. Third, correctly reflecting on the Saṃgha. Fourth, correctly reflecting on one's own and others' pure moral conduct. Fifth, correctly reflecting on one's own and others' meritorious giving. Sixth, correctly reflecting on the attainments of one's own and others' path cultivation: at these times, joy arises from within. Seventh, one should reflect: 'The World-Honored One has, for my sake, extinguished the dharmas of suffering.' Based on this right reflection, at that time joy arises from within. Eighth, one should reflect: 'The World-Honored One has, for my sake, given rise to and increased numerous dharmas of peace, benefit, and happiness.' Ninth, one should reflect: 'The World-Honored One has, for my sake, prevented and cut off immeasurable obstructive and unwholesome dharmas.' Tenth, one further reflects: 'The World-Honored One has, for my sake, given rise to and increased immeasurable wholesome dharmas that aid the path.' A person of faith, through these four reflections, at that time gives rise to joy from within.

"Elders! If a noble disciple can frequently attain the thirteen kinds of joy, based on reliance upon four dharmas, then five dharmas reach the fullness of cultivation. What are the four dharmas of reliance? First, faith and delight. Second, vigorous effort. Third, settling the mind when dwelling alone in a quiet place. Fourth, being insatiable in cultivation and cessation. What are the five dharmas that reach the fullness of cultivation? First, mental ease. Second, mental joy. Third, mental tranquility. Fourth, mental happiness. Fifth, mental concentration.

"Elders! When these five dharmas grow and reach fullness, then eight thorns are removed and destroyed. What are the eight? First, the thorn of desire. Second, the thorn of anger. Third, the thorn of delusion. Fourth, the thorn of conceit. Fifth, the thorn of craving. Sixth, the thorn of views. Seventh, the thorn of ignorance. Eighth, the thorn of doubt.

"Because these eight thorns are removed and destroyed, the noble disciple then attains the ten dharmas of one beyond training. What are the ten? First, the right view of one beyond training. Second, the right intention of one beyond training. Third, the right speech of one beyond training. Fourth, the right action of one beyond training. Fifth, the right livelihood of one beyond training. Sixth, the right effort of one beyond training. Seventh, the right mindfulness of one beyond training. Eighth, the right concentration of one beyond training. Ninth, the right liberation of one beyond training. Tenth, the right knowledge and vision of liberation of one beyond training.

"Elders! Because a noble disciple can realize the ten dharmas of one beyond training, one always abides in correspondence with them, without any falling away. This noble disciple is separated from the five parts, should be in correspondence with the six parts, has one dharma as guardian, attains the four reliances, abandons one-sided grasping at truth, has gone beyond seeking, has unclouded thinking, has pacified bodily actions, is well liberated in mind, is well liberated in wisdom, dwells alone in purity, has accomplished what was to be done. Such a one is called a supreme person.

"Elders! This final consciousness is not nourished and sustained by form, sound, or other objects. Conditioned on nothing whatsoever, at that time all subsequent rebirths are permanently severed. This is called the final end of suffering. Because this meaning is taught, it is called the Extensive Meaning of the Dharma.

"Elders! I have now taught you the Dharma: good in the beginning, good in the middle, good at the end, good in meaning and good in expression, pure and unmixed, complete and pure. I have already revealed to you the brahma conduct, namely the Extensive Meaning of the Dharma. What I earlier promised to teach, I have now taught."

At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra, having spoken this sūtra, the wise companions and the immeasurable assembly who had not yet realized the true meaning now realized it, and those who had not yet attained the fruits of the śramaṇa path now all attained them. They were delighted and joyful, and faithfully received and practiced it. The Venerable Śāriputra spoke thus in accordance with the truth.

---

This sūtra is drawn from a section of the Madhyamāgama.

In the fourth year of Tianjia of the Chen dynasty, the year guiwei, on the tenth day of the eleventh month, at the Zhizhi Monastery in Guangzhou, the Tripiṭaka Master Ācārya Paramārtha was invited to translate this sūtra.
