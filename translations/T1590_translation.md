# Twenty Verses on Consciousness-Only

## *Wéishí Èrshí Lùn* (唯識二十論)

Taishō 1590

Composed by the Bodhisattva Vasubandhu (*Shìqīn*, 世親). Translated by Xuánzàng (*Xuánzàng*, 玄奘) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is one of the foundational treatises of the Yogācāra (Consciousness-Only) school, known in Sanskrit as the Viṃśatikāvijñaptimātratāsiddhi. Vasubandhu (c. 4th-5th century CE) composed it to defend the thesis that the three realms of existence are consciousness-only (vijñaptimātra), refuting objections that the absence of external objects would make the spatial and temporal determinacy of experience, the intersubjective consistency of perception, and the causal efficacy of objects inexplicable. Through a series of verses (kārikā) and prose commentary (vṛtti), Vasubandhu deploys the analogies of dreams, hell-guardians, hungry ghosts, and the indivisibility of atoms to argue that all apparent external objects arise from the transformation of consciousness alone. This translation by Xuánzàng is the standard Chinese version and a cornerstone text of the Fǎxiàng (法相) school.*

---

To establish the Mahāyāna [thesis that] the three realms are consciousness-only, because the sūtras say "the three realms are mind-only." "Mind" (*citta*), "mentation" (*manas*), "consciousness" (*vijñāna*), and "cognition" (*vijñapti*) are different names [for the same thing]. What is here called "mind" includes the mental factors (*caitta*) as well. "Only" (*mātra*) excludes external objects but does not deny the associated [mental factors]. When inner consciousness arises, it appears as if there were external objects, like someone with an eye-disease seeing hairs, floaters, and such: in all of this, there is not the slightest real meaning.

Regarding precisely this meaning, there is an objection. The verse states:

> If consciousness has no real object,
> Then spatial determinacy, temporal determinacy,
> Indeterminacy of mental streams, and
> Functional efficacy should all be impossible.

**Commentary**: What does this say? If, apart from consciousness, there really were external dharmas such as form, and the consciousness of form and such arose without depending on form and such, then why does this consciousness arise in certain places and not everywhere? Why does consciousness arise at certain times in a given place, and not at all times? When many mental streams are in the same place at the same time, why is it not determined that each one's consciousness arises identically? Just as someone with an eye-disease sees hairs and floaters, whereas one without the disease does not have such consciousness arise. Furthermore, for what reason do the hairs and such seen by those with eye-disease lack the function of real hair, and the food, drink, weapons, poison, and clothing obtained in dreams lack real function, and gandharva-cities lack real city-function? Yet other real hairs and such do have function. If truly all are equally devoid of external objects of form and such, and there is only inner consciousness arising in the likeness of external objects, then spatial determinacy, temporal determinacy, indeterminacy of mental streams, and functionally efficacious things should all be impossible.

It is not the case that all are impossible. The verse states:

> Spatial and temporal determinacy are like dreams;
> Indeterminacy of streams is like hungry ghosts
> Who all see rivers of pus;
> Like dreams, there is effective function with real consequence.

**Commentary**: "Like dreams" means like what is seen in dreams. In dreams, although there are no real external objects, one nevertheless sees villages, gardens, men, women, and other things in certain places and not everywhere. In those very places, one sometimes sees villages and gardens and sometimes does not. Therefore, although there are no real external objects apart from consciousness, the determinacy of place and time is not impossible to establish. "Like hungry ghosts" indicates that, like hungry ghosts whose shared karmic ripening causes many of them to gather and all see rivers full of pus (called "pus-rivers" as one says "ghee-jar" to mean a jar full of ghee), it is not the case that only one sees this. The word "and so on" further indicates that they may also see feces and such, or see beings holding swords and staves, guarding and preventing them from eating. Therefore, although there are no real external objects apart from consciousness, the indeterminacy across multiple mental streams is established. Furthermore, as in dreams where, although objects are not real, there is effective consequence such as the loss of vital fluids: therefore, although there are no real external objects, the principle of illusory functional efficacy is established.

Thus, relying on separate analogies, the four principles of spatial determinacy and the rest are each established. Furthermore, the verse states:

> All is like hell:
> Hell-beings all see the guards and such,
> Who can inflict torment.
> Therefore all four principles are established.

**Commentary**: One should know that this single analogy of hell establishes all [four principles] at once. "Like hell" refers to those beings in hell who suffer tormenting afflictions. In hell, although there are no truly real sentient beings constituting the hell-guards and such, those beings, through the power of their shared karmic ripening, in the same place, at the same time, as many continuous streams, all jointly see hell-guards, dogs, crows, iron mountains, and other things coming to their location to inflict torment. Therefore, although there are no real external objects apart from consciousness, the four principles of spatial determinacy and the rest are all established.

Why should we not accept that the hell-guards and such are real sentient beings? Because it would be unreasonable. First, they should not be classified as hell-beings, since they do not suffer the same torments. If they mutually tormented each other, one could not establish them as belonging to that hell. Furthermore, since these hell-guards are equal in size, strength, and form, they should not be terrified of each other. They should be unable to endure the blazing heat of the iron ground that constantly burns, so how could they torment others there? [The objector says:] Hell-beings are not the only ones born there; just as in the heavens there are animals, so in hell there may be animals or hungry ghosts serving as hell-guards. This defense fails. The verse states:

> As with animals born in heaven,
> It is not so in hell:
> The animals and ghosts you posit
> Do not suffer the torments there.

**Commentary**: Animals born in the heavens necessarily have karma capable of producing the pleasurable environment there, and being born there, they certainly enjoy the pleasures produced by that environment. But the hell-guards do not suffer the environmental torments of hell. Therefore one should not accept that animals or hungry ghosts are born in the hells.

If so, one should accept that through the power of the karma of those in hell, extraordinary great elements are produced, giving rise to special forms, colors, sizes, and powers, upon which the designation "hell-guards" is imposed, producing terror in them, manifesting various movements of hands and feet and differentiated actions, just as the Battling-Ram Mountains alternately separate and crash together, and the Sword-Forest trees' thorns alternately lower and rise. This is not entirely without basis, but it is still unreasonable. The verse states:

> If you accept that by the power of karma,
> Extraordinary great elements are produced
> Undergoing such transformations,
> Why not accept this of consciousness instead?

**Commentary**: Why not accept that consciousness undergoes such transformations through the power of karma, rather than insisting on great elements? Furthermore, the verse states:

> Karma perfumes one locus;
> You claim the fruit is in another locus.
> That the perfumed consciousness bears fruit:
> What reason is there for not accepting this?

**Commentary**: You claim that in the hells, by the power of one's own karma, differentiated great elements are produced undergoing transformations of form and such. The perfuming of that karma should reasonably be accepted as residing in the continuum of consciousness, not elsewhere. Yet you do not accept that the perfumed consciousness undergoes fruition-transformation, while claiming that the fruit exists where there is no perfuming. What reason is there for this?

[The objector says:] "There is scripture as the reason. If there were only consciousness appearing as form and such, with no separate form and such, the Buddha should not have taught that there are sense-bases of form and such." This scripture is not valid as a reason, because it has a different intent. The verse states:

> In dependence on those to be taught,
> The World-Honored One, with hidden intent,
> Taught the existence of sense-bases of form and such,
> Just as he taught the existence of beings born by transformation.

**Commentary**: Just as the Buddha spoke of "beings born by transformation" with the hidden intent of the unbroken continuity of the mind-stream going to future lives, not affirming that there really are such transformed beings; so too, speaking of sentient beings with no real self but only dharmic causes, he taught the sense-bases of form and the other ten, also with hidden intent, adapted to those to be taught who would benefit from that teaching. It is not that [these bases] separately and really exist.

Based on what hidden intent did he teach the ten [bases] of form and such? The verse states:

> Consciousness arises from its own seed
> And manifests in the appearance of objects.
> To establish the inner and outer bases,
> The Buddha taught them as ten.

**Commentary**: Consciousness appearing as form arises from the conjunction and differentiated transformation of its own seeds. The Buddha, in dependence on those seeds and the manifested forms, taught them respectively as the eye-base and the form-base. Likewise, up to consciousness appearing as tangibles, arising from its own seeds: the Buddha taught them respectively as the body-base and the tangible-base. Based on this hidden intent, he taught the ten [bases] of form and such.

What advantage does this teaching with hidden intent have? The verse states:

> Based on this teaching, one enters
> The selflessness of persons.
> The selflessness of dharmas as conceived:
> One enters through a further teaching.

**Commentary**: Based on the teaching of the twelve sense-bases, those to be taught can enter the selflessness of persons (*pudgalanairātmya*). If one understands that from the twelve dharmas the six consciousnesses operate, with no seer, no hearer, and so on up to no knower, those who should receive the teaching of the selflessness of beings can thereby realize it. Furthermore, based on the additional teaching of consciousness-only, those to be taught can enter the selflessness of dharmas as conceived (*dharmanairātmya*). If one understands that only consciousness manifests in the likeness of dharmas such as form, and that among these there are no dharmas with the characteristics of form and such, those who should receive the teaching of the selflessness of all dharmas can thereby realize it.

If one knows that all dharmas are in every respect nonexistent and thereby enters the selflessness of dharmas, then consciousness-only too would be ultimately nonexistent: what would there be to establish? It is not by knowing that all dharmas are in every respect nonexistent that one is said to enter the selflessness of dharmas. Rather, one realizes the selflessness of dharmas by comprehending that the self-natures and distinctions universally imagined (*parikalpita*) by deluded beings are without self. It is not because the domain of all buddhas, the ineffable dharma-nature (*dharmatā*), is also entirely nonexistent that it is called "selflessness of dharmas." The nature of consciousness-only as conceived by other consciousnesses is also without substance: this is called "selflessness of dharmas." Otherwise, if the objects conceived by other consciousnesses were real, the principle of consciousness-only would be untenable, since one would be granting that those other consciousnesses have real objects. By this reasoning, the teaching of consciousness-only universally enables entry into the selflessness of all dharmas; it does not deny all existing natures.

How do we further know that the Buddha taught the sense-bases of form and such with hidden intent, and not that there really are external dharmas of form and such serving as distinct objects of the consciousnesses of form and such? The verse states:

> Because that object is neither one,
> Nor many atoms,
> Nor a combination of atoms and such:
> Because atoms are not established.

**Commentary**: If there really were external bases of form and such, each serving as a distinct object of the corresponding consciousness, such external objects should either be unitary (as the Vaiśeṣika school maintains, positing a real "whole" (*avayavin*) of form); or they should be many (holding that many real atoms individually serve as objects); or many atoms combined or aggregated should serve as the object. The external object should not be unitary, because the substance of the "whole" cannot be apprehended as distinct from its parts. Nor should it be many, because individual atoms cannot be apprehended. Nor should combinations or aggregates serve as the object, because a single real atom is logically impossible. How is it impossible? The verse states:

> When an atom combines with six others,
> It should have six parts.
> If it shares the same locus as six,
> The aggregate should be atom-sized.

**Commentary**: If a single atom combines on six sides each with one atom, it should have six parts, since one place cannot contain another. If the six atoms share the same locus as a single atom, then all composite matter should be atom-sized, since they mutually do not exceed one another's size; thus composite matter would also be invisible.

The Vaibhāṣika masters of Kaśmīra say: "Atoms do not combine, since they have no directional parts; this avoids the above fault. Only composite matter has combination, since it has directional parts." This too is wrong. The verse states:

> Since atoms have no combination,
> In the aggregate, what combines?
> Or if combination does not hold,
> It is not because of lacking directional parts.

**Commentary**: Since apart from atoms there is no separate composite matter, and atoms do not combine, what is it in the aggregate that combines? If the defense is that "composite matter also does not combine with anything," then one should not say "atoms do not combine because they lack directional parts," since aggregates, though having directional parts, are also not admitted to combine. Therefore the non-combination of atoms is not due to their lacking directional parts. Hence a single real atom is not established.

Furthermore, whether atoms are admitted to combine or not, there are faults in either case. Whether atoms are admitted to have parts or not, both lead to great failures. Why? The verse states:

> If atoms have directional parts,
> They should logically not be unitary.
> If without parts, there should be no shadow or obstruction;
> And aggregates, being no different, should be identical with a single atom.

**Commentary**: Since a single atom has different aspects in its six directions and is constituted of multiple parts, how can it be unitary? If a single atom has no different directional parts, then when the sun rises and light strikes it, how could a shadow appear on the other side, since there is no further part that light does not reach? Furthermore, if atoms lack directional parts, how can they mutually obstruct each other, since there is no further part that another cannot occupy? If they do not obstruct each other, all atoms should occupy the same locus, and all material aggregates should be identical with a single atom, as previously argued.

Why not admit that shadow and obstruction belong to aggregates, not to atoms? Can aggregates have shadow and obstruction if they are not different from atoms? No; for if they could, aggregates should not be dual. That is, if composite matter is not different from atoms, shadow and obstruction should not belong to composite matter [either]. Whether one posits atoms or aggregates based on differences of arrangement, neither is a single real thing. What use is it to deliberate whether something is an atom or an aggregate?

This has still not excluded the appearance of external form and such. What further appearance is there? Namely, what serves as the object of the eye and such: blue and other colors, which are truly the nature of form and such. We should carefully examine: is this object of the eye and such, the true nature of blue and such, one or many? Either way there are faults. The faults of multiplicity are as before. Unity is also unreasonable. The verse states:

> If one, there should be no sequential traversal,
> No simultaneous arrival and non-arrival,
> No plurality of separate things,
> And no invisible fine objects.

**Commentary**: If all blue and such that serve as objects of the eye, without any separation, were one single thing, there should be no principle of gradually traversing a great land, since putting down one foot would reach everywhere. Also, there should not simultaneously be arrival at one place and non-arrival at another, since logically one thing at one time cannot be both reached and not reached. Also, in one area there should not be many elephants, horses, and such with spaces between them; if where one is, the others are also, how could they be distinguished? Or how could two things be in one place with some reached and some not, with empty space visible between them? Also, there should be no invisible fine creatures such as water-insects, since being in the same place as coarse objects, they should be equal in size.

If one says that due to differences of characteristics these become separate objects, not due to any other principle, then one must necessarily accept that these different objects can be further analyzed into many atoms. It has already been demonstrated that atoms are not single real things. Therefore, apart from consciousness, neither the roots (eye, ear, etc.) nor the objects (form, sound, etc.) can be established. By this, the meaning of consciousness-only is well established.

The existence or nonexistence of dharmas is determined by the means of valid cognition (*pramāṇa*), and among all means of cognition, direct perception (*pratyakṣa*) is supreme. If there are no external objects, how can there be this cognition: "I now directly perceive such-and-such an object"? This proof does not hold. The verse states:

> Direct perception is like dreams and such.
> At the time the cognition of direct perception has arisen,
> Both the seeing and the object are already gone:
> How can one claim there is direct perception?

**Commentary**: Just as in dreams, although there are no real external objects, such cognition nevertheless occurs: so too should it be understood for cognition at other times. Furthermore, at the time one has the cognition "I now directly perceive such-and-such form," the seeing is already gone, since this necessarily involves the discriminating mental consciousness (*manovijñāna*), and by that time the eye-consciousness and such have already ceased. For those who hold the doctrine of momentariness, at the time of this cognition, the present object of form and such has also already perished. How can there be direct perception at that time?

[The objector says:] "Because one must have previously directly experienced [the object], the mental consciousness can remember it; therefore there certainly was a previously experienced object. Seeing this object is admitted to be direct perception. By this, the real existence of external objects is established." This too fails as proof that there must be external objects through prior experience and subsequent memory. Why? The verse states:

> As explained, consciousness resembling objects:
> From this, memory arises.

**Commentary**: As previously explained, although there are no external objects, the eye-consciousness and such appear in the likeness of external objects. From this, in a subsequent moment, the discriminating mental consciousness associated with memory appears in the likeness of the prior object. This is precisely what is called "remembering what was previously experienced." Therefore, using subsequent memory to prove that what was previously seen was a really existing external object: this argument does not succeed.

If, as in dreams where there are no real objects yet consciousness arises, the same is true in waking, then just as the world naturally knows that dream-objects are unreal, if waking is the same, why does one not naturally know this? Since one does not naturally know that waking objects are unreal, how can waking consciousness be like dream-consciousness, with no real objects at all? This too is not valid proof. The verse states:

> Before awakening, one cannot know
> That what is seen in dreams is unreal.

**Commentary**: Just as before awakening one does not know that dream-objects are not real external things, and only upon awakening does one know: so too, beings in the world, habituated to deluded discrimination as if in a dream, do not know that all they see is unreal. Until they attain true enlightenment, they cannot know this on their own. When at some point they attain the supramundane antidote, the non-discriminating wisdom (*nirvikalpajñāna*), that is called "true enlightenment." After that, when the subsequently-attained pure mundane wisdom arises, one knows as it truly is that those objects are unreal. The principle is the same.

If sentient beings, through the differentiated transformations in their own mental continua, give rise to consciousness resembling objects, and this is not produced from external objects as apprehended conditions, then the determinacy of the two kinds of consciousness [virtuous and nonvirtuous] through proximity to good or bad friends and hearing correct or incorrect teachings: without [real] friends and teachings, how can this be established? It can be established. The verse states:

[Verse on mutual influence of mental streams as dominant conditions:]

**Commentary**: The consciousnesses of all sentient beings, in their own and others' mental continua, serve reciprocally as dominant conditions (*adhipatipratyaya*). According to the case, the two kinds of consciousness are determined. Because of the differentiation of consciousness in another's continuum, differentiated consciousness arises in one's own continuum. Each becomes determined without [requiring] external objects.

If, as in dreams where objects are unreal yet consciousness arises, waking is the same, why do the virtuous and evil actions performed in dreams and in waking bear different results of pleasant and unpleasant fruition? The verse states:

> Mind impaired by sleep:
> Dream and waking fruits differ.

**Commentary**: In the dream state, the mind is impaired by sleep and its power is enfeebled. The waking mind is not so. Therefore the actions performed [in each state] bear different karmic fruition, superior and inferior, not because of external objects.

If there is only consciousness and no body, speech, and such, how are sheep and such killed by others? If sheep and such do not die from others' harm, how do butchers incur the sin of killing? The verse states:

> Through the transformation of another's consciousness,
> The act of killing occurs,
> Just as through the mental power of ghosts and such,
> Others lose mindfulness and such.

**Commentary**: Just as through the mental power of ghosts and such, other sentient beings lose mindfulness, have dreams, or become possessed, and various anomalous events occur; and just as through the mental power of those with supernatural abilities, others see various things in dreams (as when, through the power of the great Kātyāyana's aspiration, King Sarṇa and others dreamed of strange events; and as when, through the power of the forest-dwelling sage's anger, King Vaimacitra dreamed of strange events): so too, through the transformative power of another's consciousness, events that violate another's life-faculty arise. One should know that "death" means the severance of the continuum of the class-share (*nikāyasabhāga*) through the transformative differentiation of consciousness.

Furthermore, the verse states:

> How did the Daṇḍaka and other forests become empty?
> How was it through the sages' anger?
> Mental punishment is held to be the gravest sin:
> How can this further be established?

**Commentary**: If one does not accept that through the transformative power of another's consciousness, beings can die, how did the World-Honored One, in order to establish that mental punishment is the gravest sin, ask the elder Upāli: "Have you not heard what caused the Daṇḍaka forest, the Mātaṅga forest, and the Kaliṅga forest all to become empty and desolate?" The elder replied: "Gautama, I have heard it was due to the anger of sages." If one insists that divine spirits, out of respect for the sages and knowing their displeasure, killed those beings, and that it was not solely due to the sages' mental anger, how can one cite this to establish that mental punishment is the gravest sin, surpassing bodily and verbal [punishment]? Therefore one should know: solely through the sages' anger, those beings died. This is well established.

If there is only consciousness, do other-mind-cognizing wisdoms (*paracittajñāna*) know other minds or not? Either way, there are faults. If they cannot know, how are they called "other-mind-cognizing"? If they can know, consciousness-only should be untenable. Although they know other minds, they do not know them as they truly are. The verse states:

> How does the other-mind-cognizing wisdom
> Know its object not as it truly is?
> Like the wisdom that knows one's own mind,
> It does not know as the Buddha's domain.

**Commentary**: Why do other-mind-cognizing wisdoms not know their objects as they truly are? Like the wisdom that knows one's own mind. Why does the wisdom that knows one's own mind not know its object as they truly are? Because of ignorance. Both wisdoms, each obscured by ignorance, do not know the ineffable domain of the Buddha's pure wisdom. Both do not know their objects as they truly are, because they falsely manifest in the likeness of external objects, and because the discrimination of grasped and grasper has not yet been severed.

The principle of consciousness-only has boundless categories of discernment, profound and difficult to fathom. Who but a buddha could comprehensively discern them? The verse states:

> I have, according to my own ability,
> Briefly established the meaning of consciousness-only.
> Herein, all the varieties
> Are inconceivable, the domain of buddhas.

**Commentary**: The categories of the principle of consciousness-only are boundless. I have, according to my own ability, briefly established it. All the remaining varieties are beyond thought, transcending the domain of all discursive investigation. This principle is the domain of buddhas alone, for the buddha world-honored ones have unobstructed wisdom regarding all objects and all varieties.
