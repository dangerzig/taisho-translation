# 離睡經
## Sūtra on Dispelling Drowsiness

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0047

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the Deer Park at Bhesakalāvana in the land of Bhagga. At that time, the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana was in the village of Kalyāṇamitra in Magadha. The Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana, dwelling alone in a quiet place, had fallen asleep while doing walking meditation. The World-Honored One, knowing that the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana had fallen asleep while doing walking meditation alone in a quiet place, thereupon entered the samādhi corresponding to that state. With the mind of samādhi, as swiftly as a strong man might bend or extend his arm, the World-Honored One vanished from the Deer Park at Bhesakalāvana in the land of Bhagga and appeared before the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana in the village of Kalyāṇamitra in Magadha. The World-Honored One then arose from samādhi and addressed the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "Maudgalyāyana! Maudgalyāyana! Are you drowsy?"

"Indeed, World-Honored One."

"On account of what perception are you drowsy? Do not pursue perceptions, do not discriminate among perceptions, do not proliferate them; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If your drowsiness is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, recite at length and review the Dharma as you have heard and studied it; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If it is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, expound at length to others the Dharma as you have heard and studied it; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If it is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, mentally recollect and practice the Dharma as you have studied and heard it; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If it is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, splash cold water on your eyes and wash your limbs; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If it is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, rub your ears with both hands; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If it is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, go outside the lecture hall, look in all four directions, and observe the stars; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If it is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, go to an open space and walk about, guarding the sense faculties, keeping the mind on generosity, and maintaining full awareness afterward; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If it is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, return from your walking, take up your sitting cloth and spread it on the bed, and sit in the full cross-legged posture; in this way, drowsiness will be dispelled. If it is still not dispelled, then, Maudgalyāyana, return to the lecture hall, fold the uttarāsaṅga in four and spread it on the bed, place the saṃghāṭī before your head, lie on your right side with foot upon foot, cultivate the perception of light, keep the mind undistracted, and constantly maintain the intention to rise, abiding in contemplation.

"Maudgalyāyana, do not take pleasure in the bed, do not take pleasure in lying on the right side, do not take pleasure in sleep, and do not relish the world's reverence as a delight. Why is this? Maudgalyāyana, I do not say that one should draw near to all things, nor do I say that one should not draw near to all things.

"How, Maudgalyāyana, do I describe non-association? Maudgalyāyana, I say one should not be intimate with householders. Maudgalyāyana, if one dwells in intimacy with householders, there is only discussion and no shared study of the teachings. From such discussion, flattery and arrogance arise. From arrogance, jealousy arises. With jealousy, there is no peace of mind. Maudgalyāyana, when there is no peace, samādhi becomes remote. This, Maudgalyāyana, is what I call the way of non-association.

"How, Maudgalyāyana, do I describe proper association? Maudgalyāyana, go to a quiet place, make a grass mat as a seat, remain silent, keep far from all that is unwholesome, distance yourself from crowds, and constantly sit in contemplation. This, Maudgalyāyana, is what I call the way of proper association.

"Maudgalyāyana, when entering a village for alms, do not seek material gain and do not seek reverence. Maudgalyāyana, having set aside the mind that seeks gain and reverence, then enter the village for alms. Maudgalyāyana, when entering a village for alms, do not enter a household with expectations. Why is this? Maudgalyāyana, householders' homes are full of worldly entanglements. If a bhikṣu enters a householder's home and the householder does not speak with him, that bhikṣu thinks: 'Who has spoken against me to this householder, so that the householder will not speak with me?' Resentment arises. With resentment comes conceit. With conceit comes agitation. Maudgalyāyana, when there is agitation, samādhi becomes remote.

"Maudgalyāyana, when teaching the Dharma, do not seek to prevail over others; cultivate the mind of non-contention. If one seeks to prevail, excessive argumentation follows. From excessive argumentation comes conceit. From conceit comes jealousy. From jealousy comes agitation. Maudgalyāyana, with agitation, I say, samādhi becomes remote.

"Maudgalyāyana, when teaching the Dharma, make it beneficial, speak with decisiveness, do not disparage the words of others, and let your discourse be like the lion's roar. Thus, Maudgalyāyana, should you train."

Thereupon the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana rose from his seat, arranged his robe on one shoulder, joined his palms toward the World-Honored One, and addressed the World-Honored One: "World-Honored One, how does a bhikṣu reach the ultimate end, the ultimate freedom from defilement, the ultimate practice of the holy life?"

"Maudgalyāyana, whatever feelings a bhikṣu experiences, whether painful, pleasant, or neither painful nor pleasant, one should contemplate those feelings as impermanent, contemplate them as subject to decay, contemplate them as free from attachment, contemplate them as ceasing, contemplate them as rightly discerned, contemplate them as the abode of stillness; thus should one contemplate those feelings. Contemplating those feelings as impermanent, contemplating them as subject to decay, contemplating them as free from attachment, contemplating them as ceasing, contemplating them as stilled, contemplating them as the abode of stillness, one does not cling to this world. Not clinging, one is free from fear. Free from fear, one attains nirvāṇa with remainder. Birth is ended, the holy life has been established, what was to be done has been done, and one knows name-and-form as it truly is. This, bhikṣu Maudgalyāyana, is how one reaches the ultimate end, the ultimate freedom from defilement, the ultimate holy life, the ultimate practice of the holy life."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The Venerable Maudgalyāyana, having heard what the World-Honored One said, rejoiced and was delighted.
