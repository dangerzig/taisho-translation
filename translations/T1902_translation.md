# 受用三水要行法
## Essential Methods for Using the Three Types of Water

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1902

Tang dynasty, compiled by Yijing (義淨).

---

In accordance with the sacred teachings and the water currently used by monastics in the western lands, there are three distinct kinds: first, time-water; second, non-time water; third, contact-use water.

**Time-water** refers to water filtered and examined for the absence of insects by a novice or layperson with their own hands. Before noon, one may freely receive and drink it. However, if a fully ordained monk's hand touches the basin, filter-cloth, or ladle, that water is unfit to enter the mouth, let alone to be used for eating, because it has been defiled by contact. Just as the water commonly used in the monastery: how can a fully ordained monk touch it? Even though a fully ordained monk has not touched it, after noon it is not permitted to be drunk. Although the substance of the water itself has no defilement, since it has been touched by laypeople and the like, it carries grease and impurity and is not perfectly clean. Therefore it must be formally received.

**Non-time pure water** means that fully ordained bhikṣus, novices, and all persons of mindful intent must all wash with bean-powder or fine scrapings, washing from the wrist to the four fingers, until they are completely clean and free of grease. The earthenware basin and filter-cloth must all be freshly clean and not stained by grease. Only then may this water be filtered. All copper bowls and copper ladles must be scrubbed with ash to remove grease before water may be drawn. If such items cannot be obtained, wooden vessels that are not dyed, that have never been stained by greasy contact, and that are washed clean of dust every day without interruption may also be used. If water is to be stored regularly, it should be kept in a pure vessel. The pure vessel should be of earthenware, not a copper water-pot, because the inside of a copper vessel produces verdigris, which is impure, and cannot be scrubbed clean with ash. If one pulls out the copper pin and wipes it, one can tell whether it is clean or dirty. Copper is purified by scrubbing with ash: this is personally stated in the sacred teachings. If washed with bean-powder, only the food-grease is removed; the copper patina is not. One can take a copper spoon and scrub it with ash as a visual test. When the earthenware vessel is empty, it must be rinsed each time and refilled with fresh water only after the interval. In the five lands of India, no one uses a copper vessel as a pure vessel: first, because patina forms and carries defilement; second, because the metallic taste of copper harms people. This pure water, whether at proper or improper times, may be drunk at will. This is a special permission opened by the Buddha, because it is pure. There is no further need for formal reception. If a bhikṣu during the improper time boils medicine, brews tea, or makes honey-water and the like, this water should be used, not the previous time-water, because there would be a transgression. The pots, ladles, bowls, and vessels used must all be kept separate from food-contamination, and all must be scrubbed with ash before cooking. The pure water basins and vessels should be placed in a clean location. The large jar must be covered with a clean object. The vessel should be placed in a bamboo basket. It must not be casually touched with contaminated hands. When one wishes to use the water, first wash the hands clean. One may use dry cow-dung to cleanly wipe the hands until there is no grease, and only then touch the vessel. Or one may use clean silk cloth or a leaf to wrap around the vessel's neck, and only then handle it. The Vinaya says "except for water and tooth-sticks": this refers to this pure water, not the other two kinds. If the tooth-stick is a fresh, moist branch, it should be purified by fire and formally received before chewing. Thus one should know that one cannot simply take the precept text at face value. Whenever one puts water in the mouth, whether for drinking or rinsing, at proper or improper times, one must always wash the hands with bean-powder, cleanly wash both lips, and rinse the mouth two or three times before drinking water. The same applies to eating food. Furthermore, after the midday meal, if one fears the pure vessel has too little water, one should have a layperson hand over the previous time-water. After chewing the tooth-stick and rinsing, one's saliva must not yet be swallowed. One must rinse the mouth three times with this pure water. Only then is it properly pure, and one may swallow saliva. I have personally witnessed the monastic communities of the western and southern seas all practicing this method. Also, the ancient vinaya masters of this land knew of this matter. Yet those who practice it are rare. If one does not practice thus, the remaining grease is not removed, and each swallow incurs an offense. Furthermore, the fast is not validly completed.

**Contact-use water** need only be free of insects; one need not distinguish between pure and defiled, and it may be used. This means filling the contact-vessel for use in the latrine or for washing hands and feet. It must not be casually brought to the mouth for any other use, let alone for eating. These three types of water should be examined for the absence of insects. Up until dawn has not yet appeared, they may all be used as the occasion requires. Once dawn appears, they are no longer permitted, regardless of the quantity, even a single ladle-full from a vessel or a single measure from a basin. All must be examined with a copper bowl under clear light. If there are no insects, even after many days, they may be used without offense. The monastic communities of the western lands, together with the laypeople, regard the five precepts as most urgent. If a visiting monk from elsewhere does not understand this method of pure and defiled water, he is not permitted to enter the monastery. Furthermore, according to western monastic rules, if a monk is seen taking the pure vessel to the latrine or drinking from the contact-vessel, this is regarded as destroying the Dharma, and he is expelled from the monastery. In light of this, I earnestly hope that practitioners will collectively preserve and protect this, causing the Buddha-Dharma to long endure. If one can practice in accordance with the teaching, it is no different from the Buddha being present in the world.

Furthermore, the old Vinaya, the *Daśabhāṇavāra*, fascicle fifty-nine, mentions "pure water-pots" and "latrine water-pots." Fascicle forty-one mentions "pure water-vessels" and "common water-vessels." The newly translated Mūlasarvāstivāda Vinaya texts clearly distinguish between pure vessels and contact vessels. These are all the personal words of the Golden Mouth, not human inventions. How can one use a single copper vessel without distinguishing pure from defiled? Although this has been repeatedly stated, it is not taken to heart. How can one, merely because of habitual custom, violate the sacred teachings? According to this principle, the water used in various monastery rooms and on journeys and the like is in most cases improperly handled: stored in large basins, kept in jars, or placed in vessels, none of it fit for use. This is because it has been touched by monks' hands, not properly filtered, left overnight without examination, and stored for a long time so that living creatures are certainly present. Even if there are no insects, drinking from the third type of water incurs an offense, since the basin, filter-cloth, and ladle have been touched by impure hands and carry impure dust. Furthermore, water from an ordinary copper vessel, if one draws and drinks from it oneself: because it is impure and was drunk without formal reception, there is an offense of non-reception. If touched before noon and drunk after noon, there is an additional contact-offense. If touched today and drunk tomorrow, there is a stale-contact offense. Handling it without reception incurs a wrong-contact offense. Not properly washing the hands incurs an offense of handling food-vessels with soiled hands. Drinking water without properly washing the lips and mouth incurs an impurity offense. These six offenses each have associated preparatory offenses, making twelve. All carry an offense of disrespecting the sacred teachings, with their respective root causes, making twenty-four more, for a total of thirty-six. For concealment overnight, there are further root-cause offenses making seventy-two, for a running total of one hundred and eight. If one does not study these, with both not-studying and ignorance offenses, there are further root-cause offenses making four hundred and thirty-two, for a grand total of five hundred and forty offenses. Furthermore, from concealment onward, and for not-studying and ignorance, each carries a disrespecting-the-teaching offense with preparatory and root causes, adding one thousand and eight more. Combined with the five hundred and forty, the total is one thousand five hundred and forty offenses. These are just for a single swallow on a single night; the above calculations enumerate the arising of offenses. If one counts ten swallows over a single night, there are fifteen thousand four hundred and eighty offenses. If concealed over many days, the offenses multiply in succession. Until confession is made, the number is incalculable. The grave ones must all be confessed before a pure person. If one already knew beforehand, there is no ignorance offense. If one drinks salt-water or honey-water at an improper time, or if the pots, ladles, bowls, and vessels, or the water, filter-cloth, and strainer are not prepared in accordance with the Dharma, or if they carry grease or contact-defilement, or if they are mixed with food from proper times, or if hands and mouth are not clean, or if one does not receive them with the hand turned upward: all incur offenses. As for apricot-seed tea and such, the western lands do not drink these. If within the monastery, one must personally inspect. If at a layperson's house, one should carefully inquire whether it is pure. If it is not in accordance with the Dharma, one must not drink. How can one, for the sake of momentary gratification of mouth and belly, endure long suffering in the future? It is commonly known that the Nāga King Hrāḍa, for the fault of damaging a single leaf, has not yet been liberated, and the time of his release is not recorded. How much more so for this constant behavior, which is not without karmic consequences. A single swallow enters the body, and the offenses are boundless. Bearing this burden for a lifetime is truly lamentable. Therefore the sūtra says: "Do not take lightly a small offense, thinking it brings no calamity." It also says: "Guard well the leather float and watch for the smallest leak." Both Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna share this intent. I have briefly stated the essential practices; the rest is as found in the greater Vinaya. I hope all practitioners will practice in accordance with the sacred teachings.
