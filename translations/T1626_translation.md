# 大乘法界無差別論
## Treatise on the Non-Differentiation of the Mahāyāna Dharma-Realm

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1626

Composed by Bodhisattva Sthiramati (堅慧菩薩). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Devaprajñā and others (提雲般若等).

---

> I bow my head to the mind of bodhi,
> Which can serve as the supreme skillful means,
> Attaining freedom from birth, old age, death,
> And the faults of reliance on suffering.

The mind of bodhi, briefly stated, has twelve kinds of meaning. This is the body of the treatise, and the wise should know them in sequence: (1) fruit, (2) cause, (3) self-nature, (4) variant names, (5) non-differentiation, (6) stages of abiding, (7) non-defilement, (8) permanence, (9) correspondence, (10) not performing beneficial activity, (11) performing beneficial activity, and (12) single nature.

Among these, first the fruit of the mind of bodhi is shown, so that one may see its supreme benefit. Next, the cause from which it arises is explained. After that, its characteristic of production is established, and its variant names, which are without differentiation, are displayed. In all stages, it is without defilement. It is constantly in correspondence with pure dharmas. In the impure stage it has no function; in the pure stage it can perform beneficial activity, being of single-nature nirvāṇa. One should know that these twelve kinds of meaning are expounded in sequence in this treatise.

**What is the fruit of the mind of bodhi?** It is the most tranquil realm of nirvāṇa. This is realized only by all buddhas and cannot be obtained by others. Why? Because only the buddha-tathāgatas can permanently extinguish all the subtlest heat of afflictions. Therein, "without birth" means never again producing the aggregates of the mentally-produced body. "Without old age" means this meritorious quality is supreme in increase, supremely excellent, perfectly fulfilled, and ultimately without decline or change. "Without death" means permanently relinquishing the inconceivable death of transmutation. "Without illness" means all afflictive obstructions, cognitive obstructions, and habitual tendencies are permanently severed. "Without suffering" means all habitual tendencies from the ground of beginningless ignorance are permanently removed. "Without fault" means all erroneous actions of body, speech, and mind do not occur.

This is because the mind of bodhi, as the most supreme skillful means and the non-retrogressing cause, brings all meritorious qualities to ultimate fulfillment and obtains that fruit. That fruit is the realm of nirvāṇa. What is the realm of nirvāṇa? It is the inconceivable Dharma-body, the mark of the turning-of-the-basis of all buddhas. Because the mind of bodhi is the cause of the inconceivable fruit, like the first quarter of the white moon, I now bow my head in reverence.

Furthermore, a verse says:

> Able to benefit worldly wholesome dharmas,
> Noble dharmas, and all buddhas:
> The basis, treasure-ground, and likeness
> Are like the earth, the ocean, and seeds.

Furthermore, the mind of bodhi is like the earth, because it is the basis upon which all worldly wholesome sprouts grow. It is like the ocean, because it is the place where all the precious treasures of noble dharmas accumulate. It is like a seed, because it is the sequential cause from which all buddha-trees are produced. Thus the fruit of the mind of bodhi has been explained.

**How is the cause?** A verse says:

> Faith is its seed,
> Prajñā is its mother,
> Samādhi is its womb-treasury,
> And great compassion is its nursing mother.

Furthermore, how does this cause accumulate? One should know it is like the son of a wheel-turning king. Among these, deep faith in the Dharma is the seed of the mind of bodhi. Wisdom that penetrates all is its mother. Samādhi is its womb-treasury, because through the joy of abiding in concentration, all wholesome dharmas are established. Great compassion is its nursing mother, because through compassionate care for sentient beings, one does not weary of birth-and-death, and the all-knowing wisdom is brought to fulfillment.

**How is the self-nature?** A verse says:

> Its self-nature is without defilement,
> Like fire, jewels, space, and water.
> It is accomplished by white dharmas,
> Like the great king of mountains.

Furthermore, one should know that after the causes of this mind of bodhi have accumulated, there are two kinds of characteristics: the characteristic of purity through separation from defilement, and the characteristic of being composed of white dharmas.

The characteristic of purity through separation from defilement: this mind is by self-nature undefiled, and moreover, having separated from the obstructions of adventitious afflictions, it attains purity. Just as fire, maṇi-jewels, empty space, water, and the like, when covered and obscured by ashes, grime, clouds, and dust, though their self-natures are without defilement, still must be separated from ashes and the rest so that fire and the rest attain purity. Just so, the self-nature mind of all sentient beings, without differentiation, though it cannot be defiled by afflictions such as greed, still must be separated from greed and the rest so that the mind attains purity.

The characteristic of being composed of white dharmas: this naturally pure mind is the basis of all white dharmas, and all white and pure dharmas compose its nature. As it is said, Mount Sumeru is the basis of many jewels, and is itself composed of many jewels.

**How are the variant names?** A verse says:

> Upon reaching the stage of buddhahood,
> It is no longer called the mind of bodhi,
> But is named Arhat,
> With purity, self, bliss, and permanence as perfections.
> This mind's nature is bright and pure,
> Of one body with the dharma-realm.
> The Tathāgata relies upon this mind
> To teach the inconceivable Dharma.

Furthermore, this mind of bodhi, having permanently departed from all the faults of adventitious defilements, and being inseparable from the accomplishment of all meritorious qualities, obtains the four kinds of supreme pāramitās and is named the Tathāgata's Dharma-body. As it is said: "World-Honored One! The Dharma-body of the Tathāgata is the pāramitā of permanence, the pāramitā of bliss, the pāramitā of self, the pāramitā of purity." The Tathāgata's Dharma-body is a variant name for the naturally pure mind that is defiled by adventitious afflictions. As it is also said: "Śāriputra! This pure dharma-nature is the dharma-realm. I teach the inconceivable Dharma relying upon this naturally pure mind."

**How is non-differentiation?** A verse says:

> The Dharma-body, among sentient beings,
> Originally has no mark of differentiation.
> Unconditioned, without beginning or end,
> Also without any defilement.
> Known by the wisdom of the emptiness of dharmas,
> Without marks, the practice of the noble ones,
> The basis of all dharmas:
> Both eternalism and annihilationism are entirely departed from.

Furthermore, this mind of bodhi, within the bodies of all sentient beings, has ten kinds of marks of non-differentiation: (1) unconditioned, because it is unconditioned; (2) without beginning, because it is without arising; (3) without end, because it is without cessation; (4) without defilement, because it is naturally pure; (5) known by the wisdom of the emptiness of nature, because all dharmas have the single-taste mark of non-self; (6) without form, because it is without the sense-faculties; (7) the practice of the noble ones, because it is the realm of the great sage, the Buddha; (8) the basis of all dharmas, because it is the ground of defiled and pure dharmas; (9) not permanent, because the nature of defiled dharmas is impermanent; (10) not annihilated, because the nature of pure dharmas is not cut off.

**How are the stages?** A verse says:

> Impure: the realm of sentient beings.
> In defilement, yet pure: bodhisattvas.
> The most supremely pure
> Is spoken of as the Tathāgata.

Furthermore, because this mind of bodhi has marks of non-differentiation, in the impure stage it is named the realm of sentient beings; in the stage of both defilement and purity it is named bodhisattva; in the most pure stage it is called Tathāgata. As it is said: "Śāriputra! This very Dharma-body, from the very beginning, wrapped by the boundless treasury of afflictions, arising and ceasing in the courses of birth-and-death from time without beginning, tossed about and drifting, is called the realm of sentient beings. Furthermore, Śāriputra! This very Dharma-body, wearied of the suffering of being tossed about in birth-and-death, relinquishing all objects of desire, and within the ten pāramitās and eighty-four thousand Dharma-gates cultivating the practices that seek bodhi, is called bodhisattva. Furthermore, Śāriputra! This very Dharma-body, liberated from all the treasury of afflictions, far removed from all suffering, permanently removing all afflictions and secondary afflictions, pure, supremely pure, most supremely pure, abiding in the dharma-nature, reaching the ground observed by all sentient beings, exhausting the ground of all that is to be known, ascending to the place of the peerless man, obtaining unobstructed, unattached, sovereign mastery over all dharmas, is called Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One. Therefore, Śāriputra, the realm of sentient beings is not different from the Dharma-body, and the Dharma-body is not different from the realm of sentient beings. The realm of sentient beings is the Dharma-body; the Dharma-body is the realm of sentient beings. This is merely a difference of name, not of meaning."

**How is non-defilement?** A verse says:

> Just as the bright and pure sun
> Is obscured by clouds,
> When the cloud of afflictions is removed,
> The sun of the Dharma-body shines forth clearly.

How is this further explained? In the impure stage, though there are immeasurable afflictions present, they do not defile it. Just as the sun's orb, though covered by clouds, is by nature always pure. This mind is likewise; those mixed afflictions are merely adventitious.

**How is permanence?** A verse says:

> Just as the fire at the end of a kalpa
> Cannot burn empty space,
> So too, old age, illness, and death
> Cannot burn the dharma-realm.
> Just as all worldly things
> Arise and perish depending on empty space,
> The sense-faculties likewise
> Arise and cease depending on the unconditioned.

Furthermore, how can one say this is permanent when birth, old age, and death are present? Just as empty space, though the fire of kalpa-destruction arises, cannot be harmed. The dharma-realm is likewise. Therefore the sūtra says: "World-Honored One! Birth-and-death is merely a conventional expression. World-Honored One! 'Death' means the sense-faculties are hidden away; 'birth' means new sense-faculties arise. The tathāgatagarbha does not have birth, old age, death, or sinking or arising. World-Honored One! The tathāgatagarbha surpasses the marks of the conditioned realm, being tranquil, constantly abiding, unchanging, and unbroken."

**How is correspondence?** A verse says:

> Just as light, warmth, and color
> Have no mark separate from the lamp,
> So too, all buddha-dharmas
> Are likewise with respect to the dharma-nature.
> The nature of afflictions is separate from them;
> The adventitious afflictions are empty.
> Pure dharmas are constantly in correspondence:
> The not-empty, undefiled dharmas.

Furthermore, how can one say that the buddha-dharmas are in correspondence when perfect enlightenment has not yet been attained? Just as light, warmth, color, and the rest have no mark different from the lamp, the buddha-dharmas are likewise with respect to the Dharma-body. As it is said: "Śāriputra! The meritorious dharmas of the Dharma-body of all buddhas are like a lamp's light, warmth, and color, which are inseparable and undetachable. The light, color, and form of a maṇi-jewel are also the same. Śāriputra! The wisdom and meritorious dharmas that are inseparable from the Dharma-body as taught by the Tathāgata are the tathāgata-dharmas surpassing the sands of the Ganges." Furthermore, as it is said: "There are two kinds of tathāgatagarbha empty-wisdom. What are the two? The empty tathāgatagarbha: the wisdom of all afflictions being separate and liberated. The not-empty tathāgatagarbha: the wisdom of the inconceivable buddha-dharmas surpassing the sands of the Ganges being inseparable and undetachable."

**How is not performing beneficial activity?** A verse says:

> Wrapped in the treasury of afflictions,
> It cannot benefit sentient beings.
> Like a lotus not yet opened,
> Like gold in a dung-heap,
> Or like the full moon
> Devoured by an asura.

Furthermore, the Dharma-body of sentient beings is in correspondence with such meritorious qualities; why then is there no function of the Tathāgata's virtue? One should know it is like a lotus not yet opened, because it is wrapped by the leaves of evil views. Like gold fallen into a privy, because it is in the dung of deliberation and inquiry. Like the full moon being devoured, because it is seized by the Rāhu of conceit. Like pond-water being muddied, because it is mixed with the dust of desire. Like a golden mountain being obscured, because it is coated with the mud of anger. Like empty space being covered, because it is veiled by the heavy clouds of deluded foolishness. Like the sun not yet risen, because it is in the ground of the habitual tendencies of ignorance. Like the world not yet formed, because it is in the womb-treasury of the water-element of the six sense-bases. Like clouds without rain, because adverse conditions are presently manifesting. A summary verse says:

> Like the lotus, gold, and others, not yet opened and revealed,
> The Buddha-body is likewise obscured by adventitious defilements.
> At this time, its meritorious qualities cannot benefit oneself;
> The reverse of this can then accomplish great benefit.

**How is performing beneficial activity?** A verse says:

> Like a pond without grime or turbidity,
> Like a lotus, great and fully blooming;
> Like the finest true gold,
> Washed clean of all dung and filth;
> Like the pure, clear sky
> With the bright moon surrounded by stars:
> At the time of freedom from desire and liberation,
> The meritorious qualities are likewise.
> Like the sun appearing bright,
> Its awesome light pervading the world;
> Like the earth producing all grains,
> Like the ocean yielding all jewels:
> Thus benefiting sentient beings,
> Causing them to be freed from all existences.
> Knowing the nature of all existences,
> Yet arousing great compassion;
> Whether exhausted or not exhausted,
> There is no attachment to either.
> The Buddha's mind, like a great cloud,
> Abides in the emptiness of true reality.
> The dharmas of samādhi and dhāraṇī:
> Sweet rain descends according to the season,
> And all the sprouts of wholesome deeds
> Grow and flourish because of this.

The meaning of this verse is the reverse of the preceding, and one should know it refers to the pure Dharma-body, which has departed from the afflictions of adventitious defilements and has accomplished the meritorious qualities of its own nature. One who realizes this Dharma is called Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One, constantly enjoying bliss in the permanently abiding, tranquil, cool, and inconceivable realm of nirvāṇa, the refuge and aspiration of all sentient beings.

**How is single nature?** A verse says:

> This is precisely the Dharma-body,
> And is also precisely the Tathāgata;
> Just so, it is also precisely
> The noble truth's ultimate meaning.
> Nirvāṇa is not different from the Buddha,
> Just as coldness is water itself.
> Meritorious qualities are inseparable;
> Therefore there is no nirvāṇa apart from them.

If the Tathāgata's Dharma-body were different from nirvāṇa, the sūtra should not have spoken thus. As the verse says:

> "When the realm of sentient beings is purified,
> One should know it is the Dharma-body.
> The Dharma-body is nirvāṇa;
> Nirvāṇa is the Tathāgata."

Furthermore, as a sūtra says: "World-Honored One! This very unsurpassed perfect enlightenment is the realm of nirvāṇa; this very realm of nirvāṇa is the Tathāgata's Dharma-body. World-Honored One! There is no Tathāgata apart from the Dharma-body; what is called the Tathāgata is the Dharma-body." Furthermore, one should know that this is also not different from the truth of the cessation of suffering. Therefore the sūtra says: "It is not by the destruction of suffering dharmas that one speaks of the truth of the cessation of suffering. The cessation of suffering means: from the very beginning, unconditioned, without arising, without birth, without cessation, without exhaustion, free from exhaustion, constantly abiding, unchanging, without interruption, naturally pure, far removed from the entire treasury of afflictions. Complete with the inconceivable buddha-dharmas of inseparable, undetachable wisdom surpassing the sands of the Ganges: therefore it is called the Tathāgata's Dharma-body. World-Honored One! This very Dharma-body of the Tathāgata, while not yet freed from the treasury of afflictions, is called the tathāgatagarbha. World-Honored One! The wisdom of the tathāgatagarbha is the empty-wisdom of the Tathāgata. World-Honored One! The tathāgatagarbha has never been seen, never been realized by any śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha. Only the Buddha-World-Honored-One has permanently destroyed the entire treasury of afflictions and has fully cultivated the path that is the cessation of all suffering, thereby realizing it." Therefore one should know that the Buddha and nirvāṇa are without difference, just as the touch of coldness is not different from water.

Furthermore, one should know that there is only the one-vehicle path. If it were otherwise, apart from this there should be another nirvāṇa. Within the one dharma-realm, how could there be an inferior nirvāṇa and a superior nirvāṇa? Nor can one say that from lower, middling, and upper causes of varying quality one obtains a single fruit, because it is directly seen that when causes are differentiated, fruits are also differentiated. Therefore the sūtra says: "World-Honored One! Truly there are no dharmas of superior, inferior, or differentiated quality that realize nirvāṇa. World-Honored One! Equal dharmas realize nirvāṇa. World-Honored One! Equal wisdom, equal liberation, equal knowledge-and-vision of liberation realize nirvāṇa. Therefore, World-Honored One, the realm of nirvāṇa is called 'of one taste,' namely the taste of equality, the taste of liberation."

---

*Note: This treatise in the cinnabar edition differs from the National and Song editions. This is the cinnabar text, which has twenty-four verses in five-character, four-line form, with one seven-character verse interspersed, divided into twelve sections, each with its own commentary. This is the text that our patriarch Fazang's commentary elucidates. The National and Song editions have twenty verses in seven-character, four-line form, presented all at once, with sequential commentary afterward. Their opening verse reads: "The dharma-realm is neither born nor destroyed, / Without old age, illness, death, or the faults of the aggregates. / Because from it the supreme mind of bodhi is aroused, / I now bow my head in homage." Upon examining the Kaiyuan Catalog and Fazang's commentary, both treat this treatise as a single translation. Yet the National and Song editions, though their wording differs from this cinnabar edition, are not different in meaning, and must be a retranslation from a later generation after the Kaiyuan era. However, it is not known from which era or by whom the translation was made; this awaits further investigation. The attribution in the two editions directly to Devaprajñā as translator is erroneous.*
