# 毘那夜迦誐那鉢底瑜伽悉地品祕要
## Secret Essentials of the Siddhi Chapter of the Vināyaka Gaṇapati Yoga

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1273

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, recorded by Hanguang (含光).

---

Because masters both ancient and modern have kept this deeply secret, it is not recorded in the sūtras. Some have revealed the mantras but not elucidated the principles, and practitioners of later generations have been much afflicted by obstacles. Therefore, Dharma Master Hanguang personally received from Master Amoghavajra the secret meaning of this method, and now briefly raises and records it.

The mantra of Gaṇapati King:

*Namo vināyaka-śahā-asti-mukha-śa tadyathā oṃ nāyaka vināyaka trāyaka paritrāyaka śaṃkha-hasti śaṃkha-kā-citta svāhā.*

If one wishes to perform pacification, add the phrase *śānti-kara* before *svāhā*. If one wishes to seek wealth, add the phrase *puṣṭika* before *svāhā*. If one wishes to seek attraction, remove the preceding phrase and add *vaśīkaraṇa*. If one wishes to subjugate evil, remove the preceding phrase and add *abhicāruka*. If one wishes to seek longevity, remove the preceding phrase and add *jīvita*. If one wishes to summon by hook, remove the preceding phrase and add *āṅkuśa*. These various phrases contain numerous siddhi attainments.

The mantra of the dual-bodied Vināyaka who generates a joyful heart:

*Oṃ gṛ-ñak svāhā.*

A practitioner who constantly recites this mantra will have no obstacles. Within this mantra, the meanings of skillful means and ultimate reality are revealed. Why is this? *Gṛ* (*hrīḥ*) is the seed-syllable of Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva. The bodhisattva manifests this body as his wife and encourages him, causing Vināyaka to not create obstacles. There are past-life causes and conditions, as explained in other sections; this is the meaning of skillful means. Next, *ñak* (*gaḥ*) is the seed of Vināyaka. He is the "constantly-accompanying māra." What does "constantly-accompanying māra" mean? It means that he constantly follows all sentient beings, watching for their faults. However, heavenly māras and earthly māras are not like this; they come only at certain times to create obstacles. Vināyaka constantly accompanies beings and creates obstacles; therefore he is called the constantly-accompanying māra. Even Brahmā, Kauśika, and all the nāgas and devas cannot destroy such obstacles. Only Avalokiteśvara and Kuṇḍalī Bodhisattva can remove these Vināyaka difficulties.

The sūtra states: "If one knows there are various obstacles within the body, and virtuous undertakings largely do not accord with one's wishes, one should bathe this Avalokiteśvara image with fragrant water. Then take this water and empower it with the mantra 108 times, and bathe the Vināyaka image with it. Again empower it with the mantra 108 times and sprinkle it upon the practitioner's own body. All obstacles will naturally be extinguished, and everything sought will be fulfilled as desired." Use the root mantra of the Eleven-Faced Avalokiteśvara for this method. As for Kuṇḍalī Bodhisattva, there are many textual attestations of his removing Vināyaka obstacles, as extensively explained in other sections.

The mantra of the dual-bodied Vināyaka for subjugating the evil of others:

*Oṃ ñak ñak hūṃ svāhā.*

If a practitioner wishes to bind an evil person, use this mantra. If one wishes to drive away an evil person, also use this mantra. This mantra especially reveals the twofold meaning of reality. Therefore, one syllable is repeated to form two syllables. Seeking the reality, one finds the expedient. Why expedient? Because the buddhas and bodhisattvas manifest this body in order to subjugate all indolence. All obstacles arise from indolence; if indolence ceases, obstacles do not arise. skillful means depends on reality; therefore one syllable is repeated. Reality reveals skillful means; therefore know that this is a transformation of buddhas and bodhisattvas. The buddha is Vairocana Buddha, who proclaimed: "My body that reaches everywhere: in order to transform and deliver sentient beings according to their kinds, Samantabhadra ultimately manifests as Vināyaka." The bodhisattva is Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva. The buddha and bodhisattva, as male and female deva forms, transform and deliver sentient beings.

I shall now explain the various forms of Vināyaka. Some resemble humans or devas; some resemble brāhmaṇas. Some manifest handsome male or female appearances. They are divided into four groups, encompassing many types. In these various ways they create all manner of obstacles. Only the Great Holy Deva, the Joyous King, is a body provisionally manifested, as described above, who, wishing to guide and encourage all those who create obstacles and lead them into right view, therefore does not resemble other vināyakas, but manifests an elephant head. This is by way of simile: just as the elephant-king, though possessing anger and great strength, can be tamed by the one who nurtures it and by the trainer. Gaṇapati is likewise: though he manifests an obstructing body, he can be tamed by those who take refuge in him and even by those who take refuge in the Buddha. Therefore this deva manifests an elephant head. Vināyaka is also called Vināṭaka, which means "elephant-trunk." His form is like a human's, but the nose is extremely long, because he is attracted to the fragrant sense-objects. Only the present Great Holy Deva has head, eyes, ears, nose, tongue, and all features resembling an elephant's, because he can be tamed by practitioners. This deva is Gaṇapati, which means "Joyous One"; he is not the same as other vināyakas. Through the power of his compassionate good roots, he causes all vināyakas to generate a joyful heart, and then rebukes them and commands them not to create obstacles. When he is in a wrathful mood, he uses his trunk to bind beings near or far as he pleases. Therefore he surpasses all other vināyakas.

The meanings of expedient and ultimate reality regarding Vināyaka having been explained, next I explain the mudrā forms.

**First mudrā**: Both hands in external bond, both index fingers in a jewel shape. This seal is used with the first great mantra.

**Second mudrā**: Both hands in internal bond. The right middle and index fingers are joined erect, crossed over the left middle and index fingers, which are also joined erect in the same manner. Left and right index fingers are each placed alongside the backs of the middle fingers. Thumbs stand side by side. This seal is used with the second mantra.

**Third mudrā**: Following the previous mudrā, change the left and right little fingers and ring fingers to an external bond. This seal is used with the third mantra.

Next I explain the image method. Elephant head, human body. The left tusk protrudes; the right tusk is broken. The face turns slightly to the left. The trunk curves outward. The body is reddish-yellow. Six arms: the left hands hold a knife, a fruit tray, and a wheel; the right hands hold a staff, a lasso, and a tusk. Before this image, recite the first great mantra, and all that is sought will be accomplished.

Another image: two deva bodies embracing, standing upright, with dual elephant heads and human bodies. The left deva wears a celestial flower crown; the trunk and tusks are short; the eyes are narrow. It wears a red kāṣāya and merit-field robe, and its body is white, the color of flesh. The right deva's expression is unkind; the trunk is long and the eyes wide. It does not wear a celestial crown or merit-field robe. Its body is reddish-yellow, and it wears only a black garment wrapped around the neck and shoulders. This deva presses its face against the face of the female deva in front, making a gesture of cherishing affection. This image reveals the meaning of skillful means and ultimate reality. Before this image, recite the second mantra. Virtue increases and evil is extinguished.

Another image: two forms embracing and standing upright as before, but the male deva's face rests against the right shoulder of the female deva, gazing at the female deva's back, and the female deva's face rests against the right shoulder of the male deva, gazing at the male deva's back. The one with narrow eyes and short tusks is the wife deva. Both devas do not wear dharma-robes or celestial crowns, manifesting their original vināyaka bodies. Before this image, recite the third mantra. All evil affairs will be entirely extinguished.

The essential meanings, mudrā seals, and image-painting methods of the mantra of Gaṇapati King having been explained: when about to practice this method, one should first pay homage to Mahāvairocana, next pay homage to Avalokiteśvara, and then pay homage to Kuṇḍalī. Only then will one obtain efficacious results. Vairocana and Avalokiteśvara are the original presiding deities' true bodies; Kuṇḍalī is the one who subjugates. Therefore one first pays homage to them.

The Great Extensive-Wisdom Master said: "If there are obstacles caused by Vināyaka, one should practice the mantra method. Within the mantra method there are three divisions; the three divisions are three deities; the three deities are buddha, bodhisattva, and vajra. Buddha, bodhisattva, and vajra are Vairocana, Avalokiteśvara, and Kuṇḍalī. This three-division method can subdue the indolence of the three karmas. Because it eliminates indolence, obstacles are forever cut off and do not arise. Therefore, one who wishes to practice the Gaṇapati method should first pay homage to Vairocana, Avalokiteśvara, and Kuṇḍalī. These three-division deities, because they can eliminate the indolence of the practitioner's body, speech, and mind, manifest this Vināyaka body. This is not like other obstructors; only this one bears the name Gaṇapati."

The five-syllable mantra of Vairocana:

*A vi ra hūṃ khaṃ.*

The root mantra of the Eleven-Faced Bhṛkuṭī Avalokiteśvara, as spoken by all buddhas, translated by Amoghavajra:

*Oṃ dara dara dhiri dhiri dhuru dhuru iti vati cale cale pracale pracale kusume kusuma-vare ili mili cili cili citi jara-māpanāya parama-śuddha-sattva mahā-kāruṇika svāhā.*

The mantra of Kuṇḍalī Bodhisattva for removing obstacles:

*Namaḥ ratna-trayāya namaś caṇḍa-vajrapāṇaye mahā-yakṣa-senāpataye namo vajra-krodhāya oṃ huru huru tiṣṭha tiṣṭha bandha bandha dihana dihana amṛte hūṃ phaṭ.*

The wondrous and subtle secret meanings described above should be kept secret by the wise of later generations. They should be stored within a palm-leaf manuscript case. Do not recklessly transmit or release them to unfit persons. What constitutes an unfit person? Those without wisdom, those without faith, evil persons, those who cannot keep a secret, and the like are unfit persons. If one freely propagates this before such persons, there is the danger of incurring the transgression of slander. Therefore, if one does not select fit persons, obstacles will arise. This is why an additional injunction is now added. The wise who practice this will swiftly attain siddhi.
