# 提婆菩薩破楞伽經中外道小乘四宗論
## Bodhisattva Āryadeva's Refutation of the Four Doctrines of Non-Buddhists and Hīnayāna within the Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1639

Translated from the Chinese. Composed by Bodhisattva Āryadeva. Later Wei dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流支).

---

**Question:** What are the four doctrines established by the non-Buddhists that are not the buddha-dharma?

**Answer:** They are: identity, difference, both, and neither.

**Question:** What is meant by identity, difference, both, and neither?

**Answer:** There are non-Buddhists who say that all dharmas are identical. There are non-Buddhists who say that all dharmas are different. There are non-Buddhists who say that all dharmas are both. There are non-Buddhists who say that all dharmas are neither. These non-Buddhists each cling to deluded dharmas, taking them to be real entities.

**Question:** Which non-Buddhists teach that all dharmas are identical?

**Answer:** Those who say all dharmas are identical are the non-Buddhist Sāṃkhya theorists. Those who say all dharmas are different are the non-Buddhist Vaiśeṣika theorists. Those who say all dharmas are both are the non-Buddhist Nirgrantha theorists. Those who say all dharmas are neither are the non-Buddhist Jñātiputra theorists.

**Question:** How do the Sāṃkhya adherents say that all dharmas are identical?

**Answer:** The Sāṃkhya non-Buddhists say that the self and awareness are two dharmas that are one. Why? Because the differentiating characteristics of the two cannot be apprehended.

**Question:** How can the differentiating characteristics of the two not be apprehended?

**Answer:** In the case of a cow and a horse, which are different dharmas, the differentiating characteristics of the two can be seen and grasped, and one can say "this is a cow" and "this is a horse." But the self apart from awareness cannot be apprehended, and awareness apart from the self cannot be apprehended. As stated in the *Self Treatise*: "The self and awareness are in their substance and characteristics like fire and heat; the differentiating characteristics of the two dharmas cannot be apprehended."

**Question:** How can the differentiating characteristics not be apprehended?

**Answer:** Because those dharmas cannot be said to be different. For example, with a white cloth, one cannot say "this is the white" and "this is the cloth" as two different dharmas. Just as with the white cloth, so too with all dharmas of cause and effect.

**Question:** How do the Vaiśeṣika non-Buddhists say that all dharmas are different?

**Answer:** When they say "different," they mean the self is different from awareness. Why? Because they teach the dharma of difference.

**Question:** What is meant by teaching the dharma of difference?

**Answer:** Just as one says "this is the white" and "this is the cloth," and "this is the celestial virtue" and "this is the celestial virtue cloth." The self and awareness being different is likewise: "this is the self" and "this is cognition."

**Question:** What distinction makes those dharmas unable to be called identical?

**Answer:** For example, with a white cloth: "this is the white" and "this is the cloth." In this way, all causes and effects are each different and cannot be called identical.

**Question:** How do the Nirgrantha say that all dharmas are both?

**Answer:** When they say all dharmas are "both," they mean that, as with the self and awareness, one cannot say they are identical and one cannot say they are different, yet there is another meaning by which one can say they are identical and can say they are different.

**Question:** How are they neither identical nor different, yet both identical and different?

**Answer:** As with the self and life-force: in their function and characteristics there is difference, because the means are different. Just as with greed, anger, and delusion, one can say there is difference. For example, with a lamp and its light, one can say they are identical and one can say they are different. Because when this exists, that exists, and when this does not exist, that does not exist, one can say they are identical. Because the lamp is in one place and the light in another, one can say they are different. Just as with the lamp and light in their cause-and-effect relationship, so too with the white cloth and all dharmas: one can say they are identical and one can say they are different. Therefore it is called "both."

**Question:** How do the Jñātiputra non-Buddhists say that all dharmas are neither?

**Answer:** "Neither" means that all dharmas cannot be said to be identical, cannot be said to be different, because of the error of the two extreme views. Because the theorists who teach identity, difference, and both all have errors, the wise do not establish such three dharmas.

**Question:** What are these errors?

**Answer:** If apart from the white there is no separate cloth, then when the white perishes, the cloth should also perish. If the cloth is different from the white and there is a separate cloth, then there should be cloth that is not white and white that is not cloth. Therefore, I do not establish any of the dharmas of identity, difference, or both. Nevertheless, one cannot say that identity, difference, both, and the like among all dharmas are nonexistent.

**Answer:** The deluded discriminations of all these non-Buddhists are the marks of wrong views, not the marks of wisdom. They are all unwholesome. What is the meaning of this? Furthermore, the deluded discriminations of identity and the like: because one cannot say "that very dharma is that dharma, and that dharma is identical," one cannot speak of a pot; and "the pot is identical" cannot be said of a pot, since a pot is precisely a pot. Nor can one apprehend different dharmas, since "different dharma is identical" cannot be said because one cannot say "a pot and a cloth are identical," as the characteristics of a pot are different from the characteristics of a cloth. Because different dharmas are separate from different dharmas, different dharmas cannot be said to be identical or different, since different dharmas do not constitute different dharmas. Because different dharmas cannot be called different dharmas, if two dharmas are said to be identical or different, those two dharmas should be describable as identical or different. If they cannot be said to be identical or different, this is deluded discrimination. If those two dharmas are identical, one cannot say those dharmas are different. If there are not two, how can one say they are identical? Because those dharmas are established in mutual dependence, they are deluded discriminations based on conventional truth. In the truth of the ultimate meaning, there are no such deluded discriminations of the non-Buddhists, because of the error of conceptual proliferation. This is the general answer to the marks of wrong views of the four kinds of non-Buddhists.

---

From this point onward, each of the four positions is answered individually. Examining in this way one by one, the deluded discriminations of Kapila, Ulūka, and the other non-Buddhists are shown to be untenable. What is the meaning of this?

The claim that all dharmas are identical is untenable, because what perishes should perish, what does not perish should not perish, and what perishes together should not perish together. What is the meaning of this? You earlier said that the differentiating characteristics of the self and awareness cannot be apprehended, as with a white cloth. I refute this position. Why? Because this position does not accord with the various treatises. Your claim that the differentiating characteristics of dharmas cannot be apprehended is untenable. Consider the hand and fingernails: the differentiating characteristics of those two dharmas cannot be apprehended. What does this clarify? Just as nails, fingers, and palm are called "hand," and apart from these dharmas, a "hand" cannot be apprehended, so too the identity of a white cloth cannot be apprehended. Why? Because there are no different dharmas. The identity of self and awareness cannot be apprehended, and in this way the identity of a white cloth cannot be apprehended, just as with the hand and its fingers and palm.

If this perishes, then that should also perish. What is the meaning of this? If the white perishes, the cloth should perish, just as cutting the hand means cutting the fingers and palm. If you think the white perishes but the cloth does not perish, this is untenable. If the cloth does not perish, then the white also should not perish, just as cutting the hand means the fingers and palm should remain, and cutting the fingers and palm means the hand should also remain. If you think that blue, yellow, red, and other colors destroy only the white without destroying the cloth, how can you call them identical? If this is not so, then blue, yellow, red, and other colors should also not perish. If the cloth does not perish, then blue, yellow, white, and other colors also should not perish.

**Question:** I say that blue, yellow, red, and other colors cover the white but do not destroy the white. What is the meaning of this?

**Answer:** The cloth is likewise: it covers the cloth but does not destroy the cloth. Furthermore, this is untenable, because after washing the cloth, the white color is seen again. The cloth is likewise: covering the cloth does not destroy the cloth. Therefore, the white is precisely the cloth and the cloth is precisely the white. If the cloth perishes, how can blue, yellow, red, white, and other colors be seen? If you think the white perishes through covering but the cloth does not perish, then covering the cloth should not mean perishing the white. If so, there are dharmas that perish through covering and dharmas that do not perish and are not covered. How then can you call them identical? Therefore the position of identity is untenable. This concludes the answer to the Sāṃkhya theorists' claim that all dharmas are identical.

---

**Question:** The Kaṇāda non-Buddhist theorists say that all dharmas are different: the self is different from awareness, because they teach the dharma of difference. "This is the self" and "this is awareness," just as with a white cloth: "this is the white" and "this is the cloth."

**Answer:** This is untenable, because there is no valid analogy. As when a person says "this is the hand" and "this are the fingers and palm," although that person makes this statement, he cannot demonstrate different dharmas. Therefore one cannot say the self and awareness are different, as with a white cloth, because in the world there are two kinds of distinction: first, by characteristics, and second, by location. Distinction by characteristics: color, fragrance, taste, and touch have different characteristics but not different characteristics. Distinction by location: as with grain and beans, there is a white cloth that does not have different characteristics but has distinction, like color, fragrance, taste, and touch.

If this is not so, there are four kinds of error. What is the meaning of this? If the white perishes, the cloth also perishes, like color, fragrance, taste, and touch. For example, when fire combines with and bakes a pot, producing a red color, and then it becomes blue, fragrance and taste are likewise. If this is not so, color, fragrance, taste, and touch also should not perish, since the difference of that white cloth cannot be apprehended. If the white perishes, the cloth should also perish. If the cloth does not perish, the white also should not perish.

**Question:** This is untenable. Depending on that dharma, this dharma exists. For example, as with a painting on a wall: depending on the wall, there is the painting. When the wall perishes, the painting also perishes; when the painting perishes, the wall does not perish. When the white perishes, the cloth does not perish; the meaning is likewise.

**Answer:** Your analogy does not correspond. The wall exists first and the painting is made afterward, but the white and the cloth arise without priority. One cannot say "this white exists first" and "the cloth is made afterward." This concludes the answer to the Vaiśeṣika theorists' claim that all dharmas are different.

---

**Question:** The Nirgrantha non-Buddhist theorists say that all dharmas are both. The Kapila and other theorists all have errors because they teach identity and difference. Therefore I say "both" and "not both." For example, as with a lamp and its light: when this exists, that exists, and when that exists, this exists; when this does not exist, that does not exist, and when that does not exist, this does not exist. Just as when there is a lamp there is light, and when there is light there is a lamp; when there is no lamp there is no light, and when there is no light there is no lamp. As for difference: the illuminator and the illuminated, since the lamp is in one place and the light in another, therefore they are called different. So too with the self, awareness, white cloth, and the like: one can say they are identical and one can say they are different.

For example, the white within the cloth cannot be separately apprehended as "this is the white" and "this is the cloth," as in the world one says "this is a cow" and "this is a horse." The white and cloth are not like that. Therefore I do not say they are different, nor do I say they are identical. If they were identical, then when the white perishes, the cloth should perish. Also, if they were identical, one should not be able to say "red cloth," "black cloth," and so forth. Therefore I say one can say they are identical and one can say they are different. What is the meaning of this?

**Answer:** This is untenable, because the errors previously stated regarding the Sāṃkhya and Vaiśeṣika are no different from this. By what meaning is the Sāṃkhya's identity as previously stated? By what meaning is the Vaiśeṣika's difference as previously stated? What was previously stated? As previously stated, if the lamp and light are identical, the lamp is the light and the light is the lamp. There is only a difference in number and no difference in meaning. If so, the lamp should also illuminate and the light should also be a lamp. If these two dharmas are identical, how are they in different places? Just as the hand and its fingers and palm are without distinction, while the foot and the hand have distinction, the fingers and palm of the hand are without distinction. If they are identical, how can one say they are different? Therefore one cannot say "identical" or "different." This position of identity-and-difference is untenable. This concludes the answer to the Nirgrantha theorists' claim that all dharmas are both.

---

**Question:** The Jñātiputra theorists say that the Sāṃkhya and other theorists' claims of identity, difference, and both regarding all dharmas all have errors. I, Jñātiputra, do not say that all dharmas are identical, different, or both. As in the *Self Treatise*, I do not accept these positions; I accept only "neither." Therefore I am free from the errors of the Sāṃkhya and others. Nevertheless, one cannot say that "neither" does not exist. What is the meaning of this?

**Answer:** This is untenable, because there is no valid analogy. "Because there is no valid analogy" means: I say that in conventional truth there are such dharmas, but in the truth of the ultimate meaning there are no such characteristics. Therefore this establishes the meaning I have stated. What does this clarify? Because without that dharma there is no this dharma; without the substance of that dharma there is also no substance of this dharma. Because this dharma does not constitute that dharma, and that dharma does not constitute this dharma. Because this dharma is ultimately not that dharma, and that dharma is also ultimately not this dharma. Because white is not cloth, and cloth is not white. Because what perishes should not be said not to perish.

If they are identical, then white is cloth and cloth is white. If not so, what perishes perishes, and what does not perish does not perish. If so, how can one make the deluded discrimination that those dharmas are identical, different, both, or neither? If so, the cloth should also be neither cloth nor not-cloth, and the white should also be neither white nor not-white. Because the cloth is precisely the cloth and the white is precisely the white. Therefore "cloth is not cloth" and "white is not white," and therefore "not-white" cannot apprehend "white."

In this way, identity, difference, both, and neither are all deluded discriminations. There are only verbal designations without real meaning. So too with the self, awareness, cause, effect, and similar meanings. This concludes the answer to the Jñātiputra theorists' claim that all dharmas are neither.
