# 沙彌尼戒經
## Sūtra on the Precepts for Śrāmaṇerīs

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1474

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

**The first precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not kill living beings.** One should have compassion for all living beings, as parents care for their children, extending mercy to crawling creatures as if they were infants. What does "not killing" mean? Guarding body, speech, and mind. The body does not kill humans, animals, crawling or breathing creatures, neither doing so with one's own hand nor instructing others. One does not eat what one has seen killed, does not eat what one has heard being killed, does not eat what one suspects was killed, and does not eat what was killed for one's sake. The mouth does not say "you should kill," "you should harm," or "seek revenge." One also must not say "it would be good if they died" or "it would be good to kill them," or "that one is fat," "that one is thin," "that one has much good meat," or "that one has too little." The mind also does not think "it would be satisfying to rob and kill so-and-so," or "that animal is fat, that one thin." One should have compassion for all sentient beings as for one's own bone and marrow, as for a father, a mother, a child, or oneself, equally and without distinction, with a single mind always aspiring to the great vehicle. This is the beginning precept for a śrāmaṇerī in training.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not steal.** From a single coin upward, to blades of grass, hairs, or grains of rice, one must not take them. What the owner has not personally given, one must not take. The mouth does not say "take it." The mind does not think of taking. The eyes do not covet form, the ears do not covet sound, the nose does not steal fragrance, the tongue does not steal flavor, the body does not covet clothing, and the mind does not steal after desires. When the six sense-faculties are without attachment and one constantly establishes expedient wisdom, this is called "not stealing." This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not engage in sexual misconduct.** What does "must not" mean? The mind is single-pointedly pure and clean. The body does not engage in sexual misconduct, the mouth does not speak of it, and the mind does not think of it. One maintains oneself in purity and clarity, like the wind in empty space, without anything to lean upon. The body does not practice sexual misconduct, the eyes do not look with lust, the ears do not listen with lust, the nose does not delight in lustful fragrance, the mouth does not speak lustful words, and the mind does not harbor desire. Contemplating the body's four great elements as originally without any real existence, reckoning earth, water, fire, and wind: there is no self, no person, no lifespan, no life-force. What is there for sexual indulgence? What is there for attachment? One aspires to emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not engage in divisive speech or harsh words.** One's speech should be calm and measured. What one has not seen, one must not say one has seen; what one has not heard, one must not say one has heard. When one sees evil, one does not pass it on; when one hears evil, one does not spread it. One directly avoids harsh words and constantly practices the four immeasurables. One has no wrong speech; whenever one speaks, one speaks of the path. One must not discuss worldly affairs, nor speak of matters concerning kings, ministers, officials, or bandits. One should constantly praise the sūtras, the Dharma, the bodhisattvas, and the correct precepts. Aspiring to the great vehicle, one does not engage in lesser learning, but practices the four immeasurable minds. This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not drink alcohol.** One must not crave alcohol and must not taste alcohol. Alcohol has thirty-six faults: losing the path, ruining the family, endangering the body, and losing one's life are all brought about by it. One is dragged east and pulled west, carried south and placed north. One cannot recite the sūtras, does not revere the Three Honored Ones, treats teachers and friends with contempt, is unfilial to one's parents. The mind is blocked and the intellect obstructed; lifetime after lifetime one is foolish and ignorant. One does not encounter the great path, and one's mind lacks understanding. Therefore one does not drink alcohol. One wishes to depart from the five aggregates, the five desires, and the five hindrances, to attain the five supernatural powers, and to cross beyond the five destinies. This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not wear fragrant flowers or adorn oneself.** Clothing, shoes, and thread must not be of five colors. One must not adorn oneself with various jewels as necklaces. One must not wear brocade, embroidered silk, gauze, or crepe. One must not look about coquettishly. One should wear coarse garments of blue, black, or magnolia bark color, along with the nirvāṇa undergarment, and walk with the head lowered. One wishes to eliminate the six sources of decline and to use the precepts as one's fragrance. One seeks to recite the profound Dharma as one's true treasure; the thirty-two marks serve as one's necklace; the planting of the excellent secondary marks serves as one's clothing. One aspires to the six supernatural powers without obstruction and the six pāramitās for guiding others. This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not sit on gold, silver, or high beds.** Embroidered brocade quilts and jeweled cushions must not even be thought of. One must not seek or request fine beds, couches, seats, fans painted in five colors, or excellent yak-tail whisks. One must not wear bracelets or finger rings. One should simply rely on faith, precepts, modesty, shame, generosity, broad learning, and wisdom, with a single mind of dedicated focus. One constantly seeks samādhi as one's bed and couch, with the mind unshaken. The various wisdoms naturally become one's sitting-mat, and the seven factors of awakening, unwavering, aspire to the mind of the path. This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not listen to singing, dancing, music, hand-clapping, or rhythmic drumming.** One must not do these oneself, nor instruct others to. One should constantly cultivate the body, practice in accord with the correct Dharma, and not engage in deviant practices. With a single mind one takes refuge in the Buddha, reciting the sūtras and practicing rectitude as the joy of the Dharma, not indulging in worldly pleasures. One listens to the sūtras, reflects, and deeply enters the great meaning. Unless one is ill, one must not ride in a carriage, or upon a horse or elephant. One should be mindful of lightness and agility, the eight inconceivable spiritual penetrations and attainments, which serve as one's vehicle, crossing beyond the eight difficulties. This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not accumulate precious treasures.** One must not handle them, and must not instruct others to. One should constantly focus with dedication, taking the path as one's treasure, the sūtras as supreme, the meaning as wonderful, and the understanding of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness as one's foundation. Arriving at the three liberations, one does not seek after craving and desire. One wishes to depart from the nine afflictions, to abide on the path for a very long time, without end or limit, without boundary or edge, and also without any fixed abiding. This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

**Precept for a śrāmaṇerī: One must not eat at the wrong time.** One should always eat at the proper time without exceeding the limit. After the sun has passed the midpoint, one must not eat again. Even if there are sweet and wonderful, incomparable flavors, one must never eat them again. One must also not instruct others to violate this, and the mind must not think of it. Even if the unsurpassed, spontaneous food were to appear, one still must not eat it. Even for an elder or a king, after the sun has passed the midpoint, one must not take food; one would rather die than violate this. One constantly contemplates dhyāna concentration. Although one may eat various foods, one takes only enough to barely sustain life. One wishes to cause all beings to understand deeply and fulfill their vows, attaining the ten powers as their food and drink. This is the precept for a śrāmaṇerī.

---

When a śrāmaṇerī has received the ten precepts, if she traces back to the path with pure reflection and can practice these ten things, the five hundred precepts will naturally be fully complete. It is like a person whose head, hands, feet, eyes, ears, nose, mouth, body, mind, belly, and back are undamaged, with all sense-faculties intact, and whose intestines, stomach, lungs, liver, five organs, various joints, sinews, and vessels are all complete. It is like a tree whose root is secure and intact, not withered or rotten: its trunk, joints, branches, leaves, flowers, and fruit will naturally flourish. Likewise for a śrāmaṇerī: if she can fully observe the ten precepts as the foundation, all five hundred precepts will be perfectly fulfilled, and she can attain spiritual penetrations with nothing she cannot comprehend. It is like a good field where the seed is not rotten and wind and rain come in due season: the five grains ripen abundantly and the people are well governed. Likewise for a śrāmaṇerī: if she can honor the ten precepts, the five hundred precepts are thereby upheld. It is like a kingdom's ruler whose civilizing influence is universally fair: all the people are at peace. Likewise for a śrāmaṇerī: if she can fully observe the ten precepts, the five hundred precepts are naturally and completely provided. It is like parents who are kind and harmonious: their children, grandchildren, relatives, servants, and maidservants will naturally follow in obedience.

Furthermore, a śrāmaṇerī should always revere the Three Treasures and respect her teacher and preceptor a hundred thousand million times more than her parents. Parents are for one lifetime, but the preceptor guides one across the limitless and boundless. One should be mindful of repaying this debt and not act in rebellion. One should always feel disgust for the impure body, as if imprisoned in a jail or fallen into a cesspool. One should not crave the female body, nor burn with desire for beauty. It is like a great fire, like being among bandits. The mind should be mindful of all beings, as of a father, a mother, a child, or oneself. One should always feel shame for one's past conduct and attitudes, and be displeased at having obtained this body. One should understand that fundamentally there is nothing, like an illusion or magical transformation: there is no male, no female, it is all obtained from one's actions. Fundamentally there are no five destinies; how much less male or female? Those who seek the great vehicle should comprehend that everything is empty, like illusions, magical transformations, dreams, shadows, echoes, mirages, and plantain trees, like an echo in a deep mountain: they arise in response to conditions and are fundamentally without any real existence. Know that form is like a shadow, feeling is like a plantain tree, perception is like a mirage, birth and death are like bubbles, and consciousness is like an illusion. There is no self. They are compounded through causes and conditions; without conditions they are nothing. One comes alone and goes alone; not a single thing follows.

Those who wish to pursue the path should take expedient wisdom as their parents, delight in the Dharma as their siblings, never depart from the profound meaning as their preceptor, take kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity and firm abiding in the correct Dharma as their children, the six pāramitās as their companions, the wisdom of spiritual penetrations as their vehicle, and non-violation of the sūtra-precepts together with contemplation of the meaning of emptiness as their dwelling.

Furthermore, a śrāmaṇerī must not travel alone. She should have companions of the same kind, two or three persons. If there are no śrāmaṇerīs, she should travel with devout laywomen. Not even a maidservant may sit together with a senior śramaṇa or a man on the same bed or seat. One must not speak falsely. Furthermore, one must not reside adjacent to a monastery. Unless one is ill, one should not make frequent visits. If a patron invites one to read the sūtras, then one may explain them. One must not act on one's own authority. Those of equal rank should instruct each other, showing deference according to seniority and not being arrogant or willful. They should guide and instruct each other in turn. If there are faults, admonish in private, and do not speak of them before the assembly. When one hears or sees something good, one may proclaim it. When one hears or sees something evil, one must not spread it, but should only report it to the preceptor and not tell others. One should constantly reprove oneself, and upon seeing the good, aspire to emulate it. Upon seeing evil, examine oneself, and feel compassion for that person whose intention fell short.

When a śrāmaṇerī seeks a preceptor, she should obtain one who is wise, intelligent, knowledgeable, obedient to the Dharma, and capable of guiding people lifetime after lifetime. It is like a sturdy and complete boat that can ferry one across to the other shore. If the teacher is not wise and her conduct does not accord with the Dharma, she is not a great teacher. If one takes such a person as one's preceptor, it is like a broken boat: embarking to cross the sea, it sinks midway, drowning all aboard, and the teacher likewise perishes, with no one surviving. The one who first upholds the Dharma and transmits the sūtras and precepts to others is, if correct, one's teacher. If one hears of a great teacher whom one wishes to take as preceptor, but who was not one's original śrāmaṇerī master, one may respectfully invoke her name from afar and take her as teacher without necessarily meeting face to face. When the mind is close, there is closeness; when the mind is distant, there is distance. Even if one is physically near, if the mind is at odds and not in harmony, one is separated by a hundred million miles. When a śrāmaṇerī travels, she must not walk alongside a man or follow him on the same road. She must not share adjacent rooms or the same monastery with men or śramaṇas. They should each keep to their separate places: this is the great principle of the Dharma.
