# 佛說龍施女經
## Sūtra of the Girl Nāgadattā, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0557

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Vaiśālī, at the Āmrapālī garden, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and five hundred bodhisattvas.

At dawn the Buddha put on his robe, took his bowl, and entered the city to beg for food. The entire assembly followed. The offerings of incense, flowers, and music from the devas, nāgas, and spirits were double the usual.

The Buddha arrived outside the gate of the elder Supuṇya. Supuṇya had a daughter named Nāgadattā, fourteen years of age. She was in the bathing room, bathing, applying fragrant ointments, and putting on fine garments. The light of the white tuft between the Buddha's brows shone upon the seven-story tower. Looking eastward, she saw the Buddha standing outside the gate, his appearance dignified like the moon among the stars, with extraordinary marks and excellent features, golden complexion, serene, his faculties perfectly still.

The girl was filled with great joy and thought to herself: "Now I have seen the Buddha and his assembly of disciples. I should resolve to practice the bodhisattva path. May I attain the Way and become like the Buddha!"

Māra saw the girl generate this great resolve and was displeased. He thought: "This girl now produces great merit and seeks buddhahood. She will certainly pass beyond my realm and lead many people to liberation. I must go and destroy her resolve for the path."

Māra descended and took on the appearance and garments of the girl's father. He said to Nāgadattā: "What you are now contemplating is too great a burden. The buddha-path is difficult to attain, requiring tireless effort through hundreds of millions of kalpas before it can be accomplished. In this present age we are fortunate to have a Buddha. Rather than seeking buddhahood, why not seek arhatship? It is easier to attain, and both alike lead to liberation. Nirvāṇa is the same either way. Why be greedy for buddhahood and endure such long suffering? You are my daughter, and I tell you this for your sake."

Nāgadattā replied: "What father says is wrong. Although arhats and Buddhas both lead to liberation from the world, their merit is not the same. The Buddha's wisdom is vast and boundless, like the space of the ten directions, saving people without limit. The arhat's wisdom is small, lasting but a single moment. What person of superior ability would delight in the lesser?"

Māra said again: "It has never been heard that a woman can become a wheel-turning holy king, much less become a Buddha! The buddha-path is long. It would be better to seek arhatship and quickly enter nirvāṇa."

Nāgadattā replied: "I too have heard that a woman cannot become a wheel-turning holy king, cannot become Śakra, cannot become Lord Brahmā, and cannot become a Buddha. I shall practice diligently and transform this female body, receiving a male body in its place. For I have heard that the World-Honored One teaches that all who practice the bodhisattva path without faltering through countless kalpas shall in the end become Buddhas."

Māra, seeing that the girl's resolve would not turn, grew even more distressed and anxious. He pressed her further: "Those who practice the bodhisattva path must not be attached to the world and must not cling to their lives. If you are truly diligent and can throw yourself from this tower to the ground, then you may become a Buddha."

Nāgadattā thought: "It was seeing the Buddha that made me aspire to the bodhisattva path. My father has instructed me that by diligently abandoning the body, I can attain the buddha-path. Why should I cling to this fragile and perishable life?"

The girl went to the railing, joined her palms toward the Buddha, and said: "I now take refuge in the God of Gods. With compassion for all beings, may he know what I seek. I offer up this body and life. I do not abandon the bodhisattva way. I give my body to the Buddha." Having made this vow and scattered flowers, she threw herself from the tower. While still in midair, before reaching the ground, her female body transformed into that of a man.

Then the Buddha smiled, and five-colored light issued from his mouth, illuminating one buddha-land, then returned and entered through the crown of his head. The worthy Ānanda came forward and knelt, asking: "The Buddha does not smile without reason. May we hear its meaning!"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! Did you see this girl throw herself into the air and transform into a man?"

He replied: "I saw it."

The Buddha said: "In past lives this girl served ten thousand Buddhas. In the future she shall make offerings to Tathāgatas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. After seven billion six hundred million kalpas she shall become a Buddha, named Nāgaśreṣṭha, with a lifespan of one kalpa. After entering parinirvāṇa, the sūtras and the path shall flourish for half a kalpa before coming to an end. When that Buddha teaches the Dharma, she shall lead ninety-seven billion people to attain the paths of the bodhisattva and the arhat. At that time the food and drink of the people shall be like those of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven."

Then Nāgadattā, in his transformed body, stood before the Buddha and addressed his parents: "May you release me and allow me to become a śramaṇa!" His parents immediately consented. The relatives of the family, numbering five hundred in all, along with eight hundred devas, seeing the girl Nāgadattā transform into a man, all generated the mind of unsurpassed true awakening. Māra, seeing so many people seeking the buddha-path, was even more worried and unhappy. He departed in shame.

When the Buddha had spoken, all were filled with joy.
