# T1570 廣百論本

# Root Text of the Expanded Hundred Treatise (Catuḥśataka)

*Note: This text contains the root verses (mūla) of Āryadeva's (Shèngtiān 聖天) Catuḥśataka ("Four Hundred Verses"), translated by Xuanzang (Xuánzàng 玄奘) during the Tang dynasty. The full Catuḥśataka had sixteen chapters, but this translation preserves only the latter eight chapters (chapters 9 through 16 of the original), which present the philosophical core of Madhyamaka argumentation. The eight chapters systematically dismantle: permanence (including atomic theory), self (including Vaiśeṣika and Sāṃkhya conceptions), time (past, present, and future), views (contrasting Buddhist, Brahmanical, and Jain teachings), sense-faculties and their objects (perception theory), extreme grasping (the one-and-many argument), conditioned characteristics (the paradoxes of arising, abiding, and ceasing), and finally offer instructions to disciples on the correct understanding of emptiness. This is one of the foundational texts of Madhyamaka philosophy. Āryadeva was the principal disciple of Nāgārjuna, and his work extends the analysis of the Mūlamadhyamakakārikā into sustained polemical engagement with non-Buddhist and Buddhist realist positions. The text concludes with Āryadeva's famous declaration comparing himself to a fire fueled by the Tathāgata's teaching.*

## Chapter One: Breaking Permanence

> All things arise for the sake of producing results;
> Therefore they are of impermanent nature.
> Thus apart from the Buddha, no one
> Is truly called "Tathāgata."

> There is no time, place, or thing
> Whose nature is not born from conditions;
> Therefore no time, place, or thing
> Has a nature that is permanent.

> Nothing has a nature without a cause,
> And having a cause means it is not permanent;
> Therefore those who wish to establish something without a cause:
> True seers say it does not exist.

> Seeing that what is produced is impermanent,
> One might claim what is unproduced is permanent;
> But since we observe that impermanent things exist,
> We should say that permanence does not.

> Foolish people falsely discriminate,
> Calling space and such things permanent;
> But wise people, even conventionally,
> Do not perceive such a meaning.

> If a single whole pervades
> All of its parts,
> Then we know that in each and every part
> There is a separate whole.

> If a thing truly exists,
> Its contraction and expansion should be observable;
> But this would mean it is produced by something else,
> And therefore it becomes a produced result.

> If apart from the produced result
> There is no producing cause,
> Then the producing cause
> Itself becomes the produced result.

> All dharmas must undergo transformation
> Before they can serve as causes for other things;
> A cause that transforms in this way:
> How can it be called permanent?

> If what was originally nonexistent now comes to be,
> And "spontaneous nature" is posited as its cause,
> Since one already admits spontaneous nature exists,
> The positing of a cause is then superfluous.

> How can the permanent
> Give rise to the impermanent?
> Cause and result differing in nature:
> This is something the world has never seen.

> If one part is the cause
> And the remaining parts are not,
> Then it should become manifold;
> Being manifold, it is not permanent.

> The minute, round form of the cause
> Is not found in the result;
> Therefore it is not the case that atoms
> Combine with their entire bodies.

> Where one atom exists,
> No other atom is admitted;
> Therefore one should also not admit
> That cause and result are equal in size.

> If an atom has an eastern side,
> It must have an eastern part;
> If an atom has parts,
> How can it be an atom?

> One must grasp the front and release the rear
> Before one can speak of motion;
> If these two are nonexistent,
> A moving thing should not exist.

> An atom has no initial part,
> And no middle or final part either;
> Therefore it cannot be seen
> By any eye whatsoever.

> If the cause is destroyed by its result,
> That cause is not permanent;
> Otherwise one would have to admit that result and cause
> Are two entities in different locations.

> One does not observe any dharma
> That is both permanent and resistant;
> Therefore that atoms are permanent:
> This the buddhas have never taught.

> Apart from the cause of bondage and the one bound,
> There is no further true liberation;
> Since the function of arising and creation is lacking,
> Even if it existed, it would be called nonexistent.

> At the time of final nirvāṇa,
> There are no aggregates and no self;
> Without seeing one who has attained nirvāṇa,
> On what basis could there be nirvāṇa?

> In the self's time, one abandons all qualities;
> Apart from craving, what thought remains?
> If there is a self without thought,
> Its utility amounts to nothing.

> If a seedless self remains,
> It will certainly be able to produce thought;
> But if there is to be neither self nor thought,
> Then all existences will be no more.

> If apart from suffering there is a self,
> There can certainly be no nirvāṇa;
> Therefore in nirvāṇa,
> Self and all else are permanently extinguished.

> Better to seek within the world
> Than to seek in ultimate reality;
> For in the world a little can be found,
> But in ultimate reality there is nothing at all.

## Chapter Two: Breaking the Self

> The inner self is truly neither male
> Nor female nor neuter;
> It is only through ignorance
> That one calls the self "a person."

> If within the great elements
> There is no male, female, or neuter,
> How is it that from the great elements
> Male and other characteristics arise?

> What is "self" to you is "not-self" to another;
> Therefore the self has no fixed characteristic.
> Is it not the case that regarding what is impermanent,
> One falsely discriminates it as self?

> The self being identical to the body,
> It changes from life to life;
> Therefore that apart from the body there is a self
> That permanently abides: this reasoning does not hold.

> If a dharma has no tangible resistance,
> It has no motion or activity;
> Therefore bodily actions
> Are not produced by a "life-force."

> The self, being permanent, cannot be harmed;
> Why then trouble to cultivate protective causes?
> Who fears that someone will eat a diamond
> And takes up a staff to ward off insects?

> If there is memory of past lives,
> One might claim the self is permanent;
> But since we also see scars from former times,
> The body too should then be permanent.

> If the self merges with thought
> And thereby becomes a thinker,
> Thought too should become non-thought;
> Therefore the self is not permanent.

> The self merges with pleasure and other states,
> Becoming manifold like pleasure and the rest;
> Since the self is like pleasure and such states,
> It is neither one nor permanent.

> If you say the self-and-thought are permanent,
> With conditions assisting to form this wrong view:
> It would be like saying fire is permanent
> And does not depend on fuel.

> Just as when one arrives at the cessation of a moving thing,
> Its functions are no more,
> So having a self without thought:
> This reasoning does not hold.

> When the domain of arising thought is elsewhere,
> And one observes thought in a different place,
> Like an iron bar melted and reformed,
> The self's substance should be destroyed and changed.

> Thought is small, the measure of the mind;
> The self is vast like space.
> If one could only observe one's own characteristics,
> One would not perceive thought at all.

> If the self's qualities were omnipresent,
> Why would others not receive them?
> If the obstructions too are called omnipresent,
> They should not obstruct only one.

> If qualities include both compassion and thought,
> How could they create all things?
> They should then be like confusion and madness:
> Both deluded, accomplishing nothing.

> If qualities can skillfully understand
> And construct houses and such things,
> Yet do not know how to enjoy them:
> Is there any absurdity greater than this?

> What has motion is impermanent; what is like space
> Has no motion at all.
> Without function it is the same as nonexistence;
> Why not rejoice in non-self?

> Some view the self as omnipresent,
> Others see it as equal in measure to the body,
> Others hold it to be like an atom:
> The wise understand that none of these exist.

> What is permanent cannot be tormented;
> How then could abandoning torment be liberation?
> Therefore, holding the self as permanent
> And claiming to attain liberation: this reasoning does not hold.

> If the self truly has a real nature,
> One should not praise abandoning the self;
> Those who truly know reality:
> Their journey toward liberation should then be empty.

> If in liberation there is nothing,
> Then before liberation too it should not exist;
> What is seen in the unmixed state:
> That should be known as its true nature.

> If the impermanent were all annihilated,
> Why would grass and such things not perish likewise?
> If this reasoning were truly valid,
> Ignorance too would not exist.

> We directly perceive that form and other conditioned dharmas
> Arise, abide, and cease dependent on conditions;
> Therefore know that the self you grasp,
> Though you claim it exists, in truth does not exist.

> Just as from conditions sprouts and such arise,
> And from conditions seeds and such are born,
> So impermanent dharmas
> Are all produced by impermanent things.

> Because dharmas arise from conditions,
> Their substance is without annihilation;
> Because dharmas cease from conditions,
> Their substance is also not permanent.

## Chapter Three: Breaking Time

> A pot in the future
> Is neither in the past nor present;
> If the future possessed past and present,
> Then it would have no future.

> If the future has already passed away
> Yet has a future substance,
> Then it would be permanently future:
> How could it become past or present?

> If a dharma is in the future
> And currently possesses a future characteristic,
> It should then be the present;
> How could it be called future?

> If past and future are like the present in existing,
> Why do they lack the function of producing results?
> If their substance were permanently non-nonexistent,
> Why would they not be permanent?

> If the past is past,
> How can it constitute "the past"?
> If the past is not past,
> How can it constitute "the past"?

> If the future has arising,
> How is it not the present?
> If the future has no arising,
> How is it not permanent?

> If the future has no arising,
> And it is not permanent because it perishes:
> Since the past has no perishing,
> Why should it not be called permanent?

> The present is impermanent
> Not because of the past or the future;
> Apart from these two directions,
> There is no third alternative.

> If dharmas that will later arise
> Already have a fixed substance beforehand,
> Then those who assert fixed natures
> Should not be called holders of wrong views.

> If dharmas arise from causes and conditions,
> They do not have a prior substance;
> If what already exists were to arise,
> What has arisen should arise yet again.

> If one perceives past and future as existent,
> How can one not perceive them as nonexistent?
> Since one perceives that there is past and future,
> One should not say they are remote.

> If an unmade dharma already exists,
> Cultivating precepts and such would be in vain;
> If there is even the slightest purpose to be served,
> Then the result does not already exist.

> Since all conditioned dharmas are impermanent,
> Results do not permanently exist;
> If something has a beginning and an end,
> The world agrees it is impermanent.

> One should not strive for liberation:
> Liberation has no past or future;
> Or, if one admits that past and future exist,
> Craving should be found even in one free from craving.

> If you hold that the result already exists,
> Then building palaces and ornaments,
> Pillars and such, would be in vain;
> And if the result initially does not exist, likewise.

> That dharmas undergo real transformation:
> The wise have never held this;
> Only those without wisdom
> Falsely discriminate this as existing.

> Impermanence: how can there be abiding?
> And without abiding, how can there be substance?
> If in the beginning there were abiding,
> Afterward there should be no decay.

> Just as no single consciousness
> Can apprehend two objects,
> So no single object
> Can be known by two consciousnesses.

> If time has some remaining abiding,
> That abiding would not constitute time;
> If time has no remaining abiding,
> Afterward there should be no cessation.

> If dharma is different from impermanence,
> Dharma would not be impermanent;
> If dharma is identical with impermanence,
> Dharma should have no abiding.

> If initially impermanence is weak
> And the power of abiding is necessarily strong,
> For what reason do these two
> Later appear reversed?

> If impermanence pervades the substance of all dharmas
> But its power is initially weak,
> Then there should be no abiding at all,
> Or else everything should be permanent.

> If impermanence perpetually exists,
> The abiding characteristic should be permanently absent;
> Or else that dharma would first be permanent
> And only later become impermanent.

> If dharma and impermanence coexist
> Yet one says there is abiding,
> Then the impermanence characteristic would be false,
> Or the abiding characteristic would be empty.

> Seeing nothing, one sees nothing;
> The mind turns to grasp an illusory object.
> Therefore it is only a false designation
> That has the name "arising" from memory.

## Chapter Four: Breaking Views

> Those who are temperate, who seek superior wisdom:
> They should be known as vessels of the Dharma.
> Apart from these, whether teacher or student,
> There is no cause for obtaining supreme benefit.

> He taught existence and the cause of existence,
> Purity and the means of purification.
> The world itself does not understand;
> How is this fault the Sage's?

> To abandon all existences is nirvāṇa:
> This is admitted even by heterodox schools.
> True emptiness destroys all views;
> How can they not delight in it?

> Without knowing the causes for abandoning and realizing,
> There is no way to abandon or realize;
> Therefore the Sage taught:
> Apart from this cooling, there is certainly nothing else.

> If regarding what the Buddha has taught,
> Profound matters give rise to doubt,
> One may rely on signless emptiness
> And give rise to certain faith.

> Observing the present, there is still error;
> Know that conclusions about the future are certainly false.
> All who follow those teachings and practice them
> Will be deceived without end.

> The wise person's own nirvāṇa:
> This is a difficult deed well done.
> Foolish people, even meeting a good guide,
> Have no mind to follow the way.

> Not knowing does not cause fear;
> Complete knowing is likewise.
> It is precisely from partial knowing
> That fear arises.

> The dharma of going with the stream of birth and death:
> Foolish people constantly practice it.
> Since they have never cultivated going against the stream,
> This is why they give rise to fear.

> All foolish and deluded people
> Obstruct others from true seeing;
> They have no way to reach good destinies:
> How then could they realize nirvāṇa?

> Better to violate moral precepts
> Than to damage right view;
> For moral precepts lead to good destinies,
> But right view leads to nirvāṇa.

> Better to give rise to grasping of a self
> Than to hold the view of emptiness as non-self with nihilistic wrong views;
> The latter at the same time leads to evil destinies,
> While the former only turns one's back on nirvāṇa.

> Emptiness and non-self, the wondrous principle:
> The true domain of all buddhas.
> It can terrify all evil views:
> The non-dual gate of nirvāṇa.

> When fools hear the name "emptiness,"
> They all give rise to great terror;
> Like seeing a person of great strength,
> The timid all flee in panic.

> Although the buddhas have no intention
> Of teaching dharmas that crush others' theories,
> Others' theories destroy themselves,
> Like wildfire burning firewood.

> All who have awakened to the true Dharma
> Certainly do not delight in heterodox schools;
> To reveal the false gate for others' sake,
> They therefore display the meaning of true emptiness.

> If one knows what the Buddha taught,
> The principle of true emptiness and non-self,
> One follows without elation
> And departs without aversion or fear.

> Seeing the various non-Buddhist assemblies
> Committed to many meaningless causes,
> What person who delights in the true Dharma
> Would not feel deep compassion?

> The Brahmanical, Jain, and Tathāgata:
> These are the three schools of the world.
> Known through ear, eye, and mind respectively;
> Therefore the Buddha-Dharma is subtle and profound.

> What the Brahmins teach
> Mostly involves practicing deception;
> The Jain teachings
> Mostly follow foolishness.

> One respects the Brahmins
> Because they recite the sacred texts;
> One has pity for the Jains
> Because they torment their own bodies.

> Just as the fruit of ascetic practice
> Is not the true cause of liberation,
> So the fruit born of excellent bodily karma
> Is also not the realization of liberation.

> In brief, what the Buddha taught
> Has two distinctions from other schools:
> Non-harming leads to the human and heavenly realms,
> And contemplating emptiness leads to liberation.

> People of the world cling to their own school
> Like loving their native land;
> The true Dharma can crush and destroy them,
> Yet the heterodox partisans feel no delight.

> Those with wisdom who seek supreme virtue
> Should accept and trust the true teaching;
> The true Dharma is like the sun's orb:
> Those with eyes can see because of it.

## Chapter Five: Breaking Sense-Faculties and Objects

> Among the parts of a pot,
> What is visible is only its color;
> To say the whole pot is visible:
> How can one awaken to truth this way?

> All persons of superior wisdom
> Should, following the meaning already explained,
> With regard to scent, taste, and tangibles,
> Apply the same refutation throughout.

> If merely seeing the color of a pot
> Is called "seeing the pot,"
> Since one does not perceive its scent and other properties,
> One should be said "not to see the pot."

> All obstructive forms
> Are not entirely visible in substance;
> Their parts and interstices
> Are blocked by these very parts.

> Whether atoms have parts or not:
> This should be carefully and truly investigated.
> To cite what is unestablished as proof:
> The meaning can never be established.

> All dharmas with obstruction
> Are composed of many parts;
> Words and syllables are likewise;
> Therefore they are not apprehended by the sense-faculties.

> If apart from manifest color there is shape,
> How does one apprehend shape-color?
> If shape is apprehended through manifest color,
> Why is it not apprehended through the body?

> If apart from color there is a cause of color,
> It should not be seen by the eye;
> Since the two are different in substance,
> How can they not be perceived separately?

> The body perceives solidity and such;
> Together we establish names like "earth."
> Therefore only within the tangible
> Are distinctions of earth and such explained.

> When the visible aspect of a pot arises,
> No different quality is seen;
> Its substance arises just as it is seen;
> Therefore real nature is utterly nonexistent.

> Eyes and other faculties are all made of the great elements;
> Why does the eye see but not the rest?
> Therefore the fruits of karma are inconceivable:
> This the Sage has truly taught.

> Since the object of cognition does not yet exist,
> Cognition cannot precede seeing;
> Coming afterward, cognition is useless;
> Arising simultaneously, seeing serves no purpose.

> If the eye traveled to its object,
> Seeing distant forms should be slow;
> Why does it not also clearly
> Illuminate the very near and the very far?

> If it sees and then travels,
> The traveling would be useless;
> If it travels without seeing,
> There can certainly be no desire to see.

> If it observes without traveling,
> It should see all forms whatsoever;
> Since the eye has no motion,
> There is no distance and no obstruction.

> The substance, characteristics, and function of all dharmas
> Should be the same before and after;
> How then does this eye-faculty
> Not see its own eye-nature?

> In the eye there is no form or consciousness;
> In consciousness there is no form or eye;
> Within form, both are absent;
> How then can the combination see form?

> If what is heard can express meaning,
> Why does it not constitute non-sound?
> If sound is not what expresses,
> Why does hearing it give rise to understanding?

> If sound must reach the ear to be heard,
> How does one comprehend the source of the sound?
> Since sound has no principle of being spoken all at once,
> How can it be known in its entirety?

> So long as something is not heard,
> It should not have the nature of sound;
> That what was previously nonexistent later comes to be:
> This reasoning certainly does not hold.

> If the mind were separate from all sense-faculties,
> Its departing would also be useless;
> If this were so, the "life-force"
> Should permanently have no mind.

> Causing the mind to falsely grasp objects,
> Dependent on prior perception, like a mirage,
> Falsely establishing the meaning of all dharmas:
> This is what should be known as the aggregate of perception.

> With eye, form, and such as conditions,
> Like an illusion, the various consciousnesses arise;
> If one grasps them as truly real,
> The simile of illusion should not hold.

> All that exists in the world
> Is without exception difficult to fathom;
> Faculties and objects are the same in principle;
> Why should the wise be startled?

> All dharmas are like a fire-wheel,
> Transformation, dream, illusion,
> The moon in water, a comet, an echo,
> A mirage, and a floating cloud.

## Chapter Six: Breaking Extreme Grasping

> If all dharmas were truly existent,
> They should not depend on others for their establishment;
> Since they necessarily depend on others,
> We know they are certainly not truly existent.

> The pot is not identical with color,
> Nor is there a pot apart from color;
> Color does not depend on a pot,
> Nor does a pot depend on color.

> If seeing two characteristics as different,
> One says that apart from the pot there is "sameness,"
> Since the two characteristics are distinct,
> One should say that apart from the pot there is "difference."

> If "one" does not constitute "pot,"
> Then "pot" should not constitute "one";
> Since pot and oneness have never been united,
> The pot should not bear the name "one."

> If color pervades substance,
> Color should receive the name "large";
> If your opponent is truly not other,
> You should set forth your own position.

> When number and other qualifying characteristics
> Fail to establish what they qualify,
> And apart from these there is no other basis:
> All dharmas are therefore nonexistent.

> Apart from its distinct characteristics the pot does not exist;
> Therefore the pot's substance is not one.
> Since no single characteristic is the pot,
> The pot's substance is also not many.

> It is not the case that an intangible substance
> Can combine with a tangible substance;
> Therefore form and other dharmas
> Cannot be combined to make a pot.

> Color is one part of a pot;
> Therefore color's substance is not the pot.
> Since the whole is nonexistent,
> How can any part exist?

> The nature of all color and such:
> Between different colors and such there is no distinction.
> That only one class constitutes a pot
> And the rest do not: what reason is there for this?

> If color is different from taste and such
> But not different from pot and such,
> Then pot and such are identical to taste and such:
> How can color be identical to pot and such?

> Since pot and such have no cause,
> Their substance cannot constitute a result;
> Therefore if different from color and such,
> Pot and such are certainly nonexistent.

> If the cause of pot and such existed,
> It could serve as the cause of pot and such;
> Since the cause of pot and such does not exist,
> How can it produce pot and such?

> When color and such are combined,
> They never become scent and such;
> Therefore the one substance of combination
> Should, like pot and such, be nonexistent.

> Just as apart from color and such
> The pot's substance is truly nonexistent,
> The substance of color too should be likewise:
> Apart from wind and such it does not exist.

> Heat is precisely the nature of fire;
> Without heat, how could it burn?
> Therefore the substance of fuel is nonexistent;
> Apart from fuel, fire does not exist.

> If heat from elsewhere, mixed, constitutes fire,
> Why does it not constitute fire in other things?
> If other things do not constitute heat,
> The dharma of fire should not exist.

> If a fire-atom has no fuel,
> There should be fire apart from fuel;
> If a fire-atom has fuel,
> There should be no fire-atom.

> When one carefully examines all dharmas,
> Not a single substance is truly existent;
> Since one substance is nonexistent,
> Many substances should also be nonexistent.

> If a dharma has nothing further besides itself,
> You call it "one substance";
> But all dharmas have three natures;
> Therefore "one substance" is nonexistent.

> Existent, nonexistent, both, neither;
> One, not-one, both extinguished:
> These should be matched in sequence;
> The wise understand they are not true.

> Regarding the false dharma of continuity,
> Evil views take it as truly permanent;
> In the false dharma of accumulation,
> Wrong views claim it is truly existent.

> All dharmas are formed by many conditions,
> Their nature is weak and without autonomy;
> Falsely established dependent on others:
> Therefore self and dharmas are both nonexistent.

> The result is formed by the combination of many conditions;
> Apart from conditions there is no separate result.
> Thus both the combination and the result:
> The noble ones understand that both are nonexistent.

> Consciousness is the seed of all existences;
> Objects are the domain where consciousness operates.
> When one sees that objects are without self,
> The seeds of all existences are extinguished.

## Chapter Seven: Breaking Conditioned Characteristics

> If what was originally nonexistent now arises,
> Then what was previously nonexistent should not arise.
> If what originally exists now arises,
> Then what already exists should arise yet again.

> If the result could oppose the cause,
> "Previously nonexistent" would be unreasonable;
> If the result is established and the cause is without function,
> "Previously existent" also does not hold.

> At this time there is no arising;
> At that time there is also no arising.
> Since at both times there is no arising,
> At what time could arising occur?

> Just as arising of a thing's own nature
> Has no meaning as "arising,"
> So too arising from another nature:
> How could the meaning of "arising" be established?

> Beginning, middle, and end, three positions:
> Arising cannot be established beforehand.
> Since two-and-one are all nonexistent,
> How can any single one exist?

> It is not that, apart from another's nature,
> Something arises only from its own nature;
> Not from another, not from both:
> Therefore arising certainly does not exist.

> Before and after and simultaneously:
> Both together cannot be stated.
> Therefore arising, together with pot and such,
> Is only provisionally existent, not truly real.

> If the old existed before the new,
> Prior arising would be unreasonable;
> If the old comes after the new,
> Later arising would not hold either.

> The present does not arise because of the present,
> Nor because of the past or future;
> The future also does not arise because of
> Past, future, or present.

> If something is complete, it has no coming;
> Once extinguished, it should not be going.
> The nature of all dharmas being thus,
> The similes of illusion and such are not empty words.

> Arising, abiding, and ceasing, three characteristics:
> That they exist simultaneously does not hold.
> That they exist sequentially is also nonexistent:
> How can one insist they exist?

> If arising and the other characteristics
> Each had separate arising and such,
> Abiding and ceasing would be like arising,
> Or arising and abiding would be like ceasing.

> If the characterized is different from the characteristic,
> Why would its substance not be permanent?
> If not different, the four should be identical,
> Or else completely nonexistent.

> The existent does not produce the existent;
> The existent does not produce the nonexistent;
> The nonexistent does not produce the existent;
> The nonexistent does not produce the nonexistent.

> The existent does not become the existent;
> The existent does not become the nonexistent;
> The nonexistent does not become the existent;
> The nonexistent does not become the nonexistent.

> Half-arisen and half-not-yet-arisen
> Is not one single "arising-time" substance;
> Otherwise in the not-yet-arisen position
> One should also say it is the "arising-time."

> If the arising-time is a result,
> Its substance is then not "arising-time";
> If the arising-time is spontaneous,
> It should lose its arising-time nature.

> "Already arisen" is different from "not yet arisen";
> Is there separately an intermediate position?
> "Arising-time" being different from both:
> There should separately be an intermediate between them.

> If one says that "arising-time" must be relinquished
> Before one reaches "already arisen":
> Then there should be yet another stage,
> An "obtaining time" that could be observed.

> If one reaches the "already arisen" position,
> In principle there can be no "arising-time";
> If "already arisen" contains "arising-time,"
> How could it arise from the latter?

> If one has not yet reached the "already arisen" position,
> And one calls this the "arising-time":
> Why not say there is "no pot"?
> Since "not yet arisen" is no different.

> It is not that "arising-time" has a function
> That distinguishes it from "not yet arisen";
> Nor is its substance incomplete,
> Distinguishing it from "already arisen."

> In the earlier position "arising-time" does not exist;
> Only in the later position is it said to exist.
> This simultaneously establishes the "already arisen" position;
> Therefore this position is not nonexistent.

> "When it exists" is called "already arisen";
> "When it does not exist" is called "not yet arisen."
> Apart from these positions of existence and nonexistence,
> Who further speaks of "arising-time"?

> All who hold that apart from the cause
> There is a separate result:
> Neither transformation-arising nor transformation-ceasing
> Can in principle be established.

## Chapter Eight: Instructions to Disciples

> Through a few reasons,
> One may doubt emptiness and call it non-empty;
> Relying on the preceding chapters,
> The reasoning and teaching should again dispel this.

> If speaker, spoken, and listener all exist,
> The principle of emptiness would be nonexistent;
> But all dharmas are provisionally established through conditions;
> Therefore these three are nonexistent.

> If one only points out the faults of emptiness,
> The principle of non-emptiness would be established;
> But the faults of non-emptiness have already been made clear,
> So the principle of emptiness should first be established.

> All who wish to destroy others' positions
> Must establish their own thesis;
> Why do they delight in discussing others' faults
> While not establishing their own position?

> To break the attachment to "one" and such,
> One provisionally establishes "removal" as one's thesis;
> Once the other's three-fold attachment is removed,
> One's own thesis likewise does not stand.

> Admitting that attachment is direct perception,
> And that emptiness-as-cause is without capacity:
> The direct perception and causes of other schools
> Are precisely what this school does not admit.

> If there is no principle of non-emptiness,
> How can the principle of emptiness be established?
> Since you yourself do not establish emptiness,
> Non-emptiness should also not be established.

> If one admits the existence of both theses, "existence" and "nonexistence,"
> Only then can a thesis of "existence" be established;
> If the thesis of "nonexistence" does not exist,
> The thesis of "existence" should not hold either.

> If all dharmas are entirely empty,
> How could fire be called "heat"?
> This, as previously fully refuted:
> Fire-and-heat are conventional, not ultimate.

> If one says dharmas truly exist,
> And one negates this by calling it "empty,"
> Then all four propositions should all be true;
> Seeing what fault would one abandon them?

> If all dharmas were entirely nonexistent,
> Saṃsāra should not exist;
> The buddhas have never admitted
> The fixed view that dharmas are nonexistent.

> If truth is apart from existence and nonexistence,
> Why does one speak of conventional existence?
> Your own original position is likewise;
> What purpose does raising objections serve?

> If all dharmas were entirely nonexistent,
> Differences should not exist;
> Holding all dharmas as entirely existent,
> Differences should also not exist.

> If one says dharmas are nonexistent,
> And there is no cause that can break "existence":
> The cause of breaking "existence" has already been made clear;
> Why does your position not stand?

> To say the breaking cause is easy to obtain:
> This is an empty conventional statement.
> Why can you not
> Refute the principle of true emptiness?

> The name "existent" expresses that a dharma exists,
> So one claims dharmas are truly not nonexistent;
> The name "nonexistent" expresses that a dharma does not exist,
> So dharmas should truly not be existent.

> Through the name one understands the dharma as existent,
> And thereby says the dharma is not nonexistent;
> Through the name one knows the dharma as nonexistent:
> One should trust that the dharma is not existent.

> All that can be spoken of in the world
> Is all provisional, not real;
> Apart from worldly conventional language,
> That alone is real, not provisional.

> To slander all dharmas as nonexistent
> Might cause one to fall into the view of nonexistence;
> But merely to cleanse all false attachments:
> How can one say this falls into nonexistence?

> "Existent" is not truly existent;
> Therefore "nonexistent" is also not truly nonexistent.
> Since there is no true nonexistence,
> How can there be true existence?

> From a cause one proves dharmas are empty,
> So "dharma-emptiness" should not be established as a thesis;
> Since thesis and cause are not different,
> The cause's substance is truly nonexistent.

> Saying that the "empty" analogy is something separate,
> One infers that all dharmas are non-empty;
> But if only the analogy of "existence" were established,
> The inner self would be the same as a crow's blackness.

> If dharmas are originally empty in nature,
> What merit is there in seeing emptiness?
> It is false discrimination that binds;
> Realizing the view of emptiness can remove it.

> If a dharma is established as "one," it is established as nonexistent;
> This contradicts both truth and convention.
> Therefore to say it is identical with or different from "existence":
> Neither can be stated.

> Existent, nonexistent, both, and neither:
> All positions are quiescent.
> In the midst of these, whoever wishes to raise objections
> Will ultimately be unable to do so.

---

When Āryadeva Bodhisattva had completed the composition of this treatise, he restated his refutation of heterodox views in a final verse:

> "I am a blazing fire that burns heterodox doctrines,
> Fed with the ghee of the Tathāgata's true teaching,
> And fanned by the great wind of logic:
> Who dares approach like a moth drawn to fierce flames?"

This completes the Root Text of the Expanded Hundred Treatise, composed by Āryadeva Bodhisattva, translated by the Tripiṭaka Master Xuanzang of the Great Tang.
