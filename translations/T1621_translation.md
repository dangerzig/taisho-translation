# 掌中論
## Treatise in the Palm of the Hand

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1621

Composed by Dignāga (陳那). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨).

---

The treatise states: In the three realms there are only conventional names; external objects do not truly exist, being grasped through false conceptualization. Now, wishing to provide for those who have not yet realized the truth a gate for discriminatively determining the inherent nature of all dharmas and enabling a non-inverted understanding, this treatise is composed. The verse states:

> Upon a rope, the understanding of a snake arises;
> Seeing the rope, one knows the object is nonexistent.
> If one then examines its parts,
> One knows the rope-understanding is as mistaken as the snake-understanding.

The treatise states: As when in a not too distant place where things are indistinct, one sees only the rope's semblance of a snake; being unable to discern its distinctive inherent nature, deceived by confusion, one definitively determines it to be a snake. Later, having discerned its distinctive characteristics, one knows that since it arose from false conceptualization and deceptive confusion, it is merely a mistaken understanding with no real object. Furthermore, when one skillfully examines the rope's parts and their distinctions, the inherent substance of the rope itself cannot be found. Knowing this, the cognition of the rope, just like the cognition of the snake, is only confused consciousness. As with the rope, so also with its parts: at each level of examination, knowing the characteristics to be mutually dependent and without any real obtainment, the cognitions of both the rope and its parts are entirely confused consciousness. The verse states:

> All things that are conventionally designated,
> When their inherent nature is carefully examined,
> The conventional name arises from something else,
> Up to the domain of conventional knowledge.

The treatise states: As when one analyzes part by part and examines, one does not see the real substance of the rope and such things; they are known to be without real substance, merely confused mind. Thus one should know that all dharmas are only conventional names. Just as pots, garments, and other objects are constituted from clay, thread, and other components, up to and including what falls within the domain of verbal knowledge, before reaching destruction they are called "pots" and so forth. "Arising from something else" means they exist through conventional verbal designation, not in the ultimate sense. The verse states:

> The partless is not perceived;
> At the ultimate level, it is equally nonexistent.
> It is only due to the confused mind;
> The wise should not grasp at it.

The treatise states: If one further maintains that all conventional things, at the level of the ultimate paramāṇu, are indivisible, without directional parts, and truly existent, then they are just like sky-flowers and rabbit-horns: being imperceptible and lacking the power to generate a cognition of themselves as condition, the asserted paramāṇu is certainly not truly existent. One must state the reason of imperceptibility because one cannot establish the paramāṇu as truly existent. Why? Because there is differentiation of directional parts. Just as one presently sees pots, garments, and other objects having distinct parts in east, west, north, and other directions, these are all presently existent with obtainable parts. If one says the paramāṇu presently exists, it must have directional parts, because it has distinct properties. Then one must acknowledge that, having distinct parts in east, west, north, and other directions, this truly real paramāṇu logically does not hold, nor is it a single entity. Being composed of many parts and having perceivable distinctions, a single truly real paramāṇu certainly cannot be found. Thus one should abandon the doctrine of paramāṇus. Therefore the wise, knowing that the three realms are entirely products of confused conceptualization, should not grasp at them as real when seeking the wondrous truth. The verse states:

> What falsely exists is not real;
> It does not accord with what is perceived.
> Since the object-appearance is illusory,
> The perceiving cognition is also nonexistent.

The treatise states: If one says, "I also acknowledge that the inherent nature of pots, garments, and such things is unobtainable; they are all discriminated by confused consciousness. Yet the confused consciousness that perceives their appearance is truly existent. Observe how gandharva cities and illusory persons: their cognitions do exist." Even granting such cognitions, they are not real because they do not correspond with the perceived objects. This confused consciousness apprehends the perceived object as having real nature. Since that object's inherent nature has already been shown to be nonexistent, and since the object is nonexistent, the perceiving confused consciousness is also not truly existent. How can one make that confused consciousness existent? In the world, one has never seen a case where the productive cause, such as a seed, exists while the produced effect, such as a sprout, does not exist. Therefore, the analogy of illusory cities and such things logically does not hold. The verse states:

> All these are conventional designations;
> Those with good discernment can understand.
> The wise can sever afflictions
> As easily as removing the fear of a snake.

The treatise states: As stated, the three realms are only conventional names. Having eliminated the coarse cognition of pots and such things, one knows that things exist through verbal designation. Those with good discernment, having understood this, can eliminate the fear of a snake upon seeing the rope. Further, through careful reflection, discerning the distinctions, even the false grasping at the rope and such things becomes nonexistent. Observing thus, all the dharmas that produce freedom from defilement quickly and easily eliminate the net of afflictions, and karmic fruits naturally cease. There is an additional verse:

> The wise observe conventional things
> And follow along with worldly practice.
> If they wish to extinguish afflictions,
> They should investigate the true ultimate meaning.

Just as worldly people regard pots, garments, and other conventional things as truly existent, calling them "pots" and "garments," so too the wise follow along with the world and speak in these terms, knowing they are not truly existent. If one delights in examining the faults of afflictions and seeks liberation, one should thoroughly investigate this true ultimate meaning, reflecting in accordance with principle. At the places of all objects and perceiving confused consciousness, the bonds of afflictions will no longer grow.
