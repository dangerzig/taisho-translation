# 玉耶經
## Sūtra of Yujā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0143

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, teaching the Dharma for the four assemblies of disciples. At that time, the household of Anāthapiṇḍada had previously taken a daughter of a householder's family as a wife for his son. The woman's name was Yujā. She was beautiful and lovely, but she was arrogant. She did not observe the proper conduct of a wife in attending to her parents-in-law and husband.

The householder Anāthapiṇḍada and his wife discussed the matter: "Our daughter-in-law is disobedient and does not follow proper etiquette. If we resort to punishment, we would rather not take such action. But if we leave it alone without correcting her, her faults will only increase. What should we do?"

The householder said: "Only the Buddha, the Great Sage, excels at transforming beings. The obstinate submit and none dare disobey. Let us invite the Buddha to transform her."

His wife said: "Excellent!"

The next morning, properly dressed, he went to where the Buddha was, placed his head on the ground, and said to the Buddha: "I have taken a wife for my son who is exceedingly arrogant. She does not observe proper etiquette in attending to my son. I beseech the World-Honored One to condescend tomorrow, bringing your disciples, to come to our home for a meal, and also to teach the Dharma to Yujā, so that her mind may be opened and she may reform and practice the good."

The Buddha told the householder: "Excellent! Excellent!"

The householder Anāthapiṇḍada, hearing the Buddha accept the invitation, joyfully bowed at the Buddha's feet and departed. He returned home, observed the purificatory fast, and prepared the midday meal.

The next morning, the Buddha arrived at the householder's home with one thousand two hundred and fifty disciples. The householder joyfully welcomed the Buddha and bowed. When the Buddha was seated, everyone, great and small, came out to bow to the Buddha and stood aside. But Yujā hid herself and refused to bow to the Buddha.

The Buddha then exercised his supernatural powers, causing the walls and structures of the householder's home to become like lapis lazuli and crystal, transparent so that the inside and outside could see each other. Yujā saw the Buddha with his thirty-two marks and eighty minor characteristics, his body of purple-gold color, radiant and luminous. Yujā was alarmed and terrified, her hair standing on end. She immediately came out and bowed to the Buddha, repenting with her head bowed low, then stood to the right.

The Buddha told Yujā: "A woman should not rely on her beauty to look down upon her husband. What is true beauty? To remove the eighty-four kinds of perverse behavior, to settle the mind in single-pointed concentration: this is true beauty. It is not the complexion of the face, the features, or the adornment of the hair that constitutes true beauty.

"A woman's body has ten afflictions. What are the ten? First, when a girl is born and falls to the ground, the parents are not pleased. Second, the parents raise her and see no lasting value in it. Third, a woman's heart is always fearful of others. Fourth, the parents constantly worry about finding her a marriage match. Fifth, she must part from her parents upon marriage. Sixth, she is always fearful of her husband, watching his expression: if he is pleased she is happy, if he is angry she is afraid. Seventh, pregnancy and childbirth are extremely difficult. Eighth, when young, a woman is controlled by her parents. Ninth, in her prime, she is governed by her husband. Tenth, in old age, she is scolded by her children and grandchildren. From birth to death she never has independence. These are the ten afflictions. A woman is not even aware of them."

Yujā knelt with palms joined and said to the Buddha: "I have received this humble body and am ignorant of proper etiquette. I beseech the World-Honored One to fully explain the teaching on the duties of a wife."

The Buddha told Yujā: "In a wife's service to her parents-in-law and husband, there are five goods and three evils.

"What are the five goods? First, a wife should go to bed late and rise early, comb and arrange her hair, keep her clothing in order, wash her face so there is no uncleanness, attend to tasks, and first consult the elders. Her mind should always be respectful and compliant. If there are sweet delicacies, she must not eat before the others. Second, if the husband scolds or rebukes her, she must not become angry or resentful. Third, she should guard her devotion to her husband alone and must not entertain thoughts of adultery. Fourth, she should always wish her husband long life. When the husband goes out, the wife should keep the household in order. Fifth, she should always think of the husband's good qualities and not dwell on his faults. These are the five goods.

"What are the three evils? First, not observing wifely conduct in attending to the parents-in-law and husband, but only desiring fine food and eating it first, going to sleep before dark and not rising after sunrise. When the husband tries to instruct or correct her, she glares at him, retorts, and curses. Second, not directing one's heart toward the husband, but only thinking of other men. Third, wishing the husband dead so as to remarry sooner. These are the three evils."

Yujā was silent and had no words to answer the Buddha.

The Buddha told Yujā: "In the world there are seven kinds of wives: first, a wife who is like a mother; second, a wife who is like a younger sister; third, a wife who is like a good spiritual friend; fourth, a wife who is like a proper wife; fifth, a wife who is like a maidservant; sixth, a wife who is like an enemy; seventh, a wife who is like a life-taker. These are the seven kinds of wives. Do you understand them?"

Yujā said to the Buddha: "I do not know what conduct each of the seven kinds of wife should practice. I beseech the Buddha to explain them."

The Buddha told Yujā: "Listen carefully, listen carefully! Reflect well upon it. I shall analyze and explain them for you.

"What is a wife like a mother? A wife like a mother cherishes and loves her husband as a compassionate mother loves her child. She attends to him day and night, never leaving his side. She provides for him with all her heart, never missing the proper occasion. When the husband comes and goes, she fears others may take advantage of him. Seeing him, she feels compassion and her heart never wearies. She loves her husband as she would a child. This is a wife like a mother.

"What is a wife like a younger sister? A wife like a younger sister serves her husband with utmost respect and sincerity, as siblings of the same flesh and blood, closest of kin without any duplicity. She honors and reveres him as a younger sister serves an elder brother. This is a wife like a younger sister.

"What is a wife like a good spiritual friend? She attends to her husband with wholehearted love and devotion, with deep attachment, unable to part from him. In private matters she always shares her counsel. When she sees his faults, she gently admonishes him to correct his conduct. She respects what is good and helps him become more wise. She loves him and wishes to help him cross over the world, like a good spiritual friend. This is a wife like a good spiritual friend.

"What is a wife like a proper wife? She provides for her elders with wholehearted sincerity and respect, serves her husband with humility and obedience, rises early and retires late with reverence and propriety. Her mouth speaks no idle words, her body commits no idle actions. When there is good, she credits others; when there are faults, she takes them upon herself. She teaches with benevolence and generosity, and encourages advancement on the path. Her mind is focused and undivided, without any deviousness. She diligently cultivates the duties of a wife without any lapse. Advancing, she does not transgress propriety; withdrawing, she does not lose courtesy. Harmony is held most precious. This is a wife like a proper wife.

"What is a wife like a maidservant? She constantly harbors caution and reverence and does not dare be conceited. With anxious diligence she attends to tasks without avoiding anything. Her mind is always respectful and reverential, loyal, filial, and devoted in her duties. Her speech is gentle, her nature always harmonious and serene. Her mouth does not utter coarse or improper words, her body does not engage in heedless conduct. She is chaste, pure, simple, upright, and trustworthy. She always keeps herself neat and proper and conducts herself with propriety. When the husband favors her, she does not become arrogant. If the husband does not attend to her, she does not consider it a grievance. Even if beaten with a staff, she accepts it without anger. When cursed and insulted, she is silent and without resentment. She willingly and gladly accepts it all without any second thought. She encourages what her husband favors and is not jealous of other women. When she is treated unfairly, she does not complain or seek justice. She diligently cultivates the duties of a wife without being choosy about clothing or food. With focused reverence she fears only that her efforts are not enough. She respectfully serves her husband and attends to the household as a maidservant. This is a wife like a maidservant.

"What is a wife like an enemy? Seeing the husband she is not happy and constantly harbors anger. Day and night she thinks of how to separate from him. She has no heart of a wife and is always like a temporary guest. She quarrels aggressively without any fear or restraint, with disheveled hair lies about and cannot be made to do anything. She does not think of managing the household or raising the children. She may engage in wanton conduct without any sense of shame. Her behavior is like that of a dog or livestock, disgracing and humiliating her relatives. She is like an enemy. This is a wife like an enemy.

"What is a wife like a life-taker? Day and night she does not sleep, facing her husband with a mind of anger. She constantly thinks of what means she can use to be rid of him. She wishes to administer poison but fears being discovered. She may go to relatives near and far to enlist their aid. With such anger she constantly plots against him. She may use precious goods to hire someone to harm him. She may arrange for a lover to watch for the chance to kill him. She murders her husband's life. This is a wife like a life-taker. These are the seven kinds of wives."

Yujā was silent.

The Buddha told Yujā: "A wife of the five goods will always have a distinguished reputation. Her words and actions follow the Dharma, and all people love and respect her. Her clan and nine degrees of kinship all share in her glory. Devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits will all come to protect her from misfortune. After her life span, she will be born in heaven in palaces of the seven treasures, with attendants naturally at her side, a long lifespan, and pleasures beyond words as she desires. When her heavenly lifespan is ended and she descends to be reborn in the world, she will become the child or grandchild of nobles, princes, and kings, beautiful, intelligent, and revered by all.

"An evil wife will always bear an evil reputation. In her present life she will have no peace, and will be frequently afflicted by evil spirits and various poisons. She will be restless in sleeping and waking, frightened by evil dreams, her wishes will be unfulfilled, and she will often encounter calamity. After her life span, her spirit will receive a form in hell, as a hungry ghost, or as an animal, revolving through the three evil destinies for countless eons without end."

The Buddha told Yujā: "Among these seven kinds of wives, which do you wish to be?"

Yujā wept and said to the Buddha: "My mind was foolish and unwise. From this day forward I shall reform and cultivate what is good. I shall be like a maidservant wife, serving my parents-in-law and husband for the rest of my life, never daring to be arrogant."

The Buddha told Yujā: "Excellent! Excellent! Who among people is without fault? To be able to reform: there is no greater good than this."

Yujā then came forward and requested to receive the ten precepts as an upāsikā. The Buddha told Yujā: "Upholding the first precept: do not kill. Second: do not steal or take others' property. Third: do not commit adultery with another man. Fourth: do not drink alcohol. Fifth: do not speak falsely. Sixth: do not curse or revile. Seventh: do not speak frivolously. Eighth: do not be jealous. Ninth: do not be angry. Tenth: have faith that doing good brings merit and doing evil brings retribution. Have faith in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. These are the ten precepts of an upāsikā. Uphold them for the rest of your life and never dare to violate them."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, all the disciples bowed. The householder Anāthapiṇḍada, the parents-in-law, the great and small of the household, and Yujā herself all poured water for washing hands and offered the Buddha food and drink of a hundred flavors.

The Buddha told Yujā: "You should have faith in giving. By this you will always gain merit, and in your next life you will be born again into a householder's family."

Yujā said: "Yes."

When the Buddha had finished the meal, he pronounced the blessing: "May the fifty good spirits protect your person." The Buddha told Yujā: "Diligently recollect the sūtras and precepts."

Yujā said: "I have received the Buddha's grace and heard the sūtras and the Dharma." All bowed before the Buddha and departed.
