Then he reflected further: "The essentials of the three times are clear to the Buddha alone. How could mere immortals, sages, or scholars illuminate them?" The brahmin said: "What is a brahmin? What is penetrating knowledge? What is purity? What is stillness? What is a Buddha?"

The Buddha replied to the brahmin: "I shall speak true words to illuminate your understanding. Listen attentively and hold them in your heart. To attain the three supernatural powers is what is called a brahmin. To clearly discern the past, to distinguish the places of rebirth, to see with the eye of the Way so that mountains and rocks cannot obstruct, to dispel darkness and resolve doubt, to be fully clear about the three times: this is called penetrating knowledge. To attain the six supernatural powers and to have the stains of the mind completely removed: this is called stillness. When the three poisons are destroyed and the mind is like heavenly gold: this is called purity. When the root of ignorance concerning birth and death has been utterly burned away without remainder, when one walks the path of purity and is preeminent over the three realms, when all ignorance is exhausted and there is nothing ungrasped, when one attains omniscience, then the honored title is Buddha."

The brahmin, overjoyed, rose to his feet, prostrated his entire body upon the ground, touched his head to the Buddha's feet, kissed the Buddha's feet with his mouth, stroked the Buddha's feet with his hands, and declared his own name: "I am the brahmin Brahmāyu. I take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Sangha." Weeping, he said: "Sentient beings are lost in darkness, shrouded by the six obscurations. They see the Buddha and do not revere him; they see the sūtras and do not read them; they see the śramaṇas and feel no devoted love in their hearts. They do not receive the divine transformation. This is lasting decline!" His many disciples, seeing their teacher's complete devotion, looked at one another and said: "Our revered teacher, who has mastered the scriptures and seen every text, whose renown extends to the four countries and who is honored by all scholars, has now humbled himself, pressing his palms together and prostrating at Gautama's feet. How much more should we do likewise!"

The Buddha told the brahmin: "Be seated again. I perceive your mind. You have true faith and wisdom directed toward the World-Honored One." He received the instruction and took his seat. The Buddha then further spoke of the virtue of keeping the precepts, the merit of generosity, the defilement and impurity of household life, and praised the exalted practice of aspiring to the Way. The Buddha then perceived that the brahmin had the mind of a superior person, joyful and broad in understanding. The Buddha taught him the essentials of the supreme Way: "All suffering, in its ten thousand forms, arises from the body. The wise person, seeing deeply, knows that in pleasure there lies the seed of misfortune. Turning back against the stream to seek the origin, one reaches the point of fundamental non-existence. This is what the superior person calls the wisdom of the noble truth. Not knowing that the body is the chief source of suffering, and that all arises from habitual accumulation: the superior person awakens to this. This the wise call the noble truth. The three realms are like an illusion. What is combined must separate; what flourishing does not decline? When conditions combine, calamity arises; when conditions separate, suffering ceases. The superior person contemplates the origin and thereby knows its emptiness. This the wise call the noble truth. Having known fundamental non-existence, one then transcends the three realms, empties the mind, purifies one's conduct, abandons all desire, and attains the concentration of signlessness. What the mind takes hold of, the Three Treasures may be attained."

The brahmin's mind was opened, like a piece of pure white cloth, spotless and without stain, which upon being dyed takes on color. The brahmin's mind was illuminated. In past lives he had many times served the Buddhas and upheld the pure precepts. Now, hearing the Honored One's teaching, he fully understood the Way of the Unsurpassed, Perfectly Enlightened One. The stains of his mind were completely stilled. He entered the three gates of liberation and was forever separated from all suffering. He then addressed the Buddha: "Before I saw the Buddha, I held to the conduct of great error, blinded by ignorance, trusting the words of the mad and foolish and taking them for truth. Now, having first seen the Buddha, my madness is cured. The blind see, the deaf hear, the mute speak, the hunched stand straight; the prisoner is released from jail. Common people in their foolishness live and die in vain, never tasting the true Way of the Honored One among the Gods, dwelling forever in flames of torment. How painful! What good fortune that I am alive! I have been blessed to see the Buddha's supreme divinity. For my sake he has spoken the most profound Way, restoring me to the original, the unconditioned, the eternal. From this day forward, I take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Bhikṣu Sangha. I wish to become a pure lay devotee: I shall uphold benevolence and not kill, know contentment and not steal, be chaste and not engage in sexual misconduct, hold to truthfulness and not deceive, practice filial devotion and not drink intoxicants. May the Honored One among the Gods have compassion for me. Tomorrow at dawn, I wish that you and the Holy Assembly would condescend to accept a simple meal." The Buddha silently accepted.

The brahmin, his heart full of joy, bowed at the Buddha's feet and returned home, where he prepared food of a hundred flavors. At dawn, at his home, he bowed to the Buddha, knelt upright, and reverently said: "May the Buddha, at the proper time, condescend to grace us with his honored presence." The Buddha put on his Dharma robes and went together with the Holy Assembly to the brahmin's home. They all took their seats according to the Dharma. The brahmin served the water for washing hands with his own hand, and attended to them with a reverent heart. This continued for seven days. When the Buddha had finished his teaching and transforming, he returned to the country of Videha. Not long afterward, the brahmin's life came to an end. When the bhikṣus heard of this, they together addressed the Buddha: "Now that the brahmin has passed away, to what destiny has he gone?" The World-Honored One said: "That brahmin possessed a saintly mind, broad in understanding, and had penetrated to the stage of non-return. The five hindrances were exhausted, and he was pure as heavenly gold. In that state of purity he attained the path of the arhat and passed into the unconditioned."

When the Buddha had finished speaking, the bhikṣus rejoiced.
