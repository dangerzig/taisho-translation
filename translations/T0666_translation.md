# 大方等如來藏經
## The Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0666

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Buddhabhadra (佛陀跋陀羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the city of Rājagṛha on Vulture Peak Mountain, in the Jewel-Moon Lecture Hall, in a sandalwood upper chamber. Ten years after his attainment of buddhahood, he was together with a great assembly of a hundred thousand bhikṣus and bodhisattva-mahāsattvas numbering sixty Ganges-sands. All had accomplished the power of great diligence and had already made offerings to hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of buddhas. All could turn the wheel of the irreversible dharma. If any sentient being heard their names, they would never fall back from the path of supreme awakening. Their names were: the bodhisattva Dharmamati, the bodhisattva Siṃhamati, the bodhisattva Vajramati, the bodhisattva Vinītamati, the bodhisattva Sumati, the bodhisattva Candraprabha, the bodhisattva Ratnacandra, the bodhisattva Pūrṇacandra, the bodhisattva Vīrya, the bodhisattva Amitavīrya, the bodhisattva Anantavīrya, the bodhisattva Trilokatikrānta, the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, the bodhisattva Mahāsthāmaprāpta, the bodhisattva Gandhahastī, the bodhisattva Gandhottama, the bodhisattva Gandhottamamūrdha, the bodhisattva Mūrdhagarbha, the bodhisattva Sūryagarbha, the bodhisattva Dhvajalakṣaṇa, the bodhisattva Mahādhvajalakṣaṇa, the bodhisattva Vimaladhvaja, the bodhisattva Anantaprabha, the bodhisattva Prabhāmukta, the bodhisattva Vimalaprabha, the bodhisattva Premanīyarāja, the bodhisattva Nityapremanīya, the bodhisattva Ratnapāṇi, the bodhisattva Ākāśagarbha, the bodhisattva Vigatadarpa, the bodhisattva Sumeru, the bodhisattva Prabhāguṇarāja, the bodhisattva Dhāraṇīśvararāja, the bodhisattva Dhāraṇī, the bodhisattva Sarvarogacikitsaka, the bodhisattva Sarvasattvavyādhicikitsaka, the bodhisattva Ānandaprītismṛti, the bodhisattva Saṃtuṣṭamanas, the bodhisattva Nityasaṃtuṣṭa, the bodhisattva Samantāvabhāsa, the bodhisattva Candraprabha, the bodhisattva Ratnamati, the bodhisattva Strīvivartanā, the bodhisattva Mahāmeghanāda, the bodhisattva Nāyaka, the bodhisattva Amoghadarśana, the bodhisattva Sarvadharmeśvara, the bodhisattva Maitreya, and the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī. Bodhisattva-mahāsattvas numbering sixty Ganges-sands such as these, from immeasurable buddha-lands, together with countless devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas, all assembled to pay reverence and make offerings.

At that time the World-Honored One, seated in correct samādhi in the sandalwood upper chamber, manifested a spiritual transformation. Thousand-petaled lotuses as large as chariot-wheels appeared, immeasurable in number, complete in color and fragrance but not yet opened. Within every flower there was a transformation-buddha. They rose into the sky and covered the world like a jeweled canopy. Each lotus emitted immeasurable light, and all the lotuses simultaneously opened in full bloom. By the Buddha's spiritual power, in an instant they all withered and deteriorated. Within those flowers, all the transformation-buddhas sat in the cross-legged posture, each emitting countless hundreds of thousands of rays of light. At that time this realm was adorned with extraordinary magnificence. The entire great assembly, leaping with joy at what they had never before seen, all had the thought: "For what reason have these countless marvelous flowers suddenly withered, becoming black, foul, and repulsive?"

At that time the World-Honored One, knowing the doubts of all the bodhisattvas and the great assembly, addressed Vajramati: "Good man! If there is any doubt regarding the buddha-dharma, you may freely ask."

Then the bodhisattva Vajramati, knowing that the great assembly all had doubts, addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! For what reason did countless lotuses, each containing a transformation-buddha, rise into the sky and cover the world, only to wither in an instant? All the transformation-buddhas each emitted countless hundreds of thousands of rays of light, and the assembly all saw this, pressing their palms together reverently."

At that time the bodhisattva Vajramati spoke in verse:

> Never before have I beheld
> a spiritual transformation like today's.
> I see hundreds of thousands of koṭis of buddhas
> seated within those lotus treasuries,
> each emitting countless rays of light
> that cover all the lands.
> The stainless guides,
> adorning all the worlds,
> and then the lotuses suddenly withered:
> all could not help feeling revulsion.
> For what reason now
> is this spiritual transformation manifested?
> I behold Ganges-sands of buddhas
> and immeasurable spiritual transformations.
> Never have I seen the like of today:
> please distinguish and explain.

At that time the World-Honored One addressed Vajramati and all the bodhisattvas: "Good men! There is a great vaipulya sūtra called the Tathāgatagarbha. I am about to expound it, and therefore this auspicious sign has appeared. Listen attentively, ponder it well." They all said: "Excellent! We wish with joy to hear."

The Buddha said: "Good men! Just as the countless lotuses manifested by the Buddha suddenly withered, and within them immeasurable transformation-buddhas, adorned with marks and excellences, sat in the cross-legged posture emitting great radiance, and the assembly, beholding this rare sight, could not but feel reverence: in the same way, good men, I observe with the buddha-eye all sentient beings and see that within their craving, hatred, delusion, and various afflictions there exists the Tathāgata's wisdom, the Tathāgata's eye, the Tathāgata's body, sitting in the cross-legged posture, dignified and unmoving. Good men! All sentient beings, though they dwell amid the afflictions of the various destinies, possess within their bodies the tathāgatagarbha, which is always undefiled, endowed with all virtuous qualities, and no different from me.

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if a person with the divine eye were to observe flowers that have not yet opened and see the tathāgata-bodies seated in the cross-legged posture within those flowers. When the withered petals are removed, they become fully manifest. In the same way, good men, when the Buddha sees the tathāgatagarbha of sentient beings, he wishes to cause it to open and bloom, and therefore he expounds the sūtra-dharma to eliminate afflictions and reveal the Buddha-nature. Good men! Such is the nature of the buddhas' dharma: whether buddhas appear in the world or do not appear, the tathāgatagarbha of all sentient beings abides permanently and does not change. However, it is covered by those sentient beings' afflictions. When a tathāgata appears in the world and broadly teaches the dharma, he eliminates the defilements and purifies all-knowledge. Good men! If there is a bodhisattva who has faith in and delight in this dharma and cultivates it with a devoted mind, that person attains liberation and achieves perfect awakening, universally performing the buddha's work for the world."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> When the withered petals are removed,
> one sees the unobstructed guide.
> In order to sever afflictions,

> The Buddha observes all beings

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if pure honey were within a rocky cliff or a tree, and countless swarms of bees surrounded and guarded it. Then a person of clever skill and wisdom first drove away the bees and then took the honey, using it as they wished and sharing it near and far. In the same way, good men, all sentient beings possess the tathāgatagarbha. Like that pure honey within the cliff or tree, it is covered and concealed by various afflictions, just as the honey is guarded by swarms of bees. With the buddha-eye I observe them as they truly are. Through skillful means, I teach the dharma as appropriate, eliminating afflictions and revealing the buddha's knowing and seeing, universally performing the buddha's work for the world."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> It is as if honey within a rocky cliff or tree

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if unhusked grain has not been freed from its chaff. The poor and foolish despise it, thinking it fit to be discarded. But once it has been refined and purified, it always serves as the emperor's provision. In the same way, good men, I observe with the buddha-eye all sentient beings: the immeasurable knowing and seeing of the Tathāgata is covered and concealed by the chaff of afflictions. Therefore, through skillful means, I teach the dharma as appropriate to eliminate afflictions and purify all-knowledge. In all the worlds, this constitutes the most perfect awakening."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> It is as if all unhusked grain
> has not had its chaff removed.
> The poor still despise it,
> thinking it a thing to be discarded.
> Though outwardly it seems useless,
> inwardly it is truly unimpaired.
> Once the chaff has been removed,
> it becomes the emperor's meal.
> I see all beings:
> afflictions conceal the buddha-treasury.
> I teach the dharma of elimination
> so they may attain all-knowledge.
> My tathāgata-nature
> is also present in sentient beings.
> Through teaching, I cause them to be purified

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if true gold fell into an impure place, becoming hidden and invisible over the years. Though the gold is not impaired, no one knows it is there. One with the divine eye says to the people: 'Within this impure matter there is a treasure of true gold. You should extract it and use it however you wish.' In the same way, good men, the impure place is the immeasurable afflictions. The treasure of true gold is the tathāgatagarbha. The one with the divine eye is the Tathāgata. Therefore the Tathāgata broadly teaches the dharma, causing all sentient beings to eliminate afflictions and attain perfect awakening, performing the buddha's work."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> As gold lies amid impurity,
> hidden where none can see,
> one with the divine eye sees it
> and at once tells the people:
> 'If you extract it,
> wash and cleanse it,
> and use it as you wish,

> The Well-Gone One's eye is like this:

> Within the mire of afflictions,

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if a poor family possessed a treasure hidden within their house. The treasure cannot speak and say, 'I am here.' They neither know of it themselves nor has anyone told them, so they cannot open and reveal this precious treasure. All sentient beings are also like this: the Tathāgata's knowing and seeing, powers, and fearlessnesses, the great dharma-treasure, are within their bodies. But they do not hear of it and do not know it, being intoxicated and confused by the five desires, revolving through birth and death, and enduring immeasurable suffering. Therefore the buddhas appear in the world to reveal the tathāgata's dharma-treasury within the body. Those beings then develop faith, purify all-knowledge, and have the tathāgatagarbha universally revealed for sentient beings. With unhindered eloquence, they become great donors of the dharma. In this way, good men, I observe with the buddha-eye that all sentient beings possess the tathāgatagarbha, and therefore I expound this dharma for all the bodhisattvas."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> It is as if in a poor person's house

> The owner neither knows of it nor sees it,
> and the treasure cannot speak.
> Through all those years of ignorance and darkness,

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if the inner kernel of an āmra fruit is not impaired. When it is planted in the earth, it grows into a great tree-king. In the same way, good men, I observe with the buddha-eye all sentient beings: the precious treasure of the Tathāgata, within the shell of ignorance, is like the fruit's seed within its husk. Good men! That tathāgatagarbha is cool and without heat, a great assemblage of wisdom, marvelously tranquil nirvāṇa. It is called Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. Good men! Having observed sentient beings in this way, the Tathāgata reveals this meaning for the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas in order to purify the buddha-wisdom."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> It is as if the kernel of an āmra fruit
> is inwardly never impaired.
> When planted in the great earth,

> With the outflow-free eye the Tathāgata

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if a person carried a statue of pure gold and set out traveling to another country. Along a perilous road, fearing robbers, they wrapped it in tattered cloth so that none would recognize it. This person then died along the way, and the gold statue was abandoned in the wilderness, where travelers trod upon it, all thinking it was filth. But one with the divine eye saw the pure gold statue within the tattered wrappings and extracted it, whereupon all paid their respects. In the same way, good men, I see that sentient beings are ensnared by various afflictions, drifting through long nights of immeasurable birth and death. Yet within their bodies is the marvelous treasury of the Tathāgata, dignified and pure, no different from me. Therefore the Buddha teaches the dharma for sentient beings, cutting off afflictions, purifying the Tathāgata's wisdom, and continuing to guide all the world."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> It is as if a person carried a gold statue

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if a woman, poor, lowly, and ugly, despised by all, were carrying a noble child who would become a cakravartin king ruling the four continents. But this woman does not know it, and through the passing of the seasons, she always thinks of herself as inferior and imagines her child to be of low birth. In the same way, good men, the Tathāgata observes all sentient beings revolving through birth and death, enduring the bitter poisons of suffering. Within their bodies they all possess the tathāgatagarbha, just like that woman who does not know it. Therefore the Tathāgata broadly teaches the dharma, saying: 'Good men! Do not demean yourselves. You all possess the Buddha-nature within. If you diligently and vigorously strive to eliminate all faults, then you will receive the title of bodhisattva and World-Honored One, guiding and liberating immeasurable sentient beings.'"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> It is as if a poor woman,
> plain and lowly in appearance,
> carried a child of noble destiny

> endowed with the seven treasures and all virtues,

"Furthermore, good men! It is as if a foundry cast a statue of true gold. When the casting is complete, the mold is placed upside-down on the ground. Outwardly it looks scorched and black, but the gold statue within does not change. When the mold is opened, the golden radiance shines forth. In the same way, good men, the Tathāgata observes all sentient beings: the buddha-treasury within their bodies is complete with all the marks. Having observed this, he broadly reveals and expounds it. Those sentient beings then attain rest and coolness, and with the vajra wisdom-hammer they shatter afflictions and reveal the purified buddha-body, as if extracting a gold statue."

At that time the World-Honored One addressed the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Vajramati: "Whether one has gone forth or remains a householder, if a good man or good woman receives, upholds, reads, recites, copies, makes offerings to, and broadly explains to others the Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra, the merit obtained is beyond calculation. Vajramati! If there were a bodhisattva who, for the sake of the buddha-path, diligently practiced with vigor, cultivating superknowledges and entering various samādhis, wishing to plant the roots of virtue, and who made offerings to buddhas more numerous than the sands of the Ganges present in the world, constructing jeweled pavilions of the seven treasures more numerous than the sands of the Ganges, ten yojanas high and one yojana in both length and width, setting out thrones of the seven treasures spread with celestial silk, and for each buddha every day constructed Ganges-sands of such seven-jeweled pavilions, offering them to each tathāgata and all the bodhisattvas and śrāvaka assemblies, doing likewise universally for all the Ganges-sands of buddhas present in the world, continuing in this manner up to constructing over fifty Ganges-sands of jeweled pavilions, making offerings to over fifty Ganges-sands of buddhas present in the world and their bodhisattvas and śrāvaka assemblies, continuing even for immeasurable hundreds of thousands of myriads of kalpas: Vajramati! This would not compare to a person who, delighting in bodhi, receives, upholds, reads, recites, copies, makes offerings to, or explains even one simile from the Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra. Vajramati! The good man who has planted wholesome roots in the presence of the buddhas possesses immeasurable merit, yet compared to the merit obtained by a good man or good woman who upholds this sūtra, it does not amount to one-hundredth, one-thousandth, and so on, even to the point that calculation and analogy cannot reach it." At that time the World-Honored One again addressed the bodhisattva Vajramati: "In the past, long ago, immeasurable, boundless, inconceivable asaṃkhyeya kalpas ago, and even more than that, there was a buddha named the Tathāgata King of Eternal Radiance, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone One, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. Vajramati! Why was he called King of Eternal Radiance? When that buddha was formerly practicing the bodhisattva path, from the time he descended into his mother's womb he always emitted radiance that illuminated a thousand buddha-worlds' worth of lands throughout the ten directions. If any sentient being saw that light, all rejoiced, their afflictions were entirely extinguished, they became complete in physical strength, they accomplished mindfulness and wisdom, and they attained unhindered eloquence. If any in the hells, among the hungry ghosts, among animals, in Yama's realm, or among the asuras saw that light, all left the evil paths and were born among devas and humans. If any devas and humans saw the light, they attained non-retrogression from the path of supreme awakening and obtained the five superknowledges. Those already at non-retrogression all attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas and the fifty merits of the revolving dhāraṇī.

"Vajramati! The lands illuminated by that light were all adorned and purified, like celestial beryl, with cords of gold marking the eight roads, with many kinds of jeweled trees whose flowers and fruits flourished, their fragrance sweet and their scent pervading, and gentle breezes that stirred them to produce subtle and marvelous sounds, proclaiming the Three Treasures, the merits of bodhisattvas, the faculties, powers, factors of awakening, the path, the dhyānas, and the liberations. All sentient beings who heard this attained the joy of the dharma, with faith firm and unshakable, forever leaving the evil paths. Vajramati! All sentient beings in those lands of the ten directions, receiving that light, joined their palms reverently six times a day and night.

"Vajramati! From the time that bodhisattva dwelt in the womb, to his birth, and through to his attainment of buddhahood and his parinirvāṇa without remainder, he always emitted radiance. Even after his parinirvāṇa, his reliquary stūpas also always emitted light. For this reason, all devas and humans called him King of Eternal Radiance.

"Vajramati! When the Tathāgata King of Eternal Radiance, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, first attained buddhahood, there was in his dharma-assembly a bodhisattva named Anantaprabha, together with twenty koṭi of bodhisattvas as his retinue. The bodhisattva-mahāsattva Anantaprabha asked that buddha about the Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra, and the Buddha expounded it. Sitting in one place, he taught for fifty great kalpas, guarding and caring for all the bodhisattvas. His voice reached and announced to lands as many as the atoms in ten buddha-worlds, amounting to hundreds of thousands of buddha-lands. Through countless causes and conditions and hundreds of thousands of similes, he taught the bodhisattvas the Tathāgatagarbha, this great vehicle sūtra. All the bodhisattvas who heard this sūtra, received, upheld, read, recited, and practiced it as taught, have all already become buddhas, with the exception of four bodhisattvas. Vajramati! Do not regard this as something other than yourself. That bodhisattva Anantaprabha was none other than my present self. The four bodhisattvas who have not yet become buddhas are Mañjuśrī, Avalokiteśvara, Mahāsthāmaprāpta, and you, Vajramati. Vajramati! The Tathāgatagarbha Sūtra is of great benefit. Whoever hears it will accomplish the buddha-path."

> Those who obtain this sūtra
> are called masters of the buddha-dharma.
> They are protectors of the world,
> praised by all the buddhas.
> Those who uphold this sūtra
> are called dharma-kings.
> They are the eyes of the world
> and should be praised like the World-Honored One.

When the World-Honored One had finished speaking this sūtra, Vajramati and all the bodhisattvas, the four assemblies and their retinues, and the devas, humans, gandharvas, asuras, and all others who heard what the Buddha taught rejoiced and put it into practice.
