# 佛說摩訶迦葉度貧母經
## Sūtra of Mahākāśyapa Saving the Destitute Old Woman, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0497

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, teaching the people. Together with an assembly of bhikṣus, bodhisattva mahāsattvas, devas, nāgas, spirits, people of the world, and an innumerable multitude, all were gathered to hear the sūtras.

At that time, Mahākāśyapa was traveling alone to teach and transform, and he arrived in the city of Rājagṛha. He constantly practiced great compassion to bring merit to all beings, passing over the wealthy and powerful to beg from the destitute. When Mahākāśyapa was about to go on his alms round, he first entered the samādhi of right reception to perceive: "Who is the most destitute person that I should bring merit to?" He then entered the great city of Rājagṛha. He saw an old woman, all alone, the most destitute of all, in a back alley beside a great dung heap. She had hollowed out a cave in the side of the dung heap, emaciated and afflicted with illness, always lying within it, alone and without family, without clothing or food. In the cave she had set up a small fence to cover her body. Kāśyapa perceived through samādhi that this person had planted no merit in past lives, and therefore was now destitute. He knew that the old woman's life was nearing its end: "If I do not save her, she will forever lose the hall of merit."

The old woman was then hungry and in distress. A wealthy man's servant had discarded some rice-water, foul and barely drinkable. The old woman begged for it, and the servant placed it in a broken pottery shard beside her. Mahākāśyapa came to her dwelling and spoke his blessing: "If you give even a little to me, you may obtain great merit."

At that time, the old woman spoke in verse:

> My whole body is wracked with illness;
> My lonely destitution, how can I describe it?
> The poorest person in the entire country,
> Without clothing or food to cover my body.
> There are heartless people in the world
> Who still show me some pity and compassion.
> How is it that you, who are called compassionate,
> Do not understand this suffering?
> Of all the cold and misery in the world,
> None surpasses my own body.
> I beg you to show me compassion and mercy;
> It is not that I begrudge anything for your sake.

Mahākāśyapa answered in verse:

> The Buddha is the honored one of the three realms;
> I am counted among his disciples.
> I wish to remove your hunger and poverty;
> That is why I beg from the destitute.
> If you can spare even a morsel from your own mouth,
> A fraction of a measure in offering,
> Through the long night you will gain liberation,
> And in future lives obtain great wealth.

Then the old woman spoke again in verse:

> Truly it is as you say;
> I was born into the world without merit.
> Now I dwell in a dung-heap cave,
> My body smeared with filth.
> I have not a grain of rice for food;
> My wasted body is not covered.
> In such extreme poverty as this,
> My wish to give and my ability are at odds.

Mahākāśyapa spoke again in verse:

> You say your dwelling gives no joy,
> That in your hunger and poverty you have nothing to give.
> But if you have the intention to give,
> Then this is not true poverty.
> If, moreover, you know shame,
> Then this is the same as wearing the Dharma robe.
> With these two things, dear mother,
> Clothing and food are fully provided.
> There are foolish people in the world
> Whose worldly garments and stored grain are abundant,
> Yet who feel no shame and never think of giving;
> In the reckoning to come, they are the truly poor.
> To make merit in the midst of distress
> Is what is called truly rare.
> Trust in this: the workings of sin and merit
> Are spoken in truth, without falsehood.

At that time, the old woman, hearing the verses, was filled with joy. She recalled that the day before she had obtained the foul rice-water and wished to offer it, yet it was undrinkable. From afar she called to Kāśyapa: "Have compassion on me! Will you accept it?"

Mahākāśyapa answered: "Very good!"

The old woman then crawled out from inside the cave. Her body was exposed and bare; she could not carry it out, so she turned sideways and stooped, handing it over the fence. Kāśyapa received it, and with his own lips spoke the blessing: "May you receive the merit of peace."

Kāśyapa thought: "If I take this away and drink it elsewhere, the old woman will not believe me and will think I threw it away." So right there before the old woman, he drank it, rinsed his bowl, and put it back in his cloth bag.

At this, the old woman's faith deepened. Kāśyapa thought to himself: "I should display my supernatural powers so that this old woman will surely obtain great peace." He sank into the earth, then appeared in the sky. From his body he emanated water and fire: from the upper half of his body, water appeared; from the lower half, fire appeared. Then he transformed again, flying through the sky, vanishing in the east and appearing in the west, and likewise from south to north.

The old woman, seeing this, leaped with joy and knelt on one knee for a long time, gazing up at Kāśyapa from afar. Kāśyapa said to her: "Mother, what do you now wish for in your heart? Do you wish for worldly wealth and power, to be a cakravartin king, or one of the Four Heavenly Kings, or Śakra or Brahmā among the devas? Or do you wish to attain stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, arhatship, or pratyekabuddhahood? Or do you wish for anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi, the highest, true, and complete awakening? All of these may bear fruit according to your wish."

The old woman, weary of the world's suffering and having heard that the heavenly palaces are truly joyful, said to Kāśyapa: "I wish that through this small merit, I may be reborn in the heavens above." Thereupon, Kāśyapa suddenly vanished. Within a few days, the old woman's life came to an end. She was immediately reborn in the second heaven, Trāyastriṃśa. Her majesty and virtue were sublime, shaking heaven and earth, her radiance surpassing and extraordinary, like seven suns appearing all at once, illuminating the heavenly palaces.

Śakra, Lord of the Devas, was startled and alarmed: "Who is this person whose merit and virtue have caused such a tremor? Could there be someone here who surpasses me?" With his divine eye he observed and saw that it was the merit of this celestial maiden that had caused it. Śakra then asked in verse:

> From where has this maiden come?
> Her great radiance illuminates all,
> Like seven great suns
> All appearing at once.
> She shakes my palace;
> Her majestic virtue is hard to withstand.
> What merit did she cultivate
> To ascend to this heaven?

Then the celestial maiden answered Śakra in verse:

> I was formerly in Jambudvīpa,
> In a dung-heap cave, amidst filth,
> Wasted with age and afflicted with illness,
> Without food or clothing.
> In the great three-thousand world,
> Śākyamuni Buddha is the honored one.
> Next among his great disciples
> Is the one named Mahākāśyapa.
> Out of compassion he begged from me,
> Taught the Dharma, and my heart rejoiced.
> I offered him my foul rice-water;
> The gift was small but the reward is vast.
> With single-minded devotion to the field of merit,
> I wished to be reborn in the heavens.
> I cast off my body in the dung-heap cave
> And was reborn in Trāyastriṃśa Heaven.

At that time, the celestial maiden thought to herself: "This reward of merit came about because in my former life I made an offering to Kāśyapa. Even if I were to take the heavenly treasures and all manner of hundreds and thousands of precious things and offer them to Kāśyapa, I still could not repay the kindness of that single moment." She then led her attendant maidens, took heavenly incense and flowers, and suddenly descended. In the sky she scattered them upon Kāśyapa. Then she came down, prostrated with her whole body on the ground, and after making obeisance she stood to one side, joined her palms, and praised him:

> In the great three-thousand lands, the Buddha is the supreme honored one.
> Next is Kāśyapa, who can close the door of sin.
> Long ago in Jambudvīpa, before the dung-heap cave,
> For the destitute old woman, he spoke words of truth.
> The old woman rejoiced and offered him rice-water;
> The gift was like a mustard seed, the reward like a mountain.
> I have become a celestial maiden, receiving blessings naturally.
> Therefore I have come down to pay homage at the field of merit.

When the celestial maiden had finished speaking, she and her attendants returned to the heavens. Afterward, Śakra thought: "This maiden, in the foulness of Jambudvīpa, with her rice-water offering to Kāśyapa, has attained such merit. Kāśyapa is greatly compassionate, but he brings merit only to the lowly households and does not reach the great families. I should devise a plan and go down to Jambudvīpa, to Kāśyapa's dwelling, and establish merit there." Śakra, together with the heavenly queen, took food of a hundred flavors and placed it in a small bottle. He descended to the great city of Rājagṛha, and at the edge of an alley he made a small, shabby dwelling. He transformed his appearance to look like an old man, emaciated in body, stooping as he walked. The old couple together were weaving mats, appearing destitute and beggarly, without any food, grain, or cloth in store.

When Mahākāśyapa later went on his alms round, he saw these destitute people and went to beg food from them. The old man said: "We are utterly poor and have nothing. What can we do?" Kāśyapa spoke his blessing and lingered for a long time without leaving.

The old man said: "My wife and I are very old. We barely have time to weave our mats. Just now we begged a little rice and were about to eat it. We have heard of your compassionate virtue, that you beg only from the poor in order to bring them merit. Though we are destitute, we willingly give it up to offer it to the worthy one. If, as you say, this will bring us merit, let it be so." The fragrance of the heavenly food was unlike anything in the world. As soon as the bottle was opened, a rich, sweet scent arose. Kāśyapa detected it and would not accept it at all. The old man said: "Please, monk! Our humble food is not much. Hold out your bowl and take it."

Kāśyapa held out his bowl and received it. He spoke the blessing: "May the fragrance of this offering household perfume the entire great city of Rājagṛha and all its territory." Kāśyapa then suspected the fragrance was extraordinary, and immediately entered samādhi to examine its origin. As he settled into samādhi, the old man and the old woman reverted to the form of Śakra and swiftly flew away, snapping their fingers in the sky with boundless joy. Kāśyapa, through his contemplation, knew that Śakra had transformed into the old man to make this offering and increase his merit. "Since I have already accepted it, it would not be proper to return it." Kāśyapa praised him: "Excellent, Śakra! You are never weary of planting merit. You endured this lowly form and descended to plant merit. You will surely receive the corresponding reward." Śakra and the queen were even more overjoyed. Then heavenly music came to welcome them, and when Śakra returned to his palace, his joy was redoubled.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This destitute old woman: no one in the entire world can match her. Though her gift was small, the merit it repays is vast, because in her suffering she gave with an utterly sincere heart, and so she attained immeasurable merit, and the reward that corresponds to it. Śakra, in the self-indulgence of heaven above, gave up his noble station and came down to plant merit; the reward he will obtain is beyond measure. For this reason the Tathāgata declares that generosity is foremost. The people of Jambudvīpa are foolish and pitiable. Those like this old woman are very few. You should broadly proclaim the Tathāgata's true words."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the devas, nāgas, spirits, the fourfold assembly of disciples, and the bhikṣu sangha all established great merit and offered their praise, wishing that all beings, according to their aspirations, might all obtain the fruit of their reward.

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, all those in the assembly were filled with joy, and they touched their foreheads to the ground in obeisance.
