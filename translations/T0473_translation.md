# 佛說妙吉祥菩薩所問大乘法螺經
## Sūtra of the Great Vehicle Dharma-Conch as Asked by Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0473

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmabhadra (法賢)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Universal Hall, seated upon a jeweled lion throne, together with a great assembly of twelve thousand five hundred bhikṣus, as well as bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, and hundreds of thousands of those whose conditions for liberation had matured.

At that time, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva, empowered by the Buddha's spiritual authority, rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms, prostrated, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! You possess great merit. How is it to be measured? This great merit is beyond measure, numbering immeasurable koṭi-nayuta hundreds of thousands. The beings whose conditions for liberation have matured wish to have their aspirations fulfilled. How is the Tathāgata the Dharma-conch for them?"

At that time, the World-Honored One, hearing these words, told Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Mañjuśrī! Inconceivable practice, great wisdom, great loving-kindness and great compassion: this is great merit. Such great merit causes all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas to give rise to great skillful means of effort, the vows and practices of upholding the precepts, and the accomplishment of the most supreme samādhi, contemplating all beings whose conditions for liberation have matured. This is the Tathāgata's Dharma-conch."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "All the sentient beings of Jambudvīpa in the south who uphold the ten wholesome dharmas and the merit they obtain: such merit should be measured thus. A hundredfold accumulation of that merit equals the merit of one gold wheel-turning king. That wheel-turning king rules over the four great continents, complete with the seven treasures, namely: the wheel treasure, elephant treasure, horse treasure, maṇi treasure, queen treasure, minister of the treasury treasure, and general treasure. He also has a thousand sons, courageous and mighty in virtue, of dignified and handsome appearance, able to defeat all opposing armies. Mañjuśrī! Such a gold wheel-turning king possesses such great power.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! All the sentient beings of the four great continents, each one possessing merit equal to that of one gold wheel-turning king: the total merit of all those sentient beings, measured up to a thousandfold, equals the merit of one Lord Śakra. Such a Śakra possesses great spiritual power and meritorious strength.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! All the sentient beings of the four great continents, each one possessing merit equal to that of one Śakra: the total merit of all those sentient beings, measured at hundreds of thousands of times, equals the merit of one mighty Nārāyaṇa deva. Such a Nārāyaṇa deva possesses great spiritual power and meritorious strength.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! All the sentient beings of the four great continents, each one possessing merit equal to that of one mighty Nārāyaṇa deva: the total merit of all those sentient beings, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times, equals the merit of one Māra king of the Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven of the desire realm. Such a Māra king, having planted roots of merit in former lives, was born in that desire heaven, possesses great power, and is able to subdue the devas and humans.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! All the sentient beings of the four great continents, each one possessing merit equal to that of one Māra king: the total merit of all those sentient beings, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times, equals the merit of one Brahmā of two thousand worlds. That Brahmā, throughout two thousand worlds, practices the transformation of loving-kindness and compassion.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! All the sentient beings of the two thousand worlds, each one possessing merit equal to that of one Brahmā of two thousand worlds: the total of all that merit, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times, equals the merit of one lord of the three-thousand great-thousand world system, namely Maheśvara and Brahmā. Those Maheśvara and Brahmā, throughout the three-thousand great-thousand world system, practice the transformation of loving-kindness and compassion."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "You should see that at the time of the kalpa's destruction, when the great fire burns, lasting for one intermediate kalpa, at that time Maheśvara and the Brahmā king send down great rain, and the water fills the three-thousand great-thousand world system, reaching up to the Brahmā heaven. This great flood is brought about by the power of Maheśvara and the Brahmā king. Thus Maheśvara and the Brahmā king, having planted great roots of merit, were born in that heaven, complete in wisdom and great power.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! All the sentient beings of the three-thousand great-thousand world system, each one possessing merit equal to that of one lord Maheśvara and Brahmā king of the three-thousand great-thousand world system: the total merit of all those sentient beings, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭis of times, equals the merit of one greatly diligent pratyekabuddha. Mañjuśrī! Do not think that, because all the sentient beings of the three-thousand great-thousand world system each possess merit equal to the Brahmā king, the total merit equals that of a pratyekabuddha and is thus considered great. Even if the merit of all sentient beings in the buddha-lands of the ten directions were compared to the merit of one diligent pratyekabuddha, it would still not be considered great.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! All the sentient beings of the buddha-lands of the ten directions, each one possessing merit equal to that of a pratyekabuddha: the total merit of all those sentient beings, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of times, equals the merit of one bodhisattva who has first given rise to the aspiration. Mañjuśrī! Do not think that the merit of immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of all sentient beings equals the merit of one bodhisattva who has first given rise to the aspiration, and that this is great. Even if the merit of all beings throughout the ten directions to the ends of space, whether egg-born, womb-born, moisture-born, or transformation-born, with form or without form, with perception, without perception, or neither with perception nor without perception, were multiplied immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of times, it would still not equal the merit of one bodhisattva who has first given rise to the aspiration.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! All sentient beings to the ends of space, each possessing merit equal to that of one bodhisattva who has first given rise to the aspiration: the total of that merit, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of times, equals the merit of one single hair pore of the Tathāgata. The Buddhas, Tathāgatas, in each and every hair pore of their bodies, each further possess ninety-nine thousand fine and subtle hair pores. Mañjuśrī! The total merit of all such hair pores of the Buddha's body, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of times, equals the merit of one minor mark on the Tathāgata's body. Thus the eighty minor marks of the Buddha's body each fully possess merit as described above.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! The total merit of the Tathāgata's eighty minor marks, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of times, equals the merit of the auspicious patterns on the soles of the Tathāgata's hands and feet. Each and every such auspicious pattern possesses the eighty minor marks, manifesting on the soles of the hands and feet."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "All the merit of these eighty minor marks, measured at immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of times, equals the merit of one of the thirty-two marks of a great man on the Tathāgata's body. Thus each of the thirty-two marks of a great man on the Buddha's body possesses merit as described above, clearly manifesting on the Buddha's body."

In verse it is said:

> The uṣṇīṣa mark,
> Hair in spiral curls, deep blue,
> Lustrous and turning to the right,
> The forehead broad and level,

> White ūrṇā light between the brows,
> Skin sublimely soft and smooth,
> Eyes broad like blue lotus petals,
> Teeth close-set and even,

> Forty in full number,
> Four canines, all sharp,
> White like conch shell and snow,
> Jaws, cheeks, and chest

> The upper body like a lion's,
> Tongue broad and long,
> The body's form wondrously complete,
> Like a nyagrodha tree,

> Body hair turning to the right,
> Navel wheel pure and deeply hidden,
> Both thighs smooth and even,
> Two calves like a deer king's,

> Both soles of the feet level and full,
> Hands and feet both soft and smooth,
> Ten fingers long and slender,
> All with webbed markings,

> Walking with steps straight ahead,
> Tongue always perceiving the supreme flavor,
> Excellent marks adorning the body,
> Seven places all level and full,

> Soles of the feet level and flat,
> Always manifesting thousand-spoked wheels:
> Such are the great man's
> Thirty-two marks.

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Thus the merit of these thirty-two marks of a great man, in all this merit of the thirty-two marks, measured at asaṃkhyeya inconceivable, immeasurable, without equal, unspeakable upon unspeakable multiples: this is the merit of fulfilling the aspirations of beings whose conditions for liberation have matured. Therefore the Tathāgata speaks of the causes and conditions of those whose maturity is complete as the Dharma-conch."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "The Tathāgata's wondrous sound of the Dharma-conch is universally heard by all sentient beings throughout immeasurable, boundless, asaṃkhyeya worlds. Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! Not only is the conch-sound thus; the Tathāgata's bodily radiance also illuminates immeasurable, boundless, asaṃkhyeya worlds, causing sentient beings to see the Buddha's body. This is inconceivable."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "Great wisdom, great compassion: this is great merit. All śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas who well understand the power of vows and practice, with great skillful means, purely upholding the precepts, attain the most supreme samādhi.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! The Buddha's body is unconditioned and apart from all characteristics; all śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and bodhisattvas cannot see it. The Tathāgata's manifested form-body has two meanings: first, to fulfill the vow made during the causal stage to liberate sentient beings; second, because the beings to be liberated have now reached the maturation of their conditions. For this reason the Tathāgata manifests the form-body. The manifested body is pure and sublime, causing sentient beings who draw near and make offerings to receive great benefit.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! Because the Buddha's body is most supreme, the marks are most supreme. Because the marks are most supreme, the radiance is most supreme. Because the radiance is most supreme, the Brahmā-voice is most supreme. Because the Brahmā-voice is most supreme, the teaching of the Dharma is most supreme. Because the teaching of the Dharma is most supreme, the Buddha's conduct is most supreme. Therefore the Tathāgata manifests the form-body, causing sentient beings to receive great benefit.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva! Within the Tathāgata's body, all marks are fully contained. According to the varying faculties, desires, natures, and degrees of sharpness or dullness of sentient beings, the manifested form-bodies each differ, causing sentient beings each to draw near, to delight in hearing the wondrous Dharma, and all to attain liberation, so that even in their daily activities they constantly see the Buddha."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "The Tathāgata, complete in the qualities of Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, has appeared in the world out of compassion for sentient beings, for the benefit and happiness of immeasurable devas, humans, and all sentient beings."

At that time, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, prostrated to the World-Honored One, and addressed the Buddha: "I have now swiftly gained this great and excellent benefit. Thus, World-Honored One, unattached in the three realms, most honored and most victorious, you benefit all sentient beings. The Buddha contemplates all worldly dharmas, immovable like Mount Sumeru, unattached like empty space, inconceivable, neither seen nor not seen."

When the World-Honored One had finished speaking this sūtra, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva, the bhikṣus, all the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, and all the devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and others, hearing the Buddha's teaching, all greatly rejoiced, believed, accepted, and respectfully practiced it.
