# 佛說十二遊經
## Sūtra of the Buddha's Twelve Travels

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0195

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Kālodaka (迦留陀伽)

---

Long ago, in the time of asaṃkhyeya kalpas, the Bodhisattva was a king. His parents had died early, so he yielded the kingdom to his younger brother, renounced the kingdom, and set out to seek the path. From afar he saw a brāhmaṇa of the Gautama clan. The Bodhisattva then studied the path under this brāhmaṇa. The brāhmaṇa said to the Bodhisattva: "Remove and set aside the royal garments you are wearing. Braid your hair and weave grass into a robe, as I wear. Accept from me the Gautama surname." Thereupon the Bodhisattva accepted, donning the grass robe and taking the Gautama surname. He purified his resolve and entered the deep mountains, through dense forests and perilous terrain, where he sat in meditation and contemplated the path.

The brāhmaṇa said: "You are a king who has long dwelt in honor and ease, and are unaccustomed to hardship. In summer you may drink water and eat various fruits. In winter you may return to the city and beg for alms in the streets. Then return to sit beneath the trees, and meditate without ceasing."

The Bodhisattva begged for alms and returned to his own kingdom's borders. All the people of the kingdom, from the king down to the common folk, could not recognize the Bodhisattva, and called him "the young Gautama." In a sweet-fruit garden outside the city, the Bodhisattva built a hermitage and sat there alone.

At that time, five hundred great bandits in the kingdom robbed government goods and fled. Their path passed by the Bodhisattva's hermitage, and their tracks scattered about, with stolen goods left around the Bodhisattva's dwelling.

The next day, those pursuing the bandits followed the tracks to the Bodhisattva's hermitage, and so they seized the Bodhisattva. They brought him before the authorities, accusing him of being the kingdom's great bandit responsible for all past robberies, a crime punishable by more than death. The king then commanded his ministers: "For such a person, the law requires that his body be impaled upon a wooden stake." He was set up as a great post, and blood flowed from his body down to the ground.

The great Gautama in the deep mountains, seeing this with his divine eye, flew there by supernatural power and asked him: "What crime have you committed, that your suffering is so cruel? Do not your wounds cause you poisonous pain, that you endure such agony?" The Bodhisattva answered: "Outwardly I have the pain of wounds, but inwardly I harbor a compassionate mind. I do not know what crime I have committed, yet I am unjustly put to death." The great Gautama said: "You have no sons. Who will carry on your line, that you endure such pain?" The Bodhisattva answered: "My life hangs by a moment. What use is speaking of sons and descendants?"

Thereupon the king ordered his attendants to shoot him dead with powerful crossbows and swift arrows.

The great Gautama, weeping in grief, took down the corpse and placed it in a coffin for burial. He then gathered the remaining blood from the soil, formed it into two balls, and carried one in each hand back to the mountains. Returning to his hermitage, he placed the blood from the left side in a vessel on the left, and likewise the blood from the right. The great Gautama said: "You were a man of the path. If your sincerity was true, the heavenly spirits shall cause the blood to transform into human beings." After ten months, the left became a boy and the right became a girl. From then on, they bore the Gautama surname, also called Śākya.

From the coming of the Bhadrakalpa, it began with the Tathāgata Ratna, and the Śākya lifespan was five million years. Below him, twenty-five kings each had lifespans of three million years. King Māndhātā had a lifespan of one million years. The cakravartin King Mūrdhagata, and the kings of the Left Thigh and Right Thigh, each had lifespans of one hundred thousand years. From King Pramuditā and the subsequent kings, all had lifespans of eighty-four thousand years.

From the time an evil thought arose, a cakravartin killed an ox for sacrifice, taking life; he lost the golden wheel and obtained the silver wheel, ruling three continents, with a lifespan of ten thousand years. King Dṛḍhasmṛti made armor, had a lifespan of five thousand years, obtained the copper wheel, and ruled two continents, the western and southern. King Priyaghāta had a lifespan of two thousand five hundred years, obtained the iron wheel, and ruled the southern continent. That king had a crown prince who committed five evils; for killing one person, the lifespan decreased by one thousand years.

The ancients had nine afflictions: cold, heat, hunger, thirst, birth, old age, sickness, and death. Brāhmaṇas killed living beings for sacrifice, and from this arose four hundred and four diseases.

From King Siṃhasmṛti onward, people's lifespans continued to decrease, reaching one hundred and twenty years. After King Siṃhasmṛti came King Siṃhamati, and there were eighty-four kings. People's lifespans decreased, with some living eighty, seventy, fifty, thirty, twenty, or ten years. After this, there was King Siṃhāyus, named Śuddhodana, who was the Bodhisattva's father. Reckoning the Bodhisattva's lineage from beginning to end, including the eighty-four thousand cakravartin kings before him, all bore the Gautama surname, a lineage of pure ripening.

The Bodhisattva was in Tuṣita heaven and wished to descend to be born. He surveyed the world below, asking in which country he should be born. He said: "Only in the household of King Śuddhodana is it fitting to be born." Thereupon, in heaven there was a tree called the Tuṣita tree. The Bodhisattva withdrew and sat beneath another tree in contemplation; his original tree lost its radiance.

Then a deva asked: "Why has the Bodhisattva abandoned his usual seat and gone to sit beneath another tree?" Another deva, knowing the Bodhisattva's intention, replied: "Do you not know? The Bodhisattva now wishes to descend to be born in Jambudvīpa. He is considering which country is suitable. Only the household of Śuddhodana is fitting."

Thereupon all the devas said: "Now that the Bodhisattva is descending, how shall we send him off?" Each devised plans and said: "Only the four hundred and four treasures of the Suddhāvāsa heaven, wondrously carved and each of distinct kind, all sharing precious flowers, shall serve as his chariot." The nāga king Airāvaṇa was made to serve as the mount, a white elephant whose fur surpassed the whiteness of the Snow Mountains. The elephant had thirty-three heads, each head with seven tusks, each tusk with seven pools, each pool with seven utpala lotuses, and upon each lotus sat a celestial maiden. The Bodhisattva, together with eighty-four thousand devas, descended riding the white elephant treasure-chariot.

At that time, King Śuddhodana's queen, in the midst of sleep, saw the white elephant dimly, and waking with a start, she told the king.

The Bodhisattva's father was named Śuddhodana. His father had four brothers in all. King Śuddhodana had two sons: the elder named Siddhārtha, the younger named Nanda. The Bodhisattva's mother was named Māyā; Nanda's mother was named Gautamī. The Bodhisattva's first uncle was named King Amṛtodana, who also had two sons: the elder named Devadatta, the younger named Ānanda. The Bodhisattva's second uncle was named King Dhanyodana, who had two sons: the elder named Śākya-mānava, the younger Aniruddha. The Bodhisattva's youngest uncle was named King Śākyodana, who had two sons: the elder named Śākya-rāja, the younger named Śākya-kumāra.

The country of Kapilavastu had eight cities, with a total of nine hundred thousand households.

Devadatta was born on the seventh day of the fourth month. The Buddha was born on the eighth day of the fourth month. The Buddha's brother Nanda was born on the ninth day of the fourth month. Ānanda was born on the tenth day of the fourth month. Devadatta was one zhang, five chi, and four cun tall. The Buddha was one zhang and six chi tall. Nanda was one zhang, five chi, and four cun tall. Ānanda was one zhang, five chi, and three cun tall. The noble Śākyas were one zhang and four chi tall. People of other countries were all one zhang and three chi tall.

The Bodhisattva's maternal family lived eight hundred li from the city. They bore the Gautama surname and were minor kings, ruling one million households. Their king was named Koṭirāja. The Bodhisattva's wife's family bore the Gautama surname; there was a Śākya elder named Suprabuddha. His wife's mother was named Candrā. In a city near their border, at the time the daughter was born, the sun was about to set, and its remaining light shone into their house, illuminating every room. Therefore they named her Gopī, which in Chinese means "Bright Woman." Gopī was the crown prince's first wife; her father was the elder Suprabuddha. The crown prince's second wife, who bore Rāhula, was named Yaśodharā; her father was the elder Miśraka. The third wife was named Mṛgī; her father was the Śākya elder. Because there were three wives, the prince's father the king built for him three seasonal palaces. Each palace had twenty thousand attendant women; the three palaces had sixty thousand attendant women in all. Because the prince was to become a cakravartin king, sixty thousand attendant women were provided.

The Buddha left home at twenty-nine and attained the path at thirty-five. From the eighth day of the fourth month to the fifteenth of the seventh month, he sat beneath the tree; this was the first year. In the second year, in the Deer Park, he taught the Dharma to Ājñāta-Kauṇḍinya and the others; he also taught Vāṣpa and the others; he also taught Bhadrika and the other seventeen people; he also taught the great elder Yaśas and the two upāsikās; he also taught the Nirgrantha Samyaksmṛti; he also taught the forty-two people from the time of Dīpaṃkara Buddha. In the third year, he taught the three Kāśyapa brothers, Uruvilvā and the others, completing one thousand bhikṣus. In the fourth year, on Elephant Head Mountain, he taught the Dharma to nāgas, ghosts, and spirits. In the fifth year, in the Bamboo Grove, he taught Aśvajit. In the fifth year, on the way to Śrāvastī but before arriving, Śāriputra was a brāhmaṇa with one hundred and twenty-five disciples, sitting beneath a tree.

At that time, Maudgalyāyana was serving as a minister and general in the country of Mithilā. Going out, he saw Śāriputra sitting beneath a tree and asked him: "Why are you sitting here?" Śāriputra answered: "I wish to study the path." Maudgalyāyana said: "I wish to accompany you." He immediately dismissed his officials and attendants, retaining only one hundred and twenty-five people. Together, the two had two hundred and fifty people.

Śāriputra entered the city to beg for alms and saw the Buddha's disciple, the bhikṣu Aśvajit. He asked him: "What sort of practitioner are you? Your robes are different from the usual." The bhikṣu Aśvajit answered: "I am a disciple of the Buddha." Śāriputra asked: "How does the Buddha teach the Dharma?" Aśvajit said: "All dharmas arise from causes and conditions; the extinction of all suffering is complete cessation." Thereupon Śāriputra attained the path of stream-entry, and overjoyed, he returned and told Maudgalyāyana: "There is a divine person in the world." Maudgalyāyana asked: "How does he teach the Dharma?" Śāriputra explained in full from beginning to end. Maudgalyāyana then also attained the path of stream-entry. The two then led their disciples to the Buddha's presence. Before they arrived, the Buddha already knew and told the bhikṣus: "Today two worthy ones will come: one called the bhikṣu of wisdom, and one called the bhikṣu of supernatural powers. They shall arrive momentarily." The Buddha taught them the Four Noble Truths. Śāriputra attained arhatship in seven days; Maudgalyāyana attained arhatship in fifteen days.

In the sixth year, Sudatta and Prince Jeta together built a monastery for the Buddha, with twelve Buddha-image temples, seventy-two lecture halls, three thousand six hundred rooms, and five hundred multi-storied pavilions. In the seventh year, in the country of Aparagodānīya, he taught the Pratyutpanna Sūtra to the bodhisattva Bhadrapāla and the other eight. In the eighth year, in the Willow Mountains, he taught the Dharma to King Druma's younger brother. In the ninth year, in the Foul Marsh, he taught the Dharma to Aṅgulimāla. In the tenth year, he returned to the country of Magadha and taught the Dharma to King Bimbisāra. In the eleventh year, beneath the Terrifying Tree, he taught Maitreya about his origins. In the twelfth year, he returned to his father's kingdom, and at the Śākya monastery eighty li from the city, he taught the Dharma to Kṣemakara; he returned to the country and taught the Dharma to his father the king and the Śākya clan, converting eighty-four thousand people who attained the path of stream-entry.

In these fourteen countries, the Buddha traveled and taught the Dharma for twelve years. King Prasenajit, rendered in Chinese as "Harmonious Joy." Kapilavastu, rendered in Chinese as "Wondrous Virtue." The country of Śrāvastī, rendered in Chinese as "Lacking Nothing." The country of Vaiśālī, rendered in Chinese as "Vast and Great," also called "Crossing Over Birth and Death." Rājagṛha, rendered in Chinese as "Royal Residence City." The country of Kuru, rendered in Chinese as "Land of the Wise." Vārāṇasī, rendered in Chinese as "Deer Park," also called "Land of All Buddhas."

In Jambudvīpa there are sixteen great countries, eighty-four thousand cities, eight kings, and four emperors. To the east is the Emperor of Jin, whose people are thriving and numerous. To the south is the Emperor of the Land of India, whose territory is famed for elephants. To the west is the Emperor of the Land of Great Qin, whose territory is rich in gold, silver, and jade. To the northwest is the Emperor of the Yuezhi, whose territory has many fine horses. Among the eighty-four thousand cities, there are six thousand four hundred kinds of people, ten thousand kinds of sounds, and five hundred and sixty billion villages. There are six thousand four hundred kinds of fish, four thousand five hundred kinds of birds, two thousand four hundred kinds of beasts, ten thousand kinds of trees, eight thousand kinds of grasses, seven hundred and forty kinds of assorted medicines, forty-three kinds of assorted fragrances, one hundred and twenty-one kinds of precious stones, and seven principal treasures.

In the ocean there are two thousand five hundred countries. One hundred and eighty countries consume the five grains. Three hundred and thirty countries consume fish, turtles, tortoises, and crocodiles.

Five kings each rule five hundred cities. The first king rules the country of Siṃhala, whose people entirely follow the Buddha and do not worship heterodox gods. The second king rules the country of Kālā, whose territory produces the seven treasures. The third king rules the country of Pūrṇa, whose territory produces forty-two kinds of incense and white lapis lazuli. The fourth king rules the country of Jayā, whose territory produces long pepper and black pepper. The fifth king rules the country of Nāga, whose territory produces white pearls and seven-colored lapis lazuli. The people of these five great country-capitals are mostly dark-skinned and short in stature. The distance between them is six hundred and fifty thousand li. Beyond them there is only ocean water, with no people, extending one million four hundred thousand li to the Iron-Ring Mountains.
