# 佛說阿那律八念經
## The Sūtra of Anuruddha's Eight Contemplations

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 46

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by Zhi Yao (支曜).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was on Oath-Herding Mountain beneath the Teacher-Seeking Tree. The Venerable Anuruddha was in a nearby meditation grove, sitting in contemplation and thinking: "The Dharma of the path is fewness of desires; many desires are not the path. The Dharma of the path is contentment; insatiability is not the path. The Dharma of the path is seclusion; delighting in company is not the path. The Dharma of the path is diligence; laziness is not the path. The Dharma of the path is restraining the mind; letting the mind run wild is not the path. The Dharma of the path is concentration; scattered thinking is not the path. The Dharma of the path is wisdom; ignorance and darkness are not the path."

The Buddha, knowing his thoughts through his holy mind, flew to his presence in the time it takes a strong man to bend and extend his arm, and praised him, saying: "Excellent, excellent! Anuruddha! What you contemplate constitutes the contemplations of a great being. Listen to my words as I explain the eight contemplations of a great being; reflect on them well and put them into practice. You should study the four dhyānas, restraining the mind and contemplating the Dharma without stopping midway. You will surely obtain great benefit and will not fail to fulfill your aspirations. What are the four dhyānas? Solely abandoning desires, evil, and unwholesome dharmas, with the mind producing joy: this constitutes the practice of the first dhyāna. Having abandoned evil thoughts and guarding the mind in single-pointed focus, without relying on joy: this constitutes the practice of the second dhyāna. Having ceased joy and solely contemplating in accord with the Dharma, perceiving suffering and happiness: this constitutes the practice of the third dhyāna. Also abandoning suffering and happiness, with sorrow and gladness completely severed, abiding in purity: this constitutes the practice of the fourth dhyāna. Having studied thus, one then solely practices the eight contemplations of great beings. The four dhyānas serve as the method for restraining the mind, swiftly perceiving the practice, obtaining benefit and fulfilling aspirations quickly, without stopping midway. Furthermore, as for fewness of desires, the analogy is this: suppose a king has a border minister in charge of many chests and trunks filled with fine garments, yet you yourself delight in wearing coarse and shabby clothing. Fewness of desires, contentment, seclusion, diligence, restraint of mind, concentration, and wisdom: having relinquished the household life, you do not engage in playful conceit, you are without any misstep, and you are sure to bring peace to people and arrive at the gate of nirvāṇa. These are the eight contemplations of great beings. As for solely practicing the four dhyānas to restrain the mind and contemplate the Dharma, the analogy is this: suppose a king has a border minister who oversees the kitchen and harmonizes the five flavors, yet you yourself delight in begging for food, accepting just enough to sustain the body, not seeking to please your palate. Another analogy: suppose a king has pleasure towers and pavilions for excursions, yet you yourself delight in being among mountains, marshes, and trees, dwelling in seclusion and practicing diligently, without desire for the world. Another analogy: suppose a king has a border minister in charge of various fine medicines, including curds, butter, ghee, and honey, yet you yourself, when ill, delight in using only urine as medicine, accepting just enough to relieve affliction. By practicing the eight contemplations and reflecting on the four dhyānas, being diligent without lapse, the mind without any misstep, you will surely bring yourself to peace and arrive at the gate of nirvāṇa."

When the Buddha had finished speaking, he returned to Oath-Herding Mountain and told the disciples: "The path requires fewness of desires; do not have many desires. The path requires contentment; do not accumulate surplus. The path requires seclusion; do not delight in gatherings. The path requires diligence; do not be lazy. The path requires restraint of mind; do not be heedless. The path requires concentration; do not let the mind be scattered. The path requires wisdom; do not be ignorant and dull.

"That a bhikṣu should have few desires means this: that one's own person has few desires, without making it known to others that one has few desires. The meaning should be understood from this.

"That a bhikṣu should be content means this: being satisfied with one's alms bowl, Dharma robes, bedding, and medicines for illness; having received enough food, one stops and does not accumulate surplus. The meaning should be understood from this.

"That a bhikṣu should dwell in seclusion means this: avoiding people and not joining assemblies, dwelling far away in mountains, marshes, cliffs, rocks, and among trees. If any of the four assemblies, or if kings and great ministers, come to inquire about the path, one teaches them purity and wishes them to depart quickly. It is like a poor person who owes a debt to a wealthy family: when seized and dragged by his creditor, he wishes to escape and takes no pleasure in it, slipping away to hide himself in a distant place. The meaning should be understood from this.

"That a bhikṣu should be diligent means this: cutting off unwholesome dharmas and assiduously practicing the path as taught in the sūtras, never being idle or weary. In the first, middle, and last watches of the night, one walks, sits, or reclines, always with an awakened mind, mindful of purity, in order to remove the five hindrances. The meaning should be understood from this.

"That a bhikṣu should restrain the mind means this: abandoning desires, evil dharmas, and sitting with focused thought in contemplation, severing suffering and happiness, and attaining the practice of the four dhyānas. The meaning should be understood from this.

"That a bhikṣu should have a concentrated mind means this: always being single-minded, contemplating the body, contemplating the mind, and contemplating dharmas, not leaning into biased practice, gathering mindfulness and directing it toward the path, abandoning deluded and afflicted thoughts. The meaning should be understood from this.

"That a bhikṣu should have wisdom means this: knowing the four truths, namely suffering, its accumulation, its cessation, and the path. What is the truth of suffering? The suffering of birth, the suffering of old age, the suffering of illness, the suffering of death, the suffering of grief and lamentation, the suffering of separation from those one loves, the suffering of meeting those one hates, the suffering of not obtaining what one desires, and the suffering of the clinging to the five aggregates. The suffering of birth refers to how, according to one's actions, a person falls into a particular destiny, receives an embryo and is formed, comes forth and the body appears, the sense-faculties enter and the person grows. Old age refers to how a person's faculties ripen and form changes: the hair turns white, the teeth fall out, the sinews slacken, the skin wrinkles, and one walks bent over leaning on a staff. Illness refers to how, brought about by a person's unwholesome actions, there arise boils, abscesses, sores, pus, epilepsy, madness, chronic diseases, and also a hundred other kinds. Death refers to how a person's life departs and the form decays: warmth dissipates, breath ceases, and the spirit-soul departs. All of these are suffering. What is the truth of accumulation? It refers to how the lustful mind takes pleasure in delight and gives rise to attachment and affection, with the will set on craving and desire, causing further existence and outflows. Manifold actions flourish and proliferate, binding oneself through attachment. What is called love is this: the eye loves form, the ear loves sound, the nose loves scent, the tongue loves taste, the body loves fine touch, and the mind loves whatever it desires. But one regards these only as constant, takes pleasure in the prospect of them, takes them to be advantageous, and declares 'these are mine,' thereby binding oneself through attachment. From this arise the five aggregates of form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, which are loved and clung to with great intensity. Regarding them as constant and taking pleasure in them, one declares 'these are mine' and binds oneself through attachment. What is called form is the elemental matter received by the spirit-consciousness: the qualities of earth, water, fire, and wind, transformed into a body. Through what one loves and is attached to, one causes the eye to cognize form, the ear to cognize sound, the nose to cognize scent, the tongue to cognize taste, the body to cognize fine touch, and the mind to cognize and become attached to dharmas. This should be trusted as the truth of accumulation. What is the truth of cessation? Not receiving and not entering into anything, with love exhausted and without remainder, with bonds and attachments already undone: as one who sees with wisdom, there is no further existence of any kind. Worldly people have no such vision; they are attached to the five aggregates. When all that has been counted is exhausted, when the bonds of love are completely undone, when through wisdom one has seen that all is impermanent, suffering, empty, and without self, and has thereby made the cut: this is the truth of cessation. What is the truth of the path? It refers to the eightfold right path: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right concentration. What is right view? Right view has two aspects, the worldly and the transcendent. To know that there is benevolence and righteousness, to know that there are fathers and mothers, to know that there are śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, to know that there are those who have attained the path as true persons, to know that there is this world and the next, and to know that there is good and evil, transgression and merit, with conduct in this life leading to consequences in the next as verified by practice: this is worldly right view. To have already understood the four truths of suffering, accumulation, cessation, and the path, and to have already attained the wisdom-vision of emptiness, purity, and non-self: this is transcendent right view. Right thought also has two aspects: thinking of study and inquiry, thinking of harmony and respect, thinking of carefulness and restraint, and thinking of non-harm: this is worldly right thought. Thinking of renunciation, thinking of patient endurance, and thinking of extinguishing craving and exhausting attachment: this is transcendent right thought. Right speech also has two aspects: not speaking divisively, not speaking harshly, not speaking falsely, and not speaking frivolously: this is worldly right speech. Departing from the four faults of speech, reciting and discoursing on the words of the path, with the mind not fabricating, exhausting all without remainder: this is transcendent right speech. Right action also has two aspects: the body performs good deeds, the mouth speaks good words, and the mind thinks good thoughts: this is worldly right action. The body and mouth are diligent, the mind contemplates emptiness and purity, dissolving and extinguishing attachment: this is transcendent right action. Right livelihood also has two aspects: not killing, not stealing, not engaging in sexual misconduct, not boasting of oneself, and cultivating virtue and self-discipline: this is worldly right livelihood. Departing from the three bodily evils, severing the accumulation of suffering, extinguishing craving and seeking liberation: this is transcendent right livelihood. Right effort also has two aspects: seeking wealth by righteous means, not coveting wrongful gain, and not deceiving others in one's heart: this is worldly right effort. Having departed from wrong livelihoods and relinquished worldly divination, not transgressing the prohibitions of the path: this is transcendent right effort. Right mindfulness also has two aspects: not being jealous, not being angry, and not serving what is heterodox: this is worldly right mindfulness. Departing from the three mental evils, practicing the four foundations of mindfulness, being pure and unconditioned: this is transcendent right mindfulness. Right concentration also has two aspects: one's nature being sincere and well-composed, guarding goodness with firm stability, the mind not being crooked: this is worldly right concentration. Attaining the four bases of mental power, contemplating only emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, seeing the source of nirvāṇa: this is transcendent right concentration. This is the truth of the path. A bhikṣu who has relinquished the household life and given up attachment and affection, who serenely contemplates the path without longing for anything, whose mind does not follow desire, who is pure and without obstruction: this is the Dharma of the path. The meaning should be understood from this."

When the Venerable Anuruddha heard the Buddha's teaching of this sūtra, it opened and guided his mind. He received and practiced it for three months, and his outflows were exhausted and his mind was liberated. He attained the three knowledges as his realization. Having himself awakened to the attainment of arhatship, he then spoke in verse:

> One who craves insatiably, who delights in company and lets the mind run free,
> Such conduct leads to suffering; cultivating evil, one becomes deeply attached.
> Fewness of desires and knowing the practice of the path, knowing shame and not regarding oneself highly,
> This Dharma leads to purity; far from evil, one attains liberation from the world.
> The mind on the path does not crave life, nor does it take pleasure in the parting of death;
> I abide in concentration like space, and in due time all suffering will be resolved.
> Having received the teaching from the Buddha, I guard the practice and abandon desires and evils;
> The afflictions of the body have been cast off, and I have gained the benefit of the unconditioned.
> Having arrived by my own effort at the three knowledges, having uprooted the roots of attachment and affection,
> I shall, in the village of Vaiśālī, in the Bamboo Grove, enter nirvāṇa.
