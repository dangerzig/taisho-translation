# 修藥師儀軌布壇法
## Method for Arranging the Altar in the Bhaiṣajyaguru Ritual

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0928

Translated from the Chinese. Qing dynasty, supplementary translation by Āwàng Zhāshí (阿旺扎什)

---

## Preface to the Reprinted Sūtra on the Ritual for the Offerings to the Seven Medicine Buddhas

In ancient times, our Buddha was in the city of Vaiśālī, where with his Brahmā-voice he taught the *Sūtra of the Original Vows and Merits of the Seven Medicine Buddhas*. When it was transmitted to Tibet, King Songtsen Gampo had it translated into Tibetan for the convenience of recitation in that land. Later, during the present dynasty, the Fifth Dalai Lama composed the *Sūtra on the Ritual for Offerings*, which is dignified in form and thorough in its liturgical details. Those who practice according to this method in later generations, with inner sincerity and outward attention to detail, will be able to attain liberation from the manifold sufferings of the world and swiftly realize unsurpassed bodhi. The benefit is immeasurable. However, since most Chinese people are not conversant with the Tibetan script, when I later saw the Chinese translation made by the Princely Consort Gongbu Chabu of Prince Xianqin, I found the text accurate and conforming to the sūtra's meaning. Unfortunately, the original printing blocks no longer existed, and there were no instructions for the altar arrangement or for the directional placement of the buddha images. I carefully transcribed and treasured them, not daring to keep them secret, and long wished to make them public. However, lacking the means, the work was delayed. Now, the great patron Yōu Róngzhāi, a Junior Minister of the Imperial Clan, has undertaken to carve this sūtra. He directed the addition of illustrations for the altar arrangement, the directional placement of each buddha image, the forms of the Thirty-Five Buddhas, and the twenty-one forms of the Liberating Buddha-Mother, along with several lines of Brahmic script, all especially dignified. Furthermore, the patron Mr. Ding, a Secretary in the Court of Colonial Affairs, upon hearing of this carving, joyfully contributed. Having thus obtained two great patrons who generously donated funds and brought this endeavor to completion, the Chinese translation, comprising several fascicles of various sūtras, with all buddha-names written in both scripts, has been entrusted to the woodblock carvers. It is bound into volumes intended for permanent transmission, to be offered to monasteries and distributed to the faithful. May all our like-minded companions share in these wholesome causes. This undertaking was no small matter. My long-cherished wish is now fulfilled, and the material generosity of these virtuous friends is itself a gift of Dharma, which should not go unrecorded. Therefore I have composed this preface.

In the great Qing dynasty, in the *jiǎshēn* year of the Daoguang reign [1824 CE], in the auspicious month of the twelfth month.

Written by Āwàng Zhāshí, abbot of Jìngzhù-sì Temple, specially appointed head seal-keeper of Shíshèng-sì Temple in Shěngjīng.

## Method for Arranging the Altar in the Seven-Buddha Bhaiṣajyaguru Ritual

There are three sections to the practice of the Seven-Buddha Bhaiṣajyaguru Ritual: the preliminaries, the main practice, and the concluding rites.

The preliminaries: as the *Comprehensive Dhāraṇī Recitation Method of the Seven Buddhas' Original Vow of Vast Merit* states: "Pay homage to the Seven Tathāgatas." Those who wish to practice the ritual should bathe, don new and clean garments, eat pure food, and completely set aside all defiled and afflictive states of mind. One should generate deep reverence, faith, and understanding toward the merits of the Three Treasures and the benefits of this sūtra, and cultivate the contemplation of the four immeasurables. If the practitioner is a layperson, observe the purification precepts, beginning on the eighth day of the waxing moon. In a suitable place, sweep and clean thoroughly. Set up banners and streamers in abundance for adornment. Within, construct a large incense maṇḍala, using either colored powder and fine flour, or flowers and rice to lay it out. Upon it, place buddha images, whether cast, painted, or sculpted; if one has relics, all the better. The seats of all the worldly and supramundane retinue should each be arranged according to the Dharma. All the offerings should likewise be arranged with full effort. Before each of the seven buddha images, light seven lamps. Hang seven five-colored pennants. Further, set out incense, flowers, powdered incense, food, medicine, the seven treasures, the eight auspicious symbols, jeweled vases, and all other items in full.

Having thus arranged the offerings, the assembly should sit on comfortable seats. Each enters the meditation of emptiness and, according to one's capacity, recites the *Sūtra of the Original Vow Merits of the Seven Buddhas*, the *Prajñā*, and other extensive and profound sūtras. Then recite this comprehensive dhāraṇī ritual with full effort. At minimum, the practice should last seven days, or twenty-one days, thirty-five days, or, even better, forty-nine days. This is taught in other ritual manuals. I now follow that intent in composing this ritual, which has two parts: first, the altar arrangement; second, the practice method.

The altar arrangement: in a supremely auspicious place, purify and adorn the upper, lower, and four intermediate directions. In the center, set a table. If no table is available, erect a clean platform, anointed with fragrant medicine on top. Upon it, depict an incomparable palace with four sides and four gates, omitting archways. The palace design and proportions need not follow the esoteric cord-line method, but from the seats of the eight Mahāyāna figures in the central hall to the gate-curves, the measurements must not be out of proportion.

The color scheme: within the incomparable palace, at the center, erect a four-sided platform of three tiers. The upper tier is white. The middle and lower tiers: east is blue, south is yellow, west is red, north is green. On the upper platform, place an eight-petaled lotus. On the middle platform, place a sixteen-petaled lotus. On the lower platform, place a twenty-four-petaled lotus. On the center and corner petals of the upper platform, each place a lion-jewel throne. On the middle platform, each place a moon-disc jewel seat. On the lower platform, beginning from the east gate and circling to the right: the ten directional guardian deities plus the sun and moon celestials all have mountain seats. Circling to the left: the twelve yakṣa great generals all have brocade seats. At the four gates, the four celestial kings all have lotus-moon-disc seats. All the above seats may be depicted with colored powder. The outer ground is spread with jeweled sand, adorned with *uḍumbara* flowers and the like, resembling the most wondrous splendor of the Land of Ultimate Bliss. Outside is an encircling wall like an iron mountain. On the wall, bracket-supports hold jeweled eaves. This is not the same as the esoteric-ritual wall with overhanging eaves, but rather like the Brahmā-ground palace within a vast garden. The general principle should be understood.

Although many ritual manuals exist, I now follow the method composed by the Buddha-son Asaṅga Bhadanta. On the lion-jewel throne at the center of the upper platform, place a stand in the shape of a maṇḍala. Upon it, lay a sūtra-cloth; on the cloth, place the eight-hundred-verse sūtra booklet. On the east: Śākyamuni Buddha, yellow-colored, right hand in the earth-touching mudrā, left hand in the meditation mudrā. On the south: Dundubhisvara-rāja Tathāgata, yellow-colored, right hand in the wish-granting mudrā, left hand in the meditation mudrā. On the west: Aśokottamaśrī Tathāgata, light red, both hands in the meditation mudrā. On the north: Suvarna-nāma-śrī-rāja Tathāgata, yellow-colored, right hand in the fearlessness mudrā, left hand in the meditation mudrā. At the northeast corner: Bhaiṣajyaguru-vaiḍūrya-prabhā-rāja Tathāgata, blue-colored, right hand in the wish-granting mudrā holding a myrobalan fruit, left hand in the meditation mudrā with a bowl on top. At the southeast corner: Suvarṇa-vimala Tathāgata, yellow-colored, both hands in the Dharma-teaching mudrā. At the southwest corner: Dharma-sāgara-megha-garjita-ghoṣa Tathāgata, light red, both hands in the Dharma-teaching mudrā. At the northwest corner: Dharma-sāgara-jñāna-vikrīḍita-abhijña Tathāgata, red-colored, right hand in the wish-granting mudrā, left hand in the meditation mudrā. All eight Tathāgata images wear golden-red Dharma-robes, possess the auspicious marks, and sit in the vajra cross-legged posture.

On the lotus petals of the middle platform, on the east at the central seat: Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, golden-red, left hand holding a sūtra, right hand holding a sword. To his right: Mokṣa-prada Bodhisattva (Salvation Bodhisattva), white, both hands in vajra-fist mudrā pressed upon both hips, seated with a slightly proud bearing. At the northeast corner: Sūryaprabha Bodhisattva, golden-red, right hand holding an *uḍumbara* flower with a sūtra-case on top, surmounted by a sun-disc, left hand in vajra-fist mudrā at the hip. To Mañjuśrī's left: Vajrapāṇi Bodhisattva, green, right hand holding a vajra at the chest, left hand holding a jeweled garland. At the southeast corner: Candraprabha Bodhisattva, white, right hand holding an *uḍumbara* flower with a sūtra-case on top, surmounted by a crescent moon, left hand in vajra-fist mudrā at the hip.

In addition, the eleven great dhāraṇī-holding bodhisattvas, as recorded in the sequence of this sūtra: at the south, central seat: Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, white, holding a lotus. To his left: Mahāmati Bodhisattva, yellow, holding a lotus surmounted by a single eye. To his right: Maitreya Bodhisattva, yellow, holding a nāga-puṣpa tree surmounted by a water-pot. At the southwest corner: Samanta-raśmi Bodhisattva (Accumulation of Eloquence), white, holding a sword. At the west, central seat: Amogha-viṣkambhin Bodhisattva, white, holding a sword. To his left: Sudarśana Bodhisattva, white, holding a lotus-tree surmounted by a sūtra-case and jewel. To his right: Tamonudaḥ-mati Bodhisattva (Darkness-Dispelling Wisdom), white, holding a staff. At the northwest corner: Suvicāra Bodhisattva (Good Deliberation), white, holding a jeweled vase. At the north, central seat: Sumeru-saṃcaya Bodhisattva, white, holding a lotus-tree surmounted by a crescent moon. To his left: Susvara Bodhisattva (Wondrous Sound), light blue, holding an *uḍumbara* flower surmounted by a vajra and blazing jewel. To his right: Meruparidhāra-rāja Bodhisattva (Wondrous-Summit-Upholder King), yellow, holding a jeweled grain-stalk. These eleven bodhisattvas each form their own mudrā with the right hand at the chest; the left hand is in vajra-fist mudrā at the hip. Adorned with many jewels, each wears celestial garments of various colors, possessing a gentle and kind appearance, seated in the bodhisattva cross-legged posture.

On the lotus-petal seats of the lower platform, beginning from inside the east gate: to the left, Extreme Dread Yakṣa Great General, yellow, holding a vajra. To his right, Vajra Yakṣa Great General, blue, holding a sword. Next, Fierce Grip Yakṣa Great General, yellow, holding a mace. At the northeast corner, Star-Holder Yakṣa Great General, light blue, holding a mace. In the north, Wind-Holder Yakṣa Great General, red, holding a trident. Dwelling-Place Yakṣa Great General, smoke-colored, holding a sword. Power-Holder Yakṣa Great General, red, holding a mace. Drink-Holder Yakṣa Great General, yellow, holding a mace. At the northwest corner, Speech-Holder Yakṣa Great General, yellow, holding an axe. In the west, Thought-Holder Yakṣa Great General, yellow, holding a lasso. Motion-Holder Yakṣa Great General, blue, holding a mace. Circle-Maker Yakṣa Great General, red, holding a wheel. All the yakṣa great generals form their own mudrā with the right hand at the chest; the left hand holds a jewel-rat disgorging a wish-fulfilling pearl. Adorned with many jewels, wearing celestial garments, with large bellies and stout bodies, their forms are fearsome. All sit in the manner of a king at play.

Inside the east gate, on the right, on a chariot seat: the Sun Celestial, red, both hands bearing a sun-disc. To his left, on a goose seat: the Moon Celestial, white, holding a moon-disc. Next, on a goose seat: Brahmā-deva, four-faced, yellow, right hand holding a wheel, left hand holding a jeweled vase. At the southeast corner, on an elephant seat: Indra, white, holding a vajra. In the south, on a ram seat: Agni-deva, red, holding a fire-brazier. On a water-buffalo seat: Yama-deva, blue, holding a mace. On a corpse seat: Rākṣasa-deva, dark blue, holding a sword. On a sea-creature seat: Varuṇa-deva, white, both hands holding a serpent-lasso. At the southwest corner, on a deer seat: Vāyu-deva, light blue, holding a wind-banner. In the west, on a horse seat: Yakṣa-deva, yellow, holding a mace. On an ox seat: Maheśvara-deva, white, holding a trident. On a boar seat: Pṛthivī-devī, yellow, both hands holding a vase. All these devas wear jeweled crowns and celestial garments.

At the east gate, center: Dhṛtarāṣṭra Mahārāja, white, both hands holding a lute. At the south gate, center: Virūḍhaka Mahārāja, blue, holding a sword. At the west gate, center: Virūpākṣa Mahārāja, red, holding a serpent-lasso. At the north gate, center: Vaiśravaṇa Mahārāja, yellow, holding a jewel-rat. The four celestial kings all wear jeweled crowns and celestial garments, standing with both feet together. All such images, whether made of fragrant medicine, wood, clay, painting, or sculpture, are acceptable; gold and silver are even more wondrous. The essential requirement is that they be arranged according to the ritual manual, all facing toward the center.

According to ancient texts, there was originally no mention of paintings or illustrations. These are provided for convenience. If painted images are unavailable, one may use a maṇḍala laid out with flowers and rice according to the preceding ritual. Even so, one should not regard these casually or treat them carelessly. One must earnestly pray for the empowerment of authentic transmission, so that the great seat spontaneously arises and all the Buddhas are invited to descend. They do not enter the previously laid-out forms. According to the sūtra, the power of making seven buddha images is of true importance. The seven Buddhas and their retinues also manifest through this power. The wisdom of the invited guests that comes and then departs should not be doubted; it is all accomplished through the capabilities and efforts of the wise.

When the arrangement is complete, set out the offerings. Among the offerings, before each Buddha, light seven lamps and hang seven colored pennants. These are the essential offerings, as stated in the vow-text of Mokṣa-prada Bodhisattva. Also offer merit-water made with various fragrant medicines, unguent, fresh or artificial flowers, sandalwood incense, *agaru* incense, *turuga* incense, and other burned incense. Offer fragrant-flour preparations of various kinds. Offer various delicious foods as food-offerings. Offer various medicines as medicine-offerings. Banners, pennants, garments, the seven treasures, the eight jewels, the eight auspicious symbols, and other offerings should be abundant. Arranging each in seven portions is especially meritorious. Before the yakṣa great generals, directional guardians, earth spirits, and other deities, offerings may be omitted. However, according to this sūtra's ritual, food-offerings should be presented, one portion for each category. Then fill a dhāraṇī jeweled vase with fragrant medicinal water, tie silk around its neck, and insert fruit-laden branches in the mouth as adornment. The patron must devoutly and respectfully attend to the monastics in the assembly, who recite the eight-hundred-verse sūtra and the *Prajñā* and other profound sūtras as supplementary practice.

Although this ritual may be practiced at any time, if performed on the seven auspicious days, the merit exceeds that of ordinary days a hundred-thousand-million-fold. What are the seven? First, the day the Buddha entered the womb: the fifteenth of the sixth month in the *jǐwèi* year. Second, the day the Buddha was born: the seventh of the fourth month in the *gēngshēn* year. Third, the day the Buddha left home: the eighth of the third month in the *wùzǐ* year. Fourth, the day the Buddha attained the Way: the fifteenth of the fourth month in the *jiǎwǔ* year. Fifth, the day the Buddha turned the Four Noble Truths Dharma-wheel at the Deer Park: the fourth of the sixth month in the same year. Sixth, the day the Buddha descended from Trāyastriṃśa Heaven: the twenty-second of the eighth month in the *gēngzǐ* year. Seventh, the day the Buddha entered parinirvāṇa: the fifteenth of the fourth month in the *gēngchén* year. These auspicious days are fixed and without error. According to Master Sākya-wa, the birthday falls on the eighth of the third month of the *dīngmǎo* year. According to the Great Master Pugai-zi, the birth was in the *jiǎwǔ* year. According to the Kaśmīrī Paṇḍita and Master Dropu Lotsāwa, the descent-day falls on the twenty-second of the ninth month. The deep and secret meaning of what these distinguished scholars have taught is profound and difficult to fathom; I cannot exhaust it here.

What I have stated accords with common practice. I have also consulted the *Saṃyuktāgama* and the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* and other texts, which record the day the Buddha performed miracles, the day he attained bodhi, the day he first turned the Dharma-wheel, and the day he descended. On these four great auspicious days, any wholesome deed performed exceeds ordinary days by a hundred-billion-fold, and the merit is immeasurable.
