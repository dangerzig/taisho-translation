# 甚希有經
## Sūtra of the Extremely Rare

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0689

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavān was in the city of Rājagṛha, dwelling on Vulture Peak, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus, and innumerable bodhisattvas, devas, humans, and others.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, at the morning hour, for the sake of begging food, donned his robes and took up his bowl to enter the city of Rājagṛha, begging in order from house to house. From afar he saw a certain place where a great multi-storied mansion had been newly constructed, splendid and ornate, exceedingly lovely. Having seen this, he thought within himself: "If good men or good women could build such a lofty and spacious multi-storied mansion, splendid and ornate, exceedingly lovely, and offer it to the Saṃgha of the four directions and its great and virtuous assembly; and if other good men or good women, after the parinirvāṇa of all the Tathāgatas, were to build a stūpa as small as an āmalaka fruit, place within it relics of the Buddha the size of a mustard seed, erect a finial the size of a great needle, and mount upon it a parasol the size of a small jujube leaf, or make a Buddha image as small as a grain of barley: of these two kinds of merit, which would be greater?"

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, having had this thought, finished begging in order through the city of Rājagṛha and returned to his quarters. After his meal, he put away his robes and bowl, washed his feet, and in the afternoon went to the Tathāgata's place. Arriving, he bowed his head to the World-Honored One's feet, withdrew to one side, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Earlier this morning, for the sake of begging food, I donned my robes and took up my bowl and entered the city of Rājagṛha, begging in order from house to house. From afar I saw a certain place where a great multi-storied mansion had been newly constructed, splendid and ornate, exceedingly lovely. Having seen this, I thought within myself: 'If good men or good women could build such a lofty and spacious multi-storied mansion, splendid and ornate, exceedingly lovely, and offer it to the Saṃgha of the four directions and its great and virtuous assembly; and if other good men or good women, after the parinirvāṇa of all the Tathāgatas, were to build a stūpa as small as an āmalaka fruit, place within it relics of the Buddha the size of a mustard seed, erect a finial the size of a great needle, and mount upon it a parasol the size of a small jujube leaf, or make a Buddha image as small as a grain of barley: of these two kinds of merit, which would be greater?' Therefore I now ask the World-Honored One. I humbly wish the Tathāgata to have compassion and explain this for me."

At that time, the World-Honored One, in a sublime Brahmā voice, told Ānanda: "Excellent! Excellent! You now wish to benefit and bring peace and happiness to innumerable sentient beings, to have compassion for the devas, humans, and others of the world, and to enable them to obtain great benefit and happiness. You ask the Tathāgata about this great matter. Listen carefully and reflect with the utmost care! I shall explain it for you in detail."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "So be it, World-Honored One! I wish to hear with joy."

At that time, the World-Honored One told Ānanda: "Right here in this southern continent of Jambudvīpa, which spans seven thousand yojanas around, broad in the north and narrow in the south, shaped like a carriage, with the people's faces likewise: suppose its entire extent were made into a single mass, whether of sugarcane groves, reed groves, bamboo groves, or rice paddies, sesame fields, and the like, packed full without any gap. Suppose further that throughout Jambudvīpa there were stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, and pratyekabuddhas, packed full with no gap, like the sugarcane and the rest. If one household were to serve and make offerings to all those noble ones with reverence for the span of a lifetime, presenting them with the finest robes, food and drink, bedding, medicine, and all necessities; and after the parinirvāṇa of each one, were to cremate the body according to the Dharma, collect the relics, and build stūpas, lofty, spacious, and splendidly adorned, offering ointment-incense, powder-incense, burnt incense, garlands, fine banners, jeweled canopies, jeweled streamers, music, and radiant lamps in praise and offering: what do you think? By this cause and condition, would the merit produced be great or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very great, World-Honored One! Very great, Sugata!"

At that time, the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside this southern continent of Jambudvīpa. In the eastern continent of Videha, which spans eight thousand yojanas, shaped like a half-moon with the people's faces likewise: suppose its entire extent were made into a single mass, whether of sugarcane groves, reed groves, bamboo groves, or rice paddies, sesame fields, and the like, packed full without any gap. Suppose further that throughout eastern Videha there were stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, and pratyekabuddhas, packed full with no gap, like the sugarcane and the rest. If one household were to serve and make offerings to all those noble ones with reverence for the span of a lifetime, presenting them with the finest robes, food and drink, bedding, medicine, and all necessities; and after the parinirvāṇa of each one, were to cremate the body according to the Dharma, collect the relics, and build stūpas, lofty, spacious, and splendidly adorned, offering ointment-incense, powder-incense, burnt incense, garlands, fine banners, jeweled canopies, jeweled streamers, music, and radiant lamps in praise and offering: what do you think? By this cause and condition, would the merit produced be great or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very great, World-Honored One! Very great, Sugata!"

At that time, the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside the eastern continent of Videha. In the western continent of Godānīya, which spans nine thousand yojanas, shaped like the disk of the sun with the people's faces likewise: suppose its entire extent were made into a single mass, whether of sugarcane groves, reed groves, bamboo groves, or rice paddies, sesame fields, and the like, packed full without any gap. Suppose further that throughout western Godānīya there were stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, and pratyekabuddhas, packed full with no gap, like the sugarcane and the rest. If one household were to serve and make offerings to all those noble ones with reverence for the span of a lifetime, presenting them with the finest robes, food and drink, bedding, medicine, and all necessities; and after the parinirvāṇa of each one, were to cremate the body according to the Dharma, collect the relics, and build stūpas, lofty, spacious, and splendidly adorned, offering ointment-incense, powder-incense, burnt incense, garlands, fine banners, jeweled canopies, jeweled streamers, music, and radiant lamps in praise and offering: what do you think? By this cause and condition, would the merit produced be great or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very great, World-Honored One! Very great, Sugata!"

At that time, the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside the western continent of Godānīya. In the northern continent of Uttarakuru, which spans ten thousand yojanas, square in shape with the people's faces likewise: suppose its entire extent were made into a single mass, whether of sugarcane groves, reed groves, bamboo groves, or rice paddies, sesame fields, and the like, packed full without any gap. Suppose further that throughout northern Uttarakuru there were stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, and pratyekabuddhas, packed full with no gap, like the sugarcane and the rest. If one household were to serve and make offerings to all those noble ones with reverence for the span of a lifetime, presenting them with the finest robes, food and drink, bedding, medicine, and all necessities; and after the parinirvāṇa of each one, were to cremate the body according to the Dharma, collect the relics, and build stūpas, lofty, spacious, and splendidly adorned, offering ointment-incense, powder-incense, burnt incense, garlands, fine banners, jeweled canopies, jeweled streamers, music, and radiant lamps in praise and offering: what do you think? By this cause and condition, would the merit produced be great or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very great, World-Honored One! Very great, Sugata!"

At that time, the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside the northern continent of Uttarakuru. The great terrace of Śakra, Lord of the Devas, rests upon eighty-four thousand wondrous jeweled pillars. Each pillar is draped with nets of various kinds of heavenly blue lapis lazuli, wondrous gems, and pearls, with gold sand spread upon the ground, sprinkled with fragrant water. Around this terrace are eighty-four thousand wondrous jeweled windows. Each window is hung with curtains of various kinds of heavenly blue lapis lazuli, wondrous gems, and pearls, with gold sand spread below, sprinkled with fragrant water. This terrace also has eighty-four thousand wondrous jeweled battlements. Each battlement is covered with nets of various kinds of heavenly blue lapis lazuli, wondrous gems, and pearls, with gold sand spread below, sprinkled with fragrant water. Suppose good men or good women were to build a splendid multi-storied mansion, lofty, spacious, and magnificent, resembling the wondrous jeweled terrace of Śakra, and offer it to the Saṃgha of the four directions and its great and virtuous assembly. What do you think? Would such good men or good women, by this cause and condition, produce great merit or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very great, World-Honored One! Very great, Sugata!"

At that time, the World-Honored One told Ānanda: "Set aside the four continents and Śakra's great jeweled terrace. Suppose good men or good women could build a hundred thousand koṭis of multi-storied mansions, all lofty, spacious, and magnificent, like Śakra's wondrous jeweled terrace, and offer them all to the Saṃgha of the four directions and its great and virtuous assembly. And suppose other good men or good women, after the parinirvāṇa of all the Tathāgatas, were to build a stūpa as small as an āmalaka fruit, place within it relics of the Buddha the size of a mustard seed, erect a finial the size of a great needle, and mount upon it a parasol the size of a small jujube leaf, or make a Buddha image as small as a grain of barley: comparing the first merit with this merit, the first would not reach even a hundredth part, nor a thousandth, nor a hundred-thousandth, nor any fraction by counting, calculation, reckoning, or analogy, not even an upaniṣad part. Why? Ānanda! Although the first merit is great in quantity, it falls short because the Tathāgatas, throughout three great asaṃkhyeya kalpas, have cultivated and accumulated immeasurable supreme morality, concentration, and wisdom, as well as liberation and the knowledge and vision of liberation, all brought to perfect completion; because the Tathāgatas' immeasurable loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity, and their spiritual powers of teaching and transforming beings in the six destinies of the ten directions, are all brought to perfect completion; and because the Tathāgatas' immeasurable generosity, morality, patience, vigor, meditation, wisdom, and all other merits are all brought to perfect completion. Therefore you should know that the merit of making a Buddha image or building a stūpa is inconceivable and beyond all comparison."

At that time, the World-Honored One told Ānanda: "You should reverently uphold this dharma-gate."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "I have reverently upheld it. But may I ask: what is the name of this dharma-gate that has been taught? How should I uphold it?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This dharma-gate is called 'The Extremely Rare.' It enables sentient beings to plant all pure and wholesome dharmas. By this name you should uphold it. Ānanda, you should know that from long ago I have frequently taught and proclaimed this dharma-gate to devas, nāgas, yakṣas, and others, as well as to humans and non-humans. Why? Because the World-Honored One wishes to enable all such beings to plant wholesome roots with correct reverence toward the Tathāgata, so that through the long night they may obtain benefit and happiness, and swiftly attain the unsurpassed awakening of a Buddha."

When the Bhagavān had spoken this sūtra, the Venerable Ānanda, the great assembly of bhikṣus, the innumerable bodhisattvas, and all the devas, humans, asuras, and others, having heard what the Buddha said, were all filled with great joy, faithfully received it, and practiced it.
