# Record of Dunhuang

## *Dūnhuáng Lù* (燉煌錄)

Taishō 2091

Author unknown. A geographical record.

*Note: This is a local geographical account of the Dunhuang region, likely dating from the late Tang or early Song period. It describes sites, legends, and customs associated with the Dunhuang area, including the Mogao Caves, Mingsha Mountain (the "Singing Sands"), and various other landmarks.*

The city of Xiàogǔ (*Xiàogǔ*, 効穀) was originally a fishing marsh. In the time of Emperor Xiào of the Han, Cuī Bùyì (*Cuī Bùyì*, 崔不意) taught the people to cultivate the fields, and they obtained grain. It was thus named. Later it became a county.

The Spring of the Èrshī General (*Èrshī Quán*, 貳師泉) lies three stages east of the Sand City (*Shāchéng*, 沙城). In Han times, when Lǐ Guǎnglì (*Lǐ Guǎnglì*, 李廣利) was marching his army and the troops were parched with thirst, he prayed to the mountain spirit and thrust his sword into the mountain. Water then gushed forth, flowing westward for several tens of *lǐ* to the Huángzhāng Marsh (*Huángzhāng Bó*, 黃章泊). Later there was a general who, desperately thirsty, drank water beside the spring and died there. The water then ceased to flow and only reached the level ground. From then on, if the people are many, the water is plentiful; if few, the water is scant. If a large army comes to drink, the water surges forth mightily. It is so to this day. The temple of the Èrshī General stands by the roadside, long in disrepair, with only a pile of stones remaining. It is a place where camel and horse drivers and travelers pray for blessings.

Next, heading east into the territory of Guāzhōu (*Guāzhōu*, 瓜州): south of the prefecture are the Mògāo Caves (*Mògāo Kū*, 莫高窟), twenty-five *lǐ* from the prefecture. The way passes through a stone desert, following the mountain slope to reach them. At the bottom of a steep valley, to the east are the Three Dangers Mountains (*Sānwēi Shān*, 三危山), and to the west is Míngshā Mountain (*Míngshā Shān*, 鳴沙山, "Singing Sands Mountain"). In between flows a stream running from south, called the Dàng Spring (*Dàng Quán*, 宕泉). Ancient monasteries and monks' quarters are extremely numerous. There is also a great bell. At both the southern and northern ends of the valley are Heavenly King halls and spirit shrines, with wall paintings depicting the Tibetan *tsenpo* and his retinue. On the western cliff of the mountain, for two *lǐ* from south to north, all is carved: large, lofty caves in the sandstone, with sculpted and painted buddha images. Each cave cost upward of a million in taxes to construct. In front are multi-story pavilions and towers. There is a hall with a great image one hundred and sixty feet tall. The small niches are countless. All have open balustrades connecting them, forming a continuous path for pilgrimage and viewing.

Further south on the mountain is a place where Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva (*Guānyīn Púsà*, 觀音菩薩) once appeared in assembly. When people of the prefecture go there, they always walk both ways on foot. Such is their reverence.

Míngshā Mountain lies ten *lǐ* from the prefecture. The mountain extends eighty *lǐ* from east to west and forty *lǐ* from south to north. At its highest it reaches five hundred feet, all composed entirely of heaped sand. The mountain is wondrous: its peaks appear as if carved with a blade. Among them are wells that the sand cannot cover over. In the height of summer the mountain makes sounds by itself. When people and horses tread upon it, the sound reverberates for tens of *lǐ*. By custom, on the fifth day of the fifth month, the men and women of the city all climb to the high peak and then slide down together at once. The sound of the sand roars like thunder. By dawn, the steep cliffs appear as before. Since ancient times it has been called Míngshā, the "Singing Sands," and a spirit shrine is maintained there.

Nearby to the south is the Sweet Spring (*Gānquán*, 甘泉). It flows south from the sand mountains. Its upper source issues from the great Snow Mountains (*Dàxuě Shān*, 大雪山). At the border of Shòuchāng County (*Shòuchāng Xiàn*, 壽昌縣) to the southwest, it enters Dunhuang. Because of its irrigating benefit, it is commonly called the Sweet Spring.

Jīn'ān Mountain (*Jīn'ān Shān*, 金鞍山) lies to the southwest of the sand mountains. Throughout the summer it is always covered in snow. In the mountains there is a spirit shrine, very efficacious. People dare not approach it. Each year the local lord offers worship from a distance and presents fine horses, driving them into the mountains. If anyone draws too near, thunder, lightning, wind, and hail strike at once.

To the southwest of the prefecture is the Temple of King Lǐ (*Lǐ Xiānwáng Miào*, 李先王廟), the ancestral temple of the former rulers of the Western Liáng (*Xīliáng*, 西涼). In the Qiánfēng (*Qiánfēng*, 乾封) era [666–668 CE], an auspicious stone was found beside the temple, its color being jade-green with ancient red characters reading: "The divination says thirty generations, the divination says seven hundred years." People today call it the Lǐ Temple.

To the west of the prefecture is Yángguān (*Yángguān*, 陽關, "Yang Pass"), which is the old Jade Gate Pass (*Yùmén Guān*, 玉門關). When the Shāzhōu (*Shāzhōu*, 沙州) prefect Yáng Míng (*Yáng Míng*, 陽明) was summoned by imperial edict but refused to comply and fled out through this pass, people afterward called it Yáng Pass. It connects to the city of Shànshàn (*Shànshàn*, 鄯善). The terrain is treacherous and barren, lacking water and grass, impassable to travelers. The pass was later moved to the east of the prefecture.

Eighty-five *lǐ* west of the city, there is the Jade Maiden Spring (*Yùnǚ Quán*, 玉女泉). Traditions about it are rather doubtful. Each year, the prefecture would select one boy and one girl as sacrifices to the pool spirit. If this was done, the year would be one of good harvest; otherwise, the crops were damaged. Though parents grieved bitterly at parting from their children, as the children were summoned by the spirit, they took each other's hands joyfully and disappeared into the dragon's abode.

When Prefect Zhāng Xiàosōng (*Zhāng Xiàosōng*, 張孝嵩) first arrived at his post, he inquired and the people informed him. The prefect angrily said: "How can there be stream demons harming my people?" He then set up an altar and prepared offerings beside the spring, saying: "I wish to see your true form. I desire to make offerings in person." The spirit then transformed into a dragon and emerged from the water. The prefect shot it through the throat with an arrow, drew his sword, and cut off its head. He personally went to the capital to present it. Emperor Xuánzōng (*Xuánzōng*, 玄宗) praised him repeatedly and bestowed the dragon's tongue upon him. By imperial edict it was named "Dragon Tongue." The Zhāng family's deed is recorded in the official histories.

One *lǐ* to the northwest of the prefectural city there is a monastery. Ancient trees cast dense shade. Within is a small fortress, atop which are covered walkways and halls, small but complete. Previously, the Assistant Prefect of Shā, Zhāng Qiú (*Zhāng Qiú*, 張球), already advanced in years, lodged there in retirement. Though not broadly learned, he was most diligent. As the years of turmoil had been many and students were few, he gathered the younger generation to propagate the great Way. Heaven did not forsake the remnant, and the people received his beneficence.

Shígāo Mountain (*Shígāo Shān*, 石膏山) lies two hundred and fifty-six *lǐ* north of the prefecture. Gypite exudes from the stones of Wūshān Fēng Mountain (*Wūshān Fēngshān*, 烏山烽山). In the nineteenth year of Kāihuáng (*Kāihuáng*, 開皇) [599 CE], Wūshān turned white. Upon investigation, this proved true, and Daoist priests Huángfǔ Décóng (*Huángfǔ Décóng*, 皇甫德琮) and six others were dispatched to perform rites. Since then, the mountain appears like a snowy peak when viewed from afar.

Hécāng City (*Hécāng Chéng*, 河倉城) lies two hundred and thirty *lǐ* northwest of the prefecture. In ancient times, military provisions were stored there. The Long Wall (*Chángchéng*, 長城) is north of the prefecture. The wall extends sixty-three *lǐ*, heading due west into the desert. It was established during the Former Han dynasty. To the north it enters the territory of Yīzhōu (*Yīzhōu*, 伊州).
