# 佛說佛地經
## Buddhabhūmi Sūtra (Sūtra on the Buddha Stage)

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0680

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavat was dwelling in the supreme, radiant, seven-jeweled adornment, emitting great radiance that universally illuminated all boundless worlds. Marvelously adorned on every side in immeasurable directions, its circumference without limit and its extent beyond reckoning, it transcended the domain traversed by the three realms, arose from transcendent wholesome roots, was most supremely sovereign, and had pure consciousness as its nature. It was the capital of the Tathāgata, where the great bodhisattvas gathered like clouds. Countless devas, nāgas, humans, and non-humans constantly attended. Sustained by the vast joy of the dharma's flavor, it accomplished all benefits for all sentient beings, extinguished the tangled defilements of all afflictions, and was far removed from all māras, surpassing all adornments. The Tathāgata's adornment was the place where it abided. Great mindfulness and wisdom were its path of practice, and great śamatha and subtle vipaśyanā were its vehicle. Great emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness in liberation were its gates of entry. It was adorned by immeasurable merits and established by the great jeweled flower-king.

In this great palace, the Bhagavat abided in the most supremely pure awareness, the non-dual present activity inclining toward the signless dharma, dwelling in the Buddha's abode. Having attained the equality-nature of all buddhas and reached the place of non-obstruction, established in the irreversible dharma, his activity was unimpeded. His accomplishment was inconceivable. He traveled within the equal dharma-nature of the three times. His body pervaded all worlds. Toward all dharmas his wisdom was free from doubt. In all practices he had accomplished the great awakening. Regarding the wisdom of all dharmas he had no uncertainty. Whatever body he manifested was beyond discrimination. He possessed the very wisdom that all bodhisattvas seek. Having attained the non-dual abiding of the buddha and reached the victorious far shore, without any mutual interference, the Tathāgata's liberation and marvelous wisdom were brought to their ultimate perfection. He had realized the equality of the buddha-stage without center or limit, reaching to the utmost extent of the dharma-realm, exhausting the nature of space and extending to the limit of the future.

Also present were immeasurable great assemblies of śrāvakas, all well tamed and all children of the Buddha. Their minds were well liberated, their wisdom well liberated, and their moral discipline well purified. They sought the joy of the dharma, were learned and retentive in hearing. Their accumulated learning was well reflected upon; what they explained was well explained; what they accomplished was well accomplished. Their wisdom was quick, swift, keen, penetrating, superbly discerning, great, vast, and unequaled. Endowed with the jewel of wisdom, they had perfected the three knowledges, had attained the foremost happiness of realization in this present life, were great fields of pure merit, and were tranquil in deportment. Of great patience and gentle harmony, they had accomplished what is without diminishment and had well fulfilled the Tathāgata's sacred teaching.

Also present were immeasurable bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who had come together from various buddha-lands. All abided in the great vehicle and traveled within the dharma of the great vehicle. Toward all sentient beings their minds were equal. They had left behind all discrimination, non-discrimination, and various discriminations. They had vanquished all the hosts of Māra. Free from all the bondage and discriminations of the śrāvaka and pratyekabuddha vehicles, sustained by the vast joy of the dharma's flavor, they had transcended the five fears. They had conclusively entered the stage of non-retrogression. In the domain that is presently before them, they brought rest to all the suffering and distress that oppresses sentient beings. The bodhisattva Mañjuśrī was the foremost among them.

At that time the World-Honored One addressed the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī: "Mañjuśrī, you should know: there are five dharmas that encompass the great stage of awakening. What are the five? They are the pure dharma-realm, the great mirror wisdom, the wisdom of equality of nature, the wisdom of marvelous discernment, and the wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done."

"Mañjuśrī, you should know: the pure dharma-realm is like empty space. Though it pervades the various kinds of characteristics among all forms, it cannot be said to have various characteristics; its essence is only a single taste. Likewise, the Tathāgata's pure dharma-realm, though it pervades all the various kinds of characteristics in the domain of what is to be known, cannot be said to have various characteristics; its essence is only a single taste.

"Furthermore, just as empty space pervades all forms without separating from them, yet is not defiled by the faults of those forms: likewise the Tathāgata's pure dharma-realm, though it pervades the mind-nature of all sentient beings and does not separate from them because of their true reality, is not defiled by their faults.

"Furthermore, just as empty space contains all actions of body, speech, and mind, yet empty space itself has no arising or action: likewise the Tathāgata's pure dharma-realm contains all the works of wisdom-transformation that benefit sentient beings, yet the pure dharma-realm itself has no arising or action.

"Furthermore, just as various forms appear to arise and appear to cease within empty space, yet empty space itself neither arises nor ceases: likewise within the Tathāgata's pure dharma-realm, the wisdom-transformations that benefit sentient beings appear to arise and appear to cease, yet the pure dharma-realm neither arises nor ceases.

"Furthermore, just as various forms appear to increase and appear to decrease within empty space, yet empty space itself neither increases nor decreases: likewise within the Tathāgata's pure dharma-realm, the display of the Tathāgata's amṛta sacred teaching increases and decreases, yet the pure dharma-realm neither increases nor decreases.

"Furthermore, just as the forms of the ten directions within empty space are without limit, because the realm of space is without limit, yet empty space itself neither goes nor comes, neither moves nor turns: likewise within the Tathāgata's pure dharma-realm, the establishment of benefits and happiness for all sentient beings in all worlds of the ten directions, with various functions without limit, because the pure dharma-realm is without limit, yet the pure dharma-realm itself neither goes nor comes, neither moves nor turns.

"Furthermore, just as the great trichiliocosm within empty space appears to be destroyed and appears to be reconstituted, yet the realm of space is neither destroyed nor reconstituted: likewise within the Tathāgata's pure dharma-realm, immeasurable forms appear as the attainment of perfect awakening, or appear as the entry into mahāparinirvāṇa, yet the pure dharma-realm neither attains perfect awakening nor enters nirvāṇa.

"Furthermore, just as, depending on empty space, various forms can be found to decay, rot, burn, dry, and undergo transformation, yet the realm of space is not transformed by those things, nor is it wearied: likewise, depending on the Tathāgata's pure realm, within the realm of sentient beings, violations of the various trainings of body, speech, and mind can be found, yet the pure dharma-realm is not transformed by those things, nor is it wearied.

"Furthermore, just as, depending on empty space, the great earth, great mountains, radiance, water, fire, Śakra and his retinue, and even the sun and moon can variously be found, yet the realm of space does not have the characteristics of any of those things: likewise, depending on the Tathāgata's pure realm, the aggregates of moral discipline, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and the knowledge and vision of liberation can be found, yet the pure dharma-realm does not have the characteristics of any of those things.

"Furthermore, just as within empty space various causes and conditions successively give rise to the great trichiliocosm and immeasurable worlds with their recurring cycles, yet the realm of space has no arising or action: likewise within the Tathāgata's pure dharma-realm, assemblies of buddhas endowed with immeasurable marks and their recurring cycles can be found, yet the pure dharma-realm has no arising or action.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! The great mirror wisdom is like a round mirror upon which various reflections appear. Likewise, depending on the Tathāgata's wisdom-mirror, the reflections of all sense-bases, objects, and consciousnesses appear. The reason a round mirror alone is used as the analogy is that you should know the round mirror and the Tathāgata's wisdom-mirror are equal. Therefore the wisdom-mirror is called the great mirror wisdom.

"Just as a great round mirror is suspended by one of fortunate merit in a high and supreme place, without any shaking, and countless beings coming and going observe in it their own virtues and faults, wishing to preserve the virtues and discard the faults: likewise the Tathāgata suspends the round mirror wisdom in the place of the pure dharma-realm. Because it is without interruption, it is without shaking. Wishing to enable immeasurable, countless sentient beings to observe what is defiled and what is pure, in order to embrace the pure and discard the defiled.

"Furthermore, just as a round mirror, supremely well polished, is reflective, pure, stainless, and shining with pervasive radiance: likewise the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom, because it has forever departed from all the stains of affliction-obstructions and knowledge-obstructions upon the buddha's wisdom, is supremely well polished. Because it is upheld by the concentration that serves as its foundation, it is reflective, pure, and stainless. Because it accomplishes the work of benefiting and bringing happiness to sentient beings, it shines with pervasive radiance.

"Furthermore, just as a round mirror, depending on conditions and an original object, gives rise to various reflections and appearances: likewise the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom, at all times and depending on various conditions, gives rise to various wisdom-reflections and appearances.

"Just as upon a round mirror, many and various reflections arise, yet the round mirror itself has no reflections, and the round mirror has no movement or action: likewise upon the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom, many and various wisdom-reflections arise, yet upon the mirror wisdom itself there are no wisdom-reflections, and this wisdom-mirror has no movement or action.

"Furthermore, just as a round mirror is neither merged with nor separated from the various reflections, because they do not accumulate in it, yet they appear through its conditions: likewise the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom is neither merged with nor separated from the various wisdom-reflections, because they do not accumulate in it, and because they are not lost from it.

"Furthermore, just as a round mirror is polished all around its surface and serves everywhere as the dependent condition for the arising of all reflections: likewise the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom, well polished through uninterrupted immeasurable practices, serves as the dependent condition for the arising of all wisdom-reflections, namely the wisdom-reflections of the śrāvaka vehicle, the wisdom-reflections of the pratyekabuddha vehicle, and the wisdom-reflections of the supreme great vehicle. This is to enable the people of the śrāvaka vehicle to attain liberation through the śrāvaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha people to attain liberation through the pratyekabuddha vehicle, and the people of the great vehicle to attain liberation through the supreme vehicle.

"Just as within a round mirror, great reflections can be found, such as the reflections of the great earth, great mountains, great trees, great palaces, and halls, yet the round mirror is not equal to their size: likewise upon the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom, wisdom-reflections from the Stage of Joy up to the buddha-stage can be found, and the wisdom-reflections of all worldly and transcendent dharmas can be found, yet the mirror wisdom is not of those proportions.

"Furthermore, just as a round mirror does not serve as a condition for reflections to arise in things that obstruct it: likewise the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom does not serve as a condition for wisdom-reflections to arise in sentient beings who are in the company of bad friends and who hear wrong teachings, because they are not suitable vessels.

"Furthermore, just as a round mirror does not serve as a condition for reflections to arise in dark objects: likewise the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom does not serve as a condition for wisdom-reflections to arise in sentient beings who delight in evil and are covered in ignorant darkness, because they are not suitable vessels.

"Furthermore, just as a round mirror does not serve as a condition for reflections to arise in objects that are far away: likewise the Tathāgata's great mirror wisdom does not serve as a condition for wisdom-reflections to arise in sentient beings who dwell in impurity, who have committed karma leading to destitution, and who lack faith, because they are not suitable vessels.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! The wisdom of equality of nature is brought to fulfillment through ten aspects. It is brought to fulfillment through realizing the equal dharma-nature of supreme delight and love in all the marks. It is brought to fulfillment through realizing the equal dharma-nature of the dependent arising of all experience. It is brought to fulfillment through realizing the equal dharma-nature of being free from the characteristics of difference and non-characteristics. It is brought to fulfillment through the equal dharma-nature of the great loving-kindness that broadly succors. It is brought to fulfillment through the equal dharma-nature of great compassion that expects nothing in return. It is brought to fulfillment through the equal dharma-nature of manifesting in accordance with what sentient beings delight in. It is brought to fulfillment through the equal dharma-nature by which all sentient beings reverently accept what is taught. It is brought to fulfillment through the equal dharma-nature by which the worldly and the tranquil are both of a single taste. It is brought to fulfillment through the equal dharma-nature by which worldly dharmas, whether of suffering or happiness, are of a single taste. It is brought to fulfillment through the equal dharma-nature of having cultivated and fully perfected immeasurable merits.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! The wisdom of marvelous discernment is like the world that sustains the realm of sentient beings. Likewise, the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment upholds all the gates of dhāraṇī and samādhi, and the marvelous dharma of the buddhas' unhindered eloquent exposition.

"Furthermore, just as the world serves as the condition for the simultaneous arising of all sentient beings' immeasurable and various kinds of consciousness: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment serves as the condition for the simultaneous arising of unhindered marvelous wisdom regarding all that is to be known, with immeasurable and various kinds of consciousness.

"Furthermore, just as the world is adorned with various delightful parks, groves, and ponds, exceedingly pleasing: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment is adorned with various delightful pāramitās, factors of awakening, ten powers, fearlessnesses, and distinctive buddha-dharmas, exceedingly pleasing.

"Furthermore, just as the world is magnificently arrayed with continents, sun and moon, the Cāturmahārājika heaven, the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, the Yāma heaven, the Tuṣita heaven, the Nirmāṇarati heaven, the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven, the Brahmā-body heaven, and the rest: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment is magnificently arrayed with the discernment of the causes and fruits of worldly and transcendent prosperity and decline, and the full realization of the śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, and bodhisattva paths, without remainder.

"Furthermore, just as the world serves for the vast enjoyment of sentient beings: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment manifests all assemblies of buddhas, raining the great rain of dharma, so that sentient beings may enjoy the great happiness of the dharma.

"Just as the five destinies can be found in the world, namely hell, hungry ghosts, animals, humans, and devas: likewise upon the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment, the differentiated five destinies of boundless cause and effect are fully manifest.

"Just as the realms of desire, form, and formlessness can be found in the world: likewise upon the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment, the differentiated three realms of boundless cause and effect are fully manifest.

"Just as the great jeweled mountain kings such as Sumeru can be found in the world: likewise upon the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment, the vast and profound dharma-teachings drawn forth by the awesome spiritual power of the buddhas and bodhisattvas can be found.

"Just as the vast, deep, and unshakable great ocean can be found in the world: likewise upon the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment, the profound dharma-realm teachings that cannot be shaken by any celestial Māra, outsider, or heterodox theory can be found.

"Furthermore, just as the world is encircled by great and small ring-mountains: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of marvelous discernment is encircled by non-confusion regarding all specific and universal characteristics.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! The wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done is like sentient beings' diligent bodily actions, through which they pursue various things such as profit, farming, craftsmanship, and the rest. Likewise, the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs diligent bodily transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata manifests various kinds of craftsmanship and the like, vanquishing the arrogance of those proud of their skills. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' experiential bodily actions cause them to experience various objects such as forms and the rest: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs experiential bodily transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata goes to the various places where sentient beings are born, manifests birth among the same kind and occupies a position of eminence. Because of manifesting birth among the same kind, the Tathāgata gathers and subdues all beings of other kinds. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' receptive bodily actions cause them to receive the fruition of the good and evil karma they have done: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs receptive bodily transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata manifests the reception of the difficult practices of former deeds and former births. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' congratulatory verbal actions cause them to converse and console one another: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs congratulatory verbal transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata proclaims various dharmas in accordance with what beings delight in, with artful and marvelous words and meaning, so that even beings of little wisdom develop faith upon first hearing. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' expedient verbal actions cause them to instruct one another, devote themselves to their tasks, censure evil, praise good, and summon one another: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs expedient verbal transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata establishes the correct trainings, censures heedlessness, praises non-heedlessness, and establishes those who follow through faith and those who follow through dharma and the rest. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' articulate verbal actions cause them to explain and reveal to one another the meaning they do not understand, and to recite and discuss various treatises: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs articulate verbal transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata resolves the immeasurable doubts of sentient beings. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' decisive mental actions cause them to decide what should and should not be done: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs decisive mental transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata discerns the eighty-four thousand differentiated mental tendencies of sentient beings. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' creative mental actions cause them to create various kinds of actions that arise: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs creative mental transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata observes the conduct of sentient beings, what they practice and what they do not, what they attain and what they lose, and in order to cause them to embrace or discard, he creates the appropriate antidotes. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' initiating mental actions cause them to initiate their various undertakings: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs initiating mental transformation-actions. Through these, in order to explain the appropriate antidotes, the Tathāgata displays the names, sentences, and syllables that beings delight in. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation.

"Furthermore, just as sentient beings' receptive mental actions cause them to receive suffering and happiness: likewise the Tathāgata's wisdom of accomplishing what is to be done performs receptive mental transformation-actions. Through these, the Tathāgata, for the purpose of responding to questions with definitive answers, counter-questions, setting aside, or declarations, receives and comprehends the meaning of past, future, and present, as appropriate. Through this power of excellent skillful means, sentient beings are led to enter the sacred teaching, bringing them to maturity and liberation."

At that time the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Is it only the Tathāgata who receives and accords with the one-taste wisdom of activity in the pure dharma-realm, or can the bodhisattvas also do likewise?"

The Buddha addressed the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī: "They too can receive and accord with the one-taste wisdom of activity."

The bodhisattva Mañjuśrī again addressed the Buddha: "Which bodhisattvas receive and accord with the one-taste wisdom of activity?"

The Buddha addressed Mañjuśrī: "Bodhisattvas who have realized the patience of the non-arising of dharmas. Because those bodhisattvas, at the time of gaining patient understanding within the dharma of non-arising, counteract the two kinds of perception. Because they have eliminated the two perceptions of self and other, they attain an equanimous mind. From this point onward, in those bodhisattvas the differentiated perception of self and other no longer arises, and they receive and accord with the one-taste wisdom of activity."

The bodhisattva Mañjuśrī again addressed the Buddha: "May the Tathāgata please broadly explain by means of analogies, so that the bodhisattvas may awaken to this exceedingly profound meaning, and in accordance with the beings to be guided, broadly proclaim and disseminate it, so that sentient beings, having heard it, may swiftly awaken to the patience of the non-arising of dharmas."

The Buddha addressed Mañjuśrī: "It is like the gods of the Thirty-Three heaven who, before entering the Miscellaneous Grove, can never, with regard to any matter or experience, receive and enjoy things with a sense of non-self and non-mine. But upon entering the Miscellaneous Grove, they receive and enjoy without discrimination, as they wish. Because this Miscellaneous Grove has such virtue, it causes all the gods who enter it to receive and enjoy all celestial fruits, matters, and experiences without any deliberation, in unified enjoyment.

"Likewise, if a bodhisattva has not yet realized the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, that bodhisattva can never attain the equanimous mind and equanimous relinquishment, and is then no different from all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Because of having the two perceptions, such a bodhisattva cannot abide in the one-taste wisdom of activity received and accorded with. But if one has already realized the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, then because one has eliminated the two perceptions and attained the equanimous mind, one becomes different from the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Through the equanimous mind one can abide in relinquishment, receiving and according with the one-taste wisdom of activity.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! It is like various great and small streams that, before entering the great ocean, each have a separate basis, with different waters, little water, and water that increases and decreases. According to the function of each water, what it accomplishes differs. Only a small number of aquatic creatures depend on them for their lives. But upon entering the great ocean, there is no separate basis. The water is without differentiation, the water is without limit, the water neither increases nor decreases. Its function is one, and it broadly sustains the lives of aquatic creatures.

"Likewise, if a bodhisattva has not yet entered the Tathāgata's great ocean of the pure dharma-realm, each has a separate basis, with different wisdoms, little wisdom, and wisdom that increases and decreases. According to the function of each wisdom, what it accomplishes differs. Only a small portion of sentient beings' wholesome roots depends on it. But if one has already entered the Tathāgata's great ocean of the pure dharma-realm, there is no separate basis. Wisdom is without differentiation, wisdom is without limit, wisdom neither increases nor decreases. One receives and accords with the one-taste wisdom of activity, and immeasurable sentient beings' ripening of wholesome roots depends upon it."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> The true suchness of all dharmas,
> the pure characteristic of the two obstructions:
> the wisdom of dharma, and what it takes as its object,
> the sovereign and inexhaustible characteristic.
> The wisdom that pervades true suchness,
> cultivated and realized to perfect completion,
> establishing both kinds of benefit for sentient beings:
> the inexhaustible fruit of all the seeds.
> Body, speech, and mental transformation,
> and the skillful expedient activities of excellent transformation.
> Concentration and the gates of dhāraṇī,
> the boundless accomplishment of both.
> The intrinsic dharma-body, the enjoyment-body,
> and the transformation-body's differentiated turnings:
> such is the pure dharma-realm
> as all the buddhas have taught it.

When the Bhagavat had finished speaking this sūtra, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Mañjuśrī and the others, the great śrāvakas, and the devas, humans, asuras, and all the great assembly of the world, hearing what the Buddha taught, all greatly rejoiced, faithfully received it, and put it into practice.
