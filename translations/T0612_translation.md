# 身觀經
## Sūtra of Contemplation of the Body

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0612

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time the Buddha said to the bhikṣus: "This body has flesh, skin, marrow, blood, and muscle; it is full of feces and urine. Look at your own body: what is beautiful about it? It always has the nine openings, which are foul afflictions, constantly impure and constantly flowing. Is this not cause for shame? It is constantly in the company of enemies, leading you to old age and death. It is always accompanied by illness. How can you not detest it? The body will surely fall; it will surely decay. It will be cast down upon the ground, no longer useful, devoured by foxes and wolves. Seeing this, how can you not feel shame? Who speaks of craving desire? As the Buddha says: there is little pleasure and much fault. Observe this with your own mind. It is like a butcher's rack, like a butcher's chopping-block, a heap of bones. It is like a blazing fire, like a poison that painfully drags one round. The foolish take delight in it, delighting without knowing themselves. How can they not fear the net? Craving desire is delusion. Wealth, money, grain, gold, silver, oxen, horses, servants: people seek these for the sake of life. But life hangs on a single breath. From the very beginning, life itself is brief. At most, one lives a hundred-odd years, and that too is a painful gathering. Observing this, who finds any of it worthwhile?

"As time passes, life gradually diminishes. Life is used up together with the days, like the swift water of a river, like the sun and moon running out. Life passes more swiftly than these. Once a human life has gone, it never returns; thus it cannot be recaptured. When a person dies and life departs, even if one had immense wealth, sought the rarest things in the world, and possessed everything, at the time of death, when the consequences arrive, there is no joy. There is nothing to be satisfied with, nothing to delight in, nothing to please oneself with at all. One can only do good oneself. The good one has done comes to fruition naturally. If you know that death is certain, what delight can there be?

"People may fall into craving desire. Even if one lives a long time, even if one is about to depart, death is certain. Why does the mind love and delight in these things? Why not think of quieting oneself, instead of utterly indulging the mind in pleasure? When a child has died, one weeps and cries, but not for more than ten days. After ten days, one gradually forgets. The love of wives and children is also like this! Relatives, family, and friends are all like this!

"Through toil and hardship one earns a livelihood and acquires possessions. One loves one's own body and life, adorning them beautifully. But when a person dies, everything is abandoned. The body lies stiff on the ground and descends into the earth. Only the shades depart, and according to one's conduct, one is reborn and takes form again. It is like a tree whose fruit has already appeared. Thus it is with a person's mind falling into existence. All the fruits and goods in the world, even if a single person obtained them all, would not be enough. If one obtains one portion, how could one ever be sated, since there is no limit? Even if one had the thirty-five pleasures, enjoying them all, what benefit would there be?

"People have already encountered suffering and are seeking punishment for themselves, yet their minds take this as good, thinking there is some benefit in it. They do not wish to accept stillness but instead seek a poisonous scorpion for their own body. Whether little or much, it is the same! Whether much or little, it is the same! An illness, whether great or small, is still painful. It is like a bone without flesh: a dog that gets it gnaws and is never satisfied. Craving desire is like a dog's habit: it is hard to obtain, and once obtained, one should greatly fear it. Such habits do not last long, and the person also falls into evil. It is like a person who sees a dream: upon waking, one cannot recover it. Craving desire is also like this, worse than a dream. In a dream there may be some pleasure, but it is like a dark scorpion, like a baited hook. It is like the fruit of a tree: the fruit is small but the taste is great. What is lost increases one's bonds and serves as the root of evil deeds. Those who practice the Way should never use it.

"Even a person in the heavens, who has forsaken pleasure, and the color-trees of heaven; who dwells in beautiful and splendid gardens; who has obtained heavenly maidens; having obtained all this, does not grow weary of the five pleasures of heaven. How then could one grow weary of worldly pleasures?

"Take the bones of two hundred days: a hundred and twenty segments bound by sinews; nine openings that constantly leak; sixty-three kinds of things; a hundred kinds of illness at their worst; flesh and blood mixed together; raw hide and skin; within, cold, heat, and wind; feces and urine; and a thousand worms, all arising from the body, with a thousand holes within. And worse: intimacy has already corrupted others. Impurities flow forth from it: mucus flows from the nose, drool and saliva flow from the mouth, sweat flows from under the arms, feces and urine flow from below. All of this flows forth from the body most foully. A dead person at a burial ground is truly loathsome in the extreme. Set aside this most loathsome place. All the impurities of the body are like this, an impure seed. From the very beginning it has been like gold-plating some other thing, adorned for the sake of clothing with fragrant powder, cosmetic oil, rouge, and indigo. The fool sees this and the mind is confused. It is like a painted vase, red like a pit covered with grass. What people embrace and love, they will afterward regret."

The bhikṣus heard the sūtra, knelt, bowed, and received it. Such is the teaching of the Way.
