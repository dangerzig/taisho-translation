# Brief Essay on the Meaning of the Pure Land of Bliss

## *Lüè Lùn Ānlè Jìngtǔ Yì*

Taishō 1957

Composed by Tánluán (*Tánluán*), Northern Wèi dynasty.

*Note: This is an important Pure Land treatise by the Chinese monk Tánluán (476-542), a key figure in the development of East Asian Pure Land Buddhism. Written in question-and-answer format, the essay addresses five major topics: (1) the ontological status of Sukhāvatī (the Pure Land of Bliss), arguing it transcends the three realms; (2) the twenty-nine kinds of adornment that constitute the Pure Land, drawn from Vasubandhu's Treatise on Rebirth (Wúliàngshòu Lùn); (3) the three grades of rebirth (upper, middle, lower) with their respective causal conditions; (4) the special category of "womb-birth" (tāishēng) or "borderland" (biāndì) rebirth for those who cultivate with doubt, living five hundred years in a jeweled palace without seeing the Three Treasures; and (5) a sustained philosophical discussion of the "ten continuous thoughts" (shí niàn xiāngxù) required for the lowest grade of rebirth, with six vivid similes demonstrating how brief but intense effort can overcome lifetimes of evil karma, culminating in the analogy of a wax seal that perishes as its imprint is formed.*

---

Question: "Within the three realms, to which realm does the Land of Bliss belong?"

Answer: "As the *Treatise* [Dazhìdù Lùn] says: 'Such a pure land is not included within the three realms.' Why? Because it is without desire, it is not the desire realm. Because its inhabitants dwell on the ground, it is not the form realm. Because it has form and color, it is not the formless realm.

"The sūtra says: 'When Amitābha Buddha was formerly practicing the bodhisattva path, he was a bhikṣu named Dharmākara. In the presence of the Buddha Lokeśvararāja, he inquired about the practices of all buddhas' pure lands. At that time, the Buddha described for him two hundred and ten billion buddha-fields, with the good and evil of their devas and humans, the refined and the coarse of their lands, all manifestly displayed before him. Thereupon Bodhisattva Dharmākara, in the Buddha's presence, made his great vows, selecting [the best features] from all those buddha-lands.' Over immeasurable asaṃkhyeya kalpas, in accordance with his vows, he practiced the pāramitās. When his myriad virtues were complete, he attained the supreme Way. [This land] was obtained through his distinctive karma; it is not [within] the three realms."

Question: "How many kinds of adornment does the Land of Bliss possess to be called a pure land?"

Answer: "If we rely on the sūtra and take its meaning, Bodhisattva Dharmākara's forty-eight vows are precisely the substance of this. One can understand them by examining the praises; I will not repeat the sequence here.

"If we follow the *Treatise on the Sūtra of Immeasurable Life* [Wúliàngshòu Lùn], two kinds of purity encompass twenty-nine kinds of adornment and accomplishment. The two kinds of purity are: (1) the purity of the receptacle-world [the environment] and (2) the purity of the sentient-being-world [the inhabitants].

### Purity of the Receptacle-World

"The purity of the receptacle-world has seventeen kinds of adornment: (1) The features of the land surpass the paths of the three realms. (2) That land is vast, its extent like space, without limit. (3) It arises from the roots of merit of the bodhisattva's correct path, great compassion, and transcendence of the world. (4) Pure radiance fully adorns it. (5) It possesses the nature of the foremost precious jewels and produces marvelous treasures. (6) Its pure radiance constantly illuminates the world. (7) Its jeweled objects are soft; touching them brings comfort and supreme bliss. (8) Thousands and tens of thousands of jeweled flowers adorn its pools and marshes; jeweled halls, jeweled pavilions, and jeweled trees of every kind cast multicolored light and shadow throughout the world; immeasurable jeweled nets cover the sky; bells hang on all four sides, constantly emitting the sound of the Dharma. (9) In the sky, heavenly flowers, heavenly garments, and heavenly incense naturally rain down, universally pervading with adornment and fragrance. (10) The Buddha's wisdom-light illuminates and dispels the darkness of ignorance. (11) The Brahmā-voice awakens understanding and is heard far in the ten directions. (12) Amitābha Buddha, the supreme Dharma King, sustains [the land] by his wholesome power. (13) [Beings there] are born by transformation from the Tathāgata's pure flowers. (14) They delight in the taste of the Buddha-dharma and take dhyāna and samādhi as food. (15) They are forever free from all sufferings of body and mind, receiving uninterrupted bliss. (16) They do not even hear the names 'two vehicles,' 'woman,' or 'deficient faculties.' (17) Whatever beings desire, their hearts' wishes are fulfilled without exception. These seventeen constitute the purity of the receptacle-world.

### Purity of the Sentient-Being-World

"The purity of the sentient-being-world has twelve kinds of adornment: (1) An immeasurably great and precious jeweled lotus platform serves as the Buddha's seat. (2) Immeasurable marks and excellences and immeasurable radiance adorn the Buddha's body. (3) The Buddha's immeasurable eloquence teaches the Dharma in response to beings' capacities, perfectly pure and clear, making people delight in hearing; those who hear will certainly understand. His words are never spoken in vain. (4) The Buddha's true-suchness wisdom is like space, illuminating the general and specific characteristics of all dharmas, with a mind free from discrimination. (5) The assembly of devas and humans, unmoving, is vast and adorned: like Mount Sumeru reflecting the four great oceans, the Dharma King's marks are complete. (6) He has achieved the supreme fruit; nothing can equal it, let alone surpass it. (7) He is the great man among devas and humans, the tamer and guide. The great assembly reveres and surrounds him, like the lion king surrounded by lions. (8) By the Buddha's original-vow power, he adorns and sustains all merits. Those who encounter him are never without gain; he enables the swift fulfillment of the ocean of all merits. Bodhisattvas who have not yet realized the pure mind will ultimately realize the equal dharma-body; together with bodhisattvas of the pure mind and bodhisattvas of the higher stages, they will ultimately attain equal quiescence. (9) The bodhisattvas of the Land of Bliss, while their bodies do not move or waver, pervade the ten directions with various responsive transformations, practicing authentically and constantly performing the Buddha's work. (10) These bodhisattvas' responsive transformation-bodies, at all times without before or after, in one mind and one thought emit great radiance that pervades all the worlds of the ten directions, teaching and transforming beings through various skillful means. Through their cultivated practice, they extinguish the sufferings of all beings. (11) These bodhisattvas, in all worlds without remainder, illuminate all the buddhas' great assemblies without remainder, making vast, immeasurable offerings and reverently praising the merits of all the tathāgatas. (12) These bodhisattvas, in all worlds of the ten directions where the Three Treasures are absent, sustain and adorn the great ocean of meritorious virtues of the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha, universally displaying them and causing understanding, and practicing authentically.

"These eight kinds of adornment of the Dharma King and these four kinds of adornment of the bodhisattvas constitute the purity of the sentient-being-world.

"The Land of Bliss possesses these twenty-nine kinds of adornment; therefore it is called 'the pure land.'"

## The Grades of Rebirth

Question: "How many grades of beings are born in the Land of Bliss, and what are the causal conditions?"

Answer: "In the *Sūtra of Immeasurable Life* [Wúliàngshòu Jīng], there are only three grades: upper, middle, and lower. In the *Contemplation Sūtra* [Wúliàngshòu Guān Jīng], each grade is further divided into upper, middle, and lower, making nine in three groups of three, for a total of nine grades. I shall now follow the *Sūtra of Immeasurable Life* as the basis and discuss three grades.

### Upper Grade

"Those born in the upper grade have five causal conditions: (1) renouncing the household, departing from desire, and becoming a śramaṇa; (2) generating the mind of supreme bodhi; (3) single-mindedly and exclusively recollecting Amitāyus Buddha; (4) cultivating various merits; and (5) vowing to be born in the Land of Bliss. With these conditions, at the moment of death, Amitāyus Buddha and the great assembly appear before that person, who immediately follows the Buddha and is born in the Land of Bliss, spontaneously transformed within a flower of the seven jewels, abiding in non-retrogression, with wisdom courageous and mighty, and supernatural powers at will.

### Middle Grade

"Those born in the middle grade have seven causal conditions: (1) generating the mind of supreme bodhi; (2) single-mindedly and exclusively recollecting Amitāyus Buddha; (3) cultivating goodness to whatever extent, upholding fasting and precepts; (4) erecting stūpas and images; (5) providing meals for śramaṇas; (6) hanging banners, lighting lamps, scattering flowers, and burning incense; and (7) transferring this merit with the vow to be born in the Land of Bliss. At the moment of death, Amitāyus Buddha manifests his transformation-body, with radiance and marks complete like the true Buddha, appearing with the great assembly before that person. The person immediately follows the transformation-buddha and is born in the Land of Bliss, abiding in non-retrogression. The merit and wisdom are next to those of the upper grade.

### Lower Grade

"Those born in the lower grade have three causal conditions: (1) even if unable to perform various merits, one should generate the mind of supreme bodhi; (2) with single-minded concentration, even up to ten thoughts, recollect Amitāyus Buddha; and (3) with an utterly sincere mind, vow to be born in the Land of Bliss. At the moment of death, one dreams of Amitāyus Buddha and likewise attains rebirth. The merit and wisdom are next to those of the middle grade.

### Womb-Birth and Borderland

"There is also another kind of rebirth in the Land of Bliss that does not fall within the three grades: namely, those who cultivate various merits with a doubting mind and vow to be born in the Land of Bliss, yet do not comprehend the Buddha's wisdom, the inconceivable wisdom, the indescribable wisdom, the vast Mahāyāna wisdom, or the unequaled, incomparable, supreme and victorious wisdom. They doubt and disbelieve these wisdoms. Nevertheless, they still believe in the consequences of good and evil, cultivate roots of goodness, and are born in the Land of Bliss.

"They are born in jeweled palaces in the Land of Bliss that are perhaps a hundred yojanas or five hundred yojanas [in extent]. In each, they enjoy various pleasures like those of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, and all arises naturally. Yet for five hundred years they never see the Buddha, never hear the sūtras and Dharma, and never see the bodhisattvas or the śrāvaka saints. In the Land of Bliss, this is called 'the borderland' (*biāndì*) or 'womb-birth' (*tāishēng*). 'Borderland' means that, during those five hundred years, they do not see or hear the Three Treasures, which is analogous to the difficulty of a borderland. Or it means that within the Land of Bliss, they dwell at its very edge. 'Womb-birth' is a metaphor: just as a person born from a womb, at the moment of initial birth, has not yet developed fully as a human being. 'Borderland' speaks of difficulty; 'womb-birth' speaks of darkness. Both names are borrowed analogies, not literal: it is not the 'borderland' of the eight difficulties, nor is it literal birth from a womb. How do we know? Because in the Land of Bliss, all are born by transformation, not by actual womb-birth; and after five hundred years they do come to see and hear the Three Treasures, so it is not the borderland of the eight difficulties."

Question: "Those born by 'womb-birth,' dwelling in the jeweled palaces, do they experience happiness? And what do they think about?"

Answer: "The sūtra uses a simile: 'Suppose a wheel-turning king's son offends the king. He is confined in the inner palace, bound with golden chains. All provisions and necessities are complete, just as for the king himself. Yet the prince, though having fine and wonderful entertainments and amusements, finds no joy in them. He thinks only of devising means to gain release and escape.'

"Those born by 'womb-birth' are just like this. Though they dwell in jeweled palaces with fine forms, fragrances, tastes, and textures, they find no joy in them. Their only suffering is that they do not see the Three Treasures and cannot make offerings or cultivate roots of goodness. They recognize their original transgression and deeply reproach themselves with regret, seeking to leave that place. Once they do, they attain their wish and join those born in the [regular] three grades. It seems that they recognize their fault and feel remorse only toward the end of the five hundred years."

Question: "Those who are born in the Land of Bliss with a doubting mind and are called 'womb-birth': how does their doubt arise?"

Answer: "The sūtra simply says 'doubt and disbelieve' without explaining the reason for the doubt. Examining the five propositions that are 'not comprehended,' I venture to discuss the antidotes.

"'Not comprehending the Buddha's wisdom' means being unable to have faith in and comprehend the Buddha's all-knowing wisdom. Because of not comprehending, doubt arises. This first statement generally identifies the object of doubt; the following four statements provide antidotes for each doubt.

### First Doubt

"Doubt has four aspects. First: doubting that merely recollecting Amitābha Buddha will not necessarily result in rebirth in the Land of Bliss. Why? The sūtra says: 'The path of karma is like a scale; the heavier side is pulled first.' How can it be that a person who, over one lifetime, or a hundred years, or ten years, or one month, has committed every evil, merely through ten continuous thoughts of the Buddha, can be reborn, enter the assembly of the rightly determined, and never retrogress, forever separated from the sufferings of the three evil paths? If so, what of the principle that 'the heavier side is pulled first'? Furthermore, since beginningless time, one has accumulated actions, all of conditioned and defiled dharmas, bound to the three realms. How can one, without severing the bonds and delusions of the three realms, merely through a brief period of recollecting Amitābha, escape the three realms? How do we account for the binding of karma?

"The antidote to this doubt is what is called 'inconceivable wisdom.'

"'Inconceivable wisdom' means: the Buddha's wisdom-power can make the little become much and the much become little; the near become far and the far become near; the light become heavy and the heavy become light; the long become short and the short become long. Such is the Buddha's wisdom: immeasurable, boundless, and inconceivable.

"Consider: a hundred men spending a hundred years gathering firewood, piled a thousand feet high. A fire the size of a bean can consume it in half a day. Can one really say that a hundred years' firewood pile cannot be consumed in half a day?

"Again: a crippled person boards another's boat. Carried by the wind in the sails, in one day he travels a thousand leagues. Can one really say that a crippled person cannot travel a thousand leagues in one day?

"Again: a lowly, poor person finds a single auspicious treasure and presents it to the sovereign. The sovereign, delighted with what he has received, bestows lavish rewards. In an instant, wealth and honor overflow. Can one really say that because someone else has labored dozens of years in official service with utmost toil, never even advancing or returning home, that the poor man's wealth and honor are impossible?

"Again: a feeble man, using his own strength, cannot even mount a donkey. But traveling in the retinue of a wheel-turning king, he can fly through space effortlessly and naturally. Can one really say that the feeble man who could not mount a donkey can certainly not fly through space?

"Again: a rope ten arm-spans around, which a thousand men cannot sever, can be cut in two in an instant by a boy wielding a sword. Can one really say that the strength of one small child cannot cut the rope?

"Again: when the zhèn bird [a poisonous bird] enters water, fish and mollusks all perish. When a rhinoceros horn touches mud, the dead all revive. Can one really say that once life is severed, there is no possibility of revival?

"Again: when the yellow crane called out to Zǐ'ān, Zǐ'ān came back to life. Can one really say that one who has lain a thousand years beneath a burial mound can certainly never be revived?

"All myriad dharmas have both self-power and other-power, self-gathering and other-gathering, opening and closing in a thousand myriad ways, immeasurable and boundless. How can one, with an obstructed consciousness, doubt the unobstructed Dharma?

"Furthermore, among the five kinds of inconceivable things, the Buddha-dharma is the most inconceivable. To take a hundred years of evil as heavy and doubt that ten thoughts of buddha-recollection, being light, cannot attain rebirth in the Land of Bliss and entry into the assembly of the rightly determined: this is simply not so.

### Second Doubt

"Second: doubting that the Buddha's wisdom is not utterly transcendent to human understanding. Why? All names arise from mutual dependence. Awakened wisdom arises from non-awakening. Thus, knowing one's direction arises from having been lost. If being lost could be absolutely ended (no more being lost), then one who is utterly lost would never understand. If being lost can be resolved, then it must be that the lost one understands; and one could also say that the understanding one can become lost. Lost-understanding and understanding-lost are like the back and front of the hand. One can say that brightness and darkness differ, but can one really claim transcendence?

"Because such doubt arises, one doubts and disbelieves the Buddha's wisdom. The antidote to this doubt is what is called 'indescribable wisdom.'

"'Indescribable wisdom' means: the Buddha's wisdom transcends all description; it is not relative and mutual. Why? If dharmas were existent, there should be a wisdom that knows them as existent. If dharmas were non-existent, there should be a wisdom that knows them as non-existent. Since all dharmas transcend both existence and non-existence, the Buddha, fused with all dharmas, has a wisdom that transcends mutual dependence. Your analogy of understanding and delusion is still within delusion; it does not constitute true understanding of delusion. It is like explaining a dream to someone within a dream: though one speaks of 'explaining the dream,' it is not yet 'not dreaming.'

"To apprehend the Buddha through knowledge is not to know the Buddha. To apprehend the Buddha through not-knowing is not to know the Buddha. To apprehend the Buddha through neither knowing nor not-knowing is also not to know the Buddha. To apprehend the Buddha through neither 'neither-knowing' nor 'neither not-knowing' is also not to know the Buddha. The Buddha's wisdom transcends these four propositions. For one who seeks it, the activity of the mind ceases; for one who tries to diminish it, the path of language is cut off.

"Therefore the *Treatise* [Dazhìdù Lùn] says: 'If a person sees prajñā, that person is bound. If a person does not see prajñā, that too is bondage. If a person sees prajñā, that person is liberated. If a person does not see prajñā, that too is liberation.' This verse says: one who does not transcend the four propositions is in bondage; one who transcends them is liberated. Your doubt that the Buddha's wisdom is not utterly transcendent to human understanding: this is simply not so.

### Third Doubt

"Third: doubting that the Buddha truly cannot save all sentient beings. Why? In past ages there have been immeasurable asaṃkhyeya Ganges-sands of buddhas. In the present, in the worlds of the ten directions, there are also immeasurable, boundless asaṃkhyeya Ganges-sands of buddhas. If the Buddha could truly save all sentient beings, the three realms should long ago have been emptied. A second buddha should not need to again generate the bodhi-mind, perfect a pure land, and receive beings. Yet in fact there is a second buddha who receives beings, and indeed there are buddhas of the three times and ten directions, immeasurably many, who receive beings. Therefore one knows: the Buddha truly cannot save all sentient beings.

"Because such doubt arises, one conceives of Amitābha Buddha as limited. The antidote to this doubt is what is called 'the vast wisdom of the Great Vehicle.'

"'The vast wisdom of the Great Vehicle' means: there is no dharma the Buddha does not know, no affliction he has not severed, no goodness he does not possess, no sentient being he does not save. The reason there are buddhas of the three times and ten directions has five meanings:

"(1) If there were no second buddha, and indeed no asaṃkhyeya Ganges-sands of buddhas, the Buddha could not save all sentient beings. Because he truly can save all, there are immeasurable buddhas of the ten directions; these immeasurable buddhas are themselves the sentient beings previously saved by the former buddha.

"(2) If one buddha saves all sentient beings to exhaustion, there should be no further buddhas. Why? Because there would be no 'awakening others.' On what basis then would one speak of buddhas of the three times? It is because of the meaning of 'awakening others' that every buddha is said to save all sentient beings.

"(3) The later buddha's ability to save is still the earlier buddha's ability. Why? Because the later buddha exists due to the former buddha. It is like the armor of an imperial succession: the later king inherits from the former. Therefore the later king's capability is the former king's capability.

"(4) Although the Buddha's power can save all sentient beings, there must be causal conditions. If a sentient being has no karmic connection with the former buddha, a later buddha is needed. Beings without such connections may pass by hundreds of thousands of millions of buddhas without ever hearing or seeing them. This is not because the Buddha's power is deficient. It is like the sun and moon circling the four continents, dispelling all darkness, yet the blind cannot see. This is not because the sun is not bright. Thunder shatters the ears, yet the deaf cannot hear. This is not because the sound is not vigorous. To awaken to the principle of conditions and causes: this is called 'Buddha.' If beings stubbornly violate the principle of conditions, it is not the Perfectly Awakened One's [fault]. Therefore sentient beings are immeasurable, and buddhas are also immeasurable. To challenge the Buddha by asking, 'Regardless of whether there are conditions or not, why does he not save all sentient beings to exhaustion?' is an unreasonable statement.

"(5) If sentient beings were exhaustible, the world would fall into the extreme of finitude. For this reason, there are immeasurable buddhas who save all sentient beings.

"Question: 'If sentient beings are inexhaustible, then the world would also fall into the extreme of infinity. Because it is infinite, the Buddha truly cannot save all sentient beings.'

"Answer: 'The world is neither finite nor infinite, and also transcends all four propositions. The Buddha causes sentient beings to leave these four propositions; this is called "saving." In reality, it is neither saving nor not-saving, neither exhausting nor not-exhausting. It is like dreaming of crossing a great ocean, encountering waves and perils. That person, terrified, cries out, and the sound reaches outside. Someone outside awakens them, and they are at ease, without worry. One was only crossing a dream; one was not crossing a river.'

"Question: 'If speaking of both "saving" and "not-saving" both fall into extreme views, why speak only of saving all sentient beings as the vast wisdom of the Great Vehicle, and not speak of "not-saving" as the vast wisdom of the Great Vehicle?'

"Answer: 'All sentient beings loathe suffering and seek happiness, fear bondage and seek liberation. Hearing of "saving," they turn toward it. Hearing of "not-saving," they do not understand the reason for not-saving and think the Buddha lacks great compassion; then they do not turn toward him. Not turning toward him, they sleep long in their interminable dream, with no means to end it. For such people, one mainly speaks of "saving" and does not speak of "not-saving." Furthermore, the *Sūtra of the Non-Practice of All Dharmas* [Zhūfǎ Wúxíng Jīng] also says: "The Buddha does not attain the Buddha-way, nor does he save sentient beings. Ordinary beings forcibly discriminate, creating 'a buddha who saves sentient beings.'" To say "save sentient beings" is the siddhānta of the antidote. To say "not save sentient beings" is the siddhānta of the supreme meaning. Both statements have their reasons and do not contradict each other.'

"Question: 'When the dream ceases, is that not "saving"? If the dreams of all sentient beings cease, would the world not be exhausted?'

"Answer: 'It is said that the dream is the world. If the dream ceases, there is no dreamer. If there is no dreamer, one also cannot speak of "one who saves." Thus knowing that the world is itself the transcendence of the world: though one saves immeasurable sentient beings, one does not fall into error.'

### Fourth Doubt

"Fourth: doubting that the Buddha has not attained all-knowing wisdom. Why? If he could exhaustively know all dharmas, all dharmas would fall into the extreme of finitude. If he cannot exhaustively know, then it is not all-knowing wisdom.

"The antidote to this doubt is what is called 'the unequaled, incomparable, supreme and victorious wisdom.'

"'Unequaled, incomparable, supreme and victorious wisdom': the wisdom of ordinary beings is false; the Buddha's wisdom is in accordance with reality. The false and the real are profoundly different, and in principle nothing can equal it; hence 'unequaled.' Śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, when they wish to know something, enter samādhi to know it; upon leaving samādhi, they do not know. Moreover, their knowledge is limited. The Buddha has attained the samādhi of true suchness, constantly abiding in deep concentration, exhaustively knowing and illuminating all dharmas: the two and the non-dual. This profound dharma has no peer; hence 'incomparable.' Bodhisattvas of the eighth stage and above, though they have attained the reward-born samādhi and use it without entry or exit, still have subtle latent impressions that prevent the samādhi from being utterly clear and pure. Compared to the Buddha's wisdom, theirs is still 'with something above.' The Buddha's wisdom and his severance [of afflictions] are complete. He illuminates in accordance with the Dharma; because the Dharma is immeasurable, his illumination is also immeasurable. It is like a case and its lid: when the case is large, the lid is also large. Hence 'supreme.'

"These three phrases can also establish each other in sequence: because the Buddha's wisdom has nothing equal to it, it is therefore 'incomparable'; because it is incomparable, it is 'supreme and victorious.' Or: because it is supreme and victorious, it is 'unequaled'; because it is unequaled, it is 'incomparable.' But if 'unequaled' alone were sufficient, why the need for the two additional phrases? Consider: the wisdom of a stream-enterer is not equal to that of an arhat, yet it is of the same class. From the first bhūmi through the tenth, the wisdoms are also unequal, yet they are not without kinship. Why? Because they are not supreme.

"Your difficulty, that knowing makes things finite, and your doubt that the Buddha is not all-knowing: this is simply not so."

## The Ten Continuous Thoughts

Question: "In the lower grade, the text says 'ten continuous thoughts assure rebirth.' What does 'ten continuous thoughts' mean?"

Answer: "Suppose a person is in an open, desolate place and encounters vengeful bandits. They draw their swords with fierce courage and come straight at him to kill. The person flees with all his strength. He sees a river ahead; if he can cross it, he can save his life. At that moment, his only thought is the means of crossing the river: 'When I reach the bank, shall I cross with my clothes on, or shall I take them off? If I keep them on, I fear I cannot get across. If I take them off, I fear there is no time.' He has only this thought and no other concern. One such thought directed solely at crossing the river: this is one thought. In this way, a mind without distraction is called 'ten continuous thoughts.'

"The practitioner is just like this. Recollecting Amitābha Buddha as that person thinks of crossing the river, through ten thoughts: whether recollecting the Buddha's name, the Buddha's marks and excellences, the Buddha's radiance, the Buddha's supernatural powers, the Buddha's merits, the Buddha's wisdom, or the Buddha's original vows. With no other thought interposed, mind following mind in succession, up to ten thoughts: this is called 'ten continuous thoughts.'

"At first glance, ten continuous thoughts seem not difficult. Yet the mind of an ordinary person is like a wild horse; consciousness is more restless than an ape, racing through the six sense-objects without a moment's rest. One should establish firm faith and discipline one's thoughts in advance, so that through accumulated habit it becomes second nature and the roots of goodness become firm.

"As the Buddha told King Bimbisāra: 'A person who has accumulated good conduct will have no evil thoughts at death. It is like a tree that leans to the west: when it falls, it necessarily follows its inclination.' But if the blade-wind [of death] arrives and a hundred sufferings assail the body, and one has not previously cultivated [such thoughts], how can one discern [the right thought] at that moment?

"Moreover, one should gather three to five companions of like aspiration and make a mutual pact: at the moment when death approaches, they will take turns reminding one another, reciting the name of Amitābha Buddha and vowing rebirth in the Land of Bliss, voice following voice in succession, so as to complete the ten thoughts.

"It is like a wax seal pressed into clay: the seal perishes as the imprint is formed. The moment this life ends is precisely the moment of birth in the Land of Bliss. Once one enters the assembly of the rightly determined, what more is there to worry about!"
