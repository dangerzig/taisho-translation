# 佛說老女人經
## Sūtra of the Old Woman, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0559

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Daśapura, at a place called Joyful Sound, together with eight hundred bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas.

At that time a poor old woman came to the Buddha's dwelling, placed her head on the ground, paid respects to the Buddha, and said: "I wish to ask a question."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! You may ask."

The old woman said: "Where does birth come from, and where does it go? Where does old age come from, and where does it go? Where does sickness come from, and where does it go? Where does death come from, and where does it go? Where do form, feeling, thought, formations, and consciousness come from, and where do they go? Where do the eyes, ears, nose, mouth, body, and mind come from, and where do they go? Where do earth, water, fire, wind, and space come from, and where do they go?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! This question is a great delight. Birth comes from nowhere and goes nowhere. Old age comes from nowhere and goes nowhere. Sickness comes from nowhere and goes nowhere. Death comes from nowhere and goes nowhere. Form, feeling, thought, formations, and consciousness come from nowhere and go nowhere. The eyes, ears, nose, mouth, body, and mind come from nowhere and go nowhere. Earth, water, fire, wind, and space come from nowhere and go nowhere. All dharmas are like this. It is like two sticks rubbed together: fire comes forth and burns the sticks. When the sticks are consumed, the fire goes out."

The Buddha asked the old woman: "Where does this fire come from, and where does it go?"

The old woman said: "When conditions come together, fire arises. When conditions disperse, the fire goes out."

The Buddha said: "All dharmas are likewise. When conditions come together, they are formed. When conditions disperse, they cease. Dharmas come from nowhere and go nowhere. When the eye sees form, that is the mind; the mind is form. Both are empty, arising from nothing, and their cessation is the same.

"It is like a drum: it is not made of one thing alone. There is the skin, there is the frame, and there is a person who takes a mallet and strikes the drum, and the drum produces sound. This present sound is empty. The future sound is empty. The past sound is empty. The sound does not come from the skin, nor from the frame, nor from the mallet, nor from the person's hand. It is the combination of all these things that produces the drum's sound, and the sound arises from emptiness and returns to emptiness.

"All the ten thousand things are likewise. Originally they are pure, with nothing whatsoever. Through conditions, dharmas are made, and dharmas too are nothing whatsoever.

"It is like clouds arising: when they darken and gather, rain falls. The rain does not come from the dragon's body, nor from the dragon's mind. It is all produced through the dragon's conditions, and thus rain comes about. All dharmas come from nowhere and go nowhere.

"It is like a painter who first prepares a board and canvas, then mixes and blends the various colors, and paints as he pleases. The painting does not come from the board and canvas, nor from the person's hand. As the mind directs, each element is completed. Birth and death are the same: each follows the actions one has performed. Calamity, Naraka, the heavens, and the human world are all like this. If there is something remaining, one does not cling; but if one clings, then it continues to exist."

The old woman, hearing this, was filled with great joy and said: "Through the Buddha's grace, I have obtained the Dharma-eye. Though my body is frail and old, today I have been liberated."

Ānanda straightened his robes, knelt, and said to the Buddha: "How is it that this old woman has such wisdom, hearing the Buddha's words and immediately being liberated?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This old woman was my mother in a past life, when I first resolved to study the Way."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "Why is this mother so poor and destitute?"

The Buddha said: "Long ago, in the time of Krakucchanda Buddha, I wished to become a śramaṇa. This mother, out of loving attachment, would not allow me to go. I was sorrowful and did not eat for one day. Because of this, for five hundred lifetimes since then, whenever she has been born into the world she has been in poverty. When her present life ends, she shall be born in the land of Amitābha Buddha and make offerings to the Buddhas. After sixty-eight billion kalpas, she shall become a Buddha named Pojian, in a land named Transformed Flower. When she is Buddha, the clothing and food of the people shall be like those of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. The people of that land shall all live for one kalpa."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, the old woman and Ānanda, the bodhisattvas, bhikṣus, spirits, nāgas, devas, humans, and asuras were all filled with great joy. They came forward, paid respects to the Buddha, and departed.
