# 釋摩男本四子經
## The Sūtra of the Śākyan Mahānāma and the Original Four Sons

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0054

Translated by Zhi Qian of the Wu dynasty

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of the Śākyas, staying at the Nyagrodha Grove in the land of Kapilavastu, seated beneath a tree. At that time there was a Śākyan named Mahānāma who came before the Buddha, placed his head and face upon the Buddha's feet in reverence, and addressed the Buddha, saying: "I have always heard the Buddha's words and taken them to heart. I have heard the Buddha teach that the human mind has three dispositions: the disposition of lust, the disposition of anger, and the disposition of delusion. Since hearing this, I have always kept it in mind, and I think to myself: 'When there is no disposition of lust, the mind itself becomes correct; when there is no disposition of anger, the mind itself becomes correct; when there is no disposition of delusion, the mind itself becomes correct.' I think to myself: 'I always uphold these three without wavering in mind, yet for what reason do I utterly fail to understand?'"

The Buddha said: "If the minds of lust, anger, and delusion were truly resolved, why would one still dwell together with wife and children? It is because a mind of greed remains. A wise person reflects: 'Though one must endure some small suffering, in the long run there is great happiness; dwelling with wife and children is momentary pleasure, but in the long run it brings great suffering.'"

"Do wise persons know that in the world pleasure is little and suffering is great?"

The Buddha said: "Those bhikṣus who have attained the path of the arhat know that in the world pleasure is little and suffering is great. The reason I sought the path of Buddhahood was simply that I recognized how the world has little pleasure and much suffering. When I was a bodhisattva, I always reflected on how there is little pleasure and much suffering."

Mahānāma said: "Is it only the Buddha and the arhats who have this thought?"

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "Listen to what I say and hold it in your heart. What is pleasure for people in the world? There are five pleasures that people covet and delight in. The eyes covet beautiful forms; this lodges in the mind, and day and night one thinks of it, becoming attached through craving for beautiful forms. The ears hear pleasant sounds; the nose smells sweet fragrances; the tongue delights in fine tastes; the body enjoys softness and comfort; all these lodge in the mind, and one becomes attached through craving. These five things are what all people of the world covet, and all the world's pleasurable attachments arise from these five things. Do you know how much sorrow arises from them?

"People in the world: some earn their living by farming, some by craftsmanship, some by trading in the marketplace, some by serving as officials, some by raising livestock, some by painting. These people endure cold when it is cold, endure heat when it is hot, endure suffering when there is suffering, endure hunger when hungry, endure thirst when thirsty, all because of the mind of greed, all enduring cold, heat, hunger, and thirst. They lament to themselves: 'I have worked for my livelihood for so many years, suffering nearly to death, yet I have obtained no wealth. I dwell with cold and hardship, and sometimes fall ill and waste away.'"

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "This is the first suffering. The second matter concerns the mind of lustful greed. Among people, some farm, some are craftsmen, some trade in the marketplace, some serve as officials, some raise livestock, some are painters. They go about earning their living, enduring cold, heat, hunger, and thirst, amassing wealth through greed. Having obtained riches, they then harbor anxiety and fear: fearing that the authorities may confiscate their wealth, or that fire may break out and burn their wealth, or that a ship may sink and their wealth be lost, or that bandits may rob them of their wealth, or that trade may cost them their wealth, or that poor relatives may poison them, or that their own children may squander their wealth. Such people constantly dwell with heavy anxiety, harboring worry day and night with no time of relief. Among them, some travel with their wealth and encounter the authorities, or encounter flood and fire, or sell goods and are not repaid, or bury their wealth in the ground and forget the place, or someone comes to slander and falsely accuse them, or their own children spend their father's wealth. Such a person thinks: 'From youth I worked for my livelihood, enduring cold, heat, hunger, and thirst, enduring diligent hardship to amass wealth, and now it is lost again.' From such anxious thoughts one may fall ill or die, all on account of wealth and money. All of this is brought about by the mind of greed and the five pleasures. This is the second suffering.

"The third matter: in the world, because of money, fathers quarrel with sons, brothers quarrel with brothers, husbands quarrel with wives; friends and acquaintances quarrel with one another, or all within and without a household quarrel, speaking ill of each other behind their backs. All of this is brought about by craving for pleasure. In the world, because of wealth, kings fight with kings, renunciants fight with renunciants, farmers fight with farmers, craftsmen fight with craftsmen. All because of wealth, they curse each other with their mouths, beat each other with staves, hack each other with knives, or wound and kill each other. All of this is brought about by greed. This is the third suffering.

"The fourth matter: people in the world join the military and receive government pay, as is publicly known. They must go into battle, with life and death uncertain, all because of the mind of greed. Having received government pay, they cannot withdraw, and so they fight. Their heads are wounded or severed, their arms are wounded or severed, their legs are wounded or severed; they take each other's lives in succession. All of this is brought about by greed. This is the fourth suffering.

"The fifth matter: people in the world with a mind of greed travel at night to break through the walls of others' homes, or rob people on the road, or assault walled cities. When caught by the authorities, they may be beheaded, or have their hands cut off, or their feet cut off, or be stretched upon a frame, or have their flesh cut away, or be burned with fire, or have their foreheads struck with a great mallet, or be cut in two at the waist. All of this is brought about by the mind of greed. This is the fifth suffering.

"People in the world, because of wealth, ceaselessly deceive one another: deceiving with their mouths, deceiving with their bodies, deceiving with their minds. At the time they think themselves justified, without fault or transgression, not knowing that calamity and poison await them hereafter, and that they shall enter hell. If there are wise persons, or śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, they reflect for themselves: in the world, are the five pleasures more abundant, or is suffering and sorrow more abundant?"

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "When I was a bodhisattva, I always reflected on how the world has little pleasure and much suffering, and for this reason I sought the path of the unconditioned. Those who would say the world is pleasurable do not know the way of birth and death. If in the world there are those who are wise and virtuous, whose hearts and minds harbor no intent of greed, and who further wish to teach others not to be greedy, this is the greatest virtue."

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "I once went to the kingdom of Rājagṛha, where there was a mountain called Saptaparṇaguhā. I saw various kinds of Nigaṇṭhas: some walked with their hair unbound, some walked bent over, some sat upon the ground, some lay upon the ground. Their bodies were without clothing, all covered only in deerskin. The Buddha saw them from afar and went forward to speak with the Nigaṇṭhas: 'For what reason do you go about with your hair unbound? For what reason do you sit and lie upon the ground, without clothing or covering, tormenting yourselves like this?' The Nigaṇṭhas replied to the Buddha: 'It is because of evil deeds we committed in past lives that we are afflicted with such hardship in this present life. It is because the evil deeds are not yet exhausted.' The Buddha said: 'For what reason do you know that this was caused by deeds of past lives? Did you hear it from others, or do you know it yourselves?' The Nigaṇṭhas said: 'We neither know it, nor have heard it, nor have studied under a teacher.' The Buddha said: 'Through this affliction and suffering, do you attain liberation from birth and death? If you have neither heard it from others nor studied under a teacher, are you not tormenting yourselves in vain? It would be better to abandon what you are doing and come to practice the Buddha's path.' The Buddha said: 'I merely feel concern for your bodies and think of your descendants, for in future generations they will all follow this example and imitate what you are doing.' The Nigaṇṭhas all grew angry at the Buddha's words and said: 'King Bimbisāra has brought this śramaṇa Gautama into the kingdom!' The Buddha told the Nigaṇṭhas: 'Do not be angry. King Bimbisāra has received my sūtras and would perhaps not dare to speak recklessly.' The Buddha told the Nigaṇṭhas: 'Could you sit upright for seven days and seven nights without eating, drinking, or speaking? Is that pleasure? Or is the pleasure of the king's palaces and music pleasure?' The Nigaṇṭhas said: 'Śramaṇa Gautama, that is pleasure.' The Buddha said: 'Why is it pleasure? Why then does King Bimbisāra not find his kingdom pleasurable?' The Nigaṇṭhas said: 'We have few worries; for this reason, Śramaṇa Gautama, we surpass King Bimbisāra.' The Buddha told the Nigaṇṭhas: 'King Bimbisāra has the mind of lust, the mind of anger, and the mind of delusion. He also wishes to subjugate his courtiers and further wishes to subjugate the common people beyond his borders, calculating day and night: whom should I govern, whom should I imprison?' The Buddha said: 'Those possessed by lust wish both to kill themselves and to kill others; those possessed by anger wish both to kill themselves and to kill others; those possessed by delusion wish both to kill themselves and to kill others.' All the Nigaṇṭhas came forward before the Buddha and addressed him, saying: 'We too have no disposition of lust, no disposition of anger, no disposition of delusion. May we become śramaṇas?' The Buddha said: 'You should return home and inform your parents.' The Nigaṇṭhas said: 'We have already left home to cultivate the Way and have taken leave of our parents.' The Buddha said: 'Then you should receive the five precepts and return.' The Nigaṇṭhas all received the five precepts: first, not to kill; second, not to steal; third, not to violate the wives and daughters of others; fourth, not to deceive; fifth, not to drink intoxicants. Having received the five precepts, the Nigaṇṭhas put on clothing, bound up their hair, and walked with proper deportment, each returning to their own home."

The Buddha told Mahānāma: "You have heard this sūtra on the minds of lust, anger, and delusion, and you say you uphold the Buddha's teaching. If you thoroughly contemplate these five matters, would you still be the same as ordinary worldly people?"

Mahānāma said: "I shall return home to contemplate and recite this scripture, and each day I shall come before the Buddha."

Mahānāma then made obeisance to the Buddha and departed.
