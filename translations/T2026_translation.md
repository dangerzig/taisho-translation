# Account of the Compilation of the Tripiṭaka and Miscellaneous Collection

## *Zhuànjí Sānzàng Jí Zázàng Zhuàn* (撰集三藏及雜藏傳)

Taishō 2026

Translator unknown. A verse narrative.

*Note: This is a verse narrative describing the First Buddhist Council held at Saṃkāśya (Sēngjiāshī, 僧伽尸) in Magadha after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa. The text recounts the gathering of the relics, the cremation at Kuśinagara, the distribution of relics into eight portions, and then the council itself. The core narrative focuses on Mahākāśyapa assembling eighty thousand arhats and commissioning Ānanda to recite the sūtras from memory. Ānanda, initially excluded as a stream-enterer, attains arhatship under a tree and returns in triumph. The text includes a vivid scene where Māra attacks the assembly with phantom armies, and Ānanda subdues him by binding three corpses (human, dog, and snake) to his neck. The concluding section explains how the four Āgamas (Ekottara, Madhyama, Dīrgha, Saṃyukta) were organized and named, plus the Vinaya, Abhidharma, and Miscellaneous Collection as the fourth basket.*

After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, Kāśyapa (*Jiāshè*, 迦葉), Ānanda (*Ānán*, 阿難), and others compiled the Tripiṭaka and Miscellaneous Collection at the city of Saṃkāśya in the north of the kingdom of Magadha.

> First, having bowed to the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha,
> Each received the collected dharma.
> These dharma-records: remove the five hindrances,
> And with one mind receive them.
> The collected dharma is as Ānanda recited.
> Together you should trust and delight in it.
> This is Ānanda's wisdom, equal to the Buddha's.
> Listen to the collection of this dharma.

> When the Buddha entered nirvāṇa,
> Ānanda was entrusted with the dharma, pitying sentient beings,
> Protecting and upholding all dharmas
> As one reveres the World-Honored One, the unsurpassed guide.
> Regard Ānanda and the rest as a field of merit beyond compare.
> Were it not for Ānanda,
> When the Śākya clan entered nirvāṇa, the true dharma would be entirely extinguished,
> Along with the Tripiṭaka.

> The Buddha rained down all dharmas, taking pity on humans and devas,
> Receiving them in a vessel made whole.
> Ānanda upheld and received them; entrusted with earnest care.
> The dharma of Ānanda:
> This person will never let anything leak or drop away.
> The collected dharma-names
> And the dharma of distinguishing and classification
> Are like a lion's roar. What Ānanda has recited,
> One by one before the assembly:
> Bhikṣus, each attend well.

> Ānanda stands alone.
> The Buddha himself praised him:
> Whether in transcendent wisdom or worldly wisdom,
> He knows all.
> Ānanda is like the ocean. For this reason
> Have faith in Ānanda and turn toward him.
> Listen to the collected dharma and sever all suffering.

> The light of the world entered nirvāṇa.
> The earth shook. Mountains and seas surged.
> Devas and humans wept:
> "The World-Honored One appeared late; why so early the nirvāṇa?"
> Humans and devas were left orphaned.
> All the paths were made desolate.

> Those with supernatural penetration and vision,
> The arhats with miraculous powers,
> All came to Kuśinagara to make offerings to the World-Honored One.
> Eighty-four thousand had passed beyond the outflows (*āsrava*):
> Fields of merit supreme, revered by devas and humans.
> Throughout all of Jambudvīpa, the arhats all came.
> The World-Honored One had entered nirvāṇa; they assembled together.

> Additionally, there were non-returners (*anāgāmin*),
> Once-returners (*sakṛdāgāmin*),
> Stream-enterers (*srotāpanna*), and those still in training:
> Their number was double [that of the arhats].
> Ordinary bhikṣus, numbering hundreds of thousands,
> Raised their hands, wept, and walked forth:
> "World-Honored One, where have you gone?
> We are blind and deaf! The three poisons are not yet removed!
> We sink in the five destinies. Who will rescue us?"

> Bhikṣuṇīs, one thousand
> And thirty-five in number,
> Some with supernatural powers,
> Some with outflows, some without.
> Śakra, Brahmā, and countless devas,
> Bearing *mandārava* flowers and music,
> Swiftly came bearing them.
> The four heavenly kings, each with their retinue,
> Bearing sandalwood, pearl necklaces, swiftly arrived,
> Together with the devas of the desire realm,
> The form realm, and the formless realm:
> Ninety-eight hundred million all came to Kuśinagara.

> The fearless Śākya king
> With eighty thousand tusked elephants and eighty thousand fine horses
> All came to Kuśinagara.
> King Prasenajit of Śrāvastī (*Shèwèi*, 舍衛), valiant and wise,
> With seventy thousand famed elephants, all arrived at Kuśinagara.
> King Ajātaśatru (*Āshéshì*, 阿闍世),
> Courageous, with faith in the Buddha,
> With seventy-five hundred elephants, came weeping.
> King Pāñcāla, named Bright and Upright,
> With sixty thousand elephants and horses, all came to Kuśinagara.
> The kings named Mín, Nuǎn, Yùguāng, and Qīnhòu,
> Each with fifty thousand, all came to the assembly.

> The kings of the fragrant lands to the west,
> With marshaled armies in four-colored uniforms,
> Numbering hundreds of thousands,
> Came swiftly, wishing to see the Buddha's remains.
> The Dragon Kings wept and cried,
> Following the kings.
> The people of Vaiśālī (*Wéiyēlí*, 維耶離), with elephants, horses, and chariots,
> In splendor rivaling the devas, came to Kuśinagara.

> The common people were without number,
> Along with the devout lay followers.
> Having seen the Buddha depart forever,
> They came to pay respects to the nirvāṇa.
> The beings of the world, thirty-six hundred million,
> All came to Kuśinagara,
> Bearing funeral offerings. Within a hundred *yojana*s,
> Kuśinagara at the center:
> Devas and humans filled it. Not a spot of ground was empty.

> The devas scattered flowers
> And made offerings to the Buddha's remains.
> They beat celestial drums and music, raining down flowers and incense.
> The kings and their officials came to the Buddha's remains,
> All bowed at the Buddha's feet,
> And stood surrounding him.
> The kings wailed and sobbed, raising their hands in grief:
> "Never again will we see the Buddha teach the dharma for the assembly!"

> Ajātaśatru wept,
> Falling at the Buddha's feet:
> "I, a man of the five heinous crimes, to whom will you entrust me?"
> The kings circumambulated the remains, scattering jewels upon them,
> And performed all manner of music.
> They anointed the bier with fragrant paste, placed the remains in an iron coffin,
> And filled it with sesame oil.
> The earth shook. The kings wailed. The devas shed tears
> That fell visible upon the ground.
> The celestial kings rained tears; all wept together.

> Celestial sandalwood, the finest firewood, wish-fulfilling incense:
> These were piled upon the ground.
> The kings, the fourfold assembly, great Kāśyapa, and the saṃgha sat above.
> The offerings of all beings to this supreme field of merit.
> Kāśyapa, chief of the assembly,
> The venerable bhikṣuṇīs,
> Śakra, the kings: each helped to light the pyre.
> As the body burned,
> Devas and humans wailed: "Alas! How grievous!
> What shall we do?"

> For seven days they made offerings. The devas fully received them.
> [The relics] were divided into eight portions, placed in urns; a ninth [for the urn];
> Each [party] returned to its home.
> Kings and commoners; the devas returned above;
> The eight divisions of spirits returned.
> Eight portions: the remainder was obtained by devas and nāgas.
> The people of Kuśinagara
> Obtained their portion and erected stūpas: eight portions, eight stūpas.
> The ninth, the urn-stūpa;
> The tenth, the charcoal-stūpa.
> These ten stūpas were raised throughout Jambudvīpa.

---

> Kāśyapa, chief of the saṃgha,
> Departed from Kuśinagara and went to the kingdom of Magadha
> To assemble the community of monks.
> Kāśyapa told the saṃgha:
> "Bhikṣus, do not go about. We should assemble together,
> Lest the dharma be destroyed."
> In the open air, Kāśyapa struck the *gandhāra* [bell],
> Wishing the dharma to long endure and triumph over Māra.

> Kāśyapa addressed the assembly,
> Together with the devas and humans:
> "Now we shall collect the dharma and bring peace to sentient beings.
> Though the Buddha has entered nirvāṇa, the four truths still remain.
> The eightfold path still exists. Nirvāṇa can be attained.
> The earlier buddhas had long lifespans; the people had merit.
> The remnant dharma draws near; before long it will be exhausted.
> People today have short lives.
> The Buddha appeared in the seventh [age]; after his nirvāṇa,
> How long can the dharma endure?

> "Non-returners, once-returners,
> Those on the path of seeing who have not yet completed it:
> Now I assemble the true saṃgha;
> These may not enter.
> Those with the divine eye, miraculous powers,
> The six penetrations, and selflessness:
> All the arhats, I now assemble them."
> The assembly, hearing the command,
> Each came at once: eighty thousand, all without outflows.

> Kāśyapa asked his younger brother Aniruddha (*Ānàlǜ*, 阿那律):
> "Survey all the arhats. Who has not come?"
> Aniruddha surveyed the great trichiliocosm
> And saw on the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven one named Gavāṃpati (*Jiāohuán*, 憍桓).
> Pāṇṭhaka (*Bōlǜ*, 鉢律) reported to Kāśyapa:
> "Gavāṃpati is on Trāyastriṃśa, an arhat without outflows.
> He has not come to the assembly."
> Kāśyapa summoned him, but he still did not come:
> "The World-Honored One has entered nirvāṇa.
> The world is empty. What is there to seek?"

> Kāśyapa thought: "Among the bhikṣus, is there one
> Who can compile what the Buddha taught: the sūtras and dharma?"
> He looked everywhere but saw no one
> Able to collect the twelve divisions of the sūtra-dharma and their meaning
> As the Buddha had taught them.
> Only Ānanda: but he was a stream-enterer.
> A means must be devised
> To cause him to attain the state without outflows.

> The elder skillfully examined Ānanda's mind
> And knew he had compassion, having recently attained the path.
> Kāśyapa said to Ānanda:
> "You may not enter the assembly. You should not enter. Now you must leave."
> Ānanda said: "Why, Elder?
> Have I committed some offense against the Three Jewels?"
> Kāśyapa answered: "Brother, do you wish to know?
> You have committed great offenses against the Buddha and the assembly.
> Because of you, the Buddha-dharma
> Was diminished by a thousand years.
> It was you who persuaded the Buddha to ordain women.
> The minor precepts: the Buddha was about to classify them in detail.
> Why did you not ask
> About the minor precepts? What were you thinking?
> You showed contempt for the precepts.
> The Buddha has entered nirvāṇa. Whom shall we now ask?
> You stepped upon the Buddha's great robe.
> When the Buddha was thirsty and asked for water, you did not bring it.
> Are these not offenses?
> These and other faults are many; they are your doing.
> I cannot recount them all.
> Therefore you must leave."

> Ānanda sighed deeply, grieved and tearful:
> "The Buddha's skillful means have ended. On whom shall I rely?"
> Thereupon he went
> And sat beneath a tree. His feelings gathered; his outflows were exhausted.
> The Buddha-dharma was thereby revived.
> Countless millions of devas surrounded Ānanda.
> He came to the great assembly,
> Fearless as a lion.

> Kāśyapa saw him from afar and told all the monks:
> "All should quickly rise! Ānanda now approaches!"
> The eighty-thousand-and-more assembly,
> All without defilement,
> With Kāśyapa at the head, stood with palms joined to welcome him.
> Kāśyapa raised his hand: "Welcome, Ānanda!"
> [Ānanda] then ascended the high seat
> To bring order to the assembly of monks.

> "Truly great Ānanda! You are the eye for all.
> Having attended the Buddha to the end,
> Gautama's merit is complete.
> Look upon this saṃgha,
> And look upon the celestial assembly.
> The world has long been ill, afflicted by three kinds of suffering.
> The world has no master; the guide has entered nirvāṇa.
> Together with compassionate mind,
> Teach the dharma for the assembly."

> Ānanda was silent. Kāśyapa then made the request.
> The elder smiled joyfully and gave a lion's roar.
> Kāśyapa thought:
> "As hoped, he is able to teach!"
> "Today Ānanda delivers devas and humans."

> Kāśyapa raised his voice and called aloud to all beings:
> "Those who wish to cross the world,
> Come here!
> As the Buddha taught the various dharmas
> To remove the suffering of sentient beings,
> Ānanda shall now recite them. The dharma of omniscience,
> Like scattered flowers of many kinds:
> Ānanda shall now compile them, distinguishing the Tripiṭaka.
> The Tathāgata taught the dharma according to the assembly's needs.
> Ānanda, on the high seat,
> Shall once more expound it.
> The ten directions shall hear. Devas, nāgas, spirits,
> And the fourfold assembly of disciples:
> Hearing the command, come at once.
> Like a person scorched and thirsty
> Longing for a drink of water,
> Running to the river to drink there:
> Devas, nāgas, spirits, and the fourfold assembly
> Come to Ānanda
> To hear the dharma within."

> The countless others: trainees on the path of seeing,
> Once-returners, non-returners,
> Following the sound, arrived afterward.
> Of the non-returner path: twenty-one thousand.
> The once-returner assembly: forty-two thousand.
> The stream-enterer saṃgha: eighty-four thousand.
> These arrived afterward,
> Also wishing to hear the dharma.
> All the kings assembled, with their ministers and armies.

> Ānanda's bearing: all who beheld it were delighted.
> The great assembly sat in order,
> Extending twelve *yojana*s in each direction.
> Ānanda was at the center, like the full bright moon.
> Śakra was at his right; Brahmā at his left,
> Attending Ānanda
> As when the Buddha was present.

> Śakra spoke verses of praise. The devas were joyful
> To behold the great assembly.
> Ānanda was fearless, as when the dharma-wheel was turned
> And the assembly surrounded the Buddha.
> They gazed upon Ānanda's countenance, his bearing majestic.
> Brahmā likewise
> Requested, as if requesting the Tathāgata, the World-Honored One, to teach.
> Ānanda was thus.

> Māra (*Bōxún*, 波旬), hearing the renown, also came,
> Bringing his wife, children, ministers, and armies.
> Māra beheld
> The multitudes of every kind, with Ānanda at the center
> Like the radiance of the sun.
> A topknot rose from Ānanda's head, like a mountain peak.
> From his neck, sunlight
> Illumined the entire assembly.
> From Nanda's head a topknot arose; from Kāśyapa's neck, light.
> Aniruddha's penetrating vision surveyed the great trichiliocosm.
> The kings were in the assembly
> With their ministers. A thousand million people
> Were all within.
> Seeing the assembly so joyful,
> Ānanda so heroic,
> Māra was filled with misery,
> His heart trembling with fear.

> Māra thought: "One buddha has entered nirvāṇa,
> But three more have appeared!
> The Buddha's power and authority are great.
> Gautama has entered nirvāṇa;
> I thought I had escaped.
> But what these three have attained is very great."
> Having been glad at the Buddha's nirvāṇa,
> Thinking "Gautama's dharma is declining,
> With no adversary to oppose me,
> I should devise a plan to destroy this remnant dharma."

> The fourfold assembly and all the kings:
> Māra then issued a command,
> Ordering his general Lion-[Banner] (*Shīzǐ Jiàng*, 師子將):
> "Swiftly assemble the four armies! Destroy this dharma utterly!"
> At once phantom armies arose,
> With four kinds of generals, surrounding the great assembly,
> Emitting terrifying sounds.
> "Seize the practitioners! The devout men and women!
> Slaughter the kings! Destroy the dharma-site!"

> The assembly was alarmed.
> The fourfold assembly thought: "Where has this come from?"
> Before they could resolve their doubt,
> The kings, hearing the sound, were all filled with terror.
> Seeing Māra's armies,
> Each prepared their weapons.

> Ānanda thought: "Who comes to trouble us?"
> Beholding the armies, he knew it was Māra's doing.
> Ānanda then laughed
> And ordered the kings to stand down their chariots:
> "This disturber of the assembly: I myself shall subdue him."

> Ānanda, with the power of wisdom, and Kāśyapa, with the power of vigor,
> Extended their hands, seized Māra,
> And bound three corpses to his throat:
> The first, a human corpse; the second, a dog's corpse;
> The third, a snake's corpse.
> Putrid and rank, unbearable.

> Māra then surrendered.
> Kāśyapa said: "Has the arhat been captured?
> Should he be troubled by a human?"
> [Māra said:] "Kāśyapa! Previously I harassed the Buddha to the utmost,
> Yet the World-Honored One
> Never treated me as harshly as this."
> [Kāśyapa] answered: "The Buddha was compassionate,
> Wearing the great armor of compassion.
> He would never harm any living being.
> We are collecting the dharma,
> Wishing it to long endure.
> Why have you come to disturb our assembly?"

> Māra then put his palms together and addressed Ānanda:
> "Just release me! I shall trouble people no more."
> Ānanda made him swear an oath. Kāśyapa likewise.
> "If hereafter you disturb the assembly,
> These corpses shall return to shackle your neck."
> The three corpses vanished.
> Māra was freed. Māra was then filled with fear,
> Standing apart to one side.

> The devas exclaimed: "Excellent!
> The Buddha-dharma has triumphed! The remnant dharma shall long endure.
> It shall always triumph over Māra."

> Kāśyapa commanded the assembly: "All be calm and settled.
> Ānanda shall now recite, as the Buddha expounded."
> All the kings arose,
> Palms joined, waiting.
> All the devas were delighted. The worthies watched the proceedings.
> Kāśyapa told Ānanda: "The time to recite the sūtras has come.
> Open this treasure-chest
> And proclaim the supreme dharma.
> Which is the Ekottara? Which is the Ekadaśaka?
> Which is the Nidāna? Which are the Dhātus?"

> Ānanda gave a long laugh,
> A lion's roar, stretching and yawning.
> Surveying the four directions of the seated assembly,
> He said: "Thus have I heard."
> Having said "at one time,"
> The earth shook. A hundred million devas and humans
> Attained the dharma-eye.

> Śrāvastī is the Ekottara; that which is called the Ekadaśaka.
> From the Śākya [clan] comes the Nidāna; from Magadha, the Dhātus.
> The remaining sūtras likewise,
> Each expounded in its proper place.

> Ānanda, using the sūtras, taught the great assembly.
> All the sūtras were collected as the first basket.
> The Vinaya as the second basket.
> The Abhidharma (*dàfǎ*, 大法) as the third basket.
> The sūtra-records are the Āgamas;
> The precept-rules and the Abhidharma:
> Three divisions, equally balanced,
> Make the Tripiṭaka.

> Having recited the great original text,
> He then recorded the various miscellaneous dharmas,
> Gathered together as one more collection,
> A separate fourth division of the sūtras.
> These are called the Āgamas:
> Ekottara, Madhyama,
> Dīrgha, Saṃyukta: the four Āgamas.

---

### Explanation of the Names of the Four Āgamas

> The Vinaya follows the dharma: offenses are treated in the order in which they may arise.
> The middle ones [treat] long-term celibates and ascetic practices, placed afterward.
> The Abhidharma's various divisions
> Are named by what they generate.
> The first is distinguished;
> Thereafter each differs.

> Ekottara, Madhyama,
> Dīrgha, Saṃyukta: the four Āgamas.
> Kāśyapa asked Ānanda: "What does each title mean?"

> Ānanda answered: "One by one, the bhikṣu recollects the Buddha.
> By this the mind is regulated.
> Therefore it is called 'Increasing by One' (Ekottara).
> Afterward, two dharmas:
> Contemplating the wholesome mind, two dharmas arise.
> Stilling the mind and discerning:
> Three areas, three knowledges:
> Past lives, exhaustion of outflows.
> Four areas, the four truths.
> Five areas, the five faculties.
> Six areas, the six great [elements].
> Seven areas, the seven factors of awakening.
> Eight areas, the eightfold [noble path].
> Nine areas, the nine cessations.
> Ten areas, the ten powers.
> The eleventh sūtra is named 'The Cowherd Boy.'
> The compassion sūtra brings it to a close.
> The Ekottara ends.
> From this principle, one should understand each item.
> The sūtras in this [collection] are of many kinds.
> Therefore it is called 'Increasing by One.'

> "Like a painter
> Who distinguishes the colors and forms of his sections,
> This is one kind of 'increasing by one.'
> Various compositions,
> Like medicines without limit:
> According to the illness they are compounded and named such-and-such a pill.
> Therefore it is called 'Increasing by One.'

> "One by one, threads of warp and weft complete the cloth.
> By teaching one by one, the Ekottara is completed.
> Like combining various things
> And naming the collected sound 'empty assembly':
> Various sūtras, therefore named 'Increasing by One.'
> Like grass, wood, clay, and earth raising a wall
> That surrounds and covers empty space
> Until a dwelling is complete:
> So generosity, morality, rebirth in heaven, and nirvāṇa,
> Encircled by meaning, are called 'Increasing by One.'
> Like one type of clay
> Fashioned into many kinds of vessel:
> This is one 'Increasing by One.'
> Various meanings combined:
> From this one should know the various meanings taught.
> This sūtra fully emerges.
> You should uphold the Ekottara."

> "Neither too long
> Nor too short,
> Meaning bound in balanced couplets:
> This is named the Madhyama ('Middle') Āgama.
> The characters are neither coarse
> Nor extremely fine.
> Words and meaning are properly balanced.
> Therefore it is called 'Middle.'
> Removing upper and lower extremes, it teaches the middle dharma.
> Discarding 'that' and 'I,'
> Therefore it is named the 'Middle Collection.'
> Minor troubles and major troubles,
> Delusion and doubt are all destroyed
> By contemplating the noble truths correctly.
> Therefore it is called 'Middle.'"

> "In it, there are lengthy discourses
> Together with accounts of former lives and former kalpas and their cycles.
> Therefore it is called 'Long' (Dīrgha).
> It reckons various cessations,
> The happiness of the heavens above.
> Those who hear it are delighted.
> Therefore it is called 'Long.'
> The seven past buddhas and the vast Mahāyāna,
> The Buddha's nirvāṇa:
> Therefore it is called 'Long.'
> Reckonings of many jewels,
> Many wheel-turning kings:
> Those who hear it are delighted.
> Therefore it is called 'Long.'"

> "This dharma, when studied, should be grasped.
> Studying it, one joyfully forgets [worldly cares],
> Wishing to sever all fetters.
> Therefore it is called 'Miscellaneous' (Saṃyukta).
> This dharma is a 'balanced collection' (*děnghán*, 等含):
> Meaning and flavor are equally present.
> Hearing it, doubts are severed.
> Therefore it is called 'Balanced Collection.'
> It is the ground of cultivation,
> The destination of meditation and wisdom.
> Equally seeing all dharmas:
> This is called the 'Balanced Collection.'
> Within this sūtra, gathering the two ends of practice,
> Those who hear it have many doubts.
> Therefore it is called the 'Balanced Collection.'
> Miscellaneous sūtras outside the main divisions,
> Gāthās praised by the devas:
> All enter within it.
> Therefore it is called the 'Balanced Collection.'"

> "Approaching the dharma,
> One obtains a good and pure mind,
> Severing all disputes.
> Therefore it is called 'Precept-Vinaya.'
> Distinguishing the middle precepts,
> One attains pure diligence.
> All who hear are disciplined.
> This is called 'Precept-Vinaya.'
> Bhikṣus who practice this receive benefit therein,
> Crushing and shattering all fetters.
> Therefore it is called 'Precept-Vinaya.'
> Enduring all defilements and stains,
> Bhikṣus who study this
> Remove all evil conduct.
> This is called 'Precept-Vinaya.'"

> "Contemplating and examining all dharmas,
> From the dharma one gains benefit
> And arrives at the ground of ambrosia (*amṛta*).
> Therefore it is called 'Vinaya' (*Pínǐ*, 毘尼)."

> When Kātyāyana (*Jiāzhān*, 迦栴) had completed [the Abhidharma],
> He brought it to the Buddha.
> The Buddha said: "Supreme dharma!
> It shall be named 'Supreme Dharma' (Abhidharma).
> Within it, delusion is broken
> And the world is benefited.
> These collected sūtras illumine [all].
> Therefore it is called 'Great Dharma.'
> It completely upholds [the teaching] and defeats the non-Buddhists,
> Cutting off their arrogance.
> The banner and pennant of all dharmas:
> This is called 'Great Dharma.'
> Like a bright lamp illuminating all things,
> By which all forms are seen:
> Therefore it is called 'Great Dharma.'"

> "The meaning of these collected sūtras
> Is like the tip of ambrosia,
> The flavor of all dharmas.
> This meaning of the Great Dharma [and]
> The various sūtras and Vinaya:
> Diligently contemplate, uphold, and do not abandon them.
> Bind together the Tripiṭaka.
> Distinguish the characters and meaning."

> The bhikṣus and devas,
> Tens of millions, all exclaimed: "Excellent!"
> Kāśyapa further asked: "What of the fourth basket?"
> "Ānanda, please explain it for the sake of sentient beings."
> Ānanda answered:
> "The teachings in this [basket] are each different,
> Following the assembly's dispositions.
> This is called the 'Miscellaneous Collection' (*Zázàng*, 雜藏).
> The Buddha's accounts of former causes and conditions,
> Which the arhats also recited,
> Together with devas, Brahmā, and non-Buddhists:
> Therefore it is called the 'Miscellaneous Collection.'
> Within it are many gāthās, questions on the twelve links of dependent origination.
> Each of these enters differently.
> This is called the 'Miscellaneous Collection.'
> [The tales of] the three incalculable kalpas,
> The bodhisattva's lives,
> His births and the conditions he created:
> Therefore it is called the 'Three Baskets' [plus this].
> Within it are many accounts of former causes and conditions,
> Many accounts of past births, differing from the Āgamas.
> This is called the 'Miscellaneous Collection.'
> The dharma of the Miscellaneous Collection praises the bodhisattva's lives.
> The meanings within it exceed those of the [other] three baskets."

> All the dharmas were gathered together
> And bound in one place.
> "What bhikṣu can uphold them all?
> In future ages, bhikṣus will mostly be foolish.
> Such people will not be able
> To uphold the entire Tripiṭaka.
> Later, when they become teachers,
> From the sūtras they will extract verses;
> Thereby the proliferation increases.
> Therefore [the baskets] are not combined.
> In various places there is delight:
> Some delight in the four Āgamas,
> Some delight in the Vinaya,
> Some delight in the Abhidharma,
> Some delight in the external gāthās,
> Some delight in the Miscellaneous Collection.
> Therefore there is not a single name [for all].
> All the dharmas are fully recited;
> The four Āgamas are completed.
> The records of all numbers,
> Together with the Vinaya and Abhidharma,
> Are gathered as the Tripiṭaka."

> Having heard this dharma,
> Devas, spirits, and humans:
> Three thousand bhikṣus attained the exhaustion of outflows.
> Non-returners: eight thousand.
> Once-returners: ten thousand.
> Countless devas and humans
> Attained the fruit of seeing the path.
> "May this dharma long endure, for the sake of devas and humans.
> May all kings always triumph and live out a full hundred autumns."
> All devas, humans,
> Kings, and bhikṣus together exclaimed: "Excellent!
> As Ānanda has recited,
> The collection of the dharma is now complete."
> Devas and humans each returned.
> The fourfold assembly of disciples
> All returned to their places.

### Afterword

After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, Kāśyapa, Ānanda, and others, at the city of Saṃkāśya in the north of Magadha, compiled the Tripiṭaka of orthodox sūtras and the sūtras of the Miscellaneous Collection. What is commonly called "the four cases" includes the Miscellaneous [Collection]. In all, there are two hundred *śataka* sections.

The first is the Ekottara Āgama. From one to ten, the sūtras of the eleven topics are: the compilation of the various sūtras of eleven matters. The sūtra on the eleven matters of the cowherd boy comes first. The sūtra on the eleven matters of practicing compassion comes last. According to what each cites, the sūtras are brought forth. Because the matters are connected, they are combined into one fascicle.

This "Cowherd Sūtra" is the Buddha's teaching on eleven matters of herding cattle, analogized to the bhikṣu's eleven practices of the Way-equipment. The root-trunk of the Way's tree is planted; branches and leaves flourish luxuriantly, providing wide shade. Because of the cowherd boy, in seated meditation, a thought arose. The Buddha knew his intention and therefore taught the eleven matters. Through what was practiced, the cowherd at once understood and attained arhatship.
