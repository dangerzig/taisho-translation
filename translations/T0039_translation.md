## The Sūtra of the Story of King Māndhātā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0039

Translated by Fǎjù of the Western Jin dynasty

## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra of the Story of King Māndhātā, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling at Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, at Anāthapiṇḍada's park. At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, while in solitary retreat, gave rise to this thought: "All craving, with its attachments, is accumulated and stored away; craving knows no satiation." The Venerable Ānanda then rose from his seat, went to where the World-Honored One was, and upon arrival bowed his head at the World-Honored One's feet and sat to one side. After a short while, the Venerable Ānanda drew back, knelt with joined palms, and addressed the World-Honored One: "Just now, having gone to my place of meditation, this thought arose in me: 'All craving with its attachments and accumulations is without satiation.'"

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Ānanda: "Just so, just so, Ānanda! Indeed, craving, attachment, and accumulation are truly without satisfaction. Why is this so? Ānanda, in times long past, there was a great king named Māndhātā, a true dharma-king who governed the people without violence or cruelty, endowed with the seven treasures. These seven treasures were: the wheel-treasure, the elephant-treasure, the dark-blue horse-treasure, the jewel-treasure, the woman-treasure, the householder-treasure, and the general-treasure — these are called the seven treasures. He also had a thousand sons, courageous, fierce, mighty, and strong, radiant in appearance, capable of repelling enemy forces. Just as the rivers and great seas span this world, so he governed by the Dharma without recourse to weapons or punishment.

"At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā gave rise to this thought: 'I have heard that people of old had exceedingly long lifespans and were clever and wise.' He then said: 'Now I possess this Jambudvīpa with its power and supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace.' The king gave rise to this thought: 'I wish that the seven treasures would rain down upon my palace.' At that time, Ānanda, as soon as King Māndhātā had this thought, within seven days the seven treasures rained down.

"At another time, King Māndhātā again gave rise to this thought: 'I have heard that people of old had exceedingly long lifespans and were clever and wise. I have heard that Pūrvavideha is endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace. I wish to go there and govern.' At that time, Ānanda, as soon as King Māndhātā had this thought, he vanished from Jambudvīpa and appeared in Pūrvavideha, together with his fourfold army. At that time, the beings of Pūrvavideha, seeing King Māndhātā approaching from afar, gathered around him, each bearing silver bowls filled with gold dust or gold bowls filled with silver dust, and came to where King Māndhātā was. Having arrived, they addressed King Māndhātā: 'Welcome, Great King! This is the realm of Pūrvavideha, endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace. May the great king govern here; we are all the great king's subjects.'

"At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā governed there for countless hundreds of thousands of years. At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā at another time gave rise to this thought: 'I possess Jambudvīpa with its fulfillment of desires and supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace. I have already caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace for seven days. I now also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha with its supernatural faculties, abundant harvests, and teeming populace. I have heard that people of old were clever and wise and said: "There is the land of Godānīya, endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace."' The king gave rise to this thought: 'I wish to go to the realm of Godānīya and govern the people.'

"At that time, Ānanda, having given rise to this thought, King Māndhātā vanished from Pūrvavideha and went to the realm of Godānīya, together with his fourfold army. At that time, the people of Godānīya, seeing King Māndhātā approaching from afar, each bearing silver bowls filled with gold dust or gold bowls filled with silver dust, brought these offerings and came to King Māndhātā's presence. Having arrived, they addressed King Māndhātā: 'Welcome, Great King! This is the king's realm of Godānīya, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace. May the great king govern the people here in Godānīya; we are all the great king's subjects.' At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā governed the people of Godānīya for countless hundreds of thousands of years, for countless myriads of years.

"At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā again at another time gave rise to this thought: 'I possess Jambudvīpa with its abundant harvests and teeming populace, where I caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace for seven days; I also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha with its supernatural faculties, abundant harvests, and teeming populace; I also possess the realm of Godānīya with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace.' King Māndhātā then gave rise to this thought: 'I have heard that people of old were clever and wise and said: "There is the realm of Uttarakuru, endowed with supernatural faculties and a teeming populace. In that land, all people are free and without masters, fulfilling their desires at will, with exceedingly long lifespans. When they die there, all are reborn in the heavens; they eat naturally-occurring rice and wear karpāsa garments."' At that time, King Māndhātā gave rise to this thought: 'I shall go there to govern.' Having given rise to this thought, Ānanda, King Māndhātā vanished from Godānīya and went to the realm of Uttarakuru, together with his fourfold army.

"At that time, King Māndhātā, seeing from afar that the ground of that land was level and entirely of a dark blue-green color, addressed his ministers and people: 'Do you all see that the ground is level and of a dark blue-green color?' They replied: 'Indeed, Great King!' 'Know that these are the karpāsa garment trees. The people there all wear karpāsa garments; you too shall wear karpāsa garments.' At that time, King Māndhātā again saw that the ground was of a pure white color, and addressing his ministers: 'Do you all see that the ground is white?' They replied: 'Indeed.' 'This is naturally-occurring rice, without husks or stalks, requiring no threshing or winnowing. Its fragrance extends a hundred yojanas with the wind and fifty yojanas against the wind; it is supremely fragrant and delicious. The people of that land eat this rice; you, worthy ones, shall also eat this rice.' At that time, King Māndhātā again saw from afar that the ground was level and entirely of a dark blue-green color, and addressed his ministers and people: 'Do you all see this dark blue-green color?' They replied: 'Indeed, Great King!' 'This is soft grass four fingers in height, as supple as peacock feathers, each blade curling to the right, conforming to the body. The people of Uttarakuru all sit upon this grass; you too shall sit upon this grass.' At that time, King Māndhātā, seeing from afar city walls, towers, and battlements, addressed his ministers and people: 'Do you all see these towers and battlements on the level ground?' They replied: 'Indeed, Great King!' 'These are the dwellings where the people live.'

"At that time, the people of Uttarakuru, seeing King Māndhātā approaching from afar, each bearing gold bowls filled with silver grain and silver bowls filled with gold grain, addressed King Māndhātā: 'Welcome, Great King! This is the king's realm of Uttarakuru, endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a flourishing populace. May the great king govern the people of Uttarakuru; we shall follow the king's instructions.' At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā governed the people of Uttarakuru for countless hundreds and thousands of years.

"At that time, King Māndhātā again at another time gave rise to this thought: 'The domains I govern include Jambudvīpa, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace, where I caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace for seven days; I also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; I also possess the realm of Godānīya, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; I also possess the realm of Uttarakuru, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace. I have heard that wise and clever people of old said: "There are the Trāyastriṃśa heavens, where the lifespan is exceedingly long and the countenances fair, and there dwells a god named Śakra Devānām Indra." I shall now go to the Trāyastriṃśa heavens and receive the five heavenly endowments: heavenly lifespan, heavenly form, heavenly pleasure, heavenly supernatural powers, and heavenly supremacy. I now wish to go to the Trāyastriṃśa heavens.'

"At that time, Ānanda, having given rise to this thought, King Māndhātā vanished from Uttarakuru and proceeded to dwell in the Trāyastriṃśa heavens, together with his fourfold army, and went to the Sudharmā Hall. At that time, Śakra Devānām Indra, seeing King Māndhātā approaching from afar, addressed him: 'Welcome, Great King! Please come to this seat.' At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā took the seat and sat together with Śakra Devānām Indra. The two kings sat together without any distinction; their countenances, bearing, and features were exactly alike, differing only in the blinking of their eyes.

"At that time, King Māndhātā at another time gave rise to this thought: 'I possess Jambudvīpa, with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace, where I caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace for seven days; I also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha, with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace; I also possess the realm of Godānīya, with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace; I also possess the realm of Uttarakuru, with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace; and here too are these Trāyastriṃśa heavens with their long life, where I dwell at length.' At that time, the gods of Trāyastriṃśa assembled in the Sudharmā Hall and sat in their respective places. The gods of Trāyastriṃśa gave rise to this thought: 'This King Māndhātā is the king of Jambudvīpa, governing by the Dharma, possessed of the seven treasures, surrounded by a thousand sons, foremost and supreme among the four realms, governing the people by the Dharma without recourse to weapons or punishment.' At that time, Ānanda, Śakra Devānām Indra shared half his seat with King Māndhātā, and the two sat together. Their radiance and appearance were without distinction; their countenance and features were entirely the same, differing only in the blinking of their eyes.

"At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā enjoyed himself there amid the five objects of sensual desire without satiation, for countless hundreds of thousands of years, for countless myriads of years. At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā at another time gave rise to this thought: 'The domains I govern include Jambudvīpa, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace, where for seven days I caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace; I also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; I also possess the realm of Godānīya, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; I also possess the realm of Uttarakuru, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; and here too are these Trāyastriṃśa heavens with their long life and resplendent beauty. There is this Sudharmā Hall, fully appointed with four gardens. What are the four? The Nandana garden, the Jeweled Splendor garden, the Rough-and-Firm garden, and the Mixed Varieties garden — these are the four gardens. There are the Pārijāta trees and the Kovidāra trees, all in full flourish, their fragrance extending a hundred yojanas with the wind and fifty yojanas against the wind. This is where the gods of Trāyastriṃśa take their pleasure, enjoying the five objects of sensual desire through four months. This Sudharmā Hall is entirely of blue lapis lazuli; this is the throne of Śakra Devānām Indra, surrounded by a hundred terraces all made of the seven treasures. Each terrace has seven hundred pavilions; each pavilion has forty-nine women; each woman has forty-nine attendants — all under the dominion of Śakra Devānām Indra.' At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā again gave rise to this thought: 'I shall now displace Śakra Devānām Indra and govern the gods of Trāyastriṃśa myself.'

"At that time, Ānanda, as soon as King Māndhātā had this thought, he fell from Śakra Devānām Indra's seat back to Jambudvīpa, together with his fourfold army. He lost his supernatural powers, and his whole body was wracked with pain, as a man on the verge of death. The wheel-treasure disappeared, the elephant-treasure perished, the horse-treasure also came to an end, the jewel-treasure vanished, the woman-treasure perished, and the householder-treasure and the general-treasure perished.

"At that time, Ānanda, all five kinds of kinsmen of King Māndhātā gathered and came to his presence, addressing him: 'After the great king has passed away, should there be those who ask us: "What were Great King Māndhātā's final instructions at the time of his death?" what shall we answer them?' At that time, King Māndhātā replied: 'If after my death someone asks: "What were King Māndhātā's final instructions at the time of his death?" you should answer thus: "Know, worthy ones, that King Māndhātā ruled over the four continents, went to the Trāyastriṃśa heavens, and without ever finding satiation in the five objects of sensual desire, he died in that very state."'

"Ānanda, do you wish to know? Was that King Māndhātā of that time perhaps some other person? Do not think so. Why? Because that king, Ānanda, was none other than my own self. Through this illustration, Ānanda, you should know that the five objects of sensual desire are insatiable; attachment to desire, with its accumulation and storage, knows no satisfaction. What is meant by satisfaction? Only upon reaching the path of the noble ones does one truly find satisfaction."

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke these verses:

> Not through wealth and material pursuits does one come to know the satiation of desire;
> Joy is little, suffering and affliction great — the wise do not pursue this.
> If, within the five sensual pleasures, one ultimately finds no love or delight in them,
> When craving is exhausted, joy is attained — such is a disciple of the Perfectly Awakened One.
> Feeding on desire for koṭi years, in the end one enters hell;
> Where does this fundamental longing lead? Life is cut short by suffering.
> All phenomena are entirely impermanent; all that is born must decay and perish;
> Birth after birth, all returns to cessation — that cessation is the supreme joy.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, having heard what the Buddha had taught, rejoiced and undertook to practice it.
