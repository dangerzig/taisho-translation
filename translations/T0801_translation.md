# 佛說無常經
## Sūtra on Impermanence, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0801

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨)

---

> I bow my head in homage to the Unsurpassed One,
> Who ever gives rise to great vows and a heart of great compassion,
> To ferry beings across the current of birth-and-death,
> And cause them to reach nirvāṇa, the place of peace.
> Great generosity, guarding against wrong, patience without weariness,
> Single-minded skill in means and the power of right wisdom,
> Benefiting self and others, all perfectly fulfilled:
> Therefore he is called the Tamer, Teacher of Devas and Humans.
> I bow my head in homage to the treasury of the wondrous Dharma,
> Where threes, fours, twos, and fives are perfectly clear in principle,
> Where sevens and eights can open the gate of the four truths,
> And those who cultivate all arrive at the shore of the unconditioned.
> The Dharma-cloud and Dharma-rain moisten all beings,
> Removing feverish affliction and curing all ills.
> Those difficult to transform are made compliant,
> Guided according to their capacity, not by force.
> I bow my head in homage to the true and holy Saṃgha,
> The eight grades of noble ones who can leave behind defilement.
> The vajra mace of wisdom smashes the mountains of the heterodox,
> Forever severing the bonds that have bound us since beginningless time.
> From the Deer Park at the start to the Twin Trees at the end,
> Following the Buddha through a single lifetime, they spread the true teaching.
> Each, according to their original conditions, accomplished their transforming work,
> Then reduced the body to ash and extinguished knowledge in birthless quiescence.
> I bow my head in reverence to all the Three Jewels,
> The true cause that can universally bring deliverance.
> Those foolish and deluded in birth-and-death, long sunk and drowning,
> Are all caused to escape and reach bodhi.

> All who are born must return to death;
> Every countenance declines and withers.
> Even the strong are invaded by illness,
> And no one can escape this.
> Even the great Mount Sumeru,
> When the kalpa ends, is utterly destroyed.
> The great ocean, deep and bottomless,
> Likewise dries up completely.
> The great earth and the sun and moon,
> When their time comes, all perish.
> There has never been a single thing
> That was not swallowed by impermanence.
> From the highest heaven of neither-perception
> Down to the wheel-turning king,
> With seven treasures ever at his side
> And a thousand sons surrounding him:
> When his lifespan is exhausted,
> Not for an instant does it pause.
> He drifts again in the sea of death,
> Following conditions, receiving every suffering.
> Cycling through the three realms,
> Like the wheel that draws water from a well,
> Or like the silkworm making its cocoon,
> Spinning silk that binds itself.
> The unsurpassed World-Honored Ones,
> The pratyekabuddhas and assemblies of śrāvakas,
> Even they have cast off this impermanent body:
> How much more so for ordinary beings!
> Parents, wife, and children,
> Brothers and all one's kin:
> Before our eyes, birth-and-death divides us.
> How can one not lament and sigh?
> Therefore I urge all people:
> Listen carefully to the true and real Dharma.
> Together let us leave this impermanent place
> And walk through the gate of the deathless.
> The Buddha-Dharma is like sweet dew,
> Removing heat and bringing cool refreshment.
> Listen with a single mind,
> And it can extinguish all afflictions.

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "There are three kinds of dharmas in the world that are unlovely, without radiance, unpleasant, and unsatisfying. What are the three? They are old age, sickness, and death. Bhikṣus! These three, old age, sickness, and death, are truly unlovely, truly without radiance, truly unpleasant, and truly unsatisfying in the world. If old age, sickness, and death did not exist in the world, the Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened One, would not appear in the world to teach sentient beings the Dharma he has realized and the means of taming and subduing them. Therefore you should know that old age, sickness, and death are unlovely, without radiance, unpleasant, and unsatisfying in the world. Because of these three things, the Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened One, appears in the world and teaches sentient beings the Dharma he has realized and the means of taming and subduing them."

At that time the World-Honored One repeated this in verse:

> "Outward adornments all come to ruin,
> And the inner body decays and changes likewise.
> Only the supreme Dharma does not perish:
> Let all the wise discern this well.
> Old age, sickness, and death are loathed by all;
> The body becomes ugly and repulsive.
> The beauty of youth lingers but a while,
> And before long, all will see it withered and frail.
> Even if one's lifespan fills a hundred years,
> In the end one cannot escape the press of impermanence.
> The suffering of old age, sickness, and death follows always,
> Ever bringing harm to sentient beings."

When the World-Honored One had finished teaching this sūtra, the assembly of bhikṣus, the devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, and all the rest rejoiced greatly, believed, received it, and practiced accordingly.

---

> Always pursuing the objects of desire,
> Never performing wholesome deeds:
> How can one protect this body and life
> Without seeing death approaching?
> As the root of life-breath nears its end,
> The limbs and joints all separate.
> Every suffering comes together with death;
> At this time, sighs of regret are in vain.
> Both eyes roll upward,
> The knife of death descends according to one's karma.
> Thoughts are in confusion and panic;
> No one can come to the rescue.
> Long gasps press hard upon the chest,
> Short breaths leave the throat parched.
> The king of death dispatches his agents to seize the life;
> Relatives stand by to no avail.
> All the consciousnesses grow dim and dark,
> And one enters the perilous city.
> Kin and friends all abandon one,
> And one is dragged away by ropes.
> Led before King Yama,
> One receives retribution according to one's karma.
> Superior causes lead to birth in good destinies;
> Evil karma casts one into the hells.
> No eye is brighter than wisdom,
> No darkness deeper than delusion.
> No illness worse than enmity,
> No terror greater than death.
> All who live must surely die;
> Transgressions committed bring suffering close.
> Exert yourself in all three karmas,
> And constantly cultivate merit and wisdom.
> Relatives all depart;
> Wealth and goods are taken by others.
> Only the good roots one has held
> Serve as provisions on the perilous road.
> Like a tree beside the road
> Where one briefly rests, not long to stay:
> Carriages, horses, wife, and children
> Will before long be just like this.
> Like a flock of birds that roost together,
> Gathering at night but flying away at dawn:
> So at death one parts from kin and friends;
> The separation is just like this.
> Only the bodhi of the Buddha
> Is the true place of refuge.
> According to the sūtras I have briefly spoken;
> Let the wise reflect well upon this.
> Devas, asuras, yakṣas, and the rest
> Who come to hear the Dharma should do so with their whole heart.
> Guard and protect the Buddha-Dharma so it will long endure;
> Let each diligently practice the World-Honored One's teaching.
> All those who have come to listen,
> Whether on the ground or dwelling in the sky:
> Ever give rise to compassion toward the human world,
> And day and night abide in the Dharma.
> May all the worlds be forever at peace,
> And boundless merit and wisdom benefit all beings.
> May all transgressions be entirely removed,
> And may all depart from suffering and reach perfect quiescence.
> Ever anointing the body with the fragrance of precepts,
> Ever wearing the garment of concentration to sustain oneself,
> Adorned everywhere with the wondrous flowers of bodhi:
> Wherever one dwells, may there always be peace and joy.

## Instructions for the Time of Death

If a bhikṣu, bhikṣuṇī, upāsaka, or upāsikā sees a person who is about to die, whose body and mind are in pain, one should give rise to compassion and seek to deliver and benefit that person. One should instruct the dying person to bathe with fragrant water until clean and pure, put on fresh, clean garments, and sit at ease in right mindfulness and contemplation. If the sick person has no strength to do this alone, others should support and seat them. If the person cannot sit, one should simply have the dying person lie on the right side with palms joined and mind sincere, facing west.

Before the dying person, find a clean spot and, using only cow-dung fragrant paste, spread a layer on the ground, making it large or small as desired. Form a square altar, spread flowers on the ground, burn various incenses, and light lamps at the four corners. Within the altar, hang a painted image, and have the dying person gaze upon it continuously, mind after mind, contemplating its marks and excellences clearly and distinctly, so as to give rise to the aspiration for bodhi.

Then broadly explain that the three realms are difficult to dwell in, that the three evil paths of suffering and affliction are not places one should be born, and that only the bodhi of the Buddha is the true place of refuge. Because of taking refuge, one will certainly be born in the Buddha-lands of the ten directions, dwell among bodhisattvas, and receive subtle and wondrous joy.

Then ask the dying person: "In which Buddha-land do you wish to be born?" The dying person may answer: "I wish to be born in such-and-such a Buddha-world." At that time the person explaining the Dharma should, according to the dying person's wish, expound the causes and conditions of that Buddha-land, the sixteen contemplations and so forth, just as with the Western land of the Buddha of Immeasurable Life, explaining each in full, so that the dying person's mind delights in being born in that Buddha-land. After teaching the Dharma, one should further instruct the person to contemplate carefully, in whichever direction or land, the marks and excellences of that Buddha's body. After contemplating the marks, one should further instruct the person to request that Buddha and the bodhisattvas, speaking thus: "I bow my head in homage to the Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened One, and to the bodhisattva mahāsattvas. I pray you will have compassion and pity on me, and deliver and benefit me. I now earnestly request you to extinguish my many offenses. I further bring this disciple to follow the Buddhas and bodhisattvas and be born in a Buddha-land." A second and a third time one speaks thus.

After instructing the request, one should have the dying person call upon the name of that Buddha. When ten recitations are accomplished, one confers the three refuges and performs a broad and extensive confession. After the confession is complete, one further confers the bodhisattva precepts on the dying person. If the dying person is too ill to speak, someone else may receive the precepts and perform the confession on their behalf. Unless the mind is insincere, the offenses are still extinguished and the bodhisattva precepts are obtained.

After the precepts have been conferred, one supports the dying person to lie with head to the north and face toward the west. Whether the eyes are open or closed, one contemplates the Buddha's thirty-two marks and eighty secondary characteristics; and likewise for the Buddhas of the ten directions. One also teaches the person the four truths of cause and effect, the twelve links of dependent arising from ignorance through old age and death, and the contemplations of suffering, emptiness, and so forth.

When the person is about to die, the others present should simply recite the Buddha's name, voice after voice without cease. In reciting the Buddha's name, one follows the dying person's mind and calls upon that name; do not call upon a different Buddha, lest the dying person's mind give rise to doubt and confusion. As the dying person's life gradually nears its end, they will see a transformation-Buddha and an assembly of bodhisattvas, bearing wondrous fragrant flowers and coming to welcome the practitioner. When the practitioner sees them, joy arises; the body feels no pain, the mind is not scattered, and right view arises as if entering dhyāna concentration. Then immediately upon death, the person will certainly not fall back into the suffering of the hells, the animal realm, or the hungry ghost realm. Riding upon the teaching previously given, as swiftly as a strong person bending and extending the arm, one is immediately born before a Buddha.

For lay upāsakas and upāsikās, after death one should take the deceased's good new clothing and the personal items they used and divide them into three shares, offering them for the deceased to the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha. Through this, the deceased's karmic obstructions will be thoroughly eliminated, and the merit, virtue, and benefit obtained will be supreme. One should not dress the corpse in fine garments for burial. Why? Because there is no benefit in it. For monastics, bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, and novices, their clothing and non-clothing possessions should follow the regulations of the Vinaya; in other matters, the same rules apply as for laypeople.

When escorting the deceased to the place of burial, one should position the body downwind and lay it on its side, right side down, facing the sunlight. Upwind, one should set up a high seat, adorned in various ways, and invite a bhikṣu who can recite the sūtras to ascend the Dharma-seat and read the Sūtra on Impermanence for the deceased.

The mourning children should restrain their grief and not weep, and the others present should all, with their whole heart, burn incense for the deceased, scatter flowers, and make offerings to the high seat, the subtle and wondrous sūtra, and to the bhikṣu. Afterward, all should sit at ease with palms joined in reverence, and listen to the sūtra with a single mind. The bhikṣu should read it through slowly and completely.

Those who hear the sūtra should each contemplate the impermanence of their own body, that it will not last long, thinking of departing from the world and entering samādhi. After reading this sūtra, one should again scatter flowers and burn incense as offerings. Then one should further invite a bhikṣu to recite whatever dhāraṇī is appropriate, and chant it over clean, insect-free water three times seven recitations, then sprinkle it upon the deceased. One should further chant over clean yellow earth three times seven recitations and scatter it upon the deceased's body. Afterward, one may do as wished: place the remains in a stūpa, cremate them, leave them in a charnel ground, or bury them in the earth.

By the power of the merit and conditions of these acts, the deceased, throughout a hundred thousand million billion nayutas of kalpas, has whatever karmic obstructions of the ten evil deeds, the four grave offenses, the five acts of immediate retribution, or slander of the Mahāyāna sūtras: all are extinguished at once. Before all the Buddhas, the deceased obtains great merit, gives rise to wisdom and severs delusion, attains the six supernatural powers and the three knowledges, enters the first ground, journeys through the ten directions making offerings to all the Buddhas, hears and receives the right Dharma, and gradually accumulates boundless merit and wisdom. In the end, the person will certainly realize the unsurpassed bodhi, turn the right Dharma-wheel, deliver countless beings, and reach great perfect quiescence, accomplishing the most true and right awakening.
