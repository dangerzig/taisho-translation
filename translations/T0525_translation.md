# 佛說長者子懊惱三處經
## Sūtra of the Elder's Son and the Grief of Three Places, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0525

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time, in the city of Śrāvastī there was a great elder of immeasurable wealth, with fields, houses, cattle, and horses beyond counting. The family had no children of their own. By the custom of that country, if one dies without children, after death all wealth is confiscated by the government. The elder and his wife prayed and sacrificed to all the devas, the sun, moon, and stars, seeking a son to continue the family line, but they could not obtain one.

At that time, the elder's wife took refuge in the Three Treasures, received the five precepts, and practiced with diligence morning and evening, never daring to slacken. She then became pregnant. A wise woman knows five things: first, she knows her husband's mind; second, she knows whether her husband thinks of her or not; third, she knows the cause of her pregnancy; fourth, she can distinguish whether the child is male or female; fifth, she can distinguish good from bad. The elder's wife reported to the elder: "I am now pregnant." The elder was overjoyed, and day by day he provided her with offerings of clothing, food, and drink of the finest quality. When ten months were fulfilled, she gave birth to a boy. Five wet-nurses were assigned to care for the child: one for nursing, one for feeding, one for bathing, one for clothing, and one for holding. The child grew, and at fifteen or sixteen, the elder and his wife sought a bride for their son. They obtained the daughter of an elder, beautiful and lovely. At a garden pavilion outside the city, they held a great feast, inviting many guests. The food and drink were festive and the delicacies exquisite. People came from every direction and none were turned away. The feasting and entertainment continued for seven days.

Then the young husband and wife were strolling in the garden. There was a tree called the Aśoka tree, and upon it were flowers of exceptionally vivid color, like a pale crimson. The wife said to her husband: "I want those flowers." The husband climbed the tree to pick the flowers. The branch was thin and weak, and it immediately broke. The son fell to the ground and died instantly. When the father and mother heard that he had fallen from the tree and died, they rushed to the scene. The mother cradled his head, and the father held his two feet. They stroked and gazed at him, but he was gone and did not revive. The father and mother grieved, their insides torn apart. The guests, seeing this, were also moved to sorrow. How sudden is death! The guests had not even finished their feasting and entertainment, and yet the son had fallen from the tree and died. How impermanent!

At that time, the World-Honored Buddha, together with Ānanda, put on their robes, took their bowls, and entered the city on their alms round. They saw the elder and his wife, who had only this one son and he had fallen from a tree and died. Their weeping and grief were beyond words. The Buddha perceived where this child had come from: he had been reborn from Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, where his lifespan had ended, and he was reborn in the elder's family. Upon dying, he was reborn among the nāgas. The garuḍa bird-king then seized and devoured him. In three places there was grief, and in all three the funeral rites were performed.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Go to the elder's dwelling and explain the meaning, to dispel his suffering. If you do not, I fear they will die of grief."

Ānanda said: "Yes!" He then followed the Buddha and went to the elder's dwelling.

The elder, hearing that the Buddha had come to his place, was immediately overjoyed. He touched his forehead to the Buddha's feet. The Buddha asked the elder: "Why are you grieving so?"

The elder said to the Buddha: "I have no luck. I had just this one son. I arranged his marriage and held a feast for the guests, but before the entertainment was finished, he climbed a tree to pick flowers, fell to the ground, and died. My body is like stone, my heart like forged iron. I had just this one son, and he has abandoned me and died."

The Buddha told the elder: "What is born must die. What is made must decay. When the appointed time comes and life is at an end, it cannot be escaped or hidden. Set aside your worried thoughts and grieve no more."

Then the Buddha emitted radiance that illuminated the ten directions, enabling the elder to see the weeping of the parents in heaven above and among the nāgas. The Buddha said to the elder: "This child originally came from Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, where his lifespan had ended. He was reborn in your family. When his life ended, he was reborn among the nāgas, and the garuḍa bird-king immediately seized and devoured him. In three places, at the same time, there is weeping. Whose child is he?"

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> The devas in heaven above:
> Is he your child?
> Is he among the nāgas,
> A child of the nāga spirits?

Then the Buddha himself explained:

> He is not a child of the devas,
> Nor is he your child,
> Nor is he a child of the nāgas.
> The causes and conditions of birth and death
> Are impermanent, like an illusion.
> Nothing endures for long,
> Like a traveler passing through.

The Buddha said to the elder: "Death cannot be escaped. What is past cannot be pursued."

The elder said to the Buddha: "What were the sins and merits of this child's past life? He was born into a wealthy and powerful family, yet his life was cut short. How does this accord?"

The Buddha said: "In a past life, this child delighted in generosity and was respectful to others. Through the merit of this, he was born into a wealthy and powerful family. But he also delighted in hunting for sport and harmed many living creatures. Because of this, his life was cut short. Sin and merit follow a person as a shadow follows the body." The elder leaped with joy and attained the patience of the Dharma.

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the elder and his wife and the entire assembly were all filled with joy and received the teaching.
