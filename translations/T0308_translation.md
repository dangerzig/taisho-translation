# 佛說大方廣菩薩十地經
## Sūtra of the Vaipulya Ten Stages of the Bodhisattva Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0308

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Kiṅkārya (吉迦夜)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in the city of Rājagṛha, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty great bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas. Their names were: Jñānadhvaja Bodhisattva, Dharmadhvaja Bodhisattva, Candradhvaja Bodhisattva, Sūryadhvaja Bodhisattva, Anantadhvaja Bodhisattva, and the sixteen right-minded ones such as Bhadrapāla, and the sixty of the same aspiration such as Mañjuśrī, and Maitreya Bodhisattva and all the bodhisattvas of the Fortunate Kalpa.

At that time, the bodhisattva Akṣayamati rose from his seat, straightened his garments, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, scattered various jeweled flowers upon the Buddha, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I wish to ask something. May the World-Honored One open up and explain it for me."

At that time, the World-Honored One told the bodhisattva Akṣayamati: "Excellent! Excellent! Good man! If you have doubts or difficulties, ask freely. The Tathāgata shall explain them in accord with your questions, to your delight."

At that time, the bodhisattva Akṣayamati addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The bodhi-mind. The World-Honored One speaks of the bodhi-mind. How should one know that a bodhisattva accomplishes the bodhi-mind? What is the bodhi-mind, which is also no bodhi-mind? Beings also cannot be found, yet apart from bodhi, it is called the bodhi-mind. Bodhi cannot be spoken of, cannot be seen, has no counterpart, and beings also cannot be found. How can one gain understanding within these dharmas?"

The World-Honored One said: "Good man! Bodhi is all merely conventional speech and designation. Good man! Bodhi is neither speech nor convention nor designation. Good man! Just as bodhi is beyond speech, so too are beings, mind, and all dharmas. This should be known. If one understands the mind in this way, this is called the bodhi-mind. Bodhi is neither past, future, nor present. So too, beings and mind are neither past, future, nor present. If one knows in this way, the bodhisattva cannot be found, and there is absolutely nothing obtained there. To obtain nothing in all dharmas is called attaining the bodhi-mind. Just as with an arhat, the arhat's fruit also cannot be found, and there is absolutely nothing obtained there; it is merely by conventional speech that it is said to exist. To obtain nothing in all dharmas is called attaining. The bodhi-mind is also not an initial undertaking. The bodhisattva's meaning of 'crossing over' is what names a bodhisattva. There is neither that mind nor a designated mind; neither sentient beings nor designated sentient beings; neither śrāvakas nor designated śrāvakas; neither pratyekabuddhas nor designated pratyekabuddhas; neither bodhisattvas nor designated bodhisattvas; neither the conditioned nor designated conditioned dharmas; neither the unconditioned nor designated unconditioned dharmas; what can be obtained, has been obtained, or will be obtained. Yet, in accordance with the teaching, one speaks of all good roots arising equally. You should know that the first mind arises through the cause of the pāramitā of generosity, like the great earth that supports all. The second mind arises through the cause of the pāramitā of moral discipline, like the lion king of beasts, courageous and mighty. The third mind arises through the cause of the pāramitā of patience, like Nārāyaṇa, courageous and of great skillful power. The fourth mind arises to subdue all the enemies of the bonds, through the cause of the pāramitā of diligence, with the flowers of merit from various good roots blossoming. The fifth mind arises like Lord Śakra's great assembly at the kovidāra tree, through the cause of the pāramitā of dhyāna, like the sun's disc with immeasurable radiance. The sixth mind arises able to eliminate all darkness, through the cause of the pāramitā of wisdom, with the merit of one's original vows universally manifesting adornments. The seventh mind arises like a caravan leader who takes the merchants across the wilderness, through the cause of the pāramitā of skillful means, purifying all faults and fulfilling all wishes. The eighth mind arises replete with brightness and purity, like the full autumn moon fulfilling all aspirations. The ninth mind arises when all activities come to rest, like a poor person finding an inexhaustible treasury, like empty space, free from defilement, bright, and pure. The tenth mind arises reaching the ultimate shore of all merits, with inexhaustible wisdom and eloquence, the lord of all dharmas, like a wheel-turning king. Thus, good man! One who accomplishes these ten kinds of mind is called a bodhisattva, a mahāsattva, a foremost being, a most supreme being, a being free from sin, a most honored being. Yet, good man! In the ultimate meaning, neither bodhisattva nor mind can be found.

"Furthermore, good man! When the bodhisattva's first mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Dharma-Jewel Samādhi. When the second mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Immovable Samādhi. When the third mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Well-Abiding Samādhi. When the fourth mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Non-Retrogression Samādhi. When the fifth mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Gathering-Flowers Samādhi. When the sixth mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Sunlight Samādhi. When the seventh mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Meaning-Fulfillment Samādhi. When the eighth mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Wisdom-Torch Samādhi. When the ninth mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the All-Buddhas-Present-Before-One Samādhi. When the tenth mind arises, it is born through the gathering of the mind by the Śūraṅgama Samādhi.

"Furthermore, good man! The bodhisattva at the first bhūmi first beholds auspicious signs: three-thousand great-thousand buddha-lands filled with billions of rare and precious treasuries, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the second bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: three-thousand great-thousand buddha-lands flat as a palm of water, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the third bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: courageous and strong warriors holding weapons, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the fourth bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: māndārava-bearing winds coming from the four directions, scattering various flowers over the great earth, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the fifth bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: all women adorned with ornaments, wearing garlands of campaka flowers, garlands of liberation flowers, garlands of utpala flowers, and garlands of vārṣika flowers adorning their heads, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the sixth bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: many jeweled bathing pools filled with water of eight meritorious qualities, with four stairways, golden sand spread on the bottom, clear, calm, and transparent, seeing oneself sporting and playing within, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the seventh bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: seeing to the left and right great hells of cruel punishment and steep danger, seeing oneself transcending this difficulty, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the eighth bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: seeing upon one's shoulders lion kings, crowned with silk, their bodies majestic and strong, all poisonous and evil beasts subdued, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the ninth bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: a wheel-turning holy king, great ministers, kṣatriyas, and hundreds of thousands of retainers, transforming all sentient beings with the Dharma, holding silk canopies in their hands, their heads adorned with hundreds of thousands of treasures, all manifesting in one's field of vision. At the tenth bhūmi, one first beholds auspicious signs: seeing oneself become a Buddha, with a body of golden color and a circular halo of one fathom, surrounded by hundreds of billions of Brahmā devas, teaching the Dharma. Good man! These are the preliminary signs of the bodhisattva's ten bhūmis. This should be known: all are born from the samādhis of the ten bhūmis.

"Furthermore, good man! When the bodhisattva first gives rise to the aspiration, they attain the Most Supreme Dhāraṇī. At the second bhūmi, they attain the Difficult-to-Overcome Dhāraṇī. At the third bhūmi, the Well-Abiding Dhāraṇī. At the fourth bhūmi, the Hard-to-Withstand Dhāraṇī. At the fifth bhūmi, the Merit-Flower Universal Gathering of Adornments Dhāraṇī. At the sixth bhūmi, the Wisdom-Radiance Dhāraṇī. At the seventh bhūmi, the Superior-Progress Dhāraṇī. At the eighth bhūmi, eighty-four thousand dhāraṇīs, with the Calm-and-Clear Dhāraṇī as foremost. At the ninth bhūmi, sixty nayuta dhāraṇīs, with the Boundless Dhāraṇī as foremost. At the tenth bhūmi, Ganges-sand dhāraṇīs, with the Arising-of-the-Immeasurable Dhāraṇī as foremost. These are the dhāraṇīs of the bodhisattva's ten bhūmis. This should be known.

"Furthermore, good man! At the first bhūmi, the bodhisattva perfects the pāramitā of generosity. At the second bhūmi, moral discipline. At the third, patience. At the fourth, diligence. At the fifth, dhyāna. At the sixth, wisdom. At the seventh, skillful means. At the eighth, vows. At the ninth, powers. At the tenth, knowledge. All these pāramitās are accomplished throughout all the bhūmis.

"Furthermore, good man! There are ten kinds of the pāramitā of generosity. What are the ten? The pāramitā of generosity led by the root of faith. Led by samādhi. Led by great loving-kindness. Led by great compassion. Led by deep-hearted aspiration. Led by omniscience. Led by establishing sentient beings. Led by the four means of gathering. Led by protecting and upholding the correct Dharma. Led by delighting in seeking the Buddha-Dharma.

"Furthermore, there are ten kinds of the pāramitā of moral discipline. What are the ten? Led by freedom from the eight difficulties. Led by establishing the Buddha-Dharma. Led by transcending the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Led by purifying bodily karma. Led by purifying verbal karma. Led by purifying mental karma. Led by adorning the mind. Led by purifying the human world. Led by fulfilling all wishes and aspirations. Led by non-harm.

"Furthermore, there are ten kinds of the pāramitā of patience. What are the ten? Led by the power of patience. Led by maturing sentient beings. Led by aspiration. Led by patience with the profound Dharma. Led by severing afflictions. Led by freedom from anger. Led by not accounting for the body. Led by not accounting for one's life. Led by cutting off all ignorance. Led by contemplating the equality of all dharmas.

"Furthermore, there are ten kinds of the pāramitā of diligence. What are the ten? Led by the root of diligence. Led by the power of diligence. Led by equal skillful means. Led by the foundations of mindfulness. Led by acting on behalf of all sentient beings with one's body. Led by following the speech and mind of all sentient beings in their turnings. Led by non-retrogression. Led by supreme effort. Led by subduing all the enemies of afflictions. Led by the knowledge of all modes.

"Furthermore, there are ten kinds of the pāramitā of dhyāna. What are the ten? Led by the root of concentration. Led by the power of concentration. Led by equal concentration. Led by the liberations of the dhyānas. Led by good dharmas. Led by correct location. Led by non-distraction. Led by incorrect location. Led by destroying all bonds and proclivities. Led by the fulfillment of the aggregate of concentration.

"Furthermore, there are ten kinds of the pāramitā of wisdom. What are the ten? Led by the root of wisdom. Led by the power of wisdom. Led by correct views. Led by correct mindfulness. Led by skillful means regarding the aggregates. Led by contemplating the elements and sense-bases. Led by the noble truths. Led by non-obstruction. Led by removing all wrong views. Led by the patience of non-arising dharmas.

"Furthermore, there are ten kinds of the pāramitā of skillful wisdom. What are the ten? Led by the skillful practice of fulfilling the hopes of sentient beings. Led by establishing sentient beings. Led by great compassion. Led by tirelessness in transforming sentient beings. Led by transcending the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Led by entering the pāramitās. Led by contemplating all dharmas as they truly are. Led by inconceivable power. Led by non-retrogression. Led by subduing all māras.

"What is the meaning of 'pāramitā'? The meaning of the practice of surpassing progress and fulfillment is the meaning of pāramitā. The fulfillment of supreme and wondrous wisdom is the meaning of pāramitā. Not being attached to conditioned or unconditioned dharmas is the meaning of pāramitā. Perceiving the faults of birth and death is the meaning of pāramitā. Awakening those who are not yet awakened is the meaning of pāramitā. Revealing the inexhaustible Dharma-treasury is the meaning of pāramitā. The complete fulfillment of unobstructed liberation is the meaning of pāramitā. Perceiving the equality of generosity is the meaning of pāramitā. Perceiving the equality of moral discipline, patience, diligence, single-mindedness, and wisdom is the meaning of pāramitā. Definitive skillful means is the meaning of pāramitā. Knowing the dharma-nature of all sentient beings is the meaning of pāramitā. The fulfillment of the patience of non-arising dharmas is the meaning of pāramitā. The fulfillment of the stage of non-retrogression is the meaning of pāramitā. Purifying the buddha-lands is the meaning of pāramitā. Maturing sentient beings is the meaning of pāramitā. The fulfillment of the seat of enlightenment is the meaning of pāramitā. Subduing all māras is the meaning of pāramitā. The fulfillment of all Buddha-Dharmas is the meaning of pāramitā. The fulfillment of the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, and eighteen exclusive dharmas is the meaning of pāramitā. Gathering and receiving all beings to be transformed is the meaning of pāramitā. The three turnings and twelve practices of the Dharma-wheel is the meaning of pāramitā."

At that time, in the assembly there was a deva named Siṃhadhvaja-anāvaraṇa-prabhā, who addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How extraordinary and rare! This is called the complete perfection; this is called the ultimate merit of all Buddha-Dharmas."

The World-Honored One answered: "Just so, just so, devas! If there are good men and good women who hear this Dharma and correctly contemplate it, they should be known as non-retrogressing bodhisattvas. Why? Deva prince! Because the good roots of such good men and good women have matured, they are able to hear this sūtra through their ear-faculties. Because all good roots have matured, having heard this sūtra, they will never be separated from true contemplation; never separated from the Buddha World-Honored Ones; never separated from the Buddha-Dharma; never separated from turning the Dharma-wheel; never separated from the Ocean-Seal Dhāraṇī; never separated from the Inexhaustible Dhāraṇī; never separated from the Dhāraṇī of Entering the Practices of All Sentient Beings; never separated from the Inexhaustible-Light Sun-Banner Dhāraṇī; never separated from the Stainless-Moon-Mark Dhāraṇī; never separated from the Stainless-Equal-Arising-Mark Dhāraṇī. If a bodhisattva attains these dhāraṇīs, they can simultaneously transform their body into a Buddha, pervading the ten directions to teach sentient beings. Yet in all dharmas there is neither death nor departure, while they ferry beings across; sentient beings also cannot be found, and the words and syllables used to teach the Dharma also cannot be found; displaying birth and death, there is no arising; knowing the equality of all dharmas, without going and without coming, neither acting nor not-acting." When this Dharma was taught, three thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of non-arising dharmas.

"Furthermore, deva prince! If there are those who read, recite, and uphold this Dharma, they will never be separated from the Dhāraṇī That Ends All Entanglements; never separated from the Vajra-Firm Dhāraṇī That Shatters the Mountain of All Afflictions; never separated from the Dhāraṇī of Wordless Speech That Universally Enters All Pāramitās; never separated from the Dhāraṇī That Can Speak Various Named Phrases and Languages; never separated from the Stainless-Space-Born Inexhaustible-Seal Dhāraṇī; never separated from the Dhāraṇī That Accomplishes Immeasurable Buddha-Bodies and Exhausts All Births." When this Dharma was taught, immeasurable sentient beings all attained Dharma-patience, and immeasurable sentient beings gave rise to the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

When the Buddha had spoken this, Bodhisattva Akṣayamati, the deva Siṃhadhvaja-anāvaraṇa-prabhā, and all the great śrāvakas, devas, humans, and asuras, paid homage to the Buddha and all greatly rejoiced.
