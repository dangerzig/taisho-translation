# 大方廣入如來智德不思議經
## The Vaipulya Sūtra of Entering the Inconceivable Wisdom and Virtue of the Tathāgata

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0304

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Śikṣānanda (實叉難陀)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Magadha, at the site of tranquil extinction, in the Hall of Universal Light. That place was established through the gathering of immeasurable merit. All who saw it could not help but give rise to great joy and delight, forever abandoning all thoughts of disdain. There the Buddha sat upon a jeweled lotus lion-throne, having realized pure and equal awakening. His conduct was non-dual. He dwelt in the abiding of the Buddha, entirely equal to all Buddhas, having arrived at the non-retrogressive Dharma of non-obstruction. In all his actions, none could prevail over him. He constantly performed the Buddha's work without ever resting. He embodied the Dharma without marks and dwelt in the inconceivable. What the three ages produced he understood without any difference. His body filled all worlds. His wisdom comprehended all dharmas, never confused. He awakened to all actions and severed all nets of doubt. His body was subtle and marvelous, impossible to distinguish. He had reached the ultimate shore of non-dual wisdom. He was revered by all bodhisattvas. He dwelt in the Tathāgata's liberation of non-differentiation. He had entered the Buddha's level of equality, without center or border. He penetrated all of empty space and the dharma-realm. Through kalpas reaching the end of the future, he constantly turned the Dharma wheel.

He was together with a great assembly of sixty-two hundred million bhikṣus, all of whom had thoroughly comprehended the true mark of all dharmas. Their self-nature was equal, like empty space, with no attachment. They had forever departed from all hindrances of afflictions. They could enter into the wisdom and skillful means of all Tathāgatas. Within one dharma they comprehended all dharmas. The wisdom of non-discrimination was always present before them. They constantly and diligently cultivated the path toward omniscience. Their minds never retrogressed. They had all accomplished the wisdom of the other shore. According to all circumstances, their methods of skillful practice were all complete.

Their names were Śāriputra, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, Mahākātyāyana, Mahākāśyapa, Nadī-Kāśyapa, Gayā-Kāśyapa, Mahākapphiṇa, Revata, Aniruddha, Subhūti, Pūrṇa Maitrāyaṇīputra, Gavāṃpati, Cūḍapanthaka, Balapāṇḍita, Khadiravanika, Śreṣṭhin Cunda, Mahākauṣṭhila, Nanda, Rāhula, and Ānanda. These great disciples were the foremost.

There were also sixty hundred million bhikṣuṇīs, all of whom had long accumulated pure white dharmas. They were close to the Buddha's omniscience. They comprehended skillful means. They had realized that all dharmas are without nature and without marks. They rested securely in the true reality. They understood that all dharmas are without arising and without ceasing, with nothing to eliminate or sever. They dwelt in the samādhi of inconceivable liberation. According to whichever sentient beings could be tamed and subdued, they manifested various kinds of deportment and physical forms, yet within these they made no discriminations. Their names were Mahāprajāpatī and Yaśodharā, who were the foremost.

There were also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas from ten buddha-lands, numbering as many as inexpressible hundreds of thousands of koṭīs of nayutas of motes of dust, all at the stage of one remaining birth before succession, who had come from other realms and gathered here. They could universally enter all world systems in the ten directions. They had attained the wisdom of skillful means along the path to nirvāṇa. They rested securely in the Dharma-gate of skillful means whereby the bodhisattva contemplates and accomplishes the maturation of all sentient beings. They gathered in sentient beings and caused them to sever all frivolous attachments. They comprehended that all dharmas are without border or center. They knew that the wholesome and unwholesome karmic fruits of sentient beings are all unobtainable, and yet are also never destroyed.

They could also fathom the aspirations, afflictions, faculties, and actions of beings. They fully upheld the meaning and phrases of all the Dharma spoken by the Buddhas of the three ages, without any forgetting. They penetrated all conditioned and unconditioned, mundane and supramundane dharmas. They accomplished the wisdom-wheel of the Buddhas of the three ages.

In every successive thought, they manifested descent from the heavenly palace, birth, leaving home, practicing austerities, going to the bodhi tree, subduing Māra, becoming a Buddha, turning the true Dharma wheel, and entering parinirvāṇa. They never wearied of or forsook any sentient being. They awakened them and caused them to arouse the great bodhi-mind. They could, within the mental objects of one sentient being, enter the mental objects of all sentient beings. They accomplished the natural wisdom while receiving the bodhisattva body. The practice of omniscience never retrogressed in them. Though they constantly cultivated, they were without any action.

They could remain in the world for immeasurable kalpas to teach the Dharma for a single sentient being, protecting and upholding the Dharma-treasury and continuing the Buddha's lineage. In places where there was no Buddha, they manifested the appearance of a Buddha in the world, equal in number to sentient beings, demonstrating the achievement of perfect awakening. Sitting cross-legged, they filled the ten directions with the completeness of great wisdom. They purified and adorned all defiled lands. They eliminated all karmic obstructions of bodhisattvas. The merits of empty space and the dharma-realm were all fully complete in them. They realized the true reality of all dharmas without obstruction. They obtained the seal of the equal wisdom of all dharmas. With this seal they knew that the self-nature of all dharmas is equal. What they saw and heard was like a reflection and an echo.

They dwelt in the samādhi of inconceivable liberation and freely sported in the Śūraṅgama samādhi. They accomplished the dhāraṇī-gate that produces all the marks and characteristics of the Buddhas. They possessed the pure practices and vows of the Buddhas of the three ages. They accomplished the supreme aspiration of Samantabhadra.

Whenever Buddhas appeared in the world, they went to their presence and reverently urged them to teach. Within one pore, they manifested all world systems. Within one pore, they manifested in the ten directions everything from the initial descent to the final appearance of parinirvāṇa. They manifested the assemblies of all the Buddhas of the ten directions within one Buddha's assembly, and one Buddha's assembly within the assemblies of all the Buddhas of the ten directions. They manifested the worlds of the ten directions within their own bodies, and within their own bodies they manifested the bodies of all sentient beings, teaching them immeasurable essentials of the Dharma as appropriate.

They manifested the body of one Buddha entering the bodies of all Buddhas, and the bodies of all Buddhas entering the body of one Buddha. Within the body of one sentient being they manifested immeasurable sentient beings' bodies; within the bodies of all sentient beings they manifested one sentient being's body. Within one body of this life they manifested the bodies of the three ages; within the bodies of the three ages they manifested one body of this life.

They manifested the past entering the future, and the future entering the past; the past entering the present, and the present entering the past. Within one body they entered deep dhyāna and from immeasurable, countless bodies they arose; from immeasurable, countless bodies they entered deep dhyāna and from one body they arose.

Within one Buddha's body they manifested the bodies of all sentient beings; within the bodies of all sentient beings they manifested one Buddha's body. Within the body of a sentient being they manifested the pure Dharma-body; within the pure Dharma-body they manifested the body of a sentient being. They manifested one buddha-land with all its adornments as all the pure lands; they manifested all buddha-lands with their adornments as one pure land. They manifested the worlds of the ten directions entering one pore. For all sentient beings, they displayed the vow-power of all Buddhas, and universally throughout the ten directions, according to whichever beings could be taught, they manifested the unsurpassed, perfect bodhi. For countless kalpas, in each and every world, they practiced the bodhisattva path without rest. Within a single mote of dust they accommodated boundless, immeasurable, incalculable world systems, yet caused no sentient being to feel confined. They compressed immeasurable inconceivable kalpas into a single instant, and extended a single instant into immeasurable inconceivable kalpas. In a single kṣaṇa, universally throughout all world systems in the ten directions, according to the various kinds of sentient beings, whether born from eggs, wombs, moisture, or transformation; whether with form or without form, with color or without color; without feet, with two feet, four feet, or many feet; devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, Śakra, Brahmā, world-protectors, humans, non-humans, and whoever else could be tamed: they manifested all manner of deportment and conduct, yet their bodies and minds were without discriminative function.

These bodhisattvas had all attained such skillful means, together with other immeasurable asaṃkhyeya merits. Their names were Bodhisattva Samantabhadra, Bodhisattva Universal-Eye, Bodhisattva Universal-Transformation, Bodhisattva Universal-Wisdom, Bodhisattva Universal-Sight, Bodhisattva Universal-Light, Bodhisattva Universal-Radiance, Bodhisattva Universal-Illumination, Bodhisattva Universal-Banner, Bodhisattva Universal-Awakening, Bodhisattva Great-Swiftness, Bodhisattva Maintaining-Great-Swiftness, Bodhisattva Great-Miraculous-Transformation, Bodhisattva King-of-Great-Miraculous-Transformation, Bodhisattva Great-Vigor, Bodhisattva Great-Heroism, Bodhisattva Great-Impetuosity, Bodhisattva Power-of-Great-Impetuosity, Bodhisattva Great-Sentient-Being, Bodhisattva Great-Fragrant-Elephant, Bodhisattva Great-Moon, Bodhisattva Wondrous-Moon, Bodhisattva Merit-Moon, Bodhisattva Jewel-Moon, Bodhisattva Universal-Moon, Bodhisattva Dharma-Stainless-Moon, Bodhisattva Vairocana-Moon, Bodhisattva Renowned-Moon, Bodhisattva Radiant-Moon, Bodhisattva Full-Moon, Bodhisattva Brahmā-Voice, Bodhisattva Brahmā-Lord-Thunder-Voice, Bodhisattva Earth-Voice, Bodhisattva Dharma-Realm-Voice, Bodhisattva Destroyer-of-All-Māra-Voice, Bodhisattva Shaker-of-the-Dharma-Drum-Voice, Bodhisattva Universal-Awakening-Voice, Bodhisattva Non-Discriminating-Voice, Bodhisattva Above-the-Earth-Voice, Bodhisattva Eclipser-of-All-Sounds-Voice, Bodhisattva Equal-Treasury, Bodhisattva Bodhisattva Lotus-Treasury, Bodhisattva Wisdom-Treasury, Bodhisattva Great-Wisdom, Bodhisattva Superior-Wisdom, Bodhisattva Renowned-Wisdom, Bodhisattva Unsurpassed-Wisdom, Bodhisattva Bodhisattva Certain-Illumination-Lamp, Bodhisattva Moon-Lamp, Bodhisattva Sun-Lamp, Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, Bodhisattva Mahāsthāmaprāpta, Bodhisattva Vajragarbha, Bodhisattva Bodhisattva Stainless-Heroism, Bodhisattva Vajra-Wisdom, Bodhisattva Destroyer-of-All-Hindrances, Bodhisattva Subduer-of-Māra, Bodhisattva Jewel-Topknot, Bodhisattva Thousand-Lights, Bodhisattva Maitreya. These bodhisattva-mahāsattvas were the foremost.

There were also immeasurable, inconceivable, inestimable devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, Śakra, Brahmā, and world-protectors, all gathered from buddha-lands in the ten directions.

At that time, in this world there were also a hundred billion six-desire heavenly devas, with Māra-kings, princes, and chief merchants as the foremost, together with immeasurable retinues of devas, who all came to the Buddha's place to see the Tathāgata, bow, make offerings, and hear the Dharma.

There were also a hundred billion Great Brahmā Kings, up through a hundred billion Akaniṣṭha Devas with Maheśvara as the foremost, also with immeasurable retinues of devas, who all came to the Buddha's place to see the Tathāgata, bow, make offerings, and hear the Dharma. There were also a hundred billion assemblies of the eight classes and immeasurable humans, non-humans, upāsakas, upāsikās, each with their retinues, who all came to the Buddha's place to see the Tathāgata, bow, make offerings, and hear the Dharma.

There were also all the spirits of grasses, trees, thickets, forests, and herbs, and the spirits of Mount Sumeru, Mahāmeru, Mount Mucilinda, Mahāmucilinda, the Himalayas, the Iron Ring Mountains, and all other mountain spirits; and all the spirits of rivers, seas, lakes, ponds, countries, settlements, and the spirits of the eight classes' palaces, also with their retinues, who all came to the Buddha's place to see the Tathāgata, bow, make offerings, and hear the Dharma.

There were also a hundred billion sun and moon devas, and the Nāga King Anavatapta, each surrounded by immeasurable retinues, who all came to the Buddha's place to see the Tathāgata, bow, make offerings, and hear the Dharma.

These great multitudes, through the Buddha's spiritual power, did not obstruct one another and felt no confinement.

At that time, the World-Honored One's radiance shone forth brilliantly, eclipsing the entire assembly. It was like the white moon on the fifteenth night, free from all clouds, its radiance shining forth brilliantly, eclipsing all the stars. It was also like Mount Sumeru, king of mountains, resting unmoved. The Tathāgata's radiance universally eclipsed all the devas of Śakra and Brahmā, towering in majestic supremacy, just like this.

At that time, the youth Mañjuśrī addressed the Bodhisattva Destroyer-of-All-Hindrances: "The Tathāgata now rests here, his body unmoving. Do you know this?"

He answered: "Mañjuśrī! Although the Tathāgata is now in this assembly resting unmoved, there are devas and humans who see him leaving home and practicing austerities; or see him going to the bodhi tree, settling at the site of the Way, subduing the forces of Māra, and achieving perfect awakening, with devas, nāga kings, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, Śakra, Brahmā, and world-protectors all praising together: 'Excellent, Great Teacher! You have conquered your enemies!' Or they see Śakra, Brahmā, and the world-protecting devas urging him to teach the Dharma. Or they see him teaching the Dharma of generosity; or teaching the Dharma of morality, patience, vigor, meditation, wisdom, skillful means, powers, vows, and knowledge. Or they see him teaching the Dharma of the Śrāvaka Vehicle; or the Dharma of the Pratyekabuddha Vehicle; or the Dharma of the Supreme Vehicle. Or they see him teaching the dharmas of birth among animals, hungry ghosts, the realm of King Yama, the Four Heavenly Kings, the Heaven of the Thirty-Three, and up through the Brahmā palace. Or they see him teaching the dharmas of birth in the human realm; or the dharmas of becoming a wheel-turning king.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! Among this assembly, some see the Tathāgata's body as one fathom tall; or one krośa, or two krośas; or half a yojana, or one yojana, or two yojanas, or ten yojanas, or a hundred, a thousand, or ten thousand yojanas; or fifty thousand, a hundred thousand, a million, or five million, or even surpassing all calculable numbers of yojanas.

"Or they see the Buddha's body as the color of true gold, or lapis lazuli, or the color of dark-blue maṇi, or great dark-blue maṇi, or radiant maṇi, or red-lotus maṇi, or Śakrābhilagna maṇi, or vajra-radiant maṇi, or celestial-radiant maṇi, or sun-and-moon-radiant maṇi, or crystal maṇi, or sphaṭika maṇi, or sovereign-king maṇi, or gathering-all-radiance maṇi, or lion-garland maṇi, or lion-banner maṇi, or ocean-dwelling-pure-radiance maṇi, or wish-fulfilling maṇi. According to whichever form the sentient beings should see the Tathāgata in to be tamed, each sees something different. According to whichever Dharma the sentient beings should hear the Tathāgata teach to be matured, each hears something different. According to whichever teaching they should follow and practice, each practices as taught and all attain accomplishment.

"Mañjuśrī! Suppose the ten directions were filled with immeasurable, inconceivable, inestimable devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, Śakra, Brahmā, world-protectors, humans, non-humans, and the like, packed as densely as a bamboo thicket, sugarcane, or sesame seeds. If any sentient being should see the Tathāgata and thereby be tamed, each sees the Buddha's form differently, facing them directly and dwelling within one fathom, teaching them the Dharma. As taught, they practice and all attain accomplishment. Although the Tathāgata performs such varied deeds, he naturally responds and manifests without discrimination.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as the full moon at midnight appears before every sentient being in Jambudvīpa, and yet the moon never thinks or discriminates 'I cause all sentient beings to each see me appear,' but naturally such things arise: the Tathāgata is the same. Although he universally appears before all sentient beings, he does not discriminate 'I cause all sentient beings to see me present before them.' It is simply that according to whichever sentient being can be tamed, each sees the Buddha appear before them. Why? Because the Tathāgata's dharmas are unique to each being.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as all sentient beings, because of the power of upper, middle, and lower karmic fruits, produce actions of three grades, and yet these actions never themselves generate the three kinds of discrimination, but simply through karma such upper, middle, and lower actions naturally arise: the Tathāgata is the same. Because of the power of the karmic fruits of sentient beings, each sees the Buddha differently. The Tathāgata also has no thoughts of upper, middle, or lower, yet naturally responds and manifests such things.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as a pure crystal placed upon various colored cloths takes on various colors: if placed on yellow cloth it appears yellow; on blue or red cloth it appears blue or red. Wherever it is placed, though it takes on that color, the pure crystal never discriminates. The Tathāgata is the same. Moved by sentient beings, he takes on various colors. If sentient beings should see the golden color and thereby be tamed, they see the golden color. If some should see the colors of lapis lazuli, pearls, dark-blue maṇi, great dark-blue maṇi, gathering-all-radiance maṇi, ocean-dwelling-pure-radiance maṇi, lion-garland maṇi, lion-banner maṇi, lightning-lamp maṇi, crystal maṇi, or any of these jeweled colors and thereby be tamed, they see the Tathāgata in those many jeweled forms. If some should be tamed by the forms of Śakra, Brahmā, or the world-protectors, they see those forms; and so on, up to and including the forms of hell, hungry ghosts, animals, King Yama's realm, the form and formless realms, egg-born, womb-born, moisture-born, transformation-born, with form, without form, with color, without color, with perception, without perception, neither with perception nor without perception: according to whatever destiny's deportment and form would tame them, they see the Tathāgata in all manner of such forms. Yet the Buddha never discriminates in thought, saying 'I shall cause this sentient being to see only the golden color and not lapis lazuli; to see only lapis lazuli and not dark-blue; and so on, up to seeing only lion-garland maṇi and not lion-banner maṇi color.' Though he has no such differentiated thoughts, in all places these various colored forms naturally appear.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as in the place where the Sovereign Maṇi King arises, iron does not arise, and the Maṇi King never thinks 'In that jewel-ground, let only myself arise and not iron,' yet iron naturally does not arise there: so it is with the buddha-land where the Tathāgata arises. In that land there are naturally none of the heterodox doctrines, evil thieves and upheavals, the five uninterrupted sins, the ten unwholesome actions, or unlawful royal commands, nor any light of the sun, moon, devas, maṇi jewels, fire, or lightning, nor any reckoning of minutes, days, months, and years. Apart from what the Buddha manifests through transformation to mature sentient beings, though there are manifestations, there is no discrimination. On account of sentient beings, all manner of things naturally arise.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as the light of a great dark-blue maṇi jewel causes everything it touches to appear in the color of the dark-blue jewel, yet the jewel never produces a differentiated thought: the Tathāgata is the same. All who are touched by the light of the Buddha's mental focus are without exception transformed into the color of omniscience. Yet the Buddha has no differentiated thought; these things naturally arise.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as a well-polished great lapis lazuli jewel causes all ornaments of hands, feet, head, and neck placed near it, through the jewel's power, to appear brilliant and distinct: the Tathāgata is the same. Wherever there are practitioners in whatever deportment or dwelling, through the Buddha's power, their practices all naturally increase in excellence. Yet the Buddha never stirs a discriminating thought; these things naturally arise.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as all plants and trees dwell upon the earth and each grows, yet the earth has no kinds of discrimination: the Tathāgata is the same. He causes the good roots of all sentient beings to dwell upon the Tathāgata and each to grow, yet in truth there has never been a differentiated thought; these things naturally arise.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as a great cloud covers all grasses, trees, thickets, and forests, and equally pours down sweet rain. Wherever the rain of one flavor reaches, it causes all grasses and trees to grow, with various colors and flavors each different. Yet the cloud never discriminates; naturally these various differences arise. The Tathāgata is the same. Raising the cloud of perfect awakening that covers all, according to the various good roots, various aspirations, various understandings, and various liberations that sentient beings have previously accumulated, he equally pours down the rain of the Dharma, causing the good roots of all sentient beings, according to their capacity, each to grow. Yet the Tathāgata does not discriminate in thought: 'I should cause this sentient being's good roots to produce the wisdom of a śrāvaka; I should cause this sentient being's good roots to produce the wisdom of a pratyekabuddha; I should cause this sentient being's good roots to produce the wisdom of a Tathāgata; I should cause this sentient being's good roots to produce a birth in the Four Heavenly Kings' Heaven or the Heaven of the Thirty-Three, up through the Pure Abode Heavens; I should cause this sentient being's good roots to become a king, or the various places of birth among humans.' Yet though the Buddha has no discriminating thought, according to the good roots accumulated by sentient beings and the power of their aspirations, these various things naturally arise, because the Buddha has abandoned attachment to all places and is without discrimination.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as the sun, upon appearing, emits immeasurable billions of hundreds of thousands of rays that dispel all darkness throughout Jambudvīpa, and yet the sun never discriminates 'I shall dispel darkness' but naturally the event of dispelling darkness arises: the Tathāgata's sun-wheel is also like this. Having appeared in the world, it emits immeasurable billions of rays of wisdom that extinguish the darkness of all views in the world. Apart from what the Buddha manifests through his awesome power to mature sentient beings, the Tathāgata does not discriminate 'I cause sentient beings to see that I am now destroying or shall destroy.' In all places, these various things naturally arise, because the Buddha has abandoned attachment to all places and is without discrimination.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as a magician magically creates many images, and though there are many different kinds of forms, the magic has no discrimination and cannot be described; it is without arising and without ending, without words and without voice, without location and without substance, without marks, inconceivable, without duality and without conduct, without equal and without counterpart. It is only because of the magician that these various forms appear. The Tathāgata is the same. Because of sentient beings, he enters into the various places of deportment and conduct, and all see him. Yet in truth the Tathāgata cannot be described; he is without arising and without ending, without words and without voice, without location, without nature, without marks, without duality and without conduct. He is equal to the true dharma-realm and cannot be encountered.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as the sun, reflected by Mount Sumeru, causes the sentient beings of the four continents to see it in different ways: some see it just rising, some see it at noon, some see it declining, some see it just setting, some see it at midnight, some see it near dawn. Yet it is but one sun-disc, and according to the four quarters beneath the sky, the sentient beings each see it differently. The sun does not discriminate; simply because of the mountain's obstruction, the four continents naturally see it differently. The Tathāgata is the same. Within a single assembly, some see the Tathāgata about to achieve perfect awakening, or having achieved perfect awakening; about to enter nirvāṇa, or having entered nirvāṇa. Or they see that the Buddha has been teaching for ten years since his awakening, and so on up to inexpressible kalpas. Or they see that the nirvāṇa has passed by ten years, and so on up to hundreds of thousands of billions of kalpas. Or they see the Tathāgata teaching the Dharma in the world for ten, twenty, or forty years. Or they see the Dharma abiding, or they see the Dharma perishing. Yet the Buddha has never had a differentiated thought. Because of sentient beings, these various things naturally arise.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as a great wind blows through Jambudvīpa, and all the grasses and trees have their branches and leaves tossed about, swaying and rising to the east, west, south, and north. Yet the grasses and trees have no kinds of discrimination; simply because of the wind, the various appearances arise. The Tathāgata is the same. He is always without discrimination. Through the power of sentient beings, in every successive thought, there are seen to arise such immeasurable actions and deportment, up to his mental focus on sentient beings, causing them for such-and-such kalpas to be freed from the destinies of hell, animals, hungry ghosts, and King Yama.

"Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata has accomplished such immeasurable subtle and marvelous merits."

"Mañjuśrī! The mental focus of the Tathāgata in a single thought: even if the great bodhisattvas were to dwell in the samādhi of inconceivable liberation for immeasurable hundreds of thousands of nayutas of kalpas, they could not know the limit of its merit.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as the sun-disc rises from the great ocean and dwells in empty space, emitting immeasurable billions of nayutas of rays that universally illuminate all cities, settlements, and villages, dispelling great darkness, drying up stagnant pools, causing all grasses, trees, thickets, and forests to grow and ripen, setting in motion all activities and tasks. Its light and reflection universally enter all rivers and pools, yet it never departs from its original function. Though the sun has no kinds of discrimination, naturally these various things appear. The Tathāgata is the same. Having emerged from the ocean of existence and dwelling in the empty space of the Dharma, he emits immeasurable billions of rays of wisdom that universally illuminate all world systems in the ten directions, extinguishing the membrane of ignorance over the eyes of sentient beings, drying up all the turbid streams of afflictions, and causing the good roots, merit, and wisdom of sentient beings to grow and ripen. Though he manifests all these things simultaneously, he is also always serenely unmoved in his original abode. Because the Buddha has departed from thought-discrimination, he naturally responds and manifests all these appearances.

"Mañjuśrī! If there were a good man or good woman who for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, day by day offered celestial food of a hundred flavors and celestial fine garments to all the Buddhas and śrāvaka assemblies in world systems as numerous as motes of dust throughout the ten directions; and after those Buddhas' passing, for each and every Buddha built stūpas as numerous as motes of dust throughout the ten directions, filling every one of the ten-directional worlds. Each stūpa would be made of Jambu-river gold and lightning-light maṇi interspersed with gathering-all-radiance jewels, surrounded by railings, with jeweled banners raised high, jeweled bells streaming their sound, anointed with serpent-guardian sandalwood, covered with a net of sovereign maṇi kings. Above them would be clouds of celestial jeweled canopies, clouds of jeweled banners and streamers, clouds of wondrous flowers and incense, clouds of maṇi kings, and clouds of wish-fulfilling jewels, hovering and scattering through the sky, filling the three-thousand world system. Day after day, three times a day, making offerings in this way for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, while also teaching and transforming countless sentient beings: such offerings would not equal the merit of a person who hears this Dharma-gate of Entering the Inconceivable Wisdom and Virtue of the Tathāgata and gives rise to faith and understanding in the mind. That person's merit surpasses the former by immeasurable asaṃkhyeya times.

"Mañjuśrī! If there is a bodhisattva who believes in and understands this Dharma, that bodhisattva will swiftly complete the immeasurable billions of nayutas of pāramitās of the bodhisattva-mahāsattva, will realize and enter immeasurable billions of nayutas of stages, will turn away from immeasurable billions of nayutas of births and deaths, will come to know immeasurable billions of nayutas of the Buddhas' spiritual powers, will shatter the immeasurable asaṃkhyeya mountains of arrogance, will topple the immeasurable asaṃkhyeya banners of miserliness and jealousy, will dry up the immeasurable asaṃkhyeya rivers of craving, will cross the immeasurable asaṃkhyeya oceans of birth and death, will sever the immeasurable asaṃkhyeya nets of Māra, will eclipse the awesome light of all suns, moons, Śakra, Brahmā, and world-protectors. Traveling from one buddha-land to another, that bodhisattva will rescue sentient beings suffering in the realms of hell, hungry ghosts, animals, and King Yama. Always drawing near to the Buddhas and bodhisattvas, that bodhisattva will fully accomplish the Ocean-Seal Samādhi, the Upholding-All-Dharmas Samādhi, the Dharma-Sovereignty Samādhi, the Adornment-of-All-Marks Samādhi, the Jewel-Producing Samādhi, the Peace-and-Joy Samādhi, the Lotus-Adornment Samādhi, the Space-Treasury Samādhi, the Entering-the-World Samādhi, the Wondrous-Dharma-Flower Samādhi, the Sovereign-Domain Samādhi, the Great-Impetuosity Samādhi, the Space-Mind Samādhi, the Lion's-Impetuosity Samādhi, the Sun-Lamp Samādhi, the Immeasurable-Turning Samādhi, the Pouring-Sweet-Nectar Samādhi, the Vajra-Banner Samādhi, the Like-Vajra Samādhi, the Vajra-Navel Samādhi, the Earth-Upholding Samādhi, the Sumeru-Lamp Samādhi, the Sumeru-Banner Samādhi, the Jewel-Treasury Samādhi, the Mind-Sovereignty Samādhi, the Sovereignty-over-All-Sentient-Beings'-Minds Samādhi, the Increasing-All-Practices Samādhi, the Deep-Secret-Skillful-Means Samādhi, the Various-Eloquence Samādhi, the Unseen Samādhi, the Comprehending-All-Dharmas Samādhi, the Playful-Sport Samādhi, the Producing-All-Spiritual-Powers Samādhi, the Māra-Subduing Samādhi, the Manifesting-All-Forms Samādhi, the Supreme-of-All-Forms Samādhi, the Contemplating-the-Body Samādhi, the Possessing-All-Practices Samādhi, the Wisdom-Lamp Samādhi, the Bodhi-Light Samādhi, the Delighting-in-Eloquence Samādhi, the Entering-All-Merits Samādhi, the Explaining-the-True-Mark-of-All-Dharmas Samādhi, the Quiescent-Spiritual-Power Samādhi, the Śūraṅgama Samādhi, and the Ocean-Tide Samādhi. That bodhisattva will also obtain the Immeasurable-Buddha-Bodies Dhāraṇī, the Great-Wisdom Dhāraṇī, the Pure-Sound Dhāraṇī, the Inexhaustible-Casket Dhāraṇī, the Immeasurable-Turning Dhāraṇī, the Ocean-Seal Dhāraṇī, the Entering-Definitive-Eloquence Dhāraṇī, and the Buddhas'-Upholding Dhāraṇī. That bodhisattva will also gain accordance with all sentient beings, supreme practice in all dharmas, teacherless wisdom that severs all doubt regarding all dharmas, the Buddha's spiritual powers, and the bodhisattva's practice of skillful means.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as Mount Sumeru, the king of mountains, towers magnificently, eclipsing all other mountains: the merit and purity of a bodhisattva who believes in and understands this Dharma-gate, eclipsing the good roots of all sentient beings, is also like this."

At that time, Mañjuśrī addressed the Bodhisattva Destroyer-of-All-Hindrances: "Child of the Buddha! There are yet further supreme dharmas. If bodhisattvas can believe in and understand them, they will accomplish yet further supreme merits."

The Bodhisattva Destroyer-of-All-Hindrances, hearing this said, then addressed Mañjuśrī: "If there is a bodhisattva who believes in and understands five dharmas, he will be able to surpass this supreme Dharma and gain further immeasurable, surpassing merits. What are the five? First, believing and understanding that all dharmas are unborn and unperishing, indescribable, incomparable, and without counterpart. Second, believing and understanding that the Tathāgata, without effort and without discrimination, enters into the deportment and conduct of places as numerous as motes of dust surpassing those of Jambudvīpa, arising in every successive kṣaṇa without ceasing. Third, believing and understanding that when the Tathāgata Śākyamuni in the past taught King Sudāvāsa, it was solely for the sake of maturing sentient beings, for in truth he had already, for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, long since achieved perfect awakening. Fourth, believing and understanding that since the time when the Tathāgata Śākyamuni displayed the prediction given by the Buddha Dīpaṃkara until the achievement of buddhahood, the period in between during which he practiced the bodhisattva path, was in reality already for immeasurable kalpas perfectly awakened, dwelling in the Buddha's domain. Fifth, believing and understanding that the Tathāgata Śākyamuni, though manifesting birth in the royal palace and the Śākya clan's destruction, did so solely for the sake of maturing sentient beings, for in truth he had already, for immeasurable kalpas, achieved perfect awakening.

"Mañjuśrī! If a bodhisattva can give rise to belief and understanding regarding these five, then he can surpass this supreme Dharma and further accomplish surpassing merits.

"Mañjuśrī! If a good man or good woman for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges daily offered celestial food of a hundred flavors and celestial fine garments to all the arhats endowed with the six spiritual powers and the eight liberations, as numerous as motes of dust throughout the ten directions, the merit obtained would not equal that of a person who in a single day offered food to one pratyekabuddha. That person's merit surpasses the former by asaṃkhyeya times.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If a good man or good woman, for asaṃkhyeya pratyekabuddhas throughout the ten directions, built monasteries as numerous as motes of dust throughout the ten directions, each made of Jambu-river gold, with maṇi for pillars and stairs, railings, pavilions, and windows all adorned with various treasures, offering great jeweled canopies and anointed with fine sandalwood, daily with celestial food of a hundred flavors and celestial fine garments reverently making offerings for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges: this would not equal the merit of a person who merely hears the name 'Buddha,' or 'World-Honored One,' or 'Tathāgata,' or 'Omniscient One.' That person's merit surpasses the former by asaṃkhyeya times. How much greater still for one who paints or molds an image of the Tathāgata: the merit of one who gazes upon it surpasses the former by yet more asaṃkhyeya times. How much greater for one who offers lamps, oil, incense, flowers, and music in various ways: the merit surpasses by yet more asaṃkhyeya times. How much greater for one who is able, even for a single day, to protect and uphold one precept within the Buddha-Dharma: the merit surpasses by yet more asaṃkhyeya times.

"Mañjuśrī! If a good man or good woman for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges daily offered celestial food of a hundred flavors and celestial fine garments to all the Buddhas, bodhisattvas, and śrāvaka assemblies as numerous as motes of dust throughout the ten directions; and after those Buddhas' passing, for each and every one built stūpas as numerous as motes of dust throughout the ten directions, each stūpa as large as the four continents, of wondrous form and workmanship, adorned with various treasures, with banners, canopies, music, and offering-articles surpassing those described before: though all such merits were fully complete, one who has not yet believed in and understood this Dharma-gate would not equal a person who believes in and understands this Dharma-gate of Entering the Inconceivable Wisdom and Virtue of the Tathāgata, even if that person merely gives one morsel of food to an animal. That person's merit surpasses the former by asaṃkhyeya times.

"Mañjuśrī! If there is a bodhisattva who believes in and understands this sūtra and makes offerings to the Buddhas as described, and another bodhisattva, hearing of it, rejoices and gives rise to a pure mind of faith, rises from his seat, joins his palms, bows, and to the extent of his ability makes offerings in practice: that second person's merit surpasses the former by yet more asaṃkhyeya times, because such a person will soon attain the Buddha's wisdom."

When this sūtra was spoken, all the bhikṣus and the assembly of bodhisattvas, and all the world's devas, humans, asuras, and the rest, rejoiced, believed, accepted, bowed, and put it into practice.
