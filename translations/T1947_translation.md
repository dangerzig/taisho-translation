# 釋迦如來涅槃禮讚文
## Liturgy of Praise and Worship for the Nirvāṇa of Śākyamuni Tathāgata

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1947

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, composed by Renyue (仁岳).

---

### Preface

Among Confucian scholars, those who bear lifelong sorrow do so on the distant anniversary of a parent's death. How could sons of the Śākya Dharma be any different? The former grieve recalling the parents who toiled to give them life; the latter cherish the memory of the compassionate father who delivered them. The depth of that virtue is beyond measure; the filial devotion is one and the same. Yet when one looks at the monastics of this latter age, who bear the form of śramaṇas and carry the family name of Śākyamuni, those who on the day of the Buddha's nirvāṇa are unable to prepare themselves in solemn reverence and pay their respects are many indeed. I am privately grieved by this.

I once read the *Latter Part of the Nirvāṇa Sūtra*. After the Tathāgata's passing, the hosts of Brahmā and Śakra, Mahākāśyapa and others, all composed verses of lament. Moreover, the *Mahāsāṃghika Vinaya* instructs that on the day of nirvāṇa, one should extol the Buddha's virtues. Accordingly, I have traced the course of his transformative deeds and composed these words of praise: fourteen chapters in all, eight lines per chapter. The first ten chapters praise the Buddha; the next one chapter praises the Dharma; the final three chapters praise the Saṃgha. The liturgical forms for all directions are also fully provided for practical use. It is my hope that our assembly, on the fifteenth day of the second month, will solemnly attend to the sūtras and images, prepare fine offerings, and call upon one of esteemed voice and virtue to chant these words, thereby expressing the sincerity of our unfailing grief.

Formerly, the Master of Gushan, Zhongyong, composed the "Eight Virtues of Nirvāṇa Praise," modeling it upon the "White-Robed Avalokiteśvara Liturgy" by Fu Hui. However, because the phonological traditions of Wu and Shu regions differ considerably, in the Jiangsu and Zhejiang areas it was mostly not practiced. I have now composed this piece in the manner of Master Zhiyuan of Tianzhu, who composed the "Praise of Zhizhe." This is not done from a fondness for novelty but from a concern to generate goodness. Let those who understand me not use form and sound to seek the Buddha, nor take the deviant path as their guide.

### Liturgy

Reverently, the Able Benevolent One responded to the world and, in silent realization, realized the truth. He commanded all the chiliocosms and dwelt as the honored one. He embraced the four kinds of birth and regarded them as his children. His form appeared in accordance with beings; fundamentally it was identical with the body of no-characteristics. His teaching arose in response to capacities; how could it differ from the Way of no-words? Since the Dharma-wheel began turning, his transformative deeds grew ever more magnificent. First he taught the half-word, like striking the unawakened without weariness. Later he entrusted the hundred pieces of gold, as one who ransoms a life with tireless urgency. He caused all the classes of affliction to abide securely within the tathāgata-garbha. The four minds having been fulfilled, the three virtues are reached. This is what is called "not causing a single being to attain extinction alone, but causing all to attain extinction through the Tathāgata's extinction."

Therefore, entering nirvāṇa is inconceivable. We, now that the Crane Grove is so far distant, grieve the loss of former conditions. Yet the age of the semblance Dharma still endures, and we humbly follow the remaining instructions. Now, on this day in the middle of spring, we longingly recall the ceremony of lying with head to the north. With aquatic plants and brookside ferns, we modestly present our humble offerings. With rustic songs and village chants, we briefly express our sincerity of mourning. We humbly pray that the vast compassion may condescend to look upon us.

With single mind, I reverently invite the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, Śākyamuni Buddha.

We humbly pray: descend to this place of practice and accept our offerings.

With single mind, I reverently invite the Dharma-gates taught at the Nirvāṇa Assembly, the Sūtra-Piṭaka.

We humbly pray: descend to this place of practice and accept our offerings.

With single mind, I reverently invite the holy and worthy bodhisattva-saṃgha assembled at the Nirvāṇa Assembly.

We humbly pray: descend to this place of practice and accept our offerings.

With single mind, I reverently invite the holy and worthy pratyekabuddha-saṃgha assembled at the Nirvāṇa Assembly.

We humbly pray: descend to this place of practice and accept our offerings.

With single mind, I reverently invite the holy and worthy śrāvaka-saṃgha assembled at the Nirvāṇa Assembly.

We humbly pray: descend to this place of practice and accept our offerings.

> He who has compassion for the world, the Great King of Physicians:
> Both body and wisdom are in perfect quiescence.
> Within the selfless Dharma there exists the true self.
> To this one I bow my head, the Unsurpassed Honored One.

### Chapter One: Praise of the Buddha Manifesting Light and Sound to Gather the Assembly

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when emitting light and sound to gather the assembly, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> At dawn on the Fortunate River, by the firm-standing trees,
> Numinous radiance and celestial voices spread through the ten directions.
> To summon all beings and resolve their final doubts,
> He proclaimed that the Great Awakened One would return to primordial stillness.
> The worlds were adorned with jewels like a land of bliss;
> His human body manifested blood as if in lavish display.
> Celestial offerings arrived like massing clouds, yet he remained silent.
> All at that moment bowed their heads, hearts filled with grief.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Two: Praise of the Buddha Accepting Cunda's Last Offering

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when accepting Cunda's offering of food, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> The Tathāgata had long realized the Vairocana substance,
> Yet by skillful means he displayed the ceremony of a final offering.
> He who could transform a hair-tip into myriad bodies
> Accepted this offering of grain from the Flower-Clan.
> The six sense-objects, though said to be empty and without characteristics,
> The five fruits should be known to establish karmic connections.
> I now, in distant remembrance, reverently present these offerings,
> Praying to realize the true permanence and share in its wondrous meaning.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Three: Praise of the Buddha Reclining on the Jeweled Couch and Manifesting Illness

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when reclining on the jeweled couch and displaying illness, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> Entrusting Mañjuśrī with the transmission of the correct Dharma,
> He then manifested a back ailment to instruct all beings.
> Resting on his bent arm, lying on his right side, like an infant child,
> Withdrawing his gaze, wordless, like one who is ill.
> With grave solemnity, he listened to Kāśyapa's questions;
> With calm composure, he still awaited King Ajātaśatru's arrival.
> Once more he sent forth streaming light to illumine the great chiliocosm;
> All suffering within it was entirely dispelled.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Four: Praise of the Buddha Entering the Moon-Beloved Samādhi

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when entering the Moon-Beloved Samādhi, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> The Great Compassionate One, bending down in concern for Ajātaśatru,
> Accorded with Jīvaka and aroused a wholesome mind.
> From the lion seat he once more extended the Moon-Beloved Light,
> And the king's robes suddenly revealed his bodily sores healed.
> Taking refuge in the Buddha, he first understood that deviant paths are mistaken;
> Hearing the sūtra, he finally comprehended that adverse conditions are empty.
> An eranda tree among whose midst a sandalwood grows:
> How marvelous, this simile for faith without foundations!

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Five: Praise of the Buddha Displaying the Marks for Devas and Humans

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when displaying his marks for devas and humans, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> Opening his Dharma-robes with his hands, he revealed his bodily marks,
> Rising and descending in empty space, turning twenty-four times.
> On the verge of entering the nirvāṇa gate of tranquil stillness,
> Now behold the assembly of adornments in purple-gold splendor!
> With a deer's body, how can one repay the virtue of cultivating causes?
> Through dust-mote kalpas, it is hard to meet the conditions of a buddha's appearance.
> The parting instructions are earnest and entrusted to the future:
> Take refuge in the Three Treasures, and all shall receive them.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Six: Praise of the Buddha Contemplating the World in Still Concentration

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when contemplating the world in still concentration, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> Having already opened the secret treasury to benefit all beings,
> Now returning to the source, approaching the middle of the night.
> He emitted a light called "Nirvāṇa,"
> Roaming through the dhyānas, he surpassed and transcended them all.
> All-knowledge is nothing other than contemplating reality-characteristics;
> Sentient beings can hardly avoid witnessing impermanence.
> When the fuel is exhausted and the fire about to die,
> Ānanda's mind at that point fell into confusion.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Seven: Praise of the Buddha Entering the Fourth Dhyāna and Passing into Extinction

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when entering the fourth dhyāna and passing into extinction, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> Re-entering the fourth dhyāna, he relinquished regard for life
> And in that samādhi displayed his seeming passing.
> The twin trees turned crane-white, covering the golden coffin;
> The great earth shook like thunder throughout the sand-grain realms.
> Brahmā and Śakra offered verse after verse of mourning lament;
> Māras and evil ones found brief relief from the worries of battle.
> The sun of wisdom and light of compassion ceased to shine;
> In the long night of ignorance, when will dawn ever come?

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Eight: Praise of the Buddha Entering the Golden Coffin in White Cloth

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when entering the golden coffin wrapped in white cloth, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> The people of the kingdom asked about the cremation rites;
> Lord Śakra personally transmitted the words of final instruction.
> They followed the protocol for a wheel-turning king's funeral,
> To generate boundless merit among the hosts of Brahmā.
> A thousand layers of fine cloth wrapped the sage's body;
> Seven layers of jeweled coffins circled the celestial city.
> When once more they bathed and anointed with fragrant spring water,
> All beheld his wondrously adorned body, imperishable.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Nine: Praise of the Buddha Revealing Himself to Kāśyapa at the Coffin

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when showing himself to Kāśyapa at the coffin, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> The Venerable Kāśyapa, dwelling at Vulture Peak,
> Emerged from dhyāna knowing the Buddha had returned to truth.
> Gazing from afar at Kuśinagara, he came on foot,
> Arriving just as the funeral rites were being performed.
> From within the coffin first appeared his golden body;
> As Kāśyapa bowed, the Buddha displayed the wheel-marks on his soles.
> This final response to Kāśyapa's mind of mourning devotion
> Was the manifestation of his equal and compassionate nature.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Ten: Praise of the Buddha Entering the Fragrant Pavilion for Cremation

With single mind, I bow my head to the Lord of the Nirvāṇa Teaching, the World-Honored One of the Sahā Realm, in the body he manifested when entering the fragrant pavilion for cremation, Śākyamuni Buddha.

> From the svastika on his chest, the sacred fire streamed forth;
> Upon the pavilion of many fragrances, his true body was consumed.
> His jade features and golden form were finally no more,
> Yet the frost-white cotton and plain cloth remained as before.
> His crystalline śarīra were divided among the nations;
> Stūpas rose conspicuous, displayed to devas and humans.
> We who are ashamed to have been born in the age of the semblance Dharma
> Can only praise the events of that time's legacy.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Eleven: Praise of the Dharma

With single mind, I bow my head to the Dharma-gates taught at the Nirvāṇa Assembly, the Sūtra-Piṭaka.

> Beginning with the wondrous teaching revealed for Cunda,
> Ending with the subtle words proclaimed for Subhadra:
> Its power upholds the Vinaya, breaking the doubts of provisional teaching;
> It enables even the icchantikas to generate true faith.
> To drink of ghee is to surpass all other flavors;
> To bathe in the ocean is to encompass all streams.
> I vow to hear, uphold, and exhaust this final body,
> Constantly studying the perfection of the full and complete word.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Twelve: Praise of the Bodhisattva Saṃgha

With single mind, I bow my head to the holy and worthy bodhisattva-saṃgha assembled at the Nirvāṇa Assembly.

> Seeking the unsurpassed path of bodhi,
> Gathering the causes of bodhisattva-practice from dust-mote worlds:
> They do not manifest any discriminative mind within the three realms;
> They regard all beings equally as their only child.
> Guiding to the Great Vehicle, they cause aspiration;
> Carrying the weak and lost, they show them the way home.
> Free among people like sovereign dragons,
> Appearing at the right time: the benefit is inconceivable.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Thirteen: Praise of the Pratyekabuddha Saṃgha

With single mind, I bow my head to the holy and worthy pratyekabuddha-saṃgha assembled at the Nirvāṇa Assembly.

> Deeply entering the realm of dependent origination and cyclic existence,
> Through contemplative illumination they roam freely at the gate of liberation.
> Merit and wisdom have been cultivated through accumulated kalpas;
> Supernatural powers arise in boundless application.
> They have already abandoned the deer cart and walk the direct path,
> Unlike those who cling to the rhinoceros horn and guard lonely peaks.
> They come to the assembly at the twin śāla trees:
> Like mirror-reflections, they appear in accordance with form.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Chapter Fourteen: Praise of the Śrāvaka Saṃgha

With single mind, I bow my head to the holy and worthy śrāvaka-saṃgha assembled at the Nirvāṇa Assembly.

> All faculties subdued and tamed, they dwell in quiescence;
> They receive, uphold, and transmit the parting instructions in full.
> Having escaped the blazing fire in the rotting house,
> They do not gather broken tiles from the spring pond.
> Those of the four fruits are all worthy of being sons of the Buddha;
> Through the three kinds of cultivation, each can meet the King of Sūtras.
> Permanence and impermanence are ultimately the same:
> This is what it means to adorn the twin trees.

Therefore, with single mind, I take refuge and bow my head.

### Repentance

With utmost mind, I repent. I and all sentient beings of the dharmadhātu: we possess the original purity of mind, endowed with the nature of all-knowledge's wondrous illumination. When the thousand suchnesses had not yet stirred, originally there was no distinction of sage or ordinary. Yet the six kinds of delusion becoming causes, we drifted through birth and death. The pearl in the brow was suddenly lost; the film over the eye thickened. Not encountering the physician with the bright mirror, we had no opportunity for the treatment of gold.

Through what conditions of past planting were we born in the human realm, encountered the Dharma of Śākyamuni, and numbered among the bhikṣu-saṃgha? Yet because we did not carefully observe the discipline and deportment, the factors of the path were difficult to accomplish. The teaching of the complete meaning of the One Vehicle has not yet opened our true wisdom. The regulations of the seven categories for preventing error have been frequently violated with deep transgression. Sustaining the body with impure things, following ignorance in our actions. The inflated skin-bag has holes; truly we harbor the worry of drowning in the sea. The ornate house stands empty; how can we receive the gift of entering through the gate? Now, with eyes downcast, I gaze toward nirvāṇa and personally undertake the rites of repentance. Having fully confessed the root of transgression, I shall purely eliminate all karmic obstructions. The Buddha's compassion covers all; the Dharma's power pervades in hidden ways. Therefore, taking refuge, I trust that protection and mindfulness will surely be extended.

Having repented, with utmost mind I take refuge in the Three Treasures.

### Exhortation

With utmost mind, I reverently exhort:

> The buddhas of the ten directions abide in constant stillness,
> Yet for sentient beings, each appears in the world.
> Having accomplished the fruit of the Way and vanquished the māras,
> I exhort them to turn the Dharma-wheel to deliver all.
> The udumbara's auspicious petals are truly hard to see;
> The lion's heroic voice: how can it be easily heard?
> Even when, following conditions, they manifest extinction,
> I pray them, with vast compassion, to extend their lifespan through the kalpas.

Having exhorted, with utmost mind I take refuge in the Three Treasures.

### Sympathetic Joy

With utmost mind, I share in joy:

> The perfect penetration has already opened skillful means;
> The five vehicles are all nothing other than the One Vehicle.
> Throughout past, present, and future, wherever there is cultivation,
> In all deeds of body, speech, and mind, I share in joy.
> Yellow leaves called gold: even this cause is good;
> Sand gathered into stūpas: the Way is still accomplished.
> For all the jealousy and anger ever harbored in my mind,
> I now repent, and praise all equally.

Having shared in joy, with utmost mind I take refuge in the Three Treasures.

### Dedication

With utmost mind, I dedicate:

> All meritorious deeds of past and present
> I fuse entirely with the wisdom of emptiness.
> May all that I have done become the cause of the four virtues,
> Not seeking again the fruit of existence in the three realms.
> If the myriad creatures do not share in liberation,
> My mind shall never seize upon bodhi.
> As all waters flow toward the great ocean:
> So too is dedication, without any difference.

Having dedicated, with utmost mind I take refuge in the Three Treasures.

### Vow

With utmost mind, I make this vow:

> I vow in this body to cultivate prajñā,
> Constantly contemplating our Buddha abiding in nirvāṇa.
> Not departing from the mind that arises through dependent origination,
> I shall see the formless body within emptiness.
> Whether like the Snow Mountain Master writing upon stone and trees,
> Or like one in field and village raising the fish-trap and hare-snare:
> I universally pray that, like the wondrous Mañjuśrī,
> All may simultaneously awaken to the nature of the Tathāgata.

Having vowed, with utmost mind I take refuge in the Three Treasures.
