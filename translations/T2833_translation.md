# Treatise on Contemplating the Mind

## *Guānxīn Lùn* (觀心論)

Taishō 2833

Author unknown (traditionally attributed to Bodhidharma).

*Note: This is a treatise on the primacy of mind-contemplation (guānxīn) in Buddhist practice, preserved among the Dunhuang manuscripts and traditionally attributed to Bodhidharma, the legendary founder of Chan (Zen) Buddhism, though modern scholars consider the attribution uncertain. The text argues systematically that all outward Buddhist practices (building monasteries, casting images, burning incense, scattering flowers, lighting lamps, circumambulating stūpas, observing fasts, reciting the Buddha's name, and ritual bathing) should be understood as metaphors for inner contemplation. Each practice is reinterpreted: a "monastery" is the purified body; "casting an image" means cultivating awakening-practices to fashion the true likeness of the Tathāgata; "incense" refers to the five kinds of dharma-fragrance (precepts, concentration, wisdom, liberation, liberation-knowledge-and-vision); "lamps" are the light of wisdom; and so forth. The text is an important document in early Chan literature, reflecting the movement's emphasis on direct insight into the mind over ritual externalism. A colophon dates the manuscript copy to the fifth month of a gēngshēn year.*

... contemplating the mind may be called "understanding."

**Question**: Above you said that the Buddha-nature of suchness is the root of all meritorious qualities, and that awakening is the foundation. But what is the root of all evils in the mind of ignorance?

**Answer**: Although the mind of ignorance contains eighty-four thousand afflictions, passions, and immeasurable evils as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, to state the essentials, they all arise from the three poisons as their root. The three poisons are greed, anger, and delusion. These three poisonous minds are themselves fully equipped with all evils, just as a great tree, though it has only one root, produces innumerable branches and leaves. The evils born from the root of the three poisons are a hundred thousand myriad koṭi times more numerous than those branches, beyond any analogy.

Thus, within the original essence of mind, these three poisons exist by themselves. When they manifest through the six sense-faculties, they are also called the "six thieves." These six thieves are the six consciousnesses. Going out and entering through the sense-faculties, becoming attached to the myriad objects, they create evil karma and damage the essence of suchness; therefore they are called "the six thieves."

All sentient beings, because of these three poisons and six thieves, have their body and mind confused and deluded, sinking in saṃsāra, revolving through the six destinies and suffering various afflictions.

It is like rivers and streams: from a small spring source, if the trickling flow is not stopped, it can eventually become vast, with surging waves stretching ten thousand leagues. If someone cuts off the source, all the streams will cease. Those who seek liberation: if they eliminate the three poisons and the six thieves, they will naturally eliminate all suffering.

**Question**: The three realms and six destinies are vast and boundless. If one only contemplates the mind, how can one be freed from those sufferings?

**Answer**: The karmic retribution of the three realms is produced solely by the mind. If originally there were no mind, there would be no three realms. The three realms are the three poisons: greed is the desire realm; anger is the form realm; delusion is the formless realm. Because these three minds accumulate all evil karma, the retribution is accomplished, and they revolve without end: therefore they are called "the three realms."

Furthermore, because the three poisons create karma of varying weight, one is reborn in six different places: hence "the six destinies."

**Question**: How are the light and heavy [karmas] distributed into six?

**Answer**: If beings do not understand the correct cause and cultivate goodness with deluded minds, they are not yet free from the three realms and are born in the three lighter destinies. What are the three lighter? Those who universally cultivate the ten good deeds but vainly seek pleasure are not yet free from the realm of greed and are born in the deva destiny. Those who universally uphold the five precepts but vainly give rise to love and hatred are not yet free from the realm of anger and are born in the human destiny. Those who cling to conditioned practices and mistakenly seek merit through heterodox beliefs are not yet free from the realm of delusion and are born in the asura destiny. These three types are called the "three lighter." What are the "three heavier"? Those who indulge the three poisonous minds and create only evil karma fall into three heavy destinies. If greed-karma is heavy, one falls into the hungry ghost destiny. If anger-karma is heavy, one falls into the hell destiny. If delusion-karma is heavy, one falls into the animal destiny. These three heavier, together with the three lighter, constitute the six destinies.

Therefore, know that all wholesome and evil karma arise from one's own mind. If one can gather in the mind and depart from all evil, the karma of revolving through the three realms and six destinies will naturally be extinguished. To be able to extinguish all suffering is called "liberation."

**Question**: The Buddha said he practiced for three great asaṃkhyeya kalpas of immeasurable arduous effort before accomplishing the Buddha-way. Why do you now say that merely eliminating the three poisons is called "liberation"?

**Answer**: When the Buddha spoke of three great asaṃkhyeya kalpas, he meant the three poisonous minds. "Asaṃkhyeya" in Sanskrit means "incalculable" in Chinese. These three poisonous minds, in every single thought-moment, contain incalculable [evils] as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. The nature of suchness, being obscured by these three poisons, if one does not transcend these three Ganges-river-sands of poisonous evil thoughts, how can one be called "liberated"? Now, to be able to eliminate the three kinds of poisonous minds of greed, anger, and delusion: this is called having crossed over the three great asaṃkhyeya kalpas.

Beings of the latter age, foolish and of dull faculties, do not understand the Tathāgata's secret teaching of the three kinds of asaṃkhyeya, and so they say it requires kalpas of historical time. Is this not a cause for doubt?

### On the Six Pāramitās and Three Collections of Pure Precepts

Those who practice should only contemplate the mind and not cultivate disciplined conduct: how can this be adequate?

[Answer:] When one can control the three poisons, the three collections of wholesome conduct universally converge upon the mind; therefore they are called "the three collections of precepts." The pāramitās (*bōluómì*), in Sanskrit, mean "reaching the other shore" in Chinese. When the six sense-faculties are purified and not stained by worldly dust, one transcends the river of afflictions and reaches the shore of bodhi; therefore they are called "the six pāramitās."

**Question**: The sūtras say that the three collections of pure precepts are: the vow to sever all evil, the vow to cultivate all good, the vow to save all sentient beings. Now you say merely controlling the three poisonous minds: is there not a discrepancy in meaning?

**Answer**: The Buddha's words in the sūtras are true and without error. The bodhisattva, in past causal stages, when cultivating austere practices, made three vows against the three poisons: against the poison of greed, the vow to sever all evil, hence constantly cultivating precepts; against the poison of anger, the vow to cultivate all good, hence constantly cultivating concentration; against the poison of delusion, the vow to save all sentient beings, hence constantly cultivating wisdom. By upholding such precepts, concentration, and wisdom, one transcends the evil karma of the three poisons and accomplishes buddhahood.

By controlling the three poisons, all evils are extinguished: this is called "severing." By upholding the three trainings, all wholesome qualities are complete: this is called "cultivation." By cultivating the ability to sever, the myriad practices are accomplished, benefiting self and others, universally aiding all beings: this is called "saving."

### On the Six Pāramitās as Inner Practice

**Question**: The sūtras speak of the six pāramitās as giving, precept-keeping, patience, vigor, meditation, and wisdom. Now you say the six sense-faculties being pure are the six pāramitās. How do they correspond?

**Answer**: To cultivate the six perfections, one should purify the six sense-faculties. To purify the six faculties, one must first subdue the six thieves. To be able to relinquish the thief of the eye, departing from all objects of form with no attachment or reluctance: this is called "giving" (*dāna*). To be able to restrain the thief of the ear, not allowing it to run free amid sounds: this is called "precept-keeping" (*śīla*). To be able to eliminate the thief of the nose, equalizing all fragrances and odors with self-mastery and gentleness: this is called "patience" (*kṣānti*). To be able to control the thief of the tongue, not craving improper flavors, praising and expounding and teaching without weariness: this is called "vigor" (*vīrya*). To be able to subdue the thief of the body, remaining tranquil and unmoved amid all objects of touch: this is called "meditation" (*dhyāna*). To be able to gather in the thief of the mind, not following ignorance, constantly cultivating the wisdom of awakening and delighting in meritorious qualities: this is called "wisdom" (*prajñā*). Furthermore, "perfection" (*pāramitā*) means "ferrying across." The six pāramitās are likened to boats and rafts that can transport beings to the other shore; hence "the six perfections."

### On Monasteries, Images, Incense, and Flowers

**Answer**: When the Buddha spoke of the immeasurable skillful means, all beings of dull faculties and narrow capacity cannot understand deep principles, so he used conditioned phenomena as analogies for the unconditioned.

Regarding "monasteries" (*qiélán*): the Sanskrit word, translated into Chinese, means "a place of purity." If one permanently eliminates the three poisons, constantly purifies the six sense-faculties, and body and mind are tranquil, inwardly and outwardly pure: this is called "building a monastery."

Regarding "casting images": all sentient beings seeking the Buddha-way cultivate various awakening-practices to fashion the likeness of the Tathāgata. How could this mean casting figures in bronze or gold? Therefore, those who seek liberation take the body as a furnace, the Dharma as fire, wisdom as the artisan, the three collections of pure precepts and six pāramitās as the mold. They smelt and refine body and mind until the Buddha-nature of suchness pervades all, entering the mold of the precepts without flaw or crack, and naturally the true image is accomplished.

Regarding "burning incense": this is not the worldly incense of visible form, but the incense of the unconditioned, correct Dharma, which perfumes all the foulness of evil karma until it is completely purified. This correct Dharma-incense has five kinds of substance: (1) the incense of precepts, severing all evil and cultivating all good; (2) the incense of concentration, with resolute faith in the Mahāyāna and a mind of non-retrogression; (3) the incense of wisdom, constantly observing inwardly and outwardly; (4) the incense of liberation, severing all bonds of ignorance; (5) the incense of liberation-knowledge-and-vision, with the light of awareness constantly penetrating without obstruction. These five kinds of incense are unmatched in the world. When the Buddha was in the world, he commanded his disciples to use the fire of wisdom to burn such priceless incense as offerings to all the buddhas of the ten directions.

Regarding "scattering flowers": this means expounding the correct Dharma, speaking of the flowers of meritorious qualities, benefiting sentient beings and universally adorning all upon the nature of suchness. These flowers of merit are praised by the Buddha, ultimately permanent and never withering.

Regarding "lamps": the light of awakening, the correct illumination of the mind. Using wisdom's bright understanding as an analogy for a lamp: therefore all who seek liberation constantly take the body as the lamp-stand, the mind as the lamp-vessel, faith as the wick, and the increase of disciplined practice as the added oil; the bright understanding of wisdom is likened to the lamp's fire, constantly burning. Such is the lamp of suchness and correct awakening, breaking through all the darkness of ignorant delusion. If one can use this dharma to transmit it to others in mutual awakening, then one lamp lights a hundred thousand lamps, and the light, lamp after lamp, never ends. Because it never ends, it is called "the ever-burning lamp."

Regarding "the six periods of practice": in the six sense-faculties, at all times constantly practicing the Buddha-way. The "Way" means "awakening": cultivating various awakening-practices, taming the six emotions. Purified practice sustained over a long time without relinquishment is called "the six periods of practice." Regarding "circumambulating the stūpa": the stūpa is the body. Constantly causing the light of awareness and wisdom to circle around body and mind, thought after thought without stopping: this is called "circumambulating the stūpa."

### On Fasting, Prostration, Bathing, and Buddha-Recitation

Regarding "observing the fast" (*chí zhāi*): "fast" (*zhāi*) means "to regulate" (*qí*), that is, regulating and ordering body and mind so that they do not become scattered. "Observe" (*chí*) means "to guard" (*hù*), guarding the disciplined practices according to the Dharma. There are five kinds of food for fasting: (1) the food of dharma-joy, joyfully practicing according to the Tathāgata's correct Dharma; (2) the food of the bliss of meditation, with body and mind experiencing joy through inner and outer realization of tranquility; (3) the food of mindfulness, constantly being mindful of all buddhas with mind and mouth in accord; (4) the food of aspiration, always seeking wholesome vows whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down; (5) the food of liberation, with mind constantly pure and unstained by worldly dust. These five pure foods are what is called "fasting food." If anyone does not eat these five kinds of pure food, then outwardly observing a fast has no basis.

Regarding "prostration" (*lǐbài*): this should accord with the Dharma. "Prostration" means "reverence." "Bowing" means "submission." Reverently respecting the true nature and submitting the ignorant mind: this is prostration.

Regarding the "Warm-Room Sūtra" (*Wēnshì Jīng*) on bathing the saṃgha: the seven items [of bathing] correspond to seven dharmas by which all beings wash away and adorn themselves, removing the defilements of the three poisons and ignorance. The seven dharmas are: (1) pure precepts washing and cleansing body and mind, like pure water washing away dust; (2) wisdom observing inside and outside, like heating fire warming the water; (3) discernment selecting and rejecting, like bath-beans removing grease; (4) truthfulness severing all false speech, like chewing a willow-twig removing bad breath; (5) right faith with no doubt, like ash rubbed on the body warding off wind; (6) gentleness softening all harshness, like oil moistening the skin; (7) shame and remorse repenting of evil karma, like an undergarment concealing one's ugliness.

Regarding "reciting the Buddha's name" (*niànfó*): one who recites the Buddha must practice correct recitation. Correct recitation brings certain rebirth in the pure land. Incorrect recitation: how can it reach there? "Buddha" means "awakening": awakening to and investigating the mind's source, not allowing evil to arise. "Recitation" means "remembrance": firmly upholding the precepts without forgetting, diligently understanding the Tathāgata's meaning. This is called "correct recitation." Therefore, know that recitation resides in the mind, not in speech. Following the trap, one seeks the fish; having caught the fish, one forgets the trap. Following words, one seeks the meaning; having grasped the meaning, one forgets the words.

### Conclusion

The dharma-gate is profound and secret; how could it be fully stated? Speaking briefly about contemplating the mind, I have discussed a small portion.

[Colophon: Copied on the twenty-third day of the fifth month of the gēngshēn year.]
