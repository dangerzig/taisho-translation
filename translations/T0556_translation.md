# 佛說七女經
## Sūtra of the Seven Girls, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0556

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the land of Kuru, at Purṇadatta's tree garden, together with a thousand arhats, five hundred bodhisattvas, and the various devas, nāgas, and spirits.

At that time in the land of Kuru there was a brahmin named Mahāmadhu, who was miserly and greedy and did not believe in the Buddha-Dharma. He was vastly wealthy, with abundant rare treasures, jewels, cattle, horses, fields, and houses. His wisdom was unmatched, and he served as the teacher of the country, always having five hundred disciples. He was also honored by the king and his ministers. This brahmin had seven daughters, all of surpassing beauty and clever in speech. From head to foot they wore gold, silver, white pearls, and necklaces, dressed in fine garments for every occasion. They were always accompanied by five hundred women. They were proud of themselves, relying on their beauty, haughty toward everyone. They trusted in their wealth and fortune, thinking it was permanent. Whenever they debated principles with the people of the country, they always won.

At that time there was a householder named Purṇadatta who heard that these girls were very beautiful. He went to the brahmin and said: "In your house you call these girls beautiful. Even so, you should take them throughout the country and show them to people. If anyone finds fault with these girls, you shall pay me five hundred ounces of gold. If no one finds fault, I shall pay you five hundred ounces of gold." So for ninety days they went throughout the country, and no one said the girls were ugly. The brahmin thus received five hundred ounces of gold.

Purṇadatta told the brahmin: "The Buddha is nearby at the tree garden. The Buddha knows the future, the past, and the present. Moreover, he is utterly truthful and never speaks falsely. You should take them to show the Buddha."

The brahmin said: "Excellent!" Together with his retinue of five hundred brahmins and five hundred women from the country, they all went to the Buddha's dwelling.

At that time the Buddha was teaching the Dharma to countless thousands of people. Each came forward and paid respects to the Buddha, then sat to one side. The brahmin addressed the Buddha: "Gautama has traveled throughout many countries. Has he ever seen anyone as beautiful as these girls?"

The Buddha immediately rebuked him: "These girls are not beautiful. They are all ugly, without a single attractive quality."

The brahmin asked the Buddha: "In the entire country, no one has said these girls are ugly. Why does Gautama alone say they are ugly?" The brahmin then asked the Buddha: "What do worldly people consider beautiful?"

The Buddha said: "When a person's eyes do not crave sights and their ears do not listen to unwholesome sounds, this is beauty. When the nose does not seek fragrance and the mouth does not crave flavors, this is beauty. When the body does not crave fine sensations and the mind does not think unwholesome thoughts, this is beauty. When the hands do not steal others' property and the mouth does not speak ill of others, this is beauty. Not being arrogant or speaking frivolous words, knowing where life comes from and where death leads, this is beauty. Trusting that generosity will bring its reward, this is beauty. Having faith in the Buddha, faith in the Dharma, and faith in the Saṃgha of bhikṣus, this is beauty."

The Buddha told the brahmin: "Beautiful complexion is not beauty. Beautiful body is not beauty. Beautiful garments are not beauty. Double-tongued and frivolous speech is not beauty. A straight mind and upright intentions: this alone is beauty."

Purṇadatta then got his five hundred ounces of gold back.

The Buddha told the brahmin: "Long ago there was a city named Vārāṇasī. From the bottom of the earth upward, all Buddhas and future Buddhas have sat upon that ground. At that time there was a king named Jīvanī, who was a lay disciple, well versed in the sūtras, and had built a monastery for the Buddha. The king had daughters who were all lay women, learned in the sūtras and wise, of unsurpassed beauty, wearing gold, silver, amber, and jewels, with fine garments. The first daughter was named Xiūdān, the second Sudharma, the third Bhikṣuṇī, the fourth Bhikṣurāśī, the fifth Śramaṇī, the sixth Śramaṇamitra, and the seventh Saṃghadasattva. They always observed the Buddha's correct Dharma, keeping the fasts and precepts. After completing their acts of generosity, the seven girls went together to the king their father's main hall and said: 'We sisters wish to go together to visit the charnel ground.'

"The king said: 'The charnel ground is terrifying. There are only the bones and hair of the dead, scattered remains lying about, limbs strewn on the ground. Those who grieve and weep fill the place. There are tigers, wolves, wild beasts, owls, and vultures that feed on the flesh and blood of the dead. Why would you sisters go to the charnel ground? In my palace there are pleasure gardens and bathing pools, where flying birds and mandarin ducks call to one another. There are flowers of every color, bright to the eye, wondrous grasses and rare trees, refreshing fruits to eat as you please, most pleasant for sightseeing. Why would you sisters go to the charnel ground?'

"The seven girls replied: 'Great King! What benefit is there in fruits and fine food, even a ten-thousandth part? We have seen that when people in the world grow old, their lives daily approach death. No one who is born does not die. We are not small children, deceived by leftover food! If the king has compassion for us sisters, he should allow us to go outside the city to view the dead.' They asked three times in this way.

"The king said: 'Very well! I permit you sisters to do as you wish.'

"At that time the seven girls, together with five hundred attendant women, prepared their carriages and left the palace gate. The seven girls removed the necklaces from their necks and scattered them on the ground. More than a thousand people of the country saw this and followed behind, picking up the jewels. Delighted, they proceeded to the charnel ground outside the city. It was a great stench, an impure place, where only the sounds of weeping were heard. The attendant women and the people trembled, their hair standing on end. The seven girls went straight forward and looked at the various dead. Among them, some had their heads severed, some their hands and feet cut off, some their noses and ears cut away. Some were already dead, and some not yet dead. Some were in coffins, some wrapped in mats, some bound with ropes. Families wept, all wishing for release. The seven girls looked left and right; the dead were many. Still more came from all directions carrying the dead. Flying birds and running beasts competed to feed on them. The corpses were bloated, pus and blood flowing out, and countless millions of worms emerged from their bellies. The stench was unbearable. Yet the seven girls did not cover their noses but went straight forward, circled once, and said to one another: "The bodies of us sisters will not be long before they are likewise."

"The first girl said: 'Should we each compose a verse to save the spirits of the dead?' The six girls all said: 'Excellent!'

"The first girl said: 'When this person lived, they anointed their body with fine fragrances and wore beautiful new garments. Walking among the crowd, they cast lovely glances with delicate eyes and posed for others, wanting all to gaze upon them. Now they lie dead on the ground, scorched by the sun, blown by the wind. The one who posed so beautifully: where are they now?'

"The second girl said: 'A sparrow was in a bottle, its mouth sealed shut, unable to fly out. Now the bottle is broken, and the sparrow has flown away.'

"The third girl said: 'One rides in a carriage and travels. Midway, the driver abandons the carriage and leaves. The carriage cannot move on its own. The one who made the carriage go: where are they now?'

"The fourth girl said: 'It is like a person traveling by boat. Many people ride together across the water. Upon reaching the shore, they tie up the boat. Leaving the body behind is like leaving the boat behind.'

"The fifth girl said: 'There was a city, strong and solid, with many people within, all born and raised in the city. Now the city is empty; the people are nowhere to be seen. Where have they gone?'

"The sixth girl said: 'A person lies dead on the ground. Their clothes and coverings are still fine, from head to foot nothing is lacking. Yet now they cannot walk, nor can they move. Where is that person now?'

"The seventh girl said: 'One body dwelt alone. The person has departed from their house. The house is empty with no one to tend it; day by day it falls into ruin.'

"At that time the seat of Śakra, lord of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, trembled. He heard the seven girls speaking and, in the time it takes to stretch out an arm, descended from heaven. He praised the seven girls: 'What you have said is excellent! What do you wish for? Whatever your wish, I can obtain it for you.'

"The seven girls all said: 'Are you Śakra? Or Brahmā? We did not see you come; you simply appeared before us. Tell us who you are.'

"He replied: 'Young women! I am Śakra. I heard your good words and fine speech, and so I came to listen.'

"The seven girls said: 'You offered to grant us our wishes. You are the most honored in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. You should be able to obtain them for us. We sisters shall now state our wishes.'

"The first girl said: 'I wish to obtain a tree without roots, without branches, without leaves, and to be born within it. That is my wish.'

"The second girl said: 'I wish to find a place on earth without form, without distinction of light and shadow, and to be born within it.'

"The third girl said: 'When a person shouts loudly in the deep mountains, the sound echoes in all directions, but one cannot find where it comes from. I wish to be born within that.'

"Śakra replied: 'Stop! I cannot fulfill these wishes. If you wished to become Śakra, Brahmā, one of the four heavenly kings, or the most honored among the sun and moon, that could be obtained. But what you wish for, I truly do not understand.'

"The seven girls answered: 'You are the sole honored one in heaven, with divine power. How can you not fulfill these wishes? You are like an old ox that cannot pull a cart, nor can it plow a field: useless to its master.'

"Śakra replied: 'I heard you speaking of the Dharma and came to listen. This is beyond my knowledge.' He then excused himself and departed. The seven girls remained silent and did not respond.

"At that time a voice spoke from the sky: 'Kāśyapa Buddha is nearby in the village of Vairūliṅga. Why not go and ask Kāśyapa Buddha?' The seven girls were overjoyed. Together with the five hundred attendant women, those who had followed to watch, and five hundred more from the charnel ground who were mourning and weeping, they all resolved to go.

"At that time Kāśyapa Buddha was teaching the Dharma to countless thousands of people. They all came forward, paid respects to Kāśyapa Buddha, and sat to one side. Śakra addressed the Buddha: 'Earlier I heard the king's seven daughters speak and came to listen. The seven girls then asked me for these wishes: "I wish for a tree without roots, without branches, without leaves. A formless place without distinction of light and shadow. A great shout in the deep mountains whose echo is heard in all directions but whose source cannot be found." I could not answer them. May the Buddha explain their meaning to the seven girls.'

"Kāśyapa Buddha said: 'Excellent! The questions you raise go far beyond the ordinary. Even arhats and pratyekabuddhas cannot understand these things, let alone you.' At that time Kāśyapa Buddha smiled, and five-colored light issued from his mouth, illuminating the entire buddha-land, then circled his body and entered through the crown of his head.

"The attendant came forward, knelt, and asked Kāśyapa Buddha: 'The Buddha does not smile without reason. May we hear its meaning.' Kāśyapa Buddha said to Sarvapāla: 'Do you see these girls?' 'Yes, I see them.'

"'These seven daughters of the king have generated the mind of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi and have already made offerings to five hundred Buddhas. They shall yet make offerings to ten thousand more Buddhas. After ten kalpas, they shall all become Buddhas, all bearing the same name, Puṣṭarapuṇya, in a land named Śuddhavāsa. Those Buddhas shall live for thirty thousand years. At that time the clothing and food of the people shall be like those of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. After the Buddha enters parinirvāṇa, the sūtras and the path shall endure for eight thousand years before coming to an end. When those Buddhas teach the Dharma, they shall guide seventy-five billion people to attain the paths of the bodhisattva and the arhat.'

"When Kāśyapa Buddha gave the seven girls this prediction, they leaped with joy and rose into the air, hovering twenty fathoms above the ground. As they descended, they all transformed into men and attained the stage of non-retrogression. The five hundred attendant women and fifteen hundred devas and humans, seeing the seven girls transform into men, leaped with joy and all generated the mind of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi. One thousand people departed from the dust of defilements and all obtained the Dharma-eye."

The Buddha told the brahmin: "Though the king's seven daughters were wealthy, beautiful, and of noble rank, they did not rely on their bodies to make a show of beauty. Why? Because they contemplated impermanence, knowing that the body cannot last. All worldly people, through mere foolishness, fall into the twelve links of dependent origination and thus undergo birth and death. Since human life always arises from love and attachment, from birth comes old age, from old age comes sickness, from sickness comes death, and from death come weeping and pain. Since human life always comes from love and attachment, one should contemplate one's own body and also the bodies of others. In sitting and rising, one should think of the body's foul discharges, mucus and saliva, cold and heat, stench and impurity. How is this body any different? When the body decays, it turns to worms that feed on its own flesh, the joints and limbs fall apart, and it dissolves into ash and earth. One should reflect: 'When I die, my body too will be like this.' One should not rely on the body to make a show of beauty, but should contemplate impermanence. If a person practices good deeds and is not arrogant or frivolous in speech, after death they will be born in the heavens. If they practice evil, they will enter Naraka. Why do women fall into Naraka in great numbers? It is simply because of jealousy and excessive vanity."

When the Buddha spoke these words, the brahmin's daughters leaped with joy, removed the jewels from their bodies, and scattered them upon the Buddha. Through the Buddha's supernatural power, the scattered jewels halted in midair and transformed into a jeweled canopy. From within it a voice said: "Excellent! What the Buddha has spoken is without error." At that moment the Buddha was moved and released his supernatural power. From his seat he pressed the ground with his toes, and the great trichiliocosm shook mightily. Light illuminated the ten directions. Trees that had been dead for a hundred years burst into flower and fruit. Every empty ditch and ravine spontaneously filled with water. Harps and instruments sounded without being played. The pearl ornaments of women rang of their own accord. The blind gained sight, the deaf gained hearing, the mute gained speech, the hunchbacked stood straight, the lame were healed, those with diseased limbs were cured, the mad were restored to sanity, those afflicted by poison found the poison rendered harmless, and prisoners were all set free. Every manner of bird and beast cried out in harmonious song. At that time, all the people of the land of Kuru, men and women alike, were filled with great joy, their minds harmonious and at peace as if in meditation.

When the Buddha performed these transformations, the king of the land of Kuru cast away his jewels and leaped with joy. Together with his hundred ministers, the brahmin's daughters and their retinue, and the five hundred brahmins, all generated the mind of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi. Furthermore, five hundred bhikṣus attained the path of arhatship, and five hundred people of the country all attained the path of stream-entry.

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, the bodhisattvas, bhikṣus, upāsakas, upāsikās, kings, ministers, elders, people, devas, spirits, and nāgas were all filled with great joy. They came forward, touched their heads to the ground, paid respects to the Buddha, and departed.
