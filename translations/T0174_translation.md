# 菩薩睒子經
## Sūtra of the Bodhisattva Śyāma

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0174

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Virāṭa, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, as well as bodhisattvas, kings, ministers, common people, elders, householders, laymen, and laywomen, in a number beyond reckoning, who had all come to the assembly.

The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Be still and settle your minds and listen! When in the past I first sought and attained the bodhisattva path, my observance of the precepts was complete, my diligence was single-minded, I gathered wisdom, and I practiced skillful means. My accumulated merit and virtue are beyond description. No deva, Śakra, Brahmā, dragon, ghost, spirit, emperor, or person could match my practice."

Ānanda, hearing the Buddha's words, straightened his robes, knelt, joined his palms, and said to the Buddha: "I wish to hear."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Long ago, in countless past ages, there was a bodhisattva named Sarvaguṇa, who was benevolent, compassionate, and generous, saving and aiding all beings. He always practiced the four immeasurable minds, delivering the world from peril, and cherishing those who suffered. At that time, in the Tuṣita heaven, he taught and guided the celestial beings. Day and night, in each of the six periods, he settled his mind, reflected on the three realms, and with his illuminating vision surveyed the paths of good and evil of the people of the ten directions. He knew who among them was filial to parents, revered the Three Treasures, respectful of teachers and elders, and cultivated meritorious virtues. He constantly surveyed the five paths with his divine eye. At that time, in the country of Kāśi, there was an elder who was childless. Both husband and wife were blind. In their hearts, they wished to enter the mountains to seek the supreme wisdom, to cultivate a pure aspiration, and to delight in seclusion. The bodhisattva thought: 'These people have generated a subtle and wonderful aspiration, yet both their eyes cannot see. If they enter the mountains, they may fall into ditches or encounter venomous creatures and be harmed unjustly. If I, at the end of my lifespan, am reborn as their child, I can care for my parents and serve them to the end of their days.'

"Then the bodhisattva's lifespan came to an end, and he descended and was born as a child in the household of the blind parents. The parents were overjoyed and cherished him greatly. They had originally intended to enter the mountains, but because a child had been born, they were content to remain in the world. When the child was seven years old, they named him Śyāma. Śyāma was supremely filial, benevolent, and compassionate. He practiced the ten wholesome deeds: not killing, not stealing, not engaging in sexual misconduct, not deceiving, not drinking alcohol, not speaking falsely, not using frivolous speech, not being jealous, not cursing, and having faith in the path without doubt. Day and night he was diligent. He served his parents as a person serves heaven. His speech always contained a smile, never wounding another's feelings. His conduct always accorded with the Dharma, never leaning toward the crooked or deviant. His parents were pleased and joyful, free from worry. When he was past ten years old, Śyāma knelt and said to his parents: 'You originally formed the great aspiration to enter the deep mountains, to seek the serenity of emptiness and the supreme, perfect truth. How can you abandon your original vow on account of a child? People in this world are subject to impermanence and a hundred changes. Life is not made of gold or stone; death comes at an unforeseen time. I beg you to follow your original intention. Now is the right time to enter the mountains and dwell in purity. I will follow along with you. Together with my parents, I will provide for you as you wish, never missing the proper time.' The parents replied to Śyāma: 'Your filial devotion is known even to heaven. We shall not violate our original vow, and shall enter the mountains together.'

"Śyāma then gave away everything in the household to the poor people of the country, and together with his parents entered the mountains. When Śyāma reached the mountains, he made a hut of rushes for his parents, and prepared beds and mats, neither too cold nor too hot, always suited to the season. Within a year of entering the mountains, the various fruits grew abundantly, fragrant and sweet to eat. Spring water gushed forth, clear and cool. In the ponds, lotus flowers of five colors were bright and vivid. Sandalwood and mixed fragrances wafted from the luxuriant trees, which were doubly lush. The winds and rains came in season; it was neither too cold nor too hot. The leaves of the trees overlapped, shielding from rain and dew, shading from sunlight, keeping it always cool beneath. Flying birds gathered, rare and wondrous kinds, all making musical sounds to entertain the blind parents. Lions, bears, tigers, wolves, and venomous beasts all had compassionate hearts toward one another, with no thought of harming. They all ate grasses and fruits, without any fear. Deer, bears, and various beasts all came and drew near to Śyāma. Their sounds harmonized together, all making joyful music. Śyāma's filial piety and compassion were so great that when he stepped on the ground he feared the earth would feel pain. The heavenly spirits and mountain spirits all took human form, day and night comforting the three practitioners. The three practitioners settled their minds in single-pointed concentration, free from worry. Śyāma constantly gathered a hundred kinds of fruit for his parents to eat, always with surplus. When thirsty, they drank spring water, lacking nothing. When the parents were thirsty and wished to drink, Śyāma would put on his deerskin garment, take a jar, and go to fetch water. The deer and birds also came to drink at the water, without fearing one another.

"At that time, the king of Kāśi entered the mountains to hunt. From a distance, the king saw deer by the water. He drew his bow and shot at the deer. The arrow struck Śyāma in the chest by mistake. Struck by the poisoned arrow, his entire body was in pain. He cried out loudly: 'Who has shot a single poisoned arrow and killed three practitioners of the path?' The king heard a human voice and immediately dismounted. He went to where Śyāma was. Śyāma said to the king: 'The elephant dies for its tusks, the rhinoceros for its horn, the kingfisher for its feathers, the deer for its skin and flesh. I have no horn, no tusks, no feathers, and my skin and flesh cannot be eaten. For what offense do I die today?' The king asked Śyāma: 'What kind of person are you? You wear a deerskin garment and are no different from the wild beasts.' Śyāma said: 'I am a person from the king's own country. Together with my blind parents, I came into the mountains. We have been studying the path for more than twenty years. Never have we been harmed by tigers, wolves, or venomous creatures. Yet today I am shot and killed by the king.' At that very moment, a great wind arose in the mountains, breaking and toppling trees. A hundred kinds of birds cried in sorrow. Lions, bears, and running beasts all howled and cried, shaking the entire mountain. The sun lost its light, the flowing springs dried up, the flowers withered and died, and thunder and lightning shook the earth. The blind parents, startled, said to each other: 'Śyāma went to fetch water and has been gone a very long time. Could he have been harmed by tigers, wolves, or venomous creatures? The birds and beasts cry in sorrow, their voices howling, unlike normal times. The wind rises from all four sides and trees are broken. There must be some calamity.'

"At that time, the king was alarmed and deeply filled with self-reproach: 'What a terrible thing I have done! I was originally shooting at a deer, and the arrow struck him by mistake. To shoot and kill a practitioner of the path is a very grave sin. For the sake of greedily obtaining a small amount of meat, I shall receive severe retribution. I will now offer all the precious treasures of my kingdom, the contents of my treasury, my palaces, my court ladies, my cities and towns, to save this young man's life.' The king then came forward and tried to pull the arrow from Śyāma's chest, but the arrow was deep and could not be extracted. Flying birds and running beasts gathered from all directions like clouds, their cries shaking the entire mountain. The king grew even more alarmed, and all three hundred and sixty of his joints trembled. Śyāma said to the king: 'It is not the king's fault. It is due to my own past karmic offenses. I do not regret the loss of my own body and life, but I grieve for my blind parents. They are old and aged, with neither eye able to see. Once I am gone, they too will surely perish, having no one to depend upon. It is for this reason that I am filled with bitter anguish.'

"At that very moment, all the devas, dragon spirits, mountain spirits, water spirits, and tree spirits were stirred with awe. The king spoke again emphatically: 'I would rather enter hell and suffer punishment for a hundred kalpas, if only Śyāma could live.' He knelt before Śyāma and repented, saying: 'If you should die, I will not return to my kingdom. I will stay in the mountains and care for your parents as you did while you were alive. Do not be anxious. All the devas, dragons, and spirits shall witness this. I will not break this vow.' When Śyāma heard the king's solemn vow, though struck by the poisoned arrow, his heart was pleased and his mind at ease. Even in death he had no regret: 'To have the king care for my blind parents and serve the practitioners of the path: in this present life your sins will be eliminated, and you will gain immeasurable merit.' The king said: 'Tell me where your parents are, while you are still alive.' Śyāma pointed and said: 'From here along this footpath, it is not far. You will see a grass hut. My parents are inside. Go slowly, and do not alarm my parents. Use skillful and tactful words to explain to them. On my behalf, bid farewell to my parents: impermanence has arrived, and I must go to the next life. I do not regret my own life. I only think of my parents, who are old and blind. Once I am gone, they will have no one to depend upon. It is this that fills me with bitter anguish. Death is my allotted fate, brought about by past karmic offenses. No one can escape it. I now repent before my parents for all the evil deeds I have committed through countless kalpas. May those sins be extinguished and blessings arise. May I and my parents, life after life, always meet and never be separated. May my parents keep their health and lifespan, free from worry and affliction. May the devas, dragons, ghosts, and spirits always protect and assist them. May calamities be dispelled and all their wishes fulfilled, naturally and effortlessly.'

"The king then took several men and went directly to the parents. After the king departed, Śyāma suddenly died. Flying birds and beasts all cried out in grief. They circled above Śyāma's body and licked the blood from his body with their tongues. The blind parents, hearing these sounds, grew even more frightened and stood about in distress. The king walked quickly, disturbing the grasses and trees, making the sound of human footsteps. The parents, startled, said: 'Who is this? This is not the sound of our child's footsteps.' The king said: 'I am the king of Kāśi. I heard that practitioners of the path are studying in the mountains, and have come to make offerings to the practitioners.' The parents said: 'It is very good that the great king has come. We are grateful that the king's honored presence has graced this grassy wilderness. Is the king in good health? Are the palace, queen, crown prince, officials, and people all well? Are the winds and rains in harmony and the five grains abundant? Are the neighboring countries not encroaching?' The king answered the practitioners: 'Thanks to the practitioners' grace, all is well.'

"The king then inquired after the blind parents: 'Here in the mountains, with the toil and hardship, among the trees, have any birds or beasts harmed the practitioners? In the mountains, with the cold and heat of the seasons, are you at ease in this present life?' The blind parents said: 'Thanks to the great king's abundant kindness, we are always at ease. We have a filial son named Śyāma who always gathers a hundred kinds of fruit for us. The spring water is always plentiful. In the mountains, the winds and rains are in harmony, and we lack nothing. We have rush mats to sit on and fruit to eat. Śyāma has gone to fetch water and will return shortly.'

"When the king heard the blind parents' words, his heart was again deeply grieved. With tears he said: 'My offense is without measure. I entered the mountains to hunt. I saw a herd of deer by the water and drew my bow to shoot. The arrow struck the practitioners' son by mistake. Śyāma was struck by the poisoned arrow and was in great pain, and so he told me to come and speak to the two practitioners.' When the parents heard this, they threw themselves bodily to the ground like a great mountain collapsing, and the earth shook. The king went forward to support them. The parents cried out to heaven in lamentation: 'Our son Śyāma was the most filial in all the world, benevolent and compassionate beyond any other. When he stepped on the ground he feared it would feel pain. What offense has he committed that the king should shoot and kill him? Earlier, a great wind suddenly arose, breaking and toppling the trees. A hundred kinds of birds cried in sorrow, their wailing shaking the entire mountain. In our twenty-some years in the mountains, there has never been such a calamity. When our child went to fetch water and did not return for so long, we knew there must be something wrong.' All the spirits were alarmed and stirred. The mother's cries could not be stopped. The father said: 'Let us stop for now. Among those born, there is no one who does not die. Impermanence is natural and cannot be turned away.' He then asked the king: 'Where was Śyāma struck? Is he alive or dead?' The king then told the blind parents everything Śyāma had said. Hearing the king's words, they again fainted with grief: 'We shall not live without our son. We too shall die. We beg the king to lead us both to where Śyāma's body lies.'

"The king then led the blind parents to the body. The father embraced Śyāma's feet, and the mother held his head upon her lap. Each felt the arrow in his chest with one hand. They cried out to heaven: 'All the devas, dragon spirits, mountain spirits, tree spirits, water spirits! Our child was benevolent, compassionate, and supremely filial. The spirits know this. Can you not have pity on our child, this good child?' The mother then licked Śyāma's chest wound with her tongue: 'May the poison enter my mouth! I am old, and my eyes cannot see. I would give my body in exchange for my child's life. Let Śyāma live and let me die; in death I would have no regret.' Then the blind parents spoke: 'If Śyāma's sincerity and filial devotion are true, as heaven and earth know, may the arrow be drawn out, may the poison be removed, and may Śyāma live again.'

"Thereupon, the seat of the Lord Śakra in the Second Trāyastriṃśa heaven shook greatly. With his divine eye, Śakra saw the two practitioners embracing their child and weeping. The sound reached even the Fourth Tuṣita heaven, and the palaces of all the devas and the nāga palaces all shook solemnly. Śakra, Brahmā, and the Four Heavenly Kings immediately descended from the fourth heaven, and in the time it takes a person to extend an arm, they arrived and stood before Śyāma. They poured divine medicine into Śyāma's mouth. When the medicine entered his mouth, the arrow drew itself out, and he came back to life as before. The parents, overjoyed, saw that Śyāma, though dead, had come back to life, and both their eyes opened. Flying birds and beasts all made sounds of joy. The wind ceased, the clouds dispersed, and the sun shone again with doubled radiance. Spring water gushed forth, flowers of five colors and trees shone with splendor, doubly more than usual.

"At that time, the king was overjoyed beyond measure. He bowed to Śakra, and then bowed to the parents and to Śyāma: 'I wish to offer the wealth of my kingdom to the practitioners. I myself will remain and serve them, so that in this present life my sins may be eliminated and past enmities resolved.' Śyāma answered the king: 'If you wish to repay this kindness, return to your kingdom, bring the people peace, and cause them all to uphold the five precepts. Do not again hunt and kill, slaughtering birds and beasts. In this present life, your body will have no ease, and when your lifespan is ended, you will enter hell. People dwell in the world, but love and affection last only briefly. Parting and separation are long. Nothing lasts forever. Because of your past meritorious deeds, you have been born as a king. Do not, because of your power, indulge yourself.' At that time, the king deeply repented. From this day forward, he would follow Śyāma's instruction and not dare to neglect it. The several hundred people who had accompanied the king on the hunt, having seen that Śyāma had died but that celestial beings brought medicine from above and he came back to life, and that his parents' eyes opened, all leaped with joy and generated the aspiration to uphold the five precepts for the rest of their lives without transgression. When the king returned to his country, he proclaimed throughout the land that all the poor and those with blind parents, like Śyāma's, should be cared for and not abandoned. Those who violated this would be severely punished. Thereupon, the people of the country, because Śyāma had come back to life, taught and exhorted one another, high and low, to uphold the five precepts and practice the ten wholesome deeds. At death, they were all reborn in heaven, and none entered the three evil paths."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "All those who have come to this assembly: the one who in a past life was Śyāma is my present self. The blind father of that time is my present father, King Śuddhodana. The blind mother of that time is my present mother, the queen Māyā. The king of Kāśi of that time is Ānanda. Śakra of that time is Maitreya. That I swiftly attained the supreme, perfect, true path of enlightenment is entirely due to the kindness and nourishment of my parents. From death I was restored to life, moving the devas, dragons, ghosts, and spirits. The parents' grace was great, and it was the filial son who brought this about. That I have now become a Buddha, and have also saved the people of the nation, is all due to the virtue of filial devotion."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "You should widely teach this for all people. Those who have parents must not fail in filial devotion. The path must not be left unstudied. Delivering the spirit and escaping from suffering, and the attainment of the unconditioned in the end, are all brought about through compassionate filial devotion and studying the path."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, all the bodhisattvas, bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, kings, ministers, common people, elders, and householders paid their respects, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and departed.
