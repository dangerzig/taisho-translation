# 佛說越難經
## Sūtra of Yuenan, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0537

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Nie Chengyuan (聶承遠)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Vārāṇasī, at the Sage Bird settlement.

At that time, in the country there was an elder of the four castes named Yuenan, who was exceedingly wealthy, with rare pearls and treasures, cattle and horses, fields and houses in great abundance. Yuenan was stingy and jealous by nature, and did not believe in moral virtue or delight in generosity. Before sunset each day, he would instruct the gatekeeper: "If anyone comes to beg, do not let them through."

Yuenan had one son, named Candana, who was also stingy.

Yuenan later reached the end of his life and was reborn in the same country as the child of a blind beggar woman. Her husband said: "You are seriously ill, and now you are pregnant again. We are destitute and have no means for food or clothing. You must go."

The woman obeyed and left the house. Not far away, she found a great heap of dung and stopped there. In the ninth month she gave birth to a boy, and the child was also born blind. The mother went begging for food to raise him. When the child was seven years old, his mother said: "I have raised you for a long time. You must now take up your own walking-stick and begging bowl and go beg for yourself. You should speak pitifully and say: 'Of all sufferings in the world, poverty is the most grievous. I was born with ill fortune into a poor family, and both my eyes are blind. I can see nothing, and I am looked down upon by others. If anyone would give me a little food or drink to relieve my hunger, it would be like rain from heaven for one who is parched with thirst.'"

The child, hearing his mother's instructions, went begging from house to house. He came to Candana's house. Just as the child arrived, the gatekeeper happened to step out briefly. The blind child walked straight in and reached the inner courtyard, where he spoke as his mother had taught him.

At that time, Candana was on the upper gallery and heard his words. He flew into a rage and shouted to the gatekeeper: "Who let this blind beggar child in?" The gatekeeper was terrified. He seized the blind child and hurled him outside the gate, injuring his head and face, breaking his right arm, and smashing his food bowl. His food was scattered on the ground, and his body was in great pain. The child cried out in grief. His mother heard him and rushed to where he was, saying: "What wretched person has harmed my child? My child is still small, and both his eyes are blind. What offense has he committed to deserve this? What has he done to displease heaven?"

The child said to his mother: "I went begging at this gate. A man shouting in a loud voice called a strong man who seized me and threw me out, injuring my body. The pain is terrible. I shall die before long." At that moment, the guardian spirit of the gate spoke, saying: "The pain you now suffer is still but a small portion; the greater suffering comes hereafter. This is because in your past life you had wealth but did not give. Therefore you now endure hardship. Wealth and honor in this world are not permanent. To be rich and yet not give is the same as having nothing. When you die and suffer still greater pain, what good will regret do then?" At that time, the onlookers were very numerous, each speaking to one another, and the noise could be heard from afar.

At that time, the Buddha, arising from his meditation, entered the city with the bhikṣus on their alms-round. The Buddha asked Ānanda: "What is that commotion? Why is it so clamorous?"

Ānanda then told the Buddha the whole story of the blind child and his mother. He joined his palms and said to the Buddha: "May you have compassion and go to this child."

The Buddha was silent and did not respond. When the alms-round was finished and the meal had been taken, the Buddha went to see the child. Seeing the blind child's wounds and pain, the Buddha placed his hand on the child's head. The child's eyes immediately opened, and his broken injuries were healed. He then recognized his past life of his own accord. The Buddha asked: "Are you the elder named Yuenan from your past life?"

He answered: "I am."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "People dwelling in this world suffer greatly from foolishness and ignorance. Father and son of one lifetime do not recognize each other in the next."

The Buddha then spoke a sūtra to dispel his attachments, and spoke in verse:

> "People seek sons and seek wealth;
> In pursuing these two things,
> They suffer anxious toil and pain,
> While others reap the fruit.
> One cannot even preserve one's own body;
> How much less sons and wealth?
> It is like the heat of a summer day
> When one rests beneath a tree in the cool shade.
> In a moment one must depart again;
> Nothing in this world endures."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "When this child's life ends, to what destiny shall he go?" The Buddha said: "He shall enter the great Naraka hell for one night."

When the Buddha spoke this sūtra, more than eighty thousand people all cast off the three defilements and attained the Dharma-eye. The disciples were all filled with joy, made obeisance to the Buddha, and departed.
