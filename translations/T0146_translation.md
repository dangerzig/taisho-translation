# 舍衛國王夢見十事經
## Sūtra on the Ten Dreams of the King of Śrāvastī

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0146

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

The Buddha was in Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the Anāthapiṇḍada Monastery. At that time, King Prasenajit had ten dreams during the night. What were the ten things?

First, he saw three cauldrons side by side: the two on either side were full and boiling, with steam passing back and forth between them, yet it did not enter the empty middle cauldron. Second, he saw a horse that was eating from its mouth and also eating from its rump. Third, he saw small trees bearing flowers. Fourth, he saw small trees bearing fruit. Fifth, he saw a person cutting a rope, with a goat behind the person eating the rope. Sixth, he saw a fox sitting on a fine bed, eating from golden vessels. Seventh, he saw a large cow nursing from a small calf. Eighth, he saw four bulls coming from the four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight; just as they were about to clash, they disappeared and the bulls were nowhere to be found. Ninth, he saw a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges. Tenth, he saw a stream of water flowing bright red.

Having dreamed, the king immediately awoke. He was greatly frightened, fearing for the loss of his kingdom and his life. The next morning, the king summoned his ministers, officials, and those brāhmaṇas who were learned in the interpretation of dreams. They all came before the king. The king told them of his ten dreams of the night and asked who could interpret them. Those skilled in dream interpretation said: "I can interpret them, but I fear the king will not be pleased to hear it."

The king said: "Speak, according to what you know."

The brāhmaṇas interpreted for the king: "The crown prince should be killed and offered as a sacrifice to the Heavenly King. The honored queen should be killed and offered as a sacrifice to the Heavenly King. The attendant servants should be killed and offered as a sacrifice to the Heavenly King. All the white elephants should be killed and offered as a sacrifice to the Heavenly King. The prized horses should be killed and offered as a sacrifice to the Heavenly King. All the bedding and precious treasures and fine things worn on the body should all be burned as a sacrifice to the Heavenly King. Only then will the king's person be free from harm."

When the king heard the brāhmaṇas' interpretation, he was filled with great sorrow and worry. He withdrew into his purification chamber to reflect. The king had a queen named Māllī. She came to the king's purification chamber and asked: "Why has the king entered the purification chamber in sorrow? Have I done something wrong?"

The king said: "Do not ask. If you were to hear, it would only cause you grief."

The queen asked again: "What is the reason for your sorrow?"

The king said: "You need not ask further. To hear would only cause you worry."

The queen said again: "I am half of the king's own body. When the king has urgent matters, he should tell me."

The king then told her: "Last night I dreamed of ten things. First, I saw three cauldrons side by side: the two on either side were full and boiling, with steam passing back and forth, yet it did not enter the empty middle cauldron. Second, I saw a horse eating from its mouth and also from its rump. Third, I saw small trees bearing flowers. Fourth, I saw small trees bearing fruit. Fifth, I saw a person cutting a rope, with a goat behind eating the rope. Sixth, I saw a fox sitting on a fine bed, eating from golden vessels. Seventh, I saw a large cow nursing from a small calf. Eighth, I saw four bulls coming from the four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight; just as they were about to clash, the bulls were nowhere to be found. Ninth, I saw a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges. Tenth, I saw a great stream of water flowing bright red. I dreamed thus, and upon waking was terrified, fearing for the loss of my kingdom, the loss of my son, and the loss of my people. The next morning I summoned my ministers and the brāhmaṇas skilled in dream interpretation and told them my dreams. The brāhmaṇas' interpretation was that everything the king loves should be offered as a sacrifice to heaven. This is why I am in sorrow."

The queen said: "Do not be sorrowful, great king! When a person buys gold, one rubs it on a touchstone to see its quality on the stone. The Buddha is now at the Jetavana, the Anāthapiṇḍada Monastery. You may go and ask the Buddha about the meaning of your dreams. Whatever the Buddha interprets, we should follow."

The king then ordered his attendants to prepare the carriages and cavalry. When the carriages and cavalry were ready, the king mounted the high-canopied carriage called Bārāyaṇa. With several thousand cavalry, he departed from Śrāvastī and arrived at the Jetavana, the Anāthapiṇḍada Monastery. He walked slowly on the path, dismounted the carriage, and walked on foot to where the Buddha was. Seeing the Buddha, he placed his head at the Buddha's feet, then sat down and said to the Buddha:

"Last night I dreamed of ten things. First, I saw three cauldrons side by side: the two on either side were full and boiling, with steam passing back and forth, yet it did not enter the empty middle cauldron. Second, I saw a horse eating from its mouth and also from its rump. Third, I saw small trees bearing flowers. Fourth, I saw small trees bearing fruit. Fifth, I saw a person cutting a rope, with a goat behind eating the rope. Sixth, I saw a fox sitting on a fine bed, eating from golden vessels. Seventh, I saw a large cow nursing from a small calf. Eighth, I saw four bulls coming from the four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight; just as they were about to clash, the bulls were nowhere to be found. Ninth, I saw a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges. Tenth, I saw a great stream of water flowing bright red. I dreamed thus, and upon waking was terrified, fearing for the loss of my kingdom, the loss of my son, and the loss of my people."

The king said: "I beseech the Buddha to interpret these ten things for me."

The Buddha then told the king: "Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! What you dreamed is nothing else. For the king's person there is no evil, for the country there is no evil, for the prince there is no evil, and for the queen there is no evil. What the king dreamed pertains only to the future age.

"In the future age, people will not fear the Dharma. They will all be licentious and greedy, not content with one wife, frequently angry, foolish, and without shame.

"The king dreamed of three cauldrons side by side, the two on either side full and boiling with steam passing back and forth, yet not entering the empty middle cauldron: in the future age, people will not provide for the poor. Those who are closely related but both wealthy will exchange gifts among themselves. The king's first dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of a horse eating from its mouth and also from its rump: in the future age, ministers will draw provisions from the government and also take from the people. The king's second dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of small trees bearing flowers: in the future age, people not yet thirty years old will have white hair growing on their heads. The king's third dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of small trees bearing fruit: in the future age, young women will marry and carry children at an early age, without any sense of shame. The king's fourth dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of a person cutting a rope with a goat behind eating the rope: in the future age, when a man goes out on business, leaving his wife at home, the wife will secretly share her bed with another man. The king's fifth dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of a fox sitting on a fine bed, eating from golden vessels: in the future age, people of low station who come to possess wealth will sit in the seats of honor and eat the finest delicacies. The king's sixth dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of a large cow nursing from a small calf: in the future age, a mother will act as a matchmaker for her daughter, bringing a man to share the daughter's room, while the mother guards the door and takes the money earned from the daughter's licentiousness to sustain herself. The king's seventh dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of four bulls coming from the four directions, bellowing and rushing toward each other as if to fight, but just as they were about to clash they disappeared: in the future age, ministers will not fear heaven, being licentious and greedy, not content with one wife, frequently angry, foolish, without shame, and without respect for superiors or inferiors. The rain spirits will not send rain at the proper season. Emperors, officials, and the people will all pray for rain. The rain spirits, seeing that emperors, officials, and the people conduct themselves thus, will cause clouds to arise from the four directions. Emperors and officials will rejoice, saying: 'Clouds have arisen on all sides; now it will surely rain.' Yet in a moment, the clouds will each scatter and disperse. The rain spirits do this deliberately, to make emperors, officials, and the people fear heaven and earth, to stop their licentiousness and greed, to keep to one wife, and to have compassionate hearts without anger. The king's eighth dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of a great pond of water, turbid in the center but clear at the four edges: in the future age, the people within the central lands of Jambudvīpa will be unfilial to their parents, disrespectful to their elders, without loyalty or gratitude, and heedless of the future. The people of the border countries will be filial to their parents, respectful of their elders, and loyal and trustworthy. The king's ninth dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm.

"The king dreamed of a great pond of water flowing bright red: in the future age, various kings will not be satisfied with their own countries and will raise armies. The people will take up arms and fight one another. They will make war with chariots, with cavalry, and with infantry. Chariots will slaughter one another, cavalry will slaughter one another, infantry will slaughter one another, and the blood will flow bright red. The king's tenth dream pertains precisely to this. Do not be afraid! Do not be afraid! For the country, for yourself, for your wife and children, there is no harm. These dreams all pertain to events of the future age to come."

The king then knelt and said: "Having received the Buddha's teaching, my heart is immediately filled with joy. It is like a person holding a small vessel to receive oil: when the oil is too much and the vessel too small, one seeks a larger vessel. Obtaining the larger vessel and pouring the oil into it, one is immediately at ease and free from fear."

The king then bowed his head twice, placed his head at the Buddha's feet, and departed. He returned to his palace, bestowed additional rewards upon his chief queen, and confiscated the stipends of the ministers and officials who had advised him. He never again trusted the words of the brāhmaṇas.
