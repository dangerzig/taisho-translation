# 押座文類
## Collection of Seat-Settling Texts

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2845

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

## Seat-Settling Text for the Vimalakīrti Sūtra

> I bow to the Wondrous Joy Tathāgata of the Upper Fragrant-Accumulation World,
> His transformation body and manifested form.
> Though he has wife, children, and retinue,
> His mind is pure, always cultivating the Brahmā-conduct.
> His wisdom, powers, and spiritual penetrations are difficult to fathom.
> His hand shakes the sun and moon and moves Mount Sumeru.
>
> Our Buddha, the Tathāgata, in the Āmra Garden
> Proclaimed the most profound Universal-Gathering Teaching.
> The elder's body and mind were filled with joy;
> He took his jeweled canopy and went before the Tathāgata.
>
> Shimmering and swaying, it spread golden clouds;
> Bells of the seven jewels, pair by pair, wafted fragrance afar.
> He went straight to the Dharma assembly at the Āmra Garden
> And offered the jeweled canopy to the Tathāgata.
>
> Five hundred flower canopies stood before him;
> By the holy power, they merged into a single canopy.
> Sun, moon, and stars were all revealed within;
> Mountains, rivers, the great earth, and the nāga palace.
>
> When the world shook, the jeweled canopy shook;
> When the world trembled, the jeweled canopy trembled.
> All the pure lands of the ten directions,
> The tathāgatas of the three times: all appeared within.
>
> In the heart of the land of Vaiśālī,
> Jeweled trees gleamed with golden splendor.
> Many there were who excelled in kindness and compassion,
> Having cultivated practice in previous lives.
>
> The layman Vimalakīrti, honored among the assembly,
> His ten virtues perfectly bright, revered by all.
> Having been close to the boundless buddhas of the three times,
> He is therefore called "Vimalakīrti, the Elder."
>
> Five hundred śrāvakas were all reproved;
> They clung to characteristics, and the Dharma of emptiness demarcated what they had realized.
> And the assemblies of Prabhāvyūha and Maitreya
> In body and mind all bowed among the followers of the path.
>
> The non-dual true gate is of a nature self-fused.
> Only Vimalakīrti personally realized awakening.
> Manifesting illness, lying alone in his chamber,
> He broadly discussed the six chapters of inconceivability.
>
> The Great Sage Muni, deep in compassion and vow,
> One by one personally summoned the ten great disciples.
> All said they were not worthy to inquire after his illness;
> Only Mañjuśrī, teacher of a thousand buddhas.
>
> His majestic body moved the Palace of Jeweled Stars;
> Towering, the pearls shook as the jeweled seats flew.
> Eighty thousand sages, their fragrance filling the land,
> Thousands upon thousands of holy beings pervading the long sky.
>
> They requested a meal from the Fragrant-Accumulation above,
> A transformation-seat, the Lion's Roar of the Lamp King.
> All arrived at Vimalakīrti's ten-foot chamber
> To perform the Buddha's work and widely propagate it.
>
> This morning we intend to expound the most profound sūtra.
> We humbly pray that with compassion you will come to this place.
> When the listeners hear the sūtra, their transgressions are extinguished,
> And all will realize the jeweled body of bodhi and Dharma.
>
> The burning house stretches on: when will it end?
> The five desires will always result in the suffering of birth and death.
>
> Nothing compares to hearing the sūtra and seeking liberation.
> Study the Buddha and cultivate practice: can you, or can you not?
> If you can, devoutly join your palms;
> The title and name of the sūtra: let the chanter call it forth.

## Seat-Settling Text for the Three Bodies

> I always lament the many kalpas spent in transmigration.
> In the age of the declining Dharma, obstacles and difficulties abound; how shameful.
> Our World-Honored One's compassion and vow are weighty:
> He has left only the Buddha's teaching in the world.
>
> In the sahā world he serves as a vessel of passage;
> In the kalpa of the five turbidities, he serves as guide.
> But sentient beings' evil karma is so heavy
> That the mind of reverent faith is exceedingly rare.
>
> See a person committing evil: they push their heads in together.
> Hear that the sūtra is being taught: they find an excuse not to go.
> In this life, not a single good deed performed;
> In the next life, obtaining a human body will be exceedingly hard.
>
> Not knowing, not perceiving, in the greatest haste and confusion,
> Not fearing, not alarmed, constantly creating transgressions.
> Were it not that the King of Death presses one's head down,
> One would plan on a thousand years, even ten thousand years.
>
> This morning is a rare encounter with the Great Vehicle sūtra,
> Like seeing a single blossom of the uḍumbara flower.
> If one can briefly hear and listen to the wondrous Dharma,
> The evil karma of ten thousand kalpas within the body is extinguished.
>
> The wheel-turning king's topknot-jewel is encountered at this time;
> The poor son's jewel sewn in the robe is found today.
> Practice even one of the ten Dharma-practices,
> And six thousand merits adorn the body.
>
> Since you have been able to come to this place of practice,
> It must be that you wish to hear the wondrous Dharma.
> Those who do: single-mindedly join your palms;
> The title and name of the sūtra: let it be called forth.
>
> This morning the Dharma-master speaks the truth.
> Those seated below in the audience, do not delay your practice: recite the Buddha's name.
> Hurry home with haste;
> If you return too late, Grandma at home will be cross!

## Seat-Settling Text for the Eight Marks

> First, from the Tuṣita Heaven he descended to the human world;
> First, at the royal palace he displayed the sign of birth.
> Nine dragons together poured fragrant blended water,
> Competing to bathe the body upon the lotus-petal.
>
> The holy lord Māyā went to the rear garden;
> Consorts and attendant women ran about in joyful clamor.
> Fish leaping through blue waves, worthy of admiration;
> The color of the aśoka flowers, most fit for contemplation.
>
> The aśoka tree spread its leaves in full bloom;
> The queen paced slowly among them.
> Raising her hand, she happened to grasp a branch and remaining leaves;
> The holy lord Śākyamuni was born from her side.
>
> The compassionate father Śākyamuni descended at birth,
> Emerging from the left side of the womb.
> The nine dragons sprinkling water were already a lustral rite;
> Beneath his feet of a thousand spokes, auspicious lotuses appeared.
>
> The seer Asita informed the great king:
> "The prince's auspicious signs are truly portentous.
> This is no ordinary, common affair;
> He must become a great Dharma-king of bodhi."
>
> "In a former life, I formed a fine bond with Your Highness;
> Your humble consort now would not dare presume."
> That day, Yaśodharā pleaded again and again;
> The prince thereupon removed his finger-ring and gave it to her.
>
> With flowing robes, he felt no attachment to worldly glory.
> Weary of the deep palace, the prince
> Abandoned the throne of the golden wheel and the seven treasures;
> At midnight, he leaped over the city wall, vowing to go forth.
>
> Six years of austerities in the mountains;
> Birds and beasts dwelt together as his companions.
> In long hunger, not eating what is truly called the meal of cultivation,
> On hemp and wheat he would have shortly come to his end.
>
> He attained realization beneath the bodhi tree,
> Vanquishing the hosts of Māra to accomplish perfect awakening.
> On Vulture Peak he released the ray from between his brows;
> At the Deer Park he first converted the five monks.
>
> First he opened the teaching of existence to benefit all beings;
> Next he taught the doctrine of emptiness to bring about understanding.
> Later, at Spirit Mountain, he expounded the wondrous Dharma;
> Benefiting the present and the future: inconceivable.

Among these places of grievous sorrow: arriving at the southern city, one does not return. Rising at dawn, one walks early along the sandy path. At dusk, one stops to seek provisions by the road. A mother longing for her children should take thought for the long term. One should seek to meet the Tathāgata within the assembly. Today, by the merit of this sūtra-lecture, may we, upon the favorable breeze, return home soon.

Among these places of separation: every night one only hears grieving on all sides. "May I ask, what causes such sorrow?" "Yesterday, a brutal bandit seized my child. Lonely and poor, approaching old age, to suffer this! I appeal to heaven above: please look upon us. If only mother and child could meet again, it would be like a withered tree putting forth branches once more."

"Your disciple makes an offering of one bundle, divided in a time of hardship. May peace be had, and may the child soon be released. A long delay makes the lowly."

When the sun sets in this land, it illuminates the west. Do not say the sun truly disappears when it sinks in the west. When the Buddha enters extinction here, he transforms in other lands. Do not say the World-Honored One has truly entered final extinction. It is like the moon in the long sky, blocked and hidden by floating clouds so it does not come out. Within the body there is a Buddha-nature, perfectly clear, but covered and concealed by karmic obstructions, it does not appear. If one wishes the moon in the long sky to appear, one first needs the help of a keen wind. If one wishes the Buddha-nature within the body to become clear, one must diligently listen to the Great Vehicle. Once the clouds are blown and scattered by the keen wind, the moon's reflection in the long sky comes forth. While listening to the most profound and wondrous Dharma, the Buddha-nature within the body becomes perfectly clear. One sprinkle, two sprinkles, the rain of the Dharma settles the dust of the crossroads in abundance. One verse, two verses of the Great Vehicle sūtra extinguish the transgressions within the body in abundance.

I was about to invite the Buddha, but people are still taking their seats. After a little while I shall begin to expound the sūtra. Those who wish to listen, examine your minds and join your palms.

## Seat-Settling Text for the Hot-Water (Bathing) Sūtra

I bow to the Great Awakened One of the Upper Realm. I take refuge in the difficult-to-conceive, pure assembly. The four wisdoms, three bodies, following the wishes of all beings: the compassionate, sixteen-foot Śākyamuni.

For a hundred thousand million kalpas he served as a wheel-turning king, yet he took no delight in the royal palace and its bonds of affection. Giving up his life and body for a thousand million kalpas, until this present body he realized bodhi.

Long sinking in the ocean of birth and death, neither perceiving nor knowing, drawn by the force of karma. When karmic obstructions are eliminated, one now beholds the Buddha, whose light illuminates the three thousand world-systems.

In the kingdom of Vaiśālī there was the Āmra Garden. The maiden Āmrapālī was born within a mango. The jeweled tree, its branches shining with fine color, was neither ordinary nor holy: a transformation-born body.

Jīvaka was born of the mango maiden. With wondrous, penetrating medical arts he healed all beings. He could cure all manner of illness. The nation praised him as a precious great physician-king.

His father, named Jīvaka, was a kind and compassionate worthy. When he applied needles, ailments were immediately relieved. His fame was high throughout eight kingdoms as an elder. He turned back death and revived the deceased throughout Jambudvīpa.

Jīvaka thought upon the Lord Muni. He clearly directed his household to go together to the Buddha. They went straight to the Dharma assembly at Spirit Mountain and invited the Buddha and the ordinary saṃgha to bathe.

The Buddha taught that the seven items each have their merit. He did not pray for a bath to cleanse the Dharma-body. The fragrant water can purify the ordinary and holy assembly. The merit is immeasurable, fulfilling all wishes.

This morning we intend to expound the most profound text. We humbly pray that with compassion you will come to this place. When the listeners hear the sūtra, their transgressions are extinguished, and all will realize the jeweled body of bodhi and Dharma.

In this turbid and wretched Jambudvīpa, truly worthy of compassion, old age and illness throughout the day are like perpetual drunkenness. Having already abandoned the clamor, seeking to escape: sit upright and listen to the sūtra. Can you, or can you not? If you can, devoutly join your palms; the title and name of the sūtra: let the chanter call it forth.

In the west there are platforms of white silver. The four assemblies listen to the Dharma and all their minds are opened. Those who wish to hear the Dharma, join your palms. Let the lead chanter call forth the sūtra's title.
