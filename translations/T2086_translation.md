# Record of the Northern Wei Monk Huìshēng's Mission to the Western Regions

## *Běiwèi Sēng Huìshēng Shǐ Xīyù Jì* (北魏僧惠生使西域記)

Taishō 2086

Author unknown. A historical travelogue.

*Note: This text records the journey of the monk Huìshēng (*Huìshēng*, 惠生) and the Dunhuang layman Sòng Yún (*Sòng Yún*, 宋雲), sent by Empress Dowager Líng of the Northern Wei in 518 CE to obtain Buddhist scriptures from Central Asia and India. They obtained 170 texts, all Mahāyāna works.*

In the winter of the eleventh month of the first year of the Shénguī (*Shénguī*, 神龜) era of the Wei [518 CE], the Empress Dowager dispatched the bhikṣu Huìshēng of the Chónglì (*Chónglì*, 崇立) Monastery, together with the man of Dunhuang (*Dūnhuáng*, 敦煌), Sòng Yún, to travel to the Western Regions to obtain sūtras. In all, they obtained one hundred and seventy texts, all of them being wonderful scriptures of the Great Vehicle.

Setting out from the capital, they traveled west for forty days and reached Red Ridge (*Chìlǐng*, 赤嶺), which is the western frontier of the nation. The mountains have no grass or trees. There are birds and mice that share the same burrows.

They then traveled west for twenty days and reached the kingdom of Tuyuhun (*Tǔgǔhún*, 吐谷渾). They traveled further west for three thousand five hundred *lǐ* and reached the city of Shanshan (*Shànshàn*, 鄯善). They traveled west another one thousand six hundred *lǐ* and reached the city of Qiemo (*Qiěmò*, 且末). There were images of buddhas and bodhisattvas made during the time when Lǚ Guāng (*Lǚ Guāng*, 呂光) conquered the Hu people. They traveled west another one thousand three hundred and seventy-five *lǐ* and reached the city of Mò (*Mò*, 末). They traveled west another twenty-two *lǐ* and reached the city of Hànmì (*Hànmì*, 捍𡡉). There was a stūpa for offering to the Buddha built by Khotan (*Yútián*, 于闐). Beside it were several thousand small stūpas. Pennants hung in the tens of thousands. They traveled west another eight hundred and seventy-eight *lǐ* and reached the kingdom of Khotan. There was a stūpa in the form of an inverted bowl made by the king of the land. There were pratyekabuddha boots, still not decayed to this day. The territory of Khotan extends three thousand *lǐ* from east to west.

On the twenty-ninth day of the seventh month of the second year of Shénguī [519 CE], they entered the kingdom of Zhūjūbō (*Zhūjūbō*, 朱駒波). The people dwell in the mountains. They do not practice slaughter and eat only the flesh of animals that die naturally. The customs and language are the same as Khotan. Their learning is the same as the Brahmins. The territory of this kingdom can be traversed in five days.

In the eighth month they entered the territory of the kingdom of Hépántuó (*Hépántuó*, 渴盤陀). They traveled west for six hundred *lǐ* and ascended the Pamir Mountains (*Cōnglǐng*, 葱嶺). They traveled west for three more days and reached the city of Bōmèng (*Bōmèng*, 鉢孟). Three days later they reached the Poison Dragon Pool (*Dúlóng Chí*, 毒龍池). In the past, the king of Hépántuó had a Brahmin curse the dragon, and it moved to a place west of the Pamirs, more than two thousand *lǐ* from this location. From the ascent of the Pamirs, each step grew gradually higher. After four days of this, they reached the summit, which seemed approximately halfway to the heavens. The kingdom of Hépántuó is situated right on the mountaintop. From the Pamirs westward, all water flows west into the western sea. The people of the world say this is the center of heaven and earth.

In the middle of the ninth month, they entered the kingdom of Bōhé (*Bōhé*, 鉢和). High mountains and deep valleys with dangerous paths were a constant feature. The people used the mountains as their cities, wore felt clothing, and dwelt in caves. People and livestock depended on one another. Wind and snow were fierce and biting. There were great snow mountains that appeared like jade peaks.

In the first ten days of the tenth month they entered the kingdom of the Hephthalites (*Yèdá*, 嚈噠). They had no walled cities but followed the water and grass. They did not know writing. They had no surplus beyond the year and counted twelve months as one year. They received tribute from all the nations. From Tieluo (*Diéluó*, 牒羅) in the south to the Tiele (*Chìlè*, 勅勒) in the north, from Khotan in the east to Persia (*Bōsī*, 波斯) in the west, more than forty nations all came to pay tribute. They were the most powerful. The king's tent measured forty paces in circumference. His utensils were of the seven treasures. He did not believe in the buddha-dharma and killed living beings for blood food. When he saw the Wei envoy, he bowed and received the imperial edict. The distance from the capital was more than twenty thousand *lǐ*.

In the eleventh month they entered the kingdom of Persia. The territory was very narrow; it took seven months to pass through. The people dwelt in mountain valleys. The glare of the snow was blinding.

In mid-November they entered the kingdom of Shēmí (*Shēmí*, 賒彌). Gradually emerging from the Pamirs, the rocky crags were perilous and steep. People and horses could barely pass. Iron chains were suspended to traverse the gaps. Looking down, one could not see the bottom.

In the first ten days of the twelfth month they entered the kingdom of Udyāna (*Wūchǎng*, 烏場). To the north it borders the Pamirs; to the south it connects with India (*Tiānzhú*, 天竺). The climate is warm and mild. The fields are rich and productive. The people and goods are prosperous. The king eats vegetables and observes a permanent fast. Morning and night he worships the Buddha. Only after midday does he attend to the affairs of state. The sound of bells fills the land. Exotic flowers are offered. When the king heard that the Wei envoy had come, he prostrated and received the imperial edict. In this land there are places where the Tathāgata dried his garments and the stone where he set his shoes. The remaining traces of the Buddha are clearly visible wherever one goes. At each site of the Buddha's footprint there are monasteries and stūpas. The bhikṣus observe the precepts with austere practice.

In mid-April of the first year of the Zhèngguāng (*Zhèngguāng*, 正光) era [520 CE] they entered the kingdom of Gandhāra (*Qiántuóluó*, 乾陀羅). The land is similar to Udyāna. It was originally called Yèbōluó (*Yèbōluó*, 業波羅). It was destroyed by the Hephthalites, who installed a Tiele chieftain as king. The people of the land are all Brahmins. Being under the Hephthalites, the king was fond of killing and did not believe in the buddha-dharma. In conflict with Kapiśa (*Jìbīn*, 罽賓) over their border, they fought wars year after year. The soldiers were weary and the people resentful. The king received the edict while sitting, showing arrogance and discourtesy. He sent the envoy to a monastery with very meager provisions.

They traveled west to the great river of the Indus (*Xīntóu Dàhé*, 新頭大河). They traveled further west for thirteen days and reached the city of Puṣkalāvatī (*Fóshāfú*, 佛沙伏). The city walls were straight and well-constructed. Groves and springs were lush and abundant. The land was rich in precious treasures. The customs were pure and virtuous. There were eminent monks of great virtue whose practice of the way was lofty and extraordinary. The stone images were splendidly adorned, covered entirely with gold leaf. There were footprints of Kāśyapa Buddha (*Jiāshèpó Fó*, 迦葉波佛).

They traveled west for one more day and crossed a deep river of more than three hundred paces by boat. They then traveled southwest for sixty *lǐ* and reached the city of Gandhāra. There was a stūpa called Qúelí (*Qúelí*, 雀離) built by King Kaniṣka (*Jiāníjiā*, 迦尼迦) two hundred years after the Buddha's nirvāṇa. It had twelve stories, rising seven hundred feet above the ground, with a base more than three hundred paces in circumference. It was built entirely of carved stone for the steps. The buddha images inside underwent a thousand transformations. Golden discs shone brilliantly and jeweled bells rang harmoniously. Among the stūpas of the Western Regions, this was the foremost.

They traveled further northwest and crossed a great river to the kingdom of Nagarahāra (*Nàjiāluó*, 那迦邏). There were the skull-bone relic of the Buddha and a stone stūpa inscription in Sanskrit (*fànzì*, 梵字) handwritten by the Buddha.

In all, they remained in the kingdom of Udyāna for two years. In the second year of the Zhèngguāng era [521 CE], they returned to the capital.
