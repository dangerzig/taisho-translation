# 如意輪菩薩觀門義注祕訣
## Secret Instructions on the Meaning-Commentary of the Contemplation Gate of the Wish-Fulfilling Wheel Bodhisattva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1088

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

The syllable OṂ: the meaning of the influx of all dharmas. Unattainable by skillful means, awakening itself is unattainable.

The syllable VA: the meaning of all dharmas transcending. The path of speech is unattainable.

The syllable RA: the meaning of all dharmas being free from defilement. Even understanding is unattainable.

The syllable DA: the meaning of all dharmas as giving. Ultimately unattainable.

The syllable PA: the meaning of all dharmas in their supreme sense. Inherent characteristics are unattainable.

The combined syllable NAMA: all dharmas are without self. Grasping and attachment are unattainable.

The syllable HŪṂ: the ultimate meaning of all dharmas is also unattainable.

Arrange the above syllables, all in the color of turmeric-gold, and draw them from the principal deity's mouth into the wheels of body, mind, and speech. When they have been clearly arranged, contemplate each one with careful discernment. From this samaya, one swiftly attains omniscience. In the yoga method of recitation, when the count is complete, arrange the syllables and further distribute the lesser heart-mantra upon the body. Then upon the syllables, contemplate clearly and vividly, like the sun first rising, its radiance brilliant and vast beyond all limit. Through such contemplation, before long one shall realize the samādhi of the dharma-body.

> The syllable OṂ is placed at the crown.
> The syllable VA is placed at the forehead.
> The syllable RA is distributed at both eyes.
> The syllable DA is contemplated at the mouth.
> The syllable PA is placed at the heart.
> The syllable NAMA is placed at both shoulders.
> The syllable HŪṂ is placed at the navel-wheel.

> Through the above gate of contemplating the syllables,
> One swiftly awakens to the principle of non-arising.
> Before long one shall attain accomplishment
> And acquire the wisdom of the tathāgata.

The arrangement of the Sanskrit syllables of the Wish-Fulfilling Wheel heart-mantra upon the bodhicitta moon-disc.

Commentary on the meaning of the Wish-Fulfilling Wheel mantra:

*Namo ratna-trayāya namaḥ ārya-avalokiteśvarāya bodhisattvāya mahāsattvāya mahā-kāruṇikāya tadyathā*

OṂ: this syllable embodies the meaning of the three bodies. The single syllable OṂ is composed of the three letters A, MA, and so forth. The syllable OṂ means that the arising of all dharmas is unattainable. The syllable A means that all dharmas are originally non-arising. The syllable MA means that the self of all dharmas is unattainable. Another interpretation: the syllable OṂ signifies the transformation body, the syllable A signifies the reward body, and the syllable MA signifies the dharma body. Through these three syllables one accords with the principle of true reality, thus constituting the bowing in homage to all tathāgatas. This is also the meaning of the tathāgata's anointment of the crown.

*Cakra-vartī cintā-maṇi*

The meaning of contemplation; also the meaning of the tathāgata's treasure. The treasure has six kinds, as it can fulfill the wishes of all sentient beings.

*Mahā-padma*

RU RU: the meaning of being free from dust and defilement. Because dust is of two kinds, it is spoken twice. The two kinds of dust are inner dust and outer dust. Inner dust refers to the five sense-faculties: eye, ear, nose, tongue, and body. Outer dust refers to form, sound, smell, taste, and touch. With regard to these two kinds of dust, all are unattainable by skillful means, thus constituting the contemplative wisdom of yoga.

TIṢṬHA: explained as the meaning of "abiding," also as "non-abiding." Non-abiding is the foundation of all dharmas. It also means non-abiding nirvāṇa, not dwelling in saṃsāra. Through great wisdom one is not attached to saṃsāra; through great compassion one does not abide in nirvāṇa.

JVALA: explained as the meaning of "blazing." The radiance illumines and pervades the dharma-realms of space in the ten directions, startling and awakening the assemblies of buddhas, bodhisattvas, and sages, who descend and gather universally, and below summoning all the devas, nāgas, eight classes of beings, and all sentient creatures, for the purpose of benefiting and empowering them.

ĀKARṢĀYA: the meaning of "summoning." Through this summoning, the buddhas, bodhisattvas, devas, nāgas, and eight classes of beings respond to the call and arrive, gathering like clouds to empower, protect, and keep in mind.

The syllable HŪṂ: the meaning that all dharmas are without cause. It also means the site of bodhi. As the *Vajraśekhara Yoga Sūtra* says, the moment one generates the mind of bodhi, one is seated at the site of awakening, turning the wheel of the true dharma. Through this correspondence, a single syllable of this mantra enables one to awaken to all buddha-dharmas. At the moment of thought after thought awakening to the buddha-dharma, one is endowed with sarvajña-jñāna, proceeding directly to the ultimate, seated upon the vajra-throne. When the four māras appear, one enters the great compassion samādhi, and by subduing the four māras one attains true awakening. The four māras are the skandha-māra, the kleśa-māra, the death-māra, and the deva-māra. All are subdued. Furthermore, "dharmas are without cause" means that all dharmas are without cause; according to the ultimate truth, even effects are unattainable. Because there is no cause and effect, they are originally pure.

The syllable PHAṬ: the meaning of "shattering" or "destruction." Upon attaining buddhahood, the four māras are destroyed. It also signifies the virtue of cutting, among the three virtues. "Cutting" means that through wisdom one severs the afflictions and habitual tendencies of the three realms and nine grounds. The two provisions of merit and wisdom are both completely fulfilled. From beginningless time, all sentient beings have been regarded as one's own kin. Recollecting their kindness, one proceeds to the place of turning the dharma-wheel and expounds the twelve turnings of the dharma-wheel.

The syllable ṬA: the meaning that all dharmas are unattainable. In Sanskrit script, the form of ṭa is like a half-moon. It is explained as: all dharmas are without self, and unattainable by skillful means, thus constituting the emptiness of beings. Because the emptiness of beings is half, the form is half.

SVĀ and related syllables: the meaning of being beyond speech. This constitutes the wisdom of dharma-emptiness, the meaning of the fulfilled syllable. It also means the accomplishment of great meritorious virtue.

The syllable HA: the meaning of severing afflictions. It causes all sentient beings to accumulate great merit, sever the afflictions, subsidiary afflictions, and all habitual tendencies. When the teaching of syllables pervading one's own and others' bodies is realized, one attains the anupādhiśeṣa non-abiding nirvāṇa.

When the yoga practitioner forms the mudrā-seal and recites the mantra, settling the mind in single-pointed concentration upon this one syllable, through correspondence with the true principle, at the time of contemplation one can eliminate both the perceiving subject and the perceived object. Self and other become equal, with the great compassion of shared embodiment. Suddenly all merit and wisdom are gathered. In the present, all calamities and untimely death are dispelled. Worldly people who see and hear one will be delighted, and all one's words will be honored and obeyed by devas and humans, who will not dare to transgress. At the time of death, the assembly of sages will appear before one. One will be reborn in the Land of Ultimate Bliss, the pure and wondrous realm. In the midst of the great assembly, one will be born from a lotus, see Amitāyus Buddha, hear the true dharma, and suddenly realize unsurpassed bodhi.

Verse on the six arms of the Wish-Fulfilling Wheel Bodhisattva:

> "Holding the wish-fulfilling jewel in hand,
> Six-armed, the body golden in color,
> The topknot adorned with jewels,
> The Sovereign King teaches the dharma.

> The first hand is in contemplation,
> Out of compassion for sentient beings.
> The second holds the wish-fulfilling jewel,
> Able to fulfill all wishes.
> The third holds a rosary,
> To liberate beings from the suffering of the animal realm.

> The left hand presses upon the Radiant Mountain,
> Accomplishing immovability.
> The second holds a lotus hand,
> Able to purify all that is contrary to the dharma.
> The third hand holds a wheel,
> Able to turn the unsurpassed dharma.

> The jewel can give as wished,
> The mountain represents immovability,
> The lotus is unstained by nature,
> The wheel crushes obstructions and afflictions.

> The six arms in vast adornment
> Can travel through the six destinies.
> With great compassion and skillful means,
> They sever the suffering of all beings.

> The body radiates a thousand rays of light,
> Illuminating universally wherever one recollects.
> Like a bright mirror
> That can reflect myriad images.

> The self-mind is originally non-arising,
> For the dharma-nature is perfectly quiescent."

This is from the *Vajraśekhara Yoga Sūtra*.
