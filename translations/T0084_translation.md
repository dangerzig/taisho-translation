# 分別布施經
## Sūtra on Distinguishing Gifts

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0084

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the Śākya homeland, in the city of Kapilavastu, in the Nyagrodha Tree Garden, together with an assembly of bhikṣus. At that time, there was a bhikṣuṇī named Mahāprajāpatī who took a new cotton robe and came to where the Buddha was. Having arrived, she prostrated at the Buddha's feet, withdrew to stand to one side, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! This new cotton robe I have made with my own hands. I offer it to the World-Honored One. I humbly pray that you accept it, so that throughout the long night I may obtain great benefit and happiness."

At that time, the Buddha told Mahāprajāpatī: "You should take this cotton robe and give it to the great assembly. The excellent merit obtained will be equal to making an offering to me, without any difference."

Then the bhikṣuṇī Mahāprajāpatī again addressed the Buddha: "My original aspiration was solely for the World-Honored One that I made this robe. I pray the Buddha will accept it, so that throughout the long night I may obtain great benefit and happiness." Thus she earnestly entreated three times, and the Buddha likewise answered three times: "You should simply give equally to the great assembly. The excellent merit obtained will be no different from giving to me."

Then the Venerable Ānanda, attending at the Buddha's side, saw this and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! This bhikṣuṇī Mahāprajāpatī is a relative of the Buddha and has great beneficence; only the Buddha himself knows this. The robe she offers: may the Buddha accept it. Now is the right time, so that Mahāprajāpatī may throughout the long night obtain great benefit and happiness."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! So it is, so it is! This Mahāprajāpatī is my relative and has great beneficence; I myself know this well. Now she has made a robe with her own hands to give to me; this is truly a difficult matter. Why? Ānanda, you should know! For any person to give rise to pure faith and take refuge in the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṃgha is truly a difficult matter. Furthermore, to maintain the precepts of not killing, not stealing, not engaging in sexual misconduct, not lying, and not drinking intoxicants and other lay precepts: such a person is even more difficult to find. How much more so one who, before the Buddha, the World-Honored One, joins palms in reverence and practices giving, and after giving has pure faith in the Buddha without doubt, and is likewise without doubt regarding the Dharma and the Saṃgha, and has permanently severed doubtful views regarding the Four Noble Truths of suffering, origination, cessation, and the path. Ānanda! Now this bhikṣuṇī Mahāprajāpatī has given rise to pure faith, has taken refuge in the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṃgha, and maintains the precepts of not killing, not stealing, not engaging in sexual misconduct, not lying, and not drinking intoxicants and other lay precepts. Moreover, regarding the Buddha, the Dharma, the Saṃgha, and the Four Noble Truths of suffering, origination, cessation, and the path, she has already severed all doubt. This bhikṣuṇī has accomplished what is difficult to accomplish; the Buddha himself knows this.

"Ānanda! There are fourteen types of comparative giving. What are the fourteen? First, giving to those who are sick and suffering; second, giving to those who have broken the precepts; third, giving to those who maintain the precepts; fourth, giving to those who are free from defilements; fifth, giving to those progressing toward the fruit of stream-entry; sixth, giving to those who have attained the fruit of stream-entry; seventh, giving to those progressing toward the fruit of once-returning; eighth, giving to those who have attained the fruit of once-returning; ninth, giving to those progressing toward the fruit of non-returning; tenth, giving to those who have attained the fruit of non-returning; eleventh, giving to those progressing toward arhatship; twelfth, giving to those who have attained arhatship; thirteenth, giving to pratyekabuddhas; fourteenth, giving to a Tathāgata, an arhat, one of right and universal awakening. Ānanda! You should now know: giving to the sick and suffering yields twofold merit; giving to those who have broken the precepts yields a hundredfold merit; giving to those who maintain the precepts yields a thousandfold merit; giving to those free from defilements yields a hundred-thousandfold merit; giving to those progressing toward stream-entry yields immeasurable merit, how much more so for those who have attained the fruit of stream-entry; giving to those progressing toward once-returning yields immeasurable merit, how much more so for those who have attained the fruit of once-returning; giving to those progressing toward non-returning yields immeasurable merit, how much more so for those who have attained the fruit of non-returning; giving to those progressing toward arhatship yields immeasurable merit, how much more so for those who have attained arhatship; giving to pratyekabuddhas yields immeasurable merit, how much more so for a Tathāgata, an arhat, one of right and universal awakening. This is what is called the merit of the fourteen types of comparative giving.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! You should know that there are seven types of giving to the great assembly. What are they? First, giving to the assembly of bhikṣus when the Buddha is present; second, giving to the assembly of bhikṣus after the Buddha's passing; third, giving to the assembly of bhikṣuṇīs after the Buddha's passing; fourth, giving to the assemblies of both bhikṣus and bhikṣuṇīs after the Buddha's passing; fifth, giving to wandering bhikṣus who travel to teach after the Buddha's passing; sixth, giving to wandering bhikṣuṇīs who travel to teach after the Buddha's passing; seventh, giving to both wandering bhikṣus and bhikṣuṇīs who travel to teach after the Buddha's passing. These are the seven types of giving to the great assembly that one should practice.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! You should know there are four types of pure giving. What are the four? First, the giver is pure but there is no recipient; second, the gift is pure but there is no recipient; third, the recipient is pure but there is no giver; fourth, what is received is pure but there is no giver.

"Ānanda! What is meant by 'the giver is pure but there is no recipient'? This means that the giver does not grasp at characteristics: bodily action is pure, verbal action is pure, mental action is pure, livelihood is pure, and views are also pure. Being thus complete, there is no characteristic of giving; because there is no giving, there is no recipient. If the giver has any perceived characteristics, then bodily, verbal, and mental actions are not pure, livelihood is not pure, and views are not pure. If one is free from these characteristics, then both giver and recipient are pure.

"Furthermore, what is meant by 'the gift is pure but there is no recipient'? If the recipient's bodily action is not pure, verbal action is not pure, mental action is not pure, livelihood is not pure, and views are not pure, then there is a perceived characteristic of giving. If the recipient's three actions are pure, livelihood is pure, and views are pure, being thus complete there is no characteristic of giving. Because one is free from characteristics, the gift is pure.

"Furthermore, what is meant by 'the recipient is pure but there is no giver'? If the recipient's three actions of body, speech, and mind are not pure, livelihood is not pure, and views are not pure, then there is a perceived characteristic of receiving. If one is free from these characteristics, there is no giver.

"Furthermore, what is meant by 'what is received is pure but there is no giver'? This means that the giver's three actions are pure, livelihood is pure, and views are pure; thus there is nothing given. Because one is free from giving, there is nothing received; therefore what is received is pure. Ānanda! If one can understand in this way, one attains the four types of pure giving."

At that time, the bhikṣuṇī Mahāprajāpatī, having heard the Buddha proclaim these various teachings on giving, took the robe and gave it to the great assembly. The assembly of bhikṣus then accepted it.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "In ages to come, if there are faithful sons of good family who can give rise to pure faith and practice giving to the great assembly, you should know that these people will obtain immeasurable merit. How much more so for those who practice giving now in the present!"

Then the Venerable Ānanda and the bhikṣuṇī Mahāprajāpatī, having heard the Buddha's teaching, were filled with joy and delight, and faithfully received and practiced it.
