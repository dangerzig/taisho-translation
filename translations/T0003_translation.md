# 毘婆尸佛經
## 毘婆尸佛經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0003

Translated from the Chinese. 宋 法天譯

---

## Fascicle 1

At that time, the Buddha addressed the monks: "In a past age there was a great king named Bandhumat, who had a crown prince named Vipaśyin. Having long dwelt in the inner palace, [the prince] desired to go forth and travel, and told his charioteer Yoga: 'Prepare my chariot and horses according to custom; I wish to go abroad and view the gardens.' Yoga, having heard this, immediately went to the stables, prepared the chariot and horses, and brought them before the prince. [The prince] mounted and went out, and saw a sick person. The prince said: 'Why does this person look haggard and wan, with feeble and weakened energy?' Yoga replied: 'This is a sick person.' The prince said: 'What is called sickness?' Yoga replied: 'The four great elements are falsely compounded, illusory and insubstantial; when their balance is slightly upset, suffering and pain arise—this is called sickness.' The prince said: 'Can I be free from it?' Yoga replied: 'We are alike in having illusory bodies composed of the four elements; if their balance is lost, none can be free from it.' Having heard this, the prince's heart became joyless; he turned back the chariot and returned to the royal palace, where he sat upright in contemplation of the nature of sickness and suffering, true and not illusory, his mind finding no peace."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Vipaśyin the crown prince, traveling to view the gardens,
> Suddenly saw one who was ill, haggard and wan of complexion;
> He questioned the charioteer, learning the prince too could not escape—
> Sitting upright, he reflected: sickness and suffering are no delusion.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Then King Bandhumat asked Yoga: 'Why did the prince return unhappy?' Yoga replied: 'The prince went out to view the gardens and saw a sick person with a haggard, wasted appearance, his spirit ill at ease. Not recognizing him, the prince asked: "What sort of person is this?" Yoga replied: "This is a sick person." He then asked again: "Can I be free from it?" Yoga replied: "We are alike in having illusory bodies of the four elements; if their balance is lost, none can be free from it." The prince, having heard this, turned the chariot and returned to the palace, where he reflected on the nature of sickness. For this reason he is not joyful.' When King Bandhumat heard these events, he recalled the words of the astrologers spoken long ago: 'If he remains in the home life, he will succeed to the throne of the wheel-turning king consecrated by the rites; but if he goes forth and takes delight in cultivation, he will attain the fruit of Buddhahood.' Having thus reflected, the king arranged within the palace all manner of exquisite objects of the five pleasures to entertain the prince and make him cling to them, so as to sever his intention to go forth."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> King Bandhumat the father, knowing his son had returned from his excursion,
> His body and mind joyless, fearing that he sought to go forth—
> Immediately offered exquisite pleasures: forms, sounds, fragrances, tastes, and touches,
> To delight the prince's heart, that he might afterward inherit the royal throne.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Then the crown prince Vipaśyin told Yoga: 'Prepare my chariot and horses according to custom; I wish to go out and view the gardens.' Yoga, having heard this, immediately went to the stables, prepared the chariot and horses, and brought them before the prince. [The prince] mounted and went out of the city, and saw an old man with white hair and beard, his body and mind worn and feeble, walking ahead with a staff, groaning feebly. The prince said: 'What sort of person is this?' Yoga replied: 'This is an old man.' The prince said: 'What is called old age?' Yoga replied: 'The illusory body of the five aggregates undergoes transformation through the four marks: from infancy through youth, imperceptibly arriving at old age, with dimming eyes, deaf ears, a body and mind decayed and withered—this is called old age.' The prince said: 'Can I be free from it?' Yoga replied: 'Though noble and common differ, the illusory body is no different; as days pass and months go by, one must also grow old.' The prince, having heard this, returned joylessly, entered meditative stillness, and contemplated the nature of the suffering of old age—none can be free from it."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Vipaśyin the crown prince suddenly saw an old man:
> His hair and beard all white, leaning on a staff, his vitality spent.
> He entered stillness and carefully contemplated: all conditioned phenomena
> Do not abide even for a moment—there is none who can escape this.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "King Bandhumat, seeing the prince joyless, asked Yoga: 'Why is my son unhappy in heart?' Yoga replied: 'The prince went out and saw an old man. The prince said: "What sort of person is this?" Yoga replied: "This is an old man." The prince asked again: "What is called old age?" Yoga replied: "The illusory body of the five aggregates undergoes transformation through the four marks: from infancy through youth, imperceptibly arriving at old age, with dimming eyes, deaf ears, a body and mind decayed and withered—this is called old age." The prince said: "Can I be free from it?" Yoga replied: "Though noble and common differ, the illusory body is no different; as days pass and months go by, one must also grow old." The prince, having heard this, returned joylessly and entered meditative stillness; in truth there is no escaping it—for this reason he is not cheerful.' When the king heard these events, he recalled the words of the astrologers of old: 'If he remains in the home life, he will certainly become a wheel-turning king. If he goes forth, he will certainly attain the fruit of Buddhahood.' Having thus reflected, King Bandhumat once again used the delightful pleasures of the five desires to entertain and please the prince, making him cling to them so as to sever his intention to go forth."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> King Bandhumat the father, seeing his son's heart unhappy,
> Recalling the words of the astrologers of old, fearing he would seek to go forth—
> Immediately offered the wondrous five desires in their various kinds to gladden his heart,
> Like Śakra the lord of the devas receiving pleasure in the Park of Joy.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Then the crown prince Vipaśyin told Yoga: 'Prepare my chariot and horses according to custom; I wish to go out and view the gardens.' Yoga, having heard this, immediately went to the stables, prepared the chariot and horses, and brought them before the prince. [The prince] mounted and went out, and saw many people surrounding a bier, weeping aloud. The prince said: 'What sort of person is this?' Yoga replied: 'This is the appearance of death.' The prince said: 'What is called death?' Yoga replied: 'People born into this floating world have lives of varying lengths; at one moment the separation comes—the breath ceases and the spirit departs, parting forever from loved ones, lying in the wilderness, while kinsmen weep and wail—this is called death.' The prince said: 'Can I be free from it?' Yoga replied: 'The three realms are without peace; none has escaped birth and death—the prince's own body also cannot be exempted.' The prince, having heard this, his body and mind joyless, turned the chariot back to the palace, and entered meditative stillness to contemplate the nature of impermanence, which cannot be loved or delighted in: how now can I escape this suffering?"

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Vipaśyin the crown prince saw one whose life had ended,
> And asked the charioteer: none can escape this suffering.
> Sitting alone in contemplation, true and without delusion:
> My own body—how shall it escape the affliction of impermanence?

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "King Bandhumat asked Yoga: 'Why is the prince joyless?' Yoga replied: 'The prince went out of the city and saw a dead person. He asked: "What sort of person is this?" Yoga replied: "This is the appearance of death." The prince said: "What is called death?" Yoga replied: "People born into this floating world have lives of varying lengths; at one moment the separation comes—the breath ceases and the spirit departs, parting forever from loved ones, lying in the wilderness, while kinsmen weep and wail—this is called death." The prince said: "Can I be free from it?" Yoga replied: "The three realms are without peace; none has escaped birth and death—it cannot be avoided." The prince, having heard this, turned the chariot and returned to the palace, entered meditative stillness to contemplate [the teaching]: in truth there is no escaping it—for this reason he is not joyful.' When the king heard these events, he recalled the words of the astrologers of old: 'If he remains in the home life, he will certainly become a wheel-turning king; if he goes forth, he will certainly attain the fruit of Buddhahood.' Once again the king used the pleasures of the five desires to entertain and please the prince, making him delight in and cling to them, relinquishing his intention to go forth."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> King Bandhumat, [ruler of] Bandhumatī—his crown prince Vipaśyin
> Saw one whose life had ended, and lamented in sorrow and displeasure.
> The king offered exquisite pleasures: realms of form, sound, fragrance, and the like,
> Entertaining him and making him cling, that he might relinquish his intention to go forth.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Then the crown prince Vipaśyin told Yoga: 'Prepare my chariot and horses according to custom; I wish to go out and view the gardens.' Yoga, having heard this, immediately went to the stables, prepared the chariot and horses, and brought them before the prince. [The prince] mounted and went out, and saw a monk with shaved head and beard, wearing a kāṣāya robe. The prince said: 'What sort of person is this?' Yoga replied: 'This is one who has gone forth from home.' The prince said: 'What is called one who has gone forth?' Yoga replied: 'One who has awakened to old age, sickness, and death, has entered the gate of liberation, practices patience, loving-kindness, and compassion, seeks the peaceful bliss of nirvāṇa, has parted forever from those dear to him, and aspires to be a śramaṇa—this is called one who has gone forth.' The prince, having heard this, joyfully approached and said: 'Well done, well done! One who practices the equal and wholesome dharmas of loving-kindness, compassion, and patience, who can turn away from the dust and toil to seek blissful peace—I too aspire to this.' Having said this, he returned to the palace, immediately gave rise to faith, and carried out the practice of going forth, taking on the appearance of a śramaṇa."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> The prince went forth from the royal city to view the gardens and parks;
> He suddenly saw one who was ill and aged, and that one who had passed beyond—
> Moment by moment, without long abiding, ever receiving suffering of every kind.
> He also beheld one who had gone forth, with shaved head and beard,
> Wearing a kāṣāya robe of subdued color, his bearing dignified and refined,
> Ever practicing equal loving-kindness, with patience and all wholesome dharmas.
> For this reason he sought to go forth, renouncing the pleasures of the five desires;
> Father, mother, and kinsmen; the realm, the city, and all precious things—
> He took on the appearance of a śramaṇa, practicing patience and self-taming,
> Extinguishing the mind of greed and attachment, diligently seeking the bliss of liberation.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "The eighty thousand people of the city of Bandhumatī, seeing Vipaśyin renounce his royal inheritance and go forth with shaved head to become a śramaṇa, each reflected: 'The prince, of the highest lineage, has renounced the five desires to cultivate the brahma-conduct; we too should now go forth.' These eighty thousand people, having so reflected, thereupon went forth to become śramaṇas."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Those of great wisdom and supreme standing, numbering eighty thousand,
> Following Vipaśyin, went forth to cultivate the brahma-conduct.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Then the bodhisattva Vipaśyin, having gone forth, traveled to various places with those eighty thousand people and arrived at a village to spend the rains retreat. When the rains had passed, he reflected to himself: 'Why am I wandering about like a confused and intoxicated person?' Having formed this thought, his mind became pure and he returned to his original dwelling. In the night watch he again reflected: 'What use to me are worldly wealth and honor? Beings cling in attachment and revolve in birth and death; the accumulation of the aggregates of suffering continues without end.' He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of old age and death arises, that one comes to old age and death?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of birth. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of birth arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of existence. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of existence arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of grasping. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of grasping arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of craving. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of craving arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of feeling. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of feeling arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of contact. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of contact arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of the six sense bases. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of the six sense bases arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of name-and-form. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of name-and-form arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of consciousness. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of consciousness arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of formations. He reflected further: 'What is the cause from which the suffering of formations arises?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: it arises from the link of ignorance. Thus: ignorance conditions formations; formations condition consciousness; consciousness conditions name-and-form; name-and-form conditions the six sense bases; the six sense bases condition contact; contact conditions feeling; feeling conditions craving; craving conditions grasping; grasping conditions existence; existence conditions birth; birth conditions old age, death, sorrow, lamentation, and suffering—thus is assembled the one great mass of suffering.

"At that time the bodhisattva Vipaśyin further reflected: 'How can the suffering of old age and death be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when birth is extinguished, old age and death are extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of birth be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when existence is extinguished, birth is extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of existence be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when grasping is extinguished, existence is extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of grasping be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when craving is extinguished, grasping is extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of craving be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when feeling is extinguished, craving is extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of feeling be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when contact is extinguished, feeling is extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of contact be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when the six sense bases are extinguished, contact is extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of the six sense bases be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when name-and-form is extinguished, the six sense bases are extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of name-and-form be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when consciousness is extinguished, name-and-form is extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of consciousness be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when formations are extinguished, consciousness is extinguished. He reflected further: 'How can the suffering of formations be extinguished?' Entering into samādhi, he carefully contemplated this dharma: when ignorance is extinguished, formations are extinguished. Thus: when ignorance is extinguished, formations are extinguished; when formations are extinguished, consciousness is extinguished; when consciousness is extinguished, name-and-form is extinguished; when name-and-form is extinguished, the six sense bases are extinguished; when the six sense bases are extinguished, contact is extinguished; when contact is extinguished, feeling is extinguished; when feeling is extinguished, craving is extinguished; when craving is extinguished, grasping is extinguished; when grasping is extinguished, existence is extinguished; when existence is extinguished, birth is extinguished; when birth is extinguished, old age, death, sorrow, lamentation, and suffering are extinguished—thus the one great mass of suffering ceases to arise of itself."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> The bodhisattva Vipaśyin contemplated the suffering of old age and death;
> With wisdom he traced its cause: from what condition, from what dharma does it arise?
> Entering stillness, he carefully and truly contemplated: knowing it arises from the link of birth—
> And even to the cause of formations: knowing it arises from ignorance.
> He then contemplated from what it ceases: ignorance extinguished, formations extinguished;
> Even to old age and death exhausted—the mass of suffering altogether absent.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Then the bodhisattva Vipaśyin further contemplated form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness—arising and passing, not abiding, like illusion, like conjuration, without true reality. With wisdom's contemplation manifesting before him, all the afflictions of karmic habitual tendencies ceased to arise; he attained the great liberation and realized perfect, complete enlightenment."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> The bodhisattva Vipaśyin further contemplated the dharmas of the aggregates and such;
> Entering that samādhi, when wisdom's contemplation manifested before him,
> Delusion, suffering, karma, and habitual tendencies—all ceased to arise,
> Like floating tufts of tūla cotton-down that cannot abide even for a moment.
> He accomplished the bodhi of a Buddha, the auspicious fruit of nirvāṇa,
> Like a great full moon, its radiance pervading the ten directions.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "When Vipaśyin was formerly in the causal stage, he harbored two doubts: first he doubted his own body, regarding it as one confused and intoxicated; second he doubted that the afflictions of greed and such were increasing in succession. Having thus contemplated the dharma of dependent arising, he attained the great liberation."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> That Buddha Tathāgata's body—difficult to achieve, yet he achieved it;
> Contemplating the dharma of dependent arising, he further severed greed, aversion, and delusion;
> Ultimately reaching the further shore, accomplishing the great liberation,
> Like the sun upon the mountain peak, illuminating all.

## Fascicle 2

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "When the Buddha Vipaśyin had attained the way, he formed this thought: 'At what place should I first teach the Dharma to benefit sentient beings?' Carefully contemplating and reflecting, he saw that the great city where King Bandhumat held court was teeming with people, their spiritual capacities fully ripe. Having formed this thought, he rose from his seat, arranged his robes, took up his alms bowl, and went in order begging for alms; he went to the city of Bandhumatī, proceeding to the Pleasurable Deer Park, where he temporarily dwelt and rested, at ease and without fear."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> The one who among two-footed beings is perfectly, universally knowing—
> At ease he walked, holding his bowl,
> Dwelling in the Deer Park,
> Fearless as a lion.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "The Buddha Vipaśyin told the gatekeeper: 'Go on my behalf to the crown prince Khaṇḍa and the minister Tissuru; I am now dwelling in the Pleasurable Deer Park, and wish to meet with them.' The gatekeeper, having heard this, immediately went to the crown prince Khaṇḍa and the minister Tissuru and reported the matter in full: 'The Buddha Vipaśyin has attained the path of perfect enlightenment and has gone to the Pleasurable Deer Park of the city of Bandhumatī; he wishes to meet with you.' Prince Khaṇḍa, having heard this, together with Tissuru immediately mounted their chariots and horses, went out of the city of Bandhumatī, came to the Pleasurable Deer Park, and approached the Buddha Vipaśyin. They prostrated at his feet and gazed upon the Honored One's countenance without turning their eyes away for even a moment.

"At that time the Buddha Vipaśyin, wishing to gladden Khaṇḍa so that he might receive and accept the subtle Dharma, taught: 'As the Buddhas of the past have declared: if one practices generosity and upholds the precepts, practices diligently, one can be freed from the afflictions and faults of desire and form, and be born in the pure heavens.' Prince Khaṇḍa and Tissuru, having heard these words, attained purity of mind: like the mind of the perfectly enlightened Vipaśyin, they gave rise to a mind of right understanding, a mind without doubt, a wholesome mind, a pliant mind, a vast mind, an unobstructed mind, an unbounded mind, a pure mind. [The Buddha] further proclaimed the dharma-practice of the four truths—suffering, arising, cessation, and the path—opening up [the teaching] in various ways. Prince Khaṇḍa and Tissuru penetrated the four truths, seeing the Dharma, knowing the Dharma, obtaining the Dharma; firm in the Dharma, relying on the Dharma, abiding in the Dharma, unmoved by the Dharma, not abandoning the Dharma, not empty of the Dharma—like white cloth free from all dust and stain, so too was their mind of awakening.

"At that time, Prince Khaṇḍa and Tissuru addressed the Buddha Vipaśyin: 'Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One—we wish to go forth and receive the precepts of the Sugata.' The Buddha said: 'Excellent! Now is indeed the right time.' He thereupon conferred the tonsure and full ordination upon them. Thus that Buddha manifested three kinds of spiritual power before those two, causing them to give rise to diligent effort toward the wisdom of a Buddha: the first was the spiritual power of miraculous transformation, the second was the spiritual power of Dharma-teaching, and the third was the spiritual power of taming and training. Having manifested thus, Prince Khaṇḍa and Tissuru strove diligently and courageously; within a brief moment they came into accord with true wisdom, severed all outflows completely, and became arhants."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> The Blessed One Vipaśyin, teaching the Dharma in the Deer Park—
> Khaṇḍa and Tissuru both came to the presence of the Buddha,
> And with their heads bowed to his feet, gazed upon him with one-pointed mind.
> He opened up the gate of generosity and the precepts, the Dharma of suffering, arising, cessation, and the path;
> Having heard, they received it with deep faith, penetrating the unborn and unceasing.
> Both sought to go forth and immediately received the precepts of the Sugata.
> Beholding again the power of spiritual manifestation, they swiftly gave rise to diligent minds;
> In a brief moment all outflows were exhausted—they accomplished the state of arhants.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Listen carefully! Among the teeming people of the city of Bandhumatī, eighty thousand had planted wholesome roots in former lives. Hearing that Prince Khaṇḍa and Tissuru had gone forth in right faith, that the Buddha had taught the Dharma and manifested spiritual powers so that they realized the noble fruit, each of them reflected: 'There is such a going-forth, such a brahma-conduct, such a teaching of Dharma, such a taming and training—the world has rarely seen this, it has never before been heard. We too aspire to go forth.' Having formed this thought, the eighty thousand people all relinquished their household ties, went out of the city of Bandhumatī, came to the Buddha Vipaśyin in the Pleasurable Deer Park, prostrated at his feet, and sat to one side. With joined palms they gazed up without turning their eyes away for even a moment. The Buddha taught the Dharma, causing them to give rise to faith: 'As the sublime Dharma proclaimed by the Buddhas of the past: if one practices generosity and upholds the precepts, practices diligently, one can be freed from the various afflictions and faults of desire and form, and be born in the pure heavens.' The eighty thousand people, having heard these words, attained purity of mind: like the mind of the perfectly enlightened Vipaśyin, they gave rise to a mind of right understanding, a mind without doubt, a pliant mind, a wholesome mind, a vast mind, an unobstructed mind, an unbounded mind, a pure mind. [The Buddha] further proclaimed the dharma-practice of the four truths—suffering, arising, cessation, and the path—opening up [the teaching] in various ways. Those eighty thousand people penetrated the four truths, seeing the Dharma, knowing the Dharma, obtaining the Dharma; firm in the Dharma, relying on the Dharma, indestructible in the Dharma, abiding in the Dharma, unmoved by the Dharma, not abandoning the Dharma, not empty of the Dharma—like white cloth free from all dust and stain, so too were their minds. At that time the eighty thousand people all raised their voices together: 'Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Enlightened One—receive us, that we may go forth and receive the precepts of the Sugata.' The Buddha thereupon received them, conferred the tonsure, and gave the precepts; he further manifested the three spiritual powers before that assembly, causing them to give rise to diligence: the first, the spiritual power of transformation; the second, the spiritual power of Dharma-teaching; the third, the spiritual power of taming and training. Having manifested thus, the eighty thousand people strove diligently and courageously. Before long, their outflows exhausted and their minds released, they became arhants."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Within the city of Bandhumatī, a multitude of eighty thousand—
> Hearing of Khaṇḍa and the others going forth and realizing the noble path,
> All gave rise to pure minds and came to the presence of the Buddha.
> Having heard the Dharma with rejoicing hearts, they swiftly gave rise to courageous minds;
> With joined palms they addressed the Blessed One: "Please allow us to go forth
> And uphold the precepts and rules of discipline." Thereupon they were received:
> Their heads were shaved and the precepts were conferred; the spiritual powers were further manifested—
> All bonds and fetters were severed, like fire consuming the Śirīṣa forest
> Whose blazing flames are forever extinguished. They accomplished the great liberation;
> Thus this mass of suffering, their refuge, shall henceforth be extinguished and no more.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "When the Buddha Vipaśyin had guided that assembly, he departed from the Pleasurable Deer Park and went to the city of Bandhumatī; the eighty thousand monks also went to the city of Bandhumatī. They came before the Blessed One, prostrated at his feet, and sat to one side. The Buddha then fully explained the causes and conditions of attaining the way, so as to strengthen their resolve."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Difficult to do—supremely difficult to do; revolving in saṃsāra—exhausting the revolving in saṃsāra.
> These eighty thousand people forever severed all bonds and fetters,
> As did Tissuru and the sage-prince Khaṇḍa—
> All diligently sought to go forth, and together attained the fruit of liberation.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Then the Buddha Vipaśyin formed this thought: 'This great assembly of monks dwells in the city of Bandhumatī; it would be fitting to reduce their number. Let sixty-two thousand monks go to various directions, wandering among villages to practice cultivation as they please. After six years they shall return to the city of Bandhumatī to receive and uphold the Prātimokṣa.' As he formed this thought, a deva in the sky, knowing the Buddha's intention, addressed the Buddha Vipaśyin: 'Excellent! If the sixty-two thousand monks are to wander among the villages to practice cultivation as they please, and after six years return to their home country to receive and uphold the Prātimokṣa, now is indeed the right time.' The Buddha thereupon proclaimed: 'Among you in the assembly of monks, let sixty-two thousand persons wander among the villages to practice cultivation as they please; after six years return to your home country to receive and uphold the Prātimokṣa.' The sixty-two thousand persons, having heard this, went out of the city of Bandhumatī to wander and dwell in various directions."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Without outflows, equal to the unequaled—the tamer, the great man,
> Who guides the multitudes of beings, leading them to the path of tranquility.
> Now he dispatches the assembly of monks—the supreme voice-hearers of the Buddha—
> Sixty-two thousand in number, going out from the city of Bandhumatī,
> Wandering among the villages, possessed of great dragon-like majesty,
> Practicing cultivation as they please; after six years, returning to their home country.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Those sixty-two thousand people thereupon went out of the city and wandered to various villages to practice cultivation as they pleased. Thus one year passed, then two years; by the sixth year, those monks said to one another: 'Six years are now complete; we should return to our home country.' As they spoke these words, the deva-being in the sky again addressed them: 'Now is indeed the right time; return to the city of Bandhumatī to receive and uphold the Prātimokṣa.' Thereupon the sixty-two thousand monks, by their own spiritual powers and the majesty of the devas, arrived at the city of Bandhumatī in a brief moment."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Those great monks of that Buddha—sixty-two thousand in assembly,
> Wandering among the villages as they pleased, the six years now complete.
> Reflecting that they wished to return, the deva-beings all gave notice,
> Bidding them return to the city of Bandhumatī to receive the pure and cooling precepts.
> Hearing this, they rejoiced greatly, the hairs of their bodies standing upright in joy;
> Immediately employing their spiritual powers—like riding great dragon-elephants—
> In a brief moment they returned to their home city, at ease without any hindrance.
> The unsurpassed one honored among two-footed beings, arisen in this world,
> Who has excellently proclaimed the assemblies of precepts and rules, liberating all sentient beings—
> Now he shall expound and set forth the Prātimokṣa.

At that time, having spoken this verse, the Blessed One addressed the monks: "Those sixty-two thousand people arrived at the city of Bandhumatī, came to the presence of the Buddha Vipaśyin, prostrated at his feet, and sat to one side. The Buddha said: 'Listen carefully! I shall now recite the Prātimokṣa:

> Patience is supreme;
> One who can be patient attains nirvāṇa.
> As the Buddhas of the past have declared:
> Those who go forth to become śramaṇas
> Must keep far from harm and killing,
> And the seven transgressions of body and speech.
> Upholding these precepts completely,
> They give rise to great wisdom,
> Attaining the pure body of a Buddha—
> Without equal in this world—
> Bringing forth the wisdom free of outflows,
> Bringing birth and death and all suffering to an end.

"At that time, as the Blessed One proclaimed these precepts, various deva-beings, by their divine power, descended from their heavenly palaces and came before the Buddha Vipaśyin; they made obeisance with joined palms and listened to receive the Prātimokṣa."

At that time, the Blessed One spoke a verse:

> Without outflows, inconceivable, breaking through darkness to reach the further shore—
> Śakra, Brahmā, and all the devas together listened to the precepts of the great Ṛṣi.


## Fascicle 2

At that time, the World-Honored One, having spoken these verses, addressed the bhikṣus: "On one occasion I was dwelling in a quiet abode near the Seven-Leaved Rock at Rājagṛha, when I suddenly reflected: 'When the past Buddha Vipaśyin taught the Vinaya Piṭaka, it may be that some devas did not come to hear the great Seer's precepts. Let me now go to the heavens and inquire of the Brahma-assembly.' Having formed this thought, I at that time entered samādhi, and like a mighty man extending his arm, arrived in an instant at Sudṛśā Heaven. The devas there bowed their heads to my feet and said: 'Welcome, World-Honored One! It has been long since you came here. We are śrāvaka disciples of the samyak-saṃbuddha Vipaśyin. That Buddha was of Kṣatriya caste, of the Kauṇḍinya clan; he left the household life in faith. His lifespan was eighty thousand years. His father was King Bandhumā; his mother was Queen Bandhumatī. Prince Khaṇḍa and Tisya left the household life, received the precepts, and attained arhatship. The greatly worthy foremost attendant was named Aśoka. At three assemblies he taught the Dharma and broadly liberated śrāvakas: at the first great assembly, sixty-two thousand persons attained arhatship; at the second great assembly, one hundred thousand persons attained arhatship; at the third great assembly, eighty thousand persons attained arhatship. Such was Vipaśyin Buddha—such was his supreme distinction, such was his going forth, such was his realization of bodhi, such was his teaching of the Dharma, such was his discipline. He caused his disciples, wearing robes and bearing bowls, to practice the holy life, to be far removed from the five desires, to sever the afflictions and attain liberation, to realize the Dharma of non-arising, and to become anāgāmins. Likewise with the Buddha Śikhin, the Buddha Viśvabhū, the Buddha Krakucchanda, the Buddha Kanakamuni, and the Buddha Kāśyapa—through teaching the Dharma and discipline, wearing robes and bearing bowls, practicing the holy life, being far removed from the five desires, severing the afflictions and realizing the Dharma of non-arising, becoming anāgāmins—it was exactly the same.'

"At that time, the World-Honored One, accompanied by countless hundreds of thousands of devas reverently surrounding him, proceeded to Sudarśana Heaven. The devas there, upon seeing the Buddha, bowed their heads to his feet and, together with countless hundreds of thousands of devas reverently surrounding him, proceeded to Akaniṣṭha Heaven. At that time the heavenly king, seeing the World-Honored One from afar, prostrated with all five limbs to the ground, bowing to the World-Honored One's feet, and said: 'Welcome, World-Honored One! It has been long since you came here. We are śrāvaka disciples of the samyak-saṃbuddha Vipaśyin. That Buddha was of Kṣatriya caste, of the Kauṇḍinya clan; his lifespan was eighty thousand years. His father was King Bandhumā; his mother was Queen Bandhumatī; the city was likewise named Bandhumā. Prince Khaṇḍa and Tisya left the household life, received the precepts, and attained arhatship. The greatly worthy foremost attendant was named Aśoka. At three assemblies he taught the Dharma and broadly liberated śrāvakas: at the first great assembly, sixty-two thousand persons attained arhatship; at the second great assembly, one hundred thousand persons attained arhatship; at the third great assembly, eighty thousand persons attained arhatship. Such was Vipaśyin Buddha—such was his supreme distinction, such was his going forth, such was his holy life, such was his realization of bodhi, such was his teaching of the Dharma, such was his discipline. He caused his disciples, wearing robes and bearing bowls, to practice the holy life, to be far removed from the five desires, to sever the afflictions and realize the Dharma of non-arising, and to become anāgāmins. Likewise with the Buddha Śikhin, the Buddha Viśvabhū, the Buddha Krakucchanda, the Buddha Kanakamuni, and the Buddha Kāśyapa—wearing robes and bearing bowls, teaching the Dharma and discipline, practicing the holy life, being far removed from the five desires, realizing the Dharma of non-arising, becoming anāgāmins—it was exactly the same. Now the Great Sage also teaches the Dharma and the holy life, disciplines beings, and it is exactly the same.'

"At that time, the devas spoke these verses:

> The Unsurpassed Honored One of Two Feet, entering samādhi,
> Swiftly wielding great supranormal power, departing the realm of Jambudvīpa,
> Came to Sudṛśā Heaven—as a mighty man
> Swiftly extends his arm, arriving here in an instant.
> The World-Honored One is most rare: without āsravas, without obstruction,
> A pure liberated body, like a lotus untouched by water.
> Throughout a hundred thousand worlds, none is equal to the Buddha;
> He subdues the great king Māra as a river sweeps away fine grass.
> The devas of Sudṛśā and all the rest come together to bow their heads,
> Taking refuge in the Highest Honored One, the Completely Enlightened One of great compassion,
> Who disciplines all beings, with the six faculties entirely pure,
> Bringing forth unsurpassed wisdom, striving diligently in accordance with the Dharma.
> The past Buddha Vipaśyin, the Honored One of complete and perfect enlightenment—
> The subtle and wondrous Dharma he proclaimed, at three assemblies liberated śrāvakas;
> The rules of discipline and the holy life, guarded without deficiency or transgression,
> Pure and complete, like the moon on the fifteenth night.
> The World-Honored Buddha Śikhin, the Tathāgata Viśvabhū,
> Krakucchanda of the Bhadra-kalpa, Kanakamuni,
> And the Buddha Kāśyapa—all these Tathāgatas,
> The multitudes of śrāvakas they liberated have all attained the exhaustion of the āsravas,
> With no further afflictions. Ever cultivating the seven factors of awakening,
> And practicing the eightfold noble path, able to depart from the faults of the five desires,
> Penetrating to great wisdom, known to all assemblies.
> As Vaiśravaṇa ever partakes of the taste of ambrosia,
> As a king receives consecration, forever shattering all darkness,
> As the sun radiates its light—each and every Buddha World-Honored One,
> Through dignified conduct and Dharma-practice, benefits all beings;
> Opening various skillful means to guide them, there is no difference whatsoever.

At that time, the World-Honored One, having spoken these verses, addressed the bhikṣus: "When I went to those heavens and heard these things, I understood that devas and humans all come with joyful assent to the Dharma-assemblies of all Buddhas. If there is any person who delights in receiving and upholding this, who in walking, standing, sitting, and lying down reflects upon and recites it, they will have no confusion, will permanently sever the cycle of rebirth, and will attain liberation and bliss."

When the Buddha had finished expounding this sūtra, all were greatly joyful, received it with faith, and reverently put it into practice.
