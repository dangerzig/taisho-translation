# 無畏三藏禪要

## The Meditation Essentials of Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0917

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, spoken by Śubhakarasiṃha (善無畏).

---

The Tripiṭaka śramaṇa whose name was Śubhakarasiṃha (meaning "Fearless" in Chinese), of the kṣatriya caste and noble lineage, from the Bamboo Grove Monastery at Nālandā in the city of Rājagṛha, Magadha, Central India, together with the Venerable Dhyāna Master Jìngxián, a monk of great virtue at the Huìshàn Monastery on Mount Sōng, engaged in dialogue on the Buddha-dharma. He briefly set forth the essential points of the Mahāyāna, for suddenly opening the mind-ground of sentient beings so they might swiftly awaken to the Way. He also transmitted the bodhisattva-precept ordination ritual. This is recorded as follows.

---

One who wishes to enter the Mahāyāna Dharma must first arouse the mind of unsurpassed bodhi, receive the great bodhisattva precepts, and with body-vessel purified, then receive the Dharma. This is briefly divided into eleven sections:

1. The Section on Arousing the Mind
2. The Section on Offerings
3. The Section on Confession
4. The Section on Taking Refuge
5. The Section on Arousing the Bodhicitta
6. The Section on Questioning Regarding Obstructions
7. The Section on Requesting the Teacher
8. The Section on the Formal Act (*karman*)
9. The Section on Establishing the Boundary
10. The Section on Cultivating the Four Means of Attraction
11. The Section on the Ten Grave Precepts

## 1. The Section on Arousing the Mind

The disciple [name] and others take refuge in the great bodhicitta of all Buddhas and all great bodhisattvas of the ten directions, who serve as our great guides, able to deliver us from the evil destinies and able to show the path to great nirvāṇa for humans and devas. Therefore I now pay sincere homage with the crown of my head.

## 2. The Section on Offerings

The disciple [name] and others, taking all the most supreme and wondrous incense, flowers, pennants, canopies, and manifold excellent things throughout the worlds of the ten directions, make offerings to all Buddhas, all bodhisattvas, and the great bodhicitta. I now arouse the mind to make sincere offerings until the end of future time. I pay sincere homage with the crown of my head.

## 3. The Section on Confession

The disciple [name], from the beginningless past until today, through greed, hatred, delusion, and all afflictions, as well as anger, resentment, and all subsidiary afflictions, has been troubled in body and mind and has widely committed all manner of transgressions. Through unwholesome bodily actions: killing, stealing, sexual misconduct. Through unwholesome verbal actions: false speech, frivolous talk, harsh speech, divisive speech. Through unwholesome mental actions: greed, hatred, wrong views. All afflictions from beginningless time have continuously defiled body and mind, causing body, speech, and mind to create immeasurable sins: perhaps killing one's father or mother, killing an arhat, shedding the blood of a Buddha, destroying the harmony of the sangha, slandering the Three Treasures, beating and binding sentient beings, breaking fasts and violating precepts, drinking liquor and eating meat. Such sins as these are immeasurable and boundless, beyond memory or reckoning. Today, with sincere heart, I openly confess and repent. Once confessed, I shall forever cut off their continuity and dare not commit them again. I humbly ask that all Buddhas and all great bodhisattvas of the ten directions empower and protect me with their mindfulness, enabling my karmic obstacles to be extinguished. I pay sincere homage with the crown of my head.

## 4. The Section on Taking Refuge

The disciple [name], beginning from this present body until I sit at the bodhi-site, takes refuge in the Tathāgata's unsurpassed three bodies, takes refuge in the Vaipulya Mahāyāna Dharma-treasury, and takes refuge in all non-retrogressing bodhisattva-sangha. I humbly ask that all Buddhas and all great bodhisattvas of the ten directions acknowledge and witness us. I pay sincere homage with the crown of my head.

## 5. The Section on Arousing the Bodhicitta

The disciple [name], beginning from this present body until I sit at the bodhi-site, vows to arouse the mind of unsurpassed great bodhi:

> Sentient beings are boundless; I vow to save them all.
> Merit and wisdom are boundless; I vow to gather them all.
> Dharma-gates are boundless; I vow to study them all.
> Tathāgatas are boundless; I vow to serve them all.
> The unsurpassed Buddha-way: I vow to accomplish it.

The mind now aroused should further abandon the two kinds of clinging to self and to dharmas, clearly manifesting the original awakening of true suchness. With the equal and correct wisdom appearing before one, may I attain skillful wisdom and fully perfect the mind of Samantabhadra. I humbly ask that all Buddhas and all great bodhisattvas of the ten directions acknowledge and witness us. I pay sincere homage and confess.

## 6. The Section on Questioning Regarding Obstructions

First, the question: if anyone has committed the seven heinous transgressions, the teacher should not confer the precepts but should instruct them in confession. They must confess for seven days, twice seven days, up to seven times seven days, or even up to one year, with earnest confession. Auspicious signs must appear. If no auspicious signs appear, even if they receive the precepts, they do not obtain them.

"Sons of the Buddha: from the time of your birth until now, you have not killed your father? You have not killed your mother? You have not shed the blood of a Buddha? You have not killed an arhat? You have not killed your upādhyāya? You have not killed your ācārya? You have not destroyed the harmony of the sangha? If any of you have committed these seven heinous transgressions, you must openly confess before the assembly. You must not conceal them, for you would certainly fall into the Avīci hell and suffer immeasurable pain. If, in accordance with the Buddha's teaching, you openly confess and repent, your grave sins will certainly be extinguished and you will attain a pure body, enter the Buddha's wisdom, and swiftly realize unsurpassed perfect enlightenment. If you have not committed them, simply answer 'no.'"

"Sons of the Buddha: from this day until you sit at the bodhi-site, can you diligently receive and uphold the most supreme and excellent great disciplinary precepts of all Buddhas and all great bodhisattvas? These are called the three collections of pure precepts: the precepts of upholding discipline, the precepts of gathering wholesome dharmas, and the precepts of benefiting sentient beings. From this present body until you become Buddhas, during all that time, you vow never to violate them. Can you uphold them? During all that time, you will not abandon the three collections of pure precepts or the four great vows. Can you uphold them? Having aroused the bodhicitta and received the bodhisattva precepts, I humbly ask that all Buddhas and great bodhisattvas of the ten directions acknowledge, witness, empower, and protect us, causing us never to retrogress. I pay sincere homage with the crown of my head."

## 7. The Section on Requesting the Teacher

The disciple [name] and others respectfully request all Buddhas and all bodhisattvas of the ten directions: Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Maitreya Bodhisattva, Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva, Samantabhadra Bodhisattva, Vajrapāṇi Bodhisattva, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Vajragarbha Bodhisattva, Sarvanīvaraṇaviṣkambhin Bodhisattva, and all the remaining great bodhisattvas: remembering your original vows, please descend to this bodhi-site to acknowledge and witness us. I pay sincere homage with the crown of my head.

The disciple respectfully requests Śākyamuni Buddha to serve as upādhyāya, Mañjuśrī as karma-ācārya, all Buddhas of the ten directions as precept-witnessing masters, and all bodhisattva-mahāsattvas as fellow students and dharma-companions. I humbly ask that all Buddhas and all great bodhisattvas, out of compassion, kindly accept my request. I pay sincere homage with the crown of my head.

## 8. The Section on the Formal Act

"Sons of the Buddha, listen attentively. I now perform the formal act to confer the precepts upon you. This is the very moment of obtaining the precepts. Listen with sincere mind to the text of the formal act:

"All Buddhas of the ten directions and three times and all great bodhisattvas, with compassion be mindful of us. These sons of the Buddha, beginning from today until they sit at the bodhi-site, receive and study the pure precepts of all Buddhas and bodhisattvas of the past, present, and future, namely: the precepts of upholding discipline, the precepts of gathering wholesome dharmas, and the precepts of benefiting sentient beings. These three pure precepts they fully receive and uphold." I pay sincere homage with the crown of my head.

## 9. The Section on Establishing the Boundary

"Sons of the Buddha: beginning from today until you realize unsurpassed bodhi, you should fully receive and uphold the pure precepts of the Buddhas and bodhisattvas. Having now received the pure precepts, this matter is thus. Uphold them accordingly." I pay sincere homage with the crown of my head.

## 10. The Section on Cultivating the Four Means of Attraction

"Sons of the Buddha: having aroused the bodhicitta and received the bodhisattva precepts as described above, you should now cultivate the four means of attraction and the ten grave precepts, and must not violate them. The four means of attraction are: generosity, kind speech, beneficial action, and cooperation. To tame the beginningless habit of avarice and to benefit sentient beings, one should practice generosity. To tame the afflictions of anger, pride, and arrogance, and to benefit sentient beings, one should practice kind speech. To benefit sentient beings and to fulfill one's original vows, one should cultivate beneficial action. To draw near to great good teachers and to prevent wholesome thoughts from being interrupted, one should practice cooperation."

## 11. The Section on the Ten Grave Precepts

"Sons of the Buddha who uphold the bodhisattva precepts: I shall now proclaim the so-called ten grave precepts. Listen with sincere mind.

"First: one must not retreat from the bodhicitta, because it would obstruct the attainment of Buddhahood.

"Second: one must not abandon the Three Treasures and take refuge in non-Buddhist paths, because they are false dharmas.

"Third: one must not slander the Three Treasures or the scriptures of the Three Vehicles, because it goes against the Buddha-nature.

"Fourth: regarding profound Mahāyāna sūtras, where one does not understand, one must not give rise to doubt or perplexity, because they are beyond the sphere of ordinary beings.

"Fifth: if there are sentient beings who have already aroused the bodhicitta, one must not teach dharmas that cause them to retreat from the bodhicitta and incline toward the Two Vehicles, because it cuts off the seed of the Three Treasures.

"Sixth: for those who have not yet aroused the bodhicitta, one must not teach dharmas that cause them to arouse the mind of the Two Vehicles, because it contradicts the original vow.

"Seventh: before Hīnayāna practitioners or those of wrong views, one must not carelessly expound the profound and wondrous Mahāyāna, for fear they may give rise to slander and incur great calamity.

"Eighth: one must not give rise to wrong views and other such dharmas, because they cut off roots of merit.

"Ninth: before non-Buddhists, one must not declare that one possesses the wondrous precepts of unsurpassed bodhi, lest they, with angry and resentful minds, seek such things and, unable to obtain them, retreat from the bodhicitta, causing harm to both parties.

"Tenth: toward all sentient beings, any action that causes harm or is without benefit should not be performed, nor should one instruct others to do so, or rejoice upon seeing it done, because it contradicts the dharma of benefiting others and the compassionate mind."

The above completes the conferral of the bodhisattva precepts. You should maintain them in purity; do not violate them.

The reception of the three collections of pure precepts is now complete.

---

Next, one should receive the Dharma-gate of contemplative wisdom, the secret essential of meditation, the wondrous purport of the Mahāyāna. If one wishes to receive this Dharma, it is profoundly abstruse, and those who have faith are exceedingly rare. It cannot be transmitted before an assembly; the teacher must gauge the capacity and transmit it secretly. One must first employ various skillful means, reconciling them with the holy teachings, to produce firm faith and decisively remove the net of doubt. Only then can it be revealed.

Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha said: "The capacities of sentient beings are not the same, and the Great Sage's teachings are likewise not uniform. One must not cling partially to a single dharma and dispute right and wrong with others. Such a person cannot even attain the reward of humans and devas, let alone the unsurpassed Way. For some, practicing generosity alone leads to Buddhahood. For some, cultivating morality alone also leads to Buddhahood. Patience, vigor, meditation, wisdom, and so on, through to the eighty-four thousand gates of the Dharma, as numerous as particles of dust and grains of sand: entering through each and every gate, one can attain Buddhahood. Now, I shall simply rely on the *Vajraśekhara Sūtra* and establish one skillful means, practicing this all the way to Buddhahood."

When they heard this teaching, all purified their minds and abided in serene stillness. Thereupon, the Tripiṭaka Master, seated among the assembly without rising from his seat, became still and unmoving, as though entering meditative absorption. After a considerable time, he emerged from samādhi and surveyed the fourfold assembly on all sides. The fourfold assembly, with palms joined, bowed their heads, expressing deep reverence again and again.

After a long while, the Tripiṭaka Master spoke: "Although you have already received the bodhisattva pure precepts, you must now receive again the inner-realization, outflow-free, pure dharma-precepts of all Buddhas. Only then may you enter the gate of meditation. Having entered the gate of meditation, you must recite this dhāraṇī. The dhāraṇī is the ultimate extreme, identical with all Buddhas. Riding the Dharma, one awakens and enters the ocean of all-knowledge. This is called the true dharma-precept. This Dharma is secret and must not be casually heard. If one wishes to hear it, first receive a dhāraṇī:

*Oṃ samaya-stvaṃ*

"Recite this dhāraṇī three times, and then one is qualified to hear the precepts and the remaining secret dharmas. It can also fully accomplish all the pure disciplines of the bodhisattva. Its great merits are beyond complete description.

"Further, for arousing the mind, I transmit another dhāraṇī:

*Oṃ bodhicittam utpādayāmi*

"Recite this dhāraṇī three times, and from the moment the bodhicitta is aroused until the attainment of Buddhahood, it will be firm and irreversible.

"Further, for realization and entry, receive another dhāraṇī:

*Oṃ citta-prativedhaṃ karomī*

"Recite this dhāraṇī three times, and one immediately obtains the entire profound precept-treasury, and is endowed with all-knowledge wisdom, swiftly realizing unsurpassed bodhi. All Buddhas proclaim this with one voice.

"Further, for entering the rank of bodhisattva practice, I transmit another dhāraṇī:

*Oṃ vajra-maṇḍalaṃ praveśa me*

"If this dhāraṇī is recited three times, one immediately realizes the position of the abhiṣeka-maṇḍala of all, and hearing the secret teachings is without obstruction. Having entered the bodhisattva's abhiṣeka-position, one is qualified to receive the gate of meditation. The above completes the transmission of the outflow-free true dharma-precepts.

"Further, first, for protecting the practitioner, I transmit a dhāraṇī:

*Oṃ śuddha śuddha*

"First recite this 100,000 times to remove all obstacles. The three karmas become pure, sins and defilements are extinguished, māras and demons do not trouble one. Like a pure white silk that easily takes dye, so too the practitioner, once karmic obstacles are extinguished, swiftly realizes samādhi.

"Further, for the practitioner, I transmit another dhāraṇī:

*Oṃ sarvavidhi svāhā*

"The method of recitation is: of the two preceding dhāraṇīs and the two following, recite whichever one you wish. Do not recite them simultaneously, lest the mind become unfocused.

"When one wishes to enter samādhi, at the beginning stage of practice, completely cut off all external conditions, eliminate all business and affairs, and in a solitary quiet place, sit in half-lotus posture. One must first form a mudrā for protection: join the thumbs and little fingers, standing upright. Cross the ring and middle fingers, right pressing left, placed upon the backs of the hands. The index fingers are joined upright, tips touching and slightly curved, with a slight opening in the center. The thumbs are joined upright: this completes the mudrā. Having formed this mudrā, first stamp it upon the crown of the head. Next, stamp the forehead. Then stamp the right shoulder. Next, the left shoulder. Then stamp the heart. Next, stamp the right knee. Then the left knee. At each of the seven places, recite the previous dhāraṇī seven times until all seven are complete. Then release the mudrā above the crown. Then take the counting beads and recite this dhāraṇī. If one can recite many times, 200 or 300, up to 3,000 or 5,000, that too is acceptable. At each sitting, completing one full count of 100,000 produces the most remarkable accomplishment.

"After empowering the body, sit upright and properly as before in half-lotus, right pressing left. There is no need for full-lotus; full-lotus often causes pain, and if the mind fixates on the pain, it becomes difficult to attain concentration. If one has previously been able to sit in full-lotus, that is best of all. Then one may straighten the head, gaze level and forward. The eyes should not be too open, nor fully closed: wide open scatters the mind; fully closed brings torpor. Do not attend to external objects. Once seated comfortably, then one may direct the mind to make offerings and confess.

"First, set the mind to contemplate all Buddhas of the ten directions, teaching the Dharma to the fourfold assembly amid gatherings of humans and devas. Then contemplate one's own body, before each and every Buddha, with the three karmas in devout reverence, bowing and praising. When the practitioner performs this contemplation, make it vividly clear, as though present before the eyes, with the utmost clarity.

"Then direct the mind throughout the ten-direction worlds to all the most excellent incense, flowers, pennants, canopies, food and drink, jewels, and manifold offering implements in all heavens and among humans, filling empty space and pervading the dharma-realm, making offerings to all Buddhas, all great bodhisattvas, the Dharma Body, Reward Body, and Transformation Body, the teaching, principle, practice, and fruit, and the great assembly.

"After making these offerings, then direct the mind before each and every Buddha and bodhisattva, with earnest and utmost sincerity, openly confess and repent: 'From beginningless time until today, afflictions have covered my mind as I have long drifted through birth-and-death. The karmas of body, speech, and mind are difficult to fully recount. I now know only to confess broadly. Once confessed, I shall forever sever their continuity and never again commit them. I humbly ask that all Buddhas and bodhisattvas, with the power of great compassion, empower and protect me with their mindfulness, accept my confession, and cause my karmic obstacles to be swiftly extinguished.'

"Next, one should make the great vow: 'I have long been in the flow of existence. Perhaps in past lives I practiced the bodhisattva path, benefiting and bringing joy to boundless sentient beings, or cultivated meditative concentration, diligently practicing vigor, protecting the three karmas, with merit as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, leading to the fruit of Buddhahood. I humbly ask all Buddhas and bodhisattvas, with the power of compassionate vows, to empower and protect me with their mindfulness, enabling me, by riding upon this merit, to swiftly accord with all gates of samādhi, swiftly accord with all gates of dhāraṇī, and swiftly attain the complete purity of self-nature.' Making such expansive vows, ensure they never retrogress, and swiftly attain accomplishment.

"Next one should learn breath-regulation. To regulate the breath: first visualize the in-breath and out-breath flowing through every joint, sinew, and channel of one's body, then slowly exhaling from the mouth. Further visualize this breath as white as snow, moist as milk. One must also know how far it reaches. Then slowly inhale through the nose, filling the entire body again, reaching every sinew and channel throughout. Do this three times each for inhalation and exhalation. This breath-regulation ensures the body is free from ailments of cold, heat, or wind, with everything harmonious and comfortable. Then one may learn concentration."

Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha said: "You beginners often fear arising thoughts and mental activity, and wish to stop striving and cling to no-thought as the ultimate. But then, seeking growth, it cannot be obtained. Thoughts are of two kinds: first, unwholesome thoughts; second, wholesome thoughts. Unwholesome deluded thoughts should invariably be eliminated. Wholesome correct thoughts should not be extinguished. The truly correct practitioner must first increase correct mindfulness in practice, and only afterward arrive at the ultimate purity. It is like a person learning archery: after long practice and full mastery, without any deliberate thought, whether walking or standing, one is always in union with concentration. Do not fear arising thoughts; the real danger is neglecting to progress in one's practice.

"Next, one should cultivate samādhi. What is called samādhi is nothing other than this: it is simply the self-nature pure mind of all sentient beings, called the Great Perfect Mirror Wisdom. From the Buddhas above to the smallest wriggling creatures below, all are equal in this, without increase or decrease. It is only because of the covering of ignorant delusion and adventitious defilements that beings revolve in birth-and-death and fail to become Buddhas.

"The practitioner should settle the mind in stillness, without attending to any conditions whatsoever. Provisionally visualize a single luminous disk, like a pure moon, four feet from the body, directly before one, neither too high nor too low, measuring one cubit in diameter, perfectly round and complete, its color bright and lustrous, internally and externally radiant, beyond any worldly comparison. At first one may not see it, but after long and diligent investigation, one will come to see it clearly. Then further contemplate it, gradually expanding it, to four feet, then doubling, increasing until it fills the great trichiliocosm with the utmost clarity. When about to emerge from contemplation, gradually reduce it, returning to its original form.

"At the beginning of contemplation, it resembles the moon. After it pervades everywhere, it has no more directionality or shape. Having performed this contemplation, one immediately realizes the Samādhi of Liberation from All Coverings and Obstacles. One who attains this samādhi is called one of the Three Worthies before the Grounds. Relying on this, gradually progressing until it pervades the dharma-realm, this is, as the sūtras describe, the First Ground. The reason it is called the First Ground is that, having realized this dharma never before attained, one now for the first time attains it and gives rise to great joy and delight. Therefore the First Ground is called Joy.

"Do not form conceptual understandings about this. This self-nature pure mind, by three meanings, is likened to the moon: first, the meaning of self-nature purity, being free from the defilement of craving; second, the meaning of coolness, being free from the feverish torment of hatred; third, the meaning of radiance, being free from the darkness of delusion. Furthermore, the moon is constituted of the four great elements and will ultimately perish. Therefore the moon is something all people in the world can see; it is taken as a metaphor to enable one to awaken and enter.

"After the practitioner has long practiced this contemplation, when the contemplation becomes accomplished, one need not extend or contract it. One only sees bright luminosity, with nothing else whatsoever; one does not even see body or mind. The ten thousand dharmas are ungraspable, like empty space. But do not form an understanding of emptiness. Because it is equal to no-thought, it is said to be like empty space; this does not mean an imagination of emptiness. After long familiarity, in all activities, whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying, at all times and places, with or without deliberate attention, one spontaneously accords without any obstruction. All deluded thoughts, greed, hatred, delusion, and all afflictions, without needing to be cut off, naturally do not arise; the nature is always pure. Relying on this practice, all the way to Buddhahood, there is only this single path and no other principle. This is the path of inner realization of all Buddhas and bodhisattvas, not a realm accessible to the Two Vehicles or non-Buddhist paths.

"Having performed this contemplation, all Buddha-dharmas, merit as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, are understood without relying on others: with this single thread, one naturally penetrates all. One can open a single syllable and expound immeasurable dharmas. In an instant, one awakens and enters, amid all dharmas, freely and without obstruction, without going or coming, arising or ceasing, in complete equality. The signs of gradual advancement through this practice will, in time, be known through one's own realization. They cannot be fully exhausted by present explanation."

Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha said: "Having been able to practice and accomplish the contemplation of the One, you should now know that within this mind there are further five kinds of mind-aspects, which the practitioner should understand.

"First, the *kṣaṇa*-mind (momentary mind): when the initial mind first sees the Way, in a single thought of accord, it quickly slips away and is forgotten, like a flash of lightning on a dark night, momentarily appearing and instantly vanishing. Therefore it is called 'momentary.'

"Second, the flowing-and-pouring mind: once one has seen the Way, thought after thought one intensifies one's effort in continuous succession without interruption, like a stream rushing and pouring. Therefore it is called 'flowing and pouring.'

"Third, the sweet-and-delightful mind: when one has accumulated effort without ceasing, one at last attains an open, bright, clear transparency. Body and mind become light and at ease. One savors the Way with relish. Therefore it is called 'sweet and delightful.'

"Fourth, the crushing-and-scattering mind: when one suddenly arises to vigorous effort, or conversely, ceases and gives up, both are contrary to the Way. Therefore it is called 'crushing and scattering.'

"Fifth, the bright-mirror mind: having departed from the mind of scattering and confusion, one discerns with perfectly luminous clarity, unattached to anything. Therefore it is called 'bright mirror.'

"If one thoroughly understands these five minds and examines oneself by them, the positions of the Three Vehicles and the stages of ordinary beings and noble ones can be distinguished for oneself.

"You practitioners, when first learning to cultivate concentration, should practice the secret expedient empowerment-meditation method of all past Buddhas, which is one with all gates of dhāraṇī. Therefore you should receive these four dhāraṇīs:

*Oṃ sukṣma-vajra*

"This dhāraṇī enables the contemplation to succeed.

*Oṃ tiṣṭha-vajra*

"This dhāraṇī prevents the contemplation from being lost.

*Oṃ sphara-vajra*

"This dhāraṇī enables the contemplation to gradually expand.

*Oṃ saṃhāra-vajra*

"This dhāraṇī, when the contemplation has expanded, enables it to gradually contract again to its original state.

"These four dhāraṇīs belong to the profound skillful means within the self-realized dharma of the Bhagavān, opening the way for students to swiftly realize and enter. If one wishes to swiftly seek this samādhi, one should, in all four postures, constantly recite these dhāraṇīs. Apply effort with resolute mindfulness without even a moment's neglect, and there will unfailingly be swift results.

"You who practice concentration must also know the method of walking meditation. In a quiet place, level and clean the ground, making a path twenty-five cubits long. At both ends, set up posts. Between them, string a cord at chest height. Hold the cord using a bamboo tube that fits the hand, and let the tube follow the sun in a rightward rotation, walking straight back and forth. Merge the mind in universal awareness, gazing six feet ahead. Riding the awareness of samādhi, uphold the original mind, knowing it clearly and distinctly, without forgetting it. With each step taken, recite one mantra. Cycle through the four mantras from first to last, and having finished, begin again, reciting without ceasing. When one begins to feel weary, sit down wherever is convenient.

"The practitioner should know: the skillful means for entering the Way greatly aids progress. Like a diamond that cultivates the mind, unmoved and unchanged, don the great armor of vigorous effort and make a fierce resolve, vowing to accomplish it as one's goal, with no retrogression or change whatsoever. Do not let miscellaneous studies confuse the mind, causing an entire lifetime to pass in vain."

---

The Dharma has no dual aspect; mind and words are both forgotten. Without skillful means of revelation, there is no way to awaken and enter. Given the great distance between Sanskrit and Chinese, it is difficult to communicate without translation. I have merely been graced with his direct instructions and, recording from memory, have set them down to transmit to those not yet awakened. Dhyāna Master Huìjǐng of Míng Temple in the western capital had previously made a compilation; this is now further reviewed and supplemented, and may be considered fairly complete.

> Homage, bowing to the Buddhas of the ten directions,
> The gate of sweet dew in the treasury-sea of true suchness.
> The Three Worthies, Ten Stages, and those who have realized the truth:
> May you bestow your awesome spiritual power of mindful protection.
> The rare and precious essentials of dhāraṇī and meditation,
> Which can arouse the vast mind of perfect luminosity:
> I now, according to my ability, briefly extol them,
> Dedicating them to all sentient beings throughout the dharma-realm.
