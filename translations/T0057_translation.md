## Fascicle 1

### Sūtra on the Distribution of the Āsravas, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the Kuru country, at a place of practice called Dhamma. At that time, the people of the Kuru country had gathered together. The Buddha then addressed them: "O bhikkhus!" The bhikkhus responded: "So be it." The bhikkhus listened to the Buddha. The Buddha then spoke as follows: "Bhikkhus, listen. I shall expound the Dharma for you — good at the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end; with benefit and with skillful means, complete and perfectly manifest in practice. Listen carefully, receive it with proper attention, and hear what is spoken." The bhikkhus responded: "Even so." The bhikkhus then listened to the Buddha. He then said:

"Bhikkhus! You should know the āsravas, and you should know the origin of the āsravas, and you should know the misfortune received from the āsravas, and you should know the distribution of the āsravas, and you should know the cessation of the āsravas, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring the āsravas to an end.

"Bhikkhus! You should know feeling, and you should know the origin of feeling, and you should know the misfortune received from feeling, and you should know the distribution of feeling, and you should know the cessation of feeling, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring feeling to an end.

"Bhikkhus! You should know perception, and you should know the origin of perception, and you should know the misfortune received from perception, and you should know the distribution of perception, and you should know the cessation of perception, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring perception to an end.

"Bhikkhus! You should know craving, and you should know the origin of craving, and you should know the misfortune received from craving, and you should know the distribution of craving, and you should know the cessation of craving, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring craving to an end.

"Bhikkhus! You should know formations, and you should know the origin of formations, and you should know the misfortune received from formations, and you should know the distribution of formations, and you should know the cessation of formations, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring formations to an end.

"Bhikkhus! You should know suffering, and you should know the origin of suffering, and you should know the misfortune received from suffering, and you should know the distribution of suffering, and you should know the cessation of suffering, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring suffering to an end.

"Bhikkhus! You should know the āsravas, and you should know the origin of the āsravas, and you should know the misfortune received from the āsravas, and you should know the distribution of the āsravas, and you should know the cessation of the āsravas, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring the āsravas to an end.

"What does it mean to know the āsravas? There are three āsravas: the first is the āsrava of sensual desire, the second is the āsrava of existence, the third is the āsrava of delusion. This is called knowing the āsravas.

"What does it mean to know the origin of the āsravas? Delusion is the root of the āsravas; they arise from this root. This is called knowing the origin of the āsravas.

"What does it mean to know the misfortune received from the āsravas? Through delusion one acts under the āsravas; the consequences correspond to one's actions; the misfortune one receives corresponds to one's actions — one may fall into a favorable realm or fall into an unfavorable realm. This is called knowing the misfortune received from the āsravas.

"What does it mean to know the distribution of the āsravas? One may fall into the hells — this is one kind of conduct; one may fall among animals — this is another kind of conduct; one may fall among hungry ghosts — this is another kind of conduct; one may fall into the heavenly realms — this is another kind of conduct; one may fall among humans — this is another kind of conduct. This is called knowing the distribution of the āsravas.

"What does it mean to know the cessation of the āsravas? When delusion is exhausted, the āsravas are exhausted, and accordingly all is exhausted. This is called knowing the cessation of the āsravas.

"What does it mean to know what practice brings the āsravas to an end? It is this Eightfold Path: the first is right view, the second is right intention, the third is right speech, the fourth is right action, the fifth is right livelihood, the sixth is right effort, the seventh is right mindfulness, the eighth is right concentration. This is called knowing what practice brings the āsravas to an end. If, O bhikkhus, a bhikkhu has known the āsravas thus, has known the origin of the āsravas thus, has known the misfortune received from the āsravas thus, has known the distribution of the āsravas thus, has known the cessation of the āsravas thus, has known the practice that brings the āsravas to an end thus — such a one is called a bhikkhu who has turned away from the world in disenchantment, who conducts himself in purity, who has attained the path and brought the āsravas to complete cessation.

"Bhikkhus! You should know feeling, and you should know the origin of feeling, and you should know the misfortune received from feeling, and you should know the distribution of feeling, and you should know the cessation of feeling, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring feeling to an end.

"What does it mean to know feeling? There are three feelings: the first is pleasurable feeling, the second is painful feeling, the third is neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling. This is called knowing feeling.

"What does it mean to know the origin of feeling? The origin is mental intention. This is called knowing the origin of feeling.

"What does it mean to know the misfortune received from feeling? Whatever one intends, one experiences accordingly; this becomes suffering. This is called knowing the misfortune received from feeling.

"What does it mean to know the distribution of feeling? Here, O bhikkhus, when a bhikkhu experiences pleasurable feeling, he knows he is experiencing pleasurable feeling; when he experiences painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing painful feeling; when he experiences neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling; when he experiences a bodily pleasurable feeling, he knows he is experiencing a bodily pleasurable feeling; when he experiences a bodily painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a bodily painful feeling; when he experiences a bodily neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a bodily neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling; when he experiences a mental pleasurable feeling, he knows he is experiencing a mental pleasurable feeling; when he experiences a mental painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a mental painful feeling; when he experiences a mental neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a mental neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling; when he experiences a worldly pleasurable feeling, he knows he is experiencing a worldly pleasurable feeling; when he experiences a worldly painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a worldly painful feeling; when he experiences a worldly neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a worldly neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling; when he experiences an unworldly pleasurable feeling, he knows he is experiencing an unworldly pleasurable feeling; when he experiences an unworldly painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing an unworldly painful feeling; when he experiences an unworldly neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing an unworldly neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling. When he experiences a householder's pleasurable feeling, he knows he is experiencing a householder's pleasurable feeling; when he experiences a householder's painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a householder's painful feeling; when he experiences a householder's neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a householder's neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling; when he experiences a renunciant's pleasurable feeling, he knows he is experiencing a renunciant's pleasurable feeling; when he experiences a renunciant's painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a renunciant's painful feeling; when he experiences a renunciant's neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling, he knows he is experiencing a renunciant's neither-pleasurable-nor-painful feeling. This is called knowing the distribution of feeling.

"What does it mean to know the cessation of feeling? When mental intention is put aside, feeling ceases. This is called knowing the cessation of feeling.

"What does it mean to know what practice brings feeling to an end? It is this Eightfold Path: the first is right view, the second is right intention, the third is right speech, the fourth is right action, the fifth is right livelihood, the sixth is right effort, the seventh is right mindfulness, the eighth is right concentration. This is called knowing what practice brings feeling to an end. If, O bhikkhus, a bhikkhu has known feeling thus, has known the origin of feeling thus, has known the misfortune received from feeling thus, has known the distribution of feeling thus, has known the cessation of feeling thus, has known the practice that brings feeling to an end thus — such a one is called a bhikkhu who has turned away from the world in disenchantment, who conducts himself in purity, who has attained the path and brought feeling to complete cessation.

"Bhikkhus! You should know perception, and you should know the origin of perception, and you should know the misfortune received from perception, and you should know the distribution of perception, and you should know the cessation of perception, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring perception to an end.

"What does it mean to know perception? There are four perceptions: the first is limited perception, the second is extensive perception, the third is immeasurable perception, the fourth is the perception of nothingness — the sphere of no-thing whatsoever. This is called knowing perception.

"What does it mean to know the origin of perception? The origin is intention. This is called knowing the origin of perception.

"What does it mean to know the misfortune received from perception? As one perceives, so one acts; whatever action one performs — this is called action. This is called knowing the misfortune received from perception.

"What does it mean to know the distribution of perception? Perception of form is one kind; perception of sound is another; perception of smell is another; perception of taste is another; perception of coarse and subtle bodily sensation is another. This is called knowing the distribution of perception.

"What does it mean to know the cessation of perception? When perception is exhausted, perception ceases. This is called knowing the cessation of perception.

"What does it mean to know what practice brings perception to an end? It is this Eightfold Path: the first is right view, the second is right intention, the third is right speech, the fourth is right action, the fifth is right livelihood, the sixth is right effort, the seventh is right mindfulness, the eighth is right concentration. This is called knowing what practice brings perception to an end. If, O bhikkhus, a bhikkhu has known perception thus, has known the origin of perception thus, has known the misfortune received from perception thus, has known the distribution of perception thus, has known the cessation of perception thus, has known the practice that brings perception to an end thus — such a one is called a bhikkhu who has turned away from the world in disenchantment, who conducts himself in purity, who has attained the path and brought perception to complete cessation.

"Bhikkhus! You should know craving, and you should know the origin of craving, and you should know the misfortune received from craving, and you should know the distribution of craving, and you should know the cessation of craving, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring craving to an end.

"What does it mean to know craving? Craving consists of fivefold sensual desire — the desire to obtain, the desire most lodged in the heart, beloved forms one wishes to approach in attachment. What are the five? The first is forms desirable to the eye — the desire to obtain, the desire most lodged in the heart, beloved forms one wishes to approach in attachment; the second is sounds desirable to the ear — the desire to obtain, the desire most lodged in the heart, beloved forms one wishes to approach in attachment; the third is smells desirable to the nose — the desire to obtain, the desire most lodged in the heart, beloved forms one wishes to approach in attachment; the fourth is tastes obtained by the tongue — the desire to obtain, the desire most lodged in the heart, beloved forms one wishes to approach in attachment; the fifth is coarse and subtle bodily sensations known through the body — the desire to obtain, the desire most lodged in the heart, beloved forms one wishes to approach in attachment. This is called knowing craving.

"What does it mean to know the origin of craving? The origin is intention. This is called knowing the origin of craving.

"What does it mean to know the misfortune received from craving? When one has craved what is beloved, desire arises along with contention, expectation, and anticipation; the mind then becomes preoccupied with anticipating misfortune; and from this, misfortune is accordingly incurred — one may fall into a favorable realm or an unfavorable realm. This is called knowing the misfortune received from craving.

"What does it mean to know the distribution of craving? Craving for form is one kind; craving for sound is another; craving for smell is another; craving for taste is another; craving for coarse and subtle bodily sensation is another. This is called knowing the distribution of craving.

"What does it mean to know the cessation of craving? When intention is exhausted, craving ceases. This is called knowing the cessation of craving.

"What does it mean to know what practice brings craving to an end? It is this Eightfold Path: the first is right view, the second is right intention, the third is right speech, the fourth is right action, the fifth is right livelihood, the sixth is right effort, the seventh is right mindfulness, the eighth is right concentration. This is called knowing what practice brings craving to an end. If, O bhikkhus, one has known craving thus, has known the origin of craving thus, has known the misfortune received from craving thus, has known the distribution of craving thus, has known the cessation of craving thus, has known the practice that brings craving to an end thus — such a one is called a bhikkhu who has turned away from the world in disenchantment, who conducts himself in purity, who has attained the path and brought craving to complete cessation.

"Bhikkhus! You should know formations, and you should know the origin of formations, and you should know the misfortune and merit received from formations, and you should know the distribution of formations, and you should know the cessation of formations, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring formations to an end.

"What does it mean to know formations? What one contemplates and is directed toward, without departing from it — this is called formations. This is called knowing formations.

"What does it mean to know the origin of formations? From craving arises what is formed; craving is the root from which formations arise. This is called knowing the origin of formations.

"What does it mean to know the misfortune and merit received from formations? There is dark action, bringing dark misfortune, causing one to fall into lower states; there is pure-white action, bringing pure-white merit, causing one to ascend to ever higher states. This is called knowing the misfortune and merit received from formations.

"What does it mean to know the distribution of formations? There is dark action — from dark action one receives dark misfortune; there is pure-white action — from pure-white action one receives pure-white merit; there is mixed dark-and-white action — causing one to receive both dark and white misfortune and merit; there is action that is neither dark nor pure-white — causing one to receive merit from this, practicing until formations are completely ended. This is called knowing the distribution of formations.

"What does it mean to know the cessation of formations? When craving is exhausted, formations cease. This is called knowing the cessation of formations.

"What does it mean to know what practice brings formations to an end? It is this Eightfold Path: the first is right view, the second is right intention, the third is right speech, the fourth is right action, the fifth is right livelihood, the sixth is right effort, the seventh is right mindfulness, the eighth is right concentration. This is called knowing what practice brings formations to an end. If, O bhikkhus, a bhikkhu has known formations thus, has known the origin of formations thus, has known the misfortune received from formations thus, has known the distribution of formations thus, has known the cessation of formations thus, has known the practice that brings formations to an end thus — such a one is called a bhikkhu who has turned away from the world in disenchantment, who conducts himself in purity, who has attained the path and brought formations to complete cessation.

"Bhikkhus! You should know suffering, and you should know the origin of suffering, and you should know the misfortune received from suffering, and you should know the distribution of suffering, and you should know the cessation of suffering, and you should know what practice to undertake to bring suffering to an end.

"What does it mean to know suffering? One should know that birth is suffering, that aging is suffering, that illness is suffering, that death is suffering, that proximity to the uncongenial is suffering, that separation from the beloved is suffering, that not obtaining what one seeks is suffering, that the five aggregates in brief are suffering. This is called knowing suffering.

"What does it mean to know the origin of suffering? The origin is delusion; delusion is the root of suffering. This is called knowing the origin of suffering.

"What does it mean to know the misfortune received from suffering? An ignorant, uninstructed worldling who experiences acute and severe pain arising within his own body — pain most disagreeable, most excruciating — ought rightly to turn his mind toward cutting off such pain from within. Yet instead he turns outward, seeking outside, thinking: perhaps there are śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas who, through one word or two words or three words or four words or five words or a hundred words, through the recitation of spells and the performance of sacrifice, can thereby free his body from suffering. Thus he seeks to address his suffering and incurs its misfortune — this too is misfortune from suffering. This is called knowing the misfortune received from suffering.

"What does it mean to know the distribution of suffering? Some have little suffering yet experience misfortune for a long time; some have little suffering and misfortune is swiftly resolved; some have great suffering and experience misfortune for a long time; some have great suffering yet misfortune is swiftly resolved. This is called knowing the distribution of suffering.

"What does it mean to know the cessation of suffering? When delusion is exhausted, suffering ceases. This is called knowing the cessation of suffering.

"What does it mean to know what practice brings suffering to an end? It is this Eightfold Path: the first is right view, the second is right intention, the third is right speech, the fourth is right action, the fifth is right livelihood, the sixth is right effort, the seventh is right mindfulness, the eighth is right concentration. This is called knowing what practice brings suffering to an end. If, O bhikkhus, a bhikkhu has known suffering thus, has known the origin of suffering thus, has known the misfortune received from suffering thus, has known the distribution of suffering thus, has known the cessation of suffering thus, has known the practice that brings suffering to an end thus — such a one is called a bhikkhu who has turned away from the world in disenchantment, who conducts himself in purity, who has attained the path and brought suffering to complete cessation."

Thus the Buddha spoke. The bhikkhus received his words with devoted minds, rejoicing in what the Buddha had taught; through practice they attained purity and the unconditioned.
