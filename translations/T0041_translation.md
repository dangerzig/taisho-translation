## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra of King Bimbisāra, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling in Rājagṛha together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, all of them venerable elders in the Dharma and great arhants. Their outflows were entirely exhausted, what was to be done had been done; they had set down the heavy burden, attained their own benefit, completely severed the bonds of existence, and realized liberation through direct knowledge. Such an assembly numbered fully one thousand.

At that time, the World-Honored One arose in his mind this thought: "I shall now proceed to the Yaṣṭivana, to the place of the stūpa." Having formed this intention, he set out together with the assembly of bhikṣus and arrived at that place, where he took up residence. At that time, Bimbisāra, King of Magadha, heard that the Bhagavān, the World-Honored One, together with venerable elders and great arhants numbering in the full thousand, was residing at the stūpa in Yaṣṭivana. The king, wishing to go and hear the Dharma, gave the order to prepare an unusually grand retinue: twelve thousand carriages, eighteen thousand couches of fine garments and jeweled furnishings, eight musicians and four divisions of troops leading the way and following behind, with his retinue of ministers and attendants surrounding him as he proceeded. The king departed the city and went to Yaṣṭivana, approaching the Buddha's presence to pay reverence and make offerings. Many brahmins and householders also followed the king to the Buddha's presence. At that time, as the World-Honored One beheld the king's arrival, he manifested five marks: the mark of the uṣṇīṣa, the mark of the parasol, the mark of the maṇi jewel, the mark of the fly-whisk, and the mark of the jeweled sword, each adorning the Buddha's body. When the great king arrived at the Buddha's assembly, he set aside the sovereign dignity of royalty and, approaching the Buddha, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and with fine words praised the virtues of the Buddha. He touched his head to the ground in reverence at the Buddha's feet, then circumambulated three times and stood before the Buddha, declaring his own name, saying to the World-Honored One: "I am Bimbisāra, King of Magadha." He said this again: "I am Bimbisāra, King of Magadha." Thus he declared this three times. The Buddha responded three times: "So it is, so it is. You are indeed Bimbisāra, King of Magadha." And with gentle words the Buddha consoled and greeted the king, inviting the king to be seated. Hearing the Buddha's words, the king rejoiced greatly and withdrew to sit to one side. The king's retinue and ministers each turned toward the Buddha, knelt, and joined their palms; they also praised the virtues of the Buddha with fine words, and after bowing their heads to the ground in reverence, withdrew to sit to one side. Among the brahmins and householders, some praised and bowed in words, some simply joined their palms and performed obeisance, and some gazed at the Buddha from afar in silence. All such beings took their seats to one side.

At that time, those among the assembly—the brahmins and householders—suddenly beheld the venerable elder Uruvilva-Kāśyapa standing in attendance at the Buddha's side, and this thought arose in them: "Ah, the venerable elder Uruvilva-Kāśyapa has gone to practice the holy life under the great ascetic." The World-Honored One, knowing that those brahmins and householders had given rise to this doubt, immediately spoke a verse questioning Kāśyapa:

> "O Uruvilva-Kāśyapa, in former times you tended the sacred fire without ceasing.
> What benefit did you perceive, what Dharma did you attain?
> Speak this matter to me now, without delay."

At that time, the Venerable Kāśyapa replied in verse:

> "The taste of food and drink found in the world,
> and the pleasures of desire that people delight in —
> perceiving these as benefit, my mind pursued them;
> for this reason I tended the sacred fire without ceasing."

The Buddha again spoke a verse questioning Kāśyapa:

> "How is it that you were so attached to pleasures of desire,
> even coveting the taste of food and drink?
> The mind cherishes delight in the human and heavenly realms —
> speak this matter to me now, without delay."

The Venerable Kāśyapa replied in verse:

> "The highest term of stillness —
> through failing to understand it, I fell into despondency.
> Clinging solely to the five desires without discernment,
> for this reason I tended the sacred fire without ceasing.
>
> The Vedas teach that tending fire leads to liberation,
> and by this, the minds of beings are bewildered —
> the blind are no different from the dead,
> having fallen away from the highest term of stillness.
>
> I have now beheld the unconditioned Dharma of reality;
> the great Nāga, the supreme teacher, has spoken well.
> For the sake of the great benefit of sentient beings,
> the World-Honored One has appeared in the world with great vigor."

The Buddha then addressed him: "Kāśyapa! Well have you come; you dwell well, free from all erroneous thoughts; you are able to discern the supreme Dharma with skill. Kāśyapa! Now, you may transform this gathered assembly." Thereupon, the Venerable Kāśyapa, having received the Buddha's instruction, entered samādhi and manifested great supernatural powers. He vanished from the midst of the assembly and appeared in the eastern sky, displaying the four deportments of walking, standing, sitting, and lying down, and further displayed the fire samādhi. From within the fire element he emitted various lights—blue, yellow, red, white, and the color of crystal; he also emitted water from the upper part of his body and fire from the lower, and fire from the upper part and water from the lower, appearing and disappearing in unbroken succession. In the same manner, in the southern, western, and northern skies, he likewise manifested the four deportments of walking, standing, sitting, and lying down, and further entered the fire samādhi; from within the fire element he emitted various lights—blue, yellow, red, white, and the color of crystal; and he emitted water from the upper part of his body and fire from the lower, and fire from the upper part and water from the lower, appearing and disappearing in unbroken succession.

When the Venerable Kāśyapa had thus displayed supernatural transformations in the sky of the four directions, he withdrew his supernatural powers, went forward to the Buddha's presence, joined his palms, and bowed his head in reverence, then said to the Buddha: "My teacher, the World-Honored One! I am a great śrāvaka." He said this again: "My teacher, the World-Honored One! I am a great śrāvaka." The Buddha responded to Kāśyapa: "I am your teacher; you are a great śrāvaka." And again he replied: "I am your teacher; you are a great śrāvaka. Return now to your place and be seated."

At this time, the brahmins and householders in the assembly again formed this thought: "Can it be that this venerable elder, Uruvilva-Kāśyapa, has indeed been practicing the holy life under the great ascetic, the Buddha?" The Buddha, knowing their intention, addressed King Bimbisāra: "Great King! You should know that form has arising and cessation; truly knowing that this form has arising and cessation, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness likewise arise and cease; and these aggregates should be understood as possessing arising and therefore possessing cessation. Great King! With regard to this aggregate of form: if a good son can truly and fully know that what arises also ceases, he sees that the aggregate of form is fundamentally empty; since the aggregate of form is empty, arising is not truly arising; since arising is not truly arising, what ceases is there in cessation? As it is with the aggregate of form, so it is with all the aggregates. If a good son, having fully understood this, awakens to the fact that all the aggregates are without arising and without cessation, without abiding and without activity, then there is no self. I say that such a person is a true being of stillness throughout immeasurable asaṃkhyeya kalpas."

At that time, those brahmins and householders in the assembly formed this thought: "By virtue of what Dharma can one fully know no-self, and the absence of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness? What is meant by the self, person, sentient being, and life-span, and further by the master, the maker, the one who is born, the one who arises, the unmoved one, the speaker, the discriminator, and the knower—among such categories, which does not arise and which does not cease? And further, by what condition is it that beings receive the fruits of the wholesome and unwholesome karma they have performed?"

At that time, the World-Honored One, having perceived these reflections arising in the minds of those brahmins and householders, addressed the bhikṣus: "Bhikṣus! When one without a self speaks of a self, this is the notion of self among ordinary, foolish, little-learned worldlings and common beings. Bhikṣus! You should know that fundamentally there is no self and no self-possessor, and yet with regard to all the phenomena of suffering: if one forms the notion of arising, the aggregate of suffering arises; if one forms the notion of cessation, the aggregate of suffering ceases. And with regard to all the formations: if one forms the notion of arising, the formations arise; if one forms the notion of cessation, the formations cease. From this cause and this condition, the phenomena of formations arise; and through this condition of formations, there is arising and cessation. I, having thus as it truly is fully known the phenomena of arising and cessation, can then declare this to all sentient beings. Bhikṣus! With the pure divine eye surpassing the fleshly eye, I see all sentient beings—their arising and cessation, their beauty and ugliness, their noble and lowly states, their favorable and unfavorable destinations. The wholesome and unwholesome karma performed by sentient beings, and the fruits they receive—I know all of this as it truly is. Furthermore, sentient beings who possess the three kinds of unwholesome action of body, speech, and mind, who slander the holy ones and give rise to wrong view, and who on account of this wrong view perform various wrong actions and practice various wrong teachings—by reason of this, after death they fall into an evil destination and undergo the suffering of hell. Furthermore, sentient beings who possess the three kinds of wholesome action of body, speech, and mind, who do not slander the holy ones and give rise to right view, and who on account of this right view perform various wholesome actions and practice various wholesome teachings—by reason of this, after death they are born in the heavenly realm as devas. This I know as it truly is; this I see as it truly is. Bhikṣus! Yet I do not at any time say that there is a self, a person, a sentient being, or a life-span; nor do I say of those categories of knowers that such and such arises and such and such ceases, and that beings perform wholesome and unwholesome karma and receive fruits accordingly. With regard to all such phenomena, there is no notion of self; whatever five aggregates there are arise through the notion of dharmas, and through that notion of dharmas the five aggregates are produced. Furthermore, ignorance is the condition for the formations; when the formations arise, the dharma of accumulation arises; when the formations cease, the dharma of accumulation is extinguished. Bhikṣus! Thus the suffering of formations has its arising through accumulation; because accumulation is extinguished, the suffering of formations is immediately extinguished; when the phenomena of suffering are extinguished, all things that are not the Dharma are extinguished and arise no more. In this way, the phenomena of suffering reach their final limit. Bhikṣus! What is there to realize through cessation? This very limit of suffering is true quiescence, is the attainment of cool relief, is called the ultimate completion. Bhikṣus! This term of stillness means the relinquishment of all dharmas; when craving for dharmas is exhausted and the dharma of desire is extinguished, this is the stillness of nirvāṇa."

At that time, the World-Honored One again addressed the king: "Great King! What do you think? Is form permanent or impermanent?" The king said: "When form ceases, it is impermanent." The Buddha again asked: "Is it suffering or not suffering?" The king said: "World-Honored One! When suffering ceases it is no longer suffering; that which suffers is a phenomenon of inversion. Regarding this phenomenon of inversion—" "Is it that such a śrāvaka, of little knowledge and little learning, yet proclaims 'I am a great śrāvaka, I am of great wisdom,' giving rise to this notion of self, the notion of a self?" "No, World-Honored One." The Buddha then asked: "Are feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness permanent or impermanent?" The king said: "When they cease, they are impermanent." The Buddha said: "Are they suffering or not suffering?" The king said: "Suffering arises from inversion; this phenomenon of inversion is itself suffering." "Is it that such a śrāvaka, of little knowledge and little learning, gives rise to the notion of self, and therefore proclaims 'I am a great śrāvaka, I am of great wisdom'—that notion of self?" "No, World-Honored One."

The Buddha said: "Great King! So it is, so it is! Reflect carefully upon this: the phenomena of this aggregate of form—whether past, future, or present; whether inner or outer, whether in between; whether great or small, high or low, near or far—all those phenomena are fundamentally without existence, without characteristics, without self. Great King! With correct wisdom one should see them as they truly are."

The Buddha further said: "Great King! And so it is with feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness—whether past, future, or present; whether inner or outer, whether in between; whether great or small, high or low, near or far—all those phenomena are fundamentally without existence, without characteristics, without self. Great King! So it is. With correct wisdom one should see them as they truly are."

At that time, those in the assembly who were of the śrāvaka community, upon hearing this Dharma, fully understood the impermanence of form and of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness as well, and gave rise to disenchantment; through disenchantment they attained liberation; having realized liberation, correct wisdom arose before them: "I have attained liberation; my birth is exhausted; the holy life has been established; what was to be done has been done; I shall not be subject to further existence." At that time, King Bimbisāra, upon hearing this Dharma proclaimed, was freed from dust and defilement, and the Dharma-eye was purified. In the assembly, a further eighty thousand devas and humans and innumerable hundreds of thousands of brahmins and householders also had the Dharma-eye purified, freed from dust and defilement.

At that time, having had the Dharma-eye purified, having attained correct knowledge and vision, being firm and stable in the Dharma, and having departed from all desire and all affliction, King Bimbisāra attained freedom from fear within the Dharma of the Buddha. The great king then rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, joined his palms toward the Buddha with sincere faith and bowed his head in reverence, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I have attained great benefit; I have attained great benefit! I vow to take refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha, and to receive the precepts of the upāsaka. From this day forward, for the rest of my life, I shall refrain from killing and from drinking intoxicants, and so on." He further said: "I now with a devout heart invite the Bhagavān, the World-Honored One, to return to Rājagṛha. May the World-Honored One, out of compassion, accept my invitation; I wish throughout this entire life to attend upon and make offerings to the World-Honored One, including clothing, food and drink, bedding and furnishings, and medicines—all necessities of use and enjoyment, all fully provided; and so likewise for the entire assembly of bhikṣus."

At that time, the World-Honored One, having received the king's invitation, remained in silence. When the great king saw that the Buddha had silently consented, he touched his head to the ground in reverence before the Buddha and withdrew.
