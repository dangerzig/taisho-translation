## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on King Mandhātā

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at the Jetavana Anāthapiṇḍadārāma in Śrāvastī. At that time, Ānanda, sitting alone in a secluded place, reflected: "Among the people of the world, few are those who become satisfied with the five objects of desire; many are those who die without ever knowing contentment." After midday, Ānanda came before the Buddha, made obeisance, withdrew, and addressed the Buddha: "While I was sitting alone in a secluded place, I reflected: among the people of the world, few are those who become satisfied with the five objects of desire; many are those who die without ever knowing contentment."

The Buddha said: "It is indeed as Ānanda says — among the people of the world, few are those who become satisfied with the five objects of desire; many are those who die without ever knowing contentment. Why is this so? In times past there was a king whose name and title was Mandhātā, who was born from the crown of his mother's head, and was therefore called Mandhātā. He later became a cakravartin king, and the east, west, south, and north all came under his dominion. He possessed the seven treasures: first, the golden wheel; second, the white elephant; third, the sapphire-hued horse; fourth, the bright moon jewel; fifth, the jewel-woman consort; sixth, the holy counselor minister; seventh, the guide and military general minister — such were the seven treasures of this cakravartin king. The king was humane and virtuous, governed by right Dharma, and did not trouble or vex the myriad people. He had a thousand sons, all upright in appearance, of incomparable and superior talent, courageous and mighty in strength, and the four continents all submitted and came under his dominion. He reigned for several thousand years, and within his mind he thought to himself: 'I possess the four continents; the people are flourishing and numerous; grain is plentiful and cheap; the people are largely prosperous.' The king again reflected to himself: 'I have a thousand sons, all upright in appearance, of incomparable and superior talent, courageous and strong in might — how wonderful it would be if heaven were to rain down coins of gold and silver upon me for seven days and seven nights.' Heaven heard his words and, in accordance with his wish, immediately rained coins of gold and silver for seven days and seven nights. The king saw heaven rain coins of gold and silver for seven days and seven nights and was greatly delighted, and together with his retinue he took pleasure for several thousand years. The king reflected to himself: 'The four continents all belong to me; I have a thousand sons and the seven treasures, all present before me. Whatever I wished to obtain, I have already obtained; heaven moreover has not denied my wishes, but rained coins of gold and silver upon me for seven days and seven nights.' King Mandhātā heard that in the south there was the land of Jambudvīpa — greatly pleasant, with flourishing and numerous people. The king wished to go there; the moment the intention arose, he took up the seven treasures and the four kinds of troops, and together they flew and arrived. Jambudvīpa extended two hundred and eighty thousand li; upon seeing the king, that land immediately submitted and came under his dominion — this was because of the meritorious deeds performed in the king's past lives, which caused him to receive such blessings.

"He governed in Aparagodānīya for several thousand years. The king again arose with a thought, and reflected to himself: 'I possess a great country in the west named Aparagodānīya, extending three hundred and twenty thousand li in all directions; I possess the seven treasures; heaven rained coins of gold and silver upon me for seven days and seven nights; I have a thousand sons, all of upright and incomparable appearance, of superior talent, courageous and strong; I possess the southern land of Jambudvīpa, two hundred and eighty thousand li.' The king heard that in the east there was the land of Pūrvavideha, with flourishing and numerous people, grain plentiful and cheap, that land greatly pleasant. The king wished to go there; the moment the intention arose, he took up the seven treasures and the four kinds of troops, and together they flew and arrived. That country's king and people immediately submitted and came under his dominion, and the king thereupon governed the country by right Dharma.

"In this manner, after several thousand years, the king again arose with a thought and reflected: 'I possess Jambudvīpa, two hundred and eighty thousand li; I possess Aparagodānīya, three hundred and twenty thousand li; I possess Pūrvavideha, three hundred and sixty thousand li.' The king heard that in the north was Uttarakuru — the greatest delight under heaven, with flourishing and numerous people. The king wished to go to that land. Within that land there was no poverty, no distinction between the prosperous and the impoverished, the strong and the weak, no difference between master and servant, high and low — all were of a single equal rank. He thought: 'Let my people and retinue eat together of the spontaneously arising polished rice, and clothe themselves with the spontaneously arising garments, robes, and adornments of various precious things.' The moment the king's intention arose, he took up the seven treasures and the four kinds of troops, and together they flew into the territory of Uttarakuru. Looking from afar, he saw the earth was a pure blue, like the color of kingfisher feathers. The king asked his border ministers: 'Do you not see that the earth is a pure blue, like the color of kingfisher feathers?' The border ministers replied: 'Indeed we see it.' The king said: 'This, therefore, is the earth of Uttarakuru.' As the king advanced further, he again saw the earth was a pure white, like snow. The king again addressed his border ministers: 'Do you see that the earth is a pure white?' The border ministers replied: 'Indeed we see it.' The king said: 'This, therefore, is the earth of Uttarakuru — the spontaneously arising milled rice; you shall all eat of it together.' Advancing again, he looked from afar and saw the trees of the various treasures — the hundred-variety clothing trees, upon which hung gold, silver, jade rings, and jeweled garlands. The king asked his border ministers: 'Do you all see these treasure trees?' The border ministers said: 'Indeed we see them.' The king said: 'These, therefore, are the hundred-variety clothing trees, the trees of gold, silver, jade rings, and jeweled garlands — go forth and take from them together and adorn yourselves.' The king then proceeded to the land of Uttarakuru, and all its people submitted and came under his dominion.

"The king governed Uttarakuru for several thousand years, and again a thought arose; he reflected to himself: 'I possess Jambudvīpa, Aparagodānīya, Pūrvavideha, and Uttarakuru — four hundred thousand li.' The king then wished to ascend Mount Sumeru, king of the four-jeweled mountains, to the place where Śakra, King of the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, makes his abode. The moment the king's intention arose, he took up the seven treasures and his hundred officials, and together they flew to the summit of Mount Sumeru and proceeded into the palace of Śakra, King of the Devas. Śakra, seeing King Mandhātā come from afar, rose to receive him, saying: 'I have long heard of your virtuous qualities and have wished to meet you. Most excellent that you have come, worthy one!' He then took him by the hand, and they sat together, offering half of his own seat to King Mandhātā. Once seated, the king looked left and right at the heavens above. Jewel-women attendants waited upon him, and all was fashioned from the seven treasures — gold, silver, lapis lazuli, crystal, coral, amber, and agate — forming the palaces. Seeing all this, his mind gave rise to the thought: 'I possess Jambudvīpa, Aparagodānīya, Pūrvavideha, and Uttarakuru; in my treasury, heaven rained coins of gold and silver for seven days and seven nights.' King Mandhātā then thought to himself: 'If Śakra, King of the Devas, were to die and depart, I would wish to take his place and govern the heavens, just as I have governed the earth below — how wonderful that would be!' The moment the king entertained this thought, his spirit departed him and he came back down to earth, and was immediately stricken with illness, weak and feeble, confined to his bed. All the ministers and officials who had accompanied the king stood at the king's bedside and asked: 'Are there no last words to leave?' The king said: 'There are. I dare to ask this of you all: when they ask, "What last words has the king left?" tell them: "While the king was alive, he governed the four continents; heaven rained coins of gold and silver upon him for seven days and seven nights; the king had a thousand sons and the seven treasures, and was able to fly through the air. The king ascended the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven; Śakra, King of the Devas, rose to receive and welcome him with inquiries after his well-being, and offered him half of his own seat to sit upon. And yet he still gave rise to the thought of wishing to possess Śakra's position. The moment that thought arose, he came back down to earth and was immediately stricken with illness and weakness. He reproached himself, saying: People until their death have no contentment; those who know contentment are few indeed."'"

The sūtra declares: it is not because heaven rained coins of gold and silver for seven days and seven nights that he was unsatisfied — the gain was small, and the fault is great and heavy. A person of wisdom should reflect upon this matter. Moreover, to have been offered even half of Śakra's seat was still not sufficient. In one's practice of the path, one reaches Srotāpanna, Sakṛdāgāmin, Anāgāmin, Arhatship, Pratyekabuddhahood — only upon attaining the Buddha-path does one know contentment."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "King Mandhātā of that time — that was my own former body."

The Buddha having spoken thus, Ānanda rejoiced and made obeisance to the Buddha.
