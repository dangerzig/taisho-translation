# 雜阿含經
## Saṃyuktāgama

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0101

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

## (1)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Kośala, traveling with many bhikṣus. They went into a bamboo grove, stopped in one grove, and stayed among the cypress trees at the corner of the grove. At that time, a farmer brāhmaṇa of the Bhāradvāja clan was outside the grove, and many plowmen had gathered together for a meal. There were five hundred plows. The Buddha, thinking that the day was still early, went first into the grove and arrived at the farmer's place. The many plowmen were gathering for their mealtime. The Buddha then came to the place where the farmer's meal was being held. The farmer saw the Buddha approaching from afar and, having seen him, spoke thus: "I plow and sow for myself, and having plowed and sown, I eat. You, mendicant wanderer, should also plow and sow; having plowed and sown, you should eat."

The Buddha replied to the farmer: "I also plow and sow, and having plowed and sown, I eat."

The farmer replied to the Buddha: "Although the Buddha says this, I plow and sow, and having plowed and sown, I eat. But I do not see your farming implements: neither ox, nor plow-blade, nor yoke, nor goad. You merely say 'farmer' and 'sow,' but say no more. I do not see your implements. Tell me your implements so I may know your sowing."

The Buddha replied: "Faith is the seed, practice is the water, wisdom is the ox, shame is the plow, the mind is the goad, intention is the metal, guarding body and guarding speech and food are the furrows. Sincerity in cultivation without ceasing is the completion, effort without abandoning is the plow-blade, steady practice brings security. Having practiced without turning back, having practiced without sorrow: thus having sown, from this one attains the deathless. Thus sowing, one is freed from all suffering."

The farmer then filled a vessel with food and brought it to the Buddha, saying: "The Buddha truly can farm! The Buddha is indeed a great farmer! Please accept my food, out of compassion."

The Buddha replied: "Because I have taught the sūtra, I cannot eat this. A practitioner should know the Dharma and then inquire. The Buddha's teaching of the constant Dharma is like this. The higher Dharma need not be sought through this, but only through the exhaustion of fetters, the end of doubt, and the stilling of the mind. This food and drink offered as worship: from this the earth receives great merit."

The brāhmaṇa again addressed the Buddha: "To whom then should I give this food?"

The Buddha replied: "In the world, there is no one among gods, māras, brahmās, or śramaṇas, no one at all who could digest this food. Only the Buddha or one who has attained the path could. Take this food and go to water that has no living creatures and cast it in, or bury it in empty ground where there is no grass."

The brāhmaṇa, having accepted the Buddha's words, went and cast it into water with no living creatures. When he cast it in, smoke arose, it boiled, and it churned greatly with a hissing sound: just as when a lump of iron, red-hot from a day's burning in the fire, is cast into water, heat and steam and boiling and great churning arise with a sound. Just so, when the brāhmaṇa placed the food in the water, smoke arose, it boiled, and it churned greatly with a sound. The brāhmaṇa was terrified, his hair standing on end. He went and placed his head at the Buddha's feet in prostration, saying: "May I be permitted to become a śramaṇa under the Buddha, to leave evil and receive instruction, to receive practice from the Buddha."

The Buddha said: "You may follow the pure path from this point."

The brāhmaṇa received instruction from the Buddha, completed the Buddha's Dharma, and arrived at the path of non-attachment.

The Buddha spoke thus.


## (2)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the brāhmaṇa Jāṇussoṇi came to the Buddha. Having arrived, he asked after the Buddha's welfare and sat to one side. Having sat to one side, Jāṇussoṇi addressed the Buddha thus: "I have heard that the Buddha says: 'One should give only to me and not to others. Giving to me brings great merit; giving to others does not bring great merit. One should give only to my disciples and not to the disciples of others. Giving to my disciples brings great merit; giving to the disciples of others does not bring great merit.' If this is said, is it true that giving to you and your disciples brings great merit? If this is said, does it not revile the Buddha and argue with him? Are these the Buddha's words or not? Are they in accord with the Dharma or not? Do they not transgress the Dharma? Is there no fault in them?"

The Buddha told the brāhmaṇa: "If someone says the Buddha teaches thus, 'Give only to me and not to others; give to my disciples and not to others; giving to my disciples brings great merit, giving to the disciples of others brings little merit,' such words are not in accord with what I say. They misrepresent me and argue with me. Such words are not in accord with truth, not in accord with the Dharma, not in accord with any Dharma reasoning. Why? Because I do not teach this. Such teaching would destroy three paths, would destroy the merit of the giving household, would destroy the virtue of the recipient, and would destroy one's own mind.

"If someone washes out a pot or washes out a vessel and takes it to the garden and discards it in the garden, thinking: 'If the creatures in the garden eat from it, may they be safe and live from this': from this cause and condition, I say merit can be obtained. How much more so giving to a person! I only say: 'Giving to those who keep the precepts brings great merit; giving to those who do not keep the precepts brings less merit.'"

The brāhmaṇa replied to the Buddha: "I also say this: giving to those who keep the precepts brings great merit; giving to those who do not keep the precepts brings less merit."

"To all one should give according to one's wish. Giving to those without precepts brings less merit; giving to those with precepts brings great merit. Whether black or white, red or yellow, whatever the type: peacock, ox, or dove, it is the same. Each body arises from its own source; strength comes from accumulation; good and evil come forth from it. Judge only by conduct, not by appearance. People are the same: whatever body they are born with, whether a wanderer, a city dweller, a farmer, or one of the corpse-carrying caste, each has a body from which they are born. Those who keep the precepts can cross over the world; giving to them brings great merit. Those who are foolish and unable to hear, who are difficult to teach: giving to them brings less merit. Do not serve those who do not know; serve those who know, the wise disciples of the path. The disciples of the path have much faith, with branches, roots, and foundation. There are causes and conditions by which one ascends to heaven; there are causes and conditions by which one falls into evil destinies; there are causes and conditions by which one crosses over the world. All depends on causes and conditions."

The brāhmaṇa Jāṇussoṇi rose from his seat, placed his head at the Buddha's feet, and from that day took refuge in the Buddha and undertook the precepts.

The Buddha spoke thus.


## (3)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. A young brāhmaṇa from Rājagṛha came to where the Buddha was. Having arrived, he and the Buddha exchanged greetings. Having exchanged greetings, he sat to one side. Having sat, he asked the Buddha: "By what means does one distinguish and observe a bad person?"

The Buddha replied: "The bad person is compared to the moon."

He again asked the Buddha: "If one wishes to distinguish a wise person, by what means does one observe?"

"The wise person is compared to the moon."

He again asked the Buddha: "How is the unwise person like the moon?"

"Like the twenty-ninth day of the month: the light decreases, the color decreases, the fullness decreases, the visibility decreases. Past midnight it wanes further. There comes a time when the moon is entirely gone, no longer visible, like the new moon. The foolish are likewise. Having obtained teachings from those who have attained the path, having heard the sūtras and instructions, having obtained wisdom and faith, they do not practice, do not listen and accept, do not take it to heart. They abandon the teaching, do not adhere to practice, and their faith decreases, their moral conduct decreases, their learning decreases, their generosity decreases, their wisdom decreases. Past midnight they perish. There comes a time, brāhmaṇa, when the foolish person's good dharmas are entirely gone, entirely invisible, just like the moon on the twenty-ninth day. Thus the foolish person is compared to the moon."

He again asked the Buddha: "I wish to know the conduct of the wise."

"Like the moon on the fifteenth day: the light increases, the fullness increases, the visibility increases. There comes a time when the moon is complete and full. On the fifteenth day it is likewise. The wise person's virtuous words and dharmic conduct give rise to faith. From obtaining faith, one listens, applies oneself, takes it to heart, does not abandon it. The teachings gather together, and one's faith increases, moral conduct increases, learning increases, generosity increases, wisdom increases and grows lofty. Through assiduous practice, past midnight it increases to fullness. There comes a time when the wise person's practices are all complete, all conduct pure, in accord with the teaching: like the full moon on the fifteenth night. The bright and wise person sees it thus.

"Brāhmaṇa, the moon is compared to one who shifts: like the moon shining bright as it moves across the sky, surpassing all the stars of the world in brightness. One who has faith and learning is likewise: able to give without miserliness, able to give what is hard to give. In all the world, one shines through giving, like thunder rumbling and clouds flashing with lightning, abundantly pouring rain upon the earth. One who has faith and learning is likewise: able to give without miserliness, freely giving food and drink and provisions and more. Such a one has fame and renown like rain falling from the sky, with abundant merit. What is given, one receives, like rain bringing precious treasure and grain. Fame and renown also reach heaven. Having cultivated virtue, in the next life one dwells in heaven."

The young brāhmaṇa rose from his seat, placed his head at the Buddha's feet in prostration, and from that day received the Buddha's instruction and practiced accordingly.

The Buddha spoke thus.


## (4)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Once the Buddha was in the country of Uruvilvā, on the bank of the river Nairañjanā, beneath a nyagrodha tree, having just attained the path. He reflected: 'The one path by which practitioners can be purified, freed from sorrow, anguish, suffering, and displeasure, and can attain crossing over, cessation, and the true Dharma: what is this true Dharma? It is the four foundations of mindfulness. What are the four foundations of mindfulness? A bhikṣu contemplates the body in the body, abiding in mindfulness, knowing with one's own mind, free from the worldly delusion and displeasure, able to be detached. Externally, contemplating the body in the body. Internally and externally, contemplating the body in the body, abiding in mindfulness, knowing with one's own mind, free from the worldly delusion and displeasure, able to be detached. With feeling, mind, and dharmas it is likewise.

"'If a practitioner departs from these four foundations of mindfulness, one departs from the practice of the Dharma. Having departed from the practice of the Dharma, one departs from the practice of the path. Having departed from the practice of the path, one departs from the deathless. Having departed from the deathless, one cannot cross over birth, old age, death, sorrow, and affliction, cannot be free from suffering, cannot attain the essential. If a practitioner has the four foundations of mindfulness and can cross over, one can accept the practice of those who have attained the path. Having been able to accept the practice of those who have attained the path, one can attain the path. Having attained the path, one can extinguish old age, illness, death, sorrow, and affliction; one can cross over suffering and attain the essential.'

"Then Brahmā knew what the Buddha was thinking. Just as a strong person extends the arm and bends it and extends it again, so Brahmā descended from heaven and stood before the Buddha. Having stood, he said: 'It is just as the Buddha thought, just as the Buddha said: the one path by which one attains purity, freedom from sorrow, anguish, suffering, and displeasure, the ability to cross over, to attain cessation, to reach the true Dharma, to reach the four foundations of mindfulness. Contemplating the body in the body, abiding in mindfulness, knowing with one's own mind, free from worldly delusion and displeasure, able to be detached. Externally, contemplating the body in the body. Internally and externally, contemplating the body in the body, abiding in mindfulness, knowing with one's own mind, free from worldly delusion and displeasure, able to be detached. With feeling, mind, and dharmas it is likewise. If a practitioner departs from these four foundations of mindfulness, one departs from the practice of the Dharma. Having departed from the practice of the Dharma, one departs from the practice of the path. Having departed from the practice of the path, one departs from the deathless. Having departed from the deathless, one cannot be free from birth, old age, death, sorrow, and affliction, cannot be free from suffering. The essential is to know this practice and these skillful means.'

"As the goose's foot stands in the water on one leg, so the Buddha declared: 'I rightly practice.' Only receive these words and use them for self-examination, as the one path from birth and death to the essential of sorrow. The teaching of the path of liberation is taught out of compassion. Having gained the beginning, one crosses over the world from this. Crossing over now and crossing over later is also from this. The fundamental purity of the unconditioned is also from this. Birth, old age, death come to an end, through various dharmas received and practiced in reliance. This is what the one who has the eye of the path declares."

The Buddha spoke thus.


## (5)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Independent Brahmā, shining with his own light in the night, came to the Buddha. At that time, the Buddha was practicing the supernatural power of the fire element. The Independent Brahmā thought: "It is still early to visit the Buddha. The Buddha is now practicing the supernatural power of fire; let me first go elsewhere." He went to the bhikṣu Kokālika of Devadatta's faction. The Independent Brahmā arrived at the bhikṣu Kokālika of Devadatta's faction and, having arrived, told Kokālika: "Kokālika! Kokālika! You should hold good thoughts toward the bhikṣus Śāriputra and Maudgalyāyana, and toward the other wise practitioners of the path."

Kokālika of Devadatta's faction said: "Who are you?"

Brahmā said: "I am Brahmā."

Kokālika of Devadatta's faction replied: "Did not the Buddha say you are a non-returner?"

Brahmā said: "Yes."

Kokālika of Devadatta's faction replied: "Then for what reason have you come here?"

Brahmā reflected and thought: "Alas! Why does this one have no compassionate mind?" Then the Independent Brahmā spoke these concluding verses:

"One who wishes to measure the immeasurable: for this reason the world has little wisdom. One who wishes to make a measure of the immeasurable: thus the world covers itself."

Then Brahmā went to the Buddha. Having arrived, he prostrated at the Buddha's feet and stood to one side. Having stood to one side, the Independent Brahmā told the Buddha: "With my own radiance, the night being already bright, I came to the Buddha. Having arrived, at that time you were practicing the supernatural power of fire. I thought: 'It is still early to visit the Buddha.' Having arrived and seen the Buddha's supernatural power of fire, I thought: 'Let me go first.' I went to the bhikṣu Kokālika of Devadatta's faction. Having arrived, I told him: 'Kokālika! Kokālika! Hold good thoughts toward the bhikṣus Śāriputra and Maudgalyāyana, and toward the fellow practitioners of the path.' He said: 'Who are you?' I said: 'Brahmā.' He replied: 'Did not the Buddha say you are a non-returner?' I said: 'Yes.' He replied: 'Then for what reason have you come here?' I reflected and thought: 'Alas! Why does this one have no compassionate mind?'

"'One who wishes to measure the immeasurable: for this reason the world has little wisdom. One who claims to measure the immeasurable: worldly people think in their minds that they know.'"

The Buddha then said: "Kokālika of Devadatta's faction plays at destruction and slander." Then he spoke these concluding verses: "One who wishes to measure the immeasurable: therefore it is hard for the world to attain wisdom. One who claims to measure the immeasurable: from this the world covers itself."

The Buddha spoke thus.


## (6)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "In managing a livelihood there are three kinds of diligence: what has not yet brought profit can be brought to profit, and what has already been gained does not decrease. What are the three? Here, bhikṣus, a person who manages a livelihood, in the morning thinks of many skillful means and exerts full effort toward managing the livelihood. At midday likewise, and in the afternoon likewise, seeking many skillful means and exerting full effort to make it profitable.

"A bhikṣu's three dharmas of managing the spiritual livelihood are likewise: what good dharmas have not yet been obtained can be obtained, and what good dharmas have already been obtained do not decrease. What are the three? Here, bhikṣus, a bhikṣu in the morning abundantly receives the path, contemplating it in mind without parting from it. At midday and in the afternoon likewise, abundantly gathering the concentrated mind, receiving and practicing without the mind parting from it, able to greatly increase the path."

The Buddha spoke thus.


## (7)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Rājagṛha, in the Bamboo Grove at the Squirrels' Feeding Ground. At that time there was a brāhmaṇa named Akkosakabhāradvāja, residing in Rājagṛha. Akkosakabhāradvāja thought thus: "The śramaṇa Gautama is staying in Rājagṛha at the Bamboo Grove. I shall now go to the śramaṇa Gautama. Whatever sūtra the śramaṇa Gautama speaks, I shall contradict everything and not believe any of it."

So Akkosakabhāradvāja left Rājagṛha and went to where the Buddha was. At that time, the Buddha was surrounded by an assembly of more than a hundred, seated all around, teaching the Dharma. The Buddha saw Akkosakabhāradvāja approaching from afar. Having seen him, he stopped teaching the sūtra. Akkosakabhāradvāja arrived, greeted the Buddha, and sat to one side. Having sat to one side, Akkosakabhāradvāja addressed the Buddha: "I urge the Buddha to teach the sūtra; I wish to listen."

The Buddha replied: "Brāhmaṇa Akkosakabhāradvāja,

"For the obstinate the Dharma is not fitting, nor for one who does not understand the speaker, nor for one whose mind is confused, nor for one who only wishes to argue. If the mind departs from contention, the joyful one also departs from contention, and the angry one also departs from contention: such a one can understand the words of the Dharma."

Then Akkosakabhāradvāja rose from his seat, placed his head at the Buddha's feet in prostration, and awakened: "I realize I was foolish, I was deluded, I did not understand, I was unskillful, I came with an evil mind toward the Tathāgata, the Unattached One, intending to contradict and not believe. From today I repent of my past offenses and guard myself. I take refuge in the Buddha, take refuge in the Dharma, take refuge in the Saṃgha of bhikṣus, and guard my foundation."

The Buddha spoke thus.


## (8)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Buddha told Śāriputra: "I also teach the disciples' Dharma in brief, and I also teach it in full. But it is hard to find those who understand."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "Teaching in brief is also good, teaching the disciples' Dharma in full is also good. There will surely be those who understand."

The Buddha then told Śāriputra: "One should learn thus: with body and consciousness together, and externally with all perceptions of 'I am this' and 'this is mine,' the tendency toward conceit is no longer present. When the mind is liberated and wisdom is liberated, one sees the Dharma for oneself, understands for oneself, and knows that one has attained the practice. With body and consciousness together, and externally with all perceptions of 'I am this' and 'this is mine,' the tendency toward conceit is no longer present. If, Śāriputra, a bhikṣu, with body and consciousness together, and externally with all perceptions of 'I am this' and 'this is mine,' the tendency toward conceit is no longer present, and the mind is liberated and wisdom is liberated, seeing the Dharma and practicing with wisdom, seeing for oneself, knowing for oneself, seeking practice and cessation: this is called, Śāriputra, a bhikṣu without attachment, whose outflows are exhausted, who has completed the training, who has crossed over the world. For this reason I have spoken these words."

Then he spoke these concluding verses:

> Crossing the world cannot be reached by talk; destroy desire and the thoughts of desire.
> The mind cannot remain in both; also eliminate drowsiness and sleep.
> Also return from the fetters of doubt; contemplate the mind, purify and remove.
> Give rise to contemplation of the Dharma at its root. Having taught the wisdom of crossing the world, also teach the destruction of delusion.

The Buddha spoke thus.


## (9)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Buddha told the bhikṣus: "This body has skin, flesh, marrow, blood, raw flesh, and is full of feces and urine. Looking at the body, what beauty does one see? It constantly has nine orifices of foul disease, is constantly impure, constantly oozing enough to make one ashamed, constantly joined with enemies until old age and death, and illness always accompanies it. Why does one not despise the body? It will surely fall, it will surely decay. It is cast away in the burial ground, no longer used, to be devoured by foxes and wolves. Why do people see it without shame? Who speaks of craving and lust?"

As the Buddha said: "Observe well and carefully examine this with your own mind: like a butcher's tub and butcher's block, a heap of bones; like a blazing fire, like poison; feeling is agitation. The fool delights in it and is pleased, not knowing himself. Why not fear the snare? Craving and lust are delusion! Money, grain, gold, silver, oxen, horses, servants: people seek them for the sake of life, yet life hangs on a breath. One's allotted life is itself brief. Even a lifespan of a hundred-odd years is joined with suffering. Observing this, who can be pleased? As time passes, life gradually diminishes. Life and days are both exhausted, like a swift river, like the setting sun and moon. Life passes swiftly like this. When a person's life is gone, it does not return; it cannot be recovered.

"When a person dies and life departs, even if one had vast treasures and celestial rare objects, when death comes, everything comes together at once: there is no pleasure, nothing to be averse to, nothing to delight in, nothing to enjoy. There is nothing else but one's own good deeds; nothing else but one's own creation of good that naturally follows. If one knows and sees death, what kind of person would follow craving and lust? Whether one lives long or passes away, one must surely die. Why does the mind seek both the disease and its remedy?

"Why is this? Not reflecting on the tranquil ultimate, the mind loves children. When the child dies, one cries and weeps for no more than ten days; after ten days one forgets. Loving one's wife is the same; household, relatives, and acquaintances are the same. With suffering one accumulates wealth; at death others abandon it. One's beloved body and life-force are sought and discarded in confusion, entering beneath the earth, only the shadow departing. The ripe and unripe follow a person like the fruit of a tree.

"Having seen this, is there any reason for the mind to follow? Among all things in the world, one person who gains them is still not satisfied. If one gains a portion, how could one be content? Through numberless lifetimes, the five pleasures have brought self-indulgence throughout, yet what benefit have they brought? Having already suffered and accumulated punishment, does the person's mind gain any benefit? Not wishing to accept tranquility and seeking instead for snakes for oneself: whether little or much, it is the same; whether much or little, it is the same. As illness, whether great or small, is still suffering; like a bone without flesh, a dog that gets it gnaws at it without satisfaction. Desire is like this: a dog accustomed to it finds it hard to attain, and having attained it, should greatly fear it. This habituation does not last long, and one falls into evil. As a person who has a dream and then awakes can no longer enjoy it: craving and lust are likewise, more intense than a dream that has pleasure. Like a black viper, like hungry flesh, like the fruit of a tree with little substance and much loss. It increases bondage; it is the root of evil doing. The practitioner of the path never uses such a person.

"Even in heaven, having abandoned pleasures, with heavenly colored trees, even being as handsome as a pleasure garden, even obtaining heavenly princesses, having obtained them, one is not satisfied with the five heavenly pleasures. How then could one be satisfied with earthly things? This body is made of two hundred bones, one hundred bones, one hundred and twenty sections, wrapped in sinew, with nine orifices constantly leaking, with ninety-three types, with a hundred diseases at the extreme, flesh and blood combined, with living skin and muscle, with cold, heat, and wind within, with feces and urine, with a thousand worms. All arise from the body, and within there are also a thousand holes, each more grievous than the last.

"Having already destroyed intimacy with others, from this issues impurity: mucus comes from the nose, saliva comes from the mouth, flowing sweat comes from under the arms, feces and urine come from the orifices. All these issuing from the body are grievous. The dead in the cemetery are truly repulsive; when left behind they are truly repulsive. All that exists is the seed of impurity. From this origin it is like gilding: the rest is but clothing, perfume, powder, grease, rouge, red floss, indigo, and kohl. The fool sees this and is confused in mind, like a painted jar, like a pit covered with grass. What people embrace and love, they will later come to regret."

The bhikṣus knelt, bowed, and received the teaching.


## (10)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Bhikṣus, listen!" "We accept the teaching." The Buddha then said: "Bhikṣus, a person has four ties of craving attachment, some lighter and some heavier, by which one departs from the path.

"Bhikṣus, it is like a man who has four wives. The first wife is most cherished by the husband: sitting, rising, walking, moving, lying down, and resting, they are never apart. Bathing, adorning, feeding, and the five pleasures are always given to her first. In cold and heat, hunger and thirst, he caresses, soothes, and watches over her, following her every wish, never quarreling with her. The second wife: sitting, rising, and speaking, she is always at his side. If he has her, he is glad; if he does not, he is worried. She sometimes brings old age and illness, sometimes brings quarrels and lawsuits. The third wife: they sometimes meet and see each other, frequently inquiring after one another's hardships and pleasures as they please. When in poverty and extreme emaciation, they grow tired of each other. Sometimes they are far apart, and then they miss each other. The fourth wife: she serves at his command, running errands and doing work. In all difficult and painful tasks, she goes and attends to them. Yet he does not ask after her, does not speak with her, rarely watches over her, and keeps her out of mind.

"When the husband of these four wives one day faces death and must depart far away, he calls the first wife: 'You must follow me.' The first wife replies: 'I will not follow you.' The husband says: 'I loved you dearly beyond compare. In all things, great and small, I always followed your wishes, nourished and protected you, never once going against your will. Why will you not follow me?' The wife says: 'Though you loved and cherished me, I absolutely cannot follow you.' The husband departed in resentment and called the second wife: 'You must follow me.' The second wife replied: 'Even the first wife whom you loved most will not follow you. I too will absolutely not follow you.' The husband said: 'When I first sought you, the hardships were beyond words. I endured cold and heat, bore hunger and thirst, faced floods and fires, officials and thieves, and quarreled with others. With great effort I finally obtained you. Why will you not follow me?' The wife said: 'You yourself, out of greed for gain, forcefully sought me. I did not seek you. Why do you speak to me of your hardships?' The husband departed in resentment and called the third wife: 'You must follow me.' The third wife replied: 'I have received your kindness and gifts. I will escort you to outside the city walls, but I cannot travel far to where you are going.' The husband, resentful, departed. He returned and discussed with the fourth wife, saying: 'I must leave this country. You will follow me.' The fourth wife replied: 'I originally left my parents to come serve you. In death or life, suffering or happiness, I shall follow you wherever you go.' This husband could not get the three wives he valued and desired to follow him. He could only have the bitter, ugly, undesirable one go with him."

The Buddha said: "In the simile spoken above, the one person is a person's spirit-mind. The first wife is the person's body. A person loves their body even more than the first wife, but when life is exhausted and death comes, the spirit-mind must follow its merits and demerits and depart far away alone. The body lies stiff on the ground and refuses to follow."

The Buddha said: "A bhikṣu who does not follow the four practices cannot attain liberation. What are the four? First, suffering. Second, the arising of desire. Third, the exhaustion of emptiness. Fourth, the elimination of all evils. The essential path has eight practices, and sincerity lies in the Four Noble Truths. The second wife is a person's wealth and property: obtaining it brings joy, not obtaining it brings sorrow. When life is exhausted, wealth and treasure remain in the world and do not follow one. One merely sits with sorrow and suffering for nothing. The third wife represents parents, wife and children, brothers, the five relations, acquaintances, servants, and maids. In life, through affection and love they miss each other, but when life is exhausted, they cry and weep and escort the dead to the cemetery outside the city walls, then abandon the dead person and each return home. Their grief and longing last no more than ten days, and then they share food and drink and forget the dead. The fourth wife is a person's mind. In the world, no one loves and guards their own mind. Everyone lets the mind run wild, indulging in craving, anger, and disbelief in the true path. When the body dies, one must fall into evil destinies: perhaps entering hell, perhaps becoming an animal, perhaps becoming a hungry ghost. All this is brought about by indulging the mind.

"Bhikṣus! To practice the path, one should straighten the mind and set the intention right. One should remove the mind of foolish delusion and have no foolish or deluded conduct. Cease and do not practice evil. Not practicing evil, one does not receive calamity. Not receiving calamity, one is not born. Not being born, one does not grow old. Not growing old, one does not fall ill. Not falling ill, one does not die. Not dying, one attains the unconditioned path of nirvāṇa."

The Buddha spoke thus. The bhikṣus received it with joy.

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Rājagṛha, on Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "A person dwelling in the world, through a single kalpa of birth and death, if all their bones were collected and stored without rotting, dissolving, or perishing, the pile would equal Mount Sumeru. Without birth and death, one attains the path of nirvāṇa and crossing over the world."

The Buddha spoke thus.

## (16)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "There are two powers for attaining the supreme path. What are the two? The power of conscience and the power of guarding the mind.

In this life and the next, if I commit bodily misconduct, I myself will transgress, and I will also harm others. ## (17)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "There are three powers. What are the three? First, the power of faith. Second, the power of effort. Third, the power of wisdom.

Having received the Buddha's grace in practice and conduct, one speaks of the Buddha thus: the Tathāgata, the Truthful One, the Unattached One, the Truly Awakened One, the Wise One, the Essential One who has attained happiness, the Father of the World. Thus arriving at the Buddha, abandoning evil, and arriving at wise conduct: this is called the power of faith.

Seeking to practice, seeking effort, receiving right intention: evil intentions not yet arisen are not allowed to arise. Good intentions not yet arisen are sought to be born. Dharmic intentions are maintained without forgetting and without decrease, daily increasing, daily multiplying, practice and mindfulness filling the aspiration to arise, seeking, receiving effort, and controlling the mind. ## (18)

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "There are four powers. What are the four? First, the power of discernment. Second, the power of effort. Third, the power of non-transgression. Fourth, the power of association.

A bhikṣu, regarding the defiled, what is called evil, what is called transgression, what is called improper, what is called dark, what should not be followed: these categories one abandons. Regarding what is called good though defiled, what is non-transgression, what can be cultivated, what is proper, what is called bright, what is called the path: regarding these categories, does one practice with craving and desire, or does one practice with effort, receiving intention and controlling the mind? Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The body has no gratitude; the body does not remember kindness. ## (27) Sūtra on the Seven Stations and Three Contemplations

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha called the bhikṣus: "Knowing the seven stations and observing the three contemplations, one quickly abides in the Dharma of the path, liberating from fetters. Without fetters, the mind is liberated; from wisdom one attains the Dharma. Having seen the Dharma and personally realized the path, birth is ended, the path-mind is practiced, what is to be done is done, and one does not return again."

The Buddha asked the bhikṣus: "What are the seven stations? One should well know these. Bhikṣus, does one truly know form as it fundamentally is? One also knows the arising of form, also knows the cessation of form, also knows the path leading to the cessation of form, also knows the gratification of form, also knows the danger of form, also knows the escape from form, and knows it sincerely. Likewise with feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness one knows them as they fundamentally are. One knows the arising of consciousness, the cessation of consciousness, the path leading to its cessation. One also knows the gratification of consciousness, the danger of consciousness, the escape from consciousness, and knows it fundamentally and sincerely.

"What is form as it truly is? Form is based on the four elements, depends on the four great elements, and derives from the four great elements. This is how form fundamentally is; thus one knows it.

"What is the arising of form as one fundamentally knows it? The arising of craving is the arising of form; thus one knows the arising of form.

"What is the cessation of form as one sincerely knows it? The cessation of craving is the cessation of form; thus one sincerely knows the cessation of form.

"What is the path leading to the cessation of form as one sincerely knows it? Regarding this form, it is the Eightfold Path: right view through to right concentration, these eight. Thus one sincerely knows the path leading to the cessation of form.

"What is the gratification of form as one sincerely knows it? The desire and joy that arise dependent on form: this is the gratification of form, sincerely known.

"What is the danger of form as one sincerely knows it? That form is impermanent, suffering, and subject to change: this is the danger of form, sincerely known.

"What is the escape from form as one sincerely knows it? Being able to overcome desire and craving for form, being able to abandon and transcend desire: this is knowing the escape from form sincerely.

"What is feeling as one knows it? There are six kinds of feeling: feeling born of eye-contact, feeling born of ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind-contact. Thus one knows feeling.

"What is the arising of feeling? The arising of contact is the arising of feeling; thus one knows the arising of feeling.

"What is the cessation of feeling? The cessation of contact is the cessation of feeling; thus one knows the cessation of feeling.

"What is the path regarding feeling? One practices the Eightfold Path: right view through to right concentration, these eight. Thus the path leading to the cessation of feeling is the path.

"What is the gratification of feeling? The pleasure and joy that are sought and obtained through feeling: this is knowing the gratification of feeling.

"What is the danger of feeling? That feeling is impermanent, decaying, suffering, and subject to change: this is knowing the danger of feeling.

"What is the escape from feeling? Being able to live free from desire and craving for feeling, able to cross over: this is knowing the escape from feeling sincerely.

"What is perception as one knows it? There are six kinds of perception: perception born of eye-contact, perception born of ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind-contact. Thus these six are the perception one knows.

"What is the arising of perception? The arising of contact is the arising of perception; thus one knows the arising of perception.

"What is the cessation of perception? The cessation of contact is the cessation of perception; thus one knows the cessation of perception.

"What is the path leading to the cessation of perception? It is the Eightfold Path: right view through to right concentration, these eight. Thus one knows the path leading to the cessation of perception.

"What is the gratification of perception? The pleasure and joy of mind that arise dependent on perception: this is knowing the gratification of perception.

"What is the danger of perception? That perception is impermanent, exhaustible, suffering, and subject to change: this is knowing the danger of perception.

"What is the escape from perception? Being able to overcome desire for perception, being able to cut off craving for desire, being able to transcend: this is knowing the escape from perception.

"What are formations as one knows them? There are six kinds of formations: formations born of eye-contact, formations born of ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind-contact. Thus one knows formations.

"What is the arising of formations? The arising of contact is the arising of formations.

"What is the cessation of formations? The cessation of contact is the cessation of formations.

"What is the path leading to the cessation of desire for formations? It is the Eightfold Path: right view through to right concentration, these eight. Thus one knows the path leading to the cessation of formations.

"What is the gratification of formations? The pleasure and joy of mind that arise dependent on formations: this is knowing the gratification of formations.

"What is the danger of formations? That formations are impermanent, exhaustible, suffering, and subject to change: this is knowing the danger of formations.

"What is the escape from formations? Being able to avoid craving for desire regarding formations, being able to cut off craving, being able to transcend: this is knowing the escape from formations.

"What is consciousness as one knows it? The six bases of consciousness: eye-contact consciousness, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind consciousness. Thus one knows consciousness.

"What is the arising of consciousness? The arising of name-and-form is the arising of consciousness; thus one knows the arising.

"What is the cessation of consciousness? The cessation of name-and-form is the cessation of consciousness; thus one knows the cessation.

"What is the path leading to the cessation of consciousness? The Eightfold Path: right view through to right concentration, these eight. Thus one sincerely knows the path leading to the cessation of consciousness.

"What is the gratification of consciousness? The pleasure and joy of mind that arise dependent on consciousness: this is knowing the gratification of consciousness.

"What is the danger of consciousness? That consciousness is exhaustible, suffering, and subject to change: this is knowing the danger of consciousness.

"What is the escape from consciousness? Being able to cure craving for desire regarding consciousness, being able to cut off and transcend: this is knowing the escape from consciousness.

"Thus, bhikṣus, the seven stations are known. What are the seven? For each of form, arising, cessation, the path, gratification, danger, and escape, these five aggregates each have seven aspects.

"What are the three contemplations? Consciousness also has seven aspects. Adding these to the five aggregates makes six matters. Contemplating the body is the first: form. Contemplating the five aggregates is the second. Contemplating the six bases is the third. Therefore they are called the three contemplations.

"Bhikṣus! One who can understand the seven stations and can practice the three contemplations will not be long in following the path. Cutting off fetters, without fetters, the mind is liberated. From wisdom one realizes the essential. Through the single realization one receives and abides. Having cut off birth and death completely, having accomplished what was to be done, one does not return again to fall into birth and death. One has attained the path."

The Buddha spoke thus. The bhikṣus joyfully received and practiced it.
