# 沙彌尼離戒文
## Precept Text for Śrāmaṇerīs

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1475

Translator unknown.

---

The good woman named So-and-so declares: "Having received this defiled and wretched body, I am the lowest among the common stream, incapable of even the basest conduct. Reproving myself with remorse, I wish to become a disciple, to receive and uphold the correct precepts, and to practice them for my entire life."

For what reason does one become a śrāmaṇerī? Because one takes refuge in the Buddha, takes refuge in the Dharma, and takes refuge in the bhikṣu saṃgha. Because one shaves the head and wears the kāṣāya robe. How many precepts? A śrāmaṇerī has ten precepts:

1. For one's entire life, one must not kill living beings, and must not teach others to kill.

2. For one's entire life, one must not steal, and must not teach others to steal.

3. For one's entire life, one must not engage in sexual misconduct, and must not teach others to do so.

4. For one's entire life, one must not marry, and must not teach others to marry.

5. For one's entire life, one must not speak falsely, and must not teach others to speak falsely.

6. For one's entire life, one must not sing or dance, and must not teach others to sing or dance, and must not play the zither or blow the flute.

7. For one's entire life, one must not apply fragrant flowers, rouge, or powder, and must not teach others to apply rouge or powder.

8. For one's entire life, one must not sleep on high or finely carved beds, and must not teach others to make fine beds for sleeping.

9. For one's entire life, one must not drink alcohol, and must not teach others to drink alcohol.

10. For one's entire life, one must not eat again after midday, and must not teach others to eat after midday.

**The Seventy Rules of Deportment:**

> One must not wear silk or colored garments, and must not make colored garments to give to others.
> One must not use harsh speech to ridicule others, and must not teach others to speak idle words.

One must not look at the bodies of laywomen and laugh loudly together.

One must not, in a secluded place, expose one's body and fondle oneself.

One must not look in a mirror, rub and wipe one's face, or paint one's eyebrows.

One must not speak with anger, shame, or resentful irritation.

One must not think of sexual intercourse with men, or ask laywomen about such matters.

One must not sit on wool or cotton cushions. One must not wear boots or shoes, nor should one make footwear.

One must not covet the household's money and goods or forcibly demand others' possessions.

One must not sit on another woman's bed, open her containers, inspect her clothes, and say "this one is good, that one is ugly." Those aged sixteen and above may become śrāmaṇerīs. One must be originally without blemish, chaste, of good character, physically intact, and without disgrace. Only with the parents' permission may one enter the path. Those who are not originally chaste should not enter the path. Barren women and those concealing illness should not enter the path.

One must not share a room overnight with bhikṣu monks. One must not sit together with them. One must not look at each other's forms and laugh. One must not lie in another śrāmaṇerī's bedding. One must not mix up Dharma robes, share vessels, or mistakenly wear a monk's robe. One must not hand objects directly to a man. If one wishes to give something, one should place it on the ground and have him pick it up. One must not bathe openly with laywomen. One must not go alone to the monks' quarters to ask about doctrine. One must not discuss worldly affairs. One must not laugh about the sūtra's words. One must not look left and right. One must not lean on a desk with one's hands.

**Five rules for receiving instruction:** One should go together with an elder nun; sit six chi apart; kneel upright; only ask about the meaning of the teaching; one must learn to recognize the sentence punctuation.

**Four rules for visiting a sick teacher** (when there is a personal connection warranting a visit): Go with three persons; keep six chi from the bed; kneel upright and make inquiry, and depart when the conversation is finished; one must not discuss other matters.

**Five rules for sleeping at night:** One should orient one's head toward the Buddha; one should lie curled up and not stretch out the legs; one must not lie on one's back and stretch; one must not expose oneself; one's hands must not go near impure places.

**Five rules for going to a patron's house:** First go to the monastery and bow to the Buddha; next bow to the teachers and saṃgha; enter only when the laywoman invites you; one should inform the teachers and saṃgha; keep one's gaze fixed six chi ahead and sit alone on a seat.

**Five improper things when staying at a patron's house:** One must not go to the women's quarters to chat and play; one must not go sit by the stove and eat; one must not whisper privately with maidservants; one must not go alone to the back of the house, and must not go to the latrine together with others; one must not use the men's latrine.

**Five rules for entering the bath house:** One must not bathe with laywomen; one must not bathe with maidservants; one must not bathe with children; one must not take another's used water; one must not look at one's own private parts.

**Five rules for burning incense:** One must not look far to the left or right; one must not burn incense alone with a layman; one must not be alone with maidservants; one must not drag one's feet; one must not turn one's back to the image.

**Five rules for the morning:** First one should purify oneself and put on the Dharma robe; first bow to the sūtras and images; then bow to the teachers and saṃgha; inquire from six chi away; walk backward when exiting through the door.

**Five rules when the teacher speaks to you:** If one does not understand a question about the precepts or doctrine, one should request clarification; if reprimanded, one should immediately repent; one must not conceal faults; one must not justify oneself; one must not look at the teacher with angry eyes.

**Four rules for washing clothes:** One should do so in a secluded place and should kneel; one should discard dirty water in a secluded place, not on a main path; one should wait until the clothes are dry before collecting them; one must not let them fall on the ground.

**Five rules for traveling:** One should go together with three persons; one should go with an elder nun; or one should go with laywomen; one should keep one's gaze six chi ahead; one should wear the Dharma robe.

The teacher says: "A woman's nature, being greatly mixed with various tendencies and attitudes, is fond of sensual indulgence and lacking in propriety; this is why she is born a woman. Now that you have awakened, you have received the great kindness of Śākyamuni Buddha, who universally opened the field of practice across the three realms, allowing you to encounter the Dharma. Those who recognize their past conduct and take refuge in the Buddha are few.

"The Buddha says: 'Observing the human world, from the twenty-eight heavens above to the eighteen hells below, all is suffering without joy.' Therefore he established the precepts to instruct future generations. The Buddha told his disciples: 'To pursue the path is extremely difficult. Few can leave home and sever the six sense-attachments. Those who receive the Buddha's weighty precepts and abandon love and desire are rare.'

"The Buddha told his disciples: 'You must be careful not to recklessly ordain śrāmaṇerīs. Women's tendencies and attitudes are hard to ensure; in one moment pleased, in the next generating evil thoughts. They are like water bubbles, arising and vanishing, with no constancy. Only one who can perceive a person's faculties, observe their great conduct, and see that their past transgressions are now exhausted so that ordination will lead to attaining the path should be swiftly ordained. Unless one is a bodhisattva or an arhat, one should not ordain nuns.'"

**Procedure for the Confession and Precept-Recitation Ceremony (Poṣadha)**

The *karmadāna* first prepares the tally sticks, incense, fire, and the precept text. After sweeping and cleaning the monastery, the gong is struck. After burning incense, bowing to the Buddha, chanting invocations, and making aspirations, aspirations are made to Hārītī. Everyone then takes their seats, straightens their Dharma robes, joins palms, and sits in quiet silence. The *karmadāna* distributes incense and fire to each person. Each recites one verse. The *karmadāna*, standing inside the door, calls out three times: "Let laypeople depart; the confession and poṣadha is concluded." Three finger-snaps are made to send the laypeople far away, lest they hear the precept recitation. When the sound is finished, the door is opened. The *karmadāna* announces: "Sit quietly for the confession in silence; the poṣadha now begins." This is also announced three times. Then the *karmadāna* takes the tally sticks, kneels before the senior nun, and distributes one stick to each poṣadha participant, also taking one. When finished, the sticks are gathered and placed in a separate location. Taking the undistributed sticks in hand, starting from the senior nun, one asks: "Has anyone not received a stick? Those who have not received one should speak; those who have should remain silent." When finished, the *karmadāna* counts the sticks to determine how many people are present. The *karmadāna* announces: "For the confession assembly of such-and-such number of people, in such year and month, on the fifteenth [or the thirtieth], at such monastery in such county, commandery, and province, we recite the precepts. May the merit of this precept-recitation be dedicated to the great patron and their family, that all may be at peace, that those who have passed may be reborn among devas and humans with increased merit, and that beings in difficulties throughout the ten directions may all be liberated." After each has made aspirations, the *karmadāna* kneels before the senior nun and invites a qualified person to recite the precepts, and also invites one person to chant three invocations and read the sūtra. When the sūtra reading and chanting are finished, the senior nun makes universal aspirations while all junior nuns kneel to receive the aspirations. The *karmadāna* announces: "Let all bow to the Buddha together." After bowing to the Prajñāpāramitā is complete, all junior nuns bow to the senior nuns. Then the *karmadāna* kneels and announces: "Be at ease wherever you are."

The person invited to recite the precepts and the sūtra reader share a separate seat together.

---

*Colophon: Regarding this precept title, the state edition, Song edition, and the Kaiyuan Catalog all say "Śrāmaṇerī Precept Text" (沙彌尼離戒文). The Dan edition says "Śrāmaṇerī Miscellaneous Precept Text" (沙彌尼雜戒文). Examining the main text, neither is correct. The character 離 (lí) in this translation corresponds to the character 尼 (nī) in other translations; these are merely different Chinese approximations of the same Sanskrit syllable. This is not "the Lí-precept text of the śrāmaṇerī" nor "the miscellaneous-precept text of the śrāmaṇerī." Both miss the original text. Therefore it should simply be called "Śrāmaṇerī Precept Text" (沙彌離戒文). However, lacking firm evidence, I dare not emend it. I merely record my view and await a discerning scholar of refined judgment.*
