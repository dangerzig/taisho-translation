# 佛說四願經
## Sūtra of the Four Wishes, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0735

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Kuśinagara, together with five hundred bhikṣus, seated beneath a nyagrodha tree, teaching the Dharma for many tens of thousands of people. At that time in the city there was a powerful elder of immeasurable wealth named Cunda. Cunda had a son, just fourteen years old, who fell gravely ill. Unable to recover from the sickness, he passed away. His parents, brothers, and relatives both near and far all cherished him dearly. Their weeping and grief were beyond words.

At that time Cunda heard that the Buddha had come to teach. His heart was filled with great joy, and he told his wife: "The Buddha is here now; we should go to see him. All who hear the Buddha teach the sūtras find their sorrows resolved and their afflictions removed." Together with his wife, relatives, and servants, he went to the Buddha's dwelling. He bowed to the Buddha and sat to one side. The elder Cunda knelt with palms joined and addressed the Buddha: "People in this world accumulate wealth with anxious toil, not daring to spend on food or clothing, not knowing to practice generosity or uphold the sūtras and precepts, regardless of high or low station. When one who has obtained what they wished for reaches the end of their life, their parents, brothers, wife, children, and relatives weep in bitter grief. They prepare a coffin and send off the body with treasures, clothing, food, and drink. Can any of this benefit the dead?"

The Buddha told Cunda and the assembled disciples: "Listen to what I shall say, and reflect well upon it!" Cunda's family and the assembled disciples all joined their palms and listened with single-minded attention.

The Buddha said: "People have four wishes that cannot be kept forever. What are the four?

"The first wish concerns the body. One bathes and adorns it, feeds it, and gives it the five pleasures, always putting it first. Yet when sudden illness comes, one cannot stop it. When life ends, the body lies stiff upon the ground. It does not follow the person's spirit when it departs. To love and cherish it in vain: what benefit is there?

"The second wish concerns possessions, official rank, and salary. Those who obtain them are happy; those who do not are anxious. When illness comes and death arrives, all one's possessions, rank, and salary remain in this world. They do not follow the person's spirit when it departs. To grieve and suffer over them in vain!

"The third wish concerns parents, brothers, wife, children, relatives near and far, friends, acquaintances, and the joy of love and honor. When illness comes, when death arrives and life ends, none of them can save my life, nor can they follow my spirit when it departs. They weep in vain and escort me outside the city to the deep burial ground, then leave me there and each hurries home. Though they think of me with grief and sorrow, after no more than ten days the clan's men and women gather together, singing and dancing, happily eating and drinking, talking and laughing face to face, having forgotten the dead. Though one has parents, brothers, wife, children, relatives near and far, friends, and acquaintances, none of them can follow after my life. To grieve in vain: what benefit is there?

"The fourth wish concerns the mind. Few people in the world can guard their minds. All let their hearts run wild and follow their desires, indulging in the five pleasures, greedy for gain, jealous, angry, quarrelsome, and without faith in the path of virtue. When the body dies and life ends, the spirit departs, and three things pursue each other inseparably, like a sparrow in flight whose mind follows its two wings. The mind is the body's spirit; the two wings are the ethereal and corporeal souls. Those who cannot guard their minds all follow evil intentions in their actions: killing, stealing, covetousness, and sexual misconduct. Because of the sins committed during life, at death they enter the hells of Mount Tai and become hungry ghosts. When their punishment is completed, they emerge as animals to be slaughtered by humans. Because people let their minds run wild and follow their desires, they enter the three evil paths."

The Buddha told Cunda and the disciples: "You should set your minds aright and guard your intentions! Reflect carefully upon this: know that the body is not my self, and that all possessions are not truly mine. You should carefully reckon that your parents, brothers, wife, children, the five kinds of relatives, friends, acquaintances, official rank, and salary that you wish to obtain without ever being satisfied, thinking they benefit the self: when old age, illness, and death come, none of these can benefit the self, nor can any of them ward these off for me. A person who cannot extricate themselves to follow the path is like a parrot that loves its colorful tail and is caught by the hunter. The wise should clearly understand that these four wishes do not follow the spirit when it departs; one suffers over them in vain. Therefore, uproot the root of attachment and love, sever the three evil paths, and attain the three wholesome paths: first, no more old age; second, no more illness; third, no more death. Firmly guard your minds, and then you can attain liberation!"

The disciples heard this sūtra and rejoiced, bowing to the Buddha.

---

The Buddha reflected on all that lies beyond the eight directions of heaven and earth: all beings who have received life harbor sorrow and suffering. They constantly bring harm to the people. All creatures with blood and those that crawl cannot act freely. They struggle against heaven for life, yet all must return to death. Their bones decay and dissolve, sinking down into the earth; their spirits soar and fly, cycling through the five destinies. Those who do good ascend to heaven; those who do evil descend into the depths.

When an ordinary person is born, whatever good or evil they do, their spirit and souls follow according to their calamity or fortune. Those who in life are filial to parents and serve the ruler with loyalty and faithfulness, at death ascend to heaven.

Just as worldly people accumulate virtue and do good, serve in office seeking rank, and attain the status of lords and nobles: whether one is wealthy and happy or poor and destitute, all is determined by past karma. Those who commit killing and cruel oppression are born as evil persons and at death receive heavy retribution.

The mind itself is the ruler that invites a hundred misfortunes. The pleasure of an indulgent heart will surely bring later calamity. Emperors and common people alike are deluded about the path. We are entrusted to our parents. Doing good brings fortune and long life; doing evil brings poverty and suffering. Those who steal, cheat others, default on debts, or borrow without repaying: after death they become slaves, oxen, or horses, or are born as great pigs to be slaughtered, their bodies weighed and sold to repay others. Those who in life are stingy and refuse to give: at death they become hungry ghosts, unable to obtain clothing or food, like beggars who cut their own flesh and knock their heads on the ground begging for food. All this is because they were formerly greedy, cruel, and rebellious, not believing in doing good. Those who kill, steal, take others' wives, speak with duplicitous tongues, or drink wine and cause disorder: at death they enter the hells, where they are flogged, burned, and boiled. Their bodies endure the poison of scorpion stings, and the suffering and pain are without limit.

People have six sorrows, three sufferings, and four pains. The Buddha restrains and transforms the unending cycle of birth and death. He renounced his kingdom and abandoned kingship to seek the natural path, accumulating virtue over many years before attaining the truth of the path. His divine illumination shines through all, seeing completely beyond the furthest reaches of heaven and earth, knowing what humans, birds, beasts, and insects say and what their minds intend.

The Buddha reflected: a person's death is like a great wind that arrives suddenly without warning. Death comes at no fixed time. One should contend with the mind, doing good without doubt. The Buddha uses the sūtras to encourage the wise. What the eyes do not see and the ears do not hear, or rare and precious treasures: what benefit are these to oneself?

All who practice the path should believe in the sūtras and precepts, guard goodness unto death, and not be born through committing evil. The path must not be lost; virtue must not be abandoned. To be far from the path and to lose virtue is like a child born without a mother or a fish removed from the deep.

A person who dies and is reborn is like a silkworm in a murky cocoon that threads its silk and emerges to fly. The spirit remains the same; only the form and shell are changed. The path is accomplished through subtlety, and the five precepts obtain the root.

The disciples heard the sūtra and rejoiced. They came forward to receive the teaching. This is the essential knowledge of painful and pleasant feelings, to be understood as the truth.

What is perception-cognition? It is the six perceptions of the body. The eye makes perception, and the ear, nose, mouth, body, and mind make perception. Such are the six perceptions. What is cognition of the arising of perception? The arising of perception is cognition of its arising. Such is cognition of the arising of perception. What is cognition of the cessation of perception? The cessation of perception is cognition of its cessation. Such is cognition of the cessation of perception. What is cognition of the path leading to the cessation of perception? It is the cognition of the eightfold path: right view through right concentration, these eight. Such is cognition of the path leading to the cessation of perception. What is cognition of the gratification of perception? Whatever pleasure and delight of mind arise by reason of perception: such is cognition of the gratification of perception. What is cognition of the danger of perception? Whatever is impermanent, subject to cessation, suffering, and change in perception: such is cognition of the danger of perception. What is cognition of the escape from perception? The ability to remove desire and craving for perception, the ability to abandon desire and craving, the ability to liberate oneself from desire and craving: such is cognition of the escape from perception. What is cognition of birth and death? It is the six kinds of birth-and-death cognition. The eye makes birth-and-death cognition, and the ear, nose, mouth, body, and mind make action. Such is cognition of birth and death. What is cognition of the arising of birth and death? The arising of perception is cognition of the arising of birth and death. What is cognition of the cessation of birth and death? The cessation of perception is cognition of the cessation of birth and death. What is cognition of the path leading to the cessation of birth and death? It is the eightfold path: right view through right concentration, these eight. Such is cognition of the path leading to the cessation of birth and death. What is cognition of the gratification of birth and death? Whatever pleasure, delight, and satisfaction of mind arise by reason of birth and death: such is cognition of the gratification of birth and death. What is cognition of the danger of birth and death? Whatever is impermanent, subject to cessation, suffering, and change in birth and death: such is cognition of the danger of birth and death. What is cognition of the escape from birth and death? The ability to remove desire and craving for birth and death, to follow desire and craving and be able to abandon them, to be able to liberate oneself: such is cognition of the escape from birth and death. What is the cognition of the six bases of the consciousness-body? The eye makes cognition, and the ear, nose, mouth, body, and mind make cognition. Such is cognition of consciousness. What is the arising of consciousness? The arising of name is the arising of consciousness. Such is the arising of consciousness. What is the cessation of consciousness? The cessation of name is the cessation of consciousness. Such is cognition of the cessation of consciousness. What is cognition of the path leading to the cessation of consciousness? The eightfold path: right view through right concentration, these eight. Such is cognition of the path leading to the cessation of consciousness. What is cognition of the gratification of consciousness? Whatever pleasure, delight, and satisfaction of mind arise by reason of consciousness: such is cognition of the gratification of consciousness. What is cognition of the danger of consciousness? Whatever is subject to cessation, suffering, and change: such is cognition of the danger of consciousness. What is cognition of the escape from consciousness? The ability to remove desire and craving, the ability to abandon desire and craving, the ability to liberate oneself: such is cognition of the escape from consciousness.

"Thus, bhikṣus, seven places constitute awakening. What are the seven? Form, arising, cessation, the path, gratification, danger, and escape. Each of the five aggregates has these seven aspects. What are the three contemplations? They also have seven aspects each. Obtaining the five aggregates completes the six bases. Contemplating the body as form is the first; contemplating the five aggregates is the second; contemplating the six bases is the third. Therefore it is called the three contemplations. A bhikṣu who can comprehend the seven places and can also practice the three contemplations will before long walk the path, sever the fetters, be without fetters, have the mind liberated, be alive with wisdom, see the path, see the escape, and in one realization attain cessation. Having severed birth and death, with the mind's actions accomplished, one will not return again to birth and death. This is attaining the path."

The Buddha spoke thus. The bhikṣus rejoiced and practiced accordingly.
