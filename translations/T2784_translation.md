# 大乘四法經釋抄
## Commentary Notes on the Mahāyāna Sūtra on Four Dharmas

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2784

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

**The Mahāyāna Sūtra on Four Dharmas.** Regarding the establishment of sūtra titles, all are established according to four types. The first is by person, as with the Vimalakīrti Sūtra and the like. The second is by place, as with the Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra, where the World-Honored One addressed King Rāvaṇa of Laṅkā, teaching the Dharma on Mount Laṅkā, and accordingly established the title by place. The third is by dharma, as with the Great Prajñā Sūtra, which teaches that the aggregates, elements, and the like, internal and external, are all empty, and that one does not cling to the two views of a self of persons or a self of dharmas. This is called establishing the title by dharma. The fourth is by analogy, as with the Ratnamegha Sūtra and the like: when that sūtra was taught, the bodhisattvas of the ten directions all gathered like clouds, and so the analogy was taken as the title.

**Question:** Among these four types, which is this sūtra's title established by? **Answer:** This sūtra is established by dharma. What are those dharmas? They are not forsaking the mind of bodhi, and not forsaking solitary retreat, and so on.

**Regarding "the scope of practice, cultivating the Great [Vehicle]."** Broadly cultivating the practices for self and others. "Practices for self" means cultivating the six perfections and the like. Among the six perfections, the first three are practices for others, and the last two are practices for self-benefit. Vigor pertains to both. "Practices for others" means: if there is a sentient being who should be liberated through seeing a buddha-body, then one manifests a buddha-body, or a śrāvaka, or a bodhisattva, and so on. With the four dharmas of the sūtra combined, one can generate the mind of bodhi and can encompass the practices of benefiting self and others.

Another interpretation: "Not forsaking the mind of bodhi," "skillfully knowing all pleasures and dwelling in solitary retreat," and the like are practices for self. "Having a mind of patience, gentleness, and harmony" is the practice of benefiting others.

**Regarding "greatness of wisdom."** It means comprehending that both persons and dharmas are empty. This is unlike the śrāvakas, who can comprehend the emptiness of persons but not the emptiness of dharmas. Therefore they abide in nirvāṇa, without a body to benefit others. It is also unlike ordinary beings, who cannot comprehend either, and are bound by afflictions. All buddhas and bodhisattvas, because they comprehend the emptiness of persons, do not abide in saṃsāra. Because they comprehend the emptiness of dharmas, they do not abide in nirvāṇa.

**Regarding "greatness of vigor."** During the three incalculable kalpas: in the first incalculable kalpa, one cultivates the four preparatory practices to perfection. In the second incalculable kalpa, from the first ground to the eighth ground, one cultivates and accomplishes the path of seeing. In the third incalculable kalpa, from the eighth ground to the stage beyond learning. For example, when Śāriputra was practicing the bodhisattva path, a certain brāhmaṇa begged for his eye. He gave it to him. The brāhmaṇa, upon receiving the eye, crushed it under his foot. Śāriputra then asked: "Why did you crush it?" He answered: "I wanted it for the sound."

**Regarding "greatness of skillful means."** The same as the explanation of "greatness of wisdom" above.

**Regarding "greatness of activity."** To the very end of birth and death, one is able to perform the deeds of all buddhas. "Vehicle" means being able to make the four vows. "Greatness of vows" means: if there is a single sentient being who has not become a buddha, I vow not to seek unsurpassed bodhi. This is unlike the śrāvakas, who do not make great vows.

**Regarding "greatness of accomplishment."** One is able to accomplish the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the eighteen unique dharmas, and other great meritorious qualities. Each is explained in detail elsewhere. Of the above seven greatnesses, the first six serve as causes, for no single one can accomplish the ten powers and the rest; one must cultivate all six. The last one is the fruit.

**Regarding "the four dharma-boats."** These are not forsaking the mind of bodhi and delighting in dwelling in solitary retreat, and so on.

**Regarding "enumerating."** The treatise itself explains this.

**Regarding "producing."** Through cultivating these four dharmas, one can cross the ocean of death.

**Regarding "revealing and displaying."** They can reveal the exceedingly profound dharma-nature.

**Regarding "unchanging through past and present."** This corresponds to the meaning of "constancy" above. The former buddhas and later buddhas have never shifted or altered this. It is also like the twelve links of dependent origination. The two characters "dharma" and "path" likewise correspond to the above.

**Regarding "indicating the setting."** "Thus have I heard" up to "bodhisattva-mahāsattvas" is this.

**Regarding "characteristics."** These are the three characteristics: the universally imagined, and so on.

**Regarding "dharmas."** These are the five aggregates, the eighteen elements, and so on.

**Regarding "meaning."** This is what can express the substance and abiding of the five aggregates and eighteen elements.

**Regarding "the introductory sequence."** There are two types. First, the introduction prompted by a request. Second, the introduction not prompted by a request.

**Question:** Why not explain the authentication sequence at length? **Answer:** The authentication sequence is common to all sūtras. It need not be repeatedly explained. Nevertheless, I shall now briefly clarify it.

**Regarding "the Buddha's teaching conditions being fulfilled."** He only liberates those with whom he has a karmic connection; he does not liberate those without connection. The Vimalakīrti Sūtra says: "Though the Buddha's power is universal, he does not liberate those without roots. Where there is no moisture, even the rain does not moisten dry wood."

**Regarding "the four contemplations."** "Contemplating the body as impure" means: upon this body are the thirty-six substances and various impurities that together compose it. Therefore it is called "impure." "Contemplating sensations as suffering" means: painful sensations, pleasant sensations, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant sensations are all suffering. Sentient beings do not understand this and cling to them as pleasant.

"Kāśyapa Bodhisattva and the others": "others" includes Maitreya, Mañjuśrī, and so on.

**Regarding "contemplating the mind as impermanent."** This mind, thought after thought, ceases and passes away. Sentient beings do not understand this and cling to it as a permanent characteristic.

**Regarding "contemplating dharmas as without self."** Upon the five aggregates and eighteen elements, there is originally no self, person, sentient being, or the like. Sentient beings do not understand this and falsely imagine that there is a self. If one simply does not give rise to such inverted views, one can accomplish the holy path. "Mouth" [here] means "cause."

**Regarding "brahma-altar."** This is translated as "silent areca."

**Regarding "the Vinaya of Miscellaneous Matters."** This refers to what is taught in the lower fifty fascicles of the Mūlasarvāstivāda division.

**Regarding "the time of the compilation."** After the Buddha's nirvāṇa, at the Seven-Leaf Cave in Rājagṛha, five hundred great arhats were assembled. The assembly requested Ānanda to ascend the seat and teach the Dharma. The assembly raised three doubts. Then Ānanda spoke "Thus have I heard" and the rest. When the Buddha first attained the Way, at the Deer Park, he first converted the five monks such as Kauṇḍinya, teaching the Dharma of the Four Noble Truths. "Three turnings" refers to this.

**Regarding "time, place, and persons."** "One time" in that sūtra is the time. "Place" is the location. "Persons" are the listeners of the Dharma.

**Regarding "teaching."** These are the twelve divisions of the teaching.

**Regarding "principle."** This is the nature of true suchness.

**Regarding "practice."** This is the eightfold holy path.

**Regarding "fruit."** This includes superior fruits and unsurpassed fruits, and so on.

**Regarding 'obtaining the fruit according to one's vow is called suchness.'"** Generosity obtains great merit, and so on.

**Regarding "setting aside the particular and taking up the general."** There are two meanings. First, one must borrow the ear-faculty to generate consciousness, and the mind then discriminates accordingly.

**Regarding "if each were enumerated, it would become extensive."** This is an implicit objection: within the sūtra title, if one simply placed "faculty, mind, thought, consciousness," that would suffice. Why is "I heard" necessary? **Answer:** If one places the meaning, it becomes extensive. Why not mention only one? **Answer:** If one mentioned only one, its function would be incomplete. Another implicit objection: if so, why not in the sūtra...

**Regarding "there is no 'I' who heard."** To lead non-Buddhists into the Dharma, one provisionally establishes "I heard." Just as the Buddha, by expedient means, provisionally established a self for the long-nailed brāhmaṇa who questioned him.

**Regarding "skillfully according with timeliness."** If before the meal there are those who can be converted, one converts them then. If after the meal there are those who can be converted, one converts them then.

**Regarding "capacity and response being mutually fitting."** It is like clear water: according to the size of the vessel, it reflects the moon's image within, manifesting large and small reflections. "Mutually fitting and obtaining benefit" means: all buddhas and tathāgatas, according to the capacities of sentient beings in the three vehicles, teach the Dharma at the right time, causing each to obtain benefit. It is also like a physician who, according to whether the illness is cold or hot, prescribes the appropriate medicine, resulting in swift recovery.

**Regarding "non-Buddhist texts lacking such ability."** They lack the above capacity-responsive teaching, the illness-responsive medicine, and the "one-time" statement.

**Regarding "the earlier selection of teacher and listener."** In the other divisions of sūtras, the four dharmas are selected and taken up.

**Regarding "the later selection of teacher and listener."** This pertains to the before-meal and after-meal meaning.

**Regarding "if so, dharmas should not be consciousness-only."** An implicit objection: this Sūtra on Four Dharmas, if classified among the three schools of the Great Vehicle, belongs to the Consciousness-Only school. As stated above, establishing "one," "time," and "external dharmas": these dharmas should not be consciousness-only. **Answer:** The answer is the same as in the definitive text.

**Regarding "world-consciousness."** This is the world of sentient beings and the world of vessels.

**Regarding "number-consciousness."** This means one, two, three, four.

**Regarding "the most honored one."** This is the Buddha.

**Regarding "sovereign freedom."** The afflictions of the three realms, nine grounds, and the paths of seeing and cultivation cannot bind him. He has attained sovereignty over afflictions.

**Regarding "blazing ferocity."** With the fire of wisdom, he burns the fuel of afflictions.

**Regarding "dignified and majestic."** Through reverencing parents, teachers, and preceptors, one obtains the thirty-two major marks and eighty minor marks, and so on.

**Regarding "auspiciousness."** Through drawing near to, reverently serving, and universally praising, one possesses auspiciousness. It is like a blind person gaining sight, or a mute person gaining speech, and so on.

**Regarding "constantly employing skillful means to benefit and bring happiness."** "Benefit" pertains to the worldly; "happiness" pertains to the world-transcending. Or: "superior, victorious happiness"; "determinately arising benefit." Reversing the usage yields "honored and noble." This is also called "vigor."

**Regarding "the Buddha possessing the ten virtues."** An implicit objection: why not place "Omniscient One" and the rest at the beginning of the sūtra, but only use the name "Bhagavān"? **Answer:** The answer is as in the definitive text.

**Regarding "the Buddha, World-Honored One, having three bodies."** An implicit objection: a further discussion arising from this discussion. **Further question:** The Buddha has three bodies. For those of lesser faculties, one teaches through the transformation-body. The transformation-body is the sixteen-foot golden body. The reward-body is for teaching those above the grounds. "Those who should be liberated through seeing a buddha-body: one manifests a buddha-body and teaches the Dharma for them."

**Regarding "the reward-body."** The great perfect mirror wisdom is its essence. With discriminating wisdom, it conceals the one true suchness-nature as its object. Regarding the first two bodies, the Dharma-body seems to have teaching. But regarding the Dharma-body itself, there is neither teaching nor non-teaching.
