# Stele Clarifying the Root of the Buddha-Dharma

## *Míng Fófǎ Gēnběn Bēi* (明佛法根本碑)

Taishō 1954

Expounded by Zhìhuìlún (*Zhìhuìlún*, 智慧輪, "Wheel of Wisdom") of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is an esoteric Buddhist stele inscription explaining the doctrinal relationship between Vairocana, mantra-dhāraṇī, and the three collections (Tripiṭaka), from the perspective of the Mantrayāna (esoteric vehicle).*

The root of the Buddha is Bhagavān (*Bóqiéfàn*, 薄伽梵) Mahāvairocana (*Dà Pílúzhēnà*, 大毘盧遮那), upon whom all buddhas depend. The root of the dharma is the true words (*zhēnyán*, 真言) and dhāraṇī, upon which all dharmas depend.

All the tathāgatas of the dharma-realm assemblies in the dust-mote lands of the ten directions, who have fulfilled the fruit of holiness, all depend upon the pure and wondrous dharma-body of Vairocana. From this they manifest the bodies of self-enjoyment, enjoyment-for-others, and transformation. The teachings and principles they expound are all the gate of true words and dhāraṇī, which flow forth and develop into the three collections of teachings.

Since teachings follow the capacities of beings, there are the three collections of the Great Vehicle and the Small Vehicle, each realizing the precepts, concentration, and wisdom, as well as the dharmas of the aggregates, sense-bases, elements, conditions, noble truths, and sustenance. These are: *sūtra-piṭaka* (*sùdálǎn bǐzhéjiā*, 素怛覽比擿迦), called in Chinese "collection of discourses"; *vinaya-piṭaka* (*wēinàiyē bǐzhéjiā*, 微奈耶比擿迦), called in Chinese "collection of discipline"; and *abhidharma-piṭaka* (*ābǐdámó bǐzhéjiā*, 阿毘達磨比擿迦), called in Chinese "collection of higher dharma." These are the collections of discourses, discipline, and treatises, hence called the three collections (*sānzàng*, 三藏).

A single character of the true-word dhāraṇī gate completely encompasses the teaching, principle, practice, and fruit of all three collections. If one understands the dhāraṇī teaching of even a single honored one, it encompasses the three collections.

Dhāraṇī, called in Chinese "total retention" (*zǒngchí*, 總持), means: it holds all evil dharmas so that they do not arise, holds all wholesome dharmas so that they are not extinguished, crushes all defiled dharmas, and realizes the teachings that flow from the pure dharma-realm.

As for "total treasury holding" (*zǒng zàng chí*, 總藏持): within the meaning of a single character, one awakens to immeasurable hundreds of thousands of profound and wonderful meanings, and practices, expounds, and moves freely in both forward and reverse directions.

As for "samādhi total retention": by virtue of this dhāraṇī, samādhi manifests directly. One awakens to immeasurable hundreds of thousands of samādhi gates. For this reason, the bodhisattva always manifests rebirth in the six destinies without being corrupted by afflictions or secondary afflictions, realizes great spiritual powers, and accomplishes the benefit and happiness of boundless sentient beings.

As for "written-character total retention": by virtue of this dhāraṇī, within a single character, all the sūtras one has heard and recited are accomplished and never forgotten.

One should know that dhāraṇī universally holds the great meritorious dharma of the three bodies. It is the dharma-nature of the Buddha, the dharma-abiding, the dharma-realm, and the total retention of all dharmas. The tathāgatas of the three times all depend upon this gate to accomplish right awakening.

Those who delight in the practice should approach a yoga ācārya (*yúqié āshélí*, 瑜珈阿闍梨), give rise to the mind of unsurpassed great bodhi, receive the samaya precepts (*sānmóyē jiè*, 三摩耶戒), and enter the great maṇḍala (*mànnáluó*, 曼拏攞) to receive consecration (*guàndǐng*, 灌頂). Only then is one permitted to transmit, receive, and practice.

One who receives the five-wisdom consecration (*wǔzhì guàndǐng*, 五智灌頂) of Mahāvairocana and all the tathāgatas, inheriting the position of ācārya, is then permitted to transmit the teaching. Consecration means the meaning of being anointed and receiving a position. One receives the vajra name and title of the body of the Buddha's wisdom. From this point on, one receives the vast, profound, and inconceivable dharma, transcending the two vehicles and the ten stages. One is called the Great Vajrasattva (*Dà Jīngāng Sàduǒ*, 大金剛薩埵).

The ācārya inherits from the Buddha and transmits the dharma. Performing the Tathāgata's activities, practicing the precepts of the Buddha's body-secret, holding the true words of the Buddha's speech-secret, relying upon the right knowledge of the Buddha's mind-secret: these are the gate of the three secrets for delivering sentient beings, accomplishing the fruit-position of the unsurpassed three bodies.

Teaching and guiding, showing the path of refuge. Bowing in reverence to the World-Honored One, the Great Compassionate One, who opens and expounds the dharma-gate without limit. This indeed universally responds to the hearts of all beings. In broad outline, there are eighty-four thousand, all encompassed within the three collections.

For those who have not yet entered the true dharma, so that they may enter the true dharma, the sūtra-piṭaka is taught. For those who have entered the true dharma, so that they may receive, uphold, and study the training, the vinaya-piṭaka is taught. For those who have already received and upheld the training, so that they may penetrate the true nature and characteristics of all dharmas, the abhidharma-piṭaka is taught.

Since teachings follow the capacities of beings, there are the three collections of the Great and Small Vehicles, each expounding the study of precepts, concentration, and wisdom. Through faith, understanding, practice, and realization according to the teaching, one accomplishes the fruit. For the Small Vehicle: the four fruits and three awakenings. For the Great Vehicle: the pre-stage levels and the ten stages. All are taught by the Buddha. All point to the Buddha's mind.

There is also the Supreme Vehicle's three collections (*zuìshàng chéng sānzàng*, 最上乘三藏), also called the Buddha Vehicle. Through consecration, one enters and transcends to the honored positions of equality and the wonderful. Through the mutual correspondence of the three secrets and the four mudrās, one directly realizes the fruit of the three-body buddhahood.

In conclusion:

> The One Vehicle, the three collections: the root and source of the buddha-dharma.
> The Great Awakened One opens and reveals; consecration is transmitted in succession.
> Awakening enters, realization enters; the three modes of activity are clear and firm.
> Receive the precepts equal to the Buddha; ascend to the Tathāgata's meditation.
> True-word wisdom liberates; knowledge and vision are without limit.
> Clearly and distinctly, the moon is full, the pearl is round.
> Universally pervading without end; benefit and happiness follow conditions.

This teaching has one hundred thousand verses (*gāthā*, 誐他). Consecration is transmitted and received. All the tathāgatas' three-secret inner realization is the mutual empowerment of the same substance. Each practice is cultivated following the teacher's personal instruction. Giving rise to one mind is the mind of all buddhas. Cultivating one practice is the practice of all buddhas. Awakening to one dharma totally holds all dharmas. All dharmas commonly depend upon one exposition. One character gives rise to immeasurable characters; they all enter into one character.

> The substance of the character is originally empty;
> The true nature is without end.
> Holding the lands of feeling;
> The true palace of the dharma-buddha.
> Boundless holy assemblies
> Interpenetrate, layer upon layer.
> Whether gathering in or teaching forth, each marvelously rounds.
> The moon threads through; the mind-mirror illuminates and shines.
> The fruit of bodhi is fully accomplished.
> Completing and fulfilling, without abiding;
> The true eternal is never severed.
