# Record of the Transmission of the Dharma-Treasure

## *Chuánfǎ Bǎojì* (傳法寶紀)

Taishō 2838

Compiled by Dù Fěi (*Dù Fěi*, 杜朏) of the Tang dynasty. A Chan/Zen lineage history.

*Note: This is one of the earliest extant histories of the Chan lineage, discovered among the Dunhuang manuscripts. Written by the Tang literatus Dù Fěi, it traces the transmission of the dharma from Bodhidharma through Huìkě, Sēngcàn, Dàoxìn, Hóngrěn, and Fǎrú to Shénxiù. The text emphasizes mind-to-mind transmission over scriptural learning and explicitly critiques the authenticity of texts attributed to Bodhidharma. It is a key document for understanding early Chan self-understanding before the Northern/Southern school split became dominant.*

### Preface

> I bow my head to the good spiritual friend,
> Who enables us to protect the original mind.
> It is like a pearl in turbid water:
> By its power, clarity suddenly appears.
> Therefore I compose this record
> To illuminate the successive transmission of the dharma.
> May it extend to the end of the future,
> Broadly opening the buddha's knowledge and vision.

The preface says:

Our true and real dharma-body is what the dharma-buddha has attained. Apart from the verbal teachings of all transformation-buddhas, transmitted through written characters, this true-suchness gate is transmitted by the self-awakening of the realized mind. Therefore the treatise says: "All dharmas, from the very beginning, are apart from the characteristics of verbal expression, apart from the characteristics of names and words, apart from the characteristics of mental conditioning. They are ultimately equal, without any change or variation, indestructible. They are solely this one mind, and therefore are called true suchness."

It also says: "As for the mind of realization and aspiration, from the ground of the pure mind up to the ultimate: what realm is realized? It is called true suchness. Because it depends on the turning consciousness, it is spoken of as a realm. But this realization has no realm; only the wisdom of true suchness is called the dharma-body."

It is also as the sūtras say: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva, alone in a quiet place, through self-awakened contemplation, not depending on others, departing from erroneous views and false thinking, ascends progressively upward and enters the ground of the Tathāgata. This is called the holy wisdom of self-awakening."

Therefore, if one has not attained the supreme vehicle and transmitted the mind-ground, who could enter the true realm? Long ago, the Venerable Yuǎn (*Yuǎn*, 遠, Huìyuǎn) of Mount Lú, in his preface to the Chan Scripture, said: "The Buddha entrusted it to Ānanda. Ānanda transmitted it to Madhyāntika (*Mòtiándì*, 末田地). Madhyāntika transmitted it to Śāṇavāsa (*Shěnàpósī*, 舍那婆斯)." Thus we know that after the ear has received it, it does not fall to the ground but depends entirely upon the person. How profound! How could those who are attached to cause and effect, who probe and analyze sentences and meanings, ever enter this?

What the sūtras call "lineage penetration" (*zōngtōng*, 宗通) means: lineage penetration is the way attained through one's own realization, far removed from verbal expression, written characters, and false thinking, tending toward the realm of the outflow-free, the ground of self-awakening. One's own characteristics are far removed from all false and deluded perceptual characteristics. One subdues all the hosts of external-path practitioners and Māras. Tending toward one's own awakened realization, light and radiance blaze forth. This is called the characteristic of lineage penetration. This is the ground of the true ultimate; truly it is not what doctrinal explanation can enter.

Among those who set out from India and came to bring the Way to this land, there was Bodhidharma (*Pútídámó*, 菩提達摩). At the time, he transmitted to those in China who had superior wisdom. He pointed to the true realm. It was as if one who had lost his way from youth were suddenly made to turn and look within. It was also as if a great bright torch were lit in a dark room: it can be known but not expressed in words.

Subsequently, because of differences in the taste for the nature and the height of one's capacity, some playfully clung to their previous studies without seeking superior wisdom. Those who could turn around and receive the head-to-head transmission were indeed rare. Only Huìkě (*Huìkě*, 惠可) of the Eastern Wèi sought it from the Great Master at the cost of his body and life, and the Great Master transmitted it to him and departed.

Huìkě transmitted it to Sēngcàn (*Sēngcàn*, 僧璨). Sēngcàn transmitted it to Dàoxìn (*Dàoxìn*, 道信). Dàoxìn transmitted it to Hóngrěn (*Hóngrěn*, 弘忍). Hóngrěn transmitted it to Fǎrú (*Fǎrú*, 法如). Fǎrú reached Dàtōng (*Dàtōng*, 大通, i.e. Shénxiù).

From Bodhidharma onward, each master and disciple who opened the Way all skillfully used skillful means to take realization in the mind. Whatever words they happened to utter, none were attached to doctrinal explanation. Now there are texts circulating among people claiming to be treatises by Bodhidharma, but these were mostly cases where students at the time recorded words they had personally received and treated them as genuine treatises, treasuring and preserving them. Many contain errors.

As for those who transcended in enlightenment and inherited the transmission, once they attained it in the mind, there was no room for words. What use would verbal or written expression be?

Those who have not seen the ultimate should be shown the great by pointing to the small. To use a worldly analogy: if someone refines a genuine elixir and ascends to heaven in broad daylight, he must obtain it through personal, hand-to-hand transmission from an immortal. If he relies on texts written in jade script on blue paper, he will end up merely lost in confusion. This is just one conditioned thing in the world, and yet it necessarily works this way. How much more so the unsurpassed true lineage: how could it depend on verbal teaching?

Therefore this Way is subtle and secret, and rarely does one find its gate. Although the dharma does not depend on the person, and one should rely on meaning rather than on words, a true good spiritual friend is not to be lightly dismissed. Now, since these ultimate persons have left no traces to guide future generations, and some may lose their way as evening falls, I therefore compose this brief record. From Bodhidharma onward, those who inherited the transmission of the dharma are set down in order as the Record of the Transmission of the Dharma-Treasure in one fascicle.

I shall merely note the known names and traces, the places where each taught, and what the eyes, ears, and written records can attest. Since this is merged and simplified, the biographies themselves are brief. As for the realization of enlightenment and the holy way, there is no means to capture even a gourd's measure. There are portrait drawings to be made as a later record.

Nevertheless, those who inherited this Way were tranquil as the dharma-realm, truly empty and quiescent, their traces naturally dissolving. During their lifetimes they did not display anything extraordinary. No miraculous signs were heard of, and for this reason I omit such things. It is like turning back one's sleeve to wipe one's face: the light moistens, but nothing is grasped.

Furthermore, from Bodhidharma onward through the Suí and Tang, there were others of lofty awakening, profound realization, and perfect suddenness. Such persons have surely existed in every age. But since they were not part of the transmitted succession, they are listed in separate appended biographies, to illuminate the many masters of this dharma gate.

### The Lineage

Śramaṇa Bodhidharma of Shǎolín Monastery (*Shǎolín Sì*, 少林寺), Mount Sōng (*Sōngshān*, 嵩山), Eastern Wèi.

Śramaṇa Huìkě of Shǎolín Monastery, Mount Sōng, Northern Qí.

Śramaṇa Sēngcàn of Wǎngōng Mountain (*Wǎngōng Shān*, 𡷗公山), Suí dynasty.

Śramaṇa Dàoxìn of Dōngshān Monastery (*Dōngshān Sì*, 東山寺), Twin Peaks Mountain (*Shuāngfēng Shān*, 雙峯山), Tang dynasty.

Śramaṇa Hóngrěn of Dōngshān Monastery, Twin Peaks Mountain, Tang dynasty.

Śramaṇa Fǎrú of Shǎolín Monastery, Mount Sōng, Tang dynasty.

Śramaṇa Shénxiù (*Shénxiù*, 神秀) of Yùquán Monastery (*Yùquán Sì*, 玉泉寺), Dāngyáng (*Dāngyáng*, 當陽), Tang dynasty.

### Biography of Bodhidharma

Bodhidharma, of the great Brahmin caste, the third son of a king of South India. His spiritual capacity was transcendent and lucid. He transmitted the holy wisdom of self-enlightenment and broadly opened the buddha's knowledge and vision for humans and devas. For the sake of people in our land of China, he crossed the sea by ship and traversed high mountains. At the time, few recognized him. Only Dào Yù (*Dào Yù*, 道昱) and Huìkě, whose minds had long been in hidden accord, devoted themselves earnestly to seeking him.

The master, over six years, tested their will until it reached penetrating awakening. The Great Master then spoke to them at ease, saying: "Can you, for the sake of the dharma, abandon your body and life?" Huìkě severed his own arm to prove his earnest sincerity. From this point onward, the master first began to secretly open and guide them through skillful means. All such opening and guiding was between master and disciple.
