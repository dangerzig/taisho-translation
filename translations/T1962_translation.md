# 五方便念佛門
## Five Expedient Gates of Buddha-Mindfulness

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1962

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, compiled by Zhiyi (智顗).

---

The first is concentrating-mind dhyāna; the second is controlling-mind dhyāna; the third is embodying-truth dhyāna; the fourth is expedient-following-conditions dhyāna; the fifth is stilling-both-extremes-and-discrimination dhyāna.

In general, to settle the mind upon a single object is called "concentrating the mind." For example, when a practitioner, at the time of buddha-mindfulness, contemplates with careful attention the Tathāgata's jewel-tuft and golden marks, and abides in concentrated stillness, lucidly clear and thoroughly penetrating: this is called concentrating-mind dhyāna.

Next, although one has previously concentrated the mind, habitual tendencies cause it to race and scatter. One should then restrain it, causing it to return to careful contemplation of the golden marks. This is called controlling-mind dhyāna.

Furthermore, although one has previously restrained the mind and achieved stability in a concentrated state, since this is not a contemplation of principle but entirely belongs to cultivation of phenomena, one now embodies original emptiness. Who is there to restrain? There is no buddha and no mindfulness. This is called embodying-truth dhyāna.

Furthermore, although one previously claimed to embody truth, one still clings to empty quiescence, and immeasurable names and characteristics remain obscured and unknown. Now, taking non-attainment as one's expedient means, one enters from emptiness into the conventional. The myriad characteristics are thoroughly illuminated, and one is not confused or disturbed by the dust of emptiness. This is called expedient dhyāna.

Furthermore, embodying truth and expedient means each relies on emptiness or existence and does not depart from the two extremes. Now one contemplates with careful attention that stillness and disturbance are originally without characteristics, that the path of language is cut off, and that conceptual thought also ceases. This is called stilling-both-extremes dhyāna.

Proceeding from shallow to deep, one discriminates in this way. Yet fundamentally, in the perfect contemplation there is neither shallow nor deep, while shallow and deep are clearly apparent.

### Outline of the Five Gates of Buddha-Mindfulness

Gate One: The Samādhi Gate of Calling the Name for Rebirth through Buddha-Mindfulness. Gate Two: The Samādhi Gate of Contemplating Marks to Eliminate Transgressions through Buddha-Mindfulness. Gate Three: The Samādhi Gate of All Realms Being Mind-Only through Buddha-Mindfulness. Gate Four: The Samādhi Gate of Mind and Objects Both Abandoned through Buddha-Mindfulness. Gate Five: The Samādhi Gate of Nature-Arising Perfect Penetration through Buddha-Mindfulness.

All buddhas, with great compassion, constantly teach and instruct by expounding the Dharma. All bodhisattvas, through various expedient means, cause sentient beings to easily understand; moreover, they themselves, through various expedient means, reveal and demonstrate prajñā-pāramitā. For what reason is this prajñā-pāramitā taught? The Buddha said: "Because we wish to cause all bodhisattvas to increase their buddha-mindfulness samādhi." For what reason are they caused to be mindful of the buddhas? The Buddha said: "If one is mindful of the Buddha, one should know that this person is equal to Mañjuśrī, without any difference." Why is this? Because this samādhi is the sport of all buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, and is the place from which the Śūraṅgama and all other great samādhis first arise. Thus it is known that for entering all buddhas' Dharma, expedient means are truly many, but if in a single phrase one were to encompass all gates, nothing surpasses buddha-mindfulness.

Why is this? All worthies and sages are born from buddha-mindfulness, and all wisdom arises from buddha-mindfulness. For example, the bodhisattvas of the ten levels of faith and the three levels of worthiness all do not depart from mindfulness of the Buddha, mindfulness of the Dharma, and mindfulness of the Saṃgha, and even do not depart from mindfulness of all-knowledge. The bodhisattvas of the first ground through the eighth, ninth, and tenth grounds also do not depart from mindfulness of the Buddha, mindfulness of the Dharma, and mindfulness of the Saṃgha, and even do not depart from mindfulness of all-knowledge. It is only because the mindfulness and understanding differ that, following the meaning, different names are established. Sentient beings are confused by names and mistakenly generate divergent interpretations, drifting and falling into the realm of māras. They then slight the name "buddha-mindfulness," claiming they have cultivated other, superior practices. Out of compassion for them, I now speak briefly.

If one discusses those who cultivate causes, they do not depart from the three levels of worthiness and ten stages of sagehood. If one discusses those who have realized the fruit, they are the buddhas, the tathāgatas. Those who cultivate causes fix their minds in constant contemplation of all buddhas of the ten directions. Those who have realized the fruit also especially inaugurate this as the most subtle, pure, and foremost dhyāna. Thus it is known that for easy entry leading to profound realization, nothing surpasses buddha-mindfulness. Alas! Those of later generations slight its easy entry and lose its profound realization. Bound by their views, they are truly pitiable!

Moreover, for sentient beings who delight in calling the names of the buddhas and wish to be born in their lands, the buddhas reveal the gate of calling the name for rebirth. For sentient beings who delight in beholding the buddhas' forms yet fear that obstructions will prevent them from seeing, they reveal the gate of contemplating marks to eliminate transgressions. For sentient beings who are confused in mind and grasp at external objects, they reveal the gate of all realms being mind-only. For sentient beings who hold things to be truly existent, they reveal the gate of mind and objects both abandoned. For sentient beings who delight in deep, still concentration and aspire to the unborn and unceasing, they reveal the gate of nature-arising perfect penetration.

How great is the World-Honored One, who skillfully delivers us all! He has revealed one path leading directly to bodhi: the supremely profound Dharma-gate called buddha-mindfulness. Only those of great wisdom, those who are born knowing, those who deeply cultivate concentration and wisdom, and those well-versed in the sūtras and treatises are invited to examine this gate alongside the definitive-meaning Mahāyāna sūtras. If there is any meaning not fully encompassed, please try to point it out. Thus it is known that calling a single buddha-name is as deep as one's wisdom is deep; that a single phrase encompasses all gates is truly well confirmed.

### Sequence of Entry into the Five Gates

Having completed the outline of the five gates' purpose, I now further describe the sequential expedient means of entry. Suppose a practitioner, at the time of calling "Homage to Amitābha Buddha," has a mind that necessarily aspires to be born in that land. This is the gate of calling the name for rebirth. The practitioner visualizes the Buddha's body, concentrating without cease, and thereupon sees the Buddha, whose radiant light blazes forth and illuminates the practitioner. At that time, all karmic obstructions are entirely eliminated. This is the gate of contemplating marks to eliminate transgressions. One further contemplates that this Buddha arises from one's own mind and there is no separate external realm. This is the gate of all realms being mind-only. One further contemplates that this mind also has no self-nature that can be apprehended. This is the gate of mind and objects both abandoned. The practitioner then enters deep, still concentration, releasing all mind, mentation, and consciousness, and is about to enter nirvāṇa. The buddhas of the ten directions bestow their empowerment and protective mindfulness, arousing the gate of wisdom. At that time, the practitioner, in a single thought-moment, purifies buddha-lands and brings sentient beings to maturity. How is it that all the merit of the previous four gates, even in a hundred thousand ten-thousandfold, does not equal one part of this? Why? Because at the stage of effortless activity, one can make a single body into immeasurable bodies, cultivating spontaneously, because the buddhas observe and protect, because the source of all buddhas' Dharma is fathomed to the very bottom, because the vow-causes of Samantabhadra are all perfectly fulfilled, because of the power of original vows, and because the Dharma is thus. This is the gate of nature-arising perfect penetration. The above five gates are complete.

### Questions and Answers

Question: "What is the one-practice samādhi?"

Answer: "The *Mahāratnakūṭa Sūtra*, fascicle one hundred sixteen, states: 'Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how should one cultivate practice to swiftly attain anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi?" The Buddha said: "The one-practice samādhi. Good men and good women who cultivate this samādhi will swiftly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi." Mañjuśrī said: "What is called the one-practice samādhi?" The Buddha said: "The single characteristic of the dharmadhātu: to fix one's contemplation upon the dharmadhātu is called the one-practice samādhi. If good men and good women wish to enter the one-practice samādhi, they should first hear the prajñā-pāramitā and cultivate as taught; only then can they enter the one-practice samādhi. Contemplating as the dharmadhātu contemplates, it is non-retrogressing, indestructible, inconceivable, unobstructed, and without characteristics. Good men and good women who wish to enter the one-practice samādhi should dwell in an empty, quiet place, abandon all distracting thoughts, not grasp at appearances, fix their mind on a single buddha, and exclusively call that buddha's name. Facing the direction of that buddha, with body upright and correctly oriented, they should be able to maintain thought after thought in continuous succession upon that single buddha. Within this very mindfulness, they will be able to see the buddhas of the three times. Why? The merit of being mindful of a single buddha is immeasurable and boundless, equal to the merit of immeasurable buddhas and their immeasurable eloquence. One who thus enters the one-practice samādhi will fully know the dharmadhātu of all buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, without any differentiated characteristics."' This passage is excellent proof."

Question: "Many people paint images for the purpose of contemplation and worship. What scriptural authority is there for this?"

Answer: "The *Mahāratnakūṭa Sūtra*, fascicle eighty-nine, states: 'At that time, the bodhisattva Great Vigor, holding a cloth with a painted image of the Buddha, entered into deep mountains, a place of solitude without people, among birds and beasts. He unfurled and displayed the painted image, gathered grass for a seat, and before the painted image sat cross-legged, with body upright and mind focused, contemplating the Tathāgata. Having contemplated carefully, he had this thought: "The Tathāgata is rare and wondrous. Even a painted image is like this, seated in subtle wonder. How much more so the body of the Tathāgata, the One of Right and Universal Knowledge!" He further thought: "How should one contemplate the Buddha?" At that time, a forest spirit, knowing the bodhisattva's thought, addressed the bodhisattva: "Good man! You think: 'How should one contemplate the Buddha?' The answer: 'If you wish to contemplate the Buddha, contemplate this painted image. Contemplating this painted image is no different from the Tathāgata. This is called contemplating the Buddha. To contemplate the Buddha thus, to contemplate thus: this is skillful contemplation.'" Then Great Vigor had this thought: "How can I contemplate this painted image as equal to the Tathāgata?" He further thought: "This painted image is without awareness and without knowledge. All dharmas are also like this, merely having names. Such names are by nature empty and quiescent, originally without any existence. The body of the Tathāgata is characterized in the same way. A painted image is without realization and without fruit, without one who realizes and without one who attains, without one who abides and without coming or going, without birth and without extinction, without purity and without form, without greed, anger, or delusion. A painted image is without aggregates, elements, or sense-fields, without beginning, middle, or end. All dharmas are also like this. The physical characteristics of the Tathāgata are also like this. Like this painted image, which is without awareness, without knowledge, and without action, all buddhas are also like this. Contemplating the physical characteristics of the Tathāgata is also like this, and even the six sense faculties are also like this." The bodhisattva, contemplating the Tathāgata's body in this way, sat cross-legged. Within three times seven days, he accomplished the five supernatural powers and made offerings to the buddhas. Devas scattered flowers as offerings, and he was praised by the Buddha. In the assembly, twenty thousand people abided in anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, and immeasurable asaṃkhyeya numbers of people abided in the merits of the Two Vehicles. The bodhisattva Great Vigor was Śākyamuni Buddha.' This passage makes it clear."

### Analysis through the Four Teachings

The mind does not arise in isolation but necessarily relies upon conditions to arise. When a practitioner is mindful of the Buddha, mental conceptualization serves as the cause, and the Tathāgata's tuft-light serves as the condition, also called a dharma-object because it corresponds to the mental faculty. The thought that arises is the dharma produced. Contemplating this faculty and its object, the capacity and what it engages, and how the three characteristics of the conditioned shift and change, arising and ceasing in each new instant, not abiding for a single thought-moment, one analyzes them until they are found to be empty. There is no buddha and no mindfulness. This is the Piṭaka Teaching of the Lesser Vehicle.

Directly contemplating the buddha-mindfulness mind, one sees that producer and produced are all immediately empty. To falsely claim the mind arises: in reality the mind does not arise. Arising has no self-nature; embodying it, it is immediately empty. The contemplated buddha-marks are like images in a mirror or flowers in empty space. There is no buddha and no mindfulness. This is the Shared Teaching of the Great Vehicle.

Directly contemplating the buddha-mindfulness mind, one sees that arising is immediately a dharma of conventional designation. Shallow and deep are thoroughly discerned; immeasurable names and characteristics are as if seen in the palm of one's hand. One clearly knows that this mind contains the tathāgata-garbha. Through kalpas of cutting off delusion, one finally realizes the true and permanent. Transcending the extremes, the middle is revealed. There is no buddha and no mindfulness. This is the Distinct Teaching of the Great Vehicle.

Directly contemplating the buddha-mindfulness mind, one sees that arising is immediately empty, immediately conventional, immediately the middle. Whether faculty or object, all arise as the dharmadhātu. A single thought is also thus. The buddhas of dust-mote worlds are illuminated in a single thought; the sentient beings of the six destinies are universally responded to in an instant. The beginning is the end; only now does one first awaken to this. Like a greatly fortunate person who transforms stone into jewels, one certainly does not abandon mindfulness to separately seek freedom from mindfulness. The extremes are themselves the middle. There is no buddha and no mindfulness. This is the Perfect Teaching of the Great Vehicle. The *Yingluo Sūtra* clarifies sudden awakening to the Tathāgata; this is what it means.
