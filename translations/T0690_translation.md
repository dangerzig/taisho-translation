# 佛說希有挍量功德經
## Sūtra of the Rare Comparison of Merit, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0690

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, translated by Jñānagupta (闍那崛多)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada.

At that time, the Elder Ānanda was sitting alone in a secluded place, contemplating, and had this thought: "If good men and good women were to take and uphold the Three Refuges, saying: 'I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha,' how much merit would they obtain?"

Having had this thought, the Elder Ānanda, at sunrise, rose from his seat in his dwelling, arranged his robes properly, and went calmly forth to the Buddha's place. Arriving, he bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and said: "World-Honored One! Earlier, in a quiet place, I was contemplating thus: 'If good men and good women were to say: "I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha," how much merit would they obtain?' I truly do not understand. I humbly wish the Tathāgata to explain this in detail, so that sentient beings may obtain right knowledge and right views."

At that time the World-Honored One told Ānanda: "Excellent! Excellent! Good man of true and subtle wisdom, you are able to think well upon this and ask the Tathāgata about such a meaning. As you have considered: if good men and good women are able, with a pure heart, to say 'I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha,' how much merit would they obtain? Listen carefully! Listen carefully! Reflect well upon it! I shall explain it for you in detail."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "So be it, World-Honored One! I wish to hear with joy."

At that time the World-Honored One told Ānanda: "This land of Jambudvīpa is seven thousand yojanas wide, shaped like a carriage, narrow in the south and broad in the north. Suppose it were filled with stream-enterers, like bamboo, reeds, sugarcane, rice, and hemp. If a good man or good woman were for a full hundred years to take all the worldly pleasures and give them in support, and further make offerings of the four necessities with full reverence, and after their parinirvāṇa were to collect the relics and build stūpas of the seven treasures, and at each stūpa make offerings of fragrant flowers, oil lamps, music, silken banners, and jeweled canopies, with every kind of offering: what do you think? Would such a good man or good woman obtain much merit or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very much, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! It would not be as great as the merit obtained by a good man or good woman who, with a pure heart, says: 'I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha.' Compared to that merit, the former does not reach a hundredth, nor a thousandth, nor a hundred-thousand-ten-thousandth, nor can any calculation or analogy reach it."

At that time the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside Jambudvīpa. Suppose western Godānīya, eight thousand yojanas wide, its land shaped like a half-moon, were entirely filled with once-returners, like bamboo, reeds, sugarcane, rice, and hemp. If a good man or good woman were for a full two hundred years to make offerings of the worldly four necessities and all else as before, and after their parinirvāṇa were to collect relics and build stūpas of the seven treasures, and at each stūpa make offerings of flowers, incense, lamps, music, silken canopies, banners, and pennants, with every kind of offering: what do you think? Would such a good man or good woman obtain much merit or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very much, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! It would not be as great as the merit obtained by a good man or good woman who, with a pure heart, says: 'I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha.' Compared to that merit, the former does not reach a hundredth, nor a thousandth, nor a billionth, nor a hundred-thousand-billionth, nor can any calculation or analogy reach it."

At that time the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside Jambudvīpa and Godānīya. Suppose eastern Pūrvavideha, nine thousand yojanas wide, its land shaped like a full moon, were entirely filled with non-returners, like bamboo, reeds, sugarcane, rice, and hemp. If a good man or good woman were for a full three hundred years to make offerings of the four necessities and all pleasures as before, and after their parinirvāṇa were to collect relics and build stūpas of the seven treasures, and at each stūpa make offerings of flowers, incense, music, banners, and canopies, with every kind of offering: what do you think? Would such a good man or good woman obtain much merit or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very much, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! It would not be as great as the merit obtained by a good man or good woman who, with a pure heart, says: 'I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha.' Compared to that merit, the former does not reach a hundredth, nor a thousandth, nor a hundred-thousandth, nor a billionth, nor a hundred-billionth, nor a hundred-thousand-billionth, nor can any calculation or analogy reach it."

At that time the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside Jambudvīpa, Godānīya, and Pūrvavideha. Suppose northern Uttarakuru, ten thousand yojanas wide, its land square and straight, evenly proportioned on all sides, were filled with arhats whose outflows were all exhausted, free from all afflictions, whose minds had attained mastery, who possessed right liberation and the wisdom-knowledge of liberation, whose minds were tamed, great dragons among humans, who had accomplished what was to be done, cast off the heavy burden, realized their own benefit, were subject to no further existence, had established the pure conduct, with bodies and minds unobstructed, who penetrated the minds of sentient beings, had obtained the six supernatural powers and the eight liberations, like bamboo, reeds, sugarcane, rice, and hemp. If a good man or good woman were for a full four hundred years to make offerings of the four necessities with full reverence, providing all pleasures as before, and after their parinirvāṇa were to build stūpas of the seven treasures, and at each stūpa make offerings of flowers, incense, music, lamps, silken canopies, banners, and pennants, with every kind of offering: what do you think? Would such a good man or good woman obtain much merit or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very much, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! It would not be as great as the merit obtained by a good man or good woman who, with a pure heart, says: 'I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha.' Compared to that merit, the former does not reach a hundredth, nor a thousandth, nor a hundred-thousandth, nor a hundred-thousand-billionth, nor can any calculation or analogy reach it."

At that time the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside Jambudvīpa, Godānīya, Pūrvavideha, and Uttarakuru. Suppose the entire four continents were filled with solitary-awakened pratyekabuddhas, like bamboo, reeds, sugarcane, rice, and hemp. If a good man or good woman were for a full ten thousand years to make offerings of the four necessities with full reverence, providing all pleasures as before, and after their parinirvāṇa were to collect relics and build stūpas of the seven treasures, and at each stūpa make offerings of flowers, incense, music, lamps, silken canopies, banners, and pennants, with every kind of offering: what do you think? Would such a good man or good woman obtain much merit or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very much, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! It would not be as great as the merit obtained by a good man or good woman who, with a pure heart, says: 'I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha.' Compared to that merit, the former does not reach a hundredth, nor a thousandth, nor a hundred-thousandth, nor a hundred-thousand-billionth, nor can any calculation or analogy reach it."

At that time the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "Set aside the four continents, the region illuminated by a single sun and moon. Suppose there were a thousand-world system: a thousand suns and moons, a thousand great oceans, a thousand Mount Sumerus, a thousand Jambudvīpas, a thousand Godānīyas, a thousand Pūrvavidehas, a thousand Uttarakurus, a thousand heavens of the Four Kings, a thousand heavens of the Thirty-Three, a thousand Yāma heavens, a thousand Tuṣita heavens, a thousand Nirmāṇarati heavens, a thousand Paranirmitavaśavartin heavens, and a thousand Brahmā heavens, each unconnected, each with great sovereignty within its thousand-world domain. Ānanda! This is called a small thousand-world system. Ānanda! From one small thousand-world system, counting one by one until a full thousand, this is called a middle thousand-world system. Ānanda! From a middle thousand-world system, counting one by one until again a full thousand, this is called a great thousand-world system. Ānanda! Together these are called the three-thousand great-thousand world system. Suppose this three-thousand great-thousand world system were filled with Buddha Tathāgatas, like bamboo, reeds, sugarcane, rice, and hemp: all of them Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, Truly Perfected, Equally and Fully Awakened. If a good man or good woman were for twenty thousand years to constantly offer all pleasures, robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine, with every kind of offering; and after their parinirvāṇa were to collect relics and build stūpas of the seven treasures, and at each stūpa make offerings of flowers, incense, music, silken canopies, banners, pennants, fragrant lamps, and oil lamps: what do you think? Would such a good man or good woman obtain much merit or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very much, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Even if someone were to make offerings to just one Buddha, a World-Honored One, for twenty thousand years, providing the four necessities with full reverence, and after that Buddha's parinirvāṇa were to collect relics and build a stūpa of the seven treasures, and make offerings of flowers, incense, music, banners, canopies, and fragrant oil lamps, with every kind of offering: that merit would already be immeasurable and boundless, beyond all reckoning. How much more so the merit of filling the three-thousand great-thousand world system with Buddha Tathāgatas and for twenty thousand years constantly making offerings of the four necessities, and after their parinirvāṇa collecting relics and building stūpas of the seven treasures, each with flowers, incense, music, banners, canopies, and fragrant oil lamps, providing every kind of offering! Such merit would truly be immeasurable, boundless, incalculable, and innumerable in its accumulation."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! Yet it would still not be as great as the merit obtained by a good man or good woman who, with a pure heart, says: 'I now take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṃgha.' The merit of the Three Refuges surpasses the former a hundredfold, a thousandfold, ten-thousandfold, beyond all calculation, words, or analogy."

At that time the World-Honored One further told Ānanda: "If someone takes refuge in the Buddha, takes refuge in the Dharma, and takes refuge in the Saṃgha, and then, even for the span of a finger-snap, receives the ten wholesome precepts and, having received them, practices accordingly: through this cause and condition, one obtains immeasurable and boundless merit.

"Ānanda! If furthermore someone can, for one day and one night, receive and observe the eight precepts of the uposatha and practice them as taught: the merit obtained surpasses the former a thousandfold, ten-thousandfold, a hundred-thousand-ten-thousandfold, beyond all calculation or analogy.

"Ānanda! If someone can receive and uphold the five precepts for the span of a lifetime and practice them as taught: the merit obtained surpasses the former a hundredfold, a thousandfold, ten-thousandfold, a thousand-billion-ten-thousandfold, beyond all calculation or analogy.

"Ānanda! If furthermore someone receives the precepts of a śrāmaṇera or śrāmaṇerikā: this again surpasses the former a hundredfold, a thousandfold, ten-thousandfold, a thousand-billion-ten-thousandfold, beyond all calculation or analogy.

"Ānanda! If furthermore someone can receive and uphold the precepts of a śikṣamāṇā: the merit obtained again surpasses the former a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a hundred-thousand-ten-thousandfold, beyond all calculation or analogy. How much more so for one who receives and upholds the bhikṣuṇī precepts and practices according to the prātimokṣa as taught! And how much more for one who for the span of a lifetime receives and upholds the full bhikṣu precepts, practicing according to the prātimokṣa as taught, without deficiency or violation, unstained and pure in the holy life! Such a one obtains immeasurable and boundless merit, surpassing the former a hundredfold, a thousandfold, ten-thousandfold, a hundred-thousand-ten-thousandfold, beyond all calculation or analogy."

At that time, the Elder Ānanda, hearing the Buddha teach about the Three Refuges and the immeasurable merit of upholding the bhikṣu prātimokṣa for the span of a lifetime, exclaimed that this was unprecedented, and said to the Buddha: "How wondrous, World-Honored One! This sūtra is subtle, wondrous, and inconceivable, illuminating a profoundly deep meaning, with merit so vast it is difficult to compare. What should this sūtra be called? How should we uphold it?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Rare, the Rare.' You should uphold it thus. Why? Because this sūtra teaches the rare dharma. The supreme dharma it expounds is difficult to hear in all the world; therefore it is called 'The Rare.'"

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the Elder Ānanda, hearing what the Buddha said, rejoiced and practiced it.
