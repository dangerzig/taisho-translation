## Preface by the Great Ming Taizu, the Exalted Emperor

*[This preface was composed by the founding emperor of the Ming dynasty, Zhu Yuanzhang. It discusses the relationship between Buddhist teaching and governance, arguing that the Heart Sūtra's teaching on emptiness refers to the emptiness of false characteristics rather than true nature, and warns against the misunderstanding of emptiness that led several emperors to ruin.]*

## Preface to the Heart Sūtra of Prajñāpāramitā

The dharma-nature is boundless: how could it be fathomed by the mind? True suchness is without characteristics: how could it be expressed in words? Therefore, sentient beings are vast and without end, and the ocean of Dharma is boundless, without limit. If one broadly searches through texts and meanings, it is like seeking a form inside a mirror. If one further stills the mind and contemplates emptiness, it is like trying to flee one's shadow in the sunlight. This sūtra is like the great earth: what thing does not arise from the earth? All Buddhas point only to the one mind: what dharma is not established through the mind? Simply to understand the ground of mind is therefore called "complete retention." To awaken to the unborn nature of dharmas is called "wondrous awakening." In a single thought one transcends: what need is there for elaborate discourse?

---

When the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara was practicing the deep prajñāpāramitā, he illuminated the five aggregates and saw that they are all empty, and he crossed beyond all suffering and adversity.

"Śāriputra! Form is not different from emptiness; emptiness is not different from form. Form is itself emptiness; emptiness is itself form. Feeling, perception, volitional formations, and consciousness are likewise.

"Śāriputra! All these dharmas have the characteristic of emptiness: they are not produced and not destroyed, not defiled and not pure, not increased and not decreased. Therefore, in emptiness there is no form, no feeling, perception, volitional formations, or consciousness; no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind; no form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or mental object; no eye-element, and so on up to no mind-consciousness element; no ignorance and no extinction of ignorance, and so on up to no old age and death and no extinction of old age and death; no suffering, origination, cessation, or path; no wisdom and no attainment.

"Because there is nothing to attain, the bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind without obstruction. Because there is no obstruction, there is no fear. Far removed from inverted dream-thinking, one reaches ultimate nirvāṇa. All Buddhas of the three periods of time, relying on prajñāpāramitā, attain the supreme, perfect enlightenment.

"Therefore know that prajñāpāramitā is the great divine mantra, the great luminous mantra, the unsurpassed mantra, the mantra equal to the unequaled, which can remove all suffering, true and not false. Therefore the mantra of prajñāpāramitā is spoken."

The mantra is then spoken:

*Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā*
