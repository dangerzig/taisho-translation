# 弊魔試目連經
## Sūtra on the Evil Māra Testing Maudgalyāyana

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0067

Translated from the Chinese. Wu kingdom, translated by Zhi Qian

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the kingdom of Vṛji, in the Deer Park at the Fearful Assembly in the Wonderful Flower Mountain. At that time, the Worthy Mahāmaudgalyāyana, in the darkness of night, was doing walking meditation, going back and forth along a level path. At that time, the evil Māra went to where the Buddha was, transformed his body into a transparent shadow, and entered Maudgalyāyana's belly. The Worthy Mahāmaudgalyāyana thought: "Why does my belly rumble like thunder, as if I were a hungry person carrying a heavy load? I should enter my chamber and attain samādhi, to observe its source." Then Maudgalyāyana entered his chamber and in samādhi observed his body. He immediately saw the evil Māra, who had transformed into a transparent shadow and entered his belly. He addressed him: "Evil Māra! Get out, get out! Do not harass the Tathāgata or his disciples, lest through the long night you obtain suffering and unrest and fall into evil destinies."

Māra thought to himself: "This śramaṇa has never met me, nor does he know me, yet he fabricates these false words: 'Evil Māra! Get out, get out! Do not harass the Tathāgata or his disciples, lest through the long night you obtain suffering and unrest.' Even his teacher, the great sage the World-Honored One, does not know me. How much less could his disciple?"

Maudgalyāyana replied: "I also know, I also know what you are now thinking in your mind: 'Even his teacher the great sage does not know me. How much less could his disciple know where I am?'" Māra was immediately frightened: "This śramaṇa has already detected me!" He then transformed his transparent body, exited, and stood before Maudgalyāyana.

Maudgalyāyana told Māra: "Long ago in the distant past, at the time of the Buddha Krakucchanda, I was once a Māra, called Hatred. I had an elder sister named Dark. At that time, you were her son. By this you should know that you are my nephew. At that time, there was a Buddha who appeared in the world, named Krakucchanda Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha. He had two disciples: one named Great Voice, the second named Knower of Perception, the most honored and foremost, whose goodness and virtue were difficult to match. Why was the worthy one named Great Voice? Dwelling in the Brahmā heaven, he would clear his throat and raise his great voice, which was heard throughout the three-thousand great-thousand world system. Why was the worthy one named Knower of Perception? If he was in a secluded place, sitting beneath a tree, in the wilderness or mountains, he would enter samādhi in accordance with his form. Shepherds, cowherds, woodcutters, grass-carriers, farmers, and travelers, seeing him in this state, would say to one another: 'This one has died here. Let us each gather firewood and grass and cremate him together.' As they said, so they did, and they cremated him together. The bhikṣu Knower of Perception then emerged from samādhi, shook out his robes to remove the dust and ash, readjusted his Dharma robes, took his bowl, and entered the cities, towns, and villages to beg for food. The cowherds, shepherds, woodcutters, and grass-carriers were startled and amazed, and said to one another: 'In the wilderness and in that secluded place, I saw this bhikṣu sitting beneath a tree without breathing, and thought he had died. We piled firewood and grass and cremated him.' Now Knower of Perception: it was for this reason that he was called 'Knower of Perception.'

"Then Hatred, the Māra, thought to himself: 'These śramaṇas claim to keep the precepts, sitting silently in stillness, contemplating as they practice. They are like dogs and cats plotting to catch mice, sitting still and unmoving, catching them the moment they emerge. These meditating śramaṇas are just the same. They are like herons wanting to catch fish, silently and stealthily waiting, swallowing the fish the moment it appears. These śramaṇas are just the same, silently contemplating, single-mindedly seeking something. They are like great donkeys bearing heavy loads by day, exhausted and hungry by nightfall, silently craving food and drink. These śramaṇas are just the same.'

"Then Māra thought: 'I should transform the elders and brāhmaṇas of this land. Let them seize those precept-keeping śramaṇas and practitioners, beat them, abuse them, tear their robes, smash their bowls, and break their heads, causing anger to arise in them. Through that condition I will find my opportunity.' Following his thought, he immediately transformed the elders and brāhmaṇas of the land. They seized those śramaṇas who kept the precepts and upheld the Dharma, beat them, abused them, smashed their bowls, broke their heads, and tore their garments. Those śramaṇas, like cats catching mice, like herons swallowing fish, like owls catching mice among trees, those meditating śramaṇas were just the same, like hungry, weary donkeys. Then the bhikṣus, all having suffered insult and injury, walked with bowed heads to where the Buddha Krakucchanda was. The Buddha was broadly expounding the sūtra path to the four assemblies, devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits. Seeing the bhikṣus come having suffered insult and injury, he told the bhikṣus: 'Bhikṣus! Now Hatred Māra has transformed the elders and brāhmaṇas of the land, causing them to seize those precept-keeping, Dharma-upholding śramaṇas, beat them, abuse them, break their heads, smash their bowls, and tear their garments, causing their minds to turn to resentment and give rise to anger. Through that condition he finds an opportunity so that the Way is not accomplished. You should practice the four immeasurables: loving-kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity. Harbor no resentment or grudges, no angry or hateful mind. Vast and immeasurable, universally peaceful and boundless, equally throughout the ten directions. Though Māra seeks your weak point, he will never find it.' The bhikṣus received the teaching and, in secluded places in the wilderness, with single mind practiced meditation, cultivating the four immeasurable minds without increase or decrease. Then Hatred Māra, though he sought the weak point of those precept-keeping, Dharma-upholding śramaṇas, could never find it.

"At that time, those elders and brāhmaṇas who had received Māra's instruction and insulted the precept-keeping, Dharma-upholding śramaṇas, when their lives ended, all fell into evil destinies, places of painful toil, emaciation, and torment. In hell they received transformation bodies like great trees, as vast as a great wilderness, on a ground of burning iron. Naked they threw themselves down, each saying: 'Our fortune was meager, our calamity fierce and evil. We seized precept-keeping, Dharma-upholding śramaṇas and insulted and abused them. Here we cry out for refuge, unable to see those precept-keeping, Dharma-upholding śramaṇas. Seeking their weak point and an opportunity to meet them, we ourselves have created this and reaped its calamity, following Māra's instruction and failing to guard ourselves.'

"At that time, Hatred Māra thought to himself: 'By this method, seeking the weak point of those precept-keeping śramaṇas, I can never find it. I must change my approach. I shall transform the elders and brāhmaṇas to make offerings and serve the precept-keeping śramaṇas with robes, food, bedding, and medicine, causing them to crave offerings. Through that condition I will find my opportunity.' Following his plan, he immediately transformed the elders and brāhmaṇas of the land. Wherever they were, on the roads, at the crossroads, or in the lanes, if they saw precept-keeping śramaṇas and practitioners, they would spread their hair on the ground, inviting them to walk upon it, all saying: 'Precept-keeping śramaṇas, who cultivate and practice diligently, are rare and difficult to encounter. Please tread upon our hair, that through the long night we may gain immeasurable merit.' Holding garments in their hands, they would go to the śramaṇas, bow their heads and kneel: 'Please take pity and accept these robes.' With baskets filled with food, they would go to the monasteries, or at the heads of streets and lanes, making offerings: 'Precept-keeping śramaṇas are rare and difficult to encounter. Please accept these offerings, that through the long night we may gain immeasurable merit.' They would embrace them, lift them up, carry them on their backs or convey them in carriages, bringing them to their homes. Seating them on fine beds, they would bring out food, robes, kāṣāya garments, gold, silver, and the seven treasures, placing them before the śramaṇas, kneeling and saying: 'Precept-keeping śramaṇas are rare and difficult to see. Please accept these offerings. We beg you to take pity, and take whatever you desire, that through the long night we may gain immeasurable merit.'

"Then the Buddha Krakucchanda, before the four assemblies, devas, nāgas, and spirits, saw the precept-keeping śramaṇas and practitioners being honored and served with immeasurable offerings by the elders and brāhmaṇas. He told the bhikṣus: 'Now Hatred Māra has transformed the elders and brāhmaṇas, causing them to make offerings to the precept-keeping śramaṇas and practitioners with robes, food, bedding, and medicine, whatever they desire, causing them to crave offerings. Through that condition he would find an opportunity, corrupt their good minds, and prevent the Way from being accomplished. You who dwell in secluded places, in cliff-side caves, in the wilderness, should contemplate that all the myriad things are impermanent. Though you wear garments and eat food, do not be greedy or delighted. All is suffering, empty, and without self. Though Māra seeks your weak point, he will never find it.' The bhikṣus then received the teaching of the Tathāgata Krakucchanda, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, and practiced according to the Dharma. Though Māra sought their weak point, he could never find it.

"Those elders and brāhmaṇas whom Māra had taught and caused to make offerings to the precept-keeping śramaṇas, by this merit were all born in heaven. Having been born in heaven, each thought: 'Because we made offerings to those Dharma-upholding śramaṇas who kept the precepts in purity, we ourselves obtained this blessing. It came not from others, nor was it given by the devas.'

"At that time, after his meal, in the afternoon, the Tathāgata Krakucchanda, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, was walking with his great disciple Great Voice, traveling through the districts. Then the evil Māra transformed into a large, powerful man, holding a great staff, standing by the roadside. Stealthily he raised the great staff and struck Great Voice on the head, cracking his skull so that blood spilled and flowed. At that time, the disciple was behind the World-Honored One, following like a shadow, silent and without a word. Then the Tathāgata Krakucchanda, the boundless great sage, turned to look and sighed, and these words came from his mouth: 'Now Hatred Māra does not know restraint; his transgression is immense.' Then Hatred Māra, in that very body, fell and sank into hell. Writhing in hell, he was like a tadpole taken from water and placed on dry land. As if an ox were flayed alive and writhing on the ground, the pain beyond description. Māra Pāpīyas, writhing in hell in venomous agony, suffered a hundred million thousand times more than this. As if a person struck by madness runs about with no place of rest.

"Māra Pāpīyas, having fallen into the great hell with immeasurable suffering, the warden of the hell came and said to him: 'Son, you should know this: if one counting-stick appears and one bird flies past, know that a hundred million years have passed. Such a measure is nearly limitless. Evil Māra! My lifespan in this hell is like this, and only after that does one emerge from the great hell, to further encounter hardship for twenty thousand more years.' At that time, the evil Māra was in extreme anguish and torment."

The Buddha spoke this verse for Maudgalyāyana:

> What punishment did Hatred Māra receive?
> What kind of hell was it?
> At the time of the Buddha Krakucchanda,
> He misled the multitudes and the disciples.
> Whatever suffering and affliction he received,
> All was plainly punished.
> Fire blazed and burned his body,
> Flames encircled his face and form.
> Such was that hell,
> Where Hatred Māra dwelt.
> At the time of the Buddha Krakucchanda,
> Great Voice, the great disciple:
> Even standing before the Buddha
> And before the assembly of bhikṣus,
> Through causes and conditions he received punishment,
> In an instant there was disturbance.
> If one delights in disparaging
> A bhikṣu, disciple of the Buddha,
> One shall surely reap this calamity,
> Heading toward extreme suffering and affliction.
> Like one cast into a deep abyss,
> Abandoning the palace of heaven,
> No longer among celestial maidens,
> Forsaking all heavenly pleasures.
> Those who understand this:
> A bhikṣu, disciple of the Buddha,
> Arising from one's own self,
> Brings danger and falls into suffering.
> Māra, you should know who I am:
> I lean upon the gate of liberation,
> Not a deva, yet dwelling among devas and humans,
> Trāyastriṃśa, the heaven of renown.
> If one discriminates against
> A bhikṣu, disciple of the Buddha,
> One's own body committing what is wrong,
> Through this one falls into evil destinies.
> With but one toe
> He shakes the supreme palace;
> Such is the power of supernatural ability:
> Maudgalyāyana's great spiritual response.
> Those who understand this:
> A bhikṣu, disciple of the Buddha,
> Standing on his own,
> How could he fall into evil destinies?
> Suppose there were a hundred
> Delicate, beautiful celestial maidens:
> Seeing the bhikṣu in meditation,
> They would not linger in the park.
> If one discriminates against
> A bhikṣu, disciple of the Buddha,
> The bhikṣu's own created actions
> May lead to evil destinies.
> Suppose they went together in harmony
> To inquire of Śakra regarding his affairs:
> Does the lord of devas understand?
> What is the cause of liberation?
> Śakra would immediately respond,
> Answering according to the question:
> If one is without attachment,
> Then one attains liberation.
> If one understands this:
> A bhikṣu, disciple of the Buddha,
> According to one's own created actions,
> One arrives at evil destinies.
> Or one may reach the Brahmā heaven,
> To challenge the King of Brahmā:
> What caused you to reach this place,
> To be established in the Brahmā heaven?
> The Brahmā would answer at once,
> Responding according to the question:
> The place where I am now established,
> I have never harbored wrong views.
> From the Brahmā heaven seeing all,
> Radiance has its waning;
> What shall I now say?
> Does my body abide forever?
> If one understands this:
> A bhikṣu, disciple of the Buddha,
> One's own body committing what is wrong,
> Naturally returns to toil and suffering.
> Fire has no thought,
> "I shall burn the fool."
> The fool himself kindles the fire,
> And in turn burns his own body.
> Pāpīyas, you should understand this:
> Whatever intention is directed at the Tathāgata
> Returns to endanger one's own body,
> As fire burns the foolish.
> One who delights in committing evil
> Through the long night harms oneself.
> When death comes, one is unaware;
> Do not harass the bhikṣus.
> Māra, take care not to test the Buddha,
> Nor harass his disciples,
> Lest through the long night you find no peace
> And surely fall into evil destinies.
> At that time Māra was subdued,
> Sitting in fear because of the bhikṣu.
> Hearing these words, filled with sorrow,
> He suddenly vanished at that moment.

Thus spoke the Buddha. All the devas, nāgas, and spirits rejoiced.
