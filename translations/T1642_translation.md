# The Diamond Needle Treatise

## *Jīngāng Zhēn Lùn* (金剛針論)

Taishō 1642

Composed by Bodhisattva Dharmakīrti (*Fǎchēng*, 法稱). Translated by Dharmadeva (*Fǎtiān*, 法天) of the Song dynasty.

*Note: This is a polemical treatise attributed to the Buddhist logician Dharmakīrti (c. 600-660), systematically refuting the Brahmanical claim to caste supremacy. The "diamond needle" of the title pierces the fabric of caste ideology. The treatise challenges the doctrine that Brahmins are born from Brahmā's mouth and are inherently superior, arguing point by point that neither birth, lineage, knowledge of the Vedas, ascetic practice, bodily austerities, dietary restrictions, nor any external characteristic can justify the claim. It marshals a series of counter-examples from the Brahmanical tradition's own scriptures, including great sages (ṛṣi) who were born from animals, low-caste women, or celestial nymphs: Kācina born from a deer, Vyāsa from a fisherwoman, Vasiṣṭha from the apsaras Urvaśī, and Viśvāmitra from a caṇḍāla family. The treatise concludes with a dialogue between the sage Vyāsa and King Yudhiṣṭhira, establishing that true Brahminhood consists in the five qualities of non-harming, freedom from desire, sense-control, compassion, and pure conduct, not in birth or lineage.*

---

The Brahmins say: "Among all scriptures, the four Vedas (*wéituó*, 圍陀) are authoritative. Among these, the recitation (*niàn*, 念) is the authentic one. Within this recitation, the signifier and the signified are both authoritative, and of these, the signifier is authoritative. This alone is supreme; no dharma surpasses it. Were the world without this, how could ritual action be performed? Through this signifier, both love and hatred arise. Just as among all castes, the Brahmin is the highest, so too is this authoritative expression."

This reasoning is incorrect. Why? That Brahmin: what is his lineage? What is his life-principle? How is he known? What are his actions? How did he obtain the name "Brahmin"? And these Vedas: why are they called authoritative?

Indra's (*Dìshì*, 帝釋) original cause: how [did he become] an animal? How did an animal give birth to the Moon-god and the Sun-god? And the original cause further produces animals. The Wind-god, Fire-god, and Water-god's original causes are also passed on in succession. How can this be?

Furthermore, they falsely maintain that those who die in heaven are reborn in heaven, those who die as humans are reborn as humans, and animals likewise. Such claims within the four Vedas are all contrary to correct reasoning. What is this "life-principle"? For what reason is it called "life-principle"? The same applies to "Brahmin" and similar terms.

Furthermore, you non-Buddhist Brahmins say: "According to the authoritative scriptures, on Mount Bhārati (*Póluōdì*, 婆囉帝) are born seven kinds of beasts. At Mount Naṣarada and Mount Kaliṅgajāri, the parrots, egrets, geese, and deer found there are reborn in the human realm at Kurukṣetra (*Jùlǔqǐshā*, 俱嚕乞曬), and after dying there are reborn among Brahmins and understand the four Vedas."

These beasts, deer, geese, and mandarin ducks are born in the human realm. Is the life-principle of those animals a Brahmin or not a Brahmin? If it is a Brahmin, then they were not really animals. If it is not, then they are born as Brahmins [without a Brahmin life-principle]. This reasoning is incorrect.

The Brahmins maintain that the four Vedas are the root of all dharmas, calling them "suchness" (*zhēnrú*, 真如), and that they do not permit receiving food from other castes.

Yet they repeatedly accept offerings from Śūdras (*shǒutuó*, 首陀), directly violating their own doctrine. How can they be called "practitioners of purity"? Therefore they are not true Brahmins.

---

Furthermore, the four-Veda Brahmin dharma falsely maintains that there is a "correct life-principle," a "correct dharma," and a "Brahmin seed." This too is incorrect. What is this "correct dharma"? If castes are intermixed, how can one be called "supreme"? Why? If it is not the case that they are intermixed, what are the facts?

Consider: a father named Narakṣa had a son named Bhṛgu the Sage (*Bīngjié-luó Xiān*, 兵誐羅仙). A father named Agasti had a son also named Agasti. A father named Puṣyacā had a son named Kauśika (*Jiāoshījiā*, 嬌尸迦). A father named Kuśa's son was Saṃsatga. A father named Kācina had a son also named Kācina. A father named Vacagulma had a son named Gautama. A father named Karaśa had a son named Druṇacārya. A father named Ditiditri had a son named Titidri-ka. A father named Naradva had a son named Sage Jñāna ('Awakening'). A mother was a wild deer or a fisherwoman, and her son was named Vyāsa, son of a sage. A father was of the Śūdra caste, and his son was named Viśvāmitra. A mother was a Caṇḍāla; her son was named Vasiṣṭha. A mother named Urvaśī was a celestial woman, not a Brahmin.

As described above, for what reason do you stubbornly maintain that Brahmins are the highest among humans? Furthermore, the claim that the Śruti scriptures are authoritative is also incorrect. Therefore all the principles of the Brahmin dharma are likewise incorrect.

---

Furthermore, according to Brahmin dharma, raw meat, shellac dye, salt, and similar items should be received by Śūdras, and Brahmins should not receive them. Why then do [Brahmins] not follow this?

Furthermore, they maintain that a Brahmin who sells milk can walk through the sky, but if he falls, he is no [true] Brahmin. One who eats meat falls through the sky; the same incorrect reasoning applies. Therefore one should know that buying and selling milk and meat is not the dharma of the Śūdra. By this one should know that all those who do not eat meat and milk and do not engage in buying and selling should all equally be called Brahmins. Therefore one should know that the claim that selling milk and meat disqualifies one as a Brahmin is a false doctrine.

Furthermore, the worldly claim that one's caste is supreme is also not correct dharma. If the Kṣatriya, Vaiśya, and Śūdra each claim supremacy, should they all be collectively called Brahmins?

Furthermore, if mortification of the body makes one a Brahmin, then all who mortify their bodies should all be called Brahmins.

Furthermore, they falsely maintain that killing a Brahmin incurs heavy sin, and that harming their relatives also incurs heavy sin. They further maintain that Brahmins were born from the pure god's mouth, Kṣatriyas from that god's body, Vaiśyas and Śūdras from his body and feet. Therefore killing them incurs heavy sin.

This reasoning is incorrect. Why? If it follows that killing someone of another caste incurs no sin, and harming their relatives incurs no sin either, then this false doctrine does not accord with correct reasoning.

---

Furthermore, they maintain that destroying their practice, destroying their practice of giving, and [harming] their receiving of gifts, whether in wisdom or body, all incur heavy sin.

This should not be so. Why? In body and wisdom, which one is called "Brahmin"? Śūdras and others also possess body and wisdom. Should they all be called Brahmins?

Furthermore, they falsely maintain that one who understands the four Vedas, the Mīmāṃsā, Sāṃkhya, Vaiśeṣika, and all other treatises is a Brahmin.

This reasoning is also incorrect. If Śūdras and others also understand those treatises and comprehend their meaning, should they all be called Brahmins?

If practicing austerities makes one a Brahmin, then Śūdras and others can also practice them. Should they too be called Brahmins?

If understanding various arts and sciences makes one a Brahmin, then fishermen and musicians who understand various arts and sciences in all their distinctions should they too be called Brahmins?

Therefore one should know: practice does not make a Brahmin; livelihood does not make a Brahmin; the giving and receiving of gifts does not make a Brahmin.

Kṣatriyas, Vaiśyas, and Śūdras can also do all these things. Should they all be called Brahmins? Therefore one should know: it is not lineage, not livelihood, not practice, not birth, and not even virtue [alone] that makes a Brahmin.

---

What then establishes [a true Brahmin]? [One who is] like the *kunda* flower, or like the white moon; free from all defilement, who cultivates superior practice well; whose deportment is faultless, whose moral conduct is complete; who skillfully subdues the sense-faculties; who severs and eliminates afflictions; who is free from [the notion of] self and other, free from all attachment; and from whom greed, hatred, and delusion are all far removed. Such a one is called a true Brahmin.

Furthermore, one who is free from passionate attachment, even toward animals, who does not give rise to attachment, and who cultivates pure conduct, is called a Brahmin.

Therefore one should know, as the great sage Suguru has said: this "Brahmin" is not [defined by] lineage, not livelihood, not virtue, not practice, not skill. Just as a Caṇḍāla (*zhāndàluó*, 旃陀羅) who is skilled in the four Vedas and possesses artistic ability and virtuous conduct should not thereby be called a Brahmin, so one should know: it is not life-principle, not lineage, not wisdom, not body, and not livelihood that makes a Brahmin.

---

Furthermore, suppose a Śūdra practices austerities, studies, and understands the four Vedas, and attains the five supernatural powers of a sage. Why then do you Brahmins serve and honor this person of a lower caste? And if all four castes can attain the way of the sages, how can the other castes be called "not supreme"?

Furthermore, Indra cultivated wholesome karma in a former life and was thereby born in that heaven; he was originally of a lower caste. Your own authoritative texts state: "This Bhagavat and Indra were of lower castes." The same argument applies.

Furthermore, they say that the great god Maheśvara (*Dà Zìzài Tiān*, 大自在天) and the goddess produced Indra, the devas, and the world-vessel from their mouths. The world did not produce Maheśvara or the goddess. The root produces the branches; the branches do not produce the root. Therefore this claim contradicts their own authoritative teaching. If [Maheśvara] was originally of a lower caste, how can one falsely claim to be born from him? This is clearly incorrect.

Furthermore, suppose a Śūdra dies and is reborn as Maheśvara. Why then do you Brahmins serve and honor this being of a lower caste?

---

Furthermore, as you say in the Brahmin dharma: subsisting on air, taking medicines, practicing austerities, and fasting make one a Brahmin.

If Śūdras and others can also do this, should they too be called Brahmins?

Furthermore, they maintain that if one receives food from a Śūdra's hand for one month, one's present body transforms into a Śūdra body, and in the next life one is certainly reborn as a dog.

Furthermore, if a Brahmin takes a Śūdra woman as his wife, his parents and household gods all abandon him, and after death he enters hell.

This reasoning is incorrect. What difference is there between the Brahmin caste and the Śūdra?

Consider the great sage Kācina, born from a deer-womb: he practiced austerities and attained the way of a sage. Could this sage have been born from a Brahmin?

Consider the great sage Vyāsa (*Wāyěshā*, 嚩野娑), born from a fisherwoman: he practiced austerities and attained the way of a sage. Was this sage of Brahmin lineage? Therefore the false doctrine does not accord with correct reasoning.

Consider the great sage Vasiṣṭha (*Wāsīsèzhā*, 嚩斯瑟吒), born from the celestial woman Urvaśī: he practiced austerities and attained the way of a sage. Was this sage born a Brahmin?

Consider the sage Deer-Horn (*Lùjiǎo*, 鹿角), born from a deer-womb: he practiced austerities and attained the way of a sage. Was this sage a Brahmin?

Consider the great sage Viśvāmitra, born from a woman of a Caṇḍāla family. Was this sage a Brahmin?

Therefore one should know: subduing the sense-faculties, not clinging to self and other, diligently cultivating pure conduct, being far removed from attachment and desire, and permanently extinguishing all delusions: only by this is one called a true Brahmin, and not through caste lineage. How can one falsely maintain that the Brahmin caste is the highest in the world, that their conduct is pure and their lineage unmixed? This false doctrine takes what is not supreme to be supreme.

Therefore one should know: it is not lineage, not life-principle, not clan, not practice, not livelihood, not birth that makes a Brahmin.

---

Furthermore, many people of lower castes have upheld the precepts, cultivated merit, and been reborn in heaven. How can lineage [alone] lead to heavenly rebirth?

Furthermore, as your own tradition states: the great sages Kācina, Vyāsa, Vasiṣṭha, Sumedha, Viśva, Mitra, Nārāyaṇa, all these great sages were born from lower lineages, yet through the cause of ascetic practice they attained the way of sages. Why then do you falsely maintain that lineage is unmixed and the highest in the world? Therefore such false words should not be believed.

---

Furthermore, they maintain that the Brahmin caste was born from Brahmā's mouth, the Kṣatriya caste from Brahmā's arms, the Vaiśya caste from Brahmā's thighs, and from Brahmā's feet the Śūdras were born. Therefore they make many such false claims.

Furthermore, they maintain that one who practices austerities with firm resolve is a Brahmin. But fishermen, dyers, leatherworkers, and Śūdras who firmly practice austerities should all be collectively called Brahmins?

Furthermore, they maintain that the Brahmin form, with braided topknot, a cord around the waist, a staff in hand, and wearing white and eating simply, constitutes a Brahmin. But Śūdras and others can also adopt this appearance. Should they all be collectively called Brahmins?

Furthermore, they maintain that all four castes were born from Brahmā. If so, how can one father have sons of different castes? Could Śūdras and the other castes, born from one father, have sons of different castes? Since this is not the case [for ordinary people], how can it be so [for Brahmā]?

Furthermore, if Brahmins are all born from the mouth of one Brahmā, then they are siblings who mate with one another: something the world abhors. Yet you practice this. How can you be called pure? Therefore the false doctrine claims as pure what is not pure.

If one father and mother produce four sons, they cannot be of different castes. How can one falsely claim: "This is a Brahmin, this a Kṣatriya, this a Vaiśya, this a Śūdra"? How can one father's sons each be of a different caste? Therefore the distinction of four castes is a false doctrine.

It is not as with elephants, horses, cattle, sheep, camels, deer, lions, and tigers, whose forms and feet are all different: "This is a cow's track; that is an elephant's track," where one can distinguish differences. Nor is it like a single tree producing flowers and fruit that may be uniform, unlike other flowers that grow in different places and cannot be made the same. Your four castes are the same in principle. If Brahmins, Kṣatriyas, and even Śūdras are all born from one father, how can one falsely maintain that the four castes are different?

---

### Dialogue of King Yudhiṣṭhira and the Sage Vyāsa

Furthermore, there was a king named Yudhiṣṭhira (*Yùdìshèzhìluó*, 喻地瑟致囉) who reverently came with palms joined to the sage Vyāsa (*Fèishābōshā*, 吠娑波灑), bowed his head to the sage's feet, and addressed the great sage: "What constitutes Brahmin virtue? What constitutes the Brahmin characteristics? How many kinds of distinguishing characteristics are there? Please explain and help me understand."

The sage Vyāsa then told the king: "Patience, diligence, meditation (*dhyāna*), and wisdom (*prajñā*): these are called Brahmin virtues."

"Being far removed from greed and hatred, and from killing all sentient beings: this is called the first Brahmin characteristic."

"Not coveting and receiving any of others' possessions: this is called the second Brahmin characteristic."

"Being far removed from cruelty, of gentle and mild disposition, not clinging to self and other, abandoning bondage and all desire: this is called the third Brahmin characteristic."

"Toward women, whether human or divine, and even toward animals, being always free from attachment: this is called the fourth Brahmin characteristic."

"Furthermore, bringing all sentient beings to maturity, always arising compassion, subduing the sense-faculties, being pure and supreme: this is called the fifth Brahmin characteristic."

"When all five are fully present, one is called a Brahmin. If one clings to self and other and does not possess the five characteristics, one is called a Śūdra."

The sage further told Yudhiṣṭhira: "It is not clan, not lineage, and not ascetic practice that makes a Brahmin. Even a Caṇḍāla who fully possesses the five characteristics can be called a true Brahmin. By this reasoning, a [nominal] Brahmin can also be called a Śūdra, and a Śūdra can also be called a true Brahmin."

Yudhiṣṭhira addressed the sage: "If a Brahmin practices non-killing and obtains the fruit of purity, this is only a partial [claim to be called] a Brahmin."

The sage further told Yudhiṣṭhira: "The distinction of four castes is entirely due to the causes and conditions of past karma. Just as in the world all beings born from wombs are all produced from impure places at the root: what difference is there? Therefore it is through moral conduct and the cultivation of meritorious practice that one is called a Brahmin. Even a Śūdra who cultivates virtuous practice becomes a Brahmin."

"If a Brahmin does not cultivate virtuous practice, that person can also be called a lowly Śūdra."

"Furthermore, these five sense-faculties can generate evil karma and should always be subdued. Just as the great ocean drowns sentient beings, one should seek deliverance and help them cross to the other shore."

At that time, King Yudhiṣṭhira, hearing the sage's teaching, understood and leaped with joy. He dedicated what he had heard to all sentient beings without limit, wishing them all to understand, not for his own sake or out of clinging to his own life: "From this day forward, day and night I shall cultivate patience, abandon attachment to my retinue and to jealousy, never again cling to any object of desire, seek liberation, and always cultivate pure conduct."

The sage further told Yudhiṣṭhira: "Not killing sentient beings, being far from greed and hatred, pure and without comparison: this is called the conduct of a Brahmin. Subduing the sense-faculties, generosity, patience, truthful and pure conduct, compassionate care for all sentient beings, and the cultivation of wisdom: this is called the conduct of a Brahmin. Abandoning wrong austerities, enduring whatever suffering sentient beings require: this is called the conduct of a Brahmin."

Furthermore, it is said in the Brahmin Gāyatrī mantra text: "Austerity, freedom from attachment, subduing the sense-faculties, giving in all four seasons, loving all sentient beings, abandoning sleep, and always cultivating pure conduct for a thousand kalpas: only then can one be called a true Brahmin."

The sage further told Yudhiṣṭhira: "If understanding the four Vedas makes one a Brahmin, called the highest lineage, then Śūdras and others who also understand them should also be called the highest."

"It is like four castes who together visit a sacred site: the footprints left cannot be distinguished as 'this person's track' versus 'that person's track.' One caste or four castes: the same applies. Through conventional designation, there is originally no difference."

"Furthermore, just as in the world cattle and horses differ in form and appearance, yet the male and female organs of the same species are not different, so too the Brahmin compared with the Kṣatriya, Vaiśya, and Śūdra: one caste or four castes are the same in comparison."

"Furthermore, just as one person's blood, flesh, urine, feces, hands, feet, and faculties are the same as those of many other people."

"Furthermore, just as lotus flowers and *kṣattrī* flowers, the moon, conch-shells, and colors can be distinguished from one another, yet among the four castes there are no visible differences: how then can they be differentiated?"

"Furthermore, just as cattle, horses, elephants, and deer engage in mating without [inter-species] coupling that can be differentiated, today Brahmins and Kṣatriyas, Vaiśyas and Śūdras mate with each other and engage in desire, all born from wombs. What difference is there?"

"Furthermore, the daughters born to Brahmins: when they mate with other Brahmins of the same lineage, they are [mating with] siblings. Siblings as husband and wife! In the world, even Śūdras do not practice this."

"Just as in the world the *udumbara* tree may have many flowers, fruits, branches, and leaves, yet its root and trunk are one, and one cannot distinguish 'this flower' from 'that flower.' So too you Brahmins: you should not mate with sisters of the same lineage. The world abhors this; it should not be done."

"Furthermore, just as the fire of the world burns firewood without discrimination, so too the Brahmin compared with other castes: there is no difference."

"Furthermore, as your own tradition states: the great sage Vyāsa was born from a fisherman father. He was not born a Brahmin."

"Furthermore, the five Pāṇḍava brothers were born from the same mother but each had a different father. This was due to past karma: same mother, different fathers. It is not due to lineage. Yet you falsely claim distinction."

"Furthermore, just as salt in water makes the form invisible yet the salty taste does not disappear, so too past karma follows the person; its concealment and manifestation are the same. Such false doctrines should be carefully examined by the wise and should not be trusted."
