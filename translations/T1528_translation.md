# 涅槃經本有今無偈論
## Treatise on the "Originally Existent, Now Non-Existent" Verse from the Nirvāṇa Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1528

Composed by Bodhisattva Vasubandhu. Chen dynasty, translated by Paramārtha (真諦).

---

### The Meaning of the Three Times in the Nirvāṇa Sūtra

Resolving Cunda's doubt. The treatise says: Many disciples had already matured, but Cunda had not yet matured. Because Cunda had not yet matured, the Buddha manifested the great parinirvāṇa, preached the great sūtra, and received great merit, coming to the city of Kuśinagara in order to bring him to maturity. Why then did Cunda have doubt? There were two reasons: first, seeing the common characteristic without seeing the distinguishing characteristic gives rise to doubt; second, seeing the distinguishing characteristic without seeing the common characteristic gives rise to doubt. As for one who gives rise to doubt by not seeing the distinguishing characteristic: it is like seeing a tree stump from afar and doubting whether it is a person or a stump. If one sees crows gathered on top and deer passing beneath, one knows it is a stump, not a person. If one sees it raising a hand or adjusting its garment, one knows it is a person, not a stump. As for one who gives rise to doubt by seeing the distinguishing characteristic without seeing the common characteristic: empty space, for example, has the distinguishing characteristic of being permanent, while earth has the distinguishing characteristic of being impermanent. The śrāvakas, seeing their own distinguishing characteristics, generate doubt regarding them: is this permanent like empty space, or impermanent like earth? Ordinary beings generate doubt because of the common characteristic. Śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas generate doubt because of the distinguishing characteristic. Ordinary beings generate doubt because of dharmas that have arising. Śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas generate doubt because of dharmas without arising. Cunda did not generate doubt for either of these two reasons; he generated this doubt in order to benefit sentient beings. How could such a great bodhisattva generate doubt regarding the Buddha? At this great assembly, a great number of non-Buddhists had gathered. Some non-Buddhists said the Buddha dies and is reborn. Others said it is like a lamp going out and the flame being extinguished. Still others said that after the Buddha's extinction, there is both a remainder and a non-remainder. In order to resolve this doubt, the Buddha spoke this verse:

> Originally existent, now non-existent;
> Originally non-existent, now existent;
> That dharmas exist in three times:
> Such a position is untenable.

The Buddha spoke this verse for the two vehicles. Afflictions are innate; the holy is cultivated. The ordinary-being nature is innate; the holy nature is cultivated. The bondage of afflictions is innate; liberation from bondage is cultivated. Birth-and-death is innate; nirvāṇa is cultivated. "Originally innate, now cultivated" means: "originally" refers to the innate, "now" refers to the cultivated. If this explanation is made for the two vehicles, it does not slander the Great Vehicle. If this explanation is made for the Great Vehicle, it is slandering the Great Vehicle. This interpretation is incompatible with the Great Vehicle. Who can make it compatible with the Great Vehicle? Therefore, we rely on meaning, selecting and contemplating the meaning, rather than relying on words, selecting and contemplating words. We now explain this for practitioners of the Great Vehicle.

---

### Refutation of the Three Positions

Going beyond three kinds of meaning, we reveal a distinct meaning. The phrases "originally existent, now non-existent," "originally non-existent, now existent," and "existing in three times" represent three kinds of meaning that are untenable. This is an untenable position. Why are these three kinds of meaning not established?

If "originally existent, now non-existent," then all tathāgatas would have no liberation. Why? Because the nature does not abide permanently: what existed before is now non-existent. All that is truly existent would also be non-existent, and what is truly existent as well as conventionally existent would also be non-existent. Why? Because the truly existent has no difference between before and after, and the conventionally existent has no original basis. Therefore, neither the ultimate nor the conventional meaning is established. When these two meanings are not clear, the Sāṃkhya non-Buddhists also speak in this way, saying that the effect exists within the cause, like milk containing cheese, fresh ghee, and so forth. This supports and inflates the Sāṃkhya position. If what originally did not exist now has no obstruction, then what in the present could serve as an obstruction? If you argue that obstructing causes and conditions combine to serve as the obstruction, this reasoning is not valid. Why? Because there is no difference between before and after. If there is no obstruction now, why was there no obstruction before? What principle is there whereby something originally does not arise through causes and conditions, and yet later is extinguished through causes and conditions? When you say "originally," what dharma is taken as the origin? Is it the initial arising, or is the continuity taken as the origin? If the initial arising is taken as the origin, then since the initial was not produced by causes and conditions, the later should also not be produced by causes and conditions, just like the initial. If this is so, then the doctrine of the twelve links of dependent origination and the principle of suchness are all destroyed, and this would be the same as the non-Buddhist doctrine of no causation. If the continuity is taken as the origin, the continuity is also not fixed. Why? Because each individual moment is not fixed. How can the continuity serve as the origin? Therefore, all dharmas that have arising are said to have no original cause. Such a position is without reason.

"Originally non-existent, now existent": if what was formerly non-existent now exists, then there could be no one who attains liberation. Before afflictions have arisen, one would already be separate from liberation; and if afflictions subsequently arise, there is no liberation. If what was formerly non-existent now exists, then that which is most utterly non-arising should also be able to arise, like flowers appearing in empty space. If you argue that one case has no cause, this reasoning is not valid. Why? Consider a house and a flower: both were equally non-existent before. Why do causes and conditions produce a house but not a flower? Since they are equally non-existent, this reasoning is without grounds. If what was originally non-arising now comes into being, then the meaning of "original" is destroyed. This reasoning is not valid. Why? Because the first arising is the origin. If you argue that causes and conditions constitute the first arising, then it is not the first, and therefore arising is not the origin. This too is not valid. Why? Your intention is to refute "originally existent" in order to establish causes and conditions as the origin. But this does not transcend the meaning of "original." Since there is no prior dharma, from what cause does it arise? If it does arise, does it arise all at once, or does it arise part by part? If it arises all at once, does it arise simultaneously, or does it arise in sequence? If simultaneously, then cause and effect would exist at the same time and could not be distinguished. If the effect arises later, the cause has already perished earlier: who then produces the later effect? It would be like cooking a chicken and it still making sounds and coming back to life. If it arises all at once, what need is there to examine the cause? If it arises part by part, the same faults as above apply. Therefore, "originally non-existent, now existent," attempting to establish causation, is not valid.

"Existing in three times": this meaning is also untenable. If there are three times, does one meaning pervade all three times, or does each meaning have its own three times? Both alternatives are not valid. Why? If one meaning pervades all three times, then one cannot have three within a single time. Why? Because the characteristics would mutually obstruct. If the meaning depends on time, then past and future would be divisible without limit. If time depends on the meaning, then since the meaning is one, there would be no three times. Apart from meaning there is no separate time, and therefore the three times are all not established. If one entity pervades three times, that entity cannot be named. Why? Because one entity would have two established aspects. If so, then birth-and-death and nirvāṇa would be one. If each time has its own existence, and each of the three times independently exists: just as the present can produce effects, why can the past and future not also produce effects? If all could produce effects, then not a single person could attain liberation. If the past and future cannot produce effects, then who severs the retribution? To say that the past and future exist: is this said with respect to substance, or with respect to function? If said with respect to substance, how can it be broken into three divisions? If said with respect to function, the past has already perished and the future has not yet arisen: how can function be exercised? If you argue that the three times exist as something that can be spoken of, but that the three times do not serve as the speaker and the three times do not serve as substance: if they share the same substance, yet one has function and two do not, this reasoning is not valid. If you argue that it is because of temporal conditions that function may or may not be spoken of, like a plantain that bears fruit once and cannot bear fruit again, this reasoning is also not valid. Why? Because the meaning is not fixed. To whom do these three times belong? If they arise from a cause, the difficulty leads to infinite regress. If they arise without a cause, then the meaning of temporal conditions is not established. If you argue that the future is first, the present is the middle, and the past is last, thus making three times, on the grounds that the force of the future pushes forth the present, and the force of the present pushes forth the past, like the water of the Ganges, where future water pushes present water and present water pushes past water: if one time is established, then three times are established. This reasoning is not valid. Why? Because water exists simultaneously; only the locations differ. Therefore, the doctrine that dharmas exist in three times is untenable. "Such a position is untenable" means: as the Hīnayāna says, it is untenable; as the non-Buddhists say, it is untenable.

### Establishing the Correct Meaning

Having refuted the Hīnayāna and non-Buddhist positions in this way, the verse has two meanings: first, refuting the wrong meaning; second, establishing the correct meaning. Refuting the wrong meaning relies on words. Establishing the correct meaning relies on the meaning itself. What is called the correct meaning is: "originally existent, now existent," transcending the three times. This is the correct meaning. "Originally existent, now existent" means: from the initial arousing of the mind to the attainment of nirvāṇa, it is of one flavor without difference, not dependent on a cause of arising, not dependent on a cause of cessation. Existing, it is pure. The dharmas of ordinary beings cannot defile it; the dharmas of the holy cannot purify it. If one commits the four grave offenses or the five heinous acts, it cannot be diminished. If one cultivates wisdom and severs evil, it cannot be increased. If one has the eye of purity through right view, or if one has the eye of poison through wrong view: in accordance with skillful means, it transcends the path of speech and all thought. It cannot be spoken of, cannot be conceived of; it embraces cause and effect yet is neither cause nor effect. This ground cannot be measured or analyzed all at once. It is the domain of all tathāgatas. Birth-and-death and nirvāṇa are this ground in its contrary and concordant aspects: when contrary, it is birth-and-death; when concordant, it is the ground of nirvāṇa. This is the prior limit and the posterior limit, the ground of the initial arousing of the mind and the ground of the vajra-like final mind. It destroys all views and purifies all views. All sentient beings should partake of it. The Tathāgata's single body is the ultimate refuge, embracing all treasures.

This is the great nirvāṇa that transcends the three times. In terms of function, we speak of the merits of nirvāṇa. What does it mean to transcend the three times? The three times are distinguished because of arising. Since nirvāṇa is without arising, it cannot be distinguished. The three times means: what has not yet arisen comes to arise, and what has already arisen immediately perishes. Since nirvāṇa is without perishing, it is permanent. Being permanent, it is therefore sovereign. Being sovereign, it is therefore the supreme bliss. In terms of substance, we say it is pure. In terms of function, we say it is permanent, blissful, and self. In its own substance it is pure; contrasted with birth-and-death, it is permanent, blissful, and self. Furthermore, there are two kinds of meaning: if "originally existent, now existent," this would be the view of eternalism; if "transcending the three times," this would be the view of annihilationism. When both meanings are taken together, free from annihilationism and eternalism, this is the Middle Way. In this way, the conventional truth and the ultimate truth are mutually dependent, and the twelve links of dependent origination are true and real. Why? Because being free from the two extremes constitutes the true twelve links of dependent origination. If one can skillfully understand this, then one sees the Tathāgata present in the world. Therefore the Tathāgata says: "The twelve links of dependent origination are the Tathāgata's body." With respect to the conventional and ultimate truths, because they are non-dual, the twelve links are the true Buddha-path. Thus the verse has two meanings: first, the meaning that counteracts causes, severing the wrong path; second, the meaning that accords with principle, revealing the truth. These two meanings are the Tathāgata's work, relying on great wisdom and great compassion.

### Five Kinds of Permanence

There are five kinds of permanence: first, the permanence of inexhaustibility; second, the permanence of non-arising; third, the permanence of perpetual abiding; fourth, the permanence of serene stillness; fifth, the permanence of changelessness.

The permanence of inexhaustibility has ten aspects: first, permanent because causes are boundless; second, permanent because sentient beings are boundless; third, permanent because great compassion is boundless; fourth, permanent because the four bases of supernatural power are boundless; fifth, permanent because wisdom is boundless; sixth, permanent because of perpetual abiding in samādhi; seventh, permanent because of comfort, coolness, and peace; eighth, permanent because, though acting in the world, the eight worldly conditions cannot defile; ninth, permanent because of the sweet-dew stillness that is far removed from the four māras; tenth, permanent because the nature is without arising. "Causes are boundless" means: through immeasurable kalpas, one has relinquished body, life, and wealth for the sake of upholding the true Dharma. Since the true Dharma is without boundary or end, inexhaustible, this is using inexhaustible causes to obtain inexhaustible fruit. The fruit is the three bodies.

Second, the permanence of non-arising: with respect to the prior limit, it is not "originally non-existent, now existent," and it is not produced by a mind-made body. Third, the permanence of perpetual abiding: with respect to the posterior limit, it is free from inconceivable death and destruction. Fourth, the permanence of serene stillness: with respect to the middle limit, it is not destroyed by the affliction-sickness of ignorance. Fifth, the permanence of changelessness: transcending the three limits, it is not altered by the fruition of uncontaminated karma. The third, perpetual abiding, is freedom from death. The fourth, serene stillness, is freedom from sickness. As for the fifth, changelessness: from the first ground to the Tathāgata-ground, this is collectively called inexhaustibility. From the eighth ground to the Tathāgata-ground, this is called non-arising. From the ninth ground to the Tathāgata-ground, one may also partially be said to possess the permanence of changelessness. Properly speaking, all five meanings are fully present only at the Buddha-ground.

### The Verse on Impermanence

> All conditioned things are impermanent;
> They are dharmas of arising and ceasing.
> When arising and ceasing have ceased,
> Quiescent extinction is bliss.

The Tripiṭaka ācārya explains the meaning as follows: "All conditioned things are impermanent" means that conditioned things are the mental and material formations that move through the three times. Impermanence has five meanings: first, the impermanence of loss and extinction; second, the impermanence of separation of characteristics; third, the impermanence of transformation, also called the impermanence of transmutation; fourth, the impermanence of what has parts; fifth, the impermanence of intrinsic nature. "Loss and extinction" means: when the retribution of a hundred years is exhausted, one's lifespan is lost and extinguished. Second, the impermanence of separation of characteristics means the scattering and separation of bones and flesh. Third, transformation means: as bones initially are white and later turn the color of a dove. Transmutation means the turning from white to dove-color. Fourth, the impermanence of what has parts means: when the three things of sense-faculty, sense-object, and consciousness have not yet combined, this is called the impermanence of "originally non-existent"; when what has come to exist returns to non-existence, this is called the impermanence of "already existent, returning to non-existence." When sense-faculty, sense-object, and consciousness come together, this is collectively called "what has parts." Fifth, intrinsic nature means: because the preceding four meanings exist, the nature itself is called impermanent. "Are arising" refers to arising in the future. "Ceasing dharmas" refers to dharmas that have already ceased in the past. "Arising and ceasing" refers to the present. The present encompasses arising and ceasing because what arises immediately ceases; therefore arising and ceasing pertain to the present. "Quiescent extinction is bliss" means: if one says that the dharma of ceasing is bliss, this reasoning is not valid. Why? Because the present ceasing is the past, and what has already ceased is a remainder. Since there is a remainder, it is not bliss. If one says that ceasing the present arising and ceasing is bliss, this too is not valid. Why? Because the future arising is a remainder of the present. Since there is a remainder, it is not bliss. If one says that future arising is permanent, this reasoning is not valid, because what arises must also cease, and therefore it is not permanent. If one can cause dharmas that should arise in the future to not arise, only then can there be bliss. "Quiescent extinction is bliss": this is precisely that meaning. The first three lines clarify that birth-and-death is conditioned and therefore impermanent. The last line explains that nirvāṇa is unconditioned and therefore permanent.
