# 佛說法身經
## Sūtra of the Dharma Body, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0766

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmabhadra (法賢)

---

At that time the World-Honored One, amid the great assembly, spoke with a wondrous voice and said: "All Buddhas and Tathāgatas have two kinds of bodies, both endowed with merit as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. What are these two? They are the transformation body and the dharma body.

"The transformation body is shown as being born from a father and mother, endowed with the thirty-two marks and eighty minor marks that adorn its body. With the eye of wisdom it surveys all sentient beings universally. The wise who gaze upon it feel joy arise in their hearts. The three kinds of karma are pure. Each mark and sign is replete with a hundred merits. Adorned thus by the accumulation of hundreds of thousands of merits, the marks of a great being are all subsumed within the aggregate of form. Furthermore, it is endowed with the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the three non-empty dharmas, the three abodes of mindfulness, the three non-guarded dharmas, and the four immeasurable dharmas. It possesses the supreme Nārāyaṇa-strength of each and every great being. Briefly stated thus, the adornment and merit of the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One are complete and fulfilled. This is called the transformation body.

"Furthermore, the dharma body of all Buddhas, Tathāgatas, Worthy Ones, Perfectly Awakened Ones is inconceivable, immeasurable, and there is no one who can fully expound it. Even if pratyekabuddhas and śrāvakas such as Śāriputra, who possess the sharpest faculties, skillfully comprehend the profound Dharma, and with great wisdom clearly understand all manner of meanings, even they cannot fully expound the merit of the dharma body. All Buddhas and Tathāgatas are teachers of the three realms. They are the greatly compassionate ones who bring great benefit to sentient beings, equally protecting and attending to all without discrimination. They abide in śamatha and vipaśyanā, and furthermore skillfully understand the three methods of taming. They skillfully cross the four difficulties. They possess the four bases of spiritual power. Through the long night they practice the four means of attraction. They are separated from the five desires and transcend the suffering of the five destinies. They possess the six branches of the Dharma and perfectly fulfill the six pāramitās. They open the flower of the seven factors of enlightenment and expound the noble Eightfold Path. They skillfully understand the nine kinds of samāpatti and possess the ten powers of wisdom. By these powers of wisdom, their renown is heard in the ten directions, and therefore they are called the Foremost Deva of Ultimate Meaning. At all times, in all places, day and night, in the three periods of each, they constantly observe with skill. Such internal dharma-merits of all Buddhas cannot be fully expounded by anyone. Therefore I now briefly teach this Dharma.

"This dharma body is purely one, without duality, without outflows, unconditioned, and should be realized through practice. All conditioned dharmas arise from the unconditioned. Being thus truly real, it is without purity and without defilement, without thought and without support, apart from all skillful means, yet it serves as the great refuge for sentient beings. The practices performed by all sentient beings are without any fault; this is the true wholesome Dharma, apart from all conceptual recollection. At the gate of boundless samādhi, it is unmoving and unshaken, and thereby attains liberation. Through the two kinds of śamatha and vipaśyanā, it attains liberation from desire and the dispassion toward desire. From the dharmas of ignorance and desire, it attains liberation through wisdom. Dharmas of learning and non-learning are understood through mindfulness. Through the liberation of insight, it skillfully penetrates its own nature. In regard to all dharmas, it is deeply capable of sustained mindfulness. It skillfully gives rise to the dharma of āpatti from āpatti, and skillfully gives rise to the dharma of samāpatti from samāpatti. In regard to all dharmas, there is neither seeking nor realization. Apart from these two dharmas, there is nothing conditioned and nothing further to cultivate. Through the knowledge of exhaustion and the knowledge of non-arising, one ultimately accomplishes the three dharmas of ultimate attainment, the three wholesome root dharmas, and the three gates of skillful means, and apart from all false imagining, one attains true birth.

"One understands the three kinds of wisdom: hearing, thinking, and practice. One is separated from the three defilements: the defilement of afflictions, the defilement of karma, and the defilement of suffering. There are three kinds of samādhi: emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness; these are also called the three gates of liberation, namely the liberation gates of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. There are three kinds of aggregate dharmas: the aggregate of morality, the aggregate of concentration, and the aggregate of wisdom. There are three kinds of training: the training in morality, the training in concentration, and the training in wisdom. There are three kinds of cultivation: cultivation of morality, cultivation of concentration, and cultivation of wisdom, pertaining to learners, non-learners, and those who are neither learners nor non-learners. There are three kinds of path: the path of seeing, the path of cultivation, and the path of no-more-learning. There are three kinds of faculty: the faculty of coming-to-know-what-was-not-yet-known, the faculty of full knowledge, and the faculty of possessing complete knowledge. There are three kinds of practice: holy practice, heavenly practice, and Brahmā practice. There are three divisions: the aggregates, the sense-bases, and the elements. Having understood these three dharmas, one obtains a great accumulation of merit, realizes liberation, and attains quiescent nirvāṇa, with the three non-empty abodes of mindfulness. The Tathāgata equally protects and shelters all sentient beings. There are three kinds of aggregate dharmas. There are three kinds of persons: upper, middle, and lower.

"All Buddhas and Tathāgatas possess three kinds of great compassion: unconditioned great compassion, subtle and wondrous great compassion, and great compassion for all sentient beings. There are three kinds of mastery: mastery over the body, mastery over the world, and mastery over the Dharma. There are three non-guarded dharmas: the bodily karma of all Tathāgatas is pure and separated from impure dharmas; their verbal karma is pure and separated from impure dharmas; their mental karma is pure and separated from impure dharmas. There are three kinds of sword: the sword of hearing, the sword of thinking, and the sword of practice. There are three kinds of supremacy: the supremacy of concentration, the supremacy of wisdom, and the supremacy of liberation. There are three kinds of realm dharmas: right severance, dispassion, and cessation. Again there are three realms: the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm. There are three kinds of non-learner's insight: past-life insight, the divine eye for the future, and insight into the exhaustion of outflows in the present. There are three unconditioned dharmas: all formations are impermanent; all dharmas are without self; nirvāṇa is quiescence. There are three kinds of bodhi: the bodhi of śrāvakas, the bodhi of pratyekabuddhas, and the unsurpassed bodhi. There are three kinds of non-learner's wisdom: the wisdom of exhaustion, the wisdom of non-arising, and the wisdom of right view. The Three Treasures, the Three Refuges, and the three supreme wisdoms; the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of spiritual power, the four dharmas of faith, and the four phrases of liberation.

"There are four kinds of wholesome dharma: ultimate-meaning wholesomeness, intrinsic-nature wholesomeness, initiated wholesomeness, and associated wholesomeness. Four kinds of cultivation, four kinds of wisdom, the Four Noble Truths, the four dhyānas, and the four wheel-treasuries. There are four dharmas and four reliances: drawing near to good friends, hearing the right Dharma, sustaining mindfulness and reflection, and practicing in accord with principle.

"Furthermore there are four conditions: the causal condition, the immediately-preceding condition, the dominant condition, and the object-as-condition. The four stages of preliminary practice: the stage of warmth, the stage of the peak, the stage of patience, and the stage of supreme worldly dharma. There are four kinds of path: the path of preparation, the uninterrupted path, the path of liberation, and the supreme path. The four fruits of the śramaṇa, the four noble clans, the four immeasurable minds of kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. Furthermore, the four kinds of birth, the four holy abodes, the four recollections, and the four deportments. There are four gates of arising: going forth, entering, quiescence, and perfect enlightenment. Furthermore there are four stages of realization. The five aggregates of a learner, the five places of liberation, the five methods of delivering sentient beings, and the five perceptions of holy wisdom. There are five components of samādhi: the component of right severance, the component of taming, the component of separation from faults, the component of separation from characteristics, and the component of separation from intrinsic nature. Furthermore there are five kinds of supreme component, five present activities of samāpatti, the five aggregates, the five elements, and again the five grasping-aggregates. The six dharmas of merit, the six supernatural powers, and the six recollections. Furthermore the six kinds of dharma, the five kinds of dispassion, the six kinds of cultivation, the six stages of seeing the truth, the six continuous practices, and the six perceptions of attestation. The seven kinds of persons, the seven practices of great beings, the seven abodes of consciousness, the seven factors of awakening, the seven dharmas free from fault, the seven dharmas of samādhi-enjoyment, the seven wonderful dharmas, the seven element-divisions, the seven places of good understanding, and the seven paths of cultivation. The Eightfold Path, the eight kinds of persons, the eight kinds of prātimokṣa precepts, the eight liberations, the eight bases, the eight wisdoms, the eight paths, the eight precepts, the eight assemblies, and the eight worldly dharmas. The Tathāgata's continuity is truly constant, vigorous, pure, and without any attachment.

"Furthermore there are the nine kinds of past samāpatti, the nine dharmas of faith, the nine dharmas of attainment, the nine cessations of name-and-form, the nine abodes of sentient beings, the nine dharmas of reliance, the nine undefiled grounds, and the nine grounds of the cultivation-path. The ten kinds of persons: the four who are progressing toward the fruits, the four who have attained the fruits, the ninth being the pratyekabuddha, and the tenth being the Perfectly Awakened One. The ten wholesome mental factors, the ten dharmas of a learner, the ten powers of the Tathāgata, the ten wholesome courses of action, the ten unwholesome courses of action, the ten holy abodes, and the ten actions in accord with principle. The eleven dharmas of contemplating merit, marks, and signs; the eleven kinds of dharmas for generating the good understanding of complete wisdom; and the eleven dharmas of complete morality. The twelve kinds of arising speech, the twelve sense-bases, the twelve links of dependent arising, and the twelve moments of assembling to realize the holy Dharma. The thirteen dharmas of joy, the thirteen dharmas of arising, and the thirteen grounds of action. The fourteen kinds of transforming mind. The fifteen minds of the seeing-path. The sixteen minds of right mindfulness. The seventeen kinds of marks of a learner, and the seventeen marks of aspiration. The eighteen elements and the eighteen uncommon dharmas. The nineteen grounds of analysis. The twenty-two faculties. The thirty-seven factors of awakening: the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of spiritual power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, and the noble Eightfold Path. The forty-four wisdoms. Furthermore the seventy-seven wisdoms, and the one hundred sixty-two paths: these constitute the grounds of cultivation.

"Such boundless and infinite dharmas of true constancy and continuity, separated from all afflictions, profound, vast, subtle, and difficult to conceive, are understood as they truly are by those of great wisdom. This Buddha-Dharma is the supreme and wondrous Dharma of Perfectly Awakened Ones as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, the Dharma of equality. If there are those who seek to realize the wisdom of perfect enlightenment, whether bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, or even the various non-Buddhist nirgrantha practitioners, those who possess right wisdom will understand it as it truly is and will further expound it broadly for sentient beings, as the Buddha has taught. They will cause boundless, innumerable, incalculable sentient beings to all realize quiescent, fearless, and ultimate nirvāṇa."
