# 進旨
## Advancing the Teaching

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2864

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty. Translator unknown (失譯); recorded by Zhang Changi (張長繼), in the fifth year of the Kaibao reign period [972 CE].

---

When the Emperor heard these words, his eyes filled with tears. After a long silence, he spoke to the monk, saying: "Throughout my humble kingdom there have been no offerings worthy of you. The Most Venerable One has dwelt within it for several years. I never had any intention of showing disrespect. Though I reign as lord of men, I occupy an unworthy place in heaven and earth. I am ever grateful that the Most Venerable One has seen fit to visit my humble kingdom. I humbly beseech the monk, out of compassion, to remain for three to five more days. Is this possible?"

Venerable Yuan replied: "As for the meaning of nirvāṇa, it is fundamentally without grasping or clinging. If there is grasping and clinging, all of it belongs to delusion. I humbly request that Your Majesty not hold onto sorrow. This poor monk has a vow to return to the mountains."

The Emperor saw that Venerable Yuan's words were earnest, and his own feelings were difficult to suppress. An imperial decree was issued in advance, instructing the Six Palaces to prepare quietly for a farewell sendoff. At that time, the Emperor, filled with deep longing, together with all the assembled ministers of the realm, joined together in seeing him off.

Venerable Yuan set out upon the road, departing from the palace halls and bidding farewell to the Dragon Tower. Looking toward the mountain, the road stretched long before him; beholding rivers and streams, he wandered in freedom. On that day, Venerable Yuan traversed the long road and proceeded on his journey, secretly manifesting supernatural powers. Once he had departed Chang'an, clouds arose beneath his feet. Like a strong man extending his arms, in just a moment he arrived at the mountain.

Venerable Yuan did not return to his old monastery, which lay ten li away. Coming to a steep ridge, he temporarily constructed a thatched hermitage. There he sat in full lotus and once again sought out his old scrolls, raising his voice to recite the sūtras. Time passed gradually, and several more months went by.

Venerable Yuan suddenly gazed up at the high plateau and called out to ascend. In that realm the peaks were steep, cranes called in the valleys, and dragons sang in the streams below. Throughout the hundred valleys and thousand peaks, flowers bloomed in unison. On the level ground flowed ever-running waters; in the gardens bloomed imperishable flowers. This was the very place where the Tathāgata had practiced.

Thereupon Venerable Yuan sat upright and entered samādhi. With purified intention and clarified mind, he contemplated the path of the Buddha. Reflecting that this fleeting life would not endure long and that the mundane world was unbearable, he took the king of the mind of self-nature and fashioned a Dharma vessel, taking refuge in the higher realm. In building this vessel, Venerable Yuan used no ordinary worldly materials, requiring none of those things. He himself upheld the untainted. ---

**Translator's notes:** The monk referred to throughout as 遠公 (Venerable Yuan) is almost certainly Huiyuan (慧遠, 334–416 CE), the celebrated patriarch of Pure Land Buddhism who resided on Mount Lu (廬山). The mountain name in the text (㠠山) employs an unusual graph that likely represents 廬山. The narrative is hagiographic and ends abruptly mid-sentence, suggesting the surviving manuscript is a fragment. The reference to the emperor parting sorrowfully from Huiyuan in Chang'an, followed by the master's miraculous flight back to his mountain, reflects a common motif in Tang and Song dynasty Buddhist hagiography.
