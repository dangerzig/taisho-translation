# 月光菩薩經
## Sūtra of Bodhisattva Candraprabha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0166

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Faxian (法賢)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the city of Rājagṛha, in the Bamboo Grove Monastery, teaching the Dharma for the great assembly of bhikṣus. At that time, Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana came before the Buddha, prostrated themselves on the ground, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! We cannot bear to see the Buddha enter parinirvāṇa. Therefore, at this time, we wish to enter parinirvāṇa first."

At that time, among the assembly there was a bhikṣu who joined his palms toward the Buddha and spoke thus: "World-Honored One! What cause and condition leads Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana to wish to enter parinirvāṇa before the Buddha? We beseech the World-Honored One to resolve the assembly's doubt."

At that time, the World-Honored One told the bhikṣus: "Listen carefully! I shall explain for you. Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana have completely exhausted all the defilements of greed, anger, delusion, and the rest. What was to be done has been done, the holy life has been established, and they will not receive further existence. It is not only today that they wish to enter parinirvāṇa first. In a past life, in northern India there was a great city named Bhadraśilā, twelve yojanas in length and equally broad. There was a king named Candraprabha, whose lifespan was forty thousand years. He possessed the divine eye and knowledge of past lives. His body was handsome and dignified, endowed with all the marks. His radiance illuminated like the full moon in the sky. Wherever he went, he had no need of lamps, torches, or the light of the sun and moon. Therefore he was called Candraprabha. He ruled over the four continents and sixty-eight thousand kingdoms. The times were prosperous, the harvests abundant, and the people at peace. Gold, silver, treasures, food, drink, clothing, elephants, horses, and carriages were all in plentiful abundance. At the four gates of the city were towers and pavilions, their windows and balconies all adorned with various treasures. The streets and avenues were swept clean, with banners, jeweled canopies, and pearl necklaces erected. There were also the fragrances of aloeswood, powdered incense, and sandalwood. When a gentle breeze arose, it carried the fragrance throughout the entire capital, so that travelers and carriages never encountered foul odors. Everywhere there were flower and fruit trees, tamāla trees, karṇikāra trees, aśoka trees, palmyra trees, śāla trees, tilaka trees, nāga-puṣpa trees, bakula trees, atimuktaka trees, and pāṭala trees, flourishing and luxuriant. Parrots, śāri birds, kalaviṅka birds, kokila birds, and others made wondrous sounds among the trees. Within and outside the city, flowing springs and bathing pools constantly produced fine flowers: utpala flowers, kumuda flowers, puṇḍarīka flowers, and others. Such was the wealth and splendor, adorned in every way."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "At that time, the devaputra Candraprabha, in the marketplaces, streets, and at the four gates of the city, heaped up gold, silver, treasures, elephants, horses, carriages, food, drink, clothing, bedding, medicine, and various adornments. He then struck the golden drum and proclaimed to all: 'The devaputra Candraprabha gives various kinds of wealth universally to all. Whatever you need, come and ask, and it will be given.' At that time, all the people of the southern continent of Jambudvīpa came to the royal city to receive his gifts. None were left without abundance, and all obtained great wealth. Not one was impoverished or without conveyance.

"At that time, the devaputra Candraprabha further reflected: 'Although the people are no longer impoverished, what they have is still not equal to my own possessions.' He then gave the finest clothing, the most precious treasures, crowns, necklaces, bedding, food, and drink to all the people. Their wealth and adornment became equal to that of the devaputra Candraprabha himself. The cities, palaces, towers, and gardens were adorned in every way, like the Trāyastriṃśa heaven.

"There were seventy-two hundred thousand nayutas of people who constantly dwelt in this city. There were two thousand five hundred ministers. There were two chief counselors: one named Mahācandra, and the other named Dharaṇīdhara. They were handsome, richly endowed with merit and virtue, profoundly wise, broadly learned, and of great talent. They constantly used the ten wholesome deeds to transform all beings.

"At that time, Mahācandra had a dream during the night: the king's crown turned into black smoke, and a ghost came and snatched the crown from the king's head and departed. Having had this dream, he was anxious and terrified, fearing it was an ill omen. He thought: 'My king is compassionate and generous to all. He never refuses those who ask. Surely an evil person will come to beg for the king's head.' Having thought this, he fashioned a jeweled head from the seven treasures, so that if anyone came to beg, he might offer it as a substitute. At that time, the counselor Dharaṇīdhara also had a dream, in which he saw the body of Candraprabha dismembered into four parts. He immediately summoned brāhmaṇas to determine whether the dream was auspicious or inauspicious. The brāhmaṇas said: 'This dream is very evil. Surely someone from far away will come to beg for the king's head.' When Dharaṇīdhara heard this, he wept and grieved: 'How can such a great calamity befall our king?' At that time, twelve thousand five hundred ministers who held positions of trust all had evil dreams: banners fell to the ground, golden drums made no sound, loved ones were parted, and there was weeping and wailing. After these dreams, they discussed together: 'If something ill befalls the king, who will rescue and save all beings? How will we find peace?'

"Then the devaputra Candraprabha further told his ministers: 'For the remainder of my life, I shall give to all beings without interruption.'

"At that time, in the Gandhamādana Mountains, there was a great brāhmaṇa named Virūpākṣa, who was clever, intelligent, and skilled in the arts. He heard that the devaputra Candraprabha had opened a great assembly of giving at the four gates of the city, struck drums and proclaimed to all four directions that all who asked would be provided for without lack. 'I shall now go there and beg for the king's head.' Having said this, he descended from the Gandhamādana Mountains. The celestial beings of the mountain, knowing the brāhmaṇa had come to beg for the king's head, grieved and lamented: 'Alas! Alas! This king's heart is compassionate, and he benefits all living beings. If he should die, the world will lose its blessed protector.' As these words were spoken, heaven and earth turned dark, the sun and moon disappeared, springs and wells dried up, fierce winds suddenly arose, blowing sand and rolling stones, trees were snapped and broken, and the great earth shook. Such were the inauspicious signs.

"Not far from the city, there was a sage who possessed the five supernatural powers, named Viśvāmitra. Together with five hundred of his retinue, he always protected and cared for all beings with compassion. Seeing these portents, he was deeply troubled and said to his student: 'Surely a calamity threatens the sovereign of the people. How can we protect and rescue him?' In the sky, the kiṃnaras and the devas all shed tears like a light rain. All the people were alarmed and frightened.

"When the evil-eyed brāhmaṇa was about to reach the city, the guardian deity of the city came before the devaputra Candraprabha: 'Now an evil man comes from the Gandhamādana Mountains. He harbors a murderous heart and wishes to beg for the king's head. Do not grant it. You should cherish and protect yourself, and preserve the sacred body.'

"When the king heard this, his heart leaped with joy, and he exclaimed: 'Excellent! This will allow me to perfect the pāramitā of giving!'

"The evil-eyed brāhmaṇa then entered the royal city. The guardian deity of the gate saw the brāhmaṇa's hideous and evil appearance and barred him outside the gate, refusing to let him enter. The devaputra Candraprabha, knowing the brāhmaṇa had arrived but was not being allowed into the city, immediately told his chief counselor Mahācandra: 'A brāhmaṇa has come from the Gandhamādana Mountains and wishes to see me. Order the gatekeepers not to obstruct him.'

"Mahācandra received the instruction and told the guardian deity of the gate, who immediately let him enter. When Mahācandra saw him, he asked the brāhmaṇa: 'Why have you come here? What do you seek?'

"The brāhmaṇa said: 'I have heard that the devaputra Candraprabha is compassionate toward all beings and has established a great assembly of giving. Whatever is asked for, nothing is begrudged. I have come here to beg for the king's head.' Mahācandra said: 'Brāhmaṇa! The king's head is made of pus and blood, and will eventually rot and decay. If you obtained it, what use would it be? I have a jeweled head made of the seven treasures, as well as various gold, silver, and precious gems. I offer them all to you, so that your children and grandchildren may be forever wealthy.'

"The brāhmaṇa said: 'I came specifically to beg for the head, not for treasures.'

"The two ministers wept, their tears falling like rain, in grief and anguish: 'How can we prevent this harm?'

"The brāhmaṇa then went before the king. When he saw the king, he bowed his head in greeting, stood to one side, joined his palms, and said: 'I have heard that the king is compassionate and gives universally to all. I have come from afar to beg only for the king's head. I beseech you to grant it with compassion and joy.' He then spoke this verse:

> "The bodhisattva aspires to the supreme wisdom,
> Abiding in the most excellent and pure Dharma.
> May you compassionately and swiftly offer your head,
> And perfect the pāramitā of giving."

"The devaputra Candraprabha then rose, joined his palms, and spoke this verse:

> "This impure body born of father and mother,
> You ask for my head, and I joyfully give it.
> Fulfill your original vow and depart content.
> May this swiftly bring me the fruit of enlightenment."

"Having spoken this verse, he said to the brāhmaṇa: 'Do not disdain my head. It is bone, marrow, pus, and blood, skin and flesh joined together, without any purity. Yet I give it to you, to fulfill your original vow.'

"The brāhmaṇa was greatly delighted. The king, about to sever his head, first removed his crown. At that moment, throughout the southern continent of Jambudvīpa, all crowns fell to the ground, and everyone was alarmed. The two counselors could not bear to see the king give up his body and life, and right then and there they ended their own lifespans. By the power of their roots of merit, they were reborn in the Great Brahmā heaven.

"At that time, the yakṣa Bhūma cried out in the sky: 'Alas! The devaputra is now about to die!' Hundreds of thousands of millions of people rushed to the royal palace, weeping and shedding tears, grieving at the parting from their beloved. The king then taught the Dharma to comfort them and inspire them to generate the aspiration for the path.

"The brāhmaṇa said: 'If the king would give his head, he should do so in a clean place.'

"The king then said: 'I have a garden named Maṇiratnagarbha. Its flowers and fruits are luxuriant, with flowing springs and bathing pools, adorned in every way, the finest of all. I shall give my head there. What do you think?'

"The brāhmaṇa said: 'Let us go there at once.'

"The king then took his sword and went to that garden. He stood under a campaka tree and told the brāhmaṇa: 'I now offer my head. Come and sever it.'

"The brāhmaṇa said: 'If the king does not sever it himself and has me hold the blade, it is not the practice of giving.'

"At that time, the guardian deity of the garden, having seen this, wept and said to the brāhmaṇa: 'You are a great evildoer! The devaputra Candraprabha has compassion for all and universally benefits all living beings. Why do you wish to take the devaputra's life in this place?'

"The king told the deity: 'Do not speak thus, obstructing this supreme deed. In my past, through innumerable lives, as a great king, within this very garden, I have given my head a thousand times, and the devas never obstructed it. In the past, when I saved the starving tiger by giving my body and life, I surpassed the Bodhisattva Maitreya by forty kalpas. At that time, the devas also did not obstruct it. If you today generate a mind of joyful assent, you will obtain supreme benefit.'

"The devaputra Candraprabha further addressed the devas, nāgas, and the eight classes of protectors, and all the sages: 'Today I give my head. I do not seek to become a wheel-turning king, I do not seek rebirth in heaven, I do not seek to become a Māra king, I do not seek to become Śakra, I do not seek to become Brahmā. I seek only the supreme, perfect enlightenment, so that those not yet converted may turn their hearts and be converted, those already converted may swiftly attain liberation, and those who have attained liberation may perfectly realize the cessation of nirvāṇa and reach the other shore. I further vow that after my death, my relics, like white mustard seeds, shall be enshrined in a great stūpa built in the Maṇiratnagarbha garden, so that all sentient beings may bow and make offerings. Those who see, hear, or rejoice: after death may they all be born in heaven, generate the aspiration for enlightenment, and escape the realm of birth and death.'

"Having made this vow, the brāhmaṇa said: 'That the king gives his inner wealth is exceedingly rare. In a future life, you will swiftly accomplish the path of the Buddha.' As these words were spoken, the king tied his hair to a branch of the aśoka tree, grasped the sharp sword, and severed his own head.

"At that time, the three-thousand great-thousand world system shook in six ways. In the sky, celestial beings proclaimed: 'Excellent! Excellent! The devaputra Candraprabha shall attain Buddhahood.' There also rained down utpala flowers, padma flowers, kumuda flowers, mandārava flowers, and the fragrances of aloeswood, powdered incense, and sandalwood, as various kinds of offerings. The body was then cremated with sandalwood. The relics were collected, and in the Maṇi garden and at the four crossroads, stūpas were each erected, where constant offerings were made. All sentient beings, present and future, who walk, stand, sit, or recline within this garden, or who bow and make offerings before the stūpa, after death shall be born in the six desire heavens or in the Brahma heavens."

At that time, the Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The devaputra Candraprabha of the past is my present self. The two counselors Mahācandra and Dharaṇīdhara are now Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana. The evil-eyed brāhmaṇa is now Devadatta. Because of these causes and conditions, they requested to enter parinirvāṇa before the Buddha."

At that time, the bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha taught, were all greatly joyful. They received it with faith, practiced in accordance, bowed to the Buddha, and withdrew.
