# 惟日雜難經

## The Sūtra of Miscellaneous Difficult Questions of Wéirì

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0760

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhī Qiān (支謙).

---

A bodhisattva who has first received the Way is called a *caryā* bodhisattva. *Caryā* means one who has undertaken practice and advances upward until reaching avaivartika. An avaivartika is one whose mind no longer turns back. Next comes the *bodhī* bodhisattva, then the flying bodhisattva. Next is the bodhisattva who will become a buddha, also called "one who ferries others across," and also called "a person of the Way."

The bodhisattva path also transcends the twelve gates, cutting off the three evil destinies. Such a one is born before the buddhas of the ten directions, and is not born on the tenth heaven. A stream-enterer also transcends the twelve gates and cuts off the three evil destinies, but is born on the tenth heaven. Both transcend the twelve gates, yet they are born in different places because the stream-enterer has sixteen mental factors, while the bodhisattva practices thirty-four mental factors. For this reason they are born in different places.

The bodhisattva's thirty-four mental factors are the sixteen mental factors of the Four Noble Truths plus the eighteen unshared dharmas, making thirty-four in all.

The bodhisattva, with thirty-four mental factors, can govern everything. The arhat practices with sixteen mental factors.

The bodhisattva's power is greater and can govern and expound everything. From stream-enterers to arhats, they can only cut off what they encounter face to face.

When one has fulfilled the thirty-four mental factors through seated practice, one then cultivates the Buddha's sixteen mental factors, advancing from the first upward through all sixteen, each with its own division. The four dhyānas are likewise.

A bodhisattva who diligently practices for twenty kalpas may attain buddhahood. Yet due to three kinds of mental states, buddhahood is not attained. What are the three? The aspiration for buddhahood, the aspiration for pratyekabuddhahood, and the aspiration for arhatship. Because of these three mental states, buddhahood is not attained. It depends on which predominates: if the aspiration for buddhahood predominates, one attains buddhahood; if the aspiration for pratyekabuddhahood predominates, one attains pratyekabuddhahood; if the aspiration for arhatship predominates, one attains arhatship. It is like a scale: the heavier side prevails.

There are householders who attain arhatship, the state of a non-returner, a once-returner, or a stream-enterer, and there are also those who attain the stage of an avaivartika bodhisattva.

The reason for leaving home and entering the mountains involves four causes and conditions. First, lest people say: "The bodhisattva is given to sensual indulgence; how could he attain the Way?" Second, a golden wheel-turning king also always renounces the kingdom. Third, Māra will come to harass the bodhisattva. Fourth, to seek the Buddha Way one should not remain as a householder.

A bodhisattva who has already received a prediction, having cut off the new and accepted the old, can through diligent practice certainly attain buddhahood within one hundred kalpas. But for those who are not diligent, whether they attain it eventually or not, there is no set time limit.

A bodhisattva who has received a prediction repays old debts and does not create new ones. This means that from the time of receiving the prediction, one repays the transgressions committed in previous lives as well as those committed in this life before receiving the precepts, including the transgressions of countless kalpas.

From a bodhisattva who has received a prediction down to a stream-enterer, all have past transgressions. When circumstances bring one into contact with old transgressions, one follows them and is reborn accordingly, and the aspiration for the Way becomes faint.

If someone expounds a profound sūtra and its meaning is immediately comprehended, then merit and demerit are as if shown directly, as if one is not to be reborn again. This means that when a person no longer creates transgressions, they are immediately resolved. A bodhisattva who has received a prediction can attain buddhahood within one hundred kalpas.

Śākyamuni Buddha had already completed ninety-one kalpas because through diligence he gained ninety kalpas. It is like a person who must travel a thousand miles tomorrow but walks nine hundred miles today: the total distance is the same. One should count from the ninety-first kalpa for a person who should complete one hundred kalpas. An avaivartika bodhisattva must complete the marks before succeeding; the one hundred kalpas of causes must be fulfilled, never exceeding one hundred kalpas. One who has not yet attained the avaivartika stage cannot complete the marks.

When a person gives rise to a single thought of the Way, its merit surpasses one hundred thousand kalpas of evil. What is a thought of the Way? It is mindfulness of the Four Noble Truths. This is a thought of the Way. Evil is like darkness. A thought of the Way is like light. When the sun rises, the darkness of the world is extinguished. All the bodhisattvas, hearing these words, greatly rejoiced and said "Homage to the Buddha! Giving rise to a single good thought yields the merit of one hundred kalpas."

When the bodhisattva offered his body to feed the starving tiger, instead of one hundred kalpas, he attained buddhahood in ninety-one kalpas because, not perceiving any pain or suffering, nine kalpas were eliminated.

When a bodhisattva has already given rise to the aspiration, to reproach his own body or to reproach the bodies of others constitutes a double reproach.

When the Buddha was a bodhisattva, he did not reproach others. For what reason then is it said that there was reproach? "Other" means that when one thinks, one's own mind is immediately disturbed. This suffering is unwelcome, and experiencing it oneself is self-reproach. To commit transgressions and destroy one's store of merit is also a form of self-reproach. Without knowing or seeing, how can one see another person's path and reproach others for their intentions?

Thus in one's present body, from these causes and conditions there is a double reproach. In the present life, it unsettles both others and oneself. From these causes and conditions comes reproach.

A person of the Way knows that when such poison arises, it can control a person who is caught in saṃsāra. For long ages the poison of old transgressions arises, covering over the roots of goodness.

The bodhisattva has five practices. First, rising early. Second, waiting for the right time. Third, not offending others. Fourth, constant mindfulness. Fifth, reciprocity. What is rising early? It means diligently being mindful of the Way. What is waiting for the right time? It means that when someone who needs something arrives, if a good intention arises, one should give immediately and act. What is not offending others? It means being able to benefit all beings. What is constant mindfulness? It means wishing that people may gain benefit, that those with legal troubles may find resolution, that the sick may be healed, and that the poor may receive generosity. What is reciprocity? It means that for a single coin received from someone, one wishes to repay a hundredfold or a thousandfold. This is reciprocity.

The bodhisattva encounters birth and death, yet has nothing to do with birth and death: there are the events of birth and death, but the intention of birth and death is absent.

The arhat cuts off the worldly and also cuts off the intention toward it. The bodhisattva cuts off the worldly but does not cut off the intention, because the intention must remain in order to attain the Buddha Way and ferry across the people of the world.

The bodhisattva fears worldly affairs and worldly talk, and so does not transgress and therefore cuts off worldly affairs.

The bodhisattva cuts off the intention toward birth and death but does not cut off the affairs of birth and death. This means being in the world and performing actions, but simply not doing evil.

The bodhisattva only cuts off the five sense-desires but does not cut off the intention. Why? Because the bodhisattva's intention merges with birth and death, so the intention is not cut off, in order to ferry across the people of the ten directions.

The bodhisattva practices generosity, upholds the precepts, leaves the household life, cultivates wisdom, diligence, patience, truthfulness, courage, mindfulness of good, and foresight. Through these ten practices one attains the wisdom of a buddha.

A bodhisattva born into a poor family should uphold the precepts. In a wealthy family, one should practice generosity. In a powerful family, one should practice patience. In the mountains, one should practice meditation.

The bodhisattva's four foundations of mindfulness lead to the seven factors of awakening. Having attained the four foundations of mindfulness, one then attains the seven factors of awakening.

The bodhisattva, going out to practice the Way, sees four testimonies. First, seeing old age. Second, seeing sickness. Third, seeing death. Fourth, seeing suffering.

Seeing the urgency of impermanence, one therefore practices the Way. Each day people hasten toward death. Why do they not fear the approach of death? Because they fear the appointed time has arrived.

When the bodhisattva has attained the Buddha Way, he then teaches the Four Noble Truths. Why? Teaching the Four Noble Truths involves four causes and conditions. First, because they have never been heard before. Second, because of meditation. Third, because of attaining the eye. Fourth, because of attaining wisdom.

Because of old age, sickness, death, and sorrow, and the inability to escape the great prison, the Four Noble Truths are taught.

The bodhisattva teaches sūtras for four reasons. First, the king is pleased. Second, the people desire it. Third, the mind is receptive. Fourth, the timing is right. What is the right timing? It is when people are well-disposed.

The bodhisattva examined himself: "What causes and conditions bring suffering?" He reflected that suffering comes from birth. What causes birth? It comes from formations. What causes formations? They come from ignorance. What causes ignorance? It comes from craving. What causes craving? It comes from contact; without contact, one does not obtain it.

The bodhisattva views one hundred kalpas as a single night. Why? Ordinary people cannot endure a single day of illness. The bodhisattva endures suffering for one hundred kalpas as though it were a person's single day of illness.

There are times when a single day of practicing the Way yields merit that is not exhausted in one hundred kalpas; therefore it is said that one views one hundred kalpas as a single night. There are times when the bodhisattva, turning to practice and expound the sūtras, has a mind not dwelling on birth and death, and so views one hundred kalpas as a single night.

The bodhisattva has not yet attained the Buddha Way due to three things. First, not meeting good people. Second, wishing to act but having neither the causes and conditions nor the resources. Third, not examining what is right and wrong. Because of these things, the Way has not yet been attained.

The bodhisattva has not attained the Buddha Way due to three things. First, being in the world for a long time without awakening. Second, not finding a good teacher. Third, the disordered mind not being fully extinguished.

The bodhisattva has not attained the Way due to three things. First, not hearing the teaching. Second, it not arising from one's own mind. Third, having no good teacher.

The bodhisattva both enters transgression and exits transgression. The mind following the world is entering transgression. The mind being in the world is delusion; transcending the world is wisdom.

The bodhisattva has two vows. First, "May I sleep in peace," meaning not thinking of sensual desire. Second, "May I walk in peace," meaning not thinking of jealousy, anger, or ignorance. These are the two vows.

The bodhisattva, when amid merit, dislikes merit, and when amid transgression, clings to transgression. Both transgression and merit are inevitably present and cannot be separated; one must simply discern them.

A bodhisattva who does not know past lives cannot attain buddhahood. Why? Because of not recognizing transgressions and committing evil.

The bodhisattva does not perform the actions of the three evil destinies, but must repay all the debts of the three evil destinies before attaining the avaivartika stage. The bodhisattva can calculate the total amount of transgressions of the three evil destinies, and only when one hundred kalpas are complete does one attain buddhahood.

After a bodhisattva has received a prediction, all ninety-eight fetters are still present, only they are thin. Why? Because they are all within the bodhisattva's practice. With the four immeasurable minds, one compassionately thinks of the beings of the ten directions in the five destinies, wishing to liberate them all: offering the body to feed a starving tiger, giving flesh to a hawk, leaping to one's death to save someone. When the marks are complete and the merit fulfilled, the fetters are not yet entirely severed. They are cut off only upon attaining buddhahood.

The bodhisattva can give his wife, children, and even his head to others. What state of mind does this require? There are two reasons. First, thinking: "I do not wish to cause others to become angry." Second, giving to them enables the other person to attain a settled mind; not giving would cause the other person's mind to become disordered. The transgression of lust exists, but the transgression of anger is graver. Fearing that the other person will become angry, one gives one's head, wife, and children.

The bodhisattva offers his body to feed a starving tiger and gives his head to others for three reasons. First, calculating that the body will inevitably be abandoned, one uses it for generosity and the practice of merit. Second, it is like repaying an enemy: when the enemy dies, one rejoices. The bodhisattva knows that all evil arises from the body and that countless sufferings and reproaches all come from the body. Having reckoned thus, one no longer wishes to see it. The body is like an enemy; therefore one does not love it. Third, wishing to diligently advance, to fulfill merit, and to hasten along the Way.

When the bodhisattva gives his head, eyes, wife, and children: giving the eyes means not being attached to form, so one frees oneself from the eyes. Giving the head means that when evil arises, it is immediately stopped; this is cutting off the head. Giving the wife means eliminating lust, anger, and delusion.

Practicing the Way involves two things. First, examination. Second, calculation. Examination is for determining what is light and heavy. Calculation is for determining what is much and little.

A bodhisattva who has received a prediction of attaining buddhahood in one hundred kalpas may spend one kalpa as a renunciant and ninety-nine kalpas as a layperson.

When a bodhisattva gives his wife and children to another, and at that time sees them being beaten, he simply thinks of them as bones and flesh, not thinking "these are mine," and therefore gives them.

The bodhisattva, seeking buddhahood, should practice according to the Buddha's Dharma. One must obtain the thirty-two marks. If one's practice accords with the Dharma, one cannot fail. If the marks are not attained, one does not attain the Buddha Way.

A single thought of compassion from a bodhisattva surpasses meditation a hundredfold.

The bodhisattva is in the world and has shares in the three evil destinies, but does not fall into them. Why do births in the human and heavenly realms predominate? Because one does not obtain what leads to the three destinies, one no longer falls into them. When the three actions of body, speech, and mind are exhausted, the three evil destinies are also exhausted.

The bodhisattva has left the five destinies and always possesses the merit of wisdom. When a person dies, they inevitably have a share in the five destinies. The bodhisattva also has the share of wisdom and immediately knows whether the destination is near or far.

The bodhisattva's share of knowledge lies in stream-entry and once-returning; the share does not lie in non-returning. Why? Because a non-returner does not come back.

Within one buddha-field there are three-thousandfold great-thousandfold worlds. People's births and deaths pervade those worlds. There is not a place as small as a hair-tip where one has not been. There is nothing one has not been, except that one has never been an avaivartika or a once-returner. Everything else one has experienced countless times.

When the Buddha was a bodhisattva, this thought arose: "Through what kinds of practice and causes and conditions does one obtain the utmost merit in the present life?" Thus he obtained the knowledge of past lives and found it comes from these three practices: generosity, restraint, and gathering together. Generosity has two kinds: first, the gift of Dharma; second, the gift of material things. Guarding body, speech, and consciousness is restraint. Guarding the mind is gathering together. Through generosity one also attains the cutting off of greed. Through restraint one cuts off anger and mental confusion. Through gathering and self-discipline one cuts off ignorance. Through generosity one also attains right intention. Through restraint one attains right speech. Through gathering one attains peace of mind in action. Through generosity one sees the merit of generosity. Through restraint one speaks of the merit of mindfulness. Through gathering one speaks of the merit of intention.

The reason the bodhisattva practices generosity is that wherever one goes in the five destinies, one always enjoys abundance. Differing from ordinary people, one always becomes a king, with wisdom and a compassionate heart. One merely completes the repayment of transgressions without creating new ones; although amid transgressions, one does not commit transgressions.

It is the same in the human realm. A bodhisattva living with wife and children: at twenty-five, in a single day one ages by thirty days; in a month, one ages by a year; in a hundred days, one becomes fifty-one years old. The bodhisattva merely restrains evil; he does not restrain the household.

What is called a disordered mind is a transgression, not peace. One should distance oneself and withdraw from it.

An undisordered mind is merit. Even while in the household, it is like being in prison. One need only recognize the transgressions, and one will never again be born in a place of suffering. This is what it means to be a bodhisattva.

If a person seeking the Way cannot practice patience and endurance, that person attains stream-entry.

In past lives the Buddha, as a bodhisattva, said: "The Buddha Way is difficult to attain." This single statement eliminated forty kalpas. By offering his body to feed a starving tiger, he advanced beyond the other bodhisattvas by nine kalpas. As Sudāna he received a prediction. Through complete diligent practice of the Way, one will certainly attain buddhahood.

Having a wife, one cannot attain the Buddha Way. The bodhisattva, while a householder, had a wife. Why? Buddhahood was attained because he abandoned wife and children for six years before attaining the Buddha Way.

The bodhisattva regards wife and children as enemies. Why? They bring suffering, increase vexation, disturb the mind, and multiply transgressions. By following craving and attachment one cannot see the Way. Therefore they are like enemies.

When one sees wife and children, one should regard them with the mind one has toward enemies. Do not follow craving and attachment. When intention arises, immediately become aware of it. This is awakened intention. It is the same with all possessions and with one's own body.

In heaven above, on earth below, and throughout the ten directions, everything is fire. Why? Because fire already exists.

A bodhisattva takes a wife for four reasons. First, because in past lives they shared the same merit. Second, to repay transgressions. Third, because they are destined to have children together. Fourth, a wise person who takes a wife swiftly attains the Way. Without these four conditions, one would not take a wife.

The bodhisattva path is to aspire to sever the three poisons for all the people of the ten directions. For this reason one attains the Buddha Way. Some practice the Way only wishing to sever the three poisons for themselves; therefore they attain arhatship.

The bodhisattva destroys his own delusion and also destroys the delusion of the people of the ten directions. The arhat destroys his own delusion but does not destroy the delusion of others.

The bodhisattva greatly awakens and widely teaches people, enabling them to practice the Way. This is destroying people's delusion.

To be mindful of and concerned for the people of the ten directions: this is practicing the bodhisattva path. To be concerned only for oneself and not mindful of the people of the ten directions: this is the path of the arhat.

The bodhisattva destroys evil; the arhat does not destroy evil. The bodhisattva teaches people sūtras and precepts, causing them to follow the Dharma path: this is destroying evil. The arhat guards only himself; therefore it is said he does not destroy evil.

The bodhisattva severs his own suffering and also severs the suffering of the people of the ten directions; this is attaining the Buddha Way. The arhat severs his own suffering but does not sever the suffering of others; therefore he attains arhatship.

The reason a practitioner of the bodhisattva path turns and attains arhatship instead is this: the mind thinks, "The births and deaths on the Buddha path are innumerable; everything one does will have its consequences." The mind reckons this is difficult and turns toward arhatship. One further reckons that an arhat no longer repays transgressions, no longer undergoes birth and death, and directly takes liberation from the world. For this reason one turns toward arhatship. If one has no such thought, no death-turning, and does not avoid suffering, one will certainly attain the Buddha Way.

Both the bodhisattva and the arhat practice through the thirty-seven factors of awakening. The reason one attains buddhahood is that one holds four mental states that the bodhisattva always practices. What are the four? First, "Fortunately I have encountered the Buddha's teaching and recognized the Buddha. I vow that my body shall become like the Buddha." Second, "I shall always hold a mind of compassion toward the ten directions. With all my strength I shall never depart from compassion. I shall liberate all the beings and non-humans of the ten directions." Third, "My original and highest vow is for buddhahood; my mind shall not turn." Fourth, "I vow to be in the world seeking the Way, upholding the precepts, teaching others, increasing wisdom, and waiting for the appointed time." Having these four mental states, one attains buddhahood. Without these four mental states, one attains arhatship.

The Buddha's mind is equanimous. Therefore there are not two separate paths; it depends only on which intention one practices.

Following craving, one remains in the five destinies without liberation, and there is old age, sickness, death, and sorrow: these join together as suffering. When the mind does not follow craving and attachment, it is like using water to extinguish fire. When one follows craving and attachment, it is like adding firewood to increase the fire.

One should contemplate with discernment. Following discernment, one attains the Way.

A bodhisattva takes a wife for four reasons. First, fearing others will say he practices the Way because he could not find a wife. Second, fearing others will say he practices the Way because he is alone and has no wife or children. Third, the roots from past lives have not yet been exhausted. Fourth, because evil companions urge him to marry.

The bodhisattva both severs the five aggregates and does not sever them. What is severed? The five clinging-aggregates and the twelve links of dependent origination are severed. What is not severed? The bodhisattva, in practicing, follows the causes and conditions of the Way. This is what is not severed.

When the bodhisattva's transgressions are fully repaid, the Way is attained.

The bodhisattva has four kinds of fearlessness. First, not taking others' money. Second, not violating others' wives or daughters. Third, not engaging in divisive speech. Fourth, not being jealous or envious.

The bodhisattva has cut off the three evil destinies, yet they still exist; one simply does not fall into them.

The bodhisattva's three poisons have not yet arisen. At the time of buddhahood, there are no three poisons; they arise only through causes and conditions.

When the bodhisattva sat in meditation for six years, just as he was about to attain the Way, the three poisons all arose simultaneously: lust, anger, and delusion caused the mind to think, "Has Devadatta taken my wife? Has he surpassed me? Will he take my possessions?" As soon as the thought arose, it was immediately extinguished with the thought: "Since countless kalpas ago I have been cutting off these three evils." Why then did the thought arise again? As soon as the recollection came, it was extinguished, and the Way was immediately attained.

When the bodhisattva sat in meditation for six years, eating one grain of rice and one sesame seed per day, there were four reasons. First, to cut off greed. Second, to repay transgressions. Third, to demonstrate that one does not starve. Fourth, to stop hunger.

One should understand clearly: not eating is the point. He feared that people would starve themselves to death thinking this leads to the Way.

When the bodhisattva sat practicing the Way for six years, eating one grain of rice and one sesame seed per day, he entered the water to bathe and fell down, unable to rise. A deva then pressed a tree branch down low, and he grasped it and stood up.

When the bodhisattva sat beneath the tree for six years, worms, snails, mosquitoes, and gadflies did not trouble him for four reasons. First, from countless past lives he had never killed a living being. Second, he practiced the mind of equanimity. Third, devas, ghosts, and spirits protected him. Fourth, the power of the Way was strong, and also the place was pure.

The bodhisattva has four things of which he never tires. First, generosity without tiring. Second, hearing sūtras without tiring. Third, purity without tiring. Fourth, making merit without tiring.

When the bodhisattva first left home to practice the Way, the great ministers said to him: "Now you are the crown prince and live in the household. Why do you leave?" The prince replied: "I leave because of three kinds of suffering: old age, sickness, and death." The ministers replied: "Old age, sickness, and death are constant facts of life. Why leave on their account?"

The prince said: "If one catches even a single illness, one is never happy about it. How much more so when it is constant?"

When the bodhisattva was born, he fell to the ground, walked seven steps, stopped, raised his right hand, and declared: "I am the teacher above and below heaven." He then stopped and walked no more.

When the bodhisattva was a prince and went to study writing at the teacher's school, the teacher asked: "What do you wish to learn?" The prince said: "I wish to learn sixty kinds of script." The teacher asked: "What are the sixty kinds?" The prince then explained them in order. The teacher said: "I know only one kind of script. I cannot know all the rest." The prince said: "Then teach me what you know." Having received the teacher's instruction, the prince said: "This is missing two characters." The teacher then studied those two characters from the prince. The teacher said: "If the prince already knows this much, why learn from me?" The prince said: "Even so, one should observe the proper way of having a teacher."

When the bodhisattva first left home and had traveled a hundred miles, he removed his garments, ornaments, and jewels and entrusted them to Chandaka to take back. The white horse Kaṇṭhaka shed tears and licked his feet. Chandaka said: "May no one ever have such a wish as this." The prince replied: "No one in the world is as foolish as the likes of you. Why? The world contains nothing but old age, sickness, death, sorrow, and suffering. What is there to wish for? It is like a prisoner in jail: who among them is happy? One has not yet escaped this, and yet one makes more wishes. When will liberation come? It is like a deer that escapes from a hunter's net: would it ever think of going back into the net? The deer that escapes the net will never return. When I was a householder, I had this thought for a long time. It has been since countless kalpas that I have had this intention, not something that arose only today."

The bodhisattva's mother passed away seven days after his birth for four reasons. First, because she bore the bodhisattva, devas came to attend and bring her food. Second, according to the dharma of birth and death, one should bow to one's mother, but because the bodhisattva was so exalted, the mother passed away on the seventh day. Third, in past lives his mother's destiny was naturally thus. Fourth, it is like a person who has earned a reward and is enfeoffed: she ascended to be born in heaven.

When the bodhisattva had not yet attained the Buddha Way, he had five dreams. First, he used Mount Sumeru as a pillow. Second, he used the earth as a bed. Third, he covered the ocean water with his hand. Fourth, the entire world was covered in excrement, and he walked upon it without soiling his feet. Fifth, before his heart there grew a tree that reached up to the twenty-seventh heaven. Dreaming of Mount Sumeru as a pillow means that above and below heaven, none is more exalted than the Buddha. Using the earth as a bed means the Buddha's body is tall enough to reach the twenty-eighth heaven. Covering the ocean with his hand means that among all who expound sūtras and the Way, none surpasses the Buddha. Walking on excrement without soiling his feet means that the cravings and desires of the world cannot defile the Buddha's mind. The tree growing before his heart means the Buddha's voice can be heard up to the twenty-seventh heaven.

The bodhisattva follows women for four reasons. First, to encourage women in diligence. Second, also wishing women to follow the practice. Third, because of residual transgressions from past lives meeting their consequences. Fourth, because of past vows to teach women.

The devas test the bodhisattva for three reasons. First, one who will attain buddhahood must have no greed or attachment. Second, if the bodhisattva gives rise to unwholesome thoughts, they say: "You are to attain the Buddha Way; why do you act like this?" And the bodhisattva thereby increases in diligence. Third, when buddhahood is attained, they will request liberation; therefore they go to test him.

The bodhisattva practices the six pāramitās. The arhat also practices these six things. Yet the marks do not come to completion for the arhat because the arhat has only *dāna*, without *pāramitā*. *Dāna* means giving in generosity. *Pāra* means crossing over birth and death. *Mitā* means without limit. The arhat only gives generously in all ways but does not vow to ferry across the ten directions; therefore he has only *dāna*. The bodhisattva wishes to ferry across the humans and non-humans of the ten directions; therefore he has *pāramitā*. All six are likewise.

When the Buddha was a bodhisattva, he wished to make an offering of food to a buddha. He placed five small beans in the buddha's alms-bowl, but one bean fell to the ground. He later became a golden wheel-turning king for eighty lifetimes, ruling the four continents of the world: this was the merit of the four beans that entered the bowl. The bean that did not enter the bowl further earned him birth in heaven for eight months. This is what it means to have *dāna-pāramitā*.

When the bodhisattva gives material things in accord with the Dharma, the Buddha's intention follows the gift, wishing that the recipient may be at peace. If the thought arises wishing to obtain merit, one should then think: "For the hungry, the cold, and the poor, these things should be given, that all may be at peace." This is generosity according to the Dharma path. Giving a single coin with this intention surpasses ten million given otherwise. Thus a small gift can surpass a large one, because the Way-intention brings peace.

If at the time of death a person's mind is upon impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self, that person attains the avaivartika stage and receives a prediction. If one originally sought arhatship, one attains the state of a non-returner. The four contemplations of impermanence are difficult to achieve. The reason one succeeds is because of diligent practice in past lives.

The bodhisattva sat beneath a tree in meditation. A crow, a dove, and a snake asked each other: "What is the greatest suffering?" The crow said: "Hunger is the greatest suffering. Why do I know it is suffering? When hungry, one cannot walk and cannot do anything. People die of starvation. Therefore I know hunger is suffering." The dove said: "Desire alone is suffering. How do I know? The mind's craving for objects of desire is endless. People all die because of desire. Therefore I know desire is suffering." The snake said: "Anger alone is suffering. How do I know? When a person is angry, there is nowhere to flee. One wishes to kill oneself and also wishes to kill others. Therefore I know anger is suffering." The bodhisattva said: "Each of you has spoken of one thing. I shall now speak of one thing for you: only not being born is the absence of suffering. Whoever has a body cannot avoid suffering." This parable is found among the bodhisattva's one hundred and eight paths of craving.

Before the bodhisattva attained the Buddha Way, he had five teachers. First, a Nirgrantha. Second, Makkhali. Third, Ājīvika. Fourth, Rākṣasa. Fifth, Rāhula-kān.

When teaching the Dharma, one should not accept payment. The bodhisattva paid five million for a single verse. The purchase has three levels. First, a straightforward purchase. Second, a purchase of reciprocity. Third, a purchase of repaying a gift. The bodhisattva, mindful of kindness, repaid the gift; the recipient also incurred no fault.

The bodhisattva's practice requires making vows and blessings for the people of the ten directions. Yet it also says one should settle the mind in single-pointed concentration. If one's own mind is not settled, one cannot make vows and blessings either. Why? Because the mind is caught in birth and death. If one's own mind is not settled, one cannot settle others' minds either.

*Dharma* means "the teaching." *Agra* means "what is to come." *Sat* means "constant." *Pralaya* means "tears flowing." *Amṛta* means "life beyond counting."

The Bodhisattva Sadāprarudita constantly wept with tears flowing for four reasons. First, being unable to comprehend the meaning of the sūtras. Second, not knowing the person through whom, by causes and conditions, one attains the Way. Third, being mindful of the people of the ten directions. Fourth, thinking: "I wish to ferry across the ten directions; when will I attain buddhahood?"

The bodhisattva said: "One should hold to wisdom. With mindful intention, know arising and ceasing. Practice the Way in the unconditioned. One need only guard the intention and practice the thirty-seven factors of awakening." The reason one additionally practices generosity and upholds the precepts is that the bodhisattva has compassion for people. To swiftly sever birth and death, and also because merit is not yet complete, therefore one practices generosity. For the next life, one does not wish to fall into a poor family; if born into a poor family, one has nothing and falls into adverse circumstances. If born into a wealthy family, the mind is at peace and does not follow wickedness. For this reason, one practices generosity. The reason for upholding precepts is that only with a long life can one practice the Way. Without longevity, one cannot practice the Way. Why? Perhaps one lives only ten years; before any understanding is gained, life is over. Therefore one upholds the precepts. By not killing, one attains long life. The reason for not stealing is this: one considers that if the owner discovers the theft, there will be beatings, and having such evil intentions will bring further consequences. Therefore one does not steal. The reason for not engaging in divisive speech is this: the Buddha Way is truthful; divisive speech does not accord with the Way, and later one will not be trusted by others. Even now this is clearly evil. Therefore one does not engage in divisive speech. The reason for not engaging in sexual misconduct is this: it is like facing east and being unable to see the west. When the mind is upon lust, it does not follow the Way. Therefore one abstains from sexual misconduct. The reason for not drinking alcohol is this: when drunk, one engages in harsh speech, divisive speech, and acts contrary to the Dharma. Even a good person's mind becomes disordered. Therefore one does not drink alcohol.

The bodhisattva bid farewell to his parents and went to become a renunciant. His parents said: "What you are doing is upside down. The bodhisattva path requires making vows for the people of the ten directions that they may be liberated. Yet now you bring sorrow to your own parents nearby. What kind of practice is this?" The bodhisattva replied: "Because I have sorrow, my parents have sorrow. When I attain the Way, I will be free of sorrow, and my parents will also be free of sorrow. It is like when a relative has sorrow: one's own body also grieves. Or like two people traveling together: when one has sorrow, one's own body also grieves. Because the child's sorrow is not resolved, the parents are caused to sorrow. Once the child attains the Way, the parents are freed from sorrow."

Then the maiden Vimaladattā addressed Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva: "If Mañjuśrī practices the most profound path, the bodhisattva has already received a prediction. This means that from causes and conditions, the profound is profoundly severed. There is no person who follows causes and conditions to practice. Such causes and conditions neither come nor go. They cannot be observed with the eye, nor contemplated with the mind. They cannot be recognized nor practiced. If causes and conditions are profoundly severed, then there is nothing that exists and nothing to which anything belongs." Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva replied: "Originally and essentially, that which is profound is profound." Vimaladattā further said: "Since there is no original, there is no essential. In this case, Mañjuśrī, is this wisdom or not wisdom?" Mañjuśrī replied: "How is this matter? What should one say about it?" Vimaladattā further said: "What is to be said has already passed." Mañjuśrī further said: "The Tathāgata's awakened practice cannot be seen through birth and death." Mañjuśrī then fell silent and spoke no more.

The maiden bodhisattva Vimaladattā, also named "Free from Defilement," addressed Śāriputra: "Out of compassion for me." Śāriputra replied: "Wise, supremely wise, your wisdom exists. If it exists, then it should be empty and false. Being false, all things are nonexistent. Being already nonexistent, they are unborn. Being unborn, they do not come together. If this is so," Śāriputra said, "there is no wisdom."

There was a bodhisattva named Vasumitra who questioned an arhat about a sūtra. The arhat could not explain it, so with single-pointed mind he ascended to ask Maitreya. Having asked, Maitreya replied to Vasumitra, saying: "What you asked, I shall explain in due order." Vasumitra, realizing what had happened, challenged the arhat: "Did you just now, with single-pointed mind, go up to ask Maitreya?" The arhat said: "Indeed. Going up with single-pointed mind to ask involves three causes and conditions. First, minds knowing each other directly. Second, sending a transformation body to ask. Third, through practices in past lives, upon hearing one immediately understands."

The Bodhisattva Vasumitra served his teacher and recited the four Āgamas. He was then to take flowers and scatter them upon the teacher, saying: "I have now recited the four Āgamas." The teacher, however, had forgotten and could no longer recall them. Vasumitra then reflected: "I wish to gather the essential sayings from within these four Āgamas and compile them into a single sūtra, which can be taught to the four classes of disciples." When the monks heard this sūtra, they all rejoiced greatly and came in large numbers to listen, such that there was no time for sitting meditation. The monks said: "The reason we listen to sūtras is solely for the purpose of seated practice. Let them all practice the Way; we should not continue listening to sūtras but should simply go away." Vasumitra, knowing what was in their minds, put his hand into fire without being burned, and said: "Is this not diligence?" He then sat upon a great boulder. A practitioner of the Way should sit on a soft seat. Vasumitra said: "I will leap from this boulder. Before the boulder lands, I shall attain arhatship." Having leaped, he refused to stand up. A deva then pulled the boulder from above, preventing it from falling, and said: "You are pursuing the bodhisattva path. All of us shall gain liberation through you. In twenty kalpas you shall attain buddhahood. Do not destroy your good intention." Among them, there were monks who had not yet attained the Way, and they said: "This is an evil man who should not be allowed in the country." They passed written messages to this effect. Vasumitra sent someone to obtain the letter. The letter was then changed to read: "This is a good man who teaches and opens people's minds. He does not wish to exalt himself but only fears that evil people will fall into transgression. He further wishes to ferry across the people of the ten directions. His aspiration for the Way is like the sands of the Ganges. By way of analogy, in the buddha-fields of the Tathāgata, there is no attachment to perfect enlightenment. What I have said should serve all the bodhisattvas of the ten directions. It is not fully in accord with the mind. It is a delusion that has been attained. There is nothing surpassing truth. What the Buddha has attained is also thus."

The bodhisattva practices four kinds of purity through which attainment comes swiftly. What are the four? First, purity of person. Second, purity of Dharma. Third, purity of what is worthy. Fourth, purity of intention. Being in a buddha-field, therefore pure. These are the four purities that bring swift attainment.

"The bodhisattva has four practices of great love through which attainment comes swiftly. What are the four? Loving the body, loving speech, loving the mind, and loving stillness. These are the four practices of great love that bring swift attainment. There are also four kinds of conduct toward others that bring swift attainment: deeply patient conduct toward others brings swift attainment; conduct toward others that earns their recognition of one's goodness brings swift attainment; conduct of sharing merit with others brings swift attainment. These are the four kinds of conduct toward others that swiftly produce excellent disciples."

Śāriputra said: "According to what is in the Buddha's mind, I ask about what he wishes and thinks, which is the bodhisattva's pure practice. There is no other wisdom."

In the south there are bodhisattvas. The city has a circumference of sixteen thousand miles. In it is the most honored bodhisattva named Mañjuśrī. He has taught immeasurable numbers of those who have already attained buddhahood. His merit is tenfold that of the Bodhisattva Dharmodgata. The city is entirely made of the seven jewels.

The city where the Bodhisattva Dharmodgata dwells has a circumference of sixteen thousand miles. The ground is entirely of the seven jewels.

In the north, among the bodhisattvas, there is a bodhisattva named Sarvālu, whose merit ranks next after the Bodhisattva Dharmodgata.

The Buddha has eighteen unshared dharmas. From the time of first attaining unsurpassed perfect enlightenment until the final passing into nirvāṇa without remainder, these are heard throughout:

First, the Tathāgata's conduct is without error. Second, the Tathāgata's conduct is without outflows. Third, the Tathāgata's conduct: the mind never forgets its intention. Fourth, the Tathāgata's conduct never departs from samādhi. Fifth, the Tathāgata's conduct does not turn back. Sixth, the Tathāgata's conduct: there is no unexhausted contemplation. Seventh, the Tathāgata's conduct: all wishes of the mind are accomplished. Eighth, the Tathāgata's conduct: diligence is never diminished. Ninth, the Tathāgata's conduct: mindfulness never ceases midway. Tenth, the Tathāgata's conduct: samādhi is never abandoned. Eleventh, the Tathāgata's conduct: wisdom is without equal. Twelfth, the Tathāgata's conduct: the contemplation of liberation from the world is without remainder. Thirteenth, the Tathāgata's conduct: knowledge of the past is without measure. Fourteenth, the Tathāgata's conduct: knowledge of the future is without limit. Fifteenth, the Tathāgata's conduct: knowledge of the present is without error. Sixteenth, the Tathāgata's conduct: there is pervading wisdom throughout the body, and wherever it turns, it knows everything. Seventeenth, the Tathāgata's conduct: there is sharp wisdom, and whatever is spoken never departs from knowledge. Eighteenth, the Tathāgata's conduct: there is dispersing wisdom, and the mind departs from discursive thought. These are the eighteen unshared dharmas.
