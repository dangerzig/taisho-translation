# Essential Abbreviated Deportment for Bhikṣus Following the Dharmaguptaka Vinaya

## *Zōng Sìfēn Bǐqiū Suímén Yàolüè Xíngyí* (宗四分比丘隨門要略行儀)

Taishō 2791

Author unknown.

*Note: This is a practical vinaya manual for bhikṣus following the Dharmaguptaka (Sìfēn, "Four-Part") vinaya, the monastic code that became normative in Chinese Buddhism. The text is a handbook of karmic procedures (karmavācanā) and liturgical formulae for the routine operations of monastic life. It includes: the procedures for establishing a sīmā (boundary) and ordination-platform (jiètán), and for dissolving them; the formulae for the fortnightly recitation of the prātimokṣa (precepts), for the summer retreat (ānjiū), for receiving permission to leave the retreat (shòurì), for expressing one's consent to saṃgha decisions (shuōyù), for the pravāraṇā (self-examination at the end of the retreat), for distributing the possessions of deceased monks, for formally receiving and relinquishing the three robes (saṃghāṭī, uttarāsaṅga, antaravāsaka), the sitting-cloth (niṣīdana), and the alms-bowl (pātra), for the "purification through transfer" (zhǎnzhuǎn jìngshī) of surplus possessions, for receiving non-time beverages and seven-day medicines, and for the confession and repentance procedures for naihsargika-pāyantika offenses. It concludes with the "six recollections" (liùniàn) to be practiced daily and rules for purifying money, fruit, and vegetables. The text is a compact compendium of the liturgical forms essential for maintaining a Dharmaguptaka monastic community.*

## Establishing Boundaries

The natural boundary (*zìrán jiè*) of a village, each face sixty-three paces; the natural boundary of a forest hermitage (*āraṇya*), each face five leagues. When assembling before [the boundary], there are three types of markers. The inner markers are the ordination-platform boundary. Outside them, on all four sides, a strip of natural open ground, and beyond that within one league is the inner boundary of the great boundary (*dàjiè*). The outermost perimeter is the outer boundary of the great boundary. All markers must be clearly visible and unmistakable.

### Karmic Procedure for Establishing the Ordination-Platform

"Virtuous saṃgha, may you give your attention. The bhikṣus of this dwelling have marked the four-sided boundary of a small precinct. If the time is right and the saṃgha consents, may the saṃgha now, within this four-sided boundary, establish an ordination-platform. This is the motion (*bái*)."

"Virtuous saṃgha, may you give your attention. The bhikṣus of this dwelling have marked the four-sided boundary of a small precinct. The saṃgha now establishes an ordination-platform within this four-sided boundary. Will the venerable elders who consent to the saṃgha establishing an ordination-platform within this four-sided boundary remain silent? Will those who do not consent please speak?"

"The saṃgha has consented to establishing the ordination-platform within this four-sided boundary. The saṃgha consents by its silence. Let this matter be held thus."

### Karmic Procedure for Establishing the Great Boundary

"Virtuous saṃgha, may you give your attention. The bhikṣus of this dwelling have marked the four-sided great boundary. If the time is right and the saṃgha consents, may the saṃgha now, within this four-sided boundary, establish the great boundary: one dwelling-place, one recitation of precepts. This is the motion."

## Fortnightly Recitation of the Precepts

**For two or three monks, mutual face-to-face recitation:**

"Venerable, may you give your attention with one mind. Today, the fifteenth of the bright fortnight, the saṃgha recites the precepts. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, am pure." The other responds: "So be it."

**For one monk alone, mental recollection:**

[Same formula, omitting "Venerable, may you give your attention with one mind."]

## Summer Retreat

**Face-to-face declaration for the summer retreat:**

"Venerable, may you give your attention with one mind. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, now relying on such-and-such saṃghārāma, undertake the first three-month summer retreat. If the dwelling is damaged, I shall repair it. Upon whom do you rely as your vinaya-master? I rely on vinaya-master so-and-so. If I have doubts, I shall ask him." The other responds: "So be it."

[If entering after the seventeenth of the fourth month up to the sixteenth of the fifth month, one should say "the latter three-month summer retreat."]

## Expressing Consent

There are four kinds: (1) expressing one's own consent, (2) expressing consent on behalf of another, (3) expressing one's own consent together with another's, and (4) transferring consent.

**Receiving the seven-strip robe (uttarāsaṅga)** and **five-strip robe (antaravāsaka):** [Similar formulae.]

## Receiving Non-Time Beverages and Medicines

**Non-time beverage:** "Venerable, may you give your attention with one mind. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, because of illness from thirst, this is pear-juice. In order to consume it outside the proper time, I now receive it from the venerable."

**Seven-day medicine:** "Venerable, may you give your attention with one mind. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, because of such-and-such illness, this ghee is a seven-day medicine. In order to store and consume it for seven days, I now receive it from the venerable."

**Lifetime medicine:** "Venerable, may you give your attention with one mind. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, because of such-and-such illness, this ginger, pepper, and the like is a lifetime medicine. In order to store and consume it for life, I now receive it from the venerable." The other responds: "So be it."

## Recognition and Disclosure of Offenses

"The Buddha said: One should go to a pure bhikṣu, maintain proper deportment, and declare the name of the offense committed. 'Venerable, may you give your attention with one mind. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, have committed such-and-such an offense. I now disclose it before the venerable. Hereafter I shall repent according to the Dharma.'"

## The Six Daily Recollections

(1) Know whether the month is long or short. (2) Know one's food-status [whether one is eating communal food, begged food, invited food, etc.]. (3) Know the date and time of one's ordination. (4) Know whether one's robes and bowl have been received and purified [i.e., whether surplus possessions have been properly dealt with]. (5) Know whether one is eating with or apart from the assembly. (6) Know whether one is ill or not.

---

## Purification of Money, Fruit, and Vegetables

Regarding money and valuables: according to the Dharmaguptaka vinaya, keeping them is a forfeitable offense. According to the *Sarvāstivāda-vinaya-vibhāṣā*, it is permitted if purified. Since the laity's faith is weak and it is difficult to find a proper "purification-master," and since the monastic community cannot truly avoid handling money, one should at minimum, following the scriptural teaching, declare the purification formula: "These impure objects I should not keep. If they become pure, I shall receive them."

The five kinds of seeds [root-seeds, stem-seeds, joint-seeds, bark-seeds, and actual seeds] and the five kinds of fruit [stone-fruit, skin-fruit, shell-fruit, husk-fruit, and pod-fruit] must be properly rendered [by one of five methods]: (1) purification by fire, (2) purification by incision, (3) purification by knife, (4) purification by bird-pecking, (5) purification by [the seed's] inability to germinate.

## Five Rules for Entering the Assembly

As the *Ekottara Āgama* says, the Buddha told the bhikṣus: "A bhikṣu going to the assembly should first possess five things: (1) one should maintain a compassionate mind; (2) one should be humble and self-effacing, like wiping dust; (3) one should understand proper sitting and rising, neither sitting when seniors are standing nor rising when juniors are seated; (4) in the assembly, one should not engage in miscellaneous discussion of worldly affairs, but either teach the Dharma oneself or request someone to teach it; (5) if one sees something objectionable in the assembly, one should bear it in patient silence. Why? Lest the saṃgha be divided."

The flourishing or decline of the Dharma truly depends on the people. The guidelines are briefly set forth; the text may be grasped as it stands. For all other aspects of deportment, consult the detailed vinaya texts. May we fortunately receive the holy intention and heed this bright admonition.
