# 釋肇序
## Commentary on Sengzhao's Preface

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2776

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, recorded by Tiqing (體請).

---

"Depth" means profundity. "To open" means to disclose. Just as in the world, to open something deep requires the royal seal, as a simile. Therefore the *Lotus Sūtra* says: "This Dharma-flower treasury is deep, firm, secluded, and remote; no person can reach it." "Words and characteristics are quiescent." This sūtra says: "The characteristics of dharmas being thus, how can they be expressed?" and so forth. This can only be realized through wisdom.

"The sage's responses are not the same" means that the responsive manifestations differ. Sentient beings' susceptibilities differ, and the dharma-body buddha's responses differ accordingly. Since the Buddha's body follows susceptibility and accords with conditions in a thousand different ways, it is said they are "not the same." This is a general designation of the inconceivability of source and trace.

"Without the source there would be nothing from which to extend the traces; without the traces there would be nothing by which to reveal the source." This means that if the sage did not abide in the fundamental ground of non-duality, he would have nothing from which to extend the thousand different extraordinary traces. If the multitudes of beings did not encounter the thousand different extraordinary traces, they would have nothing by which to awaken to the fundamental ground of non-duality. Therefore Master Zhao said: "He relies on the effect to reveal the source; he entrusts the coarse to express the subtle." This is what he means.

The Tiantai school distinguishes source and trace in roughly five levels: (1) conventional and ultimate, (2) principle and teaching, (3) principle and practice, (4) substance and function, (5) provisional and real. In all cases, the source provides the basis from which traces are extended; by tracing the traces, one arrives at the source. Though source and trace differ, they are inconceivably one. Because source and trace are mutually dependent, they are explained together.

Therefore the *Book of Odes* says: "In the south there is a drooping tree; the kudzu vine entwines it." "Drooping below" is called *jiū*; "coiling above" is called *gōu*. If a tall pine raises its trunk above the clouds, and the kudzu vine creeps and spreads among the thorns below, then although the kudzu vine has the merit of climbing, it cannot depart from below the weeds. But if the drooping tree receives the kudzu vine's upward clinging, then even though it is a thousand fathoms of gnarled wood, one may ascend by relying upon it.

The Dharma analogy is the same. Suppose the Tathāgata rests his spirit above the nine abodes while living beings are submerged below the three evil destinies, and their mutual attraction cannot meet. Then although sentient beings possess the wisdom of original awakening, they cannot escape the suffering of cyclic existence. But if sentient beings have sincere faith and devoted aspiration, and all buddhas extend the traces of their great compassion, then although the rank of the ten powers is exalted and three incalculable kalpas are far off, through tireless self-exertion, all can be accomplished. The meaning of source and trace is thus made clear.

### Third: Explanation of the Title

From this point, the third section explains the title. "Therefore he commanded the attendant and designated it by name." In the Chapter on Entrustment, the Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'What Vimalakīrti Taught,' and is also called 'The Dharma-Gate of Inconceivable Liberation.'" He thus commanded Ānanda as the attendant. Because both source and trace are inconceivable, he caused "inconceivable" to serve as the name.

### Third: The Reason for Retranslation

The third section clarifies the reason for retranslation. Because the earlier translation missed the intended meaning, it was retranslated. The text has four parts. First, the patron of the translation: the Qin ruler, Yao Xing. Second, the time of the translation: the eighth year of the Hongshi era. Third, the translator: Dharma Master Kumārajīva. Fourth, the text translated: this very sūtra.

The first passage has two parts. First, a eulogy praising the person's virtues. Second, the fundamental purpose of the retranslation.

In the first part, "The Great King of Qin" identifies the person. "Of surpassing spirit" and so forth praises his virtues. "Great Qin" extols the greatness of his state. "King" displays the nobility of his person. "Sage virtue universally covering" is the meaning of "Heaven." "The common people looking up and returning to him" is the meaning of "King." This is the virtue of ruling with filial governance. Alternatively, "supported by the people" is the meaning of "Heaven," and "sovereign among men" is the meaning of "King." Such is the appellation of the most honored.

"Of surpassing spirit" means outstanding excellence, or surpassing genius. There is an explanation: one who surpasses ten persons is called "outstanding"; a hundred, "brilliant"; a thousand, "surpassing"; ten thousand, "a genius." "Spirit" means spiritual thought. This means a surpassing mind and a heroic equal of the age; hence "transcending the age." This praises his wisdom in governing the state.

"Whose profound mind alone comprehended" means his mind roamed the profound mirror; hence "profound mind." His subtle discernment was without peer; hence "alone comprehended." This praises his mind of comprehending principle.

"He promoted the ultimate principle above the myriad affairs" means "promoted" refers to broadly decreeing upon the ten thousand directions, hence called "great." "Ultimate" means "supreme." "Governing the myriad affairs" means the subtle mechanisms of the ten thousand matters. The *Book of Documents* says: "Anxious and diligent, with ten thousand affairs in a single day and another." This refers to the Qin king's skillful governance of state affairs: hanging his robes and sitting composedly, all things naturally came to order. Though situated amid ten thousand affairs, he did not lose the transformative power of Heaven's movement.

"He propagated the way of transformation for a thousand years below" means "propagated" refers to proclaiming and spreading. "Transformation" means the teaching of the Dharma. "A thousand years" means a thousand years after the Tathāgata's passing, more than thirteen hundred years, when the Mahāyāna supreme teaching was first transmitted to this land. The Qin ruler, corresponding to this karmic period, was therefore able to broadly promote the Dharma-teaching. Thus, because his surpassing spirit transcended the age, he was able to promote the ultimate principle above the myriad affairs. Because his profound mind alone comprehended, he was able to propagate the way of transformation for a thousand years. "Above the myriad affairs" is because they came first; "below a thousand years" is because they came afterward.

From this point, the second part clarifies the intention behind the retranslation. "He constantly studied and delighted in this scripture, making it the dwelling-place of his spirit." "Studied and delighted" means to practice. "Scripture" means the sūtra. "Making it" means using it as. "Dwelling-place" means the resting place. "Of his spirit" means where the spirit abides. Below it says "ultimately empty and quiescent," which is the meaning of "resting place" here. This clarifies that the Qin ruler obtained the old translation and constantly perused it.

"But what Zhi and Zhu produced was principle obstructed by text." "Zhi" refers to Zhi Qian. "Zhu" refers to Dharmarakṣa. "Principle obstructed by text": Zhi Qian's translation rendered "coming in the aspect of no-coming" as "insulting coming," and "seeing in the aspect of no-seeing" as "seeing characteristics," and so on. Thus the Mahāyāna principle was obstructed by the translator's text. How so? In Kumārajīva's translation, the sūtra says: "Coming in the aspect of no-coming; seeing in the aspect of no-seeing." This is precise regarding the ultimate principle, and the meaning of responsive meeting is not lost. To say "insulting coming" and "seeing characteristics" drifts into the vulgar and worldly, while the way of awakened understanding is entirely lost. It is as when people in the world say "not in person": if in the past it was "insulting coming" and "seeing characteristics," how could great bodhisattvas of the Dharma-body share the worldly grasping at characteristics?

"He constantly feared that the profound principle would fall through the translator's brush." Since the translation deviated from the sūtra's intended meaning, he feared the profound and wondrous principle would be lost at the translator's pen.

"The movement of Northern India having its appointed time." "Northern India" refers to the Northern Indian countries. "Movement" means turning and transmission. As the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* says, the Buddha predicted that after his passing, this sūtra would be transmitted to Southern India, then to Western India, and finally to Northern India, where it would disappear underground. According to the numerical reckoning, five hundred years in Southern India, five hundred years in Western India, and then it reaches Northern India. This land is precisely "Northern India," being at the eastern edge of that realm. Therefore, a thousand years later, the great teaching was destined to be transmitted to this land. This fulfilled the Buddha's words; hence "its appointed time."

The second part clarifies the time of translation. "In the eighth year of the Hongshi era, the year-star being in Quail-Fire": when the Great Year-star was in the position of Wu, the month was in the southern quarter called "Quail-Fire." The year is designated by the month's name.

"He commanded the Great General, Duke of Changshan": this is Yao Song. "The Marquis of Ancheng": this is Yao Xian. "Changshan" and "Ancheng" are the fiefs to which they were enfeoffed. "Duke" and "Marquis" are among the five ranks of nobility together with Earl, Viscount, and Baron. These are the titles of their enfeoffment.

"At the great monastery of Chang'an": the Qin ruler's given name was Chang. Being the son of Yao Chang, hence "Chang." This is the place where the translation was done. "Great Monastery" is its specific designation, that is, Caotang ("Grass Hall") Monastery, so called because the hall was roofed with grass. Where the assembly of monks gathered, it was called "great monastery." These were the designations of that time, not original names.

"Together with śramaṇas learned in doctrine": this selects those skilled in principle and distinguishes them from those of other learning. Namely Sengzhao, Sengrui, and others, twelve hundred persons in all, each outstanding in their time. The details of their illustrious deeds, the invitation sent west to request Dharma Master Kumārajīva for the translation, and related events are amply recorded in the digests and biographies.

From this point, the third part clarifies the translator. It is divided into two: first, identifying the person and praising his virtues; second, clarifying the purpose of the translation.

First, the single character "Shi" (Kumārajīva) identifies the person. "With a capacity that towered over the age" and so on praises his virtues: his wisdom and capacity were lofty and far-reaching, surpassing the age.

"His mind merged with the true realm" means his mind accorded with principle. "Having fully penetrated the pivot of the ring" means his wisdom exhausted reality-characteristics. This alludes to Zhuangzi: "Crouching, one finds the pivot of the ring and thereby responds to the inexhaustible." There, it is the cycle of affirmation and negation; the place free from affirmation and negation is the "pivot of the ring." Here we borrow that expression to illuminate reality-characteristics.

"Moreover, he was skilled in the languages of both lands" means he thoroughly understood the languages of both India and China.

From this point, the second part concerns the purpose of the translation. "At that time, holding the Indian text in his hands, he orally rendered the translation." "Indian text" means the Sanskrit manuscripts. The claim that this means "the residual teachings of the Brahmā heaven" is erroneous in calling them "text." Therefore the venerable elder of Nanshan said: "It is but a single pebble of Mount Kun." Since Dharma Master Kumārajīva was fluent in both languages, he orally translated.

"The monastic and lay assemblies were devout and reverent." "Monastic" refers to the śramaṇas learned in doctrine. "Lay" refers to the masters of Confucian learning and literary excellence. All served as persons who verified the meaning. "Devout and reverent," using the word twice, expresses the sincerity of their devotion.

"A single word was thrice reviewed" means they re-examined and settled the meaning. Therefore in the *Analerta*, Nan Rong thrice recited the verse of the white jade tablet. Hence: "A flaw in the white jade tablet can still be polished away, but a flaw in one's words cannot be undone." Therefore in reading the *Odes*, reaching this passage, they always recited it thrice. Here we adopt that expression. When text expresses correct principle, it serves as a standard for all. When the mind grasps the meaning, the fruit of the Way is thereby accomplished. If the intended meaning is lost, various wrong views arise from it. Therefore the thrice review.

"They refined and sought with precision, preserving the sage's intent." "Combining clay" is *táo* (pottery); "smelting metal" is *yě* (casting). These are similes. As the potter kneads clay and removes sand and salt, and as the goldsmith smelts and casts, extracting only the pure: thus they can form their vessels for use. Now likewise, removing superfluous words and directly conveying the sage's intended meaning, they formed the supreme Dharma-jewel. This clarifies the purpose of the translation.

From this point, the fourth part concerns the text that was translated. "Its text is concise yet reaches its destination; its meaning is gentle yet luminous." "Concise" means abbreviated. "Reaches its destination" means arrives at. This means that although the text is abbreviated, the principle reaches reality-characteristics. "Gentle yet luminous" refers to meaning. "Gentle" means smooth and harmonious. "Luminous" means clear.

For example, verses like "Prajñā is the mother of bodhisattvas": the meaning is smooth and harmonious with principle, and furthermore clearly illuminates it. The earlier preface explained that because the ancients did not comprehend the great vision, the principle was halting and deficient. Because they were not skilled in the languages, the diction was crude. Now, because Dharma Master Kumārajīva fully penetrated the pivot of the ring, "its meaning is gentle yet luminous." Because he was skilled in both languages, "its text is concise yet reaches its destination."

"Therefore the subtle and far-reaching words are herein made manifest." "Subtle" means profoundly subtle. Laozi says: "Grasp it and it cannot be caught; it is called 'subtle.'" "Far-reaching" means deeply remote. "Manifest" means clear. This means that the principle of reality-characteristics, though deep, firm, secluded, and remote, was nevertheless made manifest through the translation achieving its intended meaning. This refers to the success of the translation's transmission.

### Fourth: The Purpose of the Commentary

From this point, the fourth section clarifies the purpose of the commentary. Although the translation is clear and the principle is easy to see, when transmitted to those who have not yet awakened, one still fears it will be difficult to penetrate. Therefore commentary is needed to guide later students. The text has two parts: first, words of modesty; second, the purpose of the commentary.

In the first part, "I, being dim and shallow": "I" is the first person. "Being" means "because." "Dim" means ignorant. "Shallow" means of little depth. "At that time I attended in due order" means he participated. When Dharma Master Kumārajīva was translating the sūtra, Master Zhao personally received the venerable one's teaching during the lectures that accompanied the translation. Hence "at that time I attended in due order."

"Though my thought lacks the penetration of the profound" means his thought and wisdom were insufficient. "Lacks" means deficient. "Penetration" means entering into. "Profound" means reality-characteristics. He says his mind did not realize the principle.

"Yet I roughly grasped the meaning of the text" means that although his understanding was not precise, he roughly understood the general import.

"I therefore followed what I had heard and composed this commentary." "Therefore" means taking it upon himself. "Composed" means made.

"I briefly recorded the established words, transmitting but not creating." "Recorded" means a record. "Established words" has two interpretations. Some write "establish" meaning he established this sūtra, and "words" meaning the sūtra's words. Others write "sincere" meaning true and fitting. He recorded Dharma Master Kumārajīva's sincere and fitting words to explain this sūtra. "Transmitting but not creating": the *Analects* says: "He transmitted but did not create; he believed in and loved antiquity." This means that only a sage creates the Dharma; I merely transmit the words of the ancients and do not presume to create on my own.

The second part clarifies the purpose of the commentary. "I hope that noble persons of the future, though in a different age, may share in this." "Hope" means aspire. "Future" means times to come. "Noble persons" distinguishes them from petty persons. Laozi says: "The superior person, hearing the Way, diligently practices it. The middling person, hearing the Way, seems to retain it and seems to lose it. The inferior person, hearing the Way, laughs greatly." Since petty persons cannot practice it, he speaks only of noble persons. This is not necessarily meant to exclude them, but to encourage and exhort self-strengthening.

"Though in a different age, may share in hearing" means that although the present and future differ in time and custom, through hearing the sūtra, attaining realization and gaining benefit will be no different. Hence this is said.

I, in the second year of the Dali era, in the first month of spring, at Zisheng Monastery, in the course of transmitting the sūtra, recorded what I had heard to supplement my many lapses of memory. I hope that those who come after and comprehend the meaning will not reproach the inadequacy of this work.

Recorded by the śramaṇa Tiqing of Chongfu Monastery.
