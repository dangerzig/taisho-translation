# 維摩經疏
## Commentary on the Vimalakīrti Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2774

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

From "eloquence" onward, this praises the wisdom within existence. "Eloquence" means that his speech is clear and fitting, which is called "eloquence." "Outstanding perception surpassing others" is called "talent." "Unobstructed" means that worldly people's talent has limits and exhaustion, but the bodhisattva's eloquence is without limits or exhaustion.

"Sporting in supernatural powers" means that worldly people sport in mountains, forests, and ponds to delight their inclinations. This clarifies that this great being transforms beings through the five supernatural powers, yet in his own mind it is as if he were sporting. Another interpretation: "sporting" means going effortlessly, without applying mental exertion. This clarifies that the bodhisattva's abiding in supernatural powers is without difficulty, the meaning being the same as sporting.

"Attaining all dhāraṇīs" means obtaining the dhāraṇī of comprehensive retention. There are two kinds: first, retention of what is heard; second, retention of meaning. Retention of what is heard means that once something passes through the ear, it is never forgotten. Retention of meaning means that once the mind grasps it, it is never lost from beginning to end.

"Having no fear" means that his inner virtues being abundant, he does not dread external opponents. Another interpretation: the bodhisattva cultivates the four kinds of fearlessness. First, attaining the dhāraṇī of retaining what is heard. Second, skillfully distinguishing the roots and natures of sentient beings. Third, having no difficulty that cannot be penetrated. Fourth, skillfully resolving all doubts.

The fourth phrase, "subduing māras and adversaries," clarifies what he eliminates. Since the above already describes both practices of emptiness and existence, if the four māras were not eliminated, how could the practices of emptiness and existence be complete? Therefore it next clarifies the elimination of the four māras. The affliction-māra constitutes the cause-related burden. The aggregate-māra and death-māra constitute the result-related burden. The celestial māra is the condition that gives rise to evil. Therefore they should be eliminated.

The fifth phrase, from "entering the profound Dharma-gate" through "being firmly established in the Great Vehicle," again clarifies the practices of emptiness and existence. How does this come about? Since the above clarifies the elimination of the four māras, if one does not contemplate and comprehend the realm of emptiness, one has no means to eliminate the three māras. Without wisdom fully equipped, one cannot eliminate the celestial māra. Therefore they are explained again.

This principle being profound and hidden is called "deep." That which can serve as a standard is called "Dharma." Penetrating wisdom is called "gate." Skillfully according with the fundamental source of principle is called "entering." This refers to the realm of emptiness.

"Skilled in the perfection of wisdom" refers to the substance of wisdom itself. It is precisely because the perfection of prajñā-wisdom is complete that one can enter the profound Dharma-gate.

From "penetrating expedient means" onward through "being firmly established in the Great Vehicle," this clarifies the practice of existence. "Penetrating expedient means" means that skillful ingenuity without attachment is called "expedient means." Being unobstructed in affairs is called "penetrating." Having the ability to reach somewhere is called "arriving."

"Great vows accomplished" means that the bodhisattva has earnestly aspired to the fruit of buddhahood and vowed to liberate sentient beings. "Clearly knowing the tendencies of sentient beings' minds" means skillfully recognizing whether sentient beings' minds tend toward good or evil. "Also able to distinguish the sharpness or dullness of their faculties" means that the upper, middle, and lower grades of capacity for awakening differ in ten thousand ways, yet he can fully know them all. This is recognizing the illness.

From "long established on the buddha-path" onward, this clarifies recognizing the medicine. From the initial stage of the practice-seed nature through the Cloud of Dharma ground, being able to penetrate all the way to buddhahood is called "the buddha-path." "His mind already purely quiescent" means that "quiescent" has the meaning of "excellent." This clarifies that the bodhisattva's mind has dwelt in this Dharma for a long time and is already well practiced. "Firmly established in the Great Vehicle" means that this expression refers to the myriad practices being connected and carried as its meaning. This clarifies that his understanding of the principle of the Great Vehicle is firm and clear. Clear understanding of principle is called "firmly established."

The sixth phrase, from "all his actions" onward, clarifies the three kinds of karma. Why does this come about? It clarifies that the basis for all the above virtues is the three kinds of karma. Therefore it next clarifies that "all his actions are able to be well considered," praising his verbal karma. The treatise says: "Considered discourse is hard to defeat, hard to destroy." His mental ground of consideration and his verbal karma of teaching the Dharma are without anything that does not accord. From the eighth ground onward, being fully equipped with inconceivable wisdom, one can illuminate without deliberation and respond without discussion. Why then does it say "considered"? This clarifies that for what lies below the bodhisattva's own level, deliberation is not needed. But looking up toward the ground of buddhahood, deliberation is still required. Therefore it says "considered." Another interpretation: the bodhisattva's verbal karma of skillful teaching accords with capacity without error, and his responsive stirring merges with principle, the meaning being the same as acting after consideration.

"Abiding in the Buddha's deportment" praises his bodily karma. Bodhisattvas of the tenth ground emulate the Tathāgata's elephant-king manner of gazing, his deportment and bearing in walking, standing, sitting, and lying down.

"His mind vast as the ocean" praises his mental karma. The ocean is fathomless in its depths and limitless in its nourishing flow. This clarifies that the great being's capacity for virtue is vast and fathomless, beyond what lesser beings can fathom, with nourishing flow without limit. Therefore the simile of the ocean. Another interpretation: the ocean broadly receives all streams and is therefore called "vast." This clarifies that the bodhisattva's mind can practice all the myriad practices, wishes to liberate all sentient beings, and wishes to attain all meritorious virtues. Therefore it says "his mind is vast as the ocean."

The seventh phrase, from "all buddhas praise him" through "honored by worldly lords," summarizes the above six phrases. This clarifies that because he possesses such virtues, above he is praised and extolled by all buddhas, and below he is reverently honored by humans and devas.

From "wishing to save people, he therefore dwells in Vaiśālī" onward, this praises the virtue of external transformation. Within this, it is the second section praising the virtue of transforming beings. Within this section there are again two parts. The first, from "wishing to save people" through "entering the various wine-shops, he is able to establish their resolve," clarifies his transformative activity while dwelling in Vaiśālī. The second, from "if among the elders" onward through "benefiting sentient beings," clarifies his hidden and manifest transformative activity among humans and devas.

Within the first section there are four "banners" that clarify the meaning, each banner containing six phrases. The first banner, from "with boundless wealth" onward through "gathering in those without wisdom," clarifies how he uses the six perfections to gather in the six kinds of defilement, in sequential order. The second banner, from "though a householder in white robes" through "with the joy of dhyāna as his sustenance," clarifies household necessities in sequential order. The third banner, from "if going to a place of games and gambling" onward through "though obtaining worldly profit, he takes no delight in it," clarifies his transformative activity when encountering conditions, without specifying a fixed location. The fourth banner, from "traveling the various crossroads" onward through "he is able to establish their resolve," clarifies his transformative activity at specified locations.

"With boundless wealth, gathering in the destitute" is the first phrase of the first banner. The destitute are oppressed by poverty and hardship and have no means to receive the Way. Therefore the great being accumulates abundant wealth, first relieving the destitute and rescuing them from their acute needs, and afterward teaching them with Dharma-words. The second phrase, "gathering in those who transgress the precepts": sentient beings, unless their practice of morality is accomplished, have no means by which to be gathered in. Therefore the great being first upholds the precepts and teaches them to repent and eliminate their transgressions. The remaining four perfections can be understood by analogy.

From "though a householder in white robes" onward is the second banner, on household necessities in order. The first phrase: "though a householder in white robes, he upholds the pure disciplinary practices of a śramaṇa." As a layperson, one often seeks the ceremony of a śramaṇa. But Vimalakīrti, though outwardly a layperson, inwardly harbors the wind of the Way. Therefore he is able to uphold the precepts of a śramaṇa.

The second phrase: "though dwelling as a householder, he is not attached to the three realms." Being a layperson, he reasonably requires a dwelling and house. Worldly people develop much attachment and affection for their homes. Yet he does not even develop attachment to the three realms, how much less attachment and affection for his home.

The third phrase: "he appears to have a wife and children, yet always cultivates pure conduct." Being a householder in white robes, he reasonably has a wife and children. Worldly people's wives and children often defile their pure conduct. But Vimalakīrti, though appearing to have a wife and children, always maintains pure conduct as his abiding.

The fourth phrase: "he appears to have retainers and dependents, yet always delights in seclusion." Beyond wife and children, all maidservants, slaves, and attendants are called "retainers and dependents." Worldly people develop much attachment and possessiveness toward their retainers. But Vimalakīrti, though appearing to have them, always delights in seclusion, with no possessiveness in his heart.

The fifth phrase: "though wearing precious ornaments, he adorns his body with the marks and excellences." As a layperson with wife, children, and retainers, he should require garments and vestments. Though he has garments, caps, and hat-strings displaying precious ornaments, he constantly adorns his appearance with the thirty-two marks and eighty excellent characteristics.

The sixth phrase: "though partaking of food and drink, he takes the joy of dhyāna as his sustenance." Above it clarifies that he relies on clothing to adorn his form; he further requires food to nourish his life. Though he partakes of a hundred delicacies and fine dishes, he takes the joy of Dharma and the delight of dhyāna as his food, with no other thought of food.

"If going to a place of games and gambling, he always uses it to save people" is the first phrase of the third banner's six phrases. "Games" refers to six-piece chess; "gambling" refers to the board game of go. These are not places of proper and elegant human conduct, hence the word "always." The second phrase: "accepting the various heterodox paths, he does not damage correct faith." "Heterodox paths" are the followers of wrong views. Though he appears to join the heterodox paths, his upholding of correct faith has never been damaged. The third phrase: "though well versed in worldly classics, he always delights in the buddha-dharma." Though outwardly versed in the Five Classics and historical works, inwardly he takes the twelve divisions of the canon as the abode of his spirit. The fourth phrase: "respected by all who see him, as the foremost in offerings." Everywhere he benefits beings, and those who see him are filled with respect. He is able to produce goodness in people and is therefore the foremost in merit. The fifth phrase: "upholding the correct Dharma, he governs both young and old." In foreign lands, persons of virtue are specially appointed as arbitrators to settle disputes. If the mind harbors partiality, then feelings are biased. But Vimalakīrti, maintaining the correct Dharma as his abode, both young and old turn to him. The sixth phrase: "in all his livelihoods and trades, though he obtains worldly profit, he takes no delight in it." This clarifies that though the great being appears to seek commercial profit, he takes no delight in it: he does not value rare goods, for nothing is not a treasure. How could he be greedy for wealth and take the same delight as worldly people? When things go as wished, it is called "accord"; obtaining profit is called "success."

"Traveling the various crossroads" onward is the fourth banner, clarifying his transformative activity at specified locations. Within this there are six phrases. The first phrase: "traveling the various crossroads," where all six phrases should include "entering." It should also say "entering the various crossroads." But "traveling" and "entering" do not differ in meaning. Another interpretation: going from home to the road, "traveling" is the more fitting expression. "Crossroads" means roads in four directions.

The second phrase: "entering the government offices, he upholds the correct Dharma and rescues and protects all." According to traditional interpretation: five hundred elders took turns managing communal affairs, and when it was Vimalakīrti's turn to enter government office, he showed compassion to all the people. "Rescuing" means that when suffering has already arrived, rescue is needed. "Protecting" means that when suffering has not yet arrived, one provides assistance. Therefore it says "rescuing and protecting all."

The third phrase: "entering the places of debate, he guides them with the Great Vehicle." In India there are many heterodox schools. Each claims his own path is superior. That country specially establishes a debate hall to determine superiority and inferiority. They beat a drum, and when the assembly gathers, they go to the hall to debate. The winner becomes the teacher; the loser becomes the disciple. At that time, Vimalakīrti also ascended this hall, defeated the non-Buddhists, and then guided them with the Great Vehicle.

The fourth phrase: "entering the various schools, he guides and opens the young and unawakened." Beginning students who are immature are called "young"; those whose awareness is still dim are called "unawakened." At that time, Vimalakīrti entered these schools to guide, open, and enlighten them.

The fifth phrase: "entering the various brothels, he shows the faults of desire." He first uses the hook of desire to attract them, then teaches them with Dharma-words. Sexual desire is the root of birth and death. It ruins nations, destroys families, harms the loyal and good, and causes the loss of life, all because of this. Therefore he shows the faults and causes them to depart from it.

The sixth phrase: "entering the various wine-shops, he is able to establish their resolve." Wine has thirty-six faults. It opens the gates of transgression and can cause the spirit to become muddled and the body turbid, with much being neglected. Speaking of the faults of wine is intended to also establish their resolve. These two are mentioned as representative of each other. Another interpretation: because the faults of desire are many, their faults are especially revealed; the passage about wine then establishes their resolve.

From "if among the elders" onward, within the praise of the virtue of external transformation, this is the second section clarifying his hidden and manifest transformative activity among humans and devas. Therefore each section's purpose is that above he transforms below, like wind bending grass, with nothing that does not follow. "Elders": in cities and towns, those with virtue who can overshadow the local community are called "elders." He teaches them the supreme Dharma. Elders mostly use worldly dharmas to instruct the world. Therefore he teaches the supramundane supreme Dharma.

He teaches the elders the supreme dharma of the buddha-dharma. "Householders, he causes to cut off greed": in India, a person who has accumulated a hundred million in wealth is called a "householder." If one does not harbor greed and stinginess, how can one accumulate such enormous wealth? Therefore one must cut off greed and attachment, causing them to distribute their wealth for merit.

"To the kṣatriyas, he teaches patience." Kṣatriya means "field-lord" in Chinese. In the earliest kalpa, when people ate the fat of the earth, human sentiments became frivolous. Then there arose natural rice-grain, and human sentiments became even more frivolous. They established boundaries and developed a mind of stinginess and protectiveness. They separately appointed a virtuous person to divide and adjudicate impartially, and successive generations became kings. As the head of the kṣatriya class, these people, relying on their might and power, often give rise to violent harm. Therefore he causes them to cut off anger.

"The brahmins": in Chinese this means "outer intention" or also "pure conduct." Generation after generation they have transmitted and studied the arts of the Way. Relying on their own attainments, they are conceited and contemptuous of others. Therefore he eliminates their pride.

"The great ministers, he teaches the correct Dharma": those who serve as chief ministers and hold the affairs of state must not cause injustice and harm to people. Therefore he teaches them the correct Dharma.

"The princes, he shows loyalty and filial piety": the royal position being high, people are inclined to usurp. As subjects they must be loyal; as children they must be filial. Since princes have both obligations, he shows them loyalty and filial piety.

"The inner palace, he rectifies the conduct of the palace women": women often engage in seductive behavior. He transforms them to cultivate upright and correct conduct.

"The common people, he causes to increase their merit and strength": their planted causes are shallow and meager, and without encouragement they will go astray. Therefore he causes them to increase their merit and strength.

"Brahmā, he instructs with superior wisdom": Brahmā dwells in dhyāna and often delights in attachment to meditative absorption. Therefore he instructs with superior wisdom, not allowing one-sided attachment.

"Indra, he shows impermanence": amid the five desires, indulging freely without considering impermanence. Therefore he teaches impermanence. Another interpretation: he manifests the characteristics of impermanence, causing awakening to impermanence.

"If among the world-protectors, he causes them to protect sentient beings": the Four Heavenly Kings command the spirits and do not allow them to harm people. If stationed in their command, they cause the spirits to guard and protect, making this their business, thus protecting all sentient beings.

In this way, Vimalakīrti, through traces manifested in ten thousand different ways, appearing everywhere, uses immeasurable expedient means to compassionately shelter the six destinies. "Benefiting sentient beings" is the conclusion.
