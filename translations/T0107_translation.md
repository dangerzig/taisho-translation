# 佛說不自守意經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on Not Guarding the Mind

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 107

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty (Three Kingdoms), translated by Zhī Qiān (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus, and the bhikṣus responded: "Yes, indeed." The Buddha said: "Listen, I will speak of guarding the mind and not guarding the mind." The bhikṣus then folded their hands and listened to the Buddha.

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "What are the conditions for not guarding the mind? If one does not close and guard the eye faculty, and the eye falls upon forms, the conscious mind becomes dissipated. Once the mind becomes dissipated, one experiences further suffering. Once one experiences further suffering, one cannot attain mental concentration. Once one cannot attain mental concentration, one does not know what is true. Not knowing what is true, one does not see things as they truly are. Not knowing and not seeing things as they truly are, one neither abandons the fetters nor crosses over doubt. Not having abandoned the fetters nor crossed over doubt, one becomes subject to external conditions and knows things in a distorted way. Having known things in a distorted way, one suffers and is not at ease. So it is said: the same applies to the ear, the nose, the mouth, the body, and the mind. Such conduct is called not guarding the mind."

The Buddha further told the bhikṣus: "What are the conditions for guarding the mind? If one guards the eye faculty, restraining eye-consciousness from falling into forms, the mind does not become dissipated. Once the mind does not become dissipated, one experiences further joy. Once one experiences further joy, one attains mental concentration. Having attained mental concentration, one discerns truly; one knows things as they truly are and sees things as they truly are. Having thus truly known and truly seen, one abandons the fetters and crosses over doubt. One no longer relies on mere faith or mere sincerity, but attains wisdom and knowledge. The mind is joyful and at ease. The same is said for all six faculties. This is called guarding the mind. What has been said concerning guarding the mind and not guarding the mind is thus."

The Buddha having spoken thus, all rejoiced and received his teaching.
