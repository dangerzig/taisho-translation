# Catalog of Items Brought from Taizhou by the Great Teacher Dengyō

## *Chuánjiào Dàshī Jiānglái Táizhōu Lù* (傳教大師將來台州錄)

Taishō 2159

Compiled by Saichō (*Zuìchéng*, 最澄) of Japan. A catalog of imported texts.

*Note: This is the official catalog of texts that the Japanese monk Saichō (767–822), founder of the Tendai school, brought back from Taizhou in Tang China. Saichō traveled to China in 804–805 as part of the Japanese embassy, studying at the Tiāntái headquarters. The catalog records 230 works in 460 fascicles, comprising Tiāntái commentaries, meditation texts, Nirvāṇa and Vimalakīrti studies, dhāraṇīs, and sūtras. It includes Saichō's formal memorial to the Japanese emperor, the official Chinese certification from the Taizhou prefect, and lists of texts that were still missing from the Tiāntái corpus. The catalog was later printed with a preface by the Tendai prelate Shinchō in 1821 (Bunsei 4), the millennial anniversary of Saichō's death.*

## Catalog of Items Brought by the Great Teacher Dengyō

### Preface to the Printing of the Catalog Brought by the Fundamental Great Teacher

The Great Teacher's catalogs of texts obtained on entering Tang China are of two kinds. The first records the names of sūtras and commentaries obtained in Taizhou, called the "Perfect School Catalog" (*Yuánzōng Lù*, 圓宗錄). The second records the esoteric texts received in Yuezhou, called the "Yuezhou Catalog" (*Yuèfǔ Lù*, 越府錄). The Yuezhou Catalog has a printed edition from the Genroku era (1688–1704), but the characters are full of errors and inaccuracies.

Now, following the Great Teacher's own hand-copy preserved at the Shōzen-in of Yokawa, we have traced and produced a reproduction. As for the Perfect School Catalog, it was scattered and lost during the Ganki disturbances, and its whereabouts are unknown. Therefore, following the copy held by the Former Senior Second Rank, Former Acting Major Counselor Munetatsu, we have transcribed it. We have also collated it with a copy from the Kanji 5 (1091) held at the Kanchiin of Tōji. We have now combined both catalogs and printed them, hoping to transmit them for ten thousand ages and preserve them as a grand exemplar of the dharma-gate.

The fourth year of Bunsei (*Wénzhèng*, 文政), *xīnsì*, the early summer of the Great Teacher's millennial anniversary.

Identified by Shinchō (*Zhēnchāo*, 真超), Tendai Provisional Bishop and New Chief of the Eizan Seminary.

### Memorial to the Court

The śramaṇa Saichō speaks: I, Saichō, have heard that the hexagrams of the *Yìjīng*, exploring the subtle, are limited to the arena of birth-and-death, and that the naming of the hundred things has not yet ventured into the realm of true suchness. How could they compare with following the provisional teaching of the other and opening the three vehicles at the gate of capacity, or following the real teaching of the self and revealing the one vehicle at the site of enlightenment?

Now then: the perfect teaching is difficult to expound; the one who developed its meaning was Tiāntái. The wondrous dharma is difficult to transmit; the one who made its Way flourish was the sage emperor. I humbly reflect that Your Majesty, succeeding to the spiritual mandate, has ascended the throne. The northern barbarians come to court, requesting to offer felicitations at the New Year each year. The eastern barbarians bow northward, acknowledging their return to virtue from years past.

Thereupon, with thoughts directed toward the perfect school and yearning for the one vehicle, you have proclaimed the wondrous dharma and made it the great instruction. Thus the wondrous and perfect ultimate teaching has arisen in response to the sage's capacity and become manifest. The esoteric dharma of consecration (*abhiṣeka*), responding to the imperial affinity, has been perfected.

I, Saichō, received the mission to seek the dharma and traveled far to trace the holy vestiges. I went and ascended the Tiāntái peaks, personally copying the records of the teaching. The sūtras, commentaries, treatises, and records I obtained total 230 works in 460 fascicles.

Additionally, I now present ten fascicles of sūtras: seven fascicles of the *Saddharmapuṇḍarīka Sūtra* (*Miàofǎ Liánhuá Jīng*, 妙法蓮華經) in gold characters, one fascicle of the *Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra* in gold characters, one fascicle of the *Bodhisattva Precepts Sūtra* in gold characters, and one fascicle of the *Contemplation of Immeasurable Life Sūtra* in gold characters. Also: one painting of the spiritual responses of the Great Teacher Zhìzhě of Tiāntái, one meditation-weight (*chánzhèn*, 禪鎮) of the Great Teacher of Tiāntái, four pieces of carved tamarisk and cypress wood from the Incense-Burner Peak of Mount Tiāntái, and one white-horn *rúyì* scepter for dharma-teaching. I respectfully send my disciple Zàngjīng to present these.

If the sage's discernment illumines that the two gates are perfectly fulfilled, I cannot contain my earnest devotion. I reverently submit this memorial with trembling.

The fifteenth day of the seventh month of the twenty-fourth year of Enryaku (*Yánlì*, 延曆) [805 CE]. The śramaṇa Saichō respectfully submits.

### Catalog of Missing Tiāntái Texts

Commentaries, treatises, and other works: fourteen works in forty-one fascicles.

The above commentaries and treatises are fourteen works in forty-one fascicles. In the twenty-second year of Kaiyuan (734 CE), Dharma Master Cén of Lóngquán Monastery, together with the monk Dàomóu, had the names inscribed on the right pillar of the stele before the tomb of the Great Teacher Zhìzhě. Although the titles exist, the copies have not yet been obtained.

---

### Catalog of Tiāntái Supplementary Texts

Miscellaneous doctrinal works: fifteen items.

The above fifteen items of doctrine were privately recorded by Ānle the Recorder (*Zhāng'ān Héshàng*, 章安和尚) following the Great Teacher Zhìzhě. Their doctrinal content follows the subject matter and is organized by fascicle. They were recorded at various times, before and after, and are scattered in various places. Fearing that later students might be confused, the Lóngquán record preserved the titles. The doctrine is fully contained within the other divisions. Because it is not urgent, no separate fascicles are made here.

The above sūtras, commentaries, and records of the teaching by the Great Teacher Zhìzhě of Tiāntái, together with the doctrinal records and the biographical records of the second, fifth, and sixth patriarchs and other lineage holders, and independent extracts: in total, 120 works in 345 fascicles. Excluding the paper used for the sūtras and doctrinal records, the paper amounts to 8,532 sheets. I, Saichō, and my companions were deeply favored by the kindness of the Vice-Prefect. At the Jìngtǔ-yuan of Lóngxīng Monastery in Línhǎi District, Taizhou, in the Great Tang, we copied the texts according to number, verified and finalized them. We respectfully request the official seal of this prefecture to be shown to later students. The dharma-seeking is herewith accomplished.

Thus the Vice-Prefect's merit of dharma-giving will be inexhaustible throughout the eternal kalpas, and the dharma's benefit for all beings will be unending through the long night. May the transmitted dharma's high radiance be directed toward the Prefect, that thought after thought his merit may increase and his wisdom be perfected in an instant. Then may it extend universally to all sentient beings of the ten directions, that all may ride the one jeweled chariot and travel together the eightfold noble path, with enemies and kin equally treated, self and others alike.

The nineteenth day of the second month of the twenty-first year of Zhēnyuán (*Zhēnyuán*, 貞元) in the Great Tang, the *yǐyǒu* year [805 CE].

Recorded by the dharma-seeking monk Saichō of Hiei-zan Monastery, Japan.

The dharma-seeking interpreter-monk Gishin (*Yìzhēn*, 義真) of Japan.

The dharma-seeking attendant Tan Fukusei (*Dān Fúchéng*, 丹福成) of Japan.

The Presiding Master of the Tiāntái Perfect School Seat at the Western Capital, Preceptor Dàosuì (*Dàosuì*, 道邃).

The ācārya Saichō, though different in form and from a different land, is truly of the same source in nature. Specially endowed with innate knowledge, he comprehends all categories by analogy. He traveled far to seek the wondrous essence of Tiāntái, and also encountered the dharma-elephant Master Dàosuì. He gathered the myriad practices into one mind and thoroughly understood the three contemplations through diverse approaches. He personally received the esoteric transmission; its principle transcends name and word. Yet fearing that students in other lands might not be able to accept it, he has requested the official seal of this prefecture. How could it not serve as validation?

The twentieth day of the second month of the twenty-first year of Zhēnyuán in the Great Tang [805 CE]. Given under the hand of the Vice-Governor of Taizhou, commissioned with authority over all military affairs, Pillar of the State, Chún.
