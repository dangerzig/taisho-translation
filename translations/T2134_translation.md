# 唐梵文字
## Chinese-Sanskrit Vocabulary

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2134

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, compiled by Quanzhen (全真).

---

### Preface

Those who wish to know the speech and sounds of the two countries must have the transmission from teacher to student in succession, or be a person from the Western Country who also understands the Siddham script, the basic Sanskrit, and Chinese languages, or be a broadly learned gentleman who wishes to study Chinese-Sanskrit language. The Siddham script and the pronunciation of the Five Indias do not go beyond this path. The great yoga teaching was transmitted by the Thousand-Year Ācārya and has circulated in this land. All of it is based on Central Indian phonology, the most secret and essential script, derived from the Śabdavidyā treatise. For those who study Chinese-Sanskrit language, obtaining this is the first step; all other languages will then become accessible. Those who study both the Sanskrit and Chinese versions together should apply their minds carefully. Within one or two years, they will be capable of translating the speech and writing of both countries, with equal elegance. Those who wish to translate, maintain recitation, and practice yoga should first thoroughly master this text, the bilingual Sanskrit-Chinese translation: Sanskrit characters, Chinese characters, Chinese understanding of Sanskrit words. The Sanskrit reading of Chinese characters sometimes has many syllables, sometimes combined with two-syllable or three-syllable conjuncts, sometimes single, sometimes reduplicated. Just look at the letter-mothers; the phonology is complete in a separate volume. The two sections on sounds and rhymes properly arrange the original teachings of various schools, each with their titles and names, with Chinese characters measured beneath. The wise should examine this in detail and transmit it to later generations.

Collected by Quanzhen, the Eight-Powers Son of Beiping, Transmitter of the Five-Division Yoga Recitation Teaching.

### Verse on Dependent Origination

> The chapter on causes and karmic retribution:
> Form is entrusted to the mother's womb.
> Ignorance gives rise to formations;
> Driven on, they arise and press forward.
> Name-and-form thus exists;
> The six sense-bases open wide.
> Seeing birth, though there is joy,
> Old age and death bring sorrow again.
> Grief and sadness begin to depart,
> But suffering and affliction ultimately return.

### Verse on the Tathāgata's Teaching

> The Tathāgata, the Great Sage,
> Appeared, saw, and spread through the lands.
> He turned the excellent wheel
> For those deeply sunk and drowning.
> Beginning from the Deer Park,
> North of Kuśinagara,
> The Sanskrit texts he proclaimed
> Were numberless and inexhaustible.

### Numbers

> Ten makes a hundred; ten hundreds are called a thousand.
> Ten thousands make ten-thousand; ten ten-thousands are called a hundred-million.
> Ten hundred-millions make a capital.
> The various reversals and turns:
> The Dharma-ocean with its waves and billows
> Wishes to cause all beings
> To leave suffering and attain peace.

### Verse on the Mantra-Esoteric Teaching

> The mantra-esoteric teaching: encountering it is exceedingly rare.
> The faithful buddha-son is bestowed and should contemplate.
> Generate the mind of bodhi; enter the abhiṣeka altar.
> Relying on the honored one, take up the rules; siddhi can be attained through visualization.
> The honored Vairocana's mirror manifests water-moon and clouds.
> The extremely deep, secret treasury: the Dharma-Way King.
> The solemn, victorious vajra-body; the blue Immovable One.
> Fangs emerge in spring like thunder's roar.
> The Vajra-Jewel teaching: universal compassion, exhorting.
> Like the Tathāgata's abhiṣeka: the fire-pearl shines.
> The Dharma-nature Vajra of Contemplative Wisdom:
> The mother gives rise to the four immeasurable minds.
> The Turning-Wheel, enduring long; the Great Mantra of Karma.
> Conquering and accomplishing many fruits; joy pervades the mysterious realm.
> Following mindfulness, all is accomplished; the ocean-assembly maṇḍala's holy ones.
> The Great Sun, the four buddhas; the adorned sixteen bodhisattvas.
> Inner and outer offerings; the four means of attraction and self-benefit.
> The holy buddha, the worthy sage; beings of the Fortunate Kalpa, compassionate.
> I have already purified the three karmas; saving the world, the ācārya
> Transmits the Dharma with expedient means; the truth is inconceivable.
> With deep compassion making counsel; the solemn great teacher.
> Supreme yoga, maintaining the mantra, the caitya.
> Relying on the great teaching; the maṇḍala's secret opening
> For the benefit of the final age; the standards and school.

### Verse in Praise of the Stages

> Praising and chanting all the sages;
> Approaching the three worthies,
> Next cultivating the ten stages of the holy ones:
> Joy, Freedom from Defilement, Radiance,
> Blazing Wisdom, Hard to Conquer, Manifest,
> Far-Reaching Practice, Immovable,
> Excellent Wisdom, Dharma Cloud.
> Next, the correct wondrous awakening:
> The Tathāgata is called the great responding mirror.
> Most honored in the three realms,
> In the ten directions none compares.
> He protects and transforms beings in this realm;
> He is called Moon-Muni, the Roarer.

### Concluding Verse

> Recommending the sage: from what path does he come?
> Dhāraṇī serves as the path;
> Relying on the teaching, one practices reverently.
> With utmost mind, I prostrate in homage.
