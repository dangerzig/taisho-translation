# 佛說寶積三昧文殊師利菩薩問法身經
## Sūtra on the Dharma-Body as Asked by Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva in the Ratnakūṭa Samādhi, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0356

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in Rājagṛha, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva went to the Buddha's place and stood outside the gate. Why? Because the Buddha was seated in samādhi. Before long, the Buddha emerged and saw Mañjuśrī. He invited him in. Mañjuśrī made obeisance and stood. The Buddha said: "Please sit."

Mañjuśrī asked the Buddha: "What is the name of the samādhi in which you were just seated?"

The Buddha said: "Ratnakūṭa."

Mañjuśrī asked again: "Why is it called Ratnakūṭa?"

The Buddha said: "Just as a maṇi jewel, inherently pure and fine, is further washed with water and placed on level ground, becoming even more brilliant and luminous, with nothing it does not illuminate: so too in the samādhi I just entered, I saw the immeasurable asaṃkhyeya lands and Buddhas of the eastern direction, and then all of them disappeared. Abiding in this samādhi, there is nothing among the fundamental aspects of all dharmas that is not seen. Those who have faith thereby obtain the seal of certification, and their words are as spoken. A maṇi-jewel chamber has four corners; looking from one corner, one sees all the other corners completely without deficiency. This is why the fundamental aspects of all dharmas are seen."

The Buddha asked Mañjuśrī: "Do you know the fundamental aspect?"

He said: "I know it."

"What is it?"

He answered: "Where I am: that is the fundamental aspect. Those who desire things have a different aspect. One who abides in this aspect neither abides in dharmas nor in good or evil. All dharmas are likewise. One who knows this has truly known it. What the ordinary person knows is by non-knowing. From the very beginning it has been transmitted without any agent; therefore it is without foundation."

The Buddha asked Mañjuśrī: "What is wisdom?"

"What is truly wisdom: that is wisdom."

He asked again: "When one speaks of the path, by what contemplation is it called the path?"

He answered: "The contemplation of the path is without contemplation: that is the path."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "You say there is contemplation and then say there is no contemplation. By what dharma would you teach newly studying men and women?"

Mañjuśrī said: "There is neither emerging nor liberation. Lust, anger, and delusion are without limit. It is by this dharma that one teaches all beings, for they are without root, and therefore one cannot emerge from them and cannot be liberated from them. One who says 'I can destroy the fundamental aspect' cannot do so. One who says 'I can cut off birth' also cannot do so. Without abandoning worldly affairs and without contemplating the path of the way: acting in this way, one can teach ordinary people."

Mañjuśrī asked the Buddha: "By what dharma do you teach practitioners?"

The Buddha said: "In my teaching, I do not destroy form, feeling, perception, mental formations, birth, death, or consciousness; I destroy nothing. Nor do I teach the destruction of lust, anger, or delusion. I cause beings to attain incalculable dharmas. It is by this dharma that I teach those who practice the Buddha-path. It is because of this that I have attained Buddhahood."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Because no dharma is destroyed, one attains Buddhahood. Because no dharma is obtained, one can become a Buddha. The Buddha is the dharma-body. All the powers, the fearlessnesses: these all enter into the dharma-body. Why? Because no one can divide a single body, for the dharma-body has no number. Why? Because one does not say 'this is an ordinary person' or 'this is not an ordinary person.' The dharma-body is equal, without difference. What is without a dispersed body is the dharma-body. Just as the four rivers all return to the ocean and become one taste, so the many named dharmas become one dharma-body. Just as various kinds of seeds each have their own name, but when gathered and collected together are called 'grain,' so worldly affairs and the affairs of the path are all combined into the one dharma-body. Why? One cannot point and say 'this is a worldly affair.' The affairs of the path also cannot be spoken of. This worldly body is the dharma-body. It also cannot be seen or looked at, as I have explained the dharma-body. If there is one who has faith and understands these two, all the evil deeds they have committed will be entirely eliminated."

Mañjuśrī said: "Regarding the dharma-body, I neither see it born in the heavens, nor in the human realm, nor in the three evil paths, nor in nirvāṇa."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "What you say is just so. But if there are people who question you, saying the Buddha clearly teaches that there are five destinies, how would you explain it?"

Mañjuśrī said: "It is like a person in a dream who sees themselves enter the hells, or become an animal or a hungry ghost, or ascend to the heavens, or be among humans, but upon waking, nothing is seen. The dharma-body has no attachment. Why? It is merely because of enumeration. Enumeration belongs to the conventional. Whether śrāvaka, pratyekabuddha, or up to the Buddha, all equally share one dharma-body. Why? Because it cannot be differentiated. Just as various kinds of jewels can be distinguished, the dharma-body cannot be distinguished. Why? Because it cannot be distinguished, and because there is no birth and no death. The dharma-body has no arising and no ceasing. Why? Because it is eternal. It is neither defiled nor pure. Why? Because there is no transgressor, and there is neither liberation nor anything to be liberated from."

The Buddha, who knows all, asked Mañjuśrī again: "Do you know the dharma-body?"

Mañjuśrī said: "If it could be obtained, then it could be known."

The Buddha asked Mañjuśrī: "Do you know where the world is?"

He said: "I know."

The Buddha said: "What is it?"

Mañjuśrī said: "A conjured person placed in the world, the one who is in this world, exists only as a name. Seek even a hair-tip of it, and no one can explain it for me. Yet the world is also not separate from the dharma-body."

The Buddha asked again: "Where is the world located?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Just as clouds: where they are is nowhere. Neither weak nor strong: this is the characteristic of the world."

The Buddha asked Mañjuśrī: "Do you say I perish?"

Mañjuśrī said: "No. Why? The dharma-body has no birth. If there were birth, there would be perishing. Because the dharma-body does not arise, one knows the Buddha does not perish."

The Buddha asked Mañjuśrī: "If you heard that the Buddhas of the past, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, have all entered parinirvāṇa, do you believe it?"

He said: "I believe."

The Buddha said: "How do you believe?"

Mañjuśrī said: "All those Buddhas were transformations of the Buddha. Because the transformations enter parinirvāṇa, I believe."

The Buddha asked Mañjuśrī: "When you see a person at the time of death, do you know where they are going?"

He answered: "A person cannot even be known; how much less where they are going."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "You may gather an assembly and teach the Dharma."

Mañjuśrī said: "Who would listen?"

The Buddha said: "Those who wish to listen would gather."

Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "On the basis of what dharma would there be something to teach?"

The Buddha said: "Teach the dharma-body."

Mañjuśrī said: "I do not see the dharma-body. How would I teach it?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Since you say the dharma-body cannot be seen, those in the assembly who do not yet understand, hearing your words, may become frightened."

Mañjuśrī said: "If they are frightened, the fundamental aspect has already been frightened."

The Buddha said: "The fundamental aspect is without fear, and those who do not yet understand are also not frightened."

Mañjuśrī said: "All dharmas are without one who is frightened; they are like vajra."

The Buddha asked: "What is vajra?"

He answered: "What cannot be cut or severed: that is called vajra. The Buddha is inconceivable. All dharmas are also inconceivable. This is vajra."

The Buddha said: "What then constitutes vajra?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Because it surpasses all dharmas. The Buddha is the truth of all dharmas: this is vajra."

"By what cause is the Buddha vajra?"

He answered: "What exists is non-existent. Seeking each one, there is nothing to be found; therefore it is called empty. Emptiness is the Buddha: this is vajra. All dharmas are all Buddha, relying on what has nothing to rely upon: therefore vajra."

"By what connection is this vajra?"

He said: "What has nothing to rely upon has nothing to approach: therefore it is vajra."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "I now wish to send an emanation to cause Ānanda to come. Why? For the sake of receiving and holding all the Dharma."

Mañjuśrī asked the Buddha: "In the Dharma just spoken, there is nothing to be seen and nothing to be obtained. If Ānanda comes, what dharma would he take?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! It is as Mañjuśrī has said." The Buddha said: "I see in the eastern direction immeasurable asaṃkhyeya lands, and all the Buddhas there are also teaching this."

Śāriputra came out from his quarters and went to Mañjuśrī's place, but not finding him there, went to the Buddha's place and stood outside the gate. The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Call Śāriputra in."

Mañjuśrī asked the Buddha: "Does the fundamental aspect, the dharma-body, have an inside, an outside, or an interior? From where would one find them?"

The Buddha said: "They cannot be found."

He answered: "The fundamental aspect is without boundary." He added: "Śāriputra too is within the dharma-body. There is nowhere he comes from and nowhere he enters."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "You are giving yourself trouble, while Śāriputra is not troubled. Just as the śrāvakas are inside with me, conversing, while you stand outside and do not enter when it is time: this is not troublesome."

He answered: "Though I stand outside, I am neither troubled nor distressed."

The Buddha asked: "Why are you neither troubled nor distressed?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Because the Buddha teaches the Dharma for the śrāvakas, and I am likewise; therefore I am neither troubled nor distressed. Why? Because all the Buddhas have transcended the dharma-body."

Mañjuśrī said: "Even if for kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges I did not see the Buddha and could not enter, I would still be neither troubled nor distressed. Why? Because the Dharma taught by the Buddha is neither increased nor decreased. Why? Because all dharmas have no master. Therefore there is neither trouble nor distress. All existing names are what the Buddha uses to teach people. Why? Because the Buddha teaches by means of these."

The Buddha called out to Śāriputra from afar: "Did you hear everything Mañjuśrī said?"

Śāriputra said: "Please, Buddha, do not trouble yourself for my sake. I delighted in going there to hear his Dharma."

Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "You may have Śāriputra come in."

The Buddha called Śāriputra to come forward. Having come forward, he made obeisance and sat down.

Mañjuśrī said to Śāriputra: "In this Dharma, what do you consider to be honored, that you wish to enter and listen?"

"Having heard of the honored Dharma, with love and delight I wished to hear it, so I entered to listen."

Mañjuśrī said: "It is truly as you say. This Dharma is indeed honored, profoundly deep. Why? Because this Dharma is without duality of mind. Why? It is not what you know, and you are not within it. Arhats and pratyekabuddhas are likewise, as are those who seek the Buddha's path. Why? Because it cannot be obtained. Nor can it be obtained through aspiration. Therefore no one can be within it. Because it is originally pure, all dharmas are also pure."

Śāriputra asked Mañjuśrī: "Why is it that arhats are not within it?"

Mañjuśrī said: "When lust and anger are exhausted, this is called arhat. Without abiding and without accomplishment, where would they be within?"

Śāriputra said: "I went to where the people were but did not find them, and so I came here to listen. It was only because I wished to hear the profound Dharma."

Śāriputra said: "Whether from the Buddha or from Mañjuśrī, I hear the Dharma and am truly never satiated."

Mañjuśrī said: "To be without satiation for the Dharma: it is as Śāriputra says."

Mañjuśrī asked: "Can the dharma-body receive any dharma? Why is there no satiation?"

Śāriputra said: "The dharma-body receives nothing."

"Does the fundamental aspect receive anything?"

Śāriputra said: "It receives nothing."

Mañjuśrī answered Śāriputra: "Because the fundamental aspect receives nothing, you are without satiation." Mañjuśrī said: "If the fundamental aspect received dharmas, would you have satiation?"

Śāriputra said: "Apart from what the Buddha has said, nothing I have said is its equal."

Mañjuśrī said: "Can you have faith that your dharma reaches nirvāṇa? Can you have faith that it does not reach nirvāṇa?"

Śāriputra said: "From the very beginning, all has been parinirvāṇa."

Mañjuśrī said: "Can you trust that you are always thus, without moving or turning?"

Śāriputra said: "I trust."

He asked again: "From what do you trust?"

He answered: "The dharma-body has no arising and no ceasing; therefore I know there is no moving or turning."

He asked again: "Do you trust that the arhat has exhausted all without remainder and has nothing more to know?"

Śāriputra said: "I trust."

"From what do you trust?"

"Because what is known has nothing more to exist, there is nothing more to know. What has nothing more to know has no place to stop; therefore it is called exhaustion."

Mañjuśrī said: "The arhat's exhaustion is thus. Why?"

Śāriputra said: "Because all dharmas are completely relinquished and nothing is obtained."

Mañjuśrī asked: "Do you trust that the Ganges-sand Buddhas who have entered parinirvāṇa have not entered parinirvāṇa?"

Śāriputra said: "I trust."

"From what do you trust?"

"Because the dharma-body does not arise and does not perish, it does not enter parinirvāṇa."

Mañjuśrī asked: "Do you trust that all the Buddhas are one Buddha?"

Śāriputra said: "I trust."

"From what do you trust?"

He answered: "Because the one dharma-body is without duality."

Mañjuśrī asked: "Do you trust that all existing lands are one land?"

Śāriputra said: "I trust."

"From what do you trust?"

He answered: "Because all that exists is exhausted."

Mañjuśrī asked again: "Can you trust that all dharmas are without consciousness, without liberation, without thought, and without realization?"

Śāriputra said: "I trust."

"From what do you trust?"

"Because there is no spontaneous nature that knows the spontaneous, there is no consciousness, no liberation, no thought, and no realization. There is also no arising and no ceasing, no seeing and no being seen. The fundamental aspect has no location. Therefore I trust."

Mañjuśrī asked again: "Do you trust that the dharma-body abides without arising, without ceasing, and without stopping?"

Śāriputra said: "I trust."

"From what do you trust?"

"It is not the case that this dharma has arising, ceasing, or stopping. Therefore I trust."

Mañjuśrī asked: "Can you trust that the dharma-body, from which incalculable things emerge, can be known as to its location?"

Śāriputra said: "I trust."

"From what do you trust?"

He answered: "Because the dharma-body is also without lust, anger, or delusion, I trust that it has no location."

He asked Śāriputra again: "Do you trust that the reliance of all dharmas, the reliance of the Buddha, and non-reliance are all equal?"

He answered: "I trust."

"From what do you trust?"

He answered: "Because there is no place to stop. What has no place to stop is what is called the unseen reliance."

Mañjuśrī said: "Excellent! Excellent! Śāriputra! I have asked you about everything within your realm of understanding, and you have answered each thing that you know."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "What I have now heard, I shall keep in thought and never forget."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "If there are men and women who hear this Dharma, uphold it, recite it, read it, broadly explain it for all people, and clarify its meaning, they will swiftly attain what they wish."

Mañjuśrī said to Śāriputra: "What the Buddha has now said is without any difference."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "Those who in the past made offerings to former Buddhas have therefore all come and obtained this seal. What is the name of this Dharma? How should it be practiced?"

"Its name is 'Inquiry into the Dharma-Body,' 'Ratnakūṭa,' 'Upholding the Fundamental Aspect,' and 'Upholding What Has No Location.' What it upholds upholds all dharmas, without any obstruction. Those who hear it from you will, through this Dharma, all attain realization. Those who hear it will further teach and guide others, and they in turn will transmit it to still more. Why? Because the words are few but the understanding is great."

When the Buddha had taught this sūtra, Śāriputra and the arhats, Mañjuśrī and the bodhisattvas, and all the devas, humans, worldly people, nāgas, and spirits all rejoiced, made obeisance, and departed.
