# 相續解脫地波羅蜜了義經
## Sūtra on the Definitive Meaning of the Stages and Pāramitās in the Continuous Liberation

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0678

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

As the Continuous Liberation Sūtra states:

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! There are ten bodhisattva stages, namely the Stage of Joy, the Stage of Stainlessness, the Stage of Radiance, the Stage of Blazing, the Stage of Difficult to Conquer, the Stage of Manifest Presence, the Stage of Far-Reaching, the Stage of Immovability, the Stage of Excellent Wisdom, and the Stage of the Dharma-Cloud. The buddha-stage is the eleventh. How many kinds of purity encompass these stages, and how many portions are there?"

The Buddha addressed the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara: "There are four kinds of purity and eleven portions that encompass these stages. Avalokiteśvara! The purity of aspiration encompasses the first stage. The purity of superior moral discipline encompasses the second stage. The purity of superior mind encompasses the third stage. The purity of superior wisdom, with increasingly excellent purities, encompasses the fourth through the buddha-stage. These four kinds of purity encompass all the stages.

"What are the eleven portions? Avalokiteśvara! A bodhisattva at the stage of understanding and practice has ten dharma-practices: having well cultivated the bodhisattva's liberation and patience, upon completing this stage, the bodhisattva transcends to the stage beyond ordinary birth. That portion is fulfilled. But the bodhisattva is not yet able to dwell in correct knowledge regarding subtle violations of the precepts. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one is not yet able to fully accomplish the worldly samādhis and samāpattis, nor to fully accomplish the dhāraṇī of hearing and retention. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one is not yet able to repeatedly cultivate the dharmas of the factors of awakening as they have been attained, nor to relinquish the mind's attachment to the dharma in samāpatti. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one is not yet able to contemplate the noble truths, nor to relinquish the one-sided intention of turning away from saṃsāra toward nirvāṇa, cultivating the factors of awakening through skillful expedient gathering. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one is not yet able to directly contemplate the arising of conditioned phenomena, nor to dwell mostly in disenchantment and mostly in signlessness. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one is not yet able to dwell mostly in uninterrupted, continuous signless contemplation. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one is not yet able to relinquish the active efforts within signlessness and to attain mastery over characteristics. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one is not yet able to gain ultimate mastery in fully discriminating all characteristics, discriminating all names, and teaching the dharma in every manner. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one has not yet received the complete dharma-body. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled.

"But one has not yet attained the unobstructed and unhindered knowing and seeing of all radiant wisdom. This portion is not yet fulfilled. In order to fulfill it, one strives with skillful means until it is fulfilled. When that portion is fulfilled, all portions are fulfilled.

"Avalokiteśvara! This is called the four kinds of purity and eleven kinds of portions encompassing all the stages."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why is the first stage called the Stage of Joy, and so on up to the buddha-stage?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Because one has risen to the great purpose and attained the supreme and marvelous joy of the transcendent mind, the first stage is called the Stage of Joy. Because one is free from all subtle violations of the precepts, the second stage is called the Stage of Stainlessness. Because one relies on the immeasurable light of wisdom through samādhi and hearing-retention, the third stage is called the Stage of Radiance. Because the fire of wisdom burns away all afflictions through cultivating the factors of awakening, the fourth stage is called the Stage of Blazing. Because one skillfully cultivates the various factors of awakening with difficulty and hardship yet gains mastery, the fifth stage is called the Stage of Difficult to Conquer. Because one directly contemplates the arising of conditioned phenomena and engages in much contemplation of characteristics, the sixth stage is called the Stage of Manifest Presence. Because one's uninterrupted, continuous signless contemplation reaches far into the proximity of the stage of purity, the seventh stage is called the Stage of Far-Reaching. Because the afflictions of signlessness do not arise and do not move, without any activation or disturbance, the eighth stage is called the Stage of Immovability. Because one attains mastery in teaching the dharma in every manner and gains the vast wisdom that surpasses all error, the ninth stage is called the Stage of Excellent Wisdom. Because, like space, all faults are gone, and like a great cloud, the dharma-body universally covers all, the tenth stage is called the Stage of the Dharma-Cloud. Because the subtle afflictions and the obstructions of radiant wisdom are severed, and one attains the unobstructed and unhindered realization of all kinds of radiant wisdom, the eleventh stage is called the Buddha-Stage."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many kinds of ignorance and how many kinds of defects to be remedied are there in these stages?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are twenty-two kinds of ignorance and eleven kinds of defects to be remedied. At the first stage: the ignorance of clinging to beings and dharmas, and the ignorance of the afflictions of the evil destinies; these are the defects to be remedied. At the second stage: the ignorance of subtle precept-violations, and the ignorance of the various karmic destinies; these are the defects to be remedied. At the third stage: the ignorance of desire and craving, and the ignorance of fulfilling hearing-retention; these are the defects to be remedied. At the fourth stage: the ignorance of attachment to samāpatti, and the ignorance of attachment to the dharma; these are the defects to be remedied. At the fifth stage: the ignorance of one-sidedly turning away from saṃsāra, and the ignorance of one-sidedly turning toward nirvāṇa; these are the defects to be remedied. At the sixth stage: the ignorance regarding the direct contemplation of the arising of conditioned phenomena, and the ignorance of the many characteristics of practice; these are the defects to be remedied. At the seventh stage: the ignorance of subtle characteristic-based activity, and the ignorance of the one-sided signless contemplation's skillful active methods; these are the defects to be remedied. At the eighth stage: the ignorance of signlessness without activation, and the ignorance of mastery over characteristics; these are the defects to be remedied. At the ninth stage: the ignorance of limitless dharma-teaching, limitless dharma-words and phrases, and the superior wisdom of eloquent delight and comprehensive retention of mastery, and the ignorance of mastery in eloquent delight; these are the defects to be remedied. At the tenth stage: the ignorance of great spiritual powers, and the ignorance of entering the subtle and secret; these are the defects to be remedied. At the buddha-stage: the ignorance of the subtle samāpattis of all radiant wisdom, and the ignorance of obstructions; these are the defects to be remedied.

"Avalokiteśvara! These are called the twenty-two kinds of ignorance and eleven kinds of defects to be remedied. They are established at the various stages as being incompatible with supreme enlightenment."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "How wonderful, World-Honored One! supreme enlightenment is of great benefit and great fruition. Those bodhisattvas who break through the great net of delusion and pass beyond the great offenses attain supreme enlightenment."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many kinds of excellence are established within these stages?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are eight: the purity of aspiration, the purity of mind, the purity of compassion, the purity of the pāramitās, the purity of seeing and making offerings to buddhas, the purity of bringing sentient beings to maturity, the purity of birth, and the purity of powers. Avalokiteśvara! At the first stage there is an increase in the purity of aspiration, and so on through the purity of powers. And so for each successively higher stage, up to the buddha-stage, there is an increase in the purity of aspiration through the purity of powers. You should know that this constitutes purity. At the buddha-stage, only the purity of birth is excluded. From the first stage to each successively higher stage, those merits are equal. You should know that the merits of each stage are excellent in their own right. The stages of all bodhisattvas have merits that are surpassable; only the merits of the tathāgata-stage are unsurpassable."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! For what reason is a bodhisattva's birth supreme among all beings of existence?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are four reasons: because of the gathering of thoroughly purified roots of merit; because of taking rebirth in accordance with wisdom; because of compassionately saving all sentient beings; and because one has departed from defilement and also causes others to depart from it."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why are the bodhisattva's vows called marvelous vows, superior vows, and power-vows?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are four reasons. Those bodhisattvas are skillfully established in the happiness of nirvāṇa and are able to quickly attain it. Yet they relinquish the quick attainment and the happiness of abiding. Without any external cause or purpose, they endure suffering for a long time for the sake of sentient beings and make vows. Therefore they are called marvelous vows, superior vows, and power-vows."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many training subjects does a bodhisattva have?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "A bodhisattva has six training subjects, namely the six pāramitās: the pāramitā of giving, up to the pāramitā of prajñā."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Among these six training subjects, how many constitute the higher training in moral discipline, how many the higher training in mind, and how many the higher training in wisdom?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Giving, moral discipline, and patience, these three, constitute the higher training in moral discipline. Meditation constitutes the higher training in mind. Wisdom constitutes the higher training in wisdom. Diligence pervades all."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Among these six, how many are the equipment of merit, and how many the equipment of wisdom?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "The higher training in moral discipline is the equipment of merit. The higher training in wisdom is the equipment of wisdom. Meditation and diligence pervade all."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How does a bodhisattva train in these six training subjects?" The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are five things in accordance with the pāramitās: first, having supreme faith and understanding in the bodhisattva treasury of the correct dharma as previously taught; second, performing the ten dharma-practices within it; third, guarding the mind of enlightenment through the wisdoms of hearing, thinking, and meditation; fourth, drawing near to good spiritual teachers; fifth, skillfully cultivating uninterrupted wholesome actions."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why are these training subjects established as six?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are two reasons: first, gathering sentient beings; second, counteracting afflictions. Three trainings gather sentient beings, and three trainings counteract afflictions. Through the bodhisattva's giving of material provisions, one gathers sentient beings through the benefit of material support. Through the bodhisattva's keeping of the precepts, one does no harm and causes no fear, gathering sentient beings through the benefit of fearlessness. Through the bodhisattva's patience, one endures harm, oppression, and threats, gathering sentient beings through the benefit of forbearance. With these three trainings one gathers sentient beings. Through vigorous diligence one subdues afflictions, giving rise to the severing of afflictions and cultivating wholesome actions, which cannot be shaken by any affliction. Through meditation one subdues afflictions. Through wisdom one severs the latent tendencies. With these three trainings one counteracts afflictions."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why are the additional four pāramitās established?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Because they are companions of the six pāramitās. Three pāramitās gather sentient beings. For the bodhisattva, the skillful actions of the methods of gathering are established. Therefore I say the pāramitā of skillful means is the companion of the three pāramitās.

"Furthermore, Avalokiteśvara! A bodhisattva who is presently much afflicted in this life is unable to endure the constant cultivation of patience. Because of craving for and attachment to the lower realms, one's aspiration is weak and one cannot unify the mind internally, nor can one develop the transcendent wisdom through hearing, thinking, and meditation on the bodhisattva treasury. Wishing for one's future rebirth to have weakened afflictions: this is the pāramitā of vows. When afflictions have weakened, one can then be vigorously diligent. Therefore I say the pāramitā of vows is the companion of the pāramitā of diligence.

"Drawing near to good spiritual teachers and hearing the excellent dharma, with correct internal reflection, one transforms a weak aspiration into a powerful aspiration, one of superior excellence, enabling one to unify the mind. Therefore I say the pāramitā of power is the companion of the pāramitā of meditation.

"Through hearing, thinking, and meditation on the bodhisattva treasury: this is the pāramitā of knowledge, which is able to develop the transcendent wisdom. Therefore I say the pāramitā of knowledge is the companion of the pāramitā of prajñā."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why are the six pāramitās taught in this particular order?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Because each successively higher one is attracted and depends on the one before. The bodhisattva, relinquishing body and wealth, upholds the pure precepts. Guarding the precepts, one is patient. Being patient, one is diligent. Being diligent, one is able to meditate. When meditation is complete, one attains the transcendent wisdom."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many kinds of divisions does each pāramitā have?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Each has three kinds. The pāramitā of giving has three kinds: the giving of dharma, the giving of material wealth, and the giving of fearlessness. The pāramitā of moral discipline has three kinds: the discipline of abandoning what is unwholesome, the discipline of generating what is wholesome, and the discipline of benefiting sentient beings. The pāramitā of patience has three kinds: the patience of enduring harm, the patience of enduring hardship, and the patience of contemplating dharmas. The pāramitā of diligence has three kinds: the diligence of great vows, the diligence of skillful means, and the diligence of benefiting sentient beings. The pāramitā of meditation has three kinds: the meditation that counteracts afflictive suffering by dwelling in the tranquility and joy free from wrong thought; the meditation that develops qualities; and the meditation that benefits sentient beings. The pāramitā of prajñā has three kinds: that which takes conventional truth as its object; that which takes ultimate truth as its object; and that which benefits sentient beings."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why are these pāramitās called pāramitās?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are five reasons. First, non-obstruction: not being defiled by things contrary to the pāramitās. Second, non-regard: the mind is not bound to the rewards of the pāramitās or to present-life benefits. Third, non-error: in the pāramitās, one is free from the impure and unskillful methods of contaminated dharmas. Fourth, non-conceptualization: in the pāramitās, one does not cling to their own characteristics as verbally described. Fifth, dedication: the pāramitās, having been practiced and developed, are dedicated to the pursuit of the fruit of supreme great enlightenment."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What things are contrary to the pāramitās?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "You should know there are six things. First, delighting in wealth and seeing the merit and benefit of one's own comfort and happiness in being a sovereign master. Second, indulging body, speech, and mind as one pleases. Third, being impatient of others' slights and contempt. Fourth, not applying skillful effort to wholesome dharmas. Fifth, frequenting worldly miscellaneous and disordered activities of seeing, hearing, awareness, and knowing. Sixth, regarding worldly idle theories as beneficial."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What are the fruits of the pāramitās?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "They too are six. First, great wealth. Second, good destinies. Third, freedom from enmity. Fourth, indestructibility. Fifth, sovereignty over many joyful sentient beings. Sixth, not harming oneself while possessing great capability."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What impure dharmas are mixed with the pāramitās?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "You should know there are four kinds. First, the method without compassion. Second, the method of wrong thinking. Third, the method that is not constant. Fourth, the method that is not comprehensive. 'The method of wrong thinking' means cultivating the various pāramitās in a confused and mixed manner with the other pāramitās."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What constitutes a lack of skillful means?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Having practiced and developed the pāramitās, when gathering sentient beings, if one is pleased merely by benefiting them with a little material wealth, and does not cause sentient beings to awaken to what is unwholesome and to be established in what is wholesome: this is called a lack of skillful means. Why? This is not the bodhisattva's way of truly benefiting sentient beings. It is as if an impure thing, whether in large or small quantity, has no method by which it can be made fragrant. In the same way, sentient beings whose nature is suffering in conditioned existence cannot be made happy through a small amount of material benefit. The foremost way to gather them is to establish them in what is wholesome."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many kinds of purity do the pāramitās have?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "I do not say that the pāramitās have any purity apart from those five. However, I shall now analyze them both in general and in detail.

"In general terms, all the pāramitās' purity should be known as being of seven kinds. What are the seven? The bodhisattva does not seek others' recognition for these dharmas. The bodhisattva is not attached to these dharmas. Seeing these dharmas of the awakening vehicle, the bodhisattva does not give rise to doubt, whether affirmative or negative. The bodhisattva does not exalt himself or demean others. The bodhisattva is not proud or careless. The bodhisattva does not develop a sense of contentment from little or inferior accomplishment. The bodhisattva does not give rise to a miserly or jealous mind regarding these dharmas.

"Now I shall explain the specific purities of the pāramitās. They should be known as being of seven kinds each. What are the seven? The bodhisattva receives, upholds, and practices the seven kinds of pure giving I have taught: giving with purity of the gift, giving with purity of the precepts, purity of view, purity of mind, purity of speech, purity of wisdom, and purity from defilement. These seven kinds are called the purity of the pāramitā of giving.

"The bodhisattva well regulates all kinds of restraint-precepts, excels in the precepts of confessing wrongdoing, maintains constant precepts, firm precepts, always-practiced precepts, always-turning precepts, and learning-precepts. These seven kinds are called the purity of the pāramitā of moral discipline.

"The bodhisattva relies on karmic retribution and does not become angry at any harmful act. Whether scolded, rebuked, beaten, or quarreled with, one does not retaliate for any injury. Toward enemies and adversaries, one does not harbor resentment. If the other seeks forgiveness, one accepts it without obstruction. If another causes offense, one does not expect an apology. One compassionately receives it according to the dharma, without fear and without seeking reward. One always acts with benefit and the mind never abandons this. These seven kinds are called the purity of the pāramitā of patience.

"The bodhisattva well understands equanimous diligence and does not use diligence to exalt himself or demean others. Toward wholesome dharmas one is devoted, diligent, capable, firm, and never abandons them. These seven kinds are called the purity of the pāramitā of diligence.

"The bodhisattva well enters the signless samādhi-meditation, the fulfilling samādhi-meditation, the precept-conjoined samādhi-meditation, the great samādhi-meditation, the non-reliant samādhi-meditation, the well-purified samādhi-meditation, and the immeasurable samādhi-meditation of hearing, thinking, and meditation on the bodhisattva treasury. These seven kinds are called the purity of the pāramitā of meditation.

"The bodhisattva abandons both establishing and slandering, and the wisdom of the middle way emerges. With that very wisdom one truly understands the meaning of the gates of liberation. One truly understands the meaning of the intrinsic nature of the three gates of liberation: emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. One truly understands, regarding the three natures of conceptual construction, dependent arising, and the perfected, the meaning of their being free from intrinsic nature. One truly understands, regarding the three aspects of characteristics, causes, and ultimate meaning, the meaning of being free from intrinsic nature and the meaning of conventional truth. One truly understands the meaning of ultimate truth in the five sciences. One dwells mostly in the seven kinds of suchness, without wrong thought, free from falsity, and through the single gate rises to the immeasurable contemplation of the dharmas of the lower stages, following the dharma in accordance with the dharma. These seven kinds are called the purity of the pāramitā of prajñā."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What are the functions of those five?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "You should know there are five functions. The bodhisattva who has non-obstruction: in this present life one constantly and comprehensively practices the pāramitās without carelessness. One who has non-regard: one gathers the causes of future non-carelessness. One who has non-error: one cultivates and completes the pāramitās in purity. One who has non-conceptualization: through skillful means one swiftly brings the pāramitās to fulfillment. One who has dedication: in all births one constantly receives excellent fruition, and the pāramitās are inexhaustible, even until supreme perfect enlightenment."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Which of the pāramitās is most excellent?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Non-obstruction, non-regard, and dedication."

"Which is undefiled?"

"Non-error and non-conceptualization."

"Which is blazing?"

"That which is beyond calculation."

"Which is immovable?"

"That which enters the stage of non-retrogression."

"Which is thoroughly pure?"

"Abiding on the tenth stage and seeking the buddha-stage."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why does a bodhisattva constantly receive inexhaustible fruits and inexhaustible pāramitās?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Because they successively depend upon one another, and one cultivates through relinquishing."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why does a bodhisattva deeply delight in the fruits of the pāramitās?" The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "The bodhisattva has five reasons. First, the pāramitās are the supreme cause of joy and truth through delight. Second, they are the cause of gathering self and others. Third, they are the cause of receiving future fruition. Fourth, they are the means of separation from the matters of affliction. Fifth, they prevent one from dwelling in the evil destinies."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What virtue and power does each pāramitā possess?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "You should know each has four kinds. First, when the bodhisattva cultivates the pāramitās, one relinquishes miserliness, precept-violation, laziness, mental disturbance, and wrong views. Second, they are the true equipment for supreme enlightenment. Third, in this present life they gather self and others. Fourth, in the future one receives vast and inexhaustible fruition."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What are the cause, the fruit, and the purpose of the pāramitās?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "The cause of the pāramitās is compassion. Their fruit is receiving retribution and gathering sentient beings. Their great purpose is the fulfillment of supreme enlightenment."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If a bodhisattva has inexhaustible wealth and also has a compassionate heart, then why are there poor and destitute sentient beings in the world?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "This is entirely due to those sentient beings' own karmic faults. If it were otherwise and others could always provide for them, then they should have inexhaustible wealth, and why would there be poor and destitute beings in the world? If that were so, then the evil deeds of sentient beings should pose no obstacle.

"Avalokiteśvara! It is as if hungry ghosts, oppressed by thirst, see only empty ravines. This is not the fault of the ocean; it is only the karmic fault of those hungry ghosts. Just as the great ocean is without fault, so too the bodhisattva is without fault. Just as the hungry ghosts receive the fruition of their own karma, so too is it with the karmic retribution of sentient beings."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If all dharmas are entirely without intrinsic nature, by which pāramitā does a bodhisattva apprehend them?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "By the pāramitā of prajñā."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If the pāramitā of prajñā apprehends what is without intrinsic nature, why does it not apprehend intrinsic nature?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "I do not say that what is without intrinsic nature apprehends what is without intrinsic nature. Rather, what is without intrinsic nature is without words, and one knows it only by oneself. Yet one cannot speak of it apart from words. Therefore, apart from intrinsic nature, one apprehends the absence of intrinsic nature."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! You speak of pāramitā, superior pāramitā, and great pāramitā. Which is pāramitā? Which is superior pāramitā? Which is great pāramitā?"

The Buddha addressed the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara: "For immeasurable periods one cultivates the wholesome dharmas of giving and the rest and accomplishes them, and though afflictions still arise, one can subdue them and is not overcome by them. This refers to the stages of understanding and practice, at the gentle and moderate levels of understanding and turning. This is called pāramitā.

"Again, for immeasurable periods one cultivates such wholesome dharmas and accomplishes them, and though afflictions still arise, one can sever them and is not overcome by them. This refers to from the first stage onward. This is called superior pāramitā.

"Again, for immeasurable periods one cultivates such wholesome dharmas and accomplishes them, and all afflictions whatsoever entirely cease to arise. This refers to from the eighth stage onward. This is called great pāramitā."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many kinds of afflictive latent tendencies are there in these stages?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are three kinds. First, 'companions destroyed,' referring to the fifth stage. Avalokiteśvara! The non-innate afflictions no longer arise; the innate afflictions' companions are at that time absent. Therefore it is called 'companions destroyed.' Second, 'weakened latent tendencies,' referring to the sixth and seventh stages, where the subtle activity of cultivation suppresses them so they do not arise. Third, 'subtle latent tendencies,' referring to the eighth stage, where from that point upward all afflictions whatsoever entirely cease to arise, and the obstruction of radiant wisdom depends on very little."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many kinds of faults must the bodhisattva sever to sever these latent tendencies?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are three kinds. First, the severing of the outer faults. Second, the severing of the intermediate faults. Third, the severing of the inner faults. The complete separation from all latent tendencies: I say this belongs only to the buddha-stage."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How many asaṃkhyeya kalpas does a bodhisattva take to sever those faults?" The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "There are three immeasurable periods, whether measured in kṣaṇas, lavas, muhūrtas, half-hours, hours, days and nights, half-months, months, or years."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! When afflictions arise in the bodhisattvas of these stages, what are their characteristics, their faults, and their merits?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "Their characteristic is non-defilement. Why? Because from the first stage, when the bodhisattva has entered the dharma-realm of all things, the bodhisattva knows when afflictions arise; it is not the case that one does not know. Therefore it is called the characteristic of non-defilement. Because it cannot cause suffering to arise in one's own body, there is no fault. Because it serves as a cause for sentient beings' liberation from suffering, when bodhisattvas give rise to afflictions, there is immeasurable merit."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "How wonderful, World-Honored One! It is truly of great meaning that awakening causes the bodhisattvas' arising of afflictions to surpass the roots of merit and merits of all sentient beings, śrāvakas, and pratyekabuddhas, let alone their other merits."

The bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The World-Honored One has said that the śrāvaka vehicle and the great vehicle are precisely the one vehicle. What is the meaning of this?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "The dharma I teach for the śrāvaka vehicle, namely the five aggregates, the six internal sense-bases, the six external sense-bases, and so forth: I say this is precisely the great vehicle, the one dharma-realm, the one path. Therefore I do not say there are various vehicles. Yet those people give rise to wrong thoughts about the meaning as it was taught. Some establish it, some slander it, and they speak of various vehicles. Those who hold contradictory views argue with one another, and therefore they speak in this way."

At that time the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke in verse:

> The various intrinsic natures that are taught
> all follow the same single path.
> The lesser and the supreme vehicles
> are not of various different kinds.
> Speaking as taught, following wrong thought,
> some establish and some slander.
> Their views contradict the meaning;
> the foolish and confused have various understandings.
> The stages, their purities, their objects of remedy,
> superior birth, vows, and training:
> I have taught the path of the great vehicle.
> Diligently cultivate it and swiftly become a buddha.

At that time the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Within the Continuous Liberation Sūtra, what is this sūtra called? How should it be reverently upheld?"

The Buddha addressed Avalokiteśvara: "This sūtra is called 'The Definitive Teaching on the Stages and Pāramitās.' It should be reverently upheld in this way."

When this teaching on the definitive meaning of the stages and pāramitās was spoken, seventy-five thousand bodhisattvas attained the great vehicle samādhi of radiance.
