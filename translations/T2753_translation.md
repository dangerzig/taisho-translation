# Chapter Outlines for the Avataṃsaka Sūtra

## *Huáyán Jīng Zhāng* (華嚴經章)

Taishō 2753

Author not attributed.

*Note: This text is a guide explaining the purpose and significance of each chapter (pin, 品) of the Avataṃsaka Sūtra (Flower Garland Sūtra), based on the sixty-fascicle translation by Buddhabhadra (completed 420 CE). For each chapter, it explains why the chapter arises at that particular point in the sūtra's structure, what doctrinal themes it addresses, and how it relates to the preceding and following chapters. This kind of sequential chapter-explanation (pǐn yì, 品義) was a standard format in Chinese Buddhist exegesis, particularly in the Huayan school. The text also appends a discussion of the four great vows (sì hóngshì, 四弘誓) at the end.*

---

### Chapter on the Names [of the Buddha] (Míngháo Pǐn)

The profound and wondrous names, which are the "characteristics of no-characteristics," are simultaneously raised as names and appellations to bring about [understanding]. They are not external; therefore they are the same as external reality. Therefore they are the same as the real: hence the chapter is called "Names."

### Chapter Four: The Four Noble Truths (Sìdì Pǐn)

In this chapter, one follows the structure of the preceding and succeeding passages, listing the names of the ten directions. The text itself makes this clear. Its aim and purport [may be understood accordingly].

### Chapter Five: The Awakening of Radiant Light (Guāngmíng Jué Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the two chapters above broadly set forth the characteristics of the ultimate principle, directly illuminating the buddha-fruit, the true and wondrous function that pervades the dharma-realm. But since the meaning, though brimming and all-embracing, truly cannot be grasped except through the profound awakening [that comes when] the great sage's supernatural power and the strength of the Way give even a faint glimpse of its image, there arises a mental orientation. Therefore, the Tathāgata emits a great radiance from the wheel-marks on the soles of his feet, revealing the characteristics of true reality, containing all with its luminous power, so that all those with minds are awakened without exception. For this reason, the chapter heading takes its name: "Awakening of Radiant Light."

### Chapter Six: Illuminating Difficulties (Míng Nán Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter reveals the verification of the Awakening of Radiant Light. But the preceding [discussion of] perfect bodhi and true quiescence, the actuality of the ultimate truth, is displayed in the chapter on the Names of the Dharma-Realm. Now, the names of the dharma-realm are the "characteristics of no-characteristics"; characteristics are the same as the real. The ocean of sentient beings is likewise the same. This purport is lofty and wondrous, difficult among the difficult. Therefore, Mañjuśrī (*Wénshū Shīlì*, 文殊師利) and the great bodhisattvas of all quarters thoroughly investigate these traces, raising ten kinds of inquiry. Each is answered in accordance with the meaning, so that the profound purport becomes manifest: hence it is called "Illuminating Difficulties."

### Chapter Seven: The Practice of Purity (Jìngxíng Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter showed that the wondrous subtlety of the perfect fruit cannot be reached without causes to match it. Though the causes are immeasurable, none is other than the practice of purity. Therefore the chapter on the Practice of Purity follows next.

### Chapter Eight: Bodhisattva Xiánshǒu (Xiánshǒu Púsà Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: Mañjuśrī above illuminated the essential purity of practice, but within that, the vast and profound meaning and its characteristics had not yet been made manifest. Therefore Xiánshǒu (*Bhadraśrī*) is asked to expound the profundities: hence this eighth chapter, the Xiánshǒu Chapter.

### Chapter Nine: The Buddha Ascends to the Summit of Sumeru (Fó Shēng Xūmí Dǐng Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapters illuminated the true and actual merits of the buddha-fruit and briefly discussed the way of turning practices and skillful means toward convergence with the fruit. From here on, the text further illuminates how the Tathāgata, in supreme quiescence and void composure, yet functions throughout the dharma-realm. How do we know this? Without rising from the bodhi-tree, he is present in the six heavens, and the same is true throughout the ten directions. Subsequently, wishing to broadly discuss the stages and positions of bodhisattvas, their transcendence, and the true and actual path of escape, the chapter on Ascending Sumeru follows.

### Chapter Ten: The Assembly of Bodhisattvas Like Gathering Clouds (Púsà Yúnjí Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter illuminated the single unified characteristic of the dharma-realm in the ten directions. This chapter wishes to illuminate that all things whatsoever are of equal, true quiescence. Therefore, the great bodhisattvas, ten in number, each relying on the contemplation of reality and exhausting the profound, are called "those who see the Buddha," transcending all discrimination. Why so? To illuminate that when a bodhisattva generates the mind of bodhi, this determination is itself no different from the Buddha. Hence the chapter on the Assembly Like Gathering Clouds.

### Chapter Eleven: The Ten Abidings of Bodhisattvas (Púsà Shí Zhù Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter illuminated that true quiescence is without characteristics, wonderfully beyond discrimination, in order to open the perfect path of bodhi for the great bodhisattvas. Therefore one who has the aspiration to clearly comprehend its purport first generates the great vow, the mind merging with great quiescence, secure, firm, and unmoving: this is called "abiding" (*zhù*). Hence the chapter on the Ten Abidings.

### Chapter Twelve: The Practice of Brahma-Conduct (Fànxíng Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter illuminated how the mind merges with great quiescence, secure, firm, and unmoving, accomplishing the meaning of "abiding." This chapter illuminates the true and actual cultivation of verification, the definitive superior accumulation. At the time of the initial generation of the mind, one has already become a perfect buddha. Hence the chapter on the Practice of Brahma-Conduct.

### Chapter Thirteen: The Chapter on Merit (Gōngdé Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter illuminated the brahma-conduct, which is the essence of supreme quiescence and true reality. This chapter illuminates merit as the skillful means of adornment. The former manifests the purity and equality of the essence of practice; this chapter displays the diverse and excellent applications of merit, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. Hence the chapter on Merit.

### Chapter Fourteen: The Chapter on Illuminating the Dharma (Míngfǎ Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter illuminated the essence of brahma-conduct and merit, whose meaning is vast, established equally with the Buddha in principle. It is fitting to advance victoriously. Therefore the Bodhisattva of Wisdom (*Huì Púsà*) questions Dharma-Wisdom (*Fǎ Huì*), broadly discussing the path of transcendence and advancement, so that its characteristics become manifest: hence it is called "Illuminating the Dharma."

### Chapter Fifteen: Ascending to the Yāma Heaven Palace, Sovereign Freedom (Shēng Yèmó Tiāngōng Zìzài Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter illuminated twelve kinds of dharma as the path of victorious advance. To manifest the valiant and courageous bodhisattvas whose minds merge with the profound convergence, not proceeding by stages, the chapter on Ascending to the Yāma Palace of Sovereign Freedom follows.

### Chapter Sixteen: The Bodhisattvas Speak Verses (Púsà Shuō Jì Pǐn)

The intention and doctrinal purport of this chapter of verse-speaking are similar to the above, differing only in the names of the chapters, the stages of practice, and the locations. This can be understood by reference to the above.

### Chapter Seventeen: The Ten Practices (Shí Xíng Pǐn)

How does this chapter of the Ten Practices differ from the preceding Ten Abidings? The Ten Abidings illuminate the determined correspondence of the initial generation of the mind through faith and understanding. The present Ten Practices illuminate the meaning of practice accomplished and established, going and attending. How do we know this? Understanding is made clear through practice, and diligent practice is accomplished through understanding-awakening. Understanding and practice are mutually necessary; this is the natural order of principle. This constitutes the sequence of arising.

Moreover, the terms "understanding-practice" and "dedication" both refer to stages. The so-called true nature illuminates meaning; one engages and observes, and understanding-practice merges and accords, fusing in a single standard. In the ocean of the dharma-realm, one approaches the profound and pours into it endlessly: hence "understanding-practice," also called "dedication." This is not a distinction of before and after.

The word "practice" means: embodying the Way, returning to the fundamental, settling the mind in void quiescence, having already cultivated benevolence, advancing without obstruction. Though coursing like lightning across the eight wildernesses, one is not fettered by extraneous conditions. The light of compassion shines equally; the ten directions are unified; ten thousand vehicles are gathered in a flash; all characteristics are brought together in a single unmoving thought. Like the great mountain-king, constantly blazing. The meaning and characteristics of such special advancement are beyond measure. Why? The preceding initial generation of the mind at the Ten Abidings was said to be inexhaustible even by all buddhas. How much more so this accomplishment and establishment of practice! How could names and words exhaust it? The sequential connection of this chapter to the Ten Abidings is the same in meaning, differing only in stages of practice.

### Chapter Eighteen: The Ten Treasuries (Shí Zàng Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter discussed the Ten Practices of transcendence and advancement, reaching ultimate fulfillment and penetration, arriving at fusion without obstruction, the mind merging with the true realm of the dharma-realm treasury-ocean. Therefore it illuminates ten kinds of inexhaustible treasuries to accomplish the ten kinds of practice discussed above. Hence it further illuminates the meaning of the ten treasuries. The ten treasuries are: (1) the treasury of faith, (2) the treasury of precepts, (3) the treasury of shame, (4) the treasury of conscience, (5) the treasury of learning, (6) the treasury of giving, (7) the treasury of wisdom, (8) the treasury of right mindfulness, (9) the treasury of retention, (10) the treasury of eloquence. These are the ten. The word "treasury" is a name for the dharma-ocean. Why "treasury"? To show that the teaching is all-governing, the meaning all-embracing, the virtues complete in every capacity, and the principles constant: hence "treasury."

### Chapter Nineteen: Ascending to the Tuṣita Heaven Palace (Shēng Dōushuài Tiāngōng Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter discussed the ten kinds of treasury-ocean, illuminating that principle and practice have initially been accomplished, that victorious advance becomes ever more wondrous, that true understanding becomes ever more bright, that understanding and practice merge in their administration, that the preparatory way is complete, and that correct verification free of outflows naturally becomes present. Therefore, by analogy with the career of a one-lifetime [bodhisattva], its meaning is expressed.

### Chapter Twenty-One: The Ten Dedications (Shí Huíxiàng Pǐn), Together with the Chapter on Praise

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter illuminated that practice is accomplished. Since practice is supported by principle, true understanding cannot be displayed without practice. When understanding and practice merge in equilibrium, the path of bodhi is established; the vow to share it equally with sentient beings [is made]: though the buddha-fruit is not yet ultimately complete, the intention of non-abiding is accomplished. Therefore, even a single fine hair of goodness naturally tends toward the ocean of sarvajña (*sàpóruò*, 薩婆若, "omniscience"). Hence the chapter on Dedications.

Within the Ten Dedications there are three sections: the first three are dedications illuminated in terms of dharma; the next three are dedications illuminated in terms of the Way; the latter three are dedications illuminated in terms of the vehicle. The tenth, "Immeasurable Dharma-Realm," completes the three meanings above: the practices of the dharma-realm ocean are fully accomplished, and the meaning of aspiration and verification is beyond the power of words: hence "Immeasurable Dharma-Realm Dedication."

### Chapter Twenty-Two: The Ten Grounds (Shí Dì Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: in the preceding Dedications, the great bodhisattvas have penetrated the profound origin in their understanding; there is not a single dharma in which one can give rise to conceptual thought; practice and contemplation merge with the void teaching; one does not even see a single characteristic worth preserving. Therefore, when practice is complete and wisdom transcendent, true verification of principle is accomplished. Mind and function are entirely realized: hence this chapter follows. The word "ground" (*dì*) means: it definitely generates and brings to completion the Buddha's knowledge and upholding. Moreover, one constantly arouses the four kinds of great wisdom to convey and transform sentient beings, and further establishes the five kinds of Nārāyaṇa pillars. For these reasons, it is called a "ground": hence the chapter on the Ten Grounds.

### Chapter Twenty-Three: The Ten Kinds of Illumination (Shí Míng Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter discussed the great bodhisattvas of the ten grounds, whose virtuous practices are universally conveyed, whose way is fully complete. Taking the dharma-nature as their body and complete illumination as their function, they fully comprehend all dharmas. To manifest this meaning, the chapter on the Ten Kinds of Illumination follows. The main purport within it does not go beyond the three kinds of illumination, extended through the six superknowledges to become ten.

How do we know this? The conclusion below says: "The bodhisattva who abides in this illumination of knowledge fully attains the unobstructed wisdom-illumination of the three times."

(1) The illumination of knowing others' minds. (2) The unobstructed divine-eye illumination. (3) The illumination of the unobstructed knowledge of past lives, extending into the limitless past. (4) The illumination of the unobstructed wisdom extending into the limitless future. (5) The unobstructed pure divine-ear illumination. (6) The illumination of the wisdom-power of fearless supernatural power. (7) The illumination of the wisdom that distinguishes all sounds and languages. (8) The illumination of the wisdom that produces immeasurable and countless form-body adornments. (9) The illumination of the wisdom of the true reality of all dharmas. (10) The illumination of the wisdom of the certainty of all dharmas.

### Chapter Twenty-Four: The Ten Kinds of Patience (Shí Rěn Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter discussed the illumination of complete clarity that comprehends all dharmas. This chapter reveals that true understanding is equal and correctly abides in non-arising. Settling the mind in principle is called "patience" (*rěn*). Illuminated according to its characteristics: hence "The Ten Kinds of Patience."

### Chapter Twenty-Five: The Incalculable (Āsēngqí Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapters broadly discussed the virtuous practices of all buddhas and bodhisattvas as immeasurable, boundless, and inconceivable, all called asaṃkhyeya ("incalculable"), extending to "unspeakable," to indicate the nature of their practices. Yet the reason for this had not been explained. Therefore the Tathāgata expounds this meaning.

### Chapter Twenty-Six: Lifespan (Shòumìng Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter illuminated that virtuous practices are immeasurably vast and indescribable. To show that virtue is accomplished through practice and personhood is established through attainment, and that since the functions of personhood and dharma are accomplished and the Way extends without end, so too lifespan shares the same [boundless] nature: hence the chapter on Lifespan.

### Chapter Twenty-Seven: The Dwelling Places of Bodhisattvas (Púsà Zhùchù Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: to show that bodhisattvas are replete with virtuous practices, that their lifespan is without center or boundary, that the dharma-body is equal, and that non-abiding is their abiding: hence the chapter on the Dwelling Places of Bodhisattvas.

### Chapter Twenty-Eight: The Inconceivable Qualities of the Buddha-Dharma (Fófǎ Bùsīyì Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapters broadly illuminated the inconceivable virtuous practices of bodhisattvas, but the meaning of the merits of all buddhas had not yet been addressed. Therefore the chapter on the Inconceivable follows. Within it, the Tathāgata's eight kinds of transcendent sounds are illuminated: (1) the Brahmā-sound, (2) the deep sound, (3) the far-reaching sound (these three are the essence of sound); the next three are analogies: (4) the sound like a drum (vast and resonant), (5) the sound like a bell (clear and bright), (6) the sound like a golden bell (clear and distinct). The remaining two sounds are tentatively: (7) the sound of a bird, (8) the sound of a three-foot bird.

### Chapter Twenty-Nine: The Ocean of Characteristics (Xiānghǎi Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter discussed the inconceivable nature of the buddha-dharma, meaning that essence is fused without obstruction. The word "ocean" means that no characteristic is not real, and their fusion is without obstruction. Therefore, where there is no essence, there is no meaning-aspect that is not real; where there is no meaning-aspect, there is no essence that is not [present]. Where essence lacks no meaning, there one essence is all essences; where meaning lacks no essence, there one meaning is all meanings. Therefore essence is without essence-characteristics, and meaning is without meaning-characteristics; essence and meaning fuse in reciprocity, and all functions are then without characteristics that are not characteristics. Now, relying on the real characteristics to manifest wondrous virtues: hence "Ocean of Characteristics."

### Chapter Thirty: Minor Characteristics (Xiǎoxiàng Pǐn)

This chapter differs from the above; it is a name of particular distinction. "Minor" (*hǎo*) means following conditions in this way. Displayed and manifested, it is called "characteristic"; contemplated and appreciated, it is called "suchness." Based on this meaning, since the minor marks are distinguished separately, it is called "Minor Characteristics."

### Chapter Thirty-One: The Practices of Bodhisattva Samantabhadra (Pǔxián Púsà Xíng Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapters illuminated that the merits of the fruit pervade the dharma-realm ocean without center or boundary. Without a perfect cause that exhausts the boundary of reality, how could one accord with this? Therefore, since the perfect fruit has been manifested, the perfect cause must be displayed. It may also be said that this is the practice of the fruit, attributed to the cause for its designation. Why? Pervading the dharma-realm in extent is called "universal" (*pǔ*); being supremely compliant and harmonious is called "worthy" (*xián*); the function of the bodhi-way is called "practice": hence "Practices of Bodhisattva Samantabhadra."

### Chapter Thirty-Two: The Arising from the Nature of the Jewel-King Tathāgata (Bǎowáng Rúlái Xìngqǐ Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapters broadly discussed perfect cause and perfect fruit; the perfect cause accords with the reality of principle, the perfect fruit equals the posterior limit. The purport of the wondrous transcendence of cause and fruit is the so-called true nature. Therefore the meaning of "arising from the nature" is illuminated next.

The nature and its function have never been otherwise than suchness; they do not arise. Yet the true nature has been constantly the same from ancient times to the present. Because the nature's function constantly arises, through function there is ever more quiescence. Because the true nature is eternally the same, through quiescence it is ever more manifest. Since quiescence and function are without obstruction, how can there be any dharma of defilement or purity that can be grasped? Therefore the true nature is the origin of both adversity and compliance, the root of conditioned arising. Wondrous function arises through this; deluded characteristics cease through this. But arising and cessation proceed from deluded sentiments. For us, [the nature] has never acted or not acted, yet there is nothing it does not accomplish. Hence "arising from the nature."

"Jewel-King" is an analogy: like a wish-fulfilling gem that is sovereign at will. The true nature is likewise. "Tathāgata" manifests the nature through the display of the perfected: hence "Tathāgata." Therefore it is called the chapter on the Arising from the Nature of the Jewel-King Tathāgata.

### Chapter Thirty-Three: Transcending the World (Lí Shìjiān Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter discussed how the true nature subsumes all dharmas in utter emptiness and quiescence, all characteristics returning to the fundamental, the perfect brightness shining alone. If there were only quiescence without arising, it would be the same as empty space. How then could it be the true brightness of the nature-essence? Therefore the meaning of function is further illuminated: the dharma-realm's equal function, extending to the posterior limit, complete in every capacity: this is called "the world" (*shìjiān*). Being without any characteristics to grasp is called "transcending" (*lí*). It is not that there is some dharma that can be transcended and then called "transcending." Hence "Transcending the World."

### Chapter Thirty-Four: Entering the Dharma-Realm (Rù Fǎjiè Pǐn)

The reason this chapter arises: the preceding chapter discussed the dharma-realm's equal function, with meaning extending to the posterior limit. If this is not actualized in oneself, however wondrous, what benefit is it? Therefore "entering the dharma-realm" is next illuminated. Principle and characteristics mutually unified is called the "dharma-realm." Understanding fusing with true quiescence is called "entering." Accordance with function without obstruction is called "practice." This is the Buddha's true heir who continues the lineage of the Three Treasures. This constitutes the meaning of transmission that is unbroken: hence "Entering the Dharma-Realm."

### The Four Great Vows

The text on the four great vows: bodhi is difficult to enter; by means of vows one engages with and aspires toward it. Therefore bodhisattvas who have just generated the initial aspiration, wishing to end evil, cultivate goodness, broadly transform sentient beings, and seek the distant buddha-fruit, must make the great vows their foundation. Once the great vows are established, no aspiration fails to be fulfilled. As the Buddha praised: the Tathāgata's great wisdom, the power of meritorious exposition, the power of verified patience, wisdom, merit, and karma, the power of vows. The supreme bodhisattva's single vow surpasses the practice of a hundred thousand kalpas of a śrāvaka. The bodhisattva's practices and vows are truly immeasurable, but these four, briefly raised, completely encompass all.

**The First Vow**: "I, disciple so-and-so, vow to sever all afflictions. The buddha-dharma is profoundly deep; I vow to exhaust its source and its depths." The constant abiding of the two truths, with their principles and characteristics profoundly subtle, is called "deeply deep." One vows to exhaust the source of principle and completely sever delusion. Although one does not immediately sever them, the power of the great vow is strong: in life after life, wherever one is born, all afflictions naturally weaken. Therefore the sūtra says: "One who cultivates the mind for two lifetimes, although having afflictions, is as though without afflictions." This is the first vow.

**The Second Vow**: "I vow to cultivate all merits. Birth-and-death is fearful; I vow to constantly and joyfully dwell in the middle." Just as a person in the world cannot obtain the wish-fulfilling gem without entering the ocean, so a bodhisattva cannot obtain the unadorned wish-fulfilling gem of the buddha-fruit without entering the ocean of birth-and-death. Therefore the great bodhisattva, supremely revering the victorious fruit, vows to go forth through birth and death, broadly cultivating the ten thousand practices, not counting sufferings, taking the vow as the seed that comprehensively gathers all practices. Wherever one is born, all roots of merit naturally accumulate and increase in accordance with the vow. This is the second vow.

**The Third Vow**: "I vow to save all sentient beings. Sentient beings are without limit; I vow to save them all." Toward enemies one abandons enmity; toward kin one abandons attachment. Enemies and kin being equal, one broadly practices the four means of gathering. The vow is that all sentient beings of the dharma-realm may together, simultaneously, become buddhas. Having abandoned both enmity and attachment, one universally practices great compassion. All enmity, poison, and ill will naturally cease. Inner goodness is perfumed and cultivated; one is born in the family of the Buddha, of noble birth, beyond reproach. This is the third vow.

**The Fourth Vow**: "I vow to attain omniscience (*yīqiè zhǒngzhì*, 一切種智). The buddha-way is long and distant, but I shall certainly obtain it." One will never generate the thought of satisfaction with the superior fruits of the heavens or the nirvāṇa of the two vehicles. The roots of merit of the three times are all dedicated to unsurpassed bodhi, without counting the number of kalpas, taking the attainment of buddhahood as one's commitment. Since one's aspiration is lofty and far-reaching, not seeking worldly rewards, wherever one is born, one always dwells in the highest nobility. Though one reaches the apex of worldly glory, one does not generate deep attachment, wishing only for the most supreme path, with no other thought. This is the fourth vow.
