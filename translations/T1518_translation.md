# 佛母般若波羅蜜多圓集要義論
## Treatise on the Collected Essential Meaning of the Prajñāpāramitā, Mother of Buddhas

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1518

Composed by Dignāga (大域龍). Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護) and others.

---

Homage to the Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva Mañjuśrī-kumārabhūta and the rest.

> Prajñā and the rest are accomplished;
> Non-dual wisdom is the Tathāgata.
> The meaning therein corresponds;
> Those verbal teachings have two paths.
>
> Support and function,
> Activities practiced jointly,
> Characteristics of discrimination and faults,
> Praise explained in due order.
>
> With faith as its essence,
> Teacher and student mutually attest and explain.
> Time of teaching, place of teaching, and so forth:
> One attains one's own measure of accomplishment.
>
> The Dharma teacher should know:
> Worldly time and place are two.
> When the speaker has co-realization,
> Then the proper measure is attained.
>
> "All," "thus assembled,"
> "I have heard," and so forth are stated.
> The meaning of such combination
> Is supreme: thirty-two.
>
> Discriminating sixteen aspects,
> Emptiness in due order,
> As explained in the Eight Thousand Lines,
> Understood through different skillful means.
>
> Now in these Eight Thousand Lines,
> The meaning as stated is not diminished.
> Condensed in verses as preferred,
> The meaning is thus as stated.
>
> "Bodhisattva, I do not see":
> This statement is truly silent.
> Able to receive the internal matters,
> That is spoken of as emptiness.
>
> Form and form's self-nature,
> This too is spoken of as empty.
> These external sense-bases:
> The portion that is perceived all ceases.
>
> Form and other characteristics, that body,
> Abiding and separated from characteristics.
> If one sees toward that meaning,
> The internal is without reality.
>
> Those internal emptiness-natures,
> Their self-nature is also empty.
> All seeds of consciousness-characteristics:
> Thus arise my compassionate wisdom.
>
> Not born and not destroyed,
> Sentient beings, this and so forth are clear.
> Sentient beings' birth-death and desire:
> That is spoken of as empty.
>
> Buddha-dharmas cannot be seen,
> Bodhisattva-dharmas likewise.
> These, as stated,
> Empty are the ten powers and so forth.
>
> Each and every dharma:
> This is spoken of as the imagined nature.
> That is non-existent in the ultimate sense;
> All dharmas are spoken of thus.
>
> Those views of self and so forth are severed;
> The great being ultimately accomplishes this.
> And that selflessness of persons,
> The Buddha proclaims everywhere.
>
> All dharmas are unborn;
> What is stated here is likewise.
> Proclaiming the selflessness of dharmas,
> Truly spoken everywhere.
>
> With faults and without faults,
> Neither increasing nor decreasing.
> All conditioned and unconditioned:
> All virtues cease.
>
> Within the emptiness-nature of virtues,
> Their emergence is also inexhaustible.
> This imagined discrimination:
> That is universally subsumed as emptiness.
>
> Ten kinds of mental distraction,
> Mental distraction in different places.
> The foolish do not attain correspondence;
> Non-dual wisdom is not accomplished.
>
> Their mutual cessation
> Serves as agent and object of the antidote.
> In the Prajñā teaching,
> That comprehensive collection explains.
>
> If a bodhisattva has
> This signless discrimination,
> The teacher who stills distraction
> Explains those conventional aggregates.
>
> These Eight Thousand Lines and so forth,
> From the first word in order,
> Until completion all ceases:
> Explaining signless discrimination.
>
> Because the words are not thus,
> This only explains phenomenal characteristics.
> In sūtras like the Brahmajāla and so forth,
> All is known in accordance with principle.
>
> "Bodhisattva, I do not see":
> And these are vast.
> The World-Honored One here dispels
> The distraction of discrimination with signs.
>
> If one does not see that name,
> The domain and practice are likewise.
> Those aggregates, everywhere:
> One does not see the bodhisattva.
>
> This dispels the imagined,
> Universally subsuming what is stated here.
> Riding the cause of omniscience,
> Wisdom discriminates all characteristics.
>
> The Prajñāpāramitā
> Explains three kinds of support:
> The imagined, the dependent,
> And the perfectly accomplished nature.
>
> Without these stated phrases,
> All imagining ceases.
> Illusion metaphors and the edge of seeing:
> This explains the dependent nature.
>
> There are four kinds of purity,
> Explaining the perfectly accomplished nature.
> The Prajñāpāramitā:
> The Buddha declares without distinction.
>
> Ten discriminative distractions,
> Their antidotes explained in order.
> Having known these three kinds,
> Explained as either identical or separate.
>
> As the first words: the perfectly accomplished,
> The dependent and the imagined.
> Signless discrimination of form:
> That distraction is dispelled.
>
> That buddha is also bodhi;
> Not seeing the speaker and so forth.
> Until completion this is known:
> Dispelling the imagined nature.
>
> Self-nature is empty, that form:
> What does the combined characteristic possess?
> In these different words,
> Having understood, that ceases.
>
> "This is not empty, therefore empty":
> As such words state.
> All defamatory discriminations,
> All statements cease.
>
> As an illusion, so too the buddha;
> That is like a dream, likewise.
> Thus known in due order,
> Wisdom is determined at the edge of words.
>
> The actions of all who are equal:
> This states the buddha is like an illusion.
> Illusion metaphors and similar words:
> This explains the dependent nature.
>
> If the wisdom of ordinary beings,
> That self-nature is pure.
> Therefore it is said by that buddha:
> The bodhisattva is also like the buddha.
>
> Self-nature is covered by its own form;
> That is made by the cause of ignorance.
> Like an illusion appearing differently,
> The fruit is discarded like a dream.
>
> Non-duality is explained differently;
> The fruit and so forth are certainly defamation.
> Defamatory discriminations:
> That defamation is thus stated.
>
> Form and emptiness are not combined;
> They mutually oppose and obstruct.
> Without form, without the name of emptiness,
> The characteristic of form is self-combined.
>
> This one-nature discrimination,
> The antidote to multiplicity.
> Emptiness is not different from that form:
> What does that emptiness possess?
>
> This appears without reality;
> That arises from ignorance.
> This expresses without reality;
> Because that is said to be ignorance.
>
> Thus form is spoken of;
> The Prajñāpāramitā.
> Non-dual, dual, thus:
> Two, the antidote to discrimination.
>
> As principle states, pure nature
> Likewise cannot be obtained.
> Nature and non-nature in opposition and so forth:
> Fixed views of multiplicity.
>
> This form is spoken of as mere name;
> In truth, without self-nature.
> That self-nature discrimination,
> Once accepted, should then cease.
>
> Form and form's self-nature
> Are empty, as previously stated.
> That self-nature and combined characteristic,
> Discrimination: this is dispelled.
>
> Not born, not destroyed, and so forth:
> The contemplation of all dharmas.
> The Buddha says, if scattered and different,
> That is discriminating differences.
>
> False names and words and so forth:
> If those dharmas are discriminated,
> Sound and meaning, the two not combined,
> That is not the intent of self-nature.
>
> Prajñāpāramitā,
> Buddhas and bodhisattvas likewise.
> What is stated here is mere name,
> Apart from real-meaning discrimination.
>
> All sounds and meanings cease;
> This is not dispelling things.
> Thus the rest is also known;
> The meaning within words is determined.
>
> This non-apprehension is correct;
> All names are truly known.
> As the nature of meaning is thus,
> One does not dispel those sounds.
>
> Subhūti is separated from both;
> Sound and the meaning of sound are thus.
> "Bodhisattva" is without name:
> "I see this," it is said.
>
> Prajñāpāramitā:
> Words have no determinate arising.
> Only the wise investigate this;
> The meaning is subtle wisdom.
>
> Continuous meaning is removed;
> If separate meanings are discriminated,
> The Prajñāpāramitā:
> Those words are like an echo.
>
> In summary, such meaning:
> Prajñā and the rest as support.
> Such meaning circulates;
> Again, separate meanings serve as support.
>
> The Prajñāpāramitā
> Correctly subsumes the Eight Thousand Lines.
> The accumulation of merit obtained from it
> All arises from prajñā.
