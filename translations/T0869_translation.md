# 金剛頂經瑜伽十八會指歸

## Summary of the Eighteen Assemblies of the Vajraśekhara Yoga Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0869

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Amoghavajra (不空).

---

The Vajraśekhara Yoga Sūtra has one hundred thousand verses in eighteen assemblies.

### First Assembly

The first assembly is called the True Apprehension of All Tathāgatas, the King of Teachings. It has four great sections: the first is called Vajradhātu, the second Trailokyavijaya, the third Universal Subjugation, and the fourth Accomplishment of All Purposes. These represent the four wisdom-mudrās.

In the first section there are six maṇḍalas. The first is the Vajradhātu great maṇḍala, which also teaches how Vairocana Buddha, in his enjoyment body, through the five aspects manifests the attainment of perfect enlightenment. After attaining buddhahood, through the vajra-samādhi he generates thirty-seven wisdoms, extensively teaches the rites of the maṇḍala, and confers upon disciples the method for swiftly realizing the bodhisattva-ground and the buddha-ground. The second teaches the dhāraṇī maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven deities; in this maṇḍala the holy assembly all abide in the form of the perfections, and extensively teaches the rite for entering the maṇḍala, conferring upon disciples four kinds of vision, and teaching the rites for attraction, summoning, subjugation, and pacification. The third teaches the subtle vajra maṇḍala, also complete with thirty-seven holy deities depicted within vajras, each holding a samādhi-mudrā; it extensively teaches the rite for entering the maṇḍala, enabling disciples to have minds that are receptive, pliant, and self-mastered, teaching the subtle vajra-samādhi and the methods of cultivating the four dhyānas, the four immeasurable minds, and the three gates of liberation. The fourth teaches the great offering karma-maṇḍala of all tathāgatas, also complete with thirty-seven deities, each holding their own emblem and abiding in offerings; it extensively teaches the method of entering the maṇḍala, teaching disciples to receive the sixteen great offerings and the four kinds of secret offerings. The fifth teaches the four-mudrā maṇḍala method, in which disciples receive four kinds of swift accomplishment, and through this maṇḍala they seek the siddhis accomplished in the above four maṇḍalas, seeking accomplishment before such images. The sixth teaches the one-mudrā maṇḍala: if one holds the mantra of Vairocana and Vajrasattva Bodhisattva, it is complete with seventeen deities; the rest are each complete with thirteen. It also teaches the rite for entering the maṇḍala, conferring upon disciples the preliminary practices and cultivating the samādhi of the principal deity.

### Trailokyavijaya Section

Next is the great section of Trailokyavijaya, with six maṇḍalas. After the Tathāgata attained perfect enlightenment, on the summit of Mount Sumeru he turned the Vajradhātu wheel. Having conferred names and offices upon the bodhisattvas, Maheśvara and other beings who were stubborn and difficult to convert could not be tamed by peaceful methods. All the tathāgatas throughout the dharma-realm and empty space, with a single voice, requested Vajrasattva to be praised with one hundred and eight names. Since such devas could not be converted by peaceful methods, the Bodhisattva Vajrapāṇi, having received the request of all tathāgatas, entered the wrathful vajra-samādhi, manifested a body of great awesome power, and by various skillful means subdued them, even unto death. After Maheśvara died, he saw himself fall below through sixty-two Ganges-sands of worlds to a world called Ash-Adorned. In that world he attained perfect enlightenment with the name Terrifying Sovereign King Tathāgata. The Bodhisattva Vajradhara stepped upon him with his foot and recited the vajra-life mantra, and Maheśvara was revived. Having been converted, Vajrasattva then taught the great maṇḍala, introduced the devas, and conferred vajra-names. These devas are of five classes:

The first class: heavenly kings dwelling in the upper realms, Maheśvara and immeasurable devas with their consorts.

The second class: devas traveling through space, such as Sūrya and immeasurable devas with their consorts.

The third class: devas dwelling in space, such as the Māra king and immeasurable devas with their consorts.

The fourth class: earth-dwelling devas, such as the deity of treasuries and immeasurable devas with their consorts.

The fifth class: subterranean devas such as Vārāhī and immeasurable devas with their consorts. All having been introduced, the devas were commanded to establish maṇḍalas: "You all shall attend the assembly, and whatever siddhis you seek shall all be accomplished." These all belong to the outer vajra division.

The first teaches the great maṇḍala rite, complete with thirty-seven deities, teaching the method of subjugation and the method of cultivating supernatural powers. The second teaches the secret maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, with various sounds and vajra songs and dances. The third teaches the dharma-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, with teachings on using loving-kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity to perform abhicāruka rites and the subtle vajra mind-training ritual. The fourth teaches the karma-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for entering the maṇḍala, enabling disciples to learn the homa rite, to accomplish vast offerings to immeasurable buddhas and bodhisattvas, to swiftly obtain siddhis, and teaching twenty-five types of homa furnaces for various purposes. The fifth teaches the four-mudrā maṇḍala, complete with twenty-one, for accomplishing medicinal methods and other siddhis from the above four maṇḍalas, seeking accomplishment before images of this maṇḍala. The sixth teaches the one-mudrā maṇḍala, complete with seventeen, teaching the rite for introducing disciples and the preliminary practices.

Next, for the outer vajra division assembly, four kinds of maṇḍalas are taught, each teaching its own mantras and mudrās offered to the Buddha. The Buddha then teaches the commanded great maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, and teaching disciples the rules for employing the outer vajra division, including the great uṣṇīṣa and the flame-cluster uṣṇīṣa mantras and mudrās, which also encompass the method of the one-syllable cakra. Next the second commanded samaya-maṇḍala is taught: those deva-consorts and others each offer their own mantras, and the Buddha teaches the maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching disciples the methods for cultivating yakṣa and yakṣinī practices, with extensive rites. Next the third commanded dharma-maṇḍala is taught: the devas recite mantras and offer them to the Buddha, and the Buddha teaches the maṇḍala for them, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, and teaching disciples the dharma-mudrās of the devas; through these mudrās they do not transgress their original vow. Next the fourth commanded karma-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for introducing disciples: those devas each recite their own mantras, and the Buddha teaches the maṇḍala, teaching the rites of deva-dance and the swift methods for accomplishing all works.

### Universal Subjugation Section

Next is the great section of Universal Subjugation, with six kinds of maṇḍalas.

The first is the great maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven deities, all transformations of the Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara. It teaches the rite for introducing disciples, and within this teaches sixteen kinds of swift supernatural-power samādhi rites. The second teaches the samaya-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, all transformations of Avalokiteśvara, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, with sixteen kinds of samādhi for attraction and summoning. The third teaches the dharma-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, all transformations of Avalokiteśvara, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, with sixteen kinds of methods for cultivating the mind and seeking wisdom and eloquence. The fourth teaches the karma-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, all transformations of Avalokiteśvara, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, with the lotus-division offering rite and methods for transforming the retribution of sins, the obstacles of coverings, and the obstacles of karma. The fifth teaches the lotus-division four-mudrā maṇḍala, complete with twenty-one, all transformations of Avalokiteśvara, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, with the preliminary practices for accomplishment and the methods of the above four maṇḍalas. The sixth teaches the one-mudrā maṇḍala of the lotus division, complete with thirteen, all transformations of Avalokiteśvara, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, with the method of cultivating the principal deity, encompassing both worldly and supramundane methods.

### Accomplishment of All Purposes Section

Next, in the great section of Accomplishment of All Purposes, there are six maṇḍalas. The first is the great maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven deities. It teaches the rite for introducing disciples; by entering this maṇḍala, the karma of poverty is eliminated, and it teaches methods for seeking abundant wealth, and for seeking the stations of buddhas, bodhisattvas, and worldly glory. The second is the secret samaya-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, methods for seeking hidden treasures, and the method for swiftly fulfilling the merit-accumulation of the perfection of generosity. The third is the dharma-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, methods for cultivating samādhi within the jewel division to make the mind stable, receptive, pliant, and self-mastered, and the method for seeing the Bodhisattva Ākāśagarbha. The fourth is the karma-maṇḍala, complete with thirty-seven, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, the work of empowering the excavation of hidden treasures, and the rite of vast offerings to the buddhas within the jewel division. The fifth is the four-mudrā maṇḍala, complete with twenty-one, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, the preliminary practices, and the siddhi-methods of the four maṇḍalas. The sixth is the one-mudrā maṇḍala, complete with thirteen, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, the method of cultivating a single deity, and various medicinal samādhis. All of these are transformations of the Bhagavān Vajra Ākāśagarbha.

Next, a comprehensive teaching is given for each of the above maṇḍalas: secret auxiliary skillful means and scattered recitations. Afterward, it is shown how Śākyamuni Buddha descended to Jambudvīpa, and the eight phases of his emanation-body attaining the path were all the magical transformations of the Bodhisattva Samantabhadra. All the tathāgatas again praise Vajrasattva with one hundred and eight names. Such is the first assembly.

### Second through Eighteenth Assemblies

The second assembly is called the Secret King Yoga of All Tathāgatas, taught at the Akaniṣṭha heaven. It has four great sections, extensively teaching the subtle truth of reality, and extensively teaching the subjugation of Maheśvara, with Maheśvara offering verses in dialogue with the Vajra Bodhisattva.

The third assembly is called the Collected Teachings Yoga, taught in the dharma-realm palace. All the tathāgatas with a single voice ask the Bodhisattva Vajrasattva one hundred and eight questions, and Vajrasattva answers each one. This sūtra teaches the great maṇḍala of five divisions, with five maṇḍalas in each division, each complete with thirty-seven, forming one great maṇḍala. Each deity individually teaches four mudrās: the great mudrā, the samaya-mudrā, the dharma-mudrā, and the karma-mudrā, each with its accomplishment method. This sūtra teaches one hundred and twenty-five kinds of homa furnaces, each with its own purpose.

The fourth assembly is called the Trailokyavijaya Vajra Yoga, taught on the summit of Mount Sumeru. Vajragarbha and the other eight great bodhisattvas each teach four kinds of maṇḍalas. The first assembly teaches the subjugation of Maheśvara and the introduction of devas into the maṇḍala, conferring positions and names through four kinds of maṇḍalas: great maṇḍala, samaya-maṇḍala, dharma-maṇḍala, and karma-maṇḍala. Each deity teaches the rite for introducing disciples and the accomplishment method. Afterward, a comprehensive teaching is given on the sequence of samaya mudrā-binding for all deities, as well as the secret precepts and secret practices.

The fifth assembly is called the Worldly and Supramundane Vajra Yoga, taught in the sky above Vārāṇasī. It briefly teaches the maṇḍalas of the five buddhas, the maṇḍalas of the bodhisattvas and the outer vajra division, each maṇḍala complete with four kinds, each teaching the rite for introducing disciples and the method for seeking siddhis.

The sixth assembly is called the Great Bliss Non-Empty Samaya True Yoga, taught in the palace of the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven. This sūtra teaches the Samantabhadra maṇḍala, then the Vairocana maṇḍala, then those of Vajragarbha through Vajramuṣṭi Bodhisattva and the outer vajra division, teaching the principle of prajñā. Each deity is fully provided with four kinds of maṇḍalas, each teaching the rite for introducing disciples, conferring the Dharma of the prajñāpāramitā of the principle-truth and the reception of four kinds of mudrās. Within each section, methods for worldly and supramundane siddhis are taught.

The seventh assembly is called the Samantabhadra Yoga, taught in the palace of the Bodhisattva Samantabhadra. This sūtra teaches Samantabhadra and the bodhisattvas through Vajramuṣṭi and the outer vajra division. Each deity teaches four kinds of maṇḍalas, teaching the rite for introducing disciples, the reception of four kinds of mudrās, and the cultivation of worldly and supramundane siddhis. This sūtra teaches that the practitioner has no fixed time or direction, does not depend on worldly prohibitions, takes bodhicitta as primary, and the precept of non-action as the foundation.

The eighth assembly is called the Supreme Beginning Yoga, taught in the palace of Samantabhadra. Samantabhadra and the others through the outer vajra division each teach four kinds of maṇḍalas, teaching the truth of reality and distinguishing the rites of the various maṇḍalas. It is somewhat more extensive than the seventh assembly, though the teaching is broadly the same.

The ninth assembly is called the Assembly of All Buddhas Ḍākinī Precept-Net Yoga, taught in the mantra-palace. This teaches the yoga of establishing one's own body as the principal deity, criticizing the yoga of those who make external images as the principal form. It extensively teaches the truth of reality, the root sources of the five divisions, and teaches that the yogic method possesses nine flavors: beauty, heroism, great compassion, laughter, wrath, terror, disgust, wonder, and peace. It teaches Samantabhadra and the others through Vajramuṣṭi, each teaching four kinds of maṇḍalas, the rite for introducing disciples, the reception of four kinds of mudrās, and the songs and dances of the five divisions.

The tenth assembly is called the Great Samaya Yoga, taught in the dharma-realm palace. Samantabhadra and the others through the sixteen great bodhisattvas including Vajramuṣṭi, with the four attracting deities unified as one body, each teach four kinds of maṇḍalas, the rite for introducing disciples, and the reception of four kinds of mudrās. In this sūtra, a verse says:

> "Foolish children, covered by ignorance,
> Do not know this principle;
> They seek the Buddha elsewhere,
> Not awakening to what is here.
> In the worlds of the ten directions,
> It cannot be found elsewhere;
> The mind itself is equal awakening,
> Elsewhere, the Buddha is not taught."

The eleventh assembly is called the Mahāyāna Direct Realization Yoga, taught at the Akaniṣṭha heaven. Vairocana Buddha and the others through Vajra Viśvakarman Bodhisattva, together with the eight great offering and four attracting deities born from the same True Apprehension Yoga, each possess four kinds of maṇḍalas and four mudrās, extensively teaching the truth of reality and the mind-established rites of the maṇḍalas.

The twelfth assembly is called the Supreme Samaya Yoga, taught at the bodhi-site in empty space. Vairocana and the principal bodhisattvas of the four divisions, Vajramuṣṭi and the other eight bodhisattvas and the outer vajra division, each teach four kinds of maṇḍalas and four mudrās and other practices, totaling eight. In this sūtra, the maṇḍala is established upon one's own body, teaching the yoga of one's own body as the principal deity, and extensively teaching the gate of the syllable *A* as penetrating both defilement and purity, the conditioned and unconditioned, without obstruction.

The thirteenth assembly is called the Great Samaya Truth Yoga, taught at the Vajradhātu maṇḍala bodhi-site. All the buddhas of the ten directions with a single voice request Vajrasattva: "May you please teach the true Dharma of the samaya; we have already received it, but may Vajrasattva teach it for the bodhisattvas." Having accepted the request, he teaches the seventeen-syllable mantra of Samantabhadra Bodhisattva, the Pleasing Non-Empty maṇḍala complete with seventeen, and also teaches four kinds of maṇḍalas, one hundred and eight path-mudrās, and methods for worldly and supramundane siddhis. Following this, the bodhisattvas and the outer vajra division each teach their own maṇḍalas, mantras, and mudrās. Samantabhadra Bodhisattva teaches the seventeen deities of the secret maṇḍala, whose limbs each re-enter the body of the principal deity to form five deities sharing a single lotus platform. He teaches a one-syllable mantra: from the eyes, mouth, and all limbs, through transformation, the mudrā is formed. One simply abides in the great mudrā and binds the karma-mudrā, without the need for preliminary practices, without protective empowerment, and without the need for invitation. Past karmic obstructions cannot intrude or obstruct, and swift accomplishment is attained.

The fourteenth assembly is called the Tathāgata Samaya Truth Yoga. In this sūtra, Samantabhadra Bodhisattva and the sixteen great bodhisattvas, with the four attracting deities unified as one body, teach four kinds of maṇḍalas and four mudrās, extensively teaching the mutual interpenetration of the five divisions: the Tathāgata division is the Vajra, the Lotus division is the Jewel, they mutually pervade one another. The dharma-realm is true suchness, prajñā is the ultimate reality; in provisional designations there are differences, but in the original they are one body. Next, after Samantabhadra, the bodhisattvas and the outer vajra division each teach their own mantras, maṇḍalas, and mudrās.

The fifteenth assembly is called the Secret Gathering Yoga, taught in a secret place, namely the site of the teacher-consort, called the Palace of Prajñāpāramitā. This teaches Dharma, altars, mudrās, mantras, and the abiding precepts, using language that resembles worldly expressions of desire and attachment. In the assembly, the Bodhisattva Sarvanīvaraṇaviṣkambhin and others rose from their seats, bowed to the Buddha, and said: "World-Honored One! A great person should not utter coarse words that accord with defilement." The Buddha said: "What form do your 'pure and appropriate words' have? My words are empowered syllables, skillful means of responding to the capacities of beings, leading them into the Buddha's path. They also have no form, and they accomplish great benefit. You should not harbor doubt." From this point on, the truth of reality in samādhi is extensively taught, and each bodhisattva teaches four kinds of maṇḍalas and four mudrās.

The sixteenth assembly is called the Non-Dual Equality Yoga, taught in the dharma-realm palace. Vairocana Buddha, the bodhisattvas, and the outer vajra division each teach four kinds of maṇḍalas complete with four mudrās. This teaches the non-duality of saṃsāra and nirvāṇa, worldly and supramundane, self and other, and that the undivided mind, whether resting on sights, sounds, fragrances, tastes, touches, or mixed mental activities, whether settled or disturbed, is non-dual, identical with true suchness and the dharma-realm, and all constituting the body of all buddhas.

The seventeenth assembly is called the Like-Space Yoga, taught in the palace of the ultimate reality. Vairocana Buddha, Samantabhadra Bodhisattva, and the outer vajra division each teach four kinds of maṇḍalas complete with four mudrās. In this, the practitioner, in accordance with each deity, becomes equal in measure to empty space, corresponding to the dharma-body, free from all things. The dharma-body's radiance is equal in measure to empty space, without coming or going. This sūtra teaches the method of the samādhi of space-correspondence.

The eighteenth assembly is called the Vajra Jewel-Crown Yoga, taught at the fourth dhyāna heaven. The Bodhisattva Vajrasattva requests the Buddha to teach for Brahmā, lord of the sahā world, the yoga maṇḍala of the five divisions, the rite for introducing disciples, complete with thirty-seven, and also teaches four kinds of maṇḍalas complete with four mudrās. Down to the outer vajra division, disciples receive the practice of mental recitation: on a moon-disk, the syllables of the mantra are arrayed in a clockwise circle, and one focuses the mind on each syllable, corresponding to the truth of reality, going round and round again. This also accomplishes both worldly and supramundane siddhis. It does not depend on counting with beads as a limit; one simply realizes the gate of truth with an undistracted mind and abides in the yoga of the principal deity as the limit. In this sūtra, there is a detailed teaching on twenty kinds of signs of non-accomplishment, and many kinds of signs indicating that siddhi is near.

### Conclusion

The eighteen assemblies of the yoga teaching consist of four thousand, five thousand, or seven thousand verses each, totaling one hundred thousand verses, complete with the five divisions, four kinds of maṇḍalas, four mudrās, and thirty-seven deities. Each division is complete with thirty-seven, and even a single deity becomes thirty-seven, also possessing four maṇḍalas and four mudrās. They mutually pervade one another, like the net of Indra, whose jewels' radiance reflects and re-reflects without limit. The practitioner who well comprehends the great meaning of this yoga, like the Universally Illuminating Buddha, with each and every bodily part, each and every pore, each and every mark, each and every minor mark of excellence, each and every accumulation of merit, and each and every accumulation of wisdom, abides in the fruition-stage and proclaims the yoga that is not shared with the two vehicles. Teaching the maṇḍala samaya Dharma-gate activities, equal in measure to empty space, the realizer as described above, each in their own measure without confusion, fully realizes the four bodies: the self-nature body, the enjoyment body, the transformation body, and the equal-flow body. Through this one can instantaneously benefit and bring joy to all sentient beings, bodhisattvas, śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and all heterodox practitioners. This is called the teaching of the Yoga Vajrayāna.
