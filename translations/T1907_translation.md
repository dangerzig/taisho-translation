# Essential Record on Keeping and Violating the Bodhisattva Precepts (菩薩戒本持犯要記)

## Taisho T1907

**Author:** Wonhyo (元曉, *Yuánxiǎo*), Silla (新羅)
**Fascicles:** 1

*Note: This is a philosophical treatise on bodhisattva precept practice by the great Korean monk Wonhyo (617-686), one of the most important Buddhist thinkers in East Asian history. The text analyzes the keeping and violating of bodhisattva precepts through three gates of increasing depth: (1) the Light and Heavy Gate, classifying offenses by severity; (2) the Shallow and Deep Gate, demonstrating that superficial understanding of precepts leads to error; and (3) the Ultimate Keeping and Violating Gate, revealing the precepts' true nature as beyond existence and nonexistence. The work is notable for its sophisticated four-sentence analysis of self-praise and other-disparagement, its unflinching critique of practitioners who misuse emptiness doctrine (called "evil grasping of emptiness," echoing the Samdhinirmocana Sūtra and the Yogacarabhumi), and its dialectical method of examining apparent virtue that conceals hidden faults. The text references three precept traditions: the Dharma Precept Text (達摩戒本, with 44 light offenses), the Brahma-Net Precept Text (多羅戒本, with 48), and the Pratimoksa (別解脫戒經, with 246 light offenses).*

---

The bodhisattva precepts are the great ford for returning against the current to the source, and the essential gate for leaving what is wrong and approaching what is right. Yet the characteristics of wrong and right easily become confused, and the nature of sin and merit is hard to distinguish. Why is this? Sometimes the inner intention is truly wrong while the outer conduct appears right. Sometimes the manifest action shares in defilement while the inner mind is purely clean. Sometimes a deed that accords with small merit brings great calamity. Sometimes a mental practice that accords with what is deep and far-reaching contradicts what is shallow and near. Because of this, those who specialize in defilement and those who pursue selfish ends among the monks long specialize in seeming conduct and lose what is truly right. They constantly aim at deep precepts but seek only shallow practice. Now I wish to dispel the shallow and preserve the deep, to remove the seeming and pursue the real. Lest I forget this myself, I have gathered the essentials into a brief record. May those who share this aspiration examine it and reach a clear determination.

## The Essentials of Keeping and Violating

The essentials of keeping and violating have three gates: first, the Gate of Light and Heavy; second, the Gate of Shallow and Deep; third, the Gate of Ultimate Keeping and Violating.

## First Gate: Light and Heavy

Within this first gate there are two sections: first, a general determination of light and heavy; second, a detailed display of distinctions.

### General Determination

Among the light and heavy offenses, if one analyzes the branches in detail, the categories number eighty-four thousand. Gathering their essentials broadly, there are three classes. Either forty-four, as stated in the Dharma Precept Text (達摩戒本 *Dámó jièběn*); or forty-eight, as determined in the Brahma-Net Precept Text (多羅戒本 *Duōluó jièběn*); or two hundred forty-six light offenses, as established in the Pratimoksa Sūtra (別解脫戒經 *Biéjiětuō jiè jīng*). Within the second of these, there are shared and unshared categories; the characteristics of shared and unshared can be understood from the text. Among the heavy precepts, the general total is ten. Analyzing their types, there are also three kinds: heavy precepts shared with the lesser [vehicle], namely the first four; heavy precepts unshared, namely the latter four; and the six heavy precepts established for lay bodhisattvas, namely the first six of the ten heavy precepts. These combine both shared and unshared. The general determination of light and heavy is as described.

### Detailed Distinctions

As for the detailed distinctions, I now follow the Dharma Precept Text to analyze the characteristics and their differences. The text says: "Regarding violations, whether defiled or undefiled, whether mild, moderate, or severe, one should understand them. Whether from desire or from compassion, though the actions performed may be the same, violation and non-violation differ."

Those said to have violations refers to offenses committed through four causes. Those said to be without violation refers to actions performed through three conditions.

What are the three conditions? First, if one's mind is overwhelmed by extreme delusion. Second, if one is compelled by heavy painful feelings. Third, if one has never received the pure precept discipline. These three conditions of non-violation apply universally to all precepts. The individual cases of non-violation are as extensively explained in the text.

Among violations, there are two categories. Within the heavy, one should recognize mild, moderate, and severe grades. Within the light, one should identify defiled and undefiled.

Speaking generally, among the four causes: offenses committed through ignorance or through heedlessness are undefiled. Offenses committed through powerful afflictions or through contempt are defiled. The specific cases of defiled and undefiled can also be known from the original text.

### The Four Distinctions of Self-Praise and Other-Disparagement

Although the general explanation is as above, let me take the first precept as an example to demonstrate the characteristics.

Regarding the single matter of praise and disparagement, there are four distinctions:

1. If one praises oneself and disparages others in order to cause those people to develop the mind of faith, this is merit, not a violation.
2. If through heedlessness and a neutral state of mind one praises oneself and disparages others, this is a violation but not defiled.
3. If toward other people one has a mind of attachment or anger and praises oneself while disparaging others, this is defiled but not a heavy [offense].
4. If for the sake of greedily seeking profit and reverence one praises oneself and disparages others, this is heavy, not light.

Within the fourth category there are three grades. The causes that produce these three grades also have two routes: through the matter itself, and through the afflictions.

Through the afflictions: if the affliction manifests but is not extremely intense, or if one gives rise to shame and remorse, this is the mild grade. If extremely intense, without shame or remorse, yet one has not come to see it as a virtue, this remains the moderate grade. If one is completely without shame or remorse, deeply cherishes and delights in it, and regards it as a virtue, this is the severe grade.

Through the matter: if one disparages an individual person, this is the mild grade. If one disparages a single community, this is the moderate grade. If one universally disparages many communities, this is the severe grade.

### Six Types of Worms Eating the Lion's Body

Within the severe grade, the offenses are not of a single kind. Selecting among those that are hard to distinguish, I shall briefly indicate three pairs. Those within the Buddha-dharma mostly rely on the three trainings to produce activities resembling the Buddha Way that are actually Mara's deeds. They are like worms within the lion's own body that feed upon the lion, since nothing else has this power.

**The first pair: those relying on the training of mind [meditation].**

There are two types of worms that devour and destroy the Buddha-dharma, one arising from greed and one from pride.

*From greed:* Suppose there is a certain type of person who dwells in seclusion practicing calming meditation, free from all distraction, collecting the mind in the gate of dhyana (禪 *chán*). Because the mind is clear and still, they have vague visions, or a deviant spirit empowers their consciousness to perceive things. At that time, because of their own shallow learning, they cannot distinguish wrong from right. Moreover, wishing to attract fame, profit, and reverence, they make known to others whatever they see and perceive, dazzling worldly people so that everyone suspects them to be a sage. By exclusively exalting their seeming traces of sainthood and universally suppressing all other monks as having nothing worthy of refuge, they destroy the Buddha-dharma and therefore incur heavy offense. These are called the great thieves among the sangha.

*From pride:* Suppose there is a certain type of person who long dwells deep in the mountains, with a mind of grasping, cultivating the practice of stillness. Mara, knowing that person's mind can be shaken and ruined, produces a voice in the sky praising their practice. That person, because of this, gives rise to a mind of self-exaltation and universally suppresses all monks dwelling among people, saying: "Who could possibly praise what you practice?" This person's offense is heavier than the former. This is called a bodhisattva candala (旃陀羅 *zhāntuóluó*).

**The second pair: those relying on the training of precepts.**

There are two types of worms that devour and destroy the Buddha-dharma, one from wrong precepts and one from right precepts.

*From wrong precepts:* Suppose there is a certain type of person whose nature is not straightforward, who either inherits wrong precepts or conceives wrong ideas of their own. They do not wear silk or hemp, do not eat the five grains, cunningly seeking profit and reverence. They exalt themselves as without compare, deceiving ignorant types and hoping that the foolish masses will all admire their virtue. They universally suppress all who lack such distinctive practices. In this way they inwardly damage what is true and outwardly confuse people. No offense of damage and confusion is worse than this.

*From right precepts:* Suppose there is a certain type of person whose nature is shallow and narrow-minded. In a time when the great pattern of the world is mostly lax and lenient, they alone correct their person so that their deportment is without flaw. They then give rise to a mind of self-exaltation from their own small pool, arrogantly disparaging the multitude who ride the urgent [great vehicle] while being lax in precepts. This person completes their unwholesomeness and thereby violates the great prohibition. No transformation of merit into calamity is worse than this.

*Question:* The offense of wrong precepts should indeed be as stated. But how can one who holds the right precepts necessarily be at fault? Suppose there is a certain type of person who inwardly has no afflictions, does not observe whether others practice or not, only examines their own mind, and alone holds the right precepts. How does such a bodhisattva commit a violation?

*Answer:* If there is no defiled mind, they are not included in the foregoing discussion. Yet even for such a person, one must make distinctions. If through their solitary purity they cause all worldly people to universally regard all other monks as unworthy fields of merit, so that offerings and honor are directed exclusively to themselves, then although this accords with the sravaka (聲聞 *shēngwén*) precepts of self-liberation, it contradicts the bodhisattva precepts of the vast mind. It is like the sravaka contemplations of impermanence and so forth: though regarding shallow matters they are not inverted, regarding the Dharma-body they are inverted. One should know that the accordance and contradiction here are likewise.

If through their solitary purity they cause those in the world who have not yet believed to believe, and those who believe to increase, and they lead others to make equal offerings to all monks, then this is not only without violation but generates much merit.

However, being solitary in purity while dwelling amid the defiled, and yet hoping through this to avoid suppressing the defiled multitude while also wishing to make others generate a mind of equal reverence, is like walking with the sun and moon atop one's head while wishing not to cast shadows. Unless one is a great sage who knows the dispositions of beings, few can truly achieve this. For this reason, the great worthies of old admonished their disciples: "Be careful never to do good." The disciple replied: "Should I then do evil?" The parent said: "Good is what you should not do; how much less evil!"

**The third pair: those relying on the training of wisdom.**

There are also two types who praise themselves and disparage others: one through addition, and one through subtraction.

*Through addition:* Suppose there is a certain type of person whose nature tends to distorted listening. In order to surpass others, they extensively study various treatises. Not understanding that all dharmas transcend verbal expression, they grasp at self-nature distinctions as described in words. For the sake of gaining fame and profit, they speak thus: "I have attained the intended meaning of the Buddhas of the three times. Those who differ from this are all speaking recklessly." This person, in a single act of self-praise and other-disparagement, possesses four inversions and thereby confuses the Buddha-dharma, constituting a heavy offense.

Their deluded grasping of the view of attainment is as far from the Buddha's intention as heaven is from earth, yet they claim "I am close to the Buddha's intention": this is the first inversion. The Buddha's intention is profoundly deep, cutting off all proliferation, without any attainment whatsoever regarding any dharma, yet they cite this as identical to their own deluded view: this is the second inversion. They exalt these two inverted views and impose them upon the four assemblies: this is the third inversion. They suppress all those who speak free from extremes and place them beneath their own biased grasping: this is the fourth inversion.

*Through subtraction:* Suppose there is a certain type of person whose nature is narrow and inferior. Not approaching good spiritual friends and not studying broadly, they specialize in one portion of extremely profound sūtras and treatises. Not understanding the hidden intention, they take the meaning literally. They reject the dependent-nature (依他 *yītā*) principle of all dharmas and give rise to this view, speaking thus: "The three natures and the three truths are merely pedagogical gates. Within that which has nothing whatsoever, provisional names are established. To understand in this way is genuine truth. Those who speak differently are all engaging in proliferation." Because of this, they cling exclusively to their own view and do not accept others' words.

If they encounter someone of dull faculties and narrow learning who falls into what they refute and follows their words, they say: "This person's spirit is upright and correct." If they meet someone intelligent and literate who skillfully establishes positions without falling into their refutation, they say the person has "slipped" and that their mind is confused. Not recognizing their own understanding as dull and dim, unable to pursue the refutation, they imagine that the other's mind is not correct and has not reached their own level. It is like a house dog chasing a wild hare: when it cannot catch up, it supposes it has already surpassed it, and stops to look back.

This person of subtraction commits heavy offense through roughly two kinds of foolishness, thereby ruining the Buddha-dharma: first, the foolishness of elevating what is low to the high; second, the foolishness of relying on the small to slander the many.

**The first foolishness:** This subtractive view, among all views, is at the very bottom. It is even inferior to the heretics' view of self. Why? This person takes the most profound medicine and transforms it into a severe illness. The symptoms of severe illness closely resemble the absence of illness. Therefore, no medical art can cure this disease, and few people spontaneously recognize it as an affliction. It is like fundamental ignorance (根本無明 *gēnběn wúmíng*), the most extreme darkness, whose appearance closely resembles prajñā (般若 *bōrě*) light, because both are without subject and object. Because both are without subject and object, that ignorance is the most difficult to extinguish. This disease being difficult to cure should be understood likewise. As the verse says:

> To remove the grasping of existence, the Tathagata taught emptiness.
> If a person then grasps at emptiness, the Buddhas cannot transform them.

Furthermore, this view arises from dim understanding and reckless faith. If one relies on this view and cultivates the mind until it becomes sharp, one will surely reject even faith itself and fall into great wrong views, suffering in the Avici hell for countless kalpas. Therefore the bodhisattva, out of deep compassion for others who might follow such a person, gives advance warning, saying: "All wise companions in the holy life should not dwell together with them." All the views of self produced by all non-Buddhist practitioners, though they deviate from principle, do not have this particular danger. As the verse says:

> Better to give rise to the view of self like Mount Sumeru
> Than to give rise to the view of emptiness even as small as a hair-tip.

For these two reasons, this view is at the very bottom. Yet those who hold it do not understand, and they give rise to the conceit of superiority. It is like the lowliest novice who imagines he stands above the preceptor. This is called the foolishness of elevating what is low to the high.

**The second foolishness:** The Buddha Way is vast and boundless, without obstruction, without fixed direction, never established anywhere, yet nothing it does not fit. Therefore it is said: "All the meanings of others are entirely the meanings of the Buddha." The hundred schools of teaching are without exception correct. The eighty-four thousand Dharma gates can all lead to principle. Yet this person, from their own narrow learning, clings exclusively to their restricted view. Those who agree with their view, they consider to have attained it. Those who differ from their view, they all declare to have slipped. It is like a person peering at the sky through a reed tube, who declares that all those who do not peer through his tube fail to see the blue sky. This is called the foolishness of relying on the small to slander the many.

*Question:* The sūtra says: "Like a poor beggar who day and night counts others' treasures; one who cannot practice as taught, much learning is also like this." It also says: "If there is a person who discriminates between lust, delusion, and the Way, this person is far from the Buddha, like heaven from earth." From this we know that much learning and the view of attainment are close to the beggar and far from the Buddha Way. Though they claim to have grasped the meaning, they do not abandon words. Their pursuit of fame and profit exceeds that of worldly people. Their falling into biased grasping is evident.

Now our school of learning differs from theirs in one respect: we do not pursue fame and profit, we cast aside worldly affairs, deeply trust the Buddha-dharma, exclusively seek stillness, and delight only in cultivating the mind and purifying conduct as circumstances allow. By this verification, our understanding is not wrong but right. Moreover, grasping existence is called "addition" and grasping nonexistence is called "subtraction." The principle our school approaches dispatches both existence and nonexistence. Utterly without fixed position, this is what we contemplate. If the state of contemplation is like this, how can it be a fault?

*Answer:* Those who pursue fame and profit turn their backs on the Way and face the worldly. That they lose principle is hardly worth lamenting. Those who are capable of severing the worldly net and about to approach the Way, who take medicine and it becomes disease, are truly to be mourned. One who recognizes their own confusion is not greatly confused. One who knows their own darkness is not in the extreme of darkness.

Suppose your mental practice does not contradict the marks of dharmas and you truly do not reject the dependent-nature principle, and therefore are not grasping existence yet not falling into nonexistence. Then you yourself are free from extremes, subtly according with the Middle Way, and are not in that category. Why then do you suddenly leap to join them?

Yet even if you are yourself in the middle, if you are not in the middle regarding others, you still fall into biased grasping and are still not pure wisdom. As the sūtra verse says:

> Not accepting others' dharma, this is called a foolish person.
> All who engage in proliferation are all greatly foolish people.

If you rely on the view that only you are right and generate proliferation from that, and call this pure wisdom, then there would be nothing that is not pure wisdom.

But suppose your view and understanding do fall into the evil grasping of emptiness, and you reject conditioned existence and also reject its nonexistence, representing the extreme of subtraction without recognizing it yourself. Then you alone are the farthest from the Way, and you are actually close to the beggar. It is as if the beggar said: "One with many treasures is wealthy. One with few possessions is poor. I have neither many treasures nor few possessions. Since I am utterly without any fixed position, I am not poor." Your present words are the same as that. Thus one should know: having neither many nor few is the extreme of poverty. Rejecting both existence and nonexistence is the extreme of subtraction.

Yet the ultimate Way is dim and obscure; right and wrong cannot be distinguished. Mental practice is subtle and hidden; gain and loss are hard to discern. Only those who in previous lives have planted good roots, whose natures are straightforward, who deeply subdue their pride, and who approach good spiritual friends can look up to the holy scriptures as their mind's mirror, inwardly examine themselves, and investigate their subtle mental activities. If a person can be like this, they may cure the disease of evil grasping of emptiness.

The statement above that "the Buddhas cannot transform them" was intended to cause such people to be inwardly startled and reform themselves. Therefore they are actually transformed by the Buddhas: by the words "cannot transform," they are caused to transform themselves.

*Question:* If one wishes to examine one's own mental disease in this way, upon which scripture should one rely as the clearest mirror?

*Answer:* The Samdhinirmocana Sūtra (深密經 *Shēnmì jīng*) says:

"If there are sentient beings whose natures are not straightforward and who belong to the category of the non-straightforward, although they have the ability to think and analyze, discriminating what to adopt and reject, they remain established in the grasping of their own views. When they hear the teaching of profound hidden intention, they lack the ability to understand it as it truly is. Regarding such dharmas, although they generate faith and understanding, they grasp at the meaning in accordance with the words. They claim that all dharmas definitely all lack self-nature, definitely are unborn and unperishing, originally quiescent, and self-natured nirvāṇa. Because of these causal conditions, regarding all dharmas they acquire the view of nonexistence and the view of signlessness. Because of this view, they reject all marks as markless. They reject the three-nature characteristics of all dharmas. Why? Because it is precisely due to the existence of the dependent nature (依他起相 *yītā qǐxiàng*) and the perfected nature (圓成實相 *yuánchéngshí xiàng*) that the imagined nature (遍計所執相 *biànjì suǒzhí xiàng*) can be provisionally designated. If regarding these two natures one sees them as markless, one also rejects the imagined nature. Therefore it is said that such a person rejects all three natures. Although through generating faith and understanding regarding the Dharma their merit increases, because they grasp at what is not the meaning, they lose wisdom. Because wisdom is lost, they lose vast and immeasurable wholesome dharmas."

The Yogacarabhumi (瑜伽論 *Yújiā lùn*) says:

"Suppose there is a certain type who hears the profound sūtras connected with emptiness, whose hidden intention is not yet fully revealed. Unable to truly understand the meaning of what is taught, they give rise to improper and deluded discrimination. Through unskillful reasoning, they give rise to this view and establish this position: 'All is merely provisional; this is the genuine truth. If one contemplates in this way, it is called right contemplation.' They reject as nonexistent the real and unique matters that are the basis and locus of the provisionally designated. If all the provisionally designated were nonexistent, how could there be 'all is merely provisional' as genuine truth?

By this reasoning, they slander both the genuine and the provisional, claiming both are utterly nonexistent. One should know this is called the most extreme nihilism. Such nihilists: all wise companions in the holy life should not dwell together with them. The World-Honored One, relying on this hidden intention, said: 'Better to be like those of one type who give rise to the view of self than to be like those of one type who engage in the evil grasping of emptiness.'

What is called the evil grasping of emptiness? It means there are sramanas or brahmanas who, regarding that by reason of which something is empty, also do not accept and trust it; and regarding that within which something is empty, also do not accept and trust it. This is called the evil grasping of emptiness. Why? 'Empty by reason of that' means that thing truly does not exist. 'Empty within this' means this thing truly does exist. By this reasoning, one may speak of emptiness. If one says everything is utterly nonexistent, then where, regarding what, and for what reason would one call it empty? Nor should one say that 'by this within this it is simply called empty.' Therefore it is called the evil grasping of emptiness." And so forth extensively.

## Second Gate: Shallow and Deep

Let us set aside this digression and return to the main theme. The light and heavy aspects of keeping and violating are roughly as above. Next, the second topic: explaining the shallow and deep aspects of keeping and violating.

Continuing from the precept of praise and disparagement discussed above, I shall display the characteristics of shallow and deep keeping and violating. The Brahma-Net Precept Text says: "Always on behalf of sentient beings, accept insult and humiliation. Direct bad things toward yourself; give good things to others. If one praises and exalts one's own virtue and conceals others' good qualities, causing them to suffer humiliation, this is a parajika (波羅夷 *bōluóyí*) offense."

Based on this single passage, how do the shallow and deep understandings differ?

A person of lesser understanding, hearing this, takes the meaning just from the words: self-deprecation and praising others is always meritorious; self-praise and other-disparagement is always a violation. By thus uniformly following the words, when they try to cultivate merit, their meritorious deeds are few while their offenses are many. When they try to abandon offense, they remove one offense but eliminate three merits. This is the error of shallow understanding of keeping and violating.

A person of superior understanding, hearing this, scoops out the essential meaning. Given one corner, they immediately infer three corners. Regarding a single passage, they invariably use four sentences to judge. Because of this careful discrimination without any confusion, no merit goes uncollected and no offense goes unanalyzed. This is the virtue of deep understanding of keeping and violating.

### The Four-Sentence Analysis

What is meant by judging with four sentences?

**First sentence:** Sometimes self-deprecation and praising others is merit, and self-praise and other-disparagement is an offense. For example, if a person deeply pities sentient beings who suffer humiliation and wishes to draw the humiliation that others receive toward themselves, pushing the honor that they themselves should receive toward others. With this intention, self-deprecation and praising others is merit. If one wishes to receive honor oneself and cause others to suffer humiliation, then with this intention, self-praise and other-disparagement is an offense.

**Second sentence:** Sometimes self-deprecation and praising others is an offense, and self-praise and other-disparagement is merit. For example, if one knows the customs and habits of the times: that people generally detest those who praise themselves and disparage others, and always respect those who are self-effacing and exalt others. And one also knows that disparaging someone invariably makes them criticize oneself, while praising someone invariably makes them praise oneself in return. With this knowledge, one cunningly seeks self-exaltation through self-deprecation and praising others: this is a heavy offense. If one knows that another person's grasping is unreasonable and should be abandoned, and that one's own inner understanding is the Way and should be cultivated, directly wishing to establish the Buddha-dharma and benefit sentient beings, and one praises oneself and disparages others: this is great merit.

**Third sentence:** Sometimes whether one deprecates or praises, whether one praises or disparages, it may be offense or merit. For example, suppose there is a certain type of person whose nature tends to deception and fraud. Wishing to deceive the people of the world, they belittle others' strengths and conceal their own shortcomings. With this intention, they make distorted and confusing statements: they deprecate their own small strengths as faults, praise others' shortcomings as accomplishments, exalt their own many shortcomings as virtues, and suppress others' strengths as failures. And suppose there is another type of person whose nature is straightforward. Wishing to guide the people of the world to recognize good and distinguish evil, to abandon offense and cultivate merit, with this aspiration they speak directly without bias: seeing their own evil, they invariably criticize it; hearing of others' good, they immediately praise it; recognizing their own virtue, they also commend it; knowing others' offenses, they directly censure them. The first person's deprecation, praise, exaltation, and suppression is entirely the offense of deceit and flattery. The second person's criticism, praise, commendation, and censure is entirely the merit of loyalty and directness.

**Fourth sentence:** Sometimes one neither deprecates oneself to praise others nor praises oneself to suppress others, and this may be merit or offense. For example, suppose there is an elevated person whose nature is magnificently grand, who releases the spirit to embrace the uncarved block. Not knowing ends and beginnings, they merge calamity and fortune into one, forget self and other as non-dual. Because their spirit always delights in roaming in this realm, they neither deprecate themselves to praise others nor exalt themselves to suppress others. And suppose there is also a person of inferior foolishness whose nature is dull and plain, who does not know right from wrong, can hardly distinguish beans from wheat, does not recognize good as good, and does not understand evil as evil. Because their mind is always dim and they forget both aversion and affection, they likewise never humble themselves to honor others, and never commend themselves to disparage others. The former is the merit of supreme wisdom's genuine simplicity. The latter is the offense of inferior foolishness's muddled dullness.

By examining the first two sentences, meritorious conduct is transformed into heavy calamity, and offensive conduct becomes great good. By examining the latter two sentences, deceitful speech and loyal discourse have no gap between them, and supreme wisdom shares the same outward traces as inferior foolishness.

From this we know: the essentials of a practitioner's keeping and violating lie only in subtly examining one's own gains and losses. One must never rashly judge others' virtues and faults. Such is the meaning of shallow and deep in keeping and violating.

## Third Gate: Ultimate Keeping and Violating

Although by relying on the dharma-gates explained above one can recognize the nature of light and heavy and also know the characteristics of shallow and deep, if one does not truly understand the marks of the precepts and has not transcended the two extremes regarding offense and non-offense, one cannot ultimately keep without violating, and one cannot approach the pure precept paramita (戒波羅蜜 *jiè bōluómì*).

Why is this? Precepts do not arise on their own; they necessarily rely on a multitude of conditions. Therefore they decidedly lack self-characteristics. Identical with conditions, they are not precepts; separate from conditions, there are no precepts. If one removes both "identical with" and "separate from," one cannot find a middle ground. Seeking precepts in this way, they are never existent in such a way that one could speak of their self-nature being established. Yet relying on a multitude of conditions, precepts are also not nonexistent, for they are not like a rabbit's horn, being without causal conditions. The marks of offense should be understood in the same way. And just as with the marks of precepts and offense, so too with the marks of persons.

If within this, relying on their not being existent, one sees them as utterly nonexistent, then although one claims to have no violations, one has permanently lost the precepts, because one rejects the unique reality of the precepts. Furthermore, within this, relying on their not being nonexistent, if one conceives of them as existent, then although one claims to be keeping them, that very keeping is itself a violation, because it contradicts the true characteristics of the precepts.

The bodhisattva's cultivation of the precepts is not like either of these. Although one does not conceive of existence in a keeper or something kept, one does not reject the unique reality of the precepts. Therefore one never has the great fault of losing the precepts. Although one does not see nonexistence in offense and non-offense, one does not contradict the true characteristics of the precepts. Therefore one is forever free from the subtle offense of violating the precepts.

Through this skillful means and profound wisdom, one permanently forgets the three spheres and does not fall into the two extremes. Only then does one approach the complete precept paramita. As the sūtra says: "Because offense and non-offense are unattainable, one should fulfill the precept paramita."

The Precept Text says:

> The precept-light issues from the mouth.
> It has conditions; it is not without cause.
> It is neither form nor mind,
> Neither existent nor nonexistent,
> Not a dharma of cause and effect.
> It is the original source of all Buddhas,
> The root of all bodhisattvas.

Here, "precept-light" is used to reveal that precept and light are non-dual and without distinction, because brightness and defilement share a single flavor. Therefore, relying on the precept-light, one reveals the true characteristics of the precepts.

Precepts have no self-nature; they necessarily rely on other conditions. Therefore it says "it has conditions." The phrase "it has conditions" does not assert that they exist; it directly reveals that the causes from which they arise are not nonexistent. Therefore it says "not without cause." The precept-nature that is not without cause is not material resistance, nor is it mental cognition. Therefore it says "neither form nor mind." Although it is neither form nor mind, apart from form and mind it can never be found. Although it cannot be found, precepts are not nonexistent. Therefore it says "neither existent nor nonexistent." Although precepts are not nonexistent, separate from effect there is no cause, and separate from cause there is no effect. Therefore it says "not a dharma of cause and effect." Although the precept as causal nature cannot be found, all the Buddha-fruit necessarily relies on the precept-cause. Therefore it says "the original source of all Buddhas." Although the precept as effectual nature cannot be found, the precepts necessarily rely on the cause of the bodhicitta (菩提心 *pútíxīn*). Therefore it says "the root of all bodhisattvas."

*Question:* If the marks of the precepts are this profoundly deep and hard to understand, and understanding them is already difficult, how much more so practicing them, then we know that the practice-characteristics explained above are cultivated only by bodhisattvas of the great stages and have nothing to do with what newly resolved practitioners undertake.

*Answer:* In the sūtra, directly answering as you ask, it says: "From the time the bodhisattva first generates the aspiration, they constantly practice the dharma of non-attainment. Relying on the dharma of non-attainment, they cultivate generosity and uphold the precepts, and so forth, relying on the dharma of non-attainment, they cultivate wisdom."

The meaning of this answer is: if this practice, because it has never been cultivated, were difficult to practice and therefore not cultivated now, then because it is not practiced now, it would also not be cultivated later. In this way, as time goes on, it would become ever more difficult. Therefore one is instructed from the very beginning to look up and practice what is difficult. As practice gradually increases, it progressively becomes easy. This is the way newly practicing bodhisattvas set forth toward the great aspiration.

The essentials of ultimate keeping and violating are briefly explained as above.
