# Catalog of Dharma Teachings, Implements, and Other Items Brought Back by the Monk of Língyán Monastery

## *Língyán Sì Héshang Shōrai Hōmon Dōgu tō Mokuroku* (靈巖寺和尚請來法門道具等目錄)

Taishō 2164

Compiled by Engyō (*Engyō*, 圓行) of Japan. A bibliographic catalog.

*Note: This is a memorial and catalog submitted by the Japanese Shingon monk Engyō upon his return from Tang China in 839 CE (Jōwa 6). He studied esoteric Buddhism under Yìzhēn at Qīnglóng Monastery in Chang'an, the dharma-heir of Yìcāo, who was in turn a dharma-brother of Huìguǒ (Kūkai's teacher). Engyō received the ācārya consecration and brought back 69 sections totaling 123 fascicles, along with buddha relics, images, and ritual implements.*

The monk Engyō, who entered Tang to study and has now returned, states: In the *wùwǔ* year [838 CE], Engyō was among those commanded to seek the dharma, and he visited teachers beyond the western sea. In the twelfth month of that year he arrived in the city of Cháng'ān (*Cháng'ān*, 長安). On the thirteenth day of the first month of the *jǐwèi* year [839 CE], by imperial edict he was assigned to reside at Qīnglóng Monastery (*Qīnglóng Sì*, 青龍寺).

There he was fortunate to encounter the consecration master of that monastery, the monk whose dharma-name was Yìzhēn (*Yìzhēn*, 義真), whom he took as his teacher and master. In the lineage of great authority, Yìzhēn was a dharma-heir of Yìcāo (*Yìcāo*, 義操), a fellow disciple of the ācārya Huìguǒ (*Huìguǒ*, 惠果). He was thoroughly versed in the three teachings and profoundly accomplished in the five divisions. He was a pillar of the dharma and one to whom the nation looked.

Engyō, fortunately relying on the vast grace of the sagely court and the deep compassion of his master, resolved his doubts in the great dharma of the two divisions and gained awakening to the secret dharma of the various honored ones. On the second day of the intercalary first month, he received the consecration for the position of ācārya. The Commissioner of Merit on the Left Street, the Saṃgha Recorder, the monks of imperial service, the Great Worthy, and the entire Vajra assembly all gathered at the ritual platform and expressed their rejoicing.

This dharma: when one contemplates the moon-disc of the mind, then even while dwelling in the position of an ordinary being, one is endowed with the virtues of a buddha. When one recites the secret words of the mouth, then without passing through long kalpas, one suddenly ascends to the position of great awakening. Therefore Nāgārjuna (*Lóngshù*, 龍樹) said: "Traveling by goat-cart, one's wishes are hard to fulfill over great distances. Spurring a horse, one gradually expects to go faster. Traveling by supernatural power, one arrives the moment one sets forth." This illustrates the distinction between the exoteric and the esoteric. The esoteric scriptures also say: "If a single good man establishes a ritual platform and cultivates the mindfulness of the three mysteries, within that land there will be no calamity of the seven disasters. The king and great ministers will daily increase in merit and longevity." This is the efficacy of true words.

Although the waves churned and the sea heaved, and wind and rain battered the ships, we crossed that sea of whales and returned safely to this sagely realm. This was accomplished by the power of the sages.

We humbly reflect that His Majesty the Emperor's merit surpasses the furthest reaches and his way crowns the primordial. He glorifies the precious chart of Yáo and continues the great succession of Yǔ. Out of compassion for all living beings he wets his feet in their service; receiving the Buddha's charge he governs with effortless ease. Because His Majesty upholds and exalts the buddha-dharma, the relics of the Buddha (*shèlì*, 舍利) have crossed the waves to come from afar. Because His Majesty compassionately nurtures all within the seas, the esoteric scriptures and dharma teachings have crossed the ocean to arrive from afar. That the esoteric dharma has been transmitted here is not without reason: it is the power of merit and wisdom inherent in the Tathāgata, and the power of empowerment inherent in the dharma-realm.

Mahāvairocana (*Dàrì Rúlái*, 大日如來), Vajrasattva (*Jīngāng Sàduǒ*, 金剛薩埵), Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna, Bodhisattva Nāgabodhi (*Lóngzhì*, 龍智), Tripiṭaka Master Vajrabodhi (*Jīngāng Sānzàng*, 金剛三藏), Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra (*Bùkōng Sānzàng*, 不空三藏), Master Huìguǒ, Master Yìcāo, Master Yìzhēn: transmitted in succession, and then conferred upon Engyō.

The catalog of sūtras, dharma teachings, relics, and implements received is presented separately. I respectfully submit this, and beg pardon for this affront to your august dignity. I am filled with trepidation. The monk Engyō, in utmost dread and awe, respectfully states this.

The nineteenth day of the twelfth month of the sixth year of Jōwa (*Jōwa*, 承和) [839 CE]. Submitted by the monk Engyō, Transmission-of-the-Lamp Great Dharma Master, who entered Tang to study and has returned.

The Saṃgha Recorder of the Right Street, Great Worthy of the Three Teachings and Debate, the monk Tǐxū (*Tǐxū*, 體虛), on behalf of the envoy Cavalry General Qiú (*Qiú Piàoqí*, 仇驃騎), together with six great worthies of imperial service, went to Qīnglóng Monastery to engage in dialogue on the profound principles of the teachings with Engyō, Transmission-of-the-Lamp Great Dharma Master of Japan. Their names are listed below:

The monk Chángbiàn (*Chángbiàn*, 常辨), Great Worthy of Imperial Service and Preceptor-in-Attendance, of Bǎoshòu Monastery (*Bǎoshòu Sì*, 保壽寺).

The monk Hóngbiàn (*Hóngbiàn*, 弘辨), Great Worthy of Imperial Service in the Chan Tradition, of Zhāngjìng Monastery (*Zhāngjìng Sì*, 章敬寺).

The monk Qígāo (*Qígāo*, 齊高), Great Worthy of Imperial Service in the Teaching and Debate Tradition, of Zhāofú Monastery (*Zhāofú Sì*, 招福寺).

The monk Guānghào (*Guānghào*, 光顥), Great Worthy of Imperial Service in the Teaching and Debate Tradition, of Xīngtáng Monastery (*Xīngtáng Sì*, 興唐寺).

The monk Hǎi'àn (*Hǎi'àn*, 海岸), Great Worthy of Imperial Service in the Teaching and Debate Tradition, of Yúnhuā Monastery (*Yúnhuā Sì*, 雲花寺).

The monk Yuánjìng (*Yuánjìng*, 圓鏡), Great Worthy of Imperial Service in the Teaching and Debate Tradition, of Qīnglóng Monastery (*Qīnglóng Sì*, 青龍寺).

The topics of doctrinal discussion and examination exchanged between the above great worthies and the Great Worthy Engyō are all recorded and submitted herewith.

The fifteenth day of the first month of the fourth year of Kāichéng (*Kāichéng*, 開成) [839 CE].
