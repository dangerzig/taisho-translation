# Commentary on the Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra: Verses of Aspiration to Birth

## *Wúliàngshòu Jīng Yōubōtíshè* (無量壽經優波提舍)

Taishō 1524

Composed by Bodhisattva Vasubandhu (*Póshǒu Pándòu*, 婆藪槃豆). Translated by Bodhiruci (*Pútí Liúzhī*, 菩提流支) of the Northern Wei dynasty.

*Note: This is Vasubandhu's celebrated Upadeśa (commentary) on the Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra (Infinite Life Sūtra), the foundational treatise of East Asian Pure Land Buddhism. It consists of a series of devotional verses (gāthā) aspiring to rebirth in Amitābha's Pure Land, followed by a prose commentary explaining the "five gates of mindfulness" (wǔ niànmén, 五念門): prostration, praise, vow-making, contemplation, and merit-dedication. The contemplation gate is further analyzed into seventeen kinds of meritorious adornment of the buddha-land, eight kinds of the Buddha's own adornment, and four kinds of bodhisattva practice. The treatise culminates in the teaching that all three types of adornment are subsumed under "one dharma-phrase" (yī fǎjù, 一法句): the phrase of purity, which is the true wisdom of the unconditioned dharma-body. This text, along with the Pure Land sūtras, forms the doctrinal basis of the Jōdo (Pure Land) schools in East Asia.*

---

## Verses of Aspiration to Birth

> World-Honored One, with single mind
> I take refuge in the unobstructed light,
> The Tathāgata of the ten directions:
> I aspire to be born in the Land of Peace and Bliss.

> I rely upon the sūtras,
> On the characteristics of true merit,
> And speak these aspiration-verses as a dhāraṇī,
> In accord with the Buddha's teaching.

> Contemplating that world's characteristics,
> It surpasses the paths of the three realms,
> Ultimately like space,
> Vast and without limit.

> The great compassion of the right Way
> Is born from roots of merit that transcend the world.
> Pure radiance fills it completely,
> Like a mirror or the disk of the sun and moon.

> Endowed with the nature of every precious jewel,
> Fully equipped with wondrous adornment,
> Stainless light blazes intensely,
> Bright and pure, illumining the world.

> The jewel-natured merit-grass
> Is soft, curling left and right.
> Those who touch it give rise to supreme bliss,
> Surpassing kācilinda cloth.

> Jewel-blossoms of a thousand myriad kinds
> Cover the pools and flowing springs.
> A gentle breeze stirs the petals and leaves;
> Lights interweave and shimmer in turns.

> Palaces and various towers:
> Looking out in the ten directions, unobstructed.
> Variegated trees of wondrous light and color,
> With jeweled railings encircling everywhere.

> Immeasurable jeweled lattices
> And nets spread throughout the sky.
> Bells of every kind ring out,
> Proclaiming the wondrous dharma-sound.

> It rains flowers and garments of adornment;
> Immeasurable fragrance pervades everywhere.
> The sun of the Buddha's wisdom, bright and pure,
> Removes the world's deluded darkness.

> The Brahmā-voice speaks deep and far;
> Subtle and wondrous, heard in the ten directions.
> The truly awakened Amitābha,
> The dharma-king, skillfully upholds [all].

> The Tathāgata's pure lotus assembly:
> From the flower of true enlightenment they are born by transformation.
> They delight in the flavor of the buddha-dharma;
> Meditation and samādhi are their food.

> Forever free from affliction of body and mind,
> Receiving bliss without interruption.
> The Mahāyāna realm of roots of merit:
> Equally free from the name of blame and reproach.

> Women, those with deficient faculties,
> And the seed of the two vehicles are not born there.
> Whatever sentient beings wish and delight in,
> All can be fulfilled.

> Therefore I aspire to be born
> In the land of Amitābha Buddha.
> On the immeasurable great jewel-king's
> Subtle, pure flower-pedestal:

> Marks and radiance extending one fathom,
> His form surpasses all living beings.
> The Tathāgata's subtle voice,
> Brahmā-sound heard in the ten directions.

> Like earth, water, fire, and wind,
> And like space: without discrimination.
> The assembly of devas and humans, unmoving,
> Born from the ocean of pure wisdom.

> Like Mount Sumeru, king of mountains,
> Supreme and wondrous, none surpassing.
> The assembly of heavenly and human heroes
> Reverently surrounds and gazes up at him.

> Contemplating the Buddha's original vow-power:
> None who encounters it passes by in vain.
> It can swiftly bring to fulfillment
> The great jewel-ocean of merit.

> The Pure Land of Peace and Bliss,
> Constantly turning the stainless wheel.
> Transformation-buddhas and bodhisattva-suns,
> Abide like Mount Sumeru.

> Stainless, adorned with light,
> In a single thought and a single moment,
> Universally illumining all buddha-assemblies,
> Benefiting all sentient beings.

> Raining heavenly music, flowers, and garments,
> Wondrous fragrances and other offerings,
> Praising all the Buddha's merits,
> Without any discriminating mind.

> In whatever worlds there are none
> Of the jewel of the Buddha, Dharma, and merit,
> I aspire to be born there,
> To reveal the buddha-dharma as the Buddha does.

> I compose this treatise, speak these verses,
> Aspiring to see Amitābha Buddha,
> And universally, together with all sentient beings,
> To be born in the Land of Peace and Bliss.

---

Having completed my summary statement in verse of the Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra's chapters and phrases:

**The treatise says:** What meaning do these aspiration-verses clarify? They contemplate the world of Peace and Bliss, see Amitābha Buddha, and aspire to be born in that land.

How does one contemplate? How does one give rise to faith? If good men and good women accomplish the cultivation of the five gates of mindfulness, they will certainly be born in the Land of Peace and Bliss and see Amitābha Buddha.

What are the five gates of mindfulness? (1) The gate of prostration. (2) The gate of praise. (3) The gate of vow-making. (4) The gate of contemplation. (5) The gate of merit-dedication.

**How does one prostrate?** With bodily action, one prostrates to Amitābha Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, with the intention of being born in that land.

**How does one praise?** With verbal action, one praises, chanting that Tathāgata's name. As that Tathāgata's radiance and wisdom-characteristics are, so is the meaning of his name. One wishes to practice in true accord with this.

**How does one make the vow?** One's mind constantly makes the vow, single-mindedly and with focused attention, to be born in the Land of Peace and Bliss, wishing to practice śamatha (calm abiding) in true accord.

**How does one contemplate?** With wisdom one contemplates, with right mindfulness observing that [land], wishing to practice vipaśyanā (insight meditation) in true accord. That contemplation has three kinds. What three? (1) Contemplating the meritorious adornment of that buddha-land. (2) Contemplating the meritorious adornment of Amitābha Buddha. (3) Contemplating the meritorious adornment of the bodhisattvas there.

**How does one dedicate merit?** Without abandoning any suffering sentient beings, one's mind constantly makes the vow: dedication is foremost, in order to accomplish the mind of great compassion.

---

### Seventeen Kinds of Meritorious Adornment of the Buddha-Land

How does one contemplate the meritorious adornment of that buddha-land? The meritorious adornment of that buddha-land accomplishes inconceivable power. Like the nature of the maṇi wish-fulfilling jewel, it is a dharma of correspondence and correlation. The contemplation of the buddha-land's meritorious adornment has seventeen aspects to be known.

What are the seventeen? (1) Purity. (2) Extent. (3) Nature. (4) Form. (5) Various features. (6) Wondrous color. (7) Touch. (8) Adornment [of water, ground, and sky]. (9) Rain. (10) Radiance. (11) Sound. (12) The lord [of that land]. (13) The retinue. (14) Enjoyment. (15) Freedom from difficulties. (16) The gate of great significance. (17) Fulfillment of all wishes.

[Each of the seventeen is then correlated with the corresponding verse-line, as the treatise explains in detail.]

The pure-land reward is free from two kinds of blame: (1) in substance, and (2) in name. In substance, there are three: (1) the two-vehicle practitioner, (2) women, (3) those with incomplete faculties. Because these three are absent, it is called "free from blame in substance." In name also three: not only are the three substances absent, but one does not even hear the names "two vehicles," "woman," or "incomplete faculties." Therefore it is called "free from blame in name." "Equal" means of one uniform characteristic.

In brief, the seventeen kinds of meritorious adornment of Amitābha Buddha's land reveal the Tathāgata's great merit-power of benefiting himself, accomplishing the merit of benefiting others. The adornment of that land of Immeasurable Life, the wondrous realm of the ultimate truth: the sixteen phrases and one [additional] phrase should be understood as taught in sequence.

---

### Eight Kinds of the Buddha's Meritorious Adornment

How does one contemplate the meritorious adornment of the Buddha? There are eight aspects to know: (1) seat, (2) body, (3) speech, (4) mind, (5) assembly, (6) chief [of the assembly], (7) lordship, (8) the adornment of never acting in vain. [Each is correlated with its verse-line.]

In brief, these eight phrases reveal the Tathāgata's meritorious adornment of benefiting self and others, accomplished in sequence.

---

### Four Kinds of Bodhisattva Practice

How does one contemplate the meritorious adornment of the bodhisattvas? There are four kinds of correct cultivation: (1) Without moving from a single buddha-land, one pervades the ten directions with transformation-bodies, constantly performing buddha-deeds in true practice. (2) Those transformation-bodies, at all times without before or after, in a single mind and single thought emit great radiance pervading all worlds to teach sentient beings and eliminate all their suffering. (3) In all worlds without exception, they make vast offerings and praises to all tathāgatas. (4) In the ten directions, wherever the Three Treasures do not exist, they uphold and adorn the merit-ocean of the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṃgha, showing it universally.

---

### The One Dharma-Phrase

Furthermore, the meritorious adornment of the buddha-land, the meritorious adornment of the Buddha, and the meritorious adornment of the bodhisattvas: these three kinds of accomplishment, the adornment of the vow-mind, are in brief subsumed under one dharma-phrase. What is this one dharma-phrase? It is the phrase of purity. What is the phrase of purity? It is the dharma-body of true wisdom and unconditioned [reality].

This purity has two kinds: (1) purity of the vessel-world (the environment), and (2) purity of the sentient-being world. The purity of the vessel-world is the seventeen kinds of buddha-land adornment described above. The purity of the sentient-being world is the eight kinds of buddha-adornment and four kinds of bodhisattva-adornment described above. Thus the one dharma-phrase subsumes the two kinds of purity.

---

### Skillful Dedication and the Three Gates

When the bodhisattva has accomplished the broad and concise cultivation of śamatha and vipaśyanā and attained a pliant mind, truly knowing the broad and concise dharmas, one thereby accomplishes the skillful means of dedication. What is the bodhisattva's skillful dedication? It means that all the merit and roots of merit gathered through the five kinds of cultivation (prostration and the rest) are not sought for one's own abiding happiness, but rather, wishing to uproot the suffering of all sentient beings, one makes the vow to gather all sentient beings and together be born in the buddha-land of Peace and Bliss.

The bodhisattva, having thus skillfully understood dedication, is far removed from three kinds of dharma contrary to the gate of bodhi. What three? (1) Relying on the gate of wisdom, not seeking happiness for oneself, being far from the mind of self-centered attachment to one's own person. (2) Relying on the gate of compassion, uprooting the suffering of all sentient beings, being far from the mind that neglects sentient beings. (3) Relying on the gate of skillful means, pitying all sentient beings, being far from the mind that seeks offerings and reverence for oneself.

The bodhisattva, having distanced from these three, attains three kinds of dharma in accord with the gate of bodhi: (1) The unstained, pure mind: not seeking various pleasures for oneself. (2) The peaceful, pure mind: uprooting the suffering of all sentient beings. (3) The joyful, pure mind: causing all sentient beings to attain great bodhi. By gathering sentient beings to be born in that land, these three are fulfilled.

---

### The Five Gates of Entry and Exit

Furthermore, there are five gates that gradually accomplish five kinds of merit. What five gates? (1) The gate of approach. (2) The gate of the great assembly. (3) The gate of the dwelling. (4) The gate of the house. (5) The gate of the garden of play.

The first four gates accomplish the merit of entry. The fifth gate accomplishes the merit of exit.

Entering the first gate: by prostrating to Amitābha Buddha with the intention of being born in that land, one is born in the world of Peace and Bliss.

Entering the second gate: by praising Amitābha Buddha, chanting the Tathāgata's name in accord with its meaning, and cultivating through the contemplation of the Tathāgata's radiance, one enters the number of the great assembly.

Entering the third gate: by making the vow with single-minded focus to be born there, cultivating the practice of the śamatha samādhi of tranquility, one enters the Lotus-Treasury World.

Entering the fourth gate: by contemplating the wondrous adornment with focused attention, cultivating vipaśyanā, one arrives there and enjoys the bliss of the various dharma-flavors.

Exiting the fifth gate: with great compassion, contemplating all suffering sentient beings, one also manifests transformation-bodies that return and enter the garden of birth-and-death and the forest of afflictions, playing with supernatural powers at the ground of teaching, and through the power of one's original vows, one dedicates merit.

The bodhisattva who enters the four gates accomplishes the practice of self-benefit. The bodhisattva who exits the fifth gate accomplishes the practice of dedication for the benefit of others. The bodhisattva who thus cultivates the five-gate practice for self-benefit and the benefit of others swiftly attains anuttarā samyak saṃbodhi.

---

*Note: Regarding the phrase "Mahāyāna wholesome-root realm" (dàshèng shàngēn jiè, 大乘善根界): Tiantai Master Zhìzhě stated that the character "realm" (jiè, 界) is an error for "man/male" (nán, 男), and should be corrected accordingly. However, all the commentators have written "realm," so the present text preserves it.*
