# 佛說未生冤經
## Sūtra of Ajātaśatru, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0507

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the kingdom of Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak Mountain. The devas, nāgas, spirits, emperors, ministers, and people all came to the Buddha, prostrated, received his teaching with reverence, and performed all the rites of offering. Devadatta, seeing this, was immeasurably jealous. He returned and told Prince Ajātaśatru: "Your father spends the kingdom's treasures as tribute to the Buddha and the śramaṇas, and the national treasury is being drained. You should act quickly: seize the throne and become king. I shall raise an army and go to conquer the Buddha. If you become king and I become Buddha, we shall both have what we want. Would that not be excellent? You must carry this out."

After Ajātaśatru and Devadatta had formed this secret plot, Ajātaśatru commanded the powerful minister to muster troops: "When the king returns, seize his seal and sash and cast him into prison." When the king returned, the minister carried out the order at once, committing the king to prison. The king's mind was tranquil; he reflected on the calamities of past lives, and his heart was without fear, for he deeply trusted the Buddha's words. The king said: "What wrong have I done, that I am punished thus?" The queen, the noble ladies, and all the people of the realm, great and small, wept in grief. The king looked at those who wept and said: "The Buddha teaches that heaven and earth, the sun and moon, Mount Sumeru, and the seas: all that is created must decline. What flourishes will decay. What comes together must part. What is born must die. Through grief and sorrow one turns in the endless wheel, bringing still more suffering. Tracing it to its source and examining its origin: when causes and conditions come together, there is what is called birth. When causes and conditions disperse and dissolve, there is what is called emptiness. The body is merely the four great elements. The soul of a sentient being sojourns within it. At death, the elements return to their origin, and the soul departs into emptiness. This is what is called 'not-body.' If the body itself cannot be preserved, how can a kingdom be kept forever? When the Buddha first entered our kingdom, I had no son yet. He asked me: 'Do you know whether you will have a future king?' I replied: 'I do not know.' The World-Honored One further said: 'All things are impermanent. Reflect on this carefully.' The Buddha's admonition to me was precisely for today. Let each of you strive to build your resolve, and keep the Buddha's admonition in your hearts."

The king said to the prince: "Whenever you were ill, my heart was scorched with anxiety. I wished to give my own body and life to rescue you in danger and take your place. The loving-kindness of a parent is surpassed only by heaven itself. What mind do you harbor, that you could bear to commit this unfilial evil? One who kills a parent will, upon death, enter the Great Mountain Hell, with no respite, and you will face this. I am your elder; even one who greatly honors and is filial to one's parents still fears that their reputation will not match their deeds. How much worse to kill one's father? I give you the kingdom. I wish to go to the Buddha and become a śramaṇa. I see that lust and licentiousness are like fire burning the body. The beauty of women I regard as empty space. The blind masses are without exception deluded by it. Only when one reads the Buddha's sūtras does one perceive the extreme evil of female deception, and understand that glory and profit harm the body."

The prince said: "Say no more. I have obtained my long-held wish. How could there be a pardon?" He ordered the prison wardens: "Cut off his food. Starve him to death."

The officials led the king into prison. King Bimbisāra, facing in the direction of the Buddha, prostrated repeatedly and said: "My son has committed the greatest evil between heaven and earth, yet I bear not the slightest anger. I only hold to the Buddha's teaching: worldly happiness is not enduring; its suffering goes on for long." Entering the prison, he let down his hair, looked up to heaven, and cried out: "How agonizing! Can there be such a fate under heaven?"

The queen, consorts, noble ladies, and all the people of the realm, great and small, wept without exception. The queen said to the prince: "The great king is shackled and fettered, confined in prison. For sitting and lying he needs people's help; his pain is beyond words. Since you were born, the great king's whole heart has been devoted to you. His feelings were fixed on you; eating and resting, he never forgot you. In the waxing and waning of the four elements, he lay over you and watched you, his tears streaming, his heart scorched and body wasted. He wished to give his own body and life to die in your place. You should remember the nurture of heaven and humanity, and not commit this crime. The Buddha teaches in the sūtras: 'Of the highest good, nothing is greater than filial piety. Of the greatest evil, nothing surpasses harming one's parents.' When elders and juniors serve one another, heaven will bless them; how much more so with one's parents? You follow cruelty and violence, committing this grave evil. You will surely enter the Great Mountain Hell, where sixty billion years of the human world equal but one day and one night, and each torment extends for years. Can you endure it all? Would that not be difficult? Those who follow the pleasure of the moment invariably repent afterward."

The prince said: "Since I was small, I have had the ambition to kill my father and become king. Today I have my wish. What use is this admonition?"

The queen said: "One who refuses admonition lays the foundation for the ruin of the kingdom! I wish to see the great king. Is that permitted or not?"

The prince said: "It is permitted."

The queen purified herself and bathed, smeared her body with honeyed barley paste, and entered to see the great king. His face was emaciated beyond recognition. She raised a cry of grief; all who heard it wept. The queen said: "The Buddha teaches that glory and pleasure are impermanent, but the pain of wrongdoing endures."

The king said: "The wardens cut off my food. I have been hungry and thirsty for many days. The eighty thousand pores of my body, each containing hundreds of kinds of worms, torment me inside. My flesh and blood are consumed, and my life is nearly spent." He spoke with a choked and faltering voice, his breath failing and then resuming.

The queen said: "I see clearly how dire your suffering is. I have smeared honeyed barley paste on my body; you may eat from it. You should remember the Buddha's admonition and not be negligent."

When the king had finished eating, he faced in the direction of the Buddha, and with a choked voice, prostrated and said: "The Buddha teaches that glory and fortune are hard to preserve, like illusions and dreams. Truly it is as the Honored One teaches!" He said to the queen: "When I was king, my domain was vast, and I had fine clothing and food at will. Now I am in prison, about to die of starvation. Where did my son find such a treacherous teacher, who defied the Buddha's compassionate teaching? I do not fear death. I only regret that I cannot receive the Buddha's pure teaching face to face, and discuss the profound mysteries of the honored path with Śāriputra, Maudgalyāyana, and Mahākāśyapa!" The king further said to the queen: "The Buddha teaches that love is like a flock of birds roosting together in a tree. In the morning they scatter and depart, each following their own fortune or misfortune. Even Maudgalyāyana, who had eliminated all defilements and extinguished all evils, who had attained the six supernatural powers and the four unobstructed wisdoms, was still beaten to death by jealous brahmins. How much more so for me? Calamity and wrongdoing pursue a person as a shadow follows the body, as an echo follows a sound. The Buddha's time is rare to encounter; his divine transformations are rare to hear. The assembly of the worthy is lofty in practice, and their deportment is beyond measure; it is not what worldly scholars can follow and practice. Embrace the Buddha's sūtras and scriptures, and with benevolence transform the people. The merit of receiving offerings and the blessings of his pure teaching are truly rare to encounter! I shall now die, and my spirit will depart far away. For one who wishes to build aspiration, nothing surpasses the Buddha's teaching. Guard it carefully, and ward off future calamity!" When the queen heard the king's admonition, she again wept in grief.

The prince questioned the prison wardens: "It has been days since the king's food was cut off. Why has he not died?" They replied: "The queen entered the prison with an offering of honeyed barley paste, which has prolonged the king's life." The prince said: "Henceforth, do not allow the queen to see the king."

The king, starving, struggled to rise and face in the direction of the Buddha to prostrate. Immediately he was no longer hungry, and at night there was light. When the prince heard this, he ordered the windows and openings sealed, and the soles of the king's feet scraped away, so that he could not rise and behold the Buddha's light. The officials scraped the soles of his feet. The pain was immeasurable, yet he did not forget to think of the Buddha.

The Buddha, from afar, taught him the sūtra: "Good and evil deeds: calamity and fortune return to oneself. How can one not be careful!"

King Bimbisāra replied: "Even if my body were cut apart and sliced inch by inch, I would never harbor an evil thought."

The World-Honored One further said: "I am now the Tathāgata, the Unattached, the One of the True Path, the Most Perfectly Awakened, the Master of the Way, the Teacher of Devas and Humans. The devas, spirits, nāgas, and all beings of the three-thousand great-thousand worlds of sun and moon all prostrate before me, yet the remnants of past calamities from former lives are not released even now. How much more so for ordinary beings?"

The king received the grace of the God Among Gods. Fully perceiving the calamities of his past lives, he dared not resent his fate. He did not fear the punishment of burning and boiling in the Great Mountain Hell. His heart remained fixed on the Buddha and all his disciples. Sitting and lying down, he dared not be negligent. He then joined his palms, prostrated, and said: "Today my life ends; I am forever parted from the divine teaching." His voice quavered and choked, and in a moment his breath ceased.

All the ministers and people of the realm wept and threw themselves upon the ground, crying out to heaven: "What can be done?" King Bimbisāra immediately attained the fruit of the path, and was reborn in the heavens above. The three gates of the evil destinies were sealed, and all his sufferings were extinguished.
