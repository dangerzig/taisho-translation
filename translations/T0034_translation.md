# 法海經
## Sūtra of the Dharma Ocean

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0034

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Fǎjù (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was traveling in the country of Campā, at the Gaggarā Lotus Pool, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus. On the fifteenth day of the month, when the time had come to recite the Prātimokṣa, the Buddha took his seat. The assembly had long been gathered, yet the Tathāgata remained silent and did not recite the Prātimokṣa. His attendant Ānanda rearranged his robes, knelt, and addressed the Buddha: "The first watch of the night is nearly past; the middle watch is approaching. The great assembly has been gathered for a long time. May the World-Honored One not be fatigued—we pray that he will recite the Prātimokṣa in good time." The Buddha remained silent. After the assembly had sat for some time longer, a certain bhikṣu named Ājñātula rearranged his robes, knelt upright, and addressed the Buddha: "The first and middle watches of the night have passed; the cock will soon crow. May the World-Honored One not be fatigued? The saṃgha has been gathered for a long time. We pray the Buddha will recite the Prātimokṣa." Again the World-Honored One was silent. He spoke once more: "The morning star has already risen; the hour is passing." The Buddha said: "Bhikṣu, listen well. Among the saṃgha there is one who is impure—that is why I cannot recite the Prātimokṣa."

The venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana thought to himself: "I should settle my mind in concentration and observe who is impure." He addressed the Buddha: "I wish to settle my mind in concentration and observe who is impure, and command the impure one to leave the assembly." The World-Honored One replied: "You wish to settle your mind in concentration and observe the impure one in order to have him removed from the assembly—this intention is most excellent. You may proceed." Maudgalyāyana at once settled his mind in concentration and observed, and saw that a certain disciple had transgressed a grave precept. Maudgalyāyana arose from concentration, went to stand before the bhikṣu who had violated the precept, and rebuked him, saying: "You are a śramaṇa; the precepts are your very foundation—the precepts are like a person's own head. The conduct of a śramaṇa in the precepts should be clear and pure, like water, like jade. This is the seat of the Tathāgata, the assembly of the holy and virtuous, the gathering of those who have transcended the world, the place where those of pure virtue convene. This assembly is like a forest of candana; you, with the rank stench of the eranda plant, bring disorder into what is true and right." With his own hand Maudgalyāyana led that disciple out. "You are a person cast away; you may not take part in the pure assembly of the Tathāgata's great congregation. Do not befoul and contaminate this great gathering of the great saṃgha. The great ocean does not receive a foul corpse—consider this yourself, and do not defile the assembly of the virtuous." Once the defiled one had departed, Maudgalyāyana addressed the Buddha: "The impure and defiled person has been cast off and sent away; the assembly is now pure. We pray that the World-Honored One will recite the Prātimokṣa in good time." Yet the World-Honored One again remained silent. Maudgalyāyana was perplexed and looked in all four directions, and saw that the bhikṣu who had been dismissed was once again seated in his place. Maudgalyāyana sternly rebuked him a second time: "You are a person cast away—why do you not yourself acknowledge your transgression and defilement, but sit once more in this seat?" Maudgalyāyana sent him away again, and at last he left his seat and departed. Maudgalyāyana again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the defiled one has departed and the great assembly is pure; there is no further defilement or evil. We pray that the World-Honored One will recite the Prātimokṣa in good time, so that the saṃgha may cultivate pure karma."

The Buddha said to Maudgalyāyana: "From this day forward I shall no longer recite the Prātimokṣa—you yourselves may recite it among yourselves, as though I were reciting it. If, when I recite the Prātimokṣa before the assembly, a person who has violated a precept sits in silence without acknowledging his transgression and takes his place in the Tathāgata's assembly, this constitutes false speech through silence. For false speech through silence, the head is split into seven parts. For the Tathāgata to recite the Prātimokṣa before the great assembly is exceedingly difficult. From this day forward, you yourselves shall recite the Prātimokṣa."

Maudgalyāyana addressed the Buddha: "This disciple has heard that the dharma is first taught by the Tathāgata—it is not that disciples awaken to it of themselves and thereby achieve the path. The Tathāgata's holy virtue is as deep and weighty as heaven and earth; his words are true and essential. Disciples who recite and practice them attain the fruit of the path. The Tathāgata is like rain from heaven: all grains, grasses, and trees without exception flourish by looking up to it. This disciple's virtue is shallow and the path small; people do not take faith or confidence. May the World-Honored One have compassion on the deaf multitude of the world, so that all may obtain peace, obtain faith, obtain what is right, and have their aspirations fulfilled." Maudgalyāyana earnestly and urgently besought him, three times, four times, five times.

The World-Honored One said to Maudgalyāyana: "For the sake of all beings you have requested this of the Tathāgata, earnestly, even to four and five times. I shall now teach it to you all. The dharma of my saṃgha is like the great ocean and possesses eight qualities. Listen well, all of you.

"The waters of the great ocean neither fill to overflowing nor run dry; my dharma is the same—it neither fills to overflowing nor runs dry. This is the first quality.

"The tides of the great ocean come at their proper time without fail and never lose their constant course; those of the four assemblies of my disciples who have received my precepts do not violate the prohibitions or depart from the constant dharma. This is the second quality.

"The waters of the great ocean have but one taste; there are not many different tastes—without exception the taste is salt. My dharma is likewise: the taste of dhyāna, for those who aspire to stillness and seek through concentrated resolve to attain supernatural powers; the taste of the four noble truths, for those who aspire to the four paths and seek to loosen the bonds and fetters of obstruction; the taste of the Mahāyāna, for those who aspire to the great vow and seek to liberate all living beings. This is the third quality.

"The great ocean is both deep and vast, and none can set limits to it; the dharma of the saṃgha is likewise—nothing is not deep and wondrous. Among all things in the eight directions there is nothing greater than the dharma of the saṃgha; the dharma of the saṃgha is the most vast and great of all. This is the fourth quality.

"Within the great ocean there are gold, silver, lapis lazuli, crystal, coral, musāragalva, agate, and the wondrous maṇi gem—none are lacking. Within the dharma of my saṃgha, the wondrous treasures of the thirty-seven factors of awakening, the supernatural powers of stability and life-extension, of flying and soaring through the ten directions to wherever one wishes—in the blink of an eye one may traverse countless buddha-realms, arrive at distinguished and magnificent buddha-fields, and with this dharma transform and guide living beings and purify one's own buddha-land. This is the fifth quality.

"Within the great ocean dwell the divine nāgas: the nāga king Sāgara, Anavatapta, Nanda, Varāmaṇa, Airāvaṇa—all these nāgas, whose wondrous virtue is beyond measure, are able to fashion celestial palaces, and all the multitude of beings looks up to them in dependence. The dharma of my saṃgha is also thus: the four pairs and eight types of noble ones, the twelve sages, the bodhisattvas mahāsattvas—their work of teaching and transforming beings grows ever more flourishing and beautiful. This is the sixth quality.

"The great ocean swallows and receives the hundred rivers and ten thousand streams; the waters of rivers like the Gaṅgā are all received by it; from morning to evening, throughout day and night, it is never said to overflow, to diminish, or to be exhausted. The dharma of my saṃgha is also thus: those of the lineages of Brahmā and Śakra come to enter the dharma of the saṃgha; those of the four castes and noble families, whether of the Śākya or the Brāhmaṇa lineages; those of the royal lineage who renounce worldly power and honor and come to enter the right transformation; craftsmen and those of lesser birth who also enter the right transformation—though their lineages differ, when it comes to following and practicing the great path, they all become of one taste, and all without exception are children of the Śākyas. This is the seventh quality.

"The great ocean is pure and does not receive dead bodies; it harbors no defilements or impurities, and only what belongs to the nature of the ocean is received by it. The dharma of my saṃgha is also pure like the great ocean and does not receive the defiled or evil. Those who violate the precepts and transgress the prohibitions, who do not follow the pure brahmacarya—not one of them is received; they are cast away and kept far off, just as the ocean does not receive dead bodies. This is the eighth quality."

The Buddha said to Maudgalyāyana: "The Tathāgata's great assembly is exclusively pure. For those whose karma in the precepts is impure and who are not true offspring of the Śākya lineage, I do not recite the Prātimokṣa. You all should diligently admonish and discipline one another in the precepts, so that the right dharma may suffer no harm."

The Buddha having spoken thus, all the bhikṣus rejoiced and undertook to practice accordingly.
