# 大乘稻芉經隨聽疏決
## Commentary Following Hearing on the Mahāyāna Śālistamba Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2783

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

> Bowing in reverence to the Omniscient One, the wondrous Dharma, and the saṃgha assembly.
> The undecided meanings of the Śālistamba I shall now in sequence briefly explain.

Among the sixteen theories, the first is called "the theory that the effect exists within the cause." This holds that certain śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas give rise to the view and establish the thesis that constantly, at all times, permanently, at all times, within all causes the nature of the effect is fully present. This refers to the Sāṃkhya and other non-Buddhist schools who hold this position.

For what reason do they give rise to this view and establish this thesis, demonstrating that the nature of the effect is fully present within the cause? Because of both scripture and reasoning. "Scripture" refers to the teaching treasury composed by their former masters, transmitted from teacher to teacher, handed down to the present day, demonstrating that the nature of the effect preexists within the cause. "Reasoning" means that those śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas, being of an investigative nature, abide in the stage of reflection, the stage of self-analysis, the stage of ordinary-being, and follow the stage of reflective contemplation. They reason as follows: "From that nature, this arises." This is what all the world commonly knows and commonly establishes: "That is the cause of this, and nothing else." Furthermore, those who seek the effect go to this cause and no other. Furthermore, it is upon that very cause that one exerts effort and undertakes the tasks one seeks, and not upon anything else. Furthermore, that effect arises from that and not from anything else. Therefore, the effect already exists within the cause. If it were not so, then one should establish that everything is the cause of everything. To seek one effect, one should go to everything. One should exert effort upon everything. From everything, every effect should arise. Thus, because of designation, because of seeking, because of the determinacy of what is done, and because of production, they see that the nature of the effect is permanently present within the cause.

One should carefully question them: "What is your position? What are the characteristics of the cause? What are the characteristics of the effect? Are the two characteristics of cause and effect the same or different? If they are not different, then the two, cause and effect, are not determined. Since cause and effect are without distinction, it is unreasonable to say the effect exists within the cause. If they are different, then what is your position? Are the characteristics of the effect within the cause those of the unborn or those of the already born? If the characteristics are those of the unborn, then within the cause, the effect has not yet been born, and to say 'it exists' is unreasonable. If the characteristics are those of the already born, then the effect has already been born, and for it to be produced again from the cause is unreasonable. Therefore, the effect does not preexist within the cause. Rather, there must be a cause, and upon meeting conditions, the effect is produced."

Furthermore, an existent dharma can be known to exist within an existent dharma by five characteristics: first, it can be obtained at a given location, like water in a pitcher; second, it can be obtained at its support, like eye-consciousness in the eye; third, it can be obtained through its own characteristic, like the cause's own substance, not through inference; fourth, it can be obtained through its own function; fifth, because of the cause's transformation, there is transformation, or because of a condition's transformation, the effect is transformed. Therefore, their claim that "constantly, at all times, permanently, at all times, the effect exists within the cause" is unreasonable. For these reasons, the thesis they establish is not a reasonable statement. Thus, because of the non-different characteristic, because of the different characteristic, because of the unborn characteristic, and because of the already-born characteristic, it is unreasonable.

**Second, the theory that effects are merely manifested from conditions.** This holds that certain śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas give rise to the view and establish the thesis that the nature of all dharmas originally exists. From conditions, they are manifested; they are not produced from conditions. This refers to both the "effect exists in the cause" theorists and the "sound-characteristic" theorists who hold this position.

For what reason do the "effect exists in the cause" theorists see that within causes the nature of the effect preexists and is manifested from conditions? Because of both scripture and reasoning. "Scripture" is as previously stated. "Reasoning" means that one of an investigative nature contemplates at length, as previously described. They reason thus: "The effect previously exists, and it is produced again from the cause: this is unreasonable. Yet it is not the case that no effort is needed to bring about the effect. What then is the purpose of effort? Is it not solely to manifest the effect?" Having made this erroneous discrimination, they establish the theory of manifestation.

One should carefully question them: "What is your position? Is it that there is no obstructing condition yet there is obstruction, or is there an obstructing condition? If there is no obstructing condition, then for there to be no obstructing condition yet for there to be obstruction is unreasonable. If there is an obstructing condition, then why does the cause associated with the effect not also obstruct? Since both are existent, this is unreasonable. For example, just as darkness obstructs water in a basin and can also obstruct the basin itself, if you say the obstructing condition can also obstruct, then it should also obscure the cause, since both are obstructed. To say it manifests only the effect that is within the cause and does not manifest the cause is unreasonable."

One should further question them: "Is the existent nature the obstructing condition, or are they different? If the existent nature is the obstructing condition, then the existent nature should never be manifested, which is unreasonable. The cause is also existent; why does it not serve as an obstruction? If the nature of the effect is said to be the obstructing condition, then one and the same dharma is both cause and effect, like a sprout being the fruit of a seed and the cause of a stem. Then one and the same dharma is both manifested and not manifested, which is unreasonable."

"I now ask you; answer as you wish. Are the original dharma and its manifestation the same or different? If not different, then the dharma should always be manifest. Being manifested once and then manifested again is unreasonable. If different, is that manifestation without a cause or with a cause? If without a cause, being manifested without a cause is unreasonable. If with a cause: the nature of the effect can be manifested, but it is not the nature of the cause. For a non-manifest cause to be able to manifest the effect is unreasonable."

Thus, because of the absence of an obstructing condition, because of the presence of an obstructing condition, because of the existent characteristic, because of the effect-characteristic, because of non-difference of manifestation, and because of difference of manifestation, it is unreasonable.

**Third, the theory that past and future are truly existent.** This holds that certain śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas who belong to this school, through incorrect mental engagement, give rise to the view and establish the thesis that the past exists and the future exists, that their characteristics are established just as the present is truly existent and not merely nominal.

For what reason do they give rise to this view and establish this thesis? Because of both scripture and reasoning. "Scripture" is as previously stated. Furthermore, those who belong to this school, regarding the Tathāgata's sūtras, engage in unreasonable analysis. For instance, the sūtra says: "All that exists is the twelve sense-bases. These twelve sense-bases have a true characteristic that is real." Furthermore, the Blessed One says: "There is past karma." Also: "There is past form. There is future form." And so on extensively, up to consciousness being likewise. "Reasoning" means that one of an investigative nature contemplates at length, as previously described. They reason thus: "If a dharma abides in its own characteristic, that dharma is truly existent. If this were non-existent in the future, then at that time it should not yet have received its characteristic. If this were non-existent in the past, then at that time it should have lost its own characteristic. If that were so, the dharma's own characteristic should not be established. By this reasoning, it is also not truly real. Therefore, it is unreasonable." Through such reflection, they give rise to this view and establish this thesis that the nature and characteristics of past and future are truly existent.

One should carefully question them: "What is your position? Are the two characteristics of past and future the same as or different from the present? If the characteristics are the same, then establishing three separate temporal characteristics is unreasonable. If the characteristics are different, then for the nature and characteristics to be truly existent is unreasonable."

"Furthermore, you should state your position: are dharmas following the three times permanent or impermanent? If of permanent character, then falling within the three times is unreasonable. If of impermanent character, then being truly existent throughout the three times is unreasonable."

"I now ask you; answer as you wish. Do you hold that a future dharma comes to the present? Or that it dies there and is born here? Or that it remains in the future while conditions produce the present? Or that originally there was no karma but now there is karma? Or that originally the characteristics were not complete but now the characteristics are complete? Or that originally the characteristic was different and now it is not different? Or that within the future there is a present portion?"

"If the future dharma itself comes to the present, then this would imply spatial direction. Moreover, it should be no different from the present. Furthermore, it should be permanent. This is unreasonable. If you say the future dies and the present is born, then the future does not produce, and the present-world dharma, originally non-existent, is now born. Furthermore, for what has not yet arisen in the future to be said to have died is unreasonable."
