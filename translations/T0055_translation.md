# 苦陰因事經
## Sūtra on the Causes of the Mass of Suffering

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0055

Translated by Faju of the Western Jin dynasty.

---

### Fascicle 1: Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Causes of the Mass of Suffering

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavat was dwelling among the Śākyans at Kapilavastu, in the Nyagrodha Park. Then the Śākyan Mahānāma, in the afternoon, came walking to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he bowed at the World-Honored One's feet and sat to one side. Having sat to one side, the Śākyan great warrior addressed the World-Honored One, saying: "As the World-Honored One has taught the Dharma, I understand it all, namely the three fetters of mental attachment: the fetter of attachment to sensual desire, the fetter of attachment to anger, and the fetter of attachment to delusion. Thus I fully understand the World-Honored One's teaching. Yet now, states of sensual desire arise and attach to my mind, and states of anger and delusion arise and attach to my mind. Therefore, World-Honored One, I think to myself: 'What quality within me is not yet exhausted, such that sensual desire arises and attaches to my mind, and anger and delusion arise and attach to my mind?'"

"Great warrior, there is a quality not yet exhausted in you that causes you to dwell as a householder, not training in the path, without faith and delight in going forth from home to homelessness. Great warrior, if this quality were exhausted, you would not remain a householder; you would certainly be able to have faith and delight in going forth from home to homelessness and to train in the path. Great warrior, because that quality is not yet exhausted, you remain a householder, without faith and delight in going forth from home to homelessness to train in the path."

Then the Śākyan great warrior rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, joined his palms toward the World-Honored One, and addressed the World-Honored One, saying: "I now have faith and delight in the World-Honored One. I pray that the World-Honored One will graciously teach me the Dharma, so that I may see the truth and put an end to my doubts."

"Great warrior, there are five sensual desires connected with what is loved and cherished, beautiful in form, associated with sensuality and attachment. Forms cognized by the eye, sounds cognized by the ear, scents cognized by the nose, tastes cognized by the tongue, and tactile sensations cognized by the body: being attached among these, one delights and takes pleasure in their gratification. Thus, great warrior, there is gratification in sensual pleasures, yet among them there is much danger and ruin.

"How, great warrior, is there much danger and ruin in sensual pleasures? Here, great warrior, a son of good family learns a craft to support his livelihood, whether farming, trading, clerking, accounting, calculation, seal-cutting, composing verse, keeping accounts, teaching writing, or serving in the king's army. He is oppressed by cold when it is cold, oppressed by heat when it is hot, enduring hunger and thirst, tormented by mosquitoes, gadflies, flies, and fleas. He seeks wealth. That son of good family rises thus, works thus, and labors diligently thus, yet he cannot obtain wealth. He becomes sorrowful and unhappy, weeping, beating his breast, and striking himself, increasing in delusion, for his efforts bear no fruit. That son of good family rises thus, works thus, and labors diligently thus, and he obtains wealth. Having obtained wealth, he guards and protects it, thinking: 'May the king not seize my wealth, may thieves not steal it, may fire not burn it, may it not decay, may my investments not fail.' Yet despite guarding his wealth, it is seized by the king, stolen by thieves, burned by fire, decayed, or his investments fail. He becomes sorrowful and unhappy, weeping, beating his breast, and striking himself, increasing in delusion. Furthermore, all that has been loved and cherished throughout the long night of existence is ruined and lost. This, great warrior, is the mass of suffering visible in this present life, caused by sensual pleasures, arising from the intensification of sensual pleasures, having sensual pleasures as its condition.

"Great warrior, because of sensual pleasures, due to the intensification of sensual pleasures, mother quarrels with son, son quarrels with mother, father quarrels with son, son quarrels with father, brother quarrels with sister, sister quarrels with brother. They fight and quarrel with one another: mother speaks ill of son, son speaks ill of mother, father speaks ill of son, son speaks ill of father, brother speaks ill of sister, sister speaks ill of brother. How much more so among unrelated people! This, great warrior, is the mass of suffering visible in this present life, caused by sensual pleasures, arising from the intensification of sensual pleasures.

"Great warrior, because of sensual pleasures, due to the intensification of sensual pleasures, king quarrels with king, brāhmaṇa quarrels with brāhmaṇa, householder quarrels with householder, thief quarrels with thief, craftsman quarrels with craftsman. They fight and quarrel with each other using various weapons: with fists, with stones, with knives and clubs. Among them, some die or suffer the agony of death. This, great warrior, is the mass of suffering visible in this present life, caused by sensual pleasures, arising from the intensification of sensual pleasures.

"Great warrior, because of sensual pleasures, due to the intensification of sensual pleasures, beings don armor and take up bows and arrows, don leather armor and wield exceedingly sharp swords, and surround each other in massed battle. In their midst, they fight with elephants, with horses, with chariots, with infantry, with women, or with men. Among them, some die or suffer the agony of death. This, great warrior, is the mass of suffering visible in this present life, caused by sensual pleasures, arising from the intensification of sensual pleasures.

"Great warrior, because of sensual pleasures, due to the intensification of sensual pleasures, beings don armor and take up bows and arrows, don leather armor and wield exceedingly sharp swords, and advance upon great fortified cities to assault them. In their midst, some blow conch shells, some beat drums, some raise loud battle cries; they strike with iron maces, with battle-axes, with halberds, with sharp discuses; they shoot arrows at each other, hurl down stones, fire crossbows, or pour down molten copper. Among them, some die or suffer the agony of death. This, great warrior, is the mass of suffering visible in this present life, caused by sensual pleasures, arising from the intensification of sensual pleasures.

"Great warrior, because of sensual pleasures, due to the intensification of sensual pleasures, beings enter towns and cities, break through walls and plunder storehouses, steal others' property, waylay travelers on the road, destroy others' cities, ravage others' villages, and kill others. Officials seize them and inflict various tortures upon them: cutting off their hands, cutting off their feet, cutting off both hands and feet, cutting off their ears, cutting off their noses, cutting off their tongues, cutting off their topknots, cutting off their hair, cutting off both topknot and hair; placing them in chests; wrapping them in cloth and executing them; placing them on sand and gravel or on grass; placing them in the mouth of an iron donkey or in the mouth of an iron lion; placing them in bronze cauldrons or in iron cauldrons; cutting them to pieces; piercing them with sharp tridents; making them lie on heated iron beds; sprinkling them with boiling oil; placing them in mortars and pounding them with iron pestles; whipping them, beating them with staves, striking them with clubs; leading them to the execution stake and beheading them with a blade. This, great warrior, is the mass of suffering visible in this present life, caused by sensual pleasures, arising from the intensification of sensual pleasures.

"Great warrior, because of sensual pleasures, due to the intensification of sensual pleasures, beings commit misconduct of body, speech, and mind. At such a time, when they fall gravely ill and lie upon their beds, lying in the shade, their bodies racked with extreme painful feelings, joyless, their lives about to end, the misconduct of body, speech, and mind that they have committed hangs inverted over them at the time of death, just as when the sun is about to set and darkness comes between the great peaks, the shadows of the mountains hang inverted downward. Just so, the misconduct of body, speech, and mind, at the time of death, hangs inverted over them. They think: 'This misconduct of body, speech, and mind hangs inverted over me. I never performed good deeds, never made merit. I committed much evil, inclining toward wickedness, greed, and cruelty. I performed no meritorious deeds, no good deeds, made no refuge for myself. I will certainly fall into that destiny.' Then they are filled with remorse. Having felt remorse, their death is not good, and their rebirth is not good. This, great warrior, is the mass of suffering visible in this present life, caused by sensual pleasures, arising from the intensification of sensual pleasures.

"Great warrior, because of sensual pleasures, due to the intensification of sensual pleasures, beings commit misconduct of body, speech, and mind. Having committed misconduct of body, speech, and mind, by that cause and condition, at the breaking up of the body after death, they are reborn in an evil destiny, in the hells. This, great warrior, is the mass of suffering in the next life, caused by sensual pleasures, arising from the intensification of sensual pleasures.

"Thus, great warrior, the five sensual pleasures have much suffering, danger, and ruin. If a noble disciple does not see with right wisdom as things truly are, and commits evil and unwholesome deeds born of sensual desire, and does not enter the delight of what is called the unsurpassed peace, then, great warrior, that noble disciple remains bound up with sensual pleasures.

"Moreover, great warrior, as for me: I see sensual pleasures as yielding little gratification; I know them to be full of suffering; I know them to be ruinous. I see and know as things truly are, and I do not commit evil and unwholesome deeds born of sensual desire; I dwell in the protection of the unsurpassed peace as my happiness. Thus it is, great warrior, that I am not bound up with sensual pleasures.

"Great warrior, on one occasion I was staying at Rājagṛha, at the Saptaparṇa Cave on the flank of Vaibhāra Mountain. Great warrior, rising in the late afternoon, I went along the mountainside and from a distance I saw some Nirgranthas who were always kneeling, never sitting, undergoing extreme painful austerities. I approached them and said: 'Why is it, Nirgranthas, that you always kneel and never sit, subjecting yourselves to such extreme austerities?'

"They answered me: 'Gautama, our teacher Nirgrantha Jñātiputra has declared: "You Nirgranthas, in the past you committed evil deeds. Now by practicing these austerities you shall burn away those past evil deeds. As for your present conduct of body, speech, and mind, wherever evil is present, you shall not commit it."'

"I said to them: 'Tell me, Nirgranthas, are you able to have confidence in your teacher Nirgrantha Jñātiputra and abide by his teaching? Do you not doubt your teacher?'

"They said: 'Gautama, we do not doubt our teacher Nirgrantha Jñātiputra; we have confidence in and abide by him.'

"I replied to them: 'If it is so, then consider this, Nirgranthas: there is a Nirgrantha who in the past committed evil deeds and who now practices extreme austerities. When that Nirgrantha dies and is reborn among humans, he will once again find himself among you Nirgranthas, practicing just as you do, always kneeling, never sitting, performing austerities. So will it be with all of you.'

"They said: 'Gautama, it is not through good conduct that one obtains good rewards. King Bimbisāra dwells in happiness and ease; you, Śramaṇa Gautama, cannot equal that.'

"I said: 'Nirgranthas, you have spoken wrongly. Why? Because you are foolish, unstable, unskillful, without restraint, and without contentment, and yet you say: "King Bimbisāra constantly abides in goodness and obtains happiness and ease; Śramaṇa Gautama cannot equal that." Nirgranthas, should you not first determine how I abide in constant happiness and ease, before making comparisons between King Bimbisāra and Śramaṇa Gautama? Nirgranthas, I shall explain to you. Is it not the case that I abide in happiness and ease beyond what King Bimbisāra of Magadha can match? Rather, you should be saying: "King Bimbisāra of Magadha constantly abides in happiness and ease, which you, Śramaṇa Gautama, cannot match."'

"They said: 'Śramaṇa Gautama, we now ask you: who abides in superior happiness and ease, King Bimbisāra of Magadha or Śramaṇa Gautama?'

"'What do you think, Nirgranthas? Can King Bimbisāra of Magadha be fully at ease in mind and speech? Can he experience uninterrupted bodily happiness for seven days and seven nights?'

"'No indeed, Gautama.'

"'What of six days, five days, four days, three days, two days, or one day and one night? Can he be at ease in mind and speech and experience uninterrupted bodily happiness?'

"'No indeed, Gautama.'

"'What do you think, Nirgranthas? Am I able to be at ease in mind and speech? Do I abide in genuine bodily happiness and ease for one day and one night?'

"'Yes indeed, Gautama.'

"'What of two days, three days, on up to seven days and seven nights? Am I able to be at ease in mind and speech? Is my bodily experience one of uninterrupted genuine happiness and ease?'

"'Yes indeed, Gautama.'

"'What do you think, Nirgranthas? Between us, who constantly abides in superior happiness and ease, King Bimbisāra of Magadha or I?'

"'From what you, Śramaṇa Gautama, have said, and understanding its meaning, it is Śramaṇa Gautama who abides in superior happiness and ease, not King Bimbisāra of Magadha.'

"Great warrior, seeing that sensual pleasures yield little gratification and knowing that among them there is much suffering, danger, and ruin, the noble disciple who is unable to see with wisdom as things truly are, and who commits evil and unwholesome deeds born of sensual desire, and who does not enter the delight of the unsurpassed peace, that noble disciple, great warrior, remains bound up with sensual pleasures.

"Moreover, great warrior, as for me: I see sensual pleasures as yielding little gratification, knowing them to be full of much suffering and ruin. I see with right wisdom as things truly are; I do not engage in unwholesome states born of sensual desire but dwell in the protection of the unsurpassed peace as my own happiness. Thus it is, great warrior, that I am not bound up with sensual pleasures."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The great warrior and the bhikṣus, having heard what the World-Honored One had said, rejoiced and were glad.
