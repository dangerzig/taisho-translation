# Abridged Dharma for Bhikṣus of the Mūlasarvāstivāda to Study and Train In

## *Gēnběn Shuō Yīqiè Yǒu Bù Bìqiū Xíxué Lüèfǎ* (根本說一切有部苾芻習學略法)

Taishō 1905

Compiled by 'Phags-pa (*Báhésībā*, 拔合思巴) of the Yuan dynasty.

*Note: This is a vinaya manual compiled by the Imperial Preceptor 'Phags-pa Lama (1235-1280 CE), the Tibetan Buddhist hierarch who served as spiritual advisor to Kublai Khan and established Tibetan Buddhism as the state religion of the Yuan dynasty. The text presents an abridgment of the Mūlasarvāstivāda vinaya, the monastic code followed in Tibetan Buddhism, organized as a practical handbook for bhikṣus. It is divided into three main sections: (1) procedures for obtaining the monastic precepts (ordination rites and prerequisites), (2) methods for maintaining the precepts without transgression (including reliance on a preceptor, mindful vigilance, knowledge of the five categories of offenses totaling 253 rules, purification through the poṣadha ceremony, and the four requisites of monastic life), and (3) methods for repairing transgressions (confession, repentance, and karmic remedies). The 253 rules are enumerated in full: 4 pārājika (defeats), 13 saṃghāvaśeṣa (requiring formal proceedings), 30 naihsargika-pāyantika (offenses requiring forfeiture), 90 pāyantika (expiable offenses), 4 pratideśanīya (offenses to be acknowledged), and 112 śaikṣa (rules of training). A colophon dated 1271 CE (the eighth year of the Zhìyuán era) records that the text was compiled on imperial command by the bhikṣu Dharmatrāta ("Fǎjiù," 法救), under the direction of 'Phags-pa, and translated from the Uyghur by the Hanlin Academician Hétái Sàlǐdū Tōng.*

---

Homage to the Omniscient One.

The vinaya-piṭaka, which teaches the training in the higher morality, has three divisions:

(1) Procedures for those who have not yet obtained [the precepts] to obtain them; (2) Methods for those who have obtained the precepts to maintain them without transgression; (3) Methods for those who have transgressed to repair [their precepts].

## Part One: Obtaining the Precepts

There are four conditions that can obstruct the obtaining of the precepts, the stable maintenance of the precepts, the growth of virtuous deeds, and the dignified appearance of the assembly.

If all four adverse conditions are absent, and one additionally possesses the five favorable conditions of: [proper] place of refuge, [proper monastic] appearance, [proper] body, [proper] intention, and [proper] deportment, then one may obtain the precepts. The distinctions of meaning should be known from the ordination manual.

## Part Two: Maintaining the Precepts

There are five methods:

### 1. Reliance on a Preceptor

After receiving full ordination (*upasampadā*), once one has completed ten years [of seniority] and possesses fifteen of the twenty-one kinds of five-fold virtue, one should seek a vinaya-knowing bhikṣu as one's reliance-preceptor. In all matters of what should and should not be done, one should ask that teacher and act according to the teacher's instructions. One should rely on that teacher until one has completed ten years from one's own ordination and the three kinds of virtue are fully mature.

### 2. Vigilance Through Antidote-Contemplation

At all times, one should maintain mindfulness, awareness, and non-negligence. Possessing these three, one eliminates all adverse dharmas and accomplishes all wholesome dharmas.

### 3. Knowledge of the Offenses to Be Abandoned

The rules are collected into five categories totaling 253 offenses. Among the dharmas to be abandoned, one should maintain pure precepts, or at least precepts with proper remedies applied, with mindfulness, remembrance, and the conditions of not being a first-offender and not having abandoned the training.

**The First Category: Four Pārājika Offenses**

(1) If a bhikṣu engages in sexual intercourse through any of the three orifices, he commits the first pārājika. (2) If he steals property not belonging to himself that belongs to another, he commits the second pārājika. (3) If he takes the life of a human being or human fetus other than himself, he commits the third pārājika. (4) If he falsely claims to have attained superhuman dharmas, he commits the fourth pārājika. These four pārājika offenses all sever the root of the correct precepts.

**The Second Category: Thirteen Saṃghāvaśeṣa Offenses**

(1) Intentional emission of semen. (2) Touching a woman's body with a defiled mind. (3) Speaking coarse, lustful words to a woman. (4) Praising one's own body before a woman as worthy of being served through sexual acts. (5) Acting as a matchmaker. (6) Building a dwelling by begging for land and materials from others in three unsuitable places. (7) Building a monastic compound in an unsuitable place. (8) Groundlessly accusing a bhikṣu of a pārājika offense. (9) Groundlessly accusing a bhikṣu of a pārājika on the basis of a minor pretext. (10) Schisming the harmonious saṃgha. (11) Assisting in schisming the saṃgha. (12) When expelled by the saṃgha for corrupting families, slandering the saṃgha. (13) When another bhikṣu, out of compassion, wishes to admonish one regarding one's own precepts, refusing and saying "Do not discuss any rules on my account."

**The Third Category: Thirty Naihsargika-Pāyantika Offenses**

[First ten:] Keeping unconsecrated robes past ten nights; being separated from one of the three robes overnight; keeping cloth past thirty nights when one's three robes are insufficient; having a non-relative nun wash one's robes; accepting robes from a nun; begging robes from laypeople when one already has three; begging excessively; soliciting before the donor has given; separately soliciting two donors who each intend to give; requesting more than three times verbally or three times silently.

[Second ten:] Making a new spread with silk; making a new spread of pure black wool; mixing black and white wool for a spread; making a second spread before six years; making a new sitting-cloth without patching an old one; carrying sheep's wool more than three yojanas without a porter; having a non-relative nun card wool; keeping gems or touching them; buying and selling for profit.

[Third ten:] Keeping a proper-measure vessel unconsecrated past ten nights; seeking an extra bowl when one already has one; having a non-relative weaver weave cloth without paying wages; interfering with weaving being done for oneself; taking back a robe or bowl already given to a bhikṣu; improperly distributing rain-retreat gains; keeping a rain-bathing robe before or after the proper period; diverting communal property to oneself; keeping any of the four kinds of medicine past the allowed time-limit.

**The Fourth Category: Ninety Pāyantika Offenses**

[In nine groups of ten, covering: lying, criticizing other bhikṣus, sowing discord, teaching laypeople improperly, destroying plants, disparaging monastic officers, sleeping arrangements, handling water, interactions with nuns, eating rules, contact with laywomen, military matters, striking monks, concealing offenses, disputes about seating, various rules regarding food consumption, sleeping with non-ordained persons, speaking against the training rules, and regulations regarding measurements for robes and garments.]

**The Fifth Category: Four Pratideśanīya Offenses**

(1) Receiving food from a nun in a village or on a road. (2) Continuing to eat when a nun is directing the distribution at a layperson's house. (3) Taking food in a layperson's house that the saṃgha has restricted. (4) When appointed to watch a dangerous road, failing to keep watch and receiving food while seated.

**The Sixth Category: 112 Śaikṣa (Rules of Training)**

[Detailed rules covering: wearing the inner robe properly; covering the body correctly when going for alms; walking mindfully, looking one fathom ahead; not covering the head, not tucking up the robe, not swinging the arms, not shaking the head; sitting properly without crossing legs; receiving food attentively, not making the portions too large or too small; not opening the mouth before the food arrives; not talking with food in the mouth; not making noises while eating; not licking the fingers; not shaking the bowl; not discarding food water near other monks; not placing the bowl on dangerous edges; not washing the bowl facing a torrent; rules for teaching the Dharma (not teaching those who are standing, lying down, sitting higher, walking ahead, covering their head, wearing weapons, riding animals, etc.); not urinating while standing; not urinating on green grass or in water; not climbing trees higher than a person's head.]

---

The above: four pārājika, thirteen saṃghāvaśeṣa, thirty naihsargika-pāyantika combined with ninety single pāyantika for a total of one hundred twenty pāyantika, four pratideśanīya, and one hundred twelve śaikṣa: altogether 253 vinaya-rules. Bhikṣus who have received full ordination should diligently maintain them.

### 4. Purification of One's Own Precepts

To purify one's precepts, one performs the poṣadha (*bùsà*) ceremony. To eliminate calamities and the evil misfortunes threatening one's own and others' lives, one observes the summer retreat (*ānjiū*) for three months. At the conclusion of the summer retreat, one performs the pravāraṇā (*zìzì*, self-examination). These three constitute the fourth method of maintenance.

### 5. Reliance on Comfortable Living Conditions

Robes, food, lodging, and medicine: among these four, one should avoid the two extremes of luxury and severe austerity, abiding in the middle way.

## Part Three: Repairing Transgressions

To remove concealed offenses, one should make disclosure. To remove committed offenses, one should repent. For subtle offenses of intent not yet acted upon in body or speech, one should maintain vigilance. To prevent obstruction during karmic proceedings, one should uphold [the rules]. To ensure one does not repeat the offense, one should accept disciplinary measures.

---

[Colophon:] Respectfully, the origin of this abridgment [was as follows]: the Great Yuan World-Sovereign, the present wise ruler, whose sagacious wisdom renews daily and whose vast compassion was bestowed by heaven, in the leisure from the myriad affairs of state, revered the Buddhist teaching. There was the Imperial Preceptor ['Phags-pa], whose wisdom was fully equipped, whose renown was famous throughout the ten directions, whose understanding of doctrine was thorough, and whose majestic virtue pervaded all. He could lead the three assemblies of beings directly to the three kinds of bodhi, and further established the full ordination to perfect enlightenment. The Imperial Preceptor's vast virtue is beyond what mouth and mind can exhaust. Possessing every kind of holy ability, he caused the lamp to be transmitted in succession. The Tripiṭaka bhikṣu Dharmatrāta (*Fǎjiù*), receiving the imperial command, compiled this abridgment under the Imperial Preceptor's direction, so that it might circulate broadly. The people of the Īla kingdom were made to understand the three kinds of grammar and master both Dharma-eloquence and word-eloquence. The Hanlin Academician and Supervisor Hétái Sàlǐdū Tōng translated it into Uyghur script. The Commissionary for Buddhist Affairs of all circuits Hétái Sàlǐdū Tōng rendered it into Chinese. In the eighth year of the Zhìyuán era [1271 CE], on the fifth day after the Lantern Festival.
