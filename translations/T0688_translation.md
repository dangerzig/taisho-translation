# 佛說未曾有經
## Sūtra of the Unprecedented, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0688

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling in the city of Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, at the morning hour, donned his robes and took up his bowl to enter the city of Rājagṛha, begging for food with right mindfulness. He saw a certain place where a great new building had been constructed, a multi-storied mansion, lofty and splendid, with carved and ornamented doors and windows, its walls well-ordered, free from wind and dust, sheltered from cold and heat. When the Venerable Ānanda saw this, he thought: "If a good man or good woman were to build such a splendid building and give it to the Saṃgha of the four directions; and if, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, another good man or good woman were to take relics the size of a mustard seed and build a stūpa the size of an āmalaka fruit, with a finial like a needle, topped with a parasol like a jujube leaf, or were to make a Buddha image as small as a grain of barley: of these two merits, which would be greater?"

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda finished his begging round, returned to his quarters, finished his meal, put away his robes and bowl, and washed his feet. He then went to the Buddha's place. With single-minded reverence, he bowed his head to the ground, sat to one side, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Earlier this morning I donned my robes, took up my bowl, and entered the city of Rājagṛha to beg for food. I saw a certain place where a great new building had been built, a multi-storied mansion, lofty and splendid, with carved and ornamented doors and windows, its walls well-ordered, free from wind and dust, sheltered from cold and heat. I thought: 'If a good man or good woman were to build such a magnificent building and give it to the Saṃgha of the four directions; and if, after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, another good man or good woman were to take relics the size of a mustard seed and build a stūpa the size of an āmalaka fruit, with a finial like a needle, topped with a parasol like a jujube leaf, or were to make a Buddha image as small as a grain of barley: of these two merits, which would be greater?'"

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Ānanda: "Excellent! Excellent, Ānanda! For the sake of many people, for the peace and happiness of sentient beings, out of compassion for the world, for the sake of the great assembly, and for the abundant benefit of devas and humans, you ask the Tathāgata about this meaning. Ānanda! Listen carefully and reflect well upon it! The land of Jambudvīpa is seven thousand yojanas wide, broad in the north and narrow in the south. The people's faces are shaped like carriages. Suppose this entire land were filled with sugarcane, bamboo, reeds, rice, hemp, and thickets, without any empty space, all as one mass. Ānanda! Suppose all these grasses and trees were each to become a person, and all attained the fruits of stream-entry, once-return, non-return, arhatship, or the awakening of a pratyekabuddha. If one person were to support them all for the span of a lifetime with robes, bowls, food and drink, bedding, medicine, and dwellings, providing everything they needed; and after their parinirvāṇa were to build a stūpa for each one, and make offerings to each stūpa with reverence: fragrant flowers, music, incense, ointment, powder, banners, and jeweled canopies, all fully provided. What do you think? Would such merit be great or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very great, World-Honored One! Very great, Sugata! Such a good man or good woman would obtain great merit."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Set aside Jambudvīpa. There is also Godānīya, eight thousand yojanas wide, where people's faces are shaped like half-moons. If the people there likewise performed such great meritorious deeds as described above:

"Furthermore, Ānanda! Set aside Godānīya. There is also Pūrvavideha, nine thousand yojanas wide, where people's faces are round and full. If the people there likewise performed such great meritorious deeds as described above:

"Furthermore, Ānanda! Set aside Pūrvavideha. There is also Uttarakuru, ten thousand yojanas wide, where people's faces are square. If the people there likewise performed such great meritorious deeds as described above:"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The great palace of Śakra, Lord of the Devas, is ornamented with carvings and engravings, subtle, wondrous, and extraordinary. It has eighty-four thousand jeweled pillars, with heavenly blue lapis lazuli interlaid with gold, draped above with jeweled nets, the ground spread with gold sand, and railings of rare sandalwood.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! The great palace of Śakra has eighty-four thousand jeweled windows, also with heavenly blue lapis lazuli interlaid with gold, draped above with jeweled nets, spread with gold sand, and railings of sandalwood.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! The great palace of Śakra has eighty-four thousand more windows of heavenly dark-blue gems, subtle and splendid, ornamented as above.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! The great palace of Śakra has eighty-four thousand towers and pavilions extending in four directions all around, adorned with every kind of treasure, ornamented as above."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "If a good man or good woman were to build such a great palace as Śakra's and give it to the Saṃgha of the four directions, what do you think? Would such a good man or good woman, by this cause and condition, obtain great merit or not?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Very great, World-Honored One! Very great, Sugata! Such a good man or good woman would obtain great merit."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Set aside the merit of the four continents, and set aside the merit of Śakra's great palace. If a good man or good woman were to build a hundred thousand billion palaces like Śakra's great palace and give them all to the Saṃgha of the four directions; and another good man or good woman, after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, were to take relics the size of a mustard seed and build a stūpa the size of an āmalaka fruit, with a finial like a needle, topped with a parasol like a jujube leaf, or were to make a Buddha image as small as a grain of barley: the first merit could not match this, not a hundredfold, not a thousandfold, not ten-thousandfold, not a hundred-thousand-billion-fold, beyond all reckoning. Ānanda, you should know that this is because of the Tathāgata's immeasurable merit: his aggregate of morality, his aggregate of concentration, his aggregate of wisdom, his aggregate of liberation, and his aggregate of the knowledge and vision of liberation.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! The Tathāgata's immeasurable merit includes great spiritual powers and supernatural transformations, as well as the perfection of generosity, the perfection of morality, the perfection of patience, the perfection of vigor, the perfection of meditation, the perfection of wisdom, and all such immeasurable merits."

Then the Buddha told the Venerable Ānanda: "You should carefully uphold this sūtra."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "I accept your teaching, World-Honored One! What is the name of this dharma? How should we uphold it in the Tathāgata's teaching?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This is called 'The Unprecedented Dharma.' It is the skillful means of all pure and wondrous dharmas. For this reason I earnestly entrust it to you. You should frequently and widely proclaim it to devas, humans, asuras, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, and all other beings, human and non-human, explaining it in detail. You should plant the seeds of the Tathāgata's wholesome roots and meritorious qualities, so that all sentient beings who hear it may enter into the Tathāgata's wholesome roots and merit. Through this cause and condition, freed from all afflictions, they will all attain buddhahood."

When the bhikṣus heard this, they were filled with joy and bowed in reverence.
