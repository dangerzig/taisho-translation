# Sūtra of the Buddha's Teaching on the Great Discrimination of Wrong and Right

## *Fóshuō Dà Biàn Xiézhèng Jīng* (佛說大辯邪正經)

Taishō 2893

Translator unknown. An apocryphal text.

*Note: This is an apocryphal Chinese Buddhist text, surviving as a Dunhuang manuscript. Written in a dialogue between the Buddha and Mañjuśrī, it emphasizes the supreme importance of the "true good spiritual friend" (zhēn shànzhīshí, 真善知識) as the sole path to enlightenment. The text exhibits strong Chan (Zen) influences: the identification of mind with Buddha, the priority of direct transmission through a teacher over textual study, the metaphors of form-and-shadow and sound-and-echo for non-duality, and the reinterpretation of the thirty-two marks and eighty minor characteristics as internal spiritual transformations rather than physical attributes. The final colophon attributes the text's discovery to Xuanzang and others in the Tathāgata's Seven-Jewel Cave, a stock apocryphal provenance claim. The rough literary style and doctrinal eclecticism confirm it as a Chinese popular composition.*

## Chapter One: The Buddha Teaches the Dharma-Gate of the Great Discrimination of Wrong and Right

Thus have I heard. At one time the Buddha was in the City of Gathered Fragrances (*Zhòngxiāng Chéng*, 眾香城), leading his assembly of followers in a great dharma-teaching. In that city there were also the bodhisattvas Mañjuśrī (*Wénshū Shīlì*, 文殊師利), Master of Self-Mastery of Mind (*Shè Zìzài Xīn*, 攝自在心), Contemplator of the Two Kinds of Emptiness of the Body (*Guānshēn Èrzhǒng Kōng*, 觀身二種空), Contemplator of the Two Kinds of Reality of the Body (*Guānshēn Èrzhǒng Shí*, 觀身二種實), Contemplator of the Impurity of the Body (*Guānshēn Bùjìng*, 觀身不淨), and Contemplator of the Flawless Body (*Guānshēn Wú Xiáhuì*, 觀身無瑕穢). Beneath their seats were a further eighty-four thousand followers and bodhisattvas of the ten stages.

## Chapter Two: The Ten Stages of the Bodhisattva

Why [are they so called]? The ten stages of the bodhisattva are as follows: (1) The Bodhisattva of Determined Faith and Severed Doubt at the first stage. (2) The Bodhisattva Able to Advance in Practice at the second stage. (3) The Bodhisattva of Constant Mindfulness and Severed Delusion at the third stage. (4) The Bodhisattva of Constant Concentration and Non-Dispersal of Conditions at the fourth stage. (5) The Bodhisattva Filled with Wisdom at the fifth stage. (6) The Bodhisattva Able to Follow the Root Teacher's Commands at the sixth stage. (7) The Bodhisattva Who Continues the Ancestral Lineage at the seventh stage. (8) The Bodhisattva Who Does Not Sever the Essential Nature of the Three Treasures at the eighth stage. (9) The Bodhisattva of Myriad Practices Fully Complete at the ninth stage. (10) The Bodhisattva of Permanence, Bliss, Self, and Purity at the tenth stage.

## Chapter Three: Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī Rises to Make a Request

At that time, from the assembly a bodhisattva named Mañjuśrī rose from his seat, knelt, and with palms joined addressed the root teacher Śākyamuni Buddha: "All sentient beings, from beginningless, vast, and great kalpas, have been revolving through the six destinies, drifting in birth-and-death without any rest, enduring all manner of suffering, up to this very day. We only pray that the root teacher Śākyamuni Buddha, with great compassion and great mercy, may take pity on all sentient beings, rescue and protect all sentient beings, and briefly teach the essentials of the dharma for the assembly."

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī, listen well, listen well! Consider this carefully. I shall explain it for you in detail. All sentient beings, from beginningless, vast, and great kalpas, have been revolving through the six destinies, drifting in birth-and-death without any rest, enduring all manner of suffering, up to this very day. This is all due to their own minds. Why? Because the mind, from moment to moment, from day to day, from month to month, from year to year, pursues conditions of all kinds, is stained by worldly dust, and is constantly in disorder and unsettled. Therefore the body is likewise."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "You have now made this earnest request. Set your mind properly. What mind do you cultivate?"

The Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī said: "My earlier earnest request was to have the essentials of the dharma briefly taught for the assembly. Those who do not know, I wish to cause them to know. Those who are not awakened, I wish to cause them to awaken. Those who have not realized, I wish to cause them to realize. Those who have not penetrated, I wish to cause them to penetrate. Those who have not attained bodhi, I wish to cause them to attain bodhi. Those who do not understand how to settle the mind, I wish to cause them to understand how to settle the mind."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "If you wish to study this wondrous dharma of settling the mind, you must first take refuge in a true good spiritual friend (*zhēn shànzhīshí*, 真善知識). Why? To cause those who do not know to know, one must take refuge in a true good spiritual friend. To cause those who are not awakened to awaken, one must take refuge in a true good spiritual friend. To cause those who have not realized to realize, one must take refuge in a true good spiritual friend. To cause those who have not penetrated to penetrate, one must take refuge in a true good spiritual friend. To cause those who have not attained bodhi to attain bodhi, one must take refuge in a true good spiritual friend. To cause those who do not understand how to settle the mind to understand how to settle the mind, one must take refuge in a true good spiritual friend."

## Chapter Four: Śākyamuni Buddha Teaches Bodhisattvas of the Initial Aspiration to Sever the Six Kinds of Wrong Views

At that time the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī again addressed Śākyamuni Buddha: "The eighty-four thousand followers all simultaneously make the vow to take refuge in a true good spiritual friend. What practice should they first cultivate?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "If there are bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, or upāsikās who wish to take refuge in a true good spiritual friend, they must first sever the six [wrong] views, and only then can they take refuge in a true good spiritual friend. Why? The six views are: (1) One must not give rise to arbitrary discrimination. (2) One must not deliberately weigh right and wrong. (3) One must not give rise to doubt and regret. (4) One must not harbor doubt in one's mind. (5) One must not give rise to overweening pride (*adhimāna*). (6) One's words must not contradict the teaching. Only after severing these six kinds of wrong views can one take refuge in a true good spiritual friend."

## Chapter Five: Cultivating the Way

At that time Śākyamuni Buddha praised this true good spiritual friend as inconceivable, immeasurable, and beyond comparison. Fearing that the great assembly might give rise to doubt and regret and be unable to take refuge in a true good spiritual friend, he then related his own past causes and conditions for the great assembly.

The Buddha addressed the assembly: "Listen as I relate my own story. When I was cultivating the Way in the causal stage, what merit did I obtain? What practice did I cultivate to attain buddhahood? I recall that in the past, for three great incalculable kalpas (*asaṃkhyeyakalpa*), I cultivated conditioned merit, which had many errors and faults. I did not comprehend the principle of the unconditioned Way, because I sought through attachment to characteristics. For three great incalculable kalpas I endured all manner of suffering. Afterward I encountered the Great Awakened World-Honored One in the city of Vaiśālī (*Píyēlí*, 毘耶離), beneath the Five-Aggregate Mountain, among the twin śāla trees in the Grove of Merit. He briefly taught me the essentials of the dharma, and I was then able to practice as he had taught. The Great Awakened World-Honored One also instructed me to enter the great equal site of awakening in the city of Vaiśālī. After seven times seven, forty-nine days, I attained the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas (*anutpattikadharmakṣānti*). All good qualities were complete, the hundred blessings adorned me, the myriad practices were fulfilled, and I attained buddhahood. The Great Awakened World-Honored One then touched my crown and bestowed a prophecy, saying: 'May you in the future attain the Way, with the title Root Teacher Śākyamuni Buddha. Be forever the compassionate father of all sentient beings in the four forms of birth, the guiding teacher of the six destinies. Constantly abide in the three realms, receiving and guiding those who are lost. Transmit this dharma and hand it down in succession, never letting it be interrupted, like space that has no end.'"

"I, Śākyamuni Buddha, vowed never to violate the Great Awakened World-Honored One's command. Therefore I transmit this dharma and hand it down in succession, never letting it be interrupted. For this reason, today I exhort the great assembly: draw near to the true good spiritual friend with skillful means, and you shall certainly obtain great benefit. If you can practice as taught, you shall in the future attain the unsurpassed fruit, no different from me."

## Chapter Six: Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī Asks about the Great Benefit

At that time the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī again addressed Śākyamuni Buddha: "What is this great benefit?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī, the great benefit is nothing other than the ability to transform all evil into all good. This is called the great benefit."

The Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī said: "What kind of person can transform all evil into all good?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī, the one who can transform all evil into all good is none other than the true good spiritual friend. Why? The good spiritual friend can, within a single matter, raise ten thousand million skillful means and analogies. The good spiritual friend can break through the two views and take refuge in the one essence. The good spiritual friend can turn the three poisons of the evil mind into the one-essence Three Treasures. The good spiritual friend can tame the four poison-serpents and fashion them into the four kinds of deportment. The good spiritual friend can turn the five poisons into the five precepts. The good spiritual friend can discipline the six consciousnesses and make them into the six fasts. The good spiritual friend can, within the seventh consciousness, seek and find the seven buddhas. The good spiritual friend can turn the eight sufferings into the eight liberations. The good spiritual friend can turn delusive and inverted thoughts into merit as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. The good spiritual friend can turn ignorance into the sun of wisdom. The good spiritual friend can turn afflictions into bodhi. The good spiritual friend can turn greed, anger, and delusion into the purity of the three karmas. The good spiritual friend can turn anger and jealousy into the four immeasurable minds. The good spiritual friend can turn the eight wrong [paths] into the eightfold noble path. The good spiritual friend has great merit, inconceivable, immeasurable, and beyond comparison. Were I to explain it fully, it could not be exhausted even in an entire kalpa."

## Chapter Seven: Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī Resolves a Doubt

At that time the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī again asked Śākyamuni Buddha: "Your disciple has a small doubt. How may it be resolved?"

Śākyamuni Buddha said: "What is this small doubt?"

The Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī said: "The buddhas of the three times have established the wondrous dharma of the three vehicles and taught ten thousand million skillful means to transform all sentient beings, causing them all to direct their minds toward the Way. Why then do all sentient beings still blaze in the cycle of birth-and-death?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Listen well, listen well! Consider this carefully. I shall explain it for you in detail. All sentient beings, from beginningless time, blaze in the cycle of birth-and-death because they do not comprehend my meaning. Why? If all sentient beings could comprehend my meaning, they would be no different from me."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! All sentient beings only comprehend the dharma-gate of analogy and metaphor. How could any sentient being comprehend my Great Discrimination of Wrong and Right, this profound and wondrous dharma?"

When the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī heard this, he sighed that it was unprecedented and inconceivable. His mind gave rise to awe and trepidation. He reproached himself and threw himself to the ground, where he lay in a faint for a long time before reviving. With a long sigh he wailed: "How painful! How agonizing! Who could have thought that today I would encounter the dharma-body father and mother, who has taught me this wondrous dharma of settling the mind along with the original causes and conditions, and has also taught the profound and wondrous dharma of the Great Discrimination of Wrong and Right!"

## Chapter Eight: Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī Again Asks about the Dharma-Gate of the Great Discrimination of Wrong and Right

At that time the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī again addressed the root teacher Śākyamuni Buddha: "In this profound dharma of the Great Discrimination of Wrong and Right, what is called 'right' and what is called 'wrong'?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī, there are two kinds of right. There are also two kinds of wrong. Why? The meaning of the two kinds of wrong is extremely profound. First, to depart from the essence and seek characteristics is called 'wrong within the right.' Second, to not believe that within one's body there is a buddha, to not believe that within one's body there is dharma, to cling to worldly affairs, to follow conditions and constantly practice in an inverted manner: this is called 'wrong within the wrong.'

"The two kinds of right: their meaning is extremely profound. First, to depart from the essence and seek characteristics yet clearly know that within one's body are the complete one-essence Three Treasures, and also know that within one's body is complete merit as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and completely believe that within one's body is the tathāgatagarbha dharma-body: this is called 'right within the right.' Second, to only recognize what is external and not recognize what is internal, and to mostly seek through attachment to characteristics: this is called 'right within the wrong.'

"Why? Although I have just described four kinds of wrong and right, only one thing is true. Why? Suppose a person wishes to travel to a distant place, a journey of a million miles. There are eighty-four thousand kinds of wrong roads, but only one road is true. Aside from that, the other eighty-three thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine roads are all wrong. Only one road is true. If sentient beings wish to traverse the correct road, how can they reach their destination?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "If there are sentient beings who wish to travel the correct road, they must rely on a true good spiritual friend to set up the right markers, and then they can reach their destination. Why? Those who seek the Way must first seek the one. If one can establish the one as the foundational principle, then among the eighty-four thousand dharma-gates that the Buddha has established, if one wishes to speak of the true, all should return to the one.

"Why? The ordinary and the sacred also become one. Why? For the ordinary to seek the sacred is like discarding water to seek ice. Ice is water; water is ice. Outside of ice there is no water; outside of water there is no ice. Afflictions and bodhi also become one. Why? To turn away from afflictions and seek bodhi is like departing from a form to seek its shadow. The form is the shadow; the shadow is the form. Outside of form there is no shadow; outside of shadow there is no form. Mind and buddha also become one. Why? To turn away from mind and seek the buddha is like turning from a sound to seek its echo. The sound is the echo; the echo is the sound. Outside of sound there is no echo; outside of echo there is no sound. Non-arising is the one; illusory transformation gives it two names.

"What is this one? To explain with distinctions: to seek outside the one essence is all wrong. To seek within the one essence is to become one. Sentient beings discriminate and have three [levels]; the Buddha established the three-vehicle teaching accordingly. If one can thoroughly comprehend all dharmas, the three were originally never three. The one is also empty and merely named 'one.' When one suddenly awakens to the emptiness of the nature of all dharmas and clearly knows the ultimate quiescence, one awakens to the nature of the unconditioned great Way. Only then is one called 'without words and without teaching.'"

## Chapter Nine: Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī Again Asks about the Thirty-Two Marks

At that time the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī again addressed Śākyamuni Buddha: "What are the thirty-two marks?"

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī: "Listen well, listen well! I shall explain it for you in detail. The thirty-two marks have a twofold meaning. First, for ordinary beings, they are called the thirty-two kinds of evil marks. Second, for the worthy and sacred, they are called the thirty-two kinds of good marks. Why? The thirty-two kinds of evil marks: because the body of an ordinary being contains the eight sufferings, the eight difficulties, the eight doubts, and the eight wrong [paths]. Four times eight is thirty-two; these are called the thirty-two kinds of evil marks."

"I, Śākyamuni Buddha, say that when I was in the causal stage, before I had encountered the Great Teacher, I was likewise. After I encountered the Great Teacher, he taught me to transform the eight sufferings into the eight liberations, to transform the eight difficulties into the eight kinds of self-mastery, to transform the eight doubts into the eight kinds of right faith, and to transform the eight wrong [paths] into the eightfold noble path. Four times eight is thirty-two; these are called the thirty-two kinds of good marks. Why? Within these two kinds of marks there is complete merit as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, which cannot be described fully. Were I to describe them fully, it could not be exhausted even in an entire kalpa."

## Chapter Ten: Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī Asks about the Eighty Minor Characteristics

At that time the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī again addressed Śākyamuni Buddha: "What are the eighty minor characteristics?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Listen well, listen well! Consider this carefully. I shall explain it for you in detail. The eighty minor characteristics have an extremely profound meaning. Why? To sever the ten evils is called the ten goods. To sever the five heinous crimes is called the five acts of reverent compliance. To discipline the seven consciousnesses is called the seven buddhas. To transform the eight kinds of wrong livelihood and sycophantic crookedness into the eight kinds of subtle and wondrous fragrant breezes. To discipline the six consciousnesses produces the six pāramitās. Not being stained by the six sense-objects is called the six penetrations. To transform the six thieves into the six spirit-kings who guard the dharma-citadel. The ten goods, five acts of reverent compliance, seven buddhas, eight kinds of subtle and wondrous breezes, six pāramitās, the non-arising nature of the six penetrations, and the six spirits guarding the dharma-body together make forty-eight kinds. Adding the thirty-two marks of goodness yields a total of eighty minor characteristics."

## Chapter Eleven: Comparing Merit

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "If you are truly my foremost disciple and can repay my kindness, you should maintain your resolve. What is this resolve? If you can repay my kindness, following my every command, propagate this dharma-king and transform all sentient beings to attain the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas: this is maintaining resolve. Why? I once heard Dīpaṃkara Tathāgata (*Dìngguāng Rúlái*, 定光如來), the Great Awakened World-Honored One, bodhisattva-mahāsattvas and other worthy sages who attained the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas, all knew through maintaining resolve. Clearly, one with resolve has all myriad practices complete. Clearly, one without resolve cannot accomplish even a single practice.

"Why? In the past I once compared the merit of maintaining resolve with eighty-four thousand followers among all the gates of the myriad practices, and there was nothing surpassing this most difficult and most rare achievement.

"Why? Suppose a person were to make statues of pure copper, numbering ten thousand times ten thousand, all in human form, and maintain offerings to them. Would this be difficult? This would not yet be difficult. What would be difficult? If a good man or good woman hears this profound and wondrous dharma of the Great Discrimination of Wrong and Right and immediately gives rise to faith: this is difficult.

"Suppose a person were to make statues of pure silver, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and maintain offerings to them. Would this be difficult? This would not yet be difficult. What would be difficult? If a good man or good woman is devoted and filial to their teacher, the saṃgha, their father and mother, serving them with utmost sincerity: this is difficult.

"Suppose a person were to make statues of pure gold, as numerous as the grasses and trees of the great earth, and maintain offerings to them. Would this be difficult? This would not yet be difficult. What would be difficult? If a good man or good woman can eliminate the two kinds of self, the self of persons and the self of dharmas: this is difficult.

"Suppose a person were to build a great stūpa entirely of true sandalwood, reaching up to the Brahmā Heaven, and adorn it with the seven treasures and maintain offerings. Would this be difficult? This would not yet be difficult. What would be difficult? If a good man or good woman transmits this dharma, handing it down in succession without letting it be interrupted: this is difficult.

"Suppose a person were to build jeweled halls of the seven treasures filling all of Jambudvīpa and maintain offerings. Would this be difficult? This would not yet be difficult. What would be difficult? If a good man or good woman follows the command of the root teacher Śākyamuni Buddha, cultivates the merit of maintaining resolve, and is determined to achieve it: this is difficult."

At that time, among the assembly, eighty-four thousand followers, hearing this teaching, were joyful and exultant, sighing that it was unprecedented. They simultaneously gave rise to the great vow and cultivated the merit of maintaining resolve, practicing as taught.

> Though one upholds the twelve divisions of scripture,
> If one cannot discern wrong from right, one remains a person of wrong views,
> Not one who has awakened to the dharma.
> If mind and mouth do not commit evil,
> And the body likewise has no transgressions,
> One who can maintain these three things
> Swiftly escapes the abyss of birth-and-death.

> Though one reads a thousand chapters,
> If one does not grasp the central meaning,
> It is not as good as a single phrase
> Sufficient to attain the Way.
> All who study should examine themselves carefully:
> Do not waste your efforts,
> Forever dwelling on the road of delusion.

Xuanzang (*Xuánzàng*, 玄奘), the Elder Master Chángnián, and Xiéshē and others obtained this *Sūtra of the Tathāgata's Great Discrimination of Wrong and Right: A Profound Secret Treasury*, in one fascicle, from within the Tathāgata's Seven-Jewel Cave.
