# 佛說申日經
## Sūtra of Śṛgāla, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0535

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the royal city of Rājagṛha, on Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain, together with twelve hundred and fifty great bhikṣus.

At that time, in the royal city there was a great and wealthy elder named Candrajaya, whose treasures were beyond measure. He had faith in the Buddha-Dharma, made offerings to the sangha, and was exceedingly diligent. The elder had a younger brother named Śṛgāla, who did not believe in the Buddha-Dharma but followed heterodox arts. Seeing his brother revere the true path, he was always filled with anger and jealousy. The teacher he served was named Pūraṇa Kāśyapa. Pūraṇa Kāśyapa and his five hundred followers said to Śṛgāla: "Your brother alone does not serve us. He does not know the true path but instead believes in the Buddha."

At that time, Candrajaya, knowing what the heterodox practitioners were discussing, said to Śṛgāla: "Tomorrow I wish to invite your teachers. Would they be willing to condescend to come to my house?"

Śṛgāla answered: "I shall go and inform them."

Śṛgāla then went to his teacher's place, knelt, and said: "My brother now wishes to humbly invite the great masters. Tomorrow, would you condescend to come to his house for a meal?"

The teacher said: "Excellent!" And he accepted the invitation.

Śṛgāla returned and told his brother: "They have accepted the invitation and will come tomorrow."

Then Candrajaya sent many servants and guests to dig a great pit at the entrance inside the gate, five feet deep, and filled it with foul blue mud. They thinly covered the top to make it appear level ground. He also had five hundred chairs made beforehand, each with only one leg, draped with cloth to conceal them. He prepared five hundred bowls of soured and curdled milk. With these preparations he awaited the heterodox practitioners.

When the appointed time came the next day, they arrived at the place of invitation. The nirgranthas had no proper manners; they walked in disorder, shoulder to shoulder, rushing ahead of one another. As soon as they reached the entrance, they all stumbled and fell into the mud pit, soiling their garments. All were enraged and wished to leave.

Candrajaya sent men to hold them back and explained: "This is an old mud pit. You did not know about it and fell in. It was not done deliberately. Please do not be angry. Come in and have the meal." He provided them with clean garments and invited them all in. Inside the gate, he first gave each a bowl of milk to hold. Then he told them to sit. As soon as they settled onto the chairs, the chairs all tipped over, and all five hundred practitioners fell to the ground. The bowls of soured milk splashed all over their faces and garments. The practitioners were even more enraged and said to Śṛgāla: "You have betrayed us! You conspired with your brother to humiliate us. This is outrageous!"

At that time, Śṛgāla was also very unhappy, and the practitioners left in anger. Candrajaya again detained them and spoke allegorical words: "Just now, when you fell into the mud, your bodies were covered in black filth, extremely ugly. That is like your path. Now the milk is pure white and bright in color. That is like the Buddha's path. You could also abandon the arts you follow and come to my teaching." The heterodox practitioners refused to speak further and left in anger.

The next day, Śṛgāla went to his teacher's place, knelt, and said: "My brother acted improperly and unlawfully. I truly did not know. I beg the teacher to have compassion and not blame me. Nevertheless, I shall now avenge yesterday's humiliation for the great teacher. My brother only used a mud pit and soured milk to humiliate you; my revenge shall go further. The teacher my brother serves, I shall invite him. I shall dig inside the gate to a depth of five zhang and fill it with fire, thinly covered on top. I shall prepare a banquet and lace all the food with poison. When the Buddha comes, if he does not fall into the fire pit, I shall serve the poisoned food to kill him. What does the teacher think?"

The teacher replied: "The Buddha, the World-Honored One, is a sage of vast wisdom and great power who sees past and future events. He knows in advance what others are plotting. Though you wish to do this, I fear it will not succeed."

Śṛgāla said: "I shall invite him first. If he accepts, he knows nothing. If he is truly an all-knowing sage, he will not accept my invitation."

The teacher said: "Excellent!"

Thereupon Śṛgāla left the royal city and went to Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain. He went before the Buddha, joined his palms in greeting, and said to the Buddha: "I wish to invite the Buddha and his disciples. May you condescend to come to my humble house for a modest meal."

The Buddha said: "Excellent!"

Śṛgāla then departed joyfully. He returned and told his teacher: "The Buddha has accepted the invitation. He knows nothing. We need only dig the pit and prepare the poisoned food."

At that time, all the heterodox practitioners were overjoyed, congratulating one another with great celebration.

Śṛgāla had a son named Candradhvaja, who in past lives had studied the Buddha's sūtras and the path. He was endowed with divine power and an aspiration for the great vehicle. He said to his father: "The Buddha is a great sage whose supernatural penetration is complete. His knowledge of the future is boundless and his vision of the past limitless. The thoughts and intentions of even the tiniest crawling creatures he knows in advance. Do not heed the words of dark and evil people, lest you receive grave retribution."

Candradhvaja again said to his father: "Even if the kalpa were ending and everything ablaze with fire, pervading all the three-thousand lands, and if one gathered all the poisons and piled them as high as Mount Sumeru, still one could not stir a single hair of the Buddha, let alone harm him with this small pit. What you are doing, Father, is like a firefly thinking its tiny light can outshine the sun and moon. It is like a small bird trying to crash through the Iron-Ring Mountains with its body, only shattering itself with no effect. What the heterodox practitioners do is just like this. You need not invite the Buddha."

His father did not believe him and proceeded as planned. When the appointed time came the next day, he sent a person to tell the Buddha: "The offerings are prepared. May you condescend to come."

Thereupon the Tathāgata rose and set forth on the road, releasing a great radiance that penetrated everywhere. At that time, from the ten directions came ten bodhisattvas, all at the stage of non-retrogression. Each bodhisattva was accompanied by a hundred million billion countless bodhisattvas, and they all flew to the gathering. Each brought precious flowers, sandalwood, and fine incense, and with various musical offerings honored the World-Honored One. They came to the royal city, wishing to behold the Tathāgata's transformative display.

At that time, in Śṛgāla's house a golden radiance appeared, bright as the sun. Candradhvaja said: "Now the Buddha has manifested a golden radiance in our house. The Buddha is on his way, and this is the response. You should go and stop him; he need not come further." His father still did not believe.

At that time, Śṛgāla's chief wife, named Yueyǔ, who was Candradhvaja's mother, seeing these wondrous signs, was filled with leaping joy. She immediately gave rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed right and true enlightenment.

Thereupon Candradhvaja said to his mother: "You should now command the five hundred ladies of the household to adorn themselves and go out to see the Tathāgata. Why? The World-Honored One is rare to encounter; only once in a hundred thousand billion kalpas does a Buddha appear." The five hundred ladies all received the instruction and joyfully attended the Buddha.

When the Buddha entered the city and his foot stepped upon the gate-threshold, the three-thousand lands all quaked greatly. All who were afflicted with illness were healed. The blind could see, the deaf could hear, the mute could speak, and the lame could walk. Those afflicted by poison found the poison rendered harmless. All the musical instruments sounded without being played. Gold, silver, and the seven treasures all produced sounds. The birds and beasts cried together in harmony. At that time, the spirits of the ten directions and all the devas, each with innumerable retinues, all followed the Buddha to Śṛgāla's house.

When the Buddha stepped upon the fire pit, it transformed into a bathing pool. Within it grew lotus flowers as large as chariot wheels, with a thousand petals and stems of the seven treasures. Their colors were wondrous, and the Buddha stepped upon them. The lotuses upon which the bodhisattvas stepped each produced five hundred petals, and those upon which the disciples stepped produced five hundred petals.

Śṛgāla, seeing the fire pit transformed in this way, was alarmed and terrified. He bowed with his head to the ground. When the Buddha entered his house and the bodhisattvas and disciples had all taken their seats, Śṛgāla repented and addressed the Buddha: "I have acted improperly and unlawfully. The food I prepared is all laced with poison. I beg to be allowed to prepare it anew. Please wait a moment."

The Buddha said: "Bring the poisoned food. I will eat it myself."

Śṛgāla was delighted and did as the Buddha instructed. He distributed the food to all. The Buddha accepted it, recited blessings, and gave thanks. The poisoned food was transformed into a hundred flavors, its fragrance reaching the ten directions. All who smelled the fragrance of this food were naturally satisfied and their bodies were at ease. All gave rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed and equal awakening. When the meal was finished, the five hundred ladies and Candradhvaja made obeisance to the Buddha and stood to one side. The elder Śṛgāla took a small stool and sat before the Buddha. He addressed the Buddha: "The World-Honored One's supernatural penetration includes the three kinds of perfect knowledge. Whatever I have done, I believe the Buddha already knew. Yet you did not warn me in advance, and allowed us to establish this unlawful scheme and commit these evil deeds. Did the Buddha not know what I was plotting?"

The Buddha told Śṛgāla: "Long ago, in the past, incalculable asaṃkhyeya kalpas ago, there was a Buddha named Dīpaṃkara Tathāgata, the Perfectly Enlightened One. His radiance was immeasurable, and he liberated all the negligent. Together with a multitude of bodhisattvas and great disciples beyond count, he traveled and entered a city. At that time, there was an elder's son named Veda. Just then Veda was leaving the city when he saw a brahmin girl, radiant and without equal, holding fine flowers. The elder's son offered five hundred silver coins and obtained five stalks of flowers from the flower-seller. He scattered them above the Buddha. The Buddha then gave him a prediction: 'After innumerable incalculable asaṃkhyeya kalpas, you shall become a Buddha named Śākyamuni. When you become a Buddha, there shall be an elder named Śṛgāla who will conspire with heterodox practitioners to plot against you with a fire pit and poisoned food, seeking to test you. Though he does this evil, through you he shall attain deliverance.'"

The Buddha told Śṛgāla: "The elder's son of that time is now myself. When I received the prediction from that Tathāgata, I attained the clear and definitive samādhi, and from then on I already knew your name. How much more so the plot you devised yesterday; how could I not have known? Know that the Buddhas' wisdom of skillful means uses causes and conditions to bring about awakening."

The elder Śṛgāla, hearing what the Buddha said, immediately attained the patience of the Dharma. He further exclaimed: "The Tathāgata's wisdom delivers all without exception. Now I clearly know that I shall not receive retribution for my sin. Why? Because in the distant past, Dīpaṃkara Tathāgata foretold my name. It was ordained that I would be transformed by the Buddha. Therefore I say I shall not receive retribution."

At that time, as the Buddha taught the Dharma in the royal city, countless thousands of elders and householders, and the five hundred heterodox practitioners, all gave rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed right and true enlightenment. The five hundred ladies immediately attained the stage of non-retrogression. The Moonlight Boy rose from his seat. After praising the Buddha, he addressed the Buddha: "If in a future life I attain buddhahood, may all the people of my land be free of evil minds and be pure in nature. In lands where the people are stubborn and defiled, in the turbid age of the five corruptions, I vow to appear among them and transform them."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Did you hear what the Moonlight Boy said?"

Ānanda replied: "Yes, I have heard."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "One thousand years after my parinirvāṇa, when the sūtra Dharma is about to be extinguished, the Moonlight Boy shall be born in the land of Qin as a holy ruler. He shall uphold my sūtra Dharma and cause the path to flourish. The land of Qin and all the border countries, Shanshan, Udyāna, Kucha, Kashgar, Ferghana, Khotan, and all the Qiang and barbarian peoples, shall all revere the Buddha and honor the Dharma, and all shall become bhikṣus. If any man or woman hears the Sūtra of Śṛgāla, all the evil transgressions they have committed in the past shall be entirely removed. Know that those whom the World-Honored One is destined to deliver are thus. If even those who have committed transgressions can attain deliverance, how much more so those who wholeheartedly study the buddha-path."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, all in the assembly were filled with joy, made obeisance, and departed.
