# Sūtra of the Buddha's Mother

## *Fómǔ Jīng* (佛母經)

Taishō 2919

Translator unknown. An apocryphal text.

At that time, the World-Honored One entered parinirvāṇa (*bān nièpán*, 般涅槃). Reclining between the twin śāla trees, he addressed the great assembly: "I am now about to enter parinirvāṇa and do not see Ānanda (*Ānán*, 阿難) or my Kāśyapa (*Jiāshè*, 迦葉). When they come, tell them: 'I and you shall not meet again.'" He then sent Upāli (*Yōupōlí*, 憂波梨) to ascend to the Trāyastriṃśa (*Dāolì*, 刀利) heaven to inform his mother, saying: "My entire body is in pain beyond description. May my mother come quickly to pay reverence to the Three Treasures."

At that time, during the middle of the night, the Buddha's mother had six kinds of evil dreams. First, she dreamed that a mountain of jewels collapsed. Second, she dreamed that the four seas dried up. Third, she dreamed that frost fell in the fifth month. Fourth, she dreamed that jeweled banners and pennants were cut down, and banner-flowers toppled. Fifth, she dreamed that four fires came to burn her body. Sixth, she dreamed that milk flowed spontaneously from both her breasts.

At that time, before the Buddha's mother had finished recounting her dreams, she saw Upāli arriving through the sky. She asked: "Holy one, from where do you come? Your countenance is haggard and your complexion is without luster. You look like someone stricken with grief."

Then Upāli, his voice choked and hoarse, was unable to speak for a long time. He said: "Buddha's Mother! Buddha's Mother! Our Tathāgata, the Great Teacher, last night at the hour of the rat, relinquished the great dharma-body and entered parinirvāṇa. He therefore sent me to come and inform his family."

At that time, when the Buddha's mother heard these words, she struck herself in anguish. Like Mount Sumeru (*Xūmí*, 須彌) collapsing, her entire body turned the color of a palāśa (*bōluóshē*, 波羅奢) flower. She fainted and fell to the ground. Two celestial maidens (*tiānnǚ*, 天女) sprinkled water on her, and after a long while she revived. Then the Buddha's mother, together with her retinue, reverently surrounding her, descended from the Trāyastriṃśa heaven to the twin śāla trees (*suōluó shuāngshù*, 娑羅雙樹). She saw the Tathāgata already laid in his coffin, with only his saṃghāṭī (*sēngqiélí*, 僧伽梨) robe folded beside the coffin, and his bowl and staff hanging upon the tree. Two lions had killed themselves by cutting their own throats. The ten great disciples were crying out to heaven in grief.

At that time, the Buddha's mother took up these articles and said: "These belonged to my son. When he was alive, he used these things to guide and teach sentient beings. Now these things have no master!"

Then the Buddha's mother circumambulated the coffin three times, stood to one side, and through her tears of grief said: "Compassionate son! You are my son and I am your mother. Now that you have entered parinirvāṇa, why did you not leave even half a verse of dharma?"

At that time, the Tathāgata, hearing his mother's call, caused the golden coffin to open of itself. He sat up upon a thousand-petaled lotus throne and spoke the dharma for his mother:

> "The world is suffering and emptiness; all conditioned things are impermanent.
> This is the dharma of arising and ceasing;
> When arising and ceasing have themselves ceased, quiescent extinction is bliss."

At that time, the Buddha's mother, hearing this dharma, gave rise to joy in her heart and returned to her own heaven. Before reaching her heavenly realm, she paused in midair, and her heart filled with compassion. Crying out in great sorrow, heaven and earth trembled, and her tears fell like rain. The hundred birds within the clouds all made sounds of weeping. All bonds of love must end in parting. How then could the Tathāgata's nirvāṇa be otherwise? Thus ends the Sūtra of the Buddha's Mother from the *Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra*.
