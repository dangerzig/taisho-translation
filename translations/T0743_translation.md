# 佛說忠心經
## Sūtra of the Devoted Mind, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0743

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time the bodhisattvas, the four assemblies of disciples, devas, dragons, ghosts, spirits, emperors, ministers, and common people gathered daily to hear the sūtras. To the Buddha's right was a bhikṣu named Maudgalyāyana, whose supernatural powers were wondrously penetrating and whose wisdom was like empty space. He transformed according to the occasion, wielding both expedient and true wisdom, universally delivering sentient beings as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. All the devas bowed their heads, and none did not revere him as a teacher.

The Buddha told Maudgalyāyana: "There is a great country eight thousand leagues from here, situated on the frontier. They have not beheld the supreme spiritual power of the Three Honored Ones, and rarely hear the wondrously pure teaching of the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Awakened One. They practice the inverted ways of the many deviant paths, taking them for truth. The king and his people serve five hundred brahmins, each possessing the five supernatural powers, with the strength to move mountains and halt rivers, to divide their bodies and manifest transformations. In their country there is a great mountain that blocks the people's road, and the entire country suffers because of it. The king, considering the mountain a hardship for the people, described the situation in full to the brahmins. The brahmins replied: 'We shall remove this hazard for the people. Your Majesty need not worry!' They then sat in a circle around the mountain, each concentrating the mind singly, and with the power of their meditative concentration, the mountain began to rise."

The Buddha told Maudgalyāyana: "Go to that country. Display the supernatural transformation of the path, and forever liberate the brahmins. Deliver their king and people, causing them to leave far behind the three evil paths and forever dwell in the hall of merit."

Maudgalyāyana received the teaching. Borne by the Buddha's spiritual power, he set out radiating light that eclipsed the sun's brightness. He hovered in empty space directly above the mountain peak, and the mountain would not move.

The brahmins were startled and said: "The mountain had already risen! Who has turned it back? The sun has also lost its light. There must be some reason for this!" Among them there was one of clear insight who, with concentrated mind, examined the minds of all the disciples to see if any were impure, causing the mountain not to move. He saw that all their minds were pure in the path, and the country's glorious jeweled radiance had not stained their minds. He then raised his head and looked up, and saw a śramaṇa directly above the mountain. The brahmins all said: "This is certainly the work of a disciple of Gautama." The brahmins called out: "The king ordered us to remove this hazard for the people! Are you suppressing it?"

Maudgalyāyana replied: "I am merely hovering in space. Who has suppressed your mountain?"

The brahmins exerted their full meditative power three times, trying to make the mountain move. The mountain sank three times and became level ground. The brahmins looked at one another and said: "Whoever has penetrating understanding and the deepest virtue of the path is our teacher." All arranged their robes and bowed their heads in respect: "We wish to become disciples. Show us the highest holiness."

Maudgalyāyana replied: "You wish to leave darkness and enter the light? Good! I have a supreme teacher, called the Unsurpassed, Perfectly Awakened One, Deva above Devas, the All-Knowing One. Follow me and go to the Buddha's dwelling."

The brahmins asked: "Is the Buddha's guidance and transformation different from what you, our teacher, offer?"

Maudgalyāyana said: "The Buddha can make Mount Sumeru into a mustard seed. I can only make a mustard seed into Mount Sumeru." When he had finished praising the Buddha's virtue, he said: "Follow behind me." Maudgalyāyana carried all the brahmins, and in the time it takes a strong man to flex and extend his arm, they were before the Buddha. He recounted the entire affair in detail and addressed the Buddha: "I have relied on the supernatural transformation of the Unsurpassed, Perfectly Awakened One, and have converted these brahmins. Their inner and outer selves are already purified, like new white cloth that can easily be dyed. May the World-Honored One wash away their remaining impurities and make them truly pure." The brahmins, seeing the Buddha, opened their hearts and rejoiced, and all became śramaṇas.

The Buddha said: "I shall teach you a sūtra. The opening words are good, the middle words are good, and the closing words are good. Within its words it profoundly teaches the path of liberation from the world, and the upright mind is its foundation. Listen to what I say, so that future generations may transmit and practice it." All the bhikṣus joined their palms and received the teaching.

The Buddha said: "Within the human body there are five thieves that drag a person into the evil paths. What are the five? First, form; second, feeling; third, perception; fourth, formations; fifth, consciousness. These five are what people constantly think upon."

The Buddha said: "A person is constantly deceived by the eyes, deceived by the ears, deceived by the nose, deceived by the mouth, and deceived by the body. The eyes can only see but cannot hear. The ears can only hear but cannot see. The nose can only perceive scents but cannot perceive tastes. The mouth can only perceive tastes but cannot perceive scents. The body can only perceive cold and warmth but cannot perceive tastes. These five all belong to the mind; the mind is their root."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Those who wish to seek the path should set their minds aright. Because of delusion, people follow the twelve links of dependent arising, and thus there is birth and death. What are the twelve links? First, ignorance; second, formations; third, consciousness; fourth, name-and-form; fifth, the six sense-entries; sixth, contact; seventh, feeling; eighth, craving; ninth, grasping; tenth, becoming; eleventh, birth; twelfth, death. Those who practice good are reborn as humans. Those who practice evil enter the hells, the realm of hungry ghosts, the realm of animals, and the realm of ghosts and spirits. The Buddha sat and reflected that because of people's delusion there is birth and death.

"What is delusion? From delusion in the past one comes, and in the present life one is again deluded. The mind does not understand; the eyes do not open. One does not know where one will go at death. Seeing the Buddha, one does not ask questions. Seeing the sūtras, one does not read them. Seeing a śramaṇa, one does not attend upon them. One does not believe in the path and its virtue. Seeing one's parents, one does not show respect. One does not reflect on the suffering of the world. One does not know the severity of the punishment in the hells. This is called delusion, and because of it birth and death do not cease. A person's death is like the space of a single breath; nothing is more fragile than a human life. Within the human body there are three things that depart when the body dies: consciousness departs, the mind departs, and the will departs. These three constantly pursue one another. For those who practiced evil, they enter the hells, the realm of hungry ghosts, the realm of animals, and the realm of ghosts and spirits. For those who practiced good, the three also pursue one another, being born either in the heavens or among humans. Those who fall into these five paths of existence all do so because the mind was not set aright."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "All of you should set your eyes aright, set your ears aright, set your noses aright, set your mouths aright, set your bodies aright, set your minds aright. Then the body will be returned to the earth, but the spirit will never again fall into the hells, the realm of animals, or the realm of ghosts and spirits. Look at families with wayward sons who are taken by the authorities: all of this is because the mind was not set aright. Within the human body there are a hundred elements, just as a cart has a hundred components. People are mostly greedy and quick to anger. Not reflecting on the affairs within the body, at death they enter the hells, and afterward regret is of no use. I abandoned my kingdom and relinquished the position of a wheel-turning king, troubled by the suffering of birth and death, wishing to deliver the people of the world and enable them to attain the unconditioned path. Those of the first level of diligence immediately attain arhatship. Those of the second level of diligence attain the state of the non-returner. Those of the third level of diligence attain the state of the once-returner. Those of the fourth level of diligence attain the state of the stream-enterer. Even those who cannot practice the highest diligence should uphold the five precepts: no killing, no stealing, no sexual misconduct, no divisive speech, and no drinking wine."

The Buddha said: "A person should, in sitting and rising, constantly contemplate four things. What are the four? First, observe one's own body and observe others' bodies. Second, observe one's own feelings and observe others' feelings. Third, observe one's own mind and observe others' minds. Fourth, observe dharmas within oneself and observe dharmas within others. When inner desire again arises, one should set the mind aright and look at the body: hunger is extreme, satiety is extreme, walking is extreme, standing is extreme, sitting is extreme, cold is extreme, heat is extreme. When drowsiness comes, one should startle oneself awake and sit with a concentrated mind. If the mind is not set aright while sitting, one should stand up. If the mind is not set aright while standing, one should practice walking meditation. If the mind still wavers, one should correct it. It is like a king who leads his troops into battle: the brave are in the front. Having been in the front, if one then retreats, one is shamed before those behind.

"Bhikṣus! Having left the household life and abandoned wife and children, having shaved your beards and hair and become śramaṇas: though you toil for one lifetime, afterward you will be forever liberated. Those who have already attained the path rejoice inwardly alone. They regard a wife as a sister and regard children as acquaintances, without any mind of greed or attachment. You should always extend compassion to the people and devas of the ten directions, to those in the hells, in the realm of hungry ghosts, in the realm of animals, and to all flying and crawling creatures, enabling all of them to become wealthy, honored, peaceful, and delivered, attaining the unconditioned path. When you see insects and beasts, you should feel compassion and pity for them, knowing that in this birth they will not again be deluded. Those who can have such an intention should always remember the teacher's kindness and serve the Buddha as a person remembers their parents; as a prisoner on death row in prison whom a worthy person goes and petitions to release. The wise always remember the worthy person's kindness. A bhikṣu who has already attained the path should think of the Buddha in this way, and should think of the sūtras as a person thinks of food and drink.

"All bhikṣus should attend upon each other in turn, as a younger brother attends upon an elder. Among you, if any are confused, they should ask the wise. By teaching one another in turn, consulting the wise is like having a lamp in the darkness. You must not secretly plot evil or engage in disputes. Seeing gold and silver, regard them as dirt. You must not falsely incriminate people or bring them under the law, and your words must not falsely wound people's hearts. What you have not heard, do not say you heard; what you have not seen, do not say you saw. When walking the path, keep your head lowered and watch the ground, so as not to step on and kill insects. You must not let the eyes covet others' wives or make gestures toward others' wives. Sit and reflect upon yourself, and remove the mind of greed and attachment. Only then can you practice the path."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Those who wish to seek the path should set their minds aright. In a quiet place, sit and observe your own breathing. Know when the breath is short and when it is long. If a long breath is not returned, the body is strained. If you hold the breath without breathing, the body is also strained. Distinguish and reflect: who made this body? The mind should look inward and should also observe outward. Reflect upon yourself with joy: your mind is different from that of ordinary people. At such a time you would not trade for all the treasures in the world. As the mind gradually wishes to follow the right path, if the will stirs slightly, immediately return to guarding the mind, and the mind will return to its place. It is like a person who has a mirror: if the mirror is not bright, one cannot see one's form. Polish away the grime, and one can see one's form. When a person has removed greed, anger, and delusion, it is like polishing a mirror. Set your mind aright and reflect: nothing in the world is solid; nothing is permanent."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Hold the mind as you would a four-cornered stone. A stone placed in the courtyard: the rain that falls cannot destroy it, the sun that beats upon it cannot melt it, the wind that blows upon it cannot move it. Hold the mind as you would a stone."

The Buddha said: "The minds of people in the world are like grass and wood in flowing water. Each drifts along on its own, none looking back at the others. Those in front do not look back at those behind, and those behind do not look ahead at those in front. The grass and wood drift along, each as before. People's minds are also like this: one thought comes, one thought goes, just like grass and wood, without looking back or ahead. In the heavens above and the earth below there is nothing more to delight in. Dwelling temporarily between heaven and earth, one abandons the body and dies no more. When the path is accomplished, one then knows the teacher's kindness. Seeing the teacher, one immediately attends upon him. When not seeing the teacher, one reflects on his instructions, like a filial son remembering his parents. When the mind is settled and one-pointed, one then extends compassion to all the people and devas of the world, and to all flying and crawling creatures. Sitting alone, one rejoices that one has freed the self from the world and from the five paths: first, the path of the devas; second, the path of humans; third, the path of animals; fourth, the path of hungry ghosts; fifth, the path of the hells. Those who have already attained the path of the arhat can then fly through the air and manifest transformations. From within the body they can produce water and fire. They can enter without gaps and emerge without openings. If they wish to leave the world and take the unconditioned way, they are able to do so."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The path must not go unlearned; the sūtras must not go unread." When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the five hundred śramaṇas all attained the path of the arhat.

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "These śramaṇas heard the sūtra and their understanding was resolved. How swift it was!"

The Buddha said: "These bhikṣus, in the time of the past Buddha Kāśyapa, received and recited this sūtra. In the interval when there was no Buddha, following the world's causes and conditions, they abandoned it and did not hear it again. Now that I have taught it, they heard it and immediately attained the path."

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the bodhisattvas, the four assemblies of disciples, emperors, common people, devas, dragons, ghosts, and spirits all rejoiced without exception and bowed to the Buddha.
