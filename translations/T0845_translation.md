# 佛說尊那經
## Sūtra of Cunda, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0845

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmabhadra (法賢)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the World-Honored One was in the land of Kauśāmbī, in the Ghoṣila Grove. At that time there was a great elder named Cunda, who came to the place of the Buddha. Having arrived, he paid his respects, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, stood to one side, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Can inexhaustible merit be attained? We pray the World-Honored One will explain."

At that time the World-Honored One told the great elder Cunda: "Excellent, excellent! You are able to ask the Tathāgata about inexhaustible merit. Listen carefully, and I shall explain it for you. Cunda! This inexhaustible merit is profound, subtle, and wondrous, with renown spread far and wide. If a person devotes the mind to it, they attain great reward. This merit is of seven kinds, with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable. What are the seven?

"Cunda! If good men and good women give rise to great faith and donate groves and gardens with ponds, for the use of monks from the four directions for walking meditation and dwelling. Cunda! This is the first inexhaustible merit, with great reward and great praise, with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If good men and good women give rise to great faith and within those groves establish monasteries for the monks to dwell in. Cunda! This is the second inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If good men and good women give rise to great faith and within those monasteries donate furnishings for sitting and lying, such as beds, chairs, felt blankets, quilts, coverlets, and various articles for use. Cunda! This is the third inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If good men and good women give rise to great faith and within those monasteries donate provisions and grain to support the monks. Cunda! This is the fourth inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If good men and good women give rise to great faith and constantly give to traveling monks whatever they need. Cunda! This is the fifth inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If good men and good women give rise to great faith and give to those who are sick and suffering, and also give to those who tend the sick. Cunda! This is the sixth inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If good men and good women give rise to great faith, and when they see frost and severe cold, wind and rain, they prepare various food and drink, medicines, bedding, clothing, and shoes, and provide and make offerings to the monks. The monks, thus protected from wind and rain, not suffering from the cold, and sustained by these offerings, attain great ease and comfort. Cunda! This is the seventh inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Cunda! As for these seven kinds of inexhaustible merit, if good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and fulfill this dharma, the inexhaustible merit they obtain is inconceivable and immeasurable. The merit is extremely great, and is called a great accumulation of merit. Cunda! It is like the five great rivers that constantly flow with water, namely: the Gaṅgā, the Yamunā, the Sarayū, the Airāvatī, and the Mahī. The water of these rivers is immeasurable. If one were to fill vessels with this river water, numbering hundreds, thousands, or hundreds of billions, the water would be exceedingly great and would be called an accumulation of water. Inexhaustible merit is just like this!

"Cunda! When these seven kinds of inexhaustible merit are all fulfilled, the merit obtained by that good man or good woman is inconceivable and immeasurable, with great renown and great reward. The merit is extremely great, and is called a great accumulation of merit."

At that time the great elder Cunda again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If a person fulfills this dharma of inexhaustible merit, is it true and not false that they obtain great reward, great praise, with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast?"

The Buddha told the great elder Cunda: "As you have said, one truly obtains this inexhaustible merit. The reward is thus!"

The Buddha said: "Cunda! There are seven further kinds of inexhaustible merit, with great reward, great praise, with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable. What are the seven?

"Cunda! If there are good men and good women of pure faith who hear that the Tathāgata, or that the assembly of śrāvakas, is staying in a certain settlement or city, walking in meditation or dwelling there, and upon hearing this give rise to great joy, generate a great wholesome intention, and arouse the aspiration for bodhi: Cunda! This is the first inexhaustible merit, with great reward, great praise, with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If there are good men and good women of pure faith who hear that the Tathāgata and the assembly of śrāvakas are going to a settlement or proceeding to a city, and upon hearing this give rise to great joy, generate a great wholesome intention, and arouse the aspiration for bodhi: Cunda! This is the second inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women of pure faith are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If there are good men and good women of pure faith who hear that the Tathāgata or the assembly of śrāvakas is on the road, and upon hearing this give rise to great joy, generate a great wholesome intention, and arouse the aspiration for bodhi: Cunda! This is the third inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If there are good men and good women of pure faith who hear that the Tathāgata or the assembly of śrāvakas has arrived at a settlement or city, and upon hearing this give rise to great joy, generate a great wholesome intention, and arouse the aspiration for bodhi: Cunda! This is the fourth inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If there are good men and good women of pure faith who hear that the Tathāgata and the assembly of śrāvakas is in a settlement or city, and they personally go to draw near, bow in homage, and gaze upon them, and having gazed upon them give rise to great joy, generate a great wholesome intention, and arouse the aspiration for bodhi: Cunda! This is the fifth inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If there are good men and good women of pure faith who go to the place of the Buddha or to the place of the śrāvakas, bow in homage, gaze upon them, and listen to the wondrous Dharma, and having heard the Dharma give rise to great joy, generate a great wholesome intention, and arouse the aspiration for bodhi: Cunda! This is the sixth inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. If good men and good women are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Furthermore, Cunda! If there are good men and good women of pure faith who, at the place of the Tathāgata or the śrāvakas, having heard the Dharma, generate a great wholesome intention and take refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha, and having taken the three refuges, receive the Tathāgata's pure precepts: Cunda! This is the seventh inexhaustible merit, with great reward, and likewise with renown spread everywhere, profound and vast. Cunda! If good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, such persons, in the four deportments, will constantly increase their inexhaustible merit. The merit so increased is inconceivable and immeasurable.

"Cunda! As for these seven kinds of inexhaustible merit, if good men and good women are able to fulfill this dharma, the merit they obtain is immeasurable. Cunda! These seven kinds of inexhaustible merit, if good men and good women give rise to the most sincere mind and are able to fulfill this dharma, the inexhaustible merit they obtain is immeasurable. Such merit, renown, praise, and great reward are exceedingly great, and are called a great accumulation of merit.

"Furthermore, Cunda! It is like the five great rivers that constantly flow with water, namely: the Gaṅgā, the Yamunā, the Sarayū, the Airāvatī, and the Mahī. The water of these rivers is immeasurable. If one were to fill vessels with this river water, numbering hundreds, thousands, or hundreds of billions, the water would be exceedingly great and would be called an accumulation of water. Inexhaustible merit is just like this!

"Cunda! When these seven kinds of inexhaustible merit are all fulfilled, the merit obtained by that good man or good woman is inconceivable and immeasurable, with great renown and great reward. The merit is extremely great, and is called a great accumulation of merit."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Cultivating the practice of dhyāna in purity,
> Cultivating only among the holy traces;
> For gathering jewels, nothing surpasses the sea,
> As the river delivers sentient beings.
> The water of the rivers is boundless,
> And all the streams return again to their source;
> Giving generously brings abundant merit,
> As the rivers constantly flow into the sea."

At that time the great elder Cunda, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully made obeisance, believed, received it, and practiced accordingly.
