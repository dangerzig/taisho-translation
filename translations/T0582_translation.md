# 佛說孫多耶致經
## Sūtra of Sundara-Yati, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0582

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, staying at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada. A great crowd of disciples was gathered, and the Buddha was teaching the sūtra for them.

At that time there was a brahmin named Sundara-Yati. From a distance he saw the Buddha, stopped, leaned on his staff, stuck out his tongue, and thought: "I bathe three times a day, I eat only fruit and drink only water, and I accept nothing from others. My practice surpasses the practice of these śramaṇas. These fellows enjoy fine food, good clothes, and warm bedding. They feign virtue but are not genuine. They are no match for my way."

The Buddha knew what the brahmin was thinking and said to the śramaṇas: "There are twenty-one evils for which a person, despite their practice, does not deserve fine food or good clothes. First, the mind set on lust. Second, the mind set on anger. Third, the mind set on delusion. Fourth, the mind set on sleepiness. Fifth, the mind set on stubborn obstinacy, claiming there are no arhats. Sixth, the mind set on arrogance. Seventh, the mind set on pride. Eighth, the mind set on jealousy. Ninth, the mind set on miserliness and greed. Tenth, the mind set on cruelty, without compassion. Eleventh, the mind set on hypocrisy, with inner and outer in contradiction. Twelfth, the mind set on shamelessness, without inner remorse. Thirteenth, the mind set on impure conduct, without self-reproach. Fourteenth, the mind set on seeing others' faults without seeing one's own. Fifteenth, the mind set on lust, whose speech does not praise chastity. Sixteenth, the mind set on gossip, whose slander produces twofold evil. Seventeenth, the mind set on stirring up quarrels without settling disputes. Eighteenth, the mind set on impropriety, self-satisfied and lacking humility. Nineteenth, the mind set on persisting in error, not accepting the counsel of the wise. Twentieth, the mind set on unfiliality, destroying compassion and delighting in rebellion. Twenty-first, the mind delighting in heterodox paths, worshipping demons and sorcery, exhausting the family's wealth, making relatives destitute, not awakening to impermanence, not taking refuge in the Three Treasures, enjoying fine food, good clothes, indulgence, and luxury. With these twenty-one evils defiling and disordering the mind, it is like clean cloth that has been soiled and dyed another color: there is not the slightest benefit. Harboring these many evils, after death one enters Naraka, the realm of hungry ghosts, or the animal realm, and the suffering is beyond measure.

"The śramaṇa who beholds the Buddha's luminous precepts abandons the old and makes a new beginning, refining the passions and extinguishing desire, pure within and without. It is like clean cloth: when dyed it takes color beautifully, admired by all. When the śramaṇa beholds this light and practices with lofty conduct and sufficient wisdom, all under heaven offer him clothing, food, and dwelling, and he receives them without fault, and the merit of the giver is great. Advancing in meditation and concentration, one attains the fruits of stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, and arhatship, as people have heard. Even the śramaṇa who has not yet attained the Way, if he understands the meaning of the Buddha's sūtras, keeps his mind upright and proper, is filial and obedient to the sūtra teachings, extends great compassion universally, nourishes all beings, forgives himself in order to bring peace to others, regards enemies as he would his own children, and when a woman approaches, treats her as a sister; if upon seeing poverty and hardship he thinks of Naraka, the realm of hungry ghosts, and the animal realm, and wishes for all beings to be at peace in body and joyful in mind, to encounter the Three Treasures, have their defilements removed and their darkness extinguished, their minds purified and their sight made clear, returning to the original emptiness, in eternal peace without end: if one's aspiration and practice are like this, then fine food and good clothes are without fault from beginning to end. The fundamental nature of all beings is tainted by lust, anger, delusion, and jealousy; they are the most turbid among the turbid, the most darkened among the darkened. When the śramaṇa's mind is opened and he receives the light, all dullness and defilement become completely still. Though he dwells in a defiled world, he is like a lotus that grows in the mud, yet the mud cannot stain the flower. He receives offerings without fault. But one who harbors the three poisons and the ten evils without removing them, who lets the mind follow desire, who flatters and deceives in pursuit of gain, who does not uphold the Buddha's precepts: such a one would be better off swallowing hot iron and drinking molten copper. That death lasts but a moment. But the impure one who receives offerings: after death he enters the great mountain prison, where he drinks copper and eats charcoal, and the years are beyond counting."

The brahmin came forward and said to the Buddha: "I wish to go and bathe. There is a stream called Suśīrṣa. Those who bathe in it have the impurities of their minds washed away by the flowing water. I have heard my father's teaching: I bathe three times a day."

The Buddha said to the brahmin: "There are many streams in this land. Can people who bathe in them escape suffering? Though a thousand bathings may remove the body's grime, what of the mind's defilement? My śramaṇas sever craving and contemplate emptiness. They have no desire for the three realms. The defilements of the mind are extinguished and the pure Way is attained. From what teacher did you receive the method of bathing in water? Does it remove the mind's defilement?"

He replied: "My father said so."

The Buddha said: "And did your father attain the Way?"

He replied: "No."

The Buddha said: "My Way arises from the mind. When the mind is upright and the will is pure, then one attains the Way."

The brahmin knelt and said: "Earlier I leaned on my staff and stuck out my tongue; it was merely madness and folly. Now, having heard the Buddha's sūtra, my mind has first awakened. It is like having a lamp in the darkness; for the first time I have eyes. I beg to become a śramaṇa. May the Buddha have compassion and accept me!"

The Buddha said: "If you have family, you should inform them."

He replied: "I have no family."

The Buddha said: "Come, śramaṇa."

He immediately became a śramaṇa, sat beneath a tree, and contemplated the twenty-one matters. He removed all thoughts of desire, and suddenly, free of all conceptions, he attained the Way of arhatship. He hastened to the Buddha's presence, touched his head to the Buddha's feet, and said: "Now the blind have gained sight, and the mad have been cured."

The Buddha said: "Is this Way good? Or was your former way good?"

He replied: "All other ways are crooked. Only the Buddha's Way is straight."

The Buddha told the śramaṇas: "Thoroughly contemplate the twenty-one matters, and the Way may be attained."

The śramaṇas, hearing this sūtra, were all filled with great joy. They bowed and withdrew.
