# 常曉和尚請來目錄
## Catalog of Items Requested and Brought Back by Preceptor Jōgyō

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2163

Japanese, compiled by Jōgyō (常曉).

---

### Memorial to the Throne

The śramaṇa Jōgyō, who entered Tang China to study the Dharma, respectfully states: Jōgyō departed in the fifth month of the third year of Jōwa, bearing orders for long-term study abroad, with a distant destination of ten thousand li. That year, storms drove the ship back. In the fourth year, the sea crossing again did not succeed. In the fifth year, in the sixth month, we set out again. In the eighth month of the same year, we arrived at the Guangling hostel in Huainan and were lodged there. In early winter, the envoys entered the capital. Jōgyō was not permitted to accompany the envoys to the capital and remained at the hostel, passing many days in vain. By year's end, no imperial orders had come. Thereupon I traveled throughout the prefecture, seeking a teacher to study under. Fortunately, I encountered Wen Can, the abhiṣeka ācārya of Qiling Monastery, whose Dharma-name was Wen Can. I also met Yuanzhao, the presiding lecturer at Hualin Monastery, a great worthy of the Three Teachings. The master Wen Can was a disciple of the Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra and also the Dharma-successor of the ācārya Huiying. He was skilled in sūtras and vinaya, deeply versed in the esoteric treasury, a pillar of the Dharma, and one to whom the nation looked. In the twelfth month of the same year, I petitioned the military commissioner for assignment, and was assigned to reside at Qiling Monastery. I took Master Wen Can as my principal teacher and began studying Dharma ceremonies, while also visiting Hualin Monastery to inquire about doctrinal matters with the presiding lecturer Yuanzhao and to obtain written texts. The great master valued the continued transmission of the Buddha-dharma and lamented the possibility of saving sentient beings. He conferred upon me the great Dharma of the Vajra and granted me the position of ācārya. Whether such knowledge reached me through prostrations, I cannot say. Whether I obtained what cannot be obtained through touching his feet, I do not know. Fortunately, relying on the great favor of the nation and the compassion of the great master, I studied the Vajra Ocean Yoga and practiced the secret methods of the Great Generalissimo. This Dharma is the very heart of the Tathāgata, the parent of all sentient beings, the moat around the nation's castle, and the sinews of a person's body. As for the Great Generalissimo, within the capital it was not transmitted beyond the ten personally attending monks, and in the provinces it was not shared outside the military commissioner's residence, in order to express its conditions and inconceivable spiritual efficacy. Although images of various buddhas, bodhisattvas, vajra deities, and celestial beings had previously been brought to our country, this particular image had never been requested. Now, the maṇḍala of the Great Generalissimo and all his retinue, together with the various sacred images and required texts, have been requested and brought back. Although the waves were fierce and the Silla pirates were in revolt, we passed through those perils and arrived safely in the sacred land. This was accomplished through sacred power alone. I humbly submit that His Majesty the Emperor possesses virtue as vast as heaven, and the Sun of the Buddha turns on high. As a father to the people and a transformation of the Buddha, he has compassion for all sentient beings and wets his feet for them, upholding the Buddha's entrustment while governing from the throne. In response to His Majesty's nurturing benevolence, these auspicious sacred images and teachings have come of their own accord from afar, as if matching a tally. Who but a sage could fathom this? Jōgyō had originally intended to spend thirty years traveling throughout Chinese lands seeking the Buddha-dharma and returning to serve and protect the nation. However, the Tang court did not permit me to remain, and I had to return with the envoys. On the one hand, I am delighted that this Dharma-teaching has come to life and been brought back. I cannot contain my mixed feelings of joy and trepidation. I humbly submit this through Vice-Commissioner Fujiwara no Ason Sadatoshi, presenting this memorial for the imperial ear, together with one copy of the catalog of Dharma-teachings and other items brought back, which I respectfully submit. I beg pardon for so lightly troubling the august dignity, and am filled with trembling awe. Jōgyō states this with utmost sincerity and trepidation.

Second day of the ninth month, sixth year of Jōwa.

Jōgyō, śramaṇa who entered Tang China to study the Dharma, holding the rank of Dentō Daihōshi.

The śramaṇa Jōgyō, who entered Tang China to study the Dharma, respectfully states:

### Newly Requested Dharma-Teachings and Buddha-Images, Ritual Implements, and Related Items

The above six works were requested and brought back at the request of the study-group heads of the Sanlun school.

In general, what the Tathāgata taught is called a sūtra; what bodhisattvas taught is called a treatise; and what human masters composed is called a commentary, subcommentary, biography, or record. Why is this so? Because the Buddha's preaching of the sūtras is difficult for those of dull faculties to understand, bodhisattvas composed treatises to explain them. For those of the dullest faculties who still cannot grasp the essential meaning, human masters then composed commentaries and subcommentaries to elaborate in detail the broad meaning of sūtras and treatises. Among commentary masters there are various kinds, differing only in whether they are orthodox or heterodox. The present commentaries explain the sūtras and treatises with the most subtle refinement and resolve doctrinal principles with the utmost precision. Though their names are known in this country, in truth they have not yet been transmitted here. These are matters that have perplexed the various students. Now, the *Hundred Treatise* is a gateway to the truth. Previously, the commentary of Jiaxiang Monastery was used to explain its meaning, but it has some deficiencies. Now, with the commentary of Master Yuankang, we can settle the doctrinal principles. Furthermore, the two schools of emptiness and existence debate the principle of true nature, taking the *Śūraṅgama Sūtra* as their fundamental model. Although the sūtra text had previously been brought here, no commentary on it existed. Without a commentary, doctrinal questions are difficult to resolve. Now, with this commentary, we can adjudicate the doctrinal disputes between the two schools of emptiness and existence. If one wing is lacking, how can one fly through empty space? How much more so when the profound principle of the One Vehicle is at stake and meaning diverges from the text. Without the aid of commentarial records, the subtle and minute cannot be revealed. Though the labor of a loaded cart, with this we hope to supplement the sacred canon.

The above one work was sought and brought back at the request of the study-group heads of the Vinaya school.

The vinaya karmavācanā is the hands and feet of all practices, the lifespan of the Buddha-dharma. Although the standards and rules of the Vinaya school had mostly been brought here before, some remain incomplete. Is this text not the very one needed? Therefore, with this, we shall supplement those gaps in the śīla.

The above one work was requested and brought back at the request of the Dharma-transmitting ācāryas of the Mantra school.

The *Treatise on the Mahāyāna* horizontally explicates the exoteric teachings and vertically encompasses the esoteric treasury. Although the root treatise had previously been brought here, its meaning has not yet been settled. Now, with this commentary, we can verify the meaning of the Great Vehicle.

The *Vimalakīrti Sūtra* penetrates the subtle and exhausts all transformations; its wondrous excellence is renowned. The authors of commentaries are various, and their doctrinal discussions differ; their reasoning is loose and obstructed. It was not until Liquan of the Kaiyuan era that the great principle was thoroughly clarified, eliminating the confused overgrowth and revealing the true ultimate. Now, in examining the genuine classics of the Great Tang dynasty, recent generations of scholars studying texts and learning doctrine all take these commentaries as their guide. Therefore, every monastery that lectures on the *Vimalakīrti* to transform laypeople uses Master Liquan's commentary to guide both monastics and laity. All say: "Although other commentary masters' annotations exist, wisdom falls short. But upon studying this text, the Dharma-mirror becomes clearer and the wisdom-lamp shines brighter."

The above commentaries explain doctrine with wondrous subtlety, practiced throughout India. These masters' commentaries have recently flourished. All regard other masters' works as secondary and these commentaries as authoritative. Now, the advancement of Buddhism depends on those who can expound it; the old is to be sought and the new is to be transmitted faithfully. While the sūtra texts themselves are not to be altered, doctrinal understanding is transmitted afresh from generation to generation.

The yoga and abhiṣeka ritual manuals and other materials received in the course of study are the same as those previously brought over, with no differences, and so are not listed separately here.

The above exoteric and esoteric Dharma-teachings, whether fully mastered or not yet mastered, are not few in number. They are presented here only in terms of what is essential for the present age. As for the esoteric school, it is broadly consistent with what has already been transmitted and presents no fundamental differences.

The sands of the Ganges number as many as the Tathāgatas, who have as many lands, and each bears a different name according to his original practice. Only a buddha together with a buddha can fully fathom this. Now, the Thousand-Radiance King Buddha is the original teacher of Avalokiteśvara. This is clearly established. Yet this image had never been seen before, and so it has been requested and brought back as described.

The function of Great Compassion is to manifest in ten thousand forms, contemplating sentient beings and removing suffering to bestow joy. Therefore images are displayed to cause beings to give rise to faith. Now, observing the people of Tang China, they all regard this as a cause for eliminating calamity, and the entire realm takes it as a condition for generating merit. This image has not yet circulated in our country, and so it has been requested and brought back as described.

The Great Generalissimo is the Tathāgata's wrathful command wheel. The Buddha acts as savior and protector for all sentient beings, manifesting the form of the Great Generalissimo. When evil beings see this form, their heads split into seven pieces. When the wicked hear his name, the demonic path is scattered like dust. The original sūtra states: "The Buddha told Ānanda: 'If any king or great minister reverently prostrates and recites the dhāraṇī, within that person's territory there shall be no evil bandits, fear, calamity, epidemic, drought, or frost. The king's authority and virtue shall increase, and all the people shall dwell in peace.' And further: 'If any city, town, or village upholds this sūtra, none shall fail to receive its benefit.' And further: 'If a nation is beset by disaster and the rains are not in season, place this Great Generalissimo dhāraṇī above the four city gates, and immediately wind and rain will come in proper season, bringing stability to the land. Hostile neighbors in the four directions will not harbor rebellious intentions.'" If practiced according to the method, the nation will long endure in peace. The marvelous efficacy, whereby even a woman can surely obtain the ten benefits, is inconceivable. Such is the power of the Great Generalissimo. Now, observing the Tang court, all rely upon this as the treasure for governing the nation and the essential means for defeating enemies. Therefore, the Bodhisattva Dhyāna-sovereign King praised in verse:

> I have heard the name of the Generalissimo,
> Who rescues the world from suffering and peril.
> Among all the great spirits,
> He is marvelously unique, without compare.
> With various transformation bodies,
> He removes the suffering of sentient beings.
> Now I have heard of his deeds of old:
> His treasures are inconceivable.

This Dharma has not yet widely circulated in our court. Therefore, I have requested and brought back the essential ritual manuals and images in summary form. For a Dharma that extinguishes calamity and attracts blessings beyond compare, who would not take refuge in it?

Kumbhīra Boy is a manifestation of Śākyamuni. The sūtra states: "When the Buddha was in the Garden of Delight, teaching the Dharma for sentient beings, the Māra Pāpīyas of the non-Buddhist paths raised various evil obstacles, causing sentient beings to suffer great affliction. At that time, the Tathāgata secretly transformed his own body into Kumbhīra Boy, subduing the non-Buddhist paths and all māras, and benefiting sentient beings in the evil age." This Dharma is especially a treasure for stabilizing the nation and benefiting beings. Its Dharma-power is marvelously inconceivable. This image and its ritual manuals are kept secret by mantra-holders. They had never been seen in our country. They have been requested and brought back as described.

The Six Boys: the sūtra states that they are all transformation bodies of buddhas of the past, who manifest in order to rescue and protect all sentient beings. "If in any land this practice is performed, they shall immediately appear in person to protect the nation and benefit the people." Now, observing the people of the Tang dynasty, many cultivate this practice, and its power is profound. These sacred images had not yet circulated in our country, and so they have been requested and brought back. The ritual procedures for their practice are also quite concise.

This deity is a transformation body of the Tathāgata. In the Qin dynasty, the Tripiṭaka Master Kumārajīva, traveling through the five regions of India, gained the response of this deity, who accompanied him back. Now, in Tang China, all revere this deity as a guardian power for protecting the nation. If a person is ill, upon invoking the name, the illness is immediately removed. If a person is poor, according to their vow, they will surely become wealthy. Miraculous efficacy is clearly manifest, and there is no one who does not take refuge. The Buddha's transformative influence flowed from west to east, benefiting beings in accordance with the times. Is this not a manifestation of the Tathāgata's transformative power?

The Three Luminaries are the sun, moon, and five planets, called the Seven Luminaries. The four directional clusters of seven stars together make twenty-eight lunar mansions. The people of this country have only heard their names and seen their light, but do not know their forms and measures. Now, the forms and images of these luminaries and mansions are extremely secret and hidden, yet truly possess spiritual efficacy. Even in Tang China they are not widely transmitted, let alone in our land of the rising sun, where they have never been seen. The onmyōji and others have merely speculated and created images based on conjecture, transmitting them for divinatory use, but these do not correspond to the originals. The images now requested each have authentic models.

Bodhi King is the mountain deity of the Great Tang. At Lou Mountain in Runzhou, there was a bhikṣu who cultivated meditation practice and personally received a response from this deity, who manifested his form, displayed his spiritual power, took the precepts, and cultivated the Way, and so was named Bodhi King. He responds to people's prayers and grants their wishes with his protective blessings. He has thus become a national guardian to whom the people turn. Therefore, images have been made of him everywhere, and people everywhere revere him. I have personally witnessed these things, and they are worthy of emulation.

During the Tang dynasty, the Tripiṭaka Master Xuanzang traveled far through the five regions of India and gained the response of this deity. This is a transformation body of Vaiśravaṇa, the Heavenly King of the North. Now, the people of the Tang empire all revere this deity for rescuing from calamity and producing benefit. His efficacy is immediately manifest, and there is no one who does not practice accordingly. In both monasteries and private homes, this deity is present everywhere. I have personally seen his miraculous efficacy, which is truly inconceivable. The details are as recorded in the written account. Requested and brought back as described.

This image has manifested in the Tang dynasty, and its spiritual efficacy is profound. The people all take refuge in it. For extinguishing calamity and increasing merit, nothing surpasses this. The details are as recorded in the written account. Requested and brought back as described.

The Way at its ultimate is without words; yet without words, it cannot be revealed. True reality transcends form; yet only through form can it be realized. That which transcends form is the true-suchness Three Treasures. That which displays form is the abiding Three Treasures. The Tathāgata's original vow adapts to beings' capacities and manifests in various forms. The Great Sage's compassion responds to the times and delivers beings. The sūtra states: "For those who should be liberated through a buddha-body, he manifests a buddha-body; and so on, down to those who should be liberated through an eight-division body, he manifests each of these and teaches the Dharma for them." In the age of the true Dharma, the companions of Māra had no power, and so the Buddha's transformations mostly took the form of compassion. In the age of the semblance and final Dharma, heretical forces compete in rising, and so the Tathāgata manifests many wrathful forms. The Buddha's power is thus: truly a treasure for stabilizing the nation and benefiting the people. To receive, uphold, and venerate these images brings boundless merit. Outwardly, they crush the armies of Māra; inwardly, they subdue afflictions. Such is the wisdom and compassion of all buddhas.

### Dharma-Implements Transmitted by the Dharma-Transmitting Ācārya

The above ritual implements were all transmitted by the ācārya of Qiling Monastery.

Jōgyō departed in the middle of summer of the fifth year of Jōwa, accompanying the vice-commissioner to Tang China, Sugawara no Ason Yoshinushi, and boarded the fourth ship together. We set out for China. In the sixth month of that year, we arrived at Yangzhou and came ashore. In the latter part of the eighth month, we arrived at the Guangling hostel of the Grand Commandery of Huainan and were lodged there. In the twelfth month of the same year, I took up residence in the Mahākaruṇā Dhāraṇī Practice Hall at Qiling Monastery. Under the abhiṣeka ācārya Wen Can of the same monastery, together with the presiding lecturer Yuanzhao, the great worthy of the Three Teachings at Hualin Monastery, I studied both exoteric and esoteric Dharma in considerable breadth. On the fourth day of the first month of the sixth year, I arranged a vegetarian feast for two hundred monastics and made universal offerings to the fourfold assembly. On that day, great worthies and administrators from all monasteries came to the feast, received offerings, and rejoiced. At night, I went to my teacher to receive instruction in yoga. By day, I visited various monasteries to seek and inquire about Dharma-teachings. I then commissioned the imperially appointed artist Li Quan and others to paint the maṇḍala of the Great Generalissimo and his retinue, along with various sacred images, and to copy the required texts. Gradually, things took shape in proper order. On the nineteenth day of the second month, I received the abhiṣeka for the position of Dharma-transmitting ācārya. On that day, a great feast was arranged and universal offerings made to the great assembly. Vice-Commissioner Fujiwara no Ason Sadatoshi specially invited Dan Chi no Mahito Takashi, Shuchi no Ason Yoshimune, and others, who all attended the ritual hall. Flowers were cast to determine the principal deity, and I received the five-wisdom abhiṣeka. On the twenty-first day, following imperial orders, I departed the prefecture and returned to our homeland. Jōgyō's original training was in the branch of the Sanlun school, with the esoteric as a supplementary stream. My abilities are unheard of, and my words are of no account. Encountering the times' custom, I was placed among the long-term students with a thirty-year limit to pursue the One Vehicle. The responsibility is heavy and the person weak; morning and night I am diligent in my vows. Nevertheless, the imperial command could not be disobeyed, and forgetting my parents ten thousand li away, I set my heart on a distant land. I received some instruction in both exoteric and esoteric paths. Before I realized it, I learned that an imperial edict had arrived forbidding further stay, and I returned with the envoys. In this brief interval, what Dharma-teachings I accomplished are noted as described above.

Fifth day of the ninth month, sixth year of Jōwa.

Jōgyō, śramaṇa who entered Tang China to study the Dharma, holding the rank of Dentō Daihōshi, respectfully submitted.
