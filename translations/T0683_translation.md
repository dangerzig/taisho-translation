# 佛說諸德福田經
## Sūtra of the Field of Merit of Various Virtues, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0683

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Fali and Faju (法立、法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with twelve hundred and fifty great bhikṣus, ten thousand bodhisattvas, and an innumerable great assembly surrounding him as he taught the Dharma. At that time, Śakra, Lord of the Devas, together with thirty-two thousand deva-sons of the desire realm, each bringing retinues beyond counting, came to the Buddha's place, bowed with their heads to the ground, and sat to one side.

At that time, Śakra observed that the assembly was seated and settled. Sustained by the Buddha's spiritual power, he rose from his seat, straightened his garments, bowed, and knelt with palms joined. He said to the World-Honored One: "I wish to ask a question. May you please expound and reveal it, establishing a rule for the ages!"

The Buddha said to Śakra: "It is as if in a dark room one does not seek a lamp: how can anything be seen? It is well that you ask! I shall explain it for you in detail."

Śakra said to the Buddha: "When people plant virtue and seek the reward of merit, is there a fine field whose fruit is without limit, where by planting a virtue as slight as a hair one may harvest immeasurable merit? I humbly wish the Honored One of the Devas to set forth his gracious instruction, so that these ignorant ones may receive merit without limit!"

The Honored One of the Devas exclaimed: "Excellent, Śakra! The intent of your question touches upon the supreme Dharma! Listen carefully and reflect well! I shall fully expound it and cause you to rejoice." Śakra and the great assembly received the teaching and listened.

The Buddha said to Śakra: "Within the community of monks there are five pure virtues, called 'the field of merit.' Making offerings to them produces merit, and through this one may advance to buddhahood. What are the five? First, arousing the aspiration and departing from worldly life, because one cherishes and upholds the Way. Second, destroying one's fine appearance, because one conforms to the Dharma-robes. Third, permanently severing family affection, because one has no attachment or fondness. Fourth, relinquishing one's body and life, because one follows all wholesome deeds. Fifth, aspiring to the great vehicle, because one wishes to save all people. By these five virtues one is called a field of merit: good and fine, with nothing lost early. The merit of making offerings to them is difficult to express by analogy!"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> Destroying fine appearance, upholding the Way's discipline,
> Severing love, without partiality:
> Going forth to promote the holy Way,
> Vowing to save all people.
> These five virtues transcend worldly affairs;
> This is called the supreme field of merit.
> Making offerings brings eternal peace;
> This merit is foremost and most honored.

The Buddha said to Śakra: "There are further seven acts of broad giving, called 'fields of merit.' One who practices them obtains merit and is born in the Brahmā heaven. What are the seven? First, establishing Buddha-stūpas, monks' quarters, and halls. Second, providing gardens, fruit, bathing pools, and trees for shade and coolness. Third, always giving medicines to cure all illnesses. Fourth, building sturdy boats to ferry people across. Fifth, constructing bridges so the weak may cross. Sixth, digging wells by the roadside so the thirsty may drink. Seventh, building latrines for the convenience of all. These seven deeds produce the merit of the Brahmā heaven."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> Build stūpas, establish monasteries,
> Plant gardens and fruit for shade and coolness.
> When ill, rescue with medicines;
> Build bridges and boats to ferry the people.
> Dig fine wells along the open road,
> So the thirsty may find rest.
> Wherever born, one drinks the nectar of heaven,
> Free from illness, always at peace.
> Build latrines to bestow cleanness;
> Remove filth to bring lightness and joy.
> Afterward, no ailment of the bowels,
> Nor sight of foul and loathsome things.
> Just as five rivers flow
> Day and night without rest,
> So too is this virtue:
> In the end, one ascends to the Brahmā heaven.

At that time, a bhikṣu in the assembly named Śruti, hearing the Dharma with delight, rose from his seat, bowed to the Buddha, knelt with palms joined, and said to the World-Honored One: "The Buddha's teaching is truly supreme, vast and nourishing beyond measure. Why? Because I recall my past lives through innumerable generations. I was born in the land of Vārāṇasī as the son of a householder. Beside a great road I built a small monastery, with bedding, drink, and provisions to supply the Saṃgha. Travelers who were weary could also stop and rest. Through this merit, at the end of my life I was born in heaven as Śakra, Lord of the Devas, and then born in the human world as a wheel-turning holy king, thirty-six times each. I ruled over devas and humans, with hair growing beneath my feet, treading upon the void. For ninety-one kalpas my merit sustained me naturally. Now I have encountered the World-Honored One, who looks upon sentient beings and has cleansed my ignorance and turbidity, establishing me in pure wisdom. The planting of birth and death has withered, and I am called a True Person. The reward of merit is truly real, and it is indeed so!"

At that time Śruti spoke in verse:

> I recall in ages past
> My offerings were slight.
> Yet the reward spanned distant kalpas;
> The remaining merit allowed me to meet the Heavenly Teacher.
> Pure wisdom has severed birth and death;
> Foolish love leaves no remainder.
> The Buddha's kindness flows without end;
> Therefore I take refuge once more.

At that time Śruti bowed and returned to his seat. Then another bhikṣu named Bakkula rose from his seat, straightened his garments, bowed, knelt with palms joined, and said to the World-Honored One: "I recall a past life when I was born in the land of Kuśinagara as the son of a householder. At that time there was no Buddha in the world, and the Saṃgha was teaching. At a great assembly where the Dharma was being taught, I went to hear the sūtra. Hearing the Dharma with delight, I took a single medicinal fruit called harītakī and offered it to the Saṃgha. Through the reward of this fruit, at the end of my life I ascended to heaven; then born in the human world, I was always in a position of honor, handsome, heroic, and surpassing all others. For ninety-one kalpas I never had an illness. Through the remaining merit I encountered the Buddha, who guided my ignorant darkness, and bestowed the Dharma-medicine upon me. I have attained arhatship: my strength can move mountains; my wisdom can dispel evil. Excellent indeed is this reward of merit; it is truly real!"

At that time Bakkula spoke in verse:

> Compassion's dew nourishes the withered;
> The virtue of effort relieves suffering.
> A single fruit as the root of goodness:
> Its reward endures to this very day.
> The Buddha has bestowed the meaning of supreme truth;
> By his teaching I have transcended the abyss.
> The Saṃgha's blessing is without limit;
> I bow to the supreme field of merit.

At that time Bakkula bowed and returned to his seat. Then another bhikṣu named Sudāya rose from his seat, straightened his garments, bowed, knelt with palms joined, and said to the World-Honored One: "I recall that in a former life I was born in the land of Vaiśālī, the child of a poor family. At that time there was no Buddha, and the Saṃgha was teaching. I was carrying curds to sell in the market when I encountered the Saṃgha at a great assembly teaching the Dharma. I stopped and listened. The Dharma-words were subtle and wondrous; hearing them, I was delighted. I immediately took up my jar of curds and gave it all to the Saṃgha. The Saṃgha pronounced blessings, and I was filled with joy. Through this merit, at the end of my life I was born in heaven; then born in the human world, my wealth was without limit. For ninety-one kalpas I was distinguished, honored, and illustrious. In my final birth, because of remaining wrongdoing, when I was born in the world, my mother, several months pregnant, fell ill and died. She was buried in a tomb, and only when the months were complete was I born inside the tomb. For seven years within the tomb I drank my dead mother's milk, sustaining my own life. Through my slight remaining merit I have encountered the Buddha, who has revealed and proclaimed the bright Dharma, rescued me from the realm of death, and I have attained arhatship. Truly real are wrongdoing and merit, exactly as the Buddha teaches!"

At that time Sudāya spoke in verse:

> Once a child of a poor family,
> I sold curds to sustain myself.
> In joy I made a slight offering;
> Through this I was freed from the three kinds of suffering.
> Though through wrongdoing I was born in a tomb,
> I drank milk and survived seven years.
> By causes and conditions I obtained liberation;
> I take refuge in the holy field of merit.

At that time Sudāya bowed and returned to his seat. Then another bhikṣu named Ānanda rose from his seat, straightened his garments, bowed, knelt with palms joined, and said to the World-Honored One: "I recall a past life when I was born in the land of Rājagṛha, the child of a commoner. My body was covered with terrible sores, and no treatment could cure them. A friend who was a practitioner came and said: 'You should bathe the Saṃgha and take their bathing water to wash your sores. They will be healed, and you will also gain merit!' I was delighted and went to the monastery. With reverence and a sincere heart I dug a new well and prepared fragrant oil and bathing implements, and washed the Saṃgha. I washed my sores with the water and was immediately healed. From this cause, wherever I have been born, I have been beautiful, with a golden complexion, brilliant and lustrous, unsoiled by dust. For ninety-one kalpas I have always received the pure reward of the Saṃgha's blessing, vast and far-reaching. Now again I have encountered the Buddha; the defilements of my mind have been eliminated, and I have attained arhatship."

Ānanda spoke this verse before the Buddha:

> The noble Saṃgha is a skilled physician,
> Rescuing and relieving suffering and affliction.
> By bathing and bestowing cleanness,
> My sores were healed and I found peace.
> Wherever born, always beautiful,
> With the exceptional hue of purple gold.
> Virtue's nourishment is without limit;
> I take refuge in the fine field of merit.

Ānanda bowed and returned to his seat. At that time, a bhikṣuṇī in the assembly named Āmrapālī rose from her seat, straightened her garments, bowed, knelt with palms joined, and said to the Buddha: "I recall a former life when I was born in the land of Vārāṇasī as a poor woman. At that time there was a Buddha in the world named Kāśyapa, who was surrounded by a great assembly and teaching the Dharma. I was in the assembly and heard the sūtra with delight. I wished to make an offering but had nothing at all. Reflecting on my poverty and low station, my heart was grieved and moved. I went to someone else's garden and begged for fruit. I would offer it to the Buddha. I obtained a single nai fruit, large and fragrant. Carrying a cup of water and the single nai fruit, I offered them to the Buddha Kāśyapa and the entire Saṃgha. The Buddha knew the sincerity of my intention and pronounced blessings as he accepted them. He distributed the water and the fruit, and all were fully supplied. Through this blessing, at the end of my life I was born in heaven and became the queen of the heavens. Born again in the human world, I was not born from a womb; for ninety-one kalpas I was born from within a nai flower, beautiful and fresh, and I always remembered my past lives. Now I have encountered the World-Honored One, who has revealed the eye of the Way."

At that time Āmrapālī spoke in verse:

> The Three Honored Ones' compassion is universal;
> The ferry of wisdom makes no distinction of man or woman.
> From a water-and-fruit offering comes this vast reward;
> Through its cause I have left behind all suffering.
> In this world I was born within a flower;
> Above, I was the queen of heaven.
> I take refuge in the Saṃgha's blessing;
> This field of merit is the deepest and richest.

The bhikṣuṇī Āmrapālī bowed and returned to her seat. Then Śakra rose from his seat, bowed to the Buddha, and said to the World-Honored One: "In a former life I was born in the land of Kuru as the son of a householder. While a servant carried me through the city, I encountered the Saṃgha begging in the streets. I saw many people making offerings, and I thought: 'If only I had wealth to give to the Saṃgha, how wonderful that would be!' I immediately unfastened my pearl necklace and gave it to the Saṃgha. They all joined their hearts in prayer, and I departed with joy. Through this cause, at the end of my life I was born in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven as Śakra, Lord of the Devas. For ninety-one kalpas I have been forever free from the eight difficulties."

Then Śakra spoke in verse:

> Virtue is the highest; none surpasses it.
> It opens the gate of merit and blocks the source of calamity.
> Through the Saṃgha's spiritual power of concentration,
> Even a child's heart gives rise to joy.
> Following the crowd in a joyful offering,
> My spirit rose to rule in two heavens.
> I take refuge in the most generous in the world;
> Life after life I vow to honor the Honored One.

The Buddha said to Śakra and the great assembly: "Listen to what I shall say about my own past-life deeds. In a former life, in the land of Vārāṇasī, beside a great road, I built and provided a latrine for the use of the people of the land. All who found relief there were moved by this service. Through this merit, wherever I have been born, I have been clean and pure. Through kalpas of practicing the Way, defilement has not stained me. When my meritorious foundations were fully complete, I attained buddhahood of my own accord. My golden body is radiant; dust and water do not adhere. Food is digested of itself, and there is no ailment of the bowels."

Then the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> Enduring the foul, cultivating merit:
> This is what others cannot defile.
> Building latrines and providing convenience,
> The heavy burden becomes lightness and ease.
> This virtue removes arrogance;
> Through it, the bonds of birth and death are understood.
> One advances and ascends to the Way of Buddhahood:
> Emptiness, purity, the lofty and majestic Honored One.

The Buddha said to Śakra: "Among the ninety-six paths, the Buddha's Way is the most honored. Among the ninety-six dharmas, the Buddha's Dharma is the most true. Among the ninety-six communities, the Buddha's Saṃgha is the most righteous. Why? Because the Tathāgata, from asaṃkhyeya kalpas, made vows in truth and sincerity, sacrificed his life to accumulate virtue, and pledged for the sake of sentient beings to give his kingdom, wealth, wife, children, head, eyes, blood, and flesh, without any lingering attachment; his mind was like space, covering all without exception. The six perfections, the four immeasurables, and every wholesome quality were all complete. When virtue and wisdom were fully matured, he became a Buddha, his body the color of purple gold, his marks without compare, knowing past, present, and future with nothing unilluminated. The honored devas of the three realms cannot match him. His words are trustworthy, his virtue weighty, shaking heaven and earth. If any sentient being arouses a single thought of reverence toward the Tathāgata, the merit surpasses obtaining all the precious treasures of the great-thousand world! He teaches the thirty-seven factors of the path and the twelve divisions of scripture, distinguishing wrongdoing from merit, his words all truthful. He opens the teaching of the three vehicles, and each may practice according to their kind. Those who hear rejoice and delight in becoming śramaṇas, trusting the Buddha, practicing the Dharma, with aspirations lofty and pure. Within the Saṃgha there are the four pairs and eight grades, and the twelve sages, who have abandoned worldly craving and contention and who guide the world in opening the way of merit. The path of devas and humans is open, and this is due to the Saṃgha. This is the most honored, unsurpassed Way. All Buddhas, bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, and arhats all emerge from within it, teaching and transforming all, liberating all sentient beings."

When the Buddha had spoken this, Śakra's entire assembly all gave rise to the aspiration for the supreme, true Way, and innumerable people attained purity of the Dharma-eye. Then Ānanda knelt with palms joined and said to the Buddha: "What is this sūtra called? How should we uphold it?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Field of Merit of Various Virtues.' Always uphold it and clearly proclaim the Way of the sūtras. Do not let it diminish!"

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, Śakra's entire assembly and the whole gathering were all filled with joy, bowed, and departed.
