# 佛說須摩提菩薩經
## Sūtra on the Bodhisattva Sumatī, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0335

Translated from the Chinese. Yao Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the kingdom of Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas.

At that time, in the great kingdom of Rājagṛha, there was an elder named Ugra. Ugra had a daughter named Sumatī, who was eight years of age. Through many lifetimes she had reverently served innumerable hundreds of thousands of Buddhas of the past, and her accumulated merit was beyond reckoning. At that time, Sumatī went out from the great kingdom of Rājagṛha to Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa. She approached the Buddha, prostrated with her head at his feet, and after paying her respects, withdrew to one side. With single-minded concentration, she joined her palms and addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask some questions. May the Tathāgata, through skillful means, free me from my doubts."

The Buddha, in silence, immediately knew the girl's intention. The Buddha said to Sumatī: "Ask whatever you wish. The Tathāgata shall now explain it all for you in detail, distinguishing each matter, to bring you joy."

Sumatī asked the Buddha: "How can a bodhisattva be one whom people always rejoice to see wherever they are born? How can they obtain great wealth with an abundance of treasures? How can they never be separated from others? How can they avoid being conceived in a mother's womb and always be born by transformation in a thousand-petaled lotus, standing before the Dharma King? How can they obtain spiritual powers to travel across immeasurable koṭis of buddha-lands to visit, pay respects, and serve the Buddhas? How can they be free of enemies and those who would harm or envy them? How can their words be such that all who hear them believe, accept, and joyfully practice them? How can they be free of calamity and wrongdoing, so that no one can undermine their good deeds? How can Māra be unable to find an opening against them? How can they, at the end of life, have the Buddha standing before them to teach the Dharma, ensuring they do not fall into places of suffering? These are my questions."

The Buddha then said to Sumatī: "As to the meaning of the Tathāgata that you have asked about: excellent! How wonderful! That it should be so! If you wish to hear, listen carefully and receive it. Reflect upon it diligently! I shall explain."

The girl then said: "Very well, World-Honored One! I am glad to hear it." Thereupon Sumatī received the teaching and listened.

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva has four practices that cause all people who see them to rejoice. What are the four? First, not giving rise to anger, and regarding enemies as good spiritual friends. Second, always having a compassionate mind toward all. Third, always seeking and pursuing the supreme essential Dharma. Fourth, making images of the Buddha. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva practices these, people always rejoice upon seeing them."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Not raising anger destroys the root of affliction;
> Always practicing compassion, one obtains the essential Dharma.
> Making Buddha images, the body becomes pure and radiant;
> With a joyful mind, people delight in seeing you."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices for obtaining great wealth. What are the four? First, giving at the proper time. Second, after giving, feeling redoubled joy. Third, after giving, never feeling regret. Fourth, having given, not seeking any reward. These are the four. Because a bodhisattva practices these four, they obtain great wealth."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Giving at the proper time, without a regretful mind,
> Joyfully giving without expectation,
> Performing generosity with courageous wisdom,
> Wherever one dwells, there is always great wealth."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices for never being separated from others. What are the four? First, not passing on malicious speech to create quarrels between people. Second, guiding the ignorant to enter the Buddha Way. Third, if the true Dharma is damaged, protecting it so it does not perish. Fourth, encouraging all people, teaching them to seek Buddhahood, and making them firm and unshakable. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva practices these four, they are never separated from others."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Not passing on speech to make quarrels between people,
> Guiding the ignorant and protecting the true Dharma,
> Encouraging people to seek Buddhahood,
> No one will ever be able to separate you from others."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices for being born by transformation in a thousand-petaled lotus, standing before the Dharma King. What are the four? First, finely crushing utpala flowers, padma flowers, kumuda flowers, and puṇḍarīka flowers, making the four kinds into powder as fine as dust to fill a *kumbha* measure, and offering it to the World-Honored One, or to a stūpa, or to relics. Second, never causing others to give rise to an angry mind. Third, making Buddha images seated upon lotuses. Fourth, upon attaining the supreme awakening, abiding in joy. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva practices these four, they are born by transformation in a thousand-petaled lotus, standing before the Dharma King."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Offering the four flowers filling a *kumbha* measure,
> Removing resentment and hatred, receiving the Dharma's meaning,
> Attaining the supreme awakening, standing before the Buddha,
> Making images, one is born among the lotus flowers."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices for obtaining spiritual powers to travel from one buddha-land to another. What are the four? First, when seeing someone performing meritorious deeds, not interrupting them. Second, when seeing someone teaching the Dharma, not stopping them. Third, always lighting lamps in stūpas and monasteries. Fourth, seeking samādhi. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva practices these four, they obtain spiritual powers to travel from one buddha-land to another."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Performing merit and giving the Dharma,
> When hearing the teaching, not stopping it,
> Always lighting lamps in Buddha's monasteries,
> Entering samādhi, one travels to all lands."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices for being free of enemies and those who would harm or envy them. What are the four? First, having no flattering or deceitful mind toward good spiritual friends. Second, not being stingy or coveting others' possessions. Third, when seeing someone giving, rejoicing and assisting. Fourth, not slandering bodhisattvas in whatever they do. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva always carries out these practices, they are free of enemies and those who would harm or envy them."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Having no flattery toward good friends,
> Not being stingy over others' possessions,
> Seeing someone give and assisting with joy,
> Practicing as a bodhisattva, one has no enemies."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices so that their words are such that all who hear them believe, accept, and joyfully practice them. What are the four? First, what one says with the mouth is no different from what is in the mind. Second, always having sincerity toward good spiritual friends. Third, when hearing someone teach the Dharma, not judging it as right or wrong. Fourth, when seeing someone invited to teach a sūtra, not seeking their faults. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva practices these four, their words are such that all who hear them believe, accept, and joyfully practice them."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "What one thinks, one's words are likewise;
> Toward good friends, one has sincere faith.
> Hearing a Dharma lecture, not seeking faults,
> When a sūtra is taught, the mind leaps with joy."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices for being free of calamity and wrongdoing, so that their good deeds quickly reach a state of purity. What are the four? First, what one thinks of in mind and intention is always directed toward the good. Second, always upholding the precepts, samādhi, and wisdom. Third, from the first arising of the bodhisattva aspiration, giving rise to omniscient knowledge and liberating many beings. Fourth, always having great compassion for all. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva practices these four, they are free of calamity and wrongdoing and quickly attain a state of purity."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always aspiring to goodness and broadly liberating beings,
> Precepts, concentration, never apart from wisdom,
> Always teaching people omniscient knowledge,
> Practicing compassion, one attains a state of purity."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices so that Māra cannot find an opening against them. What are the four? First, always being mindful of the Buddha. Second, always being diligent. Third, always being mindful of the sūtras and Dharma. Fourth, always establishing merit. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva practices these four, Māra cannot find an opening against them."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always with a joyful mind, mindful of the Buddha,
> Always diligent, abiding in the profound Dharma,
> Exerting oneself to establish merit,
> Māra, because of this, cannot find an opening."

The Buddha said to Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four more practices so that at the end of life, the Buddha stands before them to teach the Dharma, ensuring they do not fall into places of suffering. What are the four? First, for the sake of all people, fulfilling all their wishes. Second, if someone has given generously but is still lacking, thinking of how to help them become sufficient. Third, when seeing someone making various offerings but falling short, assisting and supplementing them. Fourth, always being mindful of making offerings to the Three Treasures. These are the four practices. Because a bodhisattva practices these four, at the end of life they will see all the Buddhas standing before them, teaching the Dharma, ensuring that person does not fall into places of suffering."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "For all beings, fulfilling their wishes,
> With boundless compassion, encouraging the perfection of giving,
> Through various offerings, attaining discerning wisdom,
> Making offerings to the Three Treasures, one attains the presence of the Buddha."

At that time, Sumatī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! As for the forty practices you have taught, I shall carry them out without any deficiency, fulfilling them all completely, not violating a single one. If I should fail in even one, I shall be cutting off the Buddha's Dharma and destroying the assembly of disciples."

At that time, the elder Mahāmaudgalyāyana was seated in the great assembly and asked Sumatī: "These forty practices are the conduct of great beings and the deeds of bodhisattvas; they are extremely difficult to fulfill. How can a little girl like you accomplish them?"

Sumatī then answered Maudgalyāyana: "If it is truly the case that I can practice these forty things, then the great trichiliocosm shall quake in six ways for my sake, heavenly flowers shall rain down, and the musical instruments of the heavens shall sound of their own accord without being struck."

No sooner had Sumatī spoken these words than the great trichiliocosm quaked in six ways, heavenly flowers rained down, and the musical instruments resounded.

The girl said to Maudgalyāyana: "This bears witness to my sincerity! If in the future there are those who generate the bodhisattva aspiration, it shall be just like this. I too shall in the future attain the stage of Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha. Trust my words, for there is nothing false in them. All those present in this assembly shall become uniformly golden in color."

Instantly, just as she had spoken, all became golden in color.

Thereupon Maudgalyāyana rose from his seat, adjusted his robes, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms, prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and addressed the Buddha: "Now, to all those who have initially generated the great aspiration to become bodhisattvas, I shall take refuge and bow before them. Why is this? An eight-year-old girl produces such a wondrous response; how much more so the great bodhisattva-mahāsattvas!"

At that time, in the assembly there was a great bodhisattva named Mañjuśrī, who said to Sumatī: "In what dharma do you abide, that you manifest such a wondrous response?"

Sumatī answered: "All dharmas are incalculable and without any abiding. Yet you ask me in what dharma I abide? It would be better not to ask."

Mañjuśrī asked Sumatī: "How can you speak in this way, going so far as this?"

Sumatī replied to Mañjuśrī: "One does not abide in any dharma, has no doubts, and does not speak of right or wrong."

Mañjuśrī asked Sumatī: "Has the Tathāgata originally not practiced?"

Sumatī answered: "It is like the reflection of the moon in water, like a dream, a mirage, or an echo in a deep mountain. The Tathāgata's original practice is also like this."

Mañjuśrī asked Sumatī: "According to what you have said, by combining these matters, can one attain Buddhahood or not?"

Sumatī answered: "How is it? Do you think that the three things of delusion, wisdom, and practice are different? They are not different. All is one Dharma; all dharmas are in accord. Why is this? Whether it is the true Dharma or the untrue Dharma, there is nowhere to abide, nothing to grasp, and nothing to gather in. It is empty and without form."

Mañjuśrī then asked Sumatī: "How many people understand this meaning?"

Sumatī answered: "When a magician creates illusions at will, is there a limit? Even the magician's transformations are without limit; those who understand and have faith in this Dharma are also like this."

Mañjuśrī asked Sumatī: "If, like me, there are no transformations and no illusions, what practice should one cultivate to accord with the Way?"

Sumatī answered: "As you have spoken, that is truly wonderful. All dharmas and all places are neither existent nor nonexistent. As for the Tathāgata, there is neither coming together nor scattering apart."

Mañjuśrī, hearing what she said, was greatly delighted and praised her. Mañjuśrī then addressed the Buddha: "Excellent! What Sumatī has said, speaking freely and profoundly, is most marvelous and wondrous! She has deeply entered and attained the patient endurance of the birthlessness of dharmas. How long ago did she first generate this aspiration?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "This Sumatī has generated the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and abided in equanimity for an incalculable time. Thirty billion kalpas before you, she had already generated the unsurpassed aspiration for universal liberation. Only then did you enter the patient endurance of birthlessness. She was in fact your original teacher when you first generated the aspiration."

Mañjuśrī, hearing the Buddha's words, went forward and bowed, then addressed Sumatī: "We have been separated for so long, and only now do we meet. I am able to see my teacher and receive the Dharma's instruction."

Sumatī answered: "Do not think in this way. Why? In the patient endurance of birthlessness, there is nothing to be mindful of and no teacher."

Mañjuśrī asked: "Why do you not transform your female body?"

Sumatī answered: "In this there is nothing to be attained. Why? The Dharma is neither male nor female. Now I shall resolve your doubt."

Mañjuśrī said: "Excellent! I would be glad to hear it."

Sumatī said to Mañjuśrī: "Before long I too shall attain the stage of Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, Accomplished in Wisdom and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Gods and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. If this is truly so, I shall now transform into a male." No sooner had she spoken these words than she became a man; her hair fell away, a kāṣāya robe appeared on her body, and she became a novice monk.

Sumatī then said to Mañjuśrī: "Truly, when I become a Buddha in a future life, let there be three things absent from my land. What are the three? First, the deeds of Māra. Second, the hells. Third, the ways of women. If my vow is sincere, my body shall become like that of a śramaṇa of thirty years." When Sumatī spoke these words, her form and appearance became like that of a thirty-year-old.

Sumatī then said to Mañjuśrī: "When I become a Buddha, let the people of my land all be golden in color. The ground and the city walls shall be surrounded by rows of seven-jeweled trees, eight rows deep, with seven-jeweled pools of water. On all four sides, within and without, there shall grow seven-jeweled lotuses of various colors and various jewels, neither too many nor too few, all perfectly balanced."

Sumatī said: "Like your land, my buddha-field shall be just the same. If my vow is sincere, all those in this assembly shall become golden in color." No sooner had she spoken these words than the entire assembly became golden in color.

At that time, the earth deity Dhāraṇīdhara emerged from the earth, manifested a celestial body, and loudly praised Sumatī, saying: "When the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Sumatī attains Buddhahood, all the seven-jeweled pools, trees, flowers, and fruits in her land shall be just like this."

Thereupon the Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "This bodhisattva-mahāsattva Sumatī shall before long attain Buddhahood, with the name Ratnaguṇa-samaṅgī Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, Accomplished in Wisdom and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed One, who always with good intention upholds the essential Dharma, Teacher of Gods and Humans, known as the Honored One among the Heavens."

When the Buddha taught this sūtra and gave the prediction to Sumatī, thirty billion people generated the mind of unsurpassed, universal liberation and all attained the stage of non-retrogression. Sixty thousand celestial beings all attained the pure Dharma-eye.

In the assembly there were five hundred bodhisattvas who, upon hearing the profound questions of Mañjuśrī, could not understand them and were about to fall from their resolve. But seeing that Sumatī's sincere words were immediately confirmed by wondrous responses, they removed their upper garments and offered them to the Buddha, without flattery or expectation. They dedicated this merit to strengthen themselves in the unsurpassed aspiration for universal liberation, and immediately attained the stage of non-retrogression, leaping beyond ninety kalpas of birth and death. The Buddha then gave a prediction to these five hundred people: after ten kalpas, in a kalpa named Dustless, there shall be a Buddha named Dṛḍha-pratigṛhīta Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha. These five hundred people shall be born in that land, which shall be named Blazing Radiance. In that same kalpa, they shall all attain Buddhahood, all with the same name: Alaṃkṛta-pūrva-jñāna-mano-'bhijña Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha.

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Know that this sūtra brings abundant benefit. Is it not so? If, in the final age, there are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, śramaṇas, good men, or good women who seek the bodhisattva path and practice the six pāramitās but have not yet understood *upāya-kauśalya*, it would be better for them to copy, uphold, recite, and chant this sūtra, to teach others to always be mindful of its contents, and to explain it broadly for all who wish to hear it."

The Buddha further said to Mañjuśrī: "For bodhisattvas who have not yet heard what was previously unheard and have not yet practiced what was previously unpracticed, they should contemplate and uphold this teaching. Why is this? It is like when a cakravartin king rules the world: throughout his entire reign, the seven treasures are never diminished. But when the king's life ends, the seven treasures scatter. Just so, Mañjuśrī, when the Buddha's sūtras and Way abide in the world, the Buddha's seven factors of awakening are never diminished. But when the Buddha's Dharma perishes, the factors of awakening and all dharmas are entirely exhausted."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "One should seek by innumerable skillful means to gather the sūtras, diligently studying and copying them, teaching them to others, instructing all and broadly explaining their meaning. One should always be diligent; this is the Dharma teaching. Good men and good women who wish to seek the Buddha Way should never have regrets along the way."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, the bodhisattva Sumatī, the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, and all the devas and humans in the assembly, together with the asuras, gandharvas, and world-protectors, all rejoiced and delighted in what they had heard.
