# 御請來目錄
## Catalog of Items Requested by Imperial Command

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2161

Japanese, compiled by Kūkai (空海).

---

### Memorial Presenting the Catalog of Newly Requested Sūtras and Other Items

The Dharma-studying śramaṇa Kūkai who entered Tang China states: In the twenty-third year of Enryaku, Kūkai received the imperial command as one remaining to study abroad, and inquired the way across ten thousand leagues. In the twelfth month of that year, he arrived in Chang'an. On the tenth day of the second month of the twenty-fourth year, by imperial decree he was assigned to reside at Ximingsi.

Thereupon he traveled around the various monasteries, seeking and selecting a teacher to rely upon. Fortunately he encountered the consecration ācārya of Qinglongsi, whose Dharma-name was Preceptor Huiguo. He took him as his master. This great worthy was a Dharma-transmission disciple of the great Amoghavajra Tripiṭaka of Daxingshan Monastery. His grasp of the sūtras and vinaya was comprehensive, his mastery of the esoteric treasury complete. He was the guiding principle of the Dharma and the teacher of the nation.

The great master, cherishing the spread of the Buddha-Dharma and lamenting that living beings could be rescued, conferred upon me the precepts of arousing the mind of bodhi and permitted me to enter the consecration altar. I received the consecration of illumination three times and received the rank of ācārya once. Crawling on elbows and advancing on knees, I studied what I had not yet studied; prostrating my head and touching his feet, I heard what I had not yet heard. Fortunately, relying on the great grace of the nation and the compassion of the great master, I learned the great Dharma of the two divisions and practiced the yoga of all the honored ones.

This Dharma is the heart and marrow of all the buddhas, the direct path to attaining buddhahood. For the nation it is a citadel; for the people it is their very sustenance. Therefore, those of meager fortune do not even hear its name, and those of heavy defilements cannot enter it. In India, Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha cast off royal status and forgot worldly pleasures; in China, Emperor Xuanzong of Tang gazed up in admiration, forgetting the taste of food. From that time on, the sovereign and the three highest ministers followed in each other's footsteps, delighting in its study, while the fourfold assembly and the myriad people prostrated themselves and beat their chests. The lineage of the esoteric treasury has reigned supreme since then; the revealed teachings of the half-jewel have lowered their banners in submission.

Now, phoenixes take flight only when they behold a Yao or a Shun. The practice and concealment of the Buddha-Dharma unfolds and withdraws according to the times. At present, over one hundred divisions of the Vajrayāna teachings and the ocean-assembly of the two great maṇḍalas have been requested and brought here. Though the waves were vast and boundless and wind and rain lashed the ship, we crossed that whale-ocean and arrived safely in this sacred realm. This was accomplished by the power of the sages.

I humbly submit that Your Imperial Majesty's supreme virtue is like heaven, and the sun of the Buddha turns high above. As father of the people and emanation of the Buddha, you compassionately descend to moisten the feet on behalf of all beings. You have received the entrustment of the Buddha and govern effortlessly. Because Your Majesty now newly turns the celestial mechanism, the newly translated sūtras have come from afar to arrive anew. Because Your Majesty compassionately nurtures all within the seas, the ocean-assembly images have crossed the ocean to come. This coincidence is like tallies fitting together; who but a sage could fathom it?

Though the crime of Kūkai's overdue return deserves more than death, I secretly rejoice that this Dharma, so hard to obtain, has been requested and brought here in my lifetime. I cannot contain my mixture of fear and joy. I humbly attach this to Adjutant Takashina no Mahito Tōnari, Senior Sixth Rank Upper Grade, Acting Senior Inspector of Dazaifu, and present this memorial for your hearing, together with one fascicle of the catalog of newly requested sūtras and other items. I venture to present this, begging pardon for presuming upon your august dignity. In trepidation I submit this with utmost fear and sincerity.

The śramaṇa Kūkai speaks with sincerest reverence.

The twenty-second day of the tenth month of Daido 1 (806 CE).

Memorial presented by the Dharma-studying śramaṇa Kūkai who entered Tang China.

The Dharma-studying śramaṇa Kūkai who entered Tang China, in the first year of Daido, presents this catalog of sūtras, vinaya, treatises, commentaries, chapters, biographical records, and images of buddhas, bodhisattvas, vajra-beings, devas, and others, together with samaya maṇḍalas, dharma maṇḍalas, portraits of Dharma-transmitting ācāryas, ritual implements, and items entrusted by the ācārya, requested and brought from Tang China. In total there are six categories. Among them:

Newly translated sūtras and other works: a total of 142 texts in 247 fascicles.

Sanskrit (Siddhaṃ) mantras, praises, and other works: a total of 42 texts in 44 fascicles.

Treatises, commentaries, chapters, and other works: a total of 32 texts in 170 fascicles.

The above three categories together total 216 texts in 461 fascicles.

Images of buddhas, bodhisattvas, vajra-beings, devas, and others, dharma maṇḍalas, samaya maṇḍalas, and portraits of Dharma-transmitting ācāryas: altogether 10 scrolls.

Ritual implements: 9 kinds.

Items entrusted by the ācārya: 13 kinds.

### Newly Translated Sūtras

The following are not yet recorded in the Zhenyuan catalog.

The above 118 texts in 150 fascicles were translated by the great Amoghavajra Tripiṭaka, Grand Preceptor of Tang, Special Advancement, Probationary President of the Court of State Ceremonial, with additional title of Commander Unequaled in Honor, enfeoffed as Duke of Su, posthumously granted the title of Minister of Works, with the posthumous name "Great Dialectician, Upright, Great and Broadly Wise."

The Dharma-ocean is of one flavor, yet varies in depth according to capacity. The five vehicles diverge in their courses, following the vessel in sudden or gradual fashion. Within the sudden teaching there is the revealed and the esoteric. Within the esoteric treasury there are sources and tributaries. The Dharma-masters of old swam in the tributaries and climbed the branches. What is now transmitted draws from and exhausts the source. Why is this so? Long ago, Vajrasattva personally received transmission from Vairocana Tathāgata. After several hundred years, he transmitted it to Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna. Nāgārjuna transmitted it to Ācārya Nāgabodhi. Nāgabodhi transmitted it to Ācārya Vajrabodhi. Tripiṭaka Master Vajrabodhi, in the Kaiyuan era of Tang, first opened the five divisions. Though one person revered the lineage, it could not spread widely. Only our patriarch, Ācārya Amoghavajra, first received transmission from Tripiṭaka Master Vajrabodhi, then traveled to South India to Ācārya Nāgabodhi, where he gathered the complete yoga of the eighteen assemblies and thoroughly investigated the esoteric treasury of the Womb Realm and others. In the Tianbao era he returned to Tang. At that time, Emperor Xuanzong first received consecration, humbling his august person in the relationship of master and disciple. From the Suzong through the Daizong reigns, they successively received the Dharma. Within the palace, the Shenlong Chapel was established; in the capital, consecration altars were widely opened. The sovereign and the hundred officials came to the altar to receive consecration; the fourfold assembly and the multitudes advanced on their knees to study the esoteric treasury. The lineage of the esoteric treasury flourished abundantly from that day; the method of consecration has proceeded in unbroken succession since then. Furthermore, the revealed teachings speak of the remote kalpas of the three great incalculable aeons, while the esoteric treasury promises the sixteen great lives. The difference in speed and superiority is like that between supernatural flight and a lame donkey. Those who aspire to goodness should understand this purport. The superiority and inferiority of the teachings and the origin of the Dharma are extensively explained in texts such as the Five Secret Ritual Manual of Vajrasattva and the memorials and responses of Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra.

### Sanskrit Texts

The above 42 texts in 44 fascicles.

The Buddha's teaching originally comes from India. The customs of the western regions and the eastern lands are separated by heaven itself. Their language differs from the tones of Chu and Xia; their script is not the form of seal or clerical characters. Therefore, one must await translation before one can drink of the pure breeze. Yet mantras are profound and hidden, with deep meaning in every syllable. When one alters the meaning to follow the sound, the long and short become easy to confuse. One may obtain a rough approximation but cannot achieve clarity and precision. Without the Sanskrit letters, the long and short are hard to distinguish. The intent of preserving the source lies herein.

### Treatises, Commentaries, Chapters, and Other Works

The above 32 texts in 170 fascicles.

That which contains principle is the three lines of the trigrams; that which can expound is the ten wings. If one lacks the judgments and appended statements, of what use are the tortoise-shell markings? How much more so with the One Vehicle, whose principle is profound and whose meaning diverges from the text: without the aid of treatises and commentaries, the subtle words accomplish nothing. Though there is the labor of a loaded cart, one hopes to supplement the sacred canon.

### Buddha Images and Others

The above images of buddhas, bodhisattvas, vajra-beings, devas, and other figures, together with portraits of the Dharma-transmitting ācāryas: 10 scrolls.

The Dharma is originally without words; without words it cannot be revealed. True suchness transcends form; only through form can one awaken. Though one may err in mistaking the finger for the moon, there is no limit to the guidance it provides. What is valued is not the spectacle that startles the eye, but truly the treasure that protects the nation and benefits the people. Moreover, the esoteric treasury is deep and profound, and brush and ink can hardly convey it. One must further rely on paintings and illustrations to reveal what cannot be understood. The various mudrās and dignified comportments all arise from great compassion; a single glance leads to buddhahood. What the sūtras and commentaries describe in secret and abbreviated form is set forth in the images. The essential of the esoteric treasury truly rests herein. In transmitting and receiving the Dharma, whom else could one rely upon? The root source of the ocean-assembly is precisely this.

### Ritual Implements

Each of the above contains buddha relics.

The above 9 kinds, 18 items.

Wisdom being boundless, one is called a buddha. Awakening being unsurpassed, one is called a tamer. Because wisdom is boundless, there is nothing that is not known. Because awakening is unsurpassed, skillful means are unfathomable. Therefore, through the various Dharma-gates, one guides and transforms beings through the long night. The so-called vajra implements and others are all the wisdom of the Buddha and the gates of the Dharma. To uphold and venerate them brings inexhaustible blessings. Externally they crush and destroy the hosts of Māra; internally they subdue the afflictions. The beginnings of contemplative wisdom arise from here. Those who aspire to the southern direction must know this.

### Items Entrusted by the Ācārya

The above 8 kinds of items originally were brought by Ācārya Vajrabodhi from the kingdom of South India. He transmitted them to Ācārya Amoghavajra. Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra in turn transmitted them to the Qinglong Ācārya. Preceptor Huiguo of Qinglong in turn bestowed them upon Kūkai. These are the seal-credentials of Dharma-transmission and the refuge of ten thousand lifetimes.

The above 5 kinds were also entrusted by the Qinglong Ācārya.

In the late summer month of the twenty-third year of Enryaku, Kūkai accompanied the Ambassador to Tang, Fujiwara no Ason, aboard the first ship and set out for Xianyang. In the eighth month of that year, he arrived at Fuzhou and came ashore. In the last ten days of the twelfth month, he arrived at the city of Chang'an and was lodged at the official residence in Xuanyang Ward. On the eleventh day of the second month of the twenty-fourth year, the Ambassador and others returned to our homeland. Only Kūkai remained alone, assigned by imperial decree to reside at the former quarters of Preceptor Eichū at Ximingsi.

Thereupon, traveling through the capital and visiting eminent worthies, he happened to encounter the Preceptor of the East Pagoda Hall of Qinglongsi, whose Dharma-name was Ācārya Huiguo. This great worthy was a Dharma-transmission disciple of Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra of Daxingshan Monastery. In virtue he was the most revered of his time; in the Way he was the imperial master. Three reigns honored him and received consecration from him; the fourfold assembly looked up to him and studied the esoteric treasury.

Kūkai, together with five or six persons including the Dharma Master Zhiming of Ximingsi who discussed the superior Dharma, went together to see the Preceptor. The Preceptor, upon seeing them, smiled with joy and gladly declared: "I knew beforehand that you would come. I have waited for you a long time. To meet today is most excellent, most excellent! My life force is about to be exhausted, and there is no one to transmit the Dharma to. You must quickly prepare incense and flowers and enter the consecration altar. Quickly prepare incense and flowers and enter the consecration altar!"

Kūkai then returned to his quarters and prepared the offering articles. In the first ten days of the sixth month, he entered the consecration altar for studying the Dharma. On that day, he approached the Great Maṇḍala of the Compassionate Womb-realm. Following the prescribed method, he cast a flower, and it happened to land upon the body of Vairocana Tathāgata at the central platform. The ācārya praised him, saying: "Inconceivable! Inconceivable!" He praised him again and again. Kūkai then received the consecration of the five divisions and the empowerment of the three mysteries. From this time on, he received the Sanskrit ritual manuals of the Womb-realm and studied the yogic contemplative wisdom of all the honored ones.

In the first ten days of the seventh month, he again approached the Great Maṇḍala of the Vajra-realm and once more received the consecration of the five divisions. Again casting a flower, he obtained Vairocana. The Preceptor expressed amazement and praised him as before.

In the first ten days of the eighth month, he further received the consecration of the rank of Dharma-transmitting ācārya. On that day, a feast for five hundred monks was provided and offerings were made universally to the fourfold assembly. The offering-attending great worthies of Qinglongsi and Daxingshan Monastery all came to the feast and rejoiced. The mantras and secret mudrā-contracts of the yoga of the five divisions of the Vajraśekhara were received in succession. The Sanskrit letters and Sanskrit praises were studied in between.

The Preceptor said: "The sūtras and commentaries of the mantra esoteric treasury are hidden and secret; without paintings and illustrations, they cannot be transmitted." He then summoned the offering-attending painters Li Zhen and more than ten others to paint the Great Maṇḍalas of the Womb-realm, Vajra-realm, and others, ten scrolls in all. He assembled more than twenty scribes to copy the supreme-vehicle esoteric sūtras such as the Vajraśekhara. He also summoned the offering-attending casting masters Yang Zhongxin and Zhao Wu to newly fashion fifteen ritual implements. The images, paintings, and copied sūtras gradually reached completion.

The Preceptor said: "Long ago, when I was still a child, I first met the Tripiṭaka Master. After one look, the Tripiṭaka Master was especially fond of me, treating me as his own son. Whether entering the palace or returning to the monastery, I followed him like a shadow, inseparable. He privately told me: 'You have the capacity for the esoteric treasury. Strive diligently! Strive diligently!' The great Dharma of the two divisions and the secret mudrā-contracts were thus learned through this connection. As for the remaining disciples, whether ordained or lay, some learned the great Dharma of one division, or obtained one honored one and one contract, but none could encompass the whole.

"I wished to repay a debt as vast as mountains, rivers, and the boundless sky. But now my connection with this land is exhausted, and I cannot remain long. You should take these two great maṇḍalas, over one hundred divisions of the Vajrayāna Dharma, the items transmitted by the Tripiṭaka Master, and the offering implements, and request permission to return to your homeland to disseminate them throughout your country. As soon as I saw you coming, I feared my life would not suffice. Now the transmission of the Dharma is accomplished and the sūtras and images are complete. Return quickly to your country to serve the nation. Spread it throughout the land and increase the blessings of all beings. Then the four seas will be at peace and the myriad people will be happy. This is to repay the grace of the Buddha and the virtue of the master. For the nation it is loyalty; for the family it is filial piety." Yiming, the offering-attendant, transmitted this here. "Go now and transmit it to the eastern land. Strive diligently! Strive diligently!"

The entrustment of the Dharma was earnest and thorough; the parting instructions were also completed. On the fifteenth day of the twelfth month of the preceding year, the Preceptor bathed in fragrant water, formed the dharma-mudrā of Vairocana, and passed away lying on his right side. That night, while Kūkai was maintaining his practice in the hall, the Preceptor appeared vividly before him and said: "You and I have long had a covenant. We vowed to spread the esoteric treasury. When I am born in the eastern land, I will certainly become your disciple." The details need not be elaborated further. The general account of the ācārya's entrustment and the circumstances of receiving the Dharma is as above.

Tripiṭaka Master Prajñā told Kūkai: "My birthplace is the kingdom of Kapiśa. In my youth I entered the Way and traveled through the five regions of India. I have always vowed to transmit the lamp and came to sojourn here. Now I wish to set sail for the eastern sea, but having no connection, my aspiration remains unfulfilled. Please take my newly translated Avataṃsaka Sūtra, the Sūtra of the Six Pāramitās, and these Sanskrit manuscripts, and bring them as offerings. I humbly pray that they may form a connection with your country and rescue the multitudes." For fear of being too lengthy, I shall not elaborate further.

The Buddha's teaching is vast and boundless, without limit or shore. To sum it up in one phrase: it lies solely in the two benefits. To seek the fruit of eternal bliss is self-benefit; to relieve the causes of empty suffering is benefiting others. To idly wish for eternal bliss without effort, one cannot obtain it. To vainly plan to remove suffering, that too is difficult. One must certainly cultivate both merit and wisdom together, and practice both concentration and insight equally. Only then can one relieve others' suffering and attain one's own bliss.

The paths of cultivating concentration are many; some are slow, some fast. To contemplate the one mind with a sharp blade is the revealed teaching. To wield the vajra of the three mysteries is the esoteric treasury. To roam in the revealed teaching through the three incalculable aeons is endlessly remote. To maintain the body in the esoteric treasury through sixteen lives is extremely brief. The most sudden within the sudden: the esoteric treasury is precisely this. Therefore, Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha abandoned royal status and forgot worldly pleasures. Emperor Daizong humbled the North Star and never wearied of it. Preceptor Nāgabodhi lived eight hundred years without aging. Dhyāna Master Chonghui crushed heterodox views and upheld what was collapsing. Is the inconceivability of the Dharma anything beyond this treasury? Those who aspire to awakening wish to hear what they have not yet heard. A verse says:

> The Dharma has no practice or concealment; it goes and comes following people.
> Like a jewel hard to obtain; once obtained, the mind opens.
> To cast away one's body for half a verse: how could one begrudge precious wealth?
> Diligently copying and transcribing, its coming has been long indeed.
> May this small merit bring peace to the nation and prosperity to the people.
> All who hear or see it may be entirely freed from affliction.

The twenty-second day of the tenth month of Daido 1. The Dharma-studying śramaṇa Kūkai who entered Tang China.
