# 般若波羅蜜多心經還源述
## Returning to the Source: An Exposition on the Prajñāpāramitā Heart Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2746

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

"In correspondence with the person, the name is received." "Practicing the deep prajñāpāramitā" is the Dharma. Although prajñā shares the same name, in depth and shallowness its substance differs. "Practice" means entering the coarse and illuminating the signless, knowing the unborn, subtly entering the natureless, and directing the mind toward realization. Therefore it says "practicing."

"Illuminated and saw that the five aggregates are all empty" identifies the thesis. "Illuminated" is the thesis. "Empty" is the meaning. The five aggregates and other objects: the characteristics of objects are empty of themselves, not through fabrication. If there were no entering, who would illuminate them? Therefore realization is the true thesis, illuminating emptiness is the real meaning. The five aggregates do not exist. All dharmas have always been empty in nature. Therefore it says "and so forth."

"And crossed beyond all suffering and distress" reveals the function. The reason there is suffering is that it is conditioned by the discriminating mind. Since the discriminating mind is itself empty, who receives suffering and distress? This is the wondrous function of discerning dharmas.

---

"Śāriputra, form is not different from emptiness; emptiness is not different from form. Form is precisely emptiness; emptiness is precisely form. Feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness and so forth are likewise." This explains the contemplation in relation to objects. The text has four distinct sections: (1) contemplating the deluded existence of conventional truth; (2) contemplating the real existence of ultimate truth; (3) contemplating the neither-real-nor-deluded existence of the truth of the first meaning; and (4) contemplating the non-non-real-non-deluded existence of the truth beyond establishment.

"Form is not different from emptiness" is the contemplation of deluded existence. It says: there is no conventional truth with grasped or grasping, or any self-nature of all dharmas. Materiality as obstruction means form. That is, the existence and nonexistence of cold and heat, contrasting characteristics and the like. Examining existence and non-existence in terms of nature, both are one's own mind. Cold and heat take shape relative to each other and are still bound together by conditions. Apart from mind-only, there is nothing. The five aggregates, lacking self-nature, are empty. Form and emptiness in all dharmas are not obstructive of existence or nonexistence, not confined to true or conventional. Because their natures are the same, it says "not different."

"Emptiness is not different from form" is the contemplation of real existence. It says: there is no nonexistence of conventional truth with grasped or grasping, or any self-nature of all dharmas. Materiality as obstruction is emptiness. Examining conditions, there is delusion; therefore we say "real." If delusion is exhausted, how can the real remain? If one abandons the deluded existence of conventional truth and clings to the real nonexistence of ultimate truth, then this emptiness is no different from the obstruction of form.

"Form is precisely emptiness" is the contemplation of neither-real-nor-deluded existence. It says: there is no existence of ultimate truth without grasped or grasping, or any self-nature of all dharmas. Materiality as obstruction is form. Examining: conventional truth has existence-of-existence and existence-of-nonexistence; ultimate truth has nonexistence-of-nonexistence and nonexistence-of-existence. If one sees the true as different from the conventional and clings to existence as different from nonexistence, then this obstructive form is precisely emptiness. Being precisely emptiness, no nature of existence arises from that mind. Provisionally speaking, it transcends all correlatives. Confronted with this conditioned arising, categories take shape relative to each other. Emptiness is the emptiness of form; form is the form of emptiness. Because form is the form of emptiness, form is not form. Because emptiness is the emptiness of form, emptiness is not emptiness. Therefore it says "form is precisely emptiness."

"Emptiness is precisely form" is the contemplation of non-non-real-non-deluded existence. It says: there is no nonexistence of the truth of the first meaning, neither existence nor nonexistence, with grasped or grasping, or any self-nature of all dharmas. Materiality as obstruction is emptiness. Examining: the truth of the first meaning arises from the truths of the conventional and the ultimate. Departing from conventional existence and ultimate nonexistence, this is called "first." If one preserves the "first," the nature of emptiness is again confined by the nature of emptiness. Therefore we know: if emptiness is truly empty, it is only a single emptiness, and one cannot speak of "emptiness" and "form." If form is truly form, it is only a single form, and one cannot speak of "form." Form is not truly form; therefore one speaks of emptiness. Emptiness is not truly empty; therefore one speaks of form. Thus within ultimate emptiness, all dharmas are established. They are merely designations, truly without self-nature. If one further clings to the mind's measure of this emptiness, it becomes form.

"Feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness and so forth are likewise" indicates by analogy that all dharmas share these four contemplations. Examining each one in accordance with principle, one naturally enters realization. Emptiness, non-emptiness, non-non-emptiness: none departs from it. Therefore Maitreya's verse says:

> All is empty, without substance; the truly existent cannot be apprehended.
> Depending on words and expressions, the characteristics of dharmas are of four kinds.

---

"Śāriputra, the empty characteristics of all dharmas are: not born, not extinguished; not defiled, not pure; not increasing, not decreasing." This is clarifying the characteristics through the realization of emptiness. The text has two parts: (1) indicating dharma-emptiness, and (2) clarifying the characteristics of realization.

"The empty characteristics of all dharmas" indicates dharma-emptiness. It means that all dharmas are without nature and are not produced by conditions. If one realizes emptiness based on existence, this constitutes slander. One cannot speak of "dharma-emptiness."

"Not born, not extinguished," and so on, clarifies the characteristics of realization. It means that all dharmas have from the beginning never been born, and now also have no extinction. If there were birth and extinction, that would be defilement. If there were no birth and extinction, that would be purity. Since there has never been birth and extinction, therefore there is neither defilement nor purity. If there were defilement and purity, that would be increase. If there were no defilement and purity, that would be decrease. Since fundamentally there is neither defilement nor purity, and now emptiness has no birth and extinction, increase and decrease: how could they arise from conditions of real knowledge? This is stated to clarify the characteristics of realization when entering prajñā. Therefore we know: correct wisdom is without characteristics, hence without birth and extinction. True knowledge is without birth, hence without defilement and purity. Pure awakening is without nature, hence without increase and decrease. This is the initial cause of beginningless existence, and the initial portion of what has no initial non-existence.

"Therefore, within emptiness there is no form; no feeling, perception, mental formations, or consciousness; no eyes, ears, nose, tongue, body, or mind; no forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tangibles, or dharmas; no eye-consciousness realm, and so on through no mind-consciousness realm; no ignorance and no extinction of ignorance, and so on through no old age and death and no extinction of old age and death; no suffering, accumulation, cessation, or path; no wisdom and no attainment." This is a comprehensive explanation adapted to those of various capacities. The text has two parts: (1) a general explanation for the five vehicles, and (2) a specific display of the three noble ones.

For the general explanation of the five vehicles, the text has four parts: (1) no five aggregates, (2) no six sense-objects, (3) no six sense-faculties, and (4) no six consciousnesses.

For the specific display of the three noble ones, the text has three parts: (1) clarifying the middle vehicle, (2) revealing the lesser path, and (3) explaining the great Dharma.

The middle vehicle has two levels: (1) no realm of the middle vehicle, which is "no ignorance and so on through no old age and death"; and (2) no contemplation of the middle vehicle, which is "no extinction of ignorance and so on through no extinction of old age and death."

The lesser path has two levels: (1) no realm of the lesser vehicle, which is "no suffering and accumulation"; and (2) no contemplation of the lesser vehicle, which is "no cessation and path."

The great Dharma has two levels: (1) no contemplation of the Great Vehicle, which is "no wisdom"; and (2) no realm of the Great Vehicle, which is "no attainment." Therefore the *Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra* says:

> The vehicles of humans, devas, śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and tathāgatas:
> As long as mind still turns, the vehicles are not ultimate.
> If that mind is utterly extinguished, there is no vehicle and no one who rides.
> With no vehicle established, I call it the One Vehicle.

Examining: from the beginning with form and feeling through the end with wisdom and attainment, all are the mental measures of the five vehicles. None is other than the single gate of true principle. Therefore within the realization of emptiness, there are none of these characteristics. As for the explanation of names and elucidation of substance, that belongs to a separate treatise and would be adding feet to the snake. There is nothing to grasp here.

---

"Because there is nothing to attain, the bodhisattva relies on the prajñāpāramitā, and the mind is without obstruction. Because there is no obstruction, there is no fear, and being far removed from inverted dream-thinking, one reaches ultimate nirvāṇa." This demonstrates the cause by presenting the practice. It says: within ultimate emptiness there are no dharmas; this is "because there is nothing to attain." If there were something to attain, then the dharmas would not be empty. Since the dharmas are empty, what is there to attain? Because there is nothing to attain, the bodhisattva relies on the prajñāpāramitā. According to the principle being expounded, this passage should contain these words, but they were omitted in translation.

"Relying on the prajñāpāramitā, the mind is without obstruction" means that correct wisdom discerns dharmas, transcending existence and nonexistence, birth and extinction. Since these have fundamentally never been so, increase and decrease: from what conditions could they arise? Therefore it says "the mind is without obstruction."

"Because there is no obstruction, there is no fear, and being far removed from inverted dream-thinking, one reaches ultimate nirvāṇa" means that accumulated practice has its basis, the mind penetrates without abiding, progressively transcending until it reaches the utmost limit of reality. Comprehending the seeds of consciousness as without self-nature, one mirrors characteristics and perception as mind-only. Fear does not come from external objects; inversion still depends on internal imagination. When imagination and objects are quiescent and extinguished, this is provisionally called nirvāṇa. When fear and arising occur, this is forcibly called inversion. If one realizes that arising comes from quiescent extinction and that quiescent extinction comes from arising, then dream-thinking and nirvāṇa are both left behind, and inversion and ultimacy both vanish. This is the most pure awakening. Because of these three causes, within emptiness there can be no dharmas of the various vehicles.

---

"The buddhas of the three times, relying on the prajñāpāramitā, attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi." This adduces proof and demonstrates the fruit. The text has two parts: (1) adducing proof, which is the buddhas of the three times; and (2) demonstrating the fruit, which is samyak-saṃbodhi.

"The buddhas of the three times" means "pure awakening." The true buddha of the knowledge of all seeds transcends in substance past and future. It is the deluded consciousness of present sentient beings that of itself sees birth, abiding, and extinction. Conforming to worldly consciousness, it provisionally speaks of the three times. "Anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi" is translated here as "unsurpassed, correct, and universal knowing and awakening." "Unsurpassed" is above the five vehicles. "Correct" refers to the wisdom that accords with principle, entering the wondrous principle of the three kinds of non-nature. "Universal" refers to the wisdom that accords with measure, perceiving the coarse characteristics of the three kinds of existent nature. "Knowing" refers to non-discriminating wisdom, which severs the two kinds of unknowing. "Awakening" refers to the wisdom subsequent to non-discrimination, which surpasses sleep and dreams. This is the true buddha. Therefore the *Mahāyānasaṃgraha* says: "Like waking from a sleeping dream, like a lotus flower opening. Whether defiled or undefiled, when the two kinds of stupefied sleep are exhausted, one is called a buddha."

---

"Therefore know that the prajñāpāramitā is the great spiritual mantra, the great radiant mantra, the unsurpassed mantra, the unequaled mantra. It can remove all suffering. It is true and not false." This is the comprehensive conclusion and attestation. The text has two parts: (1) the comprehensive conclusion, and (2) the attestation.

"The great spiritual mantra" and so on is the comprehensive conclusion. According to the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra*, there are separately four mantras, all in Sanskrit. Here, as inner protection, they are beyond words. Examining: "great spiritual mantra" means comprehending consciousness-only. "Great radiant mantra" means comprehending mentation-only. "Unsurpassed mantra" means comprehending mind-only. "Unequaled mantra" means comprehending wisdom-only. If one does not comprehend consciousness-only, external objects are difficult to universally know. If one illuminates consciousness-only, external objects are easily known and universally comprehended. Therefore it is called "great."

Furthermore: existence and nonexistence being unfathomable, it is forcibly called "spiritual mantra," which totally retains principle and universally governs. Therefore it is called the "great spiritual mantra." If one does not comprehend mentation-only, characteristics and perception are without distinction, and deliberation is needed to understand. If one knows mentation-only, the equal wisdom constantly arises and conditioned contemplation is always bright. Therefore it is called the "great radiant mantra." If one does not comprehend mind-only, the seeds of consciousness undergo birth and extinction, perfuming the storehouse in return. If one realizes mind-only, the seeds of consciousness are without nature, the mirror-wisdom manifests, and nothing can defile it. Therefore it is called the "unsurpassed mantra." If one does not comprehend wisdom-only, although the causal seeds are pure, ultimately they give rise to further birth. If one enters wisdom-only, the nature of causes has from the beginning never arisen, and wisdom and consciousness have never from the start come into being. One perfectly pure awakening eternally illuminates without remainder. Firm as vajra, its substance is only the eternally abiding. Therefore it is called the "unequaled mantra."

"It can remove all suffering. It is true and not false." This is the attestation. Examining: correct wisdom discerns dharmas, utterly transcending the mind-source. Departing from the self-substance of existence and nonexistence, one instantly enters the realization of direct insight. Characteristics and perception have from the beginning never existed. Causal nature is by its own nature nonexistent. Conditioned illumination and both are extinguished; consciousness and wisdom are both severed. How could the crooked and deluded disturb it? How could suffering and distress encroach upon it?

"Therefore he proclaimed the prajñāpāramitā mantra, and spoke the mantra thus:"

*[Dhāraṇī]* Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā.

This is the outer protection. The text has two levels: (1) the introduction, and (2) the recitation of the mantra. Its meaning is easily understood and needs no further explanation. Examining: "mantra" means "retention." Retention is of four kinds: (1) dharma, (2) meaning, (3) phrasing, and (4) principle. The inner protection is the comprehensive retention of principle. The outer protection is the comprehensive retention of phrasing. But this phrasing pertains to the great enlightenment of the same substance, which only it can fully exhaust. Even bodhisattvas of the ten stages have not fully penetrated it. Attaining the fundamental and practicing the fundamental, it cannot be translated. Nevertheless, its general meaning has three aspects: (1) it expresses the extremely profound prajñā, (2) it names the great and powerful celestial spirits, and (3) it disperses wandering ghosts and demons. The buddhas' compassionate and wholesome secret intention lies within it. Therefore reciting and upholding it surely invites numinous assistance. This is the outer protection. The verse says:

> The ever-abiding Dharma that all buddhas take as teacher has neither form, emptiness, true, nor conventional nature.
> Having now heard it, I reverently uphold it, and universally vow that all sentient beings may trust and understand.

This sūtra was originally extracted from the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā*, like the separate circulation of the *Universal Gate* chapter from the *Lotus Sūtra*. Tripiṭaka Dharma Master Xuanzang constantly upheld it and received numinous responses. Therefore he made a separate translation to circulate it. If a person purifies the mind, bathes, puts on clean clothing, sits upright with proper posture, and recites it five hundred times in a single session, this will eliminate the ninety-five kinds of heterodox paths. Wholesome wishes will follow the mind, and all suffering and distress will be crossed over.
