# 因明入正理論
## Introduction to Logic

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1630

Composed by Bodhisattva Śaṅkarasvāmin (商羯羅主菩薩). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘).

---

> Proof and refutation,
> Together with their semblances, serve only to make others understand.
> Perception and inference,
> Together with their semblances, serve only for self-understanding.

Thus are all the essential points of logical treatises comprehensively included.

Herein, the multiple terms of thesis and so forth are called "proof." Through the multiple terms of thesis, reason, and example, one reveals to questioners meanings they have not yet understood. Herein, "thesis" refers to a proposition whose nature is a distinction made by a predicate that is well-established applied to a subject that is well-established, being that which one freely wishes to establish. This is called a thesis. For example, someone establishes: "Sound is impermanent."

The reason has three characteristics. What are these three? They are: the property of pervading the subject of the thesis, the property of being definitely present in similar instances, and the property of being universally absent from dissimilar instances. What are called "similar instances" and "dissimilar instances"? Instances that share an equal meaning with the property to be established are called similar instances. For example, in establishing "impermanent," a pot and such, being impermanent, are called similar instances. Dissimilar instances are those in which the property to be established is absent: "If something is permanent, it is seen to be not-produced, such as space and the like." Herein, the property of being-produced, or the property of being brought about by effort without interval, is the property pervading the subject of the thesis. The property of being definitely present in similar instances and universally absent from dissimilar instances constitutes the reason for impermanence and so forth.

Examples are of two kinds: first, similar examples; second, dissimilar examples. A similar example is one that, with respect to a given case, demonstrates the definite presence of the reason in similar instances of the thesis: "Whatever is produced is seen to be impermanent, like a pot and the like." A dissimilar example is one that, with respect to a given case, states the absence of the property to be established and the universal non-existence of the reason: "Whatever is permanent is seen to be not-produced, like space and the like." Herein, "permanent" expresses "not impermanent," and "not-produced" expresses "the absence of being-produced," just as what is non-existent is called "non-existent."

Having explained the thesis and so forth, when these multiple terms bring understanding to others, they are called "proof." For example, the statement "sound is impermanent" is the thesis-statement. "Because it has the property of being produced" is the reason-statement. "Whatever is produced is seen to be impermanent, like a pot and the like" is the statement of agreement with similar instances. "Whatever is permanent is seen to be not-produced, like space" is the statement of exclusion. Only these three components are called "proof."

Although one may wish to establish something, when it contradicts perception and the like, it is called a semblance of a thesis. These are: contradiction with perception, contradiction with inference, contradiction with one's own doctrine, contradiction with common knowledge, self-contradiction, a predicate not well-established, a subject not well-established, both not well-established, and redundant agreement. Among these, "contradiction with perception" is, for example, saying "sound is not heard." "Contradiction with inference" is, for example, saying "a pot and such are permanent." "Contradiction with one's own doctrine" is, for example, when a Vaiśeṣika master establishes "sound is permanent." "Contradiction with common knowledge" is, for example, saying "the hare-bearer is not the moon, because it exists," or saying "a human skull-bone is pure, because it is a part of a sentient being, like a conch shell." "Self-contradiction" is, for example, saying "my mother is a barren woman." "A predicate not well-established" is, for example, when a Buddhist, facing a Sāṃkhya master, establishes "sound is destructible." "A subject not well-established" is, for example, when a Sāṃkhya master, facing a Buddhist, says "the self is thought." "Both not well-established" is, for example, when a Vaiśeṣika master, facing a Buddhist, establishes "the self, by means of conjunction, is a cause." "Redundant agreement" is, for example, saying "sound is heard." Such multiple terms are faults of semblance of thesis because they are gates for excluding the intrinsic characteristics of dharmas, because they cannot validly be established, and because the establishment is fruitless.

Having explained the semblance of thesis, we shall now explain the semblance of reason. Non-establishment, indeterminacy, and contradiction are called the semblance of reason.

Non-establishment is of four kinds: first, non-establishment for both parties; second, non-establishment for one party; third, doubtful non-establishment; fourth, non-establishment of the locus. For example, in establishing "sound is impermanent" and so forth, if one says "because it is the object of sight," this is non-establishment for both parties. "Because it has the property of being produced," when addressed to a proponent of sound-manifestation, is non-establishment for one party. When doubt arises as to whether something is mist or the like, and one says, to establish that "fire exists through the combination of the great elements," this is doubtful non-establishment. "Space truly exists, because it is the locus of qualities," when addressed to a proponent of the non-existence of space, is non-establishment of the locus.

Indeterminacy is of six kinds: first, common; second, uncommon; third, partially present in similar instances and universally present in dissimilar instances; fourth, partially present in dissimilar instances and universally present in similar instances; fifth, partially present in both; sixth, counter-balanced determination. Among these, "common" is, for example, saying "sound is permanent, because it has the property of being knowable." Since both permanent and impermanent things share this reason, it is indeterminate. Is it the case that, like a pot and the like, because of the property of being knowable, sound is impermanent? Or is it the case that, like space and the like, because of the property of being knowable, sound is permanent? "Uncommon" is, for example, saying "sound is permanent, because it has the property of being heard." Since both permanent and impermanent things exclude this reason, and since apart from permanent and impermanent things there are no other existents, this is a reason of doubt. To what, then, is this property of being heard similar? "Partially present in similar instances and universally present in dissimilar instances" is, for example, saying "sound is not brought about by effort without interval, because of impermanence." Herein, in the thesis "not brought about by effort without interval," lightning and space are similar instances. This impermanence is present in lightning and absent in space. In the thesis "not brought about by effort without interval," a pot and the like are dissimilar instances, and in those it is universally present. Since this reason takes both lightning and the pot as instances of agreement, it too is indeterminate. Is it the case that, like a pot and the like, because of impermanence, it is brought about by effort without interval? Or is it the case that, like lightning and the like, because of impermanence, it is not brought about by effort without interval? "Partially present in dissimilar instances and universally present in similar instances" is, for example, the thesis-statement "sound is brought about by effort without interval, because of impermanence." In the thesis "brought about by effort without interval," a pot and the like are similar instances, and impermanence is universally present in them. Lightning and space are dissimilar instances; impermanence is present in part of them, namely lightning, but absent in space. Therefore, as before, this too is indeterminate. "Partially present in both" is, for example, saying "sound is permanent, because of non-tangibility." Herein, in the thesis "permanent," space and atoms are similar instances. Non-tangibility is present in space but absent in atoms. A pot and pleasure are dissimilar instances; it is present in pleasure but absent in the pot. Therefore, since this reason takes both pleasure and space as instances of agreement, it too is called indeterminate. "Counter-balanced determination" is, for example, the thesis-statement "sound is impermanent, because of the property of being produced, like a pot and the like," while another establishes "sound is permanent, because of the property of being heard, like the nature of sound." Since both of these are reasons of doubt, both are called indeterminate.

Contradiction is of four kinds: a reason contradicting the intrinsic nature of the predicate, a reason contradicting a distinction of the predicate, a reason contradicting the intrinsic nature of the subject, and a reason contradicting a distinction of the subject. Among these, "a reason contradicting the intrinsic nature of the predicate" is, for example, saying "sound is permanent, because of the property of being produced, or because of the property of being brought about by effort without interval." This reason is present only in dissimilar instances; therefore it is contradictory. "A reason contradicting a distinction of the predicate" is, for example, saying "the eye and so forth must serve the purpose of another, because of the property of being an assemblage, like a bed and the like." Just as this reason can establish that the eye and so forth must serve the purpose of another, so too it can establish the contradictory distinction of the established predicate that the assemblage serves the purpose of another, since beds and the like are used by another who is an assemblage. "A reason contradicting the intrinsic nature of the subject" is, for example, saying "existence is neither substance, nor quality, nor action, because it possesses a single substance, because it possesses quality and action, like universal-and-particular." Just as this reason can establish the negation of substance and the rest, so too it can establish the negation of existence, since both are equally determined. "A reason contradicting a distinction of the subject" is as follows: this same reason, applied to the distinction of the subject of the above thesis that functions as a condition for existence, can also establish the contradictory distinction of functioning as a condition for non-existence, since it negates substance and the rest with equal determination. Having explained the semblance of reason, we shall now explain the semblance of example.

The semblance of a similar example is of five kinds: first, non-establishment of the proving property; second, non-establishment of the property to be proved; third, both not established; fourth, absence of application; fifth, reversed application. The semblance of a dissimilar example is also of five kinds: first, non-exclusion of the property to be proved; second, non-exclusion of the proving property; third, both not excluded; fourth, non-separation; fifth, reversed separation.

"Non-establishment of the proving property" is, for example, saying "sound is permanent, because of non-tangibility. Whatever is non-tangible is seen to be permanent, like an atom." However, regarding the atom, the property to be established, permanence, is present, but the proving property, non-tangibility, is absent, since atoms are tangible in nature. "Non-establishment of the property to be proved" is, for example, saying "like a cognition." However, regarding all cognitions, the proving property, non-tangibility, is present, but the property to be established, permanence, is absent, since all cognitions are impermanent. "Both not established" is further of two kinds: existent and non-existent. Saying "like a pot" is an existent case of both not established. Saying "like space," when addressed to a proponent of the non-existence of space, is a non-existent case of both not established. "Absence of application" is where there is no synthesis, but only the joint presentation of the proving property and the property to be proved in a pot and the like. For example, saying: "In a pot, the property of being produced and the property of impermanence are both seen." "Reversed application" is where one should say "whatever is produced is impermanent," but instead says in reverse, "whatever is impermanent is produced." These are called the semblances of similar examples.

Among the semblances of dissimilar examples, "non-exclusion of the property to be proved" is, for example, someone saying "whatever is impermanent is seen to be tangible, like an atom." Since, regarding the atom, the established property of permanence is not excluded (for the proponent holds atoms to be permanent), the proving property of non-tangibility is also absent. "Non-exclusion of the proving property" is, for example, saying "like action." This only excludes the property to be proved but does not exclude the proving property, since the proponent holds that all actions are non-tangible. "Both not excluded" is, when addressed to a proponent of existence, saying "like space." Since space neither excludes permanence nor excludes non-tangibility, because space is held to be both permanent and non-tangible. "Non-separation" is, for example, saying "like a pot: the property of impermanence and the property of tangibility are both seen." "Reversed separation" is, for example, saying "whatever is tangible is impermanent."

Such semblance terms of thesis, reason, and example are not valid proof.

Furthermore, for self-understanding, one should know that there are only two valid means of knowledge: perception and inference. Herein, "perception" means that which is free from conceptual construction. If there is correct cognition regarding objects such as visible form that is free from all conceptual construction involving names, classes, and so forth, and which arises distinctly for each particular, it is called perception. "Inference" means observing an object by relying on marks. Marks are of three kinds, as previously explained. By taking them as the basis, correct cognition arises regarding the object to be inferred, and one ascertains the existence of fire or impermanence and so forth. This is called inference. In these two means of knowledge, the cognition itself is called the result, because it has the characteristic of proof. Just as something that functions and thereby becomes manifest is also called a means of knowledge.

A discriminating cognition that apprehends objects differently from what they are is called a semblance of perception. That is, all cognitions that arise with discrimination, apprehending pots, clothing, and the like, are called semblances of perception because they do not take the intrinsic characteristics of objects as their domain.

If cognition arises from a semblance of a reason as its prior condition, and if cognition arises regarding a semblance of the object to be inferred, this is called a semblance of inference. Semblances of reasons are of many kinds, as previously explained. Using these as the basis, cognitions arise regarding semblances of what is to be inferred. Since they do not yield correct understanding, they are called semblances of inference.

Furthermore, correctly revealing the faults of another's proof is called refutation. That is, revealing the faults of deficiency in the initial proof, the fault of the thesis, the fault of a non-established reason, the fault of an indeterminate reason, the fault of a contradictory reason, and the faults of examples. Statements that reveal these and make the questioner understand are called refutation. If one falsely reveals faults in another's proof, this is called a semblance of refutation. That is, statements that purport to reveal deficiency in a complete proof, that allege thesis-faults in a faultless thesis, that allege non-establishment in an established reason, that allege indeterminacy in a determinate reason, that allege contradiction in a non-contradictory reason, or that allege example-faults in a faultless example. Such statements are called semblances of refutation because they cannot reveal the faults of the other's thesis, since it has no faults. Let this matter rest here.

> Having proclaimed but a few essential points
> To lay the foundation for beginners:
> What therein is right or wrong
> Is subtly discerned elsewhere.

## Postface to the Introduction to Logic

The Introduction to Logic is the threshold for staunchly arguing and establishing one's thesis, for crushing the heterodox and revealing the orthodox. "Reason" speaks of illuminating the real. "Logic" makes manifest the revelation of principle. "Introduction" means approaching the root. "Right" means departing from the wrong. "Treatise" means comparing words to reach their import and carefully examining the essentials.

Long ago, when the Tathāgata manifested beneath the Bodhi Tree, this teaching was fully present. When he was elevated at the Deer Park, this tradition was set in motion. The six heretical teachers bowed their heads and held their tongues; the ten ṛṣis sought their lives and knew where to turn. Not that heavenly spirits dimmed their light, or that streams of heterodoxy stirred their waves. Those of identical evil intentions were indeed fierce and manifold. Therefore Vasubandhu propagated the great achievement in the past, and Dignāga continued the legacy of fragrant teaching in the future, raising the true and eliminating the false, leveling difficulties and unraveling tangles. His divine accomplishment was supreme, and the details are in other treatises.

Then came Bodhisattva Iśvarasena, born as a sub-sage of outstanding genius, who broadly researched and deeply analyzed, continuing the former tradition. He selected the essential and the subtle, adapted to the capacities of the time, opened eight gates to benefit both self and others, trimmed the five components to fix three members. His meaning is concise yet clear, his text is brief yet manifest. The talented of the western regions studied it with ever-deepening admiration. Without first treading this universal standard, one was not qualified to participate in higher discourse.

The Great Tang Emperor, arising at the proper time, opened the sagely era, wielding the golden mirror and turning the golden wheel. Succeeding to the brilliant mandate, he sounded the jade drum and regulated the jade candle, broadly disseminating the profound transformation and renewing the constant institutions. He lit the torch of wisdom to illuminate the city of darkness and moored the boat of knowledge to ferry the ocean of suffering.

Our Tripiṭaka Dharma Master Xuanzang, with divine insight fresh and keen, and lofty integrity surpassing all, practiced the four diligences as though he could never catch up, and gazed upon the three traditions with eager inquiry. The former masters of the Chinese lands each excelled in their own specialties. Whatever met his eye, he would investigate its subtlety; whatever entered his mind, he would exhaust its wonder. He lamented that the holy traces had grown distant and the semblance-teaching had declined. Never once did he face a corrupt text without sighing, nor touch a doubtful meaning without deep regret. He gazed toward the Onion Mountains with high aspiration and set his sights on Vulture Peak for a distant journey.

Then, braving dangers and perils, seeking teachers and inquiring into the Way, his travels reached Kaśmīra in northern India. There he met the great master of treatises, Saṅghayaśas, and resolved his doubts regarding the eight treasuries and investigated the five vehicles. The master, seeing that the great principles were deeply rooted and admiring his prior cultivation, taught him only the marvelous technique of Buddhist logic, which had not yet been explained to him. Upon hearing the Sanskrit sounds, the ice of doubt melted in his heart. Later, in Magadha of central India, he met Bodhisattva Śīlabhadra and further broadened his examples, expanding by analogy and refining with gentleness. Thereupon he visited all the remaining sacred sites and made thorough inquiry into the remaining achievements. Though he encountered those of the copper-bellied tradition, his free-flowing eloquence was unsurpassed, and the heterodox were toppled like grass before the wind. This was partly due to this treatise. He then returned, spreading the universal transformation, and luminous blessings converged.

In the autumn of the twenty-first year of the Zhenguan era, on the sixth day of the eighth month, the translation was completed at Hongfu Monastery by imperial command. The scribe who recorded and verified the text was Śramaṇa Mingjun of Hongfu Monastery. The one who verified the Sanskrit was Śramaṇa Xuanmo of Hongfu Monastery. The one who corrected the characters was Śramaṇa Xuanying of Dazongchi Monastery. Those who verified the great meaning were Śramaṇa Daohong of Dazongchi Monastery, Śramaṇa Mingyan of Shiji Monastery, Śramaṇa Huigui of Luohan Monastery, Śramaṇa Faxiang of Baochang Monastery, Śramaṇa Wenbei of Hongfu Monastery, Śramaṇa Daoshen of Fajiang Monastery in Kuozhou, and Śramaṇa Shentai of Qiyan Monastery in Puzhou. The imperial supervisor of the translation was Xu Jingzong, Silver-Green Grandee of Splendor, Acting Left Advisor to the Heir Apparent, Earl of Gaoyang County.

The Tripiṭaka Dharma Master, in his humility responding to beings, opened this profound gate. The ocean of meaning is vast and sourceless; the peak of words is steep and hard to look up to. The outstanding talents of foreign lands all received his personal transmission, and their recorded accounts of the profound text all circulate in the world. I, in my lack of talent, presumptuously appointed to verify the text, happened upon this excellent occasion. I have ventured to record the events of the time, to bequeath them to future generations, so that this superior model and great cause may never be extinguished in ages to come.
