# 佛說解憂經
## Sūtra of Resolving Sorrow, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0804

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmadeva (法天)

---

> I bow my head in homage to the Perfectly Awakened One,
> Who can ferry beings across the boundless ocean of great suffering.
> Ever with amṛta he nourishes the living;
> To the one who grants nirvāṇa I make prostration.
> I bow my head in homage to the treasury of the right Dharma,
> Which can halt the boundless causes of suffering,
> Revealing faults to benefit all beings;
> To that which grants quiescence I make prostration.
> I bow my head in homage to the great bhikṣus,
> Who become a field of merit for the world,
> Setting forth and diligently cultivating the cause of peace and joy;
> To those who skillfully sever saṃsāra I make prostration.

> Separation from the beloved is the greatest suffering;
> The fire of sorrow burns unceasingly.
> If one wishes to put the mind at ease,
> Sit upright and practice contemplation.
> Just as flocks of birds and herds of beasts
> Briefly gather then each flies away,
> So too with beings in birth-and-death:
> Why then harbor grief and sorrow?
> If I alone were subject to death
> While all others lived forever,
> The pain of parting would be unbearable,
> And kin would have cause to weep.
> But in the great wheel of the three realms,
> There is no one exempt from this.
> All equally receive impermanence:
> Why then harbor grief and sorrow?
> If a person gives rise to craving and love,
> Who knows the fire of craving will burn?
> Like the yak who loves its tail
> And is killed by hunters because of it.
> The people of the world are mostly drunk and deluded,
> Wishing to escape the perilous evil paths,
> Yet though they exhaust every expedient of the mind,
> They cannot gain freedom from suffering.
> Like the wild deer
> Constantly pursued by the lion,
> In the end it cannot escape:
> Why then harbor grief and sorrow?
> On the great earth and in the heavens above,
> In the three realms and the four kinds of birth,
> Never has it been seen or heard
> That any being does not receive impermanence.
> It is like wildfire on mountain and field
> That burns the grasses and trees:
> It does not spare the flowering orchard
> But turns all to ash at once.
> Foolish and deluded sentient beings
> Give rise to inverted false imaginings.
> The body is bound by the rope of impermanence,
> And no one can untie it.
> Even in the Brahmā-world of the form realm,
> Where the flavor of dhyāna is taken as bliss,
> It is still like a tree on the riverbank:
> Before long, wind and water destroy it.
> A hundred billion wheel-turning kings,
> Ten million Śakras, lord of the devas:
> Thought after thought they are impermanent,
> Like wind blowing out a lamp's flame.
> The great sages of the past,
> With five supernatural powers and minds at ease,
> Traveling back and forth through the sky:
> Even they were seized by impermanence.
> The vajra body, firm and indestructible,
> Still demonstrates cessation in the end.
> The consciousness of ordinary beings is like a plantain:
> How could it hope to endure?
> The great earth, Mount Sumeru,
> And the four great oceans:
> When the kalpa ends, even these return to emptiness.
> How much more so the destinies of sentient beings!
> The nāga dwells deep in the ocean,
> Constantly surrounded by its retinue,
> Yet the garuḍa bird can devour it:
> The suffering of parting is just like this.
> If a person were to go to another realm,
> Hoping to escape impermanence,
> It would be like entering the mouth
> Of a great makara fish to seek safety.
> Thus in the desire realm and the form realm,
> And even in the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception,
> There has never been a single thing
> That was not swallowed by impermanence.
> Only the Perfectly Awakened One
> Is the true place of refuge.
> Receive this with faith, listen carefully:
> It can resolve all grief and affliction.

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada.

At that time the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus: "Sentient beings are without number, and saṃsāra is without limit. Like ants going in circles, there is no end. Sentient beings are obstructed by craving, love, and ignorance, as if sunk in mud, unable to get out. The past beings who have gone around in saṃsāra are beyond counting. Bhikṣus! If all the earth of the great land were gathered in one place and formed into mud pellets the size of beans, and for each parent, child, and descendant of every sentient being since beginningless time one pellet were set down, the pellets would be exhausted, but the number of parents, children, and descendants could not be exhausted. Bhikṣus! Thus the sentient beings who have cycled through boundless saṃsāra, obstructed by craving, love, and ignorance, inverted and sunk in the mud of desire, revolving through birth-and-death, are beyond counting. Therefore I instruct you to learn to sever saṃsāra."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! The bones of such persons who have cycled through saṃsāra, if piled together, would be like Mount Sumeru, neither decaying nor rotting. Thus those non-learner śrāvakas who have realized the four noble truths know that this suffering is truly suffering, and that suffering is to be extinguished, and they realize the noble truth of suffering. But those persons who see these piled corpses and bones and do not know that this is suffering are unable to extinguish the afflictions of the three realms. If one extinguishes the afflictions of the three realms and realizes the non-empty Dharma of the srotāpanna, one will certainly attain bodhi. Within seven births among devas and humans, one severs saṃsāra and eliminates the afflictions. When the seven births are fulfilled, the noble truths are directly present, right-view wisdom extinguishes all remaining delusion, and one arrives at the quiescence of nirvāṇa. Only then does that person attain liberation from the suffering of saṃsāra!"

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! It is like the members of a household who delight in one another. Because of their craving and love they broadly create various karma, revolving through birth-and-death. It is like a wild elephant sunk in a mud pit, with no prospect of getting out. Furthermore, such family members, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, parents who raise and nurture them all like their own children, in future lives, according to their respective retributions, each differs: some become servants, some become enemies, mutually resenting, deceiving, insulting, beating, and cursing one another. Some become animals, mutually devouring one another, or being killed. In such various ways they revolve through the destinies, like the assembly of the seven sages, sometimes gathering, sometimes scattering, or like raindrops forming water-bubbles, sometimes arising, sometimes ceasing. Thus sentient beings, through the great power of foolishness, deluded and inverted, do not understand saṃsāra. Toward their families they falsely give rise to thoughts of happiness, creating various karma, and not for a single moment do they dwell in purity.

"Furthermore, these beings, through beginningless saṃsāra, entering the hells, have drunk molten copper in quantities exceeding the waters of the great ocean. Like pigs and dogs eating filth, the amount would be like Mount Sumeru. Furthermore, the tears shed by these beings in separation and loving attachment through birth-and-death would be like the waters of the ocean. Furthermore, the heads severed as these beings killed one another, if piled up, would surpass the Brahmā heavens, and the pus and blood consumed by worms would also be like the ocean. Furthermore, in the hungry ghost destiny, because of past stinginess and greed, they suffer hunger and thirst; if they encounter food and drink, it turns to smoke and flame. When their ghost retribution is exhausted, even if they are born among humans, they are poor, hungry, and afflicted, and their various sufferings are beyond telling.

"Furthermore, these beings, through cultivating merit and wholesome deeds, are born in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven and other such supremely excellent realms, constantly enjoying happiness. Yet their craving and love blaze fiercely like fire burning dry grass. When the retribution of their lifespan is exhausted, they immediately fall into the evil destinies. It is like a flying bird whose two wings are broken: in an instant it crashes to the ground and suffers all manner of pain. Therefore, all of you, learn to sever saṃsāra and swiftly seek liberation."

The Buddha said: "Bhikṣus! Just as rivers, the great earth, sun, moon, stars, Mount Sumeru, and all the settlements and worlds, so long as they have not been destroyed, continue to endure and remain in the world: so too this sūtra, so long as the world has not been destroyed, will endure in the Dharma. Why do you think this is? It is for the sake of bringing all sentient beings to the cessation of saṃsāra."

The bhikṣus, having heard this, received it with faith and practiced accordingly.
