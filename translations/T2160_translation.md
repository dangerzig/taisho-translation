# Catalog of Items Brought from Yuèzhōu by the Great Master of Transmitting the Teaching

## *Dengyō Daishi Shōrai Esshū Roku* (傳教大師將來越州錄)

Taishō 2160

Compiled by Saichō (*Saichō*, 最澄) of Japan. A bibliographic catalog.

*Note: This is a catalog compiled by the Japanese Tendai founder Saichō (767–822), listing texts and dharma teachings he obtained during his journey to Tang China in 804–805 CE. Saichō studied both Tiantai doctrine under Dàosuì at Mount Tiāntái and esoteric Buddhism under Shùnxiǎo at Lóngxīng Monastery in Yuèzhōu (modern Shaoxing). This catalog lists the Yuèzhōu acquisitions, including esoteric initiations and ritual implements.*

Catalog of the Japanese dharma-seeking monk Saichō.

In total, two hundred and thirty sections in four hundred and sixty fascicles.

Dharma teachings sought at Táizhōu (*Táizhōu*, 台州): in total, one hundred and twenty-eight sections in three hundred and forty-five fascicles.

The above dharma teachings for recitation and models of offering implements were obtained at Lóngxīng Monastery (*Lóngxīng Sì*, 龍興寺) in the Yuè prefectural capital. He went to the quarters of Master Shùnxiǎo (*Shùnxiǎo*, 順曉). Saichō, together with Gishin (*Gishin*, 義真), followed the master to the ritual platform on the Eastern Peak of Hújìng Mountain (*Hújìng Dōngfēng Shān*, 湖鏡東峯山). The master led the two monks in preparing the ritual platform, then led them into the consecration maṇḍala of the five divisions and personally bestowed upon them the true-word dharma. He also conferred the consecration water of true words. They then copied the above dharma teachings for recitation and the models of offering implements. The collation and verification were completed.

Saichō and the others deeply received the compassionate assistance of the Administrator (*Lángzhōng*, 郎中). The previous year, at Táizhōu, the two monks received the precepts of both the Great and Small Vehicles. They also copied several hundred fascicles of texts. This year, proceeding to the Yuè prefectural capital, the two monks entered the consecration platform of the five divisions. They also copied dharma teachings for recitation. The total of all items obtained, before and after, is two hundred and thirty sections in four hundred and sixty fascicles. The essential work being completed, we now return to our homeland. We respectfully request the official seal of this prefecture. For students from foreign lands, this will serve to show the origins of our dharma-seeking.

Thus, the merit of the Administrator's transmission of the dharma will bring blessings in the present and the future. The virtue of all beings who hear the dharma may be expected to bear fruit in wondrous awakening. We humbly pray that the Administrator may soon ascend to the position of the Three Terraces, and ultimately realize the fruit of the Three Virtues. Thereafter, may this extend vertically through the three realms and spread horizontally across the ten directions. May all sentient beings of the six destinies and four modes of birth together enter the gate of meditation and roam the garden of wisdom. Whether through faith or slander, may self and other equally obtain benefit.

The thirteenth day, *xīnsì*, of the fifth month, *jǐsì* new moon, of the twenty-first year of Zhēnyuán (*Zhēnyuán*, 貞元) of the Great Tang [805 CE], the year *yǐyǒu*.

Recorded by Saichō, dharma-seeking monk of Japan.

Gishin, dharma-seeking interpreter-monk of Japan.

Confucius said: "I have heard that in the West there is a sage. His teaching takes purity and non-action as its foundation, and non-attachment and non-clinging as its wonder. In transforming people, he fully perfects all virtues and achieves perfect illumination."

The ācārya Saichō possesses innate wisdom by nature and comes from a land of propriety and righteousness. Traveling ten thousand *lǐ* to seek the dharma, he regarded danger as level ground. He did not shrink from hardship and toil; divine power protected him. To the south he ascended the summit of Mount Tiāntái; to the west he sailed the waters of Mirror Lake. He plumbed the dharma gate of Zhìzhě (*Zhìzhě*, 智者, Tiantai Zhiyi) and fathomed the divine secrets of consecration. He may truly be called a dragon-elephant of the dharma gate, a blue lotus emerging from the pond. He shall bring this Great Vehicle to transmit it in his home country. He seeks this official seal to serve as his authorization.

Previously, the Prefect of Tái (*Lù Táizhōu*, 陸台州) had already provided an attestation. Therefore I record what I have witnessed and set it forth in straightforward writing.

The fifteenth day of the fifth month of the twenty-first year of Zhēnyuán of the Great Tang [805 CE]. Written by Zhèng Shěnzé (*Zhèng Shěnzé*, 鄭審則), Grand Master for Court Audiences, Military Commissioner of Míngzhōu (*Míngzhōu*, 明州), Pillar of State, of Xíngyáng (*Xíngyáng*, 滎陽).

## Preface to the Mountain School's Catalog of Items Brought Back

In the past, when our Three Sages entered Tang to seek the dharma, each had a catalog of items brought back. These were all previously published and circulated in the world. That this woodblock edition is now again specially produced is to ensure that the original handwriting does not perish. The flourishing of our national culture, whether among the clergy or the laity, was never greater than in the sagely reigns of Enryaku (*Enryaku*, 延曆) and Kōnin (*Kōnin*, 弘仁). As for the refinement and subtlety of the calligraphy, even calligraphers from the late Tang onward truly could not match it. Everyone knows this. The Song people's praise, "almost equal to the Two Wangs," is no exaggeration. If that was the case in those times, how much more so now, when several thousand characters survive: they may truly be called a rare treasure of the age. Nevertheless, if they are kept hidden in storage, not only will few people know of them, but there is a danger they may not survive intact for later generations. How can this not be a matter of regret?

In the summer of this *kanoto-mi* year, the Dharma Master Chō (*Chō*, 超) of Hōman brought a facsimile copy and said: "It has been one thousand years since the Great Master's passing. Today his original handwriting can still be seen. If we now carve it and install it in the pagoda hall, we can transmit it to eternity. Would this not be a magnificent sight?" At this, our group snapped their fingers in joyful assent. Recently the woodcutting was completed, and our fellow monks pressed me to write a few words. I firmly declined but was not permitted to refuse, and so, without concern for the plainness and artlessness of my writing, I record the beginning and end of this matter.

On the day of the monks' *pravāraṇā* in the *kanoto-mi* year of Bunsei (*Bunsei*, 文政) [1821 CE]. Written and inscribed by Ryōshō (*Ryōshō*, 亮照), bhikṣu of the Vairocana studies at Enryaku-ji.
