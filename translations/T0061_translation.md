## Fascicle 1

### Sūtra on Pravāraṇā Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in Mṛgāramātṛ Park in the Eastern Park of Śrāvastī, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus. At that time, on the fifteenth day of the seventh month, the Bhagavān spread his seat in the open air, with the saṃgha of bhikṣus gathered all around him. The Buddha addressed Ānanda, saying: "You should now quickly strike the gaṇḍī in the open air. The reason is that today, the fifteenth day of the seventh month, is the day for observing the pravāraṇā."

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, placing his right knee on the ground, knelt with joined palms and spoke this verse:

> Pure-eyed one, without equal,
> No matter remains unpracticed—
> Wisdom unstained and unattached:
> What, then, is called receiving the year?

At that time, the Bhagavān replied to Ānanda with this verse:

> In pravāraṇā, the three karmas are purified—
> Actions of body, speech, and mind.
> Pair by pair, bhikṣus face one another
> And each declares their own shortcomings.
> Each in turn recites their own name:
> "Today the assembly observes pravāraṇā;
> I too receive it with a purified mind.
> I humbly ask that my transgressions be forgiven."

At that time, Ānanda again questioned the ceremony with this verse:

> The Buddhas of the past, countless as Ganges sands,
> Pratyekabuddhas and śrāvakas too—
> Is this the Dharma of all the Buddhas?
> Or does it belong to Śākyamuni alone?

At that time, the Bhagavān again replied to Ānanda with this verse:

> The Buddhas of the past, countless as Ganges sands—
> Their disciples of purified mind
> All observe this; it is the Dharma of all Buddhas,
> Not the invention of the present Śākyamuni.
> Pratyekabuddhas do not have this practice;
> They have no pravāraṇā year, no disciples.
> They depart alone, without companions,
> And do not expound the Dharma to others.
> The future Buddhas, World-Honored Ones,
> Countless and incalculable as Ganges sands—
> They too will observe this pravāraṇā,
> Just as Gautama does now.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, having heard these words, was filled with joy and exultation beyond his ability to contain himself. He then ascended to the teaching hall, took the gaṇḍī in hand, and spoke thus: "I now strike this signal-drum of the Tathāgata. All who are disciples of the Tathāgata should now assemble together." At that time, Ānanda spoke this verse:

> Having subdued the power of Māra and adversaries,
> Having eliminated all fetters without remainder—
> In the open air the gaṇḍī is struck;
> Bhikṣus who hear it should assemble.
> All those who wish to hear the Dharma,
> Who cross the flowing sea of birth and death—
> Upon hearing this wondrous resonant sound,
> Let all quickly gather here.

At that time, having struck the gaṇḍī, the Venerable Ānanda went to where the Bhagavān was, bowed with his head to the Bhagavān's feet, and stood to one side. He addressed the Bhagavān, saying: "Now is the proper time. What does the Bhagavān command?"

At that time, the Bhagavān said to Ānanda: "Take your seat in proper order. The Tathāgata himself will know the proper time." At that time, the Bhagavān sat upon a grass seat and instructed the bhikṣus: "You should all likewise sit upon grass seats." The bhikṣus replied: "As you say, Bhagavān!" At that time, each bhikṣu sat upon a grass seat.

At that time, the Bhagavān silently observed the bhikṣus, then addressed them: "I wish now to observe the pravāraṇā. Have I committed any offense against any person here? Have I transgressed in body, speech, or mind?" Having spoken these words, the Tathāgata received no reply from the bhikṣus; they remained silent. The Tathāgata then addressed the bhikṣus a third time: "I wish now to observe the pravāraṇā. Have I truly committed no offense against any person here?"

At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra rose from his seat, knelt with joined palms, and addressed the Bhagavān: "The assembly of bhikṣus, having observed the Tathāgata, perceives no transgression of body, speech, or mind. The reason is this: the Bhagavān brings across those not yet brought across, liberates those not yet liberated, causes those who have not yet attained nirvāṇa to attain nirvāṇa, becomes a refuge and protector for those without refuge, becomes eyes for the blind, becomes a king of physicians for the sick. He is the sole honored one in the three realms, whom none can equal—the most revered, the most supreme. He causes those who have not yet aroused the aspiration for the Way to arouse it, causes those not yet enlightened to become enlightened, causes those who have not yet heard the Dharma to hear it, and for those who are lost he opens a path, guiding them with the right Dharma. For these reasons, the Tathāgata has committed no offense against any person, nor has he transgressed in body, speech, or mind."

At that time, Śāriputra addressed the Bhagavān: "I too now declare myself before the Tathāgata. Have I committed any offense against the Tathāgata or the saṃgha of bhikṣus?"

The Bhagavān replied: "You, Śāriputra, have committed no improper action whatsoever in body, speech, or mind. The reason is this: your wisdom is without equal—manifold wisdom, immeasurable wisdom, boundless wisdom, wisdom without peer, swift wisdom, keen wisdom, profound wisdom, equanimous wisdom. You are content with few desires, dwelling in contentment, abundant in skillful means. Your mindfulness is undistracted; you possess the samādhi of dhāraṇī. Your foundational faculties are fully endowed with moral discipline; you have accomplished samādhi, accomplished wisdom, accomplished liberation, accomplished the knowledge and vision of liberation, accomplished courageous vigor. You can endure what is said to you; you recognize evil as contrary to the Dharma; your character is orderly and composed; and you do not act with sudden violence. Just as the greatest crown prince of a cakravartin king is destined to succeed to the royal throne and turn the precious wheel, so too does Śāriputra turn the unsurpassed pure Dharma-wheel—the wheel that all the gods and people of the world, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits, Māra and the hosts of Māra, have never turned. What you speak is always in accord with the principle of the Dharma; you have never gone contrary to reason."

At that time, Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "These five hundred bhikṣus should all observe the pravāraṇā. Have all five hundred committed no offense against the Tathāgata?"

The Bhagavān replied: "I find no fault with the bodily, verbal, or mental conduct of these five hundred bhikṣus. The reason is this: within this great assembly, Śāriputra, all is supremely pure, free from all blemish. Even the most junior member of this assembly, seated in the lowest place, has attained the path of stream-entry (srotāpatti) and is certain to advance to the stage of non-retrogression (avaivartika). For this reason, I find no cause for blame with this assembly."

At that time, Vaṅgīsa, who was among the assembly, rose from his seat, approached the Bhagavān, bowed with his head to the Bhagavān's feet, and addressed him: "I now feel moved to wish to offer something." The Bhagavān replied: "If you wish to speak, now is the proper time." Vaṅgīsa then stood before the Buddha, offered praise to the Buddha and the saṃgha of bhikṣus, and spoke this verse:

> On this pure fifteenth day,
> Five hundred bhikṣus have assembled.
> All bonds and fetters have been fully dissolved;
> Craving shall arise no more.
> Like a great wheel-turning sage-king
> Surrounded by his retinue of ministers,
> He pervades all the worlds—
> The heavens above and the human realm.
> A great general guiding the multitudes,
> He serves as teacher and guide for all.
> His disciples take delight in attending him;
> The three knowledges and six super-knowledges are fully mastered.
> All are true sons of the Buddha,
> Without a single trace of defilement.
> Able to sever the thorn of sensual craving,
> On this day I take refuge.

At that time, the Bhagavān approved of what Vaṅgīsa had said. At that time, Vaṅgīsa thought to himself: "The Tathāgata has today approved of what I said." Filled with joy and exultation beyond his ability to contain himself, he rose from his seat, bowed to the Buddha, withdrew, and returned to his place.

At that time, the Bhagavān addressed the bhikṣus: "Among my śrāvaka disciples, foremost in composing verses is Vaṅgīsa the bhikṣu; and foremost in speaking without hesitation or difficulty is also Vaṅgīsa the bhikṣu."

At that time, the bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha taught, rejoiced and undertook to practice it.

Regarding this *Sūtra on Pravāraṇā*, translated by Dharmarakṣa: both the Chinese National Edition and the Song Edition place it in the *Róng* (容) section. The Khitan Canon, however, has in its *Róng* section a text titled the *Sūtra on Receiving the Year*, which differs substantially from the present sūtra. Upon consulting the *Kaiyuan Catalogue*, it becomes clear that the text in the Khitan Canon is precisely the *Sūtra on Receiving the Year* belonging to the *Róng* section. The present Song Canon sūtra and this *Sūtra on the New Year* in the *Jìng* (竟) section differ in wording but agree in meaning, and appear to be separate translations of the same original. The *Kaiyuan Catalogue*'s designation of the *Sūtra on the New Year* as a sole translation thus remains without explanation. For now, wishing to gather related texts together and await the judgment of future scholars, this sūtra has been moved and placed in the *Jìng* section.
