# 說罪要行法
## Essential Methods for Confessing Offenses

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1903

Tang dynasty, compiled by Yijing (義淨).

---

At every half-month, at the end of the month, one recalls offenses committed and confesses them in accordance with the rules.

Such as deliberate lying, drinking alcohol, eating at improper times, and eating food received by an impure or unwashed hand.

At morning or at other times, not examining water before drinking or using it. Not properly releasing living creatures, and thereby killing sentient beings. Destroying living ground oneself or causing others to destroy it. Not using the phrase "make it known and pure." Eating the five kinds of living seeds without first purifying them with fire or other means. Eating from bowls and vessels not properly washed. Eating with copper bowls and spoons and chopsticks not scrubbed with ash. All copper vessels must be scrubbed with ash to become pure; washing with bean-powder or water alone does not make them pure.

Drinking from vessels of leftover overnight water that have been defiled, or cutting food with leftover overnight contaminated knives and eating it. Drinking impure tea, broth, ghee, honey, or other liquids at improper times.

After finishing the five proper foods and changing posture, swallowing remaining saliva.

Handling gold, silver, money, and precious things oneself or causing others to handle them, without using the phrase "make it known and pure." Touching fire without mindfulness.

Touching fire when burning incense without mindfulness.

Entering a village at improper times without informing a fellow bhikṣu. Sharing a room with an unordained person for more than two nights. Reciting together with an unordained person, or chanting the Buddha's name in unison with them. These are pāyantika offenses. The above lists them by frequency of occurrence; the rest should be understood by analogy with these offenses. Furthermore, not chewing the tooth-stick each morning and after the main meal. Chewing the tooth-stick facing a stūpa. Discarding it after use without washing. Spitting or blowing the nose in the saṃgha's clean area. Discarding fleas, lice, and the like not in the proper place. Making sounds while eating. Talking with food in the mouth.

Biting food in half. Sitting or lying on the same bed or seat with a person who has not received full ordination.

Standing to urinate. Not washing after defecation or urination. When rinsing the mouth or spitting water, or blowing the nose, or discarding the tooth-stick during defecation or urination, not snapping the fingers or clearing the throat first. Handling food vessels with a soiled hand. Not properly washing the hand or rinsing the mouth before drinking water.

Eating honey at an improper time without purifying it by adding a drop of water. Looking at men or women without guarding the mind and giving rise to desire. Touching one's own body with a mind of lustful attachment. Not maintaining the visualization of light and letting the mind wander freely when sleeping. Sleeping under lamplight. Although there are permitted exceptions, touching fire without mindfulness. Toward the Three Treasures, teachers, or parents, giving rise to a disrespectful mind, or generating anger without patience. Extinguishing a fire without mindfulness. All of these are duṣkṛta offenses. This lists only the essential points; if there are other offenses, one should recall them accordingly. All of these must be confessed before another person in a single declaration of repentance. As for self-reproach: how does one reproach oneself? Whenever a renunciant, through lack of care, acts contrary to the vinaya teachings, one must immediately reproach one's own mind, saying: "This is something I should not have done. From now on I will not do this again." If one can constantly reproach oneself in this way, one will naturally not transgress the precepts. One should know that the intention of the Buddha's teaching lies precisely in this.

Furthermore, from the time a renunciant receives the ten precepts or the full precepts onward, in every matter one should inform either the preceptor or the ācārya. Only five matters do not require informing. What are the five? Defecation and urination. Drinking pure water. Chewing the tooth-stick. Within the same boundary, up to forty-nine arm-spans. Bowing to the Buddha and circumambulating the stūpa. All other matters require informing. If one does not inform the teacher, each instance incurs a duṣkṛta offense.

On the poṣadha day, one should face a pure bhikṣu who has not committed the same offense. According to seniority, one performs the proper salutation. Squatting with palms joined, recalling the names of one's offenses, one speaks as follows: "Venerable elder, please be mindful. I, Bhikṣu So-and-so, have committed such offenses as deliberate lying and so forth. I have committed numerous root pāyantika offenses and numerous associated duṣkṛta offenses. And such offenses as not chewing the tooth-stick: I have committed numerous root duṣkṛta offenses and numerous associated duṣkṛta offenses. For each of these offenses, there is also a pāyantika offense for disrespecting the sacred teaching, and an associated duṣkṛta offense, as well as a concealment offense for not revealing them to another person. These offenses I have committed, I now, before this venerable elder, from the state of purity, fully reveal and do not conceal. By revealing them, I attain peace and happiness."

The other bhikṣu should ask: "Do you see your offenses?" One answers: "I see them." He asks again: "In the future, can you properly guard the precepts?" One answers: "I can guard them." The bhikṣu who hears the confession should say "*auḍbika*." The one who confesses responds "*sādhu*." If one has committed a saṃghāvaśeṣa offense, one should for the time being reveal it before another person, and separately perform the formal procedure afterward. If one has committed a sthūlātyaya or a niḥsargika-pāyantika offense, one should remove it according to the prescribed procedure.

## Method of Making Aspirations After Each Meal

According to the vinaya teaching, when a bhikṣu has finished eating, one should recite the dāna-gāthā, meaning the verse of giving. In a quiet place, whether sitting, standing, or walking in meditation, one first recites a short sūtra passage of one or half a page, then recites the gāthā: "Having finished the meal, may all sentient beings have their virtue and practice fully accomplished and attain the ten powers. Those who practice giving will surely obtain its benefit. If one gives for the sake of happiness, one will surely obtain happiness afterward."

"May the temple, buildings, and quarters, given to the saṃgha, and the patron who built the temple, the guardian spirit of the temple, the sovereign and officials, teachers, parents, relatives by condition, and all sentient beings: may those who have already passed away be reborn in the Western Land and see Amitābha Buddha, or be reborn in Tuṣita Heaven and see the Venerable Maitreya, casting off the sandals of defilement and dust and awakening to the patience of the unborn. May those presently living be free from illness and enjoy long life, adorned with merit and wisdom. May I myself be free from all obstacles and afflictions, always uphold the pure precepts without transgressing the śīla, giving rise to great dread even toward minor offenses. May all offenses committed be fully revealed. To the end of the future, may I never lose faith. May I encounter good teachers. May I be reborn in a central land, free from the eight difficulties. May I always maintain right view and seek liberation. May I always be an uninvited friend to all sentient beings. With this merit I universally dedicate to all, that together we may leave the stream of existence and realize the unsurpassed fruit." If one does not perform this recitation and aspiration daily, one is a person of laziness and does not merit the offerings of the faithful. The method of purification:

The rooms for major and minor elimination must be in separate places, each with its own door and a crossbar. When entering the minor elimination room or going to the latrine, the proper method is: carry a water vessel; do not hold water in the mouth for washing. Clear the throat and snap the fingers two or three times before proceeding. Once inside, bar the door. Take two blocks of earth or a pinch of ash. Use half to wash the body and half to wash the left hand. Bamboo troughs, cloth wipes, and the like are not the original proper form. After leaving the latrine, place the vessel on a three-pronged wooden stand with the water stream directed toward the body, or rest it on the side to let the water pour obliquely. If there is no vessel, a basin of water may temporarily suffice. Place fifteen blocks of earth on the ground to the right, or on a brick or board. Seven blocks are used to wash the left hand alone. Seven blocks to wash both hands together. The remaining one is used to wash the vessel. The brick or board must then be washed clean. Only then should the impure vessel be placed upon it. Then take pure vessel water and rinse the mouth three times. Only then does it accord with the proper form of purity. Only then may one receive prostrations, prostrate to others, reverently serve the Three Treasures, sit on the seats, or read and recite the sūtras. If one does not practice in accordance with the vinaya in this way, every action incurs a duṣkṛta offense. When traveling on the road or riding in a boat, one should assess the circumstances accordingly. I earnestly hope that practitioners will know that these are the sacred teachings and not casually cover them with ash. In the end one will fall into the hells. Upholding them brings merit; making light of the Dharma brings offense. Since these are constant matters, they are announced clearly.
