# Sūtra of the Buddha's Teaching on Immeasurable Great Compassion

## *Fóshuō Wúliàng Dàcí Jiào Jīng* (佛說��量大慈教經)

Taishō 2903

Translator unknown. An apocryphal text.

*Note: This apocryphal sūtra, surviving as a Dunhuang manuscript fragment (the opening is lost), presents a dialogue between the Buddha and Ānanda on karmic retribution, the power of the sūtra itself, and proper Buddhist conduct. It includes vivid descriptions of hellish punishments for specific offenses (stealing from the saṃgha, breaking precepts, drinking wine), along with characteristic apocryphal tropes of self-referential sūtra-promotion. Notable is the reference to "Zhèndan" (China) as a specific country the Buddha has observed, and the practical moral instruction on temple conduct. The text's rough literary style and doctrinal eclecticism mark it as a Chinese popular composition.*

[Opening lost] ...there are. At that time Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, only the *icchantika* beings are difficult to transform and difficult to nurture."

At that time the World-Honored One answered: "Just as an ox plows the field because a person drives it, just as a monkey performs dances because a person teaches it, so too beings who commit sins do so because others urge them on."

At that time Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if there are beings who wish to be born in the Western Land, even if they should flay my body or direct impurities at my mouth and eyes, I would not refuse."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "This sūtra is supremely holy. If there are beings who diligently recite this sūtra, they shall be freed from three difficulties and sufferings. The first difficulty is imprisonment. The second difficulty is illness. The third difficulty is hell."

At that time the World-Honored One declared to Ānanda: "Because the beings of Jambudvīpa are difficult to transform and difficult to nurture, I have opened this sūtra-treasury and extracted its extraordinary and wondrous teaching to expound for you. If a person sees this sūtra and does not give rise to purity, know that such a person has no karmic connection with me.

"All sūtras extensively teach wondrous words to guide and deliver beings. If anyone utters my name even once, I fully hear and fully see it, and I follow the sound to go and rescue them.

"Those in this present life who flay and strip the saṃgha: after death they fall into the Cold Ice Hell. They are also reborn as silkworms, to be boiled and stripped by others. Such beings pass through eighty thousand kalpas in this way. In the remainder they receive the body of an animal, passing through five hundred kalpas. They take on the form of every kind of crawling creature. Afterward they receive a human body, but one that is deformed, crippled, blind, or lame. For five hundred kalpas they constantly receive the retribution of muteness.

"At that time the World-Honored One answered: "Ānanda, bhikṣuṇīs who engage in impure conduct: after death they fall into the Iron Cavern Hell. Eighty thousand blade-wheels descend upon them all at once, hacking and severing their bodies."

At that time Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "As the Buddha considers this matter, it transcends the three realms."

At that time the World-Honored One said to Ānanda: "Those who drink wine and become intoxicated and disordered: after death they fall into the Molten-Copper-Drinking Hell."

At that time Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, wine has no life. Why is it prohibited?"

At that time the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "Drinking wine leads to intoxication and disorder. One does not recognize one's own elders and kin. I have observed that in the land of Zhèndan (China) there was a person who, because of wine, committed incest with his own mother, breaking all five precepts. This is why it is prohibited."

At that time the World-Honored One addressed all the great bodhisattvas: "Since I attained buddhahood, over five hundred kalpas have passed. I have read through all the sūtras and recited them completely. Never have I heard such words; never have I heard such a teaching. If there are beings who hear this sūtra, they have planted wholesome causes and wholesome fruits in past lives. The contemplation of this sūtra is inconceivable and beyond reckoning."

At that time the Tathāgata said to the bodhisattvas: "The words I have spoken are as weighty as Mount Sumeru, yet beings who hear them treat them as lightly as a mote of dust. This dharma is difficult to hear and difficult to see."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "If there are beings who hear this sūtra and their minds give rise to sighing joy, for such persons one should explain it with all one's heart."

At that time the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "I see beings falling into the three evil paths. Even if someone were to take a knife and cut my body, I could still endure it. But I cannot endure seeing beings suffer great anguish." Grieving with bitter patience, from his body light radiated forth, illuminating the ten directions. Those who have a karmic connection with me can see my body; those without a karmic connection cannot perceive my form.

At that time Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how is it that beings of one kind, with one mind, have some who see and some who do not see?"

At that time the Tathāgata again said to Ānanda: "The sun and moon shine universally, yet the blind do not see."

At that time Ānanda again said: "World-Honored One, what does it mean to be blind, and what does it mean to have sight?"

At that time the World-Honored One answered: "Ānanda, those who cultivate merit have sight. Those who do not cultivate merit are blind."

Ānanda again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what about those who cultivate merit but still do not see?"

The World-Honored One told Ānanda: "Those who cultivate merit yet do not see my body have obstructed the Buddha's light and defiled the Three Treasures. This is why they cannot see me."

The Buddha told the bodhisattvas: "Those who hear this sūtra and give rise to joy in their hearts are like a child seeing its mother return from a long journey, like the hungry finding food, like the thirsty finding drink. For such persons, one should explain it with all one's heart."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Those who eat meat are like a pack of dogs fighting over a bone, each coveting more. Seeing pigs and sheep, they constantly harbor thoughts of slaughter. Seeing rank meat, they are like cats pursuing mice, intent and focused. Those who in this present life have faith in and understand the buddha-dharma have come from the human realm. Those who in this present life do not have faith in the Buddha have come from the animal realm.

"Those who commit sins and do not repent are like carrying gold to fill a treasury and then losing the treasure. Those who commit sins and do repent are like a sick person who obtains medicine and recovers. Like a soiled garment washed with water that becomes clean again."

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Universally Expansive (*Pǔguǎng*, 普廣): "Those who follow my words can attain buddhahood through even a single verse. Those who do not believe my words are like searching for a needle in the ocean: a mad waste of effort, with no hope of success."

The Buddha told the bodhisattvas: "Those in this present life who steal others' possessions will in future lives become stupid oxen for their victims, bitterly beaten and cruelly worked out of season. They shall receive the ox-body for five hundred kalpas, serving as slaves to others, subject to their commands and service. If their hearts seek to flee, they shall be captured and cruelly punished, branded on the face and unreasonably mistreated."

The Buddha told the bodhisattvas: "Within a monastery, there are two kinds of mind: a wholesome mind and an unwholesome mind. What is an unwholesome person? If there are beings who, upon entering the monastery, only beg and demand from the saṃgha, or seek out the faults of monastics, or eat meritorious food without any shame, taking cakes, fruits, and vegetables and carrying them home: such persons after death fall into the Iron-Fork Hell.

"What is a wholesome person? If there are beings who, upon entering the monastery, are respectful when they see monastics, bow when they see the Buddha, receive the precepts and repent, donate their wealth, manage the affairs of the Three Treasures without sparing their own lives, and protect and uphold the great dharma: for such persons, with each step they take, the heavenly realm spontaneously comes to receive them. Like Śuddhipanthaka (*Shùtíjiā*, 樹提伽): Śuddhipanthaka is called the supremely wholesome person."

The Buddha addressed the great assembly: "The various causes and effects that I have discussed above: this sūtra has three names. The first is 'Extraordinary.' The second is 'Supreme.' The third is 'Bodhisattva.' If there are beings who hear this sūtra and even once give rise to a wholesome mind, they shall be born in the Pure Land."

The Buddha told the bodhisattvas: "Those who hear my teaching and give rise to sighing joy are like parched land receiving rain: the seedlings and crops revive. Those who do not accept my words are like soaking a stone in water: there will never be a time of moistening."

At that time Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, suppose you observe in the land of Zhèndan a person who from the age of seven cultivates merit until one hundred years of age, yet at the moment of death breaks the five precepts. Does such a person obtain merit or not?"

At that time the World-Honored One again said to Ānanda: "It is like a cart going up a slope of ten thousand *lǐ*: at the very top, the cart overturns and speeds back down. What hope is there of gain? Whatever small amount was obtained is like the shadow of a cloud; when the sun appears, the light is gone. It is like a single mouthful of food: how can it long satisfy?"

The Buddha told the beings: "I and all of you have broadly spoken of causes and conditions together. May we all attain buddhahood. I universally exhort all beings to jointly cultivate the practice of purity."

All the worldly devas, humans, asuras, and others, hearing what the Buddha taught, all greatly rejoiced, exclaiming that this was unprecedented. Each gave rise to the mind of the unsurpassed, true, and right Way. They came forward, placed their foreheads upon the ground, bowed, and put it into practice.
