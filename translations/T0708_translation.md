# 了本生死經
## Sūtra of Understanding the Origin of Birth and Death

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0708

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

The Buddha said this: "If a bhikṣu sees dependent arising, they see the Dharma; having already seen the Dharma, they see me."

Thereupon the worthy Śāriputra addressed the bhikṣus: "Worthy ones! The Buddha has said: 'If bhikṣus see dependent arising, they see the Dharma; having already seen the Dharma, they see me.' What is the meaning of this? This teaching concerns conditions. If one sees that dependent arising has no life-force and no non-life-force, this is seeing the Dharma. If one sees that the Dharma has no life-force and no non-life-force, this is seeing the Buddha. One should follow this wisdom.

"There are two aspects to seeing external dependent arising and two aspects to seeing internal dependent arising, making four in total.

"What are the two aspects of seeing external dependent arising? They are the binding of causes and the binding of conditions. What is the binding of causes? From the seed comes the root, from the root the leaf, from the leaf the stem, from the stem the node, from the node the flower buds, and from the flower the fruit. This is the binding of causes. What is the binding of conditions? It is the earth element, the water element, the fire element, the wind element, and the space element. From these causes and conditions, the seed produces its growth. Earth upholds the seed, water moistens the seed, fire warms the seed, wind stirs the seed, and space allows the seed to grow without obstruction. When the proper season arrives, the seed grows. That seed does not know, 'I produce the root,' and the root does not know, 'I arise from the seed.' The root does not know, 'I produce the leaf,' and the leaf does not know, 'I arise from the root.' The leaf does not know, 'I produce the stem,' and so on through the node, flower buds, and fruit, none of which know they produce or arise from one another. Again, the earth does not know, 'I support the seed,' and the seed does not know, 'Earth supports me.' Water does not know, 'I moisten the seed,' and the seed does not know, 'Water moistens me.' So too with fire, wind, and space: none know one another. Worthy ones, from causes and conditions, when the proper occasion arrives, the seed grows. It is neither self-made nor made by another, nor does it arise without a cause. One should observe external dependent arising through five aspects. What are the five? Impermanence, non-annihilation, non-transference, indestructibility of the seed, and resemblance without identity. The seed having decayed is impermanence; the root emerging is non-annihilation; that the seed and root are separate is non-transference; a small seed producing abundant fruit is indestructibility of the seed; the fruit resembling the seed yet not being the original seed is resemblance without identity.

"Know that internal dependent arising is also seen through two aspects: the binding of causes and the binding of conditions. What is the binding of causes? Conditioned by ignorance, there are formations; conditioned by formations, consciousness; conditioned by consciousness, name-and-form; conditioned by name-and-form, the six sense bases; conditioned by the six sense bases, contact; conditioned by contact, feeling; conditioned by feeling, craving; conditioned by craving, grasping; conditioned by grasping, becoming; conditioned by becoming, birth; conditioned by birth, old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, distress, and mental anguish. Thus this is merely the complete arising of the great mass of suffering. Ignorance does not know, 'I produce formations'; formations do not know, 'I arise from ignorance.' Formations do not know, 'I produce consciousness'; consciousness does not know, 'I arise from formations.' So on through the six sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, and birth, down to old age and death: none of them know one another. From ignorance come formations, from formations consciousness, from consciousness name-and-form, from name-and-form the six sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, distress, and mental anguish, as stated above. This is merely the complete arising of the great mass of suffering. If there were no birth, then there would be no old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, distress, and mental anguish.

"Worthy ones, because dependent arising exists, Dharmas that arise from conditions exist. There is dependent arising that is not a dharma arisen from conditions; there are dharmas arisen from conditions that are not dependent arising; there is what is both dependent arising and a dharma arisen from conditions; and there is what is neither dependent arising nor a dharma arisen from conditions.

"What is dependent arising but not a dharma arisen from conditions? 'Conditioned by ignorance, formations; conditioned by formations, consciousness; conditioned by consciousness, name-and-form,' down to 'the great mass of suffering completely arises,' as stated above. This is called dependent arising but not a dharma arisen from conditions.

"What is a dharma arisen from conditions but not dependent arising? Ignorance, formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, and old age and death themselves. These are called dharmas arisen from conditions but not dependent arising.

"What is both dependent arising and a dharma arisen from conditions? Whatever arises, abides, and does not cease: the arising of old age and death. This arising, abiding, and connection of causes is proximity of causes, proximity of subtlety of causes, proximity of truth of causes, proximity of suchness of causes, proximity of non-difference, proximity of non-delusion, and proximity of dependent arising. The dharmas that arise from such conditions are: becoming, grasping, craving, feeling, contact, the six sense bases, name-and-form, consciousness, and formations. This is called both dependent arising and a dharma arisen from conditions.

"What is neither dependent arising nor a dharma arisen from conditions? That which pertains to those who have attained the path. What then is ignorance? It is like the six elements. When the six elements are received, there is female or male. What are the six? The earth element, the water element, the fire element, the wind element, the space element, and the consciousness element. When the body obtains stability, this is the earth element. What holds it together without dispersing is the water element. When food, drink, and nourishment are well digested, this is the fire element. The in-breath and out-breath within the body is the wind element. What the four great elements cannot hold is the space element. What turns along like a pair of crossed arrows is the consciousness element. The earth element is neither female nor male, neither a person nor a being, neither a body nor what belongs to a body, it is not born of a person, not a youth, not a doer nor a non-doer, not an abider nor a non-abider, not a knower nor a non-knower, not a sentient being, not 'mine,' not 'I,' not 'his,' and has no owner. So too with water, fire, wind, and space. The consciousness element is neither female nor male, neither a person nor a being, neither a body nor what belongs to a body, not born of a person, not a youth, not a doer nor a non-doer, not an abider nor a non-abider, not a knower nor a non-knower, not a sentient being, not 'mine,' not 'I,' not 'mine' nor an owner. Thus, merely from the six elements arises a single perception, then an aggregate perception, then a female perception, a male perception, a deluded perception, a body perception, a self-sovereign perception, and a forceful self-sovereign perception. Receiving various kinds of experience, this constitutes ignorance. Thus it is said: the nature is delusion; perceptions of purity, permanence, pleasure, and self; doubt, resentment, and delusion; not approaching the supreme essentials. The Buddha says this is ignorance: being dyed by objects, wisdom lacking, delusion arising, this constitutes ignorance. Because of delusion there are formations; because of knowing objects there is consciousness; because of the five natures there is name-and-form; because of relying on name-and-form as the basis there are the six sense bases; because of the meeting of three there is contact; because of the operation of contact there is feeling; because of feeling and delight there is craving; because craving spreads further there is grasping; because grasping leads to further becoming there is becoming; because the five natures are completely formed there is birth; because the various elements ripen there is old age; because the life-faculty ceases there is death. Inner burning is grief; deceptive speech is lamentation; what the five sense-consciousnesses encounter together is the suffering of the body; what the mind-consciousness encounters is distress; mental rumination and weariness is anguish. Because of becoming there is birth. Seeing and knowing in this way, obstructions are revealed. This teaching is complete and fulfilled: the great mass of suffering is fully formed. From this one receives misfortune and decline; through attachment one is again reborn. Its beginning cannot be seen or known, cannot be measured. Again, darkness is the meaning of ignorance; creating and completing is the meaning of formations; knowing is the meaning of consciousness; conditions abiding there and mutually depending is the meaning of name-and-form; being the master yet not exclusively so is the meaning of the six sense bases; encountering and meeting is the meaning of contact; knowing from which there is feeling is the meaning of feeling; thirsting and desiring to obtain objects, insatiable like fire, is the meaning of craving; seizing is the meaning of grasping; that there will again be future existence is the meaning of becoming; the five natures arising is the meaning of birth; ripening is the meaning of old age; the process declining is the meaning of death. These meanings are also the characteristics of the twelve links of dependent arising.

"Again, from ignorance, meritorious formations and demeritorious formations are produced; this is called 'conditioned by ignorance, formations.' Because there are formations, through merit and demerit there is consciousness; this is called 'conditioned by formations, consciousness.' Because consciousness produces the nature of action, name-and-form are completely formed; this is called 'conditioned by consciousness, name-and-form.' Because of this conditional arising, whenever there is action there is reception of experience; this is called 'conditioned by name-and-form, the six sense bases.' Eye-consciousness meeting constitutes contact; this is called 'conditioned by the six sense bases, contact.' As with contact, knowing feeling is likewise; this is called 'conditioned by contact, feeling.' Not knowing feeling means that because actions differ, from the appearance of craving one immediately grasps; this is called 'conditioned by feeling, craving.' From the appearance of craving one further swallows; this is called 'conditioned by craving, grasping, and becoming.' Grasping constitutes the three actions of body, speech, and mind; this is called 'conditioned by grasping, becoming.' Because of the labor of becoming, there will again be future existence completely formed as birth; this is called 'conditioned by becoming, birth.' Because the five natures have already been formed, there is old age and death. These are the twelve links of dependent arising, turning and revolving. Formations create the field of karma; consciousness creates the seed of action; ignorance creates the opposing action. Just as earth upholds the seed, water keeps the seed from dispersing, fire ripens the seed, wind stirs the seed, and space allows it to grow without obstruction, so formations create the field of karma in the same way. Craving creates the moistening action. Those formations do not know, 'I create the field of karma.' Craving does not know, 'I create the moistening action.' Consciousness does not know, 'I am the seed of action.' Ignorance does not know, 'I create the opposing action.' Just as earth does not know, 'I support the seed,' and so with water, fire, wind, and space, as stated above. Because of the labor of becoming, there will again be future existence completely formed as birth. Here too there is no one who transfers from this world, but only causes and conditions mutually supporting each other. It is like a mirror that is clean and bright: conditioned by the inside and outside, a facial image arises. The face neither dies here and is born there, nor does it enter the mirror; from the face there is a cause and condition that is not deficient. It neither dies here nor is born there; there is the experience of suffering, and the cause and condition is not deficient. From this there is reception. Just as fire receives fuel and burns, not being extinguished day and night, yet its flame does not transfer, so too consciousness does not transfer as a body and travel among the five destinies. Because there are conditions, it arises. This dharma has no owner. It is like the full moon, forty-nine yojanas away, yet its round form appears below in the water; there are conditions that are not deficient, yet the moon does not die there and come to be born here. One should contemplate birth and death in this way. This is the binding of causes.

"What is the binding of conditions? As the Buddha told Ānanda: 'Conditioned by the eye and form, eye-consciousness arises. The eye does not know, "I create the supporting action." Form does not know, "I am the object for consciousness." Light does not know, "I illuminate for consciousness." Space does not know, "I allow consciousness to be unobstructed." Consciousness does not know, "I arise here and produce existence." Eye, form, light, space, and attention cause eye-consciousness to be completely formed and to arise. Ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind conditioned by dharmas produce mind-consciousness. The eye does not know, "I support consciousness." Dharmas do not know, "I am the object for consciousness." Mind does not know, "I illuminate for consciousness." Space does not know, "I allow consciousness to be unobstructed." Consciousness does not know, "I am formed by these causes and conditions." Thus, Ānanda, conditioned by mind, dharmas, light, space, and attention, mind-consciousness is completely formed and arises. Yet this is neither self-made nor made by another, nor made by both together, nor arisen without a cause. It is not because of self, not because of other, and not without a cause.'

"One should observe internal dependent arising through five aspects. What are the five? Impermanence, non-annihilation, non-transference, small action producing great result, and resemblance without identity. At the time of death, the body having decayed is impermanence. Birth arising with a new bodily portion is non-annihilation. Some going the same way, some going differently, separate and distinct, is non-transference. Small action producing great result means that actions do not perish. As result arises from action, it is not the original identity. If one sees this dependent arising as without life-force and without non-life-force, this is seeing the Dharma. If one sees the Dharma as without life-force and without non-life-force, this is seeing the four noble truths: suffering, its origin, its cessation, and the path. It is like a person of clear vision seeing a master's completed painting and praising both the painting as beautiful and the master as skillful. One who sees the four truths is likewise: the Buddha is omniscient and omnipresent. From this one obtains joy and does not part from the Buddha. Having obtained the Dharma, the assembly of the truly real, the joy of precepts and non-separation."
