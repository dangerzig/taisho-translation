# Record of the Succession of Teachers Transmitting the Dharma of the Two Divisions

## *Liǎngbù Dàfǎ Xiāngchéng Shīzī Fùfǎ Jì*

Taishō 2081

Compiled by the bhikṣu Hǎiyún (*Hǎiyún*), Tang dynasty, 834 CE.

*Note: This is a lineage record (fùfǎ jì) documenting the teacher-to-student transmission of the two great esoteric teachings: the Vajradhātu (Jīngāng Jiè, "Diamond Realm," based on the Vajraśekhara Sūtra) and the Mahāvairocana (Dà Pílúzhēnà, "Womb Realm," based on the Mahāvairocana-sūtra). Compiled by Hǎiyún of the Jìngzhù Monastery in Cháng'ān in the eighth month of 834 CE (Dàhé 8), the first fascicle traces the Diamond Realm lineage from Vairocana Buddha through Samantabhadra (Vajrasattva), Mañjuśrī, Nāgārjuna, Nāgabodhi, Vajrabodhi (who brought the teaching from South India to China), and the mutual exchange with Śubhakarasiṃha, through Amoghavajra and his five principal disciples (Hánguāng, Huìlǎng, Tánzhēn, Juéchāo, and Huìguǒ), down to the students of Huìguǒ (including the Japanese monk Kūkai). The second fascicle traces the Womb Realm lineage from Vairocana through Vajrapāṇi, Dharmagupta at Nālandā, Śubhakarasiṃha (who translated the Mahāvairocana-sūtra with Yīxíng in 719 CE), the Korean monk Hyeoncho, and again through Huìguǒ to his successors. The text includes doctrinal summaries of each teaching (the five families, four mudrās, six maṇḍalas, four bodies, and four types of mantra recitation for the Diamond Realm; the ten stages of deluded mind, bodhicitta as cause, great compassion as root, and skillful means as culmination for the Womb Realm) and notes on the Sanskrit originals (the full version in one hundred thousand verses, the abbreviated version in four thousand verses).*

---

## Fascicle One: The Diamond Realm

A brief account of the succession of teachers transmitting the Vajraśekhara Great Teaching King Sūtra (*Jīngāng Jiè Dàjiào Wáng Jīng*).

Tripiṭaka Master Vajrabodhi (*Jīngāng Zhì*) said: "I received this Vajradhātu Sūtra of one hundred thousand verses directly from the ācārya Nāgabodhi (*Lóngzhì*) in South India."

The ācārya Nāgabodhi himself said: "From the time Vairocana Tathāgata was in the world, he entrusted this Vajradhātu teaching of the supreme vehicle to Samantabhadra Vajrasattva (*Pǔxián Jīngāng Sàduǒ*). Samantabhadra Vajrasattva entrusted it to Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva (*Miàojíxiáng Púsà*). Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, after twelve generations, entrusted the Dharma to Nāgārjuna Bodhisattva (*Lóngměng Púsà*). Nāgārjuna Bodhisattva, after several hundred more years, entrusted the Dharma to the ācārya Nāgabodhi. The ācārya Nāgabodhi, after another hundred and more years, entrusted the Dharma to Tripiṭaka Master Vajrabodhi."

### Vajrabodhi

Tripiṭaka Master Vajrabodhi was learned in both the Mahāyāna and the Hīnayāna. He belonged to the Sarvāstivāda school. His observance of the precepts was pure as frost; the Vinaya was his guiding dike. He was the third son of the king of South India. For the sake of propagating the Dharma, he journeyed with his staff through the five regions of India and came to travel in China.

### Śubhakarasiṃha

At that time, there was a tripiṭaka master from Central India, of the Śākya clan, a fifty-second generation descendant of King Droṇodana (*Hú Fàn Wáng*). His father, the king, had sent him on a military campaign. Witnessing the great slaughter and carnage, he felt no desire for the throne. Weary of the world, he left home [to become a monk], vowing to transmit the Dharma to the eastern lands.

Together with Tripiṭaka Master Vajrabodhi, he served as a national preceptor during the reign of Emperor Xuánzōng. Knowing that Vajrabodhi understood the Vajradhātu teachings, [Śubhakarasiṃha] requested him to transmit the Vajradhātu five-division Dharma. At that time, Vajrabodhi, knowing that the Central Indian Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha (*Wúwèi*) understood the Mahāvairocana teaching, exclaimed: "This Dharma is profound, difficult to encounter!" Previously, in South India, he had heard the name of the Mahāvairocana teaching and had traveled through the five regions of India seeking it, but found no one who understood it. Now, having come to Great Tang, he was delighted to encounter this teaching. He thereupon requested Śubhakarasiṃha to transmit the Mahāvairocana great teaching. Thus they mutually served as teacher and student, transmitting the two root teachings to each other.

### Amoghavajra

Tripiṭaka Master Vajrabodhi then entrusted the Vajradhātu great teaching to Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra (*Bùkōng Zhì*) of the Dàxīngshàn Monastery.

### The Five Principal Disciples

Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra then entrusted this Dharma to five disciples, the ācāryas: (1) Hánguāng, (2) Huìlǎng of the Dàxīngshàn Monastery, (3) Tánzhēn of the Qīnglóng Monastery, (4) Juéchāo of the Bǎoshòu Monastery, and (5) Huìguǒ of the East Pagoda Cloister of the Qīnglóng Monastery.

### Huìguǒ

[Huìguǒ] was thoroughly versed in the science of sound [Sanskrit grammar] and equally fluent in both Chinese and Sanskrit. His mind constantly rested in the gate of true characteristics, and his wondrous realization penetrated the principle of suchness. He regularly chanted the Vimalakīrti Sūtra. In his spare time, he also read and studied the Nirvāṇa, Avataṃsaka, Prajñā, Laṅkāvatāra, and Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa sūtras. When he received the Vajradhātu from the Tripiṭaka Master of the Dàxīngshàn Monastery [Amoghavajra], he said: "This teaching is the supreme and most wondrous. Yet the Mahāyāna mind-ground that I previously realized was also the ultimate and most wondrous. Now that I have encountered the Vajradhātu dharma-gate, it is yet more supreme. Therefore it is said: 'The ultimate has nothing above it.' The exoteric teaching of the mind-ground clarifies only the contemplation of principle. This Yoga teaching penetrates both principle and phenomenal gates. Abiding in the Vajradhātu, in a single moment of correspondence, one ascends to true enlightenment. Therefore it is said: 'The ultimate has nothing above it.'"

### Huìguǒ's Transmission

Those who received the Vajradhātu Dharma from [Huìguǒ] included: the ācārya Huìyīng (fellow student at the Dàxīngshàn Monastery in the conferral of the abhiṣeka teaching), Huìzé, Wéishàng of Chéngdū, Biànhóng of Biàn Prefecture, the Silla monk Hyeil (*Huìrì*), the Japanese monk Kūkai (*Kōnghǎi*), and from the Qīnglóng Monastery: Yìmǎn, Yìmíng, Yìcāo, Yìzhào, Yìmǐn, Yìzhèng, Yìyī, and the lay upāsaka Língyīn.

Next, the ācārya Yìcāo of the East Pagoda Cloister of the Qīnglóng Monastery transmitted the Vajradhātu great Dharma to: fellow monks of his cloister Fǎrùn, Yìzhēn, Yìzhōu, Yìyuán; Shēndá of the Jǐnggōng Monastery; Hǎiyún of the Jìngzhù Monastery; Dàyù of the Chóngfú Monastery; Cónghè and Wényuàn of the Lǐquán Monastery; Jūnliàng, a Silla monk from the Huìchāng Monastery; Chángjiān of the same cloister; and Zhìshēn and Fǎquán of the Xuánfǎ Monastery. His disciple the monk Wénmì received the Vajradhātu great teaching, and it has circulated throughout the land, its branches and leaves unceasing, the udumbara teaching fully matured.

### Doctrinal Summary of the Diamond Realm

Whoever receives the transmission of the Vajradhātu Dharma suddenly perceives bodhi. Entering the maṇḍala and receiving the ācārya's abhiṣeka is like being enthroned as a wheel-turning king. This great teaching is called "Vajradhātu": "vajra" means "adamantine," representing that the dharma-body of all tathāgatas is adamantine, indestructible, unborn, undying, beginningless, endless, forever enduring and indestructible. "Dhātu" means "nature": it illuminates the adamantine nature of all tathāgatas, which pervades the bodies of all sentient beings, originally complete and perfect in the Samantabhadra Vairocana great-function self-nature body-ocean of inherent meritorious qualities.

Therefore, the Yoga practitioner, using the samādhi of great bliss, Samantabhadra vajra-desire arrows, shatters the seeds and manifestations of the two obscurations of fundamental ignorance, as well as the seeds of the two vehicles, crushing them without remainder. In a single moment, one realizes the position of Mahāvairocana.

This sūtra is also called "Vajraśekhara" ("Diamond Pinnacle"): just as the crown of the head is the most exalted part of the body, this teaching is the most supreme among all Mahāyāna dharmas.

The sūtra has one hundred thousand verses in the Sanskrit original, an abbreviated version of four thousand verses, and an extensive version with immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayuta-particle-number verses.

The Vajradhātu Vairocana sūtra in the Sanskrit original of a hundred thousand verses expounds the teaching-site as having eighteen assemblies and four great divisions: first, the Vajradhātu; second, the Subjugation of the Three Worlds (Trailokyavijaya); third, Universal Taming (Sarvaduṣṭavinipāta); fourth, Accomplishment of All Aims (Sarvārthasiddhi). All the essential dharmas are subsumed under the four wisdom-seals, namely: the great seal (mahāmudrā), the samaya seal, the dharma-wisdom seal, and the karma-wisdom seal. Furthermore, each maṇḍala possesses six maṇḍalas: first, the great maṇḍala; second, the samaya maṇḍala; third, the dharma maṇḍala; fourth, the karma maṇḍala; fifth, the four-seal maṇḍala; sixth, the one-seal maṇḍala. Only the Trailokyavijaya maṇḍala possesses ten maṇḍalas; the rest each possess six. The sūtra expounds five families: first, the Buddha family; second, the Vajra family; third, the Ratna (Jewel) family; fourth, the Padma (Lotus) family; fifth, the Karma family. With the inner and outer offerings and the four gathering deities, they total thirty-seven. Furthermore, it expounds four kinds of dharma-body: first, the self-nature body (svabhāvakāya); second, the enjoyment body (saṃbhogakāya); third, the transformation body (nirmāṇakāya); fourth, the outflowing body (niṣyandakāya). Four stages: first, the stage of resolute faith and practice (adhimukticaryā-bhūmi); second, the stage of Samantabhadra's vows and practices; third, the stage of great Samantabhadra; fourth, the stage of universal illumination. The three worthies before the grounds constitute the stage of resolute faith and practice; from the first ground through the tenth ground is the stage of Samantabhadra's vows and practices; the bodhisattva of virtual enlightenment is the stage of great Samantabhadra; the Buddha is called the stage of universal illumination. Four kinds of mantra recitation: first, voiced recitation; second, whispered recitation; third, samādhi recitation; fourth, supreme-meaning recitation. Four kinds of petition-methods. Furthermore, the Yoga sūtra explains four kinds of eyes, four kinds of sitting postures, and five great vows. The *Sarvatathāgata-yoga* also expounds one hundred and twenty kinds of homa hearths, each with distinct seal-implements and insignia, for the swift accomplishment of worldly and supramundane fruits. The vast assemblies contain within the inner maṇḍala thirty-seven honored positions, together with the sixteen great bodhisattvas of the Bhadra Kalpa representing the one thousand buddhas of the Bhadra Kalpa. The thousand buddhas of the Bhadra Kalpa are all arranged in the four directions of the maṇḍala, surrounding the great maṇḍala, making fifty-three. Adding the twenty devas of the outer court makes seventy-three. Adding the earth, water, fire, wind, and space devas of the inner court makes seventy-seven. With the four wrathful vidyā-kings at the four corners, the total is eighty-one holy figures.

The teaching has circulated from above through successive transmissions spanning several hundred years, with only one person per several centuries in foreign lands. Our Tripiṭaka Master of the Dàxīngshàn Monastery [Amoghavajra] personally visited the five regions of India, re-seeking the supreme Dharma. The teaching flowed throughout the realm, the great Dharma was abundantly transmitted. Monks from Silla and other countries, crossing the ocean at the risk of their lives, came to our Tang to seek the supreme Dharma.

Thus the Vajradhātu has been transmitted through successive generations. Each year, by imperial decree, at the Dàxīngshàn, Qīnglóng, Bǎoshòu, Xīngtáng, Chóngfú, and Lǐquán monasteries, great abhiṣeka ceremonies are opened, transmitting the Dharma and liberating people. All of these are the dharma-grandchildren of our Tripiṭaka Master of the Dàxīngshàn Monastery. The branches and leaves of the teaching are successively transmitted and upheld, enduring forever.

The bhikṣu Hǎiyún, in a moment of leisure during the mid-autumn month, has respectfully compiled, according to the root teaching, a brief record of the teacher-to-student succession of the Vajradhātu Great Teaching King, from Samantabhadra Vajrasattva down to the present, through eight generations of transmission.

> Reverently I bow to the sovereign of the Vajradhātu,
> Samantabhadra Sattva, the vidyā-king who holds [the teaching].
> May this praise and exposition be bestowed on all beings;
> May all be reborn together in the Flower-Treasury Realm.

The great Tang, Dàhé 8th year [834 CE], the *jiǎyín* year, the twentieth day of the mid-autumn month.

Compiled by the bhikṣu Hǎiyún, teacher of Sanskrit at the Jìngzhù Monastery.

## Fascicle Two: The Mahāvairocana (Womb Realm)

A brief account of the succession of teachers transmitting the *Mahāvairocana-abhisaṃbodhi-vikurvita-adhiṣṭhāna-sūtra* (*Dà Pílúzhēnà Chéngfó Shénbiàn Jiāchí Jīng*), the great teaching.

The correct Sanskrit should be *Vairocana* (or *Vailocana*), also rendered as "Vairocana's Sūtra of Attaining Buddhahood." In Sanskrit: *Vailocana-saṃbodhi-vikurvita-sūtra*.

The Sanskrit manuscript exists in three versions. The extensive version has one hundred thousand verses; if translated from the Sanskrit in full, it would amount to over three hundred fascicles. This extensive version remains in the western lands and has not reached China. The abbreviated Sanskrit version has four thousand verses. There is also a still briefer version of two thousand five hundred verses, compiled by a great ācārya of Central India. The version currently transmitted is the four thousand-verse version.

The śramaṇa of the Śākya clan, Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha (*Shàn Wúwèi*), translated it by imperial command in the seventh year of Kāiyuán [719 CE]. The śramaṇa Yīxíng (*Yīxíng*) served as his amanuensis. This is the *Mahāvairocana-abhisaṃbodhi-vikurvita-adhiṣṭhāna-sūtra*: it is the empowerment of Vairocana Tathāgata's supernatural transformation. Translated from the Sanskrit original into six fascicles, with an additional fascicle compiling the sequential procedures for upholding and reciting the whole teaching, making seven fascicles in one complete work, entered into the great canon.

### Doctrinal Summary

At that time, Vairocana Tathāgata was in the Vajra Dharmadhātu Palace, together with Samantabhadra and the great bodhisattvas, ten buddha-field-dust-particle-number vajra secret lords, ten buddha-field-dust-particle-number vajra-holders, world-protecting devas, and others: their number immeasurable and indescribable. All were the empowerment of Vairocana Tathāgata's self-enjoyment-body in the vast dharmadhātu ocean.

The sūtra illuminates how a bodhisattva practicing the mantra-path cultivates the supreme bodhicitta, transcends one hundred and sixty kinds of deluded mental states, abides in the great bodhicitta, and in a single moment of correspondence crosses three asaṃkhyeya kalpas. At the very first generation of the mind, one attains the mind of anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi.

This sūtra is based on two kinds of practice: "bodhicitta as the cause, great compassion as the root, and skillful means as the culmination." Next it illuminates the ten metaphors of conditioned arising (*shí yuánshēng jù*), meaning that the bodhisattva practicing the mantra-path understands all dharmas as illusory and arising from conditions.

Also, in accordance with the two truths (supreme meaning and conventional): if one follows the supreme meaning, one cultivates and establishes the dharma-body maṇḍala. Therefore this sūtra first describes the maṇḍala in empty space. The original deity's dharma-body transcends form and color, like space. One abides in such samādhi. If one follows the conventional, one practices according to the four wheels (earth, water, fire, wind) as the maṇḍala.

[The text describes the maṇḍala arrangement with five buddhas and four bodhisattvas on the eight-petaled lotus, plus the three outer maṇḍalas (All-Tathāgata, Śākyamuni, and Mañjuśrī), collectively called the "Great Compassion Womb-Treasury Maṇḍala" (*Dàbēi Tāizàng Màntuóluó*).]

### The Womb Realm Lineage

Śubhakarasiṃha said: "This Dharma was entrusted by Vairocana Buddha to Vajrapāṇi Bodhisattva (*Jīngāng Shǒu Púsà*). Vajrapāṇi, after several hundred years, transmitted it to the ācārya Dharmagupta (*Dámó Jūduō*) of Nālandā Monastery in Central India. The ācārya Dharmagupta next transmitted it to the Central Indian Tripiṭaka Master Śubhakarasiṃha of the Śākya clan."

Śubhakarasiṃha was thoroughly learned in the five sciences, fluent in the three baskets, and accomplished in all the arts of the five regions of India. He was a fifty-second generation descendant of King Droṇodana. He renounced the succession to the throne, left home, and entered the Way. In the seventh year of Kāiyuán [719 CE], he came from the western lands, bringing the Mahāvairocana Sanskrit manuscripts and other texts to this country. Emperor Xuánzōng received him as a national preceptor, and he accompanied the imperial procession to both capitals [Cháng'ān and Luòyáng], translating the Mahāvairocana and other sūtras, serving as the great ācārya of the Mahāvairocana maṇḍala abhiṣeka.

Śubhakarasiṃha then transmitted the Mahāvairocana great teaching to Vajrabodhi, and Vajrabodhi transmitted the Vajradhātu great teaching to Śubhakarasiṃha. They mutually served as ācāryas, reciprocally transmitting.

Vajrabodhi then transmitted the Mahāvairocana great teaching to Amoghavajra of the Dàxīngshàn Monastery. Amoghavajra cherished the essential secrets of the five divisions and entered the ocean of Samantabhadra's nature-mind. Abiding in Yoga, he directly entered the Buddha-vehicle. When he proclaimed the mantras, the heavenly māras were crushed. Three successive emperors addressed him in state letters and bowed their heads to him: from Xuánzōng through Sùzōng to Dàizōng. Emperor Dàizōng's reverence for him was as if toward the Buddha himself, and he invited [Amoghavajra] to serve as the great abhiṣeka ācārya. He transmitted the great teaching for over forty years and translated over one hundred works of sūtras and śāstras.

After receiving the Vajradhātu Dharma from Vajrabodhi, Amoghavajra, fearing the great teaching was not yet complete, personally traveled to South India, where he reverently met the long-lived ācārya Samantabhadra (*Pǔxián*) and again sought and received the Vajradhātu five-division one-hundred-thousand-verse Dharma, obtaining the one-hundred-thousand-verse sūtra.

[The text describes the transmission through Yīxíng (who composed the commentary on the Mahāvairocana-sūtra in seven fascicles), the Silla monk Hyeoncho (*Xuánchāo*), and thence to Huìguǒ and his many disciples, with a parallel list of Womb Realm transmission recipients.]

---

These two great Teaching Kings, the secret and profound, are transmitted rarely. Over several hundred years, only one person may receive the transmission. Since the Buddha-dharma flowed eastward to China, now nearly a thousand years and more, all the secret mind-ground teachings that have circulated do not go beyond these two great Teaching Kings, which subsume all mantra-recitation teaching-gates.

Additionally, there is the Susiddhikara teaching, which broadly explains the three divisions [Buddha, Lotus, and Vajra], and also encompasses the methods of mantra-practice. This principally treats phenomenal accomplishment (*shì chéngjiù*) and its meaning and flavor intersect with the Vajradhātu and Mahāvairocana. It too is an extremely essential and wondrous Dharma, translated by Śubhakarasiṃha. Together with the previous two great teachings and the Susiddhikara, these constitute the three great divisions of the teaching. The Tripiṭaka Master of the Dàxīngshàn Monastery re-translated and transmitted them.

The bhikṣu Hǎiyún, having unworthily received the Buddha's shade and encountered the supreme teaching, having personally received the dharma-eye and been transmitted the Mahāvairocana and Susiddhikara through seven generations of successive lineage, has roughly outlined the essential points and briefly recorded the sequence as he has seen and heard. May those with wisdom kindly point out [any errors].

> Reverently I bow to the all-knowing of the three times,
> Samantabhadra, the secret vajra-holder.
> May this praise and exposition bring blessing to all beings;
> May all vow to be reborn in the Land of Bliss.

The great Tang, Dàhé 8th year [834 CE], the *jiǎyín* year, the eighth day of the first ten-day period of the tenth month.

Compiled and recorded by the bhikṣu Hǎiyún, teacher of the teaching, of the Jìngzhù Monastery.
