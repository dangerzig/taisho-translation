# 悉曇字記
## Record of Siddhaṃ Characters

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2132

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, compiled by Zhiguang (智廣).

---

Siddhaṃ is the script of India. The Record of the Western Regions states: "It was created by Brahmā. The original standard he handed down consisted of forty-seven sounds. These are compounded to represent things and applied in various ways according to context. The branches and tributaries have spread, and the sources have grown ever broader. Because of different places and different peoples, there have been slight modifications. Yet the script of central India is particularly detailed and correct. In the border regions with their different customs, people also learn corrupt forms. But speaking in general terms, the basic origins do not differ." This is the outline.

Recently, while reciting dhāraṇī, I sought out their phonetic values and found much inconsistency. Then a śramaṇa from southern India, Prajñābodhi, brought dhāraṇī manuscripts in Sanskrit. Coming from the Southern Sea, he paid his respects at Mount Wutai and lodged in a mountain hermitage. I then studied under him. Comparing these with the old Chinese transliterations and carefully examining the phonetics of central India, there were not a few discrepancies. Investigating the roots and origins, everything comes back to siddhaṃ. The Indian monk himself said: "As a youth, I studied phonetics under my former teacher Prajñāghoṣa. In the methods of phonological science, I have nearly exhausted the subtleties. The southern Indian tradition inherits the script of Maheśvara. This is it. Central India additionally uses the script from the Nāga palace. There are some differences from southern India, but the fundamental structure is necessarily the same. The Gandhāra script of the Kharoṣṭhī variety is particularly different. Yet the origin of all these scripts is siddhaṃ." I then requested what he had brought and carefully examined the transliterations and annotations, organizing them according to his framework into a systematic treatise.

Though the pronunciations may differ slightly, the principles of the script remain present. I have modeled it on the canonical forms of distant lands, not favoring the exotic. Using Chinese transliteration to approximate Sanskrit pronunciation is, after all, merely an approximation. How can it compare with viewing the original script? I have arranged it so that a student, within a day or two, can grasp Sanskrit phonetics at a distance. The total of characters exceeds seven thousand, yet the effort is small and the utility is great. How admirable is the wisdom of the sages in benefiting beings! The total retention of a single syllable, with principle encompassing manifold virtues, lies herein. Though I have not yet comprehensively surveyed the divisions of that land's historical records, the internal scriptures' applications are certainly well provided.

However, the pronunciations of the Five Indias may differ as those of Chu and Xia differ among us. Students in China should investigate and determine what is correct. I have privately recorded these notes on bamboo slips to preserve this tradition.

---

The system begins with siddhaṃ. The vowels number six; divided into long and short, there are twelve characters in all. These head the chapters that follow, matching the consonants to produce sounds. When consonants combine with vowels, characters are generated. That is: *a* in rising tone is the short pronunciation, *ā* in level tone is the long pronunciation, and so forth.

Among these, there are four characters such as *hṛ* (a combination of two sounds), which exist in siddhaṃ but are not used to generate characters. These are omitted here.

Next come the consonant characters, numbering thirty-five. Together with the preceding siddhaṃ vowels, this gives the forty-seven sounds, as stated. Of the consonants, five each are classified by place of articulation: dental, labial, lingual, guttural, and labial, corresponding to the musical tones. There are also ten characters of "all-mouth" sounds. Among these, *ra*, *ha*, and *la* are combined sounds. In generating characters, they do not apply to all chapters. The *la* sound is entirely absent from generative use. Thus, in the first chapter, *la* is subtracted, leaving one less. In the remaining single chapters, two are subtracted. In the doubled chapters, three are subtracted. In the variant chapter, six are subtracted for characters used at the end of a phrase by other characters, plus its own subtraction. The remainder can each universally generate: *ka*, *kha*, and so forth.

The chapters for generating characters number seventeen. Each generates nearly four hundred characters. Thus the Sanskrit writing system is set forth.

Beyond the regular chapters, there are isolated combination characters, compound characters formed by linking, which are the character names. There are also eleven *mātrā* marks. Two half-form characters, combined, form complete characters.

## Chapter One

The preceding thirty-four consonant characters are paired with the twelve vowels *a*, *ā*, and so forth to produce four hundred and eight characters. That is: the sound *ka* generates twelve characters below it, all using *ka* as the character body, pronounced with the vowels *a*, *ā*, and so forth, with *mātrā* marks added. Each consonant combines with each vowel to produce a distinct form. *Kha*, *ga*, and the rest follow by analogy to complete one chapter. The next fourteen chapters all use the first chapter as the character body. Each adds its respective combination. The vowels *a*, *ā*, and so forth are paired with the combined consonant characters to pronounce them. Then the *mātrā* marks are added. When two identical characters would combine, they are allowed to remain without generating, as in the doubled *ra* of the fourth chapter, the doubled *va* of the fifth chapter, the doubled *ma* of the sixth chapter, the doubled *na* of the seventh chapter, and so forth. Chapters eleven through fourteen follow the same pattern as the four chapters above, with the same subtractions.

## Chapter Two

The half-form *ya* is combined below the characters *ka*, *kā*, and so forth from the first chapter. These are named *kya*, *kyā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and ninety-six.

## Chapter Three

The character *ra* is combined below the characters *ka*, *kā*, and so forth from the first chapter. These are named *kra*, *krā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and ninety-six.

## Chapter Four

The character *la* is combined below the first chapter characters. These are named *kla*, *klā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and eighty-four.

## Chapter Five

The character *va* is combined below the first chapter characters. These are named *kva*, *kvā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and eighty-four.

## Chapter Six

The character *ma* is combined below the first chapter characters. These are named *kma*, *kmā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and eighty-four.

## Chapter Seven

The character *na* is combined below the first chapter characters. These are named *kna*, *knā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and eighty-four.

## Chapter Eight

The half-form *r* is added above the first chapter characters. These are named *rka*, *rkā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and ninety-six.

This chapter's characters are the same as the first chapter, but with the half-form *ra* added above each character, followed by the *mātrā* marks. Furthermore, this chapter serves as the character body for the next six chapters, just as the second chapter onward follows the first, but with the half-form *ra* added.

## Chapter Nine

The half-form *r* is added above the second chapter characters. These are named *rkya*, *rkyā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and eighty-four.

## Chapter Ten

## Chapter Eleven

## Chapter Twelve

The half-form *r* is added above the fifth chapter characters. These are named *rkva*, *rkvā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and eighty-four.

## Chapter Thirteen

The half-form *r* is added above the sixth chapter characters. These are named *rkma*, *rkmā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and eighty-four.

## Chapter Fourteen

The half-form *r* is added above the seventh chapter characters. These are named *rkna*, *rknā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and eighty-four.

## Chapter Fifteen

The fifth character of each group among *ka*, *ca*, *ṭa*, *ta*, *pa*, and so forth is added above the preceding four characters of that group. Also, the final character of the first group is added above the nine characters *ya* and so forth. These never double with themselves. There are only twenty-nine characters. They do not combine through vowels; this is called the variant chapter. Each is pronounced with the vowels *a*, *ā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and forty-eight.

## Chapter Sixteen

The consonant character bodies are combined with a special *mātrā*. These are called *kṛ* and so forth. Characters produced number thirty-four.

## Chapter Seventeen

The consonant character bodies are combined with each other in various mixtures, numbering thirty-three characters. Each is named according to its formation, such as *aśka* and so forth. Each is pronounced with the twelve vowels *a*, *ā*, and so forth. Generated characters number three hundred and ninety-six.

## Chapter Eighteen: Isolated Combination Characters

Beyond the regular chapters, there are isolated combination characters. Some are identical characters doubled, in which case they are simply pronounced with the full sound of the character. Some are different characters stacked, in which case the sounds are linked in sequence. Some do not take the full range of *mātrā* marks, remaining as isolated combinations. Some, though they may generate twelve forms, have irregular origins and are thus treated as isolated. Some, though stacked from different characters, are not necessarily pronounced as stacked forms. Some are two characters linked in sound, where the graphic form shows the latter character but the pronunciation foregrounds the former. Some are single characters with divided names. Some use *mātrā* characters with additional *mātrā* marks whose sounds must include both. Some have forms that are not *mātrā* but serve independently as ornamental characters. Some are formed in one way but named differently. Some have sounds but no graphic forms. Some do not derive from characters but exist independently as half-forms. Some characters have lacunae, in which case the characters *ta-da* are added, and the sound is pronounced with a clipped tone. Some originate from characters but acquire different forms. Some, when their origins are investigated, reveal different forms. These constitute the comprehensive overview of Sanskrit writing.

The preface states that *ra*, in generating characters, should not apply to all chapters. This means that when used in the second, fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh chapters, those characters belong to the eighth chapter. When used in the third and eighth chapters, it would create an identical doubling, which does not belong to this chapter. When used in the ninth through fourteenth chapters, it would create redundant doubling, which is entirely non-standard. The doubled form of *ra*, and the doubled forms within the stacked chapters, are each preserved as a single doubled character by the scribe at that point, without generating the twelve forms. Although there may be occasional uses that take three or five *mātrā* marks, since they cannot universally generate, they do not enter the count of generated characters within these chapters. Because a single doubled character is preserved, the text says "allow it to remain without generating." The eighteenth chapter says "some are identical characters doubled, simply pronounced with the full sound," referring to *ta*, *jha*, *ṭa*, *ṇa*, and the like, each of which has a doubled form. "And so forth" means all other consonants likewise have doubled forms. They are simply given their full pronunciation; one does not say *ta-ta*, *ra-ra*, and so forth.

## Siddhaṃ Vowels

*Namaḥ sarva-jñāya.* Siddhaṃ.

Short *a*.

Long *ā*.

Short *i*.

Long *ī*.

Short *u*.

Long *ū*.

Short *e*.

Long *ai*.

Short *o*.

Long *au*.

Short *aṃ*.

Long *aḥ*.

Tripiṭaka Master Yijing says: "Among the above three pairs, the upper member of the first three pairs is short and the lower is long. In the last three pairs, the upper is long and the lower is short."

The above twelve siddhaṃ characters serve as vowels for the subsequent chapters. For example, using the consonant *ka*, one pairs it with the twelve vowels *a*, *i*, *u*, and so forth to generate the twelve characters *ka*, *ki*, *ku*, and so forth below it. Next, using the consonant *kha*, one generates the twelve characters *kha*, *khi*, *khu*, and so forth. Then *ga*, *gi*, *gu*, and so forth are generated. All the rest follow by analogy. First, write the short *ka* characters in a row of twelve. From the second character onward, add the respective *mātrā* marks, and the character forms become distinct. Pronounce them with the siddhaṃ vowels to learn their names. *Kha*, *ga*, and the rest follow in the same manner down to *kṣa*, thus completing one chapter.

What was formerly called the fourteen sounds means that within the twelve siddhaṃ characters, after the *u* vowel, there are four additional characters: *hṛ*, *hrī*, *lṛ*, and *lṝ*. The last two siddhaṃ characters, known as the boundary characters, are removed, and the remainder makes fourteen. Here, for the purpose of generating characters, the four characters *hṛ* and so forth are omitted.

## Consonant Characters

*Ka*. *Kha*. *Ga*. *Gha*. *Ṅa*.

The above five characters are guttural sounds.

*Ca*. *Cha*. *Ja*. *Jha*. *Ña*.

The above five characters are palatal sounds.

*Ṭa*. *Ṭha*. *Ḍa*. *Ḍha*. *Ṇa*.

The above five characters are retroflex sounds.

*Ta*. *Tha*. *Da*. *Dha*. *Na*.

The above five characters are dental sounds.

*Pa*. *Pha*. *Ba*. *Bha*. *Ma*.

The above five characters are labial sounds.

*Ya*. *Ra*. *La*. *Va*. *Śa*. *Ṣa*. *Sa*. *Ha*. *Llaṃ*. *Kṣa*.

The above ten characters are all-mouth sounds.

The above consonant characters number thirty-five. The subsequent chapters use thirty-four as character bodies. Only *llaṃ* is entirely unable to generate. The rest generate according to their respective capabilities, as discussed in the regular chapters.
