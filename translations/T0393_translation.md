# 迦葉赴佛般涅槃經
## Sūtra of Kāśyapa Going to the Buddha's Parinirvāṇa

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0393

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

When the Buddha was in the world, Mahākāśyapa was the most senior in age among all the bhikṣus, brilliant in wisdom, and his body also bore the golden-colored marks. Whenever the Buddha taught the Dharma, Kāśyapa always sat facing him, and the people, seeing this, sometimes called him the Buddha's teacher. Because of this, Kāśyapa took leave of the Buddha and went to Mount Iśīrī. There was a mountain called Universal Ability, circumscribing several thousand li, situated twenty-six thousand li from Śrāvastī, which produced abundant seven treasures, sweet fruits beyond reckoning, famous incenses, fine medicines, and three kinds of candana: one kind fragrant, one kind that cures all human illnesses, and one kind that can be used to dye five colors. The multitude of fragrances and various medicines was beyond counting. There were also animals and birds: lions, tigers, wolves, white elephants, unicorns, vermilion sparrows, and phoenixes. There were also pure and different-practice ascetics. At that time there was a square stone, level and smooth, its color like lapis lazuli, extending one hundred and twenty li on each side. Extraordinary trees provided shade and coolness, with flowers and leaves of five colors, flourishing in both winter and summer, growing in rows upon the stone. Kāśyapa guided and taught two thousand disciples, in successive groups, all of them pure and lofty practitioners who had attained arhatship. They always sat upon this stone, reciting sūtras and practicing the path. There was also a pure spring of sweet and fragrant water, circumscribing forty li, in which there were utpala flowers, dark-blue flowers, red flowers, and purple flowers.

Seven of Kāśyapa's disciples, on the same night, had dreams. One bhikṣu dreamed that the square stone on which they sat split in two down the middle, and the trees were all uprooted. Another bhikṣu dreamed that the forty-li spring had dried up completely and all the flowers had fallen and wilted. One bhikṣu dreamed that all the seats on the bank had collapsed and crumbled. One bhikṣu dreamed that the entire Jambudvīpa had sunk and caved in. One bhikṣu dreamed that Mount Sumeru had collapsed. One bhikṣu dreamed that a golden-wheel king had died. One bhikṣu dreamed that the sun and moon fell to the earth and all the world lost its light.

At dawn, each reported their dream to Kāśyapa. Kāśyapa told them: "We previously saw great radiance, and the earth at that time shook greatly. Now you have all had these dreams. The Buddha is about to enter parinirvāṇa." He immediately ordered all his disciples to set out for the country of Kuśinagara.

On the road they met a brahmin carrying mandārava flowers. Kāśyapa immediately asked him: "Where have you come from? Where are you going? How did you obtain these heavenly flowers?"

He answered: "I have come from the country of Kuśinagara. The Buddha entered parinirvāṇa seven days ago. All the devas went to attend, bearing heavenly flowers and heavenly incense as offerings for the Buddha's body. These flowers are from there."

When Kāśyapa heard these words, he threw himself to the ground, weeping and saying: "The Buddha has now entered parinirvāṇa. The three realms have lost their light. On whom shall we rely?" He then led his disciples onward. When they were still several hundred li away, he saw the four heavenly kings, Brahmā, Śakra, and all the devas, bearing seven-jeweled parasols, famous incense, and fine flowers, all going to make offerings to the Buddha. The devas played the twelve kinds of music. There were also asura kings and great ghosts and spirits filling the sky. He also saw the king of Kuśinagara and kings of neighboring countries, each with their ministers and retinues numbering millions.

When they saw Kāśyapa arriving with his disciples, King Mallaprāpta of that country immediately ordered the people to clear the road so that Kāśyapa and his disciples could advance.

Aniruddha came out to welcome him, and when they met said: "The Buddha entered parinirvāṇa seven days ago. The cremation fire has not yet ignited; it is only waiting for the Worthy One's arrival."

When Ānanda saw Kāśyapa, he threw himself to the ground, weeping uncontrollably. An old bhikṣu named Pāvaka then stopped Ānanda, saying: "Stop! Stop! When the Buddha was alive, he always restrained and restricted us; we had no freedom. Now that the Buddha has entered parinirvāṇa, we can be at ease. Stop crying!" At that time a deva, hearing Pāvaka's words, immediately struck him with his hand. Kāśyapa went forward and restrained the deva, who then stopped. He said to Pāvaka: "The Buddha has now entered parinirvāṇa, and all have lost their refuge. You alone are so foolish as to rejoice instead!" When Pāvaka heard these words, his mind opened, and he immediately attained the path of arhatship.

Kāśyapa and his disciples then placed their foreheads on the ground, bowed in reverence, and circumambulated the coffin three times, lamenting: "We today do not know where the Buddha's head and feet are." By the Buddha's supernatural power, his feet then extended out of the coffin. All the devas and humans were moved to grief.

Thereupon Mahākāśyapa spoke verses praising the Buddha:

> The Buddha is the vehicle of the three realms,
> Ferrying all across the abyss of birth and death.
> In tranquil serenity he ascends to nirvāṇa,
> Subtle and wondrous, transcending the world.

> The Buddha is the immeasurable light,
> Illuminating the darkness of delusion.
> May he, for the sake of all,
> Display and manifest his awesome spiritual power.

> The Buddha is great compassion and pity;
> Those he has liberated are beyond number.
> His honored body rests in a golden coffin,
> Pure and peaceful in tranquil repose.

> May he, by his gentle, harmonious virtue,
> Reveal the radiance of his bodily marks,
> And universally cause all devas and humans
> To give rise to immeasurable merit.

> The Buddha opened and revealed the Dharma;
> Sentient beings received its nourishing moisture.
> Some stopped the wheel of birth and death;
> Others entered the truth of the right path.

> Having received the Tathāgata's grace,
> I bow my head to the Buddha's feet.
> Now I see only the golden coffin,
> And my heart is stricken with grief.

> Though the Buddha has entered the unconditioned,
> The sage's realization is entirely true.
> Seeing those who came later with doubts about the truth,
> He extended his feet from the golden coffin.

> The arising and departing of birth and death:
> The Buddha no longer sorrows over them.
> The Dharma-body and wisdom abide forever.
> Do not say he has entered nirvāṇa for eternity.
