# 佛說求欲經
## The Sūtra on Covetous Desire

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 49

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmaruci (法炬).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, Bhagavān was in the Deer Park at Bhesakaḷā Grove in the Suṃsumāragira country of the Bhagga people. At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra addressed the bhikṣus: "Venerables! In this world there are four kinds of people that can be recognized. What are the four? Here, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has covetous desire, and having inwardly covetous desire, he does not know it as it truly is. Here, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has covetous desire, and having inwardly covetous desire, he knows it as it truly is. Here, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has no covetous desire, and having inwardly no covetous desire, he does not know it as it truly is. Here, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has no covetous desire, and having inwardly no covetous desire, he knows it as it truly is. As for that one person who inwardly has covetous desire and, having inwardly covetous desire, does not know it as it truly is, I say that this person is the most base and wretched. As for the person who inwardly has covetous desire and, having inwardly covetous desire, knows it as it truly is, I say that this person is the most excellent. As for the person who inwardly has no covetous desire and, having inwardly no covetous desire, does not know it as it truly is, I say that this person is the most base and wretched. As for the person who inwardly has no covetous desire and, having inwardly no covetous desire, knows it as it truly is, I say that this person is the most excellent."

At that time, a certain bhikṣu rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, joined his palms toward the Venerable Śāriputra, and addressed the Venerable Śāriputra, saying: "Venerable Śāriputra, what is the cause and what is the condition that, of the first two kinds of people who both have covetous desire and both have minds of attachment, you declare one to be base and wretched and the other to be most excellent? And further, what is the cause and what is the condition that, of the latter two kinds of people who both have no covetous desire and both have no attachment, you declare one to be base and wretched and the other to be most excellent?"

"Here, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has covetous desire and, having inwardly covetous desire, does not know it as it truly is. You should know that he does not delight in practice, does not make progress in practice, and does not apply diligence. He dwells in covetous desire and dies with a mind attached to covetous desire. Having died with a mind attached to covetous desire, he does not die well and is not reborn in a good destination. Why? Because he dies with a mind attached to covetous desire. It is like this, venerables: suppose a person, whether at a marketplace or at a hired-worker's house, obtains a copper bowl that is filthy and unclean. Having obtained it, he neither washes it at the proper time, nor wipes it at the proper time, nor polishes it at the proper time, but simply leaves it in the dust. In this way, that copper bowl only accumulates more filth. Just so, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has covetous desire and, having inwardly covetous desire, does not know it as it truly is. You should know that he does not delight in practice, does not make progress in practice, and does not apply diligence. He simply dwells in covetous desire and dies with a mind inwardly attached to craving. Having died with a mind inwardly attached to craving, he does not die well and is not reborn in a good destination. Why? Because he dies with a mind inwardly attached to covetous desire.

"Here, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has covetous desire and, having inwardly covetous desire, knows it as it truly is. You should know that he delights in practice and applies diligence. His covetous desire ceases; free of covetous desire, with a mind unattached, he dies. Having died free of covetous desire, with a mind unattached, he dies well and is reborn in a good destination. Why? Because he is free of covetous desire and his mind is unattached. It is like this, venerables: suppose a person, whether at a marketplace or at a hired-worker's house, obtains a copper bowl that is filthy and unclean. Having obtained it, he washes it at the proper time, wipes it at the proper time, polishes it at the proper time, and does not leave it in the dust. Afterward, this copper bowl becomes clean and bright. Just so, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has covetous desire and, having inwardly covetous desire, knows it as it truly is. You should know that he delights in practice, makes progress in practice, and applies diligence. His covetous desire is severed; free of covetous desire, with a mind unattached, he dies. Having died free of covetous desire, with a mind unattached, he dies well and is reborn in a good destination. Why? Because he is free of covetous desire and his mind is unattached at the time of death.

"Here, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has no covetous desire and, having inwardly no covetous desire, does not know it as it truly is. You should know that he is unable to guard the dharmas of eye, ear, mind, and their objects. Having dwelt in the inability to guard the dharmas of eye, ear, mind, and their objects, his mind develops lustful desire, and with desire mingled and craving mingled, with a mind of attachment, he dies. Having died with desire mingled and craving mingled, with a mind of attachment, he does not die well and is not reborn well. Why? Because he dies with desire mingled and craving mingled, with a mind of attachment. It is like this, venerables: suppose a person, whether at a marketplace or at a hired-worker's house, obtains a clean copper bowl. Having obtained it, he neither washes it at the proper time, nor wipes it at the proper time, nor polishes it at the proper time, and leaves it in the dust. In this way, that copper bowl afterward becomes filthy and unclean. Just so, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has no covetous desire and, having inwardly no covetous desire, does not know it as it truly is. You should know that he is unable to guard the dharmas of eye, ear, mind, and their objects. Having been unable to guard the dharmas of eye, ear, mind, and their objects, he develops lustful desire; with desire mingled and craving mingled, with a mind of attachment, he dies. Having died with desire mingled and craving mingled, with a mind of attachment, he does not die well and is not reborn well. Why? Because he dies with desire mingled and craving mingled, with a mind of attachment.

"Here, venerables, there is a person who inwardly has no covetous desire and, having inwardly no covetous desire, knows it as it truly is. You should know that he is able to guard the dharmas of eye, ear, mind, and their objects. Having guarded the dharmas of eye, ear, mind, and their objects, he has no lustful desire; free from desire and free from craving, with a mind unattached, he dies. Having died free from desire and free from craving, with a mind unattached, he dies well and is reborn well. Why? Because he is free from craving and free from desire, with a mind unattached at the time of death. It is like this, venerables: suppose a person, whether at a marketplace or at a hired-worker's house, obtains a clean copper bowl. Having obtained it, he washes it at the proper time, wipes it at the proper time, polishes it at the proper time, and does not leave it in the dust. In this way, that bowl becomes even more exceedingly clean and bright. Just so, venerables, that person is also like this: inwardly having no covetous desire and, having inwardly no covetous desire, knowing it as it truly is. You should know that he is able to guard the dharmas of eye, ear, mind, and their objects. Having guarded the dharmas of eye, ear, mind, and their objects, he has no lustful desire; free from mingled desire and craving, with a mind unattached, he dies. Having died free from mingled desire and mingled craving, with a mind unattached, he dies well and is reborn well. Why? Because he is free from mingled desire and free from mingled craving, with a mind unattached at the time of death. This, venerables, is the cause and the condition by which, of the first two kinds of people with covetous desire and minds of attachment, one is declared base and wretched and the other most excellent. This is the cause and the condition by which, of the latter two kinds of people who inwardly have no covetous desire and minds without attachment, one is declared base and wretched and the other most excellent."

At that time, a certain bhikṣu rose from his seat, arranged his robe over one shoulder, joined his palms toward the Venerable Śāriputra, and addressed the Venerable Śāriputra, saying: "Venerable Śāriputra, what is called covetous desire? As for covetous desire, why is it called covetous desire?"

"Venerables, because through covetous desire one generates immeasurable evil dharmas, it is called covetous desire. Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises: he commits a minor offense and does not want others to know about it, yet he does have the offense. Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when others do come to know of that person's offense, his mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once he is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome. Here, venerables, there is a person in whom a little covetous desire arises: he has committed an offense, but he only confesses it privately to another person, not in the assembly; that is his offense. Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when that person's offense is spoken of in the assembly rather than in private, his mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome. Here, venerables, there is a person who has committed an offense and wishes to confess it to an equal, not to one who is not his equal; he has committed an offense. Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when that person's offense is confessed to one who is not his equal, and it is confessed to one who is not his equal, that is his offense, and his mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome. Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises, thinking: 'I should sit before the World-Honored One; I should question the World-Honored One, and he should teach the Dharma for the bhikṣus. No other bhikṣu should sit before the World-Honored One, question the World-Honored One, and teach the Dharma for the bhikṣus.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when another bhikṣu does sit before the World-Honored One, questions the World-Honored One, and is able to teach the Dharma for the bhikṣus, that person's mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises, thinking: 'When the bhikṣus go somewhere, let me walk at the front; do not let another bhikṣu walk at the front when the bhikṣus go somewhere.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when the bhikṣus go somewhere and a different bhikṣu walks at the front, when a different bhikṣu walks in front of that bhikṣu when the bhikṣus go somewhere, his mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises, thinking: 'When these bhikṣus enter a house, let me have the foremost seat, receive water first, and receive ball-food first; do not let another bhikṣu, when the bhikṣus enter a house, have the foremost seat, receive water first, or receive ball-food first.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when a different bhikṣu, after the bhikṣus have entered, takes the foremost seat, receives water first, and receives ball-food first. When that different bhikṣu, after the bhikṣus have entered, takes the foremost seat, receives water first, and receives ball-food first, that person's mind becomes angry. Once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises, thinking: 'When the bhikṣus have finished eating and put away their bowls, let me teach the Dharma to the householders, encourage them, instruct them, and cause them to rejoice; do not let another bhikṣu, when the bhikṣus have finished eating and put away their bowls, teach the Dharma to the householders, encourage them, instruct them, and cause them to rejoice.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when another bhikṣu, after the bhikṣus have finished eating and put away their bowls, does cause them to rejoice. When that other bhikṣu, after the bhikṣus have finished eating and put away their bowls, does cause them to rejoice, that person's mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises, thinking: 'When householders enter the park, let me converse and discuss with the householders; do not let another bhikṣu converse and discuss with the householders when they enter the park.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when a different bhikṣu, after the householders have entered the park, converses and discusses with them. When that other bhikṣu, after the householders have entered the park, converses and discusses with them, that person's mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises, thinking: 'Let kings, great ministers, brāhmaṇas, householders, and many others all know me; do not let other bhikṣus be known by kings, great ministers, brāhmaṇas, householders, and many others.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus are known by kings and great ministers, and by brāhmaṇas, householders, and many others. When those other bhikṣus are known by kings and great ministers, and by brāhmaṇas, householders, and many others, that person's mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises, thinking: 'Let me receive offerings from the fourfold assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās; do not let other bhikṣus receive offerings from the fourfold assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus receive offerings from the fourfold assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās. When those other bhikṣus receive offerings from the fourfold assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, that person's mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom covetous desire arises, thinking: 'Let me obtain robes, bedding, and medicines for illness; do not let other bhikṣus obtain robes, bedding, and medicines for illness.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus obtain robes, bedding, and medicines for illness. When those other bhikṣus obtain robes, bedding, and medicines for illness, that person's mind becomes angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is angry, whatever he proceeds to do is only unwholesome.

"Here, venerables, a person such as this, even if he has wise fellow practitioners of the holy life, should abandon these immeasurable evil and unwholesome practices of covetous desire, and should not engage in them. If he associates with those who are not true śramaṇas while acting as a śramaṇa, if he associates with those who are not wise śramaṇas while acting as a wise śramaṇa, if he should not seek a high seat yet seeks a high seat, if he has no attainment yet claims attainment, if he dwells with impurity yet claims purity: venerables, such a person, among his wise fellow practitioners of the holy life, engages in these immeasurable evil and unwholesome practices. Knowing there are such practices, knowing he associates with those who are not true śramaṇas and is not a true śramaṇa, knowing he associates with those who are not wise śramaṇas and is not a wise śramaṇa, knowing he should not seek a high seat yet seeks a high seat, knowing he has no attainment yet has no attainment, knowing he dwells with impurity and is impure. It is like this, venerables: suppose a person, whether at a marketplace or at a hired-worker's house, obtains a copper bowl filled with unclean substances, covered with another bowl on top. If he brings it into a gathering of people, those many people, seeing it, would want to eat and would want to have it, not knowing it contains unclean substances. If someone in the gathering takes it to a certain place and uncovers it, then whoever had wanted to eat no longer wants to eat. How much less would the one who brought it want to eat! Just so, venerables, a person among his wise fellow practitioners of the holy life who engages in these immeasurable evil and unwholesome practices, who associates with those who are not true śramaṇas while acting as a śramaṇa, who associates with those who are not wise śramaṇas while acting as a wise śramaṇa, who should not seek a high seat yet seeks a high seat, who has no attainment yet claims attainment, who dwells with impurity yet claims purity: venerables, such a person, among his wise fellow practitioners of the holy life, engages in these immeasurable evil and unwholesome dharmas. Knowing he associates with those who are not true śramaṇas, he is not a true śramaṇa; knowing he associates with those who are not wise śramaṇas, he is not a wise śramaṇa; he should not seek a high seat yet seeks a high seat; he dwells with non-attainment, and it is non-attainment; he dwells with impurity, and it is impurity. Therefore, venerables, one should not draw close to such a person, should not revere him, and should not serve him. Whether a bhikṣu draws close to one who is worthy of respect or one who is not, whether he pays service to one who is worthy of service or one who is not, if he follows such a person, throughout the long night of existence he will have only loss and no benefit, suffering in correspondence with evil destinies. Therefore, venerables, one should not draw close to such a person, should not revere him, and should not serve him.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'If I commit an offense, I do not want others to know.' Yet he does commit an offense. Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when others do come to know of that person's offense, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome. Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who commits an offense and confesses it to another privately, not to the assembly. Yet he does commit an offense. Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when that person's offense is spoken of in the assembly rather than confessed privately to another individual, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'If I commit an offense, I confess it to an equal, not to one who is not my equal.' Yet he does commit an offense. Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when that person's offense is confessed to one who is not his equal rather than to an equal, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'I sit before the World-Honored One, having questioned the World-Honored One, and the World-Honored One teaches the Dharma for the bhikṣus. I do not want another bhikṣu to sit before the World-Honored One, question the World-Honored One, and have the World-Honored One teach the Dharma for the bhikṣus.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when a different bhikṣu sits before the World-Honored One and the World-Honored One teaches the Dharma for the bhikṣus. When another bhikṣu sits before the World-Honored One, questions the World-Honored One, and the World-Honored One teaches the Dharma for the bhikṣus, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'When the bhikṣus go somewhere, let me walk at the front; let other bhikṣus walk at the front. When the bhikṣus go somewhere, do not let another bhikṣu walk at the front.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus walk at the front when the bhikṣus go somewhere. When another bhikṣu walks at the front when the bhikṣus go somewhere, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'When the bhikṣus enter a house, let me have the foremost seat, receive water first, and receive ball-food first; do not let another bhikṣu, when the bhikṣus enter a house, have the foremost seat, receive water first, or receive ball-food first.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when a different bhikṣu, after the bhikṣus have entered, takes the foremost seat, receives water first, and receives ball-food first, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'When the bhikṣus have finished eating and put away their bowls, let me teach the Dharma to the householders, instruct and encourage them, and cause them to rejoice; do not let another bhikṣu, when the bhikṣus have finished eating and put away their bowls, teach the Dharma to the householders, instruct and encourage them, and cause them to rejoice.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus, after the bhikṣus have finished eating and put away their bowls, teach the Dharma to the householders, encourage and instruct them, and cause them to rejoice, his mind does not become angry. Venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'When householders enter the park, let me converse with them; do not let another bhikṣu converse with the householders when they enter the park.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus, after the householders have entered the park, converse with them. When other bhikṣus, after the householders have entered the park, converse with them, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'Let kings, great ministers, brāhmaṇas, householders, and many others know me; do not let other bhikṣus be known by kings, great ministers, brāhmaṇas, householders, and many others.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus are known by kings, great ministers, brāhmaṇas, householders, and many others. When other bhikṣus are known by kings, great ministers, brāhmaṇas, householders, and many others, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'Let me receive offerings from the fourfold assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās; do not let other bhikṣus receive offerings from the fourfold assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus receive offerings from the fourfold assembly of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Here, venerables, there is a person in whom no covetous desire arises, who thinks: 'Let me obtain robes, bedding, and medicines for illness; I do not want other bhikṣus to obtain robes, bedding, and medicines for illness.' Venerables, you should recognize this situation: when other bhikṣus obtain robes, bedding, and medicines for illness. When other bhikṣus obtain robes, bedding, and medicines for illness, his mind does not become angry. Here, venerables, once his mind is free from anger, whatever he proceeds to do is only wholesome.

"Venerables, such a person, among his wise fellow practitioners of the holy life, delights in immeasurable wholesome dharma-practices. Even if people do not recognize that he associates with true śramaṇas and call him not a true śramaṇa, even if he associates with wise śramaṇas and they call him not a wise śramaṇa, even if he associates with elders and they call him not an elder, even if he dwells with attainment and they call it non-attainment, even if he dwells with purity and they call it impurity: venerables, such a person, among his wise fellow practitioners of the holy life, possesses immeasurable wholesome practices, all of which should be recognized. He associates with true śramaṇas and should be recognized as associating with true śramaṇas. He associates with wise śramaṇas and should be recognized as associating with wise śramaṇas. He associates with attainment and should be recognized as associating with attainment. He associates with purity and should be recognized as associating with purity. It is like this, venerables: suppose a person, whether at a marketplace or at a hired-worker's house, obtains a copper bowl filled with food and drink of various flavors, covered with another bowl on top. If he brings it into a gathering of people, those many people, seeing it, would not want to eat and would not want to have it, thinking it is not food. They would say: 'It is the same vessel as before! It is the same vessel as before!' When he brings it into the gathering of many people and uncovers it, then those who did not want to eat now wish to eat. How much more would those who saw it before not want to eat? Just so, venerables, a person among his wise fellow practitioners of the holy life possessing these immeasurable wholesome practices may not be recognized: he associates with true śramaṇas yet they say he is not a true śramaṇa; he associates with wise śramaṇas yet they say he is not a wise śramaṇa; he associates with elders yet they say he is not an elder; he dwells with attainment yet they say it is non-attainment; he dwells with purity yet they say it is impurity. Venerables, such a person, among his wise fellow practitioners of the holy life, possesses immeasurable wholesome practices, which are afterward recognized: he associates with true śramaṇas and is recognized as a true śramaṇa; he associates with wise śramaṇas and is recognized as a wise śramaṇa; he associates with elders and is recognized as an elder; he dwells with attainment and is recognized as having attainment; he dwells with purity and is recognized as pure. Therefore, venerables, one should revere such a person, should serve him, and should pay him respect. A bhikṣu should draw close to him, should revere whoever is worthy of reverence, and should serve whoever is worthy of service. One should always practice what should always be practiced. Following such a person, throughout the long night of existence, he will have only benefit, ease, and happiness. Therefore, venerables, one should draw close to such a person, should revere him, and should serve him."

At that time, the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana was also present in the assembly. Thereupon Mahāmaudgalyāyana said to the Venerable Śāriputra: "Venerable Śāriputra! I wish to offer a simile. Shall I speak it?"

"Please speak, Venerable Maudgalyāyana!"

"Venerable Śāriputra! Once, when I was at Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain in Rājagṛha, Venerable Śāriputra, I put on my robes in the early morning and, taking my robe and bowl, went to Rājagṛha to beg for food. While going about Rājagṛha begging for food, I came to the house of another artisan. Venerable Śāriputra, I saw Anenjaputta at that other artisan's house; I saw him observing the shaping of a wheel-axle. That Anenjaputta, at the artisan's house, had this thought arise: 'If this artisan takes this axle and trims it at this spot, then the flaw in this axle will be removed.' Then Anenjaputta, at the artisan's house, had this thought arise, and that artisan, just as he had thought, took up his axe and trimmed the wheel-axle. At that moment Anenjaputta said: 'This artisan, as if knowing my mind, took this axle and trimmed it at just that spot. Thus the axle will be improved, without any flaw.' Just so, Venerable Śāriputra, for those people who are flattering, deceitful, swindlers, without faith, lazy, lacking diligence, whose minds are scattered and without concentration, of poor wisdom and disturbed intentions, whose faculties are unsettled, and who are lax in the practice of precepts and do not discern the practice of a śramaṇa: the Venerable Śāriputra knows their thoughts and explains the teaching accordingly. As for those people, Venerable Śāriputra, who are without flattery, who are not deceitful or swindlers, who have faith and conviction, who are diligent in practice, whose minds are always concentrated, who study with wisdom, who are reverent toward the precepts, and who greatly discern the practice of a śramaṇa: when they hear the Dharma taught by the Venerable Śāriputra, it is like a satisfying drink and a satisfying meal; their mouths and minds fully receive and hold it. Just as, Venerable Śāriputra, when a kṣatriya maiden, a brāhmaṇa maiden, a householder's daughter, or a craftsman's daughter, having bathed and anointed herself with fragrance and put on white, clean garments, if a person with kindness and compassion in their heart, wishing to benefit her and to bring her peace, were to present her with a garland of blue lotuses, a garland of campaka flowers, a garland of vārṣika flowers, or a garland of atimuktaka flowers, she would receive it with both hands and place it upon her own head. Just so, Venerable Śāriputra, a person who is without flattery and without deceit, who is not without faith but has faith, who is able to practice diligently, whose mind is always concentrated, who studies with wisdom in reverence for the precepts, and who greatly discerns the practice of a śramaṇa: when he hears the Dharma taught by the Venerable Śāriputra, it is like a satisfying drink and a satisfying meal; his mouth and mind receive it fully. How wondrous this is, Venerable Śāriputra! The Venerable Śāriputra, for his fellow practitioners of the holy life, removes the unwholesome and establishes them in the wholesome. How excellent, Venerable! This is the mark of a true person."

When each had finished speaking, they delighted in one another's words, rose from their seats, and each returned to his own place.
