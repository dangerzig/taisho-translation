# 佛說大乘善見變化文殊師利問法經
## Sūtra of the Mahāyāna Sudarśana Transformation: Mañjuśrī's Inquiry about the Dharma, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0472

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Devaśāntika (天息災)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the World-Honored One was on Vulture Peak Mountain in the city of Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, and great bodhisattvas such as Mañjuśrī and others, who surrounded him. Then the Buddha addressed the youth Mañjuśrī: "Child of the Buddha! I now wish to teach the true Dharma of the Four Noble Truths for all sentient beings who, in their minds, generate inverted views regarding the Four Noble Truths, constantly abide in saṃsāra, and are unable to escape."

Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha! By what causes and conditions are sentient beings unable to distance themselves from this illusory saṃsāra, neither awakening nor knowing?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! I see sentient beings undergoing this illusory saṃsāra. Why? Good man! It is all because, since beginningless time, they have falsely generated discriminating attachment, distinguishing between self and others. Mañjuśrī! For this reason, they receive the karmic retribution of foolishness, undergoing illusory saṃsāra. Why? These foolish sentient beings, not hearing and not knowing the most supreme dharma of universal quiescence, do not examine themselves with wisdom, nor alert themselves regarding the three karmas. Indulging body, speech, and mind, they create manifold afflictions: my craving, my anger, my delusion, and so on. Now, within the Tathāgata's Dharma, I have attained this going forth, received the pure precepts, cultivated the pure practice, departed from saṃsāra, attained the path of nirvāṇa, and gained liberation from the wheel of suffering.

"One should further reflect: the nature of these afflictions is itself the nature of wholesome dharmas, is itself the nature of dharmas with outflows, is itself the nature of dharmas without outflows, is itself the dharma of saṃsāra, is itself the worldly and the supramundane, is itself wisdom and the dharma of removal, is itself the definitive dharma, is itself the contemplation of the dharma of complete wisdom, is itself the contemplation of suffering, arising, cessation, and the definitive path, and extends to the definitive dharma-realm.

"One should further reflect: all formations are false; all formations are suffering; all formations are without characteristics. If I attain this, I can then distance myself from all falsity and attain rebirth at will. If one contemplates the view of self without departing from the truth of the path, one attains that dharma and acts at will. Furthermore, regarding that dharma, one recollects without confusion. In all dharmas, the mind makes no distinctions. One who can know in this way attains freedom from disbelief, doubt, and slander. Praising this attainment, one is liberated from all the suffering of the self. At that time, there is not the slightest thing that is difficult to accomplish. If an arhat can know this self, then at the time of death, he sees his own place of rebirth, abandons the original mind, attains the bodhi of the Buddha, and goes freely wherever he wishes, thereby reaching the unconditioned realm. He thus attains the knowledge of suffering and the knowledge of dharmas. If one understands that the self gathers all dharmas, and gives rise to disbelief, doubt, slander, and various fears, and if one does not believe this dharma, then because this arising is exhausted, that person makes this reflection as a definitive truth of cessation.

"That person further reflects: this dharma should be practiced in this way, for it attains the truth of cessation. If this dharma is practiced, this definitive cessation is achieved; this is the definitive determination. If that person's mind gives rise to doubt, after death they will fall into the great hells. What then is true reflection? It is because all dharmas arise."

Then the youth Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How does one see the mind of the Four Noble Truths?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "If one sees that all dharmas are non-arising, one sees the truth of suffering. If one sees the arising and cessation of all dharmas eliminated, this is the truth of the origin. If one sees the most supreme nirvāṇa, the characteristic of the universal quiescence of all dharmas, this is the truth of cessation. If one sees the ultimate nature of all dharmas, this is the truth of the path. Mañjuśrī! If that person sees these Four Noble Truths as neither real nor unreal, as wholesome and unwholesome dharmas, as dharmas with and without outflows, as worldly and supramundane, as conditioned and unconditioned wisdom, as the dharma without change, as the contemplative and discerning knowledge of suffering and arising, as the definitive truth of cessation, and extending to the definitive dharma-realm truth of the path, which cannot be altered: why then do all foolish and deluded sentient beings indulge in pleasures and desires, and within this dharma of true reality give rise to confusion? Those sentient beings, regarding the dharma of quiescent cessation, do not truly reflect, and so the quiescent self-nature of all dharmas does not become manifest before them. Not knowing that this dharma is neither grasped nor abandoned nor apart from grasping and abandoning: within the realm of craving, nirvāṇa is made manifest; the realm of aversion, the realm of delusion, and even the realm of saṃsāra can all manifest the quiescent realm of nirvāṇa. If in all dharmas one can manifest this equality of self-nature, one will attain unobstructed freedom in all dharmas. How then can one not know the true and real dharma? If regarding the dharma of non-arising and non-ceasing, the mind becomes like empty space, then the equality of the Buddha is unobtainable; the equality of the Dharma is unobtainable; the equality of the Sangha is unobtainable; and even the equality of the quiescent cessation of nirvāṇa is also unobtainable. Thus, regarding all these unprecedented dharmas, no doubts arise, and one attains freedom from doubt. Without arising and without emerging, one reaches the most supreme quiescent realm of nirvāṇa. Mañjuśrī! Thus, in the true principle, all dharmas cannot be seen. This is why Subhūti did not go to prostrate at the Tathāgata's feet. If even Subhūti attained the selfless, how much more so the Tathāgata: how could he be seen? Do not make such an interpretation. Mañjuśrī! If within all these dharmas of non-arising one sees something, that is not seeing the Four Noble Truths."

Then the Dharma-prince Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How does one see the Four Foundations of Mindfulness?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "That Subhūti should contemplate the body as impure, seeing the foundation of mindfulness of the body. He should contemplate feelings as suffering, seeing the foundation of mindfulness of feelings. He should contemplate the mind as impermanent, seeing the foundation of mindfulness of the mind. He should contemplate dharmas as without self, seeing the foundation of mindfulness of dharmas."

Mañjuśrī again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Since this is the true reality, how should it be taught? And how does one see the true Four Foundations of Mindfulness?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Enough! This true principle spoken by the Tathāgata is difficult to understand and difficult to know."

Mañjuśrī said: "May you please teach it, explaining in detail the true Four Foundations of Mindfulness."

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! If you see the body as equal to empty space, you see this foundation of mindfulness of the body within the body. Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If within feelings, whether internal, external, or in between, there is something obtained, then one does not see the foundation of mindfulness of feelings. Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If one sees this mind's wisdom as having shape, being round or square, large or small, then one does not see this foundation of mindfulness of the mind within the mind. Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If within wholesome and unwholesome dharmas, dharmas with and without outflows, and even among afflicted, worldly, and supramundane dharmas, there is something obtained, then one does not see this foundation of mindfulness of dharmas within dharmas. Mañjuśrī! These true Four Foundations of Mindfulness should be understood in this way."

Mañjuśrī addressed the World-Honored One: "How does one see the Four Right Efforts?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "If one contemplates the twelve links of dependent arising as ultimately empty and quiescent, even as without self-nature, and all dharmas as unobtainable, one should give rise to the mind of diligent effort, in accordance with the Dharma eliminating all unwholesome karmas that have arisen. For unwholesome dharmas not yet arisen, one should ensure they do not arise. For wholesome dharmas not yet arisen, one should give rise to the mind of diligent effort to make them arise. For wholesome dharmas already arisen, one should ensure they endure long and are not forgotten; one should give rise to the mind of complete diligent effort. Just as all dharmas are beyond grasping and abandoning, and beyond being apart from grasping and abandoning: if one attains such right mindfulness, that person will no longer give rise to the discriminating mind, and will attain the practice of samādhi. How does that person reflect? By attaining the bases of spiritual power. How does one abide in the equality of all dharmas? Mañjuśrī! The Four Right Efforts should be seen in this way."

Mañjuśrī again said: "World-Honored One! How does one see the Five Faculties?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! If one sees the ultimate non-arising of all dharmas, one understands the faculty of faith. Why? Mañjuśrī! This ultimate non-arising is the faculty of faith because, among all dharmas, the mind is unobtainable; it is originally beyond this name. Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas one is free from recollection and there is no seeking, and one does not abide in any location, this is the faculty of effort. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas one is free from present clarity, far from differentiation, and the mind does not arise, this is the faculty of mindfulness. Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas one can be free from arising and ceasing, free from the perceiver and the perceived, and free from the nature of emptiness and non-emptiness, this is the faculty of concentration. Mañjuśrī! If regarding what nature exists and what does not exist, among all dharmas this is unobtainable, this is the faculty of wisdom. Mañjuśrī! The Five Faculties should be understood and known in this way."

Mañjuśrī said: "World-Honored One! How does one see the Five Powers?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! If one can see this vast mind-dharma that is free from nature and free from characteristics, this is the power of faith. Mañjuśrī! If in seeking the merit of bodhi through effort, one can be free from grasping and abandoning, and free from being apart from grasping and abandoning, this is the power of effort. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas one is free from all recollection and has no discriminating attachment, this is the power of mindfulness. Mañjuśrī! If one reaches the characteristiclessness of all dharmas, this is the power of concentration. Mañjuśrī! If one can be far from all views, even from nirvāṇa, this is the power of wisdom. Mañjuśrī! The Five Powers should be understood and known in this way."

Mañjuśrī said: "World-Honored One! How does one see the Seven Factors of Awakening?"

The Buddha said: "Mañjuśrī! If one sees that all dharmas are without self-nature and free from recollection, this is the awakening factor of mindfulness. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas, the mind cannot be fathomed or measured, and one speaks of wholesome, unwholesome, and the receiving of predictions, this is the awakening factor of investigation of dharmas. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas one can be free from grasping and abandoning, free from being apart from grasping and abandoning, and furthermore among all dharmas one abandons deliberation, this is the awakening factor of effort. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas one does not give rise to attachment, understanding all dharmas as non-arising, this is the awakening factor of joy. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas the mind gives rise to faith and delight, understanding all dharmas as unobtainable, this is the awakening factor of tranquility. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas the mind is free from agitation, this is the awakening factor of concentration. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas there is no abiding and no attachment, they are unknowable, and among all dharmas one does not give rise to craving attachment: if one attains this letting go, this is the awakening factor of equanimity. Mañjuśrī! The seven factors of bodhi should be understood and known in this way."

Mañjuśrī said: "World-Honored One! How does one see the Noble Eightfold Path?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "If one does not see 'right,' and does not see any self-nature in anything, and dharmas are without dual characteristics, and the mind is without obstruction, this is right view. Mañjuśrī! If one sees all dharmas as free from obstruction and as not apart from obstruction, and the mind has no attachment, this is right thought. Mañjuśrī! If one sees all dharmas as without limit, and in accordance with limitlessness speaks the equal and good teaching, this is right speech. Mañjuśrī! If one sees all dharmas as without action, free from the mind of sorrow and compassion, because they are originally non-arising, this is right action. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas there is neither delight nor anger, and all dharmas are non-arising, this is right livelihood. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas there is no arising or ceasing and no force or function, this is right effort. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas, moment by moment nothing arises, there is no awareness, and one is free from all deliberation, this is right mindfulness. Mañjuśrī! If among all dharmas one can distance oneself from both self-nature and non-nature, without any attachment, this is right concentration. Mañjuśrī! This Noble Eightfold Path should be understood and known in this way."

"Mañjuśrī! If there are those who see the mind of the Four Noble Truths in this way, they will see the Four Foundations of Mindfulness, the Four Right Efforts, the Four Bases of Spiritual Power, the Five Faculties, the Five Powers, the Seven Factors of Awakening, and the Eight Factors of the Noble Path. Because of their true mind, they seek the far shore, reach the ground of reality, and attain great peace and bliss. They completely cast off the heavy burden, leave dust behind and are free from defilement. Contemplating the body as without characteristics, they reach the patience of non-arising. As arhats, śramaṇas, and brāhmaṇas, reaching the pure far shore, they are called 'of great learning.' They are true children of the Buddha, children of the Sage. They can overcome enemies, cast away afflictions, attain great firmness, be without aging, without fear, without doubt, and without idle speculation. Without that or this, such a bhikṣu is called a 'banner of the noble Dharma.' Mañjuśrī! If one attains such Dharma-patience, one attains great wholesome benefit and deserves the offerings of all devas, humans, and asuras of the world. Mañjuśrī! Therefore, in all lands one receives, without any void passing, pure food and offerings, for the sake of departing from saṃsāra, reaching the shore of nirvāṇa, and escaping the wheel of suffering, up to and including the true, universal, and right awakening. Giving rise to this mind-dharma, whatever is sought will be obtained."

At that time, when the World-Honored One taught this Dharma, thirty-two thousand devas all attained this Dharma. Those devas scattered māndārava flowers and great māndārava flowers as offerings to the Tathāgata, World-Honored One, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, and to Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva, saying: "May they, within the Tathāgata's Buddha-Dharma, attain the going forth and receive the pure precepts. If this Dharma is taught, may they listen and receive it single-mindedly and attain the path of bodhi." Furthermore, eight thousand one hundred bhikṣus attained the liberation of the exhaustion of outflows and non-arising. Furthermore, forty-two thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of non-arising dharmas. At that time, throughout the three-thousand great-thousand world, all the palaces of the māra kings, the mountains, forests, wilderness, and the great earth shook in six ways. In the sky, heavenly flowers rained down, and a voice praised: "Excellent! Excellent! This Dharma is well taught; it is exceedingly rare!" This sound was heard everywhere throughout the ten directions.

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, the Dharma-prince Mañjuśrī, the great bodhisattvas, the assemblies of bhikṣus, and all the devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and others of the world, hearing the Buddha's teaching, all rejoiced, made obeisance to the Buddha, and departed.
