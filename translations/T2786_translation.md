# T2786 天請問經疏

# Commentary on the Sūtra of Questions Asked by a Deva

*Note: This is an anonymous commentary (shū 疏) on the Devapraśna-sūtra (Tiān Qǐngwèn Jīng 天請問經), a short sūtra in which a deva poses questions to the Buddha in groups of four. The commentary provides a verse-by-verse exegesis in the traditional Chinese scholastic format, with the sūtra text (jīng yuē 經曰) followed by the commentator's exposition (shù yuē 述曰). The sūtra covers nine rounds of questions and answers touching on: few desires and contentment, keeping and breaking precepts, merit and sin, good companions and enemies, the heavens and hells, love and liberation, craving as the most burning disease and the Buddha as the great physician, ignorance and delusion, miserliness and attachment, the indestructibility of merit, and the supreme self-deception of hoarding wealth without cultivating merit. The commentary draws on a wide range of canonical sources and provides detailed doctrinal explanations, including a full description of the eight hot hells, eight cold hells, and three peripheral hells. The text is preserved only in fragmentary form, covering questions five through nine of the sūtra.*

---

## [Sūtra, Question 5: Merit and Sin]

**Sūtra:** The World-Honored One declared: "Few desires bring the greatest peace and happiness. Contentment is great wealth and nobility. Keeping the precepts is always dignified and beautiful. Breaking the precepts is always ugly and debased."

**Commentary:** This is the answer, responding to the previous questions in sequence.

"Few desires bring the greatest peace and happiness" answers the first question. "Frugality" is "few." "Craving and indulgence" is "desire." The worldly indulgence in what is small or vast: the foolish mind quickly brings about destruction, and then one pursues and longs for what is lost, and suffering and distress cut to the heart. The Buddha-Dharma is the opposite of the worldly; it values and esteems non-action. Few desires and few pursuits bring the greatest peace and happiness.

Furthermore, the sūtra says: "A person of few desires is free from flattery and crookedness, and does not seek to win people's favor." Simply having few desires is already something that should be cultivated; how much more so when it can produce all manner of merit. "One who has few desires has nirvāṇa." The bliss of nirvāṇa is the supreme among all bliss. Such supreme bliss is obtained through few desires. Speaking of the cause while it contains the fruit, therefore it says "few desires bring the greatest peace and happiness."

"Contentment is great wealth and nobility" answers the second question. Worldly fools, though they obtain wealth and status, only become more intensely greedy, always harboring a sense of insufficiency. The contented person is without striving, without concerns, with nothing that can be diminished or seized, and always has a surplus. Therefore one is greatly wealthy and noble. The sūtra says: "The contented person, though lying on the ground, is still at peace and happy. The discontented person, though dwelling in a heavenly palace, is still not satisfied." The case of King Māndhātṛ is an example of this. It also says: "The discontented, though wealthy, are poor. The contented, though poor, are rich." Therefore it says "contentment is great wealth and nobility."

"Keeping the precepts is always dignified and beautiful" answers the third question. The person who keeps the precepts has pure moral conduct (śīla). In the near term, one obtains the delightful and excellent marks of the human and heavenly realms. In the long term, one obtains the dignified and beautiful dharma-body of the three vehicles. Therefore it says "keeping the precepts is always dignified and beautiful." As with King Udayana, whose consort kept the one-day precepts: that night she died and was immediately reborn in a heaven, obtaining a beautiful body. According to the king's prior agreement, she came back down to report to the king. Finding the king's body foul-smelling, she returned to heaven. This is an example.

"Breaking the precepts is always ugly and debased" answers the fourth question. The person who breaks the precepts increases in sinful karma and falls into the three evil destinies, receiving an ugly and debased body. If reborn among humans, the sense-faculties are incomplete; one is born in a border region, of low station, with uncouth appearance that others do not wish to see, for hundreds of thousands of lifetimes. Therefore it says "breaking the precepts is always ugly and debased."

## [Sūtra, Question 6: Good Companions and Enemies]

**Sūtra:** The deva again asked: "Who are good companions? Who are enemies with evil minds? What is the most extreme and heavy suffering? What is the foremost bliss?"

**Commentary:** This is the fifth set of questions and answers. This is the question. There are again four questions. First: who are good companions? Second: who are enemies with evil minds? Third: what is the most extreme suffering? Fourth: what is the foremost bliss?

"Who are good companions?" is the first question. "Companions" (juàn 眷) means household members. "Relatives" (shǔ 屬) means kin. Worldly relatives may have a father who is not kind, a son who is not filial, an elder brother who is not harmonious, a younger brother who is not respectful, or inner and outer kin who lack benevolence and righteousness. When emotions are estranged and opposed, they are like enemies. Though called companions, they are not good ones. But if the father is kind and the son filial, the elder brother harmonious and the younger respectful, and inner and outer kin are benevolent and righteous toward each other, without estrangement, blending like water and milk: such companions are called good. The question is: in the Dharma, who can be called purely good companions?

"Who are enemies with evil minds?" is the second question. Envying the worthy and being jealous of the superior is called "evil mind." Wishing to do harm is called "enemy." The question is: in the Dharma, who are the evil-minded enemies of the practitioner?

"What is the most extreme and heavy suffering?" is the third question. In the world, killing, binding, shackles, imprisonment, meeting with the hated, separation from the loved, sickness, and death, all pressing upon one: these are called suffering. The question is: in the Dharma, is there suffering that surpasses these?

"What is the foremost bliss?" is the fourth question. Worldly wealth and status, riding fat horses and wearing light silks, eyes gazing on beauty, ears listening to music, roaming in the fields hunting, indulging in lust and wine, giving oneself over to pleasure: these are called bliss. The question is: in the Dharma, is there bliss that surpasses these?

**Sūtra:** The World-Honored One declared: "Merit is good companionship. Sin is the enemy with an evil mind. Hell is the most extreme suffering. Non-arising is the foremost bliss."

**Commentary:** This is the answer, responding to the four questions in sequence.

"Merit is good companionship" answers the first question. "Merit" refers to the three virtues of giving, precepts, and meditation. Giving has three kinds: (1) giving of wealth, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-craving; (2) giving of fearlessness, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-anger; (3) giving of dharma, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-delusion. Precepts have three kinds: (1) precepts that regulate others, namely the three of body, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-craving; (2) precepts of not troubling others, namely the four of speech, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-anger; (3) precepts of benefiting others, namely receiving and upholding these two, with merit continuously arising, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-delusion. Meditation has three kinds: (1) the four dhyānas, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-craving; (2) the four immeasurables, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-anger; (3) the four formless absorptions, accomplished through the wholesome root of non-delusion. Also, the first five of the six pāramitās are likewise merit.

All such merit has the ten wholesome courses of action as its nature. Through the merit of giving and precepts, in life after life, internally one obtains a superior body in the desire realm of humans and devas, and externally one attracts pure companions of the ten wholesome actions, namely: not dying prematurely, great wealth, celibate conduct, truthful speech, always speaking gently, companions who do not separate, skillfully resolving disputes, speech that is always beneficial, without jealousy, without anger, and persons of right view as companions. Through the merit of meditation, one obtains a superior body in the form and formless realms. These three kinds of merit, in the long term, lead to the holy fruits of the three vehicles.

The intent of the answer: worldly good companions are temporarily together and permanently separated, difficult to gather and easy to scatter, not enduring to the end. In the Dharma, the three kinds of meritorious wholesome karma are truly solid and firm, always assisting the practitioner with great benefit. Among all companions, these are the best.

"Sin is the enemy with an evil mind" answers the second question. Destroying the Three Jewels, the five heinous crimes, and the ten evils are all called sin. Through this sin, sentient beings are cast into the three evil paths for long kalpas of suffering. The intent of this answer: a worldly evil-minded enemy harms only one lifetime, and is already called an enemy. In the Dharma, sinful karma can harm many lifetimes; is this not a great enemy? Therefore it says "sin is the enemy with an evil mind."

"Hell is the most extreme suffering" answers the third question. In Sanskrit, it is called naraka. The old translation "níli" (泥犁) is an error. In Chinese, it means "place of no pleasure." Since the place of no pleasure is precisely hell, and most are beneath the earth, it is called "hell" (dìyù 地獄, "earth-prison").

There are three kinds of hell: hot, cold, and peripheral.

**The hot hells** number eight: (1) Saṃjīva (Revival), (2) Kālasūtra (Black Rope), (3) Saṃghāta (Crushing), (4) Raurava (Screaming), (5) Mahāraurava (Great Screaming), (6) Tapana (Burning), (7) Pratāpana (Great Burning), (8) Avīci (Without Interval). These eight are beneath Jambudvīpa, stacked one upon another. The first two have wardens who administer punishment; the next three have few wardens; the last three have no wardens. These eight are the root hells. Each has sixteen enclosures, four on each side, sixteen in total. Together with the root hell, each has seventeen. Eight times seventeen equals one hundred and thirty-six locations. Sinners within them suffer the torment of burning heat.

**The cold hells** also number eight: (1) Arbuda (Blisters), where the cold causes fine blisters on the flesh; (2) Nirarbuda (Burst Blisters), where cold wind causes blisters all over the body; (3) Aṭaṭa, where the lips cannot move and only the tongue can, producing this sound; (4) Apapa, where the tongue cannot move and only the lips can, producing this sound; (5) Huhuva, where neither lips nor tongue can move and only vibrating breath produces this sound; (6) Utpala (Blue Lotus), whose petals are fine, and the flesh cracks finely like this flower opening; (7) Padma (Red Lotus), where the flesh cracks greatly like this flower opening; (8) Puṇḍarīka (White Lotus), where the bones crack like this flower opening. The first two are named from bodily characteristics; the next three from sounds; the last three from the appearance of the wounds. Also, the first two involve articulate screaming; the next three inarticulate screaming; the last three no screaming. These eight are located between the four continents, at the base of the Iron Ring Mountain, facing upward in the center. Sinners within them suffer the torment of cold and freezing.

**The peripheral hells** have three kinds: (1) between mountains, (2) between waters, (3) in the wilderness. They receive individual karmic retribution, with cold and heat mixed together.

The intent of this answer: although there are the sufferings of killing, binding, shackles, meeting with the hated, separation from the loved, sickness, and death in the human realm, as well as the sufferings of animals and hungry ghosts, compared to hell these do not reach one hundredth, nor even one part in a hundred thousand millions of nayutas. Therefore it says "hell is the most extreme suffering."

"Non-arising is the foremost bliss" answers the fourth question. The essence of nirvāṇa is called "non-arising." Because it is without arising, it is ultimately without cessation. When the noble one realizes this, it is tranquil nirvāṇa, permanently abiding in peace. Therefore it is supreme bliss.

The intent of this answer: worldly bliss, when pushed to the extreme, becomes suffering. How can it compare to nirvāṇa, which is without suffering and always blissful? Therefore the sūtra says: "What is high must fall. What endures must end. What is joined must separate. What is pleasant must turn to suffering." This verse illuminates the false bliss of birth-and-death, where bliss pushed to the extreme necessarily becomes suffering. It also says: "All conditioned things are impermanent; they are dharmas of arising and ceasing. When arising and ceasing are themselves extinguished, quiescent cessation is bliss." This verse illuminates nirvāṇa's freedom from suffering, being ultimately and permanently blissful. Therefore it says "non-arising is the foremost bliss."

## [Sūtra, Question 7: Love and Liberation]

**Sūtra:** The deva again asked: "What is loved but not beneficial? What is beneficial but not loved? What is the most burning disease? Who is the great physician?"

**Commentary:** This is the sixth set of questions and answers. This is the question. There are again four questions.

"What is loved but not beneficial, and what is beneficial but not loved?" are the first and second questions. For example, sweet rice mixed with poison is something loved, yet it harms the body and endangers life; it is not beneficial to eat. Fine medicine that is bitter to the mouth is not loved, yet it benefits the illness; it is beneficial to take. Flattering words that please the feelings are loved, yet they harm one's conduct; it is not beneficial to listen to them. Loyal words that offend the ear are not loved, yet they benefit one's conduct; they are beneficial to heed. The question is: in the Dharma, what do the emotions love that is not beneficial to the person? And what is beneficial to the person but not loved by the emotions?

"What is the most burning disease?" is the third question. The kāmalā disease is called the burning disease in the world. Those who contract it will certainly die; worldly physicians throw up their hands. The question is: in the Dharma, is there a disease even more extreme than this burning disease?

"Who is the great physician?" is the fourth question. "Good" (liáng 良) means skillful. "Physician" (yī 醫) means understanding. One who skillfully identifies the source of disease, is well-versed in the properties of medicines, and invariably cures those treated is called a good physician. A good physician has four qualities: (1) knowing the disease, (2) knowing the cause of disease, (3) knowing the cure, (4) knowing that once cured it will not recur. The question is: in the Dharma, is there a great physician who surpasses such a physician?

**Sūtra:** The World-Honored One declared: "All desires are loved but not beneficial. Liberation is beneficial but not loved. Craving is the most burning disease. The Buddha is the great physician."

**Commentary:** This is the answer, responding to the four questions in sequence.

"All desires are loved but not beneficial" answers the first question. "All desires" refers to the five desired objects of attractive form, sound, scent, taste, and touch. Because they can produce desiring mind and become objects of grasping, they are called desires. The sūtra says: "The five desires deceive people, like honey smeared on a knife-blade; the one who licks it wounds the tongue. They are like scratching a sore: initially there is a little pleasure, but afterward great suffering. Like holding fire against the wind: if one does not release it, it will certainly burn the hand." As when a king craved beauty and thereby lost his throne; as when a ṛṣi loved sound and fell from the sky into the forest; as when a bhikṣu smelled fragrance and the river-spirit cursed him; as when a novice tasted flavors and threw himself into a pool to become a nāga; as when Lambhūta touched fine textures and lost his supernatural powers. Furthermore, these five desires produce three kinds of suffering in the present: seeking produces the suffering of toil; guarding produces the suffering of fear; losing produces the suffering of burning distress. Furthermore, through unlawful pursuit of the five desires, one falls into hell, the realm of hungry ghosts, and the animal realm, suffering for a long time. After rebirth among humans and devas, there is the suffering of poverty, the suffering of separation from the loved, and the suffering of meeting with the hated. All such sufferings are caused by attachment to the five desires. Therefore we know that although the five desires are loved by the ordinary person's desiring mind, for the practitioner they are extremely not beneficial. Therefore it says "all desires are loved but not beneficial."

"Liberation is beneficial but not loved" answers the second question. Nirvāṇa is unconditioned, its essence severed from all bonds; hence it is called "liberation." One who realizes liberation permanently leaves birth and death, permanently abiding in clarity. Such liberation nurtures and benefits the practitioner; it is supremely beneficial. Yet the foolish emotions of ordinary people do not love it. Therefore it says "liberation is beneficial but not loved." The sūtra says: "Ordinary people would rather enjoy the body of a leprous jackal than enjoy nirvāṇa, because the jackal still has eyes, ears, and mind, while nirvāṇa has none."

"Craving is the most burning disease" answers the third question. Craving is the craving-tendency. Its nature pervades the three realms, obstructing all five questions, operating together with the six consciousnesses, equally staining the six sense-objects. It is the feet of birth-and-death and the root-source of suffering and distress. How can worldly burning diseases compare to this? As the Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra says: "The fire of desire blazes forth and burns the celestial sacrifice." Is this not craving as the most burning disease? In the present, one is already burned by fire; in future lives, how can one escape the cauldrons of boiling water? Therefore it says "craving is the most burning disease."

"The Buddha is the great physician" answers the fourth question. The Tathāgata has the five eyes; internally illuminated by the six supernatural powers, externally radiant. Vertically he surveys the three periods of time; horizontally he beholds the ten directions. As for the sources of bodily and mental disease, and the counter-remedies and medicine-methods for removing them: all are illuminated without obstruction, as if right before his eyes. Therefore the teaching he sets forth matches the capacities of beings; the medicine prescribed according to the disease invariably brings benefit. This is why the Buddha is the supremely great physician.

The Āgama sūtra says: "The Buddha has four kinds of knowledge: (1) knowing suffering, (2) knowing the cause of suffering, (3) knowing the cessation of suffering, (4) knowing the path to the cessation of suffering." Therefore he is called a good physician. Furthermore, the sūtra says: "What worldly physicians cure, though healed, recurs. What the Tathāgata cures will ultimately never arise again." Therefore it says "the Buddha is the great physician."

## [Sūtra, Question 8: What Covers the World]

**Sūtra:** The deva again asked: "Who can cover the world? By what is the world bewitched? Who causes one to abandon friends? What obstructs rebirth in heaven?"

**Commentary:** This is the seventh set of questions and answers. This is the question. There are again four questions.

"Who can cover the world?" is the first question. "Cover" (fù 覆) means to obstruct and conceal. In the world, the eyes are covered by blind darkness and cannot see forms and shapes. The question is: in the Dharma, what further thing covers the world and prevents it from recognizing the principle?

"By what is the world bewitched?" is the second question. Just as worldly ghosts bewitch people and make them foolish, now weeping, now laughing: the question is, in the Dharma, what further thing bewitches the world and causes confusion?

"Who causes one to abandon friends?" is the third question. Close associates and intimate friends are called friends. If the intimacy is based on wealth, when wealth is gone, the friendship fades. If the closeness is based on power, when power wanes, the bond is obstructed. The question is: in the Dharma, who causes sentient beings to abandon their friends?

"What obstructs rebirth in heaven?" is the fourth question. The superior reward of heaven is something all people would enjoy. Who creates the obstruction that prevents ascent?

**Sūtra:** The World-Honored One declared: "Ignorance covers the world. The world is bewitched by delusion. Miserliness and craving cause one to abandon friends. Attachment obstructs rebirth in heaven."

**Commentary:** This is the answer, responding to the four questions in sequence.

"Ignorance covers the world" answers the first question. Lacking the wisdom of the four truths of suffering, accumulation, cessation, and path; or lacking the wisdom of the two truths, conventional and ultimate: this is called ignorance. Ignorance is precisely the tendency of avidyā. Due to ignorance, one does not know the suffering-fruit and accumulation-cause of birth-and-death, does not know the path-cause and cessation-fruit of liberation, does not know the single characteristic of unconditioned ultimate truth, and does not know the three-period cause-and-effect of conventional truth. The intent of this answer: worldly blindness and darkness only cover the physical eye. Ignorance can cover the world's wisdom-eye. Therefore it says "ignorance covers the world."

"The world is bewitched by delusion" answers the second question. Delusion is another name for ignorance. Due to the bewitchment of delusion, one sees permanence in what is impermanent, sees self in what is without self, sees purity in what is impure, and sees bliss in what is suffering. All one's understanding and practice is entirely inverted. All such inversion is due to delusion. Therefore it says "the world is bewitched by delusion."

"Miserliness and craving cause one to abandon friends" answers the third question. Miserliness is stinginess about one's own possessions. Craving is desire for others' wealth. As the Vinaya relates: there was a bhikṣu practicing concentration beside a pool. The pool-nāga, respecting his virtue, wrapped its body around him. The bhikṣu, with an evil mind wishing to make it leave, skillfully recited a verse asking for the nāga's jewel: "Radiant maṇi gem, adorning the body with necklaces. If you can give it to me, you will be called a good friend." The pool-nāga, miserly, answered with this verse: "I love my maṇi gem and have met your bhikṣu-form. I will never give it to you; feel free not to be my friend." The Buddha, on account of this incident, spoke a further verse: "The excessively greedy person is detested by others. Constantly begging, friends depart. The bhikṣu begged the nāga's jewel; once gone, he never returned." Therefore we know that miserliness and craving can cause one to abandon friends.

Furthermore, due to craving one only wants others' wealth; due to miserliness one does not practice generosity. In whatever place one is reborn, one is poor and destitute, without father or mother, without wife or children, without good spiritual friends, without friends or kin. Therefore it says "miserliness and craving cause one to abandon friends."

The Satyasiddhi-śāstra enumerates five kinds of miserliness: (1) miserliness about giving, (2) miserliness about one's household, (3) miserliness about one's place, (4) miserliness about praise, (5) miserliness about the Dharma. All five cause one to abandon friends.

"Attachment obstructs rebirth in heaven" answers the fourth question. "Heaven" refers to the twenty-eight heavens. To ascend, one must become disenchanted with the lower realm. If one is attached to the lower realm, one cannot be reborn above. If one is attached to the five desires of the human realm, indulging without restraint and unwilling to become disenchanted, one cannot ascend to the six desire-heavens. As the sūtra explains at length: the Buddha's brother Nanda only abandoned Sundarī and aspired to the celestial maidens by first keeping the precepts and vowing rebirth in heaven. If one is attached to the five desires of the six desire-heavens, one cannot ascend to the form-realm heavens. If one is attached to the rewards of the form-realm heavens, one cannot ascend to the formless-realm heavens. Therefore it says "attachment obstructs rebirth in heaven."

Furthermore, the noble ones of the three vehicles are called "pure heavens." If one is attached to the conditioned rewards of the twenty-eight heavens of the three realms, one cannot enter the noble path of the three vehicles. This too is called "attachment obstructing rebirth in heaven."

## [Sūtra, Question 9: The Indestructibility of Merit]

**Sūtra:** The deva again asked: "Who is not burned by fire, not shattered by wind? Who is not decayed by water, and can buoy and sustain the world? Who can contend with kings and robbers, bravely resisting them? Who is not invaded or seized by humans or non-humans?"

**Commentary:** This is the eighth set of questions and answers. This is the question. There are two meanings to the question: first, who is free from seven difficulties; second, who has one ability?

The seven difficulties are: (1) fire, (2) wind, (3) water, (4) kings, (5) robbers, (6) humans, (7) ghosts. Fire's difficulty is burning and scorching. Wind's difficulty is scattering and shattering. Water's difficulty is drowning and decay. Kings' difficulty is oppressive power. Robbers' difficulty is plunder and murder. Humans' difficulty is invasion and harm. Ghosts' difficulty is possession and bewitchment. People of the world mostly cannot escape these seven kinds of calamity. The question is: in the Dharma, who can escape these?

The one ability is "buoying and sustaining the world." The world has two aspects: (1) the vessel-world, meaning the environment; (2) the sentient-being world, meaning living beings. Just as the water-wheel in the world buoys and sustains the earth-wheel and sentient beings, the question is: in the Dharma, who can buoy and sustain these two worlds? Because water has two functions, decay and support, the question about water's decay is immediately followed by the question about buoying and sustaining.

**Sūtra:** The World-Honored One declared: "Merit is not burned by fire, not shattered by wind. Merit is not decayed by water, and can buoy and sustain the world. Merit can contend with kings and robbers, bravely resisting them. It is not invaded or seized by humans or non-humans."

**Commentary:** This is the answer, responding to the two questions. "Merit" refers to the three kinds of merit: giving, precepts, and meditation, as explained previously in the section on good companions.

The answer regarding the seven difficulties: those who encounter the seven kinds of calamity have created the causes of the ten evils. Having cultivated the three kinds of merit, how could one suffer the seven kinds of calamity?

Question: Robbers act unlawfully, so it is fitting to resist them. But people are subject to the king's law; how can one contend with the king?

Answer: As when King Zhòu of Yīn was tyrannical and King Wǔ of Zhōu resisted him. Though one was the king's subject, there was still resistance. Furthermore, "king" is a general term that includes ministers and subordinates. Those who carry out the king's commands are also called kings. When such kings and ministers act tyrannically and cruelly, they are unjust like robbers, and may also be resisted.

For example, as a sūtra tells: a king ordered a minister to inventory his household wealth. The minister thereupon recorded the items he had already used for meritorious works. The king angrily rebuked him: "The items used for meritorious works are now entirely gone. Why did you not record the current goods in your house?" The minister answered: "The items used for meritorious works are truly my possessions. The current goods in my house originally belong to the five kinds of claimants and are not mine alone. Therefore I did not record them." The king's mind was opened and he thereupon also cultivated merit. Therefore we know that merit cannot be seized by others.

The answer regarding the one ability: through the three kinds of merit, one obtains the superior direct reward of rebirth among humans and devas, and also obtains the environmental reward of houses, resources, and various dependent fruits. Therefore it says "merit can buoy and sustain the world."

Question: Confucius, Laozi, and others said: "From the Way, one is born. From one, two are born. From two, three are born. From three, the myriad things are born." The Way means empty pervasiveness. One means primal vital energy. Two means yin and yang. Three means heaven, earth, and humans. The myriad things means birds, beasts, grasses, trees, and so forth. They also say: "The human head is round, imaging heaven; the feet are square, imaging earth. Absorbing the essence and spirit of heaven and earth, a person is formed." According to this, the external vessel-world sustains the sentient-being world. How can sentient beings' internal merit sustain the external vessel-world?

Answer: What Confucius and Laozi teach is that heaven and earth produce humans. What Buddhism illuminates is that humans produce heaven and earth. This means that through the collective karma of sentient beings, the external vessel-world and its environmental rewards of heaven and earth are produced. Whether in formation or destruction, this is as extensively explained elsewhere. Therefore the sūtra says: "All that the three realms produce is the work of one mind alone." The treatise also says: "The external is accomplished through the internal." Confucius and Laozi only observed the external appearances and their sequence, and therefore said heaven and earth produce humans. The Tathāgata, penetrating to the internal substance of cause and effect, therefore said humans produce heaven and earth. The depth and shallowness of the three teachings can be known by this.

## [Sūtra, Question 10: Self-Deception]

**Sūtra:** The deva again asked: "I still have doubts; may the Buddha remove and resolve them. From this life going to the next: who most deceives and defrauds oneself?"

**Commentary:** This is the ninth set of questions and answers. This is the question. The upper half states the question; the lower half is the actual question.

The stating of the question means: having received the extensive answers above, the previous obstructions have been analyzed. But the lower sentiments have many confusions and doubts remain. May the Buddha in his compassion remove and resolve them for me.

The actual question: "this life" means the present; "the next life" means the future. This means going from this life's body to the next body.

Question: Conditioned things are impermanent, neither coming nor going. There is also no master. Who then goes?

Answer: The prior ceases and the later arises; it is neither permanent nor annihilated. Causes attract results in succession; we provisionally speak of going and coming. It is like carrying a lamp from here to there: though moment by moment it is extinguished, we provisionally speak of going and coming.

Question: The three periods of time revolve and the fruits and causes are complete. Why does the question concern only the present and future, not the past?

Answer: Past events have already been experienced; there is no need to ask further. Future lives have not yet been received; therefore one must ask.

The intent of this question: worldly deception and fraud only intend to harm others. The question is: in the Dharma, is there one who, from the present going forward, harms oneself in the most extreme way of deception?

**Sūtra:** The World-Honored One declared: "If one has abundant precious wealth but is unable to cultivate merit: going from this life to the next, that person most deceives and defrauds oneself."

**Commentary:** This is the answer. "Precious things" (zhēn 珍) means gold, silver, and the eight kinds of precious things. "Wealth" (cái 財) means money, silk, and such things. Worldly people, confused, think they will endure forever. Not recognizing cause and fruit, they amass great wealth. Toward the field of compassion they have no thought of generosity; toward the field of reverence they have no intention of making offerings. Suddenly life ends and they have not a single merit as provision. They cycle into the three evil paths, suffering for a long time. The precious wealth and riches are used by others; oneself has not a shred of goodness. Is this not the greatest self-betrayal?

Therefore it says: "If one has abundant precious wealth but is unable to cultivate merit: going from this life to the next, that person most deceives and defrauds oneself."

As the sūtra also says: "Hoarding wealth, not distributing it, concealing and accumulating it for fear that others will find out. Abandoning the body, one departs empty-handed, and in the realm of hungry ghosts suffers hunger." And as the sūtra says: "Kin and relatives all separate; only karma does not abandon one. Good and evil, in future lives, always follow and pursue one."

Here one should cite the parable of the man with three wives, and also the parable of the man with three sons.

The sūtra also says: "One would rather cut the flesh from one's own body and eat it than take one's wealth and distribute it in charity seeking merit for future lives. Such a person will fall into the sufferings of hell, the animal realm, and the hungry ghost realm."

## [Conclusion]

**Sūtra:** At that time, the deva, having heard the World-Honored Buddha speak this sūtra...

**Commentary:** This is the third division: practicing according to the teaching, which constitutes the circulation section. It has three parts: (1) hearing the Dharma, (2) expressing respect, (3) concealing and returning. This is the hearing of the Dharma.

**Sūtra:** ...rejoiced and leapt with joy, exclaiming that this was unprecedented, and prostrated at the Buddha's feet.

**Commentary:** This is expressing respect. Having heard the excellent Dharma and feeling involuntary delight, one then expresses respect for the virtue. Therefore one exclaims and prostrates.

**Sūtra:** ...and then before the Buddha suddenly vanished.

**Commentary:** This is the concealing and returning. Having come to inquire about doubts, and having received the resolution of obstructions, the purpose fulfilled, one conceals and returns to one's palace.

This completes the Commentary on the Sūtra of Questions Asked by a Deva.
