# 善生子經
## The Sūtra of Siṅgāla's Son

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0017

Translated by Zhī Fǎdù of the Western Jin

---

## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra of Siṅgāla's Son, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the World-Honored One was dwelling at Rājagṛha on Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain. At that time, the householder Siṅgāla was gravely ill and commanded his son: "After I have passed away, you must perform the rite of reverence to the six directions." Thereupon Siṅgāla passed away in due course. His son reverently attended to the funeral rites and made offerings; when the funeral was completed, he thereupon rose early, bathed, put on new clothes, went to the water's edge, and bowed in reverence to the six directions, saying: "I humbly and respectfully bow to those born in the eastern direction; may they likewise show respect to me." He proceeded in the same manner to the south, west, north, above, and below, reciting the same words in each direction.

At that time, the Buddha in the early morning had put on his robes and taken his bowl and was about to enter the city when he saw Siṅgāla's son bowing in reverence to the six directions at the water's edge in this manner. The World-Honored One then approached and asked: "Son of the householder! What are you doing? I have recently heard that you always rise early, bathe, put on new clothes, go to the water's edge, bow in reverence to the six directions, declare 'I humbly and respectfully bow to all the directions,' and then bathe again. Whose teacher's practice is this that you follow in such reverence, son of Siṅgāla?"

Siṅgāla's son replied: "When my father was on the verge of death, he previously gave this command; therefore I follow it. I have not received this from any teacher."

The World-Honored One responded: "Son of the householder! What your father spoke of was not these six physical directions. Moreover, if one merely desires to sit and face the six directions, but has four kinds of defiled and evil conduct without being able to repent of them, then at the death of the body the spirit will be reborn in an evil realm or in hell. There are four things that bring trouble upon a person; one should know and recognize them. What are the four? First, delight in killing living beings; second, delight in theft; third, engaging in sexual misconduct; fourth, delight in false speech." The Buddha expressed this meaning in verse:

> Killing living beings and theft,
> Deception and false speech,
> Approaching another's wife:
> These are not praised by the wise.

"Furthermore, son of the householder, there are four things that lead one to an evil realm. What are the four? First, desire; second, anger; third, delusion; fourth, fear." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Possessed of desire, anger, delusion, and fear,
> Not accepting the true Dharma:
> Such a one's name falls low,
> Like the moon waning in darkness.
> Free from desire, anger, delusion, and fear,
> And accepting the true Dharma:
> Such a one's name stands high,
> Like the moon waxing full in brightness.

"Furthermore, son of the householder, there are six afflictions that dissipate wealth and lead one into an evil realm; one should know and recognize them. What are the six? First, addiction to liquor and idle wandering; second, frequenting the houses of others at improper times; third, gambling and idle wandering; fourth, excessive fondness for entertainments and music; fifth, evil companions; sixth, sloth and indolence." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Drinking liquor, frequenting others' houses,
> Gambling, fondness for entertainers and performers,
> Evil companions, and sloth and indolence:
> These are not praised by the sages and the wise.

"Liquor has six transformations one should know. What are the six? It dissipates wealth; it causes illness; it gives rise to quarrels; it leads to excessive anger; it destroys reputation; it diminishes wisdom. When one possesses this evil, one's affairs are ruined; wealth not yet acquired cannot be obtained; wealth already obtained is depleted; and accumulated stores are exhausted."

"Sexual misconduct has six transformations one should know. What are the six? One fails to protect oneself; one fails to protect one's wife and children; one fails to protect one's household members; from suspicion evil arises; enemies find opportunity; and one is surrounded by many sufferings. When one possesses this evil, one's affairs are ruined; wealth not yet acquired cannot be obtained; wealth already obtained is depleted; and accumulated stores are exhausted.

"Gambling has six transformations one should know. What are the six? In winning, enmity arises; in losing, one is afflicted at heart; friends are grieved by it; enemies rejoice at it; there is the worry of imprisonment and misfortune; and people in general regard one with suspicion. When one possesses this evil, one's affairs are ruined; wealth not yet acquired cannot be obtained; wealth already obtained is depleted; and accumulated stores are exhausted.

"Fondness for entertainment has six transformations one should know. What are the six? The mind is fixed on dancing; the mind is fixed on singing; the mind is fixed on string instruments; the mind is fixed on rhythm; the mind is fixed on drums; the mind is fixed on those performers. When one possesses this evil, one's affairs are ruined; wealth not yet acquired cannot be obtained; wealth already obtained is depleted; and accumulated stores are exhausted.

"Evil companions have six transformations one should know. What are the six? One grows accustomed to drunkenness and confusion; one grows accustomed to stupor and disorder; one grows accustomed to licentiousness and self-indulgence; one grows accustomed to frequenting taverns; one grows accustomed to base persons; one grows accustomed to coarse speech. When one possesses this evil, one's affairs are ruined; wealth not yet acquired cannot be obtained; wealth already obtained is depleted; and accumulated stores are exhausted.

"Sloth and indolence have six transformations one should know. What are the six? When full, one does not work; when hungry, one does not work; when cold, one does not work; when hot, one does not work; in the morning, one does not work; in the evening, one does not work. When one possesses this evil, one's affairs are ruined; wealth not yet acquired cannot be obtained; wealth already obtained is depleted; and accumulated stores are exhausted." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Fondness for sensual pleasures, delight in song and dance,
> Resting by day and following them by night,
> Evil companions and sloth and indolence:
> These bring great ruin to a man of worth.
>
> Gambling and liquor lead to reckless destruction;
> The mind fixed on other men's women;
> Keeping far from the wise and near to the foolish:
> Such ruin is like the waning of the moon.
>
> One who conducts himself with arrogance and conceit,
> Who demeans and despises the path of śramaṇas,
> Who holds wrong views and practices miserliness:
> Such a one is called a heedless and dissolute man.
>
> Liquor obstructs the use of wealth;
> Its benefit is small, but the thirst it brings is great;
> It is a diseased water that stirs up debt,
> Creates disorder, and endangers the body with illness.
>
> Some form friendships through liquor,
> Some transgress the law through liquor;
> If through it one could achieve fine benefit,
> That might perhaps be tolerable.
>
> Some by day appear to observe the precepts,
> But by night follow the path of debauchery,
> Constantly frequenting the wine-houses:
> Such persons one should take care never to befriend.
>
> Through all of heaven's cold and heat,
> Like grass that does not prize itself,
> If one works diligently at one's livelihood,
> Yet that very profit is thereby lost.
>
> But if one can endure cold and heat,
> Like grass that does not prize itself,
> And works diligently at one's livelihood,
> Then one will be at peace and will prosper.
>
> Consorting with those below leads gradually to decline;
> Consorting with those above has never brought harm.
> Advancing in goodness, one rises to noble distinction;
> Through goodness, one is certain to attain goodness.
>
> Great goodness encounters goodness;
> Sincere goodness can be cultivated alongside other virtues.
> What is upheld and esteemed by one's kinsfolk:
> Upholding the precepts to eliminate evil.
>
> Therefore one should cultivate this practice;
> Having walked this path, continue to walk it.
> Among one's kin, one becomes the highest,
> Like a sovereign presiding over the multitude.

"Furthermore, son of the householder, there are four who have the appearance of friends but are not true friends; one should know and recognize them. What are the four? First, those who take others' possessions; second, those who speak with flattery; third, those who show affection only to one's face; fourth, those who give evil instruction." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Friends who take others' possessions,
> Who speak fair and pleasing words,
> Who express flattering affection face to face,
> Whose evil teaching brings mutual danger and harm:
> These have the appearance of friends but are not true friends;
> The wise do not befriend them.
> Having recognized them, one should keep far away,
> As one leaves a rough and difficult road.

"Friends who take others' possessions should be known by four things. What are the four? They greedily take others' possessions; they give little while hoping to receive much; they associate out of fear; they associate for the sake of profit." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Taking another's possessions,
> Giving little while desiring much,
> Associating out of fear and associating for profit:
> Such is the way of a greedy person's friendship.
> These have the appearance of friends but are not true friends;
> The wise do not befriend them.
> Having recognized them, one should keep far away,
> As one leaves a rough and difficult road.

"Flattering friends should be known by four things. What are the four? They reveal others' private affairs; they conceal their own private affairs; they falsely praise one to one's face; and when they depart, they spread slander." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> They delight in broadcasting others' private affairs
> While concealing their own;
> They face one with praise and flattery,
> But behind one's back discuss one's faults.
> These have the appearance of friends but are not true friends;
> The wise do not befriend them.
> Having recognized them, one should keep far away,
> As one leaves a rough and difficult road.

"Friends who show affection only to one's face should be known by four things. What are the four? They speak of others' past failings; they secretly seek out future faults; what they give is of no value; and they wish misfortune upon others." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Doing what should not be done,
> Creating false speech to one's detriment,
> Giving gifts of no value,
> Wishing harm upon others for their own sake:
> These have the appearance of friends but are not true friends;
> The wise do not befriend them.
> Having recognized them, one should keep far away,
> As one leaves a rough and difficult road.

"Friends who give evil instruction should be known by four things. What are the four? They encourage others in the matter of killing living beings; they encourage others in the matters of theft, sexual misconduct, and deception." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Killing living beings and theft,
> Deception and false speech,
> Approaching another's wife:
> With these they instruct and guide others.
> These have the appearance of friends but are not true friends;
> The wise do not befriend them.
> Having recognized them, one should keep far away,
> As one leaves a rough and difficult road.

"Furthermore, son of the householder, there are four friends who are benevolent, wise, and wish to benefit others; one should know and recognize them. What are the four? First, those who share in hardship and joy alike; second, those who benefit and support one another; third, those who assist with one's fundamental livelihood; fourth, those who show compassionate sympathy." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Sharing with others in safety and danger alike,
> Guiding and supporting them with good and benefit,
> Being devoted and able to assist others in their livelihood,
> Compassionately leading them on the right path:
> These have the appearance of true friends;
> The wise speak their praises and extol their virtue.
> One should associate with such friends,
> And will certainly benefit and not be harmed.

"Friends who share in hardship and joy alike should be known by four things. What are the four? They give of what they themselves treasure; they give of what benefits their wife and children; they give of all that belongs to their household; and their words are loyal and spoken with patience." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Giving of what benefits oneself,
> When they have wealth and benefit, sharing it as well,
> Giving of the benefit of one's household,
> Speaking loyal words with patience:
> These have the appearance of true friends;
> The wise seek their intimacy and fellowship.
> One should associate with such friends,
> And will certainly benefit and not be harmed.

"Friends who benefit and support one another should be known by four things. What are the four? They do not reveal others' private affairs; they do not conceal their own private affairs; they speak good words to one's face; and when they depart, they suppress slander." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Not broadcasting others' private affairs,
> Not concealing their own,
> When they meet, speaking of and discussing goodness,
> When they depart, suppressing slander:
> These have the appearance of true friends;
> The wise seek their intimacy and fellowship.
> One should associate with such friends,
> And will certainly benefit and not be harmed.

"Friends who assist with one's fundamental livelihood should be known by four things. What are the four? They assist one's livelihood with wealth; they assist one's livelihood with their strength; they remonstrate when one is given to licentiousness and self-indulgence; and they nourish one with goodness." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Assisting one's livelihood with wealth and benefit,
> Helping to bring peace and security with their strength,
> Earnestly admonishing one's licentiousness and indulgence,
> Fostering and nurturing one's aspiration to goodness:
> These have the appearance of true friends;
> The wise seek their intimacy and fellowship.
> One should associate with such friends,
> And will certainly benefit and not be harmed.

"Friends who show compassionate sympathy should be known by four things. What are the four? They teach, encourage, and uphold one in perfecting one's faith; in perfecting one's precepts; in perfecting one's learning; and in perfecting one's generosity." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> Constantly encouraging and guiding others
> In the ways of faith, precepts, learning, and generosity:
> These have the appearance of true friends;
> The wise seek their intimacy and fellowship.
> One should associate with such friends,
> And will certainly benefit and not be harmed.

"Furthermore, son of the householder, the east direction is like the child in the presence of parents. Therefore children should, through five things, properly revere, properly support, and properly give peace to their parents. What are the five? Keeping in mind and contemplating how to repay the family; diligently fulfilling one's obligations; receiving and upholding the precepts and instructions they impart; following their guidance and making offerings to them; and bringing joy to father and mother. Parents in turn should, through five things, show love and compassion to their children. What are the five? Establishing the foundational livelihood; planning and advising in matters of profit; arranging a suitable marriage; teaching and instructing in scriptures, the path, and proper conduct; and entrusting all that one possesses to one's children. This is the twofold relationship of the east: when this is done as one wishes, in accord with the laws established by the ancient sages, whereby children are certain to be filial and parents are lovingly kind, a man of worth will grow and prosper, and the wholesome Dharma will not decline.

"The south direction is like a student in the presence of a teacher. Therefore students should, through five things, properly revere, properly support, and properly give peace to their teachers. What are the five? Being attentive and careful in listening; being devoted to learning; being diligent in practical matters; conducting oneself without transgression; and making offerings to the teacher. Teachers in turn should, through five things, show compassion and instruct their students. What are the five? Teaching them through proper study; instructing them in all the arts to the fullest; making them diligent in their studies; guiding them on the good path; and directing them toward worthy and virtuous friends. This is the twofold relationship of the south: when this is done as one wishes, in accord with the laws established by the ancient sages, whereby students are humble and teachers instruct with benevolence, a man of worth will grow and prosper, and the wholesome Dharma will not decline.

"The west direction is like a husband in the presence of his wife. Therefore husbands should, through five things, properly revere, properly support, and properly give peace to their wives. What are the five? Respecting her with a pure and sincere heart; not harboring resentment toward her; not entertaining affections for another; providing clothing and food at the proper time; and providing jewelry and adornments at the proper time. Wives in turn should attend to their husbands through fourteen things. What are the fourteen? Performing tasks well; completing things well; receiving what is entrusted with careful attention; rising early in the morning; resting at night; in all matters striving to learn; closing the door and awaiting their husband; when their husband returns, going to welcome and greet him; being harmonious in tone and manner; being gentle and compliant in speech; keeping the furniture and mats in good order; keeping food and drink clean and pure; keeping generosity in mind; and making offerings to the husband. This is the twofold relationship of the west: when this is done as one wishes, in accord with the laws established by the ancient sages and the proper conduct between husband and wife, a man of worth will grow and prosper, and the wholesome Dharma will not decline.

"The north direction is like a friend in the presence of a companion. Therefore friends should, through five things, properly revere, properly support, and properly give peace to their companions. What are the five? Respecting them with a pure and sincere heart; not harboring resentment toward them; not entertaining affections for another; sharing delicacies and pleasures from time to time; and not setting aside deep kindness and affection. Companions in turn should, through five things, receive and protect their friends. What are the five? When one is fearful, bringing one to safety; when one is given to idle wandering and indulgence, repeatedly admonishing; keeping private matters confidential; the longer one makes offerings, the greater the excellence; and speaking loyally with patience. This is the twofold relationship of the north: when this is done as one wishes, in accord with the laws established by the ancient sages and the proper conduct of friendship, a man of worth will grow and prosper, and the wholesome Dharma will not decline.

"The downward direction is like the eldest son in the presence of servants and employees. Therefore the eldest son should, through five things, properly revere, properly support, and properly give peace to servants and employees. What are the five? Assigning tasks according to their capacity; providing clothing and food at proper times; sharing delicacies and pleasures from time to time; instructing them in the practice of fasting from time to time; and allowing them to rest when they are ill. Servants and employees in turn should serve the eldest son through ten things. What are the ten? Performing tasks well; completing things well; receiving what is entrusted with careful attention; retiring at night; rising early; in all matters striving to learn; working diligently and with effort; not being disrespectful when the household is poor; not departing when resources are exhausted; and when going out, declaring, 'The eldest son of our household is intelligent and wise.' This is the twofold relationship of the downward direction: when this is done as one wishes, in accord with the laws established by the ancient sages and the proper conduct between the eldest son and his employees, a man of worth will grow and prosper, and the wholesome Dharma will not decline.

"The upward direction is like the lay person who makes offerings in the presence of śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas. Therefore when such persons come to a household, the householder should, through five things, properly revere, properly support, and properly give peace to the śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas. What are the five? Opening the door and awaiting them; going out to welcome and greet them when they arrive; providing furniture and mats for them; protecting and preserving the treasury of scriptures and the Dharma; and offering clean and pure food. Through these things one makes offerings to śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas. Śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas in turn should respond to the household that makes offerings through five things. What are the five? Teaching and instructing them so as to perfect their right faith; teaching and instructing them so as to perfect their conduct of the precepts; teaching and instructing them so as to perfect their breadth of learning; teaching and instructing them so as to perfect their generosity; and teaching and instructing them so as to perfect their wisdom. This is the twofold relationship of the upward direction: when this is done as one wishes, in accord with the laws established by the ancient sages and the proper conduct between householders and śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, a man of worth will grow and prosper, and the wholesome Dharma will not decline." He expressed this meaning in verse:

> The east is for father and mother;
> The south is proper for teacher and instruction;
> The west is for one's wife;
> Friends are positioned to the north;
> Servants and employees below;
> Śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas above.
> One should practice these forms of reverence,
> As this is also proper conduct for a householder.
>
> When a person has abundant wealth,
> One should keep in mind the benefit of others;
> Sharing wealth and benefit with others,
> The one who gives generously ascends to heaven.
>
> Sharing gained profit with others,
> One everywhere obtains peace and security;
> One who embraces the world through righteousness:
> This is the very foundation of approaching happiness.
>
> Guiding others with kindness,
> Like a mother caring for her child,
> Wisely protecting and guiding all under heaven:
> Such blessings come again and again.
>
> In the highest position, dwelling among assemblies,
> Able to bring benefit and peace,
> Perfecting in others their faith and precepts:
> Such a one will surely attain fame and renown.
>
> With intention always free from indolence,
> Abandoning the conduct of miserliness,
> Guiding others with the spirit of friendship,
> Sharing and bestowing food and drink upon one another:
>
> Coming, going, and coming again,
> In this way, one's name suffers no diminishment.
> One who can cultivate and guard oneself
> Is counted as wise among householders.
>
> One who accumulates treasures and goods
> Should arouse benevolence and righteousness;
> First let learning be the highest pursuit,
> And next attend to the management of wealth.
>
> When one seeks and obtains wealth,
> One should always divide it into four parts:
> One part for clothing and food,
> Two as capital for seeking profit,
> Store one away as a reserve
> That can provide rescue in times of hardship.
>
> The four livelihoods of farming, trade, raising cattle, and keeping sheep;
> The fifth is carefully managing the household;
> The sixth is to take a wife in marriage.
> When things are done in this way, wealth accumulates,
> Increasing day by day without end.
>
> Wealth gathers day and night,
> Like streams flowing into the sea;
> Managing wealth and seeking it gradually
> Is like the bee making honey.
>
> Having wealth but not sharing with the prosperous,
> And not giving to those in distant regions,
> Miserliness and evil conduct,
> Having strength but not sharing it with friends:
>
> In affairs, when something is useful, learn it;
> When not useful, do not obstruct yourself with it.
> Observe those who use things effectively:
> Their brilliance and excellence is like a blazing fire.
>
> Among one's clan and kinsfolk,
> Be good in both ways;
> At ease in assembly with one's kinsfolk,
> Like Śakra dwelling in his celestial palace.

Thereupon, having heard the teaching of the World-Honored One, Siṅgāla's son prostrated himself at the Buddha's feet, rose and circumambulated him three times, joyfully took refuge, and received the precepts from the Buddha.
