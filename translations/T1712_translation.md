# Brief Commentary on the Heart of the Perfection of Wisdom Sūtra (般若波羅蜜多心經略疏)

## Taisho T1712

**Author:** Fazang (法藏, 643-712), Tang dynasty
**Fascicles:** 1

*Note: This is Fazang's (法藏 Fǎzàng) commentary on the Heart Sūtra (般若心經 Bōrě xīnjīng), composed during a break from translation work at the Qingchan Temple (清禪寺 Qīngchán sì) in Chang'an in 702 CE, at the request of the official Zheng Wanjun (鄭萬鈞). Fazang is the third patriarch of the Huayan (華嚴) school. He structures his commentary through five gates of analysis: (1) the circumstances of the teaching's arising, (2) its scriptural classification, (3) its essential purport and import, (4) an explanation of the title, and (5) a line-by-line exegesis. The commentary is notable for its fourfold analysis of the relationship between form and emptiness (including the "four meanings of true emptiness" and the "four meanings of illusory form"), integrating Huayan philosophy with Prajnaparamita doctrine.*

---

## Prologue

The true source, primordial and formless, lies in silent vastness beyond all nets and snares. The wondrous awakening, profound in its workings, lies in depths that surpass words and images. Though truth and convention are both extinguished, the two truths eternally abide. Though emptiness and existence are both gone, the one flavor is constantly manifest.

Truly, true emptiness has never not been existent; it is precisely within existence that emptiness is discerned. Illusory existence has never not been empty; it is precisely within emptiness that existence is clarified. Existence has its emptiness, therefore it is not [merely] existent. Emptiness has its existence, therefore it is not [merely] empty. The emptiness that is "not empty" is empty yet not nihilistic. The existence that is "not existent" is existent yet not eternal. When the four extremes are eliminated and the hundred negations are dispatched: is this not the profound purport of Prajñā?

If the matter is set forth in full detail, the words exceed 200,000 verses. If one grasps its essential pivot, the principle is exhausted in fourteen lines. Thus we know that the teaching expressing the truth may be extensive or concise according to conditions, while the essential purport transcending words is by nature perfectly penetrating and manifests in its entirety.

The *Prajñā Heart Sūtra* (般若心經) is truly a high torch illuminating the dark road, a swift vessel crossing the ocean of suffering. For rescuing beings and guiding the lost, nothing surpasses this.

"Prajñā" (般若 *bōrě*) has spiritual discernment as its substance. "Paramita" (波羅蜜多 *bōluómìduō*) has "reaching the other shore" as its function. "Heart" (心 *xīn*) indicates the essential and wondrous to which it returns. "Sūtra" (經 *jīng*) means the thread that strings together the verbal teachings. Combining Dharma with metaphor, clarifying the purport as the title, it is therefore called the *Prajnaparamita Heart Sūtra*.

## Gate One: The Circumstances of the Teaching's Arising (教興)

According to the *Mahaprajnaparamita-upadesa* (大智度論 *Dàzhìdù lùn*): "Just as Mount Sumeru, king of mountains, is not caused to tremble by no cause or by a minor cause, so too the arising of the Prajñā teaching." There are many causes and conditions, which may be summarized as ten:

1. To destroy the various wrong views of non-Buddhist practitioners.
2. To turn the two-vehicle practitioners and cause them to enter the Great Vehicle.
3. To prevent lesser bodhisattvas from being confused about emptiness.
4. To cause awakening to the Middle Way of the two truths and generate right views.
5. To reveal the Buddha's supreme virtues and generate pure faith.
6. To cause the arousal of the great aspiration for bodhi.
7. To enable the cultivation of the bodhisattva's profound and vast practices.
8. To enable the severing of all grave obstacles.
9. To enable the attainment of the fruits of bodhi and nirvāṇa.
10. To flow onward to later generations for the benefit of sentient beings.

## Gate Two: Scriptural Classification (藏攝)

Within the Three Baskets (三藏 *sānzàng*), this belongs to the Sūtra Basket. Within the Two Collections (二藏 *èrzàng*), it is included in the Bodhisattva Collection. Within the provisional and definitive teachings, it belongs to the definitive teaching.

## Gate Three: Essential Purport (宗趣)

What the words express is called the "purport" (宗 *zōng*); what the purport returns to is called the "import" (趣 *qù*). First in general, then in particular.

In general, the three kinds of prajñā serve as the purport: (1) the prajñā of true characteristics (實相 *shíxiàng*), meaning the true nature that is contemplated; (2) the prajñā of contemplative illumination (觀照 *guānzhào*), meaning the wondrous wisdom that contemplates; (3) the prajñā of written words (文字 *wénzì*), meaning the teaching that expresses the above two. These three are not exceeded, so they serve as the purport.

In particular, there are also three pairings: (1) teaching and meaning: the verbal teaching is the purport, the other two meanings are the import; (2) object and wisdom: the true emptiness as object is the purport, contemplative wisdom is the import; (3) cause and fruit: the causal practices of bodhi are the purport, the fruit-virtues of bodhi are the import.

## Gate Four: Explanation of the Title (釋題)

This also has three levels. First, distinguishing teaching and meaning: "Prajñā Heart" is the meaning expressed; "Sūtra" is the teaching that expresses it. Second, within the expressed meaning, distinguishing Dharma and metaphor: "Prajñā" and the rest are the Dharma expressed; "Heart" is the metaphor introduced, comparing the inner essential core of Prajñā to the human heart, which is master and essential, the ultimate root. Third, within the Dharma, distinguishing substance and function: "Prajñā" is the substance, translated as "wisdom" (智慧 *zhìhuì*), meaning spiritual awakening to the profound and wondrous, and the realization of the true source; "Paramita" is the function, translated as "reaching the other shore," meaning that through this wondrous wisdom one completely transcends birth and death and arrives at the realm of true emptiness. This distinguishes it from wisdom that does not reach the other shore, and hence constitutes the name.

## Gate Five: Exegesis of the Text (解文)

Since this is the Heart Sūtra, it has no opening sequence or transmission section. The text is divided into two parts: first, the explicit prajñā; second, from "the spell is thus spoken" onward, the secret prajñā. The explicit teaching is spoken clearly to generate wisdom-understanding and extinguish the obstacle of afflictions. The spell is spoken in secret words to generate merit through recitation and extinguish the obstacle of karmic offenses. To extinguish these two obstacles and accomplish the two adornments [of merit and wisdom], these two sections are taught.

### The Brief Outline Section

Within the first part there are also two divisions: first, a brief outline of the essential framework; second, from "Sariputra! Form is not different from emptiness" onward, an extensive presentation of the real meaning.

The brief outline has four components: (1) the person who contemplates, (2) the practice performed, (3) the object of the contemplative practice, (4) the benefits of the contemplation.

**1. The person who contemplates:** "Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva" (觀自在菩薩 *Guānzìzài púsà*) is the person who contemplates. "Observing with freedom" means contemplating the realm where principle and phenomena are non-obstructing, with penetrating freedom; hence this name. Also, observing beings' capacities and going to rescue them, freely and without obstruction; hence this name. The first interpretation is from the perspective of wisdom; the second from the perspective of compassion. "Bodhi" (菩 *pú*) means awakening; "sattva" (薩 *sà*) means sentient being. This person uses wisdom to seek bodhi above and compassion to rescue beings below, taking the name from the object.

**2. The practice performed:** "When practicing the profound Prajñā Paramita" (行深般若波羅蜜多時). The wondrous practice of prajñā has two kinds: (1) the shallow, which is the prajñā of the emptiness of persons; (2) the profound, which is the prajñā of the emptiness of dharmas. Now, distinguishing the shallow from the profound, it says "practicing the profound prajñā." "At the time" (時 *shí*) means that this bodhisattva sometimes also enters the contemplation of personal emptiness like the two vehicles, as the *Lotus Sūtra* says: "For those who should be saved through a sravaka body, he appears as a sravaka body." But this is not that time; hence it says "at the time of practicing the profound."

**3. The object of contemplative practice:** "He illuminated and saw that the five aggregates are all empty" (照見五蘊皆空). This means penetrating and seeing that the self-nature of the five aggregates is entirely empty, which is the principle of the twofold emptiness as seen by the profound wisdom.

**4. The benefits:** "And crossed beyond all suffering and distress" (度一切苦厄). Having realized true emptiness through direct seeing, all suffering and affliction are exhausted. One will attain separation from the two kinds of birth and death (fractional-segment and transformation), and realize the ultimate joyous fruit of bodhi and nirvāṇa.

### The Extensive Presentation

From "Sariputra! Form is not different from emptiness" onward, the extensive presentation of real meaning has five sections: (1) dispelling doubts, (2) revealing the substance of the Dharma, (3) clarifying what is left behind, (4) discerning what is attained, (5) concluding with praise of the superior power.

**Section 1: Dispelling doubts.** "Sariputra" (舍利子 *Shèlìzǐ*) names the person with doubts. "Sari" (舍利) is a bird's name, translated as "heron" (鶖鷺鳥). His mother's intelligence was as swift as that bird's eyes, so she was given this name, and he, being her son, takes his name from his mother.

The Hinayana practitioners' doubt: "In our remaining-existence position, we see that the aggregates are devoid of a person, and also say 'dharmas are empty.' How does this differ?" The answer: "In your school, 'the aggregates are devoid of a person' is called 'aggregate-emptiness,' but the aggregates themselves are not empty. Here we clarify that the self-nature of the aggregates is fundamentally empty, which is different. Hence 'form is not different from emptiness.'"

Another doubt: "In our school, upon entering the remainderless position, body and wisdom are both exhausted, and there is also emptiness with no form. How does this differ?" The answer: "In your school, form is not itself empty; only by destroying form does emptiness obtain. Here it is different: 'form is itself emptiness,' not that form is destroyed and then empty."

**Bodhisattvas' doubts.** According to the *Ratnagotravibhaga* (寶性論), bodhisattvas confused about emptiness have three doubts: (1) suspecting that emptiness is different from form, grasping an emptiness outside of form (answered by "form is not different from emptiness"); (2) suspecting that emptiness destroys form, grasping a nihilistic emptiness (answered by "form is itself emptiness, not that form is destroyed and then empty"); (3) suspecting that emptiness is a thing, grasping emptiness as existent (answered by "emptiness is itself form; one cannot use emptiness to grasp emptiness"). When the three doubts are exhausted, true emptiness naturally manifests.

**The proper meaning.** Regarding form and emptiness in relation to each other, there are three meanings: (1) The meaning of mutual opposition: "within emptiness there is no form," because emptiness eliminates form; correspondingly, "within form there is no emptiness," because form opposes emptiness. (2) The meaning of non-obstruction: since form is illusory form, it necessarily does not obstruct emptiness; since emptiness is true emptiness, it necessarily does not obstruct illusory form. (3) The meaning of mutual creation: if illusory form were not, in its entire substance, empty, it could not be illusory form. Therefore it is precisely because form is itself emptiness that form can exist. As the *Great Perfection of Wisdom Sūtra* says: "If all dharmas were not empty, there would be no path and no fruit." The *Middle Treatise* says: "Because there is the meaning of emptiness, all dharmas are accomplished."

Therefore true emptiness has four complete meanings: (1) Setting aside itself to establish the other: since emptiness is itself form, form appears and emptiness is hidden. (2) Extinguishing the other to reveal itself: since form is emptiness, form is exhausted and emptiness is revealed. (3) Both self and other co-existing: since hidden and manifest are non-dual, this is true emptiness. "Form is not different from emptiness" means illusory form exists; "emptiness is not different from form" means true emptiness is manifest. Since they do not mutually obstruct, both co-exist. (4) Both self and other extinguished: since in their entire substance they are mutually identical, both are completely taken away and both extremes are severed.

Form in relation to emptiness also has four meanings: (1) revealing the other while the self is exhausted; (2) the self revealing while hiding the other; (3) both co-existing; (4) both extinguished.

Thus illusory form's existence and non-existence are unobstructed; true emptiness's hiddenness and manifestation are free. Together they form one flavor, perfectly penetrating and without fixed abode.

**Contemplative practice interpretation.** Three levels: (1) Contemplating that form is itself emptiness accomplishes the practice of calming (*samatha*); contemplating that emptiness is itself form accomplishes the practice of insight (*vipasyana*); when emptiness and form are non-dual, appearing in a single thought-moment, calming and insight are practiced simultaneously, which is the ultimate. (2) Seeing that form is itself emptiness accomplishes great wisdom and does not abide in birth-and-death; seeing that emptiness is itself form accomplishes great compassion and does not abide in nirvāṇa; since the objects of form and emptiness are non-dual, compassion and wisdom are not different in thought, accomplishing the practice of non-abiding. (3) Following Zhiyi's (智者大師) establishment of the three contemplations in one mind from the *Sūtra of the Necklace*: (a) the contemplation entering emptiness from the provisional, since "it is itself emptiness"; (b) the contemplation entering the provisional from emptiness, since "emptiness is itself form"; (c) the contemplation of the equality of emptiness and the provisional, since "form and emptiness are not different."

**Section 2: Revealing the substance of the Dharma.** "Sariputra, the empty characteristics of all dharmas" (是諸法空相): since aggregates and the rest are not singular, they are called "all dharmas"; displaying the aspect of their emptiness, it is called "empty characteristics." The *Madhyantavibhaga* (中邊論) says: "There is no duality, yet this non-existence exists: this is called the characteristic of emptiness." "No duality" means no grasper and no grasped. "This non-existence exists" means the non-existence of grasper and grasped exists. This non-duality is called the characteristic of emptiness.

"Not arising, not ceasing; not defiled, not pure; not increasing, not decreasing" (不生不滅，不垢不淨，不增不減): Three pairs of six negations, explained in three ways.

*First, by stage:* (1) "Not arising, not ceasing" refers to the mundane position before the path. Ordinary beings die here and are born there, cycling through long kalpas: this is the stage of arising and ceasing. True emptiness is free from this. (2) "Not defiled, not pure" refers to the bodhisattva and other positions on the path. Bodhisattvas whose defilements are not yet exhausted but whose pure practices are already cultivated are at the stage of defilement and purity. True emptiness is free from this. (3) "Not increasing, not decreasing" refers to the position of the buddha-fruit at the end of the path. The delusions and obstacles of birth and death that were previously not yet exhausted are now exhausted (this is "decrease"); the myriad virtues of cultivation that were previously not yet complete are now complete (this is "increase"). True emptiness is free from this. Also, the *Buddhagotra-sastra* (佛性論) establishes three kinds of Buddha-nature: (1) before the path, the self-abiding Buddha-nature; (2) on the path, the drawn-forth Buddha-nature; (3) after the path, the attained-fruit Buddha-nature. Buddha-nature is one, but is divided into three according to position.

*Second, by dharma:* True emptiness, though identical with form, does not arise when form arises from conditions and does not cease when form ceases. It is not stained when flowing along and not purified when emerging from obstructions. Obstacles exhausted is not decrease; virtues fulfilled is not increase.

*Third, by contemplative practice:* Establishing the three non-nature contemplations for the three natures. (1) Regarding the universally discriminated nature, practice the contemplation of signlessness. (2) Regarding the other-dependent nature, practice the contemplation of non-arising. (3) Regarding the perfectly accomplished nature, practice the contemplation of non-self-nature.

**Section 3: Clarifying what is left behind.** "Therefore, in emptiness there is no form, no sensation, perception, formations, or consciousness..." through "...no realm of visual consciousness up to no realm of mental consciousness": true emptiness leaves behind the five aggregates (combined as one form, opened into four mental aspects), the twelve sense-bases (combined into one-and-a-half mental, opened into ten-and-a-half material), and the eighteen realms (both form and mind fully opened).

"No ignorance and also no ending of ignorance, up to no old age and death and also no ending of old age and death": the forward and reverse contemplation of the twelve links of dependent origination, all empty.

"No suffering, accumulation, cessation, or path": the Four Noble Truths of defiled and pure cause and effect, all empty.

"No wisdom and also no attainment": not only are the preceding dharmas absent in emptiness, but even the wisdom that knows emptiness cannot be found, and even the emptiness that is known cannot be found.

**Section 4: Discerning what is attained.** "Because there is nothing to attain" bridges the preceding to what follows. "Because" (以 *yǐ*) means "by reason of"; "therefore" (故 *gù*) means "as the cause." By reason of the preceding "nothing to attain" as the cause, the subsequent attainments are possible. The *Great Perfection of Wisdom Sūtra* says: "Through non-attainment, one attains."

The attainments have two parts: first, the bodhisattva attains the fruit of nirvāṇa (cessation); second, all buddhas attain the fruit of bodhi (wisdom). Regarding the bodhisattva: "The mind is without obstruction" means the practice is accomplished. "Because there is no obstruction, there is no fear. Being far from inverted, dream-like thinking" means obstacles are severed: externally, no fear of demons and enemies; internally, no inverted afflictions. "Ultimate nirvāṇa" (究竟涅槃) means the fruit is attained. "Nirvāṇa" is translated as "perfect quiescence": virtue with nothing lacking is called "perfect"; obstacles with nothing remaining is called "quiescent." "Ultimate" distinguishes this from the Hinayana's provisional transformation-city.

Regarding the buddhas: "The buddhas of the three times, relying on the Prajnaparamita, attain *anuttara-samyak-sambodhi*" (阿耨多羅三藐三菩提), the unsurpassed, correct, and equal awakening. "Awakening" has two meanings: (1) "correct awakening," the wisdom of accordance with principle, correctly contemplating the ultimate truth; (2) "equal awakening," the wisdom of full measure, comprehensively contemplating the conventional truth. Both are ultimate and boundless, hence "unsurpassed."

**Section 5: Concluding praise.** "Therefore know that the Prajnaparamita is the great divine spell, the great luminous spell, the unsurpassed spell, the unequaled spell" praises four virtues. Three interpretations: (1) By dharma: removing obstacles without fail is "divine"; wisdom discerning without obscurity is "luminous"; nothing surpasses it is "unsurpassed"; uniquely peerless is "unequaled." (2) By function: it can break afflictions; it can break fundamental ignorance; it brings causal practice to completion; it brings fruit-virtue to perfection. (3) By position: it surpasses ordinary beings; it surpasses the lesser vehicles; it surpasses the causal stages; it equals the fruit-stage. "Unequaled" means the position of the unequaled mutually aids its equality, hence "unequaled equal." The *Dasabhumika-sastra* says: "'Unequaled' means the Buddha compared to other beings: they are not his equal. The repeated 'equal' means that their dharma-bodies are equal."

"It can remove all suffering; this is true and not false" is the general conclusion: the three sufferings, the eight sufferings, all suffering. Removing suffering with certainty, hence "true and not false."

### The Secret Prajñā

"Therefore the Prajnaparamita spell is spoken; the spell is thus:"

[Dhāraṇī] *Gate gate paragate parasamgate bodhi svaha*

This has two interpretations. First, it is not to be interpreted, since it is the secret language of all buddhas, not to be understood at the causal stage. One should simply recite and uphold it to remove obstacles and increase merit. Second, if one insists on interpreting: *gate* means "gone" or "crossed," the function of the profound wisdom. The repeated *gate* means crossing oneself and crossing others. *Paragate* means "gone to the other shore," the destination of crossing. *Parasamgate* means "universally gone to the other shore," self and others universally crossing, all reaching the other shore together. *Bodhi* asks: "To what other shore?" The shore of great bodhi. *Svaha* means "swiftly," causing all the preceding to be swiftly accomplished.

*Concluding verse:*

> Prajñā is profound and abstruse, difficult to encounter through accumulated kalpas.
> Praising and interpreting according to my capacity, I hope to accord with the true school.

*Brief Commentary on the Prajnaparamita Heart Sūtra, by Fazang.*

In the second year of Chang'an [702 CE], at the Qingchan Temple in the capital, during a break from sūtra translation, at the request of the Honorable Zheng of Xingyang, who served concurrently as Vice-President of the Board of Rites and Inspector of Yongzhou... who from his youth to his white-haired years had recited this Heart Sūtra tens of millions of times, his mind roaming through its wondrous meaning and his mouth chanting its numinous words... he repeatedly and earnestly requested that I produce a brief commentary. I have ventured to use my gourd-ladle to measure the high and the deep.

### Preface to the Heart Sūtra Eulogy

The myriad dharmas arise from the mind; the mind is the master of the person. The three vehicles return to the one; the one is the essential of the Dharma. Knowing that the mind has nothing to attain is true attainment. Seeing that the one has nothing it does not penetrate is profound penetration. The Tathāgata taught that the five aggregates are all empty: persons are fundamentally empty. The Tathāgata taught that the characteristics of all dharmas are empty: dharmas are also empty. Knowing the Dharma and illuminating emptiness, seeing emptiness and releasing the Dharma: these two kinds of knowing and seeing, are they not also empty? Therefore, concentration and wisdom are both dharmas within emptiness. To enter this gate is the gate of luminosity. To walk this path is the path of transcendence. Who but one who practices the profound Prajñā could realize this?

The Junior Secretary and Imperial Son-in-Law Zheng Wanjun of Xingyang, a man of profound accomplishment, whose learning was renowned and whose calligraphy achieved the pinnacle of the grass script, wielded his brush to inscribe the Heart Sūtra in stone, establishing a precious sanctuary of sacred goodness and opening a glorious enterprise for future ages.

The Buddha taught with the characteristic of non-dependence, and the Dharma fundamentally does not arise. I transmit with non-attainment, and now there is no cessation. The Way abides in written words; its meaning is as vast as heaven and earth. Zhang Yue, the Elder of State, heard of this and rejoiced, praising the Buddha's work and inscribing it upon the joyous stone.
