# 觀總相論頌
## Verses on Examining General Characteristics

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1623

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨); composed by Bodhisattva Dignāga (陳那菩薩)

---

> Those who delight in brevity have already indicated the gist of the matter;
> For those who love elaborate texts, the general characteristics are explained.
> Through sound (śabda), meaning (artha), and cognition (jñāna), those who take conventional speech as fundamental —
> These are the words on examining general characteristics. This verse concerns sound and meaning,
> That is, the two of sound and meaning together. Within these, there are two kinds of cognition:
> The cognition of name-sound and the cognition of meaning — these are what is fundamental.
> That is, with regard to the causal meaning, as taught by all worldly conventions,
> Taking sound, meaning, and cognition as fundamental, therefore this is composed into verses.
> Sound, meaning, and the significance of cognition — therefore this treatise is briefly composed.

Question: The connection between name and meaning is fundamental to conventional speech. Why then does the text say it only distinguishes sound and meaning?

Answer:

> It is not like this. Even though there is the connection of sounds [and meanings],
> There are only the two: name and meaning. For those who accept general characteristics (sāmānya),
> The expressed (vacya) and the expressing (vācaka) — the principle here confirms [this],
> And all are subsumed into this. That is, the nature of non-separability
> Is precisely what permits their connection; therefore this is not separately explained here.
> Through expressing names and so forth, limited only to the domain of connection,
> One understands the expressed meaning. Therefore, through the nature of general characteristics,
> What can be understood — this will be extensively explained later.
> Therefore, through sound, meaning, and cognition alone, the principle of connection is established.

> Furthermore, others hold a different view: within this, name and meaning are two,
> There are three kinds of distinctions — namely, sound together with the two of meaning.
