# 要行捨身經
## Sūtra on the Essential Practice of Abandoning the Body

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2895

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was together with a great assembly of bhikṣus: one thousand two hundred five arhats with Mahākāśyapa as their leader; one thousand pratyekabuddhas of supernatural flight as the foremost; eight thousand great bodhisattvas with Bodhisattva Kṣitigarbha as their leader; the Great Brahmā King, Śakra Devānām Indra, the Four Great Heavenly Kings, the eight classes of nāgas and spirits; and bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās.

At that time, the World-Honored One, in the midst of this assembly, joyfully emitted a great light that illuminated the Śītavana charnel ground and the great assembly. Shortly afterward, the light returned and entered his mouth. He sat upright, in right mindfulness, without moving.

Thereupon Kāśyapa rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, circumambulated three times, and before the Buddha placed his right knee on the ground. He addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, why has the Tathāgata, in the midst of this assembly, joyfully smiled, emitting a great light that illuminated the Śītavana charnel ground and the great assembly, and shortly afterward the light returned and entered his mouth, while he sat upright in right mindfulness without moving? We humbly ask the Tathāgata to explain its significance."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent, good man. Listen carefully, reflect well, and be mindful. I shall explain the essential Dharma for you. With the Buddha-eye, I observe that within this charnel ground, those who practice body-abandonment shall in the future attain perfect, complete awakening. If any sentient being can generate the supreme aspiration to abandon the body, with a mind of equanimity free from love and hatred, without producing miserliness, then even a single generation of this aspiration, though the body has not yet been given, already surpasses the practice of the two vehicles a hundred thousand million times over. One immediately becomes equal to the bodhisattvas and all those of the same aspiration. How much more so when one actually gives up one's body and life to benefit all sentient beings universally! Such merit, if I were to explain it fully, could not be exhausted."

Kāśyapa addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, when the Tathāgata says this is in the Śītavana charnel ground and benefits all sentient beings universally, this reasoning is flawed and cannot be called 'universal.' If it is truly universal, then sentient beings in the water have no share. We humbly ask the Tathāgata to show us the meaning of 'universal.'"

The Buddha said: "Good man, I was just about to explain this when you asked. If any sentient being can give up portions of the body, dividing it into two parts, one part for the water and one part for the land, then when that person's life is about to end, they should entrust good spiritual friends and those of the same aspiration to divide and cut the body into two parts. The merit of dividing the body in this way is no different from the merit of one who abandons the whole body."

"I recall that in the past, at the time of Dīpaṃkara Buddha, in the kingdom of Śrāvastī, I was cultivating the practice of bodhi, vowing to give up my body and life, skin, flesh, sinews, and bones, thought after thought in continuous succession. At that time, Maitreya was my good friend. When my life came to an end, my body was divided into two parts and placed according to the Dharma. Maitreya joined his palms and made a vow: 'With this small offering, may all sentient beings universally, in water, on land, and in the sky, all have food to eat. By this merit, from beginningless time onward, may we always encounter each other and be companions.' The one who subsequently became a buddha is this very person."

"Because of abandoning my body, I uttered a great vow: 'If any sentient being, when hungry, eats my flesh, and when thirsty, drinks my blood, then after eating and drinking, may all sentient beings, through eating my flesh, generate the aspiration for bodhi and be freed from the suffering of hunger and thirst. In the future, may they obtain the food of Dharma-joy and the like, nourishing the body of their own nature. May those who drink my blood be freed from all the thirst of craving and obtain the water of the Dharma, which irrigates the source of the mind. May they love the Dharma-king and the resources of the transformation-body. For this reason, may they swiftly attain unsurpassed, perfect, complete awakening.'"

"Furthermore, Kāśyapa, when I was on the causal ground and first generated the aspiration, through three lifetimes I died giving up my body. In the very fourth life I was able to give while still living, without craving or attachment. At the time of the Void-King Buddha, having awakened to the principle of emptiness, I was Prince Sattva, offering my body to the starving tigress to feed her seven cubs. I was also King Candraprabha, who broadly gave away a thousand heads. I was also Prince Dāna, who gave away his wife and children. I was also King Maitrībala, who gave his body to five yakṣas. For the sake of seeking the Dharma, on the edge of the Himalayas, I sought a half-verse and offered my body to feed a rākṣasa. If I were to speak extensively of all the lifetimes from beginningless time in which I gave up my body and life, even in a hundred thousand kalpas it could not be fully told. Why? It is all because, on the causal ground when first generating the aspiration, I died giving up my body."

Kāśyapa addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, such people practice body-abandonment and their merit is immeasurable. But I wonder: before they made their offering, in many past lives they committed transgressions. Can their former evil karma be eliminated by the merit of body-abandonment?"

The Buddha said: "Good man, if any sentient being has killed other sentient beings through many lives, filling the entire sahā world, committing the four grave offenses, the five heinous crimes, slandering the Vaipulya sūtras, and even eating the blood and flesh of sentient beings, such transgressions are immeasurable and boundless. Yet if any sentient being can give up portions of the body to benefit sentient beings universally and relieve their suffering of hunger and thirst, all such transgressions as described above are completely eliminated. All the calamities from beginningless time are swiftly extinguished without remainder. Because of abandoning the body, this is the great confession and repentance, the sincere remorse and shame. All buddhas of the ten directions jointly praise such a person."

"Good man, suppose a person, from beginningless time onward, has committed such deeds as stealing the property of the saṃgha as a whole, the permanent property of the saṃgha, or the property presently held by the saṃgha; preaching the Dharma impurely; cheating with large measures and heavy scales: such transgressions should lead to hells, animal realms, and hungry ghost realms, with no liberation for a hundred thousand kalpas. Fearing future suffering, such a person might summon and gather all the great arhats of the sahā world who possess the three insights, six supernatural powers, and eight liberations, whether ordinary or holy, all monks and nuns, assembling them in one place, hoping to eliminate those transgressions. Yet even so, they cannot be eliminated. Why? Because there are a hundred thousand millions of Ganges River sahā worlds, and all the pure saṃgha and ocean of assemblies cannot all be gathered together. For this reason, such transgressions are difficult to eliminate. This is why they are called 'permanent property of the saṃgha': it universally pervades the dharma-realm, from the buddhas above to novices below, and all have a share in it."

"It would be better for a person, in every place of birth, to vow to give up this body, skin, flesh, sinews, and bones, thought after thought in continuous succession. Such transgressions as described above are all completely eliminated. The records in the celestial bureaus and the underworld courts are naturally expunged. For this reason, I see this benefit and therefore urge all sentient beings to give up portions of the body. One will certainly attain unsurpassed, equal, complete awakening."

At that time, the World-Honored One told Bodhisattva Maitreya: "Maitreya, you and the others should protect and uphold this category of sūtra. In Jambudvīpa, spread and circulate it widely, and do not let it be cut off."

Thereupon Bodhisattva Maitreya addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, after the Tathāgata's extinction, in the evil age, if good men or good women generate a courageous mind and give their bodies in offering to all sentient beings for the sake of the perfection of giving (dāna-pāramitā), such people I shall, at the Dragon-Flower Assembly at the very first gathering, cause to attain liberation. May the World-Honored One have no further concern."

The Great Brahmā King, Śakra Devānām Indra, the Four Great Heavenly Kings, the eight classes of nāgas and spirits, the bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, such people as these we shall from this day forward guard and protect, not allowing anyone to create obstacles for them. Why? Because such people will certainly attain unsurpassed, true, complete awakening."

At that time, Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what should this be called?" The Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Sūtra on the Merit of the Essential Practice of Body-Abandonment of All Buddhas.'" At that time, all devas, humans, and asuras, having heard what the Buddha said, faithfully received it and put it into practice.

**Vow-Text for Body-Abandonment:**

All buddhas of the ten directions and three times, bear witness and know! Your disciple So-and-so and others, from beginningless time, through this present life, and to the end of future time, constantly with inner wealth, outer wealth, giving while alive, and giving in death: your disciple So-and-so, now that this present life is ending, further offers this body of limited existence, blood, flesh, skin, bone, head, eyes, marrow, brain, as well as hands and feet, to all hungry and starving sentient beings, to repay past debts and to reverently follow the traces of the bodhisattvas' austere practices. Abandoning the body, blood, and flesh, proceeding to the Śītavana charnel ground. The wholesome roots from this body-abandonment, those already gathered, those to be gathered, and those being gathered: with these wholesome merits, together with all sentient beings, in every place of birth, may we not develop attachment to body, life, or possessions. May we abandon the wrong and return to the right. May we generate the aspiration for bodhi. May the three obstructions be forever removed. May we be forever free from poverty. May we constantly see all buddhas and bodhisattvas.
