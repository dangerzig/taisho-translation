# Brilliance Praise Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra
## 光讚經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 222, 10 fascicles

Translated from the Chinese. Dharmarakṣa (竺法護), Western Jin dynasty.

---

## Fascicle One

### Chapter 1: Brilliance Praise

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of five thousand bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats whose outflows were exhausted, who were free of defilements, who had attained mastery, whose minds were liberated in wisdom, who had achieved benevolence and served as great guides. All that they needed to do was accomplished and brought to completion. They had cast off heavy burdens and attained their own benefit, ending the afflictions of saṃsāra. With impartial liberation they crossed beyond all conceptions, reaching the shore of the boundless, save only the Venerable Ānanda, a learner at the stage of stream-entry. Also present were five hundred bhikṣus, as well as bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās. There were also bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who had attained dhāraṇī and samādhi, who cultivated the practice of emptiness, who honored signlessness, who did not cling to wishes, who had attained the patient endurance of equality, who mastered the immeasurable, who had all attained the five supernatural powers. Their speech was swift and without weariness. They had renounced the desire for household profit. Their teaching of the dharma did not seek offerings. They had reached the profound and wondrous dharma, crossing to the boundless. They had attained fearlessness and transcended the affairs of Māra, freed from all the hindrances of the aggregates and coverings. They expounded on causes and conditions, following the mind's inclinations, having practiced the vow of diligent effort through innumerable kalpas. Their names included Bhadrapāla Bodhisattva, Ratnākara Bodhisattva, Mahāsubhūti Bodhisattva, Nārada Bodhisattva, Kauṇḍinya Bodhisattva, Varuṇadatta Bodhisattva, Indra Bodhisattva, Kuśalarakṣita Bodhisattva, Wonderful-Thought Bodhisattva, Dhṛtidhara Bodhisattva, Adhyāśaya Bodhisattva, Amoghadarśin Bodhisattva, Praṇidhāna Bodhisattva, Parivarta Bodhisattva, Ever-Diligent Bodhisattva, Never-Abandoning Bodhisattva, Sūryaprabha Bodhisattva, Nirātman Bodhisattva, Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Ratnakūṭamukhya Bodhisattva, Ever-Raised-Hand Bodhisattva, Ever-Lowered-Hand Bodhisattva, and Maitreya Bodhisattva. The bodhisattva assembly was thus without limit, incalculable billions of hundreds of thousands of koṭis, all of wondrous virtue, pure and identical with truth.

At that time the World-Honored One, seated upon his natural lion-throne in cross-legged posture, his body upright, his mind directed and established, entered a samādhi called "King of Settled Mind." Through this samādhi he naturally entered right absorption, pervading and entering all samādhis equally, guiding and directing them with impartial rescue.

When the Buddha entered samādhi, his mind at peace, he surveyed the world with his wisdom-eye. His body was serene, and he smiled. From the soles of his feet he emitted sixty trillion hundreds of thousands of rays of light. From his ten toes he emitted ten billion hundreds of thousands of rays. From both sides, two billion hundreds of thousands of rays each. From his knees, from his thighs, from his shoulders, from his elbows, from his navel, from his head, from his fingers, from his arms, from his eyebrows, from his neck, from his eyes, from his ears, from his nose, from his four faces, from his forty teeth, from the mark between his brows, and from the crown of his head, sixty trillion hundreds of thousands of rays each. These illuminated the entire trichiliocosm without remainder, and also universally radiated to the east throughout buddha-fields as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and likewise to the south, west, north, the four intermediate directions, above, and below. All beings who encountered this light found their minds at peace, and all aroused the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

Thereupon the World-Honored One laughed with delight. From every pore he emitted light illuminating the trichiliocosm and pervading the ten directions. On the lotuses within those lights appeared buddhas seated in cross-legged posture, teaching the dharma and expounding the six pāramitās.

The World-Honored One then manifested supernatural displays, and the trichiliocosm trembled in six ways. All beings in the hells, hungry ghost realm, and animal realm were spontaneously delivered. The blind gained sight, the deaf gained hearing, the confused regained their minds. The naked received clothing, the hungry were sated, the sick were healed. All beings attained the mind of equality, regarding one another as father and mother, as brothers and sisters, cultivating the ten wholesome deeds.

Then the World-Honored One told the Venerable Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva should diligently cultivate and study prajñāpāramitā."

Śāriputra asked: "World-Honored One, how does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, fully understanding all dharmas, study prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, abiding in prajñāpāramitā, cultivates without any fixed location, thereby perfecting dāna-pāramitā, without deficiency or diminishment in giving, without attachment or aversion. Śīla-pāramitā should be perfected as well. Through this, one never abides in offense or non-offense. One should also perfect kṣānti-pāramitā, arousing freedom from anger. One should study vīrya-pāramitā to receive the prediction. With body and mind one arouses diligence, not giving rise to outflows. One should perfect dhyāna-pāramitā. Through this, there is nothing sought or craved."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva abiding in prajñāpāramitā achieves the four foundations of mindfulness without arousing anything. One should also perfect the four right efforts, the four bases of supernatural power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold path. One should fully achieve the emptiness samādhi, the signless samādhi, and the wishless samādhi, as well as the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables, the four formless absorptions, and the eight liberations. If one wishes to understand the tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and eighteen unique dharmas of a buddha, as well as great compassion and great loving-kindness, then a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice prajñāpāramitā."

[The Buddha continues at great length, repeating the formula: "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to accomplish X, one should study prajñāpāramitā," covering: establishing all beings in morality, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge of liberation; attaining the fruits from stream-entry through arhatship and pratyekabuddhahood; attaining anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi; purifying the five eyes; hearing the dharma of all buddhas in all directions; illuminating all dark world-systems; healing all sentient beings; rescuing beings from the evil destinies; and countless other aspirations.]

The Buddha said: "Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva studies prajñāpāramitā."

From the world-system Ratnākara in the far east, the bodhisattva Samantaprabha traveled to this Sahā world with countless bodhisattvas, carrying golden lotus flowers given by his buddha Ratnakāra Tathāgata, and paid homage to Śākyamuni Buddha. Likewise, bodhisattvas arrived from the southern, western, northern, lower, and upper directions, and from the four intermediate quarters, each bearing lotuses from their respective buddhas.

### Chapter 2: Conforming to Emptiness

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices prajñāpāramitā and arouses these virtues, the Four Great Kings immediately rejoice. The gods of Trāyastriṃśa, the Yāma, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, and Paranirmitavaśavartin heavens, up to the Akaniṣṭha heaven, all leap with joy, saying, 'We shall request and encourage him to turn the dharma wheel.'"

Śāriputra asked the World-Honored One: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practice prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha said: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices prajñāpāramitā, one does not perceive the bodhisattva, nor perceive the word 'bodhisattva,' nor perceive prajñāpāramitā, nor perceive the words 'practicing prajñāpāramitā,' nor perceive non-practice. Why is this? The name 'bodhisattva' is inherently empty. What is empty has no form, no sensation, no perception, no formations, no consciousness. Form is not different from emptiness; emptiness is not different from form. Form is emptiness; emptiness is form. Sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness are likewise. Why is this? What is called a bodhisattva is merely a conventional designation. What is called the path is also a conventional designation. What is called emptiness is also a conventional designation. These dharmas are naturally without arising and without cessation, free from defilements. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices thus sees neither arising nor cessation. Why is this? Names are established through deceptive convention, arising from adventitious perceptions. From where are names established? They are merely empty words. Understanding thus, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices prajñāpāramitā."

### Chapter 3: Practicing Emptiness (Part 1)

The Buddha again told Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices prajñāpāramitā, one should contemplate thus: 'What is called a bodhisattva, what is called a buddha, these are merely conventional designations. What are called form, sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness are merely conventional designations, all arising from the notion of self. This so-called self is utterly non-existent. There is no self, no person, no life force, no lifespan, no sentient being. There is no mind, no thought. Whatever is done or created, whatever is naturally practiced or experienced, whatever is seen or known, all such things cannot be apprehended; they are empty and without attachment. They are all conventional designations, mere empty words.' A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices thus does not perceive sentient beings. Having nothing to perceive, there is also no perception, and no conventional designation to rely upon."

The Buddha said: "Suppose Śāriputra, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, and such bhikṣus filled Jambudvīpa like a bamboo grove, their wisdom fully complete; still they could not equal a bodhisattva practicing prajñāpāramitā, not by a hundred, a thousand, ten thousand, or a billion times. Why is this? The bodhisattva's wisdom aspires to liberate all sentient beings."

[The text continues with extensive discussions of how the bodhisattva's one-day practice of wisdom surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, because the bodhisattva's intention is to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi for the sake of all beings. The sun metaphor is used: as the sun illuminates all of Jambudvīpa, so the bodhisattva's practice of the six pāramitās illuminates and liberates immeasurable beings.]

Śāriputra asked: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva transcend the stages of the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas?"

The Buddha said: "From the initial arousal of the mind, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the six pāramitās, transcending emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, thereby surpassing the stages of the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas and abiding in the stage of non-retrogression."

[The fascicle continues with teachings on the bodhisattva's supreme merit, the five eyes (flesh eye, divine eye, wisdom eye, dharma eye, buddha eye), the six supernatural powers, and how the bodhisattva abiding in prajñāpāramitā perfects each of the six pāramitās while remaining in emptiness. The three hundred bhikṣus offer their ornaments and arouse the aspiration for bodhi. The Buddha smiles and predicts they will become buddhas named Mahāvīra Tathāgata after sixty-one kalpas.]

### Chapter 4: Praising Equality

Śāriputra, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, Mahākāśyapa, and the other great disciples, as well as innumerable bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, upāsakas, and upāsikās, all addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! This practice is the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's great unsurpassable pāramitā, the most supreme pāramitā, the most honored pāramitā, the unequalled pāramitā, the limitless pāramitā, the peerless pāramitā. This is the bodhisattva's emptiness pāramitā, the signless pāramitā, the wishless pāramitā. The bodhisattva-mahāsattva can equal the unequalled. The bodhisattva who practices dāna-pāramitā reaches the equality of the unequalled, attaining anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

### Chapter 5: Bestowing Predictions

The World-Honored One extended his tongue to cover the trichiliocosm. From his tongue came immeasurable light, illuminating the buddha-fields of the east. Bodhisattvas throughout the ten directions beheld this light and traveled to Śākyamuni Buddha's realm. They paid homage, offered flowers and incense, and sat to listen to the teaching. Seeing the assembly, the Buddha smiled. Ānanda asked the reason. The Buddha said that all in the assembly who had resolved upon the dharma of non-arising would, after sixty-eight billion kalpas, become buddhas named Buddhapuṣpa Tathāgata.

## Fascicle Two

### Chapter 6: Discerning Emptiness

The Buddha instructed the Venerable Subhūti to expound prajñāpāramitā for the bodhisattvas. Subhūti, knowing the assembly's thoughts, told Śāriputra: "Whatever a disciple of the Buddha teaches, all is through the Tathāgata's power."

Subhūti then said to the World-Honored One: "What is called a bodhisattva? Within this dharma, why is there the word 'bodhisattva'? I never perceive any dharma of a bodhisattva. I never perceive prajñāpāramitā or a bodhisattva. How then shall I teach prajñāpāramitā to bodhisattvas?"

The Buddha told Subhūti: "What are called prajñāpāramitā and bodhisattva are merely conventional names, without substance. Their names are not internal, not external, and not between the two. Just as what is called a 'person' is a mere conventional designation, the dharmas being without arising or cessation, all being named through the conjunction of causes and conditions according to worldly convention, merely through sound and speech, so too 'self,' 'person,' 'lifespan,' 'sentient being,' 'creator,' 'actor,' 'one who sees,' 'one who knows,' all of these are conventional dharmas. All are without arising or cessation. So too, Subhūti, prajñāpāramitā and the name 'bodhisattva' are mere conventional designations, all without arising and without cessation."

[The chapter continues with an extensive dialogue between the Buddha and Subhūti, exploring how all dharmas are conventional designations (prajñapti), analogous to names given to parts of the body (head, neck, arms, etc.) or external objects (grass, trees, flowers, fruit). All names for form, sensation, perception, formations, consciousness, the sense bases, the dhātus, the twelve links of dependent origination, the six pāramitās, the 37 aids to awakening, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique qualities of a buddha are likewise conventional designations, all without arising and without cessation, neither internal nor external. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who understands this practices prajñāpāramitā without attachment to any dharma, and thereby progresses through the teaching, purifies buddha-fields, teaches sentient beings, and approaches anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.]

The Buddha asked Subhūti: "Is form a bodhisattva? Are sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness bodhisattvas?" Subhūti answered "No" to each question. The Buddha then asked about the sense bases, the elements, the twelve links: none of these is a bodhisattva. Subhūti concluded: "Self and person cannot be apprehended. How then could one name a bodhisattva?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Subhūti, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva studying prajñāpāramitā should contemplate that sentient beings and phenomena are non-existent and unobtainable. Prajñāpāramitā is also non-existent and unobtainable."

## Fascicle Three

### Chapter 7: Understanding Emptiness

Subhūti said to the Buddha: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who wishes to perfect dāna-pāramitā should study prajñāpāramitā. One who wishes to perfect śīla, kṣānti, vīrya, and dhyāna-pāramitā should study prajñāpāramitā. One who wishes to eradicate form should study prajñāpāramitā, and likewise for sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness; for the sense bases, sense fields, and sense consciousnesses; for desire, anger, and delusion; for clinging to self-view, doubt, and violation of precepts; for the attachments to desire, form, and the formless realm."

[Subhūti lists an exhaustive catalogue of what should be studied through prajñāpāramitā, including all the dhāraṇī doors, samādhi doors (śūraṅgama samādhi, jewel-seal samādhi, lion's sport samādhi, moon-radiance samādhi, adamantine samādhi, and many others), and all aspects of the bodhisattva path.]

Śāriputra asked Subhūti how the bodhisattva avoids disputing the supreme dharma. Subhūti explained that without skillful means (upāya-kauśalya), a bodhisattva may understand emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness yet still fall into attachment to these very understandings, which constitutes "impure birth" (a birth lacking full ripening). True stillness (praśama) is when emptiness does not perceive emptiness, and each type of emptiness does not contend with any other.

Subhūti said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing prajñāpāramitā should enter into the equal and unequalled mind. The mind is inherently without mind. The mind is originally pure. The originally pure mind is naturally joyful, luminous, and clean."

Śāriputra praised Subhūti: "Excellent! Subhūti, you are the son of the World-Honored One, born from the dharma gate, always conforming to dharma. The World-Honored One praises you as foremost in the practice of emptiness."

### Chapter 8: Conventional Designations

Subhūti said to the Buddha: "As the World-Honored One has said, 'bodhisattva-mahāsattva,' I also do not perceive and cannot apprehend such a practitioner. Since I cannot perceive or apprehend a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing prajñāpāramitā, how shall I teach prajñāpāramitā to the bodhisattvas? Were I to say that all dharmas can be apprehended and thereby establish the designation 'bodhisattva,' I would fall into doubt."

[Subhūti then delivers a sustained meditation on non-apprehension: investigating every aspect of experience, from self and body to form, sensation, perception, formations, consciousness, the six senses, the eighteen elements, the twelve links, the six pāramitās, the 37 aids to awakening, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the eighteen unique qualities, all dharmas past, present, and future, and even the buddhas and their fields throughout the ten directions, he finds in each case that "when I examine its root and extremity, it is utterly unobtainable." All names are established through ignorance; all designations neither abide nor do not abide. Everything is like an echo, a reflection, a mirage, an illusion, a magical creation.]

Subhūti said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who hears prajñāpāramitā taught in this way, without being frightened, alarmed, or terrified, should be known as dwelling at the stage of non-retrogression."

## Fascicle Four

### Chapter 9: Practice

Subhūti said to the Buddha: "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva lacking skillful means practices prajñāpāramitā and practices [engaging with] form, this is practicing with conceptual proliferation. If one practices the permanence or impermanence of form, the suffering or pleasure of form, the self or non-self of form, the emptiness or non-emptiness of form, this is all practicing with conceptual proliferation."

Śāriputra asked: "Then how should a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practice prajñāpāramitā to attain it?"

Subhūti said: "With skillful means, one does not practice form, does not practice sensation, perception, formations, or consciousness. One does not practice the permanence or impermanence of form, the suffering or pleasure of form, the self or non-self of form. Why is this? Form is emptiness, and emptiness is form. Form is naturally empty. So too for consciousness and all the rest."

[The discussion continues with Subhūti explaining the "non-receiving samādhi" (anupalambha-samādhi) which is vast and limitless, beyond the reach of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Through this samādhi, the bodhisattva swiftly attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Subhūti then lists over one hundred named samādhis, including the śūraṅgama, jewel-seal, lion's sport, moon-radiance, adamantine, dharma-seal, king of settled mind, and many more.]

### Chapter 10: Illusion

Subhūti said to the Buddha: "If someone asks, 'Can this illusory person study prajñāpāramitā and attain sarvajñā (omniscience)?', how should one answer?"

The Buddha asked Subhūti: "Is form different from illusion? Is sensation different from illusion? Are perception, formations, and consciousness different from illusion?" Subhūti answered: "No, World-Honored One! Form is illusion; illusion is form. Consciousness is illusion; illusion is consciousness. The 37 aids to awakening are illusion. The ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique dharmas are illusion. Illusion is dharma; dharma is illusion."

The Buddha asked: "Does illusion have defilements or aversion?" "No." "Does illusion arise or cease?" "No." "Can what neither arises nor ceases study prajñāpāramitā and attain sarvajñā?" "No." "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who studies prajñāpāramitā in this way attains sarvajñā through non-attainment."

Subhūti said: "One who wishes to study should study as one studies illusion. Why? Observe that the five aggregates are like an illusory person. The five aggregates are naturally non-existent. What is naturally non-existent cannot be apprehended. Like a dream, an echo, a water-reflection, a mirage, an illusion."

[The text then discusses good teachers versus bad teachers. A good teacher teaches that all dharmas are unobtainable and unattainable, and uses merit to direct beings toward sarvajñā rather than toward the śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha stages. A bad teacher, or Māra in disguise, attempts to dissuade the bodhisattva from prajñāpāramitā, appearing as a buddha, a teacher, or even the bodhisattva's parents, encouraging the bodhisattva to settle for the śrāvaka path.]

## Fascicle Five

### Chapter 11: Mahāsattva

Subhūti asked the Buddha: "What does 'bodhisattva' signify? What is its meaning?"

The Buddha said: "The sentence of no-meaning is the bodhisattva's designation. The bodhisattva has no footprint, no self. Like a bird flying through empty space, there is no footprint. Like a dream, like an illusion, like a mirage, like an echo, like a reflection, like a transformation by the Tathāgata, there is no footprint."

[The Buddha then offers an extensive series of analogies: just as internal emptiness has no footprint, just as self and non-self have no footprint, just as the 37 aids, the ten powers, the eighteen unique qualities have no footprint, just as the Tathāgata's form has no footprint, just as the unconditioned realm has no footprint, just as what does not arise has no footprint, just as the sun illuminates from empty space without footprint, just as fire at the end of a kalpa destroys all without leaving a trace, so too the bodhisattva practicing prajñāpāramitā is without any trace or designation.]

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva should learn all dharmas without attachment. One should not awaken to the meaning of any dharma." He then defined all dharmas as wholesome and unwholesome, worldly and transcendent, with outflows and without outflows, conditioned and unconditioned, with adversaries and without adversaries. He explained each category in detail.

The Buddha told Subhūti why one is called "mahāsattva": one who, among all the assemblies from the eight types of noble beings through to bodhisattvas, is the most honored, with a mind like vajra. The mahāsattva resolves: "I shall remain in saṃsāra without limit. All sentient beings I shall liberate through the three vehicles. Having extinguished all beings, there is no one who attains nirvāṇa."

### Chapter 12: Equal to the Unequalled

Śāriputra asked to explain the meaning of "mahāsattva." Subhūti said: "The bodhisattva's mind equals the unequalled. It is a mind without adversaries. The minds of the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas cannot reach it. Why? The sarvajñā-mind is without outflows and without causes. If the sarvajñā-mind is without outflows and without causes, there is nothing to which that mind can be attached."

Śāriputra and Subhūti then discussed how even the minds of ordinary beings, the śrāvakas, the pratyekabuddhas, and the Buddha are without outflows, without causes, and originally pure. All dharmas including the aggregates, elements, sense bases, the twelve links, the 37 aids, the ten powers, and the eighteen unique qualities are without outflows, without causes, and originally pure.

### Chapter 13: The Great Vehicle

The Venerable Pūrṇamaitrāyaṇīputra asked: "Why is the bodhisattva-mahāsattva said to don the great armor (mahā-saṃnāha) and aspire to the Great Vehicle (mahāyāna)?"

Pūrṇa explained: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva does not practice dāna-pāramitā for a limited number of beings. Rather, for the sake of all sentient beings, one abides in dāna-pāramitā. Likewise for śīla, kṣānti, vīrya, dhyāna, and prajñā-pāramitā."

[Pūrṇa then explains in great detail how the bodhisattva, with mind fixed on sarvajñā, practices each pāramitā: giving without expecting reward, keeping precepts without directing merit toward the śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha stages, enduring insults without anger, practicing diligence without pride, maintaining meditation without attachment to absorption, and cultivating wisdom that sees all dharmas as illusory. Each pāramitā is practiced as encompassing all six. The discussion extends to the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables (loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity), and the formless absorptions, all practiced with skillful means to avoid being reborn in the corresponding heavens. This constitutes the Great Vehicle (mahāyāna).]

## Fascicle Six

### Chapter 14: Riding the Great Vehicle

Śāriputra asked Pūrṇa: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva ride the Great Vehicle?"

Pūrṇa answered: "The bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices prajñāpāramitā, rides dāna-pāramitā without apprehending it, without finding a bodhisattva or a recipient. One rides each pāramitā, the four foundations of mindfulness, the 37 aids, the ten powers, and the eighteen unique dharmas, without apprehending any of them." All phenomena are mere conventional designations, unobtainable and without arising.

From the initial arousal of the mind, the bodhisattva perfects supernatural powers, teaches sentient beings, travels from buddha-field to buddha-field, serves the buddhas, purifies buddha-lands, and never separates from this vehicle until attaining sarvajñā and turning the dharma wheel.

### Chapter 15: Without Bonds

The Buddha told Subhūti about the bodhisattva's Great Armor (saṃnāha). Donning this armor of the six pāramitās, internal and external emptiness, sarvajñā, and the buddha-form, the bodhisattva's light pervades the trichiliocosm and the ten directions. The bodhisattva manifests as a cakravartin king who gives generously to all in need, then teaches the dharma. Just as a skilled illusionist creates people at a crossroads and provides them with everything they need, yet no real giving or receiving occurs, so the bodhisattva gives without there being a giver or receiver.

[The Buddha extends this analogy to each pāramitā: the bodhisattva establishes beings in morality (like an illusionist creating people and teaching them the ten wholesome deeds), endurance (enduring even physical assault, like an illusionist creating people who are struck but feel nothing), diligence, meditation, and wisdom. In each case, neither the teacher nor the taught truly exist.]

Subhūti said: "Color is without attachment, without bondage, without liberation. Sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness are without attachment, without bondage, without liberation." He then applied "without attachment, without bondage, without liberation" to every category of Buddhist doctrine: the three times, the six pāramitās, the seven emptinesses, the 37 aids, the ten powers, the eighteen unique qualities, sarvajñā, the turning of the dharma wheel, and the establishment of beings in the three vehicles.

### Chapter 16: Samādhi

Subhūti asked the Buddha about the Great Vehicle's meaning, where it arises, where it abides, and who accomplishes it.

The Buddha said: "The six pāramitās constitute the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's Great Vehicle." He defined each pāramitā in terms of directing the mind to sarvajñā: giving without grasping, keeping precepts by practicing the ten wholesome deeds, patient endurance, diligence that does not forsake the five pāramitās, meditation with skillful means that does not follow the force of samādhi into rebirth, and wisdom that perceives all dharmas as originally pure.

The Buddha then defined the Great Vehicle as: internal emptiness, external emptiness, emptiness of emptiness, great emptiness, ultimate emptiness, emptiness of the conditioned, emptiness of the unconditioned, emptiness of natural characteristics, emptiness of all dharmas, emptiness of non-apprehension, emptiness of non-existence, and natural emptiness. Each was explained as indestructible and incorruptible because originally pure.

[The Buddha then listed 110 named samādhis constituting the mahāyāna, including the śūraṅgama, seal of truth, lion's sport, good moon, moon-banner, transcending all dharmas, observation of the pinnacle, discernment of the dharma-realm, vajra-likeness, entering the dharma-seal, radiant king, power of diligence, equal transcendence, entering responsive analysis, illuminating all directions, dhāraṇī-seal, not-taken-away, sea-seal governing all dharmas, pervading all space, vajra seat of awakening, victorious treasure, illumination, non-blinking, resolution, stainless lamp, boundless light, light-creation, universal illumination, governing purity, stainless radiance, delight, wisdom-lamp, inexhaustible, majestic power, removing exhaustion, non-differentiation, opening through, sun-lamp, moon-stainless, pure illumination, radiant effulgence, accomplishment, wisdom-flower, vajra-likeness, firm resolve, jewel-heap, surpassing dharma-seal, dharma-universality, surpassing delight, transcending the dharma-pinnacle, destruction, discernment of sentences, equal creation of letters, removing letters, cutting conditions, non-action, non-fabrication, non-ultimate practice, removing darkness, practice-trace, non-moving, transcending realms, discerning all virtues, resolved abiding, pure flower-adornment, awakening-factor sentence, boundless lamp, equal to the unequalled, transcending all dharmas, cutting all fabrications, mindless non-destruction, non-abiding, one-purity, governing affairs, surpassing affairs, removing all fabrications, entering concordant sound, transcending words and speech, blazing radiance, adorned purity, signlessness, all accomplishment, not delighting in suffering or pleasure, inexhaustibility, dhāraṇī-sentence, protecting right and wrong, entering form and formlessness, soundlessness, stainless radiance, firm governance, full moon stainless radiance, great purity, universal illumination, universal samādhi, governing emptiness, equal governance, ultimate non-delight, abiding in no-origin, body-time serenity, speech-time emptiness, and liberation from the formless.]

Each samādhi was defined: "When abiding in this samādhi, one enters all samādhis," "one seals all samādhis," "one sports in all samādhis," and so forth.

## Fascicle Seven

### Chapter 17: Contemplation

The Buddha told Subhūti: "The Great Vehicle includes the four foundations of mindfulness. What are the four? Internally observing the body, not identifying with the body, without conceptualization, without apprehending the body. Observing the external body, likewise. Observing sensations, mind, and dharmas, both internally and externally, without identification, without conceptualization, without apprehension."

[The Buddha then gave detailed instructions on contemplation of the body: awareness of postures (walking, standing, sitting, lying), mindfulness of activities (eating, drinking, moving), mindfulness of breathing (long, short, fast, slow, rough, smooth, like a potter who knows the size of his vessel), contemplation of the four elements (earth, water, fire, wind, like a butcher who divides a cow into four quarters), contemplation of bodily impurities (hair, nails, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, organs, blood, etc., like a farmer sorting different grains), and contemplation of death and decomposition (a corpse swelling, turning blue, being eaten by animals, bones scattering, turning to dust). The meditator observes: "This body is like that, subject to disintegration and impermanence, with no escape."]

The chapter continued with the four right efforts, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, the eightfold noble path, the three gates of liberation (emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness), ten types of wisdom, three types of faculties, three types of samādhi (with perception and activity, without perception with activity, without perception and without activity), ten recollections, the four dhyānas, the four formless absorptions, the four immeasurables, the eight liberations, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the four analytical knowledges, the eighteen unique qualities of a buddha, and the dhāraṇī gate with its alphabet teaching (each syllable opening the door to understanding dharmas in a different way).

### Chapter 18: The Ten Stages

The Buddha told Subhūti about the bodhisattva's ten stages (daśabhūmi). The first stage requires ten practices: cultivating one's disposition without inversion, cultivating compassion without conceptions, regarding all beings equally, practicing giving without distinction, respecting good teachers, seeking the dharma, diligently leaving home life, seeking the buddha body, opening the dharma teaching for all beings without expectation, and abandoning arrogance.

The second stage requires eight qualities: pure morality with gratitude, knowledge of repaying kindness, abiding in patient strength, constant joy, not abandoning beings, following the instructions of elders, regarding renunciants as the World-Honored One, and seeking skillful means.

The third stage requires five: insatiable inquiry, giving the dharma without expecting material support, purifying buddha-lands, encouraging merit, and having no expectations. The fourth, ten things never to abandon: solitude, few desires, contentment, meditation, precepts, non-aversion to sensory experience, not dwelling in nirvāṇa, giving all possessions, not being faint-hearted, and not coveting possessions.

The fifth, abandoning eight things: home life, association with nuns, frivolous behavior, attachment to merit, sleepiness, angry disputes, self-praise, and denigrating others.

The sixth, completing six things: the six pāramitās; not seeking the śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha vehicles; welcoming beggars with a joyful expression; giving without sorrow; and freedom from resentment.

The seventh, removing twenty things (attachment to self, person, lifespan, etc.) and perfecting twenty things (understanding emptiness, not realizing signlessness prematurely, wisdom without wishes, etc.).

The eighth, four things: entering beings' minds, surveying buddha-lands, perfecting one's own land, and observing the buddha-body with true insight.

The ninth, four things: understanding faculties, perfecting the illusion-samādhi, guiding beings to virtue, and manifesting the complete body for teaching.

The tenth, twelve things: infinite protection, fulfilling all wishes, speaking so all beings understand, great eloquence, noble birth, appropriate retinue and land, renouncing the kingdom, arriving at the bodhi-tree, complete purity, and all virtues.

"The tenth-stage bodhisattva is the Buddha."

## Fascicle Eight

### Chapter 19: From Where the Vehicle Arises

The Buddha told Subhūti: "The Great Vehicle arises from the three realms, abides in sarvajñā, is originally unborn and will never be born. Why? The mahāyāna and sarvajñā, in these two, dharmas neither come together nor separate. They are without form, without perception, without grasping or releasing, being of one characteristic, the characteristic of no-characteristic."

[The Buddha then taught that if one wishes signless dharmas to arise, it is as if one wishes the dharma-realm, the original groundlessness, the true fundamental limit, the inconceivable realm, diligent practice, and the realm of cessation to arise. All dharmas from the five aggregates through the six pāramitās, the seven emptinesses, the 37 aids, the ten powers, the three vehicles, and conventional designations are equally unborn from the three realms, and sarvajñā has no abode. All is like a dream, an illusion, a water-moon, a plantain trunk, a mirage, or a deep mountain echo.]

As for where the vehicle abides: "Mind has no abode; the vehicle has no location. All dharmas have no abode. Like the dharma-realm, like empty space, like the unborn and the unceasing, the vehicle abides in non-abiding." As for who accomplishes the vehicle: "The vehicle is unproduced. No dharma gives rise to it. Self, person, lifespan are also unproduced, unobtainable, originally pure."

### Chapter 20: Neither Coming nor Going

Subhūti said to the Buddha: "The mahāyāna, World-Honored One, is the most honored among gods and humans. Like empty space covering immeasurable beings, the mahāyāna shelters incalculable asaṃkhyeya beings. The mahāyāna is not seen coming, not seen going, not seen abiding. It does not obtain past, future, or present. Its name equals the three times; therefore it is called the vehicle."

The Buddha said: "Just so. The mahāyāna is equal to the three times." He then taught that the desire realm is originally non-existent and without foundation, equal and undifferentiated, truthful and natural, lasting and firm, without the dharma of separation, without coming together or dispersing. When the kalpa-ending fire burns, all is shown to be impermanent, without anything lasting. Likewise form, sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness are originally non-existent. "What is not existent and not non-existent, that is the mahāyāna."

[The teaching extends through all categories: the dharma-realm, the Tathāgata, the inconceivable realm, the six pāramitās, the seven emptinesses, the 37 aids, the ten powers, the dharma of the nature, the eight noble paths, the fruits from stream-entry through buddhahood, gods, humans, and asuras, the bodhisattva's mind from initial aspiration through the seat of awakening, the adamantine wisdom, the thirty-two marks, the eight kinds of voice, the turning of the dharma wheel. All are "not existent and not non-existent," and therefore constitute the mahāyāna.]

### Chapter 21: The Vehicle Equal to Emptiness

The Buddha told Subhūti: "As you say, the vehicle equals emptiness. Like empty space, one cannot measure its extent in any direction; the Tathāgata's wisdom is likewise boundless. Like empty space, it has no long or short, no square or round, no increase or decrease; so too the vehicle. Like empty space, it has no five colors; so too the vehicle. Like empty space, it has no past, future, or present; so too the vehicle. Like empty space, none can increase or diminish it. Like empty space, it has no defilements, no aversion, no birth, no cessation, no abiding, no conceptualization. Like empty space, it has no good or evil, no speech or silence, no seeing, hearing, thinking, or knowing."

[The comparison continues through all conceivable attributes: the vehicle, like empty space, is without desire or freedom from desire, without anger or freedom from anger, without delusion or freedom from delusion; it does not combine with nor separate from the three realms; it has no initial stage or tenth stage; no stream-entry through buddhahood; no form or formlessness; no permanence or impermanence; no suffering or happiness; no self or non-self; no emptiness or non-emptiness; no stillness or non-stillness; no light or darkness; no attainment or non-attainment; no speech or speechlessness.]

"Empty space is equal; the vehicle is equal. Just as empty space shelters immeasurable beings, the vehicle shelters immeasurable beings, and yet beings are non-existent, empty space is non-existent, and the vehicle is non-existent. People are non-existent, the dharma-realm is non-existent, all dharmas are non-existent, therefore the mahāyāna shelters incalculable beings."

## Fascicle Nine

### Chapter 22: Pūrṇamaitrāyaṇīputra

Pūrṇamaitrāyaṇīputra asked the Buddha: "Subhūti was instructed to teach prajñāpāramitā, but he has been teaching about the mahāyāna instead." Subhūti asked the Buddha if he had erred.

The Buddha said: "You have not erred. In teaching the mahāyāna, you follow the teaching of prajñāpāramitā. All wholesome dharmas, the dharmas of the śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and buddhas, all arise from prajñāpāramitā." He explained that the mahāyāna, prajñāpāramitā, and the five other pāramitās are not different from one another. The ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, and all buddha-dharmas are equally not different. "Therefore, Subhūti, to teach the mahāyāna is to teach prajñāpāramitā."

### Chapter 23: Equal to the Three Times

Subhūti said: "A bodhisattva does not conceive of past, future, or present. Form and sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness are without limit; the bodhisattva is without limit. Form and the bodhisattva together are non-existent and unobtainable. All bodhisattvas are unobtainable. How then can we teach bodhisattvas prajñāpāramitā? The bodhisattva is called a bodhisattva, but this is merely a name. Just as a person says 'I have a self,' yet when investigated root and branch, there is nothing at all."

[Śāriputra and Subhūti then engage in an extended dialogue. Subhūti explains that in the past, future, and present, bodhisattvas cannot be apprehended because beings are empty, beings are illusory, form is non-existent, form is empty, the six pāramitās are empty, the seven emptinesses are empty, the 37 aids are empty, the ten powers are empty, the three vehicles are empty, sarvajñā is empty. Since form has no boundary, and consciousness has no boundary, the bodhisattva has no boundary. All designations are merely names. Names are empty. What is called a bodhisattva is a conventional designation arising from ignorance.]

### Chapter 24: Contemplating Practice

Subhūti told Śāriputra: "What is called prajñāpāramitā means the complete absence of delight. Not delighting in the aggregates, not delighting in the six pāramitās, not delighting in the seven emptinesses, the 37 aids, the ten powers, the eighteen unique dharmas, or even sarvajñā."

[The text then discusses the non-arising of all dharmas: "Form through the seven emptinesses is empty; what is already empty is not form, therefore form has no arising, and not-form is not prajñāpāramitā." The teaching explores conventional versus ultimate truth: "There is attainment and there is the path, but truly there is no duality. Stream-entry, once-return, non-return, arhatship, and pratyekabuddhahood are all spoken through worldly convention. Investigating their root and end, there is no attainment, no path, no three vehicles."

Subhūti explained worldly giving versus transcendent giving: "Worldly dāna-pāramitā involves giving with three conceptualizations: self-conception, other-conception, and conception of reward. Transcendent dāna-pāramitā involves giving without apprehending self, without perceiving a recipient, without expecting reward." Likewise for all six pāramitās, worldly practice involves conceptualization, while transcendent practice is free from all conceptualization.]

When Subhūti finished teaching prajñāpāramitā, the trichiliocosm trembled in six ways. The Buddha smiled. Subhūti asked the reason. The Buddha said: "Just as I now teach prajñāpāramitā in this Sahā world, the buddhas in all ten directions are also teaching prajñāpāramitā." At that moment twelve nayutas of gods and humans attained the patient endurance of the non-arising of dharmas, and innumerable beings aroused the aspiration for anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

## Fascicle Ten

### Chapter 25: Questions

The gods of the trichiliocosm, from the realm of Śakra through the pure abodes, assembled in great numbers. Śakra asked Subhūti: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva abide in prajñāpāramitā?"

Subhūti said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva arousing the mind of sarvajñā does not conceive of form as permanent or impermanent, as suffering or pleasant, as self or non-self, as diseased or healthy, as bound or free. Neither does one conceive thus of sensation, perception, formations, consciousness, the sense bases, the elements, or the twelve links. A bodhisattva practices dāna-pāramitā without attachment, and likewise śīla, kṣānti, vīrya, dhyāna, and prajñā-pāramitā."

Subhūti told Śakra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva abides in prajñāpāramitā by understanding that form is empty and therefore the bodhisattva is empty, that consciousness is empty and therefore the bodhisattva is empty, that the six sense bases are empty, the twelve links are empty, the six pāramitās are empty, the seven emptinesses are empty, the 37 aids are empty, the ten powers are empty, the three vehicles are empty, and sarvajñā is empty. In each case, emptiness and the bodhisattva are not two and not multiple."

Subhūti then told Śakra: "A bodhisattva practicing prajñāpāramitā should not abide in form, not abide in sensation, perception, formations, or consciousness; not abide in permanence or impermanence, suffering or pleasure, self or non-self, emptiness or non-emptiness; not abide in any of the four fruits, the pratyekabuddha stage, or the buddha stage; not abide in the four dhyānas, the five supernatural powers, the five eyes, the thirty-two marks, the purification of buddha-fields, or the teaching of sentient beings." The bodhisattva should not even conceive: "I shall attain the five supernatural powers," or "I shall see the buddhas," or "I shall purify my buddha-land."

Śāriputra thought: "How does the bodhisattva practice prajñāpāramitā?"

Subhūti, knowing his thought, asked: "Where does the Tathāgata abide?"

Śāriputra said: "The Tathāgata abides nowhere and not-nowhere. The Buddha has no mind and no form. He does not abide in form, sensation, perception, formations, consciousness, the conditioned or unconditioned, emptiness, the 37 aids, or sarvajñā."

Subhūti said: "Just so. As the Tathāgata abides in non-abiding, so should the bodhisattva study prajñāpāramitā and abide in non-abiding."

The gods were perplexed, finding Subhūti's words difficult to understand. Subhūti told them: "There is not a single syllable that can be spoken, not a single thing that can be distinguished, and no one who hears. Why? Prajñāpāramitā is without letters or words. Why? The Tathāgata is without letters. Suppose the Tathāgata conjured a transformation-person, and that transformation-person conjured four assemblies and taught them the dharma. Would there be a speaker or a listener?" "No." "Just so. All dharmas are also transformations. There is neither speaker nor listener."

### Chapter 26: The Dharma Teacher Is Like an Illusion

The gods wondered how to listen to Subhūti's teaching. Subhūti told them: "The dharma teacher is like an illusion. The listener is like a transformation. Neither attains realization through this."

The gods asked: "Are persons like illusions and dharma teachers like transformations?"

Subhūti said: "Just so. Persons are like illusions; dharma teachers are like illusions. Self is like a dream; form is like a dream. Sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness are like dreams. The sense bases, the pāramitās, the emptinesses, the 37 aids, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the eighteen unique dharmas, stream-entry through buddhahood, all are like illusions, like dreams."

The gods asked: "Is even the buddha-path like an illusion and a dream?"

Subhūti said: "Even nirvāṇa is like an illusion and a dream."

The gods asked: "Nirvāṇa too?"

Subhūti said: "Even if nirvāṇa were the most honored of all dharmas, I would still call it like an illusion, like a dream. Why? Illusion, dream, and nirvāṇa are not two and not multiple. They are empty and without substance."

Śāriputra, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, Mahākauṣṭhila, Mahākātyāyana, Pūrṇamaitrāyaṇīputra, Mahākāśyapa, and innumerable bodhisattvas asked Subhūti: "This prajñāpāramitā is extremely profound. Who is able to receive it?"

Subhūti said: "The non-retrogressing bodhisattva-mahāsattva can receive this profound practice. Arhats who have seen the truth, whose aspirations are complete, who in past lives have served innumerable buddhas and planted wholesome roots, protected by good teachers, can receive this profound prajñāpāramitā." He explained that such persons understand emptiness without conceptualizing emptiness, understand form as empty without conceptualizing it, and understand all dharmas as unborn and unceasing without conceptualizing this.

### Chapter 27: Raining Dharma Treasures

Śakra thought: "Subhūti's teaching rains dharma treasures upon the trichiliocosm. I should create a rain of flowers to honor the Buddha, the bodhisattvas, the saṅgha, and Subhūti." Śakra and all the gods of the trichiliocosm scattered divine flowers upon the assembly. The flowers spread across two thousand buddha-fields, forming in the sky a magnificent multi-storied palace.

Subhūti thought: "In all my visits to the heavens, I have never seen such flowers. These are mind-created flowers, not grown from trees."

Śakra said: "These flowers have no origin. They are not from mind-trees."

Subhūti said: "If they have no origin, then they are not flowers."

Śakra said: "Just as the flowers have no origin, form has no origin, and sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness have no origin."

Subhūti said: "These flowers indeed have no origin. Form is unborn; what is unborn has no form. Consciousness is unborn; what is unborn has no consciousness. The eye is unborn; what is unborn has no eye. Dāna-pāramitā is unborn; what is unborn has no giving. Prajñāpāramitā is unborn; what is unborn has no prajñāpāramitā. The seven emptinesses, the 37 aids, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, the eighteen unique dharmas are unborn. Stream-entry through buddhahood and sarvajñā are unborn; what is unborn has no sarvajñā."

The Buddha told Śakra: "Just so. Subhūti's entry into wisdom is extremely profound. His teaching is without contention and without confusion."

Subhūti told Śakra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who studies thus does not study form, because form is not perceived. One does not study the pāramitās, the emptinesses, the 37 aids, the ten powers, or sarvajñā, because none of these are perceived."

Śakra asked: "Why are none perceived?"

Subhūti said: "Because form is empty, consciousness is empty, and all dharmas up to sarvajñā are empty. If one studies emptiness, that is not unity; therefore form through sarvajñā being empty, there is no duality. One who can study the buddha's sarvajñā studies incalculable dharmas. Having studied, one does not study form as increasing or decreasing, and likewise does not study sarvajñā as increasing or decreasing. Having thus studied the five aggregates without increase or decrease, one studies sarvajñā. Having studied sarvajñā without increase or decrease, one does not receive form and loses nothing; does not receive sarvajñā and loses nothing."

Śāriputra told Śakra: "One should seek prajñāpāramitā from the Venerable Subhūti."

Śakra asked Subhūti: "By whose power do you teach?"

Subhūti said: "By the power of the Tathāgata."

Śakra said: "But all dharmas have no establishment. How can the Tathāgata establish anything?"

Subhūti said: "Just so. The Tathāgata abides nowhere. Prajñāpāramitā has no form and no different-from-form. It has no sensation, perception, formations, or consciousness, and nothing different from these. Prajñāpāramitā, the Tathāgata, and sarvajñā: none has form, none has any dharma of form. All dharmas, within prajñāpāramitā, are without coming together and without dispersing, without form and without perception, unreceivable and ungraspable, being of one characteristic, the characteristic of non-existence."

Śakra said: "This is the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's mahā-prajñāpāramitā. It is without differentiation and without limit. Bodhisattva-mahāsattvas should study it. Through it, the three vehicles arise. Through it, the bodhisattva attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

Subhūti said: "Just so. Prajñāpāramitā is without limit, because form is without limit, consciousness is without limit, and sarvajñā is without limit. Like empty space, which has no boundary, form has no boundary, and prajñāpāramitā has no boundary. Like empty space, which has no bottom, sarvajñā has no bottom, and prajñāpāramitā has no bottom."

Śakra asked: "Why is prajñāpāramitā bottomless?"

Subhūti said: "Because sarvajñā is bottomless. Because dharmas are bottomless. Because the dharma-realm is bottomless. Because what has no origin is bottomless. Because beings are bottomless."

Śakra asked: "Why are beings bottomless?"

Subhūti said: "Where in what dharma do you find a being called a bodhisattva-mahāsattva?"

Śakra said: "There is no dharma and no teaching. The name 'person' comes adventitiously, utterly non-existent and without original form, merely a conventional designation."

Subhūti said: "Just so. Since prajñāpāramitā does not proclaim the existence of persons, how could there be a bottom? Even if the Tathāgata, with a lifespan as long as the sands of the Ganges, were to speak of persons throughout all those kalpas, would any person be born or cease to be?"

Śakra said: "No. Persons are originally pure."

Subhūti said: "Therefore persons are bottomless, and prajñāpāramitā is bottomless. A bodhisattva should understand thus, and practice prajñāpāramitā accordingly."
