# 大乘淨土讚
## Hymn of Praise to the Mahāyāna Pure Land

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2828

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

> The Dharma mirror faces the void, illuminating mind, penetrating throughout;
> The five colors appear and appear; when mind is pure, wondrous perception arises.
> The dharmadhātu too is penetrated; the mind-gem constantly self-purifies;
> Bodily light illuminates the ten directions.
>
> The ultimate mind has no place to dwell;
> In liberation attained, clear contemplation;
> Thought is like no-thought.
> Great sound, yet soundless—
> Know that there is no gate;
> The wisdom mirror slays delusion.
>
> Through empty purity, the offspring of awakening arises;
> Upon awakening, there is no object of grasping;
> In sitting and lying down, all dissolves within emptiness.
> Transcending the realm within of humans and devas,
> Briefly arriving at the other shore, standing firm,
> Washing away the mud within the mind.
>
> Pure, undefiled by dust and stain—
> May one thereby realize bodhi.
> The wisdom mirror harbors no spiritual darkness;
> The wise constantly use it, and it illuminates.
> Though the toil of dust and defilements is severed,
> The treasure-store naturally brightens.
>
> In the pond, golden-sand waters are refined;
> Within them flows the Dharma-nature;
> Flowers open, and the transformation-born child arises.
> Speaking of my original root and cause:
> Dwelling in contemplation, constantly observing—
> The Samādhi of the Precious King is true.
>
> Circling through the count of the Tripiṭaka,
> Unable to remove the mud within the mind—
> There are those who single-mindedly recite the Buddha's name.
> Reciting the Buddha, one enters deep dhyāna;
> In the early night, sitting with upright mind,
> The Western Pure Land appears before one's eyes.
>
> In recitation, one knows no-recitation;
> No-recitation is just suchness itself.
> Contemplate the meaning within this:
> This is called the exceptional Dharma-nature.
> The Pure Land is within the mind;
> Foolish people seek it outwardly.
>
> Within the depths there is a precious mirror—
> Not recognizing it, one's whole life is wasted.
> All Buddhas are within the mind;
> Seeking thus outwardly, one cannot find them.
> What a wondrous, rare thing this is!
> With urgent hands, diligently cultivate it at once.
>
> All people have a precious mirror;
> Foolish people do not know how to polish it.
> Unable to turn their hands to behold its reflection,
> Dust and defilements only multiply.
> Every household has a precious mirror;
> The wise person knows how to rub and polish it.
>
> Diligently, diligently, turn to illuminate oneself;
> Let dust and defilements not come to pass.
> The exceptional mind's name pervades throughout;
> Self-nature originally awakens to principle.
> Cultivating toward it, recite the Buddha's name—
> This is immediately entry into the unborn.
>
> Fragments not yet refined into gold ore—
> Within the ore, the gold does not appear.
> One who knows can melt and smelt it;
> True gold is seen within the interior.
> Buddha-thought empty—no-thought;
> True Suchness: quiescent, unspeaking.
>
> The mouth speaks textual teachings;
> This world's practice is deluded meditation.
> Nirvāṇa ultimately conceals the Dharma;
> The secret is not transmitted between persons.
> The penetrating mind constantly uses itself;
> With dignity, it is suited to liberate those with affinity.
>
> Even the Three Vehicles do not recognize this;
> Outsiders have never heard of it.
> The Lesser Vehicle harbors hatred and much slander.
> The vow: let it not be transmitted and spread abroad.
> On the Way, even the worthy among bridge-builders who wish to transmit it by their own hands—
> On the Way, unwavering: worthy ones, children and mothers alike, do not transmit it to one another.

---

**Translator's note:** The final two lines contain the rare character 𨕱, for which no reliable gloss is established. The translation above renders these lines according to context: a closing colophon warning against indiscriminate transmission, common in esoteric Buddhist texts. The preceding verse (誓願莫流傳, "The vow: let it not be transmitted and spread abroad") supports this reading. Some passages in the body of the text also appear textually corrupt or unusual, and have been rendered as faithfully as the Chinese permits.
