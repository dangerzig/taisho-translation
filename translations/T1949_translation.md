# Samadhi Ritual for Requesting Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva's Dharani for Subduing Poisons (請觀世音菩薩消伏毒害陀羅尼三昧儀)

## Taisho T1949

**Compiled by:** Zunshi (遵式 Zūnshì, 964-1032), Song dynasty
**Fascicles:** 1

*Note: This is a Tiantai (天台 Tiāntái) liturgical manual for the repentance and meditation ritual based on the Sutra of Requesting Avalokitesvara's Dharani for Subduing Poisons (T1043). The compiler Zunshi was a prominent Tiantai master known for systematizing repentance rituals. The text organizes the practice into ten steps following the precedent of Zhiyi's Mohe zhiguan (摩訶止觀) and the Guoqing Temple liturgical collection (百錄 bǎilù, "Hundred Records"). Each step integrates both the outer ritual actions and inner contemplative insight, reflecting the Tiantai principle that practice must unite samatha (止 zhǐ, "calming") and vipasyana (觀 guān, "insight").*

---

## Part One: Account of the Origins (敘緣起第一)

This text has been revised, based on four reasons. First, when the original was first compiled at Guoqing Temple (國清寺 *Guóqīng sì*), it was done hastily at the Lingxu site for personal practice, and because the deadline was pressing, it was completed in haste. As a result, doctrinal and practical passages were mixed together, the balance of detailed and concise was not right, and it was difficult to understand at first reading. According to the *Hundred Records*, this was titled the "Repentance Method for Requesting Avalokitesvara" (請觀音懺法). This revision stays true to the original intent, using the sutra's title to distinguish it from other compilations and from the previously revised version.

Second, the Guoqing compilation contained much ornamental language, which has all been removed. Only the words of the sutra, its commentary, and the *Mohe zhiguan* (摩訶止觀) are used. Since these citations have an established basis, later people can develop right faith.

Third, a recently obtained copy of the Guoqing compilation showed that later careless students had appended the four repentance passages and phonetic glosses from the *Lotus Repentance* text after the confession prayers. Another copy had deleted all the ritual procedures and contemplation sections, retaining only the buddha-positions and confession prayers under the title "Avalokitesvara Liturgy." Yet another copy, apparently written in someone's dotage, had entire lines dropped and coarse annotations indistinguishable from the text, with private annotation-characters inserted between the lines in an attempt to differentiate. It is painful that such people, fond of the practice yet untrained in it, rashly added and subtracted, damaging the gate of sweet-dew nectar. The harm that follows from this is not slight. This revision aims to keep far from such errors.

Fourth, all the contemplation passages have been moved to follow the ritual actions, so that the mental visualization is fully prepared and practitioners do not waste any portion of their time. One should know that the three kinds of Mahayana repentance all take insight-contemplation as primary. The *Mohe zhiguan* says: "The root of contemplative wisdom must not be lacking." The *Supplementary Practice* commentary explains: "Without contemplative wisdom, it becomes merely fruitless asceticism." The *Perfection of Meditation* says: "In all Mahayana sutras, the repentance methods all take this contemplation as primary. If one departs from this contemplation, it cannot be called a Mahayana Vaipulya repentance." The *Supplementary Ritual Manual* says: "As for repentance methods, though the world may jointly practice the outer rituals, the mental visualization is often not thorough. Some roughly read the repentance texts without even being able to recite half of them fluently. Some push forward by force while completely lacking insight-contemplation. The spirit of diligent practice is missing, and the thread of entering the gate is broken." Therefore it is said: "Diligent practice of austerities is not the cause of nirvana." Our ancestral Great Physician has clearly admonished us in this regard.

The above four points correspond to the four *siddhanta* (modes of teaching). This concludes the account of the origins.

## Part Two: Explaining the Main Practice (明正意第二)

The *Commentary on the Requesting Avalokitesvara Sutra* follows earlier authorities in explaining the method through ten points: (1) adorning and purifying the ritual space, (2) bowing, (3) burning incense and scattering flowers, (4) focusing the mind and counting the breath, (5) preparing willow branches and pure water, (6) inviting the Three Jewels, (7) reciting the dharanis, (8) confessing and disclosing, (9) prostrations, and (10) seated meditation. The commentary merely explains the sutra without having time to elaborate the method in detail, so it provisionally follows the older arrangement. Since the *Hundred Records* directly explains the ritual procedure, its arrangement differs slightly from the older version. [Details of the slight reordering between the older version and the *Hundred Records* are discussed.]

These ten points each encompass both phenomena and principle, both correspond to spiritual response, both pervade the three kinds of action (body, speech, and mind), purify the three obstacles, and accord with the three virtues. The path of liberation is truly open and smooth. From the perspective of phenomena, these are the ten categories of ritual action. From the perspective of principle, there are only two: (1) the Middle Way right contemplation in accordance with the dharani, and (2) cultivating contemplation through each ritual action. Both phenomena and principle necessarily rely on the three kinds of action. When the three actions become the proper basis, principle responds without exception. Through response, karma is purified. When karma is purified, obstacles are removed. When obstacles are removed, one accords with virtue. When one accords with virtue, there are no phenomena or principle, no response or stimulus. All is quiescent. Who then discusses ten points?

[Extended discussion of how even adorning the ritual space already encompasses phenomena, principle, response, and the meeting of virtue, with scriptural citations from the sutra about the Buddha and bodhisattvas arriving the moment the method was taught.]

## Step One: Adorning the Ritual Space (莊嚴道場)

The *Hundred Records* says: One should adorn the ritual space, plaster the ground with fragrant mud, and hang banners and canopies. Place the Buddha image facing south, and the Avalokitesvara image separately facing east. The *Mohe zhiguan* says: In a pure place, set up the ritual space. Install images of Amitabha (彌陀 *Mítuó*), Avalokitesvara, and Mahasthamaprapta facing west. Set out willow branches and pure water. If convenient, smear the body with ash on either side, bathe in pure water, and wear new clean garments. The *Hundred Records* says: The practitioners should number ten or fewer, sitting on mats facing west. If the ground is damp, use low-legged platforms. One should make offerings with full effort each day. If one cannot manage this, the first day's offerings must not be omitted. The *Supplementary Practice* says: Though body and speech are sincere, one must supplement with merit. Making offerings daily is all the more superior. If one's resources are insufficient, the first day is the minimum. One must first assess one's own financial resources and give everything possible. The *Hundred Records* says: according to the *Mahakaruna Sutra*, three periods of seven days are used; according to this sutra, seven periods of seven days. In all cases, begin on an auspicious purification day.

## Step Two: The Method of Bowing (作禮法)

The *Hundred Records* says: Each person holds an incense burner, single-mindedly and wholeheartedly. Facing the west, prostrate with the five limbs touching the ground. Have someone with a clear and resonant voice lead the chanting. The *Supplementary Practice* says: For the five-limb prostration, one should properly place both knees forward, then both elbows, and finally touch the forehead to the ground, with heart and liver committed to the earth. Visualize the feet of the Buddha and extend one's hands to embrace them as if face to face.

The commentary says: The great sage constantly wishes to rescue and deliver beings, but those who are without wounds cannot absorb the medicine of poison. Therefore one must make the three kinds of action serve as the basis for response.

Single-mindedly bow to our root teacher, Sakyamuni, World-Honored One.

Single-mindedly bow to Amitayus, World-Honored One of the West.

Single-mindedly bow to the seven buddhas of the past, world-honored ones.

Single-mindedly bow to all the buddhas, world-honored ones of the ten directions.

Single-mindedly bow to the Dharani for Subduing Poisons, the Dharani for Breaking Evil Karmic Obstacles, and the Six-Character Chapter Dharani.

Single-mindedly bow to all the honored Dharma of the ten directions.

Single-mindedly bow to Avalokitesvara, Bodhisattva-Mahasattva.

Single-mindedly bow to Mahasthamaprapta, Bodhisattva-Mahasattva.

Single-mindedly bow to all the bodhisattva-mahasattvas of the ten directions.

Single-mindedly bow to Sariputra and the other worthy sages of the sravaka and pratyekabuddha assemblies.

## Step Three: Burning Incense and Scattering Flowers (燒香散華)

The *Mohe zhiguan* says: Burn incense and direct one's thoughts in offerings of the three kinds of action. The *Supplementary Practice* says: The three kinds of action in offering are: the body kneels upright, the mouth proclaims the chant, and the mind directs the visualization. The *Hundred Records* says: After bowing, burn incense and scatter flowers while speaking these words:

All in this assembly, each kneeling reverently, solemnly holding incense and flowers, make offerings according to the Dharma, offerings to the Three Jewels of the Dharma-realm in the ten directions.

> May this cloud of incense and flowers pervade the ten-direction realm,
> Making offerings to all the buddhas, the honored Dharma, and all the bodhisattvas,
> As well as the immeasurable sravaka assembly.
> May it raise a platform of radiance crossing beyond boundless realms.
> Throughout boundless buddha-lands, may it be received and used for buddha-work,
> Universally perfuming all sentient beings, so that all may arouse the aspiration for bodhi.

## Step Four: Focusing the Mind and Counting the Breath (繫念數息)

The *Hundred Records* says: Facing west, sit in full-lotus posture, focus the mind and count the breath, keeping the mind from scattering. Do not count wind, panting, or gasping. For the sake of all beings, sustain this for ten breath-recollections. The commentary says: Ten breaths constitute one recollection; one hundred breaths constitute ten recollections.

The *Perfection of Meditation* says: Upon first reaching the meditation seat, sit comfortably. For half-lotus, place the left foot on the right thigh and draw it close to the body so the toes are level with both thighs. For full-lotus, also place the right foot on the left thigh. Next, place the left palm on the right palm, resting them on the left thigh near the body at heart-level. Sit upright with the nose aligned with the navel. Face forward and level. Close the mouth with lips and teeth barely touching. Raise the tongue to the palate. Close the eyes just enough to shut out external light. Regarding the breath: if it makes sound, it is "wind." If it is blocked, it is "panting." If unblocked, it is "qi." If it is soundless, unblocked, neither coarse nor rough nor slippery, going in and out so finely as to seem barely present, nourishing the spirit in peaceful stability, it is called "breath" (息 *xī*). Guard against wind, settle panting, quiet qi, and tend the breath until it is settled.

The *Hundred Records* says: After completing the ten recollections, next recollect the buddhas of the ten directions, the seven world-honored buddhas, their form-bodies and the true-characteristic wondrous body, which is like empty space. One should also think compassionately of all sentient beings. When making this recollection, it is like a superior meditation. After dwelling in this recollection for an extended time, slowly and peacefully emerge from it.

## Step Five: Invocation (召請)

The commentary says: Invocation has three meanings: first, invoking for oneself; second, invoking for others; third, invoking to protect the true Dharma. [Extended discussion of how these three correspond to the three categories of bodhisattva precepts, and how each invocation involves body, speech, and mind.]

The *Hundred Records* says: One person tends the incense and fire. Each person kneels in turn to make the invocation. The *Supplementary Practice* says: The posture of kneeling should have three points elevated, the body curved with palms joined, eyes fixed on the golden countenance. In recent degenerate times, there is no proper kneeling posture at all. If the banner of pride is not broken, the ocean of karma is difficult to drain. One still indulges the defiled body; how can one expect the great Way? Looking at this clear admonition, earnest diligence is essential.

Single-mindedly I invoke and request: Homage to our root teacher, Sakyamuni, World-Honored One.

[Note: Other repentance manuals include praise-verses after the invocation. This one alone lacks a praise text. Therefore, following the *Sutra of Three Requests to Avalokitesvara*, the verses are recorded and added here:]

> The greatly compassionate one of great renown, who grants people auspicious peace,
> Constantly speaks auspicious phrases, rescuing those in the extremity of suffering.
> When beings hear his name, they are freed from suffering and attain liberation.
> He also roams through the hells, with great compassion taking on suffering for others.
> Sometimes he dwells among animals, taking animal form,
> Teaching with great wisdom, causing them to arouse the unsurpassed aspiration.
> Sometimes he dwells among the asuras, with gentle words taming their minds,
> Causing them to abandon the habit of arrogance and quickly reach the shore of the unconditioned.
> He manifests as a hungry ghost, with hands emitting fragrant, colored milk,
> For those tormented by hunger and thirst, giving them their fill.
> With a heart of great kindness and great compassion, he roams and plays in the five destinies,
> Constantly gathering wholesome wisdom, with unsurpassed, supreme skillful means,
> Universally teaching all beings, causing them to leave the suffering of birth and death,
> Always to abide in peace and joy, and reach the shore of great nirvana.

## Step Six: Preparing the Willow Branches and Pure Water (具楊枝淨水)

The sutra says: "The people of Vaisali then prepared willow branches and pure water and offered them to Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva." The *Mohe zhiguan* simply says to "set out willow branches" without mentioning offering them. Following the sutra, the practitioner should kneel and offer them, chanting:

I have now prepared the willow branches and pure water. We pray the Greatly Compassionate One will mercifully accept them.

## Step Seven: Reciting the Three Dharanis (誦三呪)

The practitioner should first understand the meaning of the dharanis. The commentary says: "Dharani" means "dharani-pledge," like the method of the ichneumon wasp. This is also called "dharani-art." This art's Dharma-power is given to ten kinds of practitioners. It secretly subdues the poisons that correspond to each. [Extended discussion of how the dharani embodies the true-characteristic right contemplation, is neither empty nor existent, blocks evil karma on both extremes, upholds the Middle Way good, and encompasses the three virtues.]

The *Mohe zhiguan* says: The Dharani for Subduing Poisons can break the obstacle of retribution (as the Vaisali people were restored to health). The Dharani for Breaking Evil Karmic Obstacles can break the obstacle of karma (as those who had broken pure conduct had their defilements cleansed). The Six-Character Chapter Dharani can break the obstacle of afflictions (purifying the roots of the three poisons, attaining buddhahood without doubt). [Note that the commentary assigns the first dharani to breaking afflictions and the six-character chapter to breaking retribution, each with scriptural evidence. Since each of the three dharanis can break all three obstacles, the assignments are interchangeable.]

Having briefly understood the essentials, one should assess one's own situation: among the ten kinds of practitioners, which position does one's mind correspond to? Which obstacle does one wish to eliminate? What result does one seek? In counteracting obstacles, one should address the heaviest first. When the heavy ones are destroyed, the lighter ones will naturally follow. Resolve to be diligent and do not doubt yourself. The true Dharma-seal of the buddhas of the three times is certainly not in vain.

Before reciting the dharanis, first kneel with palms joined, single-mindedly and wholeheartedly. Recite three times the names of the Three Jewels and Avalokitesvara:

Homage to the Buddha! Homage to the Dharma! Homage to the Sangha! Homage to Avalokitesvara, Bodhisattva-Mahasattva, greatly compassionate one of great renown, who rescues and protects those in suffering and calamity!

[The three dharanis follow, with their framing narrative passages from the sutra, as in T1043.]

## Step Eight: Confessing and Disclosing (披陳懺悔)

After the dharanis, one should recollect one's past offenses and give rise to great dread. There is no evil karma that does not bring painful retribution. The *Gate of Meditation* says: "The practitioner should reflect: if one's precepts are not pure, one must certainly repent." Therefore the sutra says: "In the Buddha-dharma there are two kinds of heroes: first, one whose nature is to never do evil; second, one who, having done wrong, can repent." To know one's transgression and repent is to be a hero.

"Repentance" (懺 *chàn*) means to confess and apologize to the Three Jewels and all sentient beings. "Contrition" (悔 *huǐ*) means to feel shame, reform one's faults, and seek mercy. "If this sin of mine can now be destroyed, then in the future, even at the cost of my life, I will never commit it again." As a monk once told the Buddha: "I would rather embrace a blazing great fire than ever damage or violate the pure precepts."

[Extended confession text for all practitioners, covering offenses of the three kinds of action and six sense-faculties, the three obstacles and three poisons, wandering through the six destinies, violating the pure precepts, and praying to Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva for illumination, purification, restoration of the precept-roots, and ultimate attainment of the unsurpassed Way.]

## Step Nine: Prostrations (禮拜)

The *Hundred Records* says: Single-mindedly bow to the Three Jewels invoked above. After bowing, circumambulate the ritual space in proper fashion, either three or seven times, while reciting:

Homage to the Buddha! Homage to the Dharma! Homage to the Sangha! Homage to our root teacher Sakyamuni Buddha! Homage to Amitayus Buddha! Homage to the seven buddhas of the past! Homage to all the buddhas of the ten directions! Homage to Avalokitesvara Bodhisattva! Homage to Mahasthamaprapta Bodhisattva! Homage to all the bodhisattva-mahasattvas of the ten directions!

I take refuge in the Buddha; may all beings embody the great Way and arouse the unsurpassed aspiration.

I take refuge in the Dharma; may all beings deeply enter the sutra treasury, with wisdom vast as the ocean.

I take refuge in the Sangha; may all beings guide the great assembly, with everything unobstructed. Homage to the sacred community.

## Step Ten: Sutra Recitation (誦經)

The *Hundred Records* says: One person should ascend a high seat and chant the *Sutra of Requesting Avalokitesvara*. The *Hundred Records* says: The above method is practiced during the morning and first watch of the night. At other times, one should do seated meditation and worship according to the regular method. This constitutes the pattern for one day and one night. From the second day through seven periods of seven days, do likewise. The *Supplementary Practice* says: "The remaining four sessions must follow the regular ritual; they cannot be abandoned." This concludes the main practice.

## Part Three: Encouragement to Practice (勸修第三)

The *Mohe zhiguan* asks: "The three preceding samadhis each have an encouragement to practice. Why does this one alone lack one?" The answer: "The six coverings are not the Way but serve as the path to liberation. If one were to further encourage practice, the point would be even more lost." Why then do we now establish an encouragement contrary to the *Mohe zhiguan*? Because the practice of following one's own intention discusses cultivation through four methods; what harm is there in encouraging goodness? The reason for not encouraging is already stated in the answer. Now, separately following the sutra's method: in the sutra, the Buddha himself encourages practice. How could this be a matter of human opinion?

[Extended citation of the sutra's own words of encouragement, enumerating the many benefits of the practice: seeing Avalokitesvara in one's present body, seeing Sakyamuni's incomparable form, seeing countless buddhas at each hair-tip, having eighty billion buddhas extend their hands, attaining the dharani of unforgetting, fulfilling all good wishes, being born before the buddhas and forever parting from suffering. Further enumeration of the afflictions, karmic obstacles, and retributive sufferings that the three dharanis can break: the people of Vaisali's six sensory ailments, famine, royal persecution, beasts, bandits, imprisonment, raksasas, poisons, weapons, difficult childbirth, 404 diseases, the ten evil actions, heavy past karma, even the story of Upasena the monk who had killed immeasurably in past lives yet attained liberation. Warnings against violating the spell, with promise that the three roots of poison will be severed and buddhahood attained without doubt.]

These praises by all the buddhas are spoken with compassionate voices, sincere and not false. May the reader read them again and reflect again, courageously arouse the aspiration for the Way, and with even a small portion of cutting effort, receive a great reward. One should, for each of the ten kinds of practitioners up through the stage of virtual enlightenment, understand how the sutra's power generates good and extinguishes evil. This is explained extensively in the sutra. This concludes the brief encouragement to practice.

The above follows the sutra's three divisions. Concluding verse of dedication:

> To the ocean of great compassion in the ten directions, the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha of worthy sages,
> To Avalokitesvara, protector of the world, bestower of fearlessness:
> I have compiled these auspicious phrases for the benefit of those who are beginning,
> And pray that blessings be bestowed, so that wherever they reach, they may always circulate.
