# 阿吒薄𤘽付囑呪
## The Dhāraṇī Entrusted by Aṭavaka

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1240

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

[Dhāraṇī]

> Namo Aṭavaka-makuṭāya | mahāyakṣanāthāya | maheśvarāya phaṭ | vimacitrarājāya phaṭ | naṭa-kuvarāya phaṭ | ha ha ha ha phaṭ phaṭ phaṭ haṃ | yeṭa yeṭa yeṭa yeṭa | nīlāmbarāya | gandharvāya | piśācāya svāhā |
>
> Namo bhagavate vajrakuṇḍalīya phaṭ | vajrī-vasidhāya phaṭ | bhagavate aṭavakāya phaṭ | vaiśravaṇa phaṭ | pāpa-ṭaṭāyāya phaṭ phaṭ | indrāyakṣarājāya phaṭ | virūḍhakāya phaṭ | virūpākṣa-yakṣanātha phaṭ | brahma phaṭ |
>
> Suru suru ghu ghu svāhā | mahāyakṣa svāhā |
>
> Ga ga ga ga ra ra phaṭ phaṭ phaṭ phaṭ | maṇibhadra phaṭ | nārāyaṇa phaṭ | mahākāla phaṭ | anottāya phaṭ | upananda phaṭ | sugīrāya phaṭ | nandana phaṭ | kṛṣṇa-pūtana phaṭ | yamarājāya phaṭ | mucalindāya phaṭ | surū-kumbhāṇḍa haṃ phaṭ | śrī-guṇāya phaṭ |
>
> [To subdue enemies:] phaṭ phaṭ phaṭ phaṭ |
>
> Yakṣa yakṣa suru suru suru suru svāhā | mahāyakṣa yakṣa suru suru suru suru svāhā |
>
> A a a a a a pa pa ca ca ṭha ṭha ṭha ṭha va va ra ra | vajra ha ṭa ṭa ṭa ṭa ye phaṭ | ṭa svāhā |
>
> [Tuṃ-śe] svāhā |
>
> Aṭa aṭa aha aha ha ha haṃ phaṭ | cakragupta vajra | bala bala bala bala | va va va va | jī jī jī jī | bindhu-graha siṃharāja | hūṃ hūṃ hūṃ hūṃ ha ha ha ha phaṭ ṭa | aṭavakāya svāhā |

This dhāraṇī is fierce and terrible. If you are not ritually pure, please do not recite it carelessly. Should you recite it with a mind of desire, I will dispatch the ghost king to take the golden wheel and shatter your head.

Dharma-relic verse for recitation:

> All dharmas arise from conditions;
> the Tathāgata has declared the cause.
> When those dharmas and their conditions cease,
> thus has the great Śramaṇa proclaimed.

---

**Translator's notes:** This brief text consists almost entirely of dhāraṇī syllables, which the Chinese preserves as phonetic transcriptions of Sanskrit (and possibly mixed-language) mantric speech. The reconstruction above renders identifiable deity names in standard Sanskrit — including Aṭavaka (the titular yakṣa-king of the forest), Maheśvara, Vimacitra, Vajrakuṇḍalī, the four heavenly kings Vaiśravaṇa/Virūḍhaka/Virūpākṣa, Maṇibhadra, Mahākāla, Upananda, Mucalinda, Yamarāja, and others — while preserving ambiguous syllables phonetically. The phrase 降怨 ("subdue enemies") and the sequence 吞攝 (*tuṃ-śe*) appear to be Chinese annotations or partially garbled phonemes without recoverable Sanskrit equivalents. The closing verse is the well-known *ye dharmā* stanza, used throughout Buddhist traditions as a Dharma-body relic (法身舍利, *dharma-kāya-śarīra*) for inscription in stupas and protective ritual.
