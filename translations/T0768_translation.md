# 三慧經

## The Sūtra of Three Wisdoms

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0768

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

The Buddha always wishes to find three kinds of people. First, those who have faith. Second, those who ask questions. Third, those who practice. Some people only have faith but do not rejoice; having done something, they consider it faith but do not wish to act further.

There are three ways that joy is lost. First, not practicing generosity. Second, not observing the precepts. Third, not settling the mind.

One must extinguish discursive thoughts in order to attain the Way. The essential point lies in non-thinking. When discursive thoughts are extinguished, form is also extinguished and consciousness is also extinguished. When the mind has thoughts, this constitutes the four grounds of attachment. These are: the thought that there should be thoughts, the thought that there should be no thoughts, the thought of not departing from thoughts, and the thought of departing from thoughts. If one does not transcend thoughts, one returns to them. "There should be thoughts" refers to thoughts of the Way. "There should be no thoughts" refers to the thought of the formless realm. "Not departing from thoughts" refers to not departing from the thought of walking in practice. "Departing from thoughts" refers to departing from the thought of birth and death. "Not transcending thoughts" refers to having no thought of the Way, not transcending the twelve gates. "Returning to them" refers to a person's continued birth and death, unable to gain liberation.

The body is like the earth. Good intentions are like grain. Evil intentions are like weeds. If the weeds are not removed, the grain will not mature. If a person does not remove evil intentions, one cannot attain the Way either. When a person has anger, it is like the earth producing thorns. Good intentions are like lightning: when it comes, there is brightness; when it goes, darkness returns. Wrong thoughts are like clouds covering the sun. When one does not see one's own evil intentions arising, one does not see the Way.

For the learner, there is suffering. For the non-learner, there is no suffering. The learner has suffering, just as a person who plants crops must first plow and remove the weeds, and then obtains an abundant harvest: this is the initial suffering. The non-learner has no suffering, just as land that is not plowed continually produces thorns and various noxious things on its own: this is the non-learner having no suffering.

In practicing the Way, there are four types. First, one can endure suffering. Second, one can restrain pleasure. Third, one who cannot endure suffering. Fourth, one who cannot restrain pleasure. One who can find joy in obtaining joy can practice the Way. One who can find suffering in obtaining suffering can practice the Way.

There are people who, upon encountering suffering while practicing the Way, become fearful of birth and death. Such people can practice the Way in this manner and should not be given pleasure.

There are people who can practice the Way upon obtaining pleasure; their minds are not in suffering. Such people should not be given suffering.

There are people who can practice the Way upon obtaining pleasure and can also practice the Way upon obtaining suffering. Such people may be given pleasure but should not be given suffering.

There are people who cannot practice the Way upon obtaining suffering and also cannot practice the Way upon obtaining pleasure. Such people should be given suffering and should not be given pleasure.

In all, there are four kinds of seeking. First, seeking on account of the body. Second, seeking on account of wishes. Third, seeking on account of delusion. Fourth, seeking on account of action. Wishing to preserve the body and have long life is seeking on account of the body. Wishing to obtain power, a wife, children, and precious treasures is seeking on account of wishes. Performing sacrifices and conducting rituals to obtain blessings from them is seeking on account of delusion. Acting contrary to the Dharma while wishing for household peace and attainment of the Way is seeking on account of action.

People have three things that cannot be preserved. First, a joyful mind. Second, wealth and treasures. Third, one's life. The body is both worthy of contemplation and not worthy of contemplation. Examining the body's various impurities makes it worthy of contemplation. When the mind falls into the five pleasures, it is not worthy of contemplation.

Goodness is both worthy of contemplation and not worthy of contemplation. The attainment of the Way-mind is worthy of contemplation. Not worthy of contemplation means that having attained the Way-mind, one should progressively advance further. Evil is both worthy of contemplation and not worthy of contemplation. Having committed a fault and repenting of it oneself is worthy of contemplation. When the mind arises and falls into evil, it is not worthy of contemplation. Abandoning right mindfulness for wrong views is confusion. Not distinguishing right from wrong is doubt. Evil has two aspects: the root and the fruit. What one does and practices is the root. Receiving the fruit of merit from one's actions is the fruit. Eliminating these, one attains the Way.

Greed and attachment mean craving again for what one has already obtained. Thinking of form is the highest degree of craving. Because this is not extinguished with a single thought, guarding against craving leads to attaining the Way. Craving and attachment lead to falling into birth and death. Having obtained something and then guarding it: this is craving and attachment.

"Joyful food" refers to delight. "Satisfying food" means that when one is mindful of the thirty-seven factors of awakening, one becomes satisfied. "Consciousness-food" means delight in the Dharma. Having followed feelings and sensations as the basis of seeking, then further thinking constitutes seeking through consciousness.

All worldly affairs belong to the body. All names and designations belong to the mind. All non-transgression belongs to the precepts. Apart from these, there is nothing else; this is the Way. One dharma can destroy the practice of the Way: lack of diligence. One dharma can destroy a person: miserly greed.

When external evil causes and conditions come toward a person, not accepting them is patience. When one's own body does evil, not expressing it is self-restraint. Do not dwell on the past. Do not wait for the future. In the present, one should sever attachments. What does not belong to a person should not cause worry. All that has intention constitutes a fetter.

Good intentions are also forgotten, and evil intentions are also forgotten. This is further due to three causes and conditions. First, not practicing mindfulness. Second, not frequently being mindful. Third, not focusing the mind single-pointedly. If these three things are not present, one will not forget.

From existence one can attain non-existence. From non-existence one cannot attain existence. This is the meaning of the thirty-seven factors of awakening. Regarding the mind of birth and death: the births and deaths are without number. The reason one can awaken is that one's forgetting of previous seeds is the cause. Originally one's intention was to sit in practice for ten days. One cannot complete ten days because the merit from past lives was insufficient. Those with much merit, wishing to sit in practice for ten days, accomplish it. The body does not wish to practice because of weakness, thinness, and exhaustion. The mind does not wish to practice because one is not mindful of death, decay, suffering, and emptiness.

Wisdom has four characteristics. First, upon hearing good words, one does not waver. Second, having heard, one receives and places them in the mind. Third, having placed them in the mind, one should reflect and contemplate. Fourth, having reflected and contemplated, one further asks questions, wishing to understand the meaning. The reverse of these is lesser wisdom.

There were five hundred people practicing the Way who had attained settled mind. Fearing they would lose it again, they found someone and asked that person to kill them. The person replied: "Killing practitioners of the Way would cause me to incur a grave transgression." The practitioners said: "It is like a person who has an enemy and wishes to kill that enemy. This is not the body; it is my enemy. Kill it for me. The Buddha said: 'One should kill the mind, not the body.'"

There was a practitioner who had attained settled mind. At that time a wildfire burned toward him, but his robes did not catch fire. People who saw this thought he was a ghost and struck at him with a blade, but the blade broke and could not penetrate. Because his mind was single-pointed, the fire did not burn him. Because he was pliant, the blade could not enter.

There was a practitioner who had attained settled mind. His disciple called him to eat, but he did not notice. The disciple then went up and pulled his arm, and the arm stretched to over a *zhàng* in length. The disciple was greatly frightened and tried to tie it up. When his mind attempted to tie it, he could not, so he then tried to untie it. The master came out of meditation, and his arm ached painfully. He asked his disciple, who told him what had happened. The master said: "If you had not untied it, you would have accidentally broken my arm." When a person attains settled mind, the body becomes as soft as cotton. It is the same in the mother's womb.

There are three causes and conditions by which one can perceive that a person has no understanding. First, the answer does not match the question. Second, the person cannot ask questions. Third, the person cannot speak.

The essential teaching of the sūtras is given through a parable: a person encounters a great flood and should take only the precious treasures and go. This is a metaphor for a person in the world who should take only good intentions and go.

Without mindfulness, one does not attain the Way. Why? Because one is not mindful of the causes and conditions of the Way. When there are causes and conditions of the Way, one can attain the Way. These are the six pāramitās, ānāpāna-smṛti, and the thirty-seven factors of awakening. These are the causes and conditions of the Way.

There are five causes and conditions for trustworthiness. First, faith in the Buddha. Second, faith in the Dharma. Third, faith in the precepts. Fourth, faith in the sūtras. Fifth, faith in a good teacher. Having faith in these five things, one attains the Way.

Speech has four methods. First, direct speech. Second, analytical speech. Third, questioning speech. Fourth, silence. Direct speech means that when a wise person falls into the Way's causes and conditions, one speaks directly. Analytical speech means that when what one has received or accepted is not precise, one should analyze it from beginning to end and explain it in detail. Questioning speech means that when a person's own mind considers something to be right, one asks a question based on the causes and conditions, and the person then knows for themselves. Silence means that what the Buddha did not teach, one also does not teach.

There are four causes and conditions for asking questions. First, general questions. Second, analytical questions. Third, counter-questions. Fourth, questions met with silence. All things in the world are impermanent, suffering, and empty. Practicing the Way brings peace. Having explained this, it constitutes a response; this is the general question. If a person asks about matters of the eye, do not respond about the ear; this is the analytical question. If a person brings a white object and says it is black, then hold up a black object and ask what they see; this is the counter-question. If a person comes and asks, "What is the Way like?", then respond by asking, "What is cold like?" If they ask, "What is the mind like?", respond with, "What is wind like?" If they ask, "What is the unconditioned like?", respond with, "What is space like?" This is the question met with silence.

There are four inversions. First, what is impermanent, people's minds take to be permanent. Second, what is suffering, people call pleasure. Third, all things are empty, but people take them to be real. Fourth, what is not-self, people cling to as having a self. People fall into these inversions: taking the impermanent as permanent, taking suffering as pleasure, taking the empty as real, taking not-self as self. When a person's mind is like this, one attains the Way.

Because people's minds fall into the four inversions, they reckon that there is a self. If one carefully examines and reckons, within the body there is nothing at all. Since there is nothing at all, one falls into emptiness. Having fallen into emptiness, it becomes non-self. Being already non-self, one falls into the unconditioned.

People have four kinds of delusion and are always attached to the four inversions. First, all things are impermanent, yet people take them as permanent. Second, everything under heaven is suffering, yet people take it as pleasure. Third, everything under heaven is empty, yet people take it as existent. Fourth, the body is not-self and cannot be preserved, yet people take it as self. Fifth, when the new moon first appears, people bow to it. Sixth, on the fifteenth day when it is full and bright, people turn and sit looking at it. Seventh, when a girl is small, people hold and carry her. Eighth, when a girl grows up, she cannot be seen.

There are four things that are noble and four that are base. First, the Way is noble, people are base. Second, precious jewels are noble, people are base. Third, official rank is noble, people are base. Fourth, wisdom is noble, ignorance is base.

Ānanda said: "If a person finds a good teacher, that is like obtaining half of buddhahood." The Buddha said: "If a person finds a good teacher, that is like attaining the entire Buddha Way. A good teacher is difficult to find!"

What is meant by virtue in the Way? Faith is the Way; restraining body, speech, and mind is virtue.

A person should have three kinds of acquaintances. First, those from a wealthy family. Second, those who serve the nobility. Third, the greatly honored. Through generosity, one is like a wealthy family. Through upholding precepts, one is like one who serves the nobility. Guarding the mind and being mindful of the Way constitutes being greatly honored.

There is inner livelihood and outer livelihood. Seeking money, wealth, and precious treasures is outer livelihood. Guarding the mind and being mindful of the Way is inner livelihood. If a person cannot subdue their own mind, yet wishes to subdue the minds of others, that is impossible. If one can subdue one's own mind, then the minds of all others can be subdued.

There is inner strength and outer strength, inner form and outer form, inner consciousness and outer consciousness. Being able to restrain evil thoughts is inner strength. Being able to perform deeds, lift heavy things, and act from anger is outer strength. Feeling, perception, mental formations, birth and death, and consciousness constitute inner form. Earth, water, fire, wind, and space constitute outer form. Mental intention is inner consciousness. What the eye sees is outer consciousness.

There are four things that are very difficult. First, meeting a person who has attained the Way is very difficult, meaning the twelve worthy sages. Second, hearing a sūtra that enters the heart is difficult, meaning being in the eight difficult circumstances. Third, contemplating things as they originally are is difficult, meaning falling into the four inversions. Fourth, practicing in accordance with the Dharma is difficult, meaning being unable to uphold the precepts.

There are five things that make seeking the Way very difficult. First, old age. Second, illness. Third, legal troubles. Fourth, bandits and thieves. Fifth, hunger and thirst. These are the five things that make seeking the Way very difficult.

There are five difficult things. First, encountering a time when a buddha is in the world is difficult. Second, hearing a sūtra is difficult. Third, finding a good teacher is difficult. Fourth, finding a good person is difficult. Fifth, being born as a human being is difficult.

There are five more difficult things. First, being poor yet able to practice generosity is difficult. Second, being powerful and noble yet able to practice patience is difficult. Third, facing a legal proceeding yet not deceiving anyone is difficult. Fourth, sharing a bed with a beautiful woman yet keeping one's mind undisturbed is difficult. Fifth, having power over a person's life yet not causing harm is difficult.

There are seven difficult things. First, receiving a sūtra and being able to ask questions about it is difficult. Second, hearing a sūtra and understanding its meaning is difficult. Third, conversing with a person of great knowledge and being able to hold one's own is difficult. Fourth, being able to teach oneself and also teach others is difficult. Fifth, bringing peace to oneself and also bringing peace to others is difficult. Sixth, settling one's own mind and also settling the minds of others is difficult. Seventh, never departing from the Dharma until one attains the Buddha Way is difficult.

There are eighteen things that are extremely difficult for people in the world. First, encountering a time when a buddha is in the world is difficult. Second, even if one encounters a buddha's time, being successfully born as a human being is difficult. Third, even if successfully born as a human, being born in the central land is difficult. Fourth, even if born in the central land, being born into a family of good lineage is difficult. Fifth, even if born into a family of good lineage, having all four limbs and six senses complete is difficult. Sixth, even if all four limbs and six senses are complete, having possessions is difficult. Seventh, even if one has possessions, finding a good teacher is difficult. Eighth, even if one finds a good teacher, having wisdom is difficult. Ninth, even if one has wisdom, having a careful and cautious heart is difficult. Tenth, even if one has a careful and cautious heart, being able to practice generosity is difficult. Eleventh, even if one can practice generosity, wishing to find a worthy and virtuous person is difficult. Twelfth, even if one finds a worthy and virtuous person, going to where that person is located is difficult. Thirteenth, even if one goes there, finding a suitable occasion is difficult. Fourteenth, even if one finds a suitable occasion, being able to listen and ask questions is difficult. Fifteenth, even if one can listen and ask questions, giving loyal and correct advice is difficult. Sixteenth, even if one is loyal and correct, understanding wisdom is difficult. Seventeenth, even if one attains understanding of wisdom, being able to receive a profound sūtra is difficult. Eighteenth, even if one can understand a profound sūtra, going further and deeper is still more difficult. These are the eighteen things that are extremely difficult for people in the world.

There are people in eight situations for whom the Buddha can do nothing. First, the mute. Second, the deaf. Third, those in the hells. Fourth, those among the hungry ghosts. Fifth, those among the animals. Sixth, those in border lands who do not know the meaning of the Dharma. Seventh, those born in the long-lived heavens, the twenty-eighth heaven. Eighth, those who have taken up the practice of non-diligence. These are the people in eight situations for whom the Buddha can do nothing.

There were five hundred people who each proclaimed: "I am good." The Buddha said: "If you wish to be truly good, then follow behind me." The people said: "Very well!" The Buddha then walked into fire. All five hundred stopped and stood still; none dared to follow. The Buddha said that truly good people are extremely difficult to find.

Someone asked the Buddha: "The Buddha teaches people to do good. What benefit does it bring?" The Buddha said: "The people of the world are troubled. That is why I teach them!"

Someone further said: "People have minds, and they should follow them as they please." The Buddha said: "It is because the people of the world follow their minds as they please that I remained for one hundred kalpas before attaining the Buddha Way."

There are seven aspects to the Way. First, the mind delights in generosity and does not desire anything else. Second, only wishing to hear the Dharma. Third, only faith. Fourth, only upholding precepts. Fifth, only wishing to practice. Sixth, wishing to cultivate wisdom. Seventh, only wishing to gain liberation.

When the Buddha was in the world, people attained liberation. In later ages, people only cultivate wisdom. In still later ages, people only wish to practice. In still later ages, people only uphold precepts. In still later ages, people only have faith. In still later ages, people only wish to hear. In still later ages, people only wish to give. But one should not only give without also hearing. One should not only hear without also having faith. One should not only have faith without also upholding precepts. One should not only uphold precepts without also practicing. One should not only practice without also cultivating wisdom. One should not only cultivate wisdom without also gaining liberation. These seven things should all be practiced together.

There are five ways in which things have deteriorated. First, in ancient times people had long lives; in the present age people have short lives. Second, in ancient times people were beautiful like peach blossoms; in the present age people are ugly. Third, in ancient times many people attained the Way; in the present age people cannot attain it. Fourth, in ancient times people were broadly learned and thoroughly understood the essentials of the sūtras; in the present age people cannot thoroughly understand them. Fifth, in ancient times people were peaceful; in the present age people have many illnesses and afflictions. These are the five ways the present age has deteriorated.

There was a long-lived practitioner of the Way, extremely wealthy with immeasurable possessions, who loved to practice generosity. Someone said: "Your generosity is excessively great!" The practitioner replied: "I have heard from the Buddha that the days a person spends coming and going in birth and death in this world are beyond counting. Now, the total of all I have given is not even enough to count as one coin per day. How can it be called excessive? The Buddha said that if a person obtained all the precious treasures under heaven, it would not compare to hearing a single word of the Buddha. Why? Because merely having great wealth cannot liberate one from the world."

In the mountains there is a pheasant-like bird with long tail feathers. When its feathers become caught on something, it dares not move away; loving its feathers and fearing they might be pulled out, it stands still until it is captured by the hunter. Its body is destroyed because of a single feather. When a person's mind is scattered among thoughts of affection, love, and possessions, one cannot escape suffering; this is because of greed and desire. People making their living are like bees making honey: they gather from many flowers, toiling laboriously day after day. When the honey is complete, people come and take it away. The bees do not even get to eat it themselves; they have only exhausted themselves. People run east and run west, seeking this and doing that, accumulating wealth and treasure with unspeakable toil. When their lives end, others obtain their wealth, while they themselves incur grave transgressions and suffer immeasurably. People in the world are like someone crossing a river in a mud boat: one should cross quickly, for the boat is about to break apart. The human body is like a mud boat and cannot last long. One should hasten to practice the Way.

Gold has four tests. First, burning. Second, grinding. Third, hammering. Fourth, smelting. By analogy, people also have four tests. First, provocation. Second, working together. Third, appearances. Fourth, trying to restrain what cannot be stopped.

To understand a person's character, there are four causes and conditions. First, living together. Second, living together for a long time. Third, conversing. Fourth, working together: through these one can know a person.

There are four causes and conditions by which one knows a person of the Way. First, upon hearing something evil or disturbing, one immediately transforms the thought. Second, one does not speak of others' faults. Third, one does not fall into arguments. Fourth, one can guard oneself. By these one knows a person of the Way who guards himself.

In this life, one receives blessings through four causes and conditions. First, having the right place. Second, having the right time. Third, having the right occupation. Fourth, having the right teacher. When a person has a dwelling place and obtains peace and ease as desired, this is having the right place. For instance, a person who is destined to become wealthy at thirty: if at fifteen he seeks wealth, he cannot obtain it, but at thirty he succeeds. This is having the right time. If a person's suitable trade is dealing in white pearls or other goods, and from this comes profit, this is having the right occupation. When one encounters a wise person who analyzes and explains the sūtras, and the heart is immediately opened to understanding, this is having the right teacher.

There were three brothers, each claiming to be the bravest without equal. They took turns keeping watch at night. The two elder brothers slept first, and the youngest sat alone. A creature called "ill-omen" came and bit his thigh. The youngest pointed his finger at it, and the creature grew larger. He struck it, and it grew even larger. Enraged, the man kicked and stamped on it, exhausting himself, but the creature only grew larger and would not stop. The man then gave up and rested. The first night passed, and he called the middle brother to rise. The creature bit the middle brother, who fought with it like the youngest had, and the creature grew to the size of the room. Exhausted, he too gave up and rested. The second night passed, and he called the eldest brother to sit up. The creature bit the eldest brother. The eldest brother gently stroked it with his hand. The creature arose, so the eldest brother, getting an idea, covered it with a basin. After a while it was completely subdued, and the creature went away out from under the basin. At dawn, the two younger brothers were so exhausted they could not get up. The eldest knew they had fought with the creature and asked: "Why can you not get up?" The two brothers were ashamed and dared not speak. The eldest said: "You fought with the creature and exhausted yourselves, did you not?" The brothers said: "Indeed!" The eldest said: "If the ill-omen creature comes again in the future, just cover it with a basin. Do not point at it!" The moral is: a foolish person, when provoked, becomes angry, and thereby incurs transgression, like the brothers fighting the creature and exhausting themselves. A wise person, seeing provocation coming, avoids it, and thereby obtains merit, like covering the ill-omen creature with a basin.

Long ago there was a practitioner of the Way who was teaching sūtras to a king. The king said: "When the Buddha was in the world, many people attained the Way. Now you teach the same sūtras of the Buddha, yet people do not attain the Way. Did the Buddha take the method of the Way away with him?" The practitioner said: "Consider that the most delicious thing under heaven is no more than grape wine. One cup is enough to become intoxicated. Now pour one cup of water into one cup of wine, and drinking it, one can no longer become intoxicated. When the Buddha was in the world and taught the sūtras, he knew people's temperaments, like a person who drinks one cup of wine and becomes intoxicated. Now we do not know this. The Buddha taught the sūtras knowing people's temperaments and gave medicine suited to the illness. That is why many people attained the Way."

There was a king who offered a meal to the bhikṣus. A deva came and pointed out to the king: "This person has attained arhatship. This person is a bodhisattva. This person has attained the first fruit of the Way. This person does not uphold the precepts." The king was fully aware of all this, yet kept his heart upright and equal, without any discriminating thoughts. The devas rejoiced greatly on his behalf.

There was a king who competed with another person, bidding ever higher to bathe the Buddha's image. The king kept raising his bid without stopping. The other person said: "I now offer all my possessions, my wife and children, and my own body as a servant, in order to bathe the Buddha." The king could not exceed this.

King Aśoka built eighty thousand stūpas. When he was about to die, five hundred bodhisattvas and arhats together guarded him, taking turns teaching him sūtras, and did not allow any of the royal consorts to come near or see him. This was in order to help the king be reborn in heaven.

There was a minor king who constantly waged war and attacked others. A great king reflected: "The ruin and evil of the body all come from craving and attachment. It is better for me to give my kingdom to him!" The great king abandoned his kingdom and left, becoming a layman in another country. A long time later he returned to his former kingdom. Someone reported this, and the king ordered his men to go and arrest and kill him. Just before death, the great king called to his son and entrusted him with a single message: "Never forget this: do not harbor resentment against your enemies. You must always maintain a compassionate heart!"

There was a king whose governance was unjust: he oppressed and wronged the people and took things unlawfully. Heaven therefore did not send rain in its proper season. A woman said: "Heaven does not send rain in its proper season because the king's governance is not upright and just." When the king heard this, he summoned the woman and ordered her to bring rain. She placed three vessels on the ground and made her vow: that the rain should fall into the middle vessel, and that it should begin from one end. And it was exactly as she had wished. The king asked: "What causes and conditions produced this?" The woman said: "It is because of my sincerity." The Buddha said: "When there is earth, then there are the ten thousand things. When a person has sincerity, then there is the Way."

There was a king who went out and saw a woman of incomparable beauty. The king wished to kill her husband and take the woman. A minister said: "You should not kill him; you should entertain him instead." The king then employed the husband and gave him a golden ring, saying: "If you lose this ring, I will have you killed!" The king then secretly summoned the wife and ordered her to steal the ring. Afterward, the king summoned the husband and asked where the ring was. The husband searched but could not find it, and the king ordered his ministers to execute him. He was given fine food and drink, but the husband was afraid and dared not eat. Someone said: "You are about to die; why not eat?" Just as the husband was about to bite into a fish, he found the ring inside the fish's belly. This was brought about by his sincerity.

There was a poor practitioner of the Way who borrowed one hundred thousand coins to use as trading capital. He first took thirty thousand and gave them in charity, then used the remaining money for trade. On the road, bandits robbed him. The king then gave him precious goods in compensation. There were large sacks and small sacks. The other people each took a large sack and left. The practitioner thought to himself: "My money was small; it is not fitting for me to take a large sack." So he took a small sack and left. Inside it were nothing but white pearls, which he sold for sixty million coins. Because of his sincerity and lack of greed, he obtained these precious treasures.

Long ago there was a king who summoned all the blind people in the land and had them observe an elephant in the elephant stable. One grasped the elephant's foot. One grasped the elephant's trunk. One grasped the elephant's ear. One grasped the elephant's tail. Afterward they asked each other: "What is an elephant like?" The one who grasped the foot said: "An elephant is large, like a pillar." The one who grasped the trunk said: "An elephant is like a rope." The one who grasped the ear said: "An elephant is like a winnowing fan." The one who grasped the tail said: "An elephant is like a large stick." They all argued among themselves, each blind person confident in his own perception. This is a metaphor for people who each see only a small part of what they have experienced, without comprehending the full Dharma, yet consider themselves to have a great understanding. It is just like this.

There was a king who staged a great musical performance and entertainment outside the city walls. All the people of the land went out to watch. In the city there was one family whose father was ill and could not walk. The family all helped support him, urging him to go, saying: "We will stop just outside the city." He could not make it to the tree below where they stopped, so he told the family: "Go and watch by yourselves. When you come back, carry me home." At that time, the lord Śakra Devānām Indra transformed himself into a practitioner of the Way and passed by the sick man. He called to the sick man: "Come follow me, and I can heal your illness." The man was overjoyed and rose to follow. Śakra took him up to heaven, to his palace. Seeing the vast quantity of gold, silver, and fine things, the man wished to ask for some. Someone said: "Do not ask for those things; ask for the vase." The sick man then went before Śakra and said: "I wish to leave. Would you please give me this vase?" Śakra gave it to him and said: "There is something inside. Make your wish." The sick man took it home, and his family gathered round and reached inside. From it came everything their hearts desired: gold, silver, and precious treasures, as much as they wished. They held a great feast for all their relatives, kin, and acquaintances, and celebrated together. After they were full from drinking, the man took the vase and tossed it in the air, saying: "I am grateful for your kindness in making me wealthy!" He kept tossing and kicking it until it fell and broke. After that, nothing he wished for could be obtained again.

In the world, the number of wise people is immeasurable, yet none surpasses Maitreya. Even Maitreya continues to study without tiring. How much more so for everyone else! The Buddha, having attained the Way, sat practicing ānāpāna-smṛti. The Buddha said: "From countless ages past, I gave without tiring, and thus I attained buddhahood." How will people of later ages attain the Buddha Way!

Having received the precepts, one no longer does evil. If faithless thoughts arise and one again violates the precepts, one falls into the hells. When King Yama questions such a person, the person answers: "I did no evil." King Yama then asks: "If you did no evil, why are you here?"

There was an honored elder who was humiliated and abused by a bandit. Someone said: "Why do you not kill him?" The elder said: "Because my soldiers and horses are not yet assembled." The other person said: "I will furnish soldiers and horses for you." The elder said: "No! My forces are now fully assembled." He waited twenty-some years, and the bandit died of illness. Someone said: "You did not kill him, and now he has died on his own!" The elder said: "My soldiers and horses were indeed fully assembled. What does this mean? The fool will enter the hells; that is what it means for the soldiers and horses to be fully assembled."

Someone asked: "What is the single thing whose understanding brings all ten thousand matters to completion?" The answer: "It is having no thought and no intention. When there is no thought, all ten thousand matters are naturally completed. When the mind has a hundred thoughts, all ten thousand matters are lost."

There was a practitioner who walked at night but had not yet attained the Way. Someone followed behind him. The follower had doubts and regrets. The practitioner raised his hand, and from the tips of his five fingers fire emerged. He then used a key to open a door. The follower then awakened and realized this was a practitioner of the Way.

It is said: "The sūtras speak of six kinds of decline, yet some people say there are seven. Is the decline of one's dwelling not a decline?" The Buddha addressed the person: "I will give you yet one more decline: delusion is the greatest decline. Why? A person speaks of matters of the body but instead talks about the dwelling. This is delusion."

There was a person who fell into the sea. Someone told him to drink up all the water so he could walk out. The person said: "I have already drunk, but more water keeps coming!" The affairs of the world are just like this: one thing after another, without end.

When people wish to see one another, there are four reasons. First, because that person is beautiful. Second, because of love from past lives. Third, because of the person's renown. Fourth, because one wishes to hear a profound sūtra. What does it mean to know what is hidden and what is concealed? What is concealed means not displaying one's faults.

In the world there are one thousand and eight paths. The Buddha knows them all. In past lives, he had already studied every one of them. Through these he did not attain the Way, and so he sought further knowledge.

Someone asked: "When a person speaks, does the sound arise first, or does the thought arise first?" The answer: "The thought arises first. Why? Because the thought perceives the sound, but the sound cannot perceive the thought."

There was a person who was crossing the sea carrying a pearl when the pearl was lost. The person then took a wooden bucket and began bailing water, pouring it onto the shore. The sea deity said: "When will you ever finish bailing out all this water?" The person said: "I will bail through birth and death without ceasing." The sea deity, recognizing the greatness of his resolve, brought the pearl out and returned it.
