# 佛說如來獨證自誓三昧經
## Sūtra of the Tathāgata's Self-Attestation Samādhi of Solitary Realization, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0623

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling in the land of Kauśāmbī, in the Great Grove, in a hall of open lattice-work. The name of the site where he abided was the Self-Attestation Samādhi of Solitary Realization. When first he attained buddhahood, his radiance was supremely bright. Naturally there appeared an auspicious jeweled lotus seat. The flower's pure fragrance illuminated all the ten directions. The flower had a thousand petals, and upon each petal sat a transformation bodhisattva, attending in orderly procession, suspended in space. Each rose from their position and prostrated with their five limbs upon the ground, circumambulating a thousand times. Standing reverently before him, they all raised a great voice and exclaimed that this was unprecedented: "O World-Honored One! In our own original lands, we have seen that transformation trees of auspicious wonder have appeared. When those trees first appeared, their radiance illuminated the buddha-lands of Ganges-sand-grain worlds. The trees emitted a great sound, clear, pure, melancholy, elegant, compassionate, and harmonious, entering deeply into the hearts of all who heard it. Those who heard it leapt with joy. They perfectly and equally gave rise to the practice of the great vehicle, the six perfections carried to their limit, the thirty-seven factors of the path, and the complete fulfillment of all the deeds of a Buddha."

At that time, in the Ganges-sand-grain worlds, each Tathāgata sent bodhisattvas to proclaim the Way's teaching and to illuminate the great vehicle. Each told his bodhisattvas: "All of you, from this Buddha's land, passing through buddha-lands as numerous as two times the sands of the Ganges, will reach a buddha-land called Sahā. Its Buddha is named Śākyamuni Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, who, by means of the Dharma and Vinaya, by supernatural powers, and by the Buddha's spoken teaching, accomplishes the deeds of a Buddha." Each Tathāgata took a thousand-petaled lotus flower in hand and gave it to his bodhisattvas, saying: "Take my name and convey immeasurable respect. We joyfully acknowledge this great being whose accomplishments are complete, whose aspiration is fulfilled, whose body of the Way is replete, who has descended into the five turbidities to bear the heavy burden for all beings, who has surpassed and leapt ahead to stand before Maitreya, who with vast compassion practices the six perfections to universally save all sentient beings, whose rising and resting are light and nimble, and whose Way and teaching surpass the ordinary. Now present this flower as a Dharma-offering, and may all beings universally gather at the place of the Way."

Those bodhisattvas, sustained by the Buddhas' awesome spiritual power, each vanished from their own lands, settled into serene and quiet contemplation, and entered the samādhi of observation. In a moment they all arrived at the world of endurance. Each rose from their seat and stood with dignity and reverence. They returned in homage to the holy teaching, prostrated with their five limbs upon the ground, withdrew and circumambulated seven times, then returned to their original positions. Their supernatural presence was lofty, their deportment solemn, their Dharma-robes properly arranged. They all raised a great voice and said before the Buddha: "Our World-Honored Ones, the Tathāgatas of our original lands, convey immeasurable respect. They joyfully acknowledge your accomplishments fulfilled, your aspiration achieved, your body of the Way replete. You have descended into the five turbidities to bear the heavy burden for all beings, surpassing and leaping ahead to stand before Maitreya, with vast compassion practicing the six perfections to universally save all sentient beings, your rising and resting light and nimble, your Way and teaching surpassing the ordinary. They now present these flowers as a Dharma-offering, that all may universally gather at the place of the Way."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, O great beings! We joyfully acknowledge the wisdom-teaching of all those Tathāgatas, untiring in samādhi, thoroughly penetrating. The Dharma-body is empty and pure; the light of wisdom is vast and complete, equal here and there. We respectfully appreciate what we have heard, and our delight is immeasurable." Then the Tathāgata Śākyamuni received the flowers in his hands and smiled with joy. Light came forth from his mouth and illuminated the ten directions, the immeasurable buddha-lands of Ganges-sand-grain worlds. He then took those flowers and scattered them equally upon the immeasurable Buddhas of the Ganges-sand-grain worlds. The immeasurable Buddhas of those worlds also emitted radiance that universally penetrated and pervaded the Ganges-sand-grain worlds. Each sentient being who received the Buddha's compassionate light attained the contemplation of wisdom and recalled their past lives. The light illuminated everywhere, reaching down to the hells. Those in the three evil destinies, the eight difficulties, the heavens, and the human realm all received the compassionate light and were liberated. Hundreds of thousands of beings became of one mind and universally gave rise to the aspiration for the supreme, true Way. The light then returned and circled the Buddha's body three times before disappearing at the crown of his head. At that time, the Buddhas of the Ganges-sand-grain worlds all became mutually visible. Through the awesome spiritual power of all the Buddhas, all sentient beings universally saw the Buddhas. When these transformations were completed, everything returned to its original state.

At that time, a bodhisattva in the assembly named Bhadravidyā spoke these verses of praise before the Buddha:

> Wondrous is the great sage's transformation; he compassionately pities all beings.
> From incalculable kalpas he has accumulated merit and virtuous practice.
> Each and every virtuous practice numbers in the hundreds of thousands;
> A hundred merits complete one mark. I bow to the honored one of the three realms.
> Wondrous is the great sage's transformation; his compassion and wisdom are without limit.
> The Way's teaching is pure and precious. The Śākya Teacher, Deva among devas!
> His great wisdom is lofty and supreme; his Dharma-ship ferries all beings.
> His holy wisdom, pure and immeasurable: I bow to the honored one without peer.
> Wondrous is the great sage's transformation; his compassionate light moistens Ganges-sand-grain worlds.
> Foolish darkness is forever removed; the confused are awakened, the turbid made pure.
> His gracious rain falls as the occasion requires; with skillful means he receives all people.
> The Dharma-bridge carries all across. I bow to the honored one of the three realms.

Then the Holy Teacher said to Bhadravidyā: "When the Buddhas smile in the Dharma, there are three reasons. What are these three? The wisdom of omniscience is profound and subtle, clearly penetrating the three times and reaching the source of all sentient beings. The three vehicles, each with their own fundamental practice, roots, and faith, are fully discerned. Or there are bodhisattvas whose aspiration lies in the great vow, who don the armor of great virtue and bear the heavy burden for all, who sever the paths of transmigration and become the bridge for the world, who exclusively uphold the six perfections without abandoning anything, who abide in the Way with increasing resolve and courageous vigor, who give without thought of self, who protect their practice through morality and patience, who are undisturbed in meditation, who are clear and pure in the light of wisdom, and who advance toward non-retrogression. Such beings, Bhadravidyā, the Buddha sees with his Buddha-eye, each and every one, and gives them their prediction. Not just one Buddha gives that person a prediction; all the Buddhas of the ten directions who are presently in the world give them their prediction. This is the first reason. Furthermore, Bhadravidyā! If there are bodhisattvas who have advanced toward irreversibility, who have accumulated the foundations of virtue and are endowed with holy wisdom, who have made offerings to immeasurable Buddhas of the Ganges-sand-grain worlds, receiving a prediction from each one, prediction upon prediction illuminating and purifying the buddha-lands, equally nourishing all sentient beings in a single practice; who invite the Buddhas, the sages, the great and humane ones; who in the four swift currents become the great Dharma-ship; who dry up the ocean of the six desires, block the twelve gates, enter the five destinies, purify the five eyes, concentrate the spirit in profound stillness, abide in the Tuṣita palace and gather all bodhisattvas, accomplished ones, and great beings; who purify the practices of the three realms and turn the wheel of non-retrogression: all the Buddhas of the ten directions who are presently in the world, before the eightfold assembly, all together praise such a bodhisattva, extolling his virtue and saying, 'This one shall soon descend and become a Buddha; all sentient beings of the ten directions shall universally obtain liberation.' This is the second reason. Furthermore, Bhadravidyā! If there is a bodhisattva who, in the Tuṣita palace, has completed his heavenly lifespan and is about to descend, he enters the ultimate and extensive samādhi. The devas of the pure abodes survey the three-thousand great-thousand lands, looking for a land broad and great where sentient beings are gentle and compliant, with kṣatriyas, brahmins, elders, and householders, determining in which city or town among the billions there is the Way and virtue, purity and honesty, benevolence, harmony, compassion, and wisdom. A wheel-turning holy king resides in India; they deliberate and gather to escort the descending spirit, with the devas as his retinue. He appears in the palace, attended by women and guards; he appears to study and exhaust all worldly things and to observe the four marks of impermanence; the devas of the pure abodes urge him to leave the household life. He enters the mountains and concentrates his efforts, sits beneath the bodhi tree, removes his hair, and takes a self-vow in the form of a bhikṣu. He practices the method of the former Buddhas, taking the Dharma as his teacher and the pure abodes as his witnesses. In one night he attains the three penetrations, subdues Māra's hosts, and completely fulfills all the deeds of a Buddha. The auspicious tree universally appears throughout the Ganges-sand-grain worlds of all the Buddhas. Each Tathāgata, in their own land before the eightfold assembly, praises the merit of the bodhisattva who sits beneath the tree. Each then sends the bodhisattvas of their land to present flowers, convey respect, and praise the great vehicle. In this way, Bhadravidyā, all the Buddhas of the ten directions who are presently in the world all know of it together and celebrate with all sentient beings who universally gather at the place of the Way. This is the third reason. These bodhisattvas who have come here are all persons connected to the Tathāgata from former causes. Because of this teaching of the Dharma, all shall attain the non-arising of dharmas, or advance to the stage of the pure youth, or to the completion of birth, or to irreversibility. Therefore the Tathāgata gives each one a clear and complete prediction. You should know, O great being! The Buddha does not smile without reason." When he spoke these words, seventy billion nayutas of bodhisattvas attained the stage of the pure youth; sixty billion nayutas of bodhisattvas attained the completion of birth; thirty billion nayutas of bodhisattvas attained irreversibility; hundreds of billions of bhikṣus attained arhatship; ninety billion nayutas of people attained the fruit of stream-entry; and the devas of the three realms all attained the Dharma-eye.

Then the Holy Teacher spoke in verse:

> The dharma-realm is entirely empty; the form-body is purely true.
> The dhāraṇī of the perfection of transcendence: samādhi is without cause.
> The Buddha-realm is also not empty; pure wisdom is also not existent.
> In compassion for the world, he shows a subtle smile: O great beings, you should swiftly receive it.

At that time, a bodhisattva in the assembly named Dṛṣṭiprabha-bhadra rose, straightened his robes, bared his right arm, placed his right knee on the ground, and knelt with palms joined before the Holy Teacher: "I wish to ask a question. May the Holy Teacher, with his immeasurable wisdom-light, illuminate what has not yet been heard." He replied: "Excellent! Ask whatever you wish. I shall now fully expound the great essentials for you."

The Bodhisattva Dṛṣṭiprabha-bhadra said: "What constitutes a great being's leaving home, complete realization of the Way, and attainment of omniscience?"

Then the Holy Teacher said: "Great being! Listen well and take it to heart. When the Buddhas leave the household life, there must be an initiating impulse. They sit in concentration, contemplating stillness, profoundly and subtly penetrating the subtle. When one concentrates in meditation upon the Way, what one responds to, what is moved, are the devas of the pure abodes: Brahmā, and the sovereign Śakra. Śakra then creates the four transformations of impermanence: the forms of old age, illness, and death. Because of this teaching and attestation, one leaves behind desire and the root of suffering, and the mind's contemplation of the Way becomes pure. One should dwell among mountains and marshes to concentrate on the practice of meditation. When this aspiration has just arisen, the Four Heavenly Kings and those below come to serve and attend. One goes to the bodhi tree and recalls the method of the former Buddhas who left the household life, taking the Dharma as one's teacher and the heavenly Brahmā as one's witness. The root of faith is inwardly firm. The habitual practice of the six perfections and the thirty-seven factors of the path completely fulfills all the deeds of a Buddha, and sudden self-awakening occurs. Śakra then descends and places a razor in one's hand. The bodhisattva holds his hair in his left hand and the razor in his right, and thinks: 'I cut away the filth of habitual suffering and plant the root of non-attachment. I sever the stream that does not retreat and open the source of nirvāṇa.' From the moment the aspiration was first aroused, he has always been able to leave the household. His firm resolve is without weariness, profoundly irreversible, his faith and realization complete, his intention and understanding clear. Before the razor even touches the hair, it falls of its own accord, and the uṣṇīṣa naturally appears. The future-buddha bodhisattva then thinks: 'The former Buddhas who left home and removed their hair must have had a kaṣāya Dharma-robe to restrain the mind.' Just as this thought arises, the devas of the pure abodes present a pure, fine, heavenly-silk, naturally-produced kaṣāya. The bodhisattva receives it and puts it on his body, the Dharma-robe properly arranged. Then the immeasurable Buddhas of the Ganges-sand-grain worlds all become mutually visible, and each sends a kaṣāya to give to the bodhisattva. The bodhisattva receives them all. Through the awesome spiritual power of all those Buddhas, all the kaṣāyas they sent combine into a single robe, called the Sarva-buddha-śīrṣa-pravara. This robe is now in the Brahmā heaven. He sat in concentration for six years in order to complete his former karmic connections. After six years, in the last watch of the night, the attestation of the precepts appeared. What is the attestation of the precepts? The aspiration to dwell in solitude among mountains and marshes, receiving the Dharma; the spirit true and the conduct far-reaching; keeping the precepts and practicing the Way without sparing one's life or clinging to the body; treating all things equally without seeking gain; guarding emptiness and practicing stillness while constantly contemplating the pure Dharma; the four immeasurables of loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity, and the four kindnesses; the complete and unlimited compassion that illuminates and liberates the four dhyānas; without flaw, without impurity, without cunning thought; not following the dharma of another person: within the Buddha-Dharma one attains the path of nirvāṇa. This is the true precept of the śramaṇa."

The Buddha said: "Great being! The precepts are without form and are not attached to the three realms. There is no consciousness, no I, no self, no person, no life, no mind, no name, no species, no transformation, no counting, no action, nothing from which they come, nothing to which they go, no form, no cessation, no body, no transgression, no mouth, no speech, no mind, no thought, no worldly affairs, no reckoning, no activity, no abiding, and also no precepts, no confusion, no object of thought, no destruction. This is called the precepts. The Buddha's precepts are without flaw or impurity, and there is also no one who keeps the precepts. Without anger, without hostility, settled and pure, one approaches the Way of transcending the world. This is what it means to keep the precepts: not receiving bodily form, not receiving a lifespan, and not delighting in the five destinies. One thoroughly understands people within the Buddha-Dharma: this is keeping the precepts. It is neither in the center nor at the periphery, neither attached nor turning. It is like the wind in space. This is the precept-keeping bodhisattva. When the great being first sat beneath the tree for the pure beginning of the precept-attestation, he abandoned desire and the root of suffering, relinquished the scattered mind, and the mind had no arising thought, no thought of immovability, no thought of pride, no thought of self, no thought of other, no thought of a middle, no thought of here and there, no thought of inside or outside, no thought of the Way, no thought of the worldly, no thought of cessation. The cessation of 'no thought' is also without; the 'without' of 'no thought' is also without thought; the 'without' of 'without no thought': all and the thought of all are exhausted. Thus the great being, beneath the tree, established the attestation and purified the original eighteen hundred precepts. When this count was completed, a diamond seat suddenly burst forth from the ground, and the palace of the sixth Māra shook greatly. The devas of the three realms could not remain in their original positions. They all descended together to the bodhi tree to serve and attend. Throughout the Ganges-sand-grain worlds a great voice suddenly sounded, and within that great voice it was said: 'The bodhisattva seated beneath the tree shall this night realize the attestation.' All sentient beings each heard and saw this great being. This is the bodhisattva's complete fulfillment of the precept-attestation and attainment of omniscience: the three penetrations, the six supernatural powers, the thirty-seven factors of the path, the eighteen exclusive qualities, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, all fully complete. The three-thousand worlds shook in six ways. Through the merit of subduing Māra and the radiance that universally illuminated the Ganges-sand-grain worlds, all sentient beings, receiving this compassionate light, were at peace in a single moment and universally gave rise to the aspiration for the supreme, true Way."

When the Buddha had spoken this, eight hundred bhikṣus attained arhatship; thirty thousand devas all attained the Dharma-eye; three thousand people attained the fruit of non-returning; and Bhadravidyā and the other great beings attained the non-arising of dharmas.

Then the Holy Teacher said to Bhadravidyā: "Once I was dwelling in the land of Kauśāmbī. At that time, the demon Māra's faction suddenly came with their retinue and entered the great assembly, wearing strange garments and mixing with the assembly, stirring up contention, slandering one another, and playing both sides. Bhikṣus caught in birth and death, who did not understand the body of the Way, became angry and resentful. Each left the group and scattered. The arhat true ones each went to the mountains and forests. At that time, Bhadravidyā, the three months of the summer retreat had passed and the year's end had arrived. On the fourteenth day of the Pravāraṇā, when the morning star appeared, I instructed only Ānanda to strike the gong and spread the grass mats, and only with Ānanda did I receive the rains-retreat. At that time, the devas of the pure abodes, from space, said to the Buddha: 'World-Honored One! The assembly of bhikṣus has scattered in all directions. Now the Buddha receives the rains alone. How solitary!' The Buddha said to the devas: 'When I first left the household life, you were my witnesses. When I went to the bodhi tree, you were again my witnesses. Now when I receive the rains, you are again my witnesses. Throughout Jambudvīpa and across three thousand worlds, the immeasurable Tathāgatas who have attained omniscience, from the time they attained buddhahood until they enter nirvāṇa, you serve as witnesses for all three, your attestation-body complete. You should know, O devas! The Tathāgatas' wisdom of the Way, their supernatural penetrations that walk alone through the three realms, still require witnesses. How much more so for all ordinary beings who practice the Way: can they be without a teacher?' He further told the pure abodes: 'In the age of the decline, many people aspire to purity and honesty, with firm resolve bright and clear, not delighting in worldly affairs, dwelling in seclusion among mountains and forests, truly possessing the firm aspiration to leave the household life. If they have no teacher or companions, they should follow the method of Mahākāśyapa's leaving the household life: departing from desire, the relinquishing of desire serves as the attestation; departing from the worldly, the relinquishing of the worldly serves as the attestation; departing from fame, the relinquishing of fame serves as the attestation; departing from form, the forgetting of form serves as the attestation; departing from inner and outer seeking, the relinquishing of seeking serves as the attestation. In this way, O devas, Mahākāśyapa, through these five attestations, cut his hair, put on the kaṣāya, and took the natural Dharma as his teacher. Purely he moved the Buddhas of the ten directions, seeking their compassion, and declared to himself: "I reverently look up to the devas of the three realms." Having thus declared, these three attestations were clear and complete, and he became a bhikṣu. He exclusively practiced the twelve ascetic practices (dhūta), each one firmly established without arising thought as the attestation.

"'At that time, Kāśyapa, beneath a tree, attained the five supernatural powers. Later, when he saw the Buddha, the sixth was also complete. In this way, O devas, bhikṣus in the age of the decline should carefully consider this dharma. They should not be arrogant or seek fame by disparaging others in hopes of receiving offerings. Nor should they, merely possessing this dharma, turn their backs on the community and refuse to have a teacher. This dharma is meant for when there is truly no community of bhikṣus who can serve as teachers; then one may reverently practice this dharma. If there is a Saṃgha, one should seek the Saṃgha as one's witness. The Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha, these Three Treasures, are equal to the Tathāgata. Therefore the Tathāgatas praise the bhikṣu Saṃgha that maintains the Vinaya as foremost. You should know, O devas, that within the bhikṣu Saṃgha there are all three vehicles.'" When he had spoken these words, the devas of the pure abodes and the eightfold assembly, having heard the sūtra, were filled with joy, bowed, and departed.
