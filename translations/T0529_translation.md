# 佛說阿鳩留經
## Sūtra of Akula, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0529

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada.

The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Long ago there was a merchant named Akula, who was exceedingly wealthy, with gold, silver, and precious treasures, and many servants and maids. Akula did not believe in a future life, or that doing good brings good results, or that doing evil brings evil results. Akula said: 'When a person dies, the earth of the body returns to the outer earth, the water of the body to the outer water, the fire of the body to the outer fire, the wind of the body to the outer wind. The body simply merges back into the elements. When a person dies, they will never be born again. Whatever good or evil a person does, whatever the mind thinks, the mouth speaks, or the body performs, all of it is abandoned. After death there is no rebirth.'

"At that time, Akula took goods worth tens of millions and set out together with five hundred merchants. Akula was the most wealthy and prominent, leading the merchant caravan. They had to traverse a perilous route through a waterless and grassless wasteland. Their provisions, food, and water were all exhausted. After one day, two days, they found no water or grass. After three days, four days, still no water or grass. The merchants were all terrified and said: 'Are we all going to starve to death in this desolate wasteland?' Each wept and cried out to their fathers, mothers, wives, and children. Akula sent riders out in all four directions to search for water and grass.

"Akula himself traveled on, and from afar saw a tree with green leaves and abundant fruit. He thought: 'Beneath this tree there must be water.' He went toward the tree and saw a man of incomparable beauty. From a distance the man saw Akula coming on horseback and came forward to greet him, saying: 'How wonderful that you have arrived! Whatever you seek, ask for it.' Akula, seeing the man beneath the tree, answered him, and was filled with joy, as if he had been given life anew.

"The man beneath the tree said: 'Where have you come from? Where are you going?'

"Akula said: 'I seek to save my life and the lives of five hundred people and our livestock.'

"The man beneath the tree said: 'What do you seek?'

"He said: 'I need water.'

"The man beneath the tree raised his right hand, and water flowed from his five fingertips like a spring, exceedingly fragrant and sweet. He said to Akula: 'Drink your fill.' After drinking, Akula asked for food. The man beneath the tree raised his right hand, and fine food appeared from his fingertips.

"After Akula had eaten his fill, he suddenly raised his voice and wept loudly. The man beneath the tree asked: 'Good sir, why do you weep?'

"Akula said: 'My companions, five hundred people and our livestock, have had nothing to eat or drink for three or four days. They are suffering extreme hunger, and their lives hang by a thread. That is why I weep!'

"The man beneath the tree said to Akula: 'Go and bring the five hundred people and the livestock here. I will provide for them all.'

"Akula immediately rode back and called to his companions: 'Do not worry any longer! I have found a place with food and drink. Follow me!' The companions were overjoyed and followed him to the tree. They all joined their palms and made obeisance to the man beneath the tree.

"The man beneath the tree asked: 'What do you need?'

"They all said: 'We are dying of hunger and thirst.'

"The man beneath the tree again raised his right hand, and from his five fingertips a great stream of water flowed like a spring. All the people, horses, and livestock drank. They then asked for food. The man beneath the tree again raised his right hand, and from his five fingertips appeared fine and delicious food. He gave it to them, and all five hundred people and their livestock were satisfied.

"The man beneath the tree then asked the five hundred people: 'Where are you all heading, and what do you seek?'

"The merchants said: 'We are all heading to the great ocean to seek precious treasures.'

"The man beneath the tree then said: 'If you want precious treasures, they can come from my hand.' He raised his right hand, and from his five fingertips appeared gold, silver, crystal, lapis lazuli, coral, amber, and white pearls. The people gathered them up, each taking as much as they could carry.

"The man beneath the tree said to Akula: 'Take this gold and silver home to your country, and use it to give to the poor. Those who need food, feed them. Those who need gold, silver, money, or clothing, give to them generously. Have the monks and practitioners recite blessings for me, so that I may receive the merit, so that my hands may continue to produce treasures, and so that I may soon be freed from this desolate wasteland.'

"Akula, hearing these words, was greatly astonished and afraid. He bowed with his head to the ground and asked: 'What kind of being are you? Are you a deva? A nāga? A ghostly spirit? A human?'

"The man beneath the tree said: 'I am neither a deva, nor a nāga, nor a ghostly spirit, nor a human. I am a powerful preta. In my past life, in my country, I was extremely poor. I always sat by the city gate. Though I was poor, my mind was pure and clean, and I loved and admired the śramaṇas and practitioners. Being poor, I could not give to others, but when I saw others give, I rejoiced on their behalf. At that time, after Kāśyapa Buddha had entered parinirvāṇa, bhikṣus came to me to beg for food. I answered the bhikṣus: "I have nothing." But I would point from afar into the city, saying: "Such-and-such a household is generous, and you can obtain food there. Such-and-such a household is not generous, and you cannot obtain food there." When the bhikṣus came back from begging, if I saw they had obtained something, I rejoiced. Further, after Kāśyapa Buddha had entered parinirvāṇa, the king of that country, named Kṛkin, erected a stūpa of the seven treasures for Kāśyapa Buddha. I would place my hand upon it and say: "May I receive the merit." Whenever the king offered fine things at the Buddha's stūpa, I would place my hand upon them and say: "May I receive the merit." But because of my poverty, I never observed the purificatory fast. I ate at irregular times and I drank wine. Therefore, after death, I became a powerful preta. Yet because in my past life I rejoiced at others' good deeds and placed my hand upon those objects, my five fingertips can now produce whatever is desired. But because in life I never observed the fast, I am made to be a preta in this place.'

"Akula then reflected: 'Before, I did not believe in a future life, or that doing good brings good results, or that doing evil brings evil results. Now I have seen it with my own eyes. It is truly the case that there is a future life and rebirth, and truly the case that doing good brings good results and doing evil brings evil results. From this day forward, when I return home, I shall earnestly do good. I shall give generously to all, satisfying whatever they seek. Whoever needs gold, silver, precious treasures, food, or clothing, I shall give it all, refusing no one.'

"Akula returned home and announced to all the people of his country: 'Whoever needs gold, silver, precious treasures, clothing, or food, ask for whatever you want and come take it.' In this way he gave without limit. Each day he fed eighty-four thousand practitioners. The water from washing the food alone flooded out through the gates, and people had to pole boats through it.

"Akula practiced goodness with all his heart. After death he was reborn in the second Trāyastriṃśa heaven as a heavenly being, with his seat four hundred and eighty li from Lord Śakra's throne.

"In that country there was a beggar woman named Pín, who with a wholesome mind offered a single bowl of rice gruel to the śramaṇa Mahākāśyapa. After death, this woman was also reborn in Trāyastriṃśa heaven as a heavenly being, seated beside Lord Śakra in the third seat, and she surpassed all the other devas in five respects. What are the five? First, longevity. Second, beauty. Third, happiness. Fourth, wisdom. Fifth, divine power. She surpassed all the other devas.

"Later, when the Buddha's mother passed away and was reborn in Trāyastriṃśa heaven, the Buddha ascended to heaven to teach the sūtras for his mother. When the teaching was finished, the Buddha's mother and innumerable devas all attained the fruit of stream-entry. The Buddha then saw Akula, who had given generously to people, seated far from Lord Śakra, four hundred and eighty li away. He also saw the beggar woman, who had offered rice gruel to the śramaṇa Mahākāśyapa, seated beside Lord Śakra in the third seat, surpassing all the other devas in five respects. The Buddha perceived and recognized all of this, and called from afar to Akula: 'O generous one! Come and see me.'

"Akula immediately came forward, bowed with his head to the ground, made obeisance to the Buddha, and said: 'I gave generously in great quantities, yet I am only an ordinary heavenly being. I have not attained stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, arhatship, pratyekabuddhahood, or buddhahood. Now I see this beggar woman, who offered only rice gruel to the śramaṇa Mahākāśyapa, and in this life she is seated beside Śakra in the third seat, surpassing all the other devas in five respects.'

"A person who offers even a small thing to Mahākāśyapa obtains such great merit, and thereby attains such wealth and eminence."

The devas then made obeisance to the Buddha and departed.
