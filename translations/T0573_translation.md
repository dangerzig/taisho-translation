# 差摩婆帝授記經
## Prediction Sūtra of Kṣemāvatī

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0573

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流支)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with a great assembly of twenty thousand bhikṣus. There were also many bodhisattvas: the Bodhisattva Maitreya, Mañjuśrī, and other great bodhisattva mahāsattvas.

At that time, at the early morning hour, the World-Honored One put on his robe and took his bowl. The Bodhisattva Maitreya accompanied him. Together they entered the great city of Rājagṛha to beg for food, and in due course arrived at the great palace of King Bimbisāra. Just as they were about to enter, they saw Kṣemāvatī, the great king's consort, upon the upper story. Seeing the World-Honored One, her mind grew even more pure. She descended from the story and spread a precious cloth called Paṭuṇa for the Tathāgata to sit upon. The Bodhisattva Maitreya sat on his sitting-cloth.

When the World-Honored One and the Bodhisattva Maitreya were both seated, Queen Kṣemāvatī, her body fully adorned with every ornament, bowed at the World-Honored One's feet and also paid respects to the Bodhisattva Maitreya. Then she sat properly before the World-Honored One with a respectful heart, wishing to hear the teaching. At that time the World-Honored One, seeing Queen Kṣemāvatī adorned with all her ornaments, desiring to benefit all beings and to bring them happiness, knowingly asked Queen Kṣemāvatī: "Kṣemāvatī! What tree is that? You are now wearing such fruits upon your body, foremost in radiance, dignified and wonderful."

Queen Kṣemāvatī answered in verse:

> "The foremost Great Man
> Knowingly asks me:
> 'What tree is that?
> You are wearing such fruit.'
> That tree is named Merit.
> In past lives I planted it.
> This fruit is the fruit of that tree;
> Today I partake of it thus.
> I have also planted that tree
> Among the śrāvakas of the Perfectly Awakened,
> Abiding firmly on the path of awakening,
> Seeking the ground of perfect awakening.
> The water of giving and precepts
> Has moistened that tree and made it grow.
> After that tree has grown,
> It bears this fruit of dignity.
> The power of patience and diligence
> Can further nourish that tree.
> After that tree has grown,
> It bears this stainless fruit.
> The flowers of meditation and wisdom
> Make that tree beautiful.
> I now partake of this fruit,
> As the World-Honored One can see.
> As groves of trees and their fruits
> All depend upon the mountain,
> With branches and leaves growing,
> Above and below all unmoved,
> Just so is that great tree
> Which I in past lives well planted,
> For the benefit of all beings,
> To attain the dharmas of the Buddhas.
> Of this tree of merit,
> What I have now is only its flower.
> For now I partake of it thus;
> The fruit is still yet to come.
> The foremost fruit of awakening,
> The unsurpassed awakening of a Buddha:
> Having cast off the inferior body of a woman,
> I shall surely obtain that fruit hereafter.
> I shall attain the body of a great man,
> Supreme among all beings,
> Reaching the far shore of all dharmas,
> With omniscient vision encompassing all.
> I shall liberate all beings
> From every kind of suffering and fear.
> All shall come to me,
> For I have compassion on all beings."

At that time the World-Honored One told Queen Kṣemāvatī: "Excellent! Excellent! Kṣemāvatī! You are able to benefit many people, bring peace and happiness to many, and have compassion for the world, enriching and bringing comfort to all the great assemblies of devas and humans."

Then Queen Kṣemāvatī, hearing the Buddha's praise, said to the World-Honored One: "World-Honored One! This body with its thirty-two marks of a great man and eighty refinements: through what causes and conditions does one obtain the ten powers of a Tathāgata, the four fearlessnesses, and the four kinds of unobstructed knowledge? And the eighteen dharmas unique to a Buddha, great kindness, great compassion, great joy, great equanimity, and the various immeasurable, unequaled, indescribable, countless Buddha-dharmas: from what do they arise?"

After Queen Kṣemāvatī had asked this, the Buddha spoke in verse:

> "I too am just the same:
> In past lives I planted that tree,
> For the benefit of all beings,
> Seeking the wisdom of perfect awakening.
> Practicing giving, precepts, patience,
> Diligence, meditation, and their cultivation,
> And at all times
> Constantly practicing meditation and wisdom.
> Toward all beings
> I never once raised an unwholesome thought.
> Always practicing the mind of equanimity,
> Today I have attained buddhahood.
> Always, toward the loved and the unloved,
> Toward friends and foes equally,
> Having attained the mind of equanimity,
> The tree of merit has grown.
> This body of mine is a great tree
> Gathering immeasurable merit.
> I have been a wheel-turning king,
> And also a lord of Śakra's heaven,
> And a great Lord Brahmā,
> With manifold abundance and happiness.
> The inconceivable Buddha-dharmas,
> Their marks and such, I have now obtained."

When Queen Kṣemāvatī heard of these meritorious virtues, she praised them: "Excellent! In the World-Honored One's words I find the greatest sympathetic joy." She then addressed the Tathāgata in verse:

> "As the Victor, supreme among humans,
> Has practiced in accordance,
> As from the beginning he has learned well,
> An ocean of boundless merit,
> So now I learn the Buddha's learning,
> The far shore of all merit.
> I shall leave behind the suffering of birth and death
> And become a sage such as this.
> In all the places of my past births,
> I have practiced generosity;
> May all that merit
> Lead to the Buddha's unequaled wisdom.
> In all past and present births,
> I have upheld the precepts;
> May all that merit
> Lead to the Tathāgata's wisdom.
> I have cultivated patience, diligence,
> Samādhi, and prajñā;
> May all such merits
> Bring to completion the Buddha-dharmas.
> All the good deeds
> I have performed with body, speech, and mind:
> May they lead to great awakening
> And the merits of seeking the Buddha's wisdom.
> I shall cast off the body of a woman
> And attain the supreme body of a man.
> Having attained the body of a man,
> In due course I shall attain the body of a Buddha.
> Having attained supreme awakening,
> I shall turn the unsurpassed Dharma wheel
> And liberate beings
> Imprisoned in the jail of birth and death."

At that time the World-Honored One, in response to Queen Kṣemāvatī's verse, praised her with his own verse:

> "Excellent! These words you speak.
> Excellent! Your mind's aspiration.
> You shall vanquish Māra
> And attain perfect awakening."

When Queen Kṣemāvatī received the World-Honored One's personal prediction, she was delighted. With supreme aspiration and resolute mind, she offered the World-Honored One abundant food of various kinds, and likewise offered food to the Bodhisattva Mahāsattva Maitreya.

At that time, when the World-Honored One had eaten and finished his meal, he set aside his bowl and washed his hands. Having washed his hands, he again taught the Dharma for Queen Kṣemāvatī. Having shown, taught, encouraged, and guided her, and having gladdened her, he gave the prediction: "Kṣemāvatī! In the future, after immeasurable kalpas, you shall become a Buddha named Guṇaratnaviśiṣṭa Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha. Your buddha-world shall be supremely pure, with no evil destinies and no suffering. It shall be delightful and joyful, adorned and wonderful, supremely pure, a dwelling place for bodhisattvas. Such a magnificent and pure buddha-world you shall attain."

When the Buddha taught this Dharma-gate, many thousands of upāsakas all generated the mind of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi. Many thousands of beings, humans, devas, and worldlings, were free from doubt regarding the Dharma and attained the mind of accordance.

When the Tathāgata had finished speaking, Kṣemāvatī, the Bodhisattva Maitreya, the entire assembly, and all the devas, humans, asuras, and gandharvas, hearing the World-Honored One speak, were all filled with great joy.
