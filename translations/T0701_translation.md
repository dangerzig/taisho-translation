# 佛說溫室洗浴眾僧經
## Sūtra of the Warm-Room Bathing of the Saṃgha, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0701

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Ānanda said: "I heard from the Buddha thus:

"At one time the Buddha was in the kingdom of Magadha, on Mount Indasālaguhā. Within the city of Rājagṛha there was a great elder, the son of the mango-grove woman, named Jīvaka. He was the great physician-king who healed all illnesses. From youth he loved learning, and his skill surpassed all. His wisdom penetrated the five classics, astronomy, and geography. All whom he treated were cured without fail; the dead were brought back to life, and the funeral carriage returned. His virtues were so many they cannot all be recounted. The eight kingdoms looked up to him, and all who saw him rejoiced.

"Thereupon Jīvaka, in the night, suddenly conceived a thought: 'Tomorrow morning I shall go to the Buddha and ask about my doubts.' At dawn, he instructed his entire household and retinue, and they solemnly proceeded to the Buddha's place. Arriving at the gate of the monastery, he saw the Buddha radiantly shining, his light illuminating heaven and earth. The fourfold assembly was seated, numbering thousands upon ten thousands, and the Buddha was teaching the Dharma as they listened with one mind and in stillness. Jīvaka and his retinue alighted from their carriage and went directly forward, bowed to the Buddha, and each sat to one side.

"The Buddha greeted him warmly: 'Welcome, physician-king! If you have something to ask, do not hesitate!'

"Jīvaka knelt for a long while and said to the Buddha: 'Although I have been born into the world, I am a rustic person who has followed the common stream and never performed meritorious deeds. I now wish to invite the Buddha and all the Saṃgha, the bodhisattvas and great beings, to enter the warm room and bathe. I pray that sentient beings may be forever pure through the long night, that filth and defilement may be removed, and that they may not encounter any affliction. I humbly ask the Buddha's holy will; may he not dismiss my wish!'

"The Buddha told the physician-king: 'Excellent, wondrous intention! You have healed the illnesses of many people, and all have been cured. Near and far, all depend upon you and rejoice. Now you further invite the Buddha and all the Saṃgha to enter the warm room to bathe, wishing that the medicinal remedies of the ten directions may heal illness and that bathing may remove defilement. The merit of this is immeasurable. Listen with one mind! I shall first explain for you the rewards of bathing the Saṃgha!'

"The Buddha told Jīvaka: 'The method of bathing requires seven things and removes seven diseases, and one obtains seven rewards. What are the seven things? First, fire; second, clean water; third, bathing powder; fourth, oil; fifth, pure ash; sixth, willow twigs; seventh, an inner garment. This is the method of bathing. What are the seven diseases removed? First, the four elements are stabilized; second, wind disease is removed; third, dampness and rheumatism are removed; fourth, cold is removed; fifth, heat is removed; sixth, filth and defilement are removed; seventh, the body becomes light and nimble, and the eyes become clear and bright. These are the seven diseases removed from the Saṃgha. Through such an offering, one obtains seven rewards. What are the seven rewards? First, the four elements are free from illness; wherever one is born, one is always at peace, robust and healthy, revered by all. Second, wherever one is born, one is pure and clean, with an upright and handsome face, unsoiled by water or dust, respected by all. Third, one's body is always fragrant, one's garments always clean; all who see one rejoice and none fail to show respect. Fourth, one's body is supple and radiant, with great authority and virtue; none fail to admire, and one stands alone without equal. Fifth, one has many attendants who brush away dust and defilement; one naturally receives blessings and always remembers one's past lives. Sixth, one's mouth and teeth are fragrant and fine, white and even; whatever one teaches, all follow respectfully. Seventh, wherever one is born, one naturally has garments, ornaments, and precious treasures; all who see one are filled with awe.'

"The Buddha told Jīvaka: 'The seven rewards of bathing the Saṃgha and the bodhisattvas are such as this. From this cause and condition, one may become a minister, or an emperor, or the Sun, Moon, or Four Heavenly Kings, or Śakra, or a wheel-turning holy king, or be born in the Brahmā heaven with merit beyond measure, or become a bodhisattva who generates the aspiration and upholds the ground of practice until one's work is complete and one's aspiration fulfilled, and one thereby attains buddhahood. Such are the causes and conditions of making offerings to the Saṃgha, an immeasurable field of merit that neither drought nor flood can damage.'

"Thereupon the World-Honored One further spoke in verse for Jīvaka:

> Observe the three realms:
> Devas and humans receive radiant blessings.
> The Way and its virtue are without limit;
> Listen carefully as I explain in order.
> When a person is born into the world,
> Upright and handsome, respected by all,
> Their nature always pure:
> This is due to bathing the Saṃgha.
> If born the child of a great minister,
> Wealthy, always at peace,
> Courageous and among the worthiest,
> Coming and going without obstruction;
> All one says is followed by others;
> One's body is always fragrant and clean,
> Upright and handsome, poised and graceful:
> This is due to bathing the Saṃgha.
> If born in the family of a heavenly king,
> One is always pure from birth,
> Bathed with fragrant water,
> Perfumed with sweet incense;
> One's form surpasses all others;
> All who see rejoice:
> This is due to building warm rooms
> And bathing the Saṃgha, and its reward.
> The first, the Four Heavenly Kings,
> Who rule and guard the four quarters:
> Radiant, upright, and handsome,
> With authority guarding the four regions;
> Sun, moon, and stars
> Illuminating and dispelling darkness:
> This is due to bathing the Saṃgha;
> Its reward is like a shadow following form.
> The second, the Trāyastriṃśa heaven,
> Where Śakra, named Indra,
> Within a sixfold jeweled citadel
> And seven-jeweled palace,
> Is courageous and honored among the devas,
> Upright and handsome, with a long life:
> This is due to bathing the Saṃgha;
> Its reward is without equal.
> The wheel-turning king of the world,
> With seven treasures leading before him,
> Travels beyond the four seas,
> With eighty-four thousand troops;
> His jewel illuminates day and night,
> And his consort attends him at every season;
> Upright, with a fragrant and clean body:
> This is due to bathing the Saṃgha.
> The sixth, the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven,
> Honored above all in the desire realm;
> His heavenly form is resplendent and complete,
> His spiritual power shaking the six heavens;
> He naturally eats sweet dew,
> With celestial maidens always by his side;
> His manifold virtues are difficult to praise:
> This is due to bathing the Saṃgha.
> The Brahmā, the Māra, and the Sanpat heaven,
> The pure abodes, naturally cultivated;
> Their practice is pure and without defilement,
> And there is no female form;
> The holy life is cultivated in purity,
> With sincere aspiration toward nirvāṇa.
> To be born in that heaven:
> This is due to bathing the Saṃgha.
> The Buddha, honored in the three realms,
> Cultivated the path with extreme diligence,
> Accumulating practice through innumerable kalpas
> To now attain the truth of the Way;
> A golden body with jade ornaments,
> Unsoiled by dust,
> The circular radiance and marks complete:
> This is due to bathing the Saṃgha.
> The Buddhas emerge through practice,
> Through every kind of effort untiring.
> They give to all in the three realms
> Without omission;
> The Saṃgha, the assembly of sages,
> The four paths, the excellent field of merit:
> The Way and its virtue emerge from this;
> This practice is the most wondrous and true.

"When the Buddha had spoken the verses, he further told Jīvaka: 'Observe the three realms: the varieties of devas and humans, their rank and stature, the greater and lesser degrees of their merit: all are due to the differing intentions of their past lives. Therefore what they receive differs in this way. All the rewards of blessing are obtained through bathing the noble Saṃgha!'"

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, Ānanda said to the Buddha: "What should this sūtra be called? How should we teach and encourage others with it?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Warm-Room Bathing of the Saṃgha.' It is what all the Buddhas have taught, not my creation alone. Those who practice it attain liberation; it is not bestowed by spirits. If you seek the merit of purity, you should practice it yourself."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, Jīvaka and his retinue, hearing it, were filled with joy. They all attained the fruit of stream-entry, bowed to the Buddha, and took their leave to prepare the bathing implements. The great and small assembly all attained the first stage of the path, and together they bowed to the Buddha and departed.
