# 大乘百法明門論
## Treatise on the Hundred Dharmas Illuminating the Gate of the Mahāyāna

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1614

Composed by Vasubandhu (天親). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘).

---

### Brief Record of Names and Numbers from the Section on Principal Matters

As the World-Honored One said, "All dharmas are without self." What are "all dharmas"? What is "without self"?

All dharmas are briefly of five kinds: first, mind dharmas; second, dharmas associated with the mind; third, form dharmas; fourth, dharmas of formations not associated with the mind; fifth, unconditioned dharmas. Because [mind] is supreme over all; because [mental factors] are associated with it; because [form] is the appearance manifested by the first two; because [formations] are the distinctions of the aspects of the first three; because [the unconditioned] is what is revealed by the first four. Such is the sequence.

**First, mind dharmas** are briefly of eight kinds: (1) eye consciousness, (2) ear consciousness, (3) nose consciousness, (4) tongue consciousness, (5) body consciousness, (6) mental consciousness, (7) manas consciousness, (8) ālaya consciousness.

**Second, dharmas associated with the mind** are briefly of fifty-one kinds, divided into six categories: (i) five universals, (ii) five particular objects, (iii) eleven wholesome, (iv) six root afflictions, (v) twenty secondary afflictions, (vi) four indeterminate.

(i) The five universals are: (1) attention, (2) contact, (3) feeling, (4) perception, (5) volition.

(ii) The five particular objects are: (1) desire, (2) conviction, (3) mindfulness, (4) concentration, (5) wisdom.

(iii) The eleven wholesome are: (1) faith, (2) vigor, (3) shame, (4) embarrassment, (5) absence of greed, (6) absence of hatred, (7) absence of delusion, (8) pliancy, (9) conscientiousness, (10) equanimity, (11) non-harmfulness.

(iv) The six root afflictions are: (1) greed, (2) hatred, (3) pride, (4) ignorance, (5) doubt, (6) wrong views.

(v) The twenty secondary afflictions are: (1) anger, (2) resentment, (3) vexation, (4) concealment, (5) deceit, (6) flattery, (7) arrogance, (8) harmfulness, (9) jealousy, (10) stinginess, (11) shamelessness, (12) lack of embarrassment, (13) faithlessness, (14) laziness, (15) heedlessness, (16) torpor, (17) restlessness, (18) forgetfulness, (19) lack of introspection, (20) distraction.

(vi) The four indeterminate are: (1) sleep, (2) regret, (3) applied thought, (4) sustained thought.

**Third, form dharmas** are briefly of eleven kinds: (1) eye, (2) ear, (3) nose, (4) tongue, (5) body, (6) visible form, (7) sound, (8) smell, (9) taste, (10) tangible objects, (11) form included in the dharma-āyatana.

**Fourth, dharmas of formations not associated with the mind** are briefly of twenty-four kinds: (1) attainment, (2) life faculty, (3) similarity of kind, (4) state of being an ordinary being, (5) absorption of non-perception, (6) absorption of cessation, (7) fruition of non-perception, (8) collection of names, (9) collection of phrases, (10) collection of syllables, (11) arising, (12) aging, (13) abiding, (14) impermanence, (15) transmigration, (16) definite distinction, (17) coordination, (18) velocity, (19) sequence, (20) direction, (21) time, (22) number, (23) union, (24) disunion.

**Fifth, unconditioned dharmas** are briefly of six kinds: (1) unconditioned of space, (2) unconditioned of analytical cessation, (3) unconditioned of non-analytical cessation, (4) unconditioned of immovable cessation, (5) unconditioned of the cessation of perception and feeling, (6) unconditioned of suchness.

As for "without self," it is briefly of two kinds: first, selflessness of persons; second, selflessness of dharmas.
