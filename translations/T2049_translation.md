# Biography of Dharma Master Vasubandhu

## *Póshǒu Pándòu Fǎshī Zhuàn* (婆藪槃豆法師傳)

Taishō 2049

Translated by Paramārtha (*Zhēndì*, 真諦) of the Chén dynasty.

*Note: This is the biography of Vasubandhu (Póshǒu Pándòu, "Kinsman of Heaven," c. 4th-5th century CE), the great Indian Buddhist philosopher, as translated by the Chén-dynasty translator Paramārtha (499-569 CE). It is one of the most important sources for the life of Vasubandhu and his elder brother Asaṅga (Wúzhuó, "Unattached"). The text begins with a mythological origin story for the kingdom of Puruṣapura ("Land of the Man," i.e. Gandhāra/Peshawar), involving the god Viṣṇu's battle with the asura Indradamana and his sister Prabhāvatī. It then describes the three brothers of the Kauśika brahmin family, all named Vasubandhu ("Kinsman of Heaven"): the eldest (Asaṅga), who became a bodhisattva and Yogācāra master; the second (Vasubandhu proper), who became the great Sarvāstivāda and later Mahāyāna philosopher; and the youngest, who attained arhatship. Key episodes include: Asaṅga's initial meditation failures, his visit to Maitreya in Tuṣita heaven, Maitreya's descent to teach the Yogācārabhūmi; Vasubandhu's mastery of the Abhidharma, his composition of the Abhidharmakośa (which secretly critiqued the Vaibhāṣika school), his defeat of the Sāṃkhya philosopher from Vindhya Mountain, and his eventual conversion from Hīnayāna to Mahāyāna at the urging of Asaṅga. The biography preserves important historical data about the Vaibhāṣika council in Kashmir, the composition of the Mahāvibhāṣā, and the role of Aśvaghoṣa as its literary redactor.*

---

Dharma Master Vasubandhu (*Póshǒu Pándòu*) was a native of the kingdom of Puruṣapura in northern India. "Puruṣa" translates as "man" (*zhàngfū*); "pura" translates as "land" (*tǔ*).

## The Origin of Puruṣapura

According to tradition, the god Viṣṇu (*Písōuniǔ Tiānwáng*) was the younger brother of Indra. Indra sent him to Jambudvīpa to become king in order to subdue the asuras. When he was born in Jambudvīpa, he was the son of King Vāsudeva. There was an asura named Indradamana ("Subduer of Indra"), who was constantly at war with Indra and claimed to be able to defeat him, hence his name.

This asura had a sister named Prabhāvatī ("Radiant Lady"), who was extraordinarily beautiful. The asura, wishing to destroy Viṣṇu, sent his sister to deceive him through magical arts, transforming one area of Jambudvīpa into darkness. The asura hid himself in the dark, while his sister dwelt in the illuminated area. He told her: "If anyone wishes to take you as wife, tell him: 'My brother is of great power. If you wish to take me, you must first fight my brother. Only if you can defeat him will I consent.'"

Later, Viṣṇu saw the woman in the bright area and was greatly delighted. He asked: "Who are you?" She answered: "I am an asura maiden." The god said: "Asura women have always wedded devas. I have no wife and you have no husband. May I take you?" The woman answered as her brother had instructed. The god said: "You speak thus out of concern for me. Since you already love me, how could I abandon you? I have great power and can fight your brother." The woman consented and they became husband and wife.

Later, the asura came and confronted Viṣṇu: "How dare you take my sister as your wife?" Viṣṇu answered: "If I were not a [true] man and took your sister as wife, you might justly reproach me. But I am a man without a wife, and your sister is a maiden without a husband. My taking her is entirely proper. Why should you object?" The asura said: "What ability have you to call yourself a man? If you are truly a man, fight me and win, and then I will give you my sister." They fought from dawn to dusk. Viṣṇu's body was like Nārāyaṇa, impervious to blows. But whenever he cut off the asura's head, it grew back at once, and the same with his arms and all other limbs.

As evening fell, the asura showed no sign of dying, while Viṣṇu's strength was gradually spent. At night, the asura's power only grew stronger. Prabhāvatī, fearing her husband would be defeated, plucked an *utpala* lotus, tore it in two, and threw the halves to either side. Then she walked between them, going and returning. Viṣṇu understood her meaning. He seized the asura's body, tore it in two, threw the halves to either side, and walked between them. The asura thereby died. (Previously, the asura had obtained a boon from a sage: that any wounds inflicted on his body would instantly heal. The sage had granted this, but, wishing the devas to be able to kill him, had not granted him the boon of surviving being torn apart.)

Having thus displayed his manly prowess on this land, Viṣṇu gave the kingdom the name "Land of the Man" (Puruṣapura).

## The Three Brothers

In this land, there was a national preceptor, a brahmin of the Kauśika clan, who had three sons, all named Vasubandhu (*Póshǒu Pándòu*): "Vasu" means "deva" (*tiān*); "bandhu" means "kinsman" (*qīn*). It is the custom in India to give sons this kind of name. Though they shared the same name, each also had a distinct personal name.

### The Third Brother

The third Vasubandhu left home in the Sarvāstivāda school and attained arhatship. His personal name was Viriñciputra ("Son of Viriñcī," his mother's name).

### Asaṅga (The Eldest Brother)

The eldest Vasubandhu was a person of bodhisattva-nature. He also left home in the Sarvāstivāda school. Later, through meditation, he attained freedom from desire. He contemplated the meaning of emptiness but could not penetrate it and wished to take his own life. The arhat Piṇḍola, observing from the eastern continent Videha, came and taught him the Hīnayāna contemplation of emptiness. He practiced as taught and was able to enter it, but his mind was still not at ease; he felt the principle should not stop there.

By means of supernatural power, he traveled to Tuṣita heaven and consulted the Bodhisattva Maitreya. Maitreya taught him the Mahāyāna contemplation of emptiness. Returning to Jambudvīpa, he contemplated as taught and immediately attained awakening. At the moment of his contemplation, the earth trembled in six ways. Having attained the Mahāyāna contemplation of emptiness, he was given the name Asaṅga (*Āsēngjiā*), "the Unattached."

Thereafter, he repeatedly ascended to Tuṣita heaven to consult Maitreya about the meaning of the Mahāyāna sūtras. Maitreya explained them extensively. Each time Asaṅga returned to Jambudvīpa, he taught what he had heard to others, but most did not believe it. Asaṅga thereupon made a vow: "I now wish to cause sentient beings to believe and understand the Mahāyāna. May the Great Teacher descend to Jambudvīpa, expound the Mahāyāna, and enable all sentient beings to attain faith and understanding."

Maitreya accordingly descended at night to Jambudvīpa, emitting great light, and widely gathered those with karmic connections. In the Dharma hall, he recited the Yogācārabhūmi-śāstra (the "Seventeen Grounds"). As he recited each section, he explained its meaning. Over the course of four months of nightly sessions, the entire Yogācārabhūmi was expounded. Though all sat in the same hall listening to the Dharma, only Asaṅga could approach Maitreya directly; the others could only hear from afar. By night they all listened to Maitreya teach; by day, Asaṅga further explained Maitreya's teachings for the others. Thus the multitude came to hear, believe, and follow the Mahāyāna teaching of Maitreya.

Asaṅga then cultivated the Sunlight Samādhi (*Rìguāng Sānmódì*). Having attained this concentration, everything he had previously not understood he could now penetrate. Whatever he heard or saw, he remembered forever. He understood the meaning of all the great Mahāyāna sūtras originally taught by the Buddha, such as the Avataṃsaka and others. Maitreya, in Tuṣita heaven, explained the meaning of all the great Mahāyāna sūtras for him, and Asaṅga penetrated and retained them all. Later, in Jambudvīpa, he composed *upadeśas* (commentaries) on all the Mahāyāna sūtras, explaining all the great teachings taught by the Buddha.

### Vasubandhu (The Second Brother)

The second Vasubandhu also left home in the Sarvāstivāda school. He was broadly learned and widely read, mastering all the classics. His genius was brilliant and unmatched, his precept-practice lofty and without peer. Since the brothers had separate personal names, this Dharma Master was simply called Vasubandhu.

#### The Composition of the Mahāvibhāṣā

Five hundred years after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, there was an arhat named Kātyāyanīputra (named after his mother's clan). He had originally left home in the Sarvāstivāda school. He was a native of India who later went to Kashmir, which is to the northwest of India. Together with five hundred arhats and five hundred bodhisattvas, he compiled the Abhidharma of the Sarvāstivāda school, producing eight *grantha* sections (the "eight sections" known in Chinese as the "eight *gāndu*"). A *grantha* means "knot" or "section": the meaning-types are knotted together, hence "knot"; each meaning-type has its own limits, hence "section." This text is also called the "Treatise for Generating Wisdom."

By supernatural and vow-power, [Kātyāyanīputra] proclaimed throughout the world that anyone who had previously heard the Buddha teach Abhidharma, however much or little they had retained (even a single sentence or verse), should send it. Thereupon devas, nāgas, yakṣas, and beings up to the Akaniṣṭha heaven who had previously heard the Buddha teach Abhidharma sent whatever they had. Kātyāyanīputra, together with the arhats and bodhisattvas, selected and evaluated these teachings. Whatever was consistent with the Sūtra and Vinaya piṭakas was retained; whatever contradicted them was discarded. The selected passages were arranged by type: if a passage elucidated wisdom, it was placed in the wisdom section; if it elucidated concentration, in the concentration section; and so on. The eight sections totaled fifty thousand verses.

After completing the eight sections, [Kātyāyanīputra] wished to compose a *Vibhāṣā* (commentary) to explain them. The Bodhisattva Aśvaghoṣa, a native of the land of Vākṣita in Śrāvastī, was proficient in the eight divisions of the Vyākaraṇa, the four Vedas, and the six treatises. He understood the eighteen schools and was acknowledged as the master of the Tripiṭaka. Kātyāyanīputra sent a person to Śrāvastī to invite Aśvaghoṣa to compose the literary text. When Aśvaghoṣa arrived in Kashmir, Kātyāyanīputra expounded the eight sections in order. The arhats and bodhisattvas debated the meaning, and once the meaning was settled, Aśvaghoṣa wrote the text. Over twelve years, the Vibhāṣā was completed, totaling one million verses. "Vibhāṣā" translates as "Extensive Explanation."

When the text was completed, Kātyāyanīputra inscribed a stone proclamation: "Henceforth, those who study these texts must not leave Kashmir. The eight sections and the Vibhāṣā must not leave the country, lest other schools and the Mahāyāna corrupt this correct Dharma." The four mountain-walls of Kashmir were like a city with only one gate. The sages, by vow-power, commanded yakṣa-spirits to guard the gate. Anyone wishing to study these texts could come to Kashmir freely, and by the sages' vow-power, five hundred yakṣas served as donors, so that students lacked nothing.

#### Vasubandhu and the Abhidharmakośa

In the kingdom of Ayodhyā, there was a Dharma Master named Buddhasiṃha (*Póshā Xūbátuóluó*), of great intelligence who could memorize upon hearing. Wishing to study the eight sections and the Vibhāṣā in order to spread them beyond Kashmir, he disguised himself as a madman and went to Kashmir. He attended the great assemblies, listening to the Dharma, but his deportment was erratic and his speech strange. At times during the assembly discussions, he would interject questions about the Rāmāyaṇa. The assembly looked down on him and paid him no heed. Over twelve years, he heard the Vibhāṣā several times through, mastering both its text and meaning, retaining everything in memory.

When he attempted to leave through the gate, the yakṣa-spirits cried out: "The great Abhidharma master is trying to leave the country!" He was seized and brought back to the great assembly for examination. His answers were confused and incoherent. The assembly concluded he was mad and released him. This happened three times. On the fourth attempt, though the spirits again raised the alarm, the assembly no longer examined him and let him go.

Having reached his homeland, he proclaimed everywhere that he had learned the complete text and meaning of the Vibhāṣā. Students gathered from all directions. Though old and fearing he might die before transmitting the entire teaching, he had his students write as fast as he recited, and the entire text was preserved.

Later, [Vasubandhu] lectured on the Vibhāṣā for the multitude. Each day, he composed one verse summarizing the day's teaching, inscribed on a copper leaf. He placed the verse beneath a decorated elephant and had drums beaten announcing: "Who can refute this verse?" In this way, he composed over six hundred verses summarizing the entire Vibhāṣā, and none could refute a single one. This became the Abhidharmakośa verses (*Jùshě Lùn*).

When the verses were complete, he sent them with fifty pounds of gold to the Vaibhāṣika masters in Kashmir. They were overjoyed, thinking their correct Dharma was now widely propagated. But since the verses were profound and difficult to understand fully, they sent another fifty pounds of gold (making one hundred in total) and requested Vasubandhu to compose a prose commentary. Vasubandhu then wrote the commentary. He established the Sarvāstivāda positions but, wherever there were errors, refuted them using the positions of the Sautrāntika school. This is called the Abhidharmakośa-bhāṣya. When the Kashmir masters saw that their positions had been undermined, they were greatly distressed.

#### Defeating the Sāṃkhya Philosopher

King Vikramāditya's son, Prince Bālāditya ("New Sun"), had been sent by the king to study under Vasubandhu. The queen-mother also left home as Vasubandhu's disciple. When the prince ascended the throne, both mother and son invited Vasubandhu to remain in Ayodhyā and accept their patronage, which he agreed to.

The new king's brother-in-law, a brahmin named Vasu-rata, was an outsider Dharma master skilled in the Vyākaraṇa treatise. He challenged Vasubandhu's Kośa. Vasubandhu thereupon composed a treatise that demolished all thirty-two sections of the Vyākaraṇa treatise.

The defeated outsider then sent to India for the monk Saṃghabhadra (*Sēngjiā Fútuóluó*) to come to Ayodhyā and compose a treatise refuting the Kośa. Saṃghabhadra composed two treatises: the *Nyāyānusāra* (one hundred thousand verses, strictly elucidating the Vaibhāṣika positions) and the *Abhidharmasamayapradīpikā* ("Light of the Abhidharma Positions"). He summoned Vasubandhu to debate face-to-face. But Vasubandhu, knowing the refutations could not actually overturn the Kośa, declined, saying: "I am now old. Do as you wish. When I composed my treatise refuting the Vibhāṣā, I did not debate you face-to-face either. Now that you have composed your treatise, why must you call me? Those with wisdom will know for themselves what is right and wrong."

#### Conversion to the Mahāyāna

Vasubandhu had thoroughly mastered all eighteen schools. He was expert in the Hīnayāna and held the Hīnayāna to be correct, not believing in the Mahāyāna, saying the Mahāyāna was not taught by the Buddha.

Asaṅga, seeing his younger brother's extraordinary intelligence and fearing he would compose treatises to demolish the Mahāyāna, was dwelling in Puruṣapura. He sent a messenger to Ayodhyā, reporting: "I am gravely ill. Come quickly." Vasubandhu followed the messenger home and asked his brother about the source of his illness. Asaṅga answered: "My heart carries a grave illness, born because of you." Vasubandhu asked: "How so?" Asaṅga said: "You do not believe the Mahāyāna and constantly slander it. Because of this evil karma, you will certainly sink forever into the evil destinies. My grief over this is so great that my life will not last."

Vasubandhu was startled and alarmed. He asked his brother to explain the Mahāyāna to him. Asaṅga briefly explained its essential meaning. Being extraordinarily intelligent, Vasubandhu immediately understood that the Mahāyāna principle surpassed the Hīnayāna. He studied the full Mahāyāna doctrine under his brother, and later understood it as his brother did, penetrating everything without a single contradiction. He then verified that the Hīnayāna was in error and the Mahāyāna was correct: without the Mahāyāna, there would be no fruits of the three vehicles.

Having formerly slandered the Mahāyāna and fearing this karmic offense would lead to the evil destinies, he deeply reproached himself and wished to repent. He went to his brother, confessed his error, and asked how to atone. He said: "In the past, it was because of my tongue that I slandered. I should now cut out my tongue to pay for this sin." His brother said: "Even if you cut out a thousand tongues, you could not eradicate this sin. If you wish to eradicate it, you must find another method." Vasubandhu asked what method. His brother said: "Your tongue was skilled in slandering the Mahāyāna. If you wish to eradicate this sin, you should now skillfully explain the Mahāyāna."

After Asaṅga passed away, Vasubandhu composed Mahāyāna treatises explaining all the great Mahāyāna sūtras: the Avataṃsaka, Nirvāṇa, Lotus, Prajñāpāramitā, Vimalakīrti, Śrīmālā, and others. He also composed the Vijñaptimātratā (Consciousness-Only), the *Mahāyānasaṃgrahabhāṣya*, the *Ratnagotravibhāga*, the *Amṛtadvāra*, and other Mahāyāna treatises. All of Vasubandhu's compositions are exquisite in text and meaning. All who see or hear them come to faith and seek [the teaching]. Throughout India and the border regions, all students of both the Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna take Vasubandhu's works as their foundational texts. The masters of other schools and of the outsider traditions, upon hearing his name, are filled with awe and submission.

He passed away in Ayodhyā at the age of eighty. Though his outward traces appeared to be those of an ordinary person, his true [attainment] is in reality inconceivable.
