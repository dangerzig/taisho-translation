# 仁王般若陀羅尼釋
## Commentary on the Dhāraṇī of the Humane King Prajñāpāramitā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0996

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Amoghavajra (不空)

---

"Vajrapāṇi": the *Yoga Sūtra* commentary says that holding the vajra in the hand represents internally possessing great bodhi and externally crushing all afflictions; therefore the name Vajrapāṇi. Another explanation says: not being destroyed by the three kinds of māra, the bodhi-mind itself is firm and becomes vajra-wisdom, established by all the Tathāgatas, able to shatter the two extremes of eternalism and annihilationism. Therefore, the vajra-wisdom pestle shatters the mountain of wrong views and realizes the vajra samādhi. Constantly holding it in the palm, one is therefore called Vajrapāṇi.

What is the meaning of "bodhisattva"? One who awakens to the true and real Dharma, and having awakened, abides in saṃsāra, causing all sentient beings to awaken; therefore the name bodhisattva. Also, "bodhi" means "awakening." "Sattva" means "sentient being," also translated as "mind," also as "courageous and strong." "Mahā" means "great." "Sattva" means "courageous and strong." Without timidity or weakness, through three great asaṃkhyeya kalpas of accumulating the two kinds of provisions; therefore the name mahāsattva.

What is the meaning of "Vajra-maṇi"? "Vajra" is as explained above. "Jewel" has six meanings: first, it is difficult to obtain; second, it is pure and without stain; third, it has great awesome virtue; fourth, it adorns the world; fifth, it is supreme and peerless; sixth, it is unchanging. First, "difficult to obtain" means that the appearance of a Tathāgata in the world is extremely hard to encounter. Second, "pure and without stain" means that by practicing in accordance with the teaching, one realizes bodhi, which is pure and without stain. Third, "great awesome virtue" means possessing the six supernatural powers, with transformations at will; this is called great awesome virtue. Fourth, "adorning the world" means adorning body and mind with the three collections of bodhisattva-precepts. Fifth, "supreme and peerless" means realizing unsurpassed bodhi, uniquely honored in the three realms, supreme and peerless. Sixth, "unchanging" means realizing the ultimate unsurpassed bodhi, which is unchanging. "Vajra-maṇi" is the manifest name of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva; therefore this bodhisattva holds a vajra-jewel in his hands.

"Vajra-tīkṣṇa" (Vajra Sharp): the Prajñāpāramitā vajra sharp-sword can sever the seeds of affliction. The "all-kinds vajra sharp-one" is the manifest name of Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva; therefore this bodhisattva holds a vajra-sword in his hand.

"Vajra-yakṣa Bodhisattva": "Vajra" is as explained above. "Yakṣa" means "ferocious," also translated as "exhaustion." Among the sixteen vajra-wisdoms of Samantabhadra's practice, the fifteenth wisdom is called Vajra Exhaustion Wisdom. With the vajra-yakṣa's wisdom-fangs, one devours and consumes all afflictions and secondary afflictions until nothing remains.

"Vajra-ghaṇṭā" (Vajra Bell): it represents the meaning of Prajñāpāramitā. Ringing the bell awakens deluded and ignorant ordinary beings. Upon hearing the bell's sound once, one awakens to Prajñāpāramitā. The manifest name is All-Māra-Destroying Bodhisattva; therefore this bodhisattva holds a vajra-bell in his hand.

"Vajra-pāramitā Bodhisattva": "Vajra" is as explained above. "Pāramitā": as previous masters explained it, it means "reaching the other shore." Now, according to the grammar treatise (*śabda-vidyā*), analyzing the phrase: *pāram-itaḥ*. *Pāram* means "the other shore." *Itaḥ* means "this shore." This bodhisattva, by holding the vajra-wheel, caused Vairocana Buddha, after accomplishing Buddhahood in the upper realm, to turn the vajra-vehicle Dharma-wheel. By mounting this Dharma-wheel, the prajñā-ship, one ferries immeasurable and boundless sentient beings from this shore to the shore of non-abiding nirvāṇa. The manifest name is First-to-Generate-the-Mind-and-Turn-the-Dharma-Wheel Bodhisattva.

"*Namo ratna-trayāya*": this means "Homage to the Three Treasures." If a person who upholds this sūtra takes refuge in the Buddha-treasure, they immediately obtain the five-family Vajrapāṇi Bodhisattvas, with immeasurable retinues, as attendants who empower and protect that person. These bodhisattvas honor the bodhi-mind, because the Buddha is born from the bodhi-mind. Taking refuge in the Dharma-treasure, one then obtains Śakra-devānām-indra and his retinue, as well as the Four Heavenly Kings, who empower and protect one. Why? Because when Indra was in danger, the Prajñā empowerment brought him immediate benefit; therefore Indra honors the Dharma-treasure. Taking refuge in the Saṃgha-treasure, one then obtains the Akaniṣṭha Heavenly King, together with the five pure-abode devas and their retinues, who empower and protect the sūtra-upholder. Because the five pure-abode bodhisattva-saṃghas and śrāvaka-saṃghas constantly dwell in those heavens experiencing the bliss of the directly realized Dharma, Brahmā and all others deeply value them.

"*Namo ārya-vairocanāya tathāgatāya arhate samyak-saṃbuddhāya*": "*Namaḥ*" means "homage," also "bowing the head," also "prostrating with the crown." "*Ārya*" means "far from evil"; in our interpretive convention it means "holy one." "*Vairocanāya*" means "universal illumination," also "great sun." The meaning is that the worldly sun illuminates one side but not the other, illuminates daytime but not night, illuminates one world but not other worlds; thus it can only be called "sun," not "great sun." Vairocana, the Great Sun: his form-body and dharma-body pervade the dharma-realm and the realm of empty space, universally illuminating the boundless worlds of the ten directions. If a person knows the merit of this Buddha and takes refuge in reverence, then all the Buddhas, bodhisattvas, worthy sages, and eight classes of beings throughout empty space and the dharma-realm will together empower, protect, and be mindful of them.

"*Tathāgatāya arhate samyak-saṃbuddhāya*": "Thus Come One, Worthy of Offerings, of Right and Universal Knowledge." This has already been explained.

"*Namo ārya*": already explained.

"*Samantabhadrāya*": "*Samanta*" means "universal." "*Bhadra*" means "worthy." The "*ya*" syllable belongs to the seventh of the eight case-declensions in grammar, the vocative; the same applies below. This bodhisattva teaches the three secret gates and Samantabhadra's practice-vows. None of the Buddhas, without cultivating the three secret gates and practicing Samantabhadra's practices, have accomplished Buddhahood; this is impossible. Moreover, having accomplished Buddhahood, the three secret gates and Samantabhadra's practices never cease; this is also impossible.

"*Bodhi-sattvāya*": "Bodhisattva."

"*Mahā-sattvāya*": "Great Bodhisattva."

"*Mahā-kāruṇikāya*": "Great Compassionate One." If one takes refuge in the holy Samantabhadra Bodhisattva, then all the Buddhas and bodhisattvas of the ten directions will protect one. All the Buddhas and bodhisattvas accomplished the holy fruit through cultivating the three secret gates and practicing Samantabhadra's practices; therefore they are honored.

"*Tadyathā*": anciently rendered "that is to say" or "namely." The text above constitutes homage to the Three Treasures, Vairocana Buddha, and Samantabhadra Bodhisattva.

"*Jñāna-praṇidhe*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "wisdom-lamp." The secret explanation interprets it as "wisdom through non-apprehension as skillful means." Non-wisdom and non-attainment themselves constitute the prajñāpāramitā wisdom-lamp, which can illuminate all the Buddha-Dharma.

The syllable "*a*" (*akṣa*): in the Sanskrit original this "*a*" syllable is the vowel *ā*, but following the phonetic conventions of the text it is pronounced as a short "*a*."

"*Akṣaya-kuśe*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "inexhaustible treasury." The secret explanation takes the syllable *a* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *a* means "all dharmas are originally unborn." The syllable *a* is the mother of all syllables, able to produce all dharmas. If one understands the yoga correspondence of the *a*-syllable gate, one obtains the inexhaustible treasury of the Buddha-Dharma, and one awakens to the original unborn nature of all dharmas, like empty space, of one mark, pure, and equal. One then accomplishes non-discriminating wisdom.

"*Pratibhāna-vati*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "possessing eloquence." The secret explanation takes the syllable *pra* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *pra* means that Prajñāpāramitā is non-apprehensible. Through non-apprehension as skillful means, in the subsequently-attained wisdom one awakens to the fact that all dharmas arise through conditions and causes. Because one has realized the original unborn nature of all dharmas, in the subsequently-attained wisdom one obtains the four unobstructed knowledges and preaches the Dharma with sovereign freedom.

"*Sarva-buddhāvalokite*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "contemplated by all the Buddhas." The secret explanation takes the syllable *sa* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *sa* means the equality of all dharmas. Perceiver and perceived, subject and object, are all equally non-apprehensible. One then realizes true suchness, enters the stream of Dharma, and the boundless Buddhas observe, protect, and are mindful of one.

"*Yoga-pariṇiṣpannī*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "yoga perfectly accomplished." The secret explanation takes the syllable *yu* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *yu* means "all vehicles are non-apprehensible." If one's yoga-contemplation wisdom is in correspondence and one attains perfect accomplishment, then in all the vehicles one realizes a single true suchness dharma-nature throughout teachings, principles, practices, and fruits.

"*Gambhīra-nura-vāga-kehi*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "profoundly deep and unfathomable." The secret explanation takes the syllable *gaṃ* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *gaṃ* means "all true-suchness dharmas neither come nor go," like realizing the ocean of true suchness. The prajñā of reality cannot be expressed in words; only the Buddha's realm of self-realized holy wisdom can realize it.

"*Tryadhva-pariṇiṣpannī*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "perfectly accomplished through the three times." The secret explanation takes "*tryadhva*" as the seed-syllable. "*Tryadhva*" means "all dharmas are true suchness, equal, self-nature accomplished, surpassing Ganges-sands in number." Within the merit of true suchness, there are no deluded discriminations of past, future, or present, no non-corresponding formations-skandha, no rigid attachment, delusion, or conditioned dharmas.

"*Bodhi-citta-saṃjananī*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "able to generate the bodhi-mind." The secret explanation takes the syllable *bo* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *bo* means "all dharmas are without bondage." If one knows that within one's own body the bodhi-mind is self-nature accomplished, equal through the three times, like empty space free from all images, then one knows that the minds of all sentient beings and the minds of all the Buddhas are, like one's own mind, pure. Then great compassion arises and deep sympathy is born. Then one gives rise to all manner of skillful means, causing all sentient beings to reach the ultimate, freed from suffering and liberated, without binding and without release.

"*Sarva-abhiṣeka-viṣkṛṣṭe*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "all consecrations obtaining the supreme." The secret explanation takes the syllable *sa* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *sa* means "all dharmas are undefiled and unattached." Through contemplating that self, others, and all the Buddhas share a single true suchness, with same-body great compassion: therefore, undefiled and unattached, in the sky all the Tathāgatas pour the Dharma-water of consecration, and one obtains the empowerment of the three karmas. In the immeasurable sūtra-treasury, one preaches the Dharma with sovereign freedom.

"*Dharma-sāgara-saṃbuddhe*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "born from the Dharma-ocean." The secret explanation takes the syllable *dha* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *dha* means "the entire dharma-realm is non-apprehensible." By abiding in the mind of non-apprehension, the seeds of innate self-clinging and innate dharma-clinging in the ālaya consciousness are permanently severed without remainder by the vajra sharp-sword of the great holy Mañjuśrī. Then the pure equal-flow teachings of the dharma-realm pour forth, and the meaning of "born from the Dharma-ocean" is accomplished. The syllable *dha* is the seed-syllable of Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva.

"*Amogha-śrāvaṇī*": the manifest explanation interprets "*amogha*" as "not empty" (anciently translated as such). Now, according to the grammar treatise, it means "without interval." The syllable *a* is taken as the seed-syllable. The syllable *a* means "all dharmas are originally quiescent, originally nirvāṇa." Through realizing this seal of liberation, pervading the dharma-realm and all the Buddha-lands and great assemblies, all the teachings heard before the Buddhas are upheld without ever being forgotten.

"*Mahā-samantabhadra-bhūmi-niryāte*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "emerging from the great Samantabhadra stage." The secret explanation takes the syllable *ma* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *ma* means "all dharmas are without self." "Without self" has two kinds: no-self of persons and no-self of dharmas. If a yogin realizes the two kinds of no-self, one emerges from the great Samantabhadra stage and realizes the hundred-merit adorned, perfectly pure dharma-body of Vairocana.

"*Vyākaraṇa-pariprapanī*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "obtaining the prophecy of Buddhahood" (in the old translation, "conferring the prophecy"). The secret explanation takes "*vyā*" as the seed-syllable. "*Vyā*" means "all dharmas are universally pervading and non-apprehensible." One immediately knows that the self-nature of all dharmas is quiescent, self-nature is nirvāṇa; the realizer and the realized are both of one nature, neither increasing nor decreasing, perfectly realizing the dharma-realm.

"*Sarva-siddha-namaskṛte*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "all accomplished ones pay reverence." "Accomplished ones" is another name for bodhisattvas. The secret explanation takes the syllable *sa* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *sa* means "all dharmas are without solidity." Moment to moment, the four characteristics of arising, abiding, changing, and ceasing flow and pass away. Within the syllable *sa* there is the syllable *a*. If one realizes the *a*-syllable gate, originally unborn and unceasing, one is also eternally firm, like a vajra-mind obtaining sovereign freedom, able to manifest all manner of bodies.

"*Sarva-bodhi-sattva-saṃjananī*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "producing all bodhisattvas." The secret explanation takes the syllable *sa* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *sa* means "all dharmas are without equal." Through contemplating this syllable, the mind and prajñā become equally equal. The preceding thought-moment and the following thought-moment are of one mark, pure. One is able to produce all the pāramitās and all the stages; this is called the prajñā Buddha-Mother.

"*Bhagavatī buddha-māte*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "World-Honored One, Buddha-Mother." "*Bhagavān*" is the masculine form, "*Bhagavatī*" is the feminine form; both are interpreted conventionally as meaning "World-Honored One." According to the grammar treatise's oppositional analysis, it is not so. "*Bhaga*" means "to destroy"; "*vān*" means "capable"; one who is capable of destroying the four māras is called Bhagavān. Another explanation: *bhaga-vān*. According to the grammar treatise's syllable-analysis, *bhaga* means "to destroy," *a-vān* means "possessing knowledge." The syllable *a* means "not existing," also "not non-existing." The Buddha, by awakening to the fact that all dharmas are unborn and unceasing, neither coming nor going, neither one nor different, neither permanent nor annihilated, neither increasing nor decreasing, possesses such merit; therefore the name Bhagavān. Another explanation says Bhagavān possesses the two kinds of provisions: merit and wisdom. "Two kinds of provisions": "*Bhagavatī*" in the feminine form; its meaning is explained as before. The secret explanation takes the syllable *bha* as the seed-syllable. The syllable *bha* means "existence of all dharmas is non-apprehensible." "Existence" means the three realms of existence. Therefore, the three realms are only mind. When the mind is defiled, sentient beings are defiled; when the mind is pure, sentient beings are pure. According to the manifest teaching, contemplation-practice prajñā serves as the generating cause and the revealing cause, able to produce all the Buddhas and bodhisattvas; therefore it is called the Buddha-Mother. From the "*tadyathā*" (that is to say) onward, up to the Buddha-Mother passage, in the yoga teaching this constitutes the sixteen practices of Samantabhadra, like the sixteen practices of the seeing-the-path in the śrāvaka vehicle.

"*Arāṇī-karāṇī araṇā-karāṇī*": the *a*-syllable gate, all dharmas are originally unborn. By knowing that all dharmas are originally unborn, one enters the freedom from dust of all dharmas; therefore the *ra*-syllable gate, all dharmas are free from dust. By knowing that all dharmas are free from dust, one enters the non-contention of all dharmas; therefore the *ṇī*-syllable gate, all dharmas are without contention. By knowing that all dharmas are without contention, one enters the non-fabrication of all dharmas; therefore the *ka*-syllable gate, all dharmas are without fabrication. By knowing that all dharmas are without fabrication, one enters the purity of all dharmas; therefore the *ra*-syllable gate, all dharmas are pure. By knowing that all dharmas are pure, one enters the non-contention of all dharmas; therefore the *ṇī*-syllable gate, all dharmas are without contention. By knowing that all dharmas are without contention, one enters the original quiescence of all dharmas; therefore the *a*-syllable gate, all dharmas are originally quiescent. By knowing that all dharmas are originally quiescent, one enters the stainlessness of all dharmas; therefore the *ra*-syllable gate, all dharmas are without stain. By knowing that all dharmas are without stain, one enters the non-contention of all dharmas; therefore the *ṇa*-syllable gate, all dharmas are without contention. By knowing that all dharmas are without contention, one enters the non-fabrication of all dharmas; therefore the *ka*-syllable gate, all dharmas are without fabrication. By knowing that all dharmas are without fabrication, one enters non-discriminating wisdom; therefore the *ra*-syllable gate, all dharmas are without discrimination. By knowing that all dharmas are without discrimination, one enters the non-movement of all dharmas; therefore the *ṇī*-syllable gate, all dharmas are without movement. By knowing that all dharmas are without movement, one realizes Mahā-prajñāpāramitā.

"*Mahā-prajñā-jñāna-pāramite*": the manifest explanation interprets it as "Mahā: Great Wisdom Reaching the Other Shore." By realizing great Prajñāpāramitā, one relies upon non-abiding nirvāṇa.

"*Svāhā*": the manifest explanation interprets it as non-abiding nirvāṇa. Relying upon non-abiding nirvāṇa, until the end of future ages, one broadly benefits and brings happiness to boundless sentient beings.
