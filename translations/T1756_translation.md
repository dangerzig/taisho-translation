# Exposition on the Meaning of the Amitābha Sūtra

## *Āmítuó Jīng Yìshù*

Taishō 1756

Composed by Huìjìng (*Huìjìng*), Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a systematic commentary (yìshù, "exposition on the meaning") on the Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra (the shorter Amitābha Sūtra, T366), composed by the Tang-dynasty Pure Land exegete Huìjìng. The commentary opens with a prose preface on the dharma-body as imageless yet responsive, the Pure Land as without fixed location yet manifesting through the merit of sentient beings, and the complementary roles of Amitābha in the Pure Land and Śākyamuni in the sahā world. It then follows the standard threefold division of the sūtra (prologue, main teaching, transmission) and further subdivides the main teaching into ten chapters: (1) the environment of the Pure Land (seven treasures, pools, music, birds, trees); (2) Amitābha's attributes (infinite light, life, and retinue); (3) the vow for rebirth; (4) the practice of rebirth (recitation of Amitābha's name); (5) the attestation of buddhas in six directions; (6) the reason for the attestation; (7) the three-period vow; (8) the three-period rebirth; (9) this Buddha's praise of that [Amitābha]; and (10) that [Amitābha]'s praise of this [Śākyamuni]. The commentary draws on the Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā (Shízhù Pípóshā Lùn), the Jìngtǔ Lùn (Vasubandhu's Treatise on Rebirth), and Tiāntái analytical categories.*

---

## Preface

Secretly I reflect: the dharma-body is without image, yet images arise because of beings who have the capacity for images. The Pure Land has no fixed direction, yet direction arises because of beings whose conditions respond to that direction. Although ultimate principle is coagulated in tranquility and the Way transcends names and words, great compassion employs skillful means: following the three turnings [of the Dharma wheel], the nectar of ghee [that is, the supreme Dharma] brings countless beings beyond the mundane. Those who accord with it inevitably return to their original nature; those who are transformed by it may differ in their streams. Therefore, Amitābha, whose seed-consciousness is fully awakened, dwells in the wondrous realm to propagate the Way, while Śākyamuni the Benevolent One dwells in the sahā world to receive beings. Within the Pure Land, faculties are easily tamed; in the defiled land, the stubborn and obdurate are difficult to transform. He opens the three causes to convey the four kinds of birth, spreads the nine grades to crush the five pains. The buddhas of the six directions manifest throughout the three-thousand[-fold world], attesting that rebirth is obtained and that these words are not false. Contemplating sentient beings as empty yet not abandoning great compassion; contemplating the Tathāgata as quiescent yet not losing reverential offerings. Though one knows the Buddha is quiescent like space, he manifests all manner of pure buddha-lands. This can be called "not destroying conventional designations while teaching the true characteristics of all dharmas; not moving yet establishing all dharmas."

## Title

"The Buddha Speaks the Amitābha Sūtra" (*Fóshuō Āmítuó Jīng*): this is the sūtra that exhorts rebirth in the Pure Land and non-retrogression from bodhi. Bright as the autumn moon through the three kinds of insight, thundering like spring through the four kinds of eloquence, praising the pure realm to indicate the West, extolling the supreme cause to point to the Eastern Land. Issued from my [the Buddha's] own mouth, penetrating their [the listeners'] minds. Because awakening matches the capacity, it is called "the Buddha speaks." "Amitābha" (*Āmítuó*): this means "Immeasurable Life" (*Wúliàng Shòu*). His light, his teaching, and his assembly are all immeasurable. To attract the sentiments of beings, his name emphasizes "life" in particular; hence "Immeasurable."

"Sūtra" (*jīng*) has two meanings: "standard" and "constant." It is the path traversed by all sages. Called "sūtra" because it endures from ancient times to the present; called "constant" because it is eternally fixed. Hence the name "The Buddha Speaks the Amitābha Sūtra."

## The Opening Passage: "Thus ... the Jeta Grove, the Garden of Anāthapiṇḍada"

The sūtra opens in three divisions: (1) the prologue (*yóuxù*), (2) the main body (*zhèngtǐ*), and (3) the transmission (*liútōng*). Each of the three divisions has two sub-parts. The prologue has two: first, the general prologue establishing trustworthiness; second, the specific prologue establishing the occasion. The main body has two: first, the causes and fruits of the Pure Land of Bliss; second, the causes and fruits of Amitābha's dharma-body. The transmission has two: first, the Tathāgata having finished speaking this sūtra; second, the great assembly rejoicing with faith and acceptance.

The general and specific prologues each have six elements. The six of the general prologue are: (1) faith established, (2) hearing established, (3) time established, (4) the lord established, (5) the place established, and (6) the assembly established.

### Faith Established

"Thus" (*rúshì*): this is the word of faith and acceptance. "Faith" means the principle spoken is trustworthy; "acceptance" means the path between teacher and student is perfected. Without faith, the sūtra cannot be transmitted, whether its scope be broad or narrow; therefore it says "thus." Also, "thus" (*rú*) points to the dharma; "is" (*shì*) is the word of affirmation. Whatever the Buddha speaks is all "thus"; therefore "thus." Further, "faith" here means faith in this sūtra. As the text below says: the buddhas of the six directions, innumerable as the sands of the Ganges, manifest to attest and confirm that reciting Amitābha's name assures rebirth in the Pure Land. "Thus" at the beginning of the sūtra indicates faith; "joyfully" at the end indicates wisdom. Faith is the gate of entry; wisdom is the vehicle of crossing over. With zeal, mindfulness, and concentration encompassing the whole from beginning to end, the five faculties are complete, and one is fit to receive and transmit. Therefore it says "thus."

### Hearing Established

"I heard" (*wǒwén*): this is hearing established. "Personally received" is called "I heard"; "transmitted and received" is called "what was heard." This indicates direct reception: Ānanda personally heard from the Buddha himself the teaching about Amitābha Buddha and the Land of Ultimate Bliss. That whoever single-mindedly recites [the name] will certainly be reborn there. This is not hearsay subject to error. Although Ānanda was not yet the attendant [during the earlier period of the Buddha's teaching], and so that earlier period was not personally received, at the time of the Buddha's nirvāṇa, [the Buddha] again briefly taught him. Therefore he can properly say "I heard."

### Time Established

"At one time" (*yīshí*): this is time established. The time when the Buddha spoke this Amitābha Sūtra; the time when Śāriputra, Ānanda, and the rest listened and received; also the time when the Dharma King initiated his auspicious gathering. Therefore it says "at one time."

### Lord Established

"The Buddha" (*Fó*): this means "Awakened One" (*jué*); this is the lord established. There are five kinds of speakers of Dharma: (1) the Buddha speaks, (2) disciples speak, (3) devas speak, (4) ṛṣis speak, and (5) transformation-beings speak. This is the Buddha speaking; therefore it is called "lord established." "Buddha" is the name of the Great Teacher, with "awakening" as its meaning. Awakening has two aspects: (1) awareness through examination (*juéchá*) and (2) awareness through realization (*juéwù*). Realization means the cognitive obscurations are exhausted; examination means the afflictive obscurations are removed. Within these two kinds of awakening there are three distinctions: "self-awakened" distinguishes [the Buddha] from ordinary beings; "awakening others" distinguishes [the Buddha] from the two vehicles; and "complete awakening" distinguishes [the Buddha] from bodhisattvas. When all three are complete, then one is called "Buddha."

### Place Established

"Was dwelling in" (*zài*): this is place established. "Dwelling in" also means "abiding." There are eight kinds of abiding; here, briefly, there are two: (1) abiding in a domain and (2) abiding in a support. "In the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, the Garden of Anāthapiṇḍada": this specifies the two kinds of abiding. The domain is for transforming those who dwell in the world; the garden is the support-base for receiving those who have gone forth. "Jeta" is Prince Jeta (*Zhìtuó*), meaning "Victorious." When the prince was born, the king had just defeated a marauding enemy; the palace women, hearing the report, named him accordingly. "Anāthapiṇḍada" is originally Sudatta (*Xūdá*), meaning "Good Charity" (*Shàncí*): inwardly and outwardly charitable and wealthy, providing for the poor and caring for the widowed. The country people, admiring him, gave him this epithet. Since the two persons are of different rank, one cannot name the garden after Sudatta alone. Since trees are specific and the garden is general, one cannot simply say "Jeta's trees" for both. Because these two joined together in common purpose to build the monastery, both names are combined. Hence "the Jeta Grove, the Garden of Anāthapiṇḍada."

The dharma-body truly has no dwelling place; it is the manifested transformation that has location. Provisionally abiding in this place, [the Buddha] taught the five gates of practice for rebirth in Amitābha's [Pure Land] and the certainty of attaining it. The five gates are as stated in the *Treatise on Rebirth* (*Wǎngshēng Lùn*): (1) the gate of worship, (2) the gate of praise, (3) the gate of vow-making, (4) the gate of contemplation, and (5) the gate of merit-transference.

### Assembly Established

"Together with the great assembly of bhikṣus ... all great disciples": this is the assembly established. There are three kinds of assembly: (1) the śrāvaka assembly, (2) the bodhisattva assembly, and (3) the deva assembly.

The śrāvaka assembly has five aspects: (1) indicating the type, (2) announcing the number, (3) praising their virtues, (4) listing names, and (5) concluding with a summary.

"Together with great bhikṣus": this indicates the type. "Together" (*yǔ*) also means "with," signifying that the four [kinds of] bodies naturally include both self and others. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* (*Zhìdù Lùn*) says: "sharing one precept, one view, one place, and so forth is called 'together.'" "Great" (*dà*): in Sanskrit, *mahā*, which has three meanings: (1) great, (2) many, and (3) supreme. Therefore kings and men and devas revere them. Why "many"? They number up to one thousand two hundred and fifty. They can defeat the sixteen [kinds of] non-Buddhist treatises in debate. "Bhikṣu" (*bǐqiū*) has roughly three meanings: (1) one who frightens māra, (2) an almsman, and (3) one who destroys evil. These three correspond to the initial, middle, and final [stages of the monastic life]. "Frightening māra": when one first commits to the profound path, one already establishes the resolve to vanquish māra. Therefore, when one first shaves the head and dons the dyed robes, the palace of the heavenly māra trembles and māra is frightened. "Almsman": the body is the vessel of the Way; when the body is at ease, the Way flourishes. Having already received the Dharma-robes, one begs for food to sustain the body. "Destroyer of evil": for the sage, the path is advanced and for the ordinary being, it is diminished. When cultivation is complete, one destroys evil to realize the fruit. These three names at the causal stage, upon reaching the fruit stage, become: "frightening māra" becomes "slayer of enemies" (*shāzéi*); "almsman" becomes "worthy of offerings" (*yìnggōng*); and "destroyer of evil" becomes "non-arising" (*bùshēng*). "Saṃgha" (*sēng*) means "assembly" (*zhòng*): many bhikṣus dwelling harmoniously in one place, therefore called "assembly."

"One thousand two hundred and fifty persons together": this announces the number. "All are great arhats": this praises their virtues. "Arhat" has the three meanings corresponding to the three fruition-stage names described above. "Known by the multitude": the great assemblies of devas and humans all know their inner virtue and recognize their outward form.

"The Elder Śāriputra ... through ... Anuruddha": this lists names, specifying seventeen persons within the śrāvaka assembly. "Elder," "wise life," "pure life" are all terms of praise. Śāriputra (*Shèlìfú*) means "Son of Śārī." If dullness had a measure, then it would be Pilindavatsa (*Duàntuó*); if sharpness had a measure, then it would be Śāriputra. He reports the past and knows the future; his heroic talent is swift and keen; his divine wisdom is penetrating. Maudgalyāyana (*Mùlián*) means "Praise-reciter." Kāśyapa (*Jiāshè*) means "Light-drinker." Mahākauṣṭhila (*Jùchīluó*) means "Great Knee." Cūḍapanthaka (*Zhōulìpántuójiā*) means "Snake-slave." Nanda (*Nántuó*) means "Joy." Ānanda (*Ānán*) means "Delight." Rāhula (*Luóhóu*) means "Obstacle." Gavāṃpati (*Jiāofànbōtí*) means "Ox King." Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja (*Bīntóulú*) means "Senior Seat"; Bhāradvāja (*Pōluóduò*) means "Sharp Faculty." Kālodāyin (*Jiāliútuóyí*) means "Black Color." Kapphiṇa (*Jiébīnná*) means "Room-constellation." Bakula (*Bójūluó*) means "Fine Appearance." Anuruddha (*Ānílóutuó*) means "As-wished." "Such great disciples and others": this is the concluding summary.

### The Bodhisattva Assembly

"Together with the bodhisattvas ... such great bodhisattvas": this is the bodhisattva assembly, with three parts: first, indicating the type; next, listing names; last, the concluding summary.

"Bodhisattva" (*púsà*) means "awakened being" (*juéshì*). "Awakening" has four aspects: (1) worthy of awakening, (2) correct awakening, (3) partial awakening, and (4) full awakening. "Being" (*shì*) has two meanings: (1) governance and (2) service. Using this awakening to govern and serve, transforming sentient beings: hence "awakened being" or "mahāsattva" (*mópósà*), "great awakened being." According to Paramārtha's explanation regarding stages: in the stages before the ten bhūmis (the ten faiths, ten understandings, and ten practices), "bodhisattva" refers to self-benefit; the dedication stages are "mahāsattva," referring to benefiting others. From the first bhūmi up through the seventh, one is called "bodhisattva"; from the eighth bhūmi onward, "mahāsattva." The *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* says: "greatness" has three meanings: (1) great vow, (2) great practice, and (3) great benefit.

"Mañjuśrī" (*Wénshū*) and following: this lists names. Mañjuśrī means "Wondrous Virtue" (*Miàodé*). As the son of the Dharma King, he will succeed to the honored position. Ajita (*Āyìduō*) means "Invincible." Gandhahasti (*Qiántuóhētí*) means "Red Color." Nityodyukta (*Chángjīngjìn*), "Ever-Vigorous," cultivates body and mind in the Way as urgently as if extinguishing a fire on one's head. "Together with such bodhisattvas": this is the concluding summary.

### The Deva Assembly

"Together with Śakra Devānām-indra and the great assemblies of devas": this lists the deva assembly. In full, it should read "Śakra Devānām-indra." "Indra" (*Yīntuóluó*) means "lord." "Devānām" (*Tíhuán*) means "heaven" or "of the devas." "Śakra" (*Shìjiā*) means "able." In total: "Able Lord of the Devas." "And the rest" includes the others.

## Specific Prologue

After the general prologue, the specific prologue [describes the occasion]. The specific prologue has six elements: (1) striking the capacity, (2) indicating the place, (3) the quantity, (4) the reward-environment, (5) the main fruit, and (6) the propagation. "Striking the capacity" is the Buddha addressing Śāriputra. "Indicating the place" is pointing out the West. "The quantity" is passing beyond ten trillion buddha-lands. "The reward-environment" is the world called "Ultimate Bliss" (*Jílè*). "The main fruit" is the buddha named Amitābha. "The propagation" is presently teaching the Dharma.

## The Main Teaching

### Chapter One: The Environment of the Pure Land

"Śāriputra! Why is that land called 'Ultimate Bliss'?"

This is a question. "The sentient beings of that land are free from all sufferings; therefore it is called 'Ultimate Bliss'": this is the answer. The answer has two parts: first, a summary answer establishing the thesis; second, an expanded explanation opening out the thesis.

This is the summary answer establishing the thesis. In Amitābha's land, there are no sufferings; there are only joyful things. Once born in that land, one horizontally cuts through the five destinies, transcends the three realms, and reaches non-retrogression, [which otherwise requires] initial-stage practice over countless kalpas. The *Daśabhūmika-vibhāṣā* says: "There are two ways of cultivating the path: (1) the difficult practice and (2) the easy practice. Cultivating the path in this [sahā] world is called the difficult practice. Cultivating the path in the Western [Pure Land] is called the easy practice. It is like a lame person walking overland, which is painful, versus riding a boat on the water, which is pleasant."

### The Five Adornments of the Environment

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! In the land of Ultimate Bliss, there are seven-tiered railings ... called 'Ultimate Bliss.'"

Below, the expanded explanation has five parts: (1) jeweled railings and groves, (2) jeweled pools and radiant flowers, (3) jeweled music and supernatural powers, (4) jeweled birds and Dharma-sounds, and (5) jeweled trees and breezes.

This is the first: jeweled railings and groves, with six aspects: jeweled railings, jeweled balustrades, jeweled gauze-nets, and jeweled grove adornments. Railings maintain inner virtue; balustrades guard against the outside. Gauze-nets pledge with pearl-strands the undefiled Dharma. Groves are the trees of merit that adorn the realm of the Western Pure Land.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! The land of Ultimate Bliss ... meritorious adornment."

This is the second: jeweled pools and radiant flowers, with eight aspects: jeweled pools, jeweled water, jeweled stairs, jeweled paths, jeweled towers, jeweled pavilions, jeweled flowers, and jeweled radiant adornments. The pools are the bathing-pools of the eight liberations. The water is the eight-virtued scented water. The towers are the lofty towers of the eight concentrations. The pavilions are the multi-storied pavilions of the three emptinesses. The flowers are the awakening-flowers of the seven purities. The radiance is the light of wisdom, able to shatter the darkness of doubt about rebirth.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! Heavenly music is constantly played ... meritorious adornment."

This is the third: jeweled music and supernatural power adornments, with three aspects: (1) music-making and flower-rain, (2) offering to buddhas, (3) concluding the adornment. The heavens shower four kinds of flowers at the six times; the people of the Pure Land offer to ten thousand buddhas in the ten directions. At dawn they make offerings there [in other buddha-lands]; by mealtime they have returned here [to the Pure Land] to walk in meditation.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! In that land there are always various kinds of birds ... transformation-produced."

This is the fourth: jeweled birds and Dharma-sounds, with six aspects: (1) a general description of the auspicious birds, (2) specifying their names, (3) their singing of Dharma-sounds, (4) the listeners' mindfulness of the Three Jewels, (5) dispelling a doubt, and (6) revealing the inconceivable.

"Various kinds of marvelous birds of many colors": a general description of the auspicious and wondrous multicolored birds. "White cranes, peacocks, and jīvañjīva birds [birds sharing one body]": specifying their names, briefly listing seven kinds of birds. "At the six times they sing and proclaim the faculties, powers, factors of awakening, and path factors": they sing of the Dharma. The Buddha constantly at six times observes beings in the six destinies, and the birds likewise at six times sing of the path-factors. "The sentient beings of that land, hearing these sounds, all become mindful of the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha": this describes the benefit of hearing. "Śāriputra! You must not think that these birds are truly born as karmic retribution ... how much less could there be actual [evil destinies]": this dispels the doubt. First, presenting the doubt. Below, the explanation: "That land does not even have the name of the three evil destinies, how much less their actuality." "These various birds ... are transformation-produced": this reveals the inconceivable. These birds are in fact Amitābha's transformations, made to proclaim the sounds of Dharma and adorn the realm for the benefit of sentient beings.

"Śāriputra! In that buddha-land, when a gentle breeze stirs ... such adornment."

This is the fifth: jeweled trees and breezes, with four aspects: (1) the wind stirring the trees' sound, (2) the sound like heavenly music, (3) hearing and being mindful of the Three Refuges, and (4) concluding the adornment. "A gentle breeze stirs the rows of jeweled trees": the wind stirs the jeweled trees, which is the wondrous sound of the Dharma-teaching. "Like a hundred thousand kinds of music, the sound like heavenly music": the simile of the wondrous sound. "Those who hear simultaneously become mindful of the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha": this describes the benefit of hearing the Three Refuges. "Accomplished with such meritorious adornments": concluding the adornment.

### Chapter Two: Amitābha's Attributes

"Śāriputra! What do you think? ... Amitābha?"

Below, the second chapter on Amitābha's attributes has four aspects: (1) question, (2) answer, (3) conclusion, and (4) determination of stage. This is the question: why is [this buddha] called "Amitābha"?

"Śāriputra! That buddha's light..."

This is the answer. The answer has three parts: (1) light, (2) lifespan, and (3) retinue. This [first part] is the immeasurable light.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! His lifespan ... already ten kalpas."

This describes the immeasurable lifespan. First the general, then the specific. Generally, the lifespan of his assembly is immeasurable. Specifically, it concerns only Amitābha's lifespan. Although his lifespan is ten kalpas, the worldly cannot reckon its measure; therefore it is called "Immeasurable Life."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! That buddha has immeasurable and boundless ... bodhisattvas likewise."

This describes the retinue. The retinue has two parts: (1) the śrāvaka assembly, namely the four [pairs of] stages and eight [degrees of] fruit; and (2) the bodhisattva assembly, namely the fifty-two worthies, both wielding the authority of provisional and real [teaching], sharing the principle of the one vehicle.

"Śāriputra! Accomplished with such meritorious adornments."

This is the summary conclusion. Light, lifespan, bodhisattvas, and śrāvakas, though differing in person and dharma, are all beyond calculation; therefore "immeasurable." Named from the original [cause]: hence "Immeasurable Life."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! Those sentient beings born in the land of Ultimate Bliss ... asaṃkhyeya kalpas to describe."

Next, the determination of stage, with three points: (1) that land is the stage of avaivartya [non-retrogression], indicating the strength of the cause; (2) there are many bodhisattvas at the stage of one more birth [before buddhahood], indicating the superiority of conditions; (3) it would take asaṃkhyeya kalpas to describe [the assembly], indicating the vastness of the assembly.

### Chapter Three: The Vow for Rebirth

"Śāriputra! Those sentient beings who hear this should resolve to be born ... all meeting in one place."

Below, the third chapter on the vow for rebirth. First a general statement, then an explanation. Beings with no definite prospect [of attainment], who take partial realization along the way, should set their aspiration on the ultimate fruit, so that they are not drawn into [premature] rebirth at intermediate points.

### Chapter Four: The Practice of Rebirth

"Śāriputra! One cannot be reborn in that land through few roots of goodness, few blessings, or few karmic conditions."

Below, the fourth chapter on the practice of rebirth. First a general statement, then an explanation, then a conclusion. This is the general statement of the practice. About to reveal that rebirth comes from long-planted roots of goodness, it says "not through few."

"Śāriputra! If a good man or good woman hears the name of Amitābha Buddha ... born in the land of Ultimate Bliss."

This explains the practice, with five aspects: (1) the capacity: good men and good women; (2) the basis: the name of Amitābha Buddha; (3) the time: seven days of single-minded [recitation]; (4) that buddha comes to receive: Amitābha and his holy assembly appear at the moment of death to welcome the practitioner; (5) rebirth there: with an undistorted mind, one immediately attains rebirth.

### Chapter Five: The Six-Direction Buddhas' Attestation

"Śāriputra! I perceive this benefit ... inconceivable merit."

This is the conclusion. First concluding the earlier vow, then concluding the later practice. Once reborn in that land, one necessarily ascends to non-retrogression, horizontally cutting through the five destinies and directly transcending the three realms. Though the river of birth-and-death is dark, it will not be long before one crosses; though the path to nirvāṇa is far, it too can be ascended. Therefore: "I perceive this benefit." "Śāriputra! As I now ... praise the inconceivable merit": this concludes the later practice, and also serves as a bridge from what precedes to the attestation that follows.

"In the East, there is also Akṣobhya Buddha ... up to the upper direction ... 'Sūtra Protected and Mindfully Held by All Buddhas.'"

The fifth chapter: the buddhas of the six directions manifest to attest. The six directions are the four cardinal directions plus above and below, showing the places of attestation. The buddhas are the buddhas of the three times and three bodies, innumerable as the sands of the Ganges: the persons who attest. Manifesting means extending the tongue to cover the three-thousand[-fold world]: the form of attestation. Attesting means speaking words of truth: the teaching that attests. The text presents six directions in six sections, each direction having four aspects. The four of the East are: (1) the place: the East; (2) the persons: Akṣobhya and other buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges; (3) the form: the tongue covering the three-thousand[-fold world]; (4) the teaching: "you should have faith in this sūtra." A disciple's sūtra cites disciples as witnesses; a buddha's sūtra cites buddhas as witnesses. Therefore, the buddhas of the six directions, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, extend their tongues and speak truthfully, [attesting] that one who is mindful of Amitābha in a single lifetime will certainly be reborn in the Land of Ultimate Bliss and reach non-retrogression. The four aspects of the remaining five directions can be understood by analogy.

### Chapters Six through Ten

"Śāriputra! What do you think? ... 'Sūtra Protected and Mindfully Held [by All Buddhas].'"

The sixth chapter: explaining the reason for the attestation. First a question, then an answer. This is the question.

"Śāriputra! If a good man or good woman ... [attain] the three [kinds of] bodhi."

This is the answer. Simply hearing this sūtra's name, or the names of Amitābha and the other buddhas, and being able to uphold them without forgetting: one is protected by all the buddhas, and one does not regress from bodhi.

"Therefore, Śāriputra! All of you should believe and accept my words and what all the buddhas have spoken."

The seventh chapter: the three-period vow. The text has two parts: (1) the command to listen and accept with faith; (2) the actual three-period vow. This is the command to listen and accept with faith. "My words" means Śākyamuni's exhortation to be reborn in the Pure Land. "What all the buddhas have spoken" means the innumerable buddhas of the six directions who attest and encourage rebirth.

"Śāriputra! If anyone has already resolved, now resolves, or will in the future resolve ... [attain] the three [kinds of] bodhi."

This is the actual three-period vow. "Already resolved": Avalokiteśvara, Mahāsthāmaprāpta, Nāgārjuna, and their like. "Now resolving": those in the present who accept with faith. "Will resolve in the future": those in future ages who will believe and seek rebirth. "Wishing to be born in Amitābha's land": this is the destination of the three-period vow. "Attaining non-retrogression from bodhi": once born there, where the ground is superior and conditions are strong, one necessarily ascends to non-retrogression from supreme bodhi.

"Born in that land ... born in that land."

The eighth chapter: the three-period rebirth. "Already born": Avalokiteśvara, Mahāsthāmaprāpta, and their like. "Now being born": those in the present who accept with faith. "Will be born": those in the future who accept with faith and seek rebirth. Since both earlier sages and later saints all vow to be born there, seeing the worthy and wishing to equal them, those with faith should resolve to be born in that land. The reason all the buddhas so earnestly exhort [beings] to return to the West alone is that rebirth there assures non-retrogression.

"Śāriputra! As I now ... the inconceivable merit."

The ninth chapter: this Buddha [Śākyamuni] praises that [Amitābha]. That is, the Tathāgata Śākyamuni praises the Pure Land fruit of Amitābha and the buddhas.

"Those buddhas and the rest ... most rare and difficult deed."

The tenth chapter: those buddhas [of the six directions] praise this [Śākyamuni] for achieving the Way in the defiled sahā world and being able to perform what is difficult. The difficult deed is teaching and transforming sentient beings in the five turbidities of the evil age, causing them to believe, turn to the Pure Land, and attain non-retrogression.

The text has three levels: (1) the thesis, (2) the explanation, and (3) the conclusion. The thesis is "a most rare and difficult deed." "Most rare" has three sections. The explanation: the five turbidities, bodhi, and speaking for beings. The conclusion brings these three together again. The thesis has a general and a specific part. Generally, it indicates that those buddhas praise my [Śākyamuni's] merit. Specifically, "most rare and difficult" comprises three aspects: "most difficult" refers to the five turbidities and the evil age; "most rare" refers to supreme bodhi; "deed" refers to speaking for sentient beings, that is, cultivating causes in the defiled land and obtaining the Pure Land's fruit. This is the dharma difficult to realize.

"Able in the sahā world ... a dharma that is difficult to believe."

The explanation of the three sections: the five turbidities explains the "most difficult" section; attaining bodhi explains the "most rare" section; speaking for beings explains the "deed" section.

"Śāriputra! You should know that I in this ... is most difficult."

The conclusion of the three sections: the five turbidities and attaining bodhi, two sections, conclude self-benefit; speaking for all the world, one section, concludes benefiting others.

## Conclusion

"When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra ... joyfully believed, accepted, and practiced accordingly."

This is the transmission section. The two saints [Śākyamuni and Amitābha] face each other in dialogue; the four assemblies receive the benefit. With deep hearts they rejoiced, believed, accepted, and practiced accordingly.
