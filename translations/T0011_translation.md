# 尼拘陀梵志經
## The Sūtra of the Brahmin Nigrodha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0011

Translated by Dānapāla and others during the Song dynasty

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the World-Honored One was residing at Rājagṛha in the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove Monastery. At that time, in that city there was a householder named Sandhāna. One day, having finished his meal, he set out from Rājagṛha and proceeded toward the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove Monastery to pay his respects and draw near to the Buddha, the World-Honored One. Yet he had not gone far when this thought arose in him: "Today the early morning hours have already passed; the Buddha and the bhikṣus are each resting in their own quarters. It would be fitting to pause and not go to the Buddha's presence to pay my respects. I shall instead go to the assembly place of the brahmin Nigrodha."

At that time, the brahmin was residing in the Udumbarikā Garden, dwelling there surrounded by a company of brahmins. They were raising their voices in various discourses: discourses on kings, discourses on warfare, discourses on thieves; discourses on clothing, discourses on food, discourses on women; discourses on wine, discourses on perverse views, discourses confused and entangled; and so on, even to discourses comparing the oceans. All such discourses bound the hearts of worldly people.

At that time the brahmin Nigrodha, seeing from afar that householder Sandhāna was approaching from outside, immediately addressed the assembly: "Quiet! Quiet! Each of you lower your voices. The one approaching is a disciple of the śramaṇa Gautama, a śrāvaka who is a great householder of Rājagṛha by the name of Sandhāna. This person is by nature a man of few words, and what he has received and transmitted is likewise peaceful and still. Therefore each of you speak softly; once he knows that we are composed, he may then come near."

When the assembly of brahmins heard these words, they all fell into silence.

Then householder Sandhāna came to the brahmin Nigrodha's assembly. Upon his arrival, Nigrodha came forward to receive him, and they exchanged joyful words. When their exchange was finished, each took a seat to one side. Then householder Sandhāna addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "This assembly of yours is something remarkable. Just now I heard you all raising your voices in various discourses: discourses on kings, discourses on warfare, and so on, even to discourses comparing the oceans. All such discourses bind the hearts of worldly people. How different from my World-Honored One, the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha! The Buddha, the World-Honored One, dwells in the wilderness, sitting and reclining wherever he pleases, far removed from clamor, free of human footprints, abiding in quietude as his natural state. His body dwells in one place; his mind does not scatter; focused one-pointedly, he conducts himself as is fitting."

Then the brahmin Nigrodha addressed householder Sandhāna: "Householder! What would there be for me to discuss with that śramaṇa Gautama? If I were to put a question to him, his varied wisdom would be unable to turn, for the śramaṇa Gautama dwells in an empty dwelling: how can wisdom turn? Since his wisdom cannot turn even in an empty dwelling, how much less when he sits and reclines in the wilderness, far from clamor, free of human footprints, abiding in quietude as his natural state, his body in one place, his mind undistracted, focused on a single object, conducting himself as is fitting. Householder, consider a one-eyed ox circling along the boundary: how far can such an ox travel? The śramaṇa Gautama is just like this. Dwelling in an empty dwelling, how can his wisdom turn? Householder, if the śramaṇa Gautama were to come to this assembly, I would then engage him in discussion. I would establish a supreme argument, raise a single point of inquiry, and strike at him. I would surely prevail, and he would certainly fall in defeat, like striking an empty vessel, easily shattered to pieces."

At that time, the World-Honored One was in his own quarters, sitting in silent meditative composure. With his purified divine ear, he heard from afar the words exchanged between householder Sandhāna and the brahmin Nigrodha in their assembled gathering.

Then, in the afternoon, the World-Honored One departed from his quarters. The rain had just cleared, and sunlight shone bright and resplendent. He gradually made his way to the Pool Free from Poison and, upon reaching its bank, walked slowly back and forth in meditation. At that time the brahmin Nigrodha, seeing from afar the World-Honored One on the bank of the Pool Free from Poison walking slowly back and forth, immediately addressed his assembly: "The śramaṇa Gautama is now on the bank of the Pool Free from Poison, walking slowly in meditation. If he should come to this assembly, how shall you receive him? Shall you rise to welcome him? Or exchange words with him? Or merely rise from your seats? Or shall you vacate your own seats and invite him?" As these words were being spoken, someone naturally came and laid out a seat for the Buddha, the World-Honored One, and these words were heard: "Venerable Gautama, there is a seat here; please come and sit at your pleasure."

Then the World-Honored One, having completed his walking meditation on the bank of the Pool Free from Poison, came to the brahmin Nigrodha's place. When the brahmin saw from afar the World-Honored One approaching from outside, he addressed his assembly: "When the śramaṇa Gautama arrives at this assembly, I shall put my question: 'O Gautama, within your Dharma and discipline, what practice enables those who cultivate the śrāvaka path to reach a place of safety and security, to still the mind within, and to purify the holy life?'"

When the World-Honored One arrived at that assembly, all the assembled brahmins spontaneously gave rise to joy and delight, each rising from their seats to come forward in welcome. Then the brahmin Nigrodha, joining his palms toward the Buddha and bowing his head in reverence, said: "Welcome, Gautama! You are endowed with omniscience. Here is your seat; please take it." The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Simply take your own seat. As for the seat that has been set up for me, I myself know; I shall seat myself accordingly."

At that moment the assembled brahmins raised their voices in chorus: "How rare, how extraordinary is this śramaṇa Gautama! In this assembly, with no one indicating it to him, through supernormal power he knows his own seat." Then the brahmin Nigrodha exchanged joyful words with the Buddha, the World-Honored One, and when their exchange was finished, he withdrew and sat to one side.

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Now that the Tathāgata has come to this assembly, what discussions have you all been engaged in?" The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "When I saw you coming from afar, I immediately told the assembly: 'When the śramaṇa Gautama arrives at this assembly, I shall put my question: "O Gautama, within your Dharma and discipline, what practice enables those who cultivate the śrāvaka path to reach a place of safety and security, to still the mind within, and to purify the holy life?"' Now that you, Gautama, have arrived, I raise this very matter as my point of inquiry; this is the discussion I wish to have with you."

Then the World-Honored One addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, this matter would truly be difficult for you to understand. Why so? Because of different Dharma, different views, different teacher, different practice; it would be appropriate to raise questions within your own Dharma and teaching."

At that moment the assembled brahmins raised their voices in chorus: "How rare, how extraordinary is the śramaṇa Gautama! When asked this question, he does not answer from his own teaching, but instead bids the questioner to raise questions from within the other's own teaching, so that whatever is asked he will address accordingly."

Then the brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "If my Dharma, views, teacher, and practice are different, and within your Dharma and discipline it is difficult for me to understand, then I shall ask you now within my own Dharma and teaching: how does one practice to attain liberation and purity, the highest cleanliness, truth, and dwelling within pure truth?"

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Regarding what is practiced within your teaching, Nigrodha, I will speak briefly. You claim to fulfill the fourfold precepts, to cultivate practice, to attain the highest excellence, and to surpass the previous cultivation of renunciation, yet without diminishing desires. Nigrodha, what is this fourfold precept that you practice and uphold? It is: not killing by oneself, not instructing others to kill, not rejoicing in killing; not stealing by oneself, not instructing others to steal, not rejoicing in stealing; not lying by oneself, not instructing others to lie, not rejoicing in lying; not engaging in sexual misconduct by oneself, not instructing others to do so, not rejoicing therein. You, Nigrodha, thus hold: 'By such means I fulfill the fourfold precepts.'

"Nigrodha, what is the practice that you claim to cultivate? What you practice consists of: wandering about and abiding in high places, setting up seats, or standing on tiptoe, as religious conduct; or constantly consuming bitter, astringent, and coarse food and drink, as religious conduct; or dwelling in silent, empty places, as religious conduct; or not removing the beard and hair, as religious conduct; or lying upon thorns and brambles, or lying on planked frames, as religious conduct; or constantly dwelling in lofty, cloud-piercing elevated places, as religious conduct; or binding oneself to one place, as religious conduct; and even bathing the body three times daily. Such manifold forms of painful mortification applied to the body, as religious conduct. All such matters, Nigrodha, constitute your practice.

"What do you who practice, Nigrodha, regard as renunciation? Among the renunciation practices you cultivate, Nigrodha: going about with the body naked, claiming this as renunciation; after finishing one's meal, licking the hands clean; not accepting food and drink offered by one with a frowning face or an angry face; not eating in streets or alleyways; not dwelling near swords, staves, or weapons. Traveling through cities and villages in complete silence: not speaking of where one has come from, not speaking of where one is going, not speaking of where one stays; uttering neither words of agreement nor disagreement, uttering no variety of words, with nothing to teach or impart. Or accepting food from one household; or from two, three, or up to seven households; or accepting from only one household and no other; or not eating for one day; or not eating for two or three days, even up to seven days, or even half a month or a full month; or in one's food, not eating parched grain, not eating boiled rice, not eating legumes, fish, or meat; not consuming cow's milk, clarified butter, curd, oil, or honey; not drinking wine, not drinking sweet juices, not drinking sour juices, but drinking only the clear water of husks and bran to sustain one's life. Also constantly eating vegetables; or eating wild grasses and millets; or eating cow dung; or eating medicinal sprouts and medicinal roots; or eating dried and raw grain; or eating other coarse and inferior grasses and vegetables. Or wearing only one garment; or wearing a garment of grass; or wearing a garment of kuśa grass; or wearing a garment of tree bark; or wearing firewood as clothing; or wearing fruit-tree bark as clothing; or wearing a garment made of tangled hair gathered from charnel grounds; or wearing a garment of sheep's wool, deer's fur, or deer hide; or wearing a garment of tiriṭa bird wings; or wearing a garment of owl wings. All such matters, Nigrodha, are the renunciation practices cultivated by your practitioners.

"Nigrodha, do these practices truly and actually attain liberation and purity? Attain the highest cleanliness? Attain truth? Attain dwelling within pure truth?"

Then the brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Just so, just so, śramaṇa Gautama! These practices of mine attain liberation and purity, attain the highest cleanliness, attain truth, and attain dwelling within pure truth."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, all these matters that you practice do not constitute renunciation, do not attain liberation and purity, do not attain the highest cleanliness, do not attain truth, and do not attain dwelling within pure truth; within the practice they obtain but a small portion."

The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Śramaṇa Gautama, though what you say seems excellent, this practice of mine attains the highest renunciation, attains truth, and is unsurpassable."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Furthermore, Nigrodha, concerning your practice: you claim to fulfill the fourfold precepts, to cultivate practice, and to attain the highest excellence, surpassing the previous cultivation of renunciation, yet without diminishing desires. While upholding the fourfold precepts, you are accompanied by a mind of loving-kindness. First, you give rise to loving-kindness toward the east, contemplating its complete pervasion: vast and all-encompassing, without duality, without limit, without enmity, without harm. Then toward the south, west, and north, the four intermediate directions, above and below, throughout all worlds, accompanied by loving-kindness, complete in conduct and pervasion, likewise in all ways. Nigrodha, do you hold the view that by practicing thus you attain liberation and purity? Attain the highest cleanliness? Attain truth? Attain dwelling within pure truth?"

Then the brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Just so, just so, śramaṇa Gautama! This practice of mine truly attains liberation and purity, attains the highest cleanliness, attains truth, and attains dwelling within pure truth."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, all these matters as you practice them do not attain liberation and purity, do not attain the highest cleanliness, do not attain truth, and do not attain dwelling within pure truth. What you claim to have attained is also not genuine."

The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Śramaṇa Gautama, though what you say seems excellent, this practice of mine attains liberation and purity, attains truth, and is unsurpassable."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, furthermore, concerning your practice: you claim to fulfill the fourfold precepts, to cultivate practice, and to attain the highest excellence, surpassing the previous cultivation of renunciation, yet without diminishing desires. Through the supernormal knowledge of past abodes, you claim to know events from one, two, or three past lives up to a hundred past lives. Nigrodha, do you hold the view that by practicing thus you attain liberation and purity? Attain the highest cleanliness? Attain truth? Attain dwelling within pure truth?"

Then the brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Just so, just so, śramaṇa Gautama! This practice of mine truly attains liberation and purity, attains the highest cleanliness, attains truth, and attains dwelling within pure truth."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, all these matters as you practice them do not attain liberation and purity, do not attain the highest cleanliness, do not attain truth, and do not attain dwelling within pure truth. Though there is something attained, it is not genuine."

The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Śramaṇa Gautama, though what you say seems excellent, this practice of mine attains liberation and purity, attains truth, and is unsurpassable."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, furthermore, concerning your practice: you claim to fulfill the fourfold precepts, to cultivate practice, and to attain the highest excellence, surpassing the previous cultivation of renunciation, yet without diminishing desires. With the purified divine eye you observe all sentient beings in the world: their arising and ceasing, the beautiful and the ugly, some being reborn in good destinies, some in evil destinies, the noble and the lowly, each reaping the retribution of their karma; all are fully observed and seen. Nigrodha, do you hold the view that by practicing thus you attain liberation and purity? Attain the highest cleanliness? Attain truth? Attain dwelling within pure truth?"

Then the brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Just so, just so, śramaṇa Gautama! This practice of mine truly attains liberation and purity, attains the highest cleanliness, attains truth, and attains dwelling within pure truth."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, all these matters, as practiced by you, are pure in a certain respect; yet what I say is this: as you practice, you have not departed from various defilements that proliferate."

The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Śramaṇa Gautama, how is it that my practice, though pure in your account, has not departed from various defilements that proliferate?"

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, in your practice you wish to display the merit of your cultivation, thinking: 'Because I have accomplished such conduct, kings, ministers, kṣatriyas, and brāhmaṇas will surely honor, revere, and make offerings to me.' Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, relying on your own cultivation, you give rise to a prideful attitude, disparaging and belittling others. This is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you give rise to conceit and overweening pride. This is a proliferating defilement in your practice. Furthermore, Nigrodha, you who practice go about belittling and insulting other śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, saying: 'You śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas sustain yourselves with manifold foods; you broadly consume the five kinds of seeds in the world, namely root seeds, trunk seeds, hollow seeds, topmost seeds, and seeds-within-seeds; these five kinds serve as your sustenance.' You, Nigrodha, travel about thus, uttering words of disparagement, seeking out disputes, swift and sharp as lightning, crushing and destroying like frost and hail. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, when you see other śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas being honored, revered, and given offerings by their own communities and companions, you give rise to various minds of envy and jealousy, and say: 'You śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, who crave manifold foods to sustain yourselves, are yet honored, revered, and given offerings by others. I constantly sustain myself only on bitter, astringent, empty, and insipid things. Why, then, do others not honor and make offerings to me, Nigrodha?' This is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, when you come before the Tathāgata or the Tathāgata's disciples to present questions, resentment and hostility swiftly arise. When anger and vexation arise, they create obstruction; and through obstruction, faults and transgressions are incurred. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, when you come before the Tathāgata or the Tathāgata's disciples to inquire about the true Dharma, and the Buddha, the Tathāgata, with one-pointed mind skillfully expounds and explains with decisiveness and full appropriateness, dispelling doubts, you and your companions then cite outside teachings and point to them, contradicting and seeking to seize the reasoning, claiming instead that the question was not properly analyzed. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, knowing that the Buddha, the Tathāgata, or the Tathāgata's disciples truly possess the highest and most excellent virtue worthy of reverence and honor, you yet do not honor them. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, there are those in practice who give rise to aversion toward beneficial things, yet do not give rise to aversion toward harmful things. In these two cases, if toward harmful things you do not give rise to aversion, Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you give rise to a prideful display, manifesting outwardly: 'I am capable of practice.' Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, when you obtain fine and exquisite food and drink, you become attached to the flavor and generate discrimination: 'This I like; this I do not like. What I like, I may accept.' Through such clinging and grasping, craving and attachment arise; through craving and attachment, faults are concealed; and thus superior wisdom cannot attain liberation. As for remaining food that you do not care for, you remain greedy and reluctant, hesitating before setting it aside. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, seated in a deep and secluded place, with an outwardly virtuous appearance, sitting in silence, when someone comes and asks: 'What Dharma do you understand? What Dharma do you not understand?' you and your companions, regarding what you do understand, claim not to understand; regarding what you do not understand, claim to understand. In many such matters you profess right knowledge while speaking falsely. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you constantly give rise to anger, resentment, and spite. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, in every circumstance you are without shame and without conscience. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you are constantly slack and possessed of feeble diligence. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you constantly lose mindfulness and lack clear comprehension. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice the mind is scattered and the faculties are diminished and dull. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you give rise to harm, reinforcing prior intentions without seeking liberation, seeing only from your own perspective, genuinely generating clinging to such dharmas. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice wrong views run deep and thick, and you engage in perverted conduct. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you regard what is without limit as limited, and the views you hold are likewise. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you constantly give rise to craving and desire, and to a mind of aversion. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice, regarding all that you do, you are foolish, deluded, and dim-witted. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you are unable to listen and receive, like one who is deaf, with nothing to say or demonstrate, like a mute sheep. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you delight in performing sinful deeds, and you also delight in associating with those who commit sinful deeds, bound and under the sway of evil companions. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice.

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, in your practice you give rise to overweening pride, entertaining notions of attainment: claiming to have seen what has not been seen, claiming to have accomplished what has not been accomplished, claiming to have attained what has not been attained, claiming to know what has not been known, claiming to have realized what has not been realized. Nigrodha, this is a proliferating defilement in your practice. Nigrodha, what do you think? Among all the defilements described above, are there practitioners of a certain kind who possess all these things?"

The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Śramaṇa Gautama, not only one kind of practitioner possesses all these defilements; in my estimation, there are very many."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "As I said above, your practice aims at displaying the merit of your cultivation, thinking: 'Because I have accomplished such conduct, kings, ministers, kṣatriyas, and brāhmaṇas will honor, revere, and make offerings to me.' Nigrodha, if you practice thus, aiming to display the merit of your cultivation so that kings, ministers, and others will honor and make offerings, and even rising to overweening pride, entertaining notions of attainment: claiming to have seen what has not been seen, claiming to have accomplished what has not been accomplished, claiming to know what has not been known, claiming to have attained what has not been attained, claiming to have realized what has not been realized; all such matters are impure, every one of them constitutes a proliferating defilement, and know that they are all subsumed within the domain of defilement. Nigrodha, what do you think? All these matters, such practice: does it attain liberation and purity? Attain the highest cleanliness? Attain truth? Attain dwelling within pure truth?"

The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Just so, just so, śramaṇa Gautama! We who practice in this manner attain liberation and purity, attain the highest cleanliness, attain truth, and attain dwelling within pure truth."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "I shall now speak to you with complete truthfulness. Regarding your earlier question to me: 'Śramaṇa Gautama, within your Dharma and discipline, what practice enables those who cultivate the śrāvaka path to reach a place of safety and security, to still the mind within, and to purify the holy life?' That question is a genuine one. Know that the place of stillness for śrāvakas, whether of the lesser, middling, or highest degree, is supremely lofty and excellent: the place of stillness of all the noble ones."

Then all the assembled brahmins together offered praise: "Wonderful! Wonderful! Within the Dharma and discipline of the śramaṇa Gautama, what is cultivated is truly cool and refreshing."

At that time, householder Sandhāna, upon hearing these words, perceived that the assembled brahmins had begun to turn their hearts slightly in admiration toward the Buddha, the World-Honored One. He addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, you said earlier that you would engage in discussion with the Buddha, the World-Honored One, establish a supreme argument, raise a single point of inquiry, and strike at him; that you would surely prevail and he would certainly fall in defeat, like striking an empty vessel, easily shattered to pieces. Why then have you not raised your question?"

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "What do you think: did you truly once say such words?"

The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Śramaṇa Gautama, I truly did say such words."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, have you not heard the teachers of antiquity, the elder sages, the aged and venerable, the learned and wise, say that the Buddhas, Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas would, like you and your assembly now, gather together, raise their voices, and engage in various discourses: discourses on kings, discourses on warfare, discourses on thieves; discourses on clothing, discourses on food, discourses on women; discourses on wine, discourses on perverse views, discourses confused and entangled; and so on, even to discourses comparing the oceans? Nigrodha, have you also ever heard the teachers of antiquity say that the Buddhas, Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas, like myself at this very time, dwell in the wilderness, sitting and reclining, far from clamor, free of human footprints, abiding in quietude as their natural state, the body in one place, the mind not scattered, focused one-pointedly, conducting themselves as is fitting?"

The brahmin Nigrodha addressed the Buddha: "Just so, Gautama! I too have heard the teachers of antiquity, the elder sages, the aged and venerable, the learned and wise, say that the Buddhas, Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas do not, like us now, gather together, raise their voices, and engage in various discourses: discourses on kings, discourses on warfare, discourses on thieves; discourses on clothing, discourses on food, discourses on women; discourses on wine, discourses on perverse views, discourses confused and entangled; and so on, even to discourses comparing the oceans. I have also heard the teachers of antiquity say that the Buddhas, Tathāgatas, Arhats, Samyaksaṃbuddhas, like yourself at this very time, dwell in the wilderness, sitting and reclining, far from clamor, free of human footprints, abiding in quietude as their natural state, the body in one place, the mind not scattered, focused one-pointedly, conducting themselves as is fitting."

The Buddha addressed the brahmin Nigrodha: "Nigrodha, when you formerly heard your ancient teachers speak, did you not think thus: 'Those Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, are able to teach the Dharma as is fitting; having themselves awakened, they then teach the Dharma of awakening to others; having themselves been liberated, they teach the Dharma of liberation to others; having themselves attained safety, they teach the Dharma of safety to others; having themselves attained nirvāṇa, they teach the Dharma of nirvāṇa to others.' Yet at that time, Nigrodha, you instead said: 'The śramaṇa Gautama, speaking thus, makes distinctions regarding the practices of our teachers' Dharma.' And again you said: 'The śramaṇa Gautama, speaking thus, makes distinctions regarding the practice of dwelling in stillness.' And again you said: 'The śramaṇa Gautama, speaking thus, claims that unwholesome and blameworthy dharmas have been accumulated within Nigrodha's teachers' Dharma.' And again you said: 'The śramaṇa Gautama, speaking thus, claims that the many wholesome dharmas have been scattered and dispersed within Nigrodha's teachers' Dharma.' And again you said: 'The śramaṇa Gautama speaks thus in order to proclaim those matters of causes and conditions.' Such various statements as these should not be viewed in this way.

"Nigrodha, why should they not be viewed in this way? It is because those teachers' dharmas, those practices, and those matters of causes and conditions are all entirely different. Therefore, Nigrodha, I do not speak of those teachers' practices of Dharma, nor do I speak of those practices of dwelling in stillness, nor do I say that unwholesome and blameworthy dharmas have been accumulated within those teachers' Dharma, nor do I say that the many wholesome dharmas have been scattered and dispersed within those teachers' Dharma, nor do I wish to speak of those matters of causes and conditions.

"Nigrodha, I always speak thus: 'If there is a noble person who is without flattery, without crookedness, and without deceit, one who practices rightly, I will teach and instruct that noble person, guiding them as is appropriate. If that noble person, in accordance with my right teaching and right instruction, for seven years, or six, five, four, three, two, or one year, remains single-minded and undistracted, free from all fevers, with body and mind pure, with focused aspiration and pursuit, then I declare that such a person, seeing the Dharma and knowing the Dharma, transcending the first two fruits, advances directly to the third, the fruit of anāgāmin at the stage with remainder.'

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, I always speak thus: 'If there is a noble person who is without flattery, without crookedness, and without deceit, one who practices rightly, I will teach and instruct that noble person, guiding them as is appropriate. If that noble person, in accordance with my right teaching and right instruction, for seven months, or six, five, four, three, two, or one month, or half a month, remains single-minded and undistracted, free from all fevers, with body and mind pure, with focused aspiration and pursuit, then I declare that such a person, seeing the Dharma and knowing the Dharma, transcending the first two fruits, advances directly to the third, the fruit of anāgāmin at the stage with remainder.'

"Furthermore, Nigrodha, I always speak thus: 'If there is a noble person who is without flattery, without crookedness, and without deceit, one who practices rightly, I will teach and instruct that noble person, guiding them as is appropriate. If that noble person, in accordance with my right teaching and right instruction, for seven days, or six, five, four, three, two, or one day, or half a day, or even during the time before or after a meal, remains single-minded and undistracted, free from all fevers, with body and mind pure, with focused aspiration and pursuit, then I declare that such a person, seeing the Dharma and knowing the Dharma, transcending the first two fruits, advances directly to the third, the fruit of anāgāmin at the stage with remainder.'"

At that time, when the World-Honored One had spoken thus, all the brahmins assembled there, being deeply burdened by obstructions, could understand nothing. Their bodies and minds were confused and bewildered, sunken in torpor and darkness. Their eloquence could not be exercised; they could make no response, and with bowed heads they sat in silence, dwelling in anxious thought.

At that time, the World-Honored One, knowing this state of affairs, turned and addressed householder Sandhāna: "Householder! These people here are truly foolish. Their seeing and hearing are obscured, and their speech is entirely cut off, like a person who has stuffed their own mouth to silence. Their defilements and pollution run deep; this is the great affair of Māra. They are unable to put this question to the Buddha, the Tathāgata: 'O śramaṇa, within your Dharma and Vinaya, by what practice are those who cultivate the śrāvaka path brought to a place of safety, with the inner mind stilled and the holy life purified?'"

At that time, the World-Honored One then taught the Dharma to householder Sandhāna as was fitting, instructing, benefiting, and gladdening him. Then, emitting from his body a radiant light, vast and blazing, illuminating everywhere, he rose bodily into the sky from the midst of the assembly and returned to the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove Monastery.
