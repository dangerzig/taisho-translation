# 佛說五王經
## Sūtra of the Five Kings, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0523

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Long ago, there were five kings whose territories bordered one another. They visited back and forth, never attacking or waging war against each other, but only cultivating good friendship.

The greatest among them was named King Universal Peace. He practiced the bodhisattva path. The other four minor kings constantly followed wrong practices. The great king, out of compassion, wished to guide them. He invited them to his palace, where they entertained one another for seven days, with singing and music day and night without cease. When the seven days had passed, the four kings addressed the great king: "The affairs of our kingdoms are many. We beg leave to return to our governance."

The great king said to his attendants: "Prepare the carriages. Let all the ministers, officials, and people go together to see them off."

When they reached the halfway point, the great king, out of compassion and wishing to guide them, said to the four kings: "Each of you, describe the thing in which you take the most pleasure."

One king said: "I wish I could always enjoy the third month of spring, when the trees are lush with flowers, and roam and play in the open fields. This is my pleasure."

Another king said: "My pleasure is different. I wish I could always be a king, with fine horses and elegant attire, towered halls and grand palaces, officers and people surrounding me on all sides, with dazzling splendor, bells struck and drums beaten, and wherever I come and go, the people on the road turning their heads in awe. This is my pleasure."

Another king said: "My pleasure is different still. I wish I could have a beautiful wife and fine children, of matchless beauty, to enjoy ourselves together to the fullest. This is my pleasure."

The last king said: "My pleasure is different yet again. I wish my parents could always be with me, and that I might have many brothers, wives, and children, all gathered together, with fine clothing and delicious food to satisfy our appetites, with pure zithers and elegant garments, to enjoy ourselves together. This is my pleasure."

When each had described his pleasure, the four kings together turned to the great king and asked: "What does the king find pleasurable?"

The great king answered: "First, let me respond to each of your pleasures, and then I shall describe my own. One of you said, 'The third month of spring, when the trees are lush with flowers, to roam and play in the open fields.' But in autumn, the leaves wither and fall. This is not an enduring pleasure.

"One of you said, 'I wish always to be a king, with fine horses and elegant attire, towered halls and grand palaces, officers and people surrounding me, with dazzling splendor, bells struck and drums beaten, and wherever I come and go, the people on the road turning their heads in awe.' But the ancient kings of old, in all their pomp and thundering grandeur, enjoyed happiness without limit, until their merit was exhausted. Their kingdoms were conquered by rivals, and they suddenly perished. This is not an enduring pleasure.

"One of you said, 'I wish to have a beautiful wife and fine children, of matchless beauty, to enjoy ourselves together to the fullest.' But when one morning illness strikes, the grief is immeasurable. This is not an enduring pleasure.

"One of you said, 'I wish my parents could always be with me, and many brothers, wives, and children, all gathered together, with fine clothing and delicious food, pure zithers and elegant garments, to enjoy ourselves together.' But when one morning trouble comes and one is seized by the authorities, imprisoned and shut away, with no one to rescue you, this is not an enduring pleasure."

The four asked together: "What does the king find pleasurable?"

The king said: "My pleasure is in what is not born and does not die, what does not suffer and is not afflicted, what does not hunger and does not thirst, what does not feel cold and does not feel heat, where existence and non-existence are sovereign at will. This is my pleasure."

The four kings said together: "Where is this pleasure to be found? There must be a wise teacher."

The great king answered: "My teacher is called the Buddha. He is nearby, at the Jetavana Monastery."

The kings were overjoyed. They all went to the Buddha's dwelling and bowed their heads in reverence, then sat to one side. The great king knelt and, with palms joined, said to the Buddha: "We have now been fortunate enough to be born as humans, yet we are dull and without wisdom, deeply attached only to worldly pleasure and ignorant of sin and merit. We wish the Buddha would teach us, his disciples, the truth of suffering."

The Buddha said: "Listen well! I shall teach you. A human being, living in the world, is constantly afflicted by innumerable sufferings pressing upon the body. Now I shall briefly describe for you the eight sufferings. What are the eight sufferings? The suffering of birth, the suffering of old age, the suffering of sickness, the suffering of death, the suffering of parting from those we love, the suffering of not obtaining what we seek, the suffering of encountering those we hate, and the suffering of grief, sorrow, and affliction. These are the eight sufferings.

"What is the suffering of birth? When a person dies, the spirit does not know what destiny it is heading for. Not yet having obtained a place of rebirth, it takes on the form of an intermediate being. At the third week, the father and mother come together, and the spirit comes to be conceived. In the first week it is like thin whey. In the second week it is like thick curd. In the third week it is like coagulated butter. In the fourth week it is like a lump of flesh. By the fifth week, the five limbs are formed. The skillful wind enters the abdomen and blows upon the body; the six sense-faculties open and extend. Within the mother's belly, beneath the raw entrails and above the cooked, when the mother eats a cup of hot food, it scalds the body like being plunged into a cauldron of boiling water. When the mother drinks a cup of cold water, it is like icy cold cutting into the body. When the mother is full, the body is pressed and squeezed; the pain is beyond words. When the mother is hungry, the abdomen is empty and hollow, and it is like being hung upside down. The suffering is immeasurable. When the full month arrives and the time of birth comes, the head faces the birth canal. It is like being squeezed between two rocks crushing a mountain. At the moment of birth, the mother is in peril and the father in terror. Falling upon the grass, the body is soft and tender. The grass touches the body like treading on swords and blades. Suddenly the infant cries aloud. Is this not suffering?"

All the people answered: "This is great suffering."

"What is the suffering of old age? One's parents raise and nurture one until one is grown. One relies on one's strength and health, carrying light and heavy loads without measuring one's capacity. In cold, one endures extreme cold; in heat, extreme heat; when hungry, extreme hunger; when full, extreme excess, without any moderation. Gradually one reaches old age: the hair turns white, the teeth fall out, the eyes grow dim, the ears grow dull. Vitality departs and decline arrives. The skin loosens and the face wrinkles. All the joints ache. Walking is extremely laborious. Sitting and rising are accompanied by groans. The heart is filled with grief and affliction. The consciousness gradually fades and dims; one forgets the instant one turns around. Each day the remaining life grows shorter. Speaking of it brings tears. One cannot sit or rise without another's help. Is this not suffering?"

The great king answered: "This is truly great suffering."

"What is the suffering of sickness? A person's body is composed of the four great elements in harmony. What are the four great elements? The earth element, the water element, the fire element, and the wind element. When one element is out of balance, a hundred and one illnesses arise. When all four elements are out of balance, four hundred and four illnesses simultaneously afflict the body. When the earth element is out of balance, the whole body becomes heavy. When the water element is out of balance, the whole body swells. When the fire element is out of balance, the whole body burns with fever. When the wind element is out of balance, the whole body is rigid and stiff. All the joints are in agonizing pain, as if being beaten with rods. As the four elements advance and retreat, the hands and feet are useless. Strength is exhausted. One cannot sit or rise without another's help. The mouth is parched, the lips scorched, the sinews severed, the nose cracked. The eyes cannot see forms; the ears cannot hear sounds. Impurities flow from the body; one lies in them. The heart is filled with suffering and affliction. Every word is spoken in sorrow. The six kinds of relatives stand by one's side, watching day and night without rest. Sweet delicacies and fine foods, once in the mouth, all taste bitter. Is this not suffering?"

They answered: "This is truly great suffering."

"What is the suffering of death? When a person is about to die, four hundred and four illnesses simultaneously afflict the body. The four great elements are about to scatter, and the spirit is not at ease. At the moment of dying, the wind of the knife carves the body apart. There is no place that is not in pain. White sweat pours forth. Both hands grope at the air. The family, within and without, stand on either side, weeping with grief and tears, the pain piercing to the marrow, unable to bear it. The dead one departs. The wind goes, the breath ceases. The fire is extinguished, the body grows cold. The wind departs first, then the fire. The spirit is gone. The body lies rigid, with no more awareness. Within ten days, the flesh decays and the blood flows. The body swells, rots, and stinks, unspeakably foul. It is cast upon the open fields; the many birds eat the flesh. The flesh gone, the bones dry, the skull lies in a different place. Is this not suffering?"

They answered: "This is truly great suffering."

"What is the suffering of parting from those we love? The family, within and without, brothers, wives, and children, all cherish and long for one another. One morning, disaster strikes. They are carried off by raiders, each scattered in a different direction: the father east, the son west, the mother south, the daughter north. They are not in the same place, made servants and slaves. Each cries out in grief, the heart severed within. In the dark and distant unknown, there is no prospect of meeting again. Is this not suffering?"

They answered: "This is truly great suffering."

"What is the suffering of not obtaining what we seek? The household has wealth. One spends and pursues high office, hoping to attain wealth and honor. With strenuous effort one pursues it without cease, until at last one obtains an appointment as a prefect or magistrate of a border region. Before long, one becomes greedy for the people's goods. Someone reports it, and one morning trouble comes. One is carried away in a prisoner's cart. At the moment of execution, the grief is immeasurable, not knowing whether one will live or die, or on what day. Is this not suffering?"

They answered: "This is truly great suffering."

"What is the suffering of encountering those we hate? People in this world are petty and mean, dwelling together in the midst of desire, quarreling over trivial matters, killing one another, until a great feud develops. Each tries to avoid the other, hiding with nowhere to go. Each sharpens blades and readies arrows, takes up bows and carries clubs, afraid of meeting the enemy. When they happen to meet on a narrow road, each draws the bow and aims the arrow; two blades face each other. No one knows who will prevail. At that moment, the terror is immeasurable. Is this not suffering?"

They answered: "This is truly great suffering."

"What is the suffering of grief, sorrow, and affliction? A person born into the world, if long-lived, may reach a hundred years; if short-lived, may perish in the womb. Take the long-lived one and grant him a hundred years. Night consumes half, leaving fifty. Being drunk, ill, or unaware of one's humanity further reduces it by five years. In childhood, foolish and ignorant: fifteen years during which one does not yet understand propriety. Past eighty, old, dull, and without wisdom, deaf and blind, without any decorum: another twenty years reduced. Already ninety years are accounted for. In the remaining ten years, there are many griefs. When the realm is in turmoil, one grieves. When there is drought, one grieves. When there is flood, one grieves. When there is great frost, one grieves. When the harvest fails, one grieves. When the family, within and without, suffers many illnesses, one grieves. Managing the household wealth and livelihood, fearing loss, one grieves. When government taxes and levies have not been paid, one grieves. When a family member is caught up in a legal matter and imprisoned with no release in sight, one grieves. When brothers, wife, and children travel far and have not returned, one grieves. When the household is destitute, without clothing or food, one grieves. When neighbors and villagers have troubles, one grieves. When the community offerings are not prepared, one grieves. When a family member dies and there is no wealth for the funeral, one grieves. At spring planting time, when there are no plow-oxen, one grieves. With all these kinds of grief and sorrow, there is never a time of joy. When a festival arrives and everyone gathers, supposedly to be happy, instead they weep before one another. Is this not suffering?"

They answered: "This is truly great suffering."

At that time, the five kings, the assembled ministers, and the many thousands of people at the assembly, having heard the teaching of the truth of suffering, had their minds opened and awakened. They immediately attained the fruit of stream-entry. All were filled with great joy, and they made obeisance and departed.

The four kings addressed King Universal Peace: "The great king is truly a great bodhisattva of skillful means, who has guided and taught us and enabled us to attain a glimpse of the path. The kindness of the great king! Before, when I looked at my palaces, my heart was filled with love and attachment, unable to part from them. Now when I behold the palaces, it is as though I am looking at a foul privy. There is nothing lovable about them." They immediately abdicated their thrones, entrusting them to their younger brothers, left the household life to follow the path, and cultivated all kinds of merit, never growing weary from day to day.
