# 大黑天神法
## Method of the Great Black Deva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1287

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, recorded by Shenkai (神愷).

---

The Great Black Deva is a transformed body of Maheśvara. He is installed in all the monasteries of the five regions of India and of our own court as well. Some say: the Great Black Deva is a transformed body of the Firm-Earth Deva, installed in monasteries. Each day, a portion of the first-cooked rice is offered to this deva. In a dream-oracle he spoke thus: "If I am installed in a monastery and reverently offered to day by day, I shall cause a multitude of monks to dwell in that temple, sustaining a community of one thousand persons daily. The same applies to lay households. If a person devotes their heart exclusively to my worship for three years, I shall certainly come, bestow upon the worshipper worldly wealth and nobility, even official rank and salary; all shall be granted according to their wishes.

"My body should be made five feet tall, though three feet or two and a half feet are also acceptable. The skin is entirely black. The head wears a black cap. Everything is black. He wears trousers tucked up and not hanging loose, and a hunting cloak with short hem and narrow sleeves. The right hand forms a fist held at the right hip. The left hand holds a large bag, slung from the shoulder over the back. The bag is the color of mouse fur, hanging down to just above the buttocks.

"When the image is thus completed and installed in the great communal dining hall with reverent offerings, the hall, buildings, and chambers will naturally flourish and resources will gather and overflow. Furthermore, if a person constantly recites my mantra, at the four seasons prepares abundant dishes, wine, soups, cooked rice, and even a hundred flavors, and at the fifth watch with an unknowing multitude of people offers worship to me, I shall certainly grant wealth." The mantra:

*Oṃ micchi micchi śabarī tārā-gati svāhā.*

"The mudrā: both hands in an internal bond. Extend both earth-pinky fingers. Both water-ring fingers move back and forth. For the offering mudrā, use the standard ritual method. The worshipper must regard this deva as father and mother, as an external protector of the Three Jewels, and as a parent to all people." I have long heard that both in the customs of India and in the customs of our own court, all temples that install this deva unfailingly enjoy prosperity. Therefore, to dispel the doubts of future generations, I record this dream-account for posterity.

The teacher of the Method of the Great Black Deva said: "This is the most secret of secrets. It must not be transmitted to any but an inner-chamber disciple. Do not transmit it even for a thousand pieces of gold. Strive diligently." A hymn follows.

The seed-syllable.

The Great Black Deva belongs to the garbha-dhātu. His Sanskrit name is Mahākāla Deva, also called the Great Black Deva. He uses the universal mudrā. His samaya-form is a sword. He is blue-black with three faces and six arms. The front left and right hands hold a sword horizontally. The next left hand holds a human head; the next right hand holds a goat. The outer left and right arms stretch an elephant hide behind the back. He wears a necklace of skulls. Thus the original text says his color is a pale black.

The *Commentary on the Benevolent Kings Sūtra* by Liangben states: "The 'charnel ground' is his dwelling place. 'Mahā' translates as 'great.' 'Kāla' translates as 'black deva.' The upper phrase is Sanskrit; the lower is Chinese. The Great Black Deva is a war deity. If one venerates this deva, one's martial power increases, and all undertakings are victorious. Therefore he is worshipped. How is this known? The Tripiṭaka Master cites a separate Sanskrit text that says: The *Peacock King Sūtra* states that east of the capital of the kingdom of Uśīra, there is a forest called Śmaśāna, which means 'charnel ground.' This forest extends one yojana in every direction. There dwells the Great Black Deva, who is a transformed body of Maheśvara. Together with innumerable ghosts and spirits as retinue, he roams through the forest at night. He possesses great supernatural power and many treasures: elixirs of invisibility, medicines of immortality. He flies through the air, and barters various magical medicines with people, accepting only the blood and flesh of living persons. He first agrees upon the weight, then exchanges the medicines. If a person wishes to go there, they must first empower their body with dhāraṇī, then proceed to barter. If not empowered, those ghosts and spirits become invisible and secretly steal the person's blood and flesh, reducing the weight. They take the person's own blood and flesh, and as it is taken it diminishes; what was agreed upon is never fulfilled. They may even consume an entire person's blood and flesh without meeting the agreed weight, and the medicines cannot be obtained. But if one is empowered, one obtains jewels and various medicines, and whatever one does is accomplished. If one venerates him, he requires only human blood and flesh. Having great power, he protects that person, and all undertakings of brave combat and warfare will be victorious. Therefore the Great Black Deva is a war deity. The Seven Mother Devas serve as his retinue." The seven sisters are: first, Cāmuṇḍā; second, Gauvarī; third, Vaiṣṇavī; fourth, Kaumārī; fifth, Indrī; sixth, Raudrī; seventh, Mālāhimī. These seven names are all mantras.

The *Commentary on the Mahāvairocana Sūtra* states: "The mantra of the ḍākinīs: in the world there are practitioners of this art, also a spell-art of Maheśvara. They can know when a person is about to die; they know six months in advance. Having known, they perform the method and take that person's heart to eat. The reason is that within the human body there is a 'bezoar' (human yellow), just as cattle have bezoars. If one obtains and eats it, one achieves supremely great siddhi. In a single day one can travel the four continents, doing whatever one wishes. One can also perform various treatments on people; if one bears a grudge against someone, one can afflict them with extreme illness through the art. However, their method cannot kill a person; it must rely upon their own divination arts. They know six months ahead when a person is fated to die, and then use their art to take the heart. Although they take the heart, by their art they replace it with another substance; the person does not die but continues until their destined time, and only then does the body perish. In the main, they are yakṣas of great sovereign power. Among worldly people, they are subordinate to Mahākāla, the so-called Great Black Deva." Vairocana, through the dharma-gate of subduing with Trailokyavijaya, wished to destroy them and therefore manifested as the Great Black Deva. Surpassing them immeasurably, smearing ash upon his body, he appeared in the wilderness, and through his art summoned all to achieve siddhi, riding through the sky and walking on water without obstruction. He rebuked the ḍākinīs: "Because you constantly devour humans, I shall now eat you!" He swallowed and consumed them. Yet he did not let them die; having subdued them, he released them and made them all renounce flesh. They addressed the Buddha: "We have survived entirely by eating flesh. How shall we now sustain ourselves?" The Buddha said: "I permit you to eat the hearts of the dead." They replied: "When a person is about to die, devas, yakṣas, and others know their life is ending and compete to eat. How shall we obtain them?" The Buddha said: "I shall teach you a mantra-method and mudrā. Six months before death, you will know. Having known, protect the person with the method, preventing others from causing harm. When the life ends, I permit you to take and eat." Thus gradually guiding them toward the Way, this mantra arose: *hrīḥ ha.* The *Commentary on the Yogī Sūtra* says: *hūṃ kāra kāra.* The *Commentary on the Mahāvairocana Sūtra* says: "Mahākāla, the Great Black Deva, is also called the Great Dark Night Deva, because 'kāla' translates as 'black' or 'dark.'" The *Commentary on the Benevolent Kings Sūtra* says: "The mudrā and mantra of Mahākāla: *Oṃ mahā-kālāya svāhā.*" The *Record of Travels to the Southern Seas* states: "Furthermore, at the great monasteries of the west, beside the kitchen pillar or before the great storehouse gate, they carve a wooden figure, either two or three feet tall, in the form of a spirit-king. He sits holding a golden pouch, squatting on a small platform with one leg hanging down. He is constantly rubbed with oil, black in color, and is called Mākāhara, the Great Black Deva. The ancient tradition says he is a subordinate of Maheśvara, whose nature delights in the Three Jewels and who protects the fivefold community, preventing loss and depletion, and fulfilling the wishes of those who pray. At mealtimes, the kitchen staff always offer incense and whatever food and drink is available is set before him. I personally witnessed this at the Pāvā Temple, the place of the Great Parinirvāṇa, where the regular monastic community was over one hundred. During the spring and autumn ceremonies, as many as five hundred monks would arrive unexpectedly for the midday meal. There was no time to cook more. The steward told the kitchen: 'This sudden crowd, what shall we do?' An elderly female lay-servant said: 'This is nothing unusual; do not worry.' She then lit abundant incense, set out a generous offering of food, and addressed the Black Deva: 'The Great Saint has entered nirvāṇa, yet your followers remain. Monks from the four directions have come to pay reverence at the holy site. Do not let the food and offerings run short; this is within your power. Please act accordingly.' She then summoned the entire assembly to be seated, and the temple's regular food was served in sequence. The entire assembly was satisfied, and the surplus was the same as on an ordinary day. All praised the power of the deva and went personally to pay reverence. They beheld the Black Deva still in his place before them, the food piled in a great mound. When asked why, they were told the reason." Although previously unknown in the north, in the south there are many places of installation. Those who pray receive efficacious results; the divine way is not empty. The Mahābodhi Temple's Mucalinda Nāga is similarly wondrous.

*Oṃ micchi micchi śabarī tārā-gati ha svāhā. Oṃ mahā-kālāya svāhā.*

Oral transmission says: the standard method is the Eighteen Paths. Oral transmission says: the *Benevolent Kings Sūtra* says: "In the charnel ground, Mahākāla, the Great Black Deva." The *Commentary on the Principle of Suchness* says: "The Seven Mother Devas are the retinue of Mahākāla. They should be placed in the northeast. Most secret, most secret!"

The teacher said: "This method is the most supremely secret. It must not be transmitted to anyone who is not an inner-chamber disciple or whose name is not in the register. Do not transmit it, not for a thousand pieces of gold. Strive! Strive! First prepare the offerings: one tray of food, then a little fruit, then one cup of fragrant water, one cup of fragrant paste, one cup of flower garlands, one cup of burning incense, one cup of wine, one cup of washed rice, and one handful of silver coins. Next, in one's room, wash the hands and rinse the mouth. Next, protect the body. Next, enter the hall. Next, bow to the buddha and take one's seat. Next, apply fragrant paste to the hands. Next, empower the fragrant water. Next, sprinkle and purify the offerings. Next, empower the food and drink.

The mudrā and mantra: the left hand forms a fist pressed against the hip. The right hand has five fingers straight. Bend the middle finger at the joint. Place the space-thumb upon the fire-middle fingernail. With each recitation, rub the middle fingernail two or three times, then extend all fingers. Then tap the thumb and index finger three times, snap the fingers, and extend. The mantra:

*Namaḥ sarva-tathāgatāvalokite oṃ saṃbhara saṃbhara hūṃ.*

Next, the mudrā and vidyā for bestowing amṛta. Form the fearlessness mudrā: the right hand upright with five fingers extended straight upward. The mantra:

*Namo surūpāya tathāgatāya tadyathā oṃ suru suru prasuru prasuru svāhā.*

Next, visualize the vaṃ-syllable heart water-wheel. First form the fearlessness mudrā. In the center of the right palm, visualize the syllable and recite it seven times. Then spread the five fingers and hold the hand face-down over the food vessel while visualizing. The mantra:

*Namaḥ samanta-buddhānāṃ vaṃ.*

Next, the invitation:

"With utmost sincerity I respectfully invite Mahākāla, the Great Black Deva, together with all retinues, to descend here and receive this offering." The mantra:

*Oṃ mahā-kālāya ehi hi.*

Beckon with both thumbs. Next, the arghya water. Next, the flower-seat. Next, fragrant paste. Next, flower garlands. Next, burning incense. Next, food and drink. Next, lamp-light. Next, silver coins. Next, universal offering. Next, the hymn-mudrā. Next, contemplation of dependent origination. Next, the Heart Sūtra. Next, pray as desired. Next, the root mudrā and mantra. Next, recitation. Next, arghya water. Next, the confession: "The offerings I have prepared are laughably crude and defiled. My distracted and wandering thoughts were largely not in accord with the Dharma, and have troubled the invisible assembly. I humbly pray for your compassion, and beseech you to be pleased and to bestow your blessings."

Next, prostration. Next, dispatch. The right hand forms a fist. Press the thumb upon the index finger. Turn the palm upward and snap the fingers seven times. The mantra:

*Oṃ vajra-mukṣa muḥ.*

Next, protect the body. Next, take one's seat.

Hymn to the devas:

*Ayaṃ tunīvacā-gasura-kiṃnarī-ndra-sākara-dāyu-pravara-dharma-kīrti-dhigāla-vidharma-ca-praśama-cokya-nīmita-bhūta-nīmita-prakāśaya-tanniha-śramaṇāya dhāṃ.*

*Colophon 1*: In the third year of Shōan [1173], on the eighteenth day of the second month, at the eastern chamber of Shōguchiin at Daigoji, written and completed over two days.

*Colophon 2*: In the third year of Eishō [1506], *hinoe-tora*, on the second day of the second month, at Jakujōin of Kami-Daigoji, transmission completed from Dharma-Seal Chōe of Tanigyōjuin to Kaiyū.

*Colophon 3*: In the first year of Meiwa [1764], *kinoe-saru*, on the second day of mid-autumn, transcribed from an old manuscript and first collation completed. The śramaṇa Chikō.

*Colophon 4*: On the fifth day after the eighth month of the first year of Kyōwa [1801], collated from three to five manuscripts and given to the artisan for woodblock printing, so that people may know the spiritual efficacy of the deva. There is also an eight-armed form. Xīlín's *Phonetic Glossary* says: "The sūtra says 'Mahākāla, Great Black Deva'; the upper is Sanskrit, the lower Chinese. This deva is blue-black cloud-colored, of immeasurable lifespan, with eight arms, each holding different weapons. He wears a necklace strung with skulls, in the form of a wrathful deva. Beneath his feet is a female earth-deva, supporting his feet with both hands." Inscribed by Kaidō, a śramaṇa at rest at Sōjiin, Buzan Hasedera.

First collation with additions completed by Jijun.
