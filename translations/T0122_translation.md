# 佛說波斯匿王太后崩塵土坌身經
## Sūtra on King Prasenajit Whose Queen Mother Died and Whose Body Was Covered with Dust

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0122

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmaruci (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the queen mother of King Prasenajit of the country of Kośala had died. She was one hundred years old, with no more youthful vigor, but had diligently cultivated wholesome dharmas. King Prasenajit, having attended to the funeral and escorted his mother's remains, returned at midday. His body covered with dust, he walked on foot to the garden and came to where the World-Honored One was. He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side. The World-Honored One then asked the king: "Why is your body covered with dust today, and why have you come here on foot?"

King Prasenajit then wept, unable to restrain himself, and wiping his tears, addressed the World-Honored One: "The queen mother has died, World-Honored One! The queen mother has passed away, Tathāgata! She was at the age of a full century, without youthful strength, and had accumulated wholesome dharmas throughout her life. I grieve deeply and miss her. Day and night I served her with filial devotion, never once going against her wishes. If her life could be ransomed, I would give my own life. If elephants, horses, and chariots could ransom her life, I would offer them all. If subjects and citizens could ransom her life, I would also offer them. Do not let my mother's life pass away! If gold and silver could ransom her life, I would offer them. If precious treasures could ransom her life, I would also offer them. If gold, silver, and precious treasures could ransom her life, I would offer gold, silver, and precious treasures. If male servants could ransom her life, I would offer them. If female servants could ransom her life, I would offer them. If both could ransom her life, I would offer both. If villages could ransom her life, I would offer villages. If cities and fortresses could ransom her life, I would offer cities and fortresses. If villages and cities together could ransom her life, I would offer cities and villages. If an entire region could ransom her life, I would offer an entire region. If the land and its people could ransom her life, I would offer the land and its people. If the people of an entire region could ransom her life, I would offer the people of an entire region. Do not let my mother's life pass away!"

At that time, the World-Honored One told King Prasenajit: "Just so, great king! Just as the king has said: if white elephants could ransom her life, you could use elephants to ransom your mother's life; if horses, chariots, subjects, precious treasures, gold and silver, servants, villages, cities and fortresses, or the people of an entire region could ransom your mother's life, you could use the people of an entire region to ransom your mother's life, not letting her pass away. Therefore, great king! You should contemplate the perception of impermanence; you should widely spread the perception of impermanence; you should widely spread the perception of death."

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> All people return to death; there is no one who does not die.
> According to one's actions, one plants calamity or merit, and oneself reaps the fruit of good or evil.
> Hell awaits those who do evil; those who are good will certainly be born in heaven.
> The wise are able to distinguish: only merit can ward off evil.

"Just so, great king! There are four fearsome things, great objects of terror, that no one can escape. They cannot be avoided by strength, swords, or clubs; they cannot be avoided by spells, medicinal herbs, elephants, horses, chariots, subjects, precious treasures, gold and silver, servants, villages, cities and fortresses, or the people of an entire region. What are the four?

"Old age is a great terror: the flesh and muscles waste away. It cannot be avoided by swords or clubs, nor by anything up to the people of an entire region.

"Illness is a great terror: one has no robust vigor. It cannot be avoided by swords or clubs, nor by anything up to the people of an entire region.

"Death is a great terror: life is utterly exhausted. It cannot be avoided by swords or clubs, nor by anything up to the people of an entire region.

"Separation from what one loves is a great terror. It cannot be avoided by swords or clubs, nor by anything up to the people of an entire region.

"These, great king, are the four great terrors that cannot be avoided by swords or clubs, spells, medicinal herbs, elephants, horses, chariots, subjects, precious treasures, gold and silver, servants, villages, cities and fortresses, or the people of an entire region.

"It is like, great king, a great cloud arising with thunder, lightning, and thunderbolts, which in a moment disperses and does not remain long. Just so, great king, human life is extremely brief; the longest lifespan is a hundred years, and those who exceed it are very few indeed.

"It is like, great king, four great mountains of stone without any hollow space, all equal in size, pressing together at once. Trees, medicinal herbs: nothing can be avoided by swords or clubs. Just so, great king, these four great terrors approach, and they cannot be escaped. What are the four? Old age is a great affliction: the flesh and muscles waste away. It cannot be avoided by swords or clubs; neither spells nor medicinal herbs can avert it. Illness is a great affliction: one has no robust vigor. Death is a great affliction: the body is permanently destroyed. Separation from what one loves is a great affliction. It cannot be avoided by swords or clubs; neither spells nor medicinal herbs can avert it.

"Great king! Cultivate extensively the perception of impermanence; spread widely the perception of impermanence. Why? Having cultivated the perception of impermanence, having spread the perception of impermanence, one thoroughly cuts off all craving for sensual desire, thoroughly cuts off all craving for form, thoroughly cuts off all craving for the formless, thoroughly cuts off all ignorance, and all craving in this world is also cut off.

"It is like, great king, a pile of straw and firewood: when fire is brought to burn it, or a great thicket of forest, or a pavilion or a house, it is consumed likewise. If one cultivates the perception of impermanence, widely spreads the perception of impermanence, one thoroughly cuts off craving for desire, cuts off craving for form, cuts off craving for the formless, cuts off ignorance, and cuts off all craving in this world. Therefore, great king, you should govern with the true Dharma and not govern with what is contrary to the Dharma. Thus, great king, you should train."

At that time, King Prasenajit addressed the World-Honored One: "What is this Dharma called? How should it be upheld?"

The World-Honored One said: "It is called the Sūtra on Removing Sorrow and Affliction. This Dharma removes sorrow and affliction."

King Prasenajit then addressed the World-Honored One: "Indeed so, World-Honored One! The Sūtra on Removing Sorrow and Affliction. Indeed so, World-Honored One! The Sūtra on Removing Sorrow and Affliction. Why? Because having heard this Dharma, World-Honored One, all my longing, grief, and sorrow have been entirely removed. I feel my body becoming supple and filled with joy."

At that time, the World-Honored One taught King Prasenajit the subtle and wonderful Dharma in full, encouraging him to rejoice. King Prasenajit then rose from his seat, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated the Buddha three times, and departed.

At that time, King Prasenajit of the country of Kośala, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully practiced in accordance.
