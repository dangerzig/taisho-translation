# Treatise on the Practice of Concentration Taught Through Six Gates

## *Liùmén Jiàoshòu Xídìng Lùn* (六門教授習定論)

Taishō 1607

Root text by Asaṅga (*Wúzhuó*, 無著). Commentary by Vasubandhu (*Shìqīn*, 世親). Translated by Yìjìng (*Yìjìng*, 義淨) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a short Yogācāra treatise on meditation practice attributed to Asaṅga, with a commentary by Vasubandhu, translated into Chinese by the Tang-dynasty pilgrim-translator Yìjìng (635-713 CE). The text systematically presents the practice of concentration (samādhi) through six "gates" or topics: (1) the aspiration for liberation, (2) the accumulation of prerequisites, (3) the diligent cultivation of mental stability (the actual practice of meditation), (4) the three kinds of perfection (perfection of teacher, object, and attention), (5) the reliance on three types of concentration (with investigation and analysis, without investigation but with analysis, and without either), and (6) the fruit of meditation practice. The text is structured in verse-and-commentary format (the characteristic Yogācāra style), with Asaṅga's verses followed by Vasubandhu's prose explanations. Key topics include: the distinction between the affliction-obstruction (kleśāvaraṇa) and the knowledge-obstruction (jñeyāvaraṇa); the ālayavijñāna as the basis of both obstructions; the nine stages of mental abiding (from initial fixation through effortless flow); the five kinds of mental distraction (external, internal, wrong-object, heaviness, and attentional); the three mental images (calming, uplifting, and equanimity); and śamatha (calming) and vipaśyanā (insight) as the two wings of meditation. The treatise draws on both Āgama and Yogācāra sources.*

---

Now, wishing to benefit all sentient beings, to cause them to practice both worldly and world-transcending concentration, and to enable them to swiftly relinquish all afflictions, I set forth this method.

### Opening Verse (General Outline of the Six Gates)

> Those who seek liberation accumulate [the prerequisites];
> In abiding, they diligently cultivate.
> Having obtained the three kinds of perfection,
> They are practitioners of concentration with a basis.

**Commentary:** This initial verse is a general outline of the six gates. "Those who seek liberation" refers to persons who seek liberation. "Accumulate" means to accumulate the provisions of superior practice. "In abiding, diligently cultivate" means to cause the mind to rest well on its object, which is called "concentration" (*samādhi*), because it is non-scattered and non-agitated.

Question: How does one cultivate? Answer: Having obtained the three kinds of perfection, one is a practitioner of concentration with a basis. The three perfections are: (1) perfection of teacher, (2) perfection of object, and (3) perfection of attention. "Basis" refers to the three concentrations: (1) concentration with investigation and analysis (*savitarka-savicāra*), (2) concentration without investigation but with analysis (*avitarka-savicāra-mātra*), and (3) concentration without investigation or analysis (*avitarka-avicāra*). "Practitioner of concentration" means one who cultivates śamatha (calming) and vipaśyanā (insight).

### Gate One: The Aspiration for Liberation

> Those who delight in liberation in the three vehicles
> Are called "persons who seek liberation."
> When both kinds of obstruction are completely removed,
> This is called "liberation."
> One should know that the appropriating consciousness
> Is the essential nature of the two obstructions.
> Affliction-seeds and all-seeds
> Can bind two types of persons.
> One who has removed the affliction-obstruction
> But whose habitual tendencies are not yet eliminated:
> This is the śrāvaka vehicle.
> The rest, only a buddha can sever.
> When afflictions are absent yet one's conduct
> Appears as if afflictions remain,
> These are habitual tendencies from prior lives.
> If removed, one would be different from this.

**Commentary:** These four verses explain "those who seek liberation," referring to the distinctions among the śrāvaka vehicle and others. Those who delight in liberation within the three vehicles are called "those who seek liberation." What is liberation? When both kinds of obstruction are completely removed, this is called liberation.

What are the two obstructions, the removal of which is called liberation? One should know that the "appropriating consciousness" (*ādānavijñāna*) is the essential nature of the two obstructions. "Consciousness" here is the ālayavijñāna. "Appropriating" means "serving as basis": it is the essential nature of the two obstructions of afflictions (*kleśa*) and knowledge (*jñeya*). How so? "Affliction-seeds" are the self-nature of the affliction-obstruction. "All-seeds" are the self-nature of the knowledge-obstruction. Furthermore, "all-seeds" are the seeds of both obstructions that "bind two persons": affliction-obstruction seeds bind śrāvakas; all-seeds bind bodhisattvas.

What is the difference in the liberation of these two? Śrāvakas have not yet eliminated habitual tendencies; they sever the affliction-obstruction and realize liberation. Only the Buddha, the World-Honored One, can completely remove both.

What are habitual tendencies? When afflictions are absent yet one's conduct and demeanor appear as if afflictions remain, these are called habitual tendencies. They are the residual traces of habitual patterns from past lives. Although the afflictions are now exhausted, one's outward behavior still resembles defiled conduct: this is called "habitual tendencies."

---

### Gate Two: Accumulation of Prerequisites

> Planting various wholesome roots,
> Being free from doubt, removing feverish torment,
> Flowing purely in the Dharma:
> This is called "accumulation."
> Upholding [the teachings], delighting in hearing the Dharma,
> Skillfully removing the two views,
> Not being satisfied merely by hearing:
> These four things should be known.

**Commentary:** As the sūtra says: "This person should first cultivate extensive learning, then hear the correct Dharma; the feverish torment of wrong views should be properly eliminated; the coverings and entanglements of the mind should be properly subdued."

How does one accumulate wholesome roots? By upholding the correct Dharma. How is one free from doubt? By delighting in hearing the Dharma. How does one remove feverish torment? By removing the two views: the view of wanting others to know and recognize one, and the view of self-exaltation. How does one flow purely in the Dharma? By removing the mere satisfaction of hearing, so that one progresses to thinking and diligent practice without ceasing.

### Gate Three: Diligent Cultivation of Abiding (The Nine Mental Abidings)

[The text presents sixteen verses explaining the practice of meditation. The first verse gives a general outline; the remaining verses provide detailed explanations.]

**The three objects of meditation:** (1) the external object (visualized images such as the skeleton meditation, for beginners), (2) the higher object (the signs of calmness in the access concentration), and (3) the internal object (the image arising from mental discourse). The "self-nature" [of abiding] is the non-disturbance of mind.

**The three abidings** corresponding to external, higher, and internal objects:

(1) First abiding: the practitioner of concentration sees [the object]; the mind does not shift to other objects and continues steadily.

(2) Second abiding: the mind of weariness [with saṃsāra] is calm, concentrated, and does not shift.

(3) Third abiding: the mind is focused and settled on the internal object, concentrated and unshifting.

**The nine mental abidings** within these: (1) initial fixation, (2) continuous placement, (3) repeated placement, (4) close placement, (5) taming, (6) pacifying, (7) thorough pacifying, (8) one-pointed application, and (9) balanced placement [effortless flow].

**The four kinds of attention:** (1) effortful attention (initial fixation), (2) interrupted attention (the six middle stages, where distraction occasionally arises), (3) applied attention (uninterrupted application), and (4) effortless attention (entering the path of habituation).

**The five kinds of mental distraction:** (1) external distraction (mind wandering to other objects), (2) internal distraction (agitation, dullness, or attachment arising within meditation), (3) wrong-object distraction (nostalgic thoughts of relatives and worldly affairs during meditation), (4) heaviness distraction (the two kinds of self-grasping disturbing the mind), and (5) attentional distraction (three subtypes: discriminative analysis of the object, shifting from one vehicle to another, or shifting from one concentration to another; the first two should be abandoned, the third is not a fault).

### Gate Four: The Three Perfections

> Learned, having seen the truths, skillful in speech,
> Possessing compassion, always generating joy:
> Such a person is fit to teach concentration.
> Explaining everything fully, speaking truthfully,
> Well understanding the objects of knowledge:
> This is called "a good teacher."

**Commentary:** The first verse describes the perfection of the teacher: skillful in teaching, accomplished in realization, skillful in speech, free from defilement, and unflagging in the continuous practice of teaching the Dharma.

The second verse describes the perfection of the object: the teacher explains all matters fully and without reservation.

The third verse describes the perfection of attention: learning is the cause from which mental discourse arises, which serves as the direct cause of the noble path and nirvāṇa. The attention directed at this mental discourse is the "attention whose cause is cessation" (*nirodha-hetu-manaskāra*).

---

### Gate Five: The Three Concentrations (Śamatha and Vipaśyanā)

> Investigation and the mental discourse that follows:
> After this, one should examine subtly.
> When mental discourse ceases, that is concentration.
> The characteristics of dhyāna are three:
> No different object, no characteristics;
> The mind abides on the word.
> This is the place where the mind is still:
> It is called śamatha.
> Observing various objects
> Is called vipaśyanā.
> When both are unified in one yoga,
> It is called "concentration of one or two parts."
> The heaviness-obstruction and the view-obstruction:
> Know that the two concentrations
> Serve as antidotes for these,
> As skillful means for increasing wholesome dharmas.

**Commentary:** "Investigation" indicates the presence of vitarka (investigation); "subtle examination" indicates the absence of vitarka with the presence of vicāra (analysis); "mental discourse ceases" indicates the absence of both. Since both vitarka and vicāra have "mental discourse" (*manojalpa*) as their nature, this is explained in terms of śamatha.

Śamatha means "place of cessation" (*śama* = cessation, *stha* = place). Vipaśyanā means "manifold observation" of multiple objects. "Concentration of one or two parts" means that sometimes there is only calming without insight, sometimes only insight without calming, and sometimes both together: this is the union of calming and insight (*śamatha-vipaśyanā-yuganaddha*).

---

### The Three Mental Images (Calming, Uplifting, Equanimity)

> Calming, uplifting, and equanimity:
> Know these in sequence.
> When the mind sinks and risks drowning,
> Direct attention to a delightful object.
> When agitation arises or risks arising,
> Generate weariness to eliminate it.
> Free from both sinking and agitation,
> The mind abides in equanimity,
> Flowing effortlessly without application.
> Always cultivate the three mental images.
> The practitioner of concentration cultivates all three,
> Not just one exclusively,
> To prevent the faults of sinking and so forth,
> And to purify the mind.

---

### Gate Six: The Fruit

> Liberation and delight,
> Right abiding and capability:
> When these obstructing afflictions are all removed,
> The practitioner's mind is purified.

**Commentary:** This describes the benefit of purification, based on the *Rajoddhāraṇa Sūtra* ("Sūtra on Removing Dust"). The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "If a person wishes to purify the inner mind, there are afflictive obstructions that appear and cannot be eliminated. To sever them, one should first avoid committing great transgressions of the unwholesome paths of action, cease wrong views, and seek renunciation, aspiring to liberation. For middling afflictions, desires, hostility, and harmful intentions that give rise to unwholesome thoughts and obstruct superior delight, one should cultivate 'delight.' For subtle thoughts about relatives, worldly matters, or immortality that obstruct right abiding, one should cultivate 'right abiding.' For the effortful [concentration] that has not yet achieved effortless capability, one should cultivate 'capability.'"

---

### Conclusion

> Within this gate of concentration,
> The correct cultivation that has been taught
> Illuminates all worldly concentrations
> And also makes known the world-transcending concentration.

This verse shows that those who cultivate śamatha and vipaśyanā obtain their fruit in the present. If a person cultivates according to the concentration-characteristics taught here, they obtain the perfect fruit of all worldly [attainments] as well as the world-transcending fruit, as previously explained.

**Summary verse:**

> Revealing the disposition and its basis,
> The root basis and the correct basis,
> The perfection of worldly concentration,
> And also understanding the world-transcending:
> These are the six gates.

Briefly stated, the meaning is complete.
