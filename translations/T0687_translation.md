# 佛說孝子經
## Sūtra of the Filial Son, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0687

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

The Buddha asked the śramaṇas: "When parents give birth to a child, the mother carries it for ten months, her body burdened with grave illness. On the day of birth, the mother's life is in danger and the father trembles with fear; their feelings are beyond words. After the child is born, they sleep on the damp spot and give the dry place to the child. With the utmost devotion, their blood transforms into milk. They wash and bathe the child, clothe and feed it, teach and instruct it, pay for tutors and companions, and make tributes to the lord. When the child's face is happy, the parents too rejoice. When the child is troubled, the parents' hearts are scorched. When the child goes out, they think of it with love; when it returns, they are mindful of it. Their hearts are always anxious, fearing the child may come to no good. Such is the kindness of parents. How can one repay it?"

The śramaṇas replied: "One should exhaust all courtesy and serve them with a compassionate heart, in order to repay the parents' kindness."

The World-Honored One further said: "Suppose the child nourishes the parents with sweet dew and a hundred delicacies to gratify their mouths, entertains their ears with heavenly music and every kind of sound, clothes their bodies in famous garments and fine robes to adorn them radiantly, carries them on both shoulders and travels throughout the four seas, and does so for the full span of the child's own lifetime in order to repay the kindness of nurturing. Can this be called filial?"

The śramaṇas said: "There is no greater filiality than this!"

The World-Honored One said: "This is not yet true filiality! If the parents are obstinate and benighted and do not honor the Three Treasures; if they are cruel and violent, stealing what is not rightfully theirs; if they are given to lust and loose conduct; if they speak falsely and follow wrong paths; if they are addicted and confused; if they turn their backs on the true and the right: when perversity such as this exists, the child should earnestly admonish them to awaken and enlighten them. If they are still uncomprehending and unawakened, the child should instruct them through principle and edification, drawing analogies and citing examples, showing them the prisons of the king and the punishments and executions of the prisoners, saying: 'These people violated the law. Their bodies were struck with many torments, and they brought about their own deaths. When life ends and the spirit departs, it is bound in the great mountain of Yama, amidst boiling cauldrons and a myriad poisons, crying out alone with no one to rescue it. Because they trod the path of evil, they have incurred this grave calamity.' If the parents still do not change, the child should weep and wail, refusing all food and drink. Although the parents may not be enlightened, they will surely feel the pain of love and fear the child's death. The child should then restrain his heart, bear up with patient endurance, and uphold the Way. If the parents then change their mind and accept the Buddha's five precepts, practicing benevolence and compassion by not killing, purity and propriety by not stealing, chastity by not engaging in sexual misconduct, trustworthiness by not being deceitful, and filial devotion by not drinking intoxicants, then within the household there will be compassionate parents and filial children, an upright husband and a chaste wife, the nine degrees of kinship in harmony, and servants obedient and compliant. The benefit will spread far and wide. All that has blood and breath will receive this grace. The Buddhas of the ten directions, the devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits, any ruler who follows the Way, any loyal and fair minister, and all the people of the myriad families will revere and cherish them and bring them peace. Even if there should be a perverse government, treacherous and flattering ministers, wicked sons, and monstrous wives, and a thousand evils and ten thousand calamities, these will have no power over them. In this way, both parents will dwell in peace throughout their lives, and when their lives end, their spirits will ascend and be born in heaven, where they will meet with all the Buddhas, hear the words of the Dharma, attain the Way, and transcend the world, parting forever from suffering."

The Buddha said to the śramaṇas: "Looking at the world, there is no true filiality other than this. Only this is filiality. To cause one's parents to leave evil and turn toward the good, to uphold the five precepts, and to take the three refuges: for parents who receive this in the morning and die in the evening, the kindness exceeds that of the parents' nursing and nourishing, an immeasurable gift. If one cannot transform one's parents through the supreme teaching of the Three Treasures, then even though one supports them with offerings, it is still not true filiality. Do not, on account of an evil wife, distance yourself from the worthy and the good. A woman's feelings are full of desire, and her love of beauty is without end; this violates filiality, destroys the family, brings disorder to the governance of the state, and causes the people to become homeless refugees. One whose original aspiration is generosity, who maintains propriety and self-restraint, with a gentle mind upholding benevolence, steadily advancing in virtue, with an intent still and quiet, whose aspiration for learning reaches penetrating wisdom, whose fame moves the devas, and whose insight equals that of the sages: if such a one is defiled by the allurements of women, and his aspiration is confused by feminine beauty, lost in desire, beguiled by enchanting postures and countless wiles; then a man of shallow wisdom and narrow vision, seeing such things, does not notice the gradual decline but turns his aspiration and drowns himself, following the confusion of bewitching and cunning seductions. In some cases he endangers his parents or kills his lord, hoarding beauty while his passions run wild, filled with jealousy, indolence, and arrogance. His mind scattered and blind, he behaves like a bird or a beast. From ancient times to the present, there have been none who did not thereby destroy their lives and extinguish their families. For this reason, the śramaṇa is alone and not paired, keeping his aspiration pure and devoting himself to the Way. By upholding these bright precepts: if one is a ruler, one preserves the four seas; if one is a minister, one is loyal; if one nourishes the people with benevolence, then the father's rule is wise and the son is filially devoted, the husband is trustworthy and the wife is chaste. If upāsakas and upāsikās practice in this way, life after life they will encounter the Buddha, see the Dharma, and attain the Way."

The Buddha spoke thus, and the disciples rejoiced.
