# 大乘經纂要義
## Essential Meanings Compiled from Mahāyāna Sūtras

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2817

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

> I bow to the supreme, all buddhas,
> Who have realized the three bodies, honored among gods and humans.
> The teaching Dharma is profound and inconceivable;
> The unconditioned liberation and the saṃgha in harmony:
> These are the true refuge in the Three Jewels.
> The buddhas appear in the world like the uḍumbara flower;
> Like a blind turtle encountering a floating log,
> A human body is hard to obtain, exceedingly rare.
> Once lost, it is hard to regain.
> Within this body, one should gradually advance,
> Forever abandoning the eight difficulties,
> Following the teaching, cultivating with added vigor.
> Good and evil karma are never lost;
> As the cause meets the seed, so too is the fruit.
> Therefore, keep far from the ten evils
> And diligently cultivate the ten virtues.
> One must skillfully distinguish the two truths,
> Always abandon saṃsāra and seek liberation.
> Diligently practice the right path, increase wisdom;
> Vow to cultivate the holy traces and realize the unconditioned.

All sentient beings should first take refuge in and reverently serve the Three Jewels. What are the Three Jewels? They are the Buddha Jewel, the Dharma Jewel, and the Saṃgha Jewel.

**The Buddha Jewel.** The Dharma-body, the reward-body, and the transformation-body are fully possessed as three kinds, which together are called the Buddha Jewel. The Dharma-body possesses inconceivable, non-discriminating wisdom and freely contemplates all dharmas. The reward-body is the accomplishment of inconceivable merit, surpassing the bliss of the human and heavenly realms, fully endowed with marks and excellent features, free from birth and death, having realized the ultimate body of Vairocana. The transformation-body is the fulfillment of compassionate vows, exhausting birth and death to liberate sentient beings, guiding them according to their capacities, forever departing from all suffering: entering the mother's womb, manifesting birth, leaving the household life, vanquishing the hosts of Māra, turning the wheel of the right Dharma, and displaying the marks of nirvāṇa. Being fully endowed with these virtues, he surpasses the human and heavenly realms, appearing only at the right time. Therefore he is hard to encounter. Nothing in the world can compare. Thus, worldly treasures are borrowed as analogies to reveal the Dharma's meaning, enabling all sentient beings to attain the unsurpassed fruit, forever departing from the suffering of old age, birth, and transmigration. Even a small degree of taking refuge immediately receives protection and blessing. All evil ghosts and spirits cannot disturb one. Possessing these five meanings, he is like the wish-fulfilling jewel. In the world and beyond, he serves as teacher of gods and humans, most honored among those with two feet: a true refuge.

**The Dharma Jewel.** All the teachings, explained through conventional and ultimate truth in twelve divisions, and the cultivation of the eightfold holy path that awakens to the true nature. Originally quiescent, the non-abiding nirvāṇa: this is called the Dharma Jewel. Before the Buddha appeared in the world, the secret and essential Dharma could not be revealed. The Tathāgata's appearance in the world is exceedingly rare, and the Dharma he taught is likewise. Non-Buddhists cannot reveal its meaning. No other dharma can equal it. With this Dharma Jewel, the path of benefit and joy is no different from the Buddha. This is called the Dharma Jewel.

**The Saṃgha.** The assembly of great bodhisattvas, who do not cling to birth and death and do not abide in nirvāṇa, benefiting self and others with full accomplishment. And the various arhats, the eight types of noble persons abiding on the ground of liberation. And ordinary bhikṣus who each have faith in and delight in the holy stages and have left the household life to study the Way. Each attains liberation. Following the teaching, they cultivate practice. Though they have not yet realized the fruit, they are able to cultivate and maintain the standards and holy Dharma. Without the Buddha's appearance in the world, there would be no Saṃgha Jewel. Like the Tathāgata, it manifests a rare appearance. With all unsurpassed wisdom and virtue fully accomplished, relying on learning, reflection, and practice, gradually becoming refined and subtle, the worldly ṛṣis who possess holy wisdom cultivate the ground of peace and happiness. Thus constituted, the Saṃgha Jewel is supreme.

As stated above, the Three Jewels, through the power of all their merits, possess great compassion: most honored, most supreme, inconceivable. They can rescue sentient beings from present suffering and difficulty, forever departing from long-kalpa transmigration through birth and death. They can also protect and cause the attainment of the joy of liberation. One should make offerings to the unsurpassed Three Jewels. First one must respectfully bow and take refuge, which can produce the sprouts of all merit and forever depart from all affliction and suffering.

All buddhas and tathāgatas possess these merits; their appearance in the world is exceedingly rare. Together with all beings, they have planted the same wholesome roots. Their appearance in the world is like the uḍumbara flower. The buddhas' appearance in the world and their transmission of the profound Dharma is likewise. Though the Buddha has appeared in the world with the profound Dharma, to encounter a human body one must possess accomplished wholesome karma. This difficulty is the same. It is like a blind turtle at the bottom of a great ocean, with a lifespan of a hundred million years, rising to the surface once every hundred years. If someone were to cast a piece of wood with a single hole into the ocean, for the turtle to surface and happen upon the hole of that floating log would be exceedingly rare. Obtaining a human body is equally difficult. Though one obtains a human body with the six faculties complete, to hear the right Dharma with body and mind at ease and to cultivate practice in accordance with the teaching is the most exceedingly rare of all. The realm of sentient beings is inconceivable; among them, to obtain a human body with the six faculties complete and be born freely is the most extremely difficult. Therefore, the Tathāgata and the Dharma appearing in the world is likewise hard to encounter.

A human body is hard to obtain, yet now we have obtained it, because of the great reward. Appearing only once in many kalpas, through wholesome karma one at last obtains a human body. One must be especially diligent and not let it be wasted. Increase wisdom and the wholesome roots of the Dharma. From the holy ground onward, one should seek the supreme practices. Vow never again to fall into the destinies of hell, hungry ghosts, or animals; not to be without hearing of the Three Jewels; not to be in a border region or of lowly birth; even though one obtains a human body, not to be blind, deaf, or mute; not to suffer the difficulty of the long-life heavens; and even if born among humans, not to hold wrong views. Though the Buddha appears in the world, not to be born before or after the Buddha. Among these eight difficulties, with their various sufferings and afflictions that obstruct the path, one should never again fall but forever depart from the eight difficulties. The Tathāgata, World-Honored One, has realized the great fruit of retribution. Those who cultivate practice should increase their vigor as if extinguishing a fire on their heads, eliminating the eight difficulties and greatly accomplishing the good.

Now, good and evil karma are never lost, even by a hair's breadth. In ancient times, the sage Gautama and others, because of pricking a fly on a deer with a blade of grass, suffered the retribution of being pierced by wooden stakes in the lower realms for five hundred lifetimes, eventually leading to death. In the past, there was a child who, with a respectful mind, offered a mere handful of white stones to the saṃgha. Through this karmic connection, for immeasurable kalpas he was born into wealthy families, with the seven treasures raining down, fully accomplished in happiness and ease. Through good and evil karma, one receives a body and the fruit of suffering or happiness according to one's class, with karmic fruition varying accordingly.

Therefore, the ten unwholesome karmas and the like should not be approached but should be kept far from. What need is there for further explanation? The ten unwholesome karmas are: the body has three kinds: killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct. The mouth has four kinds: false speech, frivolous speech, harsh speech, and divisive speech. The mind has three kinds: greed, anger, and delusion. These together are called the ten evils. Through this retribution, one is born in the three lower destinies. When the ten evils are reversed, they become the ten virtues. If one abandons killing and the ten evil conditions, one is born in the heavens or among humans in a superior place. From this body onward to the holy stages, if one can cultivate practice, one can fully accomplish all.

One should know that the ten virtues should be diligently studied. One who cultivates goodness among humans and knows the characteristics of all dharmas is called a true person. One should skillfully weigh the two truths, conventional and ultimate, and awaken to the principle of the two natures of self and other. In this regard, the conventional truth pertains to the dharma-characteristics that arise from causes and conditions: illusory, like dreams, like mirages, like heat-shimmer. The sense-realms, sense-bases, and sense-elements, whether wholesome or unwholesome, meritorious or non-meritorious, worldly or world-transcending: the various characteristics of dharmas with their many aspects and distinctions are merely established through conventional designations and verbal expression to indicate all characteristics. All conditioned dharmas are by their own nature impermanent, suffering, empty, and non-self. What is called unconditioned dharmas: because they are empty and non-self. Through these two truths, one knows all dharmas of transmigration.

Ignorance, formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, sensation, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, old age and death: these twelve links of dependent origination revolve like a wheel through the six destinies of existence, receiving suffering that afflicts the body. Each being, according to their endowment of faculties, whether sharp or dull, attains accomplishment according to their class.

What is called liberation is the ground upon which the sages rely. Those of dull faculties receive the vehicle of the śrāvakas. Those of middling faculties receive the fruit of the pratyekabuddha. According to what one's faculties realize, each differs. The unsurpassed Great Vehicle constitutes three kinds in all. Those of dull faculties realize the Small Vehicle, which can only benefit oneself without benefiting others. They are weary of the suffering of birth and death and cultivate the happiness of quiescent extinction. They abandon all that is unwholesome and restrain the faculties, objects, and consciousness. First they contemplate the Four Noble Truths and cultivate and realize the ground of the stream-enterers and the eight types of noble persons, up to nirvāṇa without remainder. The pratyekabuddhas contemplate the Four Noble Truths and the dharmas of dependent origination, and thereby extinguish the source of birth and death. They realize the holy path and nirvāṇa without remainder.

Those who possess the superior wisdom take up the Great Vehicle. They do not discriminate between birth and death and nirvāṇa. Benefiting self and others: this is unsurpassed bodhi. The bodhisattvas, with great compassion, cultivate the auxiliary practices of the path. From the ground of Extreme Joy up to the Dharma Cloud, they cultivate practice in sequence, realizing the bodhisattva grounds. They further cultivate the six pāramitās: generosity, kind words, beneficial action, and working together as the four means of attraction. With merit and wisdom fully accomplished, the inconceivable, unsurpassed, supreme wisdom is attained. Possessing the ten powers and the four fearlessnesses, with full accomplishment, they realize the ground of buddhahood, benefiting and bringing joy to sentient beings. To the end of birth and death, they do not enter nirvāṇa.
