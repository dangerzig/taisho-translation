# T1527 涅槃論

# Treatise on Nirvāṇa

*Note: This treatise is attributed to Vasubandhu (Póshǒupánzhù 婆藪槃豆) and was translated by Dharmabodhi (Dámó Pútí 達磨菩提) during the Northern Wei dynasty. It is a commentary on the Mahāparinirvāṇa-sūtra (Dàbān Nièpán Jīng 大般涅槃經), structured as a doctrinal analysis of the sūtra's major sections. The treatise outlines seven divisions of the sūtra: (1) the inconceivable supernatural-reversal section, (2) the accomplishing-the-nature-and-removing-attachment section (the Cunda and Lamentation chapters), (3) the true-dharma-true-meaning section (from the Third Announcement through the Great Assembly chapter), (4) the skillful-means-cultivation section (Five Practices and Ten Merits), (5) the abandoning-heedlessness-and-entering-realization section (Lion's Roar chapter), (6) the compassion-light-skillfully-sustaining section (Kāśyapa chapter), and (7) the display-of-characteristics section (Kauṇḍinya chapter). The commentary treats major themes of the Nirvāṇa Sūtra: the indestructible vajra body, buddha-nature in all sentient beings, the distinction between provisional and complete teaching, the four inversions (permanence, bliss, self, purity), half-letter and full-letter instruction, the stages of bodhisattva development, and the relationship between conventional truth and ultimate truth. The style is dialectical, often presenting the question from Kāśyapa's perspective and then offering multiple levels of interpretation: śrāvaka understanding, bodhisattva understanding, and the ultimate nirvāṇa perspective.*

> Homage to the pure ocean of awakening,
> The one who sustains the gate of immortal nectar (amṛta),
> Homage also to the inconceivable,
> Inherently pure treasury.
> The gates of all pāramitās that rescue the world,
> The path that proceeds rightly to true truth,
> And those who study as they should study,
> Realizing the true meaning according to the Dharma,
> Out of compassion for beings long lost,
> They transmit it with sorrow throughout the world.

## The Seven Divisions of the Sūtra

From the beginning ("Thus have I heard") through the passage about "blood sprinkled on the ground" is called the division of the inconceivable supernatural-reversal demonstration.

The chapters on Cunda and Lamentation constitute the division of accomplishing the nature and removing attachment.

From the Third Announcement onward through the Great Assembly chapter is the division of the true Dharma's true meaning.

The Five Practices and Ten Merits constitute the division of skillful means, cultivation, and accomplishment.

The Lion's Roar chapter is the division of abandoning heedlessness and entering realization.

The Kāśyapa chapter is the division of the compassion-light's skillful sustaining.

The Kauṇḍinya chapter is the division of displaying characteristics.

## The Vajra Body and Longevity

"How does one obtain long life and the indestructible vajra body?" Kāśyapa wished to hear together with all sentient beings, and therefore asked. The answer's meaning: through cultivating the three kinds of karma, I obtained long life. "How does one obtain the indestructible vajra body?" All sentient beings are subject to decay. How can one obtain indestructibility? Through the practice described above, one obtains indestructibility. "How does one obtain adamantine strength?" Because the mind is free from discrimination, one obtains adamantine strength.

Without coming and without going, therefore long-lived. Inexpressible, therefore indestructible. Without flowing or moving, therefore adamantine.

How does one obtain long life? Because of the indestructible vajra body, one obtains long life. How is it indestructible? Because one obtains adamantine strength, it is indestructible.

Kāśyapa's questioning was not exhaustible with a single question because the characteristics of the Dharma are inexhaustible.

## The Subtle and Secret Meaning

"May the Buddha open the subtle and secret, and explain it broadly for sentient beings." What is subtle and secret? That the Buddha is outside the body is not secret. That the Buddha is inside the body is not secret either. That it is neither existent nor nonexistent is also not secret. That sentient beings are the Buddha: this is subtle and secret. How are sentient beings the Buddha? Sentient beings are neither existent nor nonexistent, neither non-existent nor non-nonexistent. Therefore sentient beings are the Buddha.

## Vastness and Refuge

"How does one become vast and serve as a refuge for beings?" Why is it called vast? Because there is no characteristic of consciousness, because there is nothing that is not buddha, because there is no practice that is not pure, because there is no virtue that is not complete, therefore it is said to serve as a refuge for all beings.

One who sees Śākyamuni as a refuge is not truly taking refuge. The Hīnayāna understanding is: through compassion, one causes sentient beings to take refuge. This is truly not an arhat. But to say one is equal to an arhat: formerly the teaching was that one born in the royal palace attains arhatship; now the teaching is that there was no birth in the royal palace and no extinction in the Śāla grove.

How is one who is not an arhat equal to an arhat? If Kāśyapa had not received the Buddha's instruction, he would not have asked about the four refuges.

Question: If the Tathāgata was not born in the royal palace and did not enter extinction in the Śāla grove, and obtained the precepts spontaneously without relying on the four fruits, being truly not an arhat, how then is the Tathāgata equal to an arhat?

The explanation: if the Tathāgata were truly an arhat, the four refuges could be equal to an arhat. But the Buddha is truly not an arhat; how then can one say he is equal to an arhat?

The body of Śākyamuni has two names: first, "One Who Should Come" (yìnglái 應來); second, "Bodhisattva of True Practice." Regarding "should come": from the Lotus Treasury World, the Great Adornment Buddha manifested as a prince, appearing to be born in the royal palace and entering extinction in the Śāla grove. This is the bodhisattva's play-manifestation. Second, regarding the true: there is no place from which he came. How then can he be equal to an arhat?

The Buddha has two kinds of names: first, True Buddha, who manifests in the same form as a śrāvaka arhat. But the Buddha is not truly a śrāvaka; how can he be equal to the four śrāvakas?

Explaining the former: if there had truly been an arhat, one could say "I am equal to an arhat." But there was never an arhat; saying "arhat" was my own creation. How can there be equality?

Another explanation: the body of Śākyamuni is named arhat, but the nature-stage bodhisattva: how can he be equal to an arhat? Second, the true-practice bodhisattva "who should come" can also manifest as a buddha. If Śākyamuni were truly an arhat, one could speak of equality. Since Śākyamuni was never an arhat, how can there be equality? Therefore the nature-stage bodhisattva is an arhat, and this arhat is this bodhisattva. Being truly not a buddha, how can the arhat be equal to the Buddha?

If Śākyamuni were truly an arhat, one could speak of equality. Since Śākyamuni was never an arhat, how can there be equality? Therefore the true-practice bodhisattva is an arhat. Therefore the birth in the royal palace and extinction in the Śāla grove are both play-manifestations; the bodhisattva manifests them. There was never previously a true arhat. Through my transformation, sentient beings attain arhatship; therefore I create the arhat. Second, the bodhisattva's transformation from the Lotus Treasury World is no different from me. Therefore, truly not being an arhat, one is "equal to an arhat."

If I were truly an arhat, bodhisattvas could be equal to me. Those who cling to me as an arhat: this is not the bodhisattva's nature-characteristic of transformative power; all is unreal.

The explanation of the four refuges: the first bhūmi (Joy) is the first refuge; the sixth bhūmi is the second refuge; the eighth bhūmi is the third refuge; the Dharma-Cloud bhūmi is the fourth refuge. The transformation into śrāvakas, the śrāvaka's vacuous severance: they have never been arhats. How can there be equality?

The bodhisattva is called the Dharma-Buddha, also called the Conditioned Buddha. What is the Dharma-Buddha? Born from the Dharma, through practicing the Dharma one obtains seeing; hence the name Dharma-Buddha. What is the Conditioned Buddha? Because of conditions, one sees; hence the name Conditioned Buddha.

Question: If Kāśyapa understood on his own, there was no need to ask in this way. If he had never heard or seen, how could he ask in this way?

Answer: Kāśyapa was one of the twelve youths. Through the Tathāgata's spiritual power, he was instructed and enabled to ask. Kāśyapa's questions are precisely about nirvāṇa; there is nothing else beyond this.

## Demonic Obstruction

"How does one know that the Heavenly Māra creates obstruction for beings?" Explanation: Kāśyapa's question was precisely about the Tathāgata's body, not about the future. How do we know this? Sentient beings' own bodies do not have faith. How then could an external māra come to create obstruction? Now the Tathāgata, having just attained buddhahood under the bodhi tree, with the True Dharma about to flourish: the māra fears losing his followers and therefore creates obstruction.

## True Speech of Those Tamed

"How do the tamed ones speak true truth with joyful minds?" What does "tamed" mean? Ordinary sentient beings know nothing. Hearing Mahāyāna, they take it as Mahāyāna; hearing Hīnayāna, they take it as Hīnayāna; hearing suffering, they take it as suffering; hearing bliss, they take it as bliss. How can this be called "tamed"? To speak of suffering where there is no suffering, to speak of bliss where there is no bliss, to speak of permanence where there is no permanence, formerly teaching the small and now teaching the great: this is also not called "speaking true truth with joyful mind" or "being tamed."

Now to teach that impermanence is not impermanence, that suffering and bliss are not suffering and bliss, that there is no coming and no going: this is called speaking true truth.

## The Four Inversions

"Perfect in right goodness, one expounds the four inversions." What is "perfect in right goodness"? The bodhisattva practices the four immeasurable minds and the ten pāramitās without partiality: this is called "right goodness" in characteristics. The bodhisattva's practice is entirely right goodness.

The śrāvaka, having discrimination of self and other, is not called "right goodness." The bodhisattva, without discrimination of self and other, is called "right goodness."

Second meaning of "right goodness": the former teaching was not right; the śrāvaka did not achieve completeness. Now the nirvāṇa principle is right, without coming or going, without arising or ceasing: this is called "right goodness" in completeness. Second, from the Joy bhūmi upward to the Dharma-Cloud bhūmi: this is called "accomplishing completeness."

"Expounding the four inversions": the śrāvakas say "I am permanent, blissful, self, and pure" and that "the Buddha's teaching of suffering, emptiness, and impermanence" is the inversion. The śrāvakas' suffering, emptiness, and impermanence, and the Buddha's permanence, bliss, self, and purity, are also inversions. The Buddha's permanence, bliss, self, and purity, and sentient beings' suffering, emptiness, and impermanence, are also inversions.

How does the Tathāgata expound the four inversions? He teaches them for the śrāvakas. Rightly expounding the four inversions is itself non-inversion. There is no external dharma that is inversion or non-inversion. This is called "speaking true truth with a joyful mind." The sūtra says: "Teaching the Dharma as neither existent nor nonexistent: this is called true truth."

## Buddha-Nature

"How do bodhisattvas see the nature that is difficult to see?" Kāśyapa had two intentions: first, he wished all sentient beings to know that buddha-nature exists; second, he did not wish them to see buddha-nature. Why did he not wish them to know and see? He wished the Tathāgata to explain buddha-nature profoundly. Why is it called "profound"? Buddha-nature is not something that can be made, created, cultivated, or obtained; therefore it is called profound. Śrāvakas, being narrow and incomplete, cannot see it. Bodhisattvas, practicing compassion and broad salvation, do not seek to see it. Because they act for the sake of sentient beings and are bound by this, it is called "difficult to see."

Second explanation: Buddha-nature is not a dharma that can be seen. Because it is neither the seer nor the seen, neither the knower nor the known, neither the cultivator nor the cultivated: therefore it is called "seeing the nature that is difficult to see."

## Full Letter and Half Letter

"How does one understand the full letter and the half letter?" The half letter is the gradual teaching; the full letter is nirvāṇa, the complete teaching; hence the name "full letter." It encompasses all the fruit-merits of the Buddha's teaching; hence "full letter." The teaching of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas is incomplete; hence "half letter."

Nirvāṇa is called both "sudden" and "gradual." Now, discussing nirvāṇa, with the two truths in counterpoint, the full letter is complete. In terms of practice, there is the full and the not-full; hence it is called gradual teaching. In terms of principle, there is no full or not-full. Therefore nirvāṇa is called gradual teaching in relation to the half letter. Nirvāṇa is called sudden teaching.

Second, further: the terms "full" and "half" are sentient beings' false conceptions. In principle, there is no full or half. Therefore nirvāṇa is called gradual teaching.

"How do bodhisattvas see the nature that is difficult to see?" This is merely seeing dharma. And again, "How does one understand the full letter and the half letter?" Now there is no further seeing or not-seeing.

## The Sacred Practice

"How is the sacred practice shared, like the sārasā bird?" The Tathāgata in the royal palace took a wife and had children; at other times he went forth and was together with the śrāvakas. Like the sārasā bird that lives together in a flock and cannot be distinguished: the sage, the Tathāgata, cultivating and practicing together with all sentient beings. Hence: "How is the sacred practice shared, like the sārasā bird?"

Second explanation: for ordinary people, form constitutes the sage; for śrāvakas, form and mind constitute the sage; the bodhisattva-sage is neither mind nor form. To say there is mind and form: this is therefore not a sage. The śrāvaka-sage shares in the aspect of form. The bodhisattva-sage, without blue, yellow, red, white, or mental consciousness, in principle shares with those who have mental consciousness as ordinary beings and without mental consciousness as sages; hence "sage."

The term "sārasā bird" is a general name, symbolizing the Tathāgata being indistinguishable from all sentient beings. "Kāliṅka" symbolizes nirvāṇa's distinction from all sentient beings, returning to the śrāvaka meaning.

The bodhisattva knows that the Tathāgata and all sentient beings are without difference; hence "shared." There is a shared understanding of mutual non-abandonment. Before the Tathāgata appeared in the world, there were ordinary people and sages who abandoned each other. When the Tathāgata appeared, all sentient beings did not abandon each other. This is the śrāvaka meaning. The bodhisattva's non-abandonment: even when the Tathāgata has not appeared, there is no abandonment; when the Tathāgata appears, there is also no separation.

Conventionally, there is no suffering, emptiness, or impermanence. In the ultimate truth, there is no permanence, bliss, self, or purity. Some say: there is no conventional truth; there is permanence, bliss, self, and purity. This is a shallow understanding. Others say: "existent" is conventional truth, "nonexistent" is ultimate truth; "both existent and nonexistent" is conventional truth, "neither existent nor nonexistent" is ultimate truth. Beyond "neither existent nor nonexistent" there is no further dharma; hence it is called the shared sacred practice.

## Sun, Moon, and Stars

"Kāliṅka: sun and moon, Venus and Jupiter." What do "sun and moon" signify? This sun and moon is a simile: ordinary people see the sun and moon as rising and setting, but sages have never seen rising and setting. Second, śrāvakas see the Buddha as born in the royal palace and entering extinction in the Śāla grove. Bodhisattvas have never seen the birth in the royal palace or the entering of nirvāṇa in the Śāla grove.

Third, because the sun and moon set, Venus and Jupiter appear; people of the world find this strange. When the Tathāgata enters nirvāṇa, the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas emerge, their dharma being different from the Tathāgata's; hence it is said the stars appear.

Sentient beings falsely see the sun, moon, and stars as rising and setting, but in reality there is no rising or setting. Sentient beings see the Tathāgata as having birth and death, but the Tathāgata in reality has no birth or death.

## Arousing the Aspiration

"How is it that those who have not aroused the aspiration are called bodhisattvas?" Śrāvakas have arousal and non-arousal; pratyekabuddhas also have arousal and non-arousal; bodhisattvas also have arousal and non-arousal. These three kinds of bodhisattvas are called "those who have aroused the aspiration."

What constitutes arousing the aspiration? When a different fruit can be obtained, this is called arousing the aspiration.

From the Tathāgata's initial teaching, sentient beings have both arousal and non-arousal. In the former teaching, there was arousal, but it was not called true arousal. Why was it not called true arousal? Seeing a different buddha that can be obtained and sought, this differentiation is not called arousal. What is called true arousal? Now the teaching of nirvāṇa's signless principle, through its permeation, causes all to arouse the aspiration; this is called arousal. There are no śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, or bodhisattvas with arousal or non-arousal.

Hence: "Those who have not aroused the aspiration are called bodhisattvas." Because nirvāṇa illuminates all equally, all who have not aroused the aspiration are called bodhisattvas.

Why did Kāśyapa ask "How is it that those who have not aroused the aspiration are called bodhisattvas?" Those who arouse the aspiration see the sun and moon; those who do not arouse the aspiration do not see the sun and moon. Second, those who arouse the aspiration see the permanent abiding; those who do not arouse the aspiration do not see it. Formerly, when the Tathāgata appeared, there were those who aroused and those who did not arouse; those who aroused saw, those who did not arouse did not see. Now, in nirvāṇa's equal illumination, arousing is both arousing and non-arousing.

## Fearlessness

"How does one obtain fearlessness among the great assembly?" The bodhisattva appears in the world with equal compassion, not destroying the characteristics of sentient beings; hence "fearless." The bodhisattva is fearless, and sentient beings are also fearless. Why are sentient beings not afraid? Because the bodhisattva appears in the world without destroying the characteristics of sentient beings; therefore sentient beings are not afraid.

When the Tathāgata appears, with the four immeasurable minds of loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity, equal and without differentiation, there is no heavenly māra, no non-Buddhist, not even an icchantika that is not regarded as one's own child, without fear. Why are sentient beings not afraid? Because all sentient beings regard the Tathāgata as father and mother; therefore they are not afraid.

What does "icchantika" mean? One who does not recognize the Buddha, does not recognize the inner or outer path: this is called an icchantika.

Question: The icchantika does not recognize inner or outer. How does this differ from the bodhisattva?

Answer: The bodhisattva does not recognize inner or outer and does not kill. The icchantika does not recognize them and therefore practices killing.

"Like jāmbūnada gold, whose faults none can name." This is the meaning of harmonizing the assembly. Jāmbūnada gold has four kinds: (1) blue, (2) yellow, (3) red, and (4) purple-ground. Blue symbolizes the non-Buddhists; yellow symbolizes the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas; red symbolizes the six-pāramitā bodhisattvas; purple-ground symbolizes the Tathāgata.

Jāmbūnada gold has blue, yellow, red, and white, four kinds of colors. Second, worldly fine things, however beautiful, still have some deficiency. Jāmbūnada gold is not like this; its faults cannot be named. This symbolizes the Tathāgata who has attained nirvāṇa, and likewise the various śrāvakas, non-Buddhists, and six-pāramitā bodhisattvas: like jāmbūnada gold, whose faults cannot be named.

Purple-ground gold possesses all colors; this symbolizes nirvāṇa possessing the heavenly māras, the non-Buddhists, the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, and the six-pāramitā bodhisattvas. Why is this so? Because beyond this there is no other dharma. Purple-ground gold is complete in all colors and cannot be described. The śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, the six-pāramitā practitioners, the non-Buddhists: because of their variety, they cannot be described. The principle of nirvāṇa is not a dharma of blue, yellow, red, or white, yet it is also blue, yellow, red, and white; hence it cannot be described. If there were blue, yellow, red, and white, one could name faults. But these blue, yellow, red, and white have never existed; hence one cannot describe them.

## The Lotus in Murky Water

"How does one dwell in the turbid world and remain undefiled like a lotus?" The turbid world means the five turbidities. What are the five turbidities? The kalpa-turbidity, the affliction-turbidity. Śākyamuni appeared in the five turbidities: born in the royal palace, with wife and children, gold, silver, the seven treasures, and various material wealth; all of this is called turbidity. Not being stained by it: hence "dwelling in the turbid world, undefiled like a lotus."

Second, the teachings of the second and third vehicles are called turbidity. Gold represents nirvāṇa. Not being stained by the three vehicles: hence "undefiled."

## Dwelling amid Afflictions

"How does one dwell amid afflictions, with afflictions unable to stain? Like a physician healing all diseases, not being infected by the diseases." Kāśyapa's question concerns the Tathāgata: the afflictions of the three realms and the ninety-eight latent tendencies, yet the Tathāgata appears without being defiled.

All sentient beings are defiled by the afflictions of the three realms and the ninety-eight latent tendencies. Second, the śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and six-pāramitā practitioners, conceiving of afflictions to be severed and buddha-fruit to be obtained: therefore they are defiled by afflictions. The tenth-bhūmi bodhisattva, practicing with penetrating great wisdom: not defiled by afflictions. Fourth, even the great bodhisattva, looking toward the fruit: also defiled by afflictions.

Now nirvāṇa is not something obtained through cause and fruit; therefore it is not defiled. Fifth, the four-truths teaching and up to the Prajñāpāramitā and Lotus Sūtra are also called "defiled by afflictions." Now the principle of nirvāṇa, without flowing, without gain or loss, without arising or ceasing: therefore it is not defiled. Like a physician healing all diseases, not being infected by the diseases.

The Tathāgata appears in the world: from the three refuges and five precepts at the very beginning, up through the bodhisattva precepts, from a snap of the fingers and a bow of the head, gradually teaching and transforming, healing the diseases of sentient beings. Knowing afflictions, he is not infected by diseases. Sentient beings, not knowing afflictions, are always infected by diseases.

Second, the graduated teaching is also called "healing sentient beings' diseases." Third, the teaching of spiritual power, supernatural transformations and complete subjugation, is also called "healing sentient beings' diseases." Fourth, now the teaching of nirvāṇa heals the afflictions of the former graduated teachings, and is also called "healing all diseases."

What is called "affliction"? All sentient beings who have not aroused the aspiration are called "afflicted." Seeing the sixteen-foot body and arousing the aspiration, and also understanding that it is not the sixteen-foot body that causes the aspiration: nirvāṇa illuminates equally, causing the aspiration to arise; hence it is called "healing all diseases."

## The Great Ocean of Birth and Death

"In the great ocean of birth and death, how does one serve as a ferryman?" The three realms are called birth and death. The Tathāgata's appearance in the world is also called birth and death. Why is it called birth and death? Because the Tathāgata's Dharma can ferry sentient beings beyond birth and death; hence "in the great ocean of birth and death, serving as a ferryman."

Second, the sixteen-foot body teaching the graduated dharma, up through the Lotus Sūtra, is also called the dharma of birth and death. Now the teaching of nirvāṇa, without coming or going, without arising or ceasing, ferries beyond the former birth-and-death teaching; hence "ferryman."

First explanation: the sixteen-foot body is not birth-and-death, and the teaching is not birth-or-death. Second explanation: the sixteen-foot body and the teaching are also birth-and-death; nirvāṇa is without birth or death. Third: after the Buddha enters extinction, who can ferry beyond birth and death? Only the great bodhisattva can ferry beyond birth and death. Fourth: even the bodhisattva cannot ferry; the great principle of nirvāṇa can ferry beyond birth and death.

A simile: a worldly ferryman, skilled in means, knowing the difficulties of the sea; the great ocean of afflictions, with the three realms as the boat; the Tathāgata through various skillful means teaches the three vehicles as the ferryman. A worldly ferryman points to cause and points to fruit, and sentient beings board the boat. The Tathāgata, through skillful means of the three vehicles, teaches cause and fruit, saying that leaving the three realms is still birth and death; therefore the three realms serve as the boat.

The first graduated teaching: using birth and death to ferry sentient beings; hence "ferryman." The Lotus Sūtra uses the myriad practices as the ferryman. Now nirvāṇa uses "no birth and death" as the boat.

Why was the question about being "infected by diseases" followed by "In the great ocean of birth and death, how does one serve as a ferryman?" The former question removes afflictions. And "in the great ocean of afflictions, serving as a ferryman, ferrying to the other shore."

## Shedding the Old Skin

"How does one abandon birth and death, like a snake shedding its old skin?" Kāśyapa asks: the Tathāgata appeared in the world amid birth and death, and now teaches the path of entering nirvāṇa. "I am without birth and without death, like a snake shedding its old skin, without birth or extinction."

The graduated teaching of the sixteen-foot body is the dharma of birth and death; now the teaching of nirvāṇa is without extinction. The former graduated teaching has no true principle, like a snake shedding its old skin: without benefit and without merit. Now nirvāṇa, without birth and without extinction, also does not destroy the teaching.

Second, all sentient beings, including everything spoken and unspoken, all forms and categories, are nothing but empty names. Only nirvāṇa is true principle.

## The Three Jewels as the Wish-Fulfilling Tree

"How does one contemplate the Three Jewels as the divine wish-fulfilling tree?" The Three Jewels are called "standard and model." The Tathāgata appeared to demonstrate that the Three Jewels exist. Because the Three Jewels exist, one takes the three refuges and obtains the five precepts, and also obtains the merit of a snap of the fingers, and whatever one wishes to cultivate and whatever one wishes to obtain.

The Tathāgata is also called the wish-fulfilling Three Jewels, also called the Tathāgata's wish-fulfillment and sentient beings' wish-fulfillment. How is it sentient beings' wish-fulfillment? Because one can take the three refuges and five precepts as one wishes, up through the bodhisattva practices, all obtaining their fruit; hence "sentient beings' wish-fulfillment." The Tathāgata's wish-fulfillment: responding to the capacities of sentient beings; hence "wish-fulfillment."

Now "nirvāṇa's wish-fulfillment": all suffering and bliss, good and evil, are nothing that is not principle; hence "wish-fulfillment." Hence "contemplating the Three Jewels as the divine wish-fulfilling tree."

The simile: in the Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven there is a tree that can fulfill whatever the devas wish; hence it is called the wish-fulfilling tree. The devas' practices over a long time produced this tree. The Three Jewels are likewise: sentient beings' practices over a long time produced the sixteen-foot body; hence "the Three Jewels are like the divine wish-fulfilling tree."

## The Three Vehicles

"If the three vehicles have no nature, how then can they be taught?" The three vehicles are not the Tathāgata teaching one dharma. The three vehicles exist because of sentient beings' dispositions: "with one voice the teaching is given, and beings understand according to their kind." Although the Tathāgata teaches the three vehicles, this is not the Tathāgata's original intention. What is the Tathāgata's original intention? Nirvāṇa is.

Kāśyapa's question asks: "If the three vehicles have no nature, how can they be taught?" The Tathāgata's answer: all buddhas do not teach the three vehicles for sentient beings. Now the real characteristics of nirvāṇa: the small is not small, the great is not great. One should know that the three-vehicle teaching is of one characteristic, without great or small.

Third, in the place where nirvāṇa's principle is taught, there is no "great" or "small." Because sentient beings' wisdom is differentiated, the teaching is differentiated; the principle is without the differentiation of great and small. Hence one can teach great and small. What was formerly called differentiation is precisely non-differentiation; there is nothing else beyond this.

## Pleasure Not Yet Arisen

"Like pleasure not yet arisen, how can one say one receives pleasure?" Kāśyapa asks: sentient beings do not know pleasure; how can one say they receive pleasure? Ordinary people have suffering but no pleasure. Bodhisattvas have pleasure but no suffering. Why do they have pleasure? Because the bodhisattva's wisdom penetrates to the fruit, he knows pleasure. Sentient beings do not know, and therefore suffer. The bodhisattva knows the fruit, but saying the bodhisattva's pleasure is not truly pleasure, and saying sentient beings' suffering is not truly suffering: both are equally false. Nirvāṇa is without suffering and without pleasure; this is called great pleasure. Hence "pleasure not yet arisen: how can one say one receives pleasure?"

## Not Destroying the Assembly

"How do bodhisattvas obtain the indestructible assembly?" First explanation: śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, six-pāramitā bodhisattvas, and even non-Buddhists, having distinctions of self and other, having attainment and realization: this is called "destroying the assembly." Second, the bodhisattva's secret practice and secret teaching, knowing sentient beings' roots and natures, not destroying sentient beings' characteristics: this is called "not destroying." What the bodhisattva does not destroy, the bodhisattva can destroy; what the bodhisattva cannot destroy is not the bodhisattva's domain. Able to act in this way without going against the nature of dharmas: hence "indestructible assembly."

Third, the bodhisattva knows that nirvāṇa is without blue, yellow, red, or white, without self and other; hence "indestructible assembly." Fourth, "indestructible" because the principle of nirvāṇa is not attained, not realized, not created, not made; hence "obtaining the indestructible assembly." Fifth, true principle does not destroy sentient beings, and sentient beings do not destroy principle. Why is this so? Because sentient beings are this principle; beyond the principle, there are no further sentient beings. Hence "indestructible assembly."

## Leading the Blind

"How does one serve as eyes and a guide for the blind from birth?" Kāśyapa asks: how does one serve as eyes and a guide?

First explanation: the former teaching of śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and six-pāramitā: following appearances in understanding, this is called "blind from birth." The Tathāgata explains to the śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and six-pāramitā: if they had ever attained something, it could not be opened. This dharma has never existed, and therefore can be opened.

A simile: a blind person does not know blue, yellow, red, or white. If someone describes blue, yellow, red, and white, the person becomes more confused. But if one says there is no blue, yellow, red, or white, this accords with what the blind person understands; this is called "opening."

Beings of the three vehicles assert that there are causes and fruits, differentiation, attainment and non-attainment. Now to speak of nirvāṇa: if there were long and short, attainment and non-attainment, they would become more confused. To say that nirvāṇa is without blue, yellow, red, or white, without attainment or realization, without long or short: hence it is called "opening" for the reason that the sixteen-foot body's cultivating the path and maintaining precepts and making offerings is not the cause; the cause is buddha-nature, without attainment and without cultivation.

All sentient beings regard the three-vehicle practitioners as having the Dharma; but the three-vehicle practitioners are called "blue-blind." Not only the three-vehicle practitioners; all sentient beings are also blue-blind. The Dharma is not something that can be seen or not seen, opened or not opened, reached or not reached. Because of the teaching, one speaks of "blue-blind." In principle, there is no "blue-blind" and there are no blind ones. Therefore to say "blue-blind" and "not blue-blind" does not go against the nature of dharmas.

If these two truly existed, one could say "they never existed" or "they never did not exist." Why is it that speaking of what exists and does not exist, opening and not opening, all does not go against the Dharma? Hence the word "opening."

## Teaching with Many Approaches

"How does one demonstrate the many approaches? May the Great Sage please explain!" Kāśyapa's question: the Tathāgata's initial teaching used many approaches; why does the present nirvāṇa have only one, without a second?

The Tathāgata answers: it is not I who use many approaches. Because sentient beings in the past practiced with diverse dispositions, I taught with many approaches. Hence "demonstrating the many approaches."

Second, the dharmas are many. Why are they called many? Because the nature of dharmas is such; therefore one demonstrates the many approaches.

Third, the reason for teaching many. If this dharma existed, it could be called "teaching many." This dharma has never existed; therefore it is not "teaching many."

Fourth, the principle of nirvāṇa is such by nature: it is not "many" or "not many."

Fifth, true principle from beginning to end is not a dharma of existence or nonexistence. Therefore whether one teaches or does not teach, there is no obstruction.

This completes the Treatise on Nirvāṇa, composed by Vasubandhu, translated by the Tripiṭaka Master Dharmabodhi of the Northern Wei.
