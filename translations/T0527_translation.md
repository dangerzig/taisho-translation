# 佛說逝童子經
## Sūtra of the Boy Shi, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0527

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Zhi Fadu (支法度)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Rājagṛha, on Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain. At dawn, together with the bhikṣus, he donned his kāṣāya robe, took his alms-bowl, and entered the city to beg for food. The Buddha walked toward the gate of the householder Pūrṇaka. Pūrṇaka had a son, sixteen years old, named Shi. At that time, Shi was standing inside the third gate. From afar he saw the Buddha coming, possessed of wondrous marks, with a handsome and dignified countenance, his mind calm and his faculties serene. Light emanated from behind his head, surpassingly radiant, blazing and brilliant; like the sun in its purity, like the full moon at its zenith, it illuminated all within the gates.

Shi, seeing the Buddha thus, was filled with joy and awed reverence. He quickly went upstairs and spoke to his mother in verse:

> "With golden radiance a hundredfold,
> Rare is this sight and sound.
> He has now come and stands outside;
> We should give him what he seeks.
> His dignity surpasses the king of Brahmā,
> His radiance excels all the devas.
> Out of compassion for me he has come;
> I beg you, let us offer to him."

His mother, hearing Shi's words, said: "If he is as you describe, how could such a person be poor? Why would he be a beggar? How deluded you are! What you say makes no sense at all."

At that time, the Buddha manifested his supernatural power, releasing his radiance, which shone through all seven gates, filling them with brilliant light. Shi, moved by the Buddha's divine power, again spoke to his mother in verse:

> "It is like one who sees a fire
> And throws himself straight into it.
> One who is unwholesome toward the Buddha
> Injures himself in just this way.
> Uniquely honored among devas and humans,
> The supreme sage, beyond whom there is none higher,
> He is the most worthy of offerings;
> Giving to him surely brings great benefit.
> Please take whatever food we have
> And give my portion to me.
> I wish to offer it to the supreme honored one;
> This joy is hard to encounter again."

His mother then gave Shi whatever fine clothing and food was at hand. Shi took them and went to the Buddha. He bowed with his head to the ground, making obeisance to the Buddha, then stood to one side with palms joined, and said to the Buddha: "Now that I have seen the Tathāgata, with reverent heart and my mind focused on the Buddha, I humbly ask that you show compassion and accept what I offer."

The Buddha accepted at once, and spoke a verse to Shi:

> "You have overcome the stingy mind
> And skillfully practiced giving.
> Today you make offerings to the Buddha;
> May all your thoughts be auspicious."

Shi, hearing the Buddha's words, spoke a verse:

> "I do not wish for wealth and honor,
> Nor do I aspire to Śakra or Brahmā.
> I only wish for the supreme wisdom,
> Like the Buddha, unsurpassed."

At that time, Lord Śakra descended and stood before Shi, and spoke a verse:

> "With just a single act of giving
> You wish to seek buddhahood? That is hard!
> One must give continually, life after life,
> Accumulating treasures like Mount Sumeru,
> Through a thousand billion kalpas,
> Always practicing a mind of loving-kindness.
> One cannot, by a single gift,
> Attain the unsurpassed path of awakening."

Shi then replied to Lord Śakra in verse:

> "It is like a great craftsman
> Who wishes to fell a mighty tree.
> He does not, with a single stroke of the axe,
> Cut down the great tree at once.
> Stroke by stroke, gradually,
> He can clear the forest of a great mountain.
> Merit accrues from small beginnings;
> Seeking the path is just like this.
> I know it is not by one gift alone
> That one attains the great path,
> But I have faith and diligence,
> And I shall surely be a leader of the world."

Lord Śakra again spoke a verse to Shi:

> "Better to seek to become a noble deva;
> Śakra and Brahmā are easier to attain.
> Few can fulfill the Buddha's Dharma;
> The buddha-path is exceedingly hard to attain."

Shi replied to Lord Śakra in verse:

> "Even if the whole Jambudvīpa
> Were filled with raging fire,
> I would cast my body throughout it all
> And never abandon my resolve for buddhahood.
> Even if all the people of the world
> Together tried to harm and injure me,
> I would always turn a compassionate mind toward them
> And never forsake the great path.
> How fragile are Śakra and the heavens of Brahmā!
> They are all subject to the law of death.
> I take refuge in omniscience,
> Brave as the lion-king."

Lord Śakra spoke yet another verse to Shi:

> "How wonderful that you have gained such benefit!
> Your reverence for the Buddha is profound.
> With single-minded devotion to the great path,
> I am certain you shall become a Buddha."

Shi replied to Lord Śakra in verse:

> "Heavenly King, have no more doubts
> About this unsurpassed path.
> With diligence I shall never slacken,
> And in this world I shall become a Buddha."

At this, Lord Śakra fell silent.

Then the Buddha spoke a verse to Shi:

> "In your past lives
> You served eight thousand Buddhas.
> Your mind has always aspired to the great path,
> Wishing to bring peace to all living beings.
> For the next twelve billion kalpas
> You shall never fall into evil destinies,
> But shall cultivate much virtue and goodness,
> Always upholding a noble aspiration.
> You shall a hundred billion times
> Become a cakravartin king,
> And also serve as a Four Heavenly King,
> Each time practicing the right Dharma.
> You shall also become Lord Śakra,
> Never far from pure conduct,
> And later be reborn in Tuṣita heaven;
> Then your path of virtue shall be complete.
> You shall dwell in a realm of a thousand kingdoms
> And in its center become a Buddha
> Named Sumerikalpa;
> All beings shall serve and honor you.
> Each of the thousand kingdoms shall extend
> Four hundred and eighty li in breadth,
> And the grandeur of their palace walls
> Shall all be adorned with treasures.
> At the first assembly of teaching the Dharma,
> Sixty thousand billion people,
> Becoming your disciples, shall be liberated,
> All attaining arhatship.
> At the second assembly of teaching the sūtras,
> Forty thousand billion people
> Shall all enter the wisdom of arhatship;
> Those you deliver shall be exceedingly many.
> At the third assembly of teaching the sūtras,
> Those you deliver shall be exceedingly many,
> Leaving defilements and entering pure wisdom,
> All attaining freedom from attachment.
> In that buddha-land at that time
> There shall be no disorderly or wicked multitudes;
> All shall turn toward the path and the Dharma,
> And all shall practice loyalty and uprightness.
> The griefs and sufferings of illness
> Shall have no demons there.
> The people shall all live in harmony,
> Each caring for one another's well-being.
> The heavens shall rain three times a day,
> Just enough to settle the dust.
> Cold and heat shall always be temperate,
> And you shall deliver people of every kind.
> Men and women of good families,
> If they wish to give rise to wholesome intentions
> And to revere and love the Buddha,
> Should make offerings in just this way.
> I now expound and declare
> What the bodhisattva should practice.
> Before the face of right awakening,
> One at once attains the Buddha's eye of wisdom.
> The Buddhas are without number,
> And the sūtra Dharma is inexhaustible.
> If one pays homage with countless acts of reverence,
> The reward of merit shall also be without measure."

When the Buddha had finished giving this prediction, the householder's son Shi, Lord Śakra, and all the bhikṣus, having heard the sūtra, were filled with joy. They came forward, made obeisance to the Buddha, and departed.
