# 十二因緣論
## Treatise on the Twelve Links of Dependent Origination

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1651

Composed by Śuddhamati (淨意). Translated from the Chinese. Later Wei dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流支).

---

> Homage to the Honored Sage, the wondrous Dharma, and the saṃgha of bhikṣus.
> I briefly compose this treatise on dependent origination to make its meaning manifest.
> The Sage has proclaimed the twelve supreme links;
> The dharmas arising from dependent origination are subsumed in three.
> Affliction, karma, and suffering, explained in order, should be known.
> Affliction is the first, eighth, and ninth; karma the second and tenth.
> The remaining seven are called suffering; three subsume the twelve dharmas.
> From three arise two; from two arise seven.
> From seven again arise three; therefore it turns like a wheel.
> All worldly dharmas are merely cause and effect, with no person.
> Only from empty dharmas do empty dharmas alone arise.
> Recitation, lamp, seal, mirror, echo, sun, crystal, seed, and water:
> Whether the aggregates transfer or do not transfer, the wise should carefully reflect.

There is a disciple, accomplished in practice, who can receive and retain whatever Dharma he hears, preventing it from being forgotten. Regarding the Tathāgata's Dharma, namely matters of fact and non-fact, and of nature and characteristics, he harbors doubt and confusion in his mind. Wishing to gain understanding, he asks: "Venerable One:

> The Sage has proclaimed the twelve supreme links;
> The dharmas arising from dependent origination are subsumed in three.
> Regarding these and other such matters, I now ask to understand.
> Please explain for me and sever the net of my doubts."

The teacher, seeing the disciple's mind thirst with longing for the Dharma, and that he asks with respect, answers: "Listen!

> The twelve supreme links are subsumed in three:
> Affliction, karma, and suffering, explained in order, should be known.
> Herein, ten and two make twelve;
> Since they are not different links, they are called 'supreme links.'
> Like the parts of a chariot, they are called 'links' — this should be understood."

'Sage' (muni) means 'quiescence'; it also means 'non-discrimination,' 'concentration,' and 'inexpressibility.' What the Sage has proclaimed, expounded, and eloquently explained is called 'provisional designation.' However, they are not produced by the characteristics of a great person or hero, by autonomous will, by fixed time, or by inherent nature. They are produced solely by causes and conditions. These twelve links, in affliction, karma, and suffering, with three dharmas alternately serving as mutual causes and conditions, are like the legs of a tripod. They are subsumed within these three categories.

Question: What are afflictions? What is karma? What is suffering? How do these dependent origination dharmas come to be subsumed in the supreme links?

Answer: Among these twelve supreme links, the first is ignorance, the eighth is craving, and the ninth is grasping. These three supreme links are subsumed under affliction. The second is formations and the tenth is becoming; these two supreme links are subsumed under karma. The remaining seven supreme links are subsumed under suffering. These three, affliction, karma, and suffering, subsume the twelve links. "Remaining seven" means consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, feeling, birth, and aging-and-death. Separation from the beloved, meeting with the despised, and not obtaining what one seeks: such dharmas give rise to all suffering. All these links are rooted in affliction, karma, and suffering as previously stated. The twelve links should be understood as subsumed in only three categories, with nothing remaining. In all the sūtras, there are only these links, and nothing else.

Question: The meaning of these supreme links is now understood. Please explain for me: in what do affliction, karma, and suffering reside? And how do they accomplish all things?

Answer: From three arise two. The three are afflictions and the two are karma; from afflictions, karma arises. From two arise seven. The seven are suffering; from karma, suffering arises. From seven arise three; from suffering, afflictions arise. Thus affliction, karma, and suffering alternate in producing one another, and therefore the wheel of existence turns indeterminately. "Existence" refers to the desire realm, form realm, and formless realm. Within these, without rest, there is transmigration, like a turning wheel. Because of existence, all worldly ordinary beings revolve up and down in succession, like a turning wheel, indeterminate within existence. Because of this indeterminacy, three categories are spoken of.

Question: The autonomous being who fashions all bodies — who is that? What are its activities?

Answer: The verse says: "All worldly dharmas are merely cause and effect, with no person; apart from conventional designation, there is existence. This is right reflection." Since it is not said to be intrinsic nature, the notion of a being who creates is untenable.

Question: If so, how does one go from the present world to the future world?

Answer: Not even a single hair-tip of a dharma goes from the present world to the future world. Therefore the verse says: "Only from empty dharmas do empty dharmas alone arise." This clarifies that self and mine are empty: within affliction and karma, these five dharmic processes are by nature free of self. One should understand it thus.

Question: If within dharmas whose nature is without self, what is without self proceeds, what evidence is there?

Answer: The verse says: "Recitation, lamp, seal, mirror, echo, sun, crystal, seed, and water." Such similes can be taken as evidence, for they establish that self-substance is not real but is merely a provisional name. As for going from the present world to the future world: just as the teacher's recitation does not truly transfer from teacher to student — although it does not transfer, can one say the meaning of transmission to the student is not established? Yet one cannot say the student obtains it without a cause, for this would be guarding against the fault of the wrong view of causelessness. Likewise, at the time of death, the mind-consciousness does not go to the future world, guarding against the fault of permanence. Nor does the future body come from some other place, guarding against the fault of the wrong view of causelessness. Just as the teacher's recitation serves as the cause and the student obtains it, that cannot be called identical, nor can it be called entirely different. Likewise, at the time of death, the mind-consciousness serves as the cause, and thereby the mind-consciousness of the subsequent body arises. That mind-consciousness cannot be called one, cannot be called different, is neither separate from it nor identical with it. Thus from a lamp a lamp is produced; from a seal a seal is produced; from a mirror an image is produced; from a sound an echo arises; from the sun and a crystal, fire is produced; from a seed a sprout arises; and from a pomegranate, mango, and such fruits, saliva arises in the mouth. Such dharmas are not called identical and not called different. In this way, in all dharmas of dependent origination, whether there is transfer or no transfer, the wise should carefully reflect and understand. "Aggregates" here means form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. For the being who takes rebirth, these aggregates cease, and through the cessation of those aggregates, subsequent similar aggregates arise. Yet truly not even a single hair-tip of a dharma goes from here to there. This is the meaning of the world's gradual progression. For this reason, all the world is impermanent, impure, suffering, and without self. Because one can contemplate such matters, one does not give rise to doubt and confusion regarding dharmas. Because one does not doubt, craving does not arise. Because craving does not arise, attachment does not arise. Because attachment does not arise, thirst does not arise. Because thirst does not arise, karma is not created. Because there is no karma, one does not grasp at things. Because one does not grasp, one does not create conditioned actions. Because there are no conditioned actions, there is no further birth. Because there is no birth, there is no suffering of body and mind. Thus, because the five kinds of causes are not created, in other places the seven kinds of fruit do not arise. Because there is no fruit, this is called liberation. By doing thus, one establishes the meaning of no birth and no destruction, neither permanent nor annihilated. Questions of finite and infinite and such propositions — regarding these there are verses:

> Not seeing anything born without conditions
> Is definitively the right meaning.
> Regarding the most subtle matters,
> Therefore annihilationism is not established.
>
> Herein nothing is diminished,
> And nothing is increased.
> One should see as truly real,
> In accordance with conditions, and as such.
