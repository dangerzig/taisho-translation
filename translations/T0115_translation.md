# 佛說馬有八態譬人經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Eight Behaviors of Horses as a Simile for People

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 115

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han, translated by Zhī Yào

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was staying in the country of Śrāvastī, dwelling in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park, together with many bhikṣus. The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Horses have eight bad and wicked behaviors. What are these eight? The first behavior: when the bridle and reins are loosened, the horse immediately pulls the cart and tries to bolt. The second behavior: when hitched to the cart, it bucks and kicks, wanting to bite people. The third behavior: it rears up, lifting its two front hooves, and pulls the cart away. The fourth behavior: it kicks the cart railing. The fifth behavior: it rears up standing like a person, grasps the yoke, rubs against the cart, and backs up. The sixth behavior: it goes sideways and runs crookedly. The seventh behavior: it pulls the cart and gallops wildly, then upon reaching muddy ground, it plants itself and stops, refusing to go any further. The eighth behavior: when a feed basket is hung before it, it stares fixedly but refuses to eat; when its master leads it away wanting to harness it, it then frantically chomps and bites, wanting to eat but unable to get food."

The Buddha said: "People also have eight bad and wicked behaviors. What are these eight? The first behavior: when one hears the sūtra being expounded, one immediately runs away and does not wish to listen; just as when a horse has its bridle and reins loosened and pulls the cart, trying to bolt. The second behavior: when one hears the sūtra being expounded, one does not understand its meaning and does not know where the words are pointing; one becomes angry and kicks up a fuss, not wishing to hear; just as when a horse hitched to the cart bucks and kicks, wanting to bite people. The third behavior: when one hears the sūtra being expounded, one contradicts it and refuses to accept it; just as when a horse rears up lifting its two front hooves and pulls the cart away. The fourth behavior: when one hears the sūtra being expounded, one reviles it; just as when a horse kicks the cart railing. The fifth behavior: when one hears the sūtra being expounded, one gets up and leaves; just as when a horse rears up standing like a person, grasps the yoke, rubs against the cart, and backs up. The sixth behavior: when one hears the sūtra being expounded, one refuses to listen, turns one's head aside with sidelong glances and whispers to others; just as when a horse goes sideways and runs crookedly. The seventh behavior: when one hears the sūtra being expounded, one wants to press it with difficult challenges; when questioned in return, one cannot give fitting answers, and instead stubbornly resorts to speaking falsehoods; just as when a horse reaches muddy ground and plants itself, refusing to go further. The eighth behavior: when one hears the sūtra being expounded, one refuses to listen; instead one thinks on licentiousness and excess, harboring many desires, not wishing to hear and accept it. When one dies and enters the evil destinies, one then frantically wishes to study and practice the Way, but can no longer practice the Way; just as when a feed basket is hung before a horse and it stares fixedly but refuses to eat; when its master leads it away wanting to harness it, it then frantically chomps and bites but likewise cannot get food."

The Buddha said: "I have spoken of the eight behaviors of horses; wicked people also have eight wicked behaviors in just this way."

The bhikṣus, having heard the sūtra, rejoiced, paid homage, and departed.
