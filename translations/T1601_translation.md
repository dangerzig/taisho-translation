# 辯中邊論頌
## Verses Distinguishing the Middle from the Extremes

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1601

Spoken by Bodhisattva Maitreya. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘).

---

### Chapter One: Distinguishing Characteristics

> Characteristics alone, obstruction, the truly real,
> And the cultivation of antidotes:
> The stages of that very cultivation,
> The fruit obtained, and the unsurpassed vehicle.

> False discrimination exists;
> Within it, the two are entirely absent.
> Here there is only emptiness;
> Within that, this too exists.

> Therefore, of all dharmas it is said:
> They are neither empty nor not empty.
> Because of existence, non-existence, and existence again,
> This accords with the Middle Way.

> Consciousness arises appearing as objects, sentient beings, self, and cognition.
> These objects are truly non-existent;
> Since objects are absent, consciousness too is absent.

> The nature of false discrimination
> Is thereby established in meaning:
> Neither truly existent nor entirely non-existent,
> Since its cessation is admitted as liberation.

> Only the imagined, the dependent,
> And the perfectly established nature:
> They are taught because of objects, discrimination,
> And the twofold emptiness, respectively.

> Dependent on consciousness, there is apprehension;
> From the non-apprehension of objects, non-apprehension of objects arises.
> Dependent on the non-apprehension of objects,
> Non-apprehension of consciousness arises.

> Through the apprehending nature of consciousness,
> Non-apprehension is also established.
> Therefore, know that both apprehensions
> Are equal in their nature of non-apprehension.

> The minds and mental factors of the three realms
> Are false discrimination.
> Cognizing objects alone is called mind;
> Discriminating them separately is called mental factors.

> The first is called the appropriating consciousness;
> The second is called the experiencer.
> Herein, the capacity for experiencing
> And for discriminating pertains to the mental factors.

> Covering and obstruction; establishment;
> Leading, gathering, and bringing to completion;
> The three kinds of discrimination; experiencing;
> Initiating, and also connecting;
> Manifestation, and the fruit of suffering:
> Only these afflict the world.
> Three, two, and seven defilements
> Arise from false discrimination.

> Characteristics and synonyms,
> Meaning, differentiation, and establishment:
> Know that the nature of twofold emptiness
> Is taught in brief solely by these.

> Because of the absence of duality and the presence of that absence,
> It is neither existent nor non-existent,
> Neither different nor identical:
> This is declared to be the characteristic of emptiness.

> The synonyms of emptiness in brief are:
> Suchness, the true limit,
> Signlessness, the supreme meaning,
> And the dharma-realm: thus should one know.

> Through changelessness and non-inversion,
> The cessation of marks, the domain of noble wisdom,
> And the cause of the noble dharmas:
> The meanings of the synonyms are in this order.

> This defilement and purification
> Is through being stained and unstained,
> Like water, the gold element, and space:
> Pure, it is admitted to be pure.

> Eater and eaten,
> The body it depends on and its abode,
> The capacity to see reality as such,
> And the two purities sought: these are the emptinesses.

> For the constant benefit of sentient beings,
> For not forsaking birth-and-death,
> For the inexhaustibility of wholesome deeds,
> One contemplates this as empty.

> For the purification of the spiritual lineage,
> For obtaining the marks and excellences,
> For purifying all buddha-dharmas,
> The bodhisattva contemplates emptiness.

> The person and dharmas:
> Their real nature is in both cases non-existent.
> This absence of nature and the presence of that nature:
> Therefore, two emptinesses are separately established.

> If there were no defilement here,
> All beings should be liberated of themselves.
> If there were no purification here,
> Effort would be fruitless.

> Neither defiled nor undefiled,
> Neither pure nor impure:
> The mind's nature is originally pure;
> It is defiled by adventitious stains.

### Chapter Two: Distinguishing Obstructions

> Complete, partial,
> Predominant, and equal:
> In birth-and-death, grasping and rejecting:
> Obstructions to the two natures are thus described.

> The nine characteristics of afflictions
> Are the nine bonds, beginning with craving.
> The first two obstruct weariness and renunciation;
> The remaining seven obstruct true seeing.

> Namely, those that can obstruct: the view of self,
> Its objects, cessation, the path, the jewels,
> Gain and reverence, and so forth,
> Renunciation, and thorough knowledge.

> Lack of effort, wrong place,
> Incorrect attention, non-arising,
> Not giving rise to right thought,
> Incomplete accumulations;

> Lacking the spiritual lineage, lacking good friends,
> A mind of extreme weariness,
> And lacking right practice,
> Dwelling with the base and wicked;

> Inversion, heavy burden, three residuals,
> Prajñā not yet accomplished,
> And the fundamental heavy burden,
> Laziness and heedlessness;

> Attachment to existence, attachment to wealth,
> And an inferior mind,
> Lack of faith, lack of resolve,
> Taking words at face value;

> Slighting the Dharma, prizing fame and gain,
> Lacking compassion for sentient beings,
> Deficient hearing and scant learning,
> Not cultivating excellent concentration.

> Goodness, awakening, receptivity,
> Having wisdom, freedom from disturbance, as obstructions:
> Dedication, fearlessness, and non-miserliness,
> Mastery: these are called "goodness" and so forth.

> Thus each of the ten, such as goodness,
> Has three preceding obstructions.
> For the factors of awakening, the perfections, and the grounds,
> There are distinct obstructions to be known.

> Unskillfulness regarding objects,
> Laziness; decline of concentration in two ways;
> Not planting, feeble nature,
> Seeing the heavy burden as a fault.

> Obstructing wealth and good destinies,
> Not forsaking sentient beings,
> Decrease and increase regarding faults and virtues,
> Causing entry and liberation.

> Obstructing giving and other virtues,
> Their inexhaustibility and continuity,
> Certainty in what is done,
> Enjoyment of Dharma, and ripening of beings.

> All-pervading and supreme,
> Supreme flow and without attachment,
> Continuity without difference,
> Undefiled and pure,

> Various dharmas without distinction,
> And neither increase nor decrease,
> Together with non-discrimination and others:
> Four kinds of mastery and their objects.

> In these ten dharma-realms,
> There are undefiled forms of ignorance
> That obstruct the merits of the ten grounds;
> Therefore, they are called the ten obstructions.

> The affliction obstructions
> And the cognitive obstructions have been described.
> Since the complete exhaustion of both is admitted,
> All obstructions are liberated.

### Chapter Three: Distinguishing True Reality

> True reality is only tenfold:
> Root, characteristics,
> Non-inversion, cause and fruit,
> And the coarse and subtle true reality;

> Commonly established, the domain of the pure,
> Receptivity and differentiation:
> The ten truths of skillful means
> Are all for removing the view of self.

> Regarding the three own-natures, it is held:
> Only one is always non-existent;
> One exists but is not true;
> One exists and is truly real.

> Regarding dharmas and persons,
> And regarding the grasped and the grasper,
> In the existent and non-existent nature:
> The views of superimposition and deprecation.

> Knowing this, they do not turn:
> This is called the characteristic of true reality.

> Non-nature, arising and ceasing,
> Defilement and purity: three kinds of impermanence.
> The grasped, the phenomenal aspect,
> And their combination: three kinds of suffering.
> Emptiness also has three kinds:
> Absence, difference, and intrinsic nature.
> Signlessness, different marks,
> And own-marks: three kinds of non-self.

> In sequence, four groups of three
> Depend on the root true reality.

> The three characteristics of suffering have been explained.
> The origin also has three kinds:
> Habitual tendencies, equal arising,
> And the characteristic of not yet being free from bondage.

> Intrinsic nature; two kinds of non-arising;
> Defilement and quiescence: two groups of three cessations.
> Thorough knowledge, complete abandonment,
> And realization: three kinds of the truth of the path.

> Know that conventional truth
> Has three kinds of differentiation:
> Nominal, functional, and manifest,
> Corresponding in order to the fundamental three.

> Ultimate truth also has three:
> Meaning, attainment, and right practice.
> Depending on the fundamental one, changelessness;
> Non-inversion: the second and the perfectly real.

> Worldly consensus depends on one;
> Rational consensus depends on three.
> The domain of the pure has two,
> Depending on the one perfectly established.

> Names are the imagined;
> Characteristics and discrimination are the dependent.
> Suchness and right wisdom
> Are subsumed under the perfectly established.

> Cyclic existence and establishment,
> Wrong practice depend on the first two.
> True characteristics, mere consciousness, purification,
> And right practice depend on the last one.

> Regarding the aggregates: the view of self
> As subject, unity, cause, experiencer,
> Agent, sovereign controller,
> Superior meaning, and permanence,
> The basis of defilement and purification,
> Observer, binder, and liberator in nature.

> This imagined discrimination
> And the dharma-nature meaning are found therein.

> Non-unity, and summary,
> Segmentation: the meaning of "aggregate."
> Capacity for grasping, grasped, and grasping those:
> Seed-meaning is the meaning of "element."

> Capacity to receive, and the objects cognized,
> Gate of function: the meaning of "sense-base."
> The meaning of dependent origination: regarding causes,
> Fruits, and functions, neither increase nor decrease.

> Regarding the disagreeable, agreeable, and pure,
> Innate and supreme,
> Obtaining, practicing, and lacking mastery:
> This is the meaning of "possible and impossible."

> Faculties are supreme regarding appropriation, sustenance, continuity,
> Function, and twofold purity.
> Cause and fruit, already used and not yet used:
> This is the meaning of "time," as should be known.

> Feeling and the resources of feeling,
> The actions that cause them,
> The two kinds of quiescence and their antidotes:
> This is the meaning of "truth," as should be known.

> Through merit and fault,
> And through non-discriminating wisdom,
> Dependence on another, and self-liberation:
> This is the meaning of "vehicle," as should be known.

> The conditioned and unconditioned:
> Whether nominal or causal,
> Whether characterized, or quiescent,
> Or the object of their contemplation.

### Chapter Four: Distinguishing the Cultivation of Antidotes

> Through heaviness, craving as cause,
> Self-entity, and non-delusion:
> For entering the four noble truths,
> The cultivation of the foundations of mindfulness should be known.

> Having thoroughly known obstruction and antidote,
> And all their various kinds,
> For abandoning and cultivating,
> One diligently cultivates the four right efforts.

> Relying on the capacity of abiding,
> For the accomplishment of all tasks,
> Removing the five faults,
> One diligently cultivates the eight antidotes.

> Laziness, forgetting the holy words,
> Sinking and agitation,
> Non-application and application:
> These five faults should be known.

> To eliminate laziness,
> One cultivates zeal, effort, faith, and ease:
> These are the basis, the supported,
> The cause, and the result.

> To eliminate the remaining four faults,
> One cultivates mindfulness, discernment, intention, and equanimity:
> Remembering the words, recognizing sinking and agitation,
> Suppressing activity, and the equal flow of cessation.

> Having already planted the seeds concordant with liberation,
> One further cultivates five intensifications:
> Zeal, practice, non-forgetfulness,
> Non-distraction, and analysis.

> Precisely what damages obstructions is called a power;
> Their sequence is established through cause and result.
> Concordant with decisive discrimination: two by two
> Among the five faculties and five powers.

> The factors of awakening are briefly fivefold:
> The basis, the intrinsic nature,
> The factor of emergence and benefit,
> And the three factors of non-defilement.

> Through cause and condition, the basis,
> Intrinsic nature, and differentiation of meaning:
> Therefore ease, concentration, and equanimity
> Are called the factors of non-defilement.

> Discrimination and instruction,
> Causing others to have deep faith: three each.
> Counteracting obstructions, also three:
> Therefore, the path-factors are eight.

> Indicating view, morality, and renunciation;
> Causing others' deep trust and acceptance;
> Counteracting the root afflictions, the secondary afflictions,
> And the obstructions to mastery.

> With inversion, following non-inversion;
> Without inversion, following inversion;
> Without inversion, following non-inversion:
> These are the distinctions in the cultivation of antidotes.

> What the bodhisattvas cultivate
> Differs from the two vehicles
> In object, mental engagement,
> And realization, being superior.

### Chapter Five: Distinguishing the Stages of Cultivation

> The cultivation of antidotes described above
> Has eighteen stages,
> Namely: cause, entry, practice, fruit,
> Action, non-action, superiority,

> Higher, unsurpassed, devoted practice,
> Entry, emergence, prediction, teaching,
> Consecration, and realization,
> Surpassing benefit, and accomplishment of the task.

> Know that within the dharma-realm,
> There are briefly three stages:
> Impure, pure and impure,
> And pure, as is appropriate.

> Depending on the preceding stages,
> Whatever differentiated characteristics there are:
> Corresponding to each, there are established
> The various kinds of persons.

---

### Chapter Six: Distinguishing the Fruits Obtained

> The vessel is called the maturation-fruit;
> Power is its dominant condition.
> Delight, increase, and purification
> Are in order the five fruits.

> Furthermore, the remaining fruits in brief:
> Later and later, initial, repeated practice,
> Ultimate, concordant, obstruction-cessation,
> Separation, superior, higher, and unsurpassed.

---

### Chapter Seven: Distinguishing the Unsurpassed Vehicle

> In total, through three kinds of unsurpassedness,
> It is called the unsurpassed vehicle:
> The unsurpassedness of right practice, of objects,
> And of cultivation and realization.

> Right practice has six kinds:
> Supreme, mental engagement,
> Following the Dharma, free from two extremes,
> Differentiated, and undifferentiated.

> The supreme has twelve aspects:
> Vast, long in time,
> Basis, inexhaustible,
> Uninterrupted, and without difficulty,

> Mastery, receptive, initiating,
> Attaining, outflow, and ultimate:
> Through these, the ten perfections are taught
> And called pāramitā.

> The ten pāramitās are: giving, morality, patience,
> Vigor, concentration, prajñā,
> Skillful means, vows, powers, and knowledge.

> Benefiting, not harming, enduring,
> Increasing virtue, enabling entry, liberation,
> Inexhaustible, constantly arising, certainty,
> Enjoyment of Dharma, and ripening others.

> The bodhisattva, with the three wisdoms,
> Constantly reflects on the Great Vehicle.
> The dharmas taught as established:
> This is called the right practice of mental engagement.

> This increases the wholesome realm,
> Enters into meaning, and accomplishes tasks.
> Its companions should be known
> As the ten kinds of Dharma-practice.

> These are: copying, offering,
> Giving to others, listening, reading,
> Receiving and retaining, rightly expounding,
> Reciting, reflecting, and cultivating.

> One who practices the ten Dharma-practices
> Obtains an immeasurable accumulation of merit,
> Because it is supreme, inexhaustible,
> And gathers others without cessation.

> The practice of following the Dharma has two kinds:
> Non-distraction and
> Non-inversion in transformation:
> The bodhisattvas should know.

> Emergence from concentration, streaming toward objects,
> Savoring, sinking, agitation, pretense,
> Grasping at self, and an inferior mind:
> The wise should know these.

> Wisdom sees in text and meaning,
> In mental engagement and immovability,
> In the two characteristics of defilement and purity, as adventitious.
> Without fear, lofty, without inversion.

> Knowing that merely through correspondence,
> Through habituation, or the reverse of these,
> There is meaning and non-meaning:
> This is non-inversion regarding text.

> Appearing like duality,
> As it appears, it truly is not:
> Knowing apart from existence and non-existence:
> This is non-inversion regarding meaning.

> Non-inversion regarding mental engagement:
> Knowing that verbal habituation
> And verbal mental engagement depend on each other,
> Since they are the cause of appearing as duality.

> Non-inversion regarding immovability:
> Knowing that meaning is non-existent,
> Yet not non-existent, like an illusion and the rest,
> Because one is unmoved by existence and non-existence.

> Non-inversion regarding own-characteristics:
> Knowing all is merely names,
> Free from all discrimination,
> Depending on the ultimate own-characteristic.

> Since apart from the true dharma-realm
> There is no separate dharma at all,
> One who penetrates this
> Is without inversion regarding shared characteristics.

> Knowing that inverted mental engagement
> Has not ceased and has ceased:
> Regarding defilement and purification of the dharma-realm,
> One is without inversion.

> Knowing the original nature of the dharma-realm
> Is pure like space:
> Therefore, defilement and purification have no master.
> This is non-inversion regarding the adventitious.

> Since there are no sentient beings or dharmas,
> Defilement and purification are both without nature.
> Knowing this, without fear or pride:
> This is non-inversion regarding the two.

> The nature of difference and unity,
> Non-Buddhist and śrāvaka:
> The extremes of superimposition and deprecation
> Regarding sentient beings and dharmas, each twofold.

> What is treated and what treats,
> Permanence and annihilation,
> Grasped and grasper:
> The extremes of defilement and purification, in two and three kinds.

> The discrimination of the nature of the two extremes
> Should be known further as sevenfold:
> The extremes of existence and non-existence,
> Quiescence and fear regarding the quiescent and the fearful,

> Grasped and grasper, right and wrong,
> Having function and lacking function,
> Not arising, and time and the rest:
> These are the discriminations of the two extremes.

> Differentiated and undifferentiated:
> Know that upon the ten grounds,
> The ten pāramitās
> Are cultivated with predominant emphasis and so forth.

> Objects are: settling, realm,
> Establishing and supporting, bearing,
> Stamping, internally holding, penetrating,
> Increasing, realizing, transmitting, and supreme.

> Cultivation and realization are: without deficiency,
> Not ruined, not shaken, completeness,
> Arising, firmness, pliancy,
> Non-abiding, without obstruction, and cessation.

> This treatise distinguishes the middle from the extremes,
> Its meaning profound, firm, and substantial,
> Vast and pertaining to all meanings:
> It removes all that is inauspicious.
