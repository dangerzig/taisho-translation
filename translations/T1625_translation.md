# Commentary on the Treatise on the Object of Cognition

## *Guān Suǒyuán Lùn Shì* (觀所緣論釋)

Taishō 1625

Commentary by Dharmapāla (*Hùfǎ Púsà*, 護法菩薩). Translated by Yìjìng (*Yìjìng*, 義淨) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is Dharmapāla's (530-561 CE) commentary on Dignāga's Ālambanaparīkṣā (Treatise Examining the Object of Cognition, T1624), translated into Chinese by Yìjìng (635-713 CE). Dignāga's root text is one of the most important short treatises in Buddhist epistemology, arguing that neither individual atoms (paramāṇu) nor aggregates of atoms can serve as the true object-condition (ālambana-pratyaya) of sense-cognition. The argument proceeds by showing that any valid object of cognition must satisfy two conditions: (1) it must be the cause that generates the cognition (the "causal" condition), and (2) the cognition must bear the likeness of the object (the "resemblance" condition). Individual atoms fail condition (2) because cognition does not bear the likeness of individual atoms; aggregates fail condition (1) because aggregates, being unreal (like a second moon), cannot causally generate cognition. The conclusion is that the object of cognition is internal to consciousness itself: an "internal form" (the ākāra or "aspect" of consciousness) that appears as if external. Dharmapāla's commentary unpacks each step of this argument in detail, drawing on Dignāga's logic and also engaging with Dharmakīrti's refinements. The text is dense, technical, and philosophically sophisticated, representing the mature Yogācāra-Dignāga epistemological tradition. Key terms include: paramāṇu (extreme atom), sāmānya (universal/aggregate), ālambana-pratyaya (object-condition), savitarka-savicāra (with investigation and analysis), ākāra (cognitive aspect/image), pratibhāsa (appearance), and grāhyāṃśa (apprehended aspect).*

## Opening Verse

> To the one who can make the minds of the wise poisoned [by wrong views] become supremely clear,
> And for the sake of eliminating evil:
> Having bowed in homage, I examine the meaning [of the treatise].

## Commentary

**[On the scope of the treatise:]**

The treatise says: "Those who accept that eye-cognition and the rest..." With regard to the objects that are abandoned and the objects that are gathered, whether one rejects or accepts them, this is the fruit of examination. The entities of what is to be abandoned and the causes of inversion are what is to be demonstrated.

The word "and the rest" (*děng*) here includes the five kinds of cognition admitted by others as depending on material sense-faculties, because others exclusively maintain that these cognitions take real external entities as their conditions. Mental cognition (*manovijñāna*) is not [treated] in this way, because it is not exclusively [directed at external realities]; it is admitted to be conventionally existent, taking "chariots" and such [aggregates] as its objects. Even if we grant that mental cognition has some portion that takes real entities as objects, we can still demonstrate, by separating the similar aspect of cognition, that without such [external] objects [cognition still arises]. For eye-cognition and the rest, once it is established that the object is not separate [from cognition], that serves as the basis for the further proof. Therefore, we do not exert ourselves [to prove this point] for mental cognition. Furthermore, concerning the objects known by the wisdom that is the fruit of sustained cultivation, these are truly not within the domain of logical reasoning (*tarka*), and because they are established just as they are perceived. Here we examine only the objects of the wisdom born from hearing, thinking, and innate [capacity]. As for the objects of such mental cognition, they are entirely non-existent [as external realities], because [mental cognition] cannot take its own aggregate as its object, and because it takes past and future [objects] that are not real entities, like the unconditioned. For this reason, the word "and the rest" includes the five groups of sense-cognition.

**[On atoms as objects:]**

If it is so, then what of the mental cognition that is triggered by sense-cognition? This [mental cognition] does not, together with sense-cognition at the same time or in the immediately subsequent moment, take defunct form and so on as its object. Or if it takes a present [object], this is not what sense-cognition has already apprehended. This mental cognition itself directly takes external objects as its essential nature; this would then entail that there are no deaf or blind persons, and it contradicts the inferential reasoning by which we know that distinct sense-faculties exist. This refutation of an "additional form" is what is intended here. However, with regard to mental cognition, we need not further concern ourselves.

Eye-cognition and the rest arise depending on form as both support and condition. "Non-manifest form" (*avijñaptirūpa*) is merely the nature of non-action; we ourselves admit it is non-existent. The original intention here is this: since the treatise takes the object-condition (*ālambana*) to be a matter of direct perception, and since it has the nature of being apprehended, it is deeply treading a wrong path. For this reason, the primary intent is to refute the object-condition nature, and incidentally to refute the support-nature as well. The simultaneous sense-faculty and the form that has [causal] capacity are provisionally admitted.

The term "external object" (*wàijìng*) is used because they [the opponents] maintain that apart from [cognition] there is a separate object. This reveals their error, showing that they maintain there is a distinct entity to be apprehended; therefore we call it an "object."

**[Refutation of atoms:]**

How shall we explain this? Perhaps [they say cognition] takes the aggregate as its object? But an aggregate is not rationally tenable as a real entity. Indeed, as the objection states, their own position is internally contradictory; what further fault is ours? Since they admit both taking real entities as objects and taking aggregates as objects, we wish to set forth their respective faults. Therefore, for now, we set aside this problem.

Or they may admit atoms (*paramāṇu*): although atoms are only seen to arise and perish in aggregation, each individual real entity is separately cognized, not the aggregate, just as [one distinguishes] blue from yellow. Even if all one's sense-faculties are present [simultaneously], the objects are not confused, because the capacity of each faculty is determinate. With respect to real entities, [each faculty] has discriminative power. Each individual atom becomes the object of cognition, because it has the nature of being a cause [of that cognition], because it is the cause of eye-cognition and the rest, meaning it is in the sense of being the direct factor of their arising.

However, some say: the object-condition is the cause of cognition's arising, being among the conditions. Or alternatively, the aggregate is the object. Those theorists maintain that the assemblage of many atoms is the object-condition here, because cognition bearing the likeness of the aggregate arises from it. Since understanding arises with respect to the aggregate, therefore one knows it is the object. As it is said: "If cognition bears the likeness of that [object], then that is the object of this [cognition]." Both of these theorists say "bearing its likeness," as the principle requires.

**[The two conditions for being an object:]**

If the word "cause" is not stated, then the reason [in the argument] lacks an example, like [establishing] the nature of a cause as a cause. The aggregate-nature of atoms would be established as the object-condition nature. Furthermore, if one's own position does not admit that cognition takes real external entities as objects, there would be the fault of the subject (*dharmin*) contradicting itself. However, Dharmakīrti does not admit this; rather, this is something commonly accepted by both [parties], and so it serves as an example.

If stated just as described, one should apply [the reasoning] to the thesis being established. The original treatise's two reasons merely clarify the causal [condition]; the reason why they are not themselves [sufficient as] causes is that there is no commonly established example. For this reason, one must set forth the "bearing its likeness" reason. Why is this?

**[Why atoms fail the resemblance condition:]**

Next, to show that one's own position is free from error, [the treatise] sets forth the commonly accepted [principle] in the fifth-case ending. Even granting that [atoms] are causes, like commonly accepted non-existent entities whose non-existent nature [is admitted], they are not the cause of atoms. And even provisionally granting that the individual atoms are of a causal nature, [the treatise] merely says they are not the object-condition nature, because they do not have the nature of being the likeness of atoms. This is [expressed in the verse]: "Root-cognition: atoms are not the object."

As with sense-faculties (*indriya*): just as the sense-faculty, even though it is truly the direct cause upon which cognition depends, is not the object of that cognition because cognition does not bear the likeness of the faculty, atoms are likewise. Whatever does not have the likeness [of a given entity] is not the object of that [entity's cognition]. What does this mean? For this reason, [the treatise] states the term "object" and the rest.

The term "intrinsic nature" (*svabhāva*) means both the particular and universal characteristics. "Determined" (*liǎo*) means "ascertained." How so? And how is this further called "ascertainment"? Because [cognition] arises bearing the likeness of that [object]. The meaning here is: cognition arises conforming to the form of that object; because it follows the entity of that [object], this is called "ascertaining that object." In reality, apart from cognition, there is no separate thing ascertained that could serve as the cause of cognition. Rather, it is merely that the aspect of the external object is arranged within [cognition] itself, like a mirror-image, and this is commonly called "ascertaining that object." But individual atoms, one by one, do not have cognition following their form. Therefore, atoms as the object-body, even if they have causal nature, because [cognition] does not have the nature of [bearing] their [likeness], are not the object-condition, just as the sense-faculty, though having causal nature, is not the object-condition. If by virtue of causal nature alone they are admitted as the object-condition, then the sense-faculty too would equally [qualify], which leads to an unwanted consequence.

**[Why aggregates fail the causal condition:]**

This is stated as: "bearing its likeness [should] accord with principle." Therefore, the reason has the fault of being unestablished. However, the intended meaning is to show that it is not merely causal nature that constitutes the characteristic of an object-condition for sense-cognition.

If the statement "because it is a cause" is taken as establishing the opponent's thesis, showing what is commonly accepted: at this point, the intent is to refute the other and reveal one's own [position]. The capacity of refutation being established, [the treatise] places this statement.

The thesis is certainly established [for one's own side]; the indeterminacy arises for the other's thesis, lest they not accept [it]. The indeterminacy previously given to the opponent is itself the refutation; why should one's own thesis further require an inference? In general, "indeterminacy" does not necessarily mean [the conclusion] definitely does not hold; it creates doubt. Therefore, one further needs to establish a proof.

**[The aggregate is not a real entity:]**

Or alternatively: because [cognition does not have] their likeness, atoms are not the determinate object of cognition. Only when cognition arises bearing the likeness [of the object], as described, is there determinate ascertainment. Since that [resemblance to atoms] is absent, clearly this determinate ascertainment also has no basis. One should say: [atoms are] not of the nature of determinate ascertainment. Only by setting forth this reason [can one conclude they] are not the object-condition, like the sense-faculty, like atoms.

Some further make distinctions among cognitions to establish: eye-cognition does not take the blue and other atomic aggregates as its object, because it does not bear their form, like other sense-cognitions. For other cognitions, the converse applies. The example of "like the sense-faculty" is indeed sufficient; the other examples are needed by extension.

Furthermore, even granting causal nature, [aggregates] are not of the object-condition nature. This too is the case. Indeed, the atoms of sound and so on cannot be the cause of other sense-cognitions. Some say: because cognition does not have the assembled appearance of [atoms], [they are] not the object-condition, like the atoms of a sense-faculty. Because the aspect of the object is arranged in cognition, this is the nature of bearing [its] likeness; since this is absent [for atoms], the principle is stated that [they have] no assembled appearance.

Thus, it has been stated that atoms (*paramāṇu*) are not the object-condition, because the proof adduced for them does not correspond, and because the proof that they are not of object-nature is well established.

If so, then is the aggregate the object? However, by what has been stated, all the proofs [fail]; with respect to the Sarvāstivāda thesis, all have the nature of being unestablished. In reality, the aggregate truly has "the likeness" [in cognition] and could be the object-condition. But it lacks causal nature, because cognition bearing the likeness of the aggregate cannot be generated by the aggregate; the aggregate does not generate [cognition]. Since it does not generate, how can cognition take it as its condition? The characteristic of the object-condition does not correspond; therefore it is not an object-condition. For this reason, [the treatise] previously stated: "bearing its likeness should accord with principle"; this is thus unestablished.

If so, what is the characteristic of the object-condition? Whatever is an object must generate cognition bearing the likeness of its own form. The cognition that follows the object: that [object] is the generator; that is the object-condition.

**[The second-moon analogy:]**

Some say: whatever is an object must be the cause of mind and mental factors arising. Once [cognition] has arisen, it apprehends the object and makes verbal expression about it; at that point, it is named "the object-condition." If a meaning possesses both of these two aspects, only then does it qualify as the object-condition: having the nature of generating, it is the object-condition. The āgama is cited: this is precisely what is meant by the nature of a "generative condition." Because it is a generative cause, the condition that generates cognition is commonly accepted as the object-condition. The appearance of its own form in [cognition] has no further benefit here, so it is not mentioned. [Aggregates] cannot be generators because they are not real entities. Since aggregates are not real entities, [we know this because] with respect to the assemblage, being either identical with or different from [the parts] cannot be coherently stated. Furthermore, no unreal entity has the capacity to generate an effect, like a second moon. Just as the second moon cannot generate cognition bearing the likeness of a second moon.

If so, then what causes this appearance? Damage to the sense-faculty. When the eye-faculty is impaired by cataracts and the like, its clarity is diminished, and from this impaired faculty, the vision of a second moon arises, not from a real object. Therefore, the second moon, even though cognition bears its "likeness," is not the object of [that cognition]. Like the second moon: even though cognition has the appearance of [the aggregate], because [the aggregate] does not generate [cognition], it is not its object. This is because the aggregate is not a real entity and therefore is not the generative cause of cognition, being unreal, like the second moon. From this, the establishment is: because it lacks causal nature, it is not the object-condition, just like the second moon.

**[The internal object:]**

With the above refutation of both atoms and aggregates as objects, the conclusion of the treatise is reached. The meaning of the object-condition, as established by principle, is this: [the object of cognition is] not separate from consciousness. The term "internal" (*nèi*) shows it is not separate from consciousness. The "object-body" is the apprehended aspect (*grāhyāṃśa*): it is the meaning of consciousness transforming into the aspect of an object. However, [the opponent claims it] resides separately outside consciousness, taking it as the object. The fault of contradicting the world still remains, because worldly convention commonly accepts that objects reside externally. One should say "as if external": this, not separate from consciousness, the apprehended aspect appears as if external, and one says "I see an object," giving rise to the conceit [of external perception]; this is truly the cause.

Like the appearance of hairs and such to eye-cognition: though there is no external object, we say "truly there is no object residing externally," not because it is not of the nature of cognition. When examined with reasoning, one cannot ascertain that its own-nature definitely resides externally. Even if one grants that it truly has an external aspect, it is still not the condition of cognition, because it is not of the nature of bearing its likeness. It is not that the aspect of atoms appears as if an external aspect appears; at the time of this [apparent external] appearing, this is the object-condition, because it accords with the principle of bearing its likeness.

Because "if it accords with the principle of [bearing its] likeness," this is this [object-condition], just as causal nature and so on. Because it accords with the principle of its own likeness, this further reveals the differentiated characteristic of the object-condition. As stated: "cognition bears the likeness of that," and so forth, showing that one does not need to rely on external entities as objects. As emotionally conceived, the object-aspect arises accordingly.

Furthermore, what is emotionally conceived, if separated from consciousness, does not exist externally. This object-aspect originally does not separate from consciousness; for this reason, it is called the "internal object-aspect." The word "internal" here means "not separate from consciousness." Originally there is no "external"; in relation to what is it "internal"? And from this, it arises; this arises from it. Or alternatively: from this, by the fifth [case-ending], the meaning is distinct. Because it is not the case that, apart from the object, cognition can exist; therefore, "this exists, and so cognition arises." The fifth [case] is not stated because the two dharmas are conjoined. Clarifying the object-condition, the principle being conjoined, reveals the proof. This merely takes the universal characteristic of the object as its proof.

If the differentia [is sought], then the *prajñā* [cognition] does not take external entities as its object; in the dream-state, this is demonstrated. As stated, the two [conditions] form a single proof: cognition bears the likeness [of the object] and is also generated by it; these two functions together constitute one [valid] proof.

Moreover, even granting the internal aspect, [the opponent argues:] it is merely that by observing the external object, the appearance is falsely generated, and there is no ground-aspect. As emotionally conceived, the object generates the apprehension, and the object's aspect is arrayed internally. How is this one portion able to serve as the co-arising condition? Because the apprehended aspect does not exist apart from consciousness, this one portion again generates consciousness, which would be the fault of self-contradicting the own-nature. Because it is again a portion of that nature, like the apprehending aspect: this would then become the nature of not being able to generate. Rather, consciousness arises only through the external aspect dyeing it. This means the aspect-portion and consciousness arise simultaneously; it is not the case that two simultaneous [things] have a cause-and-effect relationship, like the two horns of an ox.

Nor is it the case that, with respect to non-different entities existing simultaneously, one can coherently speak of them as "companions." Nor is there, apart from consciousness, a separately stated object; how then can it be called "companion-nature"? In reality it is so; however, through the power of the differentiation of aspects, one conjectures them as different and expresses them thus. Because there are the seeing-aspect and the form-aspect, consciousness thereby has differentiation.

If this is so, then the condition-nature should also be merely imputed, since non-conceptual entities have no intrinsic nature. This would then become a non-genuine condition-nature. This reason is contradictory, because the meaning of "condition" is commonly accepted with respect to other imputed differentiated objects, such as the immediately-antecedent-cessation [condition]. When the cognition of the same class is being analyzed, this cognition also takes the four kinds of conditions as its conditions.
