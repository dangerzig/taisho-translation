# 佛使比丘迦旃延說法沒盡偈百二十章
## One Hundred Twenty Verses Proclaimed by the Buddha's Emissary, Bhikṣu Kātyāyana, on the Complete Extinction of the Dharma

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2029

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

> The Venerable Kātyāyana, embodying the Way, cultivating the Vinaya as protection,
> Seeing those who were violent and reckless, used verses to open the path of Dharma.
> His mind always harbored grief and sorrow, reflecting on matters of sadness.
> Always diligently striving, he looked to the future with great dread.
> The correct Dharma was about to perish; people's lifespan was barely a hundred years.
> The radiance of the correct Dharma, being in the world, would not long remain before vanishing.
> The correct Dharma having been fully extinguished, the assembly of bhikṣus will be deluded.
> They will abandon the sūtras and Dharma that the holy awakened ones taught,
> And instead accept miscellaneous texts, discarding what the Buddha spoke.
> Seeing and studying shallow scriptures, their minds will be delighted.
> They will constantly quarrel and litigate, violating the teachings, turning their backs on the canonical scriptures.
> They will increasingly slander one another, each treating the others with contempt.

> Foolish, stubborn, and hard to transform, lacking wisdom like stumps and blocks:
> "You know nothing at all! You do not study the Buddha's correct Dharma!"
> Setting aside the principles of the scriptures, they mutually seek out each other's faults.
> "What I myself have heard and transmitted stands alone without peer!"
> Placing the middle at the bottom and raising the low to the middle,
> No longer recognizing proper order, valuing only endless talk.
> If their citations go astray, they speak as though there were no beginning or end.
> What they hear and accept is all superficial; their discussions have no clear discourse.

> These followers, with evil intentions, slander their preceptors.
> Seeing the venerable ones and their teachers, they are arrogant and show no respect.
> These people argue and debate, their minds filled with anguish and venom.
> Attached to the world, admiring the wealthy and powerful, bound in fetters without knowing it.
> "My understanding is truly the supreme truth; you truly know nothing!"
> "Your teaching is entirely upside down; my words accord with the canonical meaning!"
> Each quarreling with the others, they give rise to venomous and harmful minds.
> Coveting gain and offerings, they drift along with the worldly crowd.

> Habituated to the pleasures of dwelling in houses, unable to extract and liberate themselves,
> Following desire, they converse together, speaking only of worldly affairs.
> At that time, bhikṣus will abandon the spaces between trees and their times of leisure.
> They will dwell in villages, establishing monasteries between settlements.
> Delighting in commotion and disturbance, not aspiring to dwell in stillness,
> They will increasingly encroach upon and deceive one another to support their own wives and children.

> Sometimes a visiting bhikṣu may come from afar.
> The monastery head first settles himself comfortably, then allows the visitor to stay.
> He is jealous of where the visitor stays, envious of his reputation for virtue,
> Also envious of his lineage, and coveting his knowledge of the sūtras.
> Seeing the bhikṣu from afar, his expression is displeased.
> When the visitor departs, his heart is glad.
> Clinging to offerings, he gives rise to venomous jealousy.
> Proud and haughty toward one another, from this arise anger and loss.

> Always harboring anger, pride, and self-aggrandizement,
> Their desires are insatiable; they indulge themselves and follow defilement.
> They should not practice harmful deeds, yet they do not wish to recite or study the sūtras.
> All day they laugh, sing, and dance; at dusk they sleep and do not awaken.
> When they gather together, their talk does not touch on scriptural principles.
> They speak only of officials, thieves, and the comings and goings of worldly customs.
> If there is a learned one whom many people support with offerings,
> Those who envy him seek ordination, claiming to study the bhikṣu's Dharma.

> If there is a learned one whom laypeople revere,
> He busies himself with trivial matters, and through this gains fame.
> His conduct does not accord with the teachings; it arises from seeking gain.
> Though young in years, he gathers many disciples.
> His mind harbors contention and confusion; he cannot complete his studies.
> After two or three years as a śramaṇa, he broadly accumulates dependents.
> Unable to be diligent in precepts, he falls into wrong views.
> Some teach annihilationism; some teach eternalism.

> Having settled in such learning, they fall into evil yet still bear shaved heads.
> Their followers are mostly base and vulgar, young people surrounding them.
> Sometimes there is severe drought; sometimes there are great floods.
> Sparrows, rats, and locusts: disasters all converging.
> The five grains are entirely exhausted; the common people all suffer famine.
> Pressed by poverty and the struggle to eat, they leave home seeking security.
> They then become śramaṇas, undisciplined and transgressing the rules.
> Not understanding the precepts, the assemblies are without guidance.

> Careless and shameless, unable to cultivate mindful conduct,
> They do not delight in Dharma assemblies but anxiously cling to wealth and support.
> They take non-Dharma as Dharma; what they teach violates the Way.
> Assigning blame, they reverse light and heavy, confusing the scriptures, going against the wise standards.
> They gather until midnight, quarrels and disputes ever increasing.
> Only afterward do they recite sūtras, roughly and incompletely.
> Rarely and briefly they speak of precepts; fully they engage in quarrels.
> Everywhere the principles are lost; therefore the correct Dharma is extinguished.

> Having just finished quarreling, they form enmities.
> The māras and their retinues thereby gain advantage over people.
> The devas, nāgas, and spirits who come wishing to hear the teachings
> Lean forward expectantly for the precepts, but hear only disputes.
> The devas harbor resentment, disapproving of the bhikṣus' conduct.
> Coming and going, they say to one another: "The buddha-dharma is about to be extinguished!"
> "We abandoned heavenly pleasures and came here wishing to receive the Dharma,
> But cannot hear the correct Dharma. It would be better to leave."

> Those venerable spirits who delight in the buddha-dharma
> Will no longer think of the bhikṣus or extend them protection.
> At that time, evil spirits, violent and malicious, will work their harm.
> They will drain the bhikṣus' vital essence until no life remains.
> The bhikṣus will suffer many illnesses, emaciated and without strength.
> Their spirits gone, their complexions changed, they toil in suffering through many calamities.
> They increasingly hate and envy one another; in illness they do not tend to each other.
> Some will die without care, perishing suddenly and prematurely.

> Clinging to wealth and treasures, with no limit on clothing and food,
> Knowing worldly customs well, they sustain themselves through wrong livelihood.
> Trading and trafficking for profit, going in and out seeking interest,
> Their ambitions set on managing affairs, toiling ceaselessly.
> Delighting in trivial matters, seeking profit to sustain their lives,
> Abandoning the work of transcending the world, they entangle themselves in petty affairs.
> Their robes are not properly arranged; they do not cultivate proper deportment.
> Unable to conduct themselves properly, they are like wild horses and monkeys.

> Seeing from afar a worthy bhikṣu who begs alms knowing contentment,
> From a distance they revile him, saying he does not follow the precepts.
> Like today's bhikṣus, calm and without fault:
> At that time, the bhikṣus will silently commit many evils.
> Stealing, unprincipled, without shame, lazy, and harboring malice:
> Yet in the future world, these will instead be honored and served.
> The kind and worthy bhikṣus who fully know integrity and shame
> Will in that time of lost Dharma no longer be welcomed.

> Just as the lion king dwells in the forest,
> Jackals, wolves, dogs, and foxes dare not eat its flesh.
> But when it dies, worms emerge from its body and devour its own flesh.
> Day and night they consume it, destroying its form.
> After the Great Sage, the Able One, enters nirvāṇa and passes away,
> Earth, water, fire, and wind cannot destroy the buddha-dharma.
> The precious and rare treasures of the world do not suddenly perish of themselves.
> When dull gold appears in the world, refined gold no longer shines.

> The correct Dharma being in the world will never vanish of its own accord.
> It is because of the semblance Dharma that the correct Dharma is extinguished.
> Just as a ship in the sea sinks because of the greed for heavy cargo,
> The buddha-dharma is likewise: it is extinguished because of gain and offerings.
> They turn their backs on the sūtras and holy scriptures, taking this for correct Dharma.
> They make the Dharma contradict the Vinaya; they create Dharma-meanings from what is non-Dharma.
> The non-Buddhist practitioners of wrong views, and those with the five supernatural powers,
> Cannot destroy the meaning of the Dharma or the practice of giving.

> Those who follow Śākyamuni and became śramaṇas because of the Buddha:
> They will be the ones to destroy the correct Dharma and bring it to complete extinction.
> These base men who shave their heads and don the kāṣāya
> Will all defeat the correct Dharma, causing the scriptures to vanish from sight.
> They will not follow the precepts' teachings or exert themselves to preserve the Dharma's mission.
> They indulge their minds and follow their pleasures, like a donkey covered in dust.
> At that time, the students will accept and take on false responsibilities,
> Altering and fixing their documents, causing errors in what is entrusted.

> Fearing magistrates, enemies, and creditors,
> Trembling, they face hardships with dread, their hair standing on end.
> Tilling fields and managing livelihoods, encountering officials and soldiers,
> Morning and evening practicing defiled desires, afflicted by many troubles.

> There will be three evil kings: Great Qin in the foremost position,
> Bālurā behind, and Parthia in the center.
> Because of this, the correct Dharma will be abandoned and lost.
> The barbarian king, greatly fierce and evil, dwells in the north.
> He will raise armies to attack wicked kingdoms, harming tens of thousands of people.
> He will slight and disparage the śramaṇas, committing many evils.
> He will destroy buddha-stūpas and monasteries, ruining study-halls.
> At that time in the world, prefectures and kingdoms will all become ruins.

> All these people will be fearful, anxious, and distressed.
> They will abandon the northern lands and flee toward the central kingdom.
> Sick, emaciated, eyes dim, wasted and without strength:
> Those unable to leave the north will be preyed upon by them.
> At that time, the young bhikṣus who do not attend to the śramaṇa's duties
> Will remove their robes in fear, wishing to save themselves.

> Thereupon the king of the central kingdom will come to attack the barbarian king.
> Having executed the barbarian king, he will return and reside at Kaṃni.
> There will be a venerable bhikṣu named Śiṣya,
> Broadly learned and widely accomplished, able to please all the kings.
> The king, hearing Śiṣya's words, will be filled with joy.
> He will wish to invite the assembly of monks and establish a great offering.
> He will dispatch envoys to the ten directions, proclaiming the command to all kingdoms:
> "Let all people come here; there shall now be a great offering."

> All the monks will gather, numbering in the hundreds of thousands.
> Having suffered hardship, all are haggard, but glad to witness the great offering.
> The bhikṣus having assembled, hundreds of thousands fully provided for,
> They inquire after one another, exchanging greetings:
> "Where is your kind preceptor? Where has your ācārya gone?"
> "My usual attendant novice, my teacher: who now is your teacher?"
> "Some were wounded, some have died, some were driven away."
> When the bhikṣus meet one another, they weep uncontrollably.

> At that time, those assembled number in the hundreds of thousands.
> Their faces fallen with distress, yet glad to see the great offering.
> From the four directions they gather like clouds, assembling on the fifteenth day.
> They expound the Buddha's canonical precepts, but soon they quarrel again.
> Having quarreled, they increasingly cannot be at peace with one another.
> The venerable bhikṣu admonishes them: "Bhikṣus, be silent!
> I shall speak to you and demonstrate the Buddha's Dharma-vinaya.
> Listen to what I say; do not make disordered speech."

> "Considering all the śramaṇas and disciples of the Buddha in Jambudvīpa,
> They should gather in harmony; it is not fitting to persist in grudges."
> There is a great assembly of bhikṣus, numbering in the hundreds of thousands,
> Wishing to study the Way; they have come to the assembly on the fifteenth day.
> Though the great assembly of bhikṣus numbers hundreds of thousands:
> "My learning is clear and accomplished; you cannot understand it!"
> "If there is a single bhikṣu whose learning has reached awakening,
> Then let him expound from beginning to end. My learning knows these scriptures."

> At that time there is one bhikṣu whose learning is universally accomplished,
> A virtuous one named Surāta, who makes the lion's roar thus.
> He immediately rises from his seat, palms joined, standing.
> He bows at the elder's feet and then makes the lion's roar:
> "I harbor no doubts; my mind has no hesitation.
> What I have learned of sūtras and precepts, I shall now expound fully.
> I also have no difficulties; my mind does not waver.
> What I previously learned of Dharma and Vinaya, I have no doubts about."

> "I am fluent in the scriptures and clearly accomplished in the Way.
> My learning is thus. May the worthy ones uphold it."
> "You do not comprehend the many sūtras, nor do you understand the Dharma-vinaya.
> How can you so greatly praise yourself before the elders?"
> The elder master's evil disciple, of violent nature harboring murderous intent,
> Whose name is Asi, then kills Surāta.
> At that time, a great spirit who has faith in the buddha-dharma
> Takes up the vajra and strikes Asi dead.

> At that time the earth quakes in six ways.
> From the four directions spontaneous sounds arise, as though spirit drums are struck by non-human beings.
> At that time, from the four directions there will be four great columns of smoke,
> And four great fires will fall from above in the four directions.
> At that time, the world will become dark.
> From then on, without return, the people will be sunk in ignorance.
> The masses, innumerable, will grieve in distress.
> "Today is the final age, yet the Buddha's correct Dharma has not yet been exhausted."

> The spirits who once saw the Buddha and have faith in the Way
> Cast themselves upon the ground, wailing and unable to bear it.
> The bhikṣus who have suffered calamity are like people who have lost both parents.
> "Today is the final age; the Buddha's correct Dharma is extinguished."
> "From this day forward, there will be no more expounding of the scriptures.
> The Dharma-vinaya and precepts: from where shall we hear them?"
> The devas, tree-spirits, and spirits of the wilderness,
> Overcome with grief, anguished, tossing and unable to find peace.

> The lamp of the Dharma has already gone out; the canonical scriptures are already destroyed.
> In this age of ultimate collapse, the Dharma-drum sounds no more.
> The māras celebrate joyfully, gathering together to congratulate one another.
> Raising their hands in praise, they say: "This is the Buddha's final age!"
> In the future world hereafter, there will be such calamities.
> All the more should you increase your diligence and strive for liberation!
> Just as a merchant who misses the opportune time is filled with regret,
> So you should increase your earnest devotion and not have later regrets.

> While the Way of Dharma flourishes and the scriptures are widely distributed,
> While teachers of the Dharma still remain, diligently cultivate the Buddha's teachings.
> Today, the four assemblies still show mutual respect.
> While the buddha-dharma still remains, increase your diligence morning and night.
> While your body is still healthy and strong, before meeting old age, illness, and death,
> Therefore be earnest, mindful of the great perils to come.
> While the kingdoms are still at peace, without many calamities,
> While the harvest is plentiful and alms are easy to obtain, uphold and cultivate the Buddha's precepts.

> The śramaṇa Jierāchā, hearing this teaching and precept,
> Came forward and bowed in reverence to the elder Kātyāyana:
> "My body trembles, my hair stands on end, and my heart grows cold.
> I have lost my composure and no longer know the Dharma or recognize directions.
> Now hearing these words, my heart is filled with great dread.
> If in the future world one sees this, how can the heart not break?"
> The Venerable Kātyāyana, having raised this lamentation,
> Then told his disciples that the correct Dharma had not yet been exhausted:

> "For three hundred years, many will attain liberation; for three hundred years, precepts and samādhi will be heard.
> For three hundred years, buddha-monasteries will be maintained; entering the thousandth year, the teaching of the Blue Garden."
> Bhikṣus who delight in non-delight, who practice dwelling alone on beds and seats,
> Abiding in that practice without fixed abode, shall subdue all loves and desires.
