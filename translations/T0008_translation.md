"Then the chief minister, the brahmin Mahādṛḍha, immediately came to where King Reṇu was, and having arrived, offered his respects and withdrew to sit to one side. The king, pleased, looked upon him and gave words of comfort and reassurance, and the chief minister responded with respectful deference. The king said: 'Mahādṛḍha! Now on my behalf survey this kingdom and divide it into seven portions, so that I and the six youths may each govern one separately.' The chief minister, having received the king's command, immediately surveyed and measured, dividing the territory. Due north at the northern corner, the boundaries were broad and expansive; due south at the southern corner, the boundaries were narrow and contracted, like the form of a cart. The central territory, where many people gathered, was where King Reṇu resided. The kingdoms thus apportioned were: the kingdom of Kaliṅga, with the city of Dantapura; the kingdom of Aśmaka, with the city of Potana; the kingdom of Avanti, with the city of Māhiṣmatī; the kingdom of Sūvīra, with the city of Roruka; the kingdom of Mithilā, with the city of Videhī; the kingdom of Magadha, with the great city of Campā; and the kingdom of Vārāṇasī, with the great city of Kāśī. These seven kingdoms having each been given their boundaries, the six youths in their respective places received the royal consecration and each became king, ruling their own territory. From that time on there were seven kings: King Reṇu, King Jitaśatru, King Brahmadatta, King Vijiteśvara, King Vidyāpriya, King Rāṣṭrapāla, and King Mahārāṣṭrapāla. These seven kings each governed their own portion. Afterward, the six kings again gathered together and went to the brahmin chief minister, and having arrived, said to him: 'Mahādṛḍha! With the wisdom, resourcefulness, and ability you have, assisting and supporting King Reṇu the Great, we six kings also wish you to assist and support us in the same manner.' The brahmin chief minister, having heard these words, jointly assisted all seven kings, participating in counsel in all matters.

"At that time, the brahmin chief minister further instructed seven thousand brahmins in chanting those scriptures, and taught seven thousand brahmins in reading those scriptures. Then the brahmins, householders, commoners, and people of the realm, all knowing and witnessing the brahmin chief minister's such talent and wisdom, discussed it among themselves: 'This Mahādṛḍha is truly a great brahmin; he is further able to instruct the brahmin community in reading and chanting the Vedic texts.'

"At that time, the brahmin chief minister, having heard the people's discussion, reflected thus: 'These brahmins, householders, commoners, and people gather here and there to deliberate, and groundlessly praise me, speaking of my talent and wisdom, and furthermore regard me as a truly great brahmin. This is not appropriate for me. Let me examine myself: truly I am not a genuine great brahmin. I shall no longer instruct the brahmins in reading and chanting the Vedic texts. Even if one's learning is broad, this is genuinely not my excellence; moreover, in this world my physical form will not long endure. I once heard the elders, the wise great brahmins of former times, say that in brahminical practice, during the four months of summer, one should dwell quietly in one place and cultivate the meditation of compassion. If that meditation succeeds, the Great Brahmā King will appear in person and grant the wishes one seeks. If such a thing should occur, this is what I desire; I should cultivate this meditation as has been described.' Having thus reflected, the brahmin chief minister, wishing to dwell quietly in one place during the four months of summer and cultivate the meditation of compassion, went to where King Reṇu was and, having arrived, said: 'Great King! I now desire, during the four months of summer, to dwell quietly in one place and cultivate the meditation of compassion. I pray that Your Majesty grant me permission.' King Reṇu said: 'Mahādṛḍha! As you wish; now is indeed the time.'

"Then the brahmin chief minister, having received the king's permission, went to a secluded place and with focused and concentrated mind cultivated the meditation of compassion during the four months of summer. When the four months of summer had passed, on the fifteenth day of the bright fortnight, the day of the bhikṣus' poṣadha, he performed in that place, according to brahminical practice, the following: with fresh gomaya he first plastered the ground, then constructed a four-sided fire altar; in the center of the altar he also made a fire pit. The brahmin chief minister bathed his body, donned clean new garments, approached from the north to the southern boundary of the altar, scattered kuśa grass to cover the altar floor completely, sat facing north, took up the sruva, performed the fire rite, and made offerings to Brahmā heaven. Not long after the brahmin chief minister had begun the rite, suddenly from the north there appeared a great luminous sign. Upon seeing this light, the brahmin chief minister gave rise to a sense of the extraordinary; the hairs of his body stood up in delight; he became even more reverently composed and dwelt with focused mind. The radiance blazed brilliantly, the like of which he had never before seen. Not long after the Great Brahmā King revealed this light, he came from the north and hovered in the midst of space. The brahmin chief minister, with wholehearted joy, gazed up into space and beheld the Great Brahmā King dwelling in the sky. Immediately joining his palms and bowing his head in reverence, he spoke a gāthā:

> 'Endowed with divine power, form, and brilliant radiance,
> What holy one now appears in space?
> Though I now see you, I cannot know;
> I pray you tell me truly as it is.'

"Then the Great Brahmā King spoke a gāthā in reply to the brahmin chief minister:

> 'Those of pure conduct all know
> That I dwell constantly in the Brahmā realm;
> Moreover, the devas know my name —
> You, brahmin, should reflect upon this yourself.'

"The brahmin chief minister again spoke a gāthā:

> 'Pure water and a seat are here provided,
> With ghee, honey, and milk-porridge, finest in flavor;
> These I offer first with concentrated mind —
> I pray the Brahmā King compassionately receive them.'

"The Great Brahmā King again spoke a gāthā:

> 'Pure water and a seat are here provided,
> With ghee, honey, and milk-porridge, finest in flavor;
> You, brahmin, have offered these first —
> I now receive them for you as is fitting.'

"The brahmin chief minister spoke a gāthā:

> 'The domain of the five pleasures is called this world;
> Birth in the Brahmā realm is called the other world.
> I rejoice in this meaning and begin my inquiry;
> I pray the Brahmā King grant me leave to ask.'

"The Great Brahmā King spoke a gāthā:

> 'Of the two meanings, this world and the other world,
> Ask freely as you please.
> I now grant you leave without hesitation;
> Whatever you would ask, speak it quickly.'

"Then the brahmin chief minister reflected thus: 'At this time, wishing to resolve my doubts, which meaning should I first ask the Brahmā King? Should I ask about the arising of this world? Or should I ask how to attain birth in the other world?' The brahmin chief minister further considered: 'As for the meaning of this world, it arises through the five pleasures; this should not be asked. I shall now ask the Brahmā King about the meaning of attaining birth in the other world.' Having so reflected, he then asked the Great Brahmā King: 'O valiant and pure one, Great Brahmā King! I now ask you; I pray you resolve my doubts. Great Brahmā, among humans, if one wishes to attain birth in the peaceful Brahmā realm, what practice must one cultivate in order to attain birth there?'

"Then the Great Brahmā King spoke a gāthā in reply to the brahmin chief minister:

> 'One who cultivates non-self — that is pure conduct;
> Mind dwelling in one-pointedness; the liberation of compassion;
> Far from the stain of desires, afflictions removed —
> Such persons attain birth in the Brahmā realm.'

"Then the brahmin chief minister addressed the Great Brahmā King: 'As the Great Brahmā has stated in his gāthā, "one who cultivates non-self — that is pure conduct," I have now come to understand this meaning. It refers to a certain class of persons who arouse sincere faith, cultivate the dharma of renunciation, shave off beard and hair, don the kāṣāya robe, and relinquish all wealth and pleasures, whether few or many, their wisdom following in accordance. Whether born in a high lineage or a low lineage, their minds are equanimous, free from grasping and attachment. They keep only the three robes and one bowl, possessing nothing beyond these. In the various trainings, they are instructed in the methods of training; their bodily, verbal, and mental karma are completely pure; they sustain themselves through pure livelihood, free from all transgression. This is what is called "one who cultivates non-self."

"'Furthermore, as the Great Brahmā has stated, "mind dwelling in one-pointedness," I have heard these words and also understand this meaning. It refers to a certain class of those who cultivate meditative concentration. Inwardly pure of mind, abiding in the nature of a single object, without applied thought or sustained thought, with joy and bliss born of concentration, they attain the second dhyāna in its fullness of practice. This is what is called "mind dwelling in one-pointedness."

"'Furthermore, as the Great Brahmā has stated, "the liberation of compassion," I have heard these words and also understand this meaning. It refers to a certain class of those who cultivate compassion. With the mind arising simultaneously with compassion, first in the eastern direction, they extend compassion extensively. Their mind is vast, complete in its practice, equal and non-dual, likewise without limit, free from enmity and affliction. Having thus practiced in the eastern direction, they do likewise in the southern, western, and northern directions, in the four intermediate directions, above and below, throughout all the worlds, extending compassion extensively, complete in their practice. This is what is called "the liberation of compassion."

"'Furthermore, as the Great Brahmā has stated, "far from the stain of desires, afflictions removed," I have heard these words and do not understand this meaning. Great Brahmā, what are the afflictions? How can one among human beings cause the afflictions to become purified? The ocean of afflictions overflows in abundance; amid this, how can a practitioner attain birth in that peaceful Brahmā realm?'

"Then the Great Brahmā King spoke a gāthā in reply to the brahmin chief minister:

> 'Greed, hatred, delusion, conceit, doubt, anger, and concealment,
> Vexation, harmfulness, deceit, falsehood, miserliness, and envy;
> Giving rise to these defiling dharmas and slandering others —
> These are what are called the afflictions.
> Far removed from all such afflictions,
> One attains purity among humankind;
> Blocking the source of the ocean of afflictions,
> One attains birth in the peaceful Brahmā realm.'

"Then the brahmin chief minister addressed the Great Brahmā King: 'As the Great Brahmā has spoken of the dharmas of affliction, I have heard these words and fully understand this meaning. Were I to remain a householder, I would be wholly entangled. Were I to go forth from the household, I would be wholly free from transgression, and I should cultivate the pure and immaculate brahmacarya. Why so? All that is born must perish; human life is brief; if one does not awaken to this, one falls at death into the evil destinies. Therefore I now, knowing this of myself and awakening to it myself, should practice well and conduct myself in right brahmacarya, no longer creating evil karma in the world. Great Brahmā, I now relinquish the household life and seek to go forth. I pray that the Great Brahmā know my heart and mind.' The Great Brahmā King replied: 'As you wish; now is indeed the time.'

"Then the Great Brahmā King who had appeared in the sky, having spoken thus, concealed himself and was no longer seen."

Furthermore, in the assembly, the gandharva prince Pañcaśikha came forward and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Today I have heard the Brahmā King recount before the World-Honored One this story of causes and conditions, and it has suddenly occurred to me: was not that chief minister, the great brahmin Mahādṛḍha, none other than the World-Honored One himself?"

The Buddha told the gandharva prince Pañcaśikha: "Just so, just so. That chief minister, the great brahmin Mahādṛḍha, was indeed myself. I recall how in the past that chief minister, the great brahmin Mahādṛḍha, went forth from the household life and so forth. Have you heard of this before?"

Pañcaśikha replied: "No, World-Honored One. I have not heard of it before."

The Buddha said: "Pañcaśikha! I shall now tell you in sequence. Pañcaśikha! At that time, after the chief minister, the great brahmin Mahādṛḍha, had completed the fire rite, he went to where King Reṇu was. Having arrived, he knelt in obeisance and, respectfully facing the king, spoke a gāthā:

> 'I have a wish I would now declare
> To Reṇu, great king, lord of the realm:
> I relinquish my ministerial office to seek the homeless life;
> May the king govern the affairs of state himself.'

"Then King Reṇu spoke a gāthā in reply to the chief minister:

> 'If you lack any of your needs,
> Whatever you desire, I shall provide;
> If someone troubles you, tell me at once —
> I shall punish them according to the law of kings.
> You are as my father and I as your son;
> We support each other — how could we be parted?
> Though you serve as minister, you are also my teacher —
> Why do you now speak these words?'

"The brahmin chief minister spoke a gāthā:

> 'In all my needs I lack for nothing,
> Nor has any other troubled or vexed me;
> It is only that I have heard the words of truth,
> And my resolve to go forth will not be turned.'

"King Reṇu spoke a gāthā:

> 'What non-human has spoken — what truth can there be?
> Why do you hearken and believe such words?
> Do not take these words as true
> And abandon your ministerial post to seek the homeless life.'

"The brahmin chief minister spoke a gāthā:

> 'Devaputra, I first performed the fire rite,
> Boldly arousing a pure and concentrated mind;
> I laid out the altar according to the rite, offering fire to the devas,
> Employing kuśa grass in the ceremony.
> The Great Brahmā King, the great sage and holy one,
> Responding to my entreaty, manifested in person;
> I heard him speak the words of truth —
> Therefore my resolve to go forth stands firm.'

"King Reṇu spoke a gāthā:

> 'As you, my minister, have well spoken,
> I now find in myself complete faith and understanding;
> Since you have heard the words of the ancient sages,
> How could this resolve to go forth be turned?
> Your mind is pure as empty space,
> And again like a jewel of pure and wondrous vaiḍūrya;
> As you practice, so shall I follow —
> Through you I have attained awakening.'

"When King Reṇu had finished speaking this gāthā, he also said: 'Mahādṛḍha! Your mind is pure and you delight in cultivating good conduct. Do as you wish; you have found a refuge, and I too have found a refuge.'

"Then the brahmin chief minister spoke another gāthā, addressing the king:

> 'O King, you should renounce all objects of desire —
> One who clings to them is but a fool;
> You should arouse a firm mind free from attachment,
> Endowed with samāhita and the power of forbearance.
> What is here awakened to is the pure vehicle;
> This pure path is truly and permanently abiding;
> This is the gate of the true Dharma that is proclaimed —
> Through this one attains birth in the Brahmā realm.'

"Pañcaśikha! The six kings who administered the various kingdoms, having heard that the brahmin chief minister was relinquishing his ministerial post and delighting in seeking the homeless life, assembled all six kings together in one place. Then the brahmin chief minister himself went to where the six kings were. Having arrived, he knelt in obeisance and addressed the kings: 'Your Majesties! You should know that I now wish to relinquish that ministerial post. I pray that each of the kings will separately seek someone to assist with affairs of state; if there be instruction to be given, let them rely separately on teachers and exemplars. I now delight in wishing to go forth from the household life to cultivate the path. Why so? From the Great Brahmā King I heard the words of truth, namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned. From this time forth, I no longer delight in the household life with its complete entanglement. Were I to go forth, I would be wholly free from transgression, and I should cultivate the pure and immaculate brahmacarya. Why so? All that is born must perish; human life is brief; if one does not awaken to this, one falls at death into the evil destinies. Therefore I now, knowing this of myself and awakening to it myself, should practice well and conduct myself in right brahmacarya, no longer creating evil karma in the world.'

"At that time the six kings all deliberated together: 'Why is this brahmin chief minister forsaking wealth and rank to seek the homeless life? Among brahmins there are also those who love wealth and rank. We should encourage this man with wealth and rank so that he does not leave the household life.' Having thus deliberated together, the six kings all said to the brahmin chief minister: 'We six kings, with all that is desired by way of wealth and rank, implore you. The wealth and rank we now possess were all obtained in accordance with the Dharma.' Having said this, they brought forth abundant wealth, jewels, and all the implements of affluence, and presented them to the brahmin chief minister. The brahmin chief minister then addressed the six kings: 'Your Majesties! All these wealth, jewels, and implements of affluence I already possess in full abundance, and what I possess was likewise obtained in accordance with the Dharma. What I myself possess I have already fully relinquished; how much the less would I now accept the gifts of the kings? I am now firmly resolved to seek the homeless life. Why so? From the Great Brahmā King I heard the words of truth, namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned, and so forth as previously spoken in full.'

"Pañcaśikha! The six kings then deliberated again among themselves: 'Among brahmins there are also those who love beautiful dancing girls. We should present some to him.' Having thus deliberated together, the six kings presented beautiful dancing girls to the brahmin chief minister. The kings said: 'These dancing girls of ours are extraordinary in their appearance, full and fine of form, pleasant in their bearing and conduct, and moreover possessed of many accomplishments. You should accept them; do not leave the household life.' The brahmin chief minister then addressed the six kings: 'Your Majesties! In my own home I already have forty wives, extraordinary in their appearance, full and fine of form, pleasant in their bearing and conduct, perfectly equal in their comeliness. Though I already possess them, I have fully relinquished them; how much the less would I now accept the gifts of the kings? I am now firmly resolved to seek the homeless life. Why so? From the Great Brahmā King I heard the words of truth, namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned, and so forth as previously spoken in full.'

"Pañcaśikha! The six kings all then said to the brahmin chief minister: 'Since you are firmly resolved to seek the homeless life, wait yet a while. After seven years, when our princes, sons, and younger brothers have each been established, we too shall follow you into the homeless life. O Mahādṛḍha, if you have found a refuge, we kings too have found a refuge.' The brahmin chief minister then addressed the six kings: 'If I must wait seven years, that is far too long. My resolve is firm and I wish to go forth without delay. Why so? From the Great Brahmā King I heard the words of truth, namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned, and so forth as previously spoken in full.' The six kings said again: 'O Mahādṛḍha, if that cannot be, then wait six more years, or five years, or even one year.' The chief minister replied: 'If I must wait even one year, that is far too long. My resolve is firm and I wish to go forth without delay.' The six kings said again: 'If that cannot be, then wait seven more months.' The chief minister replied: 'If I must wait seven months, that is far too long. My resolve is firm and I wish to go forth without delay.' The six kings said again: 'If that cannot be, then six months, or even half a month.' The chief minister replied: 'If I must wait half a month, that is far too long. My resolve is firm and I wish to go forth without delay.' The six kings said again: 'If that cannot be, then wait seven days.' The chief minister replied: 'Your Majesties! If I must wait seven days, that would be acceptable. Going forth from the household means abandoning suffering for joy; now is indeed the time.'

"Pañcaśikha! The brahmin chief minister then went to where the seven thousand brahmins who chanted the scriptures and the seven thousand brahmins who read the scriptures were. Having arrived, he addressed in full all fourteen thousand brahmins: 'Welcome, welcome, assembly of brahmins! As for the Vedic texts you possess, whether reading them or reciting them, from this time forth each of you should separately seek a teacher to instruct you. I am now going forth from the household life and can no longer teach you. Why so? From the Great Brahmā King I heard the words of truth, namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned. From this time forth, I no longer delight in the household life with its complete entanglement. Were I to go forth, I would be wholly free from transgression, and I should cultivate the pure and immaculate brahmacarya. Why so? All that is born must perish; human life is brief; if one does not awaken to this, one falls at death into the evil destinies. Therefore I now, knowing this of myself and awakening to it myself, should practice well and conduct myself in right brahmacarya, no longer creating evil karma in the world.'

"At that time the assembly of fourteen thousand brahmins all addressed the brahmin chief minister: 'Our teacher is a man of wisdom; it would not be right for him to go forth from the household life. Why so? As for the homeless life, it has little benefit, little authority, and little renown. But as for a brahmin, he has great benefit, great authority, and great renown.' The brahmin chief minister then told those fourteen thousand brahmins: 'You brahmins, do not say this! Do not say this! You should know that the homeless life has great benefit, great authority, and great renown; whereas a brahmin has little benefit, little authority, and little renown. Whatever you brahmins know and understand, all of it arises through the condition of transmission by teachers. Therefore, do not harbor differing views.' At that time the assembly of fourteen thousand brahmins all addressed the brahmin chief minister: 'As our teacher has said, just so, just so. The homeless life has great benefit, great authority, and great renown. Indeed, whatever we know and understand, all of it arises through the condition of transmission by teachers. If our teacher now has found a refuge, we too have found a refuge.' The brahmin chief minister then again told the fourteen thousand brahmins: 'Going forth from the household means abandoning suffering for joy; now is indeed the time.'

"The brahmin chief minister then returned to his own home to where his forty wives were, and said to them: 'Welcome, welcome! Each of you should go to your respective kinsmen, or if you prefer, remain among another brahmin household. I now relinquish you, with my heart set on going forth from the household life. Why so? From the Great Brahmā King I heard the words of truth, namely, that the dharmas of affliction should be abandoned. From this time forth, I no longer delight in the household life with its complete entanglement. Were I to go forth, I would be wholly free from transgression, and I should cultivate the pure and immaculate brahmacarya. Why so? All that is born must perish; human life is brief; if one does not awaken to this, one falls at death into the evil destinies. Therefore I now, knowing this of myself and awakening to it myself, should practice well and conduct myself in right brahmacarya, no longer creating evil karma in the world.' The forty wives then all addressed the brahmin chief minister: 'O Mahādṛḍha! When you should be a teacher and venerable one, you are just that; when you should be a husband and master, you are just that; when you should be a good friend, you are just that. Now do as you wish; you have found a refuge, and we too have found a refuge.' The brahmin chief minister then again said to the forty wives: 'Going forth from the household means abandoning suffering for joy; now is indeed the time.'

"Pañcaśikha! After the brahmin chief minister had conveyed his message to all who needed to be informed, within seven days, with firm and unwavering faith, he took refuge in the Buddha and went forth from the household life. His beard and hair fell away of themselves, the kāṣāya robe appeared on his body, and he took on the appearance of a bhikṣu, complete in dignified bearing. After the brahmin chief minister had gone forth, the seven kings all relinquished their kingdoms and likewise followed him into the homeless life; the seven thousand brahmins who chanted the scriptures likewise followed him into the homeless life; his forty wives likewise followed him into the homeless life. At that time, countless hundreds of thousands of people each rejoiced and all delighted in going forth from the household life.

"Pañcaśikha! The chief minister, the great brahmin Mahādṛḍha, having gone far from all sensual pleasures, attained the fruit of arhatship. Having attained the holy fruit, he further expounded for those who practiced brahmacarya alongside him the various gateways of the Dharma for the class of śrāvakas. Having heard the Dharma, they comprehended its meaning and were to be reborn in the Brahmā realm. At that time, the śrāvakas of Mahādṛḍha further expounded for those who cultivated brahmacarya alongside them the various gateways of the Dharma for the class of śrāvakas. Having heard the Dharma, they comprehended its meaning and were reborn in the Heaven of the Four Great Kings within the realm of desire. There was another class of those who practiced brahmacarya alongside them: having heard the Dharma and comprehended its meaning, they were reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. There was another class who were reborn in the Yāma heaven; another class who were reborn in Tuṣita heaven; another class who were reborn in the Nirmāṇarati heaven; another class who were reborn in the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven.

"Pañcaśikha! Among those in that assembly, men and women, and those who practiced brahmacarya alongside him, those who harbored thoughts of transgression toward the śrāvaka Mahādṛḍha: at the dissolution of their bodies at death, they fell into the hells. Among those in that assembly, men and women, and those who practiced brahmacarya alongside him, those who harbored thoughts of pure faith toward the śrāvaka Mahādṛḍha: at the dissolution of their bodies at death, they were reborn in the heavenly realms.

"Pañcaśikha! The śrāvaka Mahādṛḍha traveled throughout cities, towns, villages, and territories, extensively teaching and benefiting all, whether kings, ministers, elders, brahmins, or ordinary people of every station, causing them to abandon wrong paths. At that time, the kings, ministers, elders, brahmins, those who cultivated brahmacarya, householders, and all ordinary people of every station throughout the realm, all said: 'We take refuge in the holy one Mahādṛḍha, chief minister of the seven kings. How excellent! Today we have gained great good fortune.'"

After the World-Honored One had thus concluded his recounting of the causes and conditions of the past, the gandharva prince Pañcaśikha's mind gave rise to joy; far from dust and free from defilement, he attained the purity of the Dharma-eye.

When the Buddha had finished expounding this sūtra, the gandharva prince Pañcaśikha and the entire great assembly, having heard what the Buddha had taught, all experienced great joy, and with faith received and upheld it.
