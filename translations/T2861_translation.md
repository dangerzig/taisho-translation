# 泉州千佛新著諸祖師頌
## Newly Composed Verses on the Patriarchs by the Thousand-Buddha Master of Quanzhou

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2861

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

### Preface

Reverend Tai of Nanyue composed five praises and ten verses, which at the time were acclaimed as admirable. Later, Xiangyan of Yuepu was especially skilled at such verses. These serve as aids to the Way. From the successive transmission of the ancestral lamp, beginning with Kāśyapa and ending at Caoxi, there are altogether thirty-three patriarchs. After the robe of faith, several more followed. What the earlier worthies had not yet praised, I considered a deficiency. Although the Baolin zhuan narrates the events and one can examine them by reading, alas, those who have forgotten their concerns or who are too lazy may find the connections too remote. On a late autumn evening, I took the above idea and requested Chan Master Cheng of Thousand-Buddha Temple. Though he was reluctant to presume, he could not refuse. Before two nights had passed, the work was complete. The language and reasoning are born of Thousand-Buddha's brush. The remarkable phrases from the dialogues of that time are sometimes blended in. Though the words are not many, those with discernment can survey the works of all the sages herein. It is also like stuffing Mount Sumeru into a mustard seed: the capacity is one. Therefore, I have taken up paper and dipped the brush to write this preface.

*The twenty-eight patriarchs of India and the six patriarchs of the Tang territory. Afterward, composed by Great Master Qing Mingjue.*

---

### First Patriarch: The Venerable Mahākāśyapa

> Magnificent Kāśyapa, who secretly received the Buddha-mind!
> His body relied on a single robe;
> His words were an ocean a thousand fathoms deep.
> His deportment was stately, his transforming Way profound.
> Not yet having met Maitreya,
> He rests in meditation at Kukkuṭapāda.

### Second Patriarch: The Venerable Ānanda

> Ānanda of much learning and joyful celebration,
> Who raised high the Dharma-banner!
> He transmitted the Buddha's golden verse,
> Continuing the ancestral silver lamp.
> Foremost in compassion, unmatched in wisdom,
> After Kāśyapa's footsteps: the moon's reflection in an autumn river.

### Third Patriarch: The Venerable Śāṇavāsa

> The venerable one robed from birth,
> A bright lamp in a dark room.
> The ears and eyes of gods and humans,
> The supporting limb of the buddha-dharma.
> Neither mind nor form, neither decrease nor increase:
> Excellent, this ultimate principle! A great roc on the ocean of awakening.

### Fourth Patriarch: The Venerable Upagupta

> Upagupta, whose eloquence poured forth the mysterious river!
> The Dharma-mountain towered steep;
> The Way-tree spread its branches.
> Tallying-sticks filled a stone chamber; corpses bound the demon of heaven.
> His nature was not of seventeen; he awakened in an instant.

### Fifth Patriarch: The Venerable Dhṛtaka

> Great Master Dhṛtaka, who left home without self!
> Penetrating the roots, reaching the objects:
> A hare, a moon, an empty flower.
> His essence was not in form or appearance;
> His principle was beyond words.
> Benefiting beings according to the occasion: how could there be a gourd?

### Sixth Patriarch: Mikkaka

> Patriarch Mikkaka, who practiced the five supernatural powers as a sage!
> Meeting the teacher's right Dharma,
> He saw that his own mind was biased.
> Awakened yet as if unawakened: mysterious upon mysterious.
> Demonstrating nirvāṇa through supernatural power, the eight classes were silently awed.

### Seventh Patriarch: The Venerable Vasumitra

> Patriarch Vasumitra entered Mikkaka's chamber.
> Delusion and awakening are originally thus;
> Self and things merge into one.
> Carrying a wine vessel level, a buddha-head on his neck:
> What is right, what is wrong? Who gains, who loses?

### Eighth Patriarch: The Venerable Buddhanandi

> Buddhanandi, who greatly transformed the deluded!
> Mind without anything outside of mind;
> His Dharma-principle was lofty.
> He debated throughout the Five Indias, a cloud-ladder for the three realms.
> Outstanding in true spirit, north, south, east, and west.

### Ninth Patriarch: The Venerable Buddhamitra

> Buddhamitra, a great vessel of late accomplishment!
> For fifty years he did not speak;
> For fifty years he did not act.
> When I met the accomplished master, I suddenly contracted the unborn.
> A pine on the cliff has integrity; an autumn rooster has no schedule.

### Tenth Patriarch: The Venerable Pārśva

> The great Venerable Pārśva, whose love and hatred
> Were as vast as empty space!
> His Way was flowing and pure.
> His true essence was natural, relying on truth to extend and rest.
> In his youth, the world was grey; his mind-horse galloped.

### Eleventh Patriarch: The Venerable Puṇyayaśas

> Puṇyayaśas, whose wisdom was like Mount Sumeru!
> His mind pointed to the Dharma's abiding;
> Beyond his body, glory and decline.
> Hidden and manifest, seeing and hearing: rare and subtle.
> Presented directly, held up: no further discrepancy.

### Twelfth Patriarch: The Venerable Aśvaghoṣa Bodhisattva

> The Venerable Aśvaghoṣa: his transforming flower became the Dharma.
> The māra-palace mists rolled away;
> The garden wind was clear.
> "I wish to know the Buddha; one who does not know is a friend."
> Nothing that is not mysterious understanding: where the foot moves, dust arises.

### Thirteenth Patriarch: The Venerable Kapimala

> The venerable and holy Kapimala, in the causal ground a māra-king!
> Relying on the teacher's instruction,
> He killed and realized the true and constant.
> What makes one foolish or wise? Who holds the measuring rod?
> His virtue was fragrant, his nature pure: orchids and frost.

### Fourteenth Patriarch: The Venerable Nāgārjuna Bodhisattva

> Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna, who transformed the dragon! This is the mist.
> His mind knew the Buddha-mind;
> He abided, yet did not abide.
> His body manifested full and clear; his Dharma flowed like nourishing rain.
> Deva's capacity met him: who else could fathom the purport?

### Fifteenth Patriarch: The Venerable Kāṇadeva

> Kāṇadeva, whose virtue-shore was ever higher!
> He turned back the fragrant elephant;
> He yawned, revealing the golden mane.
> His capacity penetrated like cliff-thunder; his eloquence poured like autumn waves.
> Beginning and end, transcending realization: do not mistake the king's sword.

### Sixteenth Patriarch: The Venerable Rāhulata

> Rāhulata's Way and virtue: how can the mouth convey them?
> Through the teacher's spoken ear,
> He soon found the human gate.
> Raising high the sun of understanding, its great light shone on heaven and earth.
> Neither grasping nor rejecting, transmitting to his descendants.

### Seventeenth Patriarch: The Venerable Saṃghanandi

> Saṃghanandi, a prince of Śrāvastī!
> He crossed the nine-layered walls
> And entered the mountains a thousand leagues.
> Concentration is likened to gold in a well; meaning diverges from beginning and end.
> His logic yielded to the teacher; he suddenly exhausted himself.

### Eighteenth Patriarch: The Venerable Gayāśata

> Gayāśata, who encountered the Buddha's capacity in his youth!
> Hand carrying a jeweled mirror,
> He faced Master Saṃghanandi.
> Inner and outer obstructions removed, brows and eyes unimpaired.
> The wind blows the bell's chime: if not I, then who?

### Nineteenth Patriarch: The Venerable Kumārata

> Kumārata, the great and constant, stopping beneath the eaves!
> Encountering the teacher's nourishing rain,
> Entrusting to the father without weariness.
> Originally not forged or tempered, why borrow hammer and tongs?
> Sitting alone on a single couch, gods and humans bowed in reverence.

### Twentieth Patriarch: The Venerable Jayata

> Patriarch Jayata, lofty in stature, ancient in bearing!
> His staff had six rings;
> His field had not half an acre.
> Beneath his words, nothing arose; where was it not universal?
> Lowering his hands, he entered the market: other lands and this land.

### Twenty-First Patriarch: The Venerable Vasubandhu

> Vasubandhu, who cultivated practice without sleeping!
> Though he endured hardship,
> He turned it into ease.
> Pointing at seeing, he saw seeing; encountering a song, he clapped in harmony.
> Bubbles and illusions have no truth: let the feelings be without fault.

### Twenty-Second Patriarch: The Venerable Manorhita

> Discerning the stūpa, subduing the brave, prince of the Sovereign King!
> Thunder shook and hibernating creatures moved;
> The heretic teacher lost his teeth.
> With the six supernatural powers divinely deployed, his Way-wind reached a thousand leagues.
> Sound and form are always true: why should one be deaf?

### Twenty-Third Patriarch: The Venerable Haklena

> The Venerable Haklena: supreme virtue is without attainment!
> Following his nature, acting freely,
> His words were strange and remarkable.
> His merit surpassed both meanings; his fame suited all lands.
> I bow my head in refuge: the bodhi-tree of the ancestral forest.

### Twenty-Fourth Patriarch: The Venerable Siṃha

> The Venerable Siṃha, revered by gods and humans!
> In the empty sky, pressing green;
> Among the clouds, a crane soaring.
> When the debate-drum sounded, the Dharma-wheel rode high.
> He felled the heretics; who could find his true abode?

### Twenty-Fifth Patriarch: The Venerable Basiasita

> Basiasita, long departed from grasping!
> Not yet having met the master,
> He never heard the fist.
> Transmitting the teacher's robe and bowl, a bridge and boat for delivering beings.
> With the wondrous vision at the heart: how could words be needed?

### Twenty-Sixth Patriarch: The Venerable Puṇyamitra

> Puṇyamitra, born to the joy of the victorious king!
> Lofty and far, an empty lady,
> Resolutely pursuing the Way's practice.
> A ridgepole and beam of the buddha-dharma, revered by the king.
> Penetrating insight like a polished gem: a golden mirror in the ancestral hall.

### Twenty-Seventh Patriarch: The Venerable Prajñātāra

> Prajñātāra, called "Necklace" in his youth!
> His parents having passed away,
> He wandered east and west.
> Once he understood the hair of the tortoise, he ever lamented the water's drying up.
> The fruit of bodhi ripened; the source of the Way was vast and far.

### The Six Patriarchs of the Tang Territory

### First Patriarch: Bodhidharma

> Bodhidharma, whose Way transformed through non-doing!
> Nine years at Shaoshi,
> Six generations of the school's teachers.
> He demonstrated that even in extinction one can hear: with a single sandal he returned west.
> Emperor Wu of Liang did not recognize him;
> Huike transmitted the robe.

### Second Patriarch: Great Master Huike

> The Second Patriarch, of great learning, whose integrity was firm and resolute!
> His mind penetrated the Three Vehicles;
> His neck bore the weight of the Five Peaks.
> A qilin in the heavens, a luan-phoenix in the human world.
> He severed his arm, establishing the spirit: blended yet not muddied.

### Third Patriarch: Great Master Sengcan

> The Third Patriarch, Great Master, a true child among kings!
> His words emerged from the subtle and profound;
> His mind knew no "that" or "this."
> Sometimes in mountain forests, sometimes in the marketplace he demonstrated:
> Where the ground has flowers, sandalwood fragrance curls.

### Fourth Patriarch: Great Master Daoxin

> The Fourth Patriarch, at fourteen, through the teacher gained liberation!
> In the world, the Way was solitary;
> His compassion was boundless.
> Forever cutting off carving and polishing, the Dharma transcended beginning and end.
> Fruit few, flowers many: patiently he transmitted the robe and bowl.

### Fifth Patriarch: Great Master Hongren

> The Fifth Patriarch, at seven, penetrated what is before words!
> A stone ox breathed mist;
> A wooden horse held its breath.
> Body and mind always still; principle and phenomena both mysterious.
> Without feeling, without seed: a thousand years, ten thousand years.

### Sixth Patriarch: Great Master Huineng

> The Master went to Huangmei, received the teaching, and came south.
> Through the meaning of the banner,
> He greatly shook the Dharma-thunder.
> Daoming met his lesson; Shenxiu encountered the turning.
> Though the robe was no longer transmitted,
> Flowers bloomed throughout the world.

---

### Reverend Rang of Nanyue

> The Reverend of Guanyin, whose name was Huairang!
> Prajñā dwelt in his spirit;
> A towering craftsman of the Chan gate.
> His virtue was high as the Five Peaks; his mind was vast as the Four Seas.
> His Dharma-heirs numbered six; Mazu arose as king.

### Reverend Xingsi of Jizhou

> The true person of Jishui, who emerged in Luling!
> He lifted up only the single vein,
> Reaching far beyond the Three Vehicles.
> A moon-candle in the ocean, water walking in fire.
> His marvelous meeting with you: speaking of the robe, the response arose.

### National Teacher Huizhong

> The National Teacher of the Tang dynasty broadly spread great plans!
> At Caoxi he explored the moon;
> On the Wei River he rode a boat.
> Two emperors sought his counsel; the four assemblies threw wide.
> His Dharma-talent was exhaustively penetrating;
> Big-Ears hung his head in shame.

### Reverend Shitou

> Shitou of Nanyue, a stream branching from Jishui!
> His hermitage nestled in the blue cave;
> His cart was drawn by a white ox.
> His learning achieved the unicorn's horn; his fame leaked through the divine boat.
> A monk asked about the Pure Land:
> "Not defiled: what need to seek?"

### Reverend Ma of Jiangxi

> Master Ma Daoyi, his practice solid as gold and stone!
> His awakening was originally transcendent;
> Searching along branches was vain labor.
> After long settling of body and mind, at once he cast it all aside.
> Greatly transforming Nanchang: a cold pine a thousand feet tall.

Recorded by the śramaṇa Daozhen of Three Realms Temple, Shazhou.
