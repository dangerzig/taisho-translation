# Treatise on the Eighteen Schools

## *Shíbā Bù Lùn* (十八部論)

Taishō 2032

Attributed to Paramārtha (Zhēndì, 真諦) of the Chen dynasty, though possibly translated by Kumārajīva. A treatise on the early Buddhist schools.

*Note: This treatise catalogs the schisms and doctrinal positions of the early Buddhist schools. It opens with a passage from the Mañjuśrī-paripṛcchā Sūtra (Wénshū Shīlì Wèn Jīng, 文殊師利問經), "Chapter on Distinguishing the Schools," in which the Buddha predicts the division of his followers into twenty schools after his parinirvāṇa. The text then provides a more detailed historical account beginning with King Aśoka 116 years after the Buddha's passing, tracing each schism chronologically. The second half presents the root doctrinal positions (mūla-dṛṣṭi) of each school: Mahāsāṃghika, Sarvāstivāda, Dharmaguptaka, Mahīśāsaka, Kāśyapīya, and others. An appended note discusses confusion in the catalogs about whether this treatise was translated by Kumārajīva or Paramārtha, since internal evidence (references to "Qin" and Kumārajīva's name) points toward a Kumārajīva-era origin.*

## Treatise on the Eighteen Schools, in One Fascicle

### From the Mañjuśrī-paripṛcchā Sūtra, Chapter Fifteen: Distinguishing the Schools

At that time Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, after the Buddha enters nirvāṇa, how will the schools of your future disciples divide? What are the root schools?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "My future disciples will have twenty schools, which will cause the dharma to abide in the world. All the schools attain the four fruits. The three baskets are equal, without lower, middle, or upper. It is like the taste of seawater, which has no differences. It is as if a person had twenty sons: all are truly what the Tathāgata has taught."

"Mañjuśrī, the two root schools emerged from the Mahāyāna. From the Prajñāpāramitā emerged the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. All buddhas likewise emerged from the Prajñāpāramitā. Mañjuśrī, just as earth, water, fire, wind, and space are the dwelling place of all sentient beings, so too the Prajñāpāramitā and the Mahāyāna are the origin of all śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and buddhas."

Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what are these schools called?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The first two schools are: (1) Mahāsāṃghika (*Móhē Sēngqí*, 摩訶僧祇), and (2) Sthavira (*Tǐpílǚ*, 體毘履). Within a hundred years after my entry into nirvāṇa, these two schools shall arise."

"Later, from the Mahāsāṃghika emerged seven schools. Within a hundred years, one school emerged named Ekavyāvahārika (*Zhíyī Yǔyán*, 執一語言, 'Adherents of One Designation'). Within a hundred years, from the Ekavyāvahārika emerged another school named Lokottaravāda (*Chūshìjiān Yǔyán*, 出世間語言, 'Transcendent Designation'). Within a hundred years, from the Lokottaravāda emerged one school named Kukkuṭika (*Gāojūlí*, 高拘梨, 'Cock-Coop Dwellers'). Within a hundred years, from the Kukkuṭika emerged one school named Bahuśrutīya (*Duōwén*, 多聞, 'Learned'). Within a hundred years, from the Bahuśrutīya emerged one school named Caityaka (*Zhǐdǐjiā*, 只底舸). Within a hundred years, from the Caityaka emerged one school named Pūrvaśaila (*Dōngshān*, 東山, 'Eastern Mountain'). Within a hundred years, from the Pūrvaśaila emerged one school named Uttaraśaila (*Běishān*, 北山, 'Northern Mountain'). These are the seven schools that emerged from the Mahāsāṃghika. Together with the original Saṃghika, they are eight schools."

"Within a hundred years, from the Sthavira school emerged eleven schools. Within a hundred years, one school emerged named Sarvāstivāda (*Yīqiè Yǔyán*, 一切語言, 'Adherents of All Existence'). Within a hundred years, from the Sarvāstivāda emerged one school named Haimavata (*Xuěshān*, 雪山, 'Snow Mountain'). Within a hundred years, from the Haimavata emerged one school named Vātsīputrīya (*Dúzǐ*, 犢子, 'Calf's Son'). Within a hundred years, from the Vātsīputrīya emerged one school named Dharmottarīya (*Fǎshèng*, 法勝, 'Dharma-Superior'). Within a hundred years, from the Dharmottarīya emerged one school named Bhadrayānīya (*Xián*, 賢, 'Worthy'). Within a hundred years, from the Bhadrayānīya emerged one school named Saṃmatīya (*Yīqiè Suǒguì*, 一切所貴, 'Honored by All'). Within a hundred years, from the Saṃmatīya emerged one school named Ṣaṇṇagarika (*Rěngshān*, 芿山). Within a hundred years, from the Ṣaṇṇagarika emerged one school named Mahāvihāravāsin (*Dà Bùkěqì*, 大不可棄, 'Great Indestructible'). Within a hundred years, from the Mahāvihāravāsin emerged one school named Dharmaguptaka (*Fǎhù*, 法護, 'Dharma-Protector'). Within a hundred years, from the Dharmaguptaka emerged one school named Kāśyapīya (*Jiāyèbǐ*, 迦葉比). Within a hundred years, from the Kāśyapīya emerged one school named Sautrāntika (*Xiūdùlù Jù*, 修妬路句, 'Sūtra-Followers'). These are the eleven schools that emerged from the Sthavira school. Together with the Sthavira, they make twelve schools."

The Buddha spoke this gāthā:

> From the Mahāsāṃghika, seven schools emerged in division;
> From the Sthavira, eleven: these are the twenty schools.
> The eighteen plus the two root schools all emerged from the Mahāyāna.
> There is neither right nor wrong; I speak of what shall arise in the future.

> Collected by Dharma Master Kumārajīva.
> After the True Awakened One's nirvāṇa,
> When just over a hundred years had passed,
> Divergent theories arose.
> As the true dharma gradually declined,
> Each gave rise to differing views,
> Establishing separate communities:
> How perilous and fearsome!
> One should give rise to weariness and detachment.
> Now, in the sūtras,
> Examine the Buddha's true teaching.
> Relying on the words of Paramārtha,
> Seek the firm and solid meaning,
> As one seeks true gold
> Amid sand and gravel.
> I have heard from earlier worthies:
> The Tathāgata is the sun among humans.

### Historical Account of the Schisms

One hundred and sixteen years after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, in the city of Pāṭaliputra, King Aśoka (*Āyùwáng*, 阿育王) ruled over Jambudvīpa and governed the world. At that time the great saṃgha divided into separate schools with differing dharmas. There were bhikṣus named (1) Bhadra ('Able'), (2) Pratyaya ('Causal Conditions'), and (3) Bahuśrutīya ('Learned'), who taught five points to instruct sentient beings, namely: that arhats can receive benefit from others, that they can have ignorance, that they can have doubt, that they can be confirmed by another's examination, and that they can attain the path through exclamation. This was the origin of the first two schools: (1) Mahāsāṃghika and (2) Sthavira.

Within these hundred-some years, from the Mahāsāṃghika arose further schools: (1) Ekavyāvahārika ('One Designation'), (2) Lokottaravāda ('Transcendent'), and (3) Kukkuṭika ('Cave-Dwellers'). Within a further hundred years, from the Mahāsāṃghika arose yet another school named Prajñaptivāda (*Shīshè Lùn*, 施設論, 'Designation'). In the second century, the non-Buddhist Mahādeva (*Móhē Típó*, 摩訶提婆) went forth as a monk and resided at Caitya Mountain, where he established three more schools from within the Mahāsāṃghika: (1) Caityaka (*Zhītíjiā*, 支提加), (2) Aparaśaila (*Fópóluó*, 佛婆羅), and (3) Uttaraśaila (*Yùduōluó Shīluó*, 欝多羅施羅).

Thus the Mahāsāṃghika divided into nine schools: (1) Mahāsāṃghika, (2) Ekavyāvahārika, (3) Lokottaravāda, (4) Kukkuṭika, (5) Bahuśrutīya, (6) Prajñaptivāda, (7) Caityaka, (8) Aparaśaila, and (9) Uttaraśaila.

In the third century, from within the Sthavira school, due to doctrinal disputes, a separate school was established: (1) Sarvāstivāda (*Sàpóduō*, 薩婆多), also called the 'Original Sthavira School Based on Reasoning.' (2) Haimavata (*Xuěshān*, 雪山). Within that same century, from the Sarvāstivāda arose the Vātsīputrīya (*Dúzǐ*, 犢子). From the Vātsīputrīya arose four schools: (1) Dharmottarīya (*Dámó Yùduōlí*, 達摩欝多梨), (2) Bhadrayānīya (*Bátuóluó Yēní*, 跋陀羅耶尼), (3) Saṃmatīya (*Mílí*, 彌離, also called Sammitīya), and (4) Ṣaṇṇagarika (*Liùchéng*, 六城, 'Six Cities'). Also within that century, from the Sarvāstivāda arose the Mahīśāsaka (*Míshā*, 彌沙). From the Mahīśāsaka arose another school named after its founding master, the Dharmaguptaka (*Tánwúdé*, 曇無德). Also within the third century, from the Sarvāstivāda arose the Kāśyapīya (*Yōulíshā*, 優梨沙). In the fourth century, from the Sarvāstivāda arose another school due to the great master Uttara, named Sautrāntika (*Sēngjiā Lánduō*, 僧迦蘭多, also called the Sūtra School).

Thus the Sthavira school divided into twelve schools: (1) Sthavira, (2) Haimavata, (3) Sarvāstivāda, (4) Vātsīputrīya, (5) Dharmottarīya, (6) Bhadrayānīya, (7) Saṃmatīya, (8) Ṣaṇṇagarika, (9) Mahīśāsaka, (10) Dharmaguptaka, (11) Kāśyapīya, and (12) Sautrāntika.

### Doctrinal Positions of the Schools

#### Root and Intermediate Views of the Mahāsāṃghika

The Mahāsāṃghika, Ekavyāvahārika, Lokottaravāda, and Kukkuṭika share the following root views: All of the Buddha's World-Honored One's [teachings] are supramundane (*lokottara*). There are no mundane dharmas of the Tathāgata. Everything the Tathāgata teaches is turning the dharma-wheel. He completely teaches all matters, all characteristics, all meanings. The Tathāgata's physical form is limitless. His radiance is immeasurable. His lifespan is immeasurable. When mindfulness, faith, and joy arise in beings, there is no satiation. The Buddha does not sleep. He answers without deliberation. He makes no verbal statements, yet constantly maintains one-pointed mind. Sentient beings, though of various kinds and without number, all hear the Tathāgata's teaching and understand. The Tathāgata, with one mind, knows all dharmas. In a single moment of corresponding wisdom, he awakens to all dharmas. At all times the Tathāgata's knowledge of exhaustion (*kṣayajñāna*) and knowledge of non-arising (*anutpādajñāna*) are perpetually manifest, until nirvāṇa.

Bodhisattvas do not take pleasure in the mother's womb. They descend into the mother's womb in the form of a white elephant. All bodhisattvas are born from the right side. Bodhisattvas have neither thoughts of attachment nor thoughts of anger or harm. For the sake of sentient beings, they vow to be born in the evil destinies, to bring to fulfillment all beings beset by afflictions. All who hear and know and contemplate are born into the noble truths. They affirm that there exists desire, separation from desire, the form realm, and the formless realm. They possess the six consciousnesses. The five sense-organs are pieces of flesh. The eye does not see form, and so forth up to the body not feeling touch. Even within the meditation intervals there is speech. They also discipline the mind and also gather contemplation. All conditioned dharmas have no fixed location. A stream-enterer, counting mental moments, knows by itself that one is an arhat.

There can be benefit from others, ignorance, doubt, confirmation through another's examination, and attainment of the path through exclamation. Through wisdom and skillful means one gains liberation from birth-and-death, and also gains peace. There is regression from the eighth [stage]. There is even regression from those of the lineage dharma. Stream-enterers have regression. Arhats also have regression. There is no mundane right view. There is mundane faith-faculty. There are no determined dharmas. In the leap to the transcendent birth, one does not cut off all fetters at once. A stream-enterer can commit any evil deed except the five heinous crimes. All sūtras rely on definitive meaning (*nītārtha*). There are nine kinds of unconditioned dharmas: analytical cessation (*pratisaṃkhyānirodha*), non-analytical cessation (*apratisaṃkhyānirodha*), space (*ākāśa*), the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, the twelve links of dependent origination, the path factors. The mind-nature is inherently pure. The Buddha is stained by adventitious afflictions. The latent tendencies (*anuśaya*) are not mental and are not mental factors; they are without objects. Tendencies differ from bonds (*paryavasthāna*), and bonds differ from tendencies. The mind is non-associated [with tendencies]. There are no past or future dharmas entering [the present]. It is not cognized by knowledge, nor is it without remedy.

Stream-enterers can attain meditative absorption (*dhyāna*). Such are the root views.

The intermediate views are as follows: Each school observes differently according to its particular standpoint. There is a small amount of self-causation. There is a small amount of other-causation. There is a small amount arising from conditions. At one time two minds can arise simultaneously. The path itself is affliction and karma. The perception-seed itself is grasping. The sense-faculties and four great elements undergo transformation and continuation. They are not mental or mental factors. The mind pervades the entire body. All these are gatherings of desire. These are called the intermediate views.

#### Root Views of the Bahuśrutīya (Learned)

The Buddha taught five kinds of supramundane dharmas: impermanence, suffering, emptiness, non-self, and the quiescent nirvāṇa and the way of liberation. The rest are mundane. Arhats can receive benefit from others. They can have ignorance and doubt. They can be confirmed through another's examination and attain the path through exclamation. In all other respects, their views are the same as the Sarvāstivāda.

#### Root Views of the Prajñaptivāda (Designation)

When one speaks of the aggregates, this is not karma. What has not yet been established as actions: these are designated through mutual interaction. There is no knowing agent or person. There is no untimely death. By the force of original karma, one obtains nourishing karma, and from the root arises all suffering. From karma arises merit; from merit arises the noble path. The path, even if not cultivated, is also not lost. In all other respects, their views are the same as the Mahāsāṃghika.

#### Root Views of the Caityaka, Aparaśaila, and Uttaraśaila

Bodhisattvas are free from the evil destinies. Offerings to stūpas yield no great reward. Arhats can receive benefit from others, can have ignorance and doubt, and can be confirmed through another's examination and attain the path through exclamation. In all other respects, their views are the same as the Mahāsāṃghika.

#### Root Views of the Sarvāstivāda

They hold that all dharmas exist in all three times (*sarvam asti*). All dharmas are subsumed under two categories: name (*nāma*) and form (*rūpa*). There is a path shared with the future. There is dharma-entry and dharma-cognition. Understanding of dharma gives rise to the conditioned characteristics of arising, abiding, and ceasing. There are three unconditioned dharmas. Three truths have conditioned characteristics; one truth has unconditioned characteristics. The Four Noble Truths are comprehended in immediate succession. Emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness: these are the three liberations. In the leap to transcendent birth, one contemplates the bonds of the desire realm and leaps beyond them. In the leap to transcendent birth, the first fifteen mind-moments constitute the path; the sixteenth mind-moment is called "abiding in the fruit." The supreme mundane dharma is a single mind-moment. The first three preparatory stages allow regression; the supreme mundane dharma does not. The fruit of stream-entry is the dharma of non-regression. Arhats can regress. Not all arhats attain the knowledge of non-arising. Ordinary beings can attain separation from desire-realm anger. Non-Buddhists can attain the five supernatural powers. Devas can also cultivate the holy life. Among the seven [factors of awakening], one attains the awakening-factors in the correct meditations, not in the remaining absorptions. Mindfulness-foundations are attained without depending on meditation. In the leap to transcendent birth, one attains arhatship. One can attain arhatship in the form realm but cannot achieve the leap to transcendent birth there. No person in Uttarakuru can attain separation from desire. Nor can they attain the noble path. Nor can beings in the heaven of unconscious beings. One need not necessarily attain the four fruits of the ascetic in sequence through the leap to transcendent birth. Through mundane [paths] one can attain the fruits of once-returner and non-returner. The four foundations of mindfulness [encompass] all dharmas. The latent tendencies are associated with the mind; all tendencies are bound yet are not tendencies [themselves]. Dependent origination is conditioned. They hold that arhats have dependent origination. Arhats have increasing merit. In the desire realm and form realm there is an intermediate existence (*antarābhava*). The five sensory consciousnesses have desire. The five sensory consciousnesses each correspond to their own [objects]. They are not contemplation; they are not mental factors. Mental events are conditioned by their own nature. Own-nature does not correspond [with itself]. The mind is non-associated. There is mundane right view. There is mundane faith-faculty. There are wishless arhats. There are no learner dharmas. All arhats attain meditation, though not necessarily manifesting it. Arhats receive karmic retribution from past lives. Ordinary beings can have unwholesome minds. At the moment of death, one is in right receptivity (*samāpatti*). There is no death in the intermediate state. The bodhisattva is an ordinary being with fetters, who has not yet achieved the leap to transcendent birth, has not yet transcended the ordinary person's stage, and receives embodiment. Sentient beings are designated as all actions that are worn away without [any] dharma going from this world to the next. In conventional terms one says there is going from this world to the next. Before life ends, when the actions of grasping have been exhausted, there is no dharma that transforms. There is supramundane meditation with initial and sustained thought. The uncontaminated is wholesome; this is the cause of meditation. Within meditation there is no speech. The eightfold noble path is the dharma-wheel. Not everything the Tathāgata teaches is turning the dharma-wheel. The Buddha does not teach all matters. Not all his words are in accord with meaning. Not all sūtras are of definitive meaning. There are such innumerable intermediate views as these.

#### Root Views of the Haimavata (Snow Mountain)

The bodhisattva is an ordinary being free from ignorance. He purifies the buddha-field. He descends into the mother's womb. Non-Buddhists do not possess the five supernatural powers. Devas cannot cultivate the holy life. Arhats can receive benefit from others, can have ignorance and doubt, and can be confirmed through another's examination and attain the path through exclamation. In all other respects, their views are the same as the Sarvāstivāda.

#### Root Views of the Vātsīputrīya (Calf's Son)

The person (*pudgala*) is neither identical with [the aggregates] nor apart from the aggregates, elements, and sense-bases; it is designated through their combination. All aggregates are momentary and do not abide. Apart from the person, there is no dharma that goes from this world to the next; one should say it is the person that goes there. Non-Buddhists have the five supernatural powers. The five sensory consciousnesses neither have desire nor are free from desire. The fetters bound to the desire realm are severed through the path of cultivation, not through the path of seeing the truths. The four stages of patient endurance (*kṣānti*), conceptual awareness, the supreme mundane dharma, and the leap to transcendent birth: twelve mind-moments constitute the path, and the thirteenth mind-moment is called "abiding in the fruit." Those who share this view include the Dharmottarīya, Bhadrayānīya, Saṃmatīya, and Ṣaṇṇagarika. Each has particular differing teachings, which are explained with reference to this gāthā:

> They separately attain and teach,
> But then fall back.
> Having fallen, they deeply cling.
> From karma they obtain karma.

#### Root Views of the Mahīśāsaka

There are no past or future worlds; there is only the present and the unconditioned. With respect to the Four Noble Truths, there is a single moment of immediate comprehension; seeing suffering is itself called seeing the truth. The latent tendencies are not mental or associated with mind. They have no object. Tendencies differ from bonds, and bonds differ from tendencies. The mind is non-associated; bonds are associated with the mind. Ordinary beings do not [attain] separation from desire-realm anger. Non-Buddhists do not have the five supernatural powers. Devas cannot cultivate the holy life. There is no intermediate existence. Arhats have no increasing merit. The five sensory consciousnesses have desire and are also free from desire. The six consciousnesses are associated with initial and sustained thought. There is no mundane right view. There is no mundane faith-faculty. There is no supramundane meditation. There are no unconditioned factors beyond the mundane. There is no wholesome cause [for supramundane dharmas]. Stream-enterers can regress. Arhats can also regress. The path-factors are subsumed under the mindfulness-foundations. There are nine unconditioned matters: analytical cessation, non-analytical cessation, space, the wholesome dharma, the unwholesome dharma, the indeterminate dharma, the path, dependent origination, and [the process] from gestation to death. The sense-faculties and great elements transform and cease of themselves. Mental and mental factors also transform and cease of themselves. Offerings given to the saṃgha that includes the Buddha yield great reward; offerings to the Buddha alone [yield] not [as great]. The Buddha and the śrāvakas share one path and one liberation. All actions are momentary. There is no dharma going from this world to the next. Such are the root views.

The intermediate views of this school hold: there are past and future worlds; there is an intermediate existence; dharma-entry and dharma-cognition; volitional karma; there is no bodily or verbal karma; initial and sustained thought are associated with the mind; the great earth abides for a kalpa; offerings to stūpas yield small reward; dharma manifesting in the present is called a tendency; the aggregates, elements, and sense-bases manifest in the present. Dharmas already present as seeds can produce all suffering, namely: ignorance, craving, views, and karma. These are the intermediate views.

#### Root Views of the Dharmaguptaka

The Buddha is not [found] within the saṃgha. Offerings to the Buddha yield great reward, but not to the saṃgha [alone]. The Buddha's path differs from the śrāvaka's path. Non-Buddhists do not have the five supernatural powers. The arhat's body is uncontaminated. In all other respects, their views are the same as the Mahāsāṃghika.

#### Root Views of the Kāśyapīya

There is severing of dharmas and severing of knowledge. There is no dharma that is unsevered while knowledge is severed. Karma ripens and then one receives retribution; unripened karma does not receive retribution. There are past causes and fruits. There are no future causes and fruits. All dharmas are momentary. There are dharmas that are cognized and have retribution. In all other respects, their views are the same as the Dharmaguptaka.

#### Root Views of the Sautrāntika (Sūtra School)

The aggregates go from this world to the next. Apart from the noble path, one cannot attain the cessation of the aggregates. The aggregates have a binding foundation. There is a primary person (*pudgala*). In all other respects, their views are the same as the Sarvāstivāda.

This is a summary account of the views of all the schools.

### Appended Note

This treatise was erroneously duplicated in the Song canon as the *Samayabhedoparacanacakra* (Treatise on the Wheel of Distinguishing Differing Tenets). The Kaiyuan Catalog says: "This *Treatise on the Eighteen Schools* is stated in all catalogs to have been translated by the Tripiṭaka Master Paramārtha of the Liang dynasty. On examination, since Paramārtha had already translated the *Treatise on the Eighteen Schools*, it would not make sense for him to also translate the *Samayabhedoparacanacakra*. The opening of this *Treatise on the Eighteen Schools* cites the Mañjuśrī-paripṛcchā Sūtra's chapter on distinguishing the schools, then states 'Collected by Dharma Master Kumārajīva,' and only afterward presents the treatise proper. If this was Kumārajīva's translation, the Mañjuśrī-paripṛcchā Sūtra did not yet exist in Chinese during the Qin era, so it would not make sense to cite it at the beginning. However, following other catalogs, the Mañjuśrī-paripṛcchā Sūtra was classified as a 'lost translator' text, in which case its presence during the Qin era is not in doubt. If it was Paramārtha's re-translation, the sub-commentaries within the text should not contain the characters 'Qin language.' On examining the text and reasoning, it appears most likely that this was translated by Kumārajīva during the Qin era, and the catalogs simply omitted it, leading to this confusion. Although Paramārtha's *Treatise on the Eighteen Schools Commentary* is in fact the *Samayabhedoparacanacakra Commentary*, and although this reasoning is sound, I dare not pronounce definitively. I leave it to later scholars of broad learning to seek the true record."
