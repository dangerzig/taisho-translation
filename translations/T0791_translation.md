# 佛說出家緣經
## Sūtra on the Conditions of Leaving Home, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0791

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavān was in Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, one thousand two hundred and fifty. At that time the upāsaka Nandika, together with five hundred upāsakas, left the city of Rājagṛha and ascended Vulture Peak to visit the Buddha. They bowed their heads at the Buddha's feet and knelt with palms joined to ask the Buddha: "Great Virtuous One! We wish to hear about the five precepts of an upāsaka. What evils befall one who damages and violates the right precepts?"

The Buddha answered: "Upāsaka Nandika! Killing living beings brings ten evils. What are these ten? In the present life one constantly harbors a harmful mind. In future lives the poisonous mind blazes forth, and one is constantly subjected to painful harm. Enmity never ceases. People do not like to see one. One's thoughts are mostly evil. Those who see one are filled with dread. One's sleep is not peaceful. One's dreams are filled with terror. Upon waking one is startled and alarmed. At the time of death one is frenzied and confused. One plants the seeds of a short lifespan. When the body perishes and life ends, one is born in the hells. Even if one is reborn as a human, one will have much illness and a short life. These are the ten evils.

"Stealing others' property brings ten evils. What are these ten? One's greed and rapacity are deeply entrenched. One is constantly suspected by all. One is capable of committing grave evils. One acts at the wrong times. One's movements violate propriety. Friends and kin become hostile, and the worthy and good become distant. One boldly breaks the precepts. One constantly fears the king's pursuit and must purchase one's life with wealth. One plants the karma of losing possessions. One's lot is poverty and privation. When the body perishes and life ends, one is born in the hells. Even if one is reborn as a human, hunger and hardship are constant, and acquiring wealth is exceedingly difficult. Even if one obtains a little wealth, five things lay claim to it: the king, thieves, water, fire, and an evil wife or children. What one hides and buries will also eventually be lost. These are the ten evils.

"Sexual misconduct brings ten evils. What are these ten? One's spouse constantly seeks to catch and punish one with cruel torment. The household is in discord. Wholesome dharmas are exhausted and unwholesome dharmas increase. One endangers one's own body. One does not watch over one's wife or guard one's possessions. One is constantly suspected by others. Relatives and kin do not trust one. Such are the karmic seeds one plants. One's household lacks purity. When the body perishes and life ends, one is born in the hells. If reborn as a woman, one will not be faithful to a single partner. If reborn as a man, one will pursue misconduct and lose one's own spouse. These are the ten evils.

"False speech brings ten evils. What are these ten? One's mouth is constantly foul and putrid. Good spirits turn away and evil ghosts encroach. Though one speaks truthfully in the world, one is not believed by all. One is excluded from important matters and not included in deliberation. Though one has committed no actual offense, evil reputation spreads far. One is scorned and slighted, with no one rising in respect. Though one's words may contain truth, people do not heed them. One is burdened with much anxiety and dread. One plants the karma of slander. When the body perishes and life ends, one is born in the hells. Even if one is reborn as a human, one is constantly slandered. These are the ten evils.

"Drinking alcohol brings thirty-five evils. What are these thirty-five? One squanders all one's wealth. One invites numerous painful afflictions. Quarrels and disputes grow severe. One exposes one's naked body. Evil reputation spreads far and near. Wisdom and clarity diminish daily. What should be gained is not gained. What has been gained is lost. One reveals one's evil deeds. Urgent matters suddenly become sources of sorrow and distress. One becomes confused and disoriented. One's appearance becomes base and ugly. One shows disrespect to elders. One does not know to make offerings to śramaṇas and brahmins. Within one's own household, one does not distinguish proper rank. One does not honor the Buddha. One does not revere the great Dharma. One does not respectfully serve the Saṃgha. Instead one befriends evil people. One distances oneself from the wise and capable. One falls into the wrong path. One has no sense of shame or conscience. One does not guard the sense-gates. One is dull and devoted to lust. One is unloved by all. People do not like to see one. Virtuous elders and experienced ones all come to reproach one. One accumulates and creates various evils. Essential power and influence are lost, and trusted responsibilities are not kept. The wise and virtuous avoid one. One cannot distinguish among types. One is far from nirvāṇa. One plants the karma of madness and delusion. When the body perishes and life ends, one is born in the hells. Even if one is reborn as a human, one will be foolish, dull, and stupid."

At that time the upāsaka Nandika and the five hundred faithful laymen, together with the devas, worldly people, and the assembly of the four groups, hearing what the Buddha had taught, vowed to receive and uphold it for the rest of their lives. They bowed their heads, circumambulated the Buddha, and departed leaping with joy.
