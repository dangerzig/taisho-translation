# 提婆菩薩傳
## Biography of Bodhisattva Āryadeva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2048

Translated from the Chinese. Later Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什).

---

Bodhisattva Deva was a person of South India, a disciple of Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna, of the brāhmaṇa caste. He was broadly learned and deeply perceptive, with eloquence and talent beyond compare. His fame was preeminent throughout India, and he was esteemed by all the kingdoms. Having plumbed the depths of his understanding, he had nothing to be ashamed of. His only concern was that people did not believe and accept his words.

In his country there was a great celestial deity. A golden image had been cast for its seat, its body two zhang tall, called "Great Self-Existent Deva." People who sought blessings from it could have their worldly wishes fulfilled.

Deva went to the temple and sought entry to bow before it. The temple-keeper said: "The divine image is supremely powerful. When people see it, they dare not look directly at it. Moreover, it causes people to retreat and lose their composure for a hundred days. You may simply go and make your request; why must you see it?"

Deva said: "If the deity can truly do as you say, then by all means let me see it. If it is not so, then it is not what I wish to see."

The people marveled at his resolve and were overawed by his forthright character. Several tens of thousands followed him into the temple. When Deva entered the temple, the divine image shook and its eyes glared at him angrily.

Deva addressed the deity: "A divine being is divine indeed, but why is it so petty? One should move people through spiritual power and subdue them with wisdom and virtue. To rely on gold to aggrandize oneself and to dazzle with crystal to delude is not what one hopes for." He then climbed a ladder and gouged out the image's eyes.

At that time, all the onlookers had doubts: "How could the Great Self-Existent Deva be overcome by a mere young brāhmaṇa? Perhaps his reputation exceeds the reality, and he has been bested in argument."

Deva addressed the crowd: "The divine spirit is vast and great. Therefore it tested me through this physical matter. I grasped its heart, and so I climbed the golden mass and extracted the crystal, to let you all know that the spirit does not depend on material form, and the essence does not lodge in a body. I have not slighted the deity, nor has it been disgraced."

Having said this, he departed. That very night, he sought various offerings and preparations. The next morning at dawn, he reverently worshipped the celestial deity.

Since Deva's reputation was already great, and his wisdom now joined with the divine compact, whatever he spoke, wherever his voice reached, there was none that did not respond. In a single night, the offering provisions and refined delicacies were complete, with every item fully prepared.

The Great Self-Existent Deva then appeared, in a fleshy form several zhang in height, with the left eye withered and dried, and took a seat. He surveyed all the offerings and sighed that they were unprecedented. Praising Deva's power and virtue that could accomplish such things, he told him: "You have grasped my heart. Others grasp my form. You make offerings with the heart; others make gifts with material things. The one who knows me and reveres me is you. The one who fears me and deceives me is those others. Your offerings are perfect in every way. The only thing you have not provided is what I most need. Can you give it to me? That would be the supreme offering."

Deva said: "The deity has discerned my heart. I obey your command."

The deity said: "What I lack is a left eye. Can you give it to me?"

Deva said: "Respectfully, as the deity commands." He then, with his left hand, removed his eye and gave it. Through the deity's power, as soon as it was removed, it grew back. The deity continued to request it without ceasing. From dawn through the entire morning, Deva removed eyes numbering in the tens of thousands.

The deity praised him: "Excellent, excellent, young man! This is truly the supreme offering. What wish do you seek? It shall be as you desire."

Deva said: "I receive my understanding from within my own mind; I do not rely on externals. My only regret is that the ignorant multitude does not believe and accept my words. If the deity grants my wish, may my words never be spoken in vain. This alone is my request; I need nothing else."

The deity said: "It shall be as you wish." With that, Deva withdrew.

He then went to Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna, received the precepts of renunciation, shaved his head, donned the Dharma-robes, and traveled widely, teaching and transforming.

The king of South India ruled over many kingdoms and believed in heterodox teachings. No Buddhist monks or disciples of the Śākya were allowed to see him. The people of the country, near and far, all followed his way.

Deva thought to himself: "If the root of a tree is not felled, the branches will not fall. If the ruler is not converted, the Way will not spread."

The laws of that kingdom were such that the royal house paid wages to hire people for guard duty. Deva then enlisted as a soldier. Bearing a halberd in the vanguard, he marshaled the ranks and commanded the units. His authority was not stern, yet his orders were obeyed. His virtue was not displayed, yet all delighted to follow him.

The king was greatly pleased and asked: "Who is this person?" His attendants answered: "This person enlisted voluntarily. He neither accepts provisions nor takes wages. Yet in his duties he is so respectful, diligent, and skilled. We do not know what he seeks or desires."

The king summoned him and asked: "Who are you?"

He answered: "I am one who knows all things."

The king was greatly astonished and said: "A person who knows all things appears only once in vast ages. You claim to be one. How can it be verified?"

He answered: "If you wish to know my wisdom, it lies in discourse. Your Majesty should put questions to me."

The king then thought to himself: "I am the lord of wisdom, a great master of debate. If I question him and he is defeated, that would not be enough for fame. But if I am outdone even once, that is no small matter. Yet if I do not ask, that itself is already a defeat." He hesitated for a long time. Having no choice, he asked: "What is heaven doing right now?"

Deva answered: "Heaven is at this moment battling the asuras."

The king, upon hearing this, was like a person choking: he could neither spit it out nor swallow it. He wished to deny the words but had no proof. He wished to accept the claim but had no way to verify it.

Before he could speak again, Deva said: "This is not empty talk seeking victory. Your Majesty, wait just a moment, and there will be evidence."

As soon as he finished speaking, weapons fell from the sky: long halberds and short blades cascading down one after another.

The king said: "Weapons and halberds are indeed implements of war. But how do you know for certain that it is heaven battling the asuras?"

Deva said: "Rather than constructing empty claims, let us verify with actual facts." As he spoke, the fingers of the asuras, together with their ears and noses, fell from the sky.

The king then bowed his head and submitted to his Dharma-transformation. Ten thousand brāhmaṇas who had been in the hall all discarded their topknots and received the full precepts.

At that time, Deva established a high seat in the royal capital and proclaimed three propositions: "Among all sages, the Buddha-sage is foremost. Among all dharmas, the Buddha-dharma is the most correct. Among all those who save the world, the Buddha-saṃgha is foremost. If any debater from the eight directions can refute these words, I shall cut off my own head in apology for my error. Why? To establish a principle unclearly is stupidity. A stupid head is of no use to me. To sever it in apology is something I do not begrudge."

When the debaters of the eight directions heard this, they each came to assemble and made their own oaths: "If we are defeated, we too shall sever our heads. A stupid head is something we too do not begrudge."

Deva said: "The Dharma I cultivate is benevolent and preserves all life. If you are defeated, I shall merely shave your beards and hair and make you my disciples. There is no need for beheading."

Having established these terms, each side composed their finest arguments and established unbounded theses. In the exchange, those of shallow wisdom and meager argument were defeated with a single word. Those of deep wisdom and extended argument lasted at most two days before their reasoning and arguments were exhausted. All then had their hair shaved. Thus, day after day, the royal household daily sent ten cartloads of robes and bowls. After three months in all, over a million people had been converted.

There was one disciple of the heterodox path, fierce and brutish, lacking wisdom. Ashamed of his master's defeat, though he outwardly followed the assembly, in his heart he harbored resentment. He gnawed a blade and swore to himself: "You conquered my master with your mouth. I shall conquer you with my blade. You used an empty blade to overcome me; I shall use a real blade to overcome you."

Having made this oath, he concealed a sharp knife and watched for his opportunity.

The outstanding debaters from all directions having been exhausted, Deva then withdrew to a quiet grove and composed the *Śataśāstra* in twenty chapters, and further composed the *Catuḥśataka* to refute heterodox views.

His various disciples scattered among the trees, sitting in meditation and contemplation. Deva emerged from meditation and was walking. The brāhmaṇa's disciple came to his side, held a knife against him, and said: "You used your mouth to defeat my master. Now see how I use my blade to defeat your belly!" He then slashed open his abdomen and his organs spilled to the ground.

Before he expired, Deva, having compassion for this foolish criminal, told him: "I have three robes and a bowl at my seat. You may take them. Quickly go up the mountain. Be careful not to descend to the level path. My disciples who have not yet attained patience with the Dharma will certainly catch you, and they may hand you over to the authorities. The king will then punish you."

"You have not yet attained the benefit of the Dharma. You cherish your body most, and your reputation next. From body and reputation, afflictions arise. From them, all misfortune is born. Body and reputation are the root of great affliction. Foolish people who have not heard the Dharma are invaded by false views. They cherish what should not be cherished and do not cherish what should be cherished. Is this not pitiable?"

"I have received the legacy of the Buddha's Dharma and am no longer like that. But I think of you and your kind, deceived by the mad mind, burned by the poison of rage, with the karmic retribution not yet ended."

"He who wails and receives this, in truth there is no master of it. He who performs this, in truth there is no person performing it. Without a person, without a master: who is it that suffers the cruelty? If one seeks it with truth, truly it cannot be obtained. Those who have not yet awakened to this are deluded by the mad mind, turned around by inversion. They see gain and the mind clings, and so there is 'I' and 'other,' 'suffering' and 'happiness.' The coming of suffering and happiness depends entirely on attachment. When attachment is released, there is nothing to depend on. Without dependence, there is no suffering. Without suffering, there is no happiness. When suffering and happiness are both no more, one is nearly at rest."

After speaking these words, his disciples who arrived first cried out. His followers each gathered from among the groves and trees. Those who had not yet attained patience with the Dharma were terrified and wailed, beating their chests and striking the ground: "Injustice! Cruelty! Who has taken our master in such a way?"

Some ran wildly, rushing to cut off the roads, dividing into groups, shouting and chasing, their cries echoing through the deep valleys.

Deva admonished his followers: "In the truth of all dharmas, who is the victim, who is the tormentor? Who cuts, who severs? In the truth of all dharmas, truly there is no one who receives and no one who harms. Who is kin, who is foe? Who is the criminal, who causes the harm? You are deceived by the poison of delusion. You falsely give rise to views of attachment and wail greatly, planting unwholesome karma. What that person harmed was the retribution of past karma, not me. Consider this carefully. Do not pursue madness with madness, or grieve over grief."

With that, he released his body, utterly without self-regard, and shed it like a cicada leaving its shell.

Because he had first given his eye to the deity, he was thereafter without one eye. People of the time called him Kāṇadeva.
