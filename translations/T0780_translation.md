# 佛說十力經
## Sūtra on the Ten Powers

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0780

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Wùtítíxīyú (勿提提犀魚).

---

### Record of the Newly Translated Sūtras Including the Daśabhūmika in the Zhenyuan Era of the Great Tang

The newly translated *Daśabhūmika Sūtra*, *Parivarta Sūtra*, and *Daśabala Sūtra* were obtained in the kingdom of Central India by the śramaṇa Wùkōng, originally named Fǎjiè, of the Zhāngjìng Monastery in the upper capital. He had been sent on a mission to Kapiśa.

The master was originally from Yúnyáng in the capital region of Jīngzhào, from the village of Qīnglóng and the hamlet of Xiàngyì. His lay surname was Chē, his given name Fèngcháo, and he was a descendant of the Tuòbá clan of the Later Wei. Blessed by heaven with intelligence, he aspired to the classics. Filial and brotherly at home, loyal and upright in serving the state. He encountered

Emperor Xuanzong, the Supreme, Greatly Sagely, Greatly Luminous, and Filially Pious Emperor, who ruled the realm through filial governance. All nations rejoiced, the eight frontiers submitted, and the four barbarian peoples admired his transforming influence. At that time, the kingdom of Kapiśa wished to become a vassal of

the Sagely Tang. They sent their chief envoy Sàbōdágān, together with the country's Tripiṭaka master Śarīpramokṣa, and in the ninth year of the Tianbao era, the gengyin year, they came to the imperial court to present tribute and seek peace, requesting an envoy for inspection. The following year, the xinmao year,

Emperor Xuanzong issued a decree commissioning the inner court eunuch, Palace Attendant of the Inner Bureau, granted the Crimson Fish-pouch, named Zhāng Tāoguāng, with state gifts and over forty officials and attendants. Through imperial grace, Fèngcháo was appointed as Commandant Extraordinary of the Zuǒwèi Jīngzhōu Fourth Gate Command, ordered to accompany the envoy via the Ānxī route. They passed through the kingdom of Shūlè, crossed the Onion Mountains, reached the Yángxīng Ridge and Bōmì River and the kingdom of Wǔchìnì. Then the kingdom of Hùmì, the kingdom of Qūwěi, the kingdom of Gělán, the kingdom of Lánpó, the kingdom of Nièhé, the kingdom of Wūzhàngná, the kingdom of Mánggébó, and the city of Gāotóu. Then the kingdom of Módàn, then the city of Xìndù. On the twenty-first day of the second month of the twelfth year, the guisi year, they arrived in the kingdom of Gandhāra. This was the eastern capital of Kapiśa. The king wintered there and summered in Kapiśa, following the warm and cool seasons.

The king received them with the utmost courtesy and honor, reverently acknowledging the imperial grace. When the envoy returned with reply gifts and tribute, preparing to go back to the Great Tang, Fèngcháo was afflicted with a serious and lingering illness and could not bear the journey. He was left behind in the kingdom of Gandhāra. After the envoy returned to court, his health gradually recovered. He vowed in his heart to take refuge in the Buddha and so went to the Tripiṭaka master Śarīpramokṣa, had his head shaved and donned the dark robes. He wished to return home soon, to meet the enlightened sovereign and attend upon his parents, fulfilling both loyalty and filial piety.

The Tripiṭaka master bestowed upon him a Dharma name: in Sanskrit, Dharmadātu, which translates into Chinese as Fǎjiè. He was twenty-seven years old when he left home, which was the second year of the Zhide era, the dingyu year, under Emperor Suzong, the Civilly Brilliant, Martially Virtuous, Greatly Sagely, and Greatly Proclaimed Filially Pious Emperor. At twenty-nine, in the kingdom of Kaśmīra, he received full ordination. He requested Mañjuśrīkṣānti as his upādhyāya, Ubhayakṣānti as his karmācārya, and Dhārivedi as his instructing ācārya. With three preceptors and seven witnesses, he was conferred the monastic precepts. At the Méngdī Monastery he recited the śrāvaka precepts. After reciting them, he studied the fundamental vinaya. In northern India, they all followed the Sarvāstivāda school. This Méngdī Monastery was built by the king of northern India after his accession. In Sanskrit it is Méngdīvihāra; vihāra means "dwelling place," and dwelling place means monastery. Next was the Amitābhavān Monastery, then the Ānanda Monastery, then the Jìzhěcén Monastery, the Nǎoyéluó Monastery, the Rěrě Monastery, the General's Monastery, and the Yělǐtèlè Monastery, established by a Turkish prince. Next was the Kèdūn Monastery, established by a Turkish empress.

This kingdom had over three hundred monasteries, with numerous sacred stūpas and miraculous images, some established by King Aśoka and the five hundred arhats. He made pilgrimages to all of them while also studying Sanskrit, traveling for four years, morning and night devoted and never slackening. The kingdom was surrounded by mountains as outer walls, with three roads and checkpoints opened. To the east it bordered Tibet, to the north connected with Bólǜ, and the west gate had one road leading to Gandhāra. There was also a separate path normally closed, opened only when the heavenly army passed through.

In his fourth year, Fǎjiè left Kaśmīra and entered the city of Gandhāra, where he was housed at the Rúluóshā King Monastery. This monastery was built by the king and named after him; the king was a descendant of the ancient Kaniṣka. Next was the Kěhūlǐ Monastery, named after a prince, the Bīnzhī Monastery named after a princess, and the Zhāntán-hūlǐ Monastery named after the king's younger brother. These were all named after their founders. Next was the Tèqín-shā Monastery, built by a Turkish prince, and the Kèdūn Monastery, built by a Turkish empress. There were also the Āsètuō Monastery, the Sàjǐn-hūlǐ Monastery, the Kaniṣka King Holy Stūpa Monastery, and the Kaniṣka King Yǎntíshā Monastery, which also contained a skull-bone relic of the Buddha Śākyamuni. There was also the monastery of the novice who subdued the nāga at the Kaniṣka King's Dragon Palace.

After two more years of pilgrimage, in the second year of the Guangde era under Emperor Daizong, the jiachen year, he traveled south to Central India to pay personal homage to the eight stūpas. He went to Kapilavastu, to the stūpa at the place of the Buddha's birth. Then to the kingdom of Magadha, to the stūpa at the Bodhi seat of awakening, where he spent the summer retreat at the Bodhi monastery. Then to the stūpa at the Deer Park of the Sages in Vārāṇasī, where the wheel of the Dharma was turned. Then to the stūpa on Vulture Peak where the Lotus Sūtra and other sūtras were taught. Then to the stūpa at Vaiśālī where inconceivable things were manifested. Then to the stūpa at Sāṃkāśya with the triple jeweled stairway where the Buddha descended from heaven. Then to the stūpa at the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park, in Śrāvastī, where the Mahāprajñāpāramitā was taught and the heterodox were liberated. Then to the stūpa at the Śāla grove in Kuśinagara where the parinirvāṇa was entered. He circumambulated and paid homage to all eight stūpas.

He then stayed at Nālandā Monastery for three years. He also visited the kingdom of Uḍḍiyāna to pay homage to sacred sites, staying at the Mánggébó Monastery. There was also the Sukhāvatī Monastery and the Padmāvatī Monastery. He traveled back and forth visiting all the sacred sites, which were consistent with what is described in the *Record of the Western Regions of the Great Tang*. Longing for the sagely court, his birth parents, and relatives near and far burned in his heart. Thinking of the deep kindness of his nurturing, boundless as heaven, he vowed to return and reverently attend upon

his sovereign and parents. He respectfully consulted the Tripiṭaka master Śarīpramokṣa. The Tripiṭaka master initially did not approve. Fǎjiè earnestly and repeatedly pled his case. The Tripiṭaka master, who had visited the Tang court in the ninth year of Tianbao and constantly praised Mahācīna, finally granted the request upon seeing such sincerity. He personally handed over the Sanskrit manuscripts of the *Daśabhūmika Sūtra*, the *Parivarta Sūtra*, and the *Daśabala Sūtra*, all bound in a single case, together with one tooth relic of the great sage Buddha Śākyamuni. All were presented with the utmost earnest reverence and tearful devotion, to serve as gifts for the sagely emperor, with the prayer that they would be transmitted and propagated in China for the broad benefit of all beings. Fǎjiè knelt and received them with tears of gratitude, bowed farewell, and intended to return by sea. But fearing the perils of the ocean waves, he instead took the northern route home to the imperial capital.

Our sagely, divinely, civilly, and martially gifted emperor's sagely virtue reached afar, his renown shaking the five regions of India. His Way surpassed the ancient sovereigns, his authority extended to the eight quarters. He revered and believed in the Three Treasures and honored the One Vehicle. Foreign lands offered gold and presented tribute anew. The relics and Sanskrit manuscripts that Fǎjiè brought from Central India to China, through all the kingdoms he passed, among the fifty-seven districts of Tokharistan, there was one city called Gǔduō. Not far east of the city was a small sea of extremely deep water. He was carrying the tooth and sūtras along the southern bank. At that time, the nāga spirit, knowing there was a relic, caused the earth to shake, dark clouds arose, thunder crashed, and hail fell in torrents. There was a large tree not far from the sea, and the group of merchants took shelter beneath it, but its branches and leaves were torn off and the hollow trunk caught fire. The chief merchant announced to all: "Who is carrying relics or extraordinary treasures? If not, why would the nāga spirit be so enraged? If someone has them, bring them out and throw them into the sea, lest the rest of us endure this terror!" At that time, Fǎjiè prayed earnestly that they reach their homeland to benefit the nation, and that the merit obtained would be dedicated to the nāga's power. From sunrise until the hour of the monkey, his prayer was so sincere that the clouds dispersed and rain ceased, and their lives were barely spared. Proceeding gradually, they reached the kingdom of Qūmìzhī, whose king was named Dùnsàn-shā. Then the kingdom of Rěsèzhī, whose king was named Hēiwèiméi. Then to the kingdom of Shìnì. Three years of such traveling, enduring extreme hardships, offering body and life with a vow to serve the nation and honor sovereign and parents with sagely compassion. Gradually reaching Shūlè, whose king was Péilěnglěng and garrison commander was Lǔyáng, he stayed five months. Then to Khotan, in Sanskrit Gostana, whose king was Wèichí Yào and garrison commander was Zhèng Jù, staying six months. Then to the Wēiróng fortress also called the kingdom of Bōhuàn, correctly called Bùwū, with garrison commander Sū Cén. Then to the fortress of Jùsèdé with officer Jiǎ Quán. Then to Ānxī, where the Military Governor of the Four Garrisons was the Grand General Guō Xīn, and the King of Kuchā was Bái Huán, in the city properly called Qūzhī. Outside the west gate was the Lotus Monastery, where a Tripiṭaka śramaṇa named Wùtítíxīyú earnestly translated the *Daśabala Sūtra*, approximately three pages, forming one fascicle. The Tripiṭaka master was fluent in the languages of all four garrisons, versed in both Sanskrit and Chinese. This *Sūtra on the Ten Powers* was taught by the Buddha at Śrāvastī.

Within the territory of Ānxī was the Qiánjiàn Mountain, the Qiánjiàn Monastery, and the Yēpósèjī Mountain, where water dripping and producing tones was collected once a year to make yeast. There was also the Yēpósèjī Monastery, and the East-West Turkish Monasteries, and the Āchēlǐ-èr Monastery. He stayed in this city for over a year. Then to the kingdom of Wūqí, whose king was Lóng Rúlín and garrison commander was Yáng Rìyòu, staying three months. From there he set out for the northern garrison of Bēitíng, where the route's Military Governor was Yáng Xígǔ. Together with the monks of the Lóngxīng Monastery, they invited the Khotanese Tripiṭaka śramaṇa Śīladharma to translate the *Daśabhūmika Sūtra*. The Tripiṭaka master read the Sanskrit text and served as interpreter; the śramaṇa Dàzhèn served as scribe; the śramaṇa Fǎchāo polished the text; the śramaṇa Shànxìn verified the meaning; and the śramaṇa Fǎjiè verified the Sanskrit text and interpretation. The *Parivarta Sūtra* was translated in the same manner.

When the translation was complete and the copying nearly finished, the court's envoy to the Four Garrisons and Bēitíng, the inner court eunuch Duàn Míngxiù, arrived at Bēitíng. On the thirteenth day of the ninth month of the fifth year of the Zhenyuan era, the jisi year, together with the route's reporting officer Niú Xīn and the Ānxī route's reporting officer Chéng È, they accompanied the envoy back to court. Because the Shā River route was impassable, they took the Uyghur route. Because the Uyghur khan did not believe in Buddhism, Fǎjiè did not dare bring the Sanskrit manuscripts, which he left at the Lóngxīng Monastery in Bēitíng. The Chinese translations accompanied the envoy to the capital.

In the second month of the sixth year, they arrived at the upper capital. By imperial decree, they were housed at the Yuèlóngmén embassy. The inner court eunuch Duàn Míngxiù then presented the Buddha Śākyamuni's authentic tooth relic and the translated sūtras to the inner court. By imperial grace, they were entrusted to the Left Shénce Army. The order was given to copy the sūtra texts, which were presented together with the Buddha-tooth relic.

The Merit Commissioner of the Left Street, Dòu Wénchǎng, had the copies prepared and presented to the court as decreed, and memorialized: "The monk Wùkōng from Ānxī, unnamed and sixty years old, originally named Fǎjiè, lay surname Chē, given name Fèngcháo, requests residence at the Zhāngjìng Monastery." On the twenty-fifth day of the second month of that year, an imperial decree was issued granting him proper ordination, with the rest as requested. The route's reporting officer also memorialized under the lay name Chē Fèngcháo. On the fifteenth day of the fifth month, an imperial decree appointed him Robust and Martial General, Grand General of the Left Golden-Crow Guard Extraordinary, concurrently Acting Grand Master of Ceremonies. There was a proclamation: "The reporting officer Niú Xīn and others of the Yīxī and Bēitíng garrisons, crossing from the Flowing Sands through the dark lands, bearing the three armies' aspiration of loyalty, expressing the sincerity of longing for the court from ten thousand leagues, through rain and snow without ceasing their march, having presented tribute and fulfilled their mission, returning with their reports: their toil surpasses Fàn Qiāng's entry to court, and their endurance consoles Bān Chāo's longing from afar. They are elevated in rank to encourage future envoys." In that year, the imperial grace granted him a proper name and official insignia. Formerly named Fǎjiè, he was now styled Wùkōng. Bearing this honor with awe and trepidation, he returned to the Zhāngjìng Monastery and then to his native village. Inquiring after his parents, he found their graves already overgrown with trees. Not one of his brothers, sons, or nephews remained in the household. Among the distant branches of the family, few could be found. In all, his travels had spanned forty years.

From the xinmao western expedition to the present gengwu year, he grieves at not having been able to provide for his parents but rejoices at meeting an enlightened age. The tooth and sūtras presented are dedicated to the sagely lifespan. The *Daśabhūmika Sūtra* presented was copied in one hundred twenty-one pages, organized into nine fascicles. This sūtra was taught by the Buddha, who, having first attained perfect awakening, after two weeks, abided in the palace of the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven in the Hall of the Maṇi Treasury. The *Parivarta Sūtra* was taught by the Buddha at the peak of the Vajra Maṇi Jewel Mountain to the great bodhisattvas, translated into three and a half pages forming one fascicle. The *Daśabala Sūtra*, as described above, was translated into three pages forming one fascicle. The three works total one hundred twenty-nine pages in eleven fascicles, bound as one set. Since these sūtras have not yet been entered into the catalog, and fearing that with the passage of years people may doubt them to be spurious, the request is now made to incorporate them into the Great Tang Zhenyuan *Continuation of the Kaiyuan Buddhist Canon Catalog*. From the time of leaving the Sagely Tang through four reigns, enduring frost and snow for forty years, visiting sacred sites and traveling through kingdoms and cities, sometimes making a single visit, sometimes staying ten days, sometimes staying for months, sometimes dwelling one, two, three, or four years, sometimes meeting with auspicious fortune and sometimes encountering bandits, with times of ease being few and times of worry being many, the heart cannot express it all nor can it be recounted in detail. Fortunate to meet an enlightened sage, this outline of the main points is humbly submitted, with the prayer that it be put into effect and transmitted for all ages.

The śramaṇa Yuánzhào, acknowledging his own humble deficiencies and lack of skill, rejoices at meeting an enlightened age and participating again in translation work, continuing the *Illustrated Record* in praise of the True Vehicle, and compiling the Great Tang Zhenyuan *Continuation of the Kaiyuan Buddhist Canon Catalog*. The Reverend Wùkōng has provided a detailed account of his journey, entrusting me to record it and append it to the illustrated catalog to verify the events. Over many years, questioning the origins and respectfully receiving his oral accounts, though the words are rough and the ideas clumsy, I have arranged them in order. May those of great elegance and broad learning examine his record in detail.

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's park.

At that time the World-Honored One addressed the monks: "You should know that all buddhas, tathāgatas, worthy ones, fully and perfectly awakened, are endowed with the ten powers. Because they possess the ten powers, they are called tathāgatas, worthy ones, fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. They are able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly they give the true lion's roar. What are the ten?

"The tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to what is the case, truly knows it to be the case; with regard to what is not the case, truly knows it to be not the case. All is known as it truly is. Because the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened truly knows what is the case and what is not the case, this is called the first power: the power of knowing what is the case and what is not the case. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to the past, future, and present actions of all sentient beings, and the ripening of dharmas through mutual contact: all is known as it truly is. Because the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened truly knows the past, future, and present actions of all sentient beings and the ripening of dharmas through mutual contact, this is called the second power: the power of knowing karmic ripening. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to all sentient beings, all dhyānas, liberations, and samādhis, whether defiled or pure, the liberation that is pure, and the distinctions of their establishment and domains: all is known as it truly is. This is called the third power: the power of knowing dhyāna, liberation, and samādhi. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to the superiority and inferiority of the faculties of all sentient beings: all is known as it truly is. This is called the fourth power: the power of knowing the superiority and inferiority of faculties. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to the various desires, inclinations, and resolute understandings of all sentient beings, and the distinctions of their domains: all is known as it truly is. This is called the fifth power: the power of knowing the various desires, inclinations, and resolute understandings. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to all sentient beings in all worlds, the diverse and various elements, natures, and domain distinctions: all is known as it truly is. This is called the sixth power: the power of knowing the various elements. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to the paths that lead to all destinations for all sentient beings: all is known as it truly is. This is called the seventh power: the power of knowing all paths leading to all destinations. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to the past innumerable former abodes of all sentient beings, whether one life or up to ten lives, twenty lives or up to a hundred lives, a thousand lives, or innumerable lives, one cycle of increase and decrease or up to hundreds of thousands of innumerable cycles of increase and decrease: all is known as it truly is. The various classes of sentient beings, with their various names, such-and-such places, such-and-such clans, such-and-such food and drink, such-and-such suffering and happiness, such-and-such lifespan, long or short; dying there and being reborn there, dying here and being reborn here; and all the formations, consciousnesses, and perceptions of those former abodes: because all is known as it truly is, this is called the eighth power: the power of knowing former abodes. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with the pure divine eye surpassing that of humans, sees sentient beings at the time of death and at the time of rebirth, their good and evil appearances, their superior and inferior conduct, and whether by practicing good or evil they receive good or evil: all is known as it truly is. He further sees sentient beings who engage in evil bodily actions and evil actions of speech and mind, who slander the noble sages and generate great wrong views, who perform actions of wrong view, and who, because of these karmic conditions, upon the dissolution of the body at death fall into the three evil destinies. He further sees sentient beings who engage in good bodily actions and good actions of speech and mind, who do not slander the noble sages, who do not destroy right faith, whose minds hold right view, who perform actions of right view, and who, because of these karmic conditions, upon the dissolution of the body at death are reborn in heaven. Because all is known as it truly is, this is called the ninth power: the power of knowing death and rebirth. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar.

"Furthermore, the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened, with regard to the cessation of all outflows, through dhyāna, liberation of mind, and liberation through wisdom, personally realizes and abides in penetrating wisdom: 'My births are exhausted, the pure conduct has been established, what was to be done has been done, I will not receive another existence.' All is known as it truly is. Because the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened personally realizes and abides in the cessation of outflows through dhyāna, liberation of mind, and liberation through wisdom, and penetrating wisdom, knowing as it truly is that 'My births are exhausted, the pure conduct has been established, what was to be done has been done, I will not receive another existence,' this is called the tenth power: the power of knowing the exhaustion of outflows. Possessing this power, one is called the tathāgata, the worthy one, the fully and perfectly awakened: supremely honored, uniquely distinguished, heroically mighty, and sovereign. One is able to turn the supreme, pure Brahma-wheel, and in the midst of the great assembly one gives the true lion's roar."

At that time, when the World-Honored One had finished teaching this sūtra, the assembly of monks, the devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, and all others greatly rejoiced, believed, received, and practiced it.
