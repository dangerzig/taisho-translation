# 佛說鴦崛髻經
## Sūtra of Aṅgulimālya Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0119

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmaruci (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, many bhikṣus, when the time came, put on their robes and took up their bowls, and entered the city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. At that time, the many bhikṣus entering the city of Śrāvastī to beg for food heard outside the gate of King Prasenajit's palace a great crowd of people, each holding the others' hands and weeping aloud, who said: "In this country there is a great evil bandit named Aṅgulimālya who kills and harms the people with violence and cruelty, without a compassionate heart. Villages and settlements have no peace, cities and fortresses have no peace, and the people have no peace. He kills people and takes one finger from each to make a flower garland. For this reason he is called Aṅgulimālya. We beseech the king to subdue this man!"

At that time, the many bhikṣus, having begged for food in the city of Śrāvastī, after the meal put away their robes and bowls, washed their hands and feet, placed their sitting cloths over their shoulders, and went to where the World-Honored One was. They bowed their heads at his feet and sat to one side. The bhikṣus then said to the World-Honored One: "We, a great number of bhikṣus, when the time came, put on our robes and took up our bowls and entered the city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. We heard that outside the palace gate of the king of Kośala there was a great crowd of people holding each other's hands and weeping, who said: 'In this territory there is a great bandit named Aṅgulimālya who kills and harms the people, taking one finger from each to make a flower garland. For this reason he is called Aṅgulimālya. We beseech the king to subdue him.'"

At that time, the World-Honored One, hearing this from the bhikṣus, immediately rose from his seat and went to the place where Aṅgulimālya was staying in three locations. At that time, many people carrying firewood and grass, as well as farmers, and travelers on the road, came to the World-Honored One and said: "Śramaṇa, do not go by this road! Why? On this road there is Aṅgulimālya, who kills and harms people without a compassionate heart toward living beings. Cities, fortresses, and villages have all been devastated by this man. He kills people and strings their fingers into a flower garland. He would assault even the World-Honored One. Any śramaṇas or ordinary people who travel this road must gather ten together before they can pass, or twenty, or thirty, or forty, or fifty, or a hundred, or a thousand, and only then can they pass. Aṅgulimālya seizes and kills whomever he pleases." At that time, the World-Honored One simply proceeded forward, without any thought of turning back.

At that time, Aṅgulimālya saw the World-Honored One approaching from afar. Seeing him, he thought: "All people who wish to pass along this road must gather ten or even a thousand together before they can pass. I kill whomever I please among them. Yet this śramaṇa comes alone without a companion. I shall surely seize and kill him." Aṅgulimālya then drew his sword from his waist and went toward the World-Honored One. At that time, the World-Honored One, seeing Aṅgulimālya coming from afar, turned around and walked back the way he had come. Aṅgulimālya ran after the World-Honored One, exerting all his strength to catch up, but could not reach him. Then Aṅgulimālya thought: "Running, I can catch an elephant, and I can catch a horse, and I can catch a chariot, and I can catch a raging bull, and I can catch any person. Yet this śramaṇa walks without haste, and though I exert all my strength, I cannot catch him."

Then Aṅgulimālya called out from afar to the World-Honored One: "Stop! Stop, śramaṇa!"

The World-Honored One said: "I stopped long ago. It is you who have not stopped."

Then Aṅgulimālya spoke this verse:

> "The śramaṇa walks yet says he has stopped; he tells me I have not stopped.
> Śramaṇa, explain this meaning: you say you have stopped, but I have not?"

Then the World-Honored One said to Aṅgulimālya: "Listen to what I say, the meaning of 'I have stopped but you have not.'" He then spoke this verse:

> "The World-Honored One has always stopped; all beings receive his grace.
> But you have a mind of killing and harming, and do not avoid evil deeds."

At that time Aṅgulimālya thought: "Am I now doing evil deeds?" Then Aṅgulimālya spoke this verse:

> "With a mind of loving-kindness toward me, the śramaṇa has spoken this verse."
> At that moment he cast aside his sword and with his five limbs took refuge in the Buddha.
> He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and begged to become a śramaṇa.
> The Buddha said: "Come, bhikṣu!" and he immediately received the complete precepts.

It is the constant practice of all Buddha-World-Honored-Ones: when a Buddha-World-Honored-One says "Welcome, bhikṣu!" the person's hair and beard immediately fall away as if shaved, and during the next seven days, whatever robes they wear become the finest and most smooth, whether of cotton cloth, kapok, or woven fabric, all transforming into a kāṣāya. The World-Honored One having spoken thus, "Welcome, bhikṣu! Cultivate the holy life in my teaching, without any thought of arrogance, and exhaust the root source of suffering."

At that time, Aṅgulimālya's hair and beard fell away by themselves, and wearing the kāṣāya he followed behind the World-Honored One. At that time, the World-Honored One, with Aṅgulimālya following behind, traveled from the Jālinī garden to the Jetavana and took his seat. Aṅgulimālya was instructed in proper deportment and etiquette by the senior bhikṣus. Having been so instructed, that son of good family, with firm faith, went forth from the household life to study the path and cultivated the unsurpassed holy life. He exhausted the source of birth and death, the holy life was established, what was to be done had been done, and he would not again be received into a mother's womb. Aṅgulimālya attained arhatship.

At that time, the Venerable Aṅgulimālya practiced the araṇya discipline in uninhabited places, always begging for food without choosing households, and wearing patched robes that others had discarded. At that time, King Prasenajit assembled his four divisions of troops. Having assembled the four divisions, he went out from the city of Śrāvastī, intending to go and kill the bandit Aṅgulimālya. Then King Prasenajit thought: "I should first go to the World-Honored One and report this matter in full, and whatever the World-Honored One instructs, I shall follow." King Prasenajit then went to the Jetavana and walked to where the World-Honored One was. As is the custom for kṣatriya kings, there are five insignia: namely the canopy, the celestial crown, the red chowrie, the sword, and the jeweled sandals. He set all of these aside, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, and sat to one side.

When King Prasenajit was seated, the World-Honored One asked: "Great king, why have you assembled the four divisions of troops, and why do you come to me with dust covering your garments?"

King Prasenajit said to the World-Honored One: "In this city of Śrāvastī there is a bandit named Aṅgulimālya who kills and harms the people without a compassionate heart. Cities, fortresses, and villages are all afflicted by him. The people have been scattered and separated. He kills people and takes their fingers to make a flower garland. I wish to go and kill that man."

The World-Honored One said: "If the king were to see Aṅgulimālya with his hair and beard shaved off, wearing the three Dharma robes, having gone forth from the household life with firm faith to study the path, what would the king do?"

The king replied: "What would I do? I would pay my respects and make offerings to him, attend to him and provide for him, without any thought of harm toward him. But, World-Honored One! Can such a fierce and evil person, without a compassionate heart toward living beings, truly practice the way of a śramaṇa?"

At that time, the Venerable Aṅgulimālya was not far from the World-Honored One, sitting cross-legged with body upright, mind correct, and mindfulness settled before him. The World-Honored One raised his right hand and pointed to where Aṅgulimālya was: "Great king! This is the bandit Aṅgulimālya." When King Prasenajit saw Aṅgulimālya, he was filled with fear and his hair stood on end. The World-Honored One told King Prasenajit: "Great king! Do not be afraid. Go to him yourself, and he will speak with the king."

King Prasenajit then went to where Aṅgulimālya was, bowed his head at his feet, and stood to one side. King Prasenajit asked Aṅgulimālya: "Venerable Aṅgulimālya, what is your name now?"

Aṅgulimālya replied: "Great king! My name is Gagga, and my mother's name is Mantāyanī."

The king said: "Strive well in your practice. For the rest of my life I shall provide the Venerable Gagga with robes, food and drink, medicine for illness, and bedding, withholding nothing. I shall always protect you with the Dharma."

King Prasenajit then bowed his head at his feet, circumambulated three times, and went to where the World-Honored One was. He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side. King Prasenajit said to the World-Honored One: "World-Honored One! You can subdue those who are unsubdued. The Tathāgata causes even the most obstinate to submit, and without applying sword or staff, you subdue living beings. I have many affairs of state and wish to return to my country."

The World-Honored One said: "Now is the right time. Go as you wish."

King Prasenajit then rose from his seat, bowed his head at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated three times, and departed.

At that time, Aṅgulimālya, on that very day, put on his robes, took up his bowl, and entered the city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. While begging for food, Aṅgulimālya saw a woman who was pregnant and about to give birth, but could not deliver at the right time. Seeing this, he thought: "How greatly this being suffers!" Aṅgulimālya, having begged for food in the city of Śrāvastī, after the meal put away his robes and bowl, washed his hands and feet, placed his sitting cloth over his shoulder, and went to where the World-Honored One was. He bowed his head at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side. Aṅgulimālya said to the World-Honored One: "Earlier, I put on my robes, took up my bowl, and entered the city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. While begging, I saw a woman who was pregnant and about to give birth, but could not deliver at the right time. Seeing this, I thought: 'How greatly these beings suffer!'"

The World-Honored One said: "Aṅgulimālya! Go to that woman and say to her: 'Since the noble ones were born, since I was born into the noble life, I do not recall ever having killed a living being. By this truthful statement, may this woman be safe and give birth in peace.'"

At that time, Aṅgulimālya said to the World-Honored One: "Would this not be a false statement on my part? Why? Because in this very body I have killed countless hundreds of thousands of living beings."

The World-Honored One said: "When you were a layperson, that was your worldly life. Now you are in the noble life, which is not the same as before. Aṅgulimālya! Enter the city of Śrāvastī and proclaim throughout the streets and lanes: 'Good people, you should guard against five things. What five? Do not kill, do not steal, do not commit sexual misconduct, do not speak falsely, and do not drink alcohol. Why? The retribution for killing is that one who gives the sword receives the sword in return. The retribution for stealing is increasing poverty. The retribution for sexual misconduct is that one's spouse will become unfaithful. The retribution for false speech is that the breath of living beings becomes foul. The retribution for drinking alcohol is increasing confusion.' Then go to that woman and say to her: 'Since I was born into the noble life, I do not recall ever having killed a living being. By this truthful statement, may this woman be safe and give birth in peace.'"

He replied: "So be it, World-Honored One."

At that time, Aṅgulimālya, when the time came, put on his robes, took up his bowl, and entered the city of Śrāvastī to beg for food. Throughout the streets and lanes he proclaimed: "Good people, you should guard against five things." And so on, until the woman was safe and gave birth in peace. He gradually made his way to that woman's place. Having arrived, he said to the woman: "Since I was born into the noble life, I do not recall ever having killed a single life. By this truthful statement, may this woman be safe and give birth in peace." No sooner had Aṅgulimālya finished speaking than the woman gave birth.

At that time, after his meal, as Aṅgulimālya was leaving the city of Śrāvastī, a certain person struck his body with a stone. Another person struck his body with a stick. Another person slashed his body with a knife. His body was injured. Aṅgulimālya, with his head broken and bleeding, left the city of Śrāvastī and went to where the World-Honored One was. The World-Honored One saw Aṅgulimālya approaching from afar, his head broken and blood flowing, staining his saṃghāṭī robe, his body injured. Seeing this, the World-Honored One said: "Endure it, and do not give rise to evil thoughts. The retribution for your former deeds would have been countless hundreds of thousands of eons in hell. What you now receive is hardly worth speaking of."

Aṅgulimālya said: "So it is, World-Honored One! So it is, Tathāgata!" Then Aṅgulimālya, with a mind of harmonious joy, spoke these verses before the Buddha:

> "My patience is exceedingly firm; I have no mind of increase or decrease.
> Now I have heard the true Dharma; therefore I am not heedless.
> Having heard the Dharma, I am also firm, with good faith in the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṃgha.
> Drawing near to good spiritual friends, I am able to distinguish the Dharma.
> I was once an evil bandit, named Aṅgulimālya.
> Swept away by the flood and drowning, I took refuge in the Three Buddhas.
> Taking refuge in the Dharma, distinguishing the Dharma within the Dharma,
> I have attained the three penetrating knowledges, and have reached the ground where the Buddha treads.
> Formerly I was heedless in conduct, killing and harming the lives of beings.
> Now I am called 'Truthful'; I no longer kill or harm people.
> What my body and mouth committed, the mind that harbored killing and harm:
> He who bears the name of 'killer' is no longer envied by others.
> A young bhikṣu who follows the Buddha's precepts and discipline:
> His light illuminates the world, like the moon when clouds and mist disperse.
> One who formerly was heedless and dissolute, but afterward reforms and no longer transgresses:
> His light illuminates the world, like the moon when clouds and mist disperse.
> Swept away by the flood, like cotton refined and made strong,
> As a skilled craftsman understands the grain of wood, so the wise person disciplines himself.
> Some are tamed by sword and staff, some by whip and bridle,
> But without force and without restraint, I was subdued by the Buddha.
> I neither hope for death nor hope for life.
> I observe the seasons and the time, calm and steady, never rash."

At that time, the World-Honored One, delighting in Aṅgulimālya, told the bhikṣus: "Have you ever seen among the bhikṣus a disciple of mine with such swift wisdom, who understands the Dharma upon hearing it, as the bhikṣu Gagga, who understands the Dharma upon hearing it?"

The bhikṣus said: "No, World-Honored One."

Then the World-Honored One told the bhikṣus: "Among my śrāvaka disciples, the foremost bhikṣu with swift wisdom is the bhikṣu Aṅgulimālya."

At that time, the bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully practiced in accordance.
