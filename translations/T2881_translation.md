# Sūtra on the Causes and Effects of Good and Evil

## *Shàn'è Yīnguǒ Jīng* (善惡因果經)

Taishō 2881

Translator unknown. (Classified as apocryphal.)

*Note: This is a sūtra, likely of Chinese composition (classified in the Taishō as apocryphal, i.e. suspected of being composed in China rather than translated from an Indian original), in which the Buddha explains to Ānanda the karmic causes behind the vast diversity of human conditions: beauty and ugliness, wealth and poverty, longevity and early death, intelligence and dullness, and so on. The Buddha systematically teaches that each present condition results from specific past-life actions. The text has three main sections: (1) a detailed list of karmic causes for various physical traits and circumstances (e.g., beauty comes from patience, ugliness from anger, poverty from miserliness, high status from prostrating to the Buddha); (2) a list of hells corresponding to specific sins (e.g., the Hell of Boiling Cauldrons for those who scald pigs and chickens, the Hell of Molten Copper for drunkards); and (3) a list of meritorious acts and their rewards (e.g., building stūpas leads to rebirth as a king, providing lamps leads to rebirth in the heaven of sun and moon). The text concludes with a teaching on the karmic fruits of the ten unwholesome actions and the merits of the ten wholesome deeds. The style is direct, vivid, and popular, designed for a lay audience. Texts of this type were extremely widespread in medieval Chinese Buddhism and remain influential in popular Buddhist piety.*

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the kingdom of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, in the Garden of the Benefactor of Orphans [Anāthapiṇḍada]. At that time, the World-Honored One, surrounded by an immeasurable assembly of bodhisattvas, humans, and devas, was teaching the Dharma, and all listened with single-minded attention.

At that time, Ānanda, for the sake of sentient beings, addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Now I see that among those born in the human realm, all of the same kind, some are beautiful and some ugly, some strong and some weak, some poor and some rich, some suffering and some happy, some noble and some lowly, with different voices and different languages. Some live to a hundred without dying, some die at thirty, some perish at fifteen, and some die in the womb.

"Some are handsome yet poor, some ugly yet rich; some are powerful yet lowly, some weak yet hold high positions; some suffer yet live long, some are happy yet die young; some do good yet encounter misfortune, some do evil yet enjoy blessings; some are plump and fair-eyed, some dark yet charming; some are short yet full of spirit, some tall yet serve others as slaves.

"Some have abundant sons and daughters, some are alone and solitary; some wander abroad hungry and cold, some live in palaces with food and clothing at their pleasure; some are poor when young and rich when old, some are unjustly imprisoned; some have loving parents and filial children discussing sūtras and teachings, some have siblings who fight and quarrel without end; some dwell peacefully with every provision, some have no house and drift from place to place.

"Some live like crows and deer among wild animals, some wear skins and eat raw blood, knowing no writing; some sit and receive their just deserts, some labor without a place to call their own; some are intelligent and sharp, some dull and witless; some toil before acquiring anything, some receive without seeking; some are rich yet miserly, some poor yet generous; some speak with harmony, some with words like thorns; some are beloved by all, some avoided by everyone; some have compassionate hearts that cherish life, some kill without compare; some are generous and win followers, some are cast off by others.

"Some mothers-in-law and daughters-in-law despise each other, some sisters-in-law play together happily; some love to hear the Dharma, some fall asleep when sūtras are read; some are warriors without manners, some love learning and literature; some take the forms of various kinds of animals.

"May the World-Honored One explain the causes and effects in detail, so that the great assembly, upon hearing, may with single mind follow what is good."

---

## The Karmic Causes of Present Conditions

The Buddha told Ānanda: "As you have asked, the reason rewards differ is that in past lives, the mind's application was unequal. Therefore what is received varies in a thousand ways.

"Those who are beautiful in this life come from patience in past lives. Those who are ugly come from anger. Those who are poor come from miserliness. Those who are noble come from prostrating [before the Three Treasures]. Those who are lowly come from arrogance. Those who are tall come from showing respect. Those who are short come from disrespecting the Dharma. Those who are fierce and obstinate come from [rebirth as] sheep. Those who have dark and sallow complexions come from obstructing the Buddha's light.

"Those with tight lips come from tasting food [meant for] the fasting community. Those with red eyes come from begrudging the light of fire. Those with night-blindness come from sewing shut the eyes of hawks. Those who are mute come from slandering the Dharma. Those who are deaf come from not liking to hear the Dharma. Those who lack teeth come from biting bones and meat. Those with blocked noses come from burning foul incense as offerings to the Buddha. Those with cleft lips come from piercing fish gills. Those with yellow hair come from singeing pigs. Those with pierced ears come from piercing [animals'] ears.

"Those with snake-like bodies come from wearing thin clothes and brushing past buddha-images. Those with dark complexions come from placing buddha-images under smoky eaves. Those who are crippled come from not rising when seeing teachers and elders. Those with hunched backs come from wearing light clothes and passing behind buddha-images. Those whose shins and foreheads [are marked] come from striking their foreheads instead of prostrating before the Buddha. Those with short necks come from retracting their heads and running from elders. Those with heart-pain come from cutting and piercing the bodies of sentient beings. Those with leprosy come from wrongfully taking others' property. Those with coughing illness come from giving others cold food in winter.

"Those without sons or daughters come from killing the offspring of birds. Those with many children come from delighting in nurturing the lives of living things. Those who are long-lived come from compassion. Those who die young come from killing. Those who are greatly wealthy come from giving. Those who have carriages and horses come from donating carriages and horses to the Three Treasures. Those who are intelligent come from studying and reciting sūtras. Those who are dull come from [having been] animals. Those who are slaves come from unpaid debts. Those who are restless and wild come from [having been] monkeys. Those with leprosy come from destroying the Three Treasures. Those whose hands and feet do not move [freely] come from binding the hands and feet of sentient beings. Those with vicious natures come from [having been] snakes and scorpions. Those whose six faculties are complete come from keeping the precepts. Those whose faculties are incomplete come from breaking the precepts.

"Those who are unclean come from [having been] pigs. Those who delight in song and dance come from [having been] performers. Those who are greedy come from [having been] dogs. Those with goiters come from eating alone. Those with foul breath come from cursing others. Those whose male organs are incomplete come from castrating pigs and dogs. Those whose tongues are short come from secretly cursing elders."

---

## Karmic Consequences Leading to Animal Rebirth

"Those who delight in adultery with others' wives will, after death, fall into [rebirth as] geese and ducks. Those who commit incest with close relatives will fall into [rebirth as] sparrows. Those who are miserly with sūtras, hiding wisdom and refusing to teach others, will after death become worms in earth and wood. Those who are fond of bearing bows and arrows and riding will fall into [rebirth among] barbarian peoples. Those who delight in killing will fall into [rebirth as] wolves. Those who are fond of wearing ornamental flowers will become flower-laden insects. Those who delight in wearing long garments will become long-tailed insects.

"Those who are fond of lying down to eat will fall into [rebirth as] pigs. Those who delight in wearing colorful clothing will become mottled birds. Those who delight in mimicking others' speech will become parrots. Those who delight in slander will fall into [rebirth as] great venomous serpents. Those who torment others without reason will become anguish-insects. Those who delight in spreading malicious reports will become owl-birds. Those who delight in speaking words of disaster will become foxes. Those who delight in frightening others will become centipedes. Those who in past lives wore wooden clogs into monasteries are now born as horses with hooves. Those who in past lives were fond of passing wind are now born as dung-beetles. Those who in past lives used the monastic community's mortar and millstone are now born as head-knocking insects. Those who in past lives rationed people's food are now born as woodpeckers.

"Those who stole the saṃgha's water are now fish and turtles in the water. Those who dirtied the saṃgha's ground are now insects in the latrine. Those who stole the saṃgha's fruit are now earth-eating worms. Those who stole the saṃgha's property are now oxen and donkeys at the grindstone. Those who forcibly begged and borrowed from the saṃgha are now white doves. Those who insulted the saṃgha are now insects in the yoke-sores of cattle. Those who ate the saṃgha's vegetables are now insects in the smartweed. Those who sat on the saṃgha's beds are now earthworms. Those who used the saṃgha's miscellaneous property are now moths flying into fire. Those who carried bones and cracked them open in the monastery are now long-beaked birds. Those who entered the monastery wearing rouge and vermilion lipstick are now red birds. Those who entered the monastery wearing colorful clothing are now yellow orioles. Those who spent the night with their spouse in a monastery are now green-headed dung beetles. Those who sat with their backs to stūpas are now camels. Those who entered stūpas and monasteries wearing shoes and boots are now toads. Those who chattered during Dharma teachings are now mynah birds. Those who defiled nuns of pure conduct will, after death, fall into the Iron Cave Hell, where a million sword-wheels descend simultaneously to cut their bodies to pieces."

## The Proper and Improper Ways to Visit a Monastery

At that time, Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "As the Buddha has taught, offenses against the saṃgha's property are truly grave. If this is so, how can the four groups of donors visit monasteries to pay their respects?"

The Buddha said: "When visiting a monastery, there are two kinds of mind: the good mind and the evil mind. What is the good mind? When visiting the saṃgha, one prostrates upon seeing the Buddha, shows respect upon seeing monks, requests sūtras, asks about the meaning, receives precepts, repents, donates wealth, tends to the Three Treasures, and protects the great Dharma without sparing one's body or life. Such a person, with each step, the heavenly realms come of their own accord. In future lives, such merit is like that of Śūra [the great donor]. This is called the most supreme good person.

"What is the evil mind? If sentient beings, upon entering a monastery, only beg, borrow, and demand from the saṃgha, or seek out the faults of monks with the intent to cause destruction, or consume the saṃgha's food without the slightest shame, taking cakes, fruit, and vegetables home with them: such persons, after death, fall into the Iron Ball Hell, the Boiling Cauldron, the Furnace of Coals, the Mountain of Blades, and the Forest of Swords, experiencing every torment. This is called the most inferior evil person."

---

## The Buddha's Admonition

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "I admonish those of future generations: those who are my disciples should be cautious and not offend against the Three Treasures. Strive diligently and do not give rise to a mind of retreat. Those who follow the Buddha's words will, when Maitreya appears in the world, attain liberation without doubt."

---

## The Hells Corresponding to Specific Sins

The Buddha said: "Those who in this life rob others of their clothing will, after death, fall into the Cold Ice Hell, and then be reborn as silkworms, scalded and stripped by others. Those who in this life do not delight in lighting lamps before sūtras and images will, after death, fall into the Dark Hell between the Iron-Encircling Mountains. Those who in this life slaughter and dismember sentient beings will, after death, fall into the Hell of Blade-Mountains and Sword-Trees. Those who in this life fly hawks and run dogs for hunting will, after death, fall into the Iron Saw Hell. Those who commit adultery will, after death, fall into the Copper Pillar and Iron Bed Hell. Those who in this life keep many wives will, after death, fall into the Iron Millstone Hell. Those women who in this life keep many husbands will, after death, fall into the Venomous Snake Hell. Those who in this life scald and burn eggs will, after death, fall into the Ash River Hell.

"Those who in this life scald pigs and chickens will, after death, fall into the Boiling Cauldron Hell. Those who in this life castrate pigs and dogs will, after death, fall into the Sharp Stone Hell. Those who in this life become drunk and disorderly from drinking will, after death, fall into the Molten Copper Hell. Those who in this life dismember sentient beings will, after death, fall into the Iron Wheel Hell. Those who in this life steal the saṃgha's fruit will, after death, fall into the Iron Ball Hell. Those who in this life eat the intestines and flesh of pigs and dogs will, after death, fall into the Excrement Hell. Those who in this life eat raw fish will, after death, fall into the Hell of Blade-Forests and Sword-Trees.

"Those who in this life, as stepmothers, mistreat the children of the first wife will, after death, fall into the Fire-Chariot Hell. Those who in this life use double-tongued speech to cause discord will, after death, fall into the Iron Plow Hell. Those who in this life use foul speech to curse others will, after death, fall into the Tongue-Pulling Hell. Those who in this life tell many lies will, after death, fall into the Iron Needle Hell. Those who in this life kill living beings to sacrifice to heterodox spirits will, after death, fall into the Iron Mortar Hell.

"Those who in this life act as spirit-mediums, using ghost-speech to deceive others and take their property, will after death fall into the Flesh-Mountain Hell. Those who act as spirit-mediums, closing their eyes and lying on the ground, deceiving others by claiming to ascend to heaven to retrieve the souls of the dead, will after death fall into the Waist-Severing Hell. Those who act as spirit-mediums, teaching others to kill living beings to propitiate great spirits, the gods of the five roads, local earth-gods, or various female spirits: all such deceptions of the foolish will, after death, lead to the Chopping Hell, where prison-wardens hack their bodies and iron birds peck out their eyes. Those who in this life act as geomancers, divining the fortune of burial sites, prognosticating household fortune, performing dragon-pacifying and silkworm-appeasing rites, and deceiving the foolish to obtain great wealth with false words of good and bad fortune: such people will, after death, fall into the Iron-and-Copper Hell, where countless evil birds gather on their bodies, eating their flesh and pecking their sinews and bones, suffering without end. Those who in this life practice as physicians but cannot cure illness and deceive others to take their property will, after death, fall into the Cauterization Hell, where their entire bodies are consumed by fire. Those who in this life destroy stūpas, demolish monasteries, rebel against teachers and monks, and are unfilial to parents will, after death, fall into the Avīci Great Hell, passing through all eight great hells and all one hundred thirty-six lesser hells, remaining for one, two, or up to five kalpas. Only then may they emerge. If they encounter a good spiritual friend and arouse the aspiration for bodhi, [they may be liberated]; if they do not, they fall back into hell."

## Animals and Their Human Counterparts

The Buddha said: "Those who are greatly foul-smelling and prone to anger come from [having been] camels. Those who walk vigorously, eat heartily, and fear no danger come from horses. Those who endure cold and heat without resentment come from cattle. Those who speak loudly without shame and have many attachments, not distinguishing right from wrong, come from donkeys. Those who are always greedy for meat and fearless in their actions come from lions. Those who are tall with round eyes, prefer open wilds, and dislike wife and children come from tigers. Those with long hair and small eyes who do not like to stay in one place come from birds. Those whose nature is faithless and who delight in killing insects come from foxes. Those who are brave and strong with little sexual desire and no love for wife and children come from wolves. Those who dislike fine clothing, spy out wrongdoing, and are angry-eyed and wrathful in youth come from dogs. Those who delight in sexual pleasure and are loved by all come from parrots. Those who enjoy being among people and are talkative and troublesome come from owls.

"Those who are small-bodied and lustful, with unfixed minds that are confused upon seeing attractive forms, come from sparrows. Those with red eyes and short teeth who drool when speaking and curl up when sleeping come from snakes and vipers. Those who speak with anger without considering the meaning, whose mouths emit fiery venom, come from scorpions. Those who dwell alone and eat greedily, sleeping little at night, come from wildcats. Those who bore through walls to steal, greedy for wealth and fierce in resentment, treating all alike whether close or distant, come from rats."

---

## Final Teachings on the Ten Unwholesome Actions

The Buddha told Ānanda: "All the various sufferings described above arise from the karma of the ten evils. The upper grade [of these offenses] leads to hell; the middle grade leads to the animal realm; the lower grade leads to the hungry ghost realm.

"The sin of killing can cause sentient beings to fall into hell, the animal realm, and the hungry ghost realm. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: short life and frequent illness. The sin of stealing also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: poverty, and inability to freely use shared wealth. The sin of sexual misconduct also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: an unfaithful spouse, and two wives quarreling so that neither follows one's wishes. The sin of lying also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: being frequently slandered, and being constantly deceived. The sin of divisive speech also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: a broken family, and wicked companions. The sin of harsh speech also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: constantly hearing unpleasant sounds, and all one's words leading to quarrels. The sin of frivolous speech also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: speaking correctly yet not being believed, and all one's words being unclear. The sin of covetousness also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: insatiable greed for wealth, and seeking much yet never finding satisfaction. The sin of anger also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: others always seeking one's faults, and being constantly harassed. The sin of wrong views also leads to the three evil destinies. If reborn among humans, one receives two kinds of fruit: always being born in a family of wrong views, and having a mind that is always crooked and deceitful.

"All Buddha's children! These ten unwholesome paths of action are the causes and conditions of the great mass of suffering."

---

## The Practice of Merit

At that time, among the great assembly, those who had committed the ten evils, upon hearing the Buddha describe the suffering of the hells, all wept loudly and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What good practice can we perform to escape this suffering?"

The Buddha said: "You should teach and transform all sentient beings to share in meritorious karma. How does one cultivate merit? If sentient beings in this life act as great patrons, building stūpas and monasteries, in future lives they will certainly become kings, commanding ten thousand people, with none failing to submit. If in this life one serves as a community leader, administrator, or superintendent, in future lives one will certainly become a royal minister, prefect, or governor, with horses, clothing, and all provisions at one's pleasure. If in this life one leads others in performing meritorious works, in future lives one will certainly become a great and wealthy elder, respected by all, with all four roads open and everything going favorably. If in this life one delights in lighting lamps and continuing their brightness, one will be reborn in the heaven of the sun and moon, with one's own radiance shining. If in this life one delights in giving and compassionately cherishing life, one will be reborn in great wealth, with clothing and food arising naturally. If in this life one delights in giving others food and drink, wherever one is reborn, celestial kitchens will come of their own accord, with perfect complexion, strength, eloquence, and long life.

"Giving to animals yields a hundredfold reward. Giving to an *icchantika* yields a thousandfold reward. Giving to a precept-keeping bhikṣu yields a ten-thousandfold reward. Giving to a Dharma-master who disseminates the Mahāyāna and expounds the Tathāgata's secret treasury, opening the mind's eye of the assembly, yields immeasurable reward. Giving to bodhisattvas and buddhas yields inexhaustible reward. Furthermore, giving to three kinds of persons yields endless reward: first, all buddhas; second, one's parents; third, the ill. Even a single meal offered can yield immeasurable reward; how much more can constant giving ever be exhausted!

"If in this life one bathes the saṃgha, wherever one is reborn, one's face will be beautiful, one will naturally have clothing, and all will show respect. If in this life one delights in praising, reading, and reciting the sūtras, wherever one is reborn, one's voice will be elegant and beautiful, and all who hear will be delighted. If in this life one delights in keeping the precepts, wherever one is reborn, one will be beautiful and supreme among humans. If in this life one delights in constructing public wells, water stations, and planting roadside trees to shade travelers, wherever one is reborn, one will always be a king, with a hundred flavors of food appearing at the thought. If in this life one delights in copying sūtras and giving them to others to read, wherever one is reborn, one will be eloquent and talented, understanding any teaching upon first hearing, always protected by buddhas and bodhisattvas, supreme among humans and always foremost. If in this life one delights in building bridges and boats to ferry people across, wherever one is reborn, one will be furnished with the seven treasures, admired and respected by all, and assisted by others wherever one goes."

---

## Conclusion

The Buddha told Ānanda: "As I have taught in sūtra after sūtra, I have explained causes and effects, urging all sentient beings to read, recite, and practice in order to escape suffering. If anyone hears this sūtra and gives rise to slander, that person's tongue will fall out in this very life."

At that time, Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What should this sūtra be called, and how should it be used for exhortation?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "This sūtra is called 'The Causes and Effects of Good and Evil.' It is also called 'The Sūtra of the Bodhisattva's Vow and Practice.' Uphold it accordingly."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, eighty thousand devas and humans in the assembly aroused the aspiration for *anuttara-samyak-saṃbodhi*; hundreds of thousands of women transformed their female bodies and attained male form; twelve hundred evil persons abandoned their poisonous intentions and came to know their past lives; immeasurable good persons attained the patience of non-arising and enjoyed eternal bliss; immeasurable righteous persons were reborn in the various pure lands as companions of the buddhas and bodhisattvas. The entire great assembly returned home to perform merit, joyfully practicing the teaching.
