# 優陂夷墮舍迦經
## Sūtra of the Upāsikā Duòshějiā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0088

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was staying in the country of Śrāvastī. East of the city, there was a woman's son named Migāra (蕪耶樓); people called her "Migāra's Mother." The Buddha was sitting in the hall of Migāra's Mother's house. There was a woman who upheld the teaching and precepts, named Duòshějiā (墮舍迦). On the fifteenth day of the month, she rose early, bathed, and had her seven daughters-in-law all bathe as well and put on fine clothes. Before midday they ate their meal, and after eating they washed their hands. Taking her seven daughters-in-law, she went to where the Buddha was and prostrated before him. The Buddha said: "Please be seated." They all withdrew to their seats. The Buddha asked the upāsikā Duòshějiā: "Why have you bathed today and put on fine clothes, coming to see the Buddha with your daughters-in-law?" Duòshějiā said: "Today is the fifteenth day. I am keeping the observance precepts. I have heard that one should observe six observance days per month. My daughters-in-law and I are all keeping the observance together, not daring to be negligent."

The Buddha told Duòshějiā: "The Buddha's correct observance has eight precepts that enable people to cross over the worldly path, never again to fall into the three evil destinies. Wherever one is born, one always has blessings and protection. The eight precepts are also the fundamental cause for ultimately attaining Buddhahood.

"What are the eight precepts of the Buddha's correct observance? In this world, there are worthy and good people who maintain the precepts, observing six days each month: the eighth day is one observance, the fourteenth is one observance, the fifteenth is one observance, the twenty-third is one observance, the twenty-ninth is one observance, and the thirtieth is one observance. On the morning of an observance day, one announces to one's household: 'Today I am keeping the observance. Everyone in the household, for this one day: do not drink intoxicants, do not quarrel or argue, do not discuss money and possessions. Whatever household affairs the mind thinks of and the mouth speaks of, all should be as if one were an arhat.' An arhat has no thought of killing. On the observance day, maintaining the precepts should be like an arhat: no thought of killing, no thought of striking, being mindful of animals and even insects, wishing them always to live, like the mind of an arhat. This is the first precept. For this day and night, maintaining the resolve not to kill so that killing does not occur, holding one's thoughts in this way is the Buddha's first precept."

The Buddha said: "On the observance day, one should maintain one's thoughts like an arhat who has no covetous mind, who covets nothing in the world, who has not even a covetous thought toward a blade of grass. On the observance day, one should thus maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. Those who are wealthy should resolve to practice generosity; those who are poor and have nothing should always resolve to give. This is the second precept."

The Buddha said: "On the observance day, one should maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. An arhat does not keep a wife, does not think of a wife, does not crave women, and has no lustful thoughts. On the observance day, one should thus maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. This is the third precept."

The Buddha said: "On the observance day, one should maintain one's thoughts like an arhat: not speaking falsely, not harming others' feelings. When speaking, one speaks of the Buddha's sūtras; when not speaking, one only thinks of various wholesome things. On the observance day, one should thus maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. This is the fourth precept."

The Buddha said: "On the observance day, one should maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. An arhat does not drink intoxicants, does not think of intoxicants, does not crave intoxicants, for intoxicants lead to evil. On the observance day, one should thus maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. This is the fifth precept."

The Buddha said: "On the observance day, one should maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. An arhat's mind is not set on singing and dancing; even hearing music gives no delight, nor any interest in fragrant flowers and perfumes. On the observance day, one should thus maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. This is the sixth precept."

The Buddha said: "On the observance day, one should maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. An arhat does not sleep on high and luxurious beds; the mind has no thought of sleeping on high and luxurious beds. On the observance day, one should thus maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. This is the seventh precept."

The Buddha said: "On the observance day, one should maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. An arhat eats at midday and at the proper time. After midday until dawn, one may not eat again, though one may drink honey water. On the observance day, one should thus maintain one's thoughts like an arhat. This is the eighth precept."

The Buddha further told the upāsikā Duòshějiā: "In this world, when a person's head is dirty and they wash their hair, after washing they are happy and say: 'The dirt on my head has been removed.' When a person maintains the eight precepts for one day and one night, the next day they are happy. Being happy, they reflect: 'The Buddha's sūtras and precepts are truly like this.' When a person has evil thoughts in their mind, the evil immediately departs and wholesome thoughts return."

The Buddha said: "When a person's body has much dirt and they bathe in water, all the dirt is removed. Coming out of the water they are happy and say: 'My dirt is gone and my body is light.' When a person observes the precepts for one day and one night, the next day they are happy and reflect on the Buddha's correct teaching, thinking: 'When shall I attain the path, so that my mind no longer wavers and no longer runs astray, so that my mind is unified, aspiring to the unconditioned path?'"

The Buddha said: "When a person's clothes have much dirt and they wash them with ash, the dirt is removed and they are happy. When a person observes the precepts for one day and one night, the next day they are happy and contemplate the Saṃgha of bhikṣus, saying: 'Among the Saṃgha of bhikṣus, there are stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, and arhats.' They contemplate the Saṃgha of bhikṣus in this way."

The Buddha said: "Like a person who has a mirror: when the mirror has tarnish and they polish away the tarnish, the mirror becomes bright. When a person observes the precepts for one day and one night, with a compassionate mind toward all under heaven, their mind open, bright, and clear like a mirror, one should not have any thoughts of anger. When a person observes the precepts for one day and one night, having sympathy for all under heaven, purifying the mind and reflecting on oneself, straightening one's thoughts, contemplating the impurities within the body: being thus, one should no longer be angry."

The Buddha told the upāsikā Duòshějiā: "The merit of maintaining the eight precepts for one day and one night without fail surpasses giving gold, silver, pearls, and jewels to the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. Under heaven there are sixteen great nations: the first is named Aṅga, the second Magadha, the third Kāśī, the fourth Kośala, the fifth Kuru, the sixth Pañcāla, the seventh Avayā, the eighth Avantī, the ninth Cedi, the tenth Vṛji, the eleventh Sūrasena, the twelfth Saurāṣṭra, the thirteenth Vatsa, the fourteenth Malla, the fifteenth Yugandhara, and the sixteenth Kamboja. If all the treasures and precious things in these sixteen great nations were given to the Saṃgha of bhikṣus, it would not equal one day and one night of observing the precepts. The observance precepts enable people to cross over the worldly path, whereas giving material wealth cannot make a person attain the path. My present attainment of Buddhahood originally arose from these eight precepts."

The Buddha told Duòshějiā: "People in the world have many worries about household affairs. For this reason, I have established six monthly observance days for maintaining the eight precepts. If there is a worthy and good person who urgently wishes to attain the path of arhatship, or who swiftly wishes to attain the path of Buddhahood, or who wishes to be reborn in the heavens, and can straighten their mind and unify their thoughts: observing fifteen days per month is also good, twenty days per month is also good. Because people have many worries about household affairs, I have given six monthly observance days. As for one who observes the six observance days: it is like the ocean, whose water cannot be measured. For one who observes the precepts for one day and one night, the merit is incalculable."

The Buddha told Duòshějiā: "Now take your daughters-in-law and return home, and recite and remember the correct observance of the eight precepts."

The upāsikā Duòshějiā prostrated before the Buddha and departed.
