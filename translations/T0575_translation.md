# 佛說大方等修多羅王經
## Sūtra of the King of Mahāvaipulya Sūtras, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0575

Translated from the Chinese. Northern Wei dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流支)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in Rājagṛha, at the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and a multitude of great bodhisattva mahāsattvas. At that time King Bimbisāra of Magadha came forth from the city of Rājagṛha and went to the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove Monastery. He came before the World-Honored One, bowed his head to the Buddha's feet, circumambulated him three times, and sat to one side.

Then the World-Honored One said to King Bimbisāra: "Great King! Suppose a man in a dream sees a group of people and beautiful women enjoying themselves together. When the man wakes, he remembers the people and women from the dream. Great King, what do you think? Are the people and women in that dream real or not?"

King Bimbisāra replied: "No, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha said to the Great King: "What do you think? This man who saw beautiful women in his dream enjoying themselves with those people, and upon waking still thinks of them: can such a man be called wise?"

The Great King replied: "No, World-Honored One! Why? Because in the dream there ultimately were no people and no women. Since the people and women themselves cannot be found, how could there have been any enjoyment together?"

The Buddha said to the Great King: "Ordinary people are just the same. When the eye sees a beautiful form, attachment arises. Once attachment has arisen, desire arises. Once desire has arisen, deeds of anger and delusion follow: deeds of the body, deeds of speech, and deeds of the mind. These deeds, once performed, then cease. Having ceased, they do not abide in the east, nor do they abide in the south, nor in the west, nor in the north; nor in the four intermediate directions, nor above, nor below. At the time of death, the active consciousness is about to cease, and one's intentions appear before the mind. Great King! This active consciousness must fully experience the karma it has created. Great King! Just as when a man wakes from a dream he no longer sees the women and people, so too when the active consciousness ceases, the initial consciousness arises in succession: one may be born among the devas, among humans, in Naraka, among animals, or among hungry ghosts. Great King! Because this initial consciousness is unbroken and the mind-stream continues, one is born in the place where one's karmic retribution is to be received. Great King! Observe all arising and ceasing: is there even a single dharma that passes from this world to the next? Great King! When the active consciousness ends, it is called 'cessation.' When the initial consciousness arises, it is called 'birth.' Great King! When the active consciousness ceases, it goes nowhere. When the initial consciousness arises, it comes from nowhere. Why? Because the nature of consciousness is apart from all such things. Great King! The active consciousness is empty of active consciousness; cessation is empty of the activity of cessation; the initial consciousness is empty of initial consciousness; birth is empty of the activity of birth. Yet the fruits of karma are not lost or destroyed. Great King, you should know: because the initial consciousness continues without interruption, the fruits of retribution are received."

Then the Well-Gone One, having spoken thus, uttered these verses:

> "All things are merely names,
> Abiding only in concepts and discriminations.
> Names and discriminations are spoken,
> Yet what is spoken is nothing at all.
> With various names
> Various dharmas are described,
> But within the dharmas there is nothing such;
> This is the true nature of all dharmas.
> Names are empty of names;
> Names are apart from names.
> All dharmas have no names;
> They are spoken of through names.
> These dharmas have no real existence;
> They arise through discrimination.
> That discrimination too is nothing;
> Emptiness is spoken of through discrimination.
> All ordinary people say
> The eye can see forms,
> But this is the world's false discrimination,
> Grasping it and taking it as real.
> What the Buddha teaches
> Is that things are seen through the gathering of conditions.
> This is the sequential process of formations;
> It is taught as the ultimate meaning.
> The eye does not see forms;
> The mind does not perceive dharmas.
> This is the supreme truth,
> Not realized by the worldly."

When the World-Honored One had spoken this sūtra, King Bimbisāra of Magadha, and all the devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and others of the world, hearing the Buddha's teaching, were all filled with great joy.
