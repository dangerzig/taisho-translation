"Furthermore, the five spheres of emancipation are taught by the Buddha: There is a learned bhikṣu who has not fully seen suffering; his mind tolerates and accepts it, yet he has not abandoned sensual desire. Pursuing objects of sensual desire, he gives rise to desirous thoughts; these neither recede nor subside, and he has not attained liberation. Through this very condition, he is later able to contemplate all objects of sensual desire and give rise to the mind of renunciation from desire; the desirous mind recedes and subsides; he delights in liberation, engages in right practice, and attains liberation of mind. With the arising of the liberated mind, he is able to distance himself from incompatible phenomena and abide with a mind free of desire. It is for this reason that, with desire as the condition, the undefiled is brought forth.

"There is a learned bhikṣu who has not fully seen suffering; his mind tolerates and accepts it, yet he has not abandoned ill will. Toward adverse and obstructing objects, he gives rise to thoughts of ill will; these neither recede nor subside, and he has not attained liberation. Through this very condition, he is later able to contemplate all adverse and obstructing objects and give rise to the mind free of ill will; the mind of ill will recedes and subsides; he delights in liberation, engages in right practice, and attains liberation of mind. With the arising of the liberated mind, he is able to distance himself from incompatible phenomena and abide with a mind free of ill will. It is for this reason that, with ill will as the condition, the undefiled is brought forth.

"There is a learned bhikṣu who has not fully seen suffering; his mind tolerates and accepts it, yet he has not abandoned harmfulness. Toward disagreeable objects, he gives rise to thoughts of harm; these neither recede nor subside, and he has not attained liberation. Through this very condition, he is later able to contemplate all disagreeable objects and give rise to the mind of non-harmfulness; the harmful mind recedes and subsides; he delights in liberation, engages in right practice, and attains liberation of mind. With the arising of the liberated mind, he is able to distance himself from incompatible phenomena and abide with a mind of non-harmfulness. It is for this reason that, with harmfulness as the condition, the undefiled is brought forth.

"There is a learned bhikṣu who has not fully seen suffering; his mind tolerates and accepts it, and he has not renounced attachment to form. Toward objects of form, he gives rise to thoughts attached to form; these neither recede nor subside, and he has not attained liberation. Through this very condition, he is later able to contemplate those objects of form and give rise to the mind free of form; the form-attached mind recedes and subsides; he delights in liberation, engages in right practice, and attains liberation of mind. With the arising of the liberated mind, he is able to distance himself from incompatible phenomena and abide with a mind free of form. It is for this reason that, with form as the condition, the undefiled is brought forth.

"There is a learned bhikṣu who has not fully seen suffering; he clings to the existing body and has not abandoned bodily attachment. Regarding the existing body, he gives rise to the notion that it truly and substantially exists; this does not recede or subside, and he has not attained liberation. Through this very condition, he is later able to contemplate the cessation of the existing body and give rise to the notion of the body's cessation; the clinging mind recedes and subsides; he delights in liberation, engages in right practice, and attains liberation of mind. With the arising of the liberated mind, he is able to distance himself from incompatible phenomena and abide with the notion of the body's cessation. It is for this reason that, with the existing body as the condition, the undefiled is brought forth. These are called the five spheres of emancipation.

"Furthermore, the five places of liberation are taught by the Buddha. It is said that a bhikṣu who draws near to a Dharma-teaching master, serving and attending him, honoring and reverencing him, cultivating the holy life without pause: through such drawing near and service, he immediately gains benefit. When he hears the teacher proclaim the true Dharma, joy arises in his mind, and he gives rise to reverence for the Dharma. With that mind having arisen, though unable to hear and retain it extensively, he can nonetheless, from within what he has received, know one Dharma; and from knowing one Dharma, immediately comprehend one meaning. If he cannot comprehend its meaning, he cannot give rise to joy toward the Dharma. Therefore, having comprehended the meaning, joy arises in his mind; by reason of mental joy, the body attains ease; by reason of bodily ease, pleasant feeling arises; by reason of pleasant feeling, the mind abides in samāhita; abiding in that mind, he is able to know as it truly is and further observe as it truly is; through such true observation, he departs from the dust of defilements and departs from craving, and attains the wisdom of liberation. When the wisdom of liberation arises, he attains: 'My birth is exhausted, the holy life has been established, what was to be done has been done, and I will not undergo further becoming.'

"A bhikṣu who draws near to a Dharma-teaching master, serving and attending him, honoring and reverencing him, cultivating the holy life without pause: through such drawing near and service, he immediately hears the Dharma. Following what he hears, joy arises in his mind, and he gives rise to reverence for the Dharma. With that mind having arisen, he is able to hear and retain it extensively, to remember it extensively; from knowing all the Dharmas, he immediately comprehends all meanings. If he cannot comprehend all meanings, he cannot give rise to joy toward the Dharma. Therefore, having comprehended all meanings, joy arises in his mind; by reason of mental joy, the body attains ease; by reason of bodily ease, pleasant feeling arises; by reason of pleasant feeling, the mind abides in samāhita; abiding in that mind, he is able to know as it truly is and further observe as it truly is; through such true observation, he departs from the dust of defilements and departs from craving, and attains the wisdom of liberation. When the wisdom of liberation arises, he attains: 'My birth is exhausted, the holy life has been established, what was to be done has been done, and I will not undergo further becoming.'

"A bhikṣu who draws near to a Dharma-teaching master, serving and attending him, honoring and reverencing him, cultivating the holy life without pause: through such drawing near and service, he immediately hears the Dharma. Following what he hears, joy arises in his mind, and he gives rise to reverence for the Dharma. With that mind having arisen, he is able to hear and retain it extensively and to remember it; the mind abides in one object, neither regressing nor sinking; following the heard Dharma with applied thought (vitarka) and sustained thought (vicāra), right wisdom arises. Following such vitarka and vicāra, he thoroughly knows all Dharmas one by one; from knowing all the Dharmas, he immediately comprehends all meanings, and broadly expounds and explains them for others. When the wisdom of liberation arises, he attains: 'My birth is exhausted, the holy life has been established, what was to be done has been done, and I will not undergo further becoming.'

"A bhikṣu who draws near to a Dharma-teaching master, serving and attending him, honoring and reverencing him, cultivating the holy life without pause: through such drawing near and service, he immediately hears the Dharma. Following what he hears, joy arises in his mind, and he gives rise to reverence for the Dharma. With that mind having arisen, he is able to hear and retain it extensively and to remember it; the mind abides in one object, neither regressing nor sinking; following the heard Dharma with vitarka and vicāra, right wisdom arises; and moreover he is able in those various gateways of samādhi to dwell well with composed mind, and as the mind so dwells, it progressively increases and excels. He thoroughly knows all Dharmas one by one; from knowing all the Dharmas, he immediately comprehends all meanings, and broadly expounds and explains them for others. If he cannot comprehend all meanings, he cannot give rise to joy toward the Dharma. Therefore, having comprehended all meanings, joy arises in his mind; by reason of mental joy, the body attains ease; by reason of bodily ease, pleasant feeling arises; by reason of pleasant feeling, the mind abides in samāhita; abiding in that mind, he is able to know as it truly is and further observe as it truly is; through such true observation, he departs from the dust of defilements and departs from craving, and attains the wisdom of liberation. When the wisdom of liberation arises, he attains: 'My birth is exhausted, the holy life has been established, what was to be done has been done, and I will not undergo further becoming.' Such are called the five places of liberation.

"Furthermore, the five realms of rebirth are taught by the Buddha: the hell realms (naraka), the hungry ghosts (preta), the animals (tiryagyoni), humans (manuṣya), and the devas.

"Furthermore, the five Pure Abodes are taught by the Buddha: Avṛha, Atapa, Sudṛśa, Sudarśana, and Akaniṣṭha.

"Furthermore, the five ways in which persons enter the Dharma are taught by the Buddha: entry in the intermediate state (antarāparinirvāyin), entry at birth (upahaccaparinirvāyin), entry with exertion (sasaṃskāraparinirvāyin), entry without exertion (asaṃskāraparinirvāyin), and entry by the upward stream (ūrdhvasrotas). These are called the five ways in which persons enter the Dharma. Such dharmas as these the Buddha, with a mind of compassionate kindness, broadly proclaimed to beings in proper accordance with principle, causing beings to cultivate practice in accordance with the teachings, to perform the holy life, and to benefit and bring joy to the world of devas and humans.

"Furthermore, the six inner āyatanas are taught by the Buddha: the eye-āyatana, the ear-āyatana, the nose-āyatana, the tongue-āyatana, the body-āyatana, and the mind-āyatana.

"Furthermore, the six outer āyatanas are taught by the Buddha: the form-āyatana, the sound-āyatana, the smell-āyatana, the taste-āyatana, the touch-āyatana, and the dharma-āyatana.

"Furthermore, the six places of delight are taught by the Buddha: seeing a lovely form is a place of delight; hearing a lovely sound is a place of delight; smelling a lovely scent is a place of delight; discerning a lovely flavor is a place of delight; sensing a lovely touch is a place of delight; discerning wholesome dharmas is a place of delight.

"Furthermore, the six recollections are taught by the Buddha: recollection of the Buddha (Buddhānusmṛti), recollection of the Dharma (Dharmānusmṛti), recollection of the Saṃgha (Saṃghānusmṛti), recollection of the precepts (Śīlānusmṛti), recollection of generosity (Tyāgānusmṛti), and recollection of the devas (Devānusmṛti).

"Furthermore, the six practices are taught by the Buddha: the practice of seeing, the practice of hearing, the practice of benefiting, the practice of learning, the practice of discernment, and the practice of recollection.

"Furthermore, the six dharmas for departing from defilements are taught by the Buddha. A bhikṣu who currently abides in bodily action performing loving-kindness: from the very beginning of his cultivation, all of his holy life is firm and unbreakable. A bhikṣu who currently abides in verbal action performing loving-kindness: from the very beginning of his cultivation, all of his holy life is firm and unbreakable. A bhikṣu who currently abides in mental action performing loving-kindness: from the very beginning of his cultivation, all of his holy life is firm and unbreakable. A bhikṣu who receives what is proper in accordance with the Dharma, begs food in accordance with the Dharma, eats whatever is obtained in accordance with the Dharma, sustains his own practice, and keeps far from the unlawful: from the very beginning of his cultivation, all of his holy life is firm and unbreakable. A bhikṣu who cultivates pure precept-conduct, neither breaking nor lacking, free from all transgressions, increasing the power of the wholesome, in all his conduct having precept-conduct complete: from the very beginning of his cultivation, all of his holy life is firm and unbreakable. A bhikṣu who does not cling to the view of the body, gives rise to the thought of going forth, seeks to cut off and exhaust suffering, and in all his conduct does not abide in any views: from the very beginning of his cultivation, all of his holy life is firm and unbreakable. Such are called the six dharmas for departing from defilements.

"Furthermore, the six roots of contention are taught by the Buddha. There is a type of pudgala who personally delights in committing offenses, and also delights in drawing near to those who commit offenses, always wishing for others to love and respect him. There is a type of pudgala who personally delights in committing offenses, and also delights in drawing near to those who commit offenses, always wishing for others to love and respect him; and moreover does not honor the Buddha, does not honor the Dharma, and is unable to contemplate the Dharma. There is a type of pudgala who personally delights in committing offenses, and also delights in drawing near to those who commit offenses, always wishing for others to love and respect him; and moreover delights in contending with the Saṃgha. There is a type of pudgala who is possessed of flattery, deceit, avarice, envy, concealment, and other secondary afflictions, and who possesses the view of the body (satkāyadṛṣṭi), wrong view (mithyādṛṣṭi), and extreme view (antagrāhadṛṣṭi), and view-clinging, unable to distance himself from them. There is a type of pudgala who, possessing the view of the body and so on, gives rise to an inverted mind, and moreover does not honor the Buddha, does not honor the Dharma, and is unable to contemplate the Dharma. There is a type of pudgala who, possessing the view of the body and so on, gives rise to an inverted mind, and moreover constantly delights in contending with the Saṃgha. Such are called the six roots of contention.

"Furthermore, the six realms of antidotal release are taught by the Buddha. A bhikṣu speaks thus: 'I cultivate the liberation-contemplation of loving-kindness; in all my undertakings I know as they truly are; I arouse the energetic antidote of loving-kindness; my mind of anger I exhaust entirely. By means of this liberation-contemplation of loving-kindness, there is no place for any mind of anger to dwell.' Rather, observing only the mind of loving-kindness present before him, the mind of anger therefore does not arise in that place. Why? Because the mind of loving-kindness is the cause of release from that place.

"A bhikṣu speaks thus: 'I cultivate the liberation-contemplation of compassion; in all my undertakings I know as they truly are; I arouse the energetic antidote of compassion; my mind of harm I exhaust entirely. By means of this liberation-contemplation of compassion, there is no place for any mind of harm to dwell.' Rather, observing only the mind of compassion present before him, the mind of harm therefore does not arise in that place. Why? Because the mind of compassion is the cause of release from that place.

"A bhikṣu speaks thus: 'I cultivate the liberation-contemplation of the signless; in all my undertakings I know as they truly are; I arouse the energetic antidote of the signless mind; my mind of sign-grasping I exhaust entirely. By means of this liberation-contemplation of the signless mind, there is no place for any mind of sign-grasping to dwell.' "A bhikṣu speaks thus: 'I cultivate the practice of decisive understanding; using the decisive Dharma as antidote, my mind of doubt I exhaust entirely. By means of this practice of decisive understanding, there is no place for any mind of doubt to dwell.' Rather, observing only the mind of decisive understanding present before him, the mind of doubt therefore does not arise in that place. Why? Because the mind of decisive understanding is the cause of release from that place. Such are called the six realms of antidotal release. "Furthermore, the seven factors of awakening (sapta bodhyaṅgāni) are taught by the Buddha: the awakening-factor of mindfulness (smṛti-saṃbodhyaṅga), the awakening-factor of investigation of dharmas (dharmavicaya-saṃbodhyaṅga), the awakening-factor of energy (vīrya-saṃbodhyaṅga), the awakening-factor of joy (prīti-saṃbodhyaṅga), the awakening-factor of tranquility (praśrabdhi-saṃbodhyaṅga), the awakening-factor of concentration (samādhi-saṃbodhyaṅga), and the awakening-factor of equanimity (upekṣā-saṃbodhyaṅga).

"Furthermore, the eight liberations (aṣṭa vimokṣāḥ) are taught by the Buddha: the liberation in which, having form internally, one observes forms externally; the liberation in which, having no form internally, one observes forms externally; the liberation of the beautiful, abiding in completeness; the liberation of the sphere of boundless space; the liberation of the sphere of boundless consciousness; the liberation of the sphere of nothingness; the liberation of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception (naivasaṃjñānāsaṃjñāyatana); and the liberation of the cessation of perception and feeling (nirodhasamāpatti).

"Furthermore, the eight masteries (aṣṭa abhibhvāyatanāni) are taught by the Buddha: having form internally, observing few forms externally — upon making this observation, superior knowledge and vision arise — this is a mastery; having form internally, observing many forms externally — upon making this observation, superior knowledge and vision arise — this is a mastery; having no form internally, observing few forms externally — upon making this observation, superior knowledge and vision arise — this is a mastery; having no form internally, observing many forms externally — upon making this observation, superior knowledge and vision arise — this is a mastery; having no form internally, observing blue forms externally — one observes, for example, utpala flowers and blue-colored garments; in both aspects of blue, all is blue-manifesting, blue-appearing, blue-luminous, extensive and pure — upon making this observation, superior knowledge and vision arise — this is a mastery; having no form internally, observing yellow forms externally — one observes, for example, kṛṣṇākara flowers and yellow-colored garments; in both aspects of yellow, all is yellow-manifesting, yellow-appearing, yellow-luminous, extensive and pure — upon making this observation, superior knowledge and vision arise — this is a mastery; having no form internally, observing red forms externally — one observes, for example, bandhūka flowers and red-colored garments; in both aspects of red, all is red-manifesting, red-appearing, red-luminous, extensive and pure — upon making this observation, superior knowledge and vision arise — this is a mastery; having no form internally, observing white forms externally — one observes, for example, white flowers and white-colored garments; in both aspects of white, all is white-manifesting, white-appearing, white-luminous, extensive and pure — upon making this observation, superior knowledge and vision arise — this is a mastery. Such are called the eight masteries.

Such dharmas as these the Buddha, with a mind of compassionate kindness, broadly proclaimed to beings in proper accordance with principle, causing beings to cultivate practice in accordance with the teachings, to perform the holy life, and to benefit and bring joy to the world of devas and humans.

"Furthermore, the nine abodes of beings (nava sattāvāsāḥ) are taught by the Buddha: various bodies with various perceptions — namely humans and devas of the desire realm — this is an abode of beings; various bodies with one perception — namely the first dhyāna heaven — this is an abode of beings; one body with various perceptions — namely the second dhyāna heaven — this is an abode of beings; one body with one perception — namely the third dhyāna heaven — this is an abode of beings; the heaven of the sphere of boundless space is an abode of beings; the heaven of the sphere of boundless consciousness is an abode of beings; the heaven of the sphere of nothingness is an abode of beings; the heaven of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception is an abode of beings; and the heaven of beings without perception (asaṃjñisattva) is an abode of beings. Such dharmas as these the Buddha, with a mind of compassionate kindness, broadly proclaimed to beings in proper accordance with principle, causing beings to cultivate practice in accordance with the teachings, to perform the holy life, and to benefit and bring joy to the world of devas and humans.

"Furthermore, the ten complete practices are taught by the Buddha: indestructible right view, indestructible right intention, indestructible right speech, indestructible right action, indestructible right livelihood, indestructible right effort, indestructible right forbearance, indestructible right concentration, indestructible right liberation, and indestructible right knowledge. Such dharmas as these the Buddha, with a mind of compassionate kindness, broadly proclaimed to beings in proper accordance with principle, causing beings to cultivate practice in accordance with the teachings, to perform the holy life, and to benefit and bring joy to the world of devas and humans."

At that time, the World-Honored One, knowing that the Venerable Śāriputra had fittingly taught the Dharma to the bhikṣus, rose with equanimity from his peaceful reclining rest and addressed the Venerable Śāriputra, saying: "Well done! Well done! Śāriputra! As you have said, this is what the Buddha has taught. This Dharma is called the 'Great Assembly of Dharma-gates.' In the final age of the world, it will be of great benefit to sentient beings. In the final age of the world, my bhikṣus who cultivate the holy life should receive, retain, recite, proclaim, and disseminate this Great Assembly of Dharma-gates. Moreover, Śāriputra, in the final age of the world, should a person obtain this Great Assembly of Dharma-gates and receive and retain it, that person, in the Dharma of future Buddhas, will be a faithful son of good family; in the Buddha's teaching and Dharma, he will give rise to deep love and delight, broadly open and comprehend it, his mind filled with great joy."

At that time, when the bhikṣus heard the Buddha praise this Great Assembly of Dharma-gates, they rejoiced and faithfully received it, paid homage to the Buddha, and withdrew.
