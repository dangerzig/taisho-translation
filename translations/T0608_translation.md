# 小道地經
## Minor Stages of the Path Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0608

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by Zhi Yao (支曜)

---

A practitioner who seeks cessation and is unable to attain it does so because of four causes and conditions. What are these four? First, relying on one's own goodness without understanding how to guard the precepts and discipline the body. Second, because one does not guard the precepts, wise thought does not arise. Because wise thought does not arise, one does not know the body. Because one does not know the body, the mind becomes confused. Third, not understanding the sūtras. Because one does not understand the sūtras, one lacks clarity. Because one lacks clarity, the mind becomes doubtful. Fourth, not frequently examining and reckoning how one's life, merit, and days are running out, and being complacent about it. Because of these four causes and conditions, one does not attain cessation.

A practitioner who seeks cessation and wishes to attain it must first understand two things in sitting and practice: first, exhalation; second, cessation. These also depend on two causes and conditions: first, birth; second, death. What is exhalation? What is cessation? When thought arises, it is exhalation. When thought ceases, it is cessation. What is birth? What is death? When thought is extinguished, it is birth. When thought arises, it is death. One must first understand these causes and conditions in order to be able to discriminate and understand the arising and ending of causes and conditions.

The matter involves four pairs. What are these four? First, not knowing how to eat, eating too much, not learning, not exercising restraint, craving flavor beyond sufficiency. Second, the mind follows after forms without carefully examining and reckoning, seeking much and desiring for oneself, planting the root of suffering. Third, the alert mind is obstructed by hindrances, sleeping too much, losing one's original mindfulness, and in a crooked direction planting seeds in dreams. Fourth, doubt and confusion cause evil to increase daily, leading to double-tongued speech, falling into error, false anger and hatred, with body and speech out of accord. Because of these, one does not fall into dhyāna-absorption: how then could one draw near to dhyāna?

One should constantly and repeatedly contemplate the arising and ceasing, the beginning and end, of the breath. With what kind of mind should one contemplate and discriminate? This too involves four causes and conditions: first, drawing near to a good teacher; second, knowing and receiving instruction without error; third, diligently reciting the sūtras, training the mind morning and night; fourth, guarding the precepts without departing from the Dharma. Then cessation is easily attained.

The body has four ailments: sometimes earth is excessive and the body cannot be at ease; sometimes water is excessive and the body cannot be at ease; sometimes fire is excessive and the body cannot be at ease; sometimes wind is excessive and the body cannot be at ease. When these four are balanced, the body can be still.

The mind has four ailments: first, when delusion is excessive, the mind cannot be still; second, when anger is excessive, the mind cannot be still; third, when lust is excessive, the mind cannot be still; fourth, when doubt is excessive, the mind cannot be still. If these four are not at ease, the mind cannot be still.

The breath also has four ailments: sometimes seeking is excessive and the breath cannot be still; sometimes thoughts are excessive and the breath cannot be still; sometimes excitement is excessive and the breath cannot be still; sometimes panting is excessive and the breath cannot be still. When a practitioner is apart from these causes and conditions, they attain concentration of mind.

If the body is swollen, scabbed, or corpulent, and one wishes to sit but the body cannot be at ease; or if one has eaten too much and fire arises, the body cannot be at ease; or if one has drunk too much and water arises, the body becomes heavy and the eyes are sticky, and the body cannot be at ease; or if after eating much one eats again, craving flavor beyond sufficiency without learning or restraint, wind arises and one cannot be at ease; the same is said of eating too little.

If delusion is excessive, one should not frequently enter among crowds and gatherings. One should first recite the sūtras and not seek much hearing, but guard oneself well. If anger is excessive, one should not dwell at home or where there is little to be had. If lust is excessive, one should not watch entertainments or beautiful forms. If doubt is excessive, one should not frequently listen to fine words and pleasant speech, but should always guard oneself, contemplate, and hold oneself accountable. If seeking is excessive, one should always be mindful of impermanence and apply the mind to it in sitting and rising. If thoughts are excessive, one should always practice confirming that all that one thinks of is the root of suffering. If excitement is excessive, one should reckon that it cannot last long and that suffering comes afterward, and should control it as one would an illness. If panting and sighing are excessive, one should always harmonize the mind and not frequently speak rough words, for sitting and creating offense. If a practitioner does not recognize these causes and conditions, he will never draw near to the Way. If he can control these, wise thought will gradually increase, and the Way is easily attained.

A practitioner who seeks the Way must first know that past thoughts and matters have already passed, and should no longer think of them. Why? Because further knowing is like planting seeds. Just as when one plants grain, planting rice one thinks of harvesting rice, planting beans one thinks of harvesting beans. Why? Because it is for the sake of producing. Thought is also like this: planting thought produces thought. Everything is gathered in the ten directions, and one must receive the consequences of calamity and merit, with no escape from suffering. Falling into killing plants the seed of killing; stealing plants the seed of stealing; sexual misconduct plants the seed of sexual misconduct; double-tongued speech plants the seed of double-tongued speech; harsh speech plants the seed of harsh speech; false speech plants the seed of false speech; frivolous speech plants the seed of frivolous speech; jealousy plants the seed of jealousy; anger plants the seed of anger; doubt plants the seed of doubt. Therefore, repeating these again and again, thought increases thought, and it is difficult to escape suffering.

How should one hold to in order to be free of all this suffering? One must enter dhyāna-absorption, for by no longer planting these ten evils, even though there may be remaining seeds, they will eventually be exhausted. Why? It is like planting grain: though one harvests much, if one does not plant again but only gradually eats the harvest, though it takes a long time, the eating will not stop, yet eventually it will be exhausted. Dhyāna-absorption is also like this. Why? Because one no longer plants. Having entered dhyāna-absorption, offenses gradually diminish. Why? Gradually, dhyāna-absorption creates merit; as merit arises, all evils come to an end. Simply planting the seed of the Way and being mindful of it, the Way arises and wisdom comes. With wisdom, one can give life to others and also save oneself.

A practitioner who seeks the Buddha's Way and in this present life wishes to clearly understand the practice of the mind must rely on three kinds of mindfulness: mindfulness of the past, mindfulness of the future, and mindfulness of the present; and there is mindfulness leading to merit and mindfulness leading to wrongdoing. Sometimes, while reading the sūtras or practicing meditation, one suddenly recalls a past event, such as being insulted by someone, or having wronged someone, or being drawn to a beautiful form. Through such recollection, thought arises and creates a beginning and an end, further increasing wrongdoing, unable to restrain itself. From such causes and conditions, one gains wrongdoing, which becomes the root of suffering: this is called past wrongdoing-mindfulness. Sometimes, from meditation or reading the sūtras, a good thought suddenly arises, and one reflects on the suffering and happiness of one's past conduct, contemplating and understanding impermanence: this is called past merit-mindfulness.

Sometimes, while at rest, a disordered thought suddenly arises, thinking of abnormal things, losing one's original mindfulness. Craving lust and seeking much, one then thinks as though one will never die: this is called future wrongdoing-mindfulness. Sometimes, if one attains stillness, a good thought of planting seeds arises, and from these causes and conditions wisdom increases: this is called future merit-mindfulness.

While properly dwelling at home, guarding oneself and keeping the precepts, a crooked thought arises. One should think: "Keeping many domestic animals only increases worry and breaks the precepts": this is called present wrongdoing-mindfulness. While dwelling at home, guarding oneself and keeping the precepts, one further increases good thoughts, constantly wishing to leave: this is called present merit-mindfulness.

One who seeks the Buddha's Way should first clearly understand these matters of wrongdoing and merit, and only then can wisdom increase. If one seeks arhatship, one severs all of these: this is seeking the Buddha's Way. Simply wishing to increase merit and wisdom to seek arhatship, one simply wishes to enter dhyāna-absorption to extinguish evil; wisdom comes afterward. To seek buddhahood and increase merit, one must hear much. For wisdom, one must recite the sūtras. To know the essentials, one must guard the precepts. Guarding the precepts enables one to understand the sūtras, and to benefit others as well as oneself.

A practitioner who seeks the Buddha's Way and in this present life wishes to understand the practice of the bodhisattva's mind must further know these three types of precepts. First, one should know how to keep the precepts and also guard the precepts. Second, one should know how not to violate the precepts and also how to endure the precepts. Third, one should know the precepts, understand the precepts, be capable of the precepts, and also protect the precepts. The first, knowing how to keep the precepts: if a person has a spouse and children, dwells at home, and always keeps the fast without fail, this is called keeping the precepts. Being alone without a spouse, guarding oneself without deviating, this is called guarding the precepts. The second, knowing how not to violate the precepts: if a person sees with the eyes and hears with the ears and is able not to fall into sounds and forms and all the rest, this is called not violating the precepts. When enduring the cold and hardship of practicing the Way, and further being insulted by others, one is able not to lose one's original mindfulness: this is called enduring the precepts, which also corresponds to patience. The third, knowing the precepts: knowing what precepts a particular person keeps, this is called knowing the precepts. Understanding the precepts: knowing that a particular person delights in the Way, but is criticized and envied by parents, relatives, and acquaintances, one does not repeatedly explain the precepts among the crowd. Being capable of the precepts: knowing what practice a person is able to do, assigning tasks according to their strength, and causing them not to fail, whether increasing or decreasing, applying the medicine to match the illness, this is called being capable of the precepts. Protecting the precepts: one should protect all, comply with and follow others to gain their confidence, be apart from bad company with a mind of vigilance, and wish to speak of the faults of all people in the ten directions, human and non-human. Whether among entertainments or among sensual attractions, one is able to teach a little or more, speaking good words, able not to disturb the mind, and further causing merit: this is called protecting the precepts. One who seeks the Buddha's Way through the bodhisattva's practice must know these things, and only then can one save others and also save oneself, benefit others and also benefit oneself.
