# 八關齋經
## Sūtra on the Eight-Precept Observance

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0089

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Jùqú Jīngshēng (沮渠京聲)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus: "I shall now teach the sacred eight-precept observance. Listen well, listen well, and reflect carefully upon it. I shall now teach it." They replied: "Yes, World-Honored One!" The bhikṣus then received the teaching from the Buddha.

The World-Honored One said: "Bhikṣus! If a faithful son or daughter of good family wishes to learn the sacred eight-precept observance, you should instruct them. The person named should speak thus:

'Just as an arhat, for the duration of life, does not kill living beings, does not teach others to kill, harbors no resentful or hateful thoughts, always feels a sense of shame, and has a compassionate mind showing kindness toward all sentient beings: so I, of such-and-such name and such-and-such surname, instructed by an arhat, from this day forward, as I wish, shall no longer kill living beings, shall harbor no resentful or hateful thoughts, shall always feel a sense of shame, and shall have a compassionate mind showing kindness toward all sentient beings.

'Just as an arhat, for the duration of life, does not steal, delights in giving, does not teach others to steal, and always takes pleasure in quiet places: so I, of such-and-such name and such-and-such surname, instructed by an arhat, as I wish, shall no longer steal, shall always be generous, and shall delight in dwelling in quiet places.

'Just as an arhat, for the duration of life, does not practice impure conduct, always cultivates the brahma conduct, pure and undefiled, delighting in it: so I, of such-and-such name and such-and-such surname, instructed by an arhat, from this day forward shall no longer engage in sexual misconduct, and shall be pure and undefiled.

'Just as an arhat, for the duration of life, does not speak falsely, always practices careful truthfulness, most honored, most valued, esteemed by elders and revered by the world: so I, of such-and-such name and such-and-such surname, instructed by that arhat, from this day forward shall never again speak falsely, nor teach others to practice false speech, but shall practice careful truthfulness, honored by the world as an elder, not engaging in false speech.

'Just as an arhat does not drink intoxicants: so I, of such-and-such name and such-and-such surname, from this day forward, as I wish, shall not drink intoxicants, nor teach others to drink intoxicants.

'Just as an arhat, for the duration of life, does not violate the observance and eats at the proper time: so I, of such-and-such name and such-and-such surname, for this one day and one night, as I wish, shall not violate the observance, nor teach others to violate it, and shall eat at the proper time.

'Just as an arhat, for the duration of life, does not sit or lie on high and fine beds: so I, of such-and-such name and such-and-such surname, for this one day and one night, shall not sit on high and broad beds, nor teach others to do so.

'Just as an arhat, for the duration of life, does not practice singing, dancing, or theatrical entertainment, nor wear decorations or perfumes or anoint the body: for this one day and one night, I shall not practice singing, dancing, or theatrical entertainment, nor wear decorations or perfumes or anoint the body.'

"Thus cultivating the sacred eight-precept observance, the merit within these eight-precept observances is immeasurable. One cannot say how much merit, how much virtue, how much karmic reward there is, for the accumulated merit is incalculable. Bhikṣus! It is like the five great rivers that all flow to one place: the Gaṅgā, the Yamunā, the Sindhu, the Aciravatī, and the Sarabhū. The water that flows from them is immeasurable. One cannot say how much water there is, how many jars, how many thousands or hundreds of thousands of jars of water. Just so, the merit of the sacred eight-precept observance is immeasurable. One cannot say how much merit, how much virtue, how much reward there is, for this great merit is incalculable."

At that time, the bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully received and practiced it.
