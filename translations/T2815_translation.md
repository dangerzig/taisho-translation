# Commentary on the Awakening of Faith

## *Qǐxìn Lùn Zhù* (起信論註)

Taishō 2815

Author unknown.

*Note: This is a fragmentary sub-commentary (shù) on the Awakening of Mahāyāna Faith (Dàchéng Qǐxìn Lùn, T1666), one of the most influential treatises in East Asian Buddhism, traditionally attributed to Aśvaghoṣa. The sub-commentary follows the standard Chinese exegetical format, quoting passages from the root treatise (marked "The treatise says," lùn yuē) and then providing explanatory glosses (marked "Commentary says," shù yuē). It covers the treatise's central doctrines: the one mind and its two gates (the gate of mind as suchness, zhēnrú mén, and the gate of mind as arising-and-ceasing, shēngmiè mén), the relationship of original awakening (běnjué) and actualized awakening (shǐjué), the ālayavijñāna as the union of the unconditioned and conditioned, the doctrine of emptiness and non-emptiness of suchness, and the six kinds of defiled mind. The fragmentary text lacks both beginning and ending, suggesting it was recovered from a partial manuscript.*

---

### The Main Body of the Treatise

The treatise says: "There is a dharma able to give rise to the roots of Mahāyāna faith; therefore it should be explained."

**Commentary**: Below, the second section sets forth the main body. Within it there are two parts: first, presenting the benefit and promising the explanation; second, the actual explanation of what is taught. This is the first part. "The treatise says" distinguishes the treatise from sūtras and identifies the explaining teaching. "There is a dharma" and so on presents the dharma explained, showing that it has surpassing benefit.

The explaining teaching contains this dharma explained: the one mind, two gates, three greatnesses, and such. It is able to give rise to the Mahāyāna faith-root of sentient beings. This explaining teaching should certainly be expounded. "Mahāyāna faith" means to have faith in one's own nature, to know that the mind moves in delusion and that there are no objects before it. This faith is certain, able to sustain and nurture both one's own and others' wholesome qualities; therefore it is called "faith-root."

### The Five-Part Division

The treatise says: "The explanation has five parts."

**Commentary**: Next, the second section gives the actual exposition. Within it there are three: first, presenting the number as a general heading; second, listing the names according to the number; third, explaining the content according to the names. This is the first.

The treatise says: "What are the five? [They are:] the part on causes and conditions, the part establishing the meaning, the part on interpretation, the part on the practice of faith, and the part on the benefits of encouraging practice."

**Commentary**: This lists the names according to the number. "Not arising by itself, the composition must have a reason": this is called "causes and conditions." "Part" indicates distinctness from other sections. Indicating distinctness from other sections, it is therefore called "part." Once the occasion has arisen, one briefly outlines the essentials to engender faith: this is called "the part establishing the meaning." The essentials being brief, they next require expanded interpretation to produce understanding: this is called "the part on interpretation." Having produced understanding through interpretation, one next needs to undertake practice; to cause practice, this is called "the part on the practice of faith." Although these are the procedures of practice, for those of dull faculties and lax effort, one presents benefits to encourage practice: this is called "the part on the benefits of encouraging practice."

### The Causes and Conditions

**Commentary**: "Causes and conditions" means: the principle of "why," the meaning of "for what purpose," and the sense of "giving rise to." Among the eight causes and conditions, the first is a general cause; the remaining seven are specific causes. The general cause has three aspects: (1) it serves as the "why" common to all treatises; (2) it serves as the "for what purpose" common to all capacities (not only for those of indeterminate disposition); (3) it serves as the "giving rise to" common to all parts (not only for the establishing-meaning part). The interpretation has three kinds: (1) displaying correct meaning, (2) counteracting wrong attachment, (3) distinguishing the path-characteristics of setting forth and progressing. "Displaying correct meaning" means correctly interpreting the Mahāyāna dharma-meaning that has been established. What is displayed is not wrong, hence "correct meaning"; elucidating it through teaching is called "displaying." Once correct principle has been presented, emotional confusion is thereby dispelled: hence the second, "counteracting wrong attachment." Wrong views and deluded clinging are called "wrong attachment"; removing them through teaching is called "counteracting." Once wrong attachment is dispelled, one ascends the stages toward what is correct: hence the third, "distinguishing the path-characteristics of setting forth and progressing."

### The One Mind and Two Gates

The treatise says: "Displaying correct meaning: in dependence on the one-mind dharma, there are two kinds of gates."

**Commentary**: Below, the third section explains the content according to the names. Explaining these three names, it is divided into three. First, interpreting "displaying correct meaning," which is further divided into two: the general interpretation (interpreting the general establishment above) and the specific interpretation. The general interpretation is further divided into three: first, opening the gates in dependence on the dharma; second, listing the two gates; third, showing that the two gates encompass all. This is the first. "The one source-mind" is the tathāgatagarbha-mind. It contains two meanings: (1) In terms of the gate of the essence free from characteristics: the one mind. Its nature is neither defiled nor pure, without differentiation. Based on this, the sūtras say "sentient beings are nirvāṇa," "afflictions are bodhi," "ordinary beings and Maitreya are of one suchness," and so forth. (2) In terms of the gate of arising and ceasing through following conditions: following ignorance-perfuming, it transforms into the defiled and pure. Though the defiled and pure are formed, the nature of the defiled and pure remains forever unmoved. Precisely because it does not move, it can produce the defiled and pure. Therefore "not moving" is also within the gate of movement: this is the meaning of "original awakening within arising-and-ceasing."

The treatise says: "What are the two? The first is the gate of mind as suchness (*xīn zhēnrú mén*). The second is the gate of mind as arising-and-ceasing (*xīn shēngmiè mén*)."

**Commentary**: This lists the two gates. The essence itself, free from characteristics, is called the gate of suchness. Following conditions and undergoing change is called the gate of arising-and-ceasing. Although the essence is not different, the meanings are distinct. Because the meanings are distinct, they are called "gates."

The treatise says: "These two kinds of gates each comprehensively encompass all dharmas."

**Commentary**: Below, the two gates encompass all. Within this there are two parts: first, direct statement; second, explanation. This is the first. The one mind encompasses all worldly dharmas and so forth. Above, through the one mind the two gates were explained; these are the general characteristics of the defiled and pure. Beyond the general characteristics there is nothing separate. Therefore the defiled and pure are all encompassed by the general characteristics. The gate of suchness comprehensively encompasses all dharmas. The gate of arising-and-ceasing: this very suchness, united with conditions, transforms into the defiled and pure, yet never loses its original suchness-nature. Therefore the gate of specific characteristics also encompasses suchness. Hence it too encompasses all dharmas.

The treatise says: "What does this mean? Because these two gates are inseparable from each other."

**Commentary**: This is the explanation. "Why is it" that the two gates, being separate, can each follow the other? If originally one mind, how can each encompass all? Hence the answer: "Because these two gates are inseparable from each other." Because the gate of suchness is the mind's principle-essence, and the gate of arising-and-ceasing is the mind's phenomenal characteristics, and because principle and phenomena are inseparable: raising principle encompasses phenomena, raising phenomena encompasses principle. Therefore the two gates each comprehensively encompass all.

### Suchness as Empty and Not-Empty

The treatise says: "What are the two [aspects of suchness]? The first is 'truly empty' (*rúshí kōng*), because it can ultimately reveal the real. The second is 'truly not-empty' (*rúshí bùkōng*), because it has its own essence endowed with the meritorious qualities of the undefiled nature."

The treatise says: "What is called 'empty': from the beginning, all defiled dharmas are incompatible [with suchness], being apart from all characteristics of differentiated dharmas, because there are no false and deluded mental thoughts."

The treatise says: "One should know that the self-nature of suchness is not the characteristic of existence, not the characteristic of non-existence, not the characteristic of not-existence, not the characteristic of not-non-existence, not the characteristic of both existence and non-existence, not the characteristic of oneness, not the characteristic of difference, not the characteristic of not-oneness, not the characteristic of not-difference, not the characteristic of both oneness and difference."

The treatise says: "In summary: because all sentient beings have deluded minds, thought after thought making discriminations, all [such discriminations] are incompatible [with suchness]; therefore it is called 'empty.'"

The treatise says: "If one departs from the deluded mind, there is truly nothing that can be called 'empty.'"

The treatise says: "What is called 'not-empty': having already revealed that the dharma-essence is empty of delusion..."

The treatise says: "This is precisely the true mind ... constant, eternal, unchanging, the pure dharmas being complete: this is called 'not-empty.'"

The treatise says: "Yet there are no characteristics that can be grasped, because it is the domain of departing from thought, known only through realization."

### Mind as Arising-and-Ceasing: The Ālayavijñāna

The treatise says: "Mind as arising-and-ceasing: in dependence on the tathāgatagarbha, there is the arising-and-ceasing mind. What is called 'the non-arising and non-ceasing united with the arising-and-ceasing.'"

The treatise says: "The non-arising and non-ceasing united with the arising-and-ceasing: neither one nor different."

The treatise says: "This is called the ālayavijñāna (*ālíyē shí*, 阿黎耶識)."

### Original Awakening and Actualized Awakening

**Commentary**: In the previous outline, "awakening" refers to original awakening, the nature-virtue of all beings. "Non-awakening" belongs to actualized awakening, within the gate of following conditions. "Awakening" in terms of nature-virtue [means that] in relation to defilement, one obtains the name by reversal. The treatise below says "original awakening": because this nature-virtue can bring about the fruit-stage, it is called "original awakening," which, returning to the original source, reveals actualized awakening as the dharma-body. Therefore the nature-virtue is called "original awakening," and this then illuminates actualized awakening.

The treatise says: "The meaning of actualized awakening (*shǐjué*): in contrast to original awakening (*běnjué*)."

The treatise says: "Furthermore, because one awakens to the source of mind, it is called 'ultimate awakening.' Because one does not awaken to the source of mind, it is called 'not-yet-ultimate awakening.'"

The treatise says: "If one attains no-thought, then one knows the characteristics of the mind: arising, abiding, changing, and ceasing. Because of no-thought and such."

The treatise says: "Yet in reality there is no difference of actualized awakening, because the four characteristics exist simultaneously, each without independent establishment; originally equal, they are of one and the same awakening."

### The Six Kinds of Defiled Mind

The treatise says: "The defiled mind has six kinds."

**Commentary**: Next, the defiled mind and its characteristics of conditioned arising. Within this there are two: first, a general heading; second, specific explanation. This is the first. This explains the sentence above about "having defiled mind due to being stained by ignorance." The defiled mind has six kinds. These six defiled [minds] are the five kinds of mentation (*manas*) from above plus consciousness (*vijñāna*). The previous passage described them from fine to coarse in order of dependent arising; now, to also show the principles of counteraction and severance, the order is from coarse to fine.

The treatise says: "What are the six? The first is the defilement associated with attachment (*zhí xiāngyìng rǎn*), which is abandoned through the liberation of the two vehicles and through the stage of faith-correspondence, by progressive distancing."

**Commentary**: The first, "defilement associated with attachment," is the coarse attachment of "I" and "mine" arising together with the above-mentioned consciousness (the sixth consciousness). It defiles the pure mind and is therefore called "defilement." For śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas at the stage of no-more-learning, all afflictions of the views and cultivation paths are ultimately abandonable. For bodhisattvas at the stage of faith-correspondence, having attained the gate of personal emptiness, afflictions of views and love no longer manifest; hence "progressive distancing" (not yet abandoning latent tendencies).

The treatise says: "The second is the defilement of unbroken [continuity] associated [with mental factors] (*bùduàn xiāngyìng rǎn*), which is progressively abandoned through skillful means of cultivation at the stage of faith-correspondence, and ultimately abandoned at the stage of the pure mind."

**Commentary**: The "defilement of unbroken continuity" is the second of the five mentation-aspects, the "continuing consciousness." Its dharma-attachment continues unbroken, hence "unbroken." It defiles the pure mind, hence "defilement." From the first bhūmi onward, bodhisattvas in the three stages of worthiness cultivate the contemplation of consciousness-only, investigating the real wisdom. The stage of the pure mind (first bhūmi and above), attaining the undefiled pure mind, realizes the three absences of own-nature and the all-pervading suchness. The dharma-attachment of discrimination, both seeds and manifest activity, is then ultimately abandoned.

The treatise says: "The third is the defilement associated with discriminating wisdom (*fēnbié zhì xiāngyìng rǎn*), which is progressively distanced from beginning at the stage of full precepts, up to ultimate abandonment at the stage of skillful means without characteristics."

**Commentary**: The "discriminating wisdom" is the third of the five mentation-aspects, the "wisdom-consciousness," which deliberates on the defiled and pure. Because it still discriminates "this is pure dharma, this is defiled dharma," it is called "discriminating wisdom." From the second bhūmi to the sixth bhūmi, entering contemplation and attending to principle, these discriminating aspects are progressively eliminated. At the seventh bhūmi, through sustained contemplation, this dharma-attachment permanently ceases to manifest. The "stage of full precepts" is the second bhūmi, where the three collections of pure precepts are complete. The "stage of skillful means without characteristics" is the seventh bhūmi, where one constantly abides in contemplation of the signless through sustained attention.

The treatise says: "The fourth is the defilement of manifesting form, not associated [with mental factors] (*xiànshè bùxiāngyìng rǎn*), which is abandoned through the stage of sovereignty over form."

**Commentary**: The "manifesting form" is the third of the five mentation-aspects. The moving mind manifests objects, hence "manifesting form." It defiles the pure mind, hence "defilement." At the eighth bhūmi one attains sovereignty over characteristics. Form-nature follows the mind, hence "sovereignty over form." One can purify the objective realm and realize the suchness of sovereignty over characteristics. At the eighth bhūmi, the bodhisattva acts spontaneously and freely. Before the eighth bhūmi, all wisdom arises in dependence on ignorance. From the eighth bhūmi onward, all wisdom arises from suchness itself, hence "sovereignty."

The treatise says: "The fifth is the defilement of the ability to perceive, not associated [with mental factors] (*néngjiàn bùxiāngyìng rǎn*), which is abandoned through the stage of sovereignty over mind."

**Commentary**: This is the second of the five mentation-aspects, the "transforming consciousness." In dependence on the moving mind, the ability to perceive is established. It defiles the pure mind, hence "perceiving defilement." At the ninth bhūmi one attains unobstructed wisdom, fully knowing the dense forest of all beings' mental activities, with self and other unobstructed: this is called "sovereignty over mind." The ignorance that obstructs the mind permanently ceases to manifest, hence "abandoned."
