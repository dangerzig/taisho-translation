# 事師法五十頌
## Fifty Verses on the Method of Serving the Teacher

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1687

Compiled by Aśvaghoṣa (馬鳴). Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Sūryakīrti (日稱) and others.

---

> Drawn from the sūtras, vinaya, and esoteric teachings,
> This brief guide to the rites of serving the teacher is set forth.
> Having heard it and delighting with a pure mind,
> One shall obtain the vajra-wisdom of the Tathāgata.
>
> If one makes prostrations three times daily
> To the teacher who has bestowed the abhiṣeka,
> This is as if one has already made offerings
> To all the tathāgatas of the ten directions.
>
> Give rise to the utmost reverence,
> Join palms and hold flowers,
> Scatter them upon the maṇḍala,
> And prostrate with the head touching the feet.
>
> Whether the teacher is a householder
> Or newly ordained with full precepts,
> Place the sūtras and images before you,
> And all doubt and slander will cease.
>
> If the student is a renunciant,
> They should always serve with a pure mind.
> When the teacher is already seated, they should rise to greet,
> Except when giving the formal prostration.
>
> Both teacher and student
> Should mutually examine each other's capacity.
> If they do not first investigate,
> Both equally incur the offense of transgressing the Dharma.
>
> If angry, without compassion,
> Greedy, easily distracted,
> Arrogant and relying on family lineage:
> One should discerningly select with wisdom.
>
> Endowed with morality, patience, compassion, and wisdom,
> Revered and without flattery or crookedness,
> Versed in the esoteric rites and protocols,
> Well-read in all treatises and discourses,
> Accomplished in the characteristics of mantras
> And the activities of the maṇḍala,
> Having realized the ten suchnesses,
> With all faculties fully purified.
>
> If the seeker of the Dharma
> Generates contempt and disdain for the teacher,
> It is as if slandering all the tathāgatas,
> And one will always obtain suffering and distress.
>
> Through supreme ignorance one obtains present retribution:
> Seized by evil constellations, bound by grave illness,
> Oppressed by the king's law, stung by venomous snakes,
> Endangered by enemies, bandits, water, and fire, exploited by non-humans.
> Those Vināyakas constantly create obstacles.
> When one dies in this way, one falls into evil destinies.
>
> Do not cause the ācārya
> Even the slightest affliction.
> If through lack of wisdom one goes against him,
> One will certainly enter the Avīci Hell,
> Undergoing various extreme sufferings
> That are deeply terrifying to describe.
> Through slandering the ācārya,
> One will constantly abide therein.
> That ācārya
> Broadly upholds the treasury of the true Dharma.
> Therefore with single mind,
> Never generate contempt or disdain.
>
> Always toward the ācārya
> Serve and make offerings.
> Give rise to a mind of reverence,
> And obstacles and afflictions will be removed.
>
> Furthermore, toward the teacher
> Delight in practicing generosity and equanimity.
> Do not be stingy with one's own body,
> How much less with material possessions.
>
> Through immeasurable koṭis of kalpas
> Of courageous and diligent cultivation,
> Only now does one begin to realize bodhi:
> This is supremely rare.
>
> Well guard your profound vows,
> Make offerings to all tathāgatas.
> Revere the ācārya
> As equal to all buddhas.
>
> Whatever one possesses,
> The most supreme precious things,
> Seeking inexhaustible bodhi,
> Offer them with sincere mind.
>
> Giving to the Buddha and ācārya,
> Thought after thought constantly increasing:
> This is the most excellent field of merit;
> One will swiftly attain the fruit of bodhi.
>
> Such a seeker of the Dharma,
> Endowed with morality, patience, and merit,
> Who does not deceive the teacher,
> Shall obtain vajra-wisdom.
>
> If one steps upon the teacher's shadow,
> The offense is like destroying a stūpa.
> Regarding the teacher's bed, seat, and belongings,
> Riding or stepping over them, the offense exceeds this.
>
> When the teacher gives instructions,
> One should happily listen and accept.
> If oneself is unable to comply,
> Then politely explain with kind words.
>
> By relying upon the teacher,
> All one's undertakings will succeed:
> Present happiness and heavenly rebirth.
> How could one dare disobey the teacher's command!
>
> Guard the teacher's possessions
> As if they were one's own body and life.
> Toward those who attend upon the teacher,
> Always serve them respectfully as kin.
>
> One should not, before the teacher,
> Cover one's head or ride about,
> Cross one's legs, put hands on hips,
> Or sit and recline at ease.
> If circumstances require one to sit,
> Do not stretch out both feet.
> Always maintain proper deportment;
> When the teacher rises, rise swiftly.
>
> In the place of walking meditation,
> One should not follow with casual steps.
> Stand properly and attentively at the side,
> And do not spit or leave nasal discharge.
>
> Also, do not before the teacher
> Whisper or speak in secret,
> Or nearby engage in talk and laughter,
> Singing, dancing, chanting, and the like.
>
> Whether asked to sit or to rise,
> Each time bow with calm composure.
> If on a dangerous road,
> Inform the teacher and serve as guide ahead.
>
> Furthermore, one should not before the teacher
> Display signs of bodily fatigue,
> Crack the knuckles making sounds,
> Or lean upon pillars or walls.
>
> For washing clothes, bathing the feet,
> And bathing and the like,
> First inform the teacher.
> Do not let such activities be seen.
>
> Furthermore, one should not
> Casually speak the teacher's name.
> If someone insistently asks,
> One may indicate but a single syllable.
>
> If the teacher assigns tasks to be managed,
> One should watch for their dispatch.
> Whatever affairs are to be done,
> Remember and never forget them.
>
> When laughing, coughing, or yawning,
> Cover the mouth with the hand.
> If one has a matter to report,
> Bow and speak in gentle words.
>
> If a lay woman
> Comes with a pure mind to hear the Dharma,
> She should join palms with proper deportment,
> Gazing attentively at the teacher's face.
> Having heard, she should practice and uphold it,
> Abandoning pride,
> Always as when first married,
> With lowered gaze and modest demeanor.
>
> Toward personal adornments,
> No longer give rise to attachment.
> Whatever is not in accord with virtue,
> Reflect upon and keep far away.
>
> Always admire the teacher's virtues
> And do not spy upon small faults.
> By following accordingly, one attains accomplishment;
> Seeking faults will only harm oneself.
>
> Teaching the Dharma, ordaining disciples,
> Performing maṇḍala rites and homa,
> Dwelling in the same town as the teacher:
> Without permission, one should not do these.
> Whatever wealth or offerings
> Are obtained from teaching the Dharma,
> All should be presented to the teacher;
> Use only what is received in return.
>
> Fellow students and Dharma descendants
> Should not be taken as one's own disciples.
> Nor should one, before the teacher,
> Accept service and reverent homage from them.
>
> When presenting objects to the teacher,
> Hold them with both hands as an offering.
> If the teacher bestows something in return,
> Receive it reverently at the crown of the head.
>
> Devote yourself to cultivating correct practice,
> Always remembering and never forgetting.
> If others act contrary to the rules,
> Instruct them with loving words.
>
> If unable to carry out the teacher's instructions
> Due to illness or other conditions,
> One should pay respects and explain the reason;
> Then there will be no fault.
>
> Always cause the teacher to be pleased
> And keep away from matters of affliction.
> One should diligently practice this;
> For fear of prolixity, I do not elaborate further.
>
> The Vajra Tathāgata
> Has personally proclaimed it thus.
> As other teachings also make clear:
> By relying on the teacher, one attains accomplishment.
>
> If the student is pure
> And able to take refuge in the Three Treasures,
> Even if their life is about to end,
> One should still proclaim the essential Dharma for them
> And bestow the esoteric teachings,
> Making them a vessel of the true Dharma.
> But if one displays the signs and recites
> For the unworthy, one will incur a root transgression.
>
> If one can follow the teacher's practice and learning,
> Then all virtues will be accomplished.
> By the good causes I have gathered here,
> May all sentient beings swiftly become buddhas.
