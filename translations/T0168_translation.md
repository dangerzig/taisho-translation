# 太子墓魄經
## Sūtra of Prince Mūrdhagata

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0168

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. He addressed the bhikṣus:

"Long ago there was a king of Vārāṇasī. The king had one crown prince named Mūrdhagata. From birth he possessed boundless brilliance. He was handsome and exquisitely pure, without equal in the world. His parents marveled at him and nurtured and cared for him, waiting for him to grow up so they could formally name him. However, the prince kept his tongue still and did not speak for thirteen years. He was calm and unadorned in disposition. His will was like dead ashes and his mind like a withered tree. His eyes did not look upon forms, his ears did not listen to sounds. He appeared like a person who was mute, deaf, and blind. His parents became worried and weary of him.

"The king said to his queen: 'What shall we do? This child will be mocked by other countries.' The queen said to the king: 'We should summon physiognomists to read his signs and determine whether he will ever speak.' The king immediately summoned the brāhmaṇa teachers and had them examine the prince. The brāhmaṇas said: 'This child is not a being of this world. He is a baleful influence. Outwardly he is handsome, but inwardly he harbors ill omens. He will endanger the kingdom and destroy the lineage before long. He cannot be raised. He should be buried alive; he must be executed and killed. If this child is not removed, the royal line will be cut off.' The king said to his queen: 'What shall we do? If we do not remove this child now, I fear there will be no future crown prince.' The queen then submitted to the king's decision.

"The king summoned the ministers of the country and discussed the matter with them. One minister said: 'He should be exiled far away to the deep mountains where no one dwells.' One minister said: 'He should be sunk in deep water.' One minister said: 'We should simply follow the teacher's words: dig a deep pit and bury him alive.' The king followed this last minister's advice. He summoned more than three thousand outer garrison soldiers to dig a subterranean chamber. Provisions were provided and five servants assigned to attend him. The prince's clothing, necklaces, pearls, and treasures were all returned to the prince. The queen was heartbroken: 'I alone was without good fortune, and my ill-fated child has met with this calamity. It cannot be stopped.' She wept and choked with sobs, unable to contain herself.

"The prince was then sent to the main hall. Five hundred court ladies saw the prince, handsome and beautiful beyond compare, and all said: 'Why does the prince not speak, that he must be buried alive?' Five hundred attendant women saw the prince, handsome and beautiful, and all bowed before the prince, saying: 'Why do you not speak, that you must be buried alive?' Each performed music for the prince. The prince was silent, neither watching nor listening.

"The prince was then sent to the outer hall. Five hundred ministers saw the prince, handsome and beautiful beyond compare, and came before the great king to say: 'The prince is not a person who cannot speak. Let him be kept a while longer; he will surely speak before long. The brāhmaṇas' words should not be taken as certain.' The king said to the ministers: 'This is a matter of state. It is not for you to know.'

"When the chamber was completed, they came to escort the prince. The king told the servants: 'Place the prince on my four-view elephant carriage and let the people of the country come and see. The prince will surely speak. If he speaks, bring him back.' The prince then rode the carriage along the road. The country's venerable elders and ministers all prostrated themselves before the carriage, saying: 'Prince! Please just speak one word. If you do not speak, let this carriage roll over us.' The guards pushed them aside and let the carriage pass. They escorted the prince to the burial place. At that time, tens of thousands of people all followed the prince to the burial place and blocked the entrance of the chamber, so the prince could not go forward. The guards waved the people back. Just as the prince was about to go forward, birds in flight and beasts running came in alarm and gathered before the entrance, circling the chamber three times and again blocking the entrance, so the prince could not go forward. Then the prince raised his right hand, stood, and spoke: 'If I simply do not speak, I am to be buried alive. If I speak, I fear I will enter hell. The reason I did not speak was because I wished to keep my body safe, avoid harm, deliver my spirit, and escape from suffering. That is why I did not speak. Yet you believe deceptive and fraudulent words, taking me for truly deaf, blind, and mute.'

"At that time, the people heard the prince speak with an extraordinarily wondrous voice, rarely heard in the world. Those who were walking stopped; those who were sitting stood up. All said: 'The prince is so divine and holy!' All came forward and knocked their heads, begging for grace and forgiveness: 'Pardon our offense.' The servants, upon hearing this, were overjoyed and rushed to report to the great king: 'The prince has spoken! His voice reaches up to the azure heaven and down to the yellow springs. Birds in flight and beasts running all come and prostrate themselves to listen before the prince.'

"When the king heard that the prince had spoken, he was overjoyed. He immediately prepared the four-view elephant carriage with his queen and went to welcome the prince. The prince looked at his royal father, descended from the carriage, stepped aside from the road, made four bows, rose, and said: 'I have troubled and burdened my royal father to come from afar to welcome me. Today, father and son have already cast each other off. The bond of love has been broken, flesh and bone severed. Such is a grave breach of duty, not to be condoned.' The king said to the prince: 'That cannot be! You are a wise one. You should forgive my shortcomings. Let us return to the kingdom together. I shall give you the throne. I myself will step aside and retire.' The prince replied: 'I once was a king. Because my conduct had flaws and gaps, I fell down into hell for more than sixty thousand years, where I was boiled, steamed, flayed, and torn apart. The pain was unbearable. At that time, could my parents have known the severity of my suffering in hell? Could they have taken a share of my bodily pain? I dread and fear the suffering of hell. That is why I tied my tongue and did not speak for thirteen years, hoping to be freed from flaw, to remove filth and defilement, to transcend the dust, and not to meet again with sin. I wish to remove worry, cast off bonds. Life in this world is but a sojourn. One must not be lenient, for every day one moves further from the path. I shall soar high and depart far, and save myself from the world. The world is impermanent, hazy as a dream. The joys of home and family last but a moment. Pleasures are brief; worry and suffering are prolonged.'

"The king, knowing that the prince's will was firm and his resolve unshakable, permitted him to study the path. The prince thereupon abandoned the kingdom and renounced kingship. He entered the mountains to seek the path, devoting himself to meditation and concentration. When his life ended, he was born in the Tuṣita heaven. When his heavenly lifespan was complete, he descended and was born in the world as a prince of the king of Kapilavastu, and ultimately attained Buddhahood."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The Prince Mūrdhagata of that time is my present self. The royal father of that time is my present father Śuddhodana. The mother of that time is my present mother Māyā. The five servants who attended me are now Ājñāta-Kauṇḍinya and the others. The brāhmaṇa who wished to bury me alive is now Devadatta. Devadatta and I have borne enmity toward each other life after life." When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, all the disciples, devas, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, emperors, and people were all greatly joyful and bowed to the Buddha.
