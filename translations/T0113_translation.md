# 佛說難提釋經
## Sūtra of the Śākyan Nandiya Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0113

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmaruci (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the country of Kośala, near a nyagrodha tree. At that time, many bhikṣus had gathered in the Kālī lecture hall among the trees, sitting together to make robes for the Buddha. Before long the summer retreat would end. When the summer was complete, the Buddha himself said: "After the three months are finished and the robes are made, I shall go to a place of many people."

The Śākyan Nandiya heard that many bhikṣus had gathered in the Kālī lecture hall among the trees, sitting together to make robes for the Buddha, and that soon the summer retreat would end. When the summer was complete, the Buddha himself said: "After the three months are finished and the robes are made, I shall go to a place of many people."

The Śākyan Nandiya, having heard this, went to where the Buddha was. Having arrived, he prostrated at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side. Having sat, the Śākyan Nandiya addressed the Buddha: "Thus I have heard: many bhikṣus have gathered in the Kālī lecture hall among the trees, sitting together to make robes for the Buddha. Soon the summer retreat will end. When the summer is complete, the Buddha himself said: 'After the three months are finished and the robes are made, I shall go to a place of many people.' Hearing this, I became sorrowful and grieved. My food lost its taste, I could not distinguish the directions, the good Dharma I had heard I could no longer keep in mind, and the worldly tasks I was doing I could no longer attend to. Why? Because it will be very long before I again see the Buddha and the bhikṣus of pure conduct."

When the Śākyan Nandiya had finished speaking, the Buddha replied: "Nandiya! Whether you see my body or do not see my body, whether you see the bhikṣus of pure conduct or do not see them, you should always practice the five inner dharmas. What are the five? First, the mind should always have faith; abandon the faithless mind. Second, one should always practice pure conduct; abandon impure conduct. Third, one should always delight in generosity; abandon miserliness and greed. Fourth, one should always have wisdom; abandon delusion. Fifth, one should always be learned; do not delight in not learning.

"Nandiya! These are the five inner matters. Furthermore, there are six recollections. What are the six? First, recollection of the Buddha. Second, recollection of the Dharma. Third, recollection of the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. Fourth, recollection of moral conduct. Fifth, recollection of generosity. Sixth, recollection of the heavens.

"Nandiya! When a wise disciple recollects the virtues of the Buddha and all the qualities the Buddha possesses, the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Awakened One, the One Accomplished in Supernatural Powers, the One Who Has Gone Well, the Measureless One, the Unsurpassed One, the Teacher of Men, the Charioteer of the Dharma, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, the Awakened One who possesses these qualities: whether among gods, humans, māras, brahmās, śramaṇas, or brāhmaṇas, he has personally realized through wisdom that birth is exhausted, pure conduct has been established, what was to be done he himself knows, and with only this body he will not receive another existence. Nandiya! At that time, the wise disciple's mind does not cling to sensual desire, does not cling to anger, does not cling to delusion, and the mind becomes straight. When the wise disciple's mind is straight, one attains the meaning, attains the Dharma, sees the Dharma, and great delight and joy arise. Having joy, happiness arises. From happiness, the body becomes tranquil. When the body is tranquil, one attains peace. Having attained peace, one attains concentration. From concentration, one knows and sees as it truly is: this is the truth of suffering, this is the arising, this is the cessation, this is the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. Nandiya! When the wise disciple has right mindfulness amid what is wrong, and when thoughts of resentment arise, they are abandoned; when the mind has attachments, they are not accepted. This is the wise disciple's delight in the path of practice: constantly recollecting the virtues of the Buddha.

"Nandiya! When a wise disciple recollects the virtues of the Dharma taught by the Buddha: it brings great peace, it can be seen here and now, it is timeless, it can be transmitted, it can be grasped and maintained, the wise can penetrate it, one can emerge and be satisfied, one can rely on the Dharma and be liberated from one's condition. Nandiya! At that time, the wise disciple's mind does not cling to sensual desire, does not cling to anger, does not cling to delusion, and the mind becomes straight. When the wise disciple's mind is straight, one attains the meaning, attains the Dharma, sees the Dharma, and great delight and joy arise. Having joy, happiness arises. From happiness, the body becomes tranquil. When the body is tranquil, one attains a mind of peace. From peace, one attains concentration. From concentration, one knows and sees as it truly is: this is the truth of suffering, this is the arising, this is the cessation, this is the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. Nandiya! When the wise disciple has right mindfulness amid what is wrong, and when thoughts of resentment arise, they are abandoned; when the mind has attachments, they are not accepted. This is the wise disciple's delight in the path of practice: constantly recollecting the virtues of the Dharma.

"Nandiya! When a wise disciple recollects the virtues of the community of bhikṣus, the Buddha's assembly of disciples who possess purity, possess concentration, possess liberation, possess the knowledge and vision of liberation, who have practice and have attainment, who are the field of merit for the world: even a small offering to them brings immeasurable merit. Within the community there are stream-enterers, to be believed as stream-enterers; there are once-returners, to be believed as once-returners; there are non-returners, to be believed as non-returners; there are arhats, to be believed as arhats. These are the excellent men, four pairs of worthy ones, eight types of individuals, the foremost among people, the firmest among people, teachers among people, those among people who deserve respectful offerings. They are the supreme persons among the three realms! Nandiya! At that time, the wise disciple's mind does not cling to sensual desire, does not cling to anger, does not cling to delusion, and the mind becomes straight. When the wise disciple's mind is straight, one attains the meaning, attains the Dharma, sees the Dharma, and great delight and joy arise. Having joy, happiness arises. From happiness, the body becomes tranquil. When the body is tranquil, one attains a mind of peace. From peace, one attains concentration. From concentration, one knows and sees as it truly is: this is the truth of suffering, this is the arising, this is the cessation, this is the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. Nandiya! When the wise disciple has right mindfulness amid what is wrong, and when thoughts of resentment arise, they are abandoned; when the mind has attachments, they are not accepted. This is the wise disciple's delight in the path of practice: constantly recollecting the virtues of the community of bhikṣus.

"Nandiya! When a wise disciple recollects one's own virtues of moral conduct, the precepts bestowed by the Buddha are not to be violated, not to be taken lightly, not to be damaged, not to be played with, not to be tested. The wise, through moral conduct, attain concentration; from concentration they become free from craving. The mind does not cling to worldly things, just as a stone once broken can never be joined again. Nandiya! At that time, the wise disciple's mind does not cling to sensual desire, does not cling to anger, does not cling to delusion, and the mind becomes straight. When the wise disciple's mind is straight, one attains the meaning, attains the Dharma, sees the Dharma, and great delight and joy arise. Having joy, happiness arises. From happiness, the body becomes tranquil. When the body is tranquil, one attains a mind of peace. From peace, one attains concentration. From concentration, one knows and sees as it truly is: this is the truth of suffering, this is the arising, this is the cessation, this is the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. Nandiya! When the wise disciple has right mindfulness amid what is wrong, and when thoughts of resentment arise, they are abandoned; when the mind has attachments, they are not accepted. This is the wise disciple's delight in the path of practice: constantly recollecting the virtues of moral conduct.

"Nandiya! When a wise disciple recollects one's own virtues of generosity: 'How fortunate that I have been born as a human being! In the midst of miserliness and greed, I have been able to pull myself free, without any thought of jealousy, always delighting in giving, trusting in what is given, knowing that hereafter I shall certainly obtain immeasurable results from even a small gift. From giving, one attains the unconditioned.' Nandiya! At that time, the wise disciple's mind does not cling to sensual desire, does not cling to anger, does not cling to delusion, and the mind becomes straight. When the wise disciple's mind is straight, one attains the meaning, attains the Dharma, sees the Dharma, and great delight and joy arise. Having joy, happiness arises. From happiness, the body becomes tranquil. When the body is tranquil, one attains a mind of peace. From peace, one attains concentration. From concentration, one knows and sees as it truly is: this is the truth of suffering, this is the arising, this is the cessation, this is the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. Nandiya! When the wise disciple has right mindfulness amid what is wrong, and when thoughts of resentment arise, they are abandoned; when the mind has attachments, they are not accepted. This is the wise disciple's delight in the path of practice: constantly recollecting the virtues of generosity.

"Nandiya! When a wise disciple recollects the virtues of the heavens: the first, the Cātummahārājika; the second, the Trāyastriṃśa; the third, the Yāma; the fourth, the Tuṣita; the fifth, the Nirmāṇarati; the sixth, the Paranirmita-vaśavartin. Whether through faith, through purity, through learning, through generosity, or through wisdom, each has practices by which one ascends to these six heavens. 'I too can practice the five dharmas of faith, moral conduct, learning, generosity, and wisdom. From these causes and conditions, I may be reborn in heaven.' Nandiya! At that time, the wise disciple's mind does not cling to sensual desire, does not cling to anger, does not cling to delusion, and the mind becomes straight. When the wise disciple's mind is straight, one attains the meaning, attains the Dharma, sees the Dharma, and great delight and joy arise. Having joy, happiness arises. From happiness, the body becomes tranquil. When the body is tranquil, one attains a mind of peace. From peace, one attains concentration. From concentration, one knows and sees as it truly is: this is the truth of suffering, this is the arising, this is the cessation, this is the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. Nandiya! When the wise disciple has right mindfulness amid what is wrong, and when thoughts of resentment arise, they are abandoned; when the mind has attachments, they are not accepted. This is the wise disciple's delight in the path of practice: constantly recollecting the virtues of the heavens."

The Buddha spoke thus. The Śākyan Nandiya, greatly delighted, received the teaching, applied his mind, and constantly practiced these six recollections. He rose from his seat, placed his head at the Buddha's feet, and paid his respects.
