# 恒水經
## Sūtra of the Gaṅgā Waters

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0033

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Fǎjù (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha traveled together with the great saṃgha of bhikṣus, various disciples, and bodhisattvas to the banks of the Gaṅgā. Innumerable devas, humans, ghosts, spirits, nāgas, those who were neither human nor non-human, and those who had newly aroused the aspiration for the Way, each bearing flowers, incense, and musical instruments, all followed after the Buddha. When they arrived at the Gaṅgā, seats were arranged and all were seated; the entire assembly settled in stillness. It being the fifteenth day of the month, the time for reciting the precepts, Ānanda rose from his seat, arranged his robes, approached and made obeisance, touching his head to the Buddha's feet, then knelt upright with joined palms and addressed the Buddha: "The disciples are seated and at ease. May the Buddha please expound the sūtra of precepts." The Buddha remained silent and did not respond. Ānanda returned to his seat and waited a very long time. When midnight arrived, Ānanda again rose, knelt upright with joined palms, and addressed the Buddha: "The night is already half over. The disciples are all seated and at ease. We wish to hear the Buddha expound the sūtra of precepts." The Buddha again remained silent and did not respond. Ānanda returned to his seat and waited a great while. When the cock was about to crow, Ānanda again rose, knelt upright with joined palms, and addressed the Buddha: "The cock is about to crow. The disciples wish to hear the Buddha expound the sūtra of precepts."

The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "Human beings revolve through birth and death, coming and going through the five destinies; in the world this entails great toil and suffering. That people do not recognize and know the origins and ends of their former lives and past karma is entirely due to their minds and intentions not being upright. A human body is exceedingly difficult to obtain; having obtained a human body, to encounter and hear the Buddha's sūtras and precepts is again difficult. Having heard the Buddha's sūtras and precepts, to enter the Buddha's Way in faith is again difficult. Having entered the Buddha's Way, to uphold and maintain the sūtras and precepts is again difficult to achieve. The Buddha wishes to expound the sūtra of precepts; but at present there is one disciple in this assembly who is unable to uphold the Buddha's sūtras and precepts. For this reason the Buddha will not expound the sūtra of precepts."

Ānanda addressed the Buddha: "I do not know which disciple it is who does not uphold the Buddha's sūtras and precepts."

Mahāmaudgalyāyana, through the penetrating vision of samādhi, saw the disciple who did not uphold the precepts. He immediately rose, went to stand before him, and said: "You are a disciple of the Buddha yet are unable to uphold the precept-dharma. You are a person who has abandoned the Way and should not share the same seat as the venerable ones. You must rise and depart; you may not return to the midst of the assembly." The Buddha said to Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "You have done well in knowing to expel the disciple who does not uphold the precepts." That one, filled with shame, immediately departed.

The Buddha addressed the disciples: "Listen well; I shall now expound the Dharma." All the disciples joined their palms and said: "Yes; we receive your teaching."

The Buddha said: "The waters of the great ocean come and go with the tides; when they come they do not exceed their ancient boundary, and when they go they likewise do not exceed their ancient boundary. All disciples must make their minds upright and rectify your intentions, turning inward to examine the five viscera of body and mind alike, and contemplating that birth and death entail great toil and suffering. You must uphold and maintain the sūtras and precepts without failing or transgressing. Those who uphold the five precepts will be reborn in the world as human beings. Those who uphold the ten wholesome deeds will be born in the heavens. Those who uphold the two hundred and fifty precepts may in this very life attain the great Way of arhat, pratyekabuddha, bodhisattva, or the nirvāṇa of the Buddha. Having received a human body through the Dharma, one must uphold the sūtras and precepts, and through death after death must not fail or transgress even by so much as a hair's breadth; just as the ocean's waters, coming and going with the tides, do not dare to exceed their ancient boundary. Within the ocean there are seven treasures. What are the seven treasures? The first is white silver; the second is yellow gold; the third is coral; the fourth is white pearls; the fifth is cakra-gems; the sixth is moonlight pearls; the seventh is maṇi pearls. These are the seven treasures within the ocean. The Buddha's Way likewise has seven treasures."

The Buddha said: "These are the treasures of the Way. The first is the stream-enterer; the second is the once-returner; the third is the non-returner; the fourth is the arhat; the fifth is the pratyekabuddha; the sixth is the bodhisattva who has aroused the aspiration and contemplates liberating all beings; the seventh is the great Way of the Buddha's nirvāṇa. These are the seven treasures. Those who wish to obtain the treasures of the Way must all abandon and relinquish licentiousness and dissipation, anger and hatred, and foolishness and delusion; they must uphold the precepts, practice with diligence, and accumulate merit and virtue; they must maintain inner and outer purity and self-restraint. Such is the conduct of a noble one who understands impermanence. The ocean does not receive foul effluents; if there are dead bodies, impure and foul-smelling places, and unclean things, the swift wind blows them ashore. So too the Buddha's Way does not receive the impure, those evil persons who do not uphold the sūtras and precepts; those who transgress the sūtras and precepts are seized by the arm and expelled.

"Consider the four kinds of mice: the first is the mouse that lives between the walls; the second is the mouse that lives in the household; the third is the mouse that lives in the wild fields; the fourth is the mouse that lives in the privy. The mouse between the walls cannot dwell on open ground; the mouse on open ground cannot dwell between the walls; the mouse of the wild fields cannot dwell in a human household; the mouse of a human household cannot dwell in the wild fields; the mouse of the privy cannot leave the privy, for it does not know that there is abundant grain in the storehouse. Among human beings there are likewise four kinds. What are the four kinds? The first kind are those who make their minds upright, rectify their intentions, uphold the precepts without transgressing, and wish to attain the way of the arhat. The second kind are those who uphold the precepts and practice with diligence, and wish to attain the way of the pratyekabuddha. The third kind are those who uphold the precepts, pursue learning, illuminate the sūtras through wisdom, contemplate liberating all beings, and wish to attain the Buddha's Way. The fourth kind are those who bear the name of disciple but are unable to uphold and maintain the bright precepts and have no wish to pursue learning; their minds and intentions waver in doubt, fearing that they may not attain the Way. These are disciples who advance and retreat; such are the four kinds of mice."

The Buddha said: "Disciples! In the world there are five great rivers. One flows east, named Shālù; one flows south, named Āyí; one flows west, named Gaṅgā; one flows north, named Mòpáihuái; and one flows through the center, simply called the River. When they all flow and enter the ocean, they abandon their original names and become the waters of the ocean."

The Buddha said: "Disciples! There are those of the brahmin lineage, those of the kṣatriya lineage, those of the artisan lineage, those of the farmer lineage, and those who beg for their livelihood. Each of these various kinds speaks of themselves saying: 'My lineage is noble and eminent,' feeling shame before those wealthier or more fortunate, and contempt for those of lower station. Yet just as the five great rivers flow into the ocean, so all the various kinds who become disciples of the Buddha must abandon their original names and designations; only then are they truly disciples of the Buddha. How can there still be distinctions of noble and base, or self-aggrandizement? Those who know first should teach those who know later. One must not say: 'I know the Way,' out of arrogant self-importance. One must not say: 'I have studied long and know many sūtras.' One must not say: 'What I intend conforms to the Way; what that other one does is wrong.' All such things constitute transgressions of the precepts, and one may not enter the assembly. In the Dharma and Way, seniors and juniors should mutually teach and protect one another, succeeding and supporting one another in sequence. For those who have not yet understood the sūtras and the Way, one must not speak with them of profound matters; this would be a great transgression.

"When the great rains fall, water flows into ditches; the ditches flow into streams and rivulets; the streams and rivulets flow into rivers; the rivers flow into the ocean. The ocean neither increases nor decreases. Disciples! Among those who cultivate the Way, some attain stream-entry; some attain once-return and non-return; some attain arhatship; some attain the way of the pratyekabuddha; some attain the stage of non-retrogression; some attain the Buddha's Way of nirvāṇa. Whether they come or go, the Buddha's Way neither increases nor decreases, just as the ocean waters neither increase nor decrease."

The Buddha said: "In the ocean there are great fish: the first is four thousand leagues in length; the second, eight thousand leagues; the third, twelve thousand leagues; the fourth, sixteen thousand leagues; the fifth, twenty thousand leagues; the sixth, twenty-four thousand leagues; the seventh, twenty-eight thousand leagues. If in one's learning one has not encountered a wise teacher, how can one know that there is a great Way in the world? If one sails about on ponds and spring-fed streams, how can one know that the world contains great rivers and the ocean? The Buddha's sūtras are like the rivers and ocean; all the worldly scriptures have emerged through the Buddha's sūtras. The sūtras are difficult to encounter and hear again; one should devote oneself to reciting and reading them. Only after tens of thousands of millions and billions of aeons will the sūtras and precepts of the Buddha appear again. The sun, moon, and stars will one day be destroyed; but the sūtras and precepts of the Buddha, once practiced, shall know no destruction. From this day forward, the Buddha will no longer expound the sūtras and precepts. The sūtras and precepts of the Buddha are of the utmost gravity; those within who have received and maintained the precepts yet commit evil transgressions will have their heads split into seven parts."

When the Buddha had finished expounding the sūtra, all the disciples were of one mind in their renewed resolve to uphold the precept-dharma. All the devas, humans, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas rose and came forward, prostrating with heads and faces to the ground in reverence to the Buddha, and then departed.
