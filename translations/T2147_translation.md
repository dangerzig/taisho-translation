Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2147

### Preface to the Catalog of Various Scriptures

The buddha-dharma has traveled east for many years now. Sanskrit scriptures have come from the west, and their circulation has gradually increased. The orthodox texts of former times were all produced through translation. Recently, having passed through an age of turmoil, the original sources have been largely lost. Earlier copies and later translations differ in plain and ornate style. A single sūtra may exist in several editions, with variations in additions and omissions. This has allowed ordinary people to freely fabricate forgeries: some take private matters and establish separate titles; some arbitrarily compose additional phrasing yet retain the authentic name; some treatises are called sūtras; some commentaries are cataloged as treatises. The great and the small are intermingled; the true and the false are confusedly mixed. Proliferation runs unchecked; the tradition remains unsettled. We fear that the sage's teachings will gradually be debased and the mind of faith will be damaged. The duty of continuing the transmission is neglected; the principle of the Buddha's entrustment is betrayed.

The Emperor deeply venerates the Three Jewels and thoroughly comprehends the five vehicles. He issued an edict to the relevant office, requesting the great worthies of Xingshan Monastery, together with the sūtra-translating śramaṇas and scholars, to examine the Dharma-treasury and determine the scriptural catalog. Distinguishing by category, the total is organized into five divisions: unique texts first, retranslations second, separately extracted texts third, collections by worthies and sages fourth, and suspected or apocryphal works fifth. The separately extracted and suspected or apocryphal texts need not be copied. The other three divisions are to be entered into the canon and appear in the catalog.

As for works such as the *Collection of Dharma-Jewels* and the *Children of Pure Abiding*, they are equivalent to excerpts and are classified under separately extracted texts. As for works such as the *Biographies of Eminent Monks*, whose language partakes of literary history and whose style is not purely orthodox: although the matters may be worth consulting, in principle they do not belong in the catalog.

Furthermore, comparing with the ancient catalogs, there are still texts that are missing. In former times, when the realm was not yet unified, they were lost and scattered in various places. Now that the whole world is united, we request that they all be sought and recovered. May the benevolent reign long endure and the gates of the Dharma be fully complete. May all living beings be fortunate, and may the benefit be inexhaustible.

The work is completed in five fascicles and is displayed below.
