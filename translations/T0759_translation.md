# 佛說較量壽命經
## Sūtra on Comparing Lifespans

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0759

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Tiānxīzāi (天息災).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the World-Honored One was in Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍada's park.

At that time the World-Honored One addressed the assembly of monks: "I shall compare the lengths of sentient beings' lifespans. Listen carefully."

The monks said: "World-Honored One! We delight in hearing."

At that time the Well-Gone One explained to the monks the comparative lifespans of sentient beings. Having heard these words, the monks were filled with joy and excitement, and again said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! We pray that you will expound this fully."

At that time the World-Honored One told the monks: "Excellent, excellent! Listen carefully, and I shall explain. The lifespan of the Avīci hell extends through an intermediate kalpa and beyond. The seven types of hell above it have lifespans of varying lengths. Monks, you should know that these are the comparative lifespans, measured in kalpas, of the hells. After suffering is completed, life comes to an end.

"Monks, you should know: thirty human days and nights constitute one day and night in the hungry ghost realm. With thirty such days and nights making one month, and twelve such months making one year, five hundred of those years is the lifespan of the hungry ghost realm. As for the four continents compared by calculation: Uttarakuru has a lifespan of one thousand years; the western and eastern continents are each half of the preceding one. Jambudvīpa is indeterminate: at the beginning of the kalpa the lifespan was incalculable, and at the end it decreases to ten years, with the period in between having no fixed length."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "The Saṃghāta hell: constant suffering and anguish,
> Karmic retribution parches the lips and throat.
> Body-hair all stands on end; bellies vast as mountains.
> Matted hair perpetually covers the body; needle-mouths thin as awl-points.
> Faces and eyes are rent and split; emaciated, the forms are exposed.
> Red hair covers the body, revealing the very bones and joints.
> They drink foul substances, with skulls about the neck,
> Constantly weeping and crying, running in all directions.
> They endure the suffering of hunger and thirst, perpetually harried by want and exhaustion.
> Their bitter wailing rises high: what one has done, one must oneself receive.
> Greed and anger arise from aversion and attachment; evil karma is self-created.
> When these obstructive offenses ripen, they are received in the hungry ghost realm.
> The people of Pūrvavideha live two hundred and fifty years.
> The people of Aparagodānīya live five hundred years.
> Those born in Uttarakuru have a fixed lifespan of one thousand years.
> Jambudvīpa's lifespan is indeterminate: at the end, ten years; at the beginning, incalculable."

The monks said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How is it that the lifespan of Uttarakuru is one thousand years, after which life comes to an end?"

At that time the World-Honored One told the monks: "You should know that in Uttarakuru there is no clinging to self, no discrimination. Because of practicing the ten wholesome deeds, the lifespan is a fixed one thousand years. When life ends there, one is reborn in heaven. Thus the lifespan of Uttarakuru is one thousand years, after which life comes to an end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Those impoverished beings, suffering, who love to steal others' wealth,
> Who have never cultivated any good deeds, are lowly and employed by others.
> Those wealthy and of great possessions, with all necessities of life complete,
> With gold, jewels, and servants: these people practiced good deeds.
> By cultivating the ten wholesome actions as cause, one obtains the fruit of great wealth and honor.
> In the present one enjoys Uttarakuru; in the future, happiness reborn in heaven.
> The people of Uttarakuru enjoy the world's greatest happiness.
> Through past cultivation of the cause of giving, the kalpa-tree bears wondrous garments as its fruit.
> There is neither cold nor heat, nor any affliction of illness.
> Their beautiful complexions are due to past causes of cultivating generosity.
> Merit so excellent it is indestructible: rice grows of its own accord,
> Pure in color and wholly white, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> Those born in Uttarakuru: maṇi jewels shine with radiance.
> Food and drink manifest through thought, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> That continent is supremely peaceful: wondrous herbs flourish abundantly.
> Form, fragrance, and flavor are all complete, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> That continent has no vegetables; they always eat fragrant rice.
> One serving of food never runs out, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> The ālam plum-fruit trees: one eats without rising from one's seat.
> When branches drop, new fruit grows back, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> The water there has eight excellent qualities; when drunk, no illness or distress.
> Peaceful and pleasant, no harm to the belly, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> In gardens and groves they enjoy happiness; wondrous music is always heard.
> Delight and joy are inexhaustible, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> The kalpa-trees that all love bring happiness as anointing fragrance rains down.
> Fragrant breezes are without end, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> Music, wondrous flowers and incense, garments and bodily adornments:
> One merely thinks and they follow the mind, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> Fragrant grasses and forest peaks, soft and all expansive:
> They love and delight in constant play, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> They are always in shared merriment; anger and resentment never arise.
> No self and no greed, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> Each night at the first watch, a fine gentle rain descends,
> Clearing the dust and purifying the ground, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> When children grow, mothers no longer recognize them; at the time of death there is no grief.
> They are free of clinging love, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> When a loving mother bears a child, she leaves it by the road and departs.
> Milk flows from the fingers to nourish it, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> At the time of death all is relinquished; not a trace of grief.
> Birds carry the body to a pure place, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> Adornment covers everything; song and dance and play.
> Contentment and happiness, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> The people of that continent all live a full thousand years.
> Untimely death is wholly absent there, through the past cause of cultivating generosity.
> They enjoy the most supreme blessings, equal and without difference.
> At death they are reborn in heaven, through the past cause of cultivating generosity."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "The lifespan of Jambudvīpa is indeterminate, ranging from ten years to hundreds, thousands, and ten thousands of years, without limit. Monks, you should know: the people of Jambudvīpa now live to one hundred years, with untimely death having no fixed pattern. Monks, you should know: the people of Jambudvīpa live at most one hundred years, with much suffering and little happiness.

"Furthermore, monks! Though this continent has a lifespan of one hundred years, there are ten stages of life: first, vālu, sleeping without awareness; second, kumāru, playing and learning life; third, yuva, delighting in the objects of desire; fourth, mālavaṃ, diligently cultivating one's livelihood; fifth, prākṛjña, with wisdom and eloquence of distinction; sixth, smṛti, clear memory and recollection; seventh, sthitu, upright and self-possessed conduct; eighth, vīru, the king showing compassion; ninth, mṛtu, complexion faded and strength diminished; tenth, ghātuyu, reaching several hundred years and coming to life's end.

"Furthermore, monks! Within the span of one hundred years, there are three seasons: cold, hot, and rainy, also called the three periods. Within the span of one hundred years, the twelve months take the names of these three seasons: four months of the cold season, four months of the hot season, and four months of the rainy season. Furthermore, within the incomplete hundred years, the twelve months contain twenty-four half-months. Within the hundred-year span there are eight months of the cold season, eight months of the hot season, and eight months of the rainy season. Furthermore, within a hundred years, the twelve months contain thirty-six day-night periods per month, and within these there are twelve cold-season periods, twelve hot-season periods, and twelve rainy-season periods. Furthermore, within a thousand years, the twelve months contain thirty-six day-night periods per month, within which there are seventy-two thousand meal-times. Among these are times of eating and times of not eating: times of anger, times of distress, times of traveling, times of fasting, times of urgent business, times of sleeping, and times of drunken torpor, during which one does not eat. There are also times when one has food but does not eat, and times when one has no food and does not eat. Thus, in summary, within the meals that are taken, there are seventy-two thousand instances of eating.

"Furthermore, monks! Concerning Jambudvīpa as described above, including nursing from the mother, months, half-months, day-night periods, years, times of eating and not eating: this is stated in approximate terms."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "I say that fifty years in the human realm constitute one day and night in the heaven of the four great kings. With such days and nights making months and twelve months making a year, the lifespan there is five hundred years. Calculated in human days and nights, their lifespan amounts to nine hundred thousand years, after which life comes to an end. Taking the five hundred years of the four great kings' heaven as one day and night of the Reviving hell, with thirty such days and nights making one month, twelve months making one year, and five hundred such years: this is the lifespan of beings in the Reviving great hell. Those five hundred years are equivalent to five million four hundred thousand years of the four great kings' heaven, which in human calculations equals sixteen thousand two hundred koṭi years. Only then does the lifespan of the Reviving great hell come to an end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Evil actions of the body are three; evil actions of speech are four.
> Actions of the mind are likewise three: when offenses are completed, one escapes the suffering.
> In the Reviving hell, those with the same evil karma all enter together.
> Sixteen thousand two hundred koṭi years of constant suffering.
> Born in that hell, enemies mutually kill one another.
> Dead, they are revived again; when karma is exhausted, suffering ends."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "One hundred human years constitute one day and night in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. With thirty such days and nights making one month and twelve months making one year, the lifespan of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven is one thousand years. A thousand years of that heaven, calculated in human years, equals eighteen koṭi years, after which life comes to an end."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "The lifespan of that heaven being one thousand years, this constitutes one day and night in the Black-Rope great hell. With thirty such days and nights making months and years, the lifespan of that great hell is one thousand years. A thousand years calculated amounts to thirty-two thousand four hundred koṭi human years, after which life in the Black-Rope great hell comes to an end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Hating teachers, father and mother, slandering the Buddha and the śrāvakas,
> Destroying harmony among people: in the Black-Rope hell one receives great suffering.
> Evil karma is self-created; hell's suffering must be endured.
> There is no way to escape or avoid it; only when karma is exhausted does one emerge."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "Two hundred human years constitute one day and night in the Yāma heaven. With those days and nights, thirty days making a month and twelve months making a year, the lifespan of the Yāma heaven is two thousand years, equivalent to thirty-six koṭi human years in calculated lifespan. After this, life comes to an end. Taking the two thousand years of that heaven as one day and night in the Saṃghāta great hell, with thirty such days and nights making one month and twelve months making one year, the lifespan of the Saṃghāta great hell is two thousand years. Calculated, this equals sixty-four thousand eight hundred koṭi human years, after which life in that hell comes to an end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Three kinds of unwholesome karma lead to birth in the Saṃghāta hell.
> The three wholesome karmas not cultivated, cattle, sheep, and deer are slaughtered.
> Pigs, dogs, and non-humans: in Saṃghāta they mutually kill.
> From the cause of suffering, birth in a place of suffering; when karma is exhausted, one finally emerges."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "Four hundred human years constitute one day and night in the Tuṣita heaven. With thirty such days and nights making one month and a year, the lifespan of the Tuṣita heaven is four thousand years, equivalent to seventy-two koṭi human years. After this, life comes to an end. Taking the four thousand years of that heaven as one day and night in the Screaming great hell, with thirty days and nights making months and years, the lifespan of the Screaming great hell is four thousand years, equivalent to four thousand three hundred and twenty koṭi years of the Tuṣita heaven, which equals one hundred twenty-nine thousand six hundred koṭi human years. After this, life in the Screaming great hell comes to an end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Killing and creating the gravest evil: in the Screaming hell one constantly receives punishment.
> Those who do evil and deceive others: when karma is exhausted, suffering finally ends."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "Eight hundred human years constitute one day and night in the Nirmāṇarati heaven. With thirty days making a month and twelve months making a year, the lifespan of the Nirmāṇarati heaven is eight thousand years, equivalent to one hundred forty-four koṭi human years. After this, life comes to an end. Taking the eight thousand years of that heaven as one day and night of the Great Screaming great hell, with thirty days and nights making months and years, the lifespan of the Great Screaming great hell is eight thousand years. Calculated, this equals two hundred fifty-nine thousand koṭi human years. After this, life comes to an end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Self-view and desire, slandering the Dharma: these offenses are the deepest.
> Creating evil with the fetters and hindrances, one is born in the Great Screaming hell.
> In the Screaming great hell, body-hair all stands on end.
> Eating and drinking are burned by flames; to escape its confines is forbidden.
> Deceit, love of killing: sword-wheels, dogs, vultures, and birds
> Eat the marrow. Old iron crows wreak havoc without exception."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "One thousand six hundred human years constitute one day and night in the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven. With thirty such days and nights making months and twelve months making years, the lifespan of the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven is sixteen thousand years, equivalent to two hundred eighty-eight koṭi human years. After this, life comes to an end. Taking the sixteen thousand years of that heaven as one day and night of the Burning great hell, with thirty days and nights making months and twelve months making years, the lifespan of the Burning great hell is sixteen thousand years. Calculated, this equals six hundred eight thousand four hundred koṭi human years. After this, life in that hell comes to an end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Tormenting father and mother, renunciants and brahmins,
> Not cultivating wholesome karma: one constantly suffers the heat of the Burning hell.
> Deceiving and deluding sentient beings: in the Burning hell one receives punishment.
> Evil deeds done do not perish; when karma is exhausted, one finally emerges."

At that time the World-Honored One told the monks: "Listen carefully to the comparative calculations of the lifespans of beings in the Blistering hells."

The monks said to the Buddha: "Well-Gone One! We delight in hearing, receiving, upholding, and remembering. We pray that you will expound the comparative lengths of lifespans of beings in the Blistering hells."

At that time the World-Honored One told the monks: "Excellent, excellent! I shall now explain for you the matter of lifespans in the Blistering hells. Imagine accumulating sesame seeds to fill one bhāra, which can hold twenty khārikā. If a heavenly being were to remove one seed every hundred years, removing them one by one until twenty khārikā were emptied: only then would the lifespan of the Blistering hell be complete.

"Furthermore, monks! I explain the comparative lifespans of sentient beings. In the Cracking hell, the lifespan equals twenty bhāra of sesame seeds. In the Aṭaṭa hell, the lifespan equals forty bhāra. In the Hahava hell, the lifespan equals sixty bhāra. In the Huhuva hell, the lifespan equals eighty bhāra. In the Blue Lotus hell, the lifespan equals one hundred bhāra. In the Red Lotus hell, the lifespan equals one hundred twenty bhāra. In the Great Red Lotus hell, the lifespan equals one hundred forty bhāra. In each case, the granaries are filled with sesame seeds, and one seed is removed every hundred years. Only when every last seed has been removed is the lifespan of those beings complete.

"Thus, monks! If Śāriputra, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, and the heavenly beings with all their retinues, or any person whose mind harbors violent evil, such a person will certainly fall into this great hell. Therefore, monks, you should know this. If a person suffers from emaciation yet does not give rise to anger, and the mind is not contemptuous, then, monks, the wise should cultivate awakening in this way."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "If a person utters evil words, harming others like a knife or axe,
> A sword-wheel severs the body: all of this comes from one's own mouth.
> Slandering the virtuous and praising with delight,
> Creating quarrels and accumulating enmity: at death one enters hell swiftly.
> Others' wealth and possessions cause jealous quarrels to arise.
> Tormenting them and destroying their peace: one will suffer the Blistering punishment.
> Slandering the Well-Gone One yet feeling satisfaction and delight:
> For hundreds of thousands of Blistering kalpas, one constantly suffers hell's torment.
> Generating evil of body, speech, and mind, slandering the noble saints:
> Five hundred thirty-six Blistering kalpas of constant suffering.
> Creating vast and boundless karma, committing offenses and deceits:
> Through long kalpas in hell, only when karma is exhausted does one later emerge."

Furthermore, the Buddha told the monks: "In the Extreme Burning great hell, the lifespan of beings is half an intermediate kalpa in comparative calculation. After this, life comes to an end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Constantly creating the cause of evil karma, yet desiring the fruit of peace and happiness.
> Far removed from human and heavenly states, one will be born in the Burning hell.
> Beating and cursing teachers, father and mother, renunciants and brahmins.
> Holding wrong views and severing roots of merit: in the Extreme Heat one receives great suffering."

Furthermore, the Buddha told the monks: "In the Avīci great hell, the comparative lifespan extends and then life comes to an end. If Devadatta or any deluded person generates an evil mind toward the Tathāgata, destroys stūpas and monasteries, burns sūtras and images, draws blood from the Buddha's body, or kills an arhat, or drives and enslaves monks and nuns, at death they will certainly fall into the Avīci great hell, to suffer without end."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this verse:

> "Slandering the three-vehicle teachings, killing noble arhats,
> The deluded who destroy seekers of the Dharma receive the punishment of the uninterrupted.
> The thorny bamboo's fruit destroys itself; evil karma leads to evil destinies.
> Such are the offenses of heedlessness: in the Avīci hell they are received."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "The lifespan of the Brahmā Assembly heaven is half a kalpa, after which life comes to an end. Monks, you should know: the lifespan of the Brahmā Attendants heaven is one kalpa, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Great Brahmā heaven is one and a half kalpas, after which life comes to an end. Monks, you should know: the lifespan of the Lesser Light heaven is two kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Immeasurable Light heaven is four kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Extreme Light Purity heaven is eight kalpas, after which life comes to an end. Monks, you should know: the lifespan of the Lesser Purity heaven is sixteen kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Immeasurable Purity heaven is thirty-two kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Universal Purity heaven is sixty-four kalpas, after which life comes to an end. Monks, you should know: the lifespan of the Cloudless heaven is one hundred twenty-five kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Merit-Born heaven is two hundred fifty kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Vast Fruit heaven is five hundred kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Non-Perception heaven is likewise. Monks, you should know: the lifespan of the Non-Affliction heaven is one thousand kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Non-Heat heaven is two thousand kalpas, after which life comes to an end. Monks, you should know: the lifespan of the Good Appearance heaven is four thousand kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Good Vision heaven is eight thousand kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Akaniṣṭha heaven is sixteen thousand kalpas, after which life comes to an end.

"Monks, you should know: the lifespan of beings in the Sphere of Infinite Space is twenty thousand great kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Sphere of Infinite Consciousness is forty thousand great kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of the Sphere of Nothingness is sixty thousand great kalpas, after which life comes to an end. The lifespan of beings in the Sphere of Neither Perception Nor Non-Perception is eighty thousand great kalpas, after which life comes to an end."

At that time the Buddha told the monks: "Thus, from the Avīci great hell up to the Sphere of Neither Perception Nor Non-Perception, the cycle of transmigration continues in this way. Monks, you should know: in this cycle of transmigration, beings of the five destinies come and go without ceasing, manifesting the three characteristics of arising, changing, and perishing. Monks, you should know: within this threefold cycle of transmigration, not even the smallest fraction should be desired or envied. Not even for a single moment does it abide permanently. Why? Monks, you should know: because of the nature of suffering, it should not be desired. If even a small amount of defilement is undesirable, how much more a great amount? If even a small defilement is not to be desired or envied, and not even for a single moment is it ultimate, why? Monks, you should know: the suffering of the cycle of transmigration should not be desired. If there are deluded beings of little learning who cycle through the five destinies, coming and going without ceasing, who do not abandon the activities of cyclic existence, they cannot escape the suffering of hell, nor can they escape the suffering of the hungry ghost realm, and they constantly fall into evil destinies. Therefore, monks, you should study thus: the great suffering of this cycle of transmigration should not be desired. Monks, you should know: with diligent effort, sever the cycle of transmigration."

At that time, when the World-Honored One had finished teaching this Dharma, all the monks with unified minds rejoiced, believed, received, and practiced it.
