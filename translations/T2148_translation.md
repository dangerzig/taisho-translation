# Catalog of Collected Scriptures

## *Zhòngjīng Mùlù* (眾經目錄)

Taishō 2148

Compiled by Jìngtài (*Jìngtài*, 靜泰) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is a bibliographic catalog of the Chinese Buddhist canon compiled by the monk Jìngtài at the Dàjìng'ài Monastery in the Eastern Capital (Luoyang) during the Lín'dé era (664-665 CE). The work was produced in connection with an imperially commissioned project to copy the entire Buddhist canon, supervised by the Crown Prince and overseen by a team of ten scholar-monks and government officials. The catalog is organized in five fascicles following the traditional classification: (1) single translations (texts with no alternative version), (2) retranslations (texts with multiple versions), (3) separately extracted texts (excerpts from larger works), (4) doubtful and spurious texts, and (5) lost texts (titles recorded in earlier catalogs but for which no copies exist). It records 816 titles in 4,066 fascicles that were actually copied and placed in the canon, plus 382 lost titles in 725 fascicles being actively sought. The catalog includes an important prefatory essay discussing the history of catalog-making from Dào'ān through Fǎshàng, Zhìtōng, and Dàoxuān.*

---

## Preface: Catalog of All Scriptures at the Great Jìng'ài Monastery of the Eastern Capital of the Great Tang

Written words are themselves the true ground of liberation, marking the principle of non-duality. Names and sentences are the mysterious gate of prajñā, opening the single path. Therefore the Dharma records gallop across time, bringing uniform culture to the hundred billion [worlds]. The ultimate teaching drives forward, circling the great chiliocosm on a single track. Jade caskets with flower-inscriptions blaze forth in the celestial city; golden tablets with jeweled words shine in the dragon-realm.

From the time the clouds of compassion first arose in the west and the rain of loving-kindness flew eastward, beginning dimly in the era of the Zhou stars and culminating in full brightness under the Han sun; from Lán [Kāśyapa Mātaṅga] and Yù [Dharmarakṣa], who first sang the way, to Zhú [Dharmarakṣa] and Shí [Kumārajīva], who joyfully followed in their footsteps: the three vehicles have sounded the whip, the eight treasuries have opened their doors, suspending the twin luminaries in high splendor, spanning the two principles in lasting permanence.

[The preface continues with an account of the dangers of confused cataloging during periods of disorder, and how the traditions of Dào'ān and Fǎshàng established the fivefold classification: single translations first, retranslations second, Indian compilations third, separately extracted texts fourth, and doubtful-or-spurious texts fifth. Although arranged in five categories, only the first three are to be copied; the separately extracted texts are not distinct from their parent works, and the spurious texts should not be preserved.]

Our August Emperor rules the world in accord with the Way of Heaven. Spreading the ten wholesome actions for his transforming influence, relying on the five vehicles to set the proper tone, he has widely established benevolent monasteries and greatly exalted the teaching.

In the Xiǎnqìng era [656-661 CE], the Xīmíng Monastery was completed. The imperially commissioned canon was further refined and purified, without any loose ends. The Vinaya Master Dàoxuān also composed a preface for a catalog. Following the Yīn and Xià ritual models without altering previous standards, he added a "miscellaneous collection" category beyond the three main sections, encompassing works like the *Dharma Garden*, *Dharma Collection*, *Biographies of Eminent Monks*, *Histories of the Saṃgha*, and similar works, included for their supporting contributions.

The Crown Prince, shining brilliantly in the Eastern Palace... on the twenty-second day of the first month of the third year of the Lóng Shuò era [663 CE], an edict was issued to copy the complete canon at the Jìng'ài Dharma-site. Furthermore, on the twenty-sixth day of the first month of the first year of the Lín'dé era [664 CE], an edict selected ten eminent śramaṇas of exemplary learning, including Huìgài, Míngỳu, Shénchá, Dàoyīng, Tánsùi, and others, to participate in reviewing, collating, and proofreading over three years. Officials were also appointed to supply provisions.

[The catalog records that 741 titles of previously existing sūtras and śāstras were copied (2,731 fascicles), along with 75 titles of newly translated works by the Great Tang Tripiṭaka Master [Xuánzàng] (1,335 fascicles), for a combined total of 816 titles in 4,066 fascicles placed in the canon.]

[The fascicle includes a note on the controversy about the sixty-fascicle *Mahāsaṃnipāta* compilation, discussing the monk Zhìtōng's objection that it improperly merged texts with different assembly contexts, Sanskrit originals, translation eras, and transmission histories. It references Bǎoguì of Xīngshàn Monastery's precedent and compares it to the earlier compilations of the *Śūraṅgamasamādhi* and *Vimalakīrtinirdeśa* by Zhī Mǐndù of the Jin dynasty.]

## Fascicle Four: Separately Extracted Texts (continued) and Doubtful and Spurious Texts

### Separately Extracted Texts (continued)
341 titles in 346 fascicles.
[Reiterates the Hīnayāna separately extracted texts with a general note: "These 342 texts following the above were all extracted by later persons from the larger works according to their own preferences, taking stanzas and making them into separate fascicles. Because of this, toward the end of the age, corruption and excess have proliferated daily. They should now be subsumed into the parent texts to strengthen the fundamentals."]

### Doubtful Texts
29 titles in 31 fascicles.
[Note: "These 29 sūtras, because of discrepancies in their colophons, have caused perplexity in the various catalogs. Their texts are also mixed, and their authenticity or spuriousness has not been determined. They require further investigation and are provisionally placed in the doubtful section."]

### Spurious and False Texts
53 titles in 93 fascicles.
[Note: "The eight texts below the *Dharmapada* were all composed by Xiāo Zǐliáng and are therefore placed in the spurious section. The preceding 53 texts all deviate from authenticity: some begin by plagiarizing golden words but end with popular prophecies; some discuss worldly arts then append Dharma language; some cite yin-yang fortune-telling, or explain the blessings and calamities of ghosts and spirits. All such texts as these are clearly false and spurious. They should now be suppressed and hidden to save the world from their harm."]
