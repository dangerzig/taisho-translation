# 永嘉證道歌
## Song of Realizing the Way by Yongjia

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2014

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, composed by Xuanjue (玄覺).

---

> Have you not seen?
> The person of the Way, beyond learning and without contrivance, at ease,
> Neither eliminating delusive thought nor seeking the true.
> The real nature of ignorance is the buddha-nature itself;
> The illusory, empty body is the dharma-body itself.
> When the dharma-body is awakened, there is not a single thing.
> The original source, the self-nature, is the innate buddha.
> The five aggregates are floating clouds, empty in their coming and going;
> The three poisons are water-bubbles, hollow in their appearing and vanishing.

> When reality-characteristics are realized, there is neither person nor dharma.
> In an instant, the karma of Avīci is extinguished.
> If these are false words that deceive sentient beings,
> I invite upon myself the tongue-pulling hell for dust-mote kalpas.

> Suddenly awakened to the Tathāgata's dhyāna,
> The six pāramitās and myriad practices are complete within its substance.
> In dream, the six destinies clearly exist;
> Upon awakening, emptiness, emptiness: no great chiliocosm.
> No transgression, no merit, no loss, no gain.
> Within the nature of quiescent extinction, do not ask or seek.
> This dusty mirror has long gone unpolished;
> Today it must be clearly analyzed.

> Who is without thought? Who is without arising?
> If truly there is no arising, there is nothing that does not arise.
> Call the wooden puppet of a mechanism and ask:
> When will seeking buddhahood through applied effort ever succeed?
> Release the four great elements; do not grasp at them.
> Within the nature of quiescent extinction, eat and drink as you will.
> All conditioned things are impermanent; everything is empty.
> This is the Tathāgata's great perfect awakening.

> Speaking with certainty, I express the true saṃgha.
> If anyone disagrees, let them challenge as they please.
> Going straight to the root-source, stamped by the Buddha:
> Plucking leaves and chasing branches is beyond me.

> The maṇi-pearl is unrecognized by people;
> It is found within the tathāgata-garbha.
> The sixfold spiritual function is both empty and not empty;
> One sphere of perfect radiance is neither form nor non-form.
> Purifying the five eyes, attaining the five powers:
> Only through realization can one know; it is hard to fathom.
> Seeing forms in a mirror is not difficult;
> But grasping the moon in water, how can it be seized?

> Always walking alone, always stepping alone,
> Those who have arrived walk together on the nirvāṇa road.
> With ancient melody, spirit clear, their bearing naturally lofty,
> Gaunt in appearance, hard in bone, no one gives them a glance.
> Impoverished sons of Śākya, they call themselves poor,
> Yet truly the body is poor while the Way is not.
> The poor body is constantly clad in patched cloth,
> But the Way treasures priceless jewels within the mind.
> These priceless jewels are used without exhaustion,
> Benefiting beings, responding to capacities, never withheld.
> The three bodies and four wisdoms are complete within the substance;
> The eight liberations and six powers are sealed in the ground of mind.

> The superior person, once it is settled, comprehends all.
> The middle and lower, hearing much, mostly do not believe.
> Just unfasten the soiled garments from within your own breast;
> Who needs to show off diligence to the outside world?
> Let others slander, let others find fault;
> Trying to burn the sky with a torch, one tires in vain.
> When I hear it, it is like drinking sweet dew,
> Melting and dissolving, I suddenly enter the inconceivable.

> To regard harsh words as merit:
> This very thing makes them my good teachers.
> If calumny and slander did not arouse enmity and affinity,
> How would the power of the patience of non-arising be shown?
> The essence penetrates, and the teaching also penetrates;
> Concentration and wisdom, perfectly bright, do not stagnate in emptiness.
> Not only I alone have arrived at this understanding;
> Buddhas as numerous as the Ganges' sands all share the same substance.
> The lion's roar is a fearless teaching;
> When the hundred beasts hear it, their skulls all split.
> The fragrant elephant stampedes, losing its composure;
> The celestial dragon listens in stillness and is born to delight.

> Traveling rivers and seas, crossing mountains and streams,
> Seeking teachers and inquiring of the Way for the sake of investigation:
> Since I recognized the road to Caoxi,
> I have known that birth and death have nothing to do with me.
> Walking is dhyāna; sitting is dhyāna.
> In speech, silence, movement, and stillness, the substance is at ease.
> Even encountering a blade, one remains ever serene.
> Even given poison, one stays completely at peace.

> My master saw Dīpaṃkara Buddha;
> For many kalpas he was the Sage of Patient Endurance.
> How many times born? How many times dead?
> Birth and death, on and on, without fixed end.
> Since the sudden awakening to non-arising,
> In all honor and disgrace, what worry or joy?

> Entering deep mountains, dwelling in a hermitage,
> Beneath tall pines on a steep and secluded ridge,
> Wandering at ease, sitting quietly, a wild monk's home,
> In profound silence, dwelling at peace, truly free.

> Awakened, it is all accomplished without applying effort.
> All conditioned dharmas are not the same.
> Giving that grasps at appearances produces heavenly merit,
> Yet it is like shooting an arrow into the sky:
> When its force is spent, the arrow falls.
> It brings a future life that is not as one wishes.
> How can it compare with the gate of the unconditioned, true-characteristic?
> One leap and you enter directly into the Tathāgata's ground.

> Just attain the root; do not worry about the branches.
> Like pure lapis lazuli containing the precious moon.
> Once you can comprehend this wish-fulfilling jewel,
> Self-benefit and benefit of others will never be exhausted.
> The moon over the river shines; the pine-wind blows.
> What is there to do in the long, clear night?
> The buddha-nature, precept-pearl, seal of the ground of mind,
> Mist, dew, clouds, and haze are garments upon its body.

> The dragon-taming bowl, the tiger-subduing staff,
> The two metal rings jingle clearly.
> These are not idle forms held as vain symbols;
> They are the precious staff of the Tathāgata, whose tracks we follow.

> Not seeking the true, not cutting off the false,
> One knows that both dharmas are empty, without characteristics.
> Without characteristics, without emptiness, without not-empty:
> This is the Tathāgata's true reality-characteristics.
> The mirror of mind is clear, reflecting without obstruction,
> Vast and luminous, penetrating worlds as numerous as sand.
> The myriad phenomena, arrayed like a forest, appear as reflections within;
> One sphere of perfect radiance is neither inside nor outside.

> To grasp at an utter emptiness that repudiates cause and effect
> Is reckless and wild, inviting calamity.
> To abandon existence and cling to emptiness is also a sickness,
> Like avoiding drowning by throwing oneself into fire.
> To abandon the deluded mind and grasp the true principle:
> The mind of grasping and abandoning becomes artful deceit.
> Students who do not understand this use it in their practice
> And deeply end up "recognizing the thief as their own son."

> Losing the Dharma-wealth, extinguishing merit:
> All because of this mind, mentation, and consciousness.
> Therefore the dhyāna school thoroughly settles the mind
> And suddenly enters the power of the knowing and seeing of non-arising.

> The great person wields the wisdom-sword:
> Its prajñā-edge gleams like vajra-flame.
> Not only does it crush the minds of non-Buddhists;
> Long ago it shattered the courage of celestial māras.
> Shaking the Dharma-thunder, beating the Dharma-drum,
> Spreading clouds of compassion, sprinkling sweet dew.
> Where dragon-elephants tread, the moisture is boundless;
> Those of the three vehicles and five natures are all awakened.
> The snow mountain's rich cream contains no impurity;
> It yields pure ghee, which I constantly receive.

> One nature, perfectly penetrating, penetrates all natures.
> One dharma universally contains all dharmas.
> One moon appears universally in all waters;
> The water-moons of all waters are embraced in one moon.
> The dharma-bodies of all buddhas enter my nature;
> My nature merges and unites with the Tathāgata.
> One ground is complete with all grounds.
> It is neither form, nor mind, nor karmic activity.
> With a snap of the fingers, eighty thousand gates are perfected.
> In an instant, three incalculable kalpas are extinguished.

> All numerical phrases and non-numerical phrases:
> What have they to do with my numinous awareness?
> It cannot be reviled; it cannot be praised.
> Its substance, like empty space, has no borders.
> Without departing from this very place, it is always clear and still.
> Seek it, and you will know you cannot find it.
> It cannot be grasped; it cannot be abandoned.
> In the midst of "cannot be attained," just so it is attained.
> Silent, it speaks; speaking, it is silent.
> The great gate of giving is open without blockage.

> If anyone asks what school I understand,
> I reply: the power of mahā-prajñā.
> Whether it is right or wrong, people do not recognize;
> Whether going against or going along, even heaven cannot fathom.
> I have already cultivated through many kalpas;
> This is no idle talk to deceive you.
> Raising the Dharma-banner, establishing the essential teaching:
> Clearly the Buddha's own decree, Caoxi is the one.
> First, Kāśyapa transmitted the lamp;
> Twenty-eight generations are recorded in the West.
> The Dharma flowed east and entered this land;
> Bodhidharma was the first patriarch.
> The robe was transmitted through six generations, known throughout the world.
> Those who afterward attained the Way, how can they be counted?

> The true need not be established; the false is originally empty.
> When both existence and nonexistence are dismissed, it is not an empty emptiness.
> The twenty gates of emptiness are originally without attachment;
> The one nature of the Tathāgata's substance is itself the same.
> Mind is the root; dharmas are the dust.
> Both are like a stain upon a mirror.
> When the stain is entirely removed, the light first appears.
> When mind and dharma are both forgotten, the nature is true.

> Alas, in the final age, the evil age of the world,
> Sentient beings have meager merit and are hard to govern.
> Far removed from the sages, wrong views are deep;
> Māras are strong, the Dharma is weak, and there is much dread of harm.
> When they hear the Tathāgata's teaching of sudden awakening,
> They wish they could smash it to pieces.
> The deed is in the mind; the calamity falls upon the body.
> Do not lay blame and accuse others.
> If you wish to avoid incurring the karma of the uninterrupted hell,
> Do not slander the Tathāgata's turning of the correct Dharma-wheel.

> In the sandalwood forest there are no mixed trees.
> In its dense, deep shade, the lion dwells.
> In the still forest, it roams alone.
> Running beasts and flying birds all keep far away.
> The lion cubs all follow behind;
> At three years of age, they can already roar greatly.
> If a jackal pursues the Dharma-King,
> Even after a hundred years, the monster opens its mouth in vain.

> The perfect, sudden teaching has no room for human sentiment.
> If there is doubt that is unresolved, it must be directly contested.
> It is not this mountain monk showing off self and other;
> I fear that practice may fall into the pit of eternalism or nihilism.
> "Wrong" is not wrong; "right" is not right.
> Miss by a hair's breadth, and you lose by a thousand miles.
> When right, the nāga-girl suddenly becomes a buddha.
> When wrong, Sunakṣatra is born into the pit.

> In my early years, I accumulated learning;
> I also studied commentaries and searched through sūtras and treatises.
> Discriminating names and characteristics without rest,
> I entered the sea to count grains of sand, tiring myself in vain.
> Then the Tathāgata sternly rebuked me:
> "What benefit is there in counting another's treasures?"
> I realized my long, stumbling journey had been empty;
> For many years I had been a traveler adrift in wind and dust.

> If the spiritual lineage is crooked and the understanding wrong,
> One does not reach the Tathāgata's perfect, sudden standard.
> The Two Vehicles are diligent but lack the mind of the Way;
> Non-Buddhists are clever but lack wisdom.
> They are both foolish and also childish,
> Making a real interpretation from a bare fist or a pointing finger.
> To mistake the finger for the moon is effort wasted.
> Fabricating marvels within the realm of faculties and objects:
> Not seeing a single dharma, this is the Tathāgata.
> Only then may one be called Avalokiteśvara.

> Understood, then karmic obstructions are originally empty;
> Not understood, one must still repay past debts.
> Starving yet unable to eat when meeting the king's feast;
> Sick yet unable to be cured when encountering the king of physicians.
> Practicing dhyāna in the midst of desire, this is the power of knowing and seeing;
> A lotus born in fire is ultimately indestructible.
> Brave-Giving committed a grave offense yet awakened to non-arising;
> Long ago he became a buddha and remains so to this day.

> The lion's roar is a fearless teaching.
> How deeply I lament the dull and obstinate.
> They know only that grave offenses obstruct bodhi,
> And do not see the Tathāgata open the secret teaching.
> Two bhikṣus committed the offenses of lust and killing.
> Upāli's firefly-light only increased the knot of guilt.
> The great worthy Vimalakīrti suddenly removed their doubt,
> Just as the blazing sun melts away frost and snow.

> The inconceivable power of liberation
> Has wondrous functions as countless as the Ganges' sands.
> If the four kinds of offerings are presented, why shrink from the toil?
> Even ten thousand taels of gold can be accepted.
> Crushing bones and pulverizing the body would still not suffice to repay it;
> A single phrase, clearly understood, surpasses a hundred billion.
> The King among all dharmas, most supreme:
> Buddhas as numerous as the Ganges' sands have all equally realized this.
> I now comprehend this wish-fulfilling jewel.
> All who trust and accept it will be in accord.

> Clearly, clearly: not a single thing is seen.
> There is no person, and there is no buddha.
> The great chiliocosm of sand-grain worlds: foam in the sea.
> All worthies and sages: a flash of lightning.
> Suppose an iron wheel were to spin upon your head:
> Concentration and wisdom, perfectly bright, would ultimately never be lost.
> The sun may become cold; the moon may become hot.
> The hosts of māras can never destroy the true teaching.
> The elephant-carriage advances majestically along the road.
> Who has ever seen a mantis able to block its wheels?
> The great elephant does not walk the rabbit's path.
> Great awakening is not confined by petty rules.
> Do not use a reed-pipe view to slander the blue sky.
> If you do not yet understand, I shall now give you the key.

### Biographical Record of the Great Master of Formlessness

Chan Master Xuanjue of Yongjia in Wenzhou was a native of Yongjia, surnamed Dai. He left home as a youth, broadly explored the Tripiṭaka, and mastered the essential meaning of the Tiantai school. He contemplated the perfectly wondrous Dharma-gate and, within the four deportments, constantly merged in dhyāna-contemplation. Later, prompted by Chan Master Lang of Zuoxi, together with Chan Master Ce of Dongyang, he journeyed to Caoxi.

When he first arrived, shaking his staff and carrying his water-bottle, he circumambulated the Sixth Patriarch three times. The Patriarch said: "A śramaṇa is endowed with three thousand rules of deportment and eighty thousand fine practices. From what direction does this great worthy come that he gives rise to such great arrogance?"

The Master said: "The matter of birth and death is great; impermanence is swift."

The Patriarch said: "Why not embody the unborn and comprehend that which is without swiftness?"

He said: "Embodying is itself the unborn; comprehending is originally without swiftness."

The Patriarch said: "So it is, so it is."

At that time, the entire assembly was astonished. The Master then performed the full ceremonial prostrations. In a short while, he announced his departure.

The Patriarch said: "Are you not returning too quickly?"

The Master said: "Fundamentally there is no movement. How can there be quickness?"

The Patriarch said: "Who knows there is no movement?"

He said: "The benevolent one himself generates discrimination."

The Patriarch said: "You have truly grasped the meaning of the unborn."

He said: "Does the unborn have any meaning?"

The Patriarch said: "Without meaning, who then discriminates?"

He said: "Discrimination is also not meaning."

The Patriarch exclaimed: "Excellent! Excellent! Please stay for one night."

At that time, people called him "the overnight awakening." Master Ce then stayed. The next day, the Master descended the mountain and returned to the Wen River. Students gathered to him in great numbers. He was given the title Great Master of True Awakening. He composed the "Essentials of Realization and Cultivation in the Chan School's Perfect Teaching," proceeding from the shallow to the deep. The Prefect of Qingzhou, Wei Jing, compiled it into ten chapters, entitled the *Yongjia Collection*. Together with the *Song of Realizing the Way* in one piece, both circulated widely throughout the world.
