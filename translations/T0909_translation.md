# 金剛頂瑜伽護摩儀軌

## Vajraśekhara Yoga Homa Ritual Manual

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0909

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Amoghavajra (不空).

---

> I now explain the homa,
> By which one swiftly attains accomplishment.
> Through the ritual procedures of homa practice,
> Yoga is maintained without interruption.
> All such matters as these
> Should be performed according to the vidyā.
> Performing homa according to its type
> Is the supreme practice of accomplishment.
> Homa is said to be of many kinds;
> In brief, there are five types.
> The extensive explanation belongs to the Great Yoga,
> Taught within the esoteric scriptures.
> I now explain them in brief:
> The sport of the vidyā-holder.
> Through the homa ritual manual,
> One accomplishes the clan-altar.
> The five kinds of homa activities
> Each have many varieties:
> Pacification and enrichment,
> The third is subjugation,
> Summoning is the fourth,
> The fifth is enchantment.
> Of these five homas,
> Enchantment is the most supreme.
> I now explain the *kuṇḍa* (fire-hearth),
> In accordance with yoga:
> For pacification, the hearth is perfectly round,
> And should be made thus.
> For enrichment, it should be perfectly square;
> Triangular for subjugation.
> A vajra-shaped *kuṇḍa*
> Is most supreme for summoning.
> Made long in lotus shape,
> It accords with enchantment.
> Having explained the five types,
> The *kuṇḍa* practice is supreme.
> Pacification begins in the first watch of night;
> Enrichment in the first part of day.
> The middle part of day is for performing
> The fierce methods of subjugation.
> Summoning may be done at any time;
> Enchantment in the last watch of night.
> These five yogas
> Accomplish their work through meditative absorption.
> Face north for pacification;
> Face east for enrichment.
> Face south while abiding
> To perform subjugation.
> Gazing upward in all directions
> Is the rite of summoning.
> For enchantment in accord,
> One should abide facing west.
> For pacification, form the Buddha-mudrā;
> For enrichment, the jewel emblem.
> Wrathful vajra for subjugation;
> Vajra hook for summoning.
> The great summoning accords
> With the Lotus Division for enchantment.
> In these five yogas,
> One should perform the homa activities.
> For pacification, burn sweet wood;
> For enrichment, use fruit wood.
> Bitter wood for subjugation;
> Thorny wood for summoning.
> Flower-wood is for enchantment:
> These five kinds of wood
> The yogin should employ.
> For the pacification hearth, inscribe a wheel;
> For enrichment, a three-pronged vajra.
> For subjugation, a single-pronged vajra;
> For summoning, make a hook.
> For enchantment, make a lotus.
> The pacification hearth dimensions:
> Full width, half-cubit height.
> Enrichment is two cubits,
> With depth at half the measure.
> The subjugation *kuṇḍa*:
> A triangle, each side one cubit.
> Summoning is one cubit long,
> Width and depth each half less.
> Enchantment is also one cubit,
> Width and depth like summoning.
> The five kinds of *kuṇḍa* altars
> Should be painted in three tiers.
> The central court has the karma-vajra,
> With lotuses painted at the four corners.
> The second court has four mudrās,
> Namely the Four Pāramitās,
> With inner offerings at the four corners.
> The third court should depict
> The eight directional devas and their retinues,
> The four corners at the four gates,
> Outer offerings and four attractors.
> Vairocana is placed at the center.
> This is the pacification *kuṇḍa*.
> The remaining four *kuṇḍa* configurations
> Follow this same three-court pattern.
> For enrichment, the central court
> Should depict the karma-jewel.
> The second court should depict
> The retinue of Ratnasambhava Buddha.
> The third court and gates
> Are also as previously described.
> For subjugation, the central court has
> A single-pronged karma-vajra,
> With lotuses at the four corners.
> The second court should depict
> The retinue of Trailokyavijaya,
> In four wrathful forms.
> The third court and gates
> Are also as previously described,
> But all in wrathful aspect.
> For summoning, the central court
> Should depict a vajra hook,
> With lotuses at the four corners.
> The second court should depict
> The retinue of Akṣobhya Buddha.
> The third court, four corners,
> Eight directions and four gates:
> Know them as in the first *kuṇḍa*.
> For enchantment, the central court
> Depicts a lotus karma-emblem,
> With three-pronged vajras at the four corners.
> The second court should depict
> The retinue of Amitāyus,
> Depicting four honored ones.
> The third court, four corners,
> Eight directions and four gates
> Are also as previously described.
> This is the five homas,
> As taught in the Yoga Sūtra;
> Practitioners should know them.

The hearth rim is two finger-breadths high and four finger-breadths wide. Inside the rim, at the base of the hearth opening, the width is two finger-breadths. The mudrā-emblem within is two finger-breadths high. Near the practitioner's side of the hearth, open a vertical neck, four finger-breadths wide and two finger-breadths long. Next, a horizontal section, ten finger-breadths long and four finger-breadths wide. Then make a lotus-petal shape of appropriate proportions. From the vertical neck to the petal tip is a total of twelve finger-breadths. The height throughout is level with the rim. All five types of hearths follow this same design. The method for preparing the ground is the same as for the great maṇḍala.

For digging the ground and empowering the spade and other implements: the mudrā is the vajra-bond with the thumbs, index fingers, and ring fingers standing upright. The mantra is recited twenty-one times:

*Oṃ nikhanavasudhā svāhā*

For empowering the clay, cow-dung, and perfumed ointment: the mudrā is both hands in añjali, bending the index and ring fingers so the second joints touch, thumbs standing upright together, the space between the index fingers forming a mouth-shape. The mantra is recited twenty-one times:

*Oṃ amṛtodbhava hūṃ phaṭ svāhā*

The mudrā and mantra for empowering the five-colored powder are as explained in the Yoga Sūtra. For empowering the ghee, honey, yogurt, milk, wood, five grains, incense, flowers, and so forth, use the Vajrakarma Bodhisattva mantra, empowering each seven times. The mudrā: with each hand, press the thumb against the ring-finger nail, the other three fingers spread open and upright like a vajra shape, then cross them with the right pressing the left. The mantra:

*Oṃ vajrakarma kaṃ*

All wood for burning in the homa is placed on the right side. The ghee goes on the lotus-leaf platform. Honey, yogurt, milk, rice-gruel, and cooked rice are placed near the right side of the hearth. On the left side, place two fragrant-water vessels. The vessels may be gold, silver, refined copper, white porcelain, or conch; the fragrance is white sandalwood, saffron, camphor, and the like. One vessel is for sprinkling the fire and offering materials; the other is for the holy assembly and Fire Deva to rinse their mouths. The sprinkling mudrā: press the thumb against the middle-finger nail, the other three fingers spread open and upright. The mantra:

*Oṃ amṛte hūṃ phaṭ*

The mouth-rinsing mudrā: the right hand in vajra-fist, extending the index finger to stir the water, empowering seven times. Then the right hand forms a scoop, ladles the water, and with arm lowered, sprinkles the fire in a rightward circuit away from the body. The mantra:

*Oṃ varada vajradhāṃ*

For pacification: the honored one, Fire Deva, hearth, clothing, food, and flowers are all white. Sit in the auspicious posture, both feet crossed with knees up and the right pressing the left, in accord with the compassionate mind. For enrichment: all yellow, in full-lotus posture. For subjugation: all black, in squatting posture, with the left big toe pressing upon the right big toenail. For summoning: all red, in half-lotus posture. For enchantment: same color as summoning, in the sage's posture, sitting on a support with feet hanging down.

For the invitation: from the samaya through the invitation, all follow the standard method, or follow the five types of homa, becoming the five-aspect body of each division's lord. After inviting, recite praises, establish the holy assembly with the four attractors surrounding the hearth-altar, then offer the *arghya*. Each forms their own karma-mudrā to establish their positions, displays the original samaya, and recites the homa mantra 108 times. Then take a flower, empower it with the Fire Deva mantra three or seven times, and cast it into the fire. Then form the Fire Deva mudrā: the left hand grasps the right wrist, the right hand extends the palm outward, the thumb lies horizontally across the palm, and the index finger moves like a hook, back and forth, to beckon. After the offering, press the thumb against the index finger: this becomes the sending-off. The mantra:

*Oṃ ehy ehi mahābhūta devāriṣiṇi vijaya satvam agṛhītvā hutaye mahāramā siddhaye santuṣṭubhava aghnāye ha viyā kaviyā vahadanāya svāhā*

After inviting, sprinkle fragrant water three times and rinse three times. Then using the main mantra, with the large ladle take three full ladles of ghee and cast them into the fire, visualizing casting them into the Fire Deva's mouth and down to the lotus in his heart. The mantra:

*Oṃ aghnāye svāhā*

Then with this same mantra, use the small ladle to make three offerings each of honey, yogurt, milk, wood, and so forth through incense and flowers. Visualize the Fire Deva with four arms: the right hand in the fearlessness gesture, the second right hand holding a rosary; the left hand holding an ascetic's staff, the second left hand holding a *kuṇḍikā* water-vessel. Visualize from his heart, pervading his entire body, flowing forth immeasurable clouds of perfume, clouds of flowers, clouds of incense, food, lamps, and manifold offerings, making offerings to all Buddhas, bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, śrāvakas, and all worldly devas. At the *svāhā* of the Fire Deva mantra, state what is sought and cast the offering. Then with the large ladle, three full ladles are offered. Empower a flower and place it at the original directional seat, requesting that he leave the hearth and return to his own seat. Then purify the fire three times. Using the four-syllable vidyā, invite the Buddhas and bodhisattvas, each to sit in their own seats. Offer three times, rinse three times, and with three full large ladles of ghee make the offering. Then with the small ladle, make three offerings each of honey, yogurt, milk, rice-gruel, cooked rice, wood, five grains, incense, flowers, and so forth, visualizing them cast into the holy honored ones' mouths, reaching their hearts.

If performing the pacification rite, add ten times the amount of sesame to the five grains. Use 108 sticks of wood, or 54, or 21. The mantra:

*Oṃ sarvapāpadahana vajrāya svāhā*

Some teachings say to use the division-mother for pacification, or the honored one's own mantra, or Vairocana's mantra. In each case, above the *svāhā*, add words for the wish to remove all calamities for self and others. Concentrate the mind upon the holy assembly within the hearth. Visualize the holy assembly, from their hearts outward, through every pore of their bodies, flowing forth clouds and oceans of offerings reaching boundless worlds, making offerings to all Buddhas and removing all suffering of the three evil destinies. When the homa is complete, offer three full large ladles of ghee to the holy assembly. The remaining five grains, incense, flowers, and so forth are gathered in one vessel and offered to the worldly devas of the ten directions.

If performing enrichment: first invite the Fire Deva as before, then offer the holy assembly three large ladles, along with wood, incense, flowers, and so forth. Burn polished rice as before. If one wishes to extend life, burn *kuṭaja* grass. The life-extension hearth is like the enrichment hearth, with an armor shape drawn outside. The polished rice and *kuṭaja* grass are increased tenfold over the other materials.

The enrichment mantra:

*Oṃ vajrapuṣṭaye svāhā*

The life-extension mantra:

*Oṃ vajrāyuṣe svāhā*

Above the *svāhā*, add the words of what the heart seeks. Concentrate the mind upon the holy assembly within the hearth. Visualize from the holy assembly's hearts outward, through every pore of their bodies, flowing forth clouds and oceans of offerings reaching boundless worlds, making offerings to all Buddhas and bodhisattvas, and with radiant light illuminating and touching all sentient beings of the six destinies and four modes of birth, all attaining prosperity, wealth, nobility, and extended lifespan. Then with this same light, visualize raining the seven treasures and all needed provisions upon one's own home, and celestial ambrosia pouring down upon one's own body, pervading every pore.

If performing subjugation: first invite the Fire Deva as before. Use rapeseed oil, mustard-seed oil, water-buffalo ghee, or *rudra*-substance. First offer the holy assembly three large ladles. Then use unscented flowers or foul-smelling flowers, *guggula* resin, salt, poison, and the like, or use only iron filings, or make a figure (*māṇuṣa*) in the shape of the target person, cut it into pieces, and cast them in. When casting offerings to the Fire Deva, visualize from the Fire Deva's heart outward, his entire body flowing forth weapons and implements that fall upon the target person. Visualize the Fire Deva and the honored one both in wrathful form. The mantra:

*Hūṃ vajrasattvāya phaṭ*

Above the *phaṭ*, add the target person's name. Or use the method of one's own honored one, or the mantra of Acala, or Trailokyavijaya, or the Six-Footed Mañjuśrī. Visualize the wrathful honored one's body flowing forth clouds and oceans of weapons and implements, making offerings to all the wrathful honored ones throughout the sky, and then these weapons and implements falling upon the target and their home.

If performing summoning: all is the same as enrichment. Only the flowers should be red flowers from thorny wood, or use whatever is burned in the method of the honored one. The mantra:

*Hūṃ vajrākarṣāya jaḥ*

Above the *jaḥ*, add the target person's name. Visualize from the honored one's heart outward, the entire body flowing forth immeasurable vajra hooks, making offerings to all Buddhas, bodhisattvas, and holy ones throughout the sky. Then with these hooks, hook and summon sentient beings of the three evil destinies, placing them in the wholesome realms of humans and devas. Then with these hooks, enter the target person's heart and summon them.

If performing the enchantment rite: all is the same as above. Only the flowers should be red flowers, or use whatever is used in the method of the honored one. The mantra:

*Hūṃ vajraveśaya jaḥ*

Above the *jaḥ*, add the target person's name. Visualize from the honored one's body flowing forth flower-arrows pervading boundless worlds, making offerings to all Buddhas and holy ones, and striking the hearts of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas to dispel their aversion, and striking the mutually hostile hearts of sentient beings in the six destinies and four modes of birth. Then with these arrows, strike the target person in five places: the forehead, the two breasts, the heart, and the lower region.

Next, the Samāpatti Method is explained. Place the object to be accomplished before the ghee vessel. If the object is large, place it to the right or left, wherever convenient. The practitioner's body, the ghee vessel, the object, the hearth, and the holy assembly: these five are assembled and should be arranged in cyclic sequence. Take the small ladle, fill it with ghee, and pour it upon the object. Recite the mantra up to the *svā* syllable, then raise the ladle and cast it into the fire. As the *hā* sound falls, draw it out long, bringing the ladle back to the object; the *hā* sound ends only then. If empowering a person, place the ladle upon their head. If the honored one's mantra has no *svāhā*, add it when reciting. If ghee is unavailable, milk may also be used. If empowering a distant person, write their name or obtain their garment, and set the mind upon them while empowering. After making offerings to the holy assembly, use the large ladle with three full ladles to offer to the holy assembly, sprinkle three times and rinse three times. Then take the small ladle and recite the mantra for extinguishing the evil destinies, performing homa seven times, or twenty-one times, for all sentient beings. Mentally send the holy assembly back to their original seats. Using the four-syllable vidyā, guide the worldly devas of the ten directions into the hearth, sprinkle and rinse as before, then cast the remaining incense, flowers, ghee, honey, and so forth, reciting each mantra once or three times. At each *svāhā*, add the words of what is sought. Then form the holy assembly's karma and samaya mudrās, recite praises, make aspirations, form the Trailokyavijaya mudrā, dissolve the boundary with a leftward rotation, and send off as in the recitation method. Then exit the arena.

Outside the arena in the eight directions, spread *kuśa* grass, lotus leaves, or other green grass, or anoint circular altars to make ten positions. At the right and left of Indra in the east, place Brahmā and the Earth Deva. If there is no room outside the arena, create a separate square boundary in front, arrange the eight directions within it, and place two positions for Brahmā and the Earth Deva in the center.

**Indra in the East**: rides a white elephant amid five-colored clouds, his body golden. His right hand holds a three-pronged vajra at his heart; his left hand rests on his left hip. His left foot hangs down. Three celestial maidens each hold lotuses, with trays of blue lotuses, or trays of assorted flowers. The mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ indrāya svāhā*

**Agni, the Fire Deva, in the Southeast**: rides a blue ram. His body is the color of red flesh, with flames blazing all around. Of his two right hands, one holds green bamboo and the other holds a *kuṇḍikā* water-vessel. Of his two left hands, one has the palm raised and the other holds a rosary. Two celestial maidens hold heavenly flowers. To his left and right are ascetic seers. His left foot hangs down, with the right foot tucked under. The mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ aghnāye svāhā*

**Yama, the Deva of the South**: rides a water-buffalo. His right hand holds a staff topped with a human head; his left hand faces upward. Two celestial maidens attend him. Two ghost messengers hold a sword and a halberd, their color reddish-black. His right foot hangs down. The mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ yamāya svāhā*

**Nirṛti, the Rākṣasa Lord, in the Southwest**: rides a white lion, his body clad in armor. His right hand holds a sword upright; his left hand has the thumb pressing the middle and little fingers. His color is reddish-white. Two celestial maidens attend to his left and right. Two rākṣasa demons hold three-pronged halberds. The mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ nairṛtye svāhā*

**Varuṇa, the Water Deva, in the West**: dwells in the water, riding a tortoise. His color is pale green. His right hand holds a sword; his left hand holds a dragon-lasso. On his crown are five dragons. Four celestial maidens hold wondrous flowers. The mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ varuṇāya svāhā*

**Vāyu, the Wind Deva, in the Northwest**: rides a deer amid the clouds, clad in armor. His left hand rests on his hip; his right hand holds a single-pronged lance, from which hangs a crimson pennant. Two celestial maidens attend him, along with a yakṣa retinue. The mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ vāyave svāhā*

**Vaiśravaṇa in the North**: sits upon two demons, his body clad in armor. His left hand holds a stūpa on his palm; his right hand holds a jeweled club. His body is golden. Two celestial maidens hold jeweled flowers and the like. The mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ vaiśravaṇāya svāhā*

**Īśāna in the Northeast** (formerly called Maheśvara, also called Mahādeva): rides a tawny bull. His left hand holds a *kalpa*-vessel filled with blood; his right hand holds a three-pronged lance. His body is pale blue-grey flesh-colored, with three eyes and a wrathful expression, two fangs protruding upward, skull-garlands for necklaces, and within his crown two upward-facing crescents. Two celestial maidens hold flowers. The mantra:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ īśānāya svāhā*

The mantra for Brahmā above:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ brahmaṇe svāhā*

At the center, place the four-armed Acala: his body is the color of blue-grey flesh. Each of his two hands forms a separate vajra-fist, with the index and little fingers each curved like hooks, placed at the two corners of his mouth to appear like fangs. His right hand holds a sword upright; his left hand holds a lasso. He sits in half-lotus with the right pressing the left upon a flat stone, with blazing flames of authority pervading his entire body like fire.

The mantra for the Earth Deva below:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ pṛthivyai svāhā*

The offerings for the ten directional devas are mixed gruel: polished rice, sesame, and mung beans cooked together, placed in one vessel. At each seat, place a clean leaf. First, pour a little fragrant water from a clean vessel onto the leaf as an offering. Next, flick a little perfumed ointment with the fingers as an offering. Next, incense. Next, music. One ladle of gruel is placed on each, empowered three times each with the corresponding mantra. Then use a small wax candle or paper taper, inserting it into the gruel. Take care to keep the candles lit until all positions have been served; while the candles still burn, the offerings should not be interrupted. Above each *svāhā*, add the words of the aspiration.

The mantra for the Seven Luminaries:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ grahāśvarya prabhāto jyotir māyā svāhā*

The mantra for the Twenty-Eight Constellations:

*Namo samantabuddhānāṃ nakṣatra junadaye svāhā*

Among the eight directions, the two positions correspond with the devas above and below. All offerings are the same. If there is a separate aspiration, one may use them individually. Within the homa altar, each is mentally established according to their proper direction.

> I now next explain
> The pouring-ladle and the casting-ladle.
> By abiding in these for accomplishment,
> The vidyā-holder swiftly attains.
> The pouring-ladle is one cubit in length,
> Made of *khadira* wood, sturdy and dense,
> Without holes or cavities.
> The bowl should be wonderfully handsome,
> Four finger-breadths in width,
> One finger-breadth in depth,
> In the shape of a *śrīvatsa* mark.
> Within it, a three-pronged vajra
> Should be made beautifully.
> The handle should be thick enough for one hand-grip.
> The casting-ladle is one cubit in length,
> Of the same wood as described before,
> Or one may use *khadira*.
> The bowl is one finger-joint in circumference,
> With depth at half.
> Within it, make a lotus
> Or a vajra emblem.
> As the Great Seer has taught,
> Those who seek siddhi should make them.
> The homa leads to swift accomplishment.

### Colophon

The *Hakkeshiroku* ("Record of the Eight Houses") states: "The *Vajraśekhara Yoga Homa Ritual Manual*, one fascicle, translated by Amoghavajra, newly entered in the Zhenyuan era catalog, transmitted by Hairen and Yun Zhen. The *Homa Ritual Manual*, one fascicle, translated by the Siṃhala tripiṭaka śramaṇa Amoghavajra, whose name was changed by imperial decree to Zhizang ('Wisdom Treasury'): this is slightly more extensive than the preceding version."

Since the Zhizang translation is already in the Ming canon, and that version has lacunae and lacks the forms of the Eight Direction Devas, while this version does not suffer those deficiencies, and the two texts differ in completeness and conciseness, it is fitting to preserve both. Therefore, based on the collated edition by Master Myōzui of Mount Kōya in the sixth year of Hōreki, *hinoe-ne* (1756), at Tō-ji, further collated against the Kōwa 5th year edition of the Koikebō sutra repository, this woodblock edition was prepared. In the first year of Kyōwa, *kanoto-tori* (1801), in the fifth month, recorded by Kaidō, a śramaṇa temporarily residing at Buzan.
