# 大乘二十頌論
## Mahāyāna Treatise of Twenty Verses

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1576

Composed by Nāgārjuna (龍樹). Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護).

---

> Homage to the inconceivable nature,
> The unattached true wisdom of all buddhas.
> Dharmas are neither words nor without words;
> The Buddha, out of compassion, skillfully proclaimed them.
>
> The ultimate truth is unborn,
> Turning in accordance yet without nature.
> Buddhas and sentient beings have one characteristic,
> Equal like empty space.
>
> This shore and the far shore are unborn;
> Inherent nature arises from conditions.
> Those formations are all empty:
> The practice of the wisdom of omniscience.
>
> The undefiled nature of true suchness,
> Nondual, equally quiescent.
> The nature of all dharmas and their inherent nature
> Are no different from reflections.
>
> The discriminating mind of ordinary beings
> Grasps at a self where there is no real self.
> Thus afflictions arise,
> Along with suffering, pleasure, equanimity, and the rest.
>
> In the world, aging, sickness, and death
> Are suffering, unlovable.
> Following karma, one falls and tumbles;
> In truth, there is no happiness here.
>
> The heavenly realm has supreme and wondrous pleasures,
> The hells have extreme and great suffering.
> These are all unreal realms;
> Through the six destinies, one constantly revolves.
>
> Sentient beings falsely discriminate,
> And the fire of afflictions blazes.
> Falling into the hells and other destinies,
> Like a wildfire burning a forest.
>
> Sentient beings are originally like illusions,
> Yet they grasp at illusory realms.
> Treading the path formed by illusion,
> They do not understand dependent origination.
>
> Like a painter in the world
> Who paints the image of a yakṣa,
> Then frightens himself with his own painting...
> This is called one without wisdom.
>
> Sentient beings generate defilements themselves,
> Creating the causes of that cycle of rebirth.
> Having created them, they fear falling;
> Without wisdom, there is no liberation.
>
> With deluded and false minds,
> Sentient beings give rise to doubt, confusion, and stains.
> Imputing nature where there is no nature,
> They endure the worst of sufferings.
>
> The Buddha, seeing them without rescue,
> Gave rise to a compassionate mind.
> Therefore he generated the aspiration for enlightenment
> And broadly cultivated the practices of awakening.
>
> Having attained the unsurpassed fruit of wisdom,
> He then observed the world
> Bound by discrimination
> And therefore acted for their benefit.
>
> From birth and after being born,
> He fully revealed the true and correct meaning.
> Later, observing the emptiness of the world,
> Free from beginning, middle, and end.
>
> Observing birth-and-death and nirvāṇa,
> Both are without self.
> Without defilement and without destruction,
> Originally pure, eternally quiescent.
>
> The realms within a dream,
> Upon awakening, are entirely unseen.
> The wise one wakes from the sleep of delusion
> And also does not see birth-and-death.
>
> Those obscured by ignorant darkness
> Fall into the ocean of birth-and-death.
> Imputing birth where there is no birth,
> They give rise to worldly discrimination.
>
> If one discriminates that there is birth,
> Sentient beings are not in accord with truth.
> Within the dharmas of birth-and-death,
> They give rise to thoughts of permanence, pleasure, and self.
>
> All of this is mind only,
> Establishing illusory appearances.
> Creating wholesome and unwholesome karma,
> They produce good and bad rebirths.
>
> If one extinguishes the wheel of mind,
> One thereby extinguishes all dharmas.
> These dharmas are without self;
> All dharmas are entirely pure.
>
> The Buddha broadly proclaimed worldly dharmas;
> One should know they are conditioned by ignorance.
> If one can refrain from giving rise to the discriminating mind,
> Where would any sentient being be born?
>
> Within the dharma-nature of those dharmas,
> Not the slightest dharma can truly be found.
> Like a magician in the world performing illusions,
> The wise should understand it thus.
>
> In the great ocean of the cycle of birth-and-death,
> The waters of sentient beings' afflictions are full.
> If one does not convey them by the Great Vehicle,
> How could they ever reach the far shore?
