# 佛說四自侵經
## Sūtra of the Four Self-Inflicted Harms, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0736

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

The Buddha said: "Not studying day and night, not ceasing lust in old age, not giving when one has wealth, and not accepting the Buddha's words: these four arise from the mind and turn back to harm oneself. The powerful and wealthy of ancient times who were arrogant, self-indulgent, and willful, the kings, emperors, and rulers, and the foolish people of the world: they only knew that days and nights pass swiftly, unaware that their lives were ending. They were constantly angry, forceful and obstinate, indulgent in lust, proud of their wealth. Where are they now? Those who do not love the path of the sūtras, who resent hearing the truth, and thereby harm themselves, who let their minds run wild and follow their desires without restraint: at such times they do not notice hunger or thirst, and they cannot reflect upon the nine orifices, which are wounds, foul and impure. Whether walking, standing, lying down, or awake, in a hundred kinds of affairs they plant the roots of suffering. They are heedless of the great Dharma and do not know how to care for themselves. When old age and illness come, their appearance changes. The desires of the five pleasures cannot be kept forever. When illness confines one to bed, there is only restless agitation. When the command of death suddenly arrives, the body must decay. How can it last? Birth and death do not cease; the evil paths do not rest. One's own lifespan is also subject to birth and death. All the suffering, sorrow, and affliction come from oneself.

"Seeing this, what is a person? The pleasures of lust, games, singing, dancing, and entertainments: how long do they last? They are like a single breath. The wise should understand this clearly, change their aspirations, and guard the body in purity. All that exists in the world ultimately returns to emptiness. Suppose one were singing and performing, but the audience found no joy in it and took no pleasure: the performer would be ashamed. The multitudes are deluded, always delighting in entertainment. When illness and sorrow arrive, only then do they become distressed. In times of ease they busy themselves with ten thousand affairs and do not plan for themselves, having already incurred ten thousand sins. Calamity and misfortune answer like an echo, yet they cannot distinguish what has brought them upon themselves. The body is like a painted vase, filled within with impurity, foul pus and blood, like a leather bag wrapping filth. Not knowing that inside and out the body must ultimately perish, they constantly adorn it with colors, creams, and powder, thinking themselves beautiful with incomparable features, not examining the wounds of the nine orifices and what flows from them. People such as these are entangled in nets, unable to observe the body clearly.

"It is like a magical illusion, vague and indistinct. Only the practitioner of the path sees how worldly people are deluded in this way, constantly attached to the body. Carefully observe all the bustling commotion: people merely quarrel about trivial matters of the throat. Calamity comes from the mouth; a thousand misfortunes and ten thousand sins turn back to entangle them. Some harm each other, and anger becomes enmity, all arising from greed, competing and quarreling over gain and desire. The deluded masses are all alike in this, not recognizing the truth of the path versus the delusion of the conventional. Old age and death arrive suddenly; one has no freedom. The benevolent and wise, if they can clearly understand that possessions are impermanent, arriving suddenly like wind and rain, like lightning, like a dream, like an illusion, like a mirage; that impermanence comes to confront us, which no one welcomes; that the chain of beginning and end binds and follows us, never separated from the prison of the five aggregates and six bases:

"Those who are unfilial to teachers and fathers, who distance themselves from the path and from wisdom, who are attached to the body, family, and flesh, whose lustful behavior does not cease: the six things of ear, eye, nose, mouth, body, and mind only plant disasters and troubles, more numerous than all the grasses and trees in the world. The mind of love and attachment is both broad and long, like a river or the sea. From birth to old age one experiences ten thousand affairs. Though one may have a beautiful and fine appearance, one should observe with composure that all is impermanent, suffering, empty, and without self. Yet instead one is deluded, seeing only perverse advantage, drowning in the five sounds, concealing one's abilities within, unable to restrain the mind, roaming freely in the four directions, contriving worldly expedients, without a moment's thought of the passing of time, without a step taken in auspiciousness. One should submit the mind to a single purpose and exert all effort to eliminate the afflictions of the multitude. Do not be like an infant who cannot distinguish good from bad, who does not know the impurity of excrement and urine!

"Diligently cultivate and advance, abandon evil and leave no trace of it. Then one can depart from the afflictions of birth, old age, illness, death, decay, and ruin. Reflect upon this: from the Trāyastriṃśa heaven of no-thought downward, all exist within the difficulties of the three realms of beginning and end. When one sets the mind on studying the path but does not discern the path clearly, being attached to the body and letting the mind scatter, one falls back into the six sense-faculties. One should reflect carefully, set the mind rightly upon birth and death, and when there is no more coming and going, beginning and end are severed. People are attached to form and taste; the various passions do not cease. They are averse to precepts and prohibitions. When the multitude of doubts is not removed, and one desperately clings to permanence, seeing only what is before the eyes: for this reason the cycling through the five destinies does not stop, and one again obtains a body. Eliminate and cut away the six sense-faculties, strip away the five aggregates, and cultivate the practice; then there is peace. When seeing what is desirable, one should examine oneself. Not accepting the six sense-entries, one is without worry or fear. Already without fear, one enters the place of the path and approaches the gate of wisdom. If the learner wavers, with a mind that is not single-pointed and constant, sometimes advancing and sometimes retreating, this causes delusion, and the coming and going does not cease.

"To speak by way of a parable: suppose one was formerly an enemy who later reconciled and became the closest of friends; or suppose one was formerly a brave warrior who later became timid and fearful. If the mind turns toward the path but midway goes astray again, falling back into the six sense-entries: this is what is meant. Falling back into the suffering of birth and death in the five destinies, one drinks bitterness and eats poison, devouring one another in turn, not avoiding even relatives, not distinguishing one's own clan, not knowing oneself. The majestic Buddha-sage sees this with his own eyes and grieves for those who have lost the path. Therefore he extends the four immeasurables of kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and protection, pitying the myriad beings. The multitudes of living beings have not seen the many types of beings who, if they cherish a single-pointed aspiration without wavering:

"If there is a person who sincerely wishes to cross over, they should carefully reflect and practice the path with single-minded determination. Do not say, 'My family and relatives,' or cling to the bonds of love. You should know that all is impermanent; this body is merely borrowed. All unions and pairings must end in separation. There is always sorrow and affliction. We are born dependent on one another, yet all things return to emptiness; none are truly ours. Those who clearly know this emptiness can then liberate the people of the ten directions throughout the world. All who practice the path of karma should not doubt in generosity, should not harbor anger, and should abandon the mind of greed and attachment. When all causes and conditions are severed, the practitioner of the path observes what should and should not be done, examining the self within. It is like a dream, like an illusion. It is like a military campaign where an army of a million relies upon a famous general to repel the enemy. The practitioner of the path subdues the mind and controls the will, cultivates the Dharma and serves the path, follows and practices the precepts and prohibitions, keeps body and mind pure and bright, spreads kindness and practices virtue, abandons anger, arrogance, and quarreling, and devotes single-minded effort to practicing the path without obstruction, with the aspiration set upon the right track. Just as a general leads the multitude, one must first set one's own mind aright, and then act with the body. When body and mind are both right, nothing is lost. When nothing is lost, there are no more companions needed. Having attained the path, one departs completely, heroic and peerless, and then one knows the honor of the path.

"It is like people in the world whose practices differ: some serve heavenly spirits, earth, water, fire, wind, the sun and moon, mountains and rivers, and various ghosts and spirits, yet this is forever without benefit. They remain within the cycle of birth and death, unable to escape the nets and prisons. They merely wish for long life, ease, and indulgence. Even if they obtained a hundred or a thousand years, what would that be worth? In the end they must return to death. One day of practicing the path surpasses a hundred years of life. Not reckoning with impermanence, they instead are greedy and attached, saying they have parents, brothers, wife, children, and relatives near and far. When sudden illness arrives and they lie weakened in bed, they tell their relatives, 'Take a share of my pain!' All say they cannot. Only then does the sick person realize.

"The five kinds of relatives whom one expected to bring benefit: one has always labored in worldly work on their behalf, providing for them in season, yet this has caused the self to fall into greed, binding and harming oneself. Worrying about the nine kinds of kin obstructs and ruins wholesome deeds and destroys the mind of the path. When old age, illness, and death come, the good and evil, suffering and happiness, one must bear alone. No one can take one's place. Those who have not attained the path all have such thoughts and cannot practice generosity or uphold the path as virtue. Those who can give generously, in this life and the next, will always obtain peace and security. It becomes provisions for the journey through birth and death. In every life they are wealthy and noble, obtaining carriages, elephants, horses, houses, gold, silver, and treasures beyond count. Parents, brothers, wife, children, and acquaintances all receive peace on their account. When one practices generosity, those nearby rejoice along with one, and the merit obtained is immeasurable. How much more so for the donor! When one personally gives with one's own hands, in future lives the reward follows the body like a shadow following form, like an echo answering a sound.

"That which is without the covering of the aggregates is called nirvāṇa. In nirvāṇa there are no five aggregates, no sun, moon, or stars, no wind, cold, light, or darkness. It is neither void nor real. There are no years. Old age, illness, and death are eliminated. The echo is unheard. One no longer takes on the body of the aggregates, being united with the path. This is true long life, always obtaining peace and security, enduring without limit, with happiness beyond measure, not something worldly people can understand. The wise who comprehend this, who distinguish and explain it, can attain the path of the Buddha, the true person. The foolish do not practice the path but plan only for the body, yearning over old age, illness, and death. The karmic deeds of danger and harm bring manifold suffering, like planting the five grains and then eating them oneself. Good and evil are thus; each receives their own. Having already planted the root of affliction, there is no use fearing it, yet people still dread old age, illness, and death. The body of the four great elements cannot escape this difficulty. Flood, fire, thieves, enemies, creditors, government officials, and myriad troubles: people fear all of these as well, yet do not realize that this is suffering. It originally arises from this, yet they pursue sensory pleasures instead. People born in the world create these worries. This is not given by heaven, nor caused by the path; it comes naturally from what one has done in the past.

"For a person who studies the path and seeks to cross over the world, it is extremely easy and not difficult, and does not require laborious toil. One should always exert the mind with diligence and seek it with effort, believing in and receiving the holy teaching, heroically subduing the will, and then one attains clear wisdom, the reward of the precious essence. It is like the majesty of a lion before whom all beasts submit. It is also like worldly people who cannot understand that suffering is suffering, like a pig in a sty that does not know the stench as stench. Or like a moth flying into a lamp's flame: the utterly foolish follow the heart's desires. Seeing only depraved lust, they throw themselves into the prison of attachment, greedy for birth and death, not knowing that they are tormented by birth and death, thinking themselves without worry, supreme and unsurpassed. Walking vainly with false steps, looking far and wide with glaring eyes, they do not know what lies beyond heaven, earth, sun, and moon, that there is an art of advancing and retreating to seek life. They only wish to bustle about, competing for honor and nobility, coveting glory and fame, arrogant and self-indulgent, wanting everyone to submit to them, their authority covering heaven and earth, wanting people to fear them, expecting reverence and service, thinking themselves fulfilled in the present age.

"The Buddha sees the ten thousand affairs of the world, all dwelling in unworthiness. One should follow the path of transformation and emulate the practices that the ancient sages abandoned as polluting and degrading. Worldly people are pitiable: they rush about seeking gain, wanting ever more to have, greedily pursuing quick wealth, increasingly attached to the five desires, turning round and round in the worldly path, unable to free themselves. They are like prisoners in jail, with locks and cangues removed: how can they save themselves and gain liberation? How bitter! Those of this kind have their aspiration set on birth and death, like a carriage wheel, without end. Sever this course of action and eliminate the multitude of desires, as if opening the prison gate, as a bird escaping a net. Those who study the path yet sit attached to relatives, spouse, and children: this is why they go astray and do not reach nirvāṇa."

All who had come to the assembly, hearing this teaching, renewed their single-minded determination to leave the conventional world, cultivated detachment with diligence, bowed, and departed.
