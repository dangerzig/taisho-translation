# 菩薩訶色欲法經
## Sūtra of the Bodhisattva's Reproach of Sensual Desire

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 615

Translated from the Chinese. Later Qin, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Female beauty is the shackles and chains of the world; ordinary beings cling to it with longing and cannot extricate themselves. Female beauty is a grave affliction of the world; ordinary beings are beset by it and cannot escape it even unto death. Female beauty is the misfortune and calamity of the world; ordinary beings who encounter it find that no disaster fails to befall them. A practitioner who has succeeded in abandoning it and then turns back to dwell upon it again is like one who escapes from prison and then longs to return; like one restored from madness who takes delight in it once more; like one who recovers from illness and then desires to fall sick again. The wise are angered by this, knowing that such madness and stumbling means death may come any day!

Ordinary beings prize sensual beauty and willingly become its servants, racing about for their whole lives and toiling on its behalf. Even if they were to be cut apart inch by inch on the chopping block, or assailed from all sides by blades and arrows, they would gladly endure it, not regarding it as a misfortune. To call them madmen delighting in their own madness is no exaggeration. If a practitioner is able to abandon it without a backward glance, this is to break the cangue and slip the chains, to abhor madness and be averse to illness, to be free from misfortune and calamity, to dwell in peace and good fortune, to escape the prison-house, forever free from affliction.

The nature of women: their words are like honey, yet their hearts are like poison. They are like a deep and still pool, clear as a mirror, yet in which a water-dragon lurks; like a mountain of gold with a treasure-cave, yet in which a lion makes its lair. One should know that this danger must not be approached. Discord in the household arises from women; the ruin of clan and family is the fault of women. Truly they are hidden thieves who extinguish the light of others' wisdom; they are also a hunting enclosure from which few escape. They are like a tall net in which a flock of birds becomes trapped and cannot beat their wings to fly; like a fine-meshed net into which a school of fish swims, only to have their entrails gutted and their flesh laid upon the chopping block; like a dark pit into which the sightless fall, like moths rushing into flame. Therefore the wise, knowing this, keep their distance and do not suffer its harm; loathing it as filth, they do not allow themselves to be deluded by such things.
