# 佛說長者女菴提遮師子吼了義經
## Sūtra of the Lion's Roar of Definitive Meaning by Āmrapālī, Daughter of the Elder, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0580

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with immeasurable bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, and a multitude of bodhisattva mahāsattvas.

At that time, more than twenty miles west of the city of Śrāvastī, there was a village called Dīrghapradeśa. There was a brahmin named Vāsiṣṭha who dwelt there. This man was widely learned and deeply trusted the inner scriptures, revering and upholding the Buddha's teachings.

At that time the brahmin wished to hold a great assembly. He went to the Jetavana to invite the Buddha and the Saṃgha. The Buddha accepted his invitation, and the brahmin returned home.

At the appointed time, the Buddha went with the great assembly to that village and arrived at the brahmin's residence. At that time the elder, seeing the Buddha, leaped with joy, unable to contain himself. He led all his household members to the Buddha's presence, and each bowed to the Buddha and stood reverently. The brahmin had an eldest daughter named Āmrapālī. She had been married and was temporarily visiting home to attend to her parents. This woman was beautiful in appearance and lofty in her understanding. Her heart was gentle and her mind was open and generous. She could harmonize husband and wife, serve and care for her relatives, and honor her husband as though keeping a sacred vow. Her deportment was without equal, surpassing all others.

Her parents and household all went out to see the Buddha, but this woman alone remained in her room. From the time of her birth, her parents could never fathom her ways, and so they named her Āmrapālī. At that time the Tathāgata knew the elder had a daughter who was inside the room and had not come out, and he knew the reason she did not come out. If she were to come out, she would benefit immeasurable great beings, as well as the devas and humans.

The Buddha then said to the elder: "Have all of your household come out?"

The brahmin knelt upright before the Buddha with joined hands. Taking the fact that his daughter had not come out as a disgrace, he was silent and did not answer.

The Buddha then knew his thoughts and said: "The midday hour approaches. Can you prepare the offerings?"

The brahmin then followed the Buddha's instruction and rose to prepare the offerings. The great assembly and the elder's household all finished their midday meal, but this woman alone had not yet eaten.

At that time the Tathāgata had intentionally left food remaining in his bowl. He sent a phantom woman to bring this leftover food to the girl Āmrapālī inside the room.

The phantom woman announced in verse:

> "This is the Tathāgata's remainder,
> A gift from the unsurpassed, honored one.
> I have come in obedience to the Buddha's instruction;
> May you receive it with purity."

The girl Āmrapālī praised this in verse:

> "How wonderful! The greatly compassionate one
> Knows I am here in my room.
> Now he bestows this single-flavored food;
> I look up and behold the sage's intent."

She replied to the phantom woman in verse:

> "I am always mindful of what I contemplate:
> The practice of the Great Sage.
> I have never been different from you;
> What is impure about this?"

When the phantom woman heard Āmrapālī's verse, she vanished. The girl Āmrapālī then silently recited a verse in her mind:

> "Where is my husband now?
> May he come out to see the Honored One.
> May he know my heart is pure;
> May he come quickly so we may hear together."

At that time, because of Āmrapālī's power of pure mind, her husband arrived at her side according to her thought. When Āmrapālī saw her husband, her mind was filled with joy, and she praised this in verse:

> "How wonderful! The great Honored One
> Has now fulfilled my wish.
> I would not refuse to set aside a minor rule,
> Lest we fail to hear the teaching together."

When her husband saw Āmrapālī speak this verse, he reproached her in verse:

> "How foolish you are!
> You do not know what is fitting for yourself.
> You trouble the sage to bestow leftover food;
> What purpose does keeping such rules serve?"

Then the girl Āmrapālī followed her husband to the Buddha's presence. Each bowed to the Buddha and the great assembly and stood reverently. Then the girl Āmrapālī praised the Buddha in verse:

> "I am mindful of the greatly compassionate one,
> The honored one who rescues and protects the ten directions.
> Wishing to reveal the treasury of secrets,
> He bestowed upon me the pure leftover food.
> The Great Sage is so difficult to encounter.
> The world's minds harbor doubts:
> Who is qualified to ask about the Dharma
> And open the foundation of awakening for all?"

At that time Śāriputra said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What woman is this who has suddenly come here and speaks such Dharma-verses, saying she received leftover food?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "This is the elder's daughter."

He asked again: "From where has she come? What cause brought her here?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "This woman has not come from far away. She was right here in this room. Although she has parents and household, her husband was not present. Because of her vow of devotion and obedience to her husband, she would not lightly leave her parents' home or appear before the great assembly."

Then Śāriputra said to the Buddha: "By what good causes was this woman born into this elder's household, with such an appearance? And by what causes and conditions did she obtain such a husband, with such strict restraint, unable to freely see the Buddha and the Saṃgha?"

The Buddha then said to Śāriputra: "Ask her yourself."

Then Śāriputra asked the girl: "By what causes and conditions were you born into this elder's household? And by what causes and conditions did you obtain such a husband, with such strict restraint, unable to freely see the Buddha and the Saṃgha?"

The girl Āmrapālī answered in verse:

> "Because I did no evil in past lives,
> I was born into this elder's household.
> Because I did not cling to the female form,
> I obtained this pure husband.
> While I was within the inner room,
> I was already at ease.
> Never once transgressing my proper place,
> The Sage knew this and bestowed the remainder upon me.
> Alas, great elder!
> You do not know the true reason.
> Not transgressing even a hair's breadth:
> This is called great freedom.
> Though I was in the inner room,
> The Honored One appeared before me as if face to face.
> You bear the title of arhat,
> Yet following him constantly, you cannot see him.
> The Great Sage is not form,
> Nor is he apart from the form-body.
> Śrāvakas see Māra
> And call him a person of great power.
> Alas, great elder!
> Following the sage with only small skillful means,
> You do not know the original source;
> Regarding me you give rise to inverted views."

At that time Śāriputra was silent, privately thinking: "What woman is this? Her eloquence is such that I cannot match it."

The Buddha then knew his thoughts and said to him: "Do not retreat from questioning and answering, or give rise to other thoughts. This woman has already encountered immeasurable Buddhas. What she speaks is the medicine of the Dharma; do not doubt it."

Then Mañjuśrī asked Āmrapālī: "Do you now understand the meaning of birth and death?"

She answered: "By the Buddha's power, I understand."

He asked again: "If you understand, then what is the meaning of birth?"

She answered: "Birth has the meaning of unborn birth."

He asked again: "How does 'unborn birth' serve as the meaning of birth?"

She answered: "If one can clearly know that the four conditions of earth, water, fire, and wind have ultimately never of themselves achieved any combination, and yet can teach in accordance with what is appropriate, this is the meaning of birth."

He asked again: "If knowing that earth, water, fire, and wind ultimately do not of themselves achieve any combination is the meaning of birth, then there should be no mark of birth. What then serves as the meaning?"

She answered: "To be in the place of birth yet have no one who is born: this is true birth, and therefore it is said to have meaning."

Mañjuśrī asked again: "What is the meaning of death?"

She answered: "Death has the meaning of undying death."

He asked again: "How does 'undying death' serve as the meaning of death?"

She answered: "If one can clearly know that earth, water, fire, and wind ultimately do not of themselves achieve any dispersal, and yet can teach in accordance with what is appropriate, this is the meaning of death."

He asked again: "If knowing that earth, water, fire, and wind ultimately do not of themselves achieve dispersal, then there should be no mark of death. What then serves as the meaning?"

She answered: "To be in the place of death yet have a mind that does not perish: this is true death, and therefore it is said to have meaning."

Mañjuśrī asked again: "What is the meaning of permanence?"

She answered: "If one can clearly know that all dharmas ultimately arise and cease, change and transform, are without fixed nature, are like an illusion, and yet can teach in accordance with what is appropriate, this is the meaning of permanence."

He asked again: "If knowing that all dharmas ultimately arise and cease, are without fixed nature, and are like an illusion, then this is the meaning of impermanence. How can you take it as the meaning of permanence?"

She answered: "Dharmas arise yet do not of themselves achieve arising; they cease yet do not of themselves achieve ceasing; and so too with change and transformation. Because they do not of themselves achieve these, this is called the meaning of permanence."

He asked again: "What is the meaning of impermanence?"

She answered: "If one knows that all dharmas ultimately do not arise and do not cease, and in accordance with this characteristic can teach as is appropriate, this is the meaning of impermanence."

He asked again: "If knowing that all dharmas ultimately do not arise and do not cease, then this is the meaning of permanence. How can you say it is the meaning of impermanence?"

She answered: "Simply because all dharmas freely change and transform without a fixed characteristic, and do not of themselves follow along: knowing in this way, therefore it is said that there is the meaning of impermanence."

He asked again: "What is the meaning of emptiness?"

She answered: "If one can know the characteristics of all dharmas, which have never emptied themselves, and does not destroy present existence, and yet does not empty emptiness or make existence of existence, then therefore it is said that there is the meaning of emptiness."

He asked again: "If one does not empty emptiness and does not make existence of existence, then there is nothing whatsoever. What then serves as the meaning of emptiness?"

The girl Āmrapālī answered in verse:

> "Alas, truly great elder!
> You do not know the true meaning of emptiness.
> Form has no self-characteristic;
> Is this not like emptiness?
> If emptiness itself were empty,
> Then it could not contain form.
> Because emptiness does not empty itself,
> All forms arise from it."

Then Mañjuśrī asked again: "Is there one who clearly knows birth yet does not know the mark of birth, and is detained by birth?"

She answered: "Yes. Although one sees clearly, if one's power is not yet full, one may be detained by birth."

He asked again: "Is there one who, without knowledge and without recognizing the nature of birth, is ultimately never detained by birth?"

She answered: "No! Why? If one does not see the nature of birth, then even though one may gain some peace through training and discipline, the characteristic of unease will always remain as an obstacle. But if one can see the nature of birth, then even while in a place of unease, auspicious signs will always appear before one. If one does not understand in this way, then even though one may have all manner of excellent eloquence and speak of the most profound scriptures, this is itself the mind of arising and ceasing. To speak of the essential, secret words of true reality is like a blind person debating about colors: because of another's words, one can speak of blue, yellow, red, white, and black, but one cannot see the true nature of color oneself. Those who today cannot see all dharmas are just like this. They are merely born for the sake of birth and die for the sake of death. For such people, there is no meaning of birth and death! Those who are bound by permanence and impermanence are likewise. You should know, great elder, that emptiness too does not of itself achieve emptiness, and therefore it is said that there is the meaning of emptiness."

Then the Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Just so! Just so! What Āmrapālī has spoken is true and without error. One could make the sun cold or the moon hot, but what Āmrapālī has spoken cannot be altered."

Then Śāriputra asked the girl again: "Your wisdom and eloquence are such that the Buddha himself praises you, and we śrāvakas cannot match you. Why then are you unable to leave behind this female body and form?"

The girl answered: "I wish to ask the great elder a question; please answer as you will. Great elder, are you presently male?"

Śāriputra said: "Although my form is male, my mind is not male."

The girl said: "Great elder! I too am just so. As you yourself say, although I am in a female form, my mind is not female."

Śāriputra said: "You are now constrained by your husband. How can you say such things?"

The girl answered: "Great elder! Can you trust your own words?"

Śāriputra said: "How could I not trust my own words?"

The girl answered: "If you trust your own words, then when you said earlier 'my form is male but my mind is not male,' your mind and form have two different functions. If you truly trust these words, then you should not give rise to evil views regarding me and my husband. It is because you yourself are male that you create my femaleness. Because of my female form, you disturb your own mind. One who sees maleness in oneself and femaleness in another cannot generate true faith in the Dharma."

Śāriputra said: "I would not dare give rise to evil views regarding you."

The girl answered: "It is only because you are before the World-Honored One that you do not dare speak the truth. If you truly do not give rise to evil views, why did you say to me 'You are now constrained by your husband'? Where did those words come from?"

Śāriputra said: "Because I have long been apart from such habits, I spoke those words, but they were not from my true mind."

The girl asked: "Great elder! I now ask you a question; please answer as you will. Since you say you have long been apart from the marks of male and female, great elder, has your form long been apart from them? Or has your mind long been apart from them?" At that time Śāriputra was silent and did not answer.

Then Āmrapālī spoke in verse:

> "If the mind has long been apart,
> Ultimately no views arise.
> Who then creates a woman,
> Or gives rise to impurity regarding form?
> If one speaks of form being long apart,
> Dharmas originally do not exist of themselves.
> They have ultimately never been defiled;
> What then could constitute wrongdoing?
> Alas, great elder of today!
> You study in vain yet cannot understand.
> From your own maleness you create my femaleness;
> Is this not a false conception?
> Repent before the great assembly.
> Do not give rise to doubt regarding the Dharma.
> What I have spoken above
> Is sustained by the Buddha's spiritual power."

When Āmrapālī had spoken these verses, among the bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, devas, and humans, more than a thousand people generated the mind of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi. Among the five thousand in the assembly, some attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, some attained the Dharma-eye, and some attained liberation of mind. Among the immeasurable śrāvaka assembly, those who felt shame regarding the Buddha-Dharma were innumerable.

Then the Buddha said to Śāriputra: "This woman is no ordinary person. She has already encountered immeasurable Buddhas and has always been able to speak this Lion's Roar Sūtra of Definitive Meaning, benefiting immeasurable beings. I myself have together with this woman served immeasurable Buddhas. This woman shall before long attain perfect enlightenment. Those in this assembly who are able to generate true faith in the essential Dharma spoken by this woman have all long heard the Dharma spoken by this woman in the past, and therefore they can now generate right faith. For this reason, you should faithfully receive this Lion's Roar Sūtra of Definitive Meaning and not doubt it."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "You should receive and uphold this sūtra of the Lion's Roar of Definitive Meaning, with its questions and answers by the elder's daughter Āmrapālī, and its chapters and sequence. I entrust it to you; you should faithfully receive it."

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "So be it, World-Honored One! I have now fully received it."

At that time the great assembly, having heard the girl Āmrapālī teach the Dharma, were filled with great joy and boundless delight, and each practiced in accordance with what was taught.
