# 佛說入無分別法門經
## Sūtra of Entering the Gate of Non-Discrimination, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0654

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was together with an assembly of innumerable hundreds of thousands. This great assembly surrounded him with reverence and listened as the Buddha taught the Dharma, expounding the great vehicle through the dharma of non-discrimination.

At that time, the World-Honored One surveyed the assembly of bodhisattvas. Then a bodhisattva in the assembly named Avikalpaprabhā rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and said: "May the World-Honored One expound the gate of entering non-discrimination, so that when the bodhisattvas hear this dharma, they may cultivate and train in accordance with principle."

The Buddha said: "Good men! Listen carefully. I shall now expound the gate of entering non-discrimination."

Then the Bodhisattva Avikalpaprabhā again addressed the Buddha: "Excellent, World-Honored One! We delight in hearing it." The bodhisattvas received the teaching and listened.

The Buddha said: "Good men! If bodhisattvas hear the supreme dharma of non-discrimination and abide in the mind of non-discrimination, they are immediately free from all characteristics of discrimination. What is called 'non-discrimination' should first be understood thus: from the beginning there is the characteristic of self-nature discrimination. The characteristic of discrimination refers to the two dharmas of grasping and rejecting. This grasping and rejecting is the characteristic of self-nature discrimination. From this arises the characteristic of conditioned activity with outflows. Because of the characteristic of outflows, there are the five appropriating aggregates. The five appropriating aggregates are: the aggregate of form with appropriation, the aggregate of feeling with appropriation, the aggregate of perception with appropriation, the aggregate of formations with appropriation, and the aggregate of consciousness with appropriation.

"Good men! How does one become free from the characteristic of self-nature discrimination? By not engaging in differentiated mental attention that is opposed to the present moment; in this way one is free from the characteristic of self-nature discrimination. If, apart from self-nature, one grasps at characteristics, this constitutes active engagement, and one cannot attain unobstructed correspondence. Therefore you should know: generosity is without form and without the characteristic of discrimination; the precepts, patience, vigor, concentration, and wisdom are without form and without the characteristic of discrimination. Why? Because self-nature is without form, function is without form, and reality is without form. If one engages in differentiated mental attention wishing to be free from the characteristic of discrimination, then afterward there would be a formless, non-discriminating characteristic to be freed from; this constitutes active engagement, and one cannot attain unobstructed correspondence. Therefore you should know: emptiness is without form and without the characteristic of discrimination.

"Suchness, the limit of reality, the ultimate truth, and the dharma-realm are without form and without the characteristic of discrimination. Why? Because self-nature is without form, function is without form, and reality is without form. Therefore, regarding the formless, non-discriminating characteristic, one should not observe it as something of a different nature, nor should one direct mental attention toward leaving it. If one directs differentiated mental attention toward leaving it, then regarding the formless, non-discriminating characteristic, there would afterward be something attained. This constitutes active engagement, and one cannot attain unobstructed correspondence with what is present. Why? Because the dharmas attained at the first ground are without form and without the characteristic of discrimination; and so too the dharmas attained up through the tenth ground are without form and without the characteristic of discrimination. The attainment of the acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas is without form and without the characteristic of discrimination. The attainment of a prediction of buddhahood is without form and without the characteristic of discrimination. The attainment of the purification of buddha-lands and the maturation of sentient beings is without form and without the characteristic of discrimination. And so on, up to the attainment of omniscience: all are without form and without the characteristic of discrimination. What is thus stated means that self-nature is without form, function is without form, and reality is without form.

"If a bodhisattva, regarding these various attainable dharmas, gives rise to a discriminating characteristic that differs from what is attained, directing differentiated mental attention, then regarding all non-discriminating characteristics, one entirely directs differentiated mental attention toward them. In this way, one does not correspond with the principle of non-discrimination, and one cannot enter the realm of non-discrimination. If a bodhisattva abides in samādhi, the mind entering the realm of non-discrimination, and regarding what is entered gives rise to the thought that nothing is attained, this is true correspondence, this is the true practice, this is the true cultivation and true activity, this is true mental attention. Abiding in the practice of non-action without awakening to anything, this is called truly entering the realm of non-discrimination with pure practice.

"Good men! Regarding what is called the realm of non-discrimination: for what reason is it called the realm of non-discrimination? It transcends all forms, transcends all discriminations, transcends all discriminating objects, transcends all representations. All afflictions and secondary afflictions are not stored within it. For this reason it is called the realm of non-discrimination. In this realm of non-discrimination there is no form, no seeing, no abiding, no obstruction, no representation, and no manifold characteristics. If a bodhisattva thus understands and thus abides in the realm of non-discrimination, this is to enter the wisdom of non-discrimination, equal to space. Regarding all dharmas, one observes without observing anything, attains the practice of great bliss, increases the great mind, attains great wisdom and great unobstructed eloquence. At all times and in all aspects, one universally accomplishes great benefit for all sentient beings. One attains growth of courageous power and attains great ease in the Buddha's work, which is without awakening to anything.

"Good men! Suppose in a certain place there is a great stone mountain, and beneath that mountain there is a great treasure-store filled with various treasures: silver treasures, gold treasures, and various other treasures, and also a great maṇi gem emitting brilliant light. At that time, a person seeking treasures comes to this mountain, wishing to find precious gems. Another person, who already knows the hidden location of the treasures, sees the seeker and says: 'You who seek treasures, you should know that beneath this great stone mountain there is a great treasure-store filled with various treasures, and there is also a great maṇi gem that emits brilliant light.' When the treasure-seeker hears these words, he immediately exerts himself with firm resolve, excavating and breaking open the mountain to obtain the great maṇi gem. Having broken it open, he finds silver treasures shining and stone without radiance. Seeing this, he does not consider it a great and precious treasure. Then the one who already knows the treasures likewise excavates the mountain to obtain the treasure. Having opened it, he finds gold treasures shining and stone without radiance. This person also does not consider it a great and precious treasure. Then the one who knows the treasures says: 'You who seek treasures, you should not exert yourself with firm resolve and intention to seek the great treasure. If you do not direct your intention toward it, you should be able to see the vast and precious treasure. Because of your intentional striving, the treasure cannot be obtained. If one obtains that great and precious treasure, one immediately becomes vastly wealthy, sovereign and free, benefiting both oneself and others. Whatever one universally undertakes is all brought to perfection.'"

The Buddha said: "Good men! These matters are explained by means of a parable. If a bodhisattva knows this meaning, then one sees this meaning as previously stated. The great stone mountain is a term for the single dharma of affliction, active engagement in two places. The treasure-store beneath the mountain is a term for the realm of non-discrimination. The person seeking the treasure is a term for the bodhisattva. The person who knows the treasure is a term for the Tathāgata. The self-nature of stone is a term for the self-nature of discrimination. Excavating and breaking open to obtain the treasure is a term for non-intentional attention. The silver radiance beneath the stone is a term for the characteristic of self-nature discrimination. The gold radiance beneath the stone is a term for the characteristic of discrimination regarding emptiness and the like. The radiance of various treasures beneath the stone is a term for the characteristic of discrimination regarding what is attained. The longing for the great treasure is a term for the desire to enter the realm of non-discrimination.

"Good men! What is the meaning of this? If a bodhisattva can observe as it truly is, as has been taught, then one enters the realm of non-discrimination. Furthermore, good men! Bodhisattvas should abide in the realm of non-discrimination as in the realm of space. Why? Because when the self-nature of form and the self-nature of discrimination are thus observed, their characteristics do not appear. If one engages in the thought 'I have form,' this is engaging in the characteristic of discrimination. If one engages in the thought 'another has form,' this is engaging in the characteristic of discrimination. If one engages in the thought that form has arising and ceasing, defilement and purity, this is engaging in the characteristic of discrimination. If regarding the self-nature of form, whether its cause, its effect, its karma, or its action, one grasps at existence or non-existence, this constitutes engaging in form; and if one so engages, this is engaging in the characteristic of discrimination. If regarding form one sees something represented, this is engaging in the characteristic of discrimination. Therefore you should know: regarding the dharma of form, in every respect, whether as obstruction, representation, or non-representation, ultimately not the slightest dharma can be found. This dharma of form is entirely without representation and without an observable nature; and apart from this dharma of form, there is also nothing without representation and without an observable nature. The obstruction and representation of that form are entirely without an observable nature. Within representation, neither a single nature nor a different nature can be observed. That very representation is also without a nature. This absence of nature is not itself the absence of nature. If one observes in this way, this is observation of equality. When one so observes, all discrimination is without discrimination. This is the realm of non-discrimination.

"Bodhisattvas should observe in this way and enter in this way. One who enters this realm of non-discrimination, regarding both discrimination and non-discrimination, is entirely without discrimination. To observe in this equality is to truly enter the realm of non-discrimination. This is called the bodhisattva's secure abiding in the realm of non-discrimination. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, as well as generosity, the precepts, patience, vigor, concentration, wisdom, and the other perfections, emptiness, suchness, the limit of reality, the ultimate truth, the dharma-realm, the dharmas realized at the ten grounds, and so on up to omniscience and the supramundane practices: all are likewise. Regarding feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, up to omniscience, if regarding their self-nature, cause, effect, karma, or action, one grasps at existence or non-existence, then regarding omniscience and the rest, this constitutes obstruction. If one so engages, this is engaging in the characteristic of discrimination. Yet omniscience and the rest are without a representable nature. If there were something representable, then engaging in omniscience and the rest would constitute engaging in the characteristic of discrimination. If regarding omniscience and the rest, within the representable there were any characteristic to be engaged in, this would be engaging in the characteristic of discrimination. If one trusts that there is no characteristic to be engaged in, this too constitutes engaging in omniscience and the rest and is the characteristic of discrimination. Therefore you should know: this representable dharma is not of a single nature that can be observed, nor of a different nature that can be observed. This representable dharma is without a nature, and is also not the absence of a nature. If in this way, regarding omniscience and the rest, whether representable or non-representable, one is entirely without discrimination, then this very non-discrimination, which does not grasp at any characteristic, is the realm of non-discrimination. Bodhisattvas should observe in this way and enter in this way. To enter in this way is to truly enter the realm of non-discrimination. This is called the bodhisattva's secure abiding in the realm of non-discrimination."

At that time the World-Honored One further spoke in verse:

> If the mind of non-discrimination is securely established,
> It is born from the true Dharma of all the Buddhas.
> All discrimination is entirely left behind;
> One's practice then attains non-discrimination.
> This dharma is serene, stainless, and supreme;
> Fame, merit, and virtue are universally gathered.
> The dharma of non-discrimination is the highest bliss;
> The bodhisattva who attains it accomplishes bodhi.
