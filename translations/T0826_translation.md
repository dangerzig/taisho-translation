# 弟子死復生經
## Sūtra of the Disciple Who Died and Returned to Life

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0826

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Jùqú Jīngshēng (沮渠京聲)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus, twelve thousand bodhisattvas, and five hundred disciples of supernatural powers.

At that time there was a worthy upāsaka who had originally followed the ninety-six non-Buddhist paths. Weary of the suffering of prayers and sacrifices, he abandoned them and entered the Dharma. He upheld the precepts without transgression, was diligent and single-minded, devoted to chanting the sūtras, delighted in giving, was restrained in patience, and always had a compassionate heart. He suddenly fell gravely ill and thereupon passed away. As he was about to die, he instructed his relatives and parents, saying: "If my illness proves fatal, do not prepare my body for seven days. If you care for me, do not disregard my words." Thereupon he suddenly became as if dead. His parents and relatives did as he had said and kept the body for seven days.

On the eighth day, the relatives said: "The dead man has been lying for eight days now. A corpse has no further awareness; we should quickly prepare the body." The parents said: "Although it has been many days, the body has neither swollen nor begun to smell. Let us keep it a little longer, until the tenth day." Before they had finished speaking, the dead man opened his eyes. The relatives, parents, old and young, leapt with excitement and joy. He was not yet able to move, so they all watched over him. By the tenth day he was able to sit up and speak clearly.

The people asked him where he had been and what he had seen. He said: "Officers and soldiers came and took me away. We went to a great city. Within it was a great prison. The prison was pitch-dark, with iron walls on all four sides, and the prison gates were all of heated iron, glowing red. The people imprisoned within were all sitting in the midst of a great fire, with fire burning and roasting them from above and below, and dark smoke rising. Some people were cutting the prisoners' flesh with knives and devouring it. Within the prison there was a king, who asked me: 'What kind of person are you? What offense did you commit to be brought here? This is the place where those who commit the five heinous acts, who are unfilial to their parents, who are disloyal to their sovereign, are punished. This is where all evildoers are dealt with. How grave must your offenses be to have ended up here?'

"I answered: 'Since my youth, I was misled by evil people and followed non-Buddhist paths. In my ignorance of the world's ways, I killed living beings for sacrifices to heaven and earth, and I drank alcohol. Furthermore, in the marketplace I sought to profit from trade, cheating with measures, scales, and lengths to enrich myself. Later I met a good teacher who taught me to do good and led me into the way of the Buddha. I met śramaṇa monks, who conferred upon me the five precepts and instructed me in the ten wholesome deeds. From that time until today, I have not again committed any evil. I rely upon Your Majesty's grace and compassion that I have not yet been overtaken by punishment.' I then bowed my head to the ground.

"The king immediately rose, joined his palms, and said to me: 'Stop, stop! A person of pure faith should not do this!' He then gave me a seat, and I sat down. The king then summoned the officer and questioned him: 'This man is a disciple of the Unsurpassed, True, and Right One. All of you should seek to be delivered through such a person. Only when his own allotted lifespan naturally comes to its end should he die! His spirit will then follow his deeds and go to receive its due. If he is to be born in the heavens, the devas will come to welcome him. If he is to be born among humans, humans will come to welcome him. How could you bring this honored person into this place of the five heinous crimes?'

"The officer answered the king: 'In the world there are many such people who do not fear the king's law, who do not fear the four seasons, whose five elemental phases cannot restrain them, and whom the ghosts, spirits, heaven, and earth have no record of. Should we not even question one or two of them? These people walk freely through the world, with nothing to constrain them. They have teachers called śramaṇas who shave their heads and wear coarse and humble garments, who consider themselves great by virtue of the Dharma and lead many disciples. They roam in all four directions without any record being kept. Whether moving, burying, marrying off daughters, or taking wives, they do not fear the four seasons. They destroy and alter everything, disregarding the king's regulations. Such people ought to be punished.'

"The king said: 'Stop, stop! Do you not understand this Dharma at all? Those who wear the Dharma-robes have nothing they hold precious or fear. All the Śakras, Brahmās, the kings among the sun and moon, down to earthly emperors, ministers, and common people, all revere and honor them. Those who revere such persons gain immeasurable merit, and they can cause people to attain the path. One must not slight such persons; those who slight them bring suffering upon themselves. Quickly check the register of lifespans: has his allotted time come to an end or not?'

"The officer reported to the king: 'According to the life-register, he should not yet have died. He still has twenty years of allotted life remaining. Because of the offenses he committed in his early youth, and because he only later wished to do good, I took him, so that his fellow devotees might humble themselves a little more.'

"The king said: 'If a person dwells in the world and commits evil deeds up to a hundred years of age, what should be the fate of such a person's offenses?'

"The officer said: 'Such a person can only be dealt with while alive, not after death. If they die, it leads to further entanglements, and they must receive the suffering of punishment for thousands and millions of kalpas without obtaining liberation.'

"Then the king said: 'The Buddha's disciples who uphold the precepts and are diligent without slackness are honored and revered by the devas and spirits. The reason is that the Buddha, with great compassion and great loving-kindness, with a guarding heart and a devoted heart, with these four immeasurable minds, cares with concern for all beings under heaven in the ten directions: all people, all creatures, everything that wriggles and crawls, the Buddha has compassion for them all. His merit and virtue flow throughout all under heaven in the ten directions. Therefore the Buddha's disciples are revered by devas, spirits, earth-gods, ghosts, nāgas, and all. How could they be constrained by kingly regulations, the four seasons, or the five elemental phases? The Buddha's grace is like the four seas: it cannot be measured or limited. Even the sands of the Ganges might be counted, grain by grain, and their number known, but the Buddha's grace cannot be measured!'

"The officer addressed the king: 'Does Your Majesty follow the Buddha's pure precepts?'

"The king said: 'It is because I did not follow the Buddha that I was condemned and brought here as the king of this prison. You see these prisoners: those whose lifespans have truly ended and who are beyond reprieve I still dare not approach. I must join my palms, rise, and go forth to welcome them reverently, and have their record of wholesome deeds examined. The spirits of their merit will come to receive them. Those who should not yet have died have their own protectors, and they will quickly recover. But if there are people who have already entered the right Dharma and then afterward regret it and return to the non-Buddhist paths, killing living beings for sacrifices, practicing the Dharma of the heterodox, then the evil ghosts and spirits see their opportunity, and such people are then without protection. Even if they have a thousand years of allotted lifespan, they will encounter the nine kinds of untimely death and die without illness. The reason is that their guardian spirits do not protect them. Such people will never attain liberation.

"'Bhikṣus who uphold the precepts and all their disciples should diligently practice six things. What are the six? First, dāna-pāramitā: one should give generously without stinginess. Second, śīla-pāramitā: one should guard the precepts carefully and not transgress. Third, kṣānti-pāramitā: one should patiently endure and restrain the mind, with neither mouth nor mind giving way to anger. Fourth, vīrya-pāramitā: one should exert oneself with diligence and not be lazy. Fifth, dhyāna-pāramitā: one should settle the mind in single-pointed concentration and not be heedless. Sixth, prajñā-pāramitā: one should diligently study the sūtras, chant and recite them, and should comprehend upāya-kauśalya. These are the six things, the foundation for a bodhisattva seeking the path.

"'There are six further things: first, the eyes; second, the ears; third, the nose; fourth, the mouth; fifth, the body; sixth, the mind. If a person wishes to seek the path and receive blessings, one should guard these six things. Guard the eyes from attachment to form. Guard the ears from attachment to sound. Guard the nose from attachment to scent. Guard the mouth from attachment to taste. Guard the body from attachment to fine touch. Guard the mind from attachment to desire. These are the six things to guard.

"'One should further extinguish three things. What are the three? First, lust; second, anger; third, delusion. These are the three poisons.

"'One should further extinguish five things. What are the five? First, sensation; second, perception; third, birth-and-death; fourth, consciousness; fifth, desire. These are the five aggregates.

"'There are further six debilities. What are the six debilities? First, the eye is debilitated by form. Second, the ear is debilitated by sound. Third, the nose is debilitated by scent. Fourth, the mouth is debilitated by taste. Fifth, the body is debilitated by fine touch. Sixth, the mind is debilitated by dharmas. These are the six debilities.

"'The five aggregates, the six sense-faculties, and the three poisons together make twenty things constantly present in the body. A practitioner of the path should always cut off these twenty things. If one cannot stop these twenty things, one will fall into transgression: if the six sense-faculties are not stopped, one falls into the eighteen hells; if the five aggregates are not stopped, one revolves through the five destinies; if the three poisons are not stopped, one enters the three evil paths.

"'Among good men and good women, those who restrain and uphold the twenty things within the body through the precepts, so that the body becomes like a mirror with its grime removed, pure and without defilement, bright within and without: among the thousands, billions, and trillions of people under heaven, is there even one such person who is a disciple of the Buddha?'

"The officer said: 'Truly, there is none.'

"The king said: 'From this we can see that the Buddha's merit and virtue are majestic and great, deep as an abyss and vast as a hall, like a mighty ocean that cannot be withstood!'

"The officer addressed the king: 'It is indeed as Your Majesty has said. This lowly officer's own offenses have caused him not to distinguish true from false. I request permission to send this man back.'

"The king said: 'Good.' The officer then made apology to the man and sent him back on his own way. The man then felt as if falling from a great height; with a sudden flash he revived and came back to life."

The parents then carried him by carriage to the Jeta Grove and reported to the Buddha. The Buddha then summoned the man and questioned him. He answered with all that he had seen and heard. The Buddha then smiled, and five-colored light came forth from the crown of his head, circled his body three times, and entered back through the navel.

Ānanda then arranged his robes, knelt with both knees on the ground, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "The Buddha does not smile without reason. When you smile, there must be a meaning. We pray the Buddha will broadly explain."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! Listen carefully and receive this for the assembly." Ānanda received the instruction and listened.

The Buddha said: "This world of Jambudvīpa is an evil age of the five turbidities. Sons are unfilial to their fathers, ministers are disloyal to their sovereigns, husband and wife deceive one another, they deceive those above and defraud those below. The people are perverse and obstinate, with little sense of righteousness. They are frivolous and arrogant, without restraint, using strength to oppress the weak. The rich follow the rich, while the poor and destitute remain in poverty. They are greedy and miserly, without compassion, wishing only to surpass others. The four heavenly kings stand guard, and malevolent ghosts and spirits watch for their opportunity. Those who transgress die, and their spirits revolve according to their deeds, going to receive their lot. They may become hungry ghosts or animals, or suffer the torments of hell: flogging, beating, whipping, hacking, boiling in cauldrons and roasting in fire. If they have some slight remaining merit and are born in the heavens, they dwell in the sixth heaven of Māra. With meager blessings and short lives, they do not receive the Dharma-teaching. Even if born as humans, they become lowly servants and slaves, or become oxen, horses, and other livestock, or mules, donkeys, camels, elephants, tigers, lions, birds, beasts, insects, and moths, in suffering too terrible to describe, for a hundred kalpas, a thousand kalpas, ten thousand kalpas without liberation. Sometimes they are born and immediately fall ill, or sometimes die at once. If they become human, their six senses are incomplete: they are crippled, deaf, blind, or mute, and the sufferings are without limit.

"Now in this world there is one person who knows there is a Buddha in the world, who has heard the sūtras and the Dharma, who has seen the bhikṣu Saṃgha, who has a good mind and a kind heart, who is respectful and compassionate, who has abandoned the ninety-six non-Buddhist paths and entered the right Dharma. This person examines and restrains himself, receives and upholds the five precepts, practices the ten wholesome deeds to extinguish the ten evils. At that time, such a person is one with a practitioner who attained the path in the time of Vipaśyin Buddha, with merit and virtue equal to that person. Whatever offenses accumulated over a hundred kalpas, a thousand kalpas, or ten thousand kalpas are all completely extinguished. After that person's death, he will never again see the three evil paths. Even if there are some offenses, the officers will investigate the gravity and bring the person before the king of hell, but the king, seeing him, will feel his hair stand on end with reverence. Emperors, kings, and all the people, without exception, will honor and revere him. Though he has not yet attained the path, his merit and virtue are magnificent, and devas, humans, nāgas, and ghosts will all extol and praise him."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "After my parinirvāṇa, people in the world will mostly not revere the Dharma. They will be self-important and arrogant, looking down on others and treating the right Dharma with contempt. They will revile the bhikṣus, refuse them alms, curse them, and throw stones at them without any restraint or fear. Such people all come from the realm of Māra. Born as humans, they are still as evil as before. Those who believe in and delight in the Buddha-Dharma are the foremost disciples of Buddhas from ages past, who can recognize truth from falsehood, follow and uphold the right Dharma, receive and keep the sūtras and precepts, and further extinguish these twenty things. They are all bodhisattva mahāsattvas, not ordinary people!

"Those who are born must die; there is no one who does not die. The practitioner of precepts and goodness does not begrudge body or life but is mindful only of great compassion and great loving-kindness, seeking to deliver and aid all, and serving as a guide for everyone. The bodhisattva does not fear the change of birth-and-death. He enters birth-and-death to deliver beings from birth-and-death. He enters the hells to teach the sūtras and precepts, stopping evil and promoting good. He enters the realm of hungry ghosts to teach generosity. He enters the animal realm to teach that lust destroys one's human birth. He is born in the heavens to teach the devas. Among humans, he transforms evil into good. When a person acts like a hell-being, hell-thoughts arise. When a person acts like an animal, animal-thoughts arise. When a person acts like a deva, deva-thoughts arise. When a person acts like a hungry ghost, hungry-ghost-thoughts arise. When a person acts like a human, human-thoughts arise. All the myriad things are ultimately without existence; they are merely named according to one's actions, and from that, thoughts arise.

"This worthy man has witnessed with his own eyes clearly and distinctly. Having already been born into the world as a human, possessing merit and upholding the right Dharma, why not exert yourself and realize Buddhahood and its honor on your own? Why engage in the karma of toil and suffering? You yourself will have to go and receive its consequences. What good is regret afterward?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "When the Dharma is about to flourish, good people appear in the world. When the Dharma is about to decline, evil people become numerous. Tell one another the good news, and each diligently and vigorously practice the sūtras and precepts, for the sake of concern over universal impermanence. The power of impermanence is great; the Buddha does not abide permanently in the world. Exert yourselves diligently. Having already ferried yourselves across, further rescue and ferry across all people and non-humans.

"All you bhikṣus! You should make your efforts while I am still in the world. Do not say 'the Buddha is always here.' If you do not exert yourselves now, what good is regret later? I now give this man who was dead the name 'See the Truth.'"

See the Truth, having heard the Buddha's teaching, together with his parents and relatives, all attained the arhat's path of the truths. See the Truth immediately attained non-retrogression, firmly established and never again wavering. The bhikṣus, bodhisattvas, great disciples, devas, nāgas, ghosts, and spirits, having heard the sūtra, all rejoiced and came forward to bow in homage to the Buddha before departing.
