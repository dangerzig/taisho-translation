# 六字神呪經
## Six-Syllable Divine Mantra Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1180

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Bodhiruci (菩提流志).

---

*Oṃ vakeda namaḥ*

I shall now explain the powers of this six-syllable mantra of Mañjuśrī. If anyone wishes to uphold this mantra and attain accomplishment, they should eat milk porridge, or vegetables, or fruit and milk; these are the foods they should eat. Three times each day they should bathe: after the fifth watch is the first time, after noon is the second time, and after dusk until the first watch of the night is the third time. At each of these three times, they should bathe once and each time put on a fresh set of clean clothes; therefore three sets of clean clothes are needed. One should recite the mantra until reaching six hundred thousand repetitions. This constitutes the preliminary service and offering to Mañjuśrī.

Furthermore, if one wishes to receive and uphold for accomplishment, one must first paint an image of Mañjuśrī. The method for painting the image: take fine white felt, making sure it is free of any hair, and do not cut or sever the felt fibers. For the pigments, do not use glue; one should mix them with fragrant juice. The image of Mañjuśrī should depict him seated in full lotus on a lotus seat, with the right hand in the teaching mudrā and the left hand resting palm-up in the lap. His body should be in the form of a youth, golden in color, with heavenly garments in white covering from the navel downward, while the rest of the body is bare. He wears a celestial crown and is adorned with necklaces, armlets, bracelets, and other ornaments.

On the left side, paint an image of Avalokiteśvara, with a body the color of white silver, adorned with necklaces and garments as usual, seated in full lotus on a lotus, the right hand holding a white whisk. On the right side, paint an image of Samantabhadra Bodhisattva, with a golden body, adorned with necklaces as usual, also seated on a lotus, the right hand holding a white whisk.

Above Mañjuśrī in the sky on both sides, paint one Śuddhāvāsa deva on each side, their hands holding garlands, appearing in the clouds with only the upper half of their bodies visible, their hands extending downward with garlands. Below the image of Mañjuśrī on the right side, paint the mantra practitioner kneeling on the right knee, holding a censer in the hands.

Below the lotus seats of Mañjuśrī and the others, paint a pool of water throughout. On both sides of the bodhisattva images, paint mountain peaks.

The painter, from the day they begin painting, should daily take the eight precepts, bathe, and put on clean clothes before painting, continuing until the painting is complete.

If there is a śarīra stūpa, place the Mañjuśrī image on the western face of the stūpa, with the image facing west. If there is no large stūpa, use a small stūpa and place the Mañjuśrī image before it, facing west. Set out various flowers, various foods, fruits, and other offerings, making offerings three times daily. Within the ritual space, burn ghee lamps. The ritual space must be in a quiet place, with only one person attending.

When actually reciting the mantra in the ritual space, any needed flowers, incense, and other items should be requested by snapping the fingers; one must not speak aloud.

Take aloeswood cut to the length of two finger-widths, and prepare tulūba fragrant oil and smokeless khadira wood charcoal. If khadira is unavailable, purple catalpa wood may be substituted. Place the aloeswood in the oil, and before the image, burn it on the khadira charcoal fire throughout the entire night. When the dawn light appears, one will see Mañjuśrī, and all wishes will be fulfilled, with the sole exception of sexual desires; all other requests will not be denied.

Another method: before the image, take sandalwood cut to the length of two finger-widths and burn it day and night in offering. At that time Mañjuśrī will appear in person, teach the dharma, heal all bodily ailments, and one will attain the freedom of the bodhisattva stages.

Another method: before the image, smear the ground with gorocana, scatter various fine flowers, and the practitioner should sit to one side of the prepared ground and recite the mantra one hundred and eight times. After one month, one will attain brilliance and master all sūtras and śāstras.

Another method: recite it daily, following one's heart without forgetting, and even fixed karmic retribution will be extinguished.

Another method: if one can recite one hundred and eight times daily, at the time of death one will see Mañjuśrī, and whatever rebirth one wishes will be obtained.

Mañjuśrī, for the benefit of all sentient beings, has spoken only a small portion of the powers among all the powers of this mantra.

Colophon: The Song edition of the canon additionally includes a "Six-Syllable Divine Mantra King Sūtra" attributed to Bodhiruci; the Tan edition does not. According to the Kaiyuan Catalog, the Cai fascicle contains a Six-Syllable Divine Mantra Sūtra translated by Bodhiruci, described as a variant translation of the Mañjuśrī mantra methods in fascicle six of the Dhāraṇī Collection Sūtra, the Sūtra of Mantra Reciting in Five Sections, and the Six-Syllable Dhāraṇī. Upon examination, this sūtra is that text, and there is no separate "Six-Syllable Divine Mantra King Sūtra with Ten Mantras" by Bodhiruci. The Song edition, seeing the words "six-syllable divine mantra" in its title, erroneously conflated the two and redundantly included it in this Cai fascicle. This is one error. Moreover, it wrongly attributed it to Bodhiruci. That attribution is hereby removed.
