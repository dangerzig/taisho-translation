# 頻毘娑羅王詣佛供養經
## Sūtra on King Bimbisāra's Visit to the Buddha to Make Offerings

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0133

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmaruci (法炬)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. People revered and looked up to him, and all came to make offerings: bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās; great kings, princes, and ministers down to the common people. All came to make offerings, providing robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine for illness. At that time, the fame and virtue of the World-Honored One were heard far and wide: "Such is the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Awakened One, the One Accomplished in Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone One, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Charioteer of the Dharma, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One." He taught the Dharma to living beings: good at the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end, with meaning deep and far-reaching, complete in the holy life.

At that time, King Bimbisāra of Magadha told his ministers: "Prepare the feathered carriage! Why? I wish to go to the country of Kāśi-Kośala to greet the World-Honored One, pay my respects, and attend upon him. It is extremely rare for a World-Honored One to appear in the world, and extremely rare to encounter one. The Tathāgata appears in the world only from time to time, just as the udumbara flower blooms only at certain times. The World-Honored One is likewise extremely rare to encounter."

They replied: "As you command, great king!"

At that time, the ministers, hearing the command of King Bimbisāra of Magadha, prepared the feathered carriage, went to where King Bimbisāra was, and reported: "The carriage is prepared. Now is the right time."

King Bimbisāra of Magadha then mounted the feathered carriage. With ministers and people before and behind as escort, he departed from the city of Rājagṛha, and with the king's authority gradually traveled to Kāśi-Kośala, reaching the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada, in the city of Śrāvastī. When the carriage reached the city gate, he dismounted and walked to the Jetavana to where the Tathāgata was. Just as a kṣatriya king sets aside the five royal insignia, he bowed his head at the World-Honored One's feet, touched the World-Honored One's feet with his hands, and announced his own name: "I am King Bimbisāra, king of kings, of the country of Magadha. This is Bimbisāra."

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed King Bimbisāra: "You are a great king of a noble kṣatriya family. I am a Śākya who has gone forth to study the path. In this physical body all virtues are complete. Yet the great king has condescended to come to my place, to inquire about my well-being, and with joined palms to attend upon me."

The king said to the World-Honored One: "By the grace of the World-Honored One, I have also seen kṣatriyas who have wisdom and great learning, brāhmaṇas who have wisdom, householders who have wisdom, and śramaṇas who have wisdom, each debating doctrines among themselves. I took these doctrines and went to ask the śramaṇa Gautama, thinking: 'If the śramaṇa Gautama answers with this doctrine, we shall answer with this doctrine. If the śramaṇa Gautama does not answer this doctrine, we shall still debate it.' And so I went to the World-Honored One."

At that time, the World-Honored One taught him the Dharma. Having heard the Dharma from the World-Honored One, the king no longer asked about debating doctrines, much less raised any objections. He took refuge in the World-Honored One, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. This is the virtue of the World-Honored One's body, teaching the wonderful and subtle Dharma, knowing the right time for action. At that time, the king attained the third joy: having joy in the World-Honored One, and further having reverence. The great assembly of śrāvakas were all of pure conduct: accomplished in morality, accomplished in concentration, accomplished in wisdom, accomplished in liberation, and accomplished in the knowledge and vision of liberation. That is to say, the four pairs and eight types of persons. This is the World-Honored One's assembly of śrāvakas, worthy of respect and worthy of esteem, the foremost and most honored, the unsurpassed field of merit for the people of the world. This is the third joy in the Perfectly Awakened One.

At that time, King Bimbisāra of Magadha, having heard the wonderful Dharma from the Buddha, said to the World-Honored One: "I beseech the Tathāgata to accept my invitation for three months, to travel to the city of Rājagṛha. I shall provide offerings of robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine for illness, for the Buddha and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus."

At that time, the World-Honored One silently accepted King Bimbisāra's invitation. When King Bimbisāra of Magadha saw the World-Honored One silently accept his invitation, he was overjoyed beyond measure. He rose from his seat, bowed his head at the World-Honored One's feet, circumambulated three times, and departed. Outside the Jetavana gate he mounted the feathered carriage and returned to the city of Rājagṛha, to his own palace.

At that time, King Bimbisāra of Magadha ordered his ministers and people: "Listen well! I wish to invite the World-Honored One for three months, together with the Saṃgha of bhikṣus, and offer them robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine for illness. You should each encourage one another in this."

They replied: "As you command, great king!"

At that time, King Bimbisāra of Magadha, sitting alone in a private place, thought: "I have the resources and ability to provide. I wish for the rest of my life to offer the World-Honored One robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine for illness, as well as to the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. I should encourage the ministers and people."

At that time, King Bimbisāra of Magadha, on that very day, encouraged his ministers: "Earlier I was sitting alone in a private place and this thought arose: 'I have the resources and ability to provide. I wish for the rest of my life to offer the World-Honored One robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine for illness, as well as to the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. I should instruct the ministers and people.' You all, according to your respective positions, should invite the Buddha and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. Through the long night you will receive merit without end."

They replied: "As you command, great king!" The ministers and people received the king's instruction.

At that time, the World-Honored One, who had been traveling in the city of Śrāvastī, went out to journey among the people, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, gradually traveling to Rājagṛha. The World-Honored One then stayed in the Bamboo Grove, the Kalandaka Garden, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time, King Bimbisāra of Magadha heard that the Buddha had arrived in the city of Rājagṛha and was staying in the Bamboo Grove, the Kalandaka Garden, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. King Bimbisāra then ordered his ministers to quickly prepare the feathered carriage and go to the Kalandaka Garden to greet the World-Honored One. The ministers, hearing King Bimbisāra of Magadha's order, went and prepared the feathered carriage, then reported to Bimbisāra: "The carriage is prepared. Now is the right time."

King Bimbisāra of Magadha then mounted the feathered carriage. With ministers and people before and behind as escort, with the king's authority he went out from the city of Rājagṛha to the Bamboo Grove, the Kalandaka Garden. He dismounted the carriage and walked into the Kalandaka Garden to where the World-Honored One was. Just as a kṣatriya king has the five insignia: the sword, the golden sandals, the canopy, the celestial crown, and the jeweled chowrie, he set all of these aside, bowed his head at the World-Honored One's feet, and sat to one side.

King Bimbisāra of Magadha then said to the World-Honored One: "Since I returned to my country, sitting alone in a private place, this thought arose: 'I rule this country's domain and have the resources and ability to provide. I wish for the rest of my life to make offerings to the Tathāgata and the assembly of bhikṣus of robes, food and drink, bedding, and medicine for illness. I should also encourage the ministers and people, so that they may attain deliverance and through the long night be freed from the three evil destinies, abiding forever in peace and safety.'"

At that time, the World-Honored One addressed King Bimbisāra of Magadha: "Excellent! Excellent! Great king! For the sake of living beings you have generated this great vow, wishing to bring peace and safety to living beings. The meaning and principle are deep and far-reaching, and both devas and humans will attain peace." The World-Honored One then taught King Bimbisāra of Magadha the wonderful and subtle Dharma, encouraging and gladdening him so that all would rejoice.

King Bimbisāra of Magadha, having heard the wonderful Dharma from the Buddha and generated a mind of joy, rose from his seat, bowed at the World-Honored One's feet, circumambulated the Buddha three times, and departed. Going out from the Kalandaka Garden gate, he mounted his carriage and returned to the city of Rājagṛha, entering his own palace and sitting upon his throne. That very day he prepared sweet and delicious food and drink of many kinds and flavors. That very day, for the Buddha and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus, he laid out the many seats. He personally held an incense burner and ascended a high tower, facing east with palms joined, concentrating his mind on the World-Honored One. He also reflected: "The time has now arrived. May the World-Honored One know the time and turn his gaze this way."

At that time, the World-Honored One, knowing the time had arrived, put on his robes and took up his bowl. With the Saṃgha of bhikṣus escorting him before and behind, he entered the city of Rājagṛha and went to the palace of King Bimbisāra of Magadha. When they arrived, the bhikṣus of the Saṃgha each sat in proper order. King Bimbisāra of Magadha, seeing that the Buddha and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus were settled in their seats, personally served the sweet and delicious food and drink of many kinds and flavors, feeding the Buddha and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. When he saw the World-Honored One had finished eating and put away his bowl, he laid out a small seat before the Tathāgata and sat down.

At that time, the World-Honored One, for King Bimbisāra of Magadha, taught the wonderful and subtle Dharma, gradually discussing various topics. The topics he discussed were: the discourse on giving, the discourse on morality, the discourse on rebirth in heaven, the impurity and defilements of desire as great suffering, and the importance of going forth. When the World-Honored One knew the king's mind was joyful beyond measure, entirely gentle and harmonious, just as the Tathāgata teaches the Dharma in the proper way, he fully taught the king the wonderful and subtle Dharma of suffering, its arising, its cessation, and the path.

At that time, two hundred and fifty palace women, right there upon their seats, attained the purity of the Dharma-eye. Having seen the Dharma and obtained the Dharma, they chose among the various dharmas, upheld the various dharmas, were free from doubt and hesitation, their hopes severed, having attained fearlessness regarding the Dharma they should learn. They took refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha, and received the five precepts.

At that time, the World-Honored One, seeing King Bimbisāra hear the wonderful Dharma and joyfully receive it, spoke this verse:

> "Among ritual fires, the offering fire is foremost; among compositions, the hymns are foremost.
> Among people, the king is foremost; among all streams, the ocean is foremost.
> Among the stars, the moon is foremost; among all lights, the sun is foremost.
> Above and below and in the four directions, among all living things that are born,
> In heaven and in the world, the Buddha is supreme, beyond all others.
> Those who wish to cultivate fields of merit should seek the Three Buddhas."

When the World-Honored One had taught this verse for the king, he rose from his seat and departed.
