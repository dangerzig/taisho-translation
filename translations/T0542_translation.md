# 佛說耶祇經
## Sūtra of Yajñika, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0542

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Juqu Jingsheng (沮渠京聲)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Kanāj. In that country there was a brahmin of great wealth, named Yajñika, who formerly worshipped the ninety-six kinds of heterodox paths, seeking blessings and protection. He heard that those who served the Buddha gained wealth and honor, long life and peace, liberation from birth and death and the receiving of blessings, and did not fall into the three evil destinies or endure further hardship. Yajñika reflected: "I should abandon the heterodox paths and make offerings to the Buddha."

He then went to the Buddha's dwelling, bowed with his head to the ground, made obeisance to the Buddha, and knelt. He addressed the Buddha: "I was formerly foolish and ignorant, knowing nothing. I have heard that the Buddha's path is vast and great, with universal compassion. The Buddha is the most honored among devas and humans, in heaven and on earth. All who follow him attain peace. I now wish to abandon the heterodox paths I formerly served and take refuge in the Buddha. May the Buddha have compassion on me. I wish to receive your teaching and precepts."

The Buddha said: "What you now say is excellent! Reflect carefully. If you cease doing evil and do good, why should you worry about not attaining peace?"

Yajñika said to the Buddha: "Because what I formerly served was not the true path, I now take refuge in the Buddha. May you have compassion on us, remove our turbid and foul practices, and give us the Buddha's words of pure resolve. If this is truly so, it would be most excellent." Yajñika then came forward and received the five precepts: first, not to kill living beings; second, not to steal; third, not to commit sexual misconduct; fourth, not to engage in double-tongued speech, harsh speech, false speech, or frivolous speech; fifth, not to drink wine. Having taken the threefold refuge, he circumambulated the Buddha three times, observed the purificatory fast for seven days, and departed.

After this, while traveling to another country, he saw people killing living creatures and hunting for sport, and stealing the property of others. Yajñika felt the urge to follow them. He saw a beautiful woman and his mind desired her. He saw people quarreling over right and wrong and wished to join in their disputes. He saw people drinking wine until drunk and disorderly and wished to follow them. His mind was unsettled, and he began to regret his vows. He thought: "I cannot practice this Buddha-Dharma. In the end I should return the five precepts to the Buddha."

He then went to the Buddha's dwelling, bowed his head, and said to the Buddha: "Previously I received the five precepts from the Buddha, but they restrict me too severely. I cannot follow my own inclinations. Now, upon reflection, I wish to quit and can no longer serve the Buddha. The Buddha-Dharma is weighty and honored, beyond what I am able to practice. May I return the five precepts? Would the Buddha allow this?"

The Buddha was silent and did not respond. Before the words had left his mouth, ghostly spirits appeared of their own accord. One held an iron mallet and struck Yajñika on the head, splitting it open. Another spirit tore off his garments. Another spirit used an iron hook to reach into his mouth and pull out his tongue. A lustful female spirit used a blade to deeply cut his private parts. Another spirit poured molten copper into his mouth. Spirits surrounded him on all sides, front and back, competing to tear his flesh apart. In this state, Yajñika's eyes bulged and stank, his face turned the color of earth, and spontaneous fire burned his body. He could not find life and could not find death. The spirits seized him relentlessly.

The Buddha, seeing this, felt compassion and pity for him. He asked Yajñika: "What will you do now?"

Yajñika's jaw was clenched and he could no longer speak. He could only raise his hand and strike himself, begging the Buddha for mercy. The Buddha then released his divine power, and the spirits were all terrified and fled. Yajñika then recovered and could breathe again. He rose and bowed his head before the Buddha, saying: "Within my heart there are five thieves who drag me into evil and sin. They caused me to speak those evil words. Now I have received this retribution. It is my own doing, and I have violated the Buddha's words. May the Buddha have compassion on me."

The Buddha said: "It was your own mind and mouth that did this. Whom should you blame?"

Yajñika said to the Buddha: "From this day forward, I shall reform myself. I shall uphold the five precepts. I shall observe the three annual fasts and the six monthly fasts. I shall burn incense and light lamps. I shall make offerings to the Three Honored Ones. My body, my speech, and my mind shall never transgress again."

The Buddha said: "Just so. Excellent! What your own eyes have seen and your own body has experienced: one reaps what one sows. Doing good, one receives good. Thinking unwholesome thoughts, one receives unwholesome results. The Buddha is the teacher of the Dharma, who teaches people to abandon evil and do good. In the end, one attains lasting liberation. Devas and humans, the foolish and ignorant, are all led to wisdom and freed from further hardship. From now on, reform and cultivate goodness. Do not let your mind and intentions lead you astray, for they are the root of people's ruin."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, Yajñika's mind was opened and he attained the fruit of stream-entry. He departed with joy.

When Yajñika returned home, he immediately instructed everyone in his household, great and small, to go to the Buddha's dwelling and receive the five precepts, with the three annual fasts and the six monthly fasts. Yajñika then renounced his household, shaved his head and beard, donned the kāṣāya robe, and became a śramaṇa under the Buddha. He eventually attained the path of arhatship.
