# Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra Spoken by Mañjuśrī

## *Wénshūshīlì Suǒshuō Bōrě Bōluómì Jīng* (文殊師利所說般若波羅蜜經)

Taishō 233

Translated by Saṃghavarman (僧伽婆羅, *Sēngqiépóluó*) during the Liang dynasty.

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the kingdom of Śrāvastī (*Shèwèi*, 舍衛), in the Jeta Grove (*Qíhuán*, 祇洹) of Anāthapiṇḍada's garden, together with a great assembly of ten thousand bhikṣus, and also one hundred thousand bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, all dwelling on the stage of non-retrogression. They had long made offerings to countless buddhas, deeply planted good roots in the presence of all buddhas, brought sentient beings to maturity, purified buddha-lands, attained dhāraṇī, gained eloquence in joyful discourse, accomplished wisdom, and were replete with merit. Through their sovereign spiritual powers they roamed the buddha-worlds, emitting immeasurable radiance and teaching the inexhaustible sublime dharma. They guided bodhisattvas to enter the gate of the single characteristic, attained fearlessness, skillfully vanquished the host of Māra, and converted those with heterodox views. If there were sentient beings who delighted in the Śrāvaka Vehicle, they taught the Śrāvaka Vehicle; for those who delighted in the Pratyekabuddha Vehicle, they taught the Pratyekabuddha Vehicle; for those who delighted in the worldly vehicle, they taught the worldly vehicle. Through generosity, moral discipline, patience, vigor, meditation, and wisdom they gathered sentient beings: those not yet crossed over, they crossed over; those not yet liberated, they liberated; those not yet at peace, they brought to peace; those who had not yet entered nirvāṇa (*nìhuán*, 泥洹), they caused to attain nirvāṇa. They perfected the practices of a bodhisattva and skillfully entered the dharma treasury of all buddhas. Such various merits they fully possessed. Their names were: Mañjuśrī (*Wénshūshīlì*, 文殊師利) Prince of the Dharma Bodhisattva, Maitreya (*Mílè*, 彌勒) Bodhisattva, Samantaprabha (*Pǔguāngmíng*, 普光明) Bodhisattva, Unrelenting Vigor (*Bùshě Yǒngměng Jīngjìn*, 不捨勇猛精進) Bodhisattva, Medicine King (*Yàowáng*, 藥王) Bodhisattva, Jewel Palm (*Bǎozhǎng*, 寶掌) Bodhisattva, Jewel Seal (*Bǎoyìn*, 寶印) Bodhisattva, Moonlight (*Yuèguāng*, 月光) Bodhisattva, Pure Sun (*Rìjìng*, 日淨) Bodhisattva, Great Strength (*Dàlì*, 大力) Bodhisattva, Immeasurable Strength (*Wúliànglì*, 無量力) Bodhisattva, Attained Diligent Vigor (*Dé Qín Jīngjìn*, 得勤精進) Bodhisattva, Banner of Strength (*Lìchuángxiàng*, 力幢相) Bodhisattva, Dharma Characteristic (*Fǎxiàng*, 法相) Bodhisattva, and Sovereign King (*Zìzàiwáng*, 自在王) Bodhisattva. Such bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, one hundred thousand in all, were gathered together, along with the remaining devas, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, and all the great assembly. They all came and gathered.

At that time, during the middle of the night, the World-Honored One emitted a great radiance: blue, yellow, red, white, and the varied colors of crystal (*pōlí*, 頗梨), universally illuminating the immeasurable worlds of the ten directions. All sentient beings touched by this light arose from sleep, and seeing this radiance, they all attained joy in the dharma. All of them wondered: "Where does this light come from? It pervades the entire world and brings peace and comfort to all sentient beings." After having this thought, from each ray of light there emerged yet greater radiance, more brilliant and extraordinary than the previous, surpassing it in splendor. In this way it continued in succession up to tenfold. All the bodhisattvas, bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, and non-humans all leaped with joy at this unprecedented event. Each thought: "It must be the Tathāgata who has emitted this light. We should hasten to the Buddha's presence to bow before, draw near, and pay reverence to the Tathāgata." At that time, Mañjuśrī and the assembly of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who encountered this light were filled with joy throughout body and mind, and each came from their dwelling to the gate of Jeta's Grove. Then Śāriputra (*Shèlìfú*, 舍利弗), Mahāmaudgalyāyana (*Dà Mùjiānlián*, 大目揵連), Pūrṇa Maitrāyaṇīputra (*Fùlóunà Míduōluóní Zǐ*, 富樓那彌多羅尼子), Mahākāśyapa (*Móhē Jiāshè*, 摩訶迦葉), Mahākātyāyana (*Móhē Jiāzhānyán*, 摩訶迦旃延), and Mahākauṣṭhila (*Móhē Jùxīluó*, 摩訶俱絺羅) all came from their dwellings to the gate of Jeta's Grove. Śakra (*Dìshì*, 帝釋), the Four Heavenly Kings, and devas up to the Akaniṣṭha (*Ājiānízhà*, 阿迦尼吒) heaven also beheld the radiance and marveled at the unprecedented event. Together with their retinues, bearing wonderful heavenly flowers, heavenly incense, heavenly music, and heavenly jeweled garments, they all came to the gate of Jeta's Grove. The remaining bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, upāsikās, and the eightfold assembly of devas and nāgas, encountering the light and rejoicing, all came to the gate.

At that time the World-Honored One, with his all-knowing wisdom (*yīqiè zhǒngzhì*, 一切種智), knew that the great assembly was all gathered outside the gate. He arose from his dwelling, went out to the gate, personally spread a dharma seat, and sat cross-legged. He addressed Śāriputra: "Have you come to the gate this early morning?" Śāriputra said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, Mañjuśrī and the other bodhisattva-mahāsattvas all arrived first."

Then the World-Honored One addressed Mañjuśrī: "Did you arrive first at the gate this early morning?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "So it is, World-Honored One. During the middle of the night, I saw a great tenfold radiance, unprecedented, and my heart was filled with boundless joy. Therefore I came to bow before, draw near to, and pay reverence to the Tathāgata, and also wished to hear the sweet-dew of the sublime dharma."

Then the World-Honored One addressed Mañjuśrī: "Do you now truly see the Tathāgata?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the dharma-body (*fǎshēn*, 法身) of the Tathāgata is fundamentally invisible. I come to see the Buddha for the sake of sentient beings. The dharma-body of the Buddha is inconceivable, without characteristic and without form, neither coming nor going, neither existent nor non-existent, neither seen nor not-seen. Like suchness (*rúrú*, 如如), like the true limit of reality (*shíjì*, 實際), neither going nor coming, neither non-existent nor not-non-existent, neither located nor not-located, neither one nor two, neither pure nor defiled, neither arising nor ceasing. My seeing of the Tathāgata is also like this."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Is this how you see the Tathāgata?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I truly have no seeing, nor any characteristic of seeing."

Then Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "I do not understand what you have said. How is this seeing the Tathāgata?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "Venerable Śāriputra, I do not see the Tathāgata in this way."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "What you say becomes ever more incomprehensible." Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "What is incomprehensible is precisely prajñāpāramitā (*bōrě bōluómì*, 般若波羅蜜). Prajñāpāramitā is neither comprehensible nor incomprehensible."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you give rise to a mind of kindness and compassion toward sentient beings? Do you practice the six pāramitās for sentient beings? Do you enter nirvāṇa for sentient beings?"

Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "As you have said, I give rise to kindness and compassion for sentient beings, practice the six pāramitās, and enter nirvāṇa. Yet sentient beings truly cannot be found; they are without characteristic and without form, neither increasing nor decreasing. Śāriputra, do you constantly think thus: 'In each and every world, buddhas numerous as the sands of the Ganges dwell for Ganges-sands of kalpas, teaching one dharma after another, converting and liberating Ganges-sands of sentient beings, and each of those sentient beings attains cessation'? Do you have such thoughts?"

Śāriputra said: "Mañjuśrī, I do constantly think thus."

Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "Just as space is numberless, sentient beings are also numberless. Space cannot be exhausted, and sentient beings also cannot be exhausted. Why? Because all sentient beings are equal to space. How then do the buddhas convert sentient beings?"

Śāriputra said: "If all sentient beings are equal to space, why do you teach the dharma to sentient beings to enable them to attain bodhi?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "Bodhi truly cannot be attained. What dharma should I teach to make sentient beings attain it? Why? Śāriputra, bodhi and sentient beings are neither one nor two, neither different nor conditioned, without name and without characteristic. In reality there is nothing at all."

At that time the World-Honored One emitted from the great man's mark, the uṣṇīṣa (*ròujì*, 肉髻), a light of extraordinary rarity beyond description. It entered the crown of the head of Mañjuśrī, Prince of the Dharma, bodhisattva-mahāsattva, and from the crown it emerged again to illuminate the entire assembly. Having illuminated the assembly, it then pervaded all the worlds of the ten directions. At that time, the great assembly, touched by this light, experienced physical and mental bliss unprecedented in their experience. They all rose from their seats and gazed up at the World-Honored One and Mañjuśrī, all thinking: "Today the Tathāgata has emitted this marvelous and wonderful light, which entered the crown of Mañjuśrī, Prince of the Dharma, emerged from the crown to illuminate the assembly, and then pervaded the ten directions. This is not without cause; surely the sublime dharma will be taught. We should only be diligent in vigor and delight in practicing as taught." After thinking thus, they each said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the Tathāgata has today emitted this light. This is not without cause; surely the sublime dharma will be taught. We thirst with longing and delight in practicing as taught." Having said this, they remained silent.

Then Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the Tathāgata has emitted this light and empowered me with spiritual force. This light is extraordinary: neither color nor characteristic, neither going nor coming, neither moving nor still, neither seen nor heard, neither perceived nor known. All sentient beings have nothing to observe or see in it, neither joy nor fear, without any discrimination. Bearing the Buddha's sacred intent, I shall explain this radiance and cause all sentient beings to enter the wisdom beyond conceptual thought (*wúxiǎng huì*, 無想慧)."

Then the Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! You explain well. I rejoice in your words."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, this radiance is prajñāpāramitā. Prajñāpāramitā is the Tathāgata. The Tathāgata is all sentient beings. World-Honored One, this is how I cultivate prajñāpāramitā."

Then the Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Good son, you now speak of the profound prajñāpāramitā. I now ask you: if someone were to ask, 'How many are the realms of sentient beings?' how would you answer?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if someone asked such a question, I would answer: 'The number of the realms of sentient beings is the same as the number of the realm of the Tathāgata.'"

"Mañjuśrī, if someone further asked, 'How vast or narrow is the realm of sentient beings?' how would you answer?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if someone asked such a question, I would answer: 'It is as vast or narrow as the buddha-realm.'"

"Mañjuśrī, if someone further asked, 'Where is the realm of sentient beings bound?' how would you answer?" "World-Honored One, I would answer: 'As the Tathāgata is bound, so too are sentient beings.'"

"Mañjuśrī, if someone further asked, 'Where does the realm of sentient beings abide?' how would you answer?" "World-Honored One, I would answer: 'It abides in the realm of nirvāṇa.'"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "When you cultivate prajñāpāramitā in this way, does prajñāpāramitā have a dwelling place?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, prajñāpāramitā has no dwelling place."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "If prajñāpāramitā has no dwelling place, how do you cultivate it? How do you study it?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if prajñāpāramitā had a dwelling place, then there would be no cultivation or study."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "When you cultivate prajñāpāramitā, are good roots increased or decreased?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, there are no good roots that can be increased or decreased. If there were increase or decrease, this would not be cultivating prajñāpāramitā. World-Honored One, not increasing dharmas and not decreasing dharmas: this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Not cutting off the dharmas of ordinary people and not grasping the dharmas of the Tathāgata: this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? World-Honored One, prajñāpāramitā is not cultivated for the sake of attaining dharmas, nor for the sake of not attaining dharmas; not for the sake of cultivating dharmas, nor for the sake of not cultivating dharmas. World-Honored One, without attainment and without relinquishment: this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Not for the faults of birth and death, and not for the merits of nirvāṇa. World-Honored One, cultivating prajñāpāramitā in this way means not grasping and not accepting, not abandoning and not releasing, not increasing and not decreasing, not arising and not ceasing. World-Honored One, if a good man or good woman thinks, 'This dharma is superior, this dharma is middling, this dharma is inferior,' this is not cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because there is no superior, middling, or inferior dharma. World-Honored One, this is how I cultivate prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Are not all buddha-dharmas supreme?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, buddha-dharmas, bodhisattva-dharmas, śrāvaka-dharmas, pratyekabuddha-dharmas, even the dharmas of ordinary people, none can be found. Why? Because they are ultimately empty. In ultimate emptiness, there are no buddha-dharmas and no dharmas of ordinary people. In the dharmas of ordinary people, there is no ultimate emptiness. Why? Because emptiness and non-emptiness cannot be found."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Are not the buddha-dharmas unsurpassed?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, there is not a single dharma, even as small as a mote of dust, that can be called 'unsurpassed.' Why? The perfection of giving (*dān bōluómì*, 檀波羅蜜) is empty of the perfection of giving, and so on up to prajñāpāramitā being empty of prajñāpāramitā. The ten powers are empty of the ten powers. The four fearlessnesses, the eighteen exclusive qualities, and even sarvajña (*sàpóruò*, 薩婆若, 'omniscience') is empty of sarvajña. In emptiness there is no 'unsurpassed'; in the unsurpassed there is no emptiness. Because emptiness and non-emptiness ultimately cannot be found. World-Honored One, the inconceivable dharma is prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you not contemplate the buddha-dharmas?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if I contemplated the buddha-dharmas, then I would see the buddha-dharmas as unsurpassed. Why? Because the unsurpassed is without existence. World-Honored One, the five aggregates, the twelve sense-bases, and the eighteen elements ultimately cannot be found, and all buddha-dharmas also cannot be found. Within the unattainable, there is neither attainable nor unattainable. World-Honored One, within prajñāpāramitā, from ordinary beings up to buddhas, there is neither dharma nor non-dharma. What dharma should I contemplate?"

The Buddha said: "Good son, if there is no contemplation, you should not say, 'This is the dharma of ordinary people, this is the dharma of pratyekabuddhas,' and so on up to, 'This is the buddha-dharma.' Why? Because they cannot be found." "World-Honored One, I truly do not speak of the dharmas of ordinary people up to the buddha-dharmas. Why? Because I do not cultivate prajñāpāramitā in that way."

The Buddha said: "Good son, you also should not think, 'This is the desire realm, this is the form realm, this is the formless realm.' Why? Because they cannot be found." "World-Honored One, the desire realm is empty of the nature of the desire realm, and so on up to the formless realm being empty of the nature of the formless realm. Within emptiness there is no speaking, and I also do not speak. World-Honored One, in cultivating prajñāpāramitā, one does not see superior and does not see not-superior. Why? World-Honored One, in cultivating prajñāpāramitā, one does not grasp the buddha-dharmas and does not abandon the dharmas of ordinary people. Why? Because in ultimate emptiness there is no grasping or abandoning."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! You are able to speak thus of the profound prajñāpāramitā. This is the seal (*yìn*, 印) of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. Mañjuśrī, if good men and good women had merely planted good roots in the presence of a thousand or ten thousand buddhas, they could hear this dharma; but only those who have deeply planted good roots in the presence of immeasurable and boundless buddhas can hear this most profound prajñāpāramitā without giving rise to fear."

Mañjuśrī again said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, bearing the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, I will further speak of the most profound prajñāpāramitā." The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! I freely permit you to speak."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if one does not apprehend the arising of dharmas, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because all dharmas are without arising. If one does not apprehend the abiding of dharmas, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because all dharmas are as they truly are. If one does not apprehend cessation, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because all dharmas are quiescent.

"World-Honored One, if one does not apprehend form, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā, and so on up to not apprehending consciousness being the cultivation of prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because all dharmas are like an illusion, like a mirage.

"World-Honored One, if one does not apprehend the eye, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā, and so on up to not apprehending the mind. If one does not apprehend form through dharmas, does not apprehend the eye-element, the form-element, or the eye-consciousness-element, and so on up to not apprehending the dharma-element or the mind-consciousness-element, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. If one does not apprehend the desire realm, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā, and so on through the formless realm.

"World-Honored One, if one does not apprehend the perfection of giving, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā, and so on up to not apprehending prajñāpāramitā being the cultivation of prajñāpāramitā. If one does not apprehend the ten powers of the Buddha, the four fearlessnesses, through the eighteen exclusive qualities, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because of internal emptiness, through the emptiness of both existent and non-existent dharmas.

"World-Honored One, if one apprehends arising, abiding, and cessation, this is not cultivating prajñāpāramitā. If one apprehends the five aggregates, the twelve sense-bases, and the eighteen elements, this is not cultivating prajñāpāramitā. If one apprehends the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm, this is not cultivating prajñāpāramitā. If one apprehends the perfection of giving through the perfection of wisdom, if one apprehends the ten powers of the Buddha through the eighteen exclusive qualities, this is not cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because of apprehension.

"World-Honored One, if good men and good women hear this most profound prajñāpāramitā without alarm, without doubt, without fear, and without retreat, one should know that such people have long ago, under former buddhas, deeply planted good roots."

Mañjuśrī again said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if one does not see defiled dharmas or pure dharmas, does not see the fruit of birth and death, does not see the fruit of nirvāṇa, does not see a buddha, does not see a bodhisattva, does not see a pratyekabuddha, does not see a śrāvaka, and does not see an ordinary being, this is cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because all dharmas are without defilement and without purity, and so on through being without ordinary beings. World-Honored One, if one sees defilement and purity, through seeing ordinary beings, this is not cultivating prajñāpāramitā. World-Honored One, if one sees distinctions in defiled dharmas, distinctions in pure dharmas, through seeing distinctions in the buddha-dharmas and distinctions in the dharmas of ordinary people, this is not cultivating prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because prajñāpāramitā is without distinctions."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! This is the true cultivation of prajñāpāramitā. Mañjuśrī, how do you make offerings to the Buddha?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, when the mental activities of an illusory person cease, then I make offerings to the Buddha."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you not abide in the buddha-dharma?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "The Buddha has no dharma in which one can abide. How could I abide?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "If no dharma can be found, who possesses the buddha-dharma?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, there is no one who possesses the buddha-dharma."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Have you already arrived at non-attachment?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "If there is non-attachment, there is no arriving. Why, World-Honored One, do you ask whether I have arrived at non-attachment?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you abide in bodhi?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the Buddha himself does not abide in bodhi. How much less should I abide in bodhi?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "What do you rely upon to speak thus?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "I rely on nothing to speak thus."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "If you rely on nothing, for whom do you speak?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "Just so, World-Honored One. I have nothing to say. Why? Because all dharmas are without names."

Then the Elder Śāriputra said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva hears this profound dharma without alarm, doubt, or fear, will such a person certainly draw near to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?"

Then Maitreya Bodhisattva said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if bodhisattva-mahāsattvas hear this profound dharma without alarm, doubt, or fear, will they draw near to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?"

Then there was a celestial maiden (*tiānnǚ*, 天女) named Animitta (*Wúyuán*, 無緣) who said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if good men and good women hear this profound dharma without alarm, doubt, or fear, will they attain the śrāvaka-dharma, the pratyekabuddha-dharma, the bodhisattva-dharma, and the buddha-dharma?"

Then the Buddha said to Śāriputra: "So it is, so it is, Śāriputra! If bodhisattva-mahāsattvas hear this profound dharma without alarm, doubt, or fear, they will certainly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Such good men and good women shall become great benefactors, foremost benefactors, supreme benefactors. They shall fully possess moral discipline, patience, vigor, meditation, and wisdom. They shall be replete with all merits and accomplish the marks of excellence. They themselves will be without fear and will cause others to be without fear, having perfected prajñāpāramitā and, through the unattainable, the signless, and the unconditioned, having accomplished the foremost inconceivable dharma."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "What do you see, and what do you delight in, that you seek anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, it is because I have no seeing and no delight that I seek bodhi."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "If there is no seeing and no delight, there should also be no seeking." Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "Just so, World-Honored One. I truly do not seek. Why? If there were seeking, this would be the characteristic of an ordinary person."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you truly and really not seek bodhi?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "I truly and really do not seek bodhi. Why? If one seeks bodhi, this is the characteristic of an ordinary person."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you definitely seek, or definitely not seek?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "To say one definitely seeks, definitely does not seek, definitely both seeks and does not seek, or definitely neither seeks nor does not seek: all of these are the characteristics of an ordinary person. Why? Because bodhi has no dwelling place."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! You are able to speak thus of prajñāpāramitā. You have already, in the presence of immeasurable buddhas, deeply planted good roots and long cultivated the holy life (*fànxíng*, 梵行). All bodhisattva-mahāsattvas should practice as you have described." Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "I have not planted good roots, nor have I cultivated the holy life. Why? If I planted good roots, then all sentient beings would also plant good roots. If I cultivated the holy life, then all sentient beings would also cultivate the holy life. Why? Because all sentient beings are themselves the characteristic of the holy life."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "What do you see and what do you realize that you speak thus?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "I have no seeing and no realization, and I also have nothing to say. World-Honored One, I do not see ordinary people, do not see learners, do not see those beyond learning, and do not see those who are neither learners nor beyond learning. Because I do not see, I do not realize."

Then Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you see the Buddha?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "I do not even see a śrāvaka. How much less would I see a buddha? Why? It is because one does not see any dharmas that one is called a bodhisattva."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you definitely not see any dharmas?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "Venerable Elder, please stop. There is no need to speak further."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "As for what is called 'buddha,' whose words are these?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "'Buddha' and 'non-buddha' cannot be found. There is no one who speaks and no one who teaches. Śāriputra, bodhi cannot be expressed in words. How much less can there be a buddha to be spoken of or described? Furthermore, Venerable Śāriputra, you say, 'Whose words are "buddha"?' These words neither combine nor scatter, neither arise nor cease, neither go nor come. There is not a single dharma that can correspond to them; they are without letters and without phrases. Venerable Śāriputra, if you wish to see the Buddha, you should study in this way."

Then Śāriputra said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what Mañjuśrī has spoken, newly-aspiring bodhisattvas cannot understand."

Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "So it is, so it is, Venerable Śāriputra! Bodhi is not something that can be understood. How could newly-aspiring ones understand it?"

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "Have the buddhas, the tathāgatas, not awakened to the dharma-realm (*fǎjiè*, 法界)?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "Even buddhas cannot be found. How can there be buddhas who awaken to the dharma-realm? Śāriputra, the dharma-realm itself cannot be found. How could the dharma-realm be awakened to by buddhas? Śāriputra, the dharma-realm is bodhi, and bodhi is the dharma-realm. Why? Because all dharmas are without boundaries. Venerable Śāriputra, the dharma-realm and the buddha's domain (*fó jìngjiè*, 佛境界) are without distinction. What is without distinction is uncreated (*wúzuò*, 無作). What is uncreated is unconditioned (*wúwéi*, 無為). What is unconditioned is inexpressible. What is inexpressible is without anything at all."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "Are the entire dharma-realm and the buddha's domain without anything at all?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "Neither having nor not-having. Why? Having and not-having are of one characteristic, which is no characteristic; they are neither one nor two."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "Will one who studies in this way attain bodhi?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "One who studies in this way has nothing to study. One neither is born in a good destiny nor falls into an evil path, neither attains bodhi nor enters nirvāṇa. Why? Śāriputra, because prajñāpāramitā is ultimately empty. In ultimate emptiness there is no one, no two, no three, no four; there is no going or coming; it is inconceivable. Venerable Śāriputra, if someone says, 'I have attained bodhi,' this is the talk of overweening pride (*zēngshàngmàn*, 增上慢). Why? Because to claim attainment where there is no attainment. A person of such overweening pride is not worthy of receiving offerings of faith; people of faith should not make offerings to such a one."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "What do you rely upon to speak thus?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "I rely on nothing to speak thus. Why? Because prajñāpāramitā is equal to all dharmas. All dharmas are without reliance, because of equality."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you not use wisdom to sever and eliminate afflictions?" Mañjuśrī replied to Śāriputra: "Are you an arhat whose outflows are exhausted?" Śāriputra said: "No." Mañjuśrī said: "I too do not use wisdom to sever and eliminate afflictions."

Śāriputra said: "What do you rely upon to speak thus, without fear?" Mañjuśrī said: "I myself cannot even be found. What self could there be to give rise to fear?"

Śāriputra said: "Excellent, Mañjuśrī! You speak well of this most profound prajñāpāramitā."

Then the Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Good son, is there a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who abides in the bodhi-mind and seeks unsurpassed bodhi?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, there is no bodhisattva who abides in the bodhi-mind and seeks unsurpassed bodhi. Why? The bodhi-mind cannot be found, and unsurpassed bodhi also cannot be found. The five heinous acts (*wú wújiān zuì*, 五無間罪) are the nature of bodhi. There is no bodhisattva who gives rise to the mind of the heinous acts and seeks the fruit of the heinous acts. How could there be a bodhisattva who abides in the bodhi-mind and seeks unsurpassed bodhi? Bodhi is all dharmas. Why? Because form and non-form cannot be found, through consciousness and non-consciousness also not being found. The eye cannot be found, through the mind not being found. Form cannot be found, through dharmas not being found. The eye-element through the dharma-element also cannot be found. Birth cannot be found through aging and death also not being found. The perfection of giving cannot be found through prajñāpāramitā also not being found. The ten powers of the Buddha cannot be found through the eighteen exclusive qualities also not being found. The bodhi-mind and unsurpassed bodhi both cannot be found. Within the unattainable there is neither attainable nor unattainable. Therefore, World-Honored One, there is no bodhisattva who abides in the bodhi-mind and seeks unsurpassed bodhi."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you hold the view that the Tathāgata is your teacher?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "I hold no view that the Buddha is my teacher. Why? World-Honored One, I myself cannot even be found. How much less could I hold the view that the Buddha is my teacher?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you have doubt regarding me?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I do not even have certainty. How much less would I have doubt? Why? Because certainty must precede doubt."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you not hold it as certain that the Tathāgata was born?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "If the Tathāgata were born, the dharma-realm would also be born. Why? Because the dharma-realm and the Tathāgata are of one characteristic without a second; a second characteristic cannot be found."

"Mañjuśrī, do you believe that all buddhas, the tathāgatas, enter nirvāṇa?" Mañjuśrī said: "All buddhas are the characteristic of nirvāṇa. The characteristic of nirvāṇa is neither entering nor not-entering."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you say that the buddhas undergo transmigration?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, even non-transmigration cannot be found. How much less can transmigration be found?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "The Tathāgata is without mental activity (*wúxīn*, 無心). Only in the presence of the Tathāgata can such words be spoken, or in the presence of arhats whose outflows are exhausted, or in the presence of non-retrogressing bodhisattvas. If others hear these words, they will not give rise to faith and will be alarmed and doubtful. Why? Because this most profound prajñāpāramitā is difficult to believe and difficult to understand."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what kind of person can believe this profound dharma?" The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "All ordinary people can believe this dharma. Why? The Tathāgata is without mental activity, and all ordinary people are also without mental activity."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, why do you teach the dharma in this way? Newly-aspiring bodhisattvas and arhats all have doubts. We wish to hear the explanation."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "In suchness (*rú*, 如), in the true characteristic (*shíxiàng*, 實相), in the dharma-nature (*fǎxìng*, 法性), in the dharma-abiding (*fǎzhù*, 法住), in the dharma-position (*fǎwèi*, 法位), and in the true limit of reality (*shíjì*, 實際), is there any distinction between buddhas and ordinary beings?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "No, World-Honored One." The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "If there is no distinction, why give rise to doubt?"

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, within non-distinction, are there buddhas and ordinary beings?" The Buddha said: "There are. Why? Because the Buddha and ordinary beings are without duality and without distinction, being of one characteristic, which is no characteristic."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you believe that the Tathāgata is supreme among all sentient beings?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I believe the Tathāgata is supreme among all sentient beings. World-Honored One, if I believe the Tathāgata is supreme among all sentient beings, then the Tathāgata would become not-supreme."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you believe the Tathāgata has accomplished all inconceivable dharmas?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I believe the Tathāgata has accomplished all inconceivable dharmas. World-Honored One, if I believe the Tathāgata has accomplished all inconceivable dharmas, the Tathāgata would then become conceivable."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you believe all śrāvakas are taught and converted by the Tathāgata?" "World-Honored One, I believe all śrāvakas are taught and converted by the Tathāgata. World-Honored One, if I believe all śrāvakas are taught and converted by the Tathāgata, then the dharma-realm would become something that can be taught and converted."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Do you believe the Tathāgata is the unsurpassed field of merit?" "World-Honored One, I believe the Tathāgata is the unsurpassed field of merit. World-Honored One, if I believe the Tathāgata is the unsurpassed field of merit, the Tathāgata would then not be a field of merit."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "What do you rely upon to answer me thus?" Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I rely on nothing to answer thus. World-Honored One, within non-reliance, there is neither supreme nor not-supreme, neither conceivable nor inconceivable, neither teaching nor not-teaching, neither field of merit nor non-field of merit."

At that time, through the Buddha's spiritual power, the earth trembled in six ways. Sixteen thousand bhikṣus, through a mind of non-grasping, attained liberation. Seven hundred bhikṣuṇīs, three thousand upāsakas, and forty thousand upāsikās were freed from dust and defilement and attained the purity of the dharma eye. Sixty thousand koṭis of nayutas of devas were freed from dust and defilement and attained the purity of the dharma eye.

At that time, the Elder Ānanda (*Ānán*, 阿難) rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms reverently, and said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what cause and condition has made this earth tremble greatly?" Then the Buddha said to Ānanda: "This teaching of prajñāpāramitā was spoken here. All buddhas of the past also taught this dharma at this place. For this reason the earth trembles."

Then the Elder Śāriputra said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what Mañjuśrī has spoken is inconceivable."

Then the World-Honored One said to Mañjuśrī: "As Śāriputra has said, what Mañjuśrī has spoken is inconceivable."

Then Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if it is inconceivable, then it is inexpressible. If it is expressible, then it is conceivable. The inconceivable is without anything at all. All sounds are also inconceivable. The inconceivable is without sound."

The Buddha said: "Have you entered the inconceivable samādhi?" Mañjuśrī said: "No, World-Honored One. I am myself the inconceivable. I do not see any mind that could engage in conceptual thought. How can you say that I enter the inconceivable samādhi? When I first aspired to enter this samādhi, upon reflection now, there is truly no mental characteristic that enters samādhi. It is like a person learning archery who, after long practice, becomes skillful. Later, without conscious thought, through long habit, every arrow hits the mark. I am the same. When I first studied the inconceivable samādhi, I focused the mind on a single object. After long practice and accomplishment, there is no longer any mental conception; one is constantly merged with samādhi."

Śāriputra said to Mañjuśrī: "Is there a more excellent quiescent samādhi?" Mañjuśrī said: "If the inconceivable samādhi existed, you could ask, 'Is there a more quiescent samādhi?' As I understand it, the inconceivable samādhi itself cannot be found. How can you ask me about the quiescent samādhi?"

Śāriputra said: "Cannot the inconceivable samādhi be found?" Mañjuśrī said: "The conceivable samādhi is the characteristic of what can be found; the inconceivable samādhi is the characteristic of what cannot be found. All sentient beings truly accomplish the inconceivable samādhi. Why? Because all mental characteristics are fundamentally non-mental. This is called the inconceivable samādhi. Therefore the characteristics of all sentient beings and the characteristic of the inconceivable samādhi are equal and without distinction."

The Buddha praised Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! Over a long time, in the presence of all buddhas, you have deeply planted good roots and cultivated the holy life in purity, and so you are able to expound this most profound samādhi. You now abide peacefully in this prajñāpāramitā." Mañjuśrī said: "If I abided in prajñāpāramitā and could make such statements, this would be conceptual thought, and I would be abiding in the notion of self. If one abides in conceptual thought and the notion of self, prajñāpāramitā would have a location. If prajñāpāramitā abided in nothingness, this too would be the notion of self and would constitute a location. Departing from these two locations, one abides in non-abiding, as all buddhas abide: peacefully established in quiescence, which is a realm beyond conceptual thought. Such inconceivability is called the dwelling place of prajñāpāramitā. The dwelling place of prajñāpāramitā is where all dharmas are without characteristics and all dharmas are without activity. Prajñāpāramitā is the inconceivable. The inconceivable is the dharma-realm. The dharma-realm is without characteristics. Being without characteristics is the inconceivable. The inconceivable is prajñāpāramitā. Prajñāpāramitā and the dharma-realm are without duality and without distinction. Being without duality and without distinction is the dharma-realm. The dharma-realm is without characteristics. Being without characteristics is the realm of prajñāpāramitā. The realm of prajñāpāramitā is the inconceivable realm. The inconceivable realm is the realm without arising and without ceasing. The realm without arising and without ceasing is the inconceivable realm."

Mañjuśrī said: "The realm of the Tathāgata and my realm are the non-dual characteristic. To cultivate prajñāpāramitā in this way is not to seek bodhi. Why? Because the characteristic of bodhi being separate is precisely prajñāpāramitā. World-Honored One, if one knows the characteristic of self without attachment, such knowing without attachment is what the Buddha knows. The inconceivable, without knowing and without attachment: this is what the Buddha knows. Why? The essential nature of knowing is fundamentally without any characteristic. How could it transform the dharma-realm? If one knows that the essential nature is without substance and without attachment, this is called 'nothing.' If there is nothing, there is no location, no reliance, no abiding. Without reliance and without abiding means without arising and without ceasing. Without arising and without ceasing: this is the merit of conditioned existence. If one knows in this way, there is no mental conception. If there is no mental conception, how could one know the merit of conditioned and unconditioned? Non-knowing is the inconceivable. The inconceivable is what the Buddha knows. It is also without grasping and without not-grasping. It does not see the characteristics of the three times, of going and coming. It does not grasp arising and ceasing or any activity. It is neither annihilated nor eternal. Such knowing is called right knowledge (*zhèngzhì*, 正智), inconceivable knowledge. Like space, it is without this and without that, beyond comparison, without good or bad, without equal or unequal, without characteristic and without appearance."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "If one knows in this way, it is called non-retrogressing knowledge." Mañjuśrī said: "Non-active knowledge is called non-retrogressing knowledge. It is like a gold ingot: only by first hammering and striking it can one know whether it is good or bad; without refining, no one can know. The characteristic of non-retrogressing knowledge is likewise: it requires encountering the realm of experience, yet is without thought and without attachment, without arising and without activity, replete and unmoving, without arising and without ceasing. Only then does it become manifest."

Then the Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "When the tathāgatas themselves speak of their own knowledge, who could believe them?" Mañjuśrī said: "Such knowledge is neither the dharma of nirvāṇa nor the dharma of birth and death. It is quiescent practice. It does not sever greed, anger, and delusion, yet is not without severing. Why? Because it is without exhaustion and without annihilation. It is not separate from birth and death, yet is not inseparable. It is not separate from the cultivation of the path, yet is not without cultivation. One who understands in this way is said to have right faith."

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! As you have spoken, you deeply understand this meaning."

Then the Elder Mahākāśyapa said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, who in future ages will be able to believe this profound dharma? Who will delight in hearing this dharma?" The Buddha said to Kāśyapa: "The fourfold assembly of the present day, the bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, in future ages will be able to believe this dharma. Hearing this profound prajñāpāramitā, they will know this dharma and seek this dharma. Kāśyapa, it is like a householder or householder's son who has lost a great jewel worth a hundred million gold pieces and is deeply distressed. If later he finds it again, he is overcome with joy and all his distress vanishes. Just so, Kāśyapa, bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās in future ages who hear this most profound prajñāpāramitā sūtra and resonate with prajñāpāramitā will, upon hearing it, give rise to joy; their hearts will be at peace and free from distress. They will say: 'Today we have seen the Tathāgata and made offerings to the Tathāgata. Why? Because we have heard this most profound and subtle six pāramitās.'

"Kāśyapa, it is like the devas of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven who, seeing the pārijāta (*bōlìzhìduōluó*, 波利質多羅) tree first produce buds, think thus: 'Before long these buds will certainly burst into bloom.' Just so, Kāśyapa, bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās who hear this prajñāpāramitā sūtra and their hearts fill with joy, it is the same. In a future age they will certainly attain this dharma. Kāśyapa, this profound prajñāpāramitā, after the Tathāgata's passing, will endure without being extinguished, circulating everywhere. Kāśyapa, through the Buddha's power, in future ages good men and good women will attain this profound prajñāpāramitā.

"Kāśyapa, it is like a maṇi-jewel master (*móní zhūshī*, 摩尼珠師) who, upon seeing a maṇi jewel, is filled with joy, and without further deliberation immediately knows whether it is genuine or counterfeit. Why? Because of long experience. Just so, Kāśyapa, if someone hears a dharma in accord with this prajñāpāramitā and, upon hearing it, is filled with joy and gives rise to a mind of faith and delight, one should know that this person in a past life has already heard this prajñāpāramitā and, from distant kalpas past, has already made offerings to all buddhas."

Kāśyapa said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, these good men and good women who now hear this dharma will, in future ages, have even deeper faith and understanding." The Buddha said to Mahākāśyapa: "So it is, so it is, as you have said."

Then Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, this dharma is without practice and without characteristics. How then does the World-Honored One speak of practice and characteristics for those who teach this dharma?"

The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "When I was formerly practicing the bodhisattva path, cultivating all good roots, wishing to dwell on the stage of avaivartika (*āwéiyuèzhì*, 阿惟越致, 'non-retrogression'), I studied prajñāpāramitā. Wishing to accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, I studied prajñāpāramitā. If good men and good women wish to understand the characteristics of all dharmas, to know that the mental realms of all sentient beings are all equal, they should study prajñāpāramitā. Mañjuśrī, if one wishes to study all buddha-dharmas and be complete and unobstructed, one should study prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to know the characteristics, dignified comportment, and immeasurable dharma-forms of all buddhas when they attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, one should study prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to know all the dharma-forms, comportment, and dignified manners of all buddhas who do not attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, one should study prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because in the empty dharma, one does not see bodhi or any such thing among the buddhas. If good men and good women wish to know such characteristics without doubt, they should study prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because prajñāpāramitā does not see any dharma as arising or ceasing, as defiled or pure. Therefore, good men and good women should study prajñāpāramitā in this way. If one wishes to know that all dharmas are without past, future, and present characteristics, one should study prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because the nature of the dharma-realm is without past, future, and present. If one wishes to know that all dharmas equally enter the dharma-realm, the mind being without obstruction, one should study prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to attain the three turnings and twelve aspects of the dharma wheel (*sān zhuǎn shí'èr xíng fǎlún*, 三轉十二行法輪) and to personally know and realize it without grasping or attachment, one should study prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to attain a compassionate mind that covers all sentient beings without limit, yet without conceiving the notion of sentient beings, one should study prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to be free of contention with all sentient beings, yet also not to grasp the characteristic of non-contention, one should study prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to know what is so and what is not so, the ten powers, the fearlessnesses, and to abide in the Buddha's wisdom with unobstructed eloquence, one should study prajñāpāramitā."

Then Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I observe the true dharma (*zhèngfǎ*, 正法): unconditioned and without characteristics, without attainment and without benefit, without arising and without ceasing, without going and without coming, without knower, without seer, and without doer. I do not see prajñāpāramitā, nor do I see the realm of prajñāpāramitā. It is neither realized nor not-realized, without conceptual elaboration, and without any discrimination. All dharmas are inexhaustible and beyond exhaustion, without the dharmas of ordinary people, without the dharma of śrāvakas, without the dharma of pratyekabuddhas, and without the buddha-dharma. It is neither attained nor not-attained, does not abandon birth and death, and does not realize nirvāṇa. It is neither conceivable nor inconceivable, neither active nor inactive. The characteristic of dharmas being thus, I do not know how one should study prajñāpāramitā."

Then the Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "If one can know the characteristics of all dharmas in this way, this is called studying prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to study the Bodhi Sovereignty Samādhi (*pútí zìzài sānmèi*, 菩提自在三昧), and having attained this samādhi, to illuminate all the most profound buddha-dharmas and know all buddhas' names and fully comprehend all buddha-worlds without obstruction, one should study as Mañjuśrī has spoken within prajñāpāramitā."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, why is it called prajñāpāramitā?" The Buddha said: "Prajñāpāramitā is without boundary and without limit, without name and without characteristics, beyond conceptual reckoning. It is without refuge and without haven, without transgression and without merit, without darkness and without light. Like the dharma-realm, it is without division and without enumeration. This is called prajñāpāramitā. It is also called the domain of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. It is a non-domain of non-practice and non-non-practice, entering entirely into the One Vehicle. It is called a non-domain. Why? Because it is without thought and without activity. It is the mother of all buddhas, that from which all buddhas arise. Why? Because it is without arising.

"Therefore, Mañjuśrī, if good men and good women wish to practice the bodhisattva path and fully accomplish all the pāramitās, they should cultivate this prajñāpāramitā. If they wish to sit upon the site of awakening and accomplish unsurpassed bodhi, they should cultivate this prajñāpāramitā. If they wish to cover all sentient beings with great kindness and great compassion, they should cultivate this prajñāpāramitā. If they wish to give rise to all samādhi and skillful means, they should cultivate this prajñāpāramitā. If they wish to attain all samāpattis (*sānmóbátí*, 三摩跋提), they should cultivate this prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because all samāpattis are without purpose. All dharmas are without escape and without a place of escape. If someone wishes to follow these words, one should cultivate prajñāpāramitā. All dharmas are truly unattainable. If one wishes to delight in knowing this, one should cultivate prajñāpāramitā. All sentient beings, for the sake of bodhi, cultivate the path of bodhi, yet in reality there are no sentient beings and no bodhi. If someone wishes to believe and delight in this dharma, one should cultivate prajñāpāramitā. Why? Because all dharmas are truly equal to bodhi in their suchness. The conduct of sentient beings is non-conduct. Such non-conduct is bodhi. Such bodhi is the dharma-realm. If one wishes not to be attached to this dharma, one should cultivate prajñāpāramitā.

"Mañjuśrī, if a bhikṣu, bhikṣuṇī, upāsaka, or upāsikā receives and upholds even a single four-line verse of prajñāpāramitā and explains it to others, I say this person has attained the dharma of non-falling. How much more so for one who truly cultivates it in practice! One should know that such a good man or good woman abides in the buddha's domain.

"Mañjuśrī, if good men and good women hear this most profound prajñāpāramitā without giving rise to fear, one should know that this person has received the seal of the buddha-dharma (*fóyìn*, 佛印). This dharma-seal is created by the Buddha and treasured by the Buddha. Why? Because with this dharma-seal, one seals the unattached dharma. If a good man or good woman has been sealed by this seal, one should know that this person follows the bodhisattva vehicle and will definitely not retrogress, not falling to the stage of a śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha."

At that time, Śakra Devānām Indra (*Shìtíhuányīn*, 釋提桓因) and all the devas, from the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, rained fine powdered sandalwood and fine gold dust, and also scattered utpala flowers, padma flowers, kumuda flowers, puṇḍarīka flowers, and mandārava flowers, as an offering to prajñāpāramitā. After making their offerings, they said: "We have now made offerings to the unsurpassed, unattached, foremost dharma. May we in a future age again hear this profound prajñāpāramitā. May those who have already been sealed by this profound prajñāpāramitā seal, in the future, again receive and hear it, and ultimately accomplish sarvajña (*sàpóruò zhì*, 薩婆若智, 'omniscient knowledge')."

Then Śakra said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, if good men and good women hear this prajñāpāramitā even once, I shall, for the sake of increasing the buddha-dharma, protect those people. Within a hundred yojanas around them, I will not permit non-human beings to gain any advantage over them. Such good men and good women will ultimately attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. I shall go to them daily and make offerings."

Then the Buddha said to Śakra: "So it is, so it is, Kauśika (*Jiāoshījiā*, 憍尸迦)! One should know that such good men and good women are replete with the buddha-dharma and will certainly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

Then Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "May the World-Honored One, through his awesome spiritual power, uphold this prajñāpāramitā so that it long endures in the world, for the benefit of all sentient beings."

When Mañjuśrī spoke these words, through the Buddha's spiritual power, the great earth trembled in six ways. Then the World-Honored One smiled and emitted a great radiance that pervaded the three-thousand great-thousand world-system. Through his awesome spiritual power, he upheld this prajñāpāramitā to endure long in the world.

Then Mañjuśrī again said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, is this emitted radiance the sign of upholding prajñāpāramitā?" The Buddha said to Mañjuśrī: "So it is, so it is, Mañjuśrī! I emit this light as the sign of upholding prajñāpāramitā. Mañjuśrī, you should now know that I have already upheld this prajñāpāramitā to endure long in the world. If there is a person who does not disparage this dharma and does not speak of its faults, one should know that this person has already been sealed by this profound prajñāpāramitā seal. Therefore, Mañjuśrī, from long ago I have been peacefully established in this seal. If a person has been sealed by this seal, one should know that this person will not be taken advantage of by the Māra King."

The Buddha said to Śakra: "You should receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra, and widely disseminate it, so that good men and good women of future ages will attain this dharma-seal." He further said to Ānanda: "You too should receive, uphold, and recite it, and widely explain it to others."

Then Śakra and the Elder Ānanda said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what should this sūtra be called? How should we uphold it?" The Buddha said: "This sūtra is called 'Spoken by Mañjuśrī,' and is also called 'Prajñāpāramitā.' Uphold it in this way. If a good man or good woman, for Ganges-sands of kalpas, were to bestow priceless jewels upon Ganges-sands of sentient beings, and those sentient beings, having received them, all gave rise to the aspiration for the path, and then the donor, according to what was fitting, instructed, taught, benefited, and delighted them, enabling them to attain the fruit of stream-entry (*xūtuóhuán*, 須陀洹) through the fruit of arhatship: is the merit gained by this person great?" Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Extremely great, World-Honored One."

The Buddha said: "Good son, if a person gives rise to a single thought of faith in this prajñāpāramitā sūtra, without disparaging it, compared to the previous merit, it exceeds it a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a hundred-thousand-million-billion-fold, so much so that calculations and analogies cannot fathom it. How much more so for one who fully receives, upholds, reads, recites, and explains it to others! The merit gained by this person is immeasurable and boundless; even all the buddhas, the tathāgatas, could not fully describe it. Why? Because it can give rise to the sarvajña of all buddhas. If space had an end, then the merit of this sūtra would have an end. If the dharma-nature had an end, then the merit of this sūtra would have an end. Therefore, Mañjuśrī, good men and good women should diligently practice, advance with vigor, and protect this sūtra. This sūtra can extinguish all the fears of birth and death, can demolish the victory banner erected by the celestial Māra, can bring bodhisattvas to the fruit of nirvāṇa, and instruct and guide them away from the two vehicles."

Then Śakra and the Elder Ānanda both said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, so it is, so it is! Truly, as the Buddha has said, we will receive and uphold this upon our heads, and widely disseminate it. May the Tathāgata not be concerned on our account." Three times they said: "May you not be concerned. We will receive and uphold this upon our heads."

When the Buddha finished teaching this sūtra, Mañjuśrī and all the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, Śāriputra and all the bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, and non-humans, the entire great assembly, having heard what the Buddha taught, all greatly rejoiced, faithfully received it, and put it into practice.
