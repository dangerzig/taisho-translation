# 佛說四不可得經
## Sūtra of the Four Things That Cannot Be Obtained, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0770

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling at the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada in Śrāvastī, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, one thousand two hundred and fifty, and various bodhisattvas.

At dawn the Buddha put on his robes and took up his bowl to enter the city for alms. The four assemblies all followed him, and the devas, nāgas, and spirits each brought flowers, incense, and music in attendance above.

With the eye of the path the Buddha perceived four brothers, born of the same parents, who had left their homes and abandoned their livelihoods to dwell in the mountains in seclusion. They had attained the five supernatural powers and were all called sages. Their destined end had come, and knowing their lifespans were exhausted, they each wished to escape death. Each one deliberated: "Our supernatural feet enable us to fly and soar freely, reaching wherever we wish without obstruction. Yet now we are about to be overtaken by impermanence, imperiling our very lives. We should devise a plan to escape this calamity; we cannot simply submit to it."

Thereupon one of them leapt into the sky and hid his form: "How would the adversary of impermanence know where I am?" Another entered the marketplace, into the midst of a vast crowd of people, and hid his life there: "The adversary of impermanence need only catch one person; why should he seek me?" Another retreated into the great ocean, three million three hundred and sixty thousand leagues deep, neither touching the bottom nor reaching the surface, and stayed in the middle: "What would the adversary of impermanence seek there?" Another calculated and stole away to a great mountain where no one dwelt, split the mountain open in two, entered within, and closed it again: "How would the adversary of impermanence know where I am?"

At that time the four each tried to escape death, but none could avoid it. The one hiding in the sky fell to the ground like ripe fruit dropping. The one in the mountain perished there. The one in the great ocean died at once, devoured by fish and turtles. The one in the marketplace ended his life there among the crowd.

Then the World-Honored One, seeing this, said of these four men that they were benighted and unenlightened, wishing to flee their destined end. They had not removed the three poisons and had not attained the three penetrations and boundless wisdom. From ancient times to the present, who has ever escaped this calamity? The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Though one would hide in the sky,
> Or dwell amid the great ocean,
> Or even enter the mountains,
> Seeking to conceal one's form,
> Wishing to find a deathless land:
> Such a place can never be found.
> Therefore study with diligence;
> Only in bodilessness is there peace."

The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "There are four things in the world that cannot be obtained. What are these four?

"First, in youth one's complexion is radiant, one's hair black and teeth white, one's appearance lustrous and one's strength vigorous. One walks and moves freely, mounts carriages and rides horses, and all who see one look up with affection and respect. Then suddenly one grows old: the head turns white, the teeth fall out, the face wrinkles and the skin sags. The body grows heavy, one leans on a staff, one pants and groans. To wish for perpetual youth without reaching old age: this can never be obtained.

"Second, the body is strong and healthy, the bones and marrow are firm, one walks without equal, eats and drinks at will, adorns one's head and considers oneself peerless, draws the bow and wields weapons, harms others without regard for right or wrong, curses freely and considers oneself mighty, thinking 'I shall never decline.' Then illness suddenly strikes: one lies prostrate in bed, unable to move. The body aches as if beaten. Ears, nose, mouth, and eyes no longer perceive sound, scent, fine flavor, or subtle touch. One needs others to help one sit and rise, filth issues forth on its own, and the body lies in it. The afflictions are beyond description. To wish for permanent health and freedom from illness: this can never be obtained.

"Third, to wish for long life without end in this world. When sickness and death come, one's life proves very short. Harboring plans for ten thousand years yet with a brief lifespan and many worries, heedless of impermanence, one indulges in the five pleasures at will, releasing the mind to idle thoughts, killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, divisive speech, harsh speech, false speech, frivolous speech, covetousness, wrong views, being unfilial to parents, disobedient to teachers and friends, and slighting elders. Acting rebelliously without principle, hoping for power and wealth as if it could last forever, mocking the holy path, considering heterodox ways to be without equal, studying astrology and prognostication, admiring worldly glory without understanding the inner and outer workings of heaven and earth, not discerning how the four great elements combine through conditions like illusion and transformation, not comprehending the ages that have arisen since ancient times, not accepting guidance, not knowing whence life comes or whither death goes, believing heaven and earth to be one's own possession: when the adversary of impermanence arrives like wind scattering clouds, though one yearns for long life, life suddenly ends, and one cannot control it. To wish that it were otherwise: this can never be obtained.

"Fourth, parents, brothers, household and kin, friends and acquaintances, loving bonds and shared joys, wealth and riches, official rank and salary, riding and sightseeing, wives, concubines, and children: through all these one indulges oneself, eating and drinking to one's heart's content, surrounded by guests and servants, looking at one's shadow as one walks, despising others and considering oneself without equal, cursing servants and treating livestock like animals, going about at will without any measure, heedless of what comes before and after, thinking that one's family, retainers, and attendants can always be kept. When the destined end suddenly arrives like hot water melting snow, the mind fills with terror, and though one begs for relief, how can one's wishes be fulfilled? In a single gasp life is cut short, and the soul departs alone. Parents, brothers, wife, children, kin, friends, acquaintances, and loved ones all remain behind alone. Official rank, wealth, and servants scatter and flee like shooting stars. To wish for deathlessness: this cannot be obtained."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "From ancient times to the present, since heaven and earth were established, no one has been exempt from these four difficult afflictions. It is because of these four sufferings that the Buddha appears in the world. Were there no such difficulties, the Buddha would not manifest his body to teach and transform the multitudes. It is as if, from the four directions, great stone mountains, vast and towering, covered with grasses, trees, fruit, and medicinal plants all flourishing, were suddenly engulfed by wildfire, flames advancing from all four mountains toward the center, faster than the course of the sun. If someone reported to the king about this calamity, could it be avoided?"

The answer came: "It cannot be obtained, Deva above Devas! Only supernatural power could save one."

The Buddha said: "Those with mind, thought, and consciousness, who do not comprehend the profound and wondrous wisdom of emptiness, whose minds grasp at a self, who are bound by the five aggregates and deluded by the six sense-declines, who wish to avoid old age and illness and to escape these evils of parting and separation, aspiring to permanence: this can never be obtained. Only by accomplishing the dharma body, when the aggregates and the sense-declines are all removed, with nothing internal and nothing external, advancing and retreating freely, can one escape these four difficult afflictions. It is as if one plants grain in spring and wishes it not to ripen in autumn: this can never be obtained. Having planted the roots of old age, illness, death, and separation, to wish for them not to end is not as one's aspiration would have it. It is like a tree that bears fruit: to wish the fruit not to fall can never be obtained. It is like a person who drinks wine and wishes not to become intoxicated: who has ever had such a wish fulfilled? Having planted all the roots, to wish for them not to end cannot be obtained. It is like a person who takes poison and wishes not to die: who has ever had such a wish fulfilled? Having planted the roots of separation, to wish for them not to part can never be obtained. It is like a person who enters a privy and wishes it not to stink: who has ever had such a wish fulfilled? Having planted old age, illness, and death, to wish to escape these afflictions has never been as one wished.

"People who do not recognize these four sufferings release their minds to indulgence and sink into the five destinies, like a wheel that cannot leave the ground, with grieving and lamenting, yearning for one another without end. It is like a madman who goes about naked, babbling deliriously and calling it truth. How painful! Who comprehends this meaning? Only those who understand the path know it."

A bhikṣu addressed the Buddha: "By what means can one be saved from these difficult sufferings?"

The World-Honored One said: "One should seek liberation." "What is liberation?" The Buddha said: "Guard the body, speech, and mind. Be good at the beginning, in the middle, and at the end. Do not practice the way of the śrāvakas alone. The body does not commit the three offenses, the mouth does not commit the four faults, the mind does not contemplate the three evils: this is being good at the beginning, in the middle, and at the end. Furthermore, when body, speech, and mind are harmonized and take refuge in the Three Treasures, when one eliminates the three poisons and enters the dharmas of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, approaching the three gates of liberation: this is the virtue of being good at the beginning, in the middle, and at the end. The three realms are all suffering. Regarding the body as an enemy in the face of birth, old age, illness, and death, one practices the four immeasurable minds: kindness, compassion, joy, and protection. But if one merely follows the great kindness and boundless compassion and seeks only to free one's own body and reach nirvāṇa without thinking of all beings, one attains arhatship but does not reach the ten directions. Though one escapes the four afflictions, one still has limitations."

The Buddha said: "Generate the bodhisattva aspiration: universally cause all sentient beings to be ever mindful and to follow the six boundless practices of the pāramitās. Being good at the beginning, in the middle, and at the end means the bodhisattva who generates the aspiration.

"Good at the beginning means regarding all people as one's father, mother, and own body, always equal and without distinction. Good in the middle means not fearing toil and hardship, remaining in birth-and-death through innumerable kalpas without considering it wearisome. Good at the end means discerning the wisdom of emptiness without seeing any self.

"Furthermore, good at the beginning means originally generating the great aspiration to deliver all beings, not calculating for oneself. Good in the middle means practicing the four immeasurable minds: kindness, compassion, joy, and protection. Good at the end means extending boundless compassion, wishing to guide the multitudes who encounter all manner of affliction.

"Good at the beginning means contemplating the body as composed of the four great elements, originally without substance, a combination arising from conditions, conditioned upon the unconditioned. Good in the middle means there is no self, no person, no lifespan, and no life force; yet because of these four things one receives a body. Without conditions, without grasping, from what would causes arise? It is like building a house: there are timber, earth, water, and grass. If these four things are separated, each scattered in a different place, a person assembles them into a dwelling and thereby obtains the name 'house.' Calculating the four things of the body is also like this: each stands on its own side, yet the mind grasps at 'self,' 'person,' 'lifespan,' and 'life force,' and the four great elements combine to give the name 'person.' Good at the end means clearly knowing there is no body, not relying on the three realms, and that all is empty.

"Good at the beginning means the path of giving, morality, patience, vigor, one-pointed concentration, and wisdom. Good in the middle means understanding the six supernatural powers and entering the patient acceptance that accords with the Dharma. Good at the end means knowing the body is naturally so, all dharmas are naturally so, people and things are naturally so, and all is like illusion and transformation, fundamentally without existence.

"Good at the beginning means generating the aspiration for the unsurpassed, true, and right path. Good in the middle means comprehending the wisdom of echoing sounds and attaining the patient acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas. Good at the end means reaching the station of one more birth before Buddhahood, with a courageous and mighty bearing, coursing through all forms of existence, perceiving non-arising, and saving all beings, like the sun that shines universally with nothing left unilluminated. This is the bodhisattva who is good at the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end."

The Buddha said: "The bodhisattva who practices the path delivers a great multitude. Like the moon that alone shines among all the stars; like the sun when it first rises, illuminating everywhere at once; like a torch that lights wherever it goes; healing all illnesses like a good physician; ferrying the multitudes like a helmsman; bringing peace to the three realms like a sovereign; subduing heterodox paths like a lion; the mind of the path is as universal as space; the mind is as equal as the earth; it washes away defilements like water; it burns away all offenses like fire; it moves without obstruction like the wind. This is the work of the bodhisattva who is good at the beginning, in the middle, and at the end, brought to its ultimate fulfillment."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, all the bodhisattvas and the assembly of bhikṣus, and the devas, nāgas, spirits, and asuras, hearing the sūtra, were all filled with joy.
