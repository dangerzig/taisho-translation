# Siddhaṃ Chapter of the Chan Gate from the Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha

## *Fóshuō Léngqié Jīng Chánmén Xītán Zhāng* (佛說楞伽經禪門悉談章)

Taishō 2779

Author unknown. A Dunhuang Chan liturgical text.

*Note: This is a remarkable Dunhuang text combining Siddhaṃ (Sanskrit syllabary) recitation with Chan (Zen) meditation instruction. It presents eight stages of Chan practice, each framed by a Siddhaṃ syllable formula, a doctrinal teaching, an exhortation, and a refrain. The text claims descent from Bodhidharma's transmission of the Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra and credits the monk Dìnghuì of Sōngshān Huìshàn Monastery with composing the Siddhaṃ chapter. The phonetic refrains (e.g., "mólǐ mó lǔliú lúlóu") reflect a Sinified adaptation of Sanskrit syllable practice.*

All you children of the Buddha, join your palms and listen wholeheartedly. I now wish to expound the Siddhaṃ (*xītán*, 悉談) chapter of the Mahāyāna Laṅkāvatāra (*Léngqié*, 楞伽).

As for the Siddhaṃ chapter: in the past, the Mahāyāna was on Laṅkā Mountain. It was then that Bodhidharma (*Pútídámó*, 菩提達摩), in the first year of the Sòng [i.e. Liu Song dynasty], came from the kingdom of South India bringing the Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra to the Eastern Capital. The tripiṭaka dharma master Buddhabhadra (*Bátuó*, 跋陀) received it and translated it. The sūtra has five fascicles forming one complete work. The text is vast and the meaning difficult to understand. The master, with compassion, broadly aided all classes of beings. He penetrated the sūtras, inquired into the way, and grasped the profound principle. He fathomed the root source, and all received his direct instruction. Furthermore, the śramaṇa Dìnghuì (*Dìnghuì*, 定慧) of Huìshàn Monastery (*Huìshàn Sì*, 會善寺) on Mount Sōng (*Sōngshān*, 嵩山) translated the Siddhaṃ chapter, broadly opening the Chan gate without obstructing the study of wisdom. It does not cling to written characters and accords entirely with Chinese pronunciation, corresponding to the phonetics of Dharma Master Kumārajīva (*Jiūmóluóshí*, 鳩摩羅什). It begins with *lǔ liú lú lóu*.

### First: Renouncing Conditions and the Pure Seat

*Pōluóduò pōluóduò.* The first is to renounce conditions and sit in purity. When the myriad things do not arise, this is the true selflessness. Proceed directly to bodhi, leaving behind cause and effect. Mind after mind is quiescent extinction, free of calamity and misfortune. Thought after thought is non-thought: this is to be affirmed and approved.

*Módǐlì mó lǔliú lúlóu pōluóduò.* Children of the Buddha, do not be lazy or slack; exhort yourselves. The river of craving and sea of suffering must be crossed. Thinking of food but not eating, one is always hungry. If wood is not rubbed, fire does not emerge. *Yēluóluó*, sit upright. *Suōhē yē*, do not lie down.

### Second: Abiding in Mind and Always Contemplating Purity

*Zhǐlǐngshèng zhǐlǐngshèng.* The second is to abide in mind and always contemplate purity. Seeing and hearing are without gazing or listening. When arising and ceasing are both forgotten, one is still not yet fully realized. Receiving instruction from the teacher, samādhi is then manifest. To see the dharma-body of the Buddha is without duality.

*Xìng dǐnglǐng jìng lǔliú lúlóu zhǐlǐngshèng.* Children of the Buddha, do not be angry or deceitful. When the three poisons suddenly arise, there is no buddha-nature. The deluded and mad mind runs wild, troubling the worthy and holy. The eye craves visible forms, the ear is bound by sound. Turning away from heaven, one heads down the evil path. *Yínglìngjīn*, cultivate samādhi. *Suōhē yē*, return to what is right.

### Third: Contemplating Mind and the Need to Be Upright

*Àlàngyǎng àlàngyǎng.* The third is to contemplate the mind and be upright. Sweep away defilement and remove disaster's obstacles. Form is precisely emptiness: realize non-thought. Deluded discrimination is a measure of the mind. Recognize the substance upon the substance: truly there is no slander.

*Bàng dǐlì bàng lǔliú lúlóu àlàngyǎng.* Children of the Buddha, do not slander. All beings have karmic obstacles. When others hear a sound, they do not let it go. What is a three-inch tongue-root aimed at? Speaking of the long and criticizing the short only vexes the mind-king. If the mind-king does not understand, it speaks of the short and long. In the next life, on the road of karma, one receives suffering and misfortune. *Yáng liángliang*, sweep clean. In the hall, make offerings.

### Fourth: The Eight Consciousnesses United with the Sixth and Seventh

*Fúsùzhì fúsùzhì.* The fourth is the eight consciousnesses merged with the sixth and seventh. Contemplating the mind, the mind itself is the Chan chamber. The dharma-body and the body of dharma: wisdom is not one. The five eyes and six supernatural powers are the light of the wisdom-sun. Under the words, one directly awakens: truly there is no secret.

*Mì dǐlì mì lǔliú lúlóu fúsùzhì.* Children of the Buddha, do not be heedless. Since beginningless time you have dwelt in a dark room. In the flow of birth and death, you cannot get out. Only because of foolish delusion do you obstruct the wisdom-sun. *Yì sù mì yì sù mì. Suōhē*, straight and true.

### Fifth: Illumination within the Gate of True Characteristics

*Xiǎoliáoyào xiǎoliáoyào.* The fifth is illumination within the gate of true characteristics. All fame and profit is but deluded calling. As it already is, breath ceases; appearance is not appearance. Neither cause nor effect: without anger or laughter. Looking at the nature upon the nature: the wondrous within the wondrous.

*Yào dǐlǐ yào lǔliú lúlóu xiǎoliáoyào.* Children of the Buddha, do not laugh in anger. Sorrow, grief, anger, and laughter all obstruct the way. In this gate of the way, there is no anger or laughter. Still the mind and look, illuminating both within and without. When there is a film in the eye, it must be polished bright. A bronze mirror unpolished will not serve for reflection. *Yáo liáo liào*, do well. *Suōhē yē*, do not be vexed.

### Sixth: The Mind Leaves the Chan Gate and Contemplates

*Ànlàipàn ànlàipàn.* The sixth is the mind leaving the Chan gate to contemplate. Neither coming nor going, without cliff or boundary. Contemplating awakening upon awakening, removing fixation and disorder. The buddha's children and sentient beings share the same substance. The original source is pure: polish away the dust and it scatters.

*Tàn dǐlì tàn lǔliú lúlóu ànlàipàn.* Children of the Buddha, do not stare blankly. On the way, there are great rākṣasas calling. Foolish people, coming and going, are always tied and bound. Clinging to forms and dust, the mind is tangled in confusion. Walking, standing, sitting, lying down: without composure. Amidst the crowd, calling out wildly. When others advise and admonish, they become stubborn and hostile. *Yēluóluó*, irrigate! *Suōhē yē*, dull fellow!

### Seventh: Perfectly Bright Great Wisdom-Awakening

*Pǔlùyù pǔlùyù.* The seventh is the perfectly bright great wisdom-awakening. The four gates and eighteen: leave behind names and numbers. Arising and ceasing, wondrous existence: penetrating and crossing freely. The great teacher of the three realms is truly hard to encounter. Birth-and-death and nirvāṇa are not to be crossed. Going against the current up the river of craving, not lingering. The immediate mind is non-mind: Māra naturally departs.

*Qù dǐlì qù lǔliú lúlóu pǔlùyù.* Children of the Buddha, always be awakened. One thought of the pure mind is without defilement. All the hosts of Māra naturally depart. *Lǘ lǘ lǜ*, concentrate. *Suōhē yē*, great awakening.

### Eighth: The Chan Gate Transcends All Measures

*Àlüèyào àlüèyào.* The eighth is the Chan gate transcending all measures. Neither high nor low, without towers or walls. Neither going out nor entering, without city or ramparts. When thoughts manifest sound, this is the first step of learning. When the mind stirs with thought, do not allow attachment. Sitting long, exerting effort: acting yet not acting. Without any bliss to enjoy: this is the constant bliss. The lamp of wisdom, once lit, illuminates three thousand realms. The water of samādhi, always clear, refines eighty thousand. The buddhas of the ten directions all share one awakening.

*Jué dǐlì bó lǔliú lúlóu àlüèyào.* Children of the Buddha, act freely without constraint. The four directions, above and below, are immeasurable. Abide in quiescent nirvāṇa, sharing one vast expanse. Most peaceful and blissful, without attachment. *Suōhē yē*, equal awakening.
