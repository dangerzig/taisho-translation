# Essential Verses for Exhorting Kings

## *Quànfā Zhūwáng Yàojì* (勸發諸王要偈)

Taishō 1673

Composed by Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna (*Lóngshù Púsà*, 龍樹菩薩). Translated by Sanghavarman (*Sēngqiébámó*, 僧伽跋摩) of the Liú-Sòng dynasty. A didactic letter in verse.

*Note: This is a Chinese translation of Nāgārjuna's famous "Letter to a Friend" (Suhṛllekha), a practical guide to Buddhist ethics and soteriology addressed to a king. Cast entirely in verse, it covers the six recollections, the ten wholesome actions, the pāramitās, the dangers of the five sense-pleasures, the contemplation of the body's impurity, the sufferings of the six destinies (with vivid descriptions of hell, hungry-ghost, and animal realms), the twelve links of dependent origination, the Four Noble Truths, the Eightfold Path, and the aspiration to buddhahood. Despite its doctrinal comprehensiveness, the tone is warm and personal, befitting a letter from a spiritual teacher to a royal patron.*

> O king of illustrious and supreme merit,
> I have no other object of desire.
> From the dharma taught by all buddhas,
> Of what meaning is its adornment?
> I have briefly compiled these verses of the worthy and holy,
> Which a great king ought to hear.

> Just as from various kinds of wood
> One fashions an image of the Tathāgata,
> The wise bow in reverence,
> Honoring the image because of the Buddha.
> Now with my lack of eloquence
> I broadly proclaim the treasury of truth.
> The wise should trust and delight in this,
> Relying on the dharma, listening to what is set forth.

> Great king, though you have often heard
> The Tathāgata's Brahmā-voice teaching,
> Superior understanding comes from much hearing;
> Repeated hearing deepens faith.
> As sunlight shines upon white cloth,
> How could it not increase its brightness?

> The Three Jewels, generosity, precepts, and the heavens:
> The Most Excellent One teaches the six recollections.
> Following all forms of merit,
> Observe them truly and well.

> In body, speech, and mind, constantly practice
> The pure ten courses of wholesome action.
> Keep far from wine and do not become intoxicated;
> Abandon wrong livelihood and cultivate right livelihood.

> Know that wealth belongs to five claimants,
> Impermanent and not enduring.
> Give generously to the virtuous,
> The poor and suffering, and one's kin.

> What follows one through every birth:
> Generosity is the most supreme.
> It is not cut off, nor does it perish;
> It does not depart, and it does not seek the fruit.

> Such are the pure precepts:
> One should well receive and uphold them.
> They are the fertile field
> That gives rise to all forms of merit.

> Generosity, precepts, patience, diligence,
> Meditation, and immeasurable wisdom:
> These pāramitās
> The wise should cultivate.
> They can cross the sea of the three existences
> And attain the honored rank of the Muni.

> If a person honors father and mother
> And serves them with all sincerity,
> This is called the gate of rites and teaching.
> The pure heavenly noble clan,
> With fame spreading far,
> Upon leaving the body is born in heaven.

> Abstain from killing, stealing, lust, deception,
> Drinking wine, and eating at the three improper times.
> Accomplish the eight-part fast,
> Following the example of all buddhas.
> Upon leaving the body, one is born in the six heavens,
> And all desires are fulfilled at will.

> Miserliness, guile, deception, arrogance,
> Laziness, greed, anger, and delusion,
> Clan, beauty, good looks,
> Youth, wide learning, and pleasures:
> All such things that lead one astray
> Should be regarded as enemies.

> If one cultivates heedfulness,
> This is the path of deathlessness.
> Heedlessness is the path of death:
> So the World-Honored One has said.
> To increase wholesome dharmas,
> One should cultivate heedfulness.

> If a person first does evil
> But later becomes heedful,
> Such a one illumines the world,
> Like the moon revealed when clouds disperse.

> Patience has no equal;
> Do not follow the mind of anger.
> The Buddha says that one who can keep far from it
> Attains the path of non-return.

> Some anger is like writing on water,
> Some like writing on earth or stone.
> If one speaks of transcending afflictions,
> The first type of person is superior.
> Reforming evil and cultivating loving patience,
> The third type is supreme.

> The Most Excellent One says that beings
> Use three kinds of speech, good and bad.
> The first is called "attracting the heart,"
> Sweet words like candy and honey.
> The second is called "truthful speech,"
> Like a wondrous flower in bloom.
> The third is called "untruthful speech,"
> Base and foul like dung and filth.
> The wise should distinguish among them:
> Abandon the last and cultivate the first two.

> From light, light leads to the end.
> From darkness, darkness leads to the end.
> Some go from darkness into light;
> Some go from light into darkness.
> The wise should carefully understand:
> Abandon three and ascend to the first, the light.

> Some appear raw but are ripe;
> Some appear ripe but are raw.
> Some are both raw and ripe.
> The discerning should carefully distinguish.

> Do not gaze upon another's wife;
> If you see her, regard her as mother or daughter.
> If desire still arises,
> One should cultivate the contemplation of impurity.

> The mind tends to run wild;
> Diligently guard and hold it well,
> As one protects a famous reputation,
> A treasure-store, a beloved child, one's life.

> One should contemplate the five sense-pleasures
> As evil venomous snakes,
> As enemies, knives, and fire;
> Devise means to cultivate revulsion.

> The five pleasures give rise to what is not righteous,
> Like the *bimba* fruit:
> Covering their true nature, they skillfully deceive,
> Binding people in birth-and-death.
> The wise should observe and examine them;
> Cast them aside, do not be defiled.

> The sense-faculties are always light and restless,
> Racing and scattering among the six sense-objects.
> If one can well tame and subdue them,
> This is the truly great and valiant one.

> This body is a privy for the practices;
> Nine orifices constantly flow with filth.
> Riddled with holes, impossible to fill,
> Thin skin hides what is impure.
> The foolish are deceived by it;
> The wise should feel revulsion.

> Like a person with scabies-mites
> Who approaches a fire to relieve the ailment:
> A little pleasure, then suffering increases.
> Craving desire is just like this.

> One who well knows the faults of desire
> Is thereby freed from all suffering.
> To see the ultimate truth,
> The Buddha teaches: contemplate dependent origination.
> One should diligently cultivate this practice;
> Nothing surpasses it.

> Clan, bodily beauty,
> Wide learning as one's garland:
> If one does not cultivate precepts and wisdom,
> These are not truly distinguished.
> One who possesses these two merits,
> Even without the other three, is remarkable.

> Gain and loss, slander and praise,
> Fame and blame, suffering and happiness:
> If the eight worldly winds do not shake you,
> This is the holy king.

> Do not, for the sake of devas, spirits,
> Śramaṇas, brāhmaṇas,
> Clan, relatives, or guests,
> Take life or create evil karma.
> At death one enters hell
> And suffers alone; they do not take your place.

> If a person commits evil deeds
> And does not immediately receive punishment,
> At life's end one receives the bitter retribution.
> Later regret: of what use will it be?

> Faith, precepts, generosity, wide learning,
> Wisdom, a sense of shame and conscience:
> The Buddha says these are treasures not shared.
> All other treasures are shared by all.

> Gambling and great assemblies,
> Laziness, keeping bad company,
> Drinking and dissipated wandering,
> Roaming at night without shame:
> These six stain one's reputation.
> The wise should keep far from them.

> Contentment is the greatest wealth:
> So the World-Honored One has praised.
> If one can cultivate contentment,
> Though poor and lowly, one is truly rich.

> Like a many-headed dragon,
> Many heads mean much suffering.
> Of a naturally resentful disposition:
> This is called the enemy-wife.
> Haughty and disobedient:
> This is called the contemptuous wife.
> Squandering her husband's wealth:
> This is called the thief-wife.
> Beware, worthy husband:
> Keep far from these three wives.

> The one who follows along is like a sister.
> The one who delights in love is like a good friend.
> The one who comforts is like a mother.
> The one who complies is like a maidservant.
> These four are worthy, good wives,
> True kin to their husbands.

> Food and drink are medicine;
> Without greed, anger, or delusion, they are your garment.
> Eat only to stop the body's suffering;
> Do not eat to fatten yourself in heedlessness.

> By day, diligently cultivate your practice;
> The early and late parts of night likewise.
> Even in the middle of the night, maintain right mindfulness;
> Do not let it pass in empty dreams.

> Loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity:
> Day and night, constantly cultivate them.
> Even if one has not yet left the world,
> The merit surpasses that of the Brahmā heaven.

> Freed from desire, the awakening of joy;
> Through suffering and pleasure, cultivate the four dhyānas.
> Brahmā-radiance, purity, and the fruit of fruition:
> Receive these heavenly joys.

> If a person commits few evils
> And extensively cultivates immeasurable good,
> It is like casting a pinch of salt
> Into the great Ganges:
> If a person commits many evils
> And cultivates little pure merit,
> It is like placing much poison
> Into a small vessel of food.

> The five aggregates are dark thieves
> Who rob people of their wholesome treasures.
> The warriors of the five faculties of faith
> Can well guard and protect.

> Birth, old age, sickness, death, suffering,
> Separation from what one loves,
> Sinking without rescue:
> All this is due to one's own karma.

> Seek rebirth in heaven and liberation;
> Diligently cultivate right view.
> With wrong views, though one practices good,
> All results in suffering.

> Impermanence, suffering, impurity:
> One should well observe and examine these.
> If one does not think rightly,
> The four inversions blind the eye of wisdom.

> Beautiful form is not the self;
> The self is not the master of form.
> The four aggregates are likewise:
> All are merely empty, suffering clusters.

> Not from time, not without cause,
> Not from inherent nature,
> Not from a creator god:
> From ignorance and craving, karma arises.

> The view of a self in the body, clinging to rules and rites, and doubt:
> These three obstruct liberation.
> The holy wisdom opens the gate of freedom;
> By one's own power, not through another.

> Pure precepts, study of meditation,
> Diligent cultivation of the four dhyānas:
> The higher training in precepts, mind, and wisdom,
> One should always diligently study.

> All precepts, wisdom, and samādhi
> Are contained within the three trainings.
> The mindfulness of the body, that great power:
> The Buddha teaches it as the one-vehicle path.

> Always bind the mind in mindfulness,
> Guarding it skillfully with expedient means.
> If one forgets right mindfulness,
> One loses all wholesome dharmas.

> Body and life are extremely fragile,
> Like bubbles on water blown by the wind.
> From dream to waking, nothing can be assured;
> The outbreath has no certain return.
> In a flash, all becomes fine dust.
> Know that nothing is solid.

> The great earth, Mount Sumeru, and the oceans:
> Seven suns will burn them all,
> Utterly, leaving not a trace.
> How much more this fragile body?

> Impermanence cannot be relied upon,
> Nor is it a sheltering dharma.
> This body cannot be trusted.
> How can one not give rise to revulsion?

> Like a blind turtle in the ocean
> Encountering a hole in a floating log:
> For an animal to regain a human body
> Is even more difficult than this.
> Being in the human path,
> How can one not cultivate the supreme fruit?
> A jeweled vessel filled with dung:
> Such a person is a fool.
> Having obtained the precious human body
> And using it to create evil deeds:
> Know that such a person
> Is the most extremely foolish of all.

> To be born in a land where the Way exists,
> To encounter a good spiritual teacher,
> To have right view accomplished in the heart,
> And merit from past lives:
> With four precious wheels complete,
> One can escape the road of birth-and-death.
> Drawing near to a good spiritual teacher,
> Fully cultivating the holy life:
> The Buddha says that such a person's
> Mind always attains quiescence.

> Wrong views, the three evil destinies,
> Not hearing the sound of the buddha-dharma,
> Borderlands, places of darkness,
> Deafness, muteness, and the long-life heavens:
> The king has already left these eight difficulties
> And obtained this unobstructed body.
> It is fitting to cultivate wholesome karma
> And devote oneself to seeking nirvāṇa.

> In the long night of birth-and-death,
> Immeasurable varieties of suffering:
> We revolve, taking turns as the six kinds of kin;
> Noble and lowly have no fixed order.

> Through the eternal kalpas of birth-and-death,
> There has never been a time one was not someone's child.
> Reckoning the loving mother's milk one has drunk,
> It would fill by analogy the four great seas.
> Ordinary beings who are yet to be reborn
> Will drink yet more than this.
> The bones of a single person from the beginning
> Would pile as high as Mount Sumeru.

> The human and heavenly realms one has passed through
> Are as numerous as the dust-motes of the great earth.
> Once a wheel-turning king,
> Later a menial servant.
> Or above, becoming Śakra,
> Attended by all the devas;
> Below, born amid dung and dirt:
> The going and returning is beyond reckoning.

> Sometimes born in heaven,
> Sporting with celestial maidens in the utmost pleasure,
> Eyes dazzled by wondrous forms,
> Ears hearing myriad sounds,
> Touch all soft and fine,
> Happiness beyond description.

> Later falling into hell,
> Experiencing every kind of bitter poison.
> Born among the sword-forest trees,
> Head and body fall with every blade.
> Or roaming atop Mount Sumeru,
> Rising and descending as one wills.

> Together with heavenly maidens,
> Bathing in the Mandārava pool,
> Jeweled flowers arrayed in splendor,
> Cool and supremely pleasant.
> Then entering the Boiling-Ash River,
> Simmered and boiled until utterly dissolved.

> The joys of the five pleasures in the six heavens,
> The pleasure of freedom from desire in the Brahmā world:
> After death, entering the Avīci Hell,
> Enduring every kind of bitter torment.

> Sometimes becoming a deva of the sun or moon,
> With radiance illuminating the four regions;
> Later born in the Dark Hell,
> Unable even to see one's own form.

> O king, you should light the lamp of wisdom;
> Do not follow the eternal darkness.

> In the eight great hells,
> The suffering of burning, roasting, slaughter, and rending,
> Enduring every kind of torment:
> Immeasurable, beyond all analogy.

> If a person follows delusion
> And creates every kind of evil karma,
> And between the outbreath and inbreath
> Hears of these great sufferings
> Yet is not frightened in heart:
> That one is a person of wood and stone.

> Seeing with the eye the images of retribution,
> Further hearing the words of the wise,
> Examining the sūtras of the Buddha,
> Reflecting rightly in the mind:
> One should feel great dread.
> How much more so to experience them oneself!

> Of all the sufferings one can bear,
> Avīci is the greatest.
> Of all the joys one can receive,
> The ending of craving is the foremost joy.

> Day and night, in each of the three periods,
> Three hundred lances piercing the body:
> Even this, compared to Avīci's suffering,
> Is not a hundredth, not a thousandth, of the analogy.

> Immeasurable torments,
> Seeking death and finding it impossible.
> Receiving punishment for hundreds and thousands of years,
> Until the evil karma is exhausted.

> Impure, suffering, bitter retribution:
> The seeds are the karma of body and speech.
> If the seeds are not planted, there is no fruit.
> O king, you should cut the root of suffering.

> If one falls into the animal realm,
> There is the suffering of bondage, slaughter, and harm.
> With covetous, harmful, and deranged minds,
> Bound by enmity, they devour one another.

> Some are killed for their pearls and jewels,
> Their hair, tails, hides, flesh, and bones.
> Because of this they lose their lives,
> Suffering the pain of being flayed, hacked, and severed.

> The swift-footed and powerful
> Have their necks pierced, bearing the suffering of servitude.
> The wild and unruly,
> Are disciplined with goad and whip.

> Hungry ghosts yearn for food and drink,
> But what they desire never appears.
> Tormented by hunger, thirst, cold, and heat,
> Through the long night, without rest.

> Some have bodies like great mountains
> But throats as narrow as a needle's point.
> Hunger and thirst burn them within;
> Before food, they find no way to eat.

> Some see pus, blood, phlegm, and spittle
> And run in a group, racing to reach it.
> Arriving, it vanishes of itself;
> Hope is cut off, and suffering increases.

> Hunger and thirst scald them within;
> Tumors, goiters, and abscesses erupt.
> They claw and grab at one another,
> Gnawing and sucking pus and blood.

> Emaciated, skin stretched over bones,
> Naked, draped in long hair,
> Bodies tall as withered trees.
> Blazing flames issue from their mouths,
> Burning back upon their own bodies,
> Like torching a palmyra tree.

> In summer, hoping for the night's coolness,
> The moonlight only increases their heat.
> In winter, longing for the day's warmth,
> The rising sun only deepens the ice.
> Approaching a tree, the fruit vanishes;
> Going to a river, the water dries up.
> For fifteen thousand years,
> Karma sustains a life that does not end.

> Long enduring immeasurable suffering,
> All due to past karmic causes.
> Every kind of torment presses upon them,
> Pure suffering without a moment's respite.
> Extreme miserliness and clinging attachment:
> The Buddha says these are the causes of the hungry-ghost realm.

> Though born in heaven brings great joy,
> When merit is exhausted, the suffering is extreme.
> This is not the fruit of the worthy and holy;
> The wise do not rely upon it.

> The body loses its radiance;
> One no longer delights in one's former seat.
> The flower-crown suddenly wilts and falls;
> Dust and filth suddenly cling to the body.
> Sweat begins to flow from beneath the arms:
> Know that the time of death has arrived.

> When the pure karma of fortunate destinies is exhausted,
> One again falls into the three evil paths.
> Or one is born among the asuras,
> Where craving and jealousy bring constant suffering.
> Though possessing wisdom and intelligence,
> One never sees the true principle.

> In the six destinies of birth-and-death,
> The wheel turns without cease.
> In the supreme dharma, there is no birth;
> Birth is the vessel of all suffering.
> Even if your head were on fire,
> With right thought, do not give it a moment's attention.
> The karma of not receiving future existence:
> Devote your mind to diligent cultivation.

> The precepts, the qualities of meditation, and wisdom,
> Tranquility, taming, and immovable stillness:
> One should seek the path of nirvāṇa,
> Ultimately leaving birth-and-death.

> Mindfulness, investigation, diligence,
> Joy, ease, samādhi, and equanimity:
> These seven factors of awakening
> Are the pure path of the sweet dew.

> Without wisdom there is no meditation;
> Without meditation there is no wisdom.
> When both are accomplished,
> One can escape the stream of birth-and-death.
> The boundless great sea of suffering
> Is seen as mere water in a hoof-print.

> The fourteen unanswerable questions:
> The Buddha says one should not think on them.
> They are not the path of peace,
> Nor are they the place of quiescence.

> Ignorance conditions the formations;
> Conditioned by formations, consciousness arises.
> Name-and-form arises from consciousness;
> The six sense-bases arise from name-and-form.
> The six sense-bases give rise to the six contacts;
> From contact arise the various feelings.
> The feelings are the cause of craving;
> From craving arise the four graspings.
> The four graspings produce the three existences;
> Because of existence, there is future birth.
> From birth come old age and death,
> Worry, grief, and all suffering.
> The immeasurable mass of suffering:
> When birth is ended, all is extinguished.

> What the Most Excellent One has revealed,
> The profound dharma of dependent origination:
> If one can rightly observe it,
> This is the supreme among true visions.
> Such a true vision
> Is itself the seeing of the Buddha.

> Right view, right thought,
> Right speech, right action, right livelihood,
> Right mindfulness, right effort,
> And right samādhi:
> The eightfold noble path,
> Leading to quiescence: one should cultivate it.

> Birth is the truth of suffering.
> Affectionate attachment is the truth of accumulation.
> The cessation of suffering is called liberation.
> Reaching the other shore is the Eightfold Path.

> To see these noble truths,
> Constantly and diligently cultivate right wisdom.
> Though dwelling amid the five pleasures,
> The wise can gain release.

> All who realize the true dharma
> Begin from the state of an ordinary person.
> They do not fall from empty space,
> Nor do they spring from the earth.

> O wise and fearless king,
> Grasping the essentials without needing elaboration:
> You should cultivate the bridge of the true dharma
> And cross over the abyss of birth-and-death.

> Such profound dharmas as these above:
> Even for the renunciant they are difficult to master.
> How much more for a ruler of the world
> To practice them all completely?
> Cultivate them gradually as occasion allows;
> Do not let a single day pass in vain.

> For all people who cultivate goodness,
> Always give rise to sympathetic joy.
> In the three kinds of karma of one's own practice,
> Rightly transfer the merit toward the buddha-way.
> In the future world, one shall
> Receive this immeasurable merit,
> Always born among devas and humans,
> Becoming a sovereign king.
> Together with the great bodhisattva assembly,
> Sporting in all supernatural powers,
> Skillfully transforming sentient beings,
> Adorning and purifying the buddha-land.

> Generosity, precepts, and wisdom as the seeds,
> Going and returning among humans and devas,
> An untainted, pure, and famous name
> Spreading through the lands of the ten directions.

> A worldly guide, a lord of people,
> Born above as king of the Transformation Heaven,
> Causing beings to abandon the five pleasures
> And keep far from all heedlessness.

> Beings who have lost the true crossing
> Drift in the four floods.
> From the immeasurable suffering of birth-and-death,
> Ferry them across to the other shore.
> Through this, accomplish the buddha-way:
> The ultimate, the great nirvāṇa.
