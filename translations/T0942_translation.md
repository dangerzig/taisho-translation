# 釋迦佛讚
## Praise of Śākyamuni Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0942

Translated from the Chinese. Qing dynasty, translated by Dalai Lama Samdandarji (達喇嘛薩穆丹達爾吉)

---

*Namo śākyamunaye arhat guṇa āryāya*

> In the three times he released enmity and attained the Way with the nāgas;
> Upon the supreme jeweled throne, wondrously adorned beneath the lotus.

> His golden light shines bright, a body of awesome virtue;
> His voice of gandharva music is beyond compare.
> His mind, pure as the light of a billion suns,
> Is Śākyamuni, the guide of all beings.
> Like the stars arrayed across a clear sky,
> Illuminating the ocean depths with perfect clarity,
> Within the ocean of sincerity, the image of all virtues
> Stands distinct and peerlessly manifest.
> His fingers joined together, held at his heart,
> Like the moon reflected in a shallow pool.
> With single-minded presence before the Honored One,
> I offer this humble praise and pray for his compassion.
> Surrounded by all the courageous bodhisattvas,
> With hosts of arhats assembled before him,
> Like stars in heaven all paying homage to the moon,
> He is the supreme lord among the herd.
> A net of a thousand rays of light encircles his body,
> Its radiance pervading the entire realm of space.
> With various spiritual transformations, he bestows without obstruction,
> Descending like the king of geese in flight.
> At that time, through past cultivation of wholesome karma,
> Hundreds of thousands of devas and humans were brought to ripeness.
> Merely beholding the Honored One's face, they were liberated;
> All bonds and a hundred transgressions, all violent minds were abandoned.
> Seated heroically beneath the bodhi tree,
> With the troops of compassion and wisdom he subdued Māra;
> His awesome power vanquished all without remainder,
> Like a fierce wind sweeping away black clouds.

> Without donning armor, weapons, or equipment,
> Alone against a billion māras,
> He won complete victory over every army.
> Apart from the Honored One, who could do this?
> The World-Honored One's fire of benevolence and compassion
> Utterly scorches the heart of the deva of desire.
> His ocean of loving-kindness toward sentient beings
> Is equal to all, whether intimate or stranger.
> For the benefit of beings,
> He is never weary for even a moment.
> Beings in the Honored One's presence
> Are likewise never weary of reciting his virtues.

> His mind is deep and unfathomable like the ocean;
> His speech is fine and wondrous like the sound of a celestial drum.
> His body appears supreme like the summit of Mount Sumeru.
> Seeing, hearing, or recollecting him is never in vain.
> All the sentient beings of every world,
> At one time bringing forth their various doubts,
> Before each and every one of them equally,
> He simultaneously manifests both body and speech,
> And all their doubts are instantly and completely severed.

> The secret principle of the holy body, speech, and mind:
> Bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, and arhats,
> No matter how they investigate, cannot reach this domain of wisdom.
> How much less the worldly ones such as Brahmā and Śakra!
> Like the Garuḍa king traversing empty space,
> When his strength sometimes falters he pauses and turns back,
> Yet empty space alone has no end or limit.
> So too are the Buddha's merits:
> Like the great roc spreading its wings in flight,
> While a tiny sparrow strains to follow along the way.
> The domain traversed by the ten powers and sovereign freedom,
> Though one may wish to enter that supreme path,
> The wisdom-eye that sees ultimate truth is blind,
> And the mind of renunciation for bodhi is exhausted.
> The powerful enemy of afflictions constantly harasses,
> And one sinks deeper into the pit of self-grasping.
> In such a defiled and pitiable state,
> Even if the compassionate Honored One were to abandon them again,
> His great compassion takes pity on all the lowly.
> If not taking refuge in the Benevolent One, who else is there to rely upon?
> In the five turbidities, the evil age of confused conditions,
> When other honored ones abandon them, the Benevolent One fully protects.
> All Buddhas and bodhisattvas together praise him.
> He is truly like a white lotus; what can compare?
> The World-Honored One's deeds are for the purpose of taming beings.
> Though there is not even the slightest one he does not observe,
> It is entirely because of evil conditions and self-made transgressions
> That there is never any fault on the part of the Honored One.
> I vow that from now on I shall cultivate bodhi
> Until I attain the Way and subdue Māra.
> In life after life, may the World-Honored One always guard and protect me,
> And may I receive his sweet-dew words without weariness.

The above is the praise of Śākyamuni, which is called the cause for swiftly obtaining his grace. The disciple Jiangcuo, who upholds the Tripiṭaka, requested the greatly learned and virtuous master Huixian to compose this at the divine shrine of Wode Gong in the Snow Mountain Cave of Tibet.

> As for the Tathāgata's subtle and wondrous body,
> His retinue, his life-span,
> His realm, and his title:
> May we all likewise attain these.
> By the slight merit of praising and honoring the World-Honored One,
> May we, wherever we may be,
> See all illness, māras, poverty, and strife entirely eliminated,
> And may goodness and auspiciousness increase; we pray that all be granted.
> May all the Buddhas appear in the world,
> May the holy teaching shine bright as sunlight,
> May those who uphold the teaching be harmonious as brothers,
> And may the right teaching abide eternally in auspiciousness.

The above twelve lines are the reciter's prayer, to be recited following the preceding praise.
