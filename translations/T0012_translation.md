# 大集法門經
## Sūtra of the Great Assembly of Dharma Teachings

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0012

Translated into Chinese by Dānapāla (Shī Hù), Song dynasty

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the World-Honored One was traveling and arrived at the city of the Mallas, together with a community of bhikṣus in assembly. At that time, in that city there was a faithful upāsaka, also named Malla. Within the city he had newly built a rest house, adorned with various decorations, clean and spacious. This house had never before been used by any śramaṇa or brāhmaṇa to stay within. Then the upāsaka Malla, hearing that the Buddha, the World-Honored One, had come traveling there with the community of bhikṣus, felt joy arise in his heart and went to the Buddha's presence. Having arrived, he bowed his head to the World-Honored One's feet. After paying homage, he joined his palms, withdrew to one side, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I am an upāsaka named Malla. I have given rise to pure faith in the World-Honored One. In this city I have newly built a rest house that is clean and spacious. This house has never before been used by any śramaṇa or brāhmaṇa to stay within. I now invite the Buddha and the community of bhikṣus to reside in my rest house. May the Buddha, the World-Honored One, out of compassion for me, accept my invitation."

At that time, the World-Honored One silently accepted. The upāsaka Malla, knowing the Buddha had silently accepted his invitation, thereupon bowed his head to the World-Honored One's feet, circumambulated him three times to the right, and departed from the Buddha's assembly. He returned to that rest house, further adorned it, and throughout the entire building, sprinkled the ground with fragrant water, making everything inside and out thoroughly clean. Then the upāsaka Malla, having thus cleaned and adorned the rest house he had built, returned to the Buddha's presence. Having arrived, he again paid homage at the World-Honored One's feet and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The rest house that was built has been sprinkled throughout with fragrant water, and it is clean inside and out. May the Buddha, the World-Honored One, and the community of bhikṣus proceed to reside in that rest house. Now is the proper time."

At that time, the World-Honored One, surrounded respectfully by the great community of bhikṣus, proceeded to the new rest house built by the upāsaka Malla. Having arrived at the rest house, the Buddha first washed his feet, then entered the building. Once inside, he looked about in all directions, observing all around, and then sat down serenely in the center of the hall. The bhikṣus also each washed their feet and entered in order. Having paid homage at the Buddha's feet, they sat down in order behind the Buddha. The upāsaka Malla entered the hall last, paid homage at the World-Honored One's feet, joined his palms reverently, bowed to all the bhikṣus in turn, and then sat to one side before the Buddha. Then the World-Honored One consoled and encouraged the upāsaka Malla in various ways, and taught him the essentials of the Dharma as was fitting, instructing, benefiting, and gladdening him. The upāsaka Malla, hearing the Dharma, was filled with joy and gave rise to pure faith. Thus the World-Honored One taught the Dharma to the upāsaka as was fitting, instructing, benefiting, and gladdening him. As much of the night had passed, the Buddha then said: "Malla! The night is far spent; you should know the time."

At that time, the upāsaka Malla, having heard the Buddha's words, rose from his seat, paid homage at the World-Honored One's feet, joined his palms reverently, bowed to all the bhikṣus in turn, circumambulated the Buddha three times, and departed from the assembly.

Then the World-Honored One, seeing that the upāsaka Malla had not long departed from the assembly, addressed the venerable Śāriputra: "These śrāvaka bhikṣus of mine have left behind sleep; they are all a great assembly free from defilement and pure. If any bhikṣu wishes to expound the Dharma, let him teach as is fitting, bringing benefit as he is able, and should not cease." The venerable Śāriputra received the instruction and remained.

Then the World-Honored One folded his saṃghāṭī robe into four layers, lay down on the lion's couch with his right side touching the ground in auspicious repose, and with his feet placed one upon the other, rested. The Buddha had not been resting long when, at another place, the heterodox Nirgrantha Jñātaputra and his followers disparaged the śrāvaka bhikṣus, seeking to cause harm, wishing to provoke quarrels, uttering unlawful words and various slanders, saying: "The Dharma that we know, those śrāvakas cannot understand. Their teachings we truly know. Wrong conduct is yours; right conduct is ours. Benefit is ours; no benefit is yours. What you teach, if the former words happen to be right, the latter words are then wrong; and if the latter words happen to be right, the former words are again wrong. You cannot make the great lion's roar to teach the Dharma for the benefit of others." The Nirgrantha Jñātaputra and his followers, wishing to provoke a great quarrel, when uttering such slanderous words, looked at each other with grim and dreadful expressions. They further said: "The śrāvaka bhikṣus, in their appearance and deportment, are not tranquil. They cannot abandon desire, have not attained liberation, cannot see the Dharma, cannot properly know the path of renunciation, and cannot realize the holy fruit toward which they aspire. The teachings they practice were not taught by the Samyaksaṃbuddha." They uttered such slanderous words and provoked quarrels.

Then the venerable Śāriputra, learning of this matter, thought to himself: "The great Teacher, the Tathāgata, has only recently lain down to rest; I should not disturb the World-Honored One with this matter." Having thought thus, he addressed the bhikṣus: "You should know that at another place, the heterodox Nirgrantha Jñātaputra and his followers have disparaged the śrāvaka bhikṣus, seeking to cause harm, wishing to provoke quarrels, uttering unlawful words and various slanders. They say: 'The Dharma that we know, the śrāvakas cannot understand. Their teachings we truly know. Wrong conduct is yours; right conduct is ours. Benefit is ours; no benefit is yours. What you teach, if the former words happen to be right, the latter words are then wrong; and if the latter words happen to be right, the former words are again wrong. You cannot make the great lion's roar to teach the Dharma for the benefit of others.' Bhikṣus! The Nirgrantha Jñātaputra and his followers, wishing to provoke a great quarrel, when uttering such slanderous words, look at each other with grim and dreadful expressions. They further say: 'The śrāvaka bhikṣus, in their appearance and deportment, are not tranquil. They cannot abandon desire, have not attained liberation, cannot see the Dharma, cannot properly know the path of renunciation, and cannot realize the holy fruit toward which they aspire. The teachings they practice were not taught by the Samyaksaṃbuddha.' They utter such slanderous words and provoke quarrels. Bhikṣus! You should now know that we śrāvakas of this great assembly are all those whose minds are free from defilement and pure, who have directly realized the dharmas, who properly understand the paths of renunciation, and who have each attained the holy fruits they have realized. The teachings that we śrāvakas practice are each and every one personally taught by the great Teacher, the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Samyaksaṃbuddha, each and every one true and without falsehood. Bhikṣus! You should know that the Buddha has taught the sūtras, geyas, vyākaraṇas, gāthās, itivṛttakas, jātakas, nidānas, vaipulyas, and adbhutadharmas. All such teachings the Buddha, with a compassionate heart, has extensively taught to sentient beings in accordance with reason, causing sentient beings to practice accordingly, to cultivate the holy life, and to benefit and bring peace to the world of devas and humans.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! You should know one dharma taught by the Buddha: all sentient beings depend upon food for their subsistence. This is the one dharma. Such teachings the Buddha, with a compassionate heart, has extensively taught to sentient beings in accordance with reason, causing sentient beings to practice accordingly, to cultivate the holy life, and to benefit and bring peace to the world of devas and humans.

"Furthermore, two dharmas are taught by the Buddha: the first is name, the second is form. Such teachings the Buddha, with a compassionate heart, has extensively taught to sentient beings in accordance with reason, causing sentient beings to practice accordingly, to cultivate the holy life, and to benefit and bring peace to the world of devas and humans.

"Furthermore, three kinds of karma are taught by the Buddha: first, bodily karma; second, verbal karma; third, mental karma. These three kinds of karma are further of two types: wholesome and unwholesome. What is wholesome? Wholesome conduct of body, wholesome conduct of speech, and wholesome conduct of mind. What is unwholesome? Unwholesome conduct of body, unwholesome conduct of speech, and unwholesome conduct of mind.

"Furthermore, three unwholesome kinds of thought are taught by the Buddha: thought of sensual desire, thought of ill-will, and thought of harmfulness.

"Furthermore, three wholesome kinds of thought are taught by the Buddha: thought of renouncing desire, thought free from ill-will, and thought of non-harmfulness.

"Furthermore, three unwholesome roots are taught by the Buddha: the unwholesome root of desire, the unwholesome root of hatred, and the unwholesome root of delusion.

"Furthermore, three wholesome roots are taught by the Buddha: the wholesome root of non-desire, the wholesome root of non-hatred, and the wholesome root of non-delusion.

"Furthermore, three outflows are taught by the Buddha: the outflow of sensual desire, the outflow of existence, and the outflow of ignorance.

"Furthermore, three searches are taught by the Buddha: the search for sensual desire, the search for existence, and the search for the holy life.

"Furthermore, three kinds of craving are taught by the Buddha: craving for the desire realm, craving for the form realm, and craving for the formless realm.

"Furthermore, three realms are taught by the Buddha: the desire realm, the form realm, and the formless realm.

"Furthermore, three unwholesome elements are taught by the Buddha: the element of lustful desire, the element of ill-will, and the element of harmfulness.

"Furthermore, three wholesome elements are taught by the Buddha: the element of non-desire, the element of non-ill-will, and the element of non-harmfulness.

"Furthermore, three existences are taught by the Buddha: existence in the desire realm, existence in the form realm, and existence in the formless realm.

"Furthermore, three groups are taught by the Buddha: the group of wrong certainty, the group of right certainty, and the group of the undetermined.

"Furthermore, three feelings are taught by the Buddha: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and feeling that is neither painful nor pleasant.

"Furthermore, three kinds of suffering are taught by the Buddha: the suffering of conditioned existence, the suffering of suffering, and the suffering of change.

"Furthermore, three kinds of rebirth through desire are taught by the Buddha: rebirth with desire for what is presently at hand, rebirth with desire for what is created for enjoyment, and rebirth with desire over that created by others at will.

"Furthermore, three kinds of rebirth in happiness are taught by the Buddha. There are beings who, having been born, experience happiness, as among a certain class of humans: this is called the first kind of rebirth in happiness. There are further beings who long experience joy and happiness, this happiness being vast, delightful, and blissful, as in the Ābhāsvara heaven: this is called the second kind of rebirth in happiness. There are further beings who, for their entire lifespan, experience happiness to the full, as in the Śubhakṛtsna heaven: this is called the third kind of rebirth in happiness.

"Furthermore, three kinds of meritorious activities accomplished through wisdom-practice are taught by the Buddha: the meritorious activity of generosity adorned with the accomplishment of wisdom-practice, the meritorious activity of moral discipline adorned with the accomplishment of wisdom-practice, and the meritorious activity of meditative absorption adorned with the accomplishment of wisdom-practice.

"Furthermore, three samādhis are taught by the Buddha: samādhi with initial and sustained application, samādhi without initial application but with sustained application only, and samādhi without either initial or sustained application. There are further three samādhis taught by the Buddha: the samādhi of emptiness liberation, the samādhi of wishlessness liberation, and the samādhi of signlessness liberation.

"Furthermore, three abodes are taught by the Buddha: the divine abode, the brahma-abode, and the noble abode.

"Furthermore, three faculties are taught by the Buddha: the faculty of 'I shall come to know what is not yet known,' the faculty of higher knowledge, and the faculty of one who has fully known.

"Furthermore, three predominances are taught by the Buddha: the predominance of the world, the predominance of the Dharma, and the predominance of the self.

"Furthermore, three Buddhas are taught by the Buddha: the Buddhas of the past, the Buddhas of the future, and the Buddhas of the present.

"Furthermore, three bases of discourse are taught by the Buddha: discourse regarding the past, discourse regarding the future, and discourse regarding the present.

"Furthermore, three eyes are taught by the Buddha: the fleshly eye, the divine eye, and the wisdom eye.

"Furthermore, three knowledges are taught by the Buddha: the knowledge and insight of recollecting past lives, the knowledge and insight of the arising and passing away of beings, and the knowledge and insight of the destruction of the outflows.

"Furthermore, three demonstrations are taught by the Buddha: the demonstration of supernatural power, the demonstration of teaching the Dharma, and the demonstration of instruction.

"Furthermore, three impurities are taught by the Buddha: impurity of body, impurity of speech, and impurity of mind.

"Furthermore, three purities are taught by the Buddha: purity of body, purity of speech, and purity of mind.

"Furthermore, three trainings are taught by the Buddha: the training in moral discipline, the training in concentration, and the training in wisdom.

"Furthermore, three categories are taught by the Buddha: the category of moral discipline, the category of concentration, and the category of wisdom.

"Furthermore, three fires are taught by the Buddha: the fire of desire, the fire of hatred, and the fire of delusion.

"Furthermore, three stages are taught by the Buddha: the stage of arising, the stage of maturation, and the stage of the Dharma. Such teachings the Buddha, with a compassionate heart, has extensively taught to sentient beings in accordance with reason, causing sentient beings to practice accordingly, to cultivate the holy life, and to benefit and bring peace to the world of devas and humans.

"Furthermore, the four foundations of mindfulness as contemplation are taught by the Buddha: contemplating the body as impure, without giving rise to wrong conceptions, subduing ignorance, and becoming free from afflicted feelings; contemplating feelings as suffering, contemplating the mind as arising and passing away, and skillfully contemplating all dharmas, likewise in the same way.

"Furthermore, the four right efforts are taught by the Buddha: regarding unwholesome dharmas that have already arisen, one rouses diligent effort, focuses the mind and directs attention, and completely abandons them; regarding unwholesome dharmas that have not yet arisen, one rouses diligent effort, focuses the mind and directs attention, and prevents them from arising; regarding wholesome dharmas that have not yet arisen, one rouses diligent effort, focuses the mind and directs attention, and causes them to arise; regarding wholesome dharmas that have already arisen, one rouses diligent effort, focuses the mind and directs attention, and causes them all to increase and reach completion. These are called the four right efforts.

"Furthermore, the four bases of spiritual power are taught by the Buddha: the basis of spiritual power endowed with the samādhi of aspiration and the effort of abandoning; the basis of spiritual power endowed with the samādhi of energy and the effort of abandoning; the basis of spiritual power endowed with the samādhi of mind and the effort of abandoning; the basis of spiritual power endowed with the samādhi of investigation and the effort of abandoning.

"Furthermore, the four meditative absorptions are taught by the Buddha. If a bhikṣu has already separated from desires and unwholesome dharmas, with initial and sustained application, this is called the first absorption: the absorption of joy and happiness born of separation. If further a bhikṣu stills initial and sustained application, with inner clarity of mind, abiding in a single thought, without initial or sustained application, this is called the second absorption: the absorption of joy and happiness born of concentration. If further a bhikṣu does not cling to joy and abides in the practice of equanimity, with the body attaining light ease and subtle happiness, this is called the third absorption: the absorption of subtle happiness free from joy. If further a bhikṣu abandons the thought of pleasure and is also without the thought of pain, without delight of mind and without distress of mind, without suffering and without pleasure, this is called the fourth absorption: the absorption of purity through equanimity and mindfulness. Such are the four meditative absorptions.

"Furthermore, the four immeasurables are taught by the Buddha. If a bhikṣu gives rise to a mind of loving-kindness, first practicing loving-kindness toward the east, then likewise toward the south, west, and north, the four intermediate directions, above, and below, practicing loving-kindness. That mind of loving-kindness extends to all places, all worlds, and all kinds of beings, vast and immeasurable, without boundary or limit. This is called the immeasurable of loving-kindness. Compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity are likewise. These are called the four immeasurables.

"Furthermore, the four formless attainments are taught by the Buddha. If a bhikṣu separates from all form, without obstruction or hindrance and without mental engagement, contemplates the boundlessness of space: this contemplative aspect is called the attainment of the sphere of boundless space. Further separating from the sphere of space and no longer contemplating it, but contemplating only the boundlessness of consciousness: this contemplative aspect is called the attainment of the sphere of boundless consciousness. Further separating from the sphere of consciousness and no longer contemplating it, but contemplating that there is nothing whatsoever: this contemplative aspect is called the attainment of the sphere of nothingness. Further separating from the aspect of the sphere of nothingness: this is called the attainment of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Such are the four formless attainments.

"Furthermore, four knowledges are taught by the Buddha: knowledge of dharmas, knowledge of non-arising, inferential knowledge, and knowledge of others' minds.

"Furthermore, four steadfast abidances are taught by the Buddha: abiding in all practices, abiding in the practice of equanimity, abiding in the practice of tranquility, and abiding in the practice of wisdom.

"Furthermore, the four noble truths are taught by the Buddha: the noble truth of suffering, the noble truth of the origin of suffering, the noble truth of the cessation of suffering, and the noble truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering.

"Furthermore, four kinds of purity in giving are taught by the Buddha. There is giving in which the giver is pure but not the recipient. There is giving in which the recipient is pure but not the giver. There is giving in which neither the giver nor the recipient is pure, but that which is given is pure. There is giving in which both the giver and the recipient are pure.

"Furthermore, four kinds of birth are taught by the Buddha: birth from a womb, birth from an egg, birth from moisture, and spontaneous birth.

"Furthermore, four kinds of matters regarding the mother's womb are taught by the Buddha. There are those who can understand the matter of entering the mother's womb, the matter of abiding in the mother's womb, and the matter of emerging from the mother's womb: this is the first matter of the womb. There are those who can understand the matter of entering the mother's womb and the matter of abiding in the mother's womb, but cannot understand the matter of emerging from the mother's womb: this is the second matter of the womb. There are those who can understand the matter of entering the mother's womb but cannot understand the matter of abiding in the mother's womb or the matter of emerging from the mother's womb: this is the third matter of the womb. There are those who cannot understand the matter of entering the mother's womb, the matter of abiding in the mother's womb, or the matter of emerging from the mother's womb: this is the fourth matter of the womb. Such are the four kinds of matters regarding the mother's womb.

"Furthermore, four stations of consciousness are taught by the Buddha. Form arises, form conditions, form abides, and joy and its activity expand and increase: this is a station of consciousness. Feeling arises, feeling conditions, feeling abides, and joy and its activity expand and increase: this is a station of consciousness. Perception arises, perception conditions, perception abides, and joy and its activity expand and increase: this is a station of consciousness. Formations arise, formations condition, formations abide, and joy and its activity expand and increase: this is a station of consciousness. Such are the four stations of consciousness.

"Furthermore, four Dharma phrases are taught by the Buddha: the Dharma phrase of supernatural power, the Dharma phrase of freedom from anger, the Dharma phrase of equanimity, and the Dharma phrase of equanimous samādhi.

"Furthermore, four śramaṇa dharmas are taught by the Buddha. If there is present happiness, this results in suffering as its consequence. If there is present suffering, this results in suffering as its consequence. If there is present suffering, this results in happiness as its consequence. If there is present happiness, this results in happiness as its consequence. These are the four śramaṇa dharmas.

"Furthermore, four approaches are taught by the Buddha: without patience, with patience, with discipline, and with tranquility.

"Furthermore, four paths of spiritual penetration are taught by the Buddha: the painful and slow path of spiritual penetration, the painful and swift path of spiritual penetration, the pleasant and slow path of spiritual penetration, and the pleasant and swift path of spiritual penetration.

"Furthermore, four aspects of the stream-enterer are taught by the Buddha. There is one type of stream-enterer who has unshakable faith in the Buddha, the Tathāgata, who does not revile śramaṇas, brāhmaṇas, devas, Māra, or Brahmā, and who understands worldly dharmas. There is one type of stream-enterer whose mind has become pure, who has realized the Buddha's Dharma, who possesses right view and right practice, and who understands the dharmas of one's own cultivation. There is one type of stream-enterer who gives rise to joy and delight, and upon seeing householders and renunciants who keep the pure precepts, gives rise to respect. There is one type of stream-enterer who personally cultivates the pure precepts completely and without impairment, with sharp wisdom, well-composed and tranquil. Such are the four aspects of the stream-enterer.

"Furthermore, four fruits of the śramaṇa are taught by the Buddha: the fruit of the Srota-āpanna, the fruit of the Sakṛdāgāmin, the fruit of the Anāgāmin, and the fruit of the Arhat.

"Furthermore, four kinds of clinging are taught by the Buddha: clinging to sensual desire, clinging to views, clinging to rules and rituals, and clinging to the doctrine of a self.

"Furthermore, four samādhi perceptions are taught by the Buddha. There is the turning of attaining the practice of happiness through seeing dharmas: this is a samādhi perception. There is the turning of knowledge and vision: this is a samādhi perception. There is the turning of wisdom and discernment: this is a samādhi perception. There is the turning of the body attaining the exhaustion of the outflows: this is a samādhi perception.

"Furthermore, four powers are taught by the Buddha: the power of wisdom, the power of energy, the power of being without fault, and the power of gathering.

"Furthermore, four types of persons are taught by the Buddha. There is a person who says: 'I can practice, I keep the precepts, I am in accord with the Dharma, but others cannot practice, others do not keep the precepts, others are not in accord with the Dharma.' There is a person who says: 'Others can practice, others keep the precepts, others are in accord with the Dharma, but I cannot practice, I do not keep the precepts, I am not in accord with the Dharma.' There is a person who says: 'I can practice and others can also practice; I keep the precepts and others also keep the precepts; I am in accord with the Dharma and others are also in accord with the Dharma.' There is a person who says: 'I cannot practice and others also cannot practice; I do not keep the precepts and others also do not keep the precepts; I am not in accord with the Dharma and others are also not in accord with the Dharma.' Such are the four types of persons.

"Furthermore, four matters of communal conduct are taught by the Buddha: sharing the same dwelling place with the community, sharing the same food and drink with the community, sharing the same confession with the community, and sharing the same requisites with the community.

"Furthermore, four great wheels are taught by the Buddha: skillfully teaching the sublime Dharma, relying upon upright persons, aspiring with an equanimous mind, and cultivating prior wisdom-practice.

"Furthermore, four means of attraction are taught by the Buddha: giving, loving speech, beneficial action, and working together.

"Furthermore, four unhindered knowledges are taught by the Buddha: unhindered knowledge of meaning, unhindered knowledge of dharmas, unhindered knowledge of eloquence, and unhindered knowledge of analytical ability.

"Furthermore, four floods are taught by the Buddha: the flood of sensual desire, the flood of existence, the flood of views, and the flood of ignorance.

"Furthermore, four yokes are taught by the Buddha: the yoke of sensual desire, the yoke of existence, the yoke of views, and the yoke of ignorance.

"Furthermore, four bodily bonds are taught by the Buddha: the bodily bond of ignorance, the bodily bond of hatred, the bodily bond of attachment to rules and rituals, and the bodily bond of clinging to all things.

"Furthermore, four arisings of craving are taught by the Buddha. There are bhikṣus who, because of robes, give rise to craving; and because craving arises, attachment is born. There are bhikṣus who, because of food and drink, give rise to craving; and because craving arises, attachment is born. There are bhikṣus who, because of bedding and seats, give rise to craving; and because craving arises, attachment is born. There are bhikṣus who, because of various requisites, give rise to craving; and because craving arises, attachment is born. These are the four arisings of craving.

"Furthermore, four kinds of nutriment are taught by the Buddha: material food, nutriment of contact, nutriment of volition, and nutriment of consciousness.

"Furthermore, four things the Tathāgata need not guard are taught by the Buddha. The Tathāgata need not guard bodily karma, for his body is free from all faults. The Tathāgata need not guard verbal karma, for his speech is free from all faults. The Tathāgata need not guard mental karma, for his mind is free from all faults. The Tathāgata need not guard his lifespan, for his life suffers no diminishment.

"Furthermore, four inversions are taught by the Buddha. Taking the impermanent as permanent gives rise to the inversion of perception, the inversion of mind, and the inversion of view. Taking suffering as happiness gives rise to the inversions of perception, mind, and view. Taking non-self as self gives rise to the inversions of perception, mind, and view. Taking the impure as pure gives rise to the inversions of perception, mind, and view. Such are the four inversions.

"Furthermore, four kinds of unwholesome speech are taught by the Buddha: false speech, frivolous speech, divisive speech, and harsh speech.

"Furthermore, four kinds of wholesome speech are taught by the Buddha: truthful speech, straightforward speech, non-divisive speech, and speech in accord with the Dharma.

"Furthermore, four non-noble practices are taught by the Buddha: not having seen, one claims to have seen; not having heard, one claims to have heard; having lost mindfulness, one claims to have been mindful; not knowing, one claims to know.

"Furthermore, four noble practices are taught by the Buddha: having truly seen, one says one has seen; having truly heard, one says one has heard; not having lost mindfulness, one says one has been mindful; truly knowing, one says one knows.

"Furthermore, four kinds of response are taught by the Buddha: the definitive response, the analytical response, the counter-question response, and the silent response. Such teachings the Buddha, with a compassionate heart, has extensively taught to sentient beings in accordance with reason, causing sentient beings to practice accordingly, to cultivate the holy life, and to benefit and bring peace to the world of devas and humans.

## Fascicle 2

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! You should know that the five aggregates of clinging are taught by the Buddha: the aggregate of clinging to form, the aggregate of clinging to feeling, the aggregate of clinging to perception, the aggregate of clinging to formations, and the aggregate of clinging to consciousness.

"Furthermore, five desires are taught by the Buddha. The eye sees forms and the mind delights in desiring them; with a mind of delightful desire, one clings to the sense object of form. The ear hearing sounds, the nose smelling scents, the tongue discerning tastes, and the body feeling tactile sensations are likewise.

"Furthermore, five hindrances are taught by the Buddha: the hindrance of sensual desire, the hindrance of ill-will, the hindrance of sloth and torpor, the hindrance of remorse, and the hindrance of doubt.

"Furthermore, five fetters pertaining to the lower portion of afflictions are taught by the Buddha: the fetter of sensual desire, the fetter of ill-will, the fetter of self-identity view, the fetter of attachment to rules and rituals, and the fetter of doubt.

"Furthermore, five kinds of stinginess are taught by the Buddha: stinginess with food and drink, stinginess with good deeds, stinginess with material gains, stinginess with physical appearance, and stinginess with the Dharma.

"Furthermore, five faculties of feeling are taught by the Buddha: the faculty of pleasant feeling, the faculty of painful feeling, the faculty of joy, the faculty of grief, and the faculty of equanimity.

"Furthermore, five dominant faculties are taught by the Buddha: the faculty of faith, the faculty of energy, the faculty of mindfulness, the faculty of concentration, and the faculty of wisdom.

"Furthermore, five powers are taught by the Buddha: the power of faith, the power of energy, the power of mindfulness, the power of concentration, and the power of wisdom.

"Furthermore, five training powers are taught by the Buddha: the training power of faith, the training power of energy, the training power of mindfulness, the training power of concentration, and the training power of wisdom.

"Furthermore, five elements of renunciation are taught by the Buddha. There are bhikṣus of great learning who cannot see suffering; their minds accommodate it. They have not yet separated from desire and, following objects of desire, give rise to desiring minds, neither retreating nor subsiding, unable to attain liberation. Yet by this very condition, they later come to observe objects of desire and give rise to a mind of renouncing desire. The desiring mind retreats and subsides; they take delight in liberation, properly cultivate right practice, and attain liberation of mind. When the liberated mind arises, they are able to distance themselves from incompatible dharmas and abide in a mind free from desire. For this reason, desire itself serves as the condition that gives rise to the uncontaminated. There are bhikṣus of great learning who cannot see suffering; their minds accommodate it. They have not yet separated from ill-will and, toward disagreeable objects, give rise to minds of ill-will, neither retreating nor subsiding, unable to attain liberation. Yet by this very condition, they later come to observe disagreeable objects and give rise to a mind free from ill-will. The mind of ill-will retreats and subsides; they take delight in liberation, properly cultivate right practice, and attain liberation of mind. When the liberated mind arises, they are able to distance themselves from incompatible dharmas and abide in a mind free from ill-will. For this reason, ill-will itself serves as the condition that gives rise to the uncontaminated. There are bhikṣus of great learning who cannot see suffering; their minds accommodate it. They have not yet separated from harmfulness and, toward displeasing objects, give rise to minds of harmfulness, neither retreating nor subsiding, unable to attain liberation. Yet by this very condition, they later come to observe displeasing objects and give rise to a mind of non-harmfulness. The mind of harmfulness retreats and subsides; they take delight in liberation, properly cultivate right practice, and attain liberation of mind. They have not yet separated from form and, toward objects of form, give rise to minds attached to form, neither retreating nor subsiding, unable to attain liberation. Yet by this very condition, they later come to observe those realms of form and give rise to a mind free from attachment to form. The mind attached to form retreats and subsides; they take delight in liberation, properly cultivate right practice, and attain liberation of mind. When the liberated mind arises, they are able to distance themselves from incompatible dharmas and abide in a mind free from attachment to form. For this reason, form itself serves as the condition that gives rise to the uncontaminated. There are bhikṣus of great learning who cannot see suffering; their minds accommodate it. They cling to the existence of a self, have not separated from the mark of a self, and regarding the existing self, give rise to the perception of its reality, neither retreating nor subsiding, unable to attain liberation. Yet by this very condition, they later come to contemplate that the existing self must be extinguished, and give rise to the perception of the extinction of the self. The clinging mind retreats and subsides; they take delight in liberation, properly cultivate right practice, and attain liberation of mind. When the liberated mind arises, they are able to distance themselves from incompatible dharmas and abide in the perception of the extinction of the self. For this reason, the existing self serves as the condition that gives rise to the uncontaminated. Such are the five elements of renunciation.

"Furthermore, five places of liberation are taught by the Buddha. Following the arising of that mind, though they are not yet able to listen broadly, receive, and remember, they are nevertheless able to understand one dharma. Following their understanding of one dharma, they comprehend one meaning. If they do not comprehend its meaning, they cannot give rise to joy toward the Dharma. "Furthermore, five destinations are taught by the Buddha: the hells, the hungry ghosts, the animals, humans, and the devas.

"Furthermore, six kinds of remedial elements of renunciation are taught by the Buddha. There are bhikṣus who say: 'I cultivate the contemplation of the liberation of the mind through loving-kindness. At that time, the World-Honored One, knowing that the venerable Śāriputra had taught the Dharma to the bhikṣus as was fitting, rose serenely from his repose and addressed the venerable Śāriputra: "Excellent! Excellent! Śāriputra! What you have taught is what the Buddha teaches. This Dharma is named the 'Great Assembly of Dharma Teachings.' In the final age to come, it can bring great benefit to sentient beings. In the final age, those bhikṣus of mine who cultivate the holy life should receive, uphold, read, and recite this Great Assembly of Dharma Teachings, and propagate and circulate it widely. Furthermore, Śāriputra! In the final age to come, if a person receives and upholds this Great Assembly of Dharma Teachings, that person shall be, within the Dharma of the Buddhas to come, a faithful son of good family who deeply delights in the Buddha's teaching, who is broadly and greatly illuminated, and whose mind is filled with great joy."

At that time, the bhikṣus, having heard the Buddha praise this Great Assembly of Dharma Teachings, joyfully accepted it with faith, paid homage to the Buddha, and withdrew.
