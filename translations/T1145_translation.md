# 虛空藏菩薩能滿諸願最勝心陀羅尼求聞持法
## Method of Seeking Retentive Memory through the Supreme Heart Dhāraṇī of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva Who Fulfills All Wishes

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1145

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Śubhakarasiṃha (善無畏).

---

Extracted from the Vajraśekhara Sūtra, Chapter on the Accomplishment of All Meanings.

At that time the Bhagavān entered the samādhi of the equality-nature of all the pāramitās. Rising from samādhi, he then spoke this Supreme Heart Dhāraṇī of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva Who Fulfills All Wishes:

*Namo ākāśagarbhāya oṃ ari kamāri mūri svāhā*

The Bhagavān said: "This dhāraṇī has been spoken in common by all buddhas of the past and present. If one can constantly recite this dhāraṇī, all the karmic obstacles from beginningless time, including the five unremitting offenses, are all eliminated. One will constantly receive the protective care of all buddhas and bodhisattvas. Until one attains buddhahood, in every place of birth, Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva will always follow and guard one, causing sentient beings to always delight in seeing one, and whatever wholesome wishes one has will all be fulfilled. All suffering and affliction will be entirely removed. One will always be reborn among devas and humans and never fall into evil destinies. In every life one will always recall past lives. Even without performing the special ritual, merely by constantly reciting, one obtains blessings such as these.

"If one wishes to perform the ritual and uphold this dhāraṇī to seek retentive memory, one should on silk, white cotton cloth, or a clean wooden board first paint a full moon, and within it paint an image of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva, measuring at minimum not less than one cubit, or larger as one's means allow. The bodhisattva and the full moon should be proportionate in size. The body is golden in color. He sits in half-lotus posture upon a jeweled lotus, with the right leg over the left, his countenance supremely wonderful, with a joyful and delighted expression. Upon the jeweled crown are images of five buddhas, seated cross-legged. The bodhisattva's left hand holds a white lotus with a slight reddish hue. Upon the lotus pedestal is a wish-fulfilling jewel of vaiḍūrya color, emitting yellow radiant flames. The right hand forms the wish-granting mudrā, with five fingers hanging down and the palm facing outward: this is the sign of the wish-granting mudrā.

"When the painting is complete, at a secluded and quiet place, whether in a clean room, a pagoda temple, a mountaintop, or beneath a tree, place the image wherever one chooses, facing due west or alternatively facing north, and cover it with a clean cloth. Separately make a square wooden maṇḍala, at minimum one cubit, or larger as one's means allow. The altar should have four legs beneath it, or be assembled with joints, and the upper surface should be exactly four finger-widths above the ground. If the board is made of sandalwood or aloeswood, this is most excellent; otherwise, cypress or other fragrant wood may also be used. When properly made, place it before the image.

"Next one should prepare five kinds of offerings: perfume, flowers, incense, food and drink, and lamplight. For perfume, grind white sandalwood. For flowers, use whatever seasonal medicinal herbs produce them. If there are no seasonal flowers, one may use polished rice, roasted buckwheat, or leaves of citrus or cypress, or use cloves in place of flowers. For incense, use aloeswood, sandalwood, or camphor as appropriate. Food should exclude pungent items and must always be fresh and pure. For lamps, use cow's ghee; oil is also permissible.

"When about to prepare these items, one must wash hands and face at dawn, maintaining purity in accordance with the dharma. When all preparations are complete, place them beside the altar, then go outside. Wash the hands again with clean water, then form the mudrā, cupping clean water in the palm, and recite the dhāraṇī three times, then drink it. The mudrā is formed as follows: first extend the right hand palm-up with all five fingers spread, then bend the index finger to touch the thumb as if pinching incense. This is the mudrā of the wish-fulfilling jewel of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva that accomplishes all things. Then again with this mudrā cup water as before, recite the dhāraṇī three times, and sprinkle it on the crown and body, thereby purifying everything inside and out.

"Next proceed to the image, bow with utmost sincerity, and sit facing the bodhisattva in half-lotus posture. Lift the cloth covering the image. Then one must form the body-protection mudrā. The mudrā is formed as follows: first raise the right hand, then touch the index finger to the thumb as if pinching incense, with the second joint of the index finger bent and the first joint held perfectly straight, and only then is the mudrā properly formed. Having properly formed this mudrā, place it upon the crown and recite the dhāraṇī once, then place it on the right shoulder and recite once more, then likewise for the left shoulder, heart, and throat. Having performed this body-protection method, all buddhas and Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva will gather in and protect this person. All karmic obstacles are immediately eliminated, body and mind are purified, merit and wisdom increase, and all māras and vināyakas can find no advantage.

"Again form the previous mudrā to cup clean water, recite the dhāraṇī once, and sprinkle it on the perfume and other offering items, the altar, and the ground near the altar. Again form the body-protection mudrā as before, place it upon the perfume, and recite the dhāraṇī once. For the flowers, incense, and so on up to the wooden altar, perform the same for each. Having done this, the flowers, incense, and other items become purified.

"Again form the body-protection mudrā and turn it clockwise three times, also pointing above and below, moving only the mudrā while the body remains still. Recite the dhāraṇī seven times, and according to one's own mind, near or far, seal the boundaries of the ten directions.

"Next close the eyes and contemplate: the true body of Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva is identical to this image, without any difference. Again using the body-protection mudrā, with intention invite Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva by reciting the dhāraṇī twenty-five times, then lift the thumb inward to beckon once, keeping the index finger as before. Again form this mudrā, recite the dhāraṇī three times, and with the lotus on the painting as a seat, visualize the bodhisattva coming to sit upon this lotus. Then open the eyes, and upon seeing the bodhisattva, generate a mind of wonder and understand it as the true body. Recite again three times with the mudrā as before, and think: 'Now the bodhisattva has come to this place. This is through the power of the dhāraṇī, not through my own ability. I beseech the honored one to remain here for a while.'

"Next take perfume, recite the dhāraṇī once, and apply it to the altar. Next take flowers, also recite once, and scatter them upon the altar. For the incense, food and drink, and lamplight, take each in turn, reciting once for each, hold them in offering, and place them beside the altar. Then make this thought: 'All buddhas and bodhisattvas, through the fragrant cultivation of merit and wisdom, with banners, canopies, pure fragrant flowers, and all manner of jeweled adornments, all splendidly arrayed.' Again form the mudrā, recite the dhāraṇī once, and as before visualize all offering items fully accomplished, thereby making offerings to all tathāgatas and all bodhisattvas. This mental offering is the supreme among offerings. If one cannot obtain perfume and the other physical offerings, one may simply perform this second method of mental offering, and the dharma will likewise be accomplished.

"Then with the mudrā, count the beads, reciting the dhāraṇī and carefully recording the number of recitations. While reciting, close the eyes and visualize a full moon upon the bodhisattva's heart. The syllables of the dhāraṇī being recited appear within the full moon, all in golden color. The syllables then flow out from the full moon and pour into the practitioner's crown, then exit from the mouth and enter the bodhisattva's feet, as if one were speaking one's own words of petition. From beneath the bodhisattva's feet, without the dhāraṇī recitation stopping, the visualized syllables circulate back and forth, continuing without interruption, turning like a wheel. When body and mind grow weary, one should stop, gaze with utmost sincerity, sit, and bow. Close the eyes again and contemplate the full-moon bodhisattva with utmost clarity, then further exert the mind to cause it to gradually expand and pervade the dharmadhātu. Then gradually contract the visualization, and at the very end, when it has returned to its original size, only then emerge from contemplation.

"Again form the previous mudrā, recite the dhāraṇī three times, and lift the thumb to dispatch the bodhisattva, thinking: 'I beseech you to bestow compassionate joy. When next the dharma ceremony is held, may you graciously descend again.'

"Thus recite the dhāraṇī, and according to one's ability, practice either once or twice daily. From beginning to end, each session should follow the same procedure as the first day, and the number of recitations should also be the same as the first session, neither increased nor decreased. The cumulative total from beginning to end should reach one million recitations, at which point the count is complete. There is no time limit, but during the process there must be no interruption.

"Afterward, at the time of a solar or lunar eclipse, according to one's means, give alms of food, drink, and material goods as offerings to the Three Treasures. Then move the bodhisattva image and altar to an open-air clean place. Take one ounce of cow's ghee and place it in a vessel of worked copper. Also take seven leaves and one branch from a milky tree and place them beside the altar. Double the usual quantities of flowers, incense, and other items. Perform the offering method exactly as before.

"When the offering is complete, arrange the tree leaves again within the altar, and upon the leaves place the ghee vessel. Again form the mudrā, recite the dhāraṇī three times, and empower the ghee. Then with the tree branch, stir the ghee without stopping the hand, while watching the sun or moon and simultaneously looking at the ghee. Recite the dhāraṇī an unlimited number of times. From the beginning of the eclipse until the shadow recedes but has not yet fully cleared, the ghee will manifest one of three signs: first, vapor; second, smoke; third, fire. Among these lower, middle, and upper grades, if any one of the three signs appears, the method has succeeded. Having obtained this sign, the ghee becomes a divine medicine. If one eats this medicine, one will attain retentive memory: whatever passes through one's ears or eyes, both text and meaning are comprehended, and they are committed to the heart and never forgotten. The remaining merit and benefits are immeasurable and boundless; here I have merely outlined a small portion.

"If by the time the eclipse has fully receded and the disc is restored the three signs have not appeared, the method has not succeeded. One should begin again from the start and perform it up to seven times. Even if one has the five unremitting offenses and other extremely grave karmic obstacles, they will all be eliminated and the method will certainly succeed."
