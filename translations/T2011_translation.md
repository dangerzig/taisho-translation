# Treatise on the Supreme Vehicle

## *Zuìshàngshèng Lùn* (最上乘論)

Taishō 2011

Expounded by Hóngrěn (*Hóngrěn*, 弘忍) of the Tang dynasty. A Chan treatise.

*Note: This treatise is attributed to Hóngrěn (601-674), the Fifth Patriarch of Chinese Chan Buddhism and teacher of both Huìnéng (the Sixth Patriarch) and Shénxiù. Written in question-and-answer format, it teaches a single, direct method: "guarding the original true mind" (shǒu běn zhēnxīn, 守本真心). Hóngrěn argues that the inherently pure mind is the fundamental teacher, superior even to recollecting the buddhas of the ten directions. The text draws on the Daśabhūmika Sūtra, the Vimalakīrti Sūtra, the Diamond Sūtra, the Lotus Sūtra, the Nirvāṇa Sūtra, and the Heart-King Sūtra. The treatise culminates in practical meditation instructions: sitting in correct posture, closing the eyes, visualizing the sun, adjusting the breath, and watching the mind without grasping. The printing colophons indicate Korean and Japanese editions from the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries.*

---

For all who approach the holy Way and wish to realize the true teaching, this treatise on the essentials of cultivating the mind [is offered]. If one does not protect and purify [the mind], all practices will be without means to attain [their goal]. I pray that good spiritual friends who copy this text will apply their minds with care, allowing no omissions or errors, lest they mislead later generations.

---

The fundamental essence of cultivating the Way requires one to recognize that one's own present mind is originally pure, unborn and undying, without any discrimination. The self-nature is perfectly complete: the pure mind. This is the fundamental teacher, and it surpasses recollecting all the buddhas of the ten directions.

**Question:** How do we know that our own mind is originally pure?

**Answer:** The *Daśabhūmika Sūtra* (*Shídì Jīng*, 十地經) says: "Within the bodies of sentient beings there exists the vajra buddha-nature. Like the sun, its essence is bright, perfectly complete, vast, and boundless. It is merely covered by the black clouds of the five aggregates, like a lamp inside a jar whose light cannot shine forth." Suppose, for example, that clouds and mist arise in all eight directions, and the whole world is cast in darkness. Is the sun destroyed? Why is there no light? The light was never destroyed; it is merely covered by clouds and mist.

The pure mind of all sentient beings is likewise. It is merely covered by the clinging to conditions, deluded thoughts, afflictions, and the black clouds of various views. If one can simply settle the mind in stillness, without generating deluded thoughts, the dharma of nirvāṇa will naturally become manifest. Therefore we know that our own mind is originally pure.

**Question:** How do we know that our own mind is originally unborn and undying?

**Answer:** The *Vimalakīrti Sūtra* (*Wéimó Jīng*, 維摩經) says: "Suchness has no birth; suchness has no death." "Suchness" means true suchness, the buddha-nature, self-nature purity. Purity is the source of the mind. True suchness originally exists and does not arise from conditions.

It also says: "All sentient beings are suchness. All the worthy sages are also suchness." "All sentient beings" means us. "All the worthy sages" means the buddhas. Although the names and characteristics differ, the true suchness dharma-nature within the body is the same. Because it is unborn and undying, it says "all are suchness." Therefore we know that our own mind is originally unborn and undying.

**Question:** Why is one's own mind called the "fundamental teacher"?

**Answer:** This true mind naturally exists; it does not come from outside. It is not bound by cultivation across the three times. Among all that is most intimate, nothing surpasses guarding one's own mind. If one recognizes the mind and guards it, one reaches the other shore. If one is deluded about the mind and abandons it, one falls into the three evil paths. Therefore we know that the buddhas of the three times take their own mind as the fundamental teacher. As the treatise says: "Clearly guarding the mind, deluded thoughts do not arise. This is non-arising." Therefore we know that the mind is the fundamental teacher.

**Question:** Why is one's own mind said to surpass recollecting that buddha [i.e. the external buddha]?

**Answer:** Constantly recollecting that buddha does not free one from birth-and-death. Guarding one's own fundamental mind reaches the other shore. The *Diamond Sūtra* (*Jīngāng Jīng*, 金剛經) says: "If you see me through form or seek me through sound, that person practices a deviant path and cannot see the Tathāgata." Therefore it says: "Guarding the fundamental true mind surpasses recollecting another buddha."

It also says: "Surpasses" is merely a word of encouragement, urging people to practice. In truth, the ultimate fruit-essence is equal and non-dual.

**Question:** Since sentient beings and the Buddha share the same true essence, why do all the buddhas experience neither birth nor death and enjoy immeasurable happiness, free and unobstructed, while we sentient beings fall into birth-and-death and suffer various kinds of pain?

**Answer:** The buddhas of the ten directions awakened to and penetrated the dharma-nature. They all naturally illumine the mind-source. Deluded thoughts do not arise; right mindfulness is not lost. The mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished; therefore they do not undergo birth-and-death. Not undergoing birth-and-death, they are ultimately quiescent. Therefore we know that all happiness naturally returns [to them].

All sentient beings are deluded about the true nature. Not recognizing the mind's foundation, they pursue various deluded conditions. Not cultivating right mindfulness, they give rise to attachment and aversion. Because attachment and aversion arise, the vessel of the mind is cracked and leaks. Because the vessel of the mind is cracked, there is birth-and-death. Because there is birth-and-death, all sufferings naturally manifest.

The *Heart-King Sūtra* (*Xīnwáng Jīng*, 心王經) says: "The true-suchness buddha-nature is submerged in the ocean of the six consciousnesses of knowing and seeing. Sinking and drowning in birth-and-death, one cannot attain liberation." Strive to understand this! Guard the fundamental true mind. When deluded thoughts do not arise and the mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished, one is naturally equal to the Buddha, without any duality.

**Question:** If the true-suchness dharma-nature is one and non-dual, then delusion should be universal, and awakening should be universal. Why is it that the Buddha awakens to the [true] nature while sentient beings are confused?

**Answer:** From this point onward, we enter the domain of the inconceivable, beyond the reach of ordinary understanding. One who recognizes the mind therefore awakens. One who loses [contact with] the nature is therefore deluded. When the conditions converge, they converge; nothing definitive can be said. Simply trust in the true principle and guard your own fundamental mind.

As the *Vimalakīrti Sūtra* says: "There is no self-nature and no other-nature. Dharmas were originally unborn and are therefore now unceasing." This awakening immediately transcends the two extremes and enters the wisdom of non-discrimination. If you understand this meaning, simply know the essentials of the dharma in your practice: guarding the mind is first. This guarding of the mind is the root of nirvāṇa, the essential gate for entering the Way, the source of the twelve divisions of the canon, and the ancestor of the buddhas of the three times.

**Question:** How do we know that guarding the fundamental true mind is the root of nirvāṇa?

**Answer:** Nirvāṇa: its essence is quiescent, unconditioned, and blissful. Since my mind is the true mind, deluded thoughts are severed. When deluded thoughts are severed, right mindfulness is complete. When right mindfulness is complete, the wisdom of quiescent illumination arises. When the wisdom of quiescent illumination arises, one penetrates the dharma-nature. When one penetrates the dharma-nature, one attains nirvāṇa. Therefore we know that guarding the fundamental true mind is the root of nirvāṇa.

**Question:** How do we know that guarding the fundamental true mind is the essential gate for entering the Way?

**Answer:** Even raising a single hand to draw an image of the Buddha, or accumulating merit as numerous as the sands of the Ganges: all of this is simply the Buddha using expedient means to guide sentient beings who lack wisdom, creating causes for future superior retribution and the conditions for seeing the Buddha. If one wishes to quickly attain buddhahood, understand this: guard the fundamental true mind. The buddhas of the three times, innumerable and boundless: if there were even one person who did not guard the true mind yet attained buddhahood, that would be impossible.

As the sūtra says: "Concentrating the mind in one place, there is nothing that cannot be accomplished." Therefore we know that guarding the fundamental true mind is the essential gate for entering the Way.

**Question:** How do we know that guarding the fundamental true mind is the source of the twelve divisions of the canon?

**Answer:** Throughout all the sūtras, the Tathāgata teaches about all sins and merits, all causes, conditions, and fruits, drawing upon all mountains, rivers, the great earth, grasses, trees, and every kind of thing to create innumerable, boundless analogies, or manifesting innumerable supernatural powers and various transformations. All of this is simply the Buddha using expedient means to guide sentient beings who lack wisdom and who, having minds of various desires and ten thousand different mental dispositions, are taught by the Tathāgata through the appropriate mental gate, leading them into the one vehicle.

Since I already know by direct experience that the buddha-nature of sentient beings is originally pure, like the sun beneath clouds, I simply need to clearly guard the fundamental true mind. When the clouds of deluded thought are gone, the sun of wisdom naturally appears. Why would one need to study further, accumulating knowledge that generates the suffering of birth-and-death, along with all doctrinal principles and matters of the three times?

It is like polishing a mirror: when the dust is gone, the brightness naturally appears. Now, whatever is learned within the mind of ignorance will ultimately prove inadequate. If one can clearly maintain right mindfulness and learn within the unconditioned mind, this is true learning. Although I speak of "true learning," ultimately there is nothing learned. Why? Because both "I" and nirvāṇa are empty. There is no further duality, no unity. Therefore there is nothing to learn.

Although the dharma-nature is empty, one must clearly guard the fundamental true mind. When deluded thoughts do not arise and the mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished, the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* (*Nièpán Jīng*, 涅槃經) says: "To know that the Buddha does not teach the dharma: this is called being fully endowed with extensive learning." Therefore we know that guarding the fundamental true mind is the source of the twelve divisions of the canon.

**Question:** How do we know that guarding the fundamental true mind is the ancestor of the buddhas of the three times?

**Answer:** The buddhas of the three times all arise from the mind-nature. First one guards the true mind. When deluded thoughts do not arise and the mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished, one subsequently attains buddhahood. Therefore we know that guarding the fundamental true mind is the ancestor of the buddhas of the three times.

Through the above four questions and answers, even if one wished to expound at length, there would be no end. My present wish is simply that you recognize your own fundamental mind as the buddha. Therefore I earnestly exhort you: a thousand sūtras and ten thousand treatises are no match for guarding the fundamental true mind. This is the essential.

I now exert myself. Following the *Lotus Sūtra* (*Fǎhuá Jīng*, 法華經), I reveal to you the great cart, the jewel treasury, the bright pearl, and the wondrous medicine. If you do not take and use them, what can be done about your poverty and suffering?

Understand this: when deluded thoughts do not arise and the mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished, all merit and virtue is naturally complete. There is no need to seek externally, returning only to the suffering of birth-and-death. In every situation, with right mindfulness, examine the mind. Do not love the pleasures of the present, thereby planting the suffering of the future. Do not deceive yourself and others; one will not escape birth-and-death.

Strive! Strive! Though now we are impermanent, let us together create the causes for future buddhahood. Do not let three lifetimes pass in vain, wasting our effort wildly.

The sūtra says: "Constantly dwelling in the hells as if touring a garden; being in the other evil paths as if in one's own home." We sentient beings are presently in exactly this state, unaware and unknowing. It is fearsome enough to kill, yet we have no thought of escape. How strange! How painful!

If there are beginners who wish to sit in meditation, relying on the *Contemplation of Immeasurable Life Sūtra* (*Guān Wúliàngshòu Jīng*, 觀無量壽經), sit upright with right mindfulness. Close the eyes and mouth. Gaze evenly before the mind, at whatever distance feels natural. Visualize the sun and guard the true mind, thought after thought without fixation.

Then skillfully adjust the breath. Do not let it be now coarse, now fine, for that will cause illness and suffering.

When sitting in meditation at night, if you see all kinds of wholesome or evil mental images, or enter various samādhis of blue, yellow, red, white, and so forth, or see the body emitting great radiance, or see the physical marks of the Tathāgata, or see all manner of transformations: simply gather the mind and do not attach to any of them. They are all empty, deluded thoughts made visible.

The sūtra says: "The lands of the ten directions are all like empty space; the three realms are illusory, created solely by the one mind."

If you do not attain concentration and do not see any mental images, you need not be alarmed. Simply in walking, standing, sitting, and lying down, constantly and clearly guard the fundamental true mind. Understand this: when deluded thoughts do not arise and the mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished, the ten thousand dharmas do not go beyond your own mind.

The reason all the buddhas have extensively taught so many words, teachings, and analogies is simply that sentient beings' dispositions and practices are varied, so the gates of the teaching differ. In truth, the eighty-four thousand dharma-gates, the three vehicles, the eight stages of the path, the seventy-two ranks of the worthy, and the essence of all practice: none goes beyond one's own mind as the foundation.

If one can recognize one's own fundamental mind and, thought after thought, polish it without fixation, one will see the buddha-nature of oneself. In every thought-moment, one constantly makes offerings to all the buddhas of the ten directions, numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and constantly turns the twelve divisions of the canon.

If you thoroughly penetrate this mind-source, all the meanings of all mental phenomena naturally become manifest. All vows are fulfilled, all practices are complete, all is accomplished, and one does not undergo further existence.

Understand this: when deluded thoughts do not arise and the mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished, having abandoned this body, one is certain to attain the inconceivable [state of] non-arising.

Strive! Do not create [deluded thoughts]! What I have said is true and not false. It is difficult to hear; among those who hear it and can practice, among the multitudes as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, perhaps not even one. Among those who practice and can arrive, among beings over countless kalpas, perhaps one is rare. Take good care; calm yourself. Skillfully regulate the sense-faculties. Constantly illumine and examine the mind-source. Perpetually maintain clear and pure illumination. Do not allow the mind of indeterminate (*avyākṛta*) quality to arise.

**Question:** What is the "indeterminate mind"?

**Answer:** When those who gather the mind, on account of external conditions, have their coarse mind slightly stilled, yet internally the true mind is not yet pure: at such a time, in walking, standing, sitting, and lying down, one vigilantly watches the mind. Yet one is still unable to achieve clear and pure solitary illumination of the mind-source. This is called the "indeterminate mind." It is also called the "leaking mind," and it still does not free one from the great illness of birth-and-death.

How much worse is it for those who do not guard the true mind at all! Such people sink in the ocean of birth-and-death's suffering. When will they escape? How pitiable! Strive!

The sūtra says: "If sentient beings' sincerity does not arise from within, then even encountering buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges throughout the three times would be of no avail."

The sūtra says: "Sentient beings must cross themselves through the discriminating mind; the Buddha cannot cross sentient beings. If the Buddha could cross sentient beings, then with all the past buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, how is it that we have not yet attained buddhahood? It is simply because sincerity has not arisen from within. Therefore we sink in the ocean of suffering."

Strive! Strive! Diligently seek the fundamental mind. Do not allow deluded leaking. The past is not known; it has already passed and is irretrievable. In this present body, having encountered and heard the wondrous dharma, I clearly exhort you with a definitive explanation of these words: understand that guarding the mind is the foremost Way.

If one is unwilling to generate a mind of utmost sincerity, aspiring to attain buddhahood and enjoy immeasurable, free, and blissful ease, but instead merely bustles about following the world, coveting fame and profit, then in the future one will fall into the great hells and suffer various kinds of torment. What use will [that realization] be then?

Alas! Alas! Strive! Strive! If one can only wear tattered robes and eat coarse food, clearly guarding the fundamental true mind, feigning foolishness and not understanding words: this is the most energy-conserving approach, and yet it can achieve great results. This is a person of great diligence.

Worldly deluded people do not understand this principle. Within the mind of ignorance, they undertake many difficult labors, extensively cultivating merit based on appearances, hoping to attain liberation, but only returning to birth-and-death. If one clearly maintains right mindfulness and thereby delivers sentient beings, this is a bodhisattva of great power.

I tell you plainly: guarding the mind is foremost. If you do not diligently guard it, you are a very foolish person. Unwilling to endure the suffering of this single present life, you wish to take on the calamities of ten thousand future kalpas. Listen: I have nothing more to entrust to you. One who is unmoved by the eight winds is truly a mountain of precious treasure.

If one knows the fruit-essence, then facing the ten thousand conditions, one gives rise to function as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, with eloquence flowing like water, prescribing medicine according to the illness. Yet one's deluded thoughts do not arise and the mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished: such a one is truly a heroic being who has transcended the world. If the Tathāgata were present, what praise could exhaust [such a person's merit]?

My purpose in speaking these words is to earnestly exhort you: when deluded thoughts do not arise and the mind of "I" and "mine" is extinguished, this is a being who has transcended the world.

**Question:** What is the extinction of the mind of "I" and "mine"?

**Answer:** If one has even the slightest thought of being superior to others, thinking "I am able to do this": this is the mind of "I" and "mine." It is the illness within nirvāṇa. Therefore the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* says: "It is like empty space, which can contain all things. Yet this empty space does not itself think: 'I can contain all this.'" This metaphor illustrates the extinction of the mind of "I" and "mine," progressing toward the vajra-samādhi.

**Question:** Those practitioners who seek the true, constant, and quiescent merely delight in mundane, impermanent, coarse goodness and do not delight in the true, constant, and wondrous goodness of the first principle. They have not yet seen its truth. They merely wish to generate a mind that conditions meaning, and pursue thoughts. But the arising of the mind of awareness is itself the leaking mind. They merely wish to extinguish the mind, but this is the darkness of ignorance and torpor, which is also not in accord with the principle. They merely wish not to halt the mind and not to condition meaning, but this is grasping at emptiness in a wrong way. Although one has received a human body, one practices as an animal.

At such a time, there are no skillful means of concentration and wisdom, and one cannot clearly understand and see the buddha-nature. This is merely the place where practitioners sink. How can one transcend to reach the nirvāṇa without remainder? Please reveal the true mind.

**Answer:** Understand this! With faith sufficient and aspiration accomplished, calmly and gently still the mind. I will instruct you once more.

Take good care. Be at ease. Quiet the body and mind. Relinquish all conditions. Sit upright with right mindfulness. Skillfully adjust the breath. Discipline the mind: not inside, not outside, not in the middle. Gently, like suchness, steadily observe. Look carefully. Then clearly see this mind-consciousness flowing and moving like water's current or a mirage, shimmering and unsettled.

Having seen this consciousness, simply do not [fix it] as inside or outside. Gently, like suchness, steadily observe. Look carefully. Then, turning back, it dissolves, becoming empty, congealed, limpid, and still. This flowing, moving consciousness suddenly ceases of itself.

The cessation of this consciousness is the cessation of the obstructive delusions of the assembly of bodhisattvas from the first through the tenth stage. When this consciousness ceases, the mind is then empty, congealed, quiescent, calm, bright, and serene. I can say no more about its form. If you wish to attain it, take up the third fascicle of the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra*, the chapter on the Vajra Body, and the third fascicle of the *Vimalakīrti Sūtra*, the chapter on Seeing Akṣobhya Buddha. Calmly reflect, carefully search, and study them thoroughly.

If these sūtras are truly mastered, and one can, in walking, standing, sitting, and lying down, and in the face of the five desires and eight winds, not lose this mind, then this person's pure practice is established, and what was to be done has been done. One will ultimately never again receive the body of birth-and-death.

The five desires are form, sound, smell, taste, and touch. The eight winds are gain and loss, defamation and praise, fame and blame, suffering and pleasure. These are the grounds upon which the practitioner polishes the buddha-nature.

Do not be alarmed that in this present body you have not attained freedom. The sūtra says: "In the mundane world, where no buddha abides, the bodhisattva cannot manifest its use." One must shed this retribution-body.

The roots of sentient beings from past lives are sharp or dull and cannot be judged uniformly. For the superior, it is a single thought-moment. For the inferior, it is immeasurable kalpas. When one has the strength, according to the nature of sentient beings, raise the wholesome roots of the bodhisattva. Benefit self and others. Adorn the buddha-land. One must thoroughly comprehend the four reliances (*sìyī*, 四依) to fully penetrate true reality (*shíxiàng*, 實相). If one clings to the letter, one loses the true teaching.

All bhikṣus: you have studied and left the household life to cultivate the Way. This "leaving home" is leaving the shackles of birth-and-death. This is called "leaving home." When right mindfulness is complete and the Way is cultivated to accomplishment, even at the dissolution of the body, at the moment of death, if one does not lose right mindfulness, one attains buddhahood.

Your disciple, in compiling this treatise above, has simply relied on faith, taking meaning from the text, and spoken thus. This is truly not the speech of one who has clearly realized. If it departs from the holy principle, I pray for repentance and its elimination. If it accords with the holy Way, I dedicate it to sentient beings, wishing that all may recognize the fundamental mind and attain buddhahood simultaneously.

To those who hear: strive! In the future, when you attain buddhahood, may you be among the first to deliver me, your disciple.

**Question:** This treatise, from beginning to end, reveals entirely that one's own mind is the Way. I do not yet know whether the two gates of fruition and practice belong to which classification.

**Answer:** This treatise reveals the one vehicle as its school. Yet its ultimate meaning is that the deluded Way tends [only toward] self-liberation from birth-and-death and the ability to deliver others. Stated directly, it benefits the self without discussing the benefit of others: it is classified under the gate of practice.

If anyone practices according to this text, that person will attain buddhahood before me. If I have deceived you, in the future I will fall into the eighteen hells. I point to heaven and earth as my oath. If you do not believe me, in every life you will be devoured by tigers and wolves.

### Colophons

With this aspiration:

May the sage's life endure ten thousand years, and may all sentient beings throughout the ten directions and the dharma-realm together enter the fruit-ocean of the Tathāgata.
