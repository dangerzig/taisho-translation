# 文殊師利問菩提經
## Sūtra of Mañjuśrī's Inquiry about Bodhi

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0464

Translated from the Chinese. Yao Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, when the Buddha had first attained the path, he was in the country of Magadha at the mountain shrine of Gayā, together with a great assembly of one thousand bhikṣus. All of these had formerly been matted-hair ascetics, and all were arhats. What they needed to do was done, their minds had attained sovereignty, they had obtained their own benefit, exhausted all bonds of existence, and were liberated through correct knowledge. There were also ten thousand bodhisattvas who had gathered from the worlds of the ten directions. They had great awesome virtue, had attained various patiences, various dhāraṇīs, and various profound samādhis, and were endowed with all supernatural powers. Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Mahāsthāmaprāpta Bodhisattva, Gandhahastī Bodhisattva, Vīradāna Bodhisattva, and Anujñānacārī Bodhisattva were their leaders. Such bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, in a great assembly of hundreds of thousands of millions of billions, their number immeasurable, were present; together with devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, non-humans, and a great assembly surrounding him.

At that time, the World-Honored One entered the profoundly deep samādhi of all Buddhas, contemplating the nature and characteristics of all dharmas as they truly are, and he thought: "I have attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, attained omniscience, cast off all heavy burdens, crossed the perilous paths of the three realms of existence, extinguished ignorance and attained true illumination, pulled out the arrows of wrong views and severed the thirst of craving, built the Dharma-ship, struck the Dharma-drum, blown the Dharma-conch, and raised the Dharma-banner. I have turned the seeds of birth and death, revealed the nature of nirvāṇa, blocked the paths of evil, opened the correct road, freed beings from all evil karma, and shown the field of merit. I should now contemplate: who has attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi? Is it attained through the body or through the mind?

"If it is attained through the body, the body has no knowledge and no action. It is like grass, wood, tiles, or stones, composed of the four elements, born from father and mother, sustained by clothing, food, bedding, and bathing, and destined to decay and perish in impermanence. Whereas bodhi is merely a name spoken by convention. It is without form, without color, without fixity, without characteristics, without direction, without entry, without path. It transcends all speech and is beyond the three realms. It cannot be seen, heard, perceived, or known. It is also without anything obtained and without conceptual proliferation, without questions or demonstrations, without written characters, and beyond the path of language.

"If it is attained through the mind, the mind arises from conditions. What arises from conditions is empty, like an illusion: without location, without characteristics, without nature, and without any existence. If within this, one attains bodhi, then the dharma by which one attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, that dharma is entirely empty, merely a name. By convention there is speech, but all of it is conceptual discrimination. Truly nothing exists. There is no root and no substance. There is no receiving, no attachment, no defilement, and no separation. It is of one characteristic, which is the characteristic of no characteristics. Therefore, in this Dharma, there is no one who attains. There is no dharma to be used, and there is also no bodhi. To penetrate in this way is what is called anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

At that time, the Dharma-Prince Mañjuśrī was in the great assembly, standing to the Buddha's right, holding a great jeweled canopy to cover the Buddha above. Mañjuśrī, silently knowing the World-Honored One's thoughts, immediately addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If the characteristic of bodhi is thus, how should good men and good women give rise to the mind?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Good men and good women should give rise to the mind in accord with the characteristic of bodhi."

"World-Honored One! What should one say regarding the characteristic of bodhi?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The characteristic of bodhi transcends the three realms, is beyond worldly dharmas, and is where the path of language is cut off. It extinguishes all arising and non-arising: this is the arising of bodhi. Mañjuśrī! Therefore, a bodhisattva should extinguish all arising and give rise to the bodhi-mind. Non-arising is the arising of bodhi. To give rise to the bodhi-mind is to be like suchness, like the dharma-nature, like the true limit: without discrimination, without conditioning of body or mind. This is the arising of bodhi. Not attached to any dharma, neither increasing nor decreasing, neither different nor one: this is the arising of bodhi. Like an image in a mirror, like the shimmer of heat, like a shadow, like an echo, like the moon reflected in water: one should give rise to the bodhi-mind in this way."

At that time, in the assembly there was a deva prince named Candraśuddhaprabhaguṇa, who had attained non-retrogression. He asked the Dharma-Prince Mañjuśrī: "For what reason does a bodhisattva practice the bodhisattva path?"

Mañjuśrī said: "You may ask this question of the World-Honored One."

The Buddha then told Mañjuśrī: "You answer the deva prince Candraśuddhaprabhaguṇa's question about the practice."

Mañjuśrī said to the deva prince: "Listen well; I shall now explain. Deva prince! You should know that the bodhisattva path has great compassion as its basis, with sentient beings as its object."

The deva prince said: "What is the basis of the bodhisattva's great compassion?"

Mañjuśrī said: "A straightforward mind is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of a straightforward mind?"

He answered: "An equal mind toward all sentient beings is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of an equal mind?"

He answered: "Practice without discrimination is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of practice without discrimination?"

He answered: "A deeply pure mind is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of a deeply pure mind?"

He answered: "The mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?"

He answered: "The six pāramitās are the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of the six pāramitās?"

He answered: "The wisdom of skillful means is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of the wisdom of skillful means?"

He answered: "Non-negligence is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of non-negligence?"

He answered: "The three good conducts are the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of the three good conducts?"

He answered: "The ten wholesome courses of action are the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of the ten wholesome courses of action?"

He answered: "Restraint of the six sense-faculties is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of restraining the six sense-faculties?"

He answered: "Correct mindfulness is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of correct mindfulness?"

He answered: "Correct contemplation is the basis."

He asked again: "What is the basis of correct contemplation?"

He answered: "Firm mindfulness that does not forget is the basis."

The deva prince said: "Mañjuśrī! How many kinds of mind does a bodhisattva have that can gather the causes and gather the fruits?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Deva prince! Bodhisattvas have four kinds of mind that can gather the causes and gather the fruits. What are the four? First, the initial mind of aspiration. Second, the mind of practicing the path. Third, the mind of non-retrogression. Fourth, the mind of one who is one birth from Buddhahood. The initial mind is the cause and condition for the mind of practicing the path. The mind of practicing the path is the cause and condition for the mind of non-retrogression. The mind of non-retrogression is the cause and condition for the mind of one birth from Buddhahood.

"Furthermore, deva prince! You should know that the initial mind is like planting grain in a field. The mind of practicing the path is like the grain growing. The mind of non-retrogression is like the flowers and fruit first forming. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood is like the flowers and fruit being of use. The initial mind is like a cartwright gathering materials. The mind of practicing the path is like planing the wood. The mind of non-retrogression is like assembling the parts. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood is like the cart being finished and ready for use. The initial mind is like the new moon. The mind of practicing the path is like the moon on the fifth day. The mind of non-retrogression is like the moon on the tenth day. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood is like the moon on the fourteenth day. The Tathāgata's wisdom is like the moon on the fifteenth day. The initial mind can transcend the śrāvaka stage. The mind of practicing the path can transcend the pratyekabuddha stage. The mind of non-retrogression can transcend the indeterminate stage. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood abides securely in the determinate stage. The initial mind is like beginning to study the first chapter. The mind of practicing the path is like studying the second chapter. The mind of non-retrogression is like being able to apply the chapters. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood is like penetrating the profound sūtras. The initial mind arises from causes. The mind of practicing the path arises from wisdom. The mind of non-retrogression arises from cutting off. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood arises from fruit. The initial mind has the power of causes. The mind of practicing the path has the power of wisdom. The mind of non-retrogression has the power of cutting off. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood has the power of fruit. The initial mind is like a sick person seeking medicine. The mind of practicing the path is like distinguishing the medicines. The mind of non-retrogression is like the sick person taking the medicine. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood is like the illness being cured. The initial mind is birth into the Dharma-king's family. The mind of practicing the path is studying the Dharma-king's Dharma. The mind of non-retrogression is fully completing the study of the Dharma-king's Dharma. The mind of one birth from Buddhahood is attaining sovereignty in the study of the Dharma-king's Dharma."

At that time, in the great assembly there was a deva prince named Dīptaprabhādhipati, who was non-retrogressing from anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. The deva prince Dīptaprabhādhipati said to the Dharma-Prince Mañjuśrī: "What is the bodhisattva-mahāsattva's concise path by which one swiftly attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Deva prince! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva's concise path has two aspects by which one swiftly attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. What are the two? First, skillful means. Second, wisdom. Gathering good dharmas is called skillful means. Analyzing all dharmas is called wisdom. Skillful means is following the practices of sentient beings. Wisdom is not turning away from the characteristics of all dharmas. Skillful means is attending to the minds of sentient beings. Wisdom is not attending to any dharma. Skillful means is combining dharmas. Wisdom is letting go of dharmas. Skillful means is giving rise to causes and conditions. Wisdom is ceasing causes and conditions. Skillful means is knowing how to discriminate all dharmas. Wisdom is not discriminating the dharma-nature. Skillful means is adorning the buddha-lands. Wisdom is adorning the buddha-lands without discrimination. Skillful means is knowing the sharp and dull faculties of sentient beings. Wisdom is not finding any sentient being. Skillful means is being able to reach the seat of awakening. Wisdom is being able to attain all Buddha-Dharmas.

"Deva prince! You should know that the bodhisattva-mahāsattva has a further two paths by which one swiftly attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. What are the two? First, the path of assistance. Second, the path of severance. The path of assistance is the five pāramitās. The path of severance is the prajñāpāramitā.

"There are a further two paths. What are the two? First, the path of bondage. Second, the path of non-bondage. The path of bondage is the five pāramitās. The path of non-bondage is the prajñāpāramitā.

"There are a further two paths. First, the path of the measurable. Second, the path of the immeasurable. The path of the measurable is grasping at characteristics and discriminating. The path of the immeasurable is not grasping at characteristics and not discriminating.

"There are a further two paths. First, the path of knowledge. Second, the path of severance. The path of knowledge is from the first bhūmi to the seventh. The path of severance is from the eighth bhūmi to the tenth."

At that time, in the assembly there was a bodhisattva named Anujñānavīracārī, who asked the Dharma-Prince Mañjuśrī: "What is meant by the meaning of 'bodhisattva'? What is meant by 'bodhisattva wisdom'?"

Mañjuśrī said: "Good man! 'Meaning' is without function. 'Wisdom' has function. Why is 'meaning' without function? Meaning is the unconditioned. The unconditioned has no function in dharmas and is not function. Furthermore, meaning is neither the characteristic of defilement nor the characteristic of freedom from defilement. This meaning has no function in dharmas and is not function. Furthermore, meaning neither increases nor decreases, and has no function in dharmas and is not function. Deva prince, what is wisdom? Wisdom is the path of patience. It is what the mind uses; it is not without function. Therefore wisdom has function and is not without function. The accomplishment of wisdom culminates in cutting off. Therefore wisdom has function and is not without function. Wisdom is skillful knowledge of the five aggregates, the twelve sense-bases, the eighteen elements, the twelve links of dependent arising, and what is appropriate and what is not. Therefore wisdom has function and is not without function.

"Furthermore, deva prince! Bodhisattvas have ten kinds of wisdom. What are the ten? First, the wisdom of causes. Second, the wisdom of fruits. Third, the wisdom of meaning. Fourth, the wisdom of skillful means. Fifth, the wisdom of prajñā. Sixth, the wisdom of gathering. Seventh, the wisdom of the pāramitās. Eighth, the wisdom of great compassion. Ninth, the wisdom of maturing sentient beings. Tenth, the wisdom of non-attachment to all dharmas.

"Furthermore, deva prince! Bodhisattvas have ten kinds of arising. What are the ten? First, bodily arising, to cause sentient beings to purify their bodily karma. Second, verbal arising, to cause sentient beings to purify their verbal karma. Third, mental arising, to cause sentient beings to purify their mental karma. Fourth, inner arising, without attachment to any inner possession. Fifth, outer arising, to cause sentient beings to abide in correct practice. Sixth, arising of wisdom, to perfect the Buddha's wisdom. Seventh, arising of loving-kindness, contemplating all merits and adornments. Eighth, arising of maturing sentient beings, guarding the medicine of wisdom. Ninth, arising of conditioned wisdom, to perfect the community of the determinate. Tenth, arising of unconditioned wisdom, so the mind does not attach to the three realms.

"Furthermore, deva prince! Bodhisattvas have ten kinds of practice. What are the ten? First, pāramitā practice. Second, gathering practice. Third, wisdom practice. Fourth, skillful-means practice. Fifth, great-compassion practice. Sixth, the practice of seeking dharmas that aid wisdom. Seventh, the practice of seeking dharmas that aid knowledge. Eighth, the practice of purifying the mind. Ninth, the practice of contemplating the truths. Tenth, the practice of non-attachment to everything beloved.

"Furthermore, deva prince! Bodhisattvas have a further ten kinds of exhaustion through reflection. What are the ten? First, the exhaustion of reflecting on affairs. Second, the exhaustion of reflecting on feeling. Third, the exhaustion of reflecting on dharmas. Fourth, the exhaustion of reflecting on afflictions. Fifth, the exhaustion of reflecting on views. Sixth, the exhaustion of reflecting on wrong views. Seventh, the exhaustion of reflecting on craving. Eighth, the exhaustion of reflecting on non-attachment. Ninth, the exhaustion of reflecting on bonds and proclivities. Tenth, the exhaustion of reflecting on the practices at the seat of awakening.

"Furthermore, deva prince! Bodhisattvas have a further ten remedial dharmas. What are the ten? First, remedying the miserly and greedy mind by raining the rain of generosity. Second, remedying the mind of broken precepts by the purity of the three dharmas. Third, remedying the angry mind by cultivating loving-kindness and patience. Fourth, remedying the lazy mind by seeking the Buddha-Dharma without weariness. Fifth, remedying the mind of unwholesome thoughts by attaining sovereignty in dhyāna concentration and liberation. Sixth, remedying the deluded mind by generating dharmas that aid the definitive prajñāpāramitā. Seventh, remedying the mind of afflictions by generating dharmas that aid the path. Eighth, remedying the mind of inverted paths by cultivating dharmas that aid the Four Truths. Ninth, remedying the mind through sovereign practice in timely and untimely situations. Tenth, remedying the view of self through the dharma of non-self.

"Furthermore, deva prince! Bodhisattvas have a further ten wholesome grounds. What are the ten? First, bodily goodness, through freedom from the three bodily evils. Second, verbal goodness, through freedom from the four verbal evils. Third, mental goodness, through freedom from the three mental evils. Fourth, inner goodness, through non-attachment to the view of the body. Fifth, outer goodness, through non-attachment to all dharmas. Sixth, the goodness of non-attachment to aids of knowledge, through not coveting the dharmas that aid the path. Seventh, the goodness of non-conceit, through reflecting on the nature of the noble path. Eighth, the goodness of removing the body, through cultivating the prajñāpāramitā. Ninth, the goodness of freedom from inversion, through not deceiving any sentient being. Tenth, the goodness of not sparing body or life, through transforming sentient beings with great compassion.

"Furthermore, deva prince! Bodhisattvas who value practice in accord with the Dharma can attain bodhi; those who do not value practice in accord with the Dharma cannot. Those who practice in accord with the Dharma act as they speak. Those who do not practice in accord with the Dharma have only words and cannot practice what they preach.

"Furthermore, deva prince! Bodhisattvas have a further two kinds of practice in accord with the Dharma. What are the two? First, practicing the path. Second, practicing severance. There are a further two kinds. What are the two? First, personally cultivating the good with one's body. Second, teaching and transforming sentient beings. A further two kinds. What are the two? First, practicing the practice of wisdom. Second, not practicing the practice of wisdom. A further two kinds. What are the two? First, skillfully discriminating the various stages. Second, not discriminating between what is a stage and what is not. A further two kinds. What are the two? First, knowing the faults of the various stages and being able to advance. Second, skillfully knowing what is complete, progressing from one stage to the next. A further two kinds. What are the two? First, skillfully knowing the paths of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Second, skillfully knowing the Buddha's path, the practice of non-retrogression."

At that time, the Buddha praised the Dharma-Prince Mañjuśrī: "Excellent! Excellent! You have been able to teach the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas about their fundamental career and path. It is truly as you have said."

When this Dharma was taught, ten thousand bodhisattvas attained the patience of non-arising dharmas. The Dharma-Prince Mañjuśrī, and all the devas, humans, and asuras of the world, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully believed and accepted it.
