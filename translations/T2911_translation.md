# 讚僧功德經
## Sūtra in Praise of the Merit of the Saṃgha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2911

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

> Briefly compiled from the Āgama sūtras, praising the saṃgha-jewel, that great field of merit.
> Great Brahmā and Śakra, with the voice of Brahmā, praised the saṃgha-jewel.
> The earth-supporting spirits and all deities praised the entire assembly of the saṃgha-jewel.
> We who have gone forth in the age of the declining Dharma are the ever-abiding saṃgha-jewel, the ever-abiding saṃgha.

> The saṃgha-jewel, together with the saṃgha, upholds and promotes the buddha-dharma, aspiring toward the wondrous fruit of bodhi.
> In this turbid, suffering, evil world, they ever abide in the Tathāgata's pure assembly.
> Within the saṃgha, some seek the four fruits; some have already realized the fruits and remain in the saṃgha.
> These eight classes of noble persons are never absent from the harmonious saṃgha.

> Some practice dhūta austerities and constantly beg for food; some delight in solitude in the mountains.
> Even regarding the most subtle precepts, they do not transgress the Tathāgata's strict command.
> Some broadly and deeply study wisdom; some still their thoughts and cultivate the various dhyānas.
> All are gathered in the saṃgha assembly, like a hundred rivers flowing into the great sea.

> The surpassingly wondrous jeweled saṃgha of great virtue nurtures the seeds of merit in all beings.
> Those who can bestow the excellent fruit of gods and humans are none other than the assembly of the buddha-dharma-saṃgha jewels.
> One who with a good heart offers a handful of water to the saṃgha obtains merit exceeding the volume of the great ocean.
> Even dust-motes may have a limit to their counting, but offerings of treasure to the saṃgha are without end.

> If in the future one wishes to seek liberation, crossing over the river of saṃsāric poverty,
> One should swiftly and sincerely plant the seeds of cause and fruit within the saṃgha-jewel.
> In this most excellent and wholesome field of merit, if one plants seeds of meritorious virtue,
> The future harvest will be without bound, like a great cloud containing vast rain.

> The giver who does not calculate or measure, who offers equally with an undivided mind,
> Is the one truly capable of receiving the excellent fruit among gods and humans.
> Adorned with immeasurable merit and virtue, the disciples of the greatly compassionate World-Honored One:
> Ordinary people with fleshly eyes can hardly distinguish them, like fire concealed beneath ashes.

> Some outwardly manifest the appearance of breaking precepts, while inwardly concealing immeasurable merit and virtue.
> One should have faith in, follow, and reverently honor them; the worthy and the foolish cannot be fathomed.
> Some outwardly manifest perfect deportment; some appear as if they have not yet abandoned desire.
> Though their outward form appears to be that of an ordinary person, there is no hindrance to their being sages within.

> Inwardly, they are like the four kinds of āmra fruit, whose ripeness and unripeness are hard to distinguish.
> The Tathāgata's disciples are just so: those with precepts and those without are also hard to discern.
> Therefore I earnestly exhort all people: do not slander or revile the saṃgha-jewel assembly.
> If one wishes not to sink in the sea of suffering, one should always reverently cultivate this wholesome field.

> Those who constantly wish to receive happiness among humans should also make offerings to the bhikṣu-saṃgha.
> Do not with the inferior mind of an ordinary person make discriminations among the Tathāgata's disciples.
> If any faithful laymen or laywomen can generate faith in even ten moments of thought,
> And equally make offerings to the bhikṣu-saṃgha, such persons will obtain immeasurable reward.

> If one gives rise to wrong views toward the saṃgha, one will certainly fall into the three evil destinies in the future.
> The World-Honored One himself, with the voice of Brahmā, proclaimed this from his golden mouth: it is truly not false.
> Better to cut out one's own tongue with a sharp blade, or crush one's body with a pestle,
> Than to harbor even a single thought of angry resentment, slandering the Tathāgata's pure saṃgha.

> Better to swallow a great burning iron ball, better to have fierce flames issue from one's mouth,
> Than to speak even one word in jest, reviling the pure assembly of those who have gone forth.
> Better to dismember oneself with a sharp blade, maiming limbs and destroying flesh,
> Than to jest and mock the foolish. How much worse to curse and strike the bhikṣu assembly!

> Better to gouge out both eyes with one's own hands, better to be born blind for many kalpas,
> Than to look with malicious eyes upon those who practice renunciation of desire.
> Better to destroy a vihāra or a caitya, better to burn a stūpa of the seven jewels containing śarīra,
> Than to speak evil words within the saṃgha, slandering the Tathāgata's pure assembly.

> One who destroys a stūpa brings ruin upon themselves, suffering through immeasurable kalpas.
> But one who speaks of the saṃgha's shortcomings brings ruin upon themselves and also drags down immeasurable others.
> Therefore the wise should reflect carefully and not give rise to contempt toward the saṃgha.
> Guard well against transgressions of speech-karma; do not discuss who upholds and who violates the precepts.

> If with a single evil word one slanders a śramaṇa, one will fall into Naraka and suffer extreme torment.
> Emerging from hell and obtaining a human body, one will immediately incur the retribution of deafness, blindness, and muteness.
> Many foolish and inferior people in the world speak of the faults and evils of monks and nuns.
> Because of this, they fall into the evil paths and forever sink in the sea of suffering.

> The greatly compassionate World-Honored One himself bowed to the great assembly, revering the harmonious saṃgha of great virtue.
> Even the buddhas themselves are so earnest in this; how much less should ordinary people treat the assembly with contempt?
> Many people of faith in the world who honor the World-Honored One's disciples,
> Upon hearing others speak of the Three Treasures' shortcomings, develop wrong views toward the saṃgha.

> Because of this, they cause the retreat and ruin of good people, destroying the Tathāgata's pure assembly.
> They do not see the thousand world-honored ones of this Fortunate Kalpa. Therefore the wise should ponder this.
> In the past, the bhikṣu Kokālika, with a single evil word, slandered the saṃgha assembly.
> He fell into the Padma hell, and his tongue was plowed by blades tens of thousands of times.

> There was also a disciple of Kāśyapa Buddha who slandered immeasurable worldly people.
> By the force of that evil karma, after his broken body was discarded, he again suffered the tongue-plowing torment of hell.
> Even a śramaṇa who harbors resentment and slanders others incurs immeasurable retribution of speech-karma.
> How much less can a precept-less layperson who reviles the saṃgha escape falling into the evil paths?

> Therefore the wise should not revile even grass, trees, bricks, or tiles,
> Much less slander the pure ones who have gone forth, who practice renunciation of desire and wholesome dharmas.
> Even if the fire of desire blazes in the heart and one defiles the pure precepts of śīla,
> Before long one can repent and purify oneself, returning to the ranks of the Tathāgata's holy assembly.

> Just as a person briefly loses their way but, having eyes, can find the original path again,
> So a bhikṣu, though transgressing the World-Honored One's prohibitions, can still quickly eliminate the transgression.
> Just as a person stumbles on level ground but, having feet, can swiftly rise again,
> So a bhikṣu, though briefly lacking in śīla, can still repair the breach before long.

> Just as a golden vessel in the world, though broken, is still of one kind of value,
> A wooden vessel, though perfectly intact, cannot be compared to a broken jeweled vessel.
> A precept-breaking bhikṣu, though without precepts, surpasses in merit from the initial resolve to go forth
> A hundred thousand million billion laypeople, whose merit, though great, does not equal his.

> Disciples who have gone forth are capable of continuing the Tathāgata's Dharma in the latter age.
> Ten thousand virtuous laypeople cannot, even for a moment, propagate the holy teaching.
> Even the lowest precept-breaking, precept-violating monk: offerings to him still yield rewards of ten thousand billions.
> Therefore the World-Honored One praised this excellent cause: one receives honor among gods and humans.

> Therefore I earnestly exhort all people: do not slander the Tathāgata's saṃgha-jewel assembly.
> Because of habituating evil causes and conditions in this life, in the future the karma thus formed will also slander the Buddha.
> Because of these karmic branches of body, speech, and mind, one permanently severs one's capacity for rebirth among gods and humans,
> And falls into the three evil paths, sinking for a billion kalpas without rest.

> If toward the pure assembly one gives rise to right faith, free of slander, one will be without the transgression of slandering the saṃgha.
> One will always be able to guard against transgressions of speech-karma, and will not speak about the Tathāgata's saṃgha-jewel assembly.
> If anyone has committed the transgression of reviling the saṃgha, one should sincerely and swiftly seek repentance.
> Do not give rise to a mind of arrogance toward the saṃgha, lest in the next life one suffer and surely regret.

> A single moment's merit within the saṃgha: its blessings cannot be contained by the great earth.
> How much more the merit of upholding the Tathāgata's strict precepts through months and accumulating years!
> The merit-reward of such a person's precept-keeping: even the Buddha in one kalpa could not fully describe it.
> How much less can other ordinary people know its extent? Such blessings equal empty space, without measure.

> Know then that the saṃgha assembly of Śākyamuni Tathāgata is broadly adorned with merit and virtue.
> Therefore laypeople are not permitted to insult, strike, or revile the monastic saṃgha.
> Even when one sees a śramaṇa transgressing the precepts, one should broaden one's mind and not disparage them,
> Just as one entering a fragrant thicket to pick fine blossoms should not pluck the withered branches and leaves.

> In the vast and pure ocean of the buddha-dharma, there are many who uphold the precepts and cultivate with diligence.
> Though among them there may be some who transgress deportment, laypeople should not give rise to slander.
> Just as in a field of newly planted grain, among it there are also tares and weeds,
> One should reverently treat the whole good field alike, and should not pick and choose with discriminating mind.

> Therefore the World-Honored One has enjoined all people not to slander the śramaṇa assembly.
> One should only honor them with a mind of reverence, and thereby receive the excellent reward of the gods.
> Though the sun of the Buddha has long since set, the saṃgha-jewel carries on the Dharma-lamp in continuous radiance,
> Like the Dragon King sending down sweet rain that moistens and nourishes all the sprouts of the great earth.

> The harmonious saṃgha-jewel is just so, raining the Tathāgata's wondrous Dharma-rain,
> Nourishing all parched and thirsty beings, nurturing the wholesome sprouts and seeds of merit.
> Through many kalpas of planting in past fields, they became the Tathāgata's disciples,
> Dwelling in the ocean of the Dharma of worthies and sages, drinking the sweet-dew taste of wondrous liberation.

> Transmitting and upholding the World-Honored One's teaching in the latter age, spreading the transformation to all lands in the ten directions,
> Benefiting all sentient beings, causing the wheel of the buddha-dharma to turn without ceasing.
> When the buddha-dharma at last disappears, the monasteries and vihāras will be destroyed and become ruins.
> The niches, stūpas, and honored images will all be desolate. Even if one wished to make offerings, it would be hard to find the opportunity.

> Not even a painting of a monk's form on a wall could be seen, much less could one hear the correct Dharma.
> A human body is hard to obtain, yet one has been born among humans; the buddha-dharma is hard to encounter, yet one has now met it.
> How could one fail, in this excellent and wholesome field of merit, to plant the seeds of future merit and virtue?
> The road through darkness is distant beyond reach; one should prepare provisions for the journey ahead.

> If in this wholesome field one does not plant, then on the future perilous road, one will lack provisions.
> Therefore all people should reflect well: having heard the sūtra, one should make charitable offerings within the saṃgha.
> Based on the sūtras, I have briefly praised the saṃgha-jewel, whose merit and virtue are immeasurable, pervading empty space.
> I dedicate this to all sentient beings: may we together in the future meet Maitreya.
