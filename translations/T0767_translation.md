# 佛說三品弟子經
## Sūtra of the Three Grades of Disciples, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0767

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus in assembly, teaching the sūtras.

The Worthy Ānanda rose from his seat and addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask a question. May the Deva above Devas explain it and resolve my doubt!"

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Ask as you wish. The Tathāgata shall explain it for you."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "Among upāsakas who study the path, there are upper, middle, and lower grades. I pray the Buddha will explain these!"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "You have raised this question for the sake of those in future ages to come. The Tathāgata shall now explain it for you. Listen carefully and take it to heart!"

Ānanda said: "Yes, I receive the teaching."

The Buddha said: "Upper-grade upāsakas and upāsikās receive and uphold the five precepts without transgressing them even by so much as a hair. If they go about instructing and guiding people, they cause all of them to generate the bodhisattva mind. What is the bodhisattva mind? It is to think of all people in the ten directions as one would regard one's own infant child, to guide people into the path, and to practice the Mahāyāna conduct. One gives instruction fully without any hope of return, not seeking offerings of robes, food, jewels, money, or goods. One does not follow the lesser path but takes the deliverance of others as one's foundation."

"What is the lesser path?" The Buddha said: "Having entered the great Dharma and practicing the Mahāyāna conduct, to seek only to become a srotāpanna, sakṛdāgāmin, anāgāmin, arhat, or pratyekabuddha: this is the lesser path, not the bodhisattva way. The bodhisattva's Dharma is to instruct all beings and cause them to enter prajñāpāramitā, to comprehend upāyakauśalya, and to attain the wisdom of sarvajñā. You should know that such a person's practice extends far into the past. This person lived in the time of Buddhas of the past and made offerings to teachers as though they were the Buddha, without any difference. One who practices in this way is an upper-grade upāsaka or upāsikā.

"Middle-grade upāsakas and upāsikās also receive the five precepts without transgressing them even by so much as a hair. They too are people from the time of Buddhas of the past. Originally they studied the path but did not encounter an enlightened teacher. They did not hear of prajñāpāramitā and did not understand upāyakauśalya. They practiced only one pāramitā: giving, morality, patience, vigor, or concentration, but did not perceive wisdom within the sūtras and Dharma-teachings. Through their former merit they entered the human path. They possess some recognition from past lives and have obtained the gate of the Dharma. They guard the precepts completely and regard their teachers as though they were the Buddha, without any difference. They never transgress the precepts. One who practices in this way is a middle-grade upāsaka or upāsikā.

"Lower-grade upāsakas and upāsikās, though they receive the five precepts, violate every one of them. If they encounter an enlightened teacher or a worthy one who understands the Dharma, they follow along, ask about the essentials of the Dharma, and feel joy at the time. Facing that teacher, they repent of their transgressions and vow to be more diligent with the five precepts, yet they repeatedly lapse. Harboring thoughts of desire, they no longer uphold the Buddha's precepts. They become arrogant and turn to resenting the enlightened teachers and worthy ones, slandering and finding fault with them: 'I will go find another teacher with deeper precepts and wisdom, one who will protect me with skillful means!' They do not know that this teacher only feeds on offerings. Instead they accept such words and believe them, neglecting the sūtras and precepts. Such upāsakas and upāsikās are like carriers of the dead; one should not gather or sit together with them. They corrupt the good intentions and practices of others. They initiate and instruct people, accepting their Dharma-vows, but only in hopes of offerings, wanting to obtain money, grain, and cloth to support their wives and children. They borrow the Buddha's spiritual authority, yet their minds do not think of the beings in the five destinies of the ten directions, wishing to deliver them. Instead they create false impressions for those who receive their teaching and do not speak of Mahāyāna wisdom. This is to not be genuine in the Buddha's true and right Dharma. They tell those who receive the sūtras: 'You should avoid worldly entanglements. A little drinking does no harm. The Buddha had a hundred kinds of fine food; he did not forbid people from drinking. If members of your household do not keep the precepts, or if servants and slaves are involved, just have someone else do the killing. Simply do not raise your own hand to it.' These are not the Buddha's disciples. Furthermore, they limit the Buddha's merit."

"What does it mean to limit the Buddha's merit?" The Buddha said: "While going about initiating and instructing people, they do not wish for the four assemblies to hear and know of it."

Ānanda knelt and asked the Buddha: "Why do they not wish the four assemblies to hear and know?"

"Such upāsakas and upāsikās are foolish in mind and do not understand. They consider themselves clever, but truly they do not comprehend the profound and essential wisdom within the sūtras. Feeling no shame at their ignorance, they instead create obstacles and limitations for newly studying worthy ones, not wishing them to meet enlightened and skillful teachers. Why? Because they want to monopolize the offerings, and so they obstruct the great path. Such upāsakas and upāsikās, though they undertake to deliver people, do not perceive prajñāpāramitā and do not understand upāyakauśalya. They are blind and benighted, exclusively practicing the lesser path. Sometimes they teach people to do meritorious deeds but without the four immeasurable minds, and their giving is not universal. They invite this one and dismiss that one; they do not employ the methods of the path. Then the princes and envoys of the Four Heavenly Kings and the spirits who protect the Buddha's path record each of their deeds one by one, and gradually withdraw from them. They direct the spirits of destiny to reckon and accumulate their transgressions, itemize their names, and report them to the Lord Above. Before their lifespans have ended, malevolent spirits are dispatched who, seizing upon their violations of the precepts, take their remaining years. They naturally fall into the mud-hells and must pass through the punishment of the eighteen hells. Only when heaven and earth are consumed by fire do they emerge, perhaps to be reborn as an animal, or perhaps to enter the human path. If they are born among humans, they will be born in families of foolishness and ignorance, where the Dharma is not seen."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! Those on the path should frequently gather in assembly to discuss and expound the Dharma. Its meaning must not be obstructed. Foolish persons on the path will bring about the severing of the Dharma and the transgression of destroying the Buddha's teaching."

When all the disciples heard the Buddha speak this sūtra, they were all filled with awe. With upright minds they received the teaching and made obeisance to the Buddha.
