# One Hundred and Fifty Verses in Praise of the Buddha

## *Yībǎi Wǔshí Zàn Fó Sòng* (一百五十讚佛頌)

Taishō 1680

Composed by Mātṛceṭa (*Mózhǐlǐ Zhìzhā*, 摩咥里制吒). Translated by Yìjìng (*Yìjìng*, 義淨) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is Mātṛceṭa's celebrated devotional poem, the Śatapañcāśatka-stotra ("Hymn of 150 Verses"), one of the most famous works of Indian Buddhist poetry. Mātṛceṭa (c. 2nd century CE) was praised by later tradition as the greatest Buddhist poet before Aśvaghoṣa. The hymn methodically praises the Buddha's virtues: his supreme merit, impartiality, generosity, moral purity, patience, tireless compassion, wisdom, teaching skill, and skillful means in converting beings of all capacities. The poem also celebrates the Buddha's body adorned with marks, his dharma teachings as rain that quenches the dust of craving, and his final entry into nirvāṇa as the completion of his twofold benefit. Yìjìng translated this text during his years in India and at the Tang court, and considered it one of the essential devotional texts of Indian Buddhism.*

---

> The World-Honored One is supremely distinguished,
> Having well severed the seeds of all afflictions.
> Immeasurable and excellent qualities
> Are gathered in the Tathāgata's body.

> Only the Buddha is worthy of refuge,
> Worthy of praise, worthy of service.
> Those who think according to principle
> Should abide in this teaching.

> All evil afflictions and their residual tendencies
> Have been removed by the Protector of the World.
> Merit and wisdom are both perfected;
> Only the Honored One never declines or sinks.

> Even those with evil views
> Who harbor resentment toward the Honored One,
> Searching for faults in body, speech, and action,
> Can find no flaw.

> Remember: I have obtained a human body
> And heard the Dharma with joyful heart.
> It is like a blind turtle in the great ocean
> Encountering the hole in a floating log.

> Deluded thoughts constantly pursue us;
> Afflicted karma plunges us into the deep pit.
> Therefore, with words,
> I praise the Buddha's true qualities.

> The Sage's realm is immeasurable,
> His holy virtue without limit.
> For my own benefit,
> I now praise but a small part.

> Homage to the wisdom attained without a teacher,
> To the rare nature of all his deeds.
> His merit, wisdom, and radiant power:
> Who can know their measure?

> The Tathāgata's virtues are without limit,
> Without equal, beyond expression.
> I now seek merit and benefit,
> Offering praise through these words.

> My wisdom and strength are shallow,
> The Buddha's virtue without shore.
> May his great compassion
> Rescue me, who have no refuge.

> Toward enemies and friends alike,
> He arises in great compassion without condition.
> Throughout the entire realm of beings,
> He constantly serves as a true and good friend.

> He can give even his inner wealth;
> How much more his outer possessions!
> The Honored One has no stingy or grudging mind;
> He fulfills the wishes of all who seek.

> With his own body he protects others' bodies;
> With his own life he ransoms others' lives.
> He gave his whole body to save a single dove,
> Joyfully, without miserliness.

> The Honored One does not fear the evil destinies,
> Nor does he crave the good destinies.
> Only because his mind is pure and clean
> Does moral conduct arise from this.

> Ever departing from crookedness and deceit,
> Always befriending the upright,
> Knowing all actions are empty in their essential nature,
> He dwells only in ultimate truth.

> When many sufferings press upon his body,
> The Honored One skillfully brings peace to his mind.
> Right knowledge severs all afflictions;
> For those with faults, he always arouses compassion.

> Sacrificing his life to relieve others' hardships,
> He gives rise to immeasurable joy,
> Like one who died and suddenly revives:
> This joy surpasses even that.

> Though enemies harm his body,
> Tormenting him at all times,
> He does not regard their faults and evils,
> But constantly arises in great compassion.

> The seed of right and perfect awakening
> His mind always treasures and enjoys.
> The great hero's hard-to-conquer wisdom
> Has no one who can equal it.

> The peerless fruit of awakening
> Has ascetic practice as its cause.
> Therefore he did not spare his body,
> But diligently cultivated all superior qualities.

> The noble and the lowly alike
> He draws forth with great compassion.
> Among all such distinctions,
> He has no thought of high and low.

> The fruit of superior meditative absorption:
> His mind has no attachment to it.
> Universally relieving all beings,
> His great compassion is uninterrupted.

> Though the Honored One encounters extreme suffering,
> He does not seek pleasure for himself.
> His wondrous wisdom and qualities
> Are surpassingly beyond compare.

> Among the mixed dharmas of purity and defilement,
> He discards the false and takes the true,
> Like the pure royal goose
> That drinks the milk and leaves the water.

> For immeasurable billions of kalpas,
> Courageously he pursued awakening.
> In life after life,
> He gave up his body in quest of the wondrous Dharma.

> Through three incalculable eons,
> Diligent and never weary,
> Taking this as his supreme companion,
> He realized wondrous awakening.

> The Honored One has no envious heart;
> Toward the lesser, he removes thoughts of contempt.
> Equal and without contention,
> His superior practices are all perfected.

> The Honored One values the cause-stage practice,
> Not seeking the perfection of the fruit-stage.
> Pervading all with his superior deeds,
> His manifold virtues naturally become complete.

> Diligently cultivating the dharma of liberation,
> He ascends to the summit of all practices.
> Whether sitting, lying, or walking in meditation,
> Every place becomes a supreme field of merit.

> Uprooting all faults and defilement,
> Increasing pure virtue,
> All accomplished through accumulated practice:
> Only the Honored One is supreme above all.

> All merits are perfectly fulfilled,
> All faults completely removed.
> The Tathāgata's pure Dharma body:
> The dust of habits has all been severed.

> Provisions gathered again and again,
> Their merit returns to the Trainer's body.
> If one seeks a comparison,
> Nothing can equal the Buddha.

> Surveying all the worlds:
> Disasters, adversities, many obstacles and troubles.
> Even when there is a small measure of goodness,
> It is easy to find its match.

> But departing from all faults and troubles,
> Tranquil, serene, unmoved,
> The supreme wholesome roots:
> Nothing can serve as their analogy.

> The Tathāgata's wisdom is deep and far-reaching,
> Without bottom, without limit.
> Comparing worldly things to the Buddha's body
> Is like an ox's hoofprint to the great ocean.

> His deep benevolence supports all beings;
> In the world there is no comparison.
> The great earth bears heavy burdens,
> But to compare it with this is truly light.

> The darkness of delusion has been removed;
> The Sage's light shines universally.
> Worldly wisdom cannot serve as analogy,
> Like a firefly before the sun's light.

> The Tathāgata's three actions are pure,
> Like an autumn moon, a clear and bright pool.
> Worldly purity compared to the Buddha's body
> Only reveals its own dusty and turbid nature.

> All such analogies cited above,
> The most outstanding things in the world:
> The Buddha's Dharma far surpasses them.
> Worldly matters are worthy of pity.

> The precious gathering of holy Dharma:
> The Buddha stands at its summit.
> Among all that is supreme and unequaled,
> Only Buddha is equal to Buddha.

> Of the Tathāgata's ocean of holy wisdom,
> I praise but a small part as my joy permits.
> With humble words praising supreme virtue,
> I am truly ashamed before this.

> When the world witnessed his conquest of Māra,
> All universally submitted.
> Contemplating that as equal to true nature,
> I regard it as light as a feather.

> Suppose in a great battle
> Wisdom and courage can crush the enemy;
> Yet holy virtue transcends the world,
> And conquering that cannot serve as analogy.

> Just after conquering Māra,
> In the last watch of the night,
> He severed all afflictions and their residual tendencies,
> And his supreme virtues were all perfected.

> Holy wisdom dispels all darkness,
> Surpassing the light of a thousand suns.
> Crushing all heterodox schools:
> How rare! Beyond compare!

> The three wholesome roots perfected,
> Attachment, aversion, and delusion forever extinguished.
> Seeds and habits all removed:
> His purity is beyond analogy.

> The wondrous Dharma, the Honored One always praises;
> What is not the true Dharma, he always rejects.
> At these junctures of the deviant and the correct,
> His mind has neither hatred nor love.

> Toward his holy disciples
> And the followers of other teachers:
> Amid their opposition and compliance,
> The Buddha's mind is never divided.

> Toward virtue he feels no attachment;
> The virtuous, too, he does not covet.
> How excellent! Utterly without stain!
> His holy wisdom is ever perfectly pure.

> All faculties are always tranquil and still,
> Forever free from deluded and false mind.
> Among all objects and domains,
> He perceives directly through personal witness.

> Mindfulness and wisdom reach the ultimate truth,
> Unfathomable by ordinary fools.
> Skillfully establishing language,
> He realizes that place beyond words.

> Serene, unobstructed light,
> Bright and pure, surpassing all radiance.
> His wondrous form, rare in the world:
> Who would not harbor a reverential mind?

> Whether one briefly glances for the first time
> Or constantly gazes upon him,
> His wondrous marks are ever the same;
> Before and after, all equally rejoice.

> The supremely distinguished and virtuous body:
> Those who behold it never tire.
> Even through immeasurable kalpas,
> Their joyful admiration seems like the first glance.

> The body that supports virtue
> And the mind that embodies virtue:
> Nature and characteristics are both fused;
> Subject and object were never different.

> Such virtues of the Well-Gone One
> Are all gathered in the Tathāgata's body.
> Apart from the Buddha's body of marks and excellences,
> There is no other place for them to abide.

> Through my merit from former lives,
> I have been fortunate to encounter the Trainer.
> Looking up, I praise the mountain of qualities,
> Repaying from afar the Honored One's prophecy.

> All sentient beings
> Are held captive by afflictions.
> Only the Buddha can skillfully remove them;
> Through compassion he long abides in the world.

> To whom should one first pay homage?
> Only to the Buddha, the greatly compassionate Honored One.
> His holy virtue transcends the world;
> By compassionate vow he dwells in birth-and-death.

> The Honored One abides in the bliss of quiescence,
> Yet for beings he remains in the turbid world.
> Through eternal kalpas of diligent effort,
> His loving mind is for all.

> From the true he returns to benefit the worldly,
> Drawn forth by compassion.
> Like a mantra drawing out a hidden dragon
> To raise clouds and pour sweet rain.

> Ever dwelling in the station of supreme absorption,
> He regards enemies and friends equally.
> Even the fierce and violent and clamorous
> Cast themselves upon his holy virtue for refuge.

> With spiritual powers, the lion's roar,
> He proclaims himself the Honored One of the three realms.
> Long wearied of fame and renown,
> Yet through compassion he praises himself.

> Always cultivating the practice of benefiting others,
> He has never had a thought of self-benefit.
> His loving mindfulness pervades all beings;
> Toward himself he has no special affection.

> His compassionate vow is without limit;
> He transforms beings according to their capacities.
> Everywhere he provides benefit,
> Like scattering offerings of food.

> With profound mind he remembers all beings,
> Never abandoning them for even a moment.
> Though he benefits them and is repaid with insult,
> The fault is not the Buddha's doing.

> With a compassionate voice he expounds wondrous meaning,
> Sincere and true, never speaking falsely.
> Whether brief or extensive, as the occasion requires,
> He turns the half and full teachings according to the time.

> If one hears the Honored One's discourse,
> Who would not exclaim how rare!
> Even those harboring evil minds,
> If they have wisdom, all come to faith.

> His meaning and words are always skillful.
> Sometimes he utters coarse speech,
> But the benefit is never in vain;
> Therefore all become truly wondrous.

> Gentle or rough,
> He transforms beings as the occasion requires.
> His holy wisdom is an unobstructed mind;
> One flavor, all equal.

> How excellent! His stainless actions!
> Skillful, like a master artisan.
> He has accomplished this subtle and wondrous body
> And expounds these precious sentences.

> Those who behold him all rejoice;
> Those who hear his teaching all have their minds opened.
> His beautiful countenance speaks wondrous words,
> Like the moon pouring out sweet dew.

> Clouds of compassion pour Dharma rain,
> Able to cleanse the dust of craving desire.
> Like the great golden-winged king [Garuḍa]
> That swallows and destroys all dragon-poisons.

> Able to annihilate the darkness of ignorance,
> Like the light of a thousand suns.
> Crushing the mountain of pride,
> Like the vajra of the Lord of the Gods.

> Direct realization, never false or erroneous;
> Meditative absorption removes the disordered mind.
> Practicing truly and skillfully,
> Three things are all perfected.

> Upon first hearing what the Buddha teaches,
> The mind rejoices and is already illumined.
> From this, through proper reflection,
> All defilements are eliminated.

> For those encountering suffering, he provides comfort;
> For the heedless, he arouses fear.
> For those attached to pleasure, he urges weariness of mind.
> According to each situation, he guides and induces.

> Those of superior wisdom realize the joy of Dharma;
> Those of middling faculties give rise to resolute faith;
> Those of shallow capacity develop trusting hearts:
> The Honored One's words benefit all universally.

> He skillfully uproots all wrong views,
> Guiding them toward nirvāṇa.
> Sins and defilements he can wash away,
> By the Honored One's descent of Dharma rain.

> Omniscient wisdom, unobstructed,
> Always abiding in right mindfulness.
> The Tathāgata's prophecies
> Are in every case never false.

> There is nothing that is not the right time or place,
> Nor any improper vessel to which he turns.
> The Honored One's words are never uttered in vain;
> Those who hear them all diligently practice.

> One path, the supreme skillful means,
> Unmixed, fit for study.
> Beginning, middle, and end are all excellent:
> No other teaching possesses all this.

> Such a teaching, entirely wholesome:
> The mad and foolish give rise to slanderous minds.
> If one reproaches this teaching,
> No enemy is equal to this.

> Through kalpa after kalpa, for the confused multitudes,
> He has fully experienced all manner of suffering.
> Even if this teaching were not wholesome,
> One should still practice by remembering the Buddha.

> How much more when it brings great benefit
> And expounds profound and wondrous meaning!
> Even if one's head were ablaze,
> One should first rescue this teaching.

> The autonomous bliss of awakening:
> The holy virtue is ever serene and calm.
> All of this arises from this teaching,
> Realizing that place beyond words.

> The World Hero's true teaching:
> Heterodox schools, hearing it, are all startled.
> The Māra-king harbors a troubled mind;
> Humans and gods give rise to supreme joy.

> The great earth, without discrimination,
> Equally supports all universally.
> This holy teaching benefits all beings;
> Both the deviant and the correct receive its benefit.

> Briefly hearing what the Buddha teaches,
> The diamond seed is already formed.
> Even while one has not yet escaped the cage,
> One will ultimately transcend the realm of death.

> Hearing the Dharma, then reflecting on its meaning,
> Practicing truly and skillfully:
> In sequence, the three wisdoms are perfected.
> No other teaching has all this.

> Only the Bull-King Sage
> Has wondrously realized the true and perfect principle.
> If one does not diligently practice this teaching,
> How could any enmity surpass this?

> Briefly hearing it removes the thirst of craving;
> Wrong views transform into faithful mind.
> Those who listen give rise to joyful hearts;
> Relying on this, they fully realize pure conduct.

> At his birth, all the world rejoiced;
> Growing up, the world was all delighted.
> His great transformation benefits all beings;
> Demonstrating cessation, he arouses sorrowful longing.

> Praising and chanting remove all poisons;
> Recollecting him brings celebration.
> Seeking and investigating, the light of wisdom dawns;
> With understanding, the mind is perfectly pure.

> Meeting him makes one honored and noble;
> Reverently serving him, a supreme mind is born.
> Attending upon him yields the cause of merit;
> Personally serving him removes grief and suffering.

> His moral conduct is pure and clean;
> His meditative absorption is calm and still.
> His wisdom is perfectly fused with knowledge:
> Gathered from merit as [numerous as] the sands of the Ganges.

> The Honored One's form, the Honored One's teaching,
> And the Dharma the Honored One has realized:
> Among what is seen, heard, thought, and known,
> This treasure is the most distinguished.

> For those adrift, he makes an island;
> For those harming themselves, he ever provides protection.
> For the frightened, he provides refuge;
> He guides them and causes their liberation.

> Pure conduct becomes a wondrous vessel;
> The good field produces supreme fruit.
> A good friend can bring benefit;
> The wisdom-life is accomplished by this.

> He practices kindness, and harmony, and patience;
> All who see him are delighted.
> Widely gathering a benevolent and compassionate mind,
> His qualities are without limit.

> In body and speech there is no fault or evil;
> Affection and reverence arise from this.
> Auspiciousness and manifold benefits
> All depend on the Well-Gone One's virtue.

> The Guide skillfully induces:
> The fallen and proud, he makes diligent.
> By balanced absorption he straightens crooked minds;
> Those on the wrong path he returns to the right way.

> For those whose wholesome roots have ripened,
> He conveys them by the three vehicles.
> The obstinate and unruly:
> Through compassion he temporarily sets them aside.

> For those encountering adversity, he establishes
> Peace and joy, urging them toward wholesome practice.
> Compassionate toward suffering beings,
> He benefits and delights all.

> Toward those who oppose and harm him, he arouses loving thoughts;
> For those who lose their way, he feels concern.
> Toward the violent and cruel, he arouses compassion:
> Such holy virtue, none can praise it fully.

> His kindness is deep beyond all measure;
> The whole world knows this.
> Even toward those who repay kindness with enmity,
> The Honored One always arises in loving compassion.

> He abandons his body to rescue all beings;
> Over his own affairs he generates no worry.
> For those who are falling and collapsing,
> He personally provides support and protection.

> His beneficent actions span both worlds,
> Surpassing all the world.
> In darkness he always illumines:
> The Honored One is the wick of the wisdom-lamp.

> What humans and gods experience and enjoy
> Varies according to their kind.
> Only the Honored One's taste of true Dharma
> Is equal, without distinction.

> He does not consider clan or lineage,
> Appearance, strength, or years.
> Whoever has wholesome roots,
> All who seek are granted fulfillment.

> Widely displaying various marvels,
> Without condition he arises in great compassion.
> Saints and ordinary people, humans and gods,
> All join palms and draw near.

> Alas, the terror of birth-and-death!
> With the Buddha's arising, there is radiance.
> Benefiting all sentient beings,
> He is able to fulfill all their wishes.

> Evil persons dwell together with him;
> He takes upon himself their sorrow and danger while giving them joy.
> They slander, torment, and harm his body,
> Yet he receives it as if receiving supreme virtue.

> For beings he practices diligent hardship,
> Never with a mind of attachment.
> The World-Honored One's rare virtue
> Is difficult to express in words.

> The Honored One traveled dangerous and evil roads:
> Horse-grain and ox-dung,
> Six years of ascetic practice,
> He peacefully bore it all without retreat.

> The Honored One occupies the most exalted position,
> Compassionately transforming all beings.
> Even encountering those of low and base station,
> In body and speech he is ever more humble and reverent.

> Whether in the position of honored lord,
> He never has a proud and arrogant mind.
> Humbling himself to serve all beings,
> As lowly and respectful as a servant.

> The temperaments of beings are of billions of kinds;
> Debates and challenges of a thousand sorts.
> With the Tathāgata's compassionate and wholesome voice,
> A single answer severs all doubts.

> His kindness is deeper than heaven and earth that cover and support us;
> Those who repay virtue with deep enmity:
> The Honored One regards those most hostile
> Just as he regards those most deeply kind.

> When enemies increase their harm toward the Honored One,
> The Honored One becomes even more intimate with enemies.
> They constantly seek the Buddha's faults;
> The Buddha treats them as a kindness.

> Heterodox teachers, with jealous hearts, invite him:
> Poisoned food and a pit of fire.
> His compassionate vow transforms them into a clear pool;
> He changes poison into sweet dew.

> With patience he quells angry rage;
> With true words he dispels slander.
> With the power of compassion he subdues Māra's enmity;
> With right knowledge he overcomes the poison of heresy.

> The confused multitudes, from beginningless kalpas,
> Through habitual evil have formed their nature.
> Only the Honored One, with wondrous practice perfected,
> In a single thought-moment causes them to turn toward the good.

> Gentleness overcomes violence;
> Generosity destroys miserliness.
> Good speech overcomes coarse words:
> Only the Honored One possesses such supreme skillful means.

> Nanda, whose immense pride was crushed;
> Aṅgulimāla, in whom compassion was aroused:
> The hard to tame, he skillfully tames.
> Who would not praise such rarity!

> Only the Honored One's holy disciples
> Delight their spirits with the taste of Dharma.
> A grass seat they find peaceful;
> A golden bed they do not value.

> Skillfully knowing faculties, dispositions, and natures,
> He gathers and transforms according to the occasion.
> Sometimes he waits for a request;
> Sometimes he teaches without being asked.

> First he presents generosity, morality, and such;
> Gradually, step by step, pure mind arises.
> Then he speaks of the true and real Dharma,
> Ultimately causing perfect realization.

> In the fearsome place of drifting [in saṃsāra],
> Only the Buddha is worthy of refuge.
> The courageous, greatly compassionate Honored One
> Rescues and relieves all beings.

> Body-clouds pervade the dharma-realm;
> Dharma rain falls on the dusty regions.
> His responsive manifestations each differ,
> Varying according to beings' capacities.

> Wholesome, pure, without contention:
> Only the Honored One is worthy of service.
> Broadly benefiting all humans and gods,
> All should make offerings to him.

> In body and speech, without deliberate action,
> His wholesome transformation pervades all regions.
> What he teaches is wondrously fitting;
> This virtue belongs to the Honored One alone.

> Through long cultivation his three actions are pure;
> Wondrous auspicious signs appear without limit.
> Surveying all the worlds,
> There has never been such supreme virtue.

> How much more so that toward the most evil,
> He practices only the highest compassion,
> Broadly benefiting all sentient beings,
> Courageous and diligent in vigor.

> Those śrāvakas who know the Dharma
> Constantly serve the Honored One.
> Even should they realize nirvāṇa,
> In the end they are called debtors.

> For those holy assemblies
> Who study only for themselves,
> Because they have abandoned the mind that benefits beings,
> They are not called those who repay their debt.

> Awakened from the sleep of ignorance,
> His compassionate gaze pervades all regions.
> Shouldering the burden with uplifted diligence:
> The holy and the good should draw near.

> The harm and trouble of Māra the enemy
> The Buddha's power has already removed.
> Among his fearless qualities,
> This shows but a small part.

> His compassionate mind transforms all beings;
> His holy intention has no hope or expectation.
> Benefit and joy: there is nothing he does not bestow.
> His work of capability is entirely complete.

> If the Tathāgata's supreme wondrous Dharma
> Could somehow be transferred elsewhere,
> Devadatta and Sunakṣatra
> Would not have joined this teaching.

> In the beginningless course of transmigration,
> Beings have mutually harmed one another.
> Because of this the Buddha appeared in the world,
> To reveal and teach and transform all beings.

> At the Deer Park he liberated Kauṇḍinya;
> In the Śāla grove he converted Subhadra.
> The karmic affinities of this land exhausted,
> No further debts remained.

> The Dharma wheel, long since turned,
> Has awakened the confused multitudes.
> Beings as numerous as the Ganges sands who have studied under him
> Are all able to benefit the three realms of existence.

> With the supreme diamond samādhi,
> He himself shattered his adamantine body.
> Not abandoning great compassion,
> He distributed his relics as his final transformation.

> The twofold benefit-practice already fulfilled,
> The form-body and Dharma-body both perfected.
> Rescuing even the icchantika,
> In the twin Śāla trees he revealed Buddha-nature.

> His compassionate mind pervades the three realms of existence;
> His form-body responds to all regions.
> His relics, like grains of millet, distributed as his body:
> Only then did he dwell in perfect quiescence.

> How excellent! His extraordinary practices!
> Rare virtue-body!
> The great awakened one's Dharma gates:
> What the world has never before encountered.

> His flowing kindness pervades all sentient beings;
> Body and speech are ever serene.
> Ordinary fools turn their backs on his holy kindness,
> Toward the Honored One raising slander and anger.

> The treasury of Dharma, the true treasure, without limit;
> The source of virtue, the ocean of merit, truly beyond measure.
> If there is a being who has once bowed to the Honored One,
> Then to bow to that being is also called a worthy bow.

> The holy virtue, the spiritual power, without end:
> My present wisdom is as feeble as a grain of dust.
> Wishing to praise the Tathāgata's mountain of qualities,
> I gaze at the cliff, shrink back in awe, and here I stop.

> Immeasurable, uncountable, without limit,
> A principle hard to conceive, hard to see, hard to realize:
> Only the Buddha's holy wisdom alone can know it.
> How could ordinary fools be able to praise it?

> A single hair, a single mark, fills the dharma-realm;
> A single practice, a single virtue, pervades the source of mind.
> Pure, vast, likened to a fragrant pool,
> Able to cure the thirst of sentient beings' afflictions.

> I have praised the Sage's ocean of qualities.
> Relying on this wholesome karma, may I proceed toward awakening.
> May all sentient beings universally give rise to the supreme mind,
> Forever departing from the false consciousness of ordinary fools.
