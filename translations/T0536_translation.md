# 申日兒本經
## Sūtra of the Past of Śṛgāla's Son

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0536

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the kingdom of Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak.

At that time, the king, the great ministers, the local officials, and the people all revered and honored the Buddha and received his precepts. The Buddha taught the king, the elders, the officials, and the people, commanding that they must not kill living beings, steal, violate another person's wife, engage in double-tongued speech, harsh speech, false speech, or frivolous speech, and must not be jealous, greedy, or skeptical. They should believe that doing good brings good results and doing evil brings evil results, and that what one does not wish done to oneself one should not inflict upon others.

Those who studied other heterodox paths and did not receive the Buddha's precepts were no longer respected by the king, the elders, the officials, or the people. The reason they were not respected was that their path was not the true path. They did not teach people through moral virtue. They themselves killed living beings and taught others to kill. They themselves stole the property of others and taught others to steal. They themselves violated the wives of others and taught others to do the same. They themselves engaged in double-tongued speech, harsh speech, false speech, and frivolous speech, and taught others to do the same. They themselves were jealous, greedy, and skeptical, and taught others to be the same.

In that country there was an elder of the four castes named Śṛgāla, who was exceedingly wealthy, with gold, silver, and precious treasures beyond counting. Śṛgāla followed heterodox paths and did not follow the Buddha's path or believe in the Buddha's sūtras. The heterodox practitioners were all jealous of the Buddha. They assembled at Śṛgāla's house and conferred, saying: "The Buddha is now revered by the king, the great ministers, the officials, and the people, while we alone are not respected by the king, the great ministers, the officials, or the people. The Buddha always says: 'I know the affairs of past, present, and future, and I know in advance what others say and think in their minds.' We should test whether this is truly so."

Śṛgāla said: "How shall we test him?"

The heterodox practitioners instructed Śṛgāla: "Prepare a meal and invite the Buddha. Place poison in the food. Dig inside the gate to a depth of five zhang, place fire within, and cover it thinly with earth. Have the Buddha walk over it when he enters. If the Buddha truly knows the affairs of past, present, and future, he will not accept the invitation. If he accepts, then he does not know the affairs of past, present, and future."

They then dug the pit, prepared the food, and placed poison in it. Śṛgāla said: "Agreed! Excellent!" He then went and invited the Buddha.

The Buddha said: "Excellent!"

Śṛgāla then told the heterodox practitioners. They said: "He knows nothing. Just dig the pit."

Śṛgāla had a son named Candradhvaja, sixteen years old, who in past lives had studied the buddha-path and could know the affairs of past, present, and future. He said to his father: "The Buddha already knows what the heterodox practitioners have plotted. You need not test him. Do not heed the words of evil people and cast yourself into boiling fire."

Śṛgāla did not believe his son's words. He had the pit dug, fire placed within, the food prepared, and poison added.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Instruct the bhikṣus that tomorrow none may walk ahead of the Buddha. When they go to the meal, they must all follow behind the Buddha." The Buddha sat in meditation on the true path, and all the heavenly kings were stirred.

Śṛgāla sent a person to tell the Buddha: "The time has arrived. May you come to the meal."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Have the bhikṣus don their robes, take their alms-bowls, and follow behind the Buddha."

The bhikṣus received the instruction. They donned their robes, took their bowls, and all followed the Buddha. The heavenly kings and innumerable ghostly spirits all accompanied the Buddha. The Buddha immediately turned the ground to gold, all the way to Śṛgāla's gate. As the Buddha set forth on the road, he released his radiance, which filled Śṛgāla's house with a golden color.

Candradhvaja then said to his father: "Now the Buddha has set forth on the road. In all ten directions it is bright as the sun. The Buddha, with the bhikṣus and innumerable devas, has set out together. The Buddha with the bhikṣus and innumerable devas traveling together is like the moon among the stars. See how bright father's house now is! You need not test the Buddha." Śṛgāla did not heed his son's words.

When the Buddha reached the city gate and his foot stepped upon the gate-threshold, the entire city quaked greatly. The sick were healed. The blind could see, the deaf could hear, the mute could speak. Those afflicted by poison found the poison rendered harmless. The deranged were restored to sanity. The hunched could stand straight. The wrathful became joyful. Harps and instruments sounded without being played. The jeweled ornaments of the women all made sounds of their own accord. The hundred birds and livestock cried together in harmony.

When the Buddha arrived at Śṛgāla's gate, the five-zhang fire pit was transformed into a bathing pool. Within it grew lotus flowers, each with a thousand petals. The lotuses upon which the disciples stepped each produced a hundred petals. All walked upon the lotuses.

Śṛgāla, seeing the fire pit transformed into a pool of water with lotus flowers growing within, was greatly terrified. He bowed with his head to the ground and confessed: "The meal I prepared for the Buddha is all laced with poison. I beg to be allowed to withdraw it and prepare fresh food."

The Buddha said: "There is no need to cook again. Bring the poisoned food and I will eat it." The Buddha took his seat, and all the bhikṣus took their seats.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Instruct the bhikṣus not to eat yet. Wait for the Buddha's command before eating."

The Buddha then recited this spell: "In all the world there are three poisons: first, the poison of lust; second, the poison of anger; third, the poison of ignorance. The Buddha has none of these three poisons; therefore poison cannot affect him. For one who sincerely upholds the sūtra Dharma, poison also cannot take effect. For those bhikṣus whose path is upright, poison also cannot take effect." When the Buddha's words were finished, the poison in the food all vanished.

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Tell the bhikṣus they may all eat. The poison has already gone."

When the Buddha and the bhikṣus had all finished eating, Śṛgāla came forward and knelt. He bowed with his head at the Buddha's feet and said to the Buddha: "I have acted improperly, heeding the words of evil people who led me to do evil. I beg the Buddha to have compassion and forgive my transgression."

The Buddha then taught the Dharma to Śṛgāla, and he attained the first fruit of stream-entry. Śṛgāla then received the five precepts and became a layman: first, not to kill; second, not to steal; third, not to violate another person's wife; fourth, not to engage in double-tongued speech; fifth, not to drink wine.

After receiving the five precepts, Śṛgāla bowed with his head at the Buddha's feet and made obeisance to the Buddha.
