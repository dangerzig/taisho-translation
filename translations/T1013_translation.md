# 阿難陀目佉尼呵離陀經

## The Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1013

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling in Vaiśālī, among the great nyagrodha trees. There was a monastery called Kūṭāgāra, and he was together with a great assembly of three hundred thousand mahā-bhikṣus. At that time the Buddha wished to relinquish his bodily life; he had set a period of three months before he would enter parinirvāṇa.

Then the Buddha told the Worthy Mahāmaudgalyāyana to go throughout the great trichiliocosm and summon all the śrāvakas, those of the pratyekabuddha lineage, and those who had set forth on the Mahāyāna, to come and gather at the assembly. Maudgalyāyana immediately received the instruction, prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and departed. Through his own merit, he lifted one foot and stepped upon the summit of Mount Sumeru. Relying on the Buddha's divine power, he sat down and reflected: "What samādhi should I enter so that the great trichiliocosm will hear my invitation?" Immediately the samādhi arose as he conceived it, and all in the ten directions heard his voice. At that time a million bhikṣus came to gather at the monastery.

The Worthy Śāriputra then thought: "I too should enter samādhi to learn the locations, near and far, of all the bhikṣus in Jambudvīpa and cause them to assemble." It happened just as he intended, and all assembled simultaneously, their locations known. At that time four hundred thousand bhikṣus came to gather at the monastery.

The Buddha then addressed Akṣayamati Bodhisattva, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Vigata-duṣkara Bodhisattva, Apagata-sarva-duḥkha-tamas Bodhisattva, Sarva-āvaraṇa-vigata Bodhisattva, Sarva-īśvara Bodhisattva, Avalokiteśvara-mahāghoṣa-viśva-darśana-samanta-sukhā Bodhisattva, Gandhahastin Bodhisattva, Ekavāk-koṭi-niyuta-ghoṣa Bodhisattva, and Maitreya Bodhisattva: "Go forth to the buddha-lands of the ten directions, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and summon all who have generated the bodhisattva aspiration: those who have already attained avaivartika and those who have not, those who have already attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas and those who have not. Cause them all to come and gather at the Kūṭāgāra Monastery."

The ten bodhisattvas each received the Buddha's instruction, prostrated, and departed, traveling to the buddha-lands of the ten directions as numerous as the sands of the Ganges. At that time, eighty billion hundred-thousand bodhisattvas who were one-birth-from-buddhahood came to the assembly, along with a billion hundred-thousand avaivartika bodhisattvas, thirty billion bodhisattvas who had attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, and sixty billion bodhisattvas of pure understanding and liberation. Those who had first generated the great aspiration and those abiding in accordance with the dharma were incalculable. All were of one kind: relying on the Buddha's divine power, each flew from the ten directions and arrived here. They prostrated before the Buddha, and all sat together in the assembly.

At that time, Śāriputra saw the varied ranks of the bodhisattva assembly, each dwelling at different levels, yet all arriving as one kind, flying together to the gathering. He was startled and awed, saying: "This must be brought about by the Buddha's merit and divine power. For what reason have they gathered thus?"

Śāriputra then rose, straightened his robes, prostrated before the Buddha, knelt, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "My doubts and amazement I now wish to bring before the Buddha. May the bodhisattvas assembled here attain direct faith. Having heard what the Buddha speaks, may each attain their proper place. May the sūtras and dharma spoken by buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges be heard equally by all and attained in purity. May countless people with all manner of questions all be able to give answers. What dharma should one practice and abide in, never losing one's aspiration, to swiftly attain the unsurpassed, equal, most perfectly awakened path?"

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! Śāriputra! That you ask this meaning on behalf of the bodhisattva mahāsattvas is truly wonderful!" The Buddha said: "Listen attentively and well uphold these words. I shall deeply expound and explain for you, so that all who hear may swiftly attain the unsurpassed, equal, most perfectly awakened path."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "These bodhisattvas have already attained and should practice this dhāraṇī chapter, for it has already illuminated all dharmas and serves as a guide for all dharmas. Why? Because it opens the way for all beings."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva who practices four things attains this dharma. What are the four? First, one's bodily conduct is always careful and disciplined; second, one's speech is always sincere and truthful; third, one's thoughts are always gentle and compliant; fourth, one uses skillful means to rescue and protect all beings. These are the four things through which one attains this dharma."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva who has another four things attains the inexhaustible empty-body wisdom. What are the four? First, abiding in purity, burning away all hardships, dissolving the defilements of doubt, and liberating all beings of the world. Second, abiding in purity, upholding the sūtras and dharma to guide and benefit all. Third, abiding in purity, maintaining one's accomplished merit to enrich and benefit all. Fourth, purifying all buddha-lands, helping all beings transcend and attain the buddha-dharma. These are the four things: the empty-body, pure-abiding wisdom is inexhaustible."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva who has another four things upholds the dhāraṇī-gateway, enters into saṃsāra, and swiftly attains it. What are the four? First, the dhāraṇī-gateway, by means of the world's name and form, quickly attains the pure dharma. Second, the dhāraṇī-gateway enters into patience. Third, the dhāraṇī-gateway enters into the roots of all thoughts. Fourth, the dhāraṇī-gateway enters into the dharma of all wholesome and unwholesome actions, and swiftly attains it. These are the four things through which one swiftly attains this dhāraṇī-gateway."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva who wishes to swiftly attain the Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī should first learn and understand the forty-eight names. What are the forty-eight?

"Unconditioned, non-acting, universal-gate, energetic, quiescent, illuminating, path-following, constantly mindful, as mindfully contemplated, wondrous control, wondrous phrase, having mind without thought, mind without anything upon which mind rests, liberated practitioner, unmoving, the other beyond, without liberation, without arising, without defilement, practice-adorning, pure renown, existence and non-existence, unobstructed, harmonized concentration, far-reaching radiance, action-conjunction, supremely brave, greatly brave, praised phrases, immovable, unmoving, equal movement, sequentially firm, abiding in truth, unobstructed practice, universally honored, energetic practice, Sumeru-abiding, sparse-yet-firm, strong power, attaining strong power, great radiance, perpetual illumination, reaching all, protecting, unbroken, all-inclusive dhāraṇī gate.

"The buddhas of the present have spoken these forty-eight names of the divine dhāraṇī."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva contemplates and studies the dhāraṇī, regarding both what is liberated and what is not liberated, one does not form such thoughts, neither knowing nor comprehending. Within it there is nothing to increase and nothing to decrease. One does not see liberation nor non-liberation, does not see arising nor ceasing, does not see future, past, or present, does not see coming nor going. One does not seek the Buddha nor turn the mind. One does not contemplate marks, does not contemplate the kinds of excellences, does not contemplate the retinue. There are no precepts, no samādhi, no wisdom, no liberation, no seeing of liberation-wisdom. There is no seated practice, no attainment, no removal of defilement. Neither wisdom nor non-wisdom. Neither instruction nor purification of all. Neither self nor person. Neither conditioned dharmas nor vigor. Neither practice nor purity. Neither body nor mind nor speech. Neither future nor past nor present. Neither for one's own sake nor for another's.

"Thus, Śāriputra! This dharma is called the supreme dharma among all dharmas. It both gathers all dharmas and enters into all dharmas. This is called mindfulness of the buddha-dharma. This dhāraṇī is called the fierce abbreviation that gathers all dharmas. It is called the clear understanding chapter that purifies all beings and fulfills all wishes. It is called the samādhi of awakened-mind-and-hearing. It is called the self-completion of fundamental merit. It is called the dharma-seed treasury that arouses the roots of all intentions, producing the supremely honored marks and excellences of adorned dignity that none can shake. Even if the heavenly māras come, they cannot destroy it. Nothing can seize it, nothing can approach it.

"Thus, Śāriputra! If any bodhisattva hears the Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī, they have already attained avaivartika and the unsurpassed, equal path. Why? From this dhāraṇī, the practices of all buddhas are completely fulfilled. It acts for all beings, destroying the five destinies of birth, old age, sickness, death, and all the bonds of doubt, and swiftly attains the dhāraṇī."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Do not merely think 'dharmas are empty';
> Do not lightly speak of attainment or non-attainment.
> Believe directly in the dharma without mid-course doubt,
> And you will swiftly attain the dhāraṇī.
> Beware of being bound by this very emptiness;
> Discerning emptiness, one attains buddhahood quickly.
> This essential wisdom has no shore or bottom;
> Swiftly draw near to the dhāraṇī.
> The bodhisattva who holds the dhāraṇī
> Gathers and embraces all dharma principles.
> Hearing the path-teaching of the buddhas of the ten directions,
> And all wisdom, one attains them all.
> Understanding the nirhāra-dhāraṇī
> Is like the sun's great radiance.
> Receiving the buddhas' names and definitive dharma,
> One purely receives, holds, and gains dharma-understanding.
> This mukhā-dhāraṇī
> Manifests all things before one.
> Among all dharmas it is the supreme;
> It equally protects the people of the world.
> If the beings of the ten directions, human and non-human,
> Were to question wisdom's meaning for an entire kalpa,
> One could resolve all their doubts;
> When the kalpa ends, such wisdom is not exhausted.
> If one receives and trusts in the sūtra-path,
> And with vigor upholds the central, right dharma,
> One draws near to the stage one-birth-from-buddhahood
> And becomes a true dharma-prince.
> If one holds this dhāraṇī,
> It is compassion for all people.
> Renown pervading Jambudvīpa,
> Praised by the World-Honored One.
> The one who holds this sūtra
> At the end of life will surely see
> Eighty billion koṭi of buddhas
> Extending their hands to receive them into the honored place.
> If one has studied this dhāraṇī,
> Throughout thousands of billions of myriads of kalpas
> Whatever sins, evils, and darkness one has created
> Are all extinguished within a single month.
> If a bodhisattva creates a field of merit
> Tirelessly through myriads of billions of kalpas,
> It does not compare to studying this dhāraṇī:
> A single month equals all of that.
> Having fully received and recited the dhāraṇī,
> One attains all samādhis and wisdom.
> Having attained, the mind never regresses;
> One will become a buddha with a buddha-land.
> If one makes offerings to this sūtra,
> Even if all the three realms become māra's domain,
> Wishing to harass and destroy one's mind,
> They will never be able to shake this person.
> All emptiness is clearly understood;
> From this, numberless buddhas are born.
> Such words are without difference;
> The dhāraṇī is inexhaustible.
> In this very place, hearing this matter,
> Dīpaṃkara gave me the prediction.
> Buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges
> At that time I immediately saw them all.
> Sincerely receive and uphold this sūtra chapter;
> All wishes will be swiftly attained.
> Entering the dharma of all buddha-lands,
> According with what is appropriate, one can guide and save.
> Always purifying all buddha-lands,
> Among the sangha there is no flaw or stain.
> If one asks about the names of the deep and subtle dharma,
> This sūtra chapter can fully answer.
> All sensual indulgence has been exhausted;
> In contemplation, the seven factors of awakening are present.
> Eighty billion buddhas
> All bestow the dhāraṇī upon one.
> Always still the mind and do not think wrongly;
> What should not be thought, take care not to think.
> Thinking thus, not failing to think:
> The merit one later attains is immeasurable.
> Always understand the matters of this sūtra;
> Take care never to have the fox's doubt.
> Like one who enters the great ocean,
> One should never say treasures are hard to find.
> Receiving the merit of devas and humans' bliss,
> Such things are never far away.
> One will also swiftly attain buddhahood;
> Therefore, let mindfulness never depart."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva who practices four things swiftly attains the dhāraṇī dharma. What are the four? First, distance oneself from worldly desires and cravings. Second, do not speak of others' strengths and weaknesses, and do not take advantage of others. Third, when anyone makes a request, do not go against their wishes; give without reservation and without later regret. Fourth, day and night, with vigor, always aspire to seek all dharmas. These are the four."

"Śāriputra! If a bodhisattva practices these, one swiftly attains the dhāraṇī."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Distance yourself from all evils and from sensual desires;
> Creating sins leads to falling into hell.
> Without the three poisons, calamity does not arise;
> Renounce what you cherish and attain this sūtra.
> Do not be jealous or envious of others;
> Do not deceive your own kin and relatives.
> Always regard all with an equal mind;
> The body will become greatly dignified.
> Always abandon bodily pleasures;
> In the world, be without anger or dispute.
> Do not engage in contentious affairs with others,
> And you will draw near to the dhāraṇī.
> Morning and night, always study with diligence;
> Among non-Buddhist paths, place no hopes.
> Always seek the supreme dharma in this way,
> And the dhāraṇī will naturally manifest."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva who practices four things swiftly attains this dhāraṇī. What are the four? First, dwell alone in a secluded place. Second, abide in patience with the profound dharma. Third, when receiving gifts, do not take delight in them. Fourth, do not cherish one's own body and life, how much less material wealth. These are the four."

"Śāriputra! If a bodhisattva practices these, one attains the dhāraṇī."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "In a secluded place, contemplate the right dharma;
> Do not treat others with contempt.
> Birth and death blaze like a fire on one's head;
> Having attained a human body, one should practice.
> Always keep the mind in deep patience;
> Regarding expenses, sufficiency is enough.
> Rising from the ancestral hall with an equal mind,
> Do not be partial toward your own lineage.
> Simply make offerings to the Buddha and the Dharma;
> Abiding in wholesome power, transcend the world's difficulties.
> Then shave the head and cut the hair,
> And attain merit, entering the subtle and wondrous.
> Know emptiness and worldly affairs;
> For this reason, abandon all possessions.
> The foolish practitioner who craves profit
> Has neither precepts, patience, nor wisdom."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva who practices another four things attains this dhāraṇī. What are the four?

"First, there are eight syllables that open entry into the understanding of wisdom. What are the eight? The first is named *pa*: it causes all dharmas of attachment to enter the dharma of emptiness. The second is named *ra*: whether the tathāgata manifests marks and excellences or does not, with his body he enters all dharmas. The third is named *bha*: whether the dharma of ignorance or the dharma of wisdom, it causes entry into the dharma of understanding and wisdom. The fourth is named *ka*: knowing where all calamities and sins arise, it is able to cause entry into the merit and blessing. The fifth is named *ja*: knowing birth, old age, sickness, and death, one enters therein into formlessness. The sixth is named *da*: observing all the grounds of the world as empty, without being startled or amazed, entering the wishless. The seventh is named *śa*: bringing all dharmas to rest, causing entry into the dharma of cool tranquility. The eighth is named *kṣa*: knowing that all dharmas are empty. These are the eight.

"There are four more things: first, constantly contemplate the eight syllables. Second, if one copies this sūtra, copy it carefully and correctly. Third, if one holds this sūtra, hold it carefully, practicing such careful holding and reading up to the fifteenth day. Fourth, practice in accordance with the dharma, contemplating the eight syllables. These are the four."

"Śāriputra! A bodhisattva should practice in this way to swiftly attain the dhāraṇī."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always contemplate the eight syllables;
> Copy, hold the chapter, and sincerely practice.
> On the fifteenth day, recite without ceasing;
> Encourage all to seek the buddha-path.
> One must learn to open and guide others;
> Attaining wisdom, one swiftly draws near the Buddha.
> One sees everywhere the buddhas of the ten directions,
> All standing manifest before one."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva who has another four things and contemplates the dhāraṇī attains the dharma's benefits. What are the four? First, one is always remembered by the world-honored ones of the ten directions. Second, whatever one does, māra cannot destroy one's aspiration. Third, the sins of past lives are all extinguished. Fourth, one's sincere and truthful speech is never cut off, and one can resolve all questions that arise. These are the four."

"Śāriputra! These are the bodhisattva's benefits from attaining the dhāraṇī dharma."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always remembered by all buddhas,
> Māra cannot find an opening.
> Whatever sins one has created are immediately extinguished;
> One resolves all doubts for all."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "In the distant past, through innumerable kalpas, doubled and redoubled beyond measure, in that immeasurably long and incalculable time, there was a buddha in the world named Ratna-saṃpad-guṇa-caryā-rāja Tathāgata, who had crossed beyond the four paths and was not subject to them, who was equally and perfectly awakened, at ease among devas above and below, honored among devas. That buddha's teaching of the dharma for all beings was unsurpassed.

"At the time of his parinirvāṇa, there was a cakravartin king named Dhāru who ruled the four continents. His son was named Asmṛti Yaśo-guṇa-saṃpad, and was sixteen years old. At that time the prince went to the Buddha and heard that buddha expound the dhāraṇī sūtra. Having heard it, he rejoiced and immediately attained this sūtra. Having attained it, he recited, studied, constantly contemplated, and upheld it with vigor. Without sleeping, his side never touching a bed, for seven thousand years; without thinking of desires for seven thousand years; without thinking of wealth for seven thousand years; without asking about other affairs for seven thousand years; always dwelling alone in one place with unmoving mind for seven thousand years.

"At that time he heard the wisdom-dharma spoken by ninety-nine billion incalculable buddhas. Having heard it, he abandoned his kingdom and kingship and became a śramaṇa for ninety-nine thousand years, practicing the Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī, and also teaching it to all. In a single lifetime he taught and guided the people of the world, establishing eighty billion myriads of nayutas of people in the aspiration for *anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi*, bringing them to attain avaivartika.

"At that time there was also a householder's son named Candradhvaja, who amid the great assembly heard this dhāraṇī and, having heard it, encouraged others and helped them uphold it. Through the merit of such encouragement and assistance, he traveled to ninety myriads of billions of buddha-lands, made offerings to all buddhas, and established merit. In all those buddha-lands he attained all dhāraṇīs and immediately reached the stage of one-birth-from-buddhahood, becoming a flying bodhisattva. The Buddha told the bodhisattvas: 'Throughout three kalpas he made offerings to all those buddhas. After the three kalpas, he attained the unsurpassed, equal, most perfectly awakened path.'

"That householder's son Candradhvaja is Dīpaṃkara Buddha. King Dhāru's son, Asmṛti Yaśo-guṇa-saṃpad, is Amitābha Buddha."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "In the Bhadra-kalpa, I and the bodhisattvas spoke this dhāraṇī. Those who heard this sūtra, rejoiced on behalf of others, and encouraged all: the merit of their encouragement and assistance enabled them to leap beyond four hundred thousand kalpas without further hardship. Later they will attain the path and fulfill their aspirations at will. Furthermore, having made offerings to ninety myriads of buddhas, they will attain the unsurpassed, equal, most perfectly awakened path."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "If any bodhisattva, even the last to hear and attain this dhāraṇī, upholds it, encourages others, and rejoices on their behalf, the merit is as described. If any bodhisattva, through the merit of joyful encouragement, with a compassionate heart, sheds tears and has their body-hair stand on end because of this sūtra, they will all attain avaivartika, being drawn onto the unsurpassed, equal path. Whether through expedient wisdom one copies it, holds it, studies it, recites it, or reads it, Śāriputra, the merit of such people is immeasurable, incalculable, inexpressible. No person is able to reckon or fathom it."

The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "If, having heard, one adds encouragement and assistance,
> Or copies, holds, recites, and reads:
> Let the devas and humans reckon such merit;
> None can know how vast is the blessing.
> Wherever one is born, one always sees the Buddha;
> In the profound sūtras, one attains wisdom-confirmation.
> Firmly holding the dharma, none can shake one;
> One swiftly attains clear understanding and awakening.
> The mind always settled, neither increasing nor decreasing;
> Truly never losing the dhāraṇī.
> Without form or thought, one establishes marks and excellences,
> Becoming the hero of the world, the unsurpassed.
> The householder's son who heard this sūtra,
> Wherever born, knew his past lives.
> He always saw innumerable honored ones;
> As he wished, he immediately became a buddha.
> If you wish to swiftly become a buddha
> And quickly be free from all the māras,
> Wishing to attain the marks of a hundred merits:
> Always be mindful of this, and attainment will not be long.
> If one filled buddha-lands as numerous as the Ganges' sands
> With precious treasures and gave them in charity,
> It would not compare to one who copies and holds this;
> No comparison could ever match it."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "If any bodhisattva holds the mind in contemplation and studies the dhāraṇī, there is a mountain named Himavān where eight spirit-deities dwell. They constantly together protect and guard, opening the person's aspiration. Who are the eight? The first is named the Spirit of Courageous Strength, the second the Spirit Illuminating-the-Ten-Directions, the third the Spirit of Great Benefit, the fourth the Dragon-King Spirit of Great Power, the fifth the Spirit of Sincere Practice, the sixth the Spirit Able-to-Tame-the-Untamed, the seventh the Youth Spirit, and the eighth the Spirit of Swift Arms. These are the eight spirit names.

"If one who studies this sūtra wishes these spirits to come, one should purify oneself with bathing, wear new garments, and when walking, always hold a compassionate heart toward all beings throughout all kalpas. Then with a focused mind, recite the dhāraṇī. One should firmly receive and hold in accordance with this sūtra, and one will swiftly gain understanding and wisdom. At that time the spirits will stand before one."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "There are also eight bodhisattvas in the desire-heavens who constantly and equally protect the people and bestow the sūtra-path. Who are the eight? The first is named the Celestial Son Illuminating-the-Ten-Directions, the second the Celestial Son World-Radiance, the third the Celestial Son Wisdom-Light, the fourth the Celestial Son Sunlight, the fifth the Celestial Son Supreme-Examination, the sixth the Celestial Son Wish-Fulfilled, the seventh the Celestial Son Star-King, and the eighth the Celestial Son Practice-Cultivation. These are the eight celestial sons' names.

"They constantly and equally protect the people and encourage mindfulness in those who hold this sūtra, enabling them to swiftly settle the mind and attain the dhāraṇī. One who seeks this sūtra should always concentrate on contemplating the dhāraṇī, teaching the single dharma and practicing it without violating the sūtra's precepts. One's speech should be sincere and truthful, and one's conduct likewise. One should always be filial and respectful toward all elders, regarding all people as no different from the Buddha. One should not take lightly what is cherished and honored. Always be mindful of returning kindness, and one will obtain good reward and attain the eye of deep patience, as well as the vision of the dharma of non-arising."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, bodhisattvas as numerous as thirty-two times the sands of the Ganges all attained the dhāraṇī and established themselves on the avaivartika ground. Sixty billion celestial beings attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas. Thirty-two thousand devas and humans generated the aspiration for *anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi*. At that time the great trichiliocosm trembled in six ways, heavenly flowers rained down, and celestial harps and instruments sounded of themselves without being struck.

Then Śāriputra came forward and asked the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is this sūtra called? How should it be practiced?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "This sūtra is called the Ānandamukhanirhāridhāraṇī. It swiftly enables people to attain omniscience."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, Śāriputra and the innumerable bodhisattvas of the ten directions, devas and humans, gandharvas, asuras, and world-protectors were all joyful. They came forward, prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and departed.
