# 蟻喻經
## Sūtra of the Ant-Hill Simile

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0095

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護)

---

At that time, the World-Honored One radiated great light and illuminated all around, then addressed the assembly of bhikṣus: "You should know: at one time there were ant hills that emitted smoke at night and burst into flame by day. A brāhmaṇa, seeing this, spoke thus: 'If someone holds a quick and sharp blade, they will surely be able to break apart this mound.' Having said this, next he saw a great turtle. The brāhmaṇa also spoke thus: 'If someone holds a quick and sharp blade, they will surely be able to destroy it.' Next he saw water-mother worms, then a leech, then āśīviṣa worms, then a great snake, then a ruddha-patha worm, then a kavāṭa worm. The brāhmaṇa, having seen all these creatures, spoke as before about each. Finally he saw a great dragon. The brāhmaṇa said: 'As for what I have seen, what does it mean? Only the Buddha, the World-Honored One, can fully understand.' He then went to a bhikṣu and presented these matters in full, saying: 'Bhikṣu! Please ask the Buddha about this on my behalf, so that my doubts may be resolved! Whatever the Buddha says, I shall remember and uphold. Why? Bhikṣu! I do not see among gods, humans, śramaṇas, brāhmaṇas, or any in the world anyone who could ask the Buddha about this meaning. Therefore doubting minds cannot be resolved.'

"Then that bhikṣu, following those words, came to where I was. Having arrived, he prostrated at my feet, withdrew to one side, and presented those matters in full. He then asked: 'As for the ant hills that the brāhmaṇa saw, what does it mean? The smoke emitted at night and the flames by day, what do these signify? And the great turtle, the water-mother worm, the leech, the āśīviṣa worm, the great snake, the ruddha-patha worm, the kavāṭa worm, and the great dragon: what are the characteristics of all these things seen? And who is this brāhmaṇa? Why is it said that a sharp blade can break them apart? May the Buddha explain!'

"Bhikṣus! At that time I told that bhikṣu: 'The ant hill is the body of the five aggregates of all sentient beings. The smoke emitted at night is the mental seeking and deliberation that sentient beings give rise to. The flames by day are the bodily and verbal actions that sentient beings undertake in accordance with their seeking and deliberation. The great turtle is the five hindrances, the defiling dharmas. The water-mother worm is anger and resentment. The leech is miserliness and jealousy. The āśīviṣa worm is the dharmas of the five desires. The snake is ignorance. The ruddha-patha worm is doubt and confusion. The kavāṭa worm is the conceit of self. The dragon is the arhats. The brāhmaṇa is the Tathāgata, the arhat, the one of right and universal awakening. The sharp and quick one is a person of wisdom. The blade is wisdom. Breaking apart is the arousing of vigorous and excellent practice.'

"Bhikṣus! What do you think? What was seen, stated briefly, is nothing other than the body of the five aggregates of all sentient beings: the kalala and other stages, born from the impurity of father and mother, composed of the four great elements, a false physical form, coarse, vile, and weak, an accumulation of suffering, ultimately subject to destruction. Yet sentient beings cannot perceive this. Day and night they give rise to seeking and deliberation, and their bodily and verbal actions are unskillfully performed. They are covered and obstructed by the five hindrances and afflictions, attached to the five desires, increasing ignorance, giving rise to the conceit of self, doubtful and irresolute regarding the sacred Dharma, with anger, resentment, miserliness, and jealousy arising moment by moment, never seeking liberation. Therefore the Tathāgata, the arhat, the one of right and universal awakening, wishes to cause all persons of wisdom to arouse vigorous practice, cultivate wisdom, sever all afflictions, and progress toward the realization of the sacred fruit. You bhikṣus, having exhausted all defilements and realized the fruit of arhatship, are therefore said to be like dragons.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus! The Buddhas, World-Honored Ones, of past and future, out of compassion and for the benefit and happiness of all sentient beings, wishing them to sever all afflictions and progress toward the realization of the sacred fruit, have widely taught this meaning to their śrāvaka disciples. Today I too, like all the Buddhas, have taught this to you for these reasons. You bhikṣus! Remembering these matters, whether in the wilderness, in empty dwellings, in the mountains, under trees, in caves, or in hermitages, in all quiet places, you should concentrate your minds, contemplate, and examine this meaning. Do not be heedless or give rise to thoughts of regression. Furthermore, explain, instruct, and teach this to others, that all may practice and obtain great benefit and happiness."

At that time, the World-Honored One, having spoken thus to the bhikṣus, the bhikṣus all faithfully accepted it.
