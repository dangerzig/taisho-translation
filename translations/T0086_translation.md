# 泥犁經
## Sūtra on the Hells

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0086

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "Ordinary people have three things that mark them as foolish and unworthy. What are the three? A fool's thoughts are evil, a fool's words are evil, a fool's deeds are evil. In this very life such a person receives calamity: suffering in body, suffering in mind, and suffering from worry.

"What is suffering from worry? A fool accompanies a wise person, and the wise person explains that the fool's evil deeds will lead to hell after death. The fool then thinks about what the wise person said: 'Now I do evil, and after death I will enter hell.' This is suffering from worry.

"What is suffering in mind? A fool sees a thief taken for seizing another's property, cruelly bound with shackles, hands and feet cut off, beaten with bamboo rods and whips, fed to tigers, or wrapped in straw and set on fire, or chopped to pieces on a block, or thrown to the ground and trampled by elephants, or steamed in a cauldron, or with all four limbs pulled apart alive. The fool then thinks: 'Evildoers who act without shame are punished thus by the authorities. If the authorities were to discover my evil deeds, they would treat me the same way.' This is suffering in mind.

"What is suffering in body? A fool, lying down and rising up morning and evening, is never at peace. The mind constantly thinks of evil, the mouth constantly speaks evil, the body constantly does evil. After such evil, when illness comes, the fool then sees the fiery cauldrons of hell and the people in them, sees people being burned and people being boiled. Past misdeeds gradually come to torment the person, just as a shadow gradually descends after midday. As the person gradually sinks into hell, the evil one thinks: 'While I was in the world, I loved killing, loved stealing, loved committing adultery, loved deceiving others, loved divisive speech, loved harsh words, loved idle talk, loved jealousy, loved miserly greed. I did not believe in the Buddha, did not believe in the sūtras, did not believe that actions have consequences of fortune and misfortune, did not believe in future lives. Now after death I must enter hell.' This is suffering in body."

The Buddha said: "If evil people had eyes like mine and could see the places of punishment and suffering to which evil people go, they would be so terrified that their hearts would crack and burst, vomiting boiling blood and dying."

The Buddha said: "If you wish to know the most unbearable suffering, it is in hell alone. The suffering of hell is so extreme that it cannot be fully described."

The bhikṣus knelt and said: "We wish to hear a parable about the suffering of hell."

The Buddha said: "It is like an officer who captures a traitor and brings the person before the king, saying: 'This person is a rebel who harbored evil thoughts against the nation.' The king orders the officer: 'Pierce this person with a hundred spear wounds.' The next day, the king asks: 'How is this person?' The officer replies: 'Still alive.' The king says: 'Pierce another hundred wounds.' The next day, the king asks again: 'How is this person?' The officer replies: 'Still alive.' The king says: 'Pierce another hundred wounds.'"

The Buddha asked the bhikṣus: "After this person has received three hundred spear wounds, is there a single uninjured spot as large as a jujube leaf?"

The bhikṣus said: "There is not a single uninjured spot."

The Buddha asked the bhikṣus: "Is the pain of three hundred spear wounds severe?"

The bhikṣus said: "Even a single wound causes the whole body to ache. How much more so three hundred!"

The Buddha held a small stone in his hand and showed it to the bhikṣus: "Is this stone large, or is a great mountain large?"

The bhikṣus said: "The stone in the Buddha's hand is small. How can it be compared to a mountain? Even a hundred million times over, it still would not match the mountain's size."

The Buddha said: "The suffering of hell compared to the pain of spear wounds is more than a hundred million times greater. The small stone in my hand is like the three hundred spear wounds, and the mountain is like the suffering in hell. A fool's thoughts are evil, words are evil, deeds are evil. After death, the fool falls into hell.

"In hell there are demon beasts that seize the person, hooking the upper jaw and hooking the lower jaw, prying the mouth wide open, then pouring molten copper into the person's mouth. Lips, tongue, intestines, and stomach are all scorched and burned away; the copper flows down and out. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because in their previous life in the world they sought wealth and profit by dishonest means with food and drink, they are now given molten copper. Such is the suffering of hell.

"The hell demons seize the person, hooking upper and lower jaws, prying the mouth open, and thrusting a red-hot iron pestle into the person's throat. Lips, tongue, intestines, and stomach are all scorched and burned away. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"The hell demons seize the person and force them up an iron mountain. The mountain is heated with fire until it glows red. Running up and running down, the agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"The hell demons seize the person and with red-hot axes chop off hands, feet, and every joint, severing each one. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"The hell demons seize the person and with an iron adze hew the body, stripping all flesh and bone entirely. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"In hell there are birds with iron beaks that peck open people's heads and eat their brains. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"In hell there are also packs of wild beasts that seize and devour people, gnawing and biting. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"The hell demons seize the person, flay the skin from both shoulders down to the ribs with a knife, and use the rib flesh to yoke an iron chariot, forcing the person to pull it while running over fire. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"The hell demons seize the person by both feet and throw them upside down into a cauldron of boiling water that surges and roils. At the bottom they are cooked, at the top they are cooked, tossed up and down without any part left uncooked. It is like cooking beans, cooked whether covered or exposed, cooked above and below; the people in hell are cooked everywhere, with no east, west, above, or below spared. In their previous life in the world, they indulged their mind, indulged their mouth, indulged their body, and this is the consequence. Such is the suffering of hell.

"The hell demons seize the person, lay them on red-hot ground, and torment them with the five punishments. A red-hot nail is driven through the left palm, another through the right palm, another through the right foot, another through the left foot, and another through the heart, all the way down through to the ground. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"The hell demons drag people by the arm into the iron city of hell. The iron city is perfectly square, with gates on all four sides. On all four sides the walls are firm, and the gates all have demon guards. The walls and ground of the city are all iron. The city is roofed with iron so that nothing can escape. The ground is heated until it glows red. The city is four thousand *li* in circumference. Flames from the east wall reach to the west wall; flames from the west wall reach to the east wall; flames from the south wall reach to the north wall; flames from the north wall reach to the south wall; flames from above reach down to the ground; flames from the ground reach up above. All evil persons who have committed any of these ten transgressions fall into this place: those who kill, those who steal, those who commit adultery, those who deceive, those who speak divisively, those who use harsh speech, those who speak idly, those who are jealous, those who are miserly and greedy, and those who do not believe in the Buddha, do not believe in the sūtras, and do not believe that actions have consequences. Such people fill hell.

"After tens of millions of years of extreme agony in hell, the person sees the eastern gate open in the distance and runs toward it. Where their feet touch the ground, the flesh is scorched; when they lift their feet, the flesh grows back as before. Those whose time has come to be released pass through and escape. Those whose time has not come find the gate closed again. Seeing others escape while they cannot, they stare in despair and collapse on the ground. The gate-keeping demon says: 'You wretched dead! You have come to my gate. What do you seek?' They say: 'I am hungry and thirsty.' The demon then hooks their upper and lower jaws, pries the mouth open, and pours molten copper into their mouth. Lips, tongue, intestines, and stomach are all scorched and burned; the copper flows down and out. In their previous life in the world, they sought wealth and profit by dishonest means, and the evil they committed is why they receive this punishment. Such is the suffering of hell.

"There is another hell, like the charcoal furnaces of the world, glowing red, extending thousands of *li*. People all run out of the city and enter the charcoal-fire hell, scorched and burned without rest. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"Next they enter the ice hell, extending thousands of *li*. People who enter it are all frozen, trembling, shattered, and cracked apart. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"Next they enter the boiling-excrement hell, extending thousands of *li* in circumference. The excrement is boiling hot and steaming. People rush toward it. Those who enter it are cooked within. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person cannot die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"Next they enter the pus-and-blood hell, extending thousands of *li* in circumference. The stench is beyond words. The pus and blood all boil. Those who fall in are cooked and decomposed, their bodies ruined and decayed, eaten by ravens and birds. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person cannot die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"Next they enter the razor mountain, extending thousands of *li* in circumference. Running from the pus-and-death hell, they try to climb the mountain. On the mountain there are blades that slash their feet; when they try to hold on, the blades slash their hands; when they try to go forward, the blades slash their bellies; when they try to lean back, the blades slash their backs; when they try to sit, the blades slash their buttocks; when they try to lean to the side, the blades slash their ribs. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"Next they enter the sword trees. The tree branches are all like swords. People who enter are pierced in the chest, pierced in the ribs, pierced in the back, pierced in the hands, pierced in the feet, pierced in the body, front and back, all the way through. The agonizing pain is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"Next they enter the iron bamboo grove, extending thousands of *li*. The tree leaves are all like sharp blades. When people enter, the wind blows and the bamboo shakes. The leaves pierce through their flesh and sever their bones. The body has no uninjured spot. The painful agony is unbearable. Because past evils are not yet expiated, the person does not die. Such is the suffering of hell.

"Next there is the salt-water hell, extending thousands of *li*. The salt water is as salty as brine, boiling hot and surging. In the water there are birds with iron beaks that peck through flesh and bite through bone. Unable to endure the pain, people try to cross the water and leave. The hell-guarding demon says: 'You wretched dead! What do you seek?' The person says: 'I suffer from hunger and thirst.' The demon then hooks their upper and lower jaws, pries the mouth open, and pours molten copper into their mouth. Lips, tongue, and throat are all scorched; all the five organs in the belly are burned away. The copper flows down and out. Unable to endure any longer, the person goes back into the boiling salt water. The painful agony is as before, and unable to endure, they go back into the iron bamboo grove. The painful agony is as before, and unable to endure, they go back into the sword trees. The painful agony is as before, and unable to endure, they go back into the razor mountain. The painful agony is as before, and unable to endure, they go back into the pus-and-blood hell. The painful agony is as before, and unable to endure, they go back into the boiling-excrement hell. The painful agony is as before, and unable to endure, they go back into the charcoal-fire hell. The painful agony is as before, and unable to endure, they go back into the iron city of hell, where the painful agony is unbearable. The suffering at the eastern gate is thus; the suffering at the southern gate is thus; the suffering at the western gate is thus; the suffering at the northern gate is thus. Such is the suffering of hell."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "The sufferings of hell are countless. I have only given you a brief and rough account."

The Buddha said: "Those who do evil are reborn among animals, feeding on grass and straw, licking with their tongues and grinding with their teeth. What are those who lick and grind? Cattle, horses, mules, donkeys, elephants, camels, and their kind, such as these many creatures. In their previous lives in the world, their thoughts were evil, their words were evil, their deeds were evil. After death, through many transformations, they become these animals. Such is their suffering."

The Buddha said: "There are creatures born in darkness, growing in darkness, and dying in darkness. What are those born in darkness? Snakes, rats, badgers, otters, worms, ants, and their kind, such as these many creatures. In their previous lives in the world, their thoughts were evil, their words were evil, their deeds were evil. After death, through many transformations, they become these creatures. Such is their suffering."

The Buddha said: "There are scaly creatures born in water, growing in water, and dying in water. What are those born in water? Sea serpents, dragons, fish, turtles, soft-shelled turtles, alligators, and their kind, such as these many creatures. In their previous lives in the world, their thoughts were evil, their words were evil, their deeds were evil. After death, through many transformations, they become these scaly creatures. Such is their suffering."

The Buddha said: "There are vermin born in filth, dying in filth, and growing in filth. What are those born in filth? Worms of damp places, worms of ditches, worms of latrines, and their kind, such as these many creatures. In their previous lives in the world, their thoughts were evil, their words were evil, their deeds were evil. After death they become these vermin. Such is their suffering."

The Buddha said: "There are creatures that mainly feed on what is impure. When a person relieves themselves, they smell the stench from afar and run toward it, saying, 'I have found food.' What are those that mainly feed on what is impure? Dogs, pigs, flies, dung beetles, and creatures of foul and filthy things, such as these many creatures. In their previous lives in the world, their thoughts were evil, their words were evil, their deeds were evil. After death, through many transformations, they become these creatures. Such is their suffering."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Animals are very numerous. I have only given you a brief and rough account."

The Buddha said: "Those who do evil and are reborn in the realm of hungry ghosts always feed on boiling excrement and urine. Why do they always feed on boiling excrement and urine? In their previous lives in the world, their thoughts were evil, their words were evil, their deeds were evil. Being miserly and stingy with food and drink, they end up in the hungry ghost realm.

"Furthermore, there are hungry ghosts who feed on pus and blood. In their previous lives in the world, they did evil and craved fine flavors; now they feed on pus and blood.

"Among the hungry ghosts there are black dogs and white dogs that mainly feed on the flesh of the hungry ghosts. Among the hungry ghosts there are ravens that mainly feed on their brains.

"Some have not seen water for ten years, and some for a hundred years have never obtained water. Sometimes they see a flowing stream, perfectly clear, and rush toward it to drink, but the water is suddenly gone. Sometimes there is water, but it turns to molten copper. Sometimes it is boiling salt water like scalding liquid. When they try to approach and drink, demons beat them. Such is the suffering of the hungry ghost realm."

The Buddha said: "Hungry ghosts are very numerous. I have only given you a rough account."

The Buddha said: "A person in the three evil destinies finds it very difficult to escape. It is like an ocean, eighty-four thousand *li* in circumference, in which there is one blind turtle. On the surface of the water floats a single piece of wood with one hole in it. Once every hundred years the turtle surfaces. Can it happen to put its head through the hole in the wood?"

The bhikṣus said: "Even in hundreds of thousands of tens of thousands of years, it would be difficult. Why? Sometimes the wood is in the east while the turtle is in the west; sometimes the wood is in the west while the turtle comes up in the east; sometimes the wood is in the south while the turtle comes up in the north; sometimes the wood is in the north while the turtle comes up in the south; sometimes the turtle comes up and the wood has been blown to shore by the wind."

"The turtle surfaces once in a hundred years, and yet there are still times when its head enters the hole. For a person in the three evil destinies, the difficulty of becoming human again exceeds even this turtle's difficulty. Why? The people in the three evil destinies have no knowledge, no laws or order, no understanding of good and evil, no understanding of parents, and no understanding of generosity. They devour one another, the strong feeding on the weak. Such people's bodies are never free from the slaughtering, flaying, pus, blood, and wounds. Going from suffering into suffering, from darkness into darkness: this is what evil people experience."

The Buddha said: "It is like a gambler who at first loses much, until he has lost wife, children, fields, and home, left destitute and naked with nothing remaining, and still owing more. The creditor presses hard, smoking and roasting the debtor."

The Buddha said: "Such a gambler's losses are still comparatively small. At first losing much, losing wife, children, fields, and home, then still owing more and being smoked and roasted by others, this is but one lifetime of poverty, and the remaining years can be counted. But a person whose thoughts are evil, words are evil, and deeds are evil, who after death enters the three evil destinies, suffers worse than this poverty. In the three evil destinies, the time is immeasurable. Even if one is released from the three evil destinies and born again among humans, one will be born in an artisan's family, or among the poor and destitute in the wilderness who beg for a living, or among those who support themselves by manual crafts, unable to feed themselves fully or obtain fine clothing. Though born in such a family, one may be lame, crippled, deaf, or blind, never equal to others. Or one may be born in a family of butchers, or in a family that sells cattle, sheep, fish, fowl, chickens, or dogs. Having come out of the evil destinies and been born into such families, one does evil again, and after death must return to the evil destinies."

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. The Buddha admonished the bhikṣus: "With my divine eye I observe the people of the world: their births and deaths, their beauty and ugliness, the exalted and the lowly, those who after death attain a good destiny and those who attain an evil destiny. Those who in the world commit evil in body, speak evil with their mouths, think evil in their minds, and who constantly delight in slaughtering and offering sacrifices to spirits and gods: after death they enter hell. Those who constantly do good in body, constantly speak good with their mouths, and constantly think good in their minds: after death they are born in the heavens."

The Buddha said: "People are like bubbles that form in rainwater. As the rain drops from above, one bubble bursts and another forms. People born into the world, the living and the dying, are like the forming and bursting of bubbles. With the divine eye I observe the people of the world: those who go to heaven and those who enter hell, the poor and the rich, the exalted and the lowly, and all the good and evil that people do."

The Buddha said: "I know it all. It is like a dark night at a city gate with great torches burning on both sides. Among the thousands of people going in and coming out, all those in the darkness can see the people entering and leaving the firelight. With the divine eye I observe those who ascend to heaven and those who enter hell, as one sees people entering and leaving the firelight from the darkness. It is like a person on a high tower, with thousands of homes below, who looking down from above can see all the homes."

The Buddha said: "I see the people of the world, those who after death ascend to heaven and those who enter hell, as a person on a high tower looking down at the homes below."

The Buddha said: "It is like a person sailing on clear water, who can see all the fish and stones in the water. With the divine eye I observe the people of the world, those born in the heavens and those who enter hell, just as a person looking into clear water. In the world there are bright moon-pearls strung on five-colored threads. A person looking at the pearl can see all five colors distinctly, knowing each thread and knowing the pearl threaded through them. The Buddha sees the births, deaths, good and evil, and transformations of the people of the world, just as a person sees the pearl."

The Buddha said: "I see the people of the world who are not filial to father and mother, who do not serve śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, who do not respect elders, who do not fear consequences, who do not fear this world or the next, who are neither alarmed nor afraid. Such people after death enter hell.

"Upon meeting King Yama, they immediately turn from evil and incline toward good. The chief hell warden, named Pāṇa, leads the person to King Yama's presence. The hell warden Pāṇa reports: 'While this person was in the world, they were not filial to father and mother, did not serve śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, did not respect elders, did not delight in generosity, did not fear this world or the next, and did not fear the precepts. May the King judge this person's offenses.'

"The King then calls the person forward and says: 'When you were in the world, why did you not think of your parents who raised and nurtured you, who placed you in the dry and kept the wet for themselves, who nursed and raised you? Why were you not filial to your parents?' The person replies: 'I was truly foolish and arrogant.' The King says: 'The punishment for your offenses is not the fault of your parents, nor of heaven, nor of the emperor, nor of śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas. What your body has done, you yourself must receive.' This is King Yama's first question.

"'Did you not see people in the world when they are seriously ill, weakened and feeble in the extreme, with hands and feet numb?' The person says: 'I truly saw it.' The King says: 'Why did you not reform and do good?' The person says: 'I was truly foolish and arrogant.' The King says: 'What your body has done, you yourself must receive. This too is not the fault of your parents, nor of heaven, nor of the emperor, nor of śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas. What your body has done, you yourself must receive.' This is King Yama's second question.

"'Did you not see in the world how men and women, when they grow old, cannot see with their eyes, cannot hear with their ears, walk with a staff, their black hair turning white, no longer as in their youth?' The person replies: 'I truly saw old people walking with staffs.' 'At that time, why did you not reform and do good?' The person says: 'I was truly foolish and arrogant.' The King says: 'This too is not the fault of your parents, nor of heaven, nor of the emperor, nor of śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas. What your body has done, you yourself must receive.' This is King Yama's third question.

"'When you were in the world, did you not see men and women who have died, from one day to seven days, their bodies putrefied and decayed, eaten by worms and ants, reviled by everyone? When you saw this, why did you not reform and do good?' The person says: 'I truly saw it. I was foolish and arrogant.' 'In your conduct, why did you not correct your deeds, correct your speech, correct your mind? This too is not the fault of your parents, nor of heaven, nor of the emperor, nor of śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas. What your body has done, you yourself must receive.' This is King Yama's fourth question.

"'When you were in the world, did you ever see officers capture robbers and murderers, bind them behind their backs, and send them to prison for interrogation and punishment? Were some led out along the road to be executed, and some pulled apart alive? Did you see this or not?' The person says: 'I truly saw it.' 'Why then did you not reform and do good? When you were a person, why did you not correct your body, correct your speech, correct your mind?' The person says: 'I was truly foolish and arrogant.' 'What your body has done, you yourself must receive. This too is not the fault of your parents, nor of heaven, nor of the emperor, nor of śramaṇas or brāhmaṇas. What your body has done, you yourself must receive.' This is King Yama's fifth question.

"After the questioning is complete, the hell warden Pāṇa then leads and takes the person out to an iron city, the first hell, called the Avīci Hell. The city has four gates, four thousand *li* in circumference. In it there is a great cauldron, forty *li* wide and forty *li* deep. The hell warden pierces the person with a spear and throws them into the cauldron to be boiled. Such are the countless iron cities, all containing fire. People see them from afar, and all become anxious, terrified, and trembling. Tens of millions of people enter thus, with the hell wardens driving them in. Day and night they cannot get out. They run in all four directions trying to find an exit, but the gates are all closed. After tens of millions of years, the fire still does not go out and the people still do not die.

"After a long time they see the eastern gate open in the distance. Everyone runs toward it, but just as they reach the gate, it closes again. Those who wanted to get out fight and struggle at the gate. After a long time they see the southern gate open in the distance. Everyone runs there, but the gate closes again, and they fight and struggle to get out. After a long time they see the northern gate open in the distance. Everyone runs there, but the gate closes again, and they fight and struggle. After a long time they see the western gate open. Everyone runs there, but the gate closes. After a long time, all four gates open, and everyone runs and succeeds in getting out, thinking they have escaped.

"They then enter the second hell, the Kūṭa Hell. Running, where their feet touch the ground the flesh is scorched; when they lift their feet, the flesh grows back as before. Some run east, some west, some south, some north. The ground is scorched everywhere. After tens of millions of years this finally ends, and they think they have escaped.

"They then enter the third hell, the Mīlimade Hell. In it there are worms, called 'pecking worms,' with bodies like iron and black heads. When the worms see people they rush forward and peck them, consuming flesh, bone, and marrow entirely. After tens of millions of years this finally ends, and they think they have escaped.

"They then enter the fourth hell, the Bengluoduo Hell. In it there are stones like sharp blades. People all run up to the summit, and some run down, all seeking to escape, not knowing which way to go. Their feet are all severed and flayed, for the ground and stones are all like sharp blades. After tens of millions of years this finally ends, and they think they have escaped.

"They then enter the fifth hell, the Ayiboduhuan Hell. In it there is hot wind, hotter than the furnace charcoal of the world. The wind comes and scorches people's bodies. Everyone tries to avoid it, but they constantly encounter the hot wind and cannot escape. They seek death but cannot die, seek life but cannot live. After tens of millions of years this finally ends, and they get out, thinking they have escaped.

"They then enter the sixth hell, the Ayucanboli Hell. In it there are many trees, and among the trees there are demons. When people enter, fire comes out of the demons' heads, fire comes out of their mouths, and there are sixteen spines on each of their bodies. When the demons see people approaching, they become greatly enraged, fire bursting forth, and they rush forward to devour the person's flesh. The sixteen spines pierce through the person's body, tearing and consuming them. Everyone tries to get out, but running about they constantly encounter these demons. After tens of millions of years this finally ends, and they think they have escaped.

"They then enter the seventh hell, the Shuxiwu Hell. In it there are flying insects called 'dun.' When people enter, the insects fly into people's mouths and eat their bodies from within. Everyone runs frantically trying to escape, but the insects eat without ceasing. Running in all four directions, they cannot escape. After tens of millions of years this finally ends, and they think they have escaped.

"They then enter the eighth hell, the Tanniyù Hell. In it there is flowing water. People all fall into the water. On the banks there are thorn bushes. This water is hotter than boiling liquid in the world, boiling and surging, and people are all cooked and decomposed. Running, they try to reach the bank, but demons on the bank thrust spears at them, forcing them back in and not letting them out. People enter and are carried downstream. Downstream there are more demons, who block and hook them, asking: 'Where have you come from? What are you doing here?' They say: 'I do not know where I came from, nor do I know where I should go. I only suffer from extreme hunger and thirst, and so I followed in search of food.' The demon says: 'I will give you food.' He then takes molten copper and pours it into the person's mouth, burning everything within. Seeking death they cannot die, seeking life they cannot live. In their previous life in the world, they did evil; therefore seeking release they find no release.

"All the people in the hells then get out again, thinking they have escaped. They return and enter the seventh hell again. The demons of the seventh hell greet them and ask: 'You left. Why have you come back?' The people all say: 'I only suffer from hunger and thirst.' They then re-enter the sixth hell, from the sixth back to the fifth, from the fifth to the fourth, from the fourth to the third, from the third to the second, from the second back to the first, the Avīci Hell, seeking to get out. Seeing the iron city in the distance, they are all overjoyed and cry out together in celebration, calling out ten thousand years of life. King Yama hears them and asks the hell warden Pāṇa: 'What is that sound?' The hell warden Pāṇa replies: 'That crying out is from those who previously passed through the hells and are now leaving.' King Yama says: 'These are all those who were not filial to parents, who did not fear heaven, did not fear the emperor, did not respect ancestors, did not serve śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, and did not fear the precepts.'

"King Yama, seeing them again, says: 'Are you not under King Yama's jurisdiction? Now that you are all released, when you become human again and are born as someone's child, you should be filial. You should serve elders well. You should fear heaven. You should fear the emperor. You should serve śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas. You should correct your mind, correct your speech, correct your body. Being born in the human world, offenses are small and light; offenses in hell are great and heavy. If you encounter śramaṇas and brāhmaṇas, you should serve them. Only then will you be able to escape the evil destinies; the places of suffering will all be closed off to you.' After the questioning is complete, all the people in hell are allowed out beyond the city walls. At night they all die.

"Those who die, in their previous lives as humans, though they did much evil, still had some small good. Those returning from hell all reform and follow the correct path. Having come out of hell, those who correct their minds and correct their conduct will not return to hell again. Hell does not summon people; it is one's evil deeds that bring one there. Having experienced the cruel and agonizing suffering of hell, one can also reflect and one can also do good."

The Buddha taught thus, and the bhikṣus were all delighted.
