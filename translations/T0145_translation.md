# 佛母般泥洹經
## Sūtra on the Parinirvāṇa of the Buddha's Mother

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0145

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Huijian (慧簡)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Vaiśālī, staying at the Kūṭāgāraśālā Hall at the Monkey Pond. Mahāprajāpatī the bhikṣuṇī, who was the Buddha's foster mother, was at that time in the country of Vaiśālī, together with five hundred bhikṣuṇīs. All were arhats who had attained the six superknowledges, the four analytical knowledges, and the supernatural power of transformation. They were advanced in years and exalted in virtue, their spiritual radiance majestic and awe-inspiring. Their monastery was in the Royal Garden. Those they had liberated were beyond measure. Having deeply entered the universal wisdom concentration, she perceived that the World-Honored One, together with Ānanda, Śāriputra, and Mahāmaudgalyāyana, had completed their work of liberation and that the day of their passing was approaching. "I cannot bear to see the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata, the Worthy, the Perfectly Enlightened One, and the other arhats enter nirvāṇa. I shall first extinguish my spirit and return to original non-existence."

The Buddha, with his omniscience, fully perceived this. He then told Ānanda: "Mahāprajāpatī is thinking: 'I cannot bear to see the World-Honored One and the other arhats enter nirvāṇa. I wish to first enter parinirvāṇa.'"

Ānanda, hearing this instruction, immediately bowed his head and said: "Now hearing this honored command, my four limbs are weak and limp, my mind is obstructed and my wisdom exhausted. I cannot even recognize the names of the four directions."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Do you think that Mahāprajāpatī, in entering parinirvāṇa, will take with her the seed of the precepts, the seed of wisdom, the seed of concentration, the seed of liberation, the seed of the knowledge and vision of liberation: the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right efforts, the four bases of supernatural power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, or the eightfold noble path?"

Ānanda replied: "No. I was only thinking that the Buddha was born seven days before the Great Queen passed away, and that his foster mother had great kindness and grace toward the Buddha."

The World-Honored One sighed and said: "Truly as you say. My mother indeed had the great kindness of nursing and the grace of nourishment toward me. But I also have grace beyond reckoning toward my mother. It is because of me that she was able to take refuge in the Buddha, take refuge in the Dharma, take refuge in the holy Saṃgha, and take refuge in the practice of the path to cessation, from darkness to brilliant light. She has no doubts about the Three Treasures, and has fully understood the path-eye of suffering, its arising, its cessation, and the path, and has attained liberation without attachment. If a person can awaken another from the confusion of delusion and lead them into the right truth, to take refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the holy Saṃgha, and to take refuge in the practice of the path to cessation, and the disciple who receives the path were to use all the precious treasures in the world, for the span of a lifetime, to make offerings of clothing, food, bedding, and medicine to the teacher, even ten thousand lifetimes would not repay a fraction. The kindness of taking refuge in the Three Treasures exceeds Mount Sumeru, while the disciple's repayment is but a mustard seed. Therefore, Ānanda, my grace toward Mahāprajāpatī is great and immeasurable."

At that time, Mahāprajāpatī, together with the five hundred bhikṣuṇīs, came to where the Buddha was. They all placed their heads at the Buddha's feet and withdrew, joining their palms. Mahāprajāpatī said to the Buddha: "I cannot bear to see the Buddha and the other arhats enter parinirvāṇa. I wish to first enter nirvāṇa." The Buddha silently assented. Mahāprajāpatī touched the Buddha's feet with her hand and said: "I am now freed from seeing the Tathāgata, the Worthy, the Perfectly Enlightened One, the Master of the Dharma, the Teacher of Devas and Humans, the Light of the Three Realms. From this day forward, I shall never again see him." The five hundred bhikṣuṇīs spoke in the same manner, and the Buddha likewise assented. He then taught them about the suffering of bodily affliction, birth and death, grief and sorrow, and the difficulty of what is disagreeable, and about the peace of desirelessness, purity, emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness, and cessation, in various pure categories. All the bhikṣuṇīs were joyful. They circumambulated the Buddha three times, bowed their heads, and departed.

They returned to their monastery and spread out five hundred seats. Each took her seat. Mahāprajāpatī manifested her supernatural merit and virtue. From her seat she sank into the earth, then emerged from the east, in the empty sky, transforming. She ascended from the height of one tree up to seven trees. She walked about in the empty sky. At times she sat, at times she reclined. From the upper body came water and from the lower body came fire; from the lower body came water and from the upper body came fire. She also sank into the earth and came from the east, her disappearances and appearances as before, in the eight directions, above and below. She emitted a great radiance that illuminated all beings in darkness and shone upward to the heavens. The five hundred bhikṣuṇīs performed the same transformations. All entered nirvāṇa at the same time.

The Buddha told the Venerable Ānanda: "Tomorrow at dawn, enter the city and go to the house of the householder Yaśoda. Tell him: 'The Buddha's mother and five hundred senior bhikṣuṇīs have all entered parinirvāṇa. The Buddha urges the householder to prepare five hundred biers, sesame oil, incense, flowers, and firewood of camphor, catalpa, and other fine woods, five hundred portions each, as well as proper music for making offerings. The reason is that all these bhikṣuṇīs attained the six superknowledges, the four analytical knowledges, and the pure concentration of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. Now that they have attained nirvāṇa, they are praised by all Buddhas. The merit of making offerings to them at this time is beyond reckoning.'" Ānanda bowed his head to the ground, and with sorrowful reverence agreed. At dawn he entered the city and arrived at the gate of the householder's house. He told the gatekeeper: "Go in and announce that I have come." The gatekeeper went in and reported. At that time, the householder was in a high tower with musicians, enjoying themselves together. Hearing that Ānanda had come, his heart was alarmed and his hair stood on end. He immediately descended from the tower, rushed out, prostrated himself on the ground, placed his hands at Ānanda's feet, knelt, and said: "Venerable Ānanda! You have come so early today. This is not ordinary. What is the reason?" Ānanda then conveyed the Buddha's instruction in full to the householder. When the householder heard it, he immediately threw himself to the ground, crying out in grief: "Have we not been disciples of the Buddha? Have we committed some unworthy act that we have been abandoned by the bhikṣuṇīs, who have departed forever without leaving a final teaching?" Sobbing, he continued: "Venerable Ānanda! The monastery of Vaiśālī will now be all empty and desolate. On the royal roads and at the four crossroads, we will never again see the supernaturally gifted bhikṣuṇīs walking along the streets of the country. The streets of the country will be empty. How great is this pain!" Ānanda replied: "The Buddha has said that although heaven and earth endure long, what begins must eventually end. What flourishes must eventually decline. Those held dear must eventually part. Those who see impermanence and wish for permanence will follow their actions and receive their consequences. The three realms are impermanent, like illusions and dreams. From ancient times, the suffering of impermanence and bodily affliction has been boundless in its misfortune, yet the deluded do not see this; they may be called blind. To live and seek not to die, to meet and wish never to part: this can never be attained. The worthy disciple who perceives the profound meaning of the Buddha's sūtras and understands the four marks of impermanence is like a blind person gaining sight. Strive diligently and make effort, and you may be freed from the weight of suffering." Ānanda cited various essential teachings to release the householder from his bonds. The householder's mind was eased, and he was gladdened.

Ānanda then went to the brāhmaṇas and householders. At that time, the assembly was in the lecture hall engaged in a different discussion. He then addressed them: "The Buddha urges all of you to prepare funeral offerings for five hundred people. The reason is that the Buddha's mother and the five hundred bhikṣuṇīs have all entered parinirvāṇa." When the brāhmaṇas and householders heard Ānanda's words, every one of them threw themselves to the ground, beating their chests and tearing their hair, rolling about and crying out in grief: "What shall we do? We are orphaned and exposed. Upon whom shall we henceforth rely?" Ānanda again spoke: "The three realms are illusions; all is impermanent. The body is a vessel of suffering, an assemblage of pain. Only nirvāṇa is peace. Therefore the Three Treasures take refuge in it." The householders' minds were eased, and they bowed at his feet. Ānanda returned to where the Buddha was and reported everything.

The brāhmaṇas and householders immediately prepared the funeral offerings and hastened to the monastery. At that time, the garden gate was closed. The householder sent a person to climb in and open the gate, and they sought to enter the lecture hall. There were three female novice nuns: the first had attained the path of the non-returner, the next was a once-returner, and the youngest was a stream-enterer. They told the householder: "Our teachers are in seated meditation and have now attained the concentration of stillness. Take care not to disturb them." He replied: "The teachers have already entered parinirvāṇa. They are not in meditation." When the novice nuns heard this, they threw themselves to the ground and lost consciousness. After a while they revived, and cried out in grief: "Who will henceforth instruct us with the holy teaching? We who have been left behind are lost!" When the householder saw this, everyone raised their voices in grief. When the grieving was done, he told the novice nuns: "The Buddha originally said in the sūtras: 'Though those held dear may meet, they must eventually part. Worldly glory is hard to preserve. Only the path can endure.' You should only build your resolve, make progress, and strive to attain arhatship, so as to end the suffering of the three realms. Let go of worldly grief."

The householder performed the cremation. He brought the relics before the Buddha. The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Face the east, join your palms, lower your right knee, and say: 'Those of right faith and right practice who have attained the three kinds of supernatural knowledge, the six kinds of superknowledge, and whose supernatural powers and merit are complete, let them all come to this place. The reason is that the Buddha's mother, together with the five hundred bhikṣuṇīs, have all now departed in goodness. We should assemble.' Likewise for all four directions." Thereupon, from each of the four directions came two hundred and fifty arhats, who flew with supernatural powers, came, and bowed their heads at the Buddha's feet. The Buddha arose and went to where the relics of Mahāprajāpatī were. One thousand bhikṣus followed, and all took their seats. He told Ānanda: "Take the relics, place them in a bowl, and put them in my hand." Ānanda did as commanded, placed the relics in the bowl, knelt, and offered them to the Buddha. The Buddha received them with both hands and told the assembly of bhikṣus: "These collected relics were originally a defiled body: violent, foolish, impetuous, frivolous, fickle, jealous, and scheming, the kind that would first ruin the path and undermine virtue and bring about disorder. Now the mother has uprooted the defilement and foolishness of womanhood, has practiced the conduct of a man, and has attained the path of an arhat. Her spirit has returned to original non-existence, purer than empty space, her practice lofty and without peer. How strong she was!" The Buddha told the bhikṣus and the assembly of householders: "Together erect a stūpa for the mother and the arhat bhikṣuṇīs." All replied: "So be it!" Thereupon devas, humans, ghosts, and nāgas erected a stūpa and raised a banner. With flowers, incense, and music, they circumambulated the stūpa three times. The grieving shook the country. All who came before the Buddha in faith were told: "Birth and death bring suffering. The three realms are without ease." This relieved those who came, and all were joyful. They bowed their heads and departed.

## Record of Changes After the Buddha's Parinirvāṇa

One hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, my disciples, the śramaṇas, will be wise and intelligent, no different from me. Two hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, King Aśoka will request the eight bushels and four pecks of relics from the eight kings and in a single day erect eighty-four thousand Buddha stūpas. Three hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, if one goes forth and becomes a śramaṇa, one will attain the path in a single day. Four hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, people will constantly recollect the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus, and will make offerings to their preceptors and ācāryas. Five hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, there will be no śramaṇa, brāhmaṇa, or layperson who does not weep in recollection of the Buddha. Six hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, śramaṇas will practice by entering mountains, dwelling under trees, and sitting among burial mounds, seeking the path. Seven hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, people will study both inner and outer teachings. If any śramaṇa or brāhmaṇa asks them a question, there will be nothing they cannot explain clearly, and they will defeat the ninety-six kinds of non-Buddhist paths. Eight hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, people will again devote themselves to building Buddha stūpas, one after another without ceasing. Nine hundred years after my parinirvāṇa, people will devote themselves to commerce and trade, seeking profit in harmful places. One thousand years after my parinirvāṇa, people will follow kings and engage in military strategy and battle formations, themselves practicing slaughter, and will take wives and marry women.
