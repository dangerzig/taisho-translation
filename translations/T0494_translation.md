# 阿難七夢經
## Sūtra of Ānanda's Seven Dreams

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0494

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Ānanda, while in the kingdom of Śrāvastī, had seven kinds of dreams and came to ask the Buddha about them. The first: fire from a marsh blazed up to the heavens. The second: in his dream, the sun and moon set, and the stars also disappeared. The third: in his dream, ordained bhikṣus had sunk down into a pit of filth, while lay white-robed people climbed over their heads and got out. The fourth: in his dream, herds of pigs came and rooted up and attacked a sandalwood grove, which was strange. The fifth: in his dream, he carried Mount Sumeru on his head without feeling it heavy. The sixth: in his dream, great elephants abandoned small elephants. The seventh: in his dream, a lion king named Huāsǎ, with seven fine hairs upon its head, lay dead upon the ground. All birds and beasts who saw it were struck with fear. Afterwards, worms were seen emerging from within its body and eating it. He came to the Buddha with these evil dreams to ask about them.

At that time, the Buddha was at the Hall of Universal Assembly in the kingdom of Śrāvastī, expounding the Dharma to King Prasenajit: that suffering, its arising, its cessation, and the attainment of the path are bliss. He saw that Ānanda's countenance bore an expression of sorrow and distress beyond words. The Buddha said to Ānanda: "These dreams of yours are all foretellings of the five turbid ages of the evil world to come. They do no harm to you; why do you wear such a sorrowful expression?

"The first dream; fire from a marsh blazing up to the heavens: in the evil world to come, bhikṣus' wholesome minds will grow ever fewer, and evil and wickedness will blaze up fiercely. They will kill one another in numbers beyond reckoning.

"The second dream; the sun and moon setting, with the stars also disappearing: after the Buddha enters nirvāṇa, all the śrāvakas will follow the Buddha into nirvāṇa and no longer remain in the world. The eyes of sentient beings will be extinguished.

"The third dream; ordained bhikṣus sinking into a pit of filth while lay white-robed people climb over their heads and get out: in the world to come, bhikṣus will harbor poison and jealousy, going so far as to kill one another. Religious practitioners will be beheaded. Lay people will observe this and admonish and rebuke them, but they will not comply; upon death, these practitioners will enter hell. The lay people who practice diligently will, upon death, be reborn in the heavens.

"The fourth dream; herds of pigs coming and rooting up and attacking a sandalwood grove, which was strange: in the world to come, lay people will enter stūpas and monasteries, slander the sangha, seek out their faults and failings, destroy stūpas, and harm bhikṣus.

"The fifth dream; carrying Mount Sumeru on your head without feeling it heavy: after the Buddha enters nirvāṇa, Ānanda will serve as the teacher of the sūtras for a thousand arhats. Not a single phrase will be forgotten, and many will receive understanding through him, yet he will not find it a burden.

"The sixth dream; great elephants abandoning small elephants: in the time to come, wrong views will blaze forth mightily, destroying my Buddha-Dharma. People of virtue will all go into hiding and not appear.

"The seventh dream, the death of the lion king: one thousand four hundred and seventy years after the Buddha enters nirvāṇa, all my disciples who cultivate virtue in their hearts will not be disturbed or harassed by evil demons. As for the seven hairs, these refer to events seven hundred years hence."
