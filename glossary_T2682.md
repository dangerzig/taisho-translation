# Glossary: Essentials of Birth in the Pure Land (往生要集)

## Key Terms

### Pure Land Core Terms

| Chinese | Sanskrit | English | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|
| 念佛 | buddhānusmṛti | buddha-recollection | The central practice of the entire text. Genshin uses the term to encompass vocal recitation, contemplative visualization, and meditative absorption on the Buddha. |
| 極樂 | Sukhāvatī | Sukhāvatī | The Land of Utmost Bliss, Amitābha's pure land in the west. Also rendered as the Land of Peace and Bliss (安樂). |
| 淨土 | buddhakṣetra | Pure Land | A purified buddha-field. Throughout the text, usually refers specifically to Amitābha's Sukhāvatī. |
| 往生 | upapatti | birth [in the Pure Land] | The central aspiration of the text: attaining rebirth in Amitābha's Pure Land at the moment of death. |
| 穢土 | — | defiled land | The impure world of saṃsāra, contrasted with the Pure Land. No standard Sanskrit equivalent. |
| 娑婆世界 | Sahā | Sahā world | The "Endurance World," our present world-system where Śākyamuni teaches and sentient beings suffer. |
| 安樂 | sukhāvatī | Peace and Bliss | Alternative rendering of Sukhāvatī used throughout the text. |
| 本願 | pūrvapraṇidhāna | original vow | The vows made by Amitābha (as the Bhikṣu Dharmākara) to establish and draw beings into his Pure Land. A foundational concept. |
| 四十八願 | aṣṭacatvāriṃśat-praṇidhāna | forty-eight vows | The specific set of vows made by the Bhikṣu Dharmākara, forming the basis of Amitābha's Pure Land. |
| 九品 | navapadma | nine grades [of birth] | The nine levels of rebirth in the Pure Land, classified into three ranks (upper, middle, lower) each with three grades. |
| 來迎 | — | welcoming [at death] | The coming of Amitābha and the holy assembly to receive the dying practitioner. A distinctive Pure Land concept. |
| 蓮華 | padma | lotus | The lotus flower upon which practitioners are reborn in the Pure Land. Also the vehicle for the welcoming at death. |
| 七寶 | saptaratna | seven jewels | Gold, silver, lapis lazuli, crystal, coral, red pearl, and agate, composing the adornments of the Pure Land. |
| 十念 | daśa-smṛti | ten moments of mindfulness | Ten recitations of Amitābha's name at the moment of death, sufficient for attaining birth in the Pure Land. |
| 稱名 | nāma-saṃkīrtana | name-recitation | The vocal practice of calling out Amitābha's name, a key method of buddha-recollection. |
| 般舟三昧 | pratyutpanna-samādhi | pratyutpanna samādhi | The "samādhi of the buddha standing before one," a practice of continuous walking and recollection lasting ninety days. |
| 五念門 | pañca-smṛti-dvāra | five gates of mindfulness | Vasubandhu's system of practice: prostration, praise, vow-making, contemplation, and merit-dedication. |
| 法藏比丘 | Dharmākara | Dharmākara | The bodhisattva who, through his vows and practice, became Amitābha Buddha. |

| 善財 | Sudhana | Sudhana | The youth in the Avataṃsaka's "Entering the Dharma Realm" chapter who visits fifty-three teachers. |

### Contemplation and Practice

| Chinese | Sanskrit | English | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|
| 禮拜 | vandana | prostration | The first of Vasubandhu's five gates. Bodily karma brought into accord with awakening. |
| 讚歎 | stuti | praise | The second gate. Verbal karma, including recitation of verses and the Buddha's name. |
| 作願 | praṇidhāna | vow-making | The third gate. Mental karma of aspiring to birth in the Pure Land. |
| 觀察 | vipaśyanā | contemplation | The fourth gate. Visualization of the Buddha's marks and the adornments of the Pure Land. |
| 迴向 | pariṇāmanā | merit-dedication | The fifth gate. Turning all cultivated merit toward birth in the Pure Land and supreme awakening. |
| 觀佛三昧 | buddhānusmṛti-samādhi | samādhi of contemplating the Buddha | A meditative practice of visualizing the Buddha's form, marks, and radiance in progressive detail. |
| 白毫 | ūrṇā | white ūrṇā | The curl of white hair between the Buddha's brows, the primary focus of the condensed contemplation practice. |
| 觀想 | — | visualization | The practice of forming a mental image of the Buddha's marks and the Pure Land's adornments. |
| 三心 | tricitta | three minds | The three dispositions for birth: utmost sincerity, deep faith, and the mind of merit-dedication. From the Contemplation Sūtra. |
| 至誠心 | — | mind of utmost sincerity | The first of the three minds: genuine and real commitment in body, speech, and mind. |
| 深心 | — | deep mind | The second of the three minds: truly knowing oneself as an ordinary being and trusting Amitābha's vow. |
| 四修 | — | four modes of practice | Long-term, sincere, uninterrupted, and exclusive practice, drawn from the Compendium of the Mahāyāna. |
| 常行三昧 | pratyutpanna-samādhi | constantly-walking samādhi | The ninety-day practice of unceasing walking, chanting, and recollection described in the Mohe zhiguan. |
| 懺悔 | kṣamā | repentance | Practice of confessing transgressions before the Buddha, both through ritual acts and through contemplation of emptiness. |
| 事懺 | — | repentance through practice | Repentance through ritual acts: prostration, confession, and recitation. |
| 理懺 | — | repentance through principle | Repentance through contemplating that all sins are empty by nature, without self-nature. |
| 臨終 | — | deathbed | The critical moment at life's end when right mindfulness determines one's rebirth. |

### Cosmology and Rebirth

| Chinese | Sanskrit | English | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|
| 六道 | ṣaḍgati | six destinies | The six realms of rebirth: hells, hungry ghosts, animals, asuras, humans, and celestial beings. Gate One treats all six. |
| 三惡道 | tryapāya | three evil paths | The hells, hungry ghosts, and animals. The text devotes extensive description to their sufferings. |
| 地獄 | naraka | hell | The lowest of the six destinies. Gate One describes eight great hells and their subsidiary places in vivid detail. |
| 餓鬼 | preta | hungry ghost | Beings tormented by insatiable hunger and thirst. The text describes many varieties. |
| 畜生 | tiryagyoni | animal realm | The realm of animals, including birds, beasts, insects, and aquatic creatures. |
| 阿修羅 | asura | asura | Powerful beings prone to jealousy and combat, dwelling at the bottom of the ocean or in mountains. |
| 天 | deva | celestial being | Beings in the heavens, whose bliss is temporary and ends in the five signs of decay. |
| 三界 | trailokya | three realms | The desire realm, form realm, and formless realm comprising all of saṃsāric existence. |
| 欲界 | kāmadhātu | desire realm | The lowest realm, where beings are driven by sensory desire. Includes the six heavens and lower destinies. |
| 色界 | rūpadhātu | form realm | The realm of meditative absorption with subtle material form. |
| 無色界 | ārūpyadhātu | formless realm | The highest saṃsāric realm, where beings exist in pure consciousness without form. |
| 有頂 | bhavāgra | Summit of Existence | The highest point of the formless realm, from which beings still eventually descend. |
| 輪迴 | saṃsāra | saṃsāra | The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth driven by karma and affliction. |
| 中陰 | antarābhava | intermediate state | The transitional existence between death and rebirth. Described vividly for those entering the hells. |
| 由旬 | yojana | yojana | A unit of distance (roughly 7-15 km). Used throughout to describe the vast dimensions of the hells and heavens. |
| 劫 | kalpa | kalpa | An immensely long cosmic time period. Used to measure lifespans in the hells and heavens. |
| 閻浮提 | Jambudvīpa | Jambudvīpa | The southern continent in Buddhist cosmology, corresponding to the human world. |
| 須彌山 | Sumeru | Mount Sumeru | The great mountain at the center of the world-system, used as a measure of immensity. |

### The Eight Great Hells

| Chinese | Sanskrit | English | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|
| 等活 | Saṃjīva | Hell of Repetition | The first hell. Sinners are killed and revived repeatedly. Lifespan equals 500 years measured in Cāturmahārājika time. |
| 黑繩 | Kālasūtra | Hell of Black Ropes | The second hell. Sinners are marked with blazing ropes and hacked apart. For killing and stealing. |
| 眾合 | Saṃghāta | Hell of Crushing Together | The third hell. Iron mountains close together and crush sinners. For killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct. |
| 叫喚 | Raurava | Hell of Screaming | The fourth hell. Sinners scream in terror from scorching griddles and boiling cauldrons. For the above plus intoxicants. |
| 大叫喚 | Mahāraurava | Hell of Great Screaming | The fifth hell. Tenfold suffering of the fourth. For the above plus false speech. |
| 焦熱 | Tapana | Hell of Scorching Heat | The sixth hell. Sinners are beaten, skewered, and roasted. For the above plus wrong views. |
| 大焦熱 | Pratāpana | Hell of Great Scorching Heat | The seventh hell. Tenfold the sixth. For the above plus defiling women of pure precepts. Lifespan: half an intermediate kalpa. |
| 阿鼻 | Avīci | Avīci | The hell "without interval," at the very bottom of the desire realm. The most extreme suffering. For the five heinous crimes. |

| Chinese | Sanskrit | English | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|
| 糞屎泥 | — | Place of Excrement and Mire | Subsidiary of the Hell of Repetition. Scalding excrement-mire with diamond-beaked worms. |
| 刀輪 | — | Place of Sword Wheels | Iron walls, raging fire, sword-forests, and showers of blades. |
| 鑊湯 | — | Place of Cauldron Cooking | Sinners boiled in iron cauldrons like beans. |

| 刀葉林 | asipattravana | Forest of Blade-Leaves | Common subsidiary. Blade-leaves fall and hack every joint and limb. |
| 鐵鑊摩梨 | śālmalī | Forest of Iron Śālmalī Trees | Common subsidiary. Thorns pierce sinners as they climb; iron-beaked birds peck out their eyes. |

### Doctrinal Terms

| Chinese | Sanskrit | English | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|
| 菩提心 | bodhicitta | mind of awakening | The aspiration for supreme enlightenment. Gate Four devotes extensive discussion to its character, benefits, and critical distinctions. |
| 發菩提心 | bodhicittotpāda | arousing the mind of awakening | The foundational act of generating the aspiration for buddhahood for the sake of all beings. |
| 四弘誓願 | catvāri praṇidhānāni | four universal vows | Sentient beings are numberless (I vow to liberate them); afflictions are inexhaustible (I vow to sever them); dharma gates are boundless (I vow to know them); bodhi is unsurpassed (I vow to realize it). |
| 煩惱 | kleśa | afflictions | Mental factors that disturb the mind and perpetuate suffering. The text compares eighty-four thousand gates of affliction with eighty-four thousand pāramitās. |
| 無明 | avidyā | ignorance | Fundamental misunderstanding of reality, the root of delusion that drives beings through saṃsāra. |
| 貪 | rāga | greed | Desire for pleasant objects; one of the three poisons. |
| 瞋 | dveṣa | anger | Hostility toward unpleasant objects; one of the three poisons. Called the worst thief of meritorious karma. |
| 癡 | moha | delusion | Fundamental confusion about reality; one of the three poisons. |
| 五陰 | pañcaskandha | five aggregates | Form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. Also rendered as the "five burning aggregates" as a form of suffering. |
| 無常 | anitya | impermanence | The universal truth that all conditioned phenomena are transient. A major theme of Gate One's treatment of the human realm. |
| 苦 | duḥkha | suffering | The pervasive unsatisfactoriness of conditioned existence. Analyzed in eight forms throughout the text. |
| 空 | śūnyatā | emptiness | The teaching that all dharmas are empty of intrinsic existence. Central to the repentance through principle and the contemplation of the dharma-body. |
| 不淨 | aśubha | impurity | The meditation on the foulness of the body, described in graphic anatomical detail in Gate One's section on humans. |
| 三毒 | triviṣa | three poisons | Greed, anger, and delusion; the root afflictions that drive saṃsāric existence. |
| 真如 | tathatā | suchness | The ultimate nature of reality, beyond conceptual elaboration. Used in the contemplation of the dharma-body Buddha. |
| 中道 | madhyamā pratipad | Middle Way | Each single form, each single fragrance, is none other than the Middle Way. The text invokes the threefold truth. |
| 法性 | dharmatā | nature of dharmas | The essential nature of phenomena, equivalent to suchness and emptiness. |
| 法界 | dharmadhātu | dharma-realm | The totality of all phenomena viewed as one reality. Merit is dedicated "to the dharma-realm." |
| 佛性 | buddhadhātu | buddha-nature | The inherent capacity for awakening in all sentient beings. The Nirvāṇa Sūtra's teaching on this is cited at length. |
| 如來藏 | tathāgatagarbha | tathāgatagarbha | The "womb of the Tathāgata" present in all beings. Cited from the Prajñāpāramitā: "all existence is entirely tathāgatagarbha." |
| 三諦 | trisatya | three truths | Emptiness, conventional existence, and the Middle Way. The text states that the three truths are mutually interpenetrating. |
| 業 | karma | karma | Intentional action and its results. The driving force behind rebirth in the six destinies. |
| 方便 | upāya | skillful means | Expedient methods for guiding beings; applied to the Buddha's specific recommendation of the western Pure Land. |

### Meditation and Mental States

| Chinese | Sanskrit | English | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|
| 三昧 | samādhi | samādhi | Meditative concentration. The text discusses buddha-recollection samādhi as supreme among all practices. |
| 禪定 | dhyāna | meditation | States of deep concentration; the fifth pāramitā. The text discusses the four dhyānas as dwelling places for the pratyutpanna samādhi. |
| 止觀 | śamatha-vipaśyanā | calming and contemplation | The paired practice of mental quiescence and insight. The Mohe zhiguan is cited extensively. |
| 念佛三昧 | buddhānusmṛti-samādhi | buddha-recollection samādhi | The specific samādhi attained through sustained mindfulness of the Buddha. Called the "mother of all buddhas." |
| 四禪 | catvāri dhyānāni | four dhyānas | The four meditative absorptions of the form realm. The pratyutpanna samādhi may arise in any of them. |
| 正念 | samyak-smṛti | right mindfulness | Correct attentiveness, especially at the moment of death. The text emphasizes that accumulated practice ensures right mindfulness at death. |
| 散心 | vikṣipta-citta | scattered mind | The distracted, unfocused state of ordinary consciousness that must be overcome through practice. |
| 定善 | — | meditative good | Goodness cultivated through meditative concentration, as opposed to that cultivated through active practices. |
| 散善 | — | non-meditative good | Wholesome deeds performed outside of formal meditation: recitation, precepts, giving, and so forth. |
