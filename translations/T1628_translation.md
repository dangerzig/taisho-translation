# 因明正理門論本
## Nyāyadvāratārkasāstra (Gateway to Correct Reasoning)

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1628

Composed by Dignāga (陳那). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘)

---

In order to discern and uphold what is genuine in the meanings of proof and refutation, this treatise is composed.

> The thesis and so forth, stated in multiple propositions, constitute the proof.
> Among these, only what accords with one's own intention
> Is called the thesis, the proposition to be established;
> It cannot be refuted by a meaning contradictory to it.

"The thesis and so forth, stated in multiple propositions, constitute the proof": through multiple propositions expressing the thesis (*pakṣa*), reason (*hetu*), and example (*dṛṣṭānta*), one explains a meaning not yet understood by the other. Therefore, these multiple propositions are called "proof" in the *Nyāyamukha* and similar works. Further, the statement of proof in a single proposition serves to indicate the unified nature of proof as a whole. From this one should understand that any deficiency constitutes a fault of proof. The phrase "among these" serves to introduce the discussion or to delimit; it refers to "among the thesis and so forth," hence "among these." The word "only" serves to distinguish and delimit. "Accords with one's own intention" indicates that one establishes the thesis following one's own intention, without deference to the tenets of others. "The proposition to be established" means that what is not intended to serve as the means of establishment is what is to be established. If it were otherwise and one designated what is to be established differently, then a pseudo-reason or pseudo-example should also be called a thesis. To show that the thesis is free from other faults of thesis-formulation, it says "it cannot be refuted by a meaning contradictory to it." If it is refuted by words expressing a contradictory meaning, as when one asserts "all statements are false"; or if it contradicts a thesis previously established, as when the son of a jackal (*Huṇa-putra*) asserts "sound is permanent"; or again, if within it, because there is nothing in common, there is no inference, and it is refuted by a meaning contradictory to an established term, as when one says "the hare-bearer [i.e., the moon] does not exist, because it is not the moon"; or further, if in the subject (*dharmin*), what is asserted is refuted by a meaning contradictory to what is established through perception or inference, as when one establishes "sound is not audible" or "a pot is permanent" and so forth.

Those who say that a contradiction between thesis and reason is called a "thesis-contradiction" are mistaken; this is not a fault of the thesis. For when one asserts in this context "sound is permanent, because everything is impermanent," this is a faulty establishment of the dissimilar example through the device of the example, because the concomitant example shows that it is not everything. This reason does not hold, because sound is included in "everything." Or because it is one part of what is to be established, this meaning does not hold, and it is named a fault of the reason. The example also has a fault, because the dissimilar example should first show the absence of the thesis-property, then state the absence of the reason. One should say: "'impermanent everything' means 'not not-everything'"; however, this is stated in reverse as "everything is impermanent," and therefore the example also has a fault here.

Thus the thesis and pseudo-thesis have been explained. The reason and pseudo-reason, which are mostly properties of the thesis-subject: their distinguishing characteristics shall now be shown.

> The thesis-property in the similar class:
> Present, absent, or both.
> In the dissimilar class, likewise three each:
> Present, absent, and both.

Is not the entire proposition, combining what one wishes to establish, collectively called the thesis? How then can one say that "thesis" here refers only to the subject (*dharmin*)? There is no fault here, because the general term also applies to particulars, as when one says "burning a garment." Or the word "thesis" may denote only the property (*dharma*). The thesis-property here is taken only as what is definitely accepted by both the proponent and the opponent. The present, absent, and so forth in the similar class are likewise. Why? Because here we rely only on the evidential reason (*jñāpaka-hetu*), through which the stated meaning is understood by the power of cognition alone, not like a generative cause (*janaka-hetu*) that operates through productive action. If so, since cognition is taken as the evidential cause, the statement would lose its capacity as means of establishment. This too is not so, because it causes the other to recall what is already mutually established. Therefore, in this context, only what is definitely accepted by both parties is well stated. Accordingly, if something is not mutually accepted, it is definitely not a thesis-property, as when one establishes "sound is impermanent, because it is visible to the eye." Or if the opponent does not accept it, as when against one who holds the theory of manifestation (*āvirbhāva*), one says "because it is produced." Or if there is doubt, as when, upon seeing smoke and so forth and becoming uncertain, one establishes "the great elements in combination possess fire, because smoke is presently observed." Or in a case where the subject is not established, as when one establishes "the self is all-pervading, because it gives rise to pleasure and so forth in all places." All statements of this kind, of whatever category, do not constitute proof. Regarding the present, absent, and so forth in the similar class, one should explain accordingly as appropriate.

In the reasons, contradictory reasons, and inconclusive reasons to be discussed, only propositions consisting of mutually established and definite words are called proof or refutation. Words that are mutually unestablished or doubtful require further establishment.

Now, to establish a thesis-property, one should logically use another property as a reason to establish it. If one directly establishes that the subject exists or does not exist, as when one establishes "the Supreme (*Pradhāna*) exists, because individual things are seen to have a universal class"; or establishes it as "non-existent, because it is unapprehended": what is the meaning? Here one merely establishes as the thesis that "individual things definitely have a single cause," not that "the Supreme exists," so there is no such fault. If one establishes it as "non-existent," one provisionally posits the "property of being unapprehended," and therefore there is no fault of the subject. If one uses one subject to establish another subject, or to establish a property, as when one uses smoke to establish fire, or uses fire to establish tactile sensation: what is the meaning? Here one does not establish "fire" or "tactile sensation" as the thesis, but only establishes a thing correlated with these. If it were otherwise, establishing fire from smoke or tactile sensation from fire would make one part of the thesis-meaning serve as the reason. Also, one does not here wish to establish the existence of fire or tactile sensation, since these are commonly known to exist. Also, here one observes what is to be established and posits the property and the subject, not attribute and substance, so there is no fault. A verse restates this:

> The subject is not established by the subject, nor by the property;
> Nor is the subject established by this.
> But through property alone, one establishes the property,
> And thus one establishes the subject.

If one establishes "sound is not permanent" by saying "because action and so forth should then be permanent" or "because the permanent should be apprehensible," how can this be called a thesis-property? This states the opponent's fault through the thesis and reason gates. Since there is something to be established and one uses the word "should" (*ying*), and since one previously established "permanent, because it has no physical resistance," here one merely states the thesis and criticizes the opponent's faulty reason. If one establishes in this way, "sound is impermanent: what is produced is not permanent; what is permanent is not produced," what then? This is a device of the example. The similar and dissimilar examples, in their respective order, declare that the reason definitely follows the thesis, and that where the thesis is absent, the reason is also absent. Through the concomitant example, one shows that the reason of "being produced" is such that this sound is definitely produced and not non-produced, and that this "being produced" is definitely a thesis-property. A verse restates:

> State the reason: the thesis follows it.
> Where the thesis is absent, the reason does not exist.
> The example is shown through the fifth [i.e., the ablative case];
> Through concomitance, one knows the reason.

By this, the device of counter-refutation has also been explained. Since "being produced" is seen in the impermanent and not seen in the permanent, one thus establishes "sound is not permanent; it should not be produced." Therefore, the forward proof and the counter-refutation device are not separate explanations of the reason. As in refuting the Sāṃkhya, I have already discussed this at length, so let us set aside extensive disputation on tangential topics.

Thus the thesis-property has three kinds of differentiation: present in the similar class, absent from it, and both. First, remove the word "and." Here, if a class is proximate and equal to the property to be established, it is called the "similar class" (*sapakṣa*), because all meanings can be called "classes." If the property to be established is absent, it is called the "dissimilar class" (*vipakṣa*), not "contradictory to" or "different from" the similar class. If contradictory, there would be only exclusion. If merely different, there would be no reason. By this logic, "because it is produced" can establish both "impermanent" and "non-self" and so forth, since these are not contradictory. If a property can establish what contradicts the thesis, that is the fault of contradiction, and it is called a pseudo-reason. Just as with a non-contradictory property, so with a contradictory one: where the established property is absent, it is definitely not present. It is not like a pot and so forth, where the reason produces doubt, because in the latter there is presence among what is mutually absent. Since "being produced" is observed apart from pots in garments and so forth, and does not exist apart from "impermanent" in "non-self" and so forth, this reason holds. How can a distinct property operate in a distinct domain? Because of their similarity, one does not use a different name but says it is the same, so there is no fault. If one does not call it different, how can this reason be called a thesis-property? Here one merely says it is definitely a thesis-property, not that it is exclusively a thesis-property. If so, should the similar class also be called the thesis? No, because it is said elsewhere to be what is to be established. The reason must be undifferentiated to constitute inference, so the cases are not similar.

Moreover, each of these has three varieties: among all those present in the similar class, in the dissimilar class there is either presence, absence, or both. For those absent from the similar class and those with both, there are likewise three varieties each. If the thesis "impermanent" has no dissimilar class at all, when addressing one who does not posit the existence of space and so forth, how can one say "this is absent there"? If it is absent there, it does not operate there, and there is absolutely no doubt, so there is no fault here.

Thus, altogether nine varieties of thesis-property are formed. In their order, their characteristics are briefly distinguished as follows: asserting "sound is permanent, because it is knowable (*prameya*)"; or asserting "impermanent, because it is produced (*kṛtaka*)"; or asserting "produced by effort and volition, because it is impermanent"; or asserting "permanent, because it is produced"; or asserting "permanent, because it is audible (*śrāvaṇa*)"; or asserting "permanent, because it is produced by effort and volition"; or "not produced by effort and volition, because it is impermanent"; or asserting "impermanent, because it is produced by effort and volition"; or asserting "permanent, because it is intangible (*asparśa*)." These nine are subsumed in two verses:

> Permanent, impermanent, effort-produced,
> Eternal, abiding, firmly fixed,
> Not effort-produced, changing, unchanging:
> Nine, through "knowable" and so forth.
>
> Knowable, produced, impermanent,
> Produced, audible, effort-produced,
> Impermanent, effort-produced, intangible:
> Nine, based on "permanent" and so forth.

The distinctions thus analyzed are called reason, contradictory, and inconclusive. Therefore the root verse says:

> Present in the similar and in both,
> Absent in the dissimilar: this is a reason.
> The reverse is called contradictory.
> All the rest are inconclusive.

Here, only two varieties are called "reason": one where the reason is universally present in the similar class and universally absent from the dissimilar class; and one where the reason is variably present and absent in the similar class but universally absent from the dissimilar class. From the first and last groups of three, one takes the middle one respectively. Again, only two varieties are called "contradictory," because they can establish the opposite: where the reason is present in the dissimilar class, or present in both; while in the similar class it is universally absent. From the second group of three, one takes the first and last. The remaining five varieties are inconclusive for both reason and contradiction; this means they are "doubtful reasons." Moreover, in all characteristics of reasons and so forth, one speaks of what is stated as being of one number and one kind. One should not say that two mutually contradictory characteristics collected in one place still constitute a reason and so forth, or that because of performing the same function in one characteristic, one has a non-pervasive reason.

Logically there should be four kinds of inconclusive reasons, since both are present. What about "audible"? Because it is uncommon (*asādhāraṇa*). If something is uncommon, all the distinctions of the property to be established pervasively include everything and are all doubtful reasons. Because only that has the nature of only being there, and because it is entirely separate, all those that are common lack a distinguishing reason. This is only non-contradictory with both there, and is of the nature of a doubtful reason. If in that context both parts are present, it is also a definitive reason, because it distinguishes from the rest; this is the distinction. If, addressed to one who accepts that the nature of sound is permanent, this should constitute a reason. If at that time there is no indication that "being produced" and so forth are reasons for impermanence, this meaning is possible. However, since when both are obtained they contradict in a single meaning, it is impossible, so it is a doubtful reason. Moreover, in this context, the force of perception and scripture is superior, so one should rely on these to seek a determination. A summary verse says:

> If a dharma is uncommon, common, or of determinate contradiction,
> Pervading everything in that regard,
> All are of the nature of doubtful reasons.
>
> Wrongly establishing the property or the subject,
> Whether by intrinsic nature or by distinction:
> This constitutes a contradictory reason
> If there is no counter-harm.
>
> Observing the thesis-property and examining carefully:
> If what is desired meets with counter-harm,
> Producing hesitation or reversal,
> Beyond this there are no pseudo-reasons.

Thus have reasons and pseudo-reasons been explained. Examples and pseudo-examples I shall now discuss.

> State the reason: the thesis follows it.
> Where the thesis is absent, the reason does not exist.
> These two are called examples.
> All the rest are similar to these [i.e., pseudo-examples].

Examples are of two kinds: similar (*sādharmya*) and dissimilar (*vaidharmya*). A similar example is as follows: "Sound is impermanent, because it is produced by effort and volition. All that is produced by effort and volition is seen to be impermanent, like a pot and so forth." A dissimilar example is: "Whatever is permanent is seen to be not produced by effort and volition, like space and so forth." The first affirms and denies; the second only prevents error. Because through agreement and separation one infers the meaning. Therefore, although addressed to one who does not posit the real existence of space and so forth, one can still demonstrate that where the thesis is absent, the meaning of the absence of the reason is established.

Moreover, for what reason does the first statement say the reason follows the thesis, and the second say where the thesis is absent the reason does not exist, without saying where the reason is absent the thesis does not exist? Because stating it this way can demonstrate that the reason is definitely present in the similar class and universally absent from the dissimilar class, without stating it in reverse. Further, a verse says:

> One should prove permanence by non-production,
> Or prove production by impermanence.
> If otherwise, what is not stated would result:
> Non-pervasion, undesired concomitance and separation.

Thus have the two examples of agreement and separation in forward and reverse been explained. The rest that are similar to these constitute pseudo-examples. What is meant by "the rest"? Cases where, although the subject, the predicate, and the dissimilar class do have agreement and separation, they are stated in reverse order. Or cases where agreement and separation are not performed, but one merely presents the co-presence of the subject and predicate and the co-absence in the dissimilar class. In either case, one or both may be unestablished or unexcluded. For example, asserting "sound is permanent, because it is intangible." The similar example states: "Whatever is intangible is seen to be permanent, like karma, like atoms, like a pot" and so forth. The dissimilar example states: "Whatever is impermanent is seen to be tangible, like atoms, like karma, like space" and so forth. By this is shown that in the similar example, the subject is not established, namely for one who does not accept permanent space and so forth.

Must one have both kinds of example-statements to constitute proof? Or, like the reason, is stating just one sufficient? If one follows correct logic, one should state both, because the complete demonstration that the thesis does not depart from its reason fully shows presence in the similar class and universal absence from the dissimilar class, and can properly counteract contradiction and inconclusiveness. If one part is already established here, then stating just one part also constitutes proof. If, as with sound, both meanings are mutually accepted, neither need be stated. Or by implication, one can display both.

Furthermore, in inference, one sees only this principle: where the inferential subject has its characteristic firmly determined, one recalls that in other similar cases it is definitely present, and where it is absent one recalls its universal absence; thus one generates a definitive understanding. Therefore the root verse says:

> Having reached certainty oneself,
> Wishing to generate certainty in others,
> One states the thesis-property, its correspondence,
> And what is established; all else is removed.

To show the thesis-property in the inferential subject, one states the reason. To show that it is inseparable there, one states the example. To show the inferential subject, one states the thesis. In the inferential subject, apart from these, there are no other components. By this, other elements such as examination (*ūha*), application (*upanaya*), and conclusion (*nigamana*) are excluded. If so, the example-statement should not be a separate component, since it displays the meaning of the reason. Though this is actually so, the reason-statement is only for displaying the thesis-property nature, not for displaying the presence or absence in similar and dissimilar classes, so one must separately state the similar and dissimilar examples.

If only the meaning expressed by the reason-statement is called the reason, what fault is there? What virtue is there in separately stating the example? Stating it separately is the virtue. It should be like the devices spoken of in the world, which have nothing to do with the meaning of the reason. If so, what fault is there? It would merely be analogizing the thesis-meaning with no function, not the meaning of proof, because it merely says "because of being produced" and classifies it with the similar example, without stating the meaning of proof and what is to be established. Moreover, if reason and example are separate, this has the thesis-subject's similar and dissimilar dharmas, but can never show the inseparability of the reason and what is established. Therefore it merely has the analogizing of the thesis-meaning but no function. Why is it without function? Because in the similar example, the thesis-property and thesis-meaning need not be of the same kind; and since this in turn would need another analogy to be established, it would lead to infinite regress. Also, there need not be fixed categories, and without showing absence in the dissimilar class, there is no delimitation that can serve as an example. Therefore a verse says:

> If the reason merely resembles the thesis
> Or resembles a distinction,
> The example should lead to infinite regress
> And the exclusion of dissimilar classes.

The world merely shows the co-presence of thesis, reason, and dissimilar class as the dissimilar example, not the absence of the reason where the thesis is absent; therefore it definitely has no function. If only the thesis-property is the nature of the reason, then an inconclusive one should also qualify as a reason. How can both kinds of example, possessing what is to be established, what establishes, and the dissimilar class, have this fault? If at that time the dissimilar class of what is to be established is not of a single kind, then this fault arises, as in the last example of the first and last groups of three. Therefore, the three characteristics are solely for displaying the reason.

By this logic, although all components can serve as reasons displaying what is established, only one component is provisionally called the "reason." Thus have the thesis and so forth, and their pseudo-forms, been briefly explained. These multiple propositions are called "proof" and "pseudo-proof." As appropriate, one states these proofs and pseudo-proofs to enlighten others.

For one's own enlightenment, there are only perception (*pratyakṣa*) and inference (*anumāna*). Verbal testimony (*śabda*), analogy (*upamāna*), and the rest are subsumed within these two, so there are only two means of valid knowledge. Because through these one can apprehend particular characteristics (*svalakṣaṇa*) and universal characteristics (*sāmānyalakṣaṇa*), there is no object of knowledge apart from these two for which one would establish additional means of knowledge. Therefore the root verse says:

> Perception is free from conceptual construction;
> The rest is generated from the stated reason.

Here, "perception is free from conceptual construction" means: if there is a cognition regarding objects such as color and so forth that is entirely free from all kinds, names, conventional designations, non-differentiation, and conceptual construction through various categories, and that operates distinctly through an unshared condition presenting each particular, it is called perception. Therefore a verse says:

> The object is not of a single characteristic;
> The sense faculty does not operate on everything.
> Only inwardly realized, beyond words:
> This is the domain of the sense faculties regarding color and so forth.

In the mental domain too there is cognition free from conceptual construction, operating only through direct realization. Also, the self-awareness (*svasaṃvedana*) of desire and so forth, and the cognition of meditators free from scriptural conceptualization: all these are perception. Moreover, there is no separate result of the means of knowledge here, because this very cognition arises resembling the object and is provisionally called a "means of knowledge" because it functions as if having that use. If the self-awareness of desire and so forth is also perception, why does one here exclude conceptual cognition? One does not here exclude the perception of self-awareness, because it is free from conceptual construction. It is only the aspect of this cognition that apprehends other objects that is not called perception. By this it is also said that memory, inference, desire, doubt, and confused cognition, regarding gross objects such as desire and so forth, are all not perception, because they operate through conceptual construction based on previously received impressions. Likewise, among all conventional existents, cognitions of pots, numbers, lifting, existence, pot-nature, and so forth are all pseudo-perception, because in regard to what truly exists, they operate through conceptual construction in different modes, falsely combining with other meanings.

Having explained perception, inference shall now be discussed. "The rest is generated from the stated reason" means: the cognition in question is a prior cognition; the rest arises from a reason that constitutes the stated proof, and takes that meaning as its object. This is of two kinds: the cognition of careful examination of the inferential subject, arising from perception or inference; and the recollection that this reason is inseparable from the thesis to be established. Through the former, by the power of what has been stated, one recalls that the reason is definitely present in the similar class and so forth. Because these are the proximate and remote causes of inference, both are called "inference." This is stated with respect to both the instrument and the agent.

Thus one should understand that inference for others also does not depart from this in constituting proof. Therefore a verse says:

> One thing has many dharmas;
> A characteristic does not operate on everything.
> Only by distinguishing from the rest
> Does one indicate definite accompaniment.
> The indicator likewise has many dharmas;
> Only by not exceeding the indicated
> Does it serve as an indicator, not otherwise.

Why is inference here established differently from perception? To show two gates. Here too one should call the result of inference "inference," and there too one should call the cause of perception "perception"; neither is precluded.

Having explained proof and pseudo-proof, refutation and pseudo-refutation shall now be discussed. The verse says:

> Refutation consists in words showing deficiency and so forth.
> Pseudo-refutation consists in the various sophisms.

Here, "refutation consists in words showing deficiency and so forth" means: the previously described words showing deficiency and other faults in the various components. Each such statement is called a refutation, because each can show that the opponent's thesis is not well stated.

"Pseudo-refutation consists in the various sophisms (*jāti*)" means: sophistical objections based on similar dharmas and so forth, called "similar faults" (*jāti*), are called pseudo-refutation, because for the most part they are devised against sound inferences to confuse others. They cannot show that the opponent's thesis is not well stated, because they refute without reason and are devised where refutation applies. Because they are of that type, they are called "similar faults." If such devices are applied to an inference that is itself unsound, or if one does not understand the faults of the inference, or if the devices are used precisely to reveal those faults, they are not called "similar faults."

> Showing a dissimilar class, by establishing difference through similar dharmas,
> Is called "similarity of similar dharmas." The rest is through dissimilar dharmas.
> Analyzing distinction is called "analytical similarity."
> "Should become one" establishes the fault of non-difference.
> Showing another reason for what is established
> Is called "similarity of apprehension."
> Challenging the distinct meaning as a doubtful reason
> Is called "hesitation."
> Because it states the meaning of the dissimilar class,
> What is undesired is called "implication."

[The treatise continues with detailed analysis of each type of sophistical objection, explaining how each constitutes a pseudo-refutation rather than a genuine refutation, examining cases involving similarity of similar dharmas, similarity of dissimilar dharmas, analytical similarity, non-difference similarity, apprehension similarity, hesitation similarity, and implication similarity. Each is shown to fail as genuine refutation because it either attacks a sound inference without proper grounds, confuses the characteristics of the reason, or misapplies logical categories.]

[The final sections address pseudo-refutations based on causal contact (*prāpti/aprāpti*) and temporal priority, as well as pseudo-refutations of "absence of statement," "production/non-production," and "specific action," showing how each constitutes a pseudo-refutation rather than a genuine objection.]

The closing verse:

> To open the medicine of wisdom for the intelligent,
> This gateway of correct reasoning and subtle meaning is revealed.
> May all those bewildered by heterodox measurements
> Cross beyond wrong paths and accord with true meaning.
