# 大乘北宗論
## Treatise on the Northern School of the Mahāyāna

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2836

Composed in Chinese. Tang dynasty, by the monk Huiguang (慧光)

---

## The Mahāyāna Mind

> I do not even give rise to the mind of generosity — how much less to the mind of miserliness and greed.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of upholding the precepts — how much less to the mind of transgression.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of patience — how much less to the mind of killing and harm.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of diligence — how much less to the mind of laziness.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of meditation — how much less to the mind of distraction.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of wisdom — how much less to the mind of foolishness.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of heaven — how much less to the mind of hell.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of compassion — how much less to the mind of poison and harm.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of purity — how much less to the mind of defilement.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of joy — how much less to the mind of anger and hatred.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of benefiting others — how much less to the mind of robbery and plunder.
> I do not even give rise to the vast and expansive mind — how much less to the narrow and contracted mind.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of emptiness and non-being — how much less to the mind of grasping at views.
> I do not even give rise to the upright mind — how much less to the mind of perversity and crookedness.
> I do not even give rise to the truly correct mind — how much less to the inverted mind.
> I do not even give rise to the Mahāyāna mind — how much less to the Śrāvaka mind.
> I do not even give rise to the bodhisattva mind — how much less to the ordinary person's mind.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of bodhi — how much less to the mind of afflictions.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of liberation — how much less to any mind of liberation.
> I do not even give rise to the mind of nirvāṇa — how much less to the mind of saṃsāra.

And again, in verse:

> Sorrow arises from the mind as sorrow; joy arises from the mind as joy.
> If one is deluded about the mind, what sorrow or joy is there to speak of?

> Having writing and words is called saṃsāra; having no writing and no words is called nirvāṇa.
> Having speech and explanation is called saṃsāra; having no speech and no explanation is called nirvāṇa.
> Having practice and study is called saṃsāra; having no practice and no study is called nirvāṇa.
> Having knowledge and wisdom is called saṃsāra; having no knowledge and no wisdom is called nirvāṇa.
> Having anger and dharmas is called saṃsāra; having no anger and no dharmas is called nirvāṇa.
> Having precepts and rules is called saṃsāra; having no precepts and no rules is called nirvāṇa.
> Having conditioned activity and fabrication is called saṃsāra; having no conditioned activity and no fabrication is called nirvāṇa.
> Cutting off afflictions is called saṃsāra; not cutting off afflictions is called nirvāṇa.
> Seeing liberation is called saṃsāra; not seeing liberation is called nirvāṇa.
> Seeing nirvāṇa is called saṃsāra; not seeing nirvāṇa is called nirvāṇa.
> Being averse to the world is called saṃsāra; not being averse to the world is called nirvāṇa.
> Delighting in the Mahāyāna is called saṃsāra; not delighting in the Mahāyāna is called nirvāṇa.
> Seeing the pāramitās is called saṃsāra; not seeing the pāramitās is called nirvāṇa.

The Mahāyāna has ten of these.

---

A few notes on this text: T2836 is an original Chinese composition rather than a translation from Sanskrit or Pali, so the header reflects authorship rather than translation. The penultimate line 我尚不起解脫心何況解脫心 repeats "mind of liberation" in both halves, which appears intentional: even the aspiration toward liberation is itself a mental construction to be released. The closing 大乘有十也 ("The Mahāyāna has ten of these") likely refers to a broader doctrinal framework not preserved in this fragment; the count does not align with the thirteen paired statements in the preceding section. The text reflects the Northern School's emphasis on mental quiescence and the transcendence of all conceptual distinctions, including the very categories of Buddhist practice.
