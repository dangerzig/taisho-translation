# 能斷金剛般若波羅蜜多經論頌
## Verses on the Treatise on the Vajracchedikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1514

Composed by Asaṅga (無著). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨).

---

> Supreme benefit, one should know,
> Pertains to oneself and those one belongs to:
> Attaining, not yet attained, and non-retrogression.
> This is called the supreme entrustment.
>
> Regarding the mind: expansive, supreme,
> Ultimate, and without inversion,
> The locus of beneficial aspiration:
> This vehicle's merit is complete.
>
> All six pāramitās are called giving,
> Being of wealth, fearlessness, and Dharma.
> Herein, one, two, and three
> Are called practice without abiding.
>
> For one's own person, in gratitude for kindness,
> One is unattached to any reward.
> To depart from non-arising,
> And to depart from practicing for the remainder.
>
> Subjugation and gathering are in the three spheres.
> Regarding characteristics, the mind dispels them.
> The successive doubts that arise
> Are all removed as they occur.
>
> If one would consider them as constructed and fabricated,
> The excellent marks are not the supreme characteristic.
> Because the three marks undergo change,
> Without this, one speaks of the Tathāgata.
>
> Cause and fruit are both profound.
> In that evil age they are proclaimed.
> This is not without benefit,
> Because of the eminence of three kinds of bodhisattvas.
>
> Because at former buddhas' places
> They upheld and practiced the training in morality,
> And because they planted roots of merit,
> They are called "endowed with morality, endowed with virtue."
>
> Able to sever the notion of self
> And also the notion of dharmas,
> These are called "endowed with wisdom."
> Two times four makes eight in distinction.
>
> A separate substance arising in continuity,
> Abiding until the end of life,
> Further seeking another destiny:
> There are four kinds of the notion of self.
>
> All are non-existent, hence not real;
> Being existent, they are inexpressible.
> Being the cause of verbal expression,
> There are four kinds of the notion of dharmas.
>
> Through the power of their faith and understanding,
> Through faith they generate the thought of reality.
> Not grasping according to the words,
> Grasping them as the correct teaching.
>
> The Buddha's understanding of the fruit is not inferential.
> Through the wisdom of his vow he knows.
> For those who seek profit and reverence,
> He blocks their own proclamations.
>
> Realizing non-abiding in dharmas,
> Being in accordance with this,
> Like abandoning a raft:
> This hidden intent should be known.
>
> The transformation-body is not the true Buddha,
> Nor is it the Dharma-speaker.
> The spoken Dharma is not the two graspings;
> What is spoken transcends verbal explanation.
>
> Self-benefit and teaching others:
> Accumulating merit is not without benefit.
> Merit does not sustain bodhi;
> Those two are what sustain it.
>
> Because of attaining the cause of one's intrinsic nature,
> The rest are what arise.
> Because they are exclusively buddha-dharmas,
> They accomplish the supreme merit.
>
> Because one does not grasp one's own fruit,
> It is neither graspable nor expressible.
> Because of liberation from the two obstructions,
> Subhūti is said to be "without contention."
>
> In the presence of Dīpaṃkara Buddha,
> He said "no dharma of realization was grasped."
> Through this, the dharma of realization was accomplished,
> Neither graspable nor expressible.
>
> The flow of wisdom is consciousness-only in nature.
> The buddha-land is not what is clung to.
> Being formless, and being supreme,
> Adornment is not permitted as the nature of adornment.
>
> Just as with Mount Sumeru,
> Regarding enjoyment there is no grasping.
> Not being of a nature with outflows,
> It is also not fabricated by causes.
>
> To display the many distinctions
> And to accomplish the supremacy,
> The merit of before and after differ;
> Further similes are presented.
>
> Both accomplish the quality of being honored.
> Through the supremacy of the corresponding outflow,
> Being the nature of the cause of afflictions,
> Through what is inferior also being supreme.
>
> That fruition surpasses suffering.
> Being a matter difficult to encounter,
> The object-shore is beyond knowing,
> Being unshared with anything else.
>
> Being of a nature extremely profound,
> Surpassing the rest in concise expression,
> The lineage being lofty and supreme:
> Compared to merit, this merit is supreme.
>
> At the time of practicing patient endurance,
> Though there is suffering, one practices what is wholesome.
> That virtue is immeasurable.
> For this reason it is called a supreme matter.
>
> Because there is no feeling of anger or rage,
> It is not called the nature of suffering.
> With peace and joy and great compassion,
> When practiced, it is not a suffering-fruit.
>
> The cause of generating the mind is not abandoned.
> Therefore one should firmly seek it:
> This is called the stage of attaining patience
> And the skillful means of this mind.
>
> One should know that right practice
> Is the cause of benefiting living beings.
> Regarding the characteristics of affairs concerning sentient beings,
> One should know they are all thoroughly dispelled.
>
> Those affairs are called the aggregate of names.
> The Supreme One dispels their notion.
> All world-honored ones are incomparable,
> Through according with the vision of reality.
>
> The fruit does not abide in the causal stage;
> This is the cause of attaining that fruit.
> Because the World-Honored One speaks truly,
> One should know there are four kinds.
>
> Establishing the essential teaching of the lesser vehicle,
> And expounding the meaning of the great vehicle,
> Through the various matters of conferring predictions:
> All are without any difference.
>
> Not attaining it in conformity,
> It is neither real nor false.
> For those who grasp according to the words,
> In response to them this is proclaimed.
>
> At all times and in all places it exists;
> In the true nature it is not obtained.
> Through ignorance there is abiding;
> Wisdom, non-abiding, attains the real.
>
> Without wisdom, it is like darkness;
> In leisure, wisdom is like light.
> That which opposes and that which is cured:
> Gain and loss are present before one.
>
> Through such right practice,
> One obtains such a measure of merit.
> For the practitioner of the Dharma's right practice,
> The function shall now be explained.
>
> Regarding persons, there are three kinds:
> Upholding, hearing, and broadly expounding.
> The meaning is obtained from others,
> And through what one has heard and reflected upon.
>
> This means ripening what is internal;
> The rest involves maturing other sentient beings.
> Through the greatness of the matter, the time, and the nature,
> Compared to merit, this merit is supreme.
>
> It is not the domain of an object; it is unique.
> The support is a great person,
> And it is difficult to hear.
> The unsurpassed cause increases.
>
> If one merely upholds the true Dharma,
> The supporting locus becomes a vessel.
> Karmic obstructions are swept away,
> And one swiftly attains the nature of wisdom and supernatural powers.
>
> Worldly wondrous affairs are perfected,
> And the maturation-fruit is most honored.
> In practicing this Dharma,
> One should know one obtains such karma.
>
> Through one's own conduct at the time of practice,
> Considering oneself already a bodhisattva,
> This is called an obstruction of the mind,
> Contrary to the mind of non-abiding.
>
> Because the prediction was conferred later,
> The practice at Dīpaṃkara's time was not yet supreme.
> The bodhisattva's practice was the same as that:
> Not real, because it was fabricated by causes.
>
> Without those characteristics as characteristics,
> This reveals it is not false.
> Because dharmas are buddha-dharmas,
> All are without conditioned characteristics.
>
> By the Dharma-body Buddha,
> One should know the simile of a great person:
> An unobstructed, perfectly endowed body,
> Being of a pervasive nature,
> And because of the greatness of the virtue-substance,
> Also called "great body."
> It is not a body that exists;
> He spoke of it as "non-body."
>
> Not understanding the dharma-realm,
> Generating the thought of liberating sentient beings
> And of purifying the land:
> This is called delusion.
>
> Regarding bodhisattvas, sentient beings,
> And all dharmas without self-nature,
> If one understands, though not yet a saint,
> One is called "endowed with a saint's wisdom," one should know.
>
> Though one does not see dharmas,
> This is not being without eyes.
> The Buddha fully possesses all five kinds.
> Because objects are illusory and false,
>
> The mind of various kinds turns in transmigration,
> Separated from the abode of mindfulness.
> That which lacks a holder constantly turns;
> Therefore it is called illusory and false.
>
> One should know that wisdom upholds.
> Merit is not illusory and false.
> To display the cause of this merit,
> The simile is again presented.
>
> Regarding the true Dharma-body,
> There are no minor marks perfectly complete.
> It is also not endowed with the major marks.
> It is of a non-body nature, one should know.
>
> In the Dharma-body there is no distinction.
> The Tathāgata is not two.
> He again speaks of possessing marks,
> Because the two substances are both non-existent.
>
> As the Buddha expounded, it too is non-existent.
> Speaking of the two is an attachment.
> Because they are not separate from the dharma-realm,
> The teaching too is without self-nature.
>
> Though what is spoken and the speaker are profoundly deep,
> It is not the case that there are none who have faith and reverence.
> Because they are neither sentient beings nor non-sentient beings,
> Neither saints nor non-saints, they accord with the true nature.
>
> Because there is not the slightest dharma,
> unsurpassed enlightenment should be known:
> Because the dharma-realm does not increase,
> The nature of purity and equality.
>
> And the unsurpassed skillful means:
> Because the nature of outflows is non-dharma,
> Therefore it is not a wholesome dharma.
> For this reason it is called wholesome.
>
> Though the spoken Dharma is neutral,
> It is not that attainment is absent, one should know.
> By this single Dharma-jewel,
> One surpasses immeasurable other jewels.
>
> Among the various classes of calculation and power,
> The cause also has distinctions.
> Any investigation in the world:
> Similes cannot reach it.
>
> Because the dharma-realm is equal,
> The Buddha does not liberate sentient beings.
> Among the various aggregates of names,
> None stand outside the dharma-realm.
>
> If one gives rise to attachment to dharmas,
> The fault is the same as attachment to self.
> Fixedly clinging to liberating sentient beings
> Is an attachment without attachment, a deluded attachment.
>
> One should not take the color-body
> As solely the Tathāgata's Dharma-body,
> Lest that wheel-turning king
> Be equated with the Tathāgata.
>
> The fruition-reward of possessing marks:
> The perfect merit is not permitted
> To bring about the Dharma-body,
> Because of the different nature of skillful means.
>
> One who merely sees color and hears sound,
> That person does not know the Buddha.
> This true suchness Dharma-body
> Is not the domain of consciousness.
>
> That merit is not lost;
> Its reward does not cease.
> The attainment of patience also does not cease,
> Because one has obtained the stainless.
>
> To further discuss the cause of merit,
> For this the simile is presented.
> Because that merit has no reward,
> It is rightly taken, not excessively taken.
>
> That merit brings about the transformation-fruit,
> Performing the work of benefiting sentient beings.
> That work, through natural spontaneity,
> Accomplishes buddhahood, manifesting in all directions.
>
> Coming, going, and the rest are transformation;
> perfect enlightenment is always unmoving.
> Within the locus of the dharma-realm,
> They are neither one nor different, one should know.
>
> Taking atoms to make ink:
> A simile displaying the dharma-realm.
> This discussion of making ink
> Is to reveal the exhaustion of afflictions.
>
> Not being an aggregate nor a collection in nature
> Reveals that this is not a unity.
> Regarding the collective nature of the whole,
> This makes clear that it is not difference.
>
> Not understanding, merely conventional speech:
> All the foolish wrongly cling.
> Severing the two, self and dharmas:
> Not because of realizing their non-existence.
>
> Therefore, seeing is non-seeing.
> Without an object, there is illusory clinging.
> Because this is a subtle obstruction,
> By knowing it thus, one severs it.
>
> Through attaining the two kinds of wisdom,
> And because of that concentration, it is removed.
> Presenting merit makes clear the transformation-body:
> Not without inexhaustible merit.
>
> When buddhas speak the Dharma,
> They do not say the body is a transformation.
> Because they do not themselves declare it,
> This is their truthful speech.
>
> The Tathāgata's nirvāṇa-realization
> Is neither fabricated nor different.
> This collection and fabrication have nine aspects,
> Through right wisdom's contemplation.
>
> Seeing, characteristics, and consciousness,
> Dwelling-place, body, and enjoyment,
> The past, the presently existing,
> And the not-yet-arrived: examine them in detail.
>
> Through contemplating characteristics,
> Enjoyment, and transmigration,
> Within conditioned affairs,
> One attains stainless sovereign mastery.
