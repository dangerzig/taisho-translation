# 般若守護十六善神王形體
## Bodily Forms of the Sixteen Benevolent Spirit-Kings Who Guard the Prajñā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1293

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Vajrabodhi (金剛智).

---

The Benevolent Spirit Dhṛtarāṣṭra: blue-green in color, with open mouth showing a wrathful expression. He wears armor and red garments. His right hand holds a great sword; his left hand cradles a halberd. His hair is purple.

The Benevolent Spirit Virūḍhaka: reddish-purple in color, with a wrathful expression and closed lips. His right hand holds a vajra; his left presses against his hip. He wears armor and blue-white garments. His sideburns and hair are dark blue.

The Benevolent Spirit Who Crushes Poison and Harm: his hair stands bristling upward. He is flesh-colored. His right hand holds a great sword. His left hand faces outward at chest level. He wears armor and white garments. The rest is as standard.

The Benevolent Spirit of Increase: flesh-colored. He has four arms and a wrathful demon-like countenance. His first right hand holds a great sword; his second right hand grasps a sword-wheel. His first left hand holds a willow branch; his second left hand cradles a half-moon. He wears a green kāṣāya, with a red skirt and jeweled necklace hanging from his neck.

The Benevolent Spirit of Joy: green in color. Atop his head is a peacock, golden in color. His right hand holds a single-pronged hook. His left hand presses against his hip. He wears armor and red garments. His sideburns and hair are vermilion. His face and body show a wrathful expression.

The Benevolent Spirit Who Removes All Obstacles and Difficulties: yellow in color, with an angry wrathful expression. He has six arms and wears bright white garments. His right hands hold a trident, a sūtra text, and a śarīra jeweled stūpa. His left hands hold a red lotus, a jeweled hook, and a jeweled conch. He wears a jeweled crown and a kāṣāya, with celestial garments billowing in the wind, and necklaces and bracelets as standard.

The Benevolent Spirit Who Removes the Stains of Sin: naked, wearing a red-green kāṣāya. His hair stands bristling upright, reddish-yellow in color. His right hand holds a staff with five prongs. His left hand is in a fist placed atop his head. He has wrathful eyes and open mouth, with sharp fangs protruding above and below, extremely terrifying.

The Benevolent Spirit of Endurance: sky-colored. His right hand holds a great sword; his left holds a lance. He wears a head-cloth, white in color, reaching down to his ankles where just the edge of his armor is visible. His sideburns and hair are purple.

The Benevolent Spirit Vaiśravaṇa: blue-black in color, showing the wrathful expression of a king, with closed lips. His right hand holds a vajra club; his left hand cradles a whole-body śarīra jeweled stūpa. He wears armor and red garments. His sideburns and hair are purple.

The Benevolent Spirit Virūpākṣa: flesh-colored, with a black silk cord on his arm, holding a brush in the posture of writing. He wears armor and green garments. His sideburns and hair are red. He has a gently smiling expression.

The Benevolent Spirit Free from All Fear: his bodily form is like that of Śakra Devendra, except that atop his head is a skull, and above the skull is a three-pronged form.

The Benevolent Spirit Who Rescues and Protects All: blue-white in color. His appearance is like that of Vaiśravaṇa Deva. Both hands are joined in añjali, and at the tips of all ten fingers are slightly open red lotus flowers. His garments are reddish-yellow.

The Benevolent Spirit Who Subdues All Māras: his hair stands bristling upright. He is dark flesh-colored. His appearance is adorned with jeweled necklaces. His right hand holds a sword; his left hand hangs down with palm facing outward. His garments are yellow.

The Benevolent Spirit Who Rescues All Beings: white-green in color. His countenance is like jade. Both hands are held in a hollow-palmed añjali with both index fingers bent to rest against the backs of the middle fingers at the first joint. Over his armor he wears a white kāṣāya. His skirt is red.

The Benevolent Spirit of Lionlike Valor: flesh-colored. He wears a lion jeweled crown. His first right hand holds an axe; his second right hand holds a sword. His first left hand holds a Brahmā manuscript; his second left hand holds a trident. He wears a kāṣāya, dark blue in color, with an undergarment of tiger skin. He is extremely fierce and wrathful.

The Benevolent Spirit of Courageous Mind-Ground: he wears armor. He is green in color. Like the Spirit Who Rescues All Beings, his two hands are in an external bond at chest level. He has a gently smiling expression.

---

*Colophon 1*: The Honjō states: Furthermore, the *Collected Dhāraṇī Sūtra*, fascicle three, gives the names and collective mantra of the sixteen benevolent spirits. Also, the monk Kū dreamed this and felt moved at his own stūpa-compound's Vinaya Master.

*Colophon 2*: The Kokkyō-shū, fascicle eight, states: In the world there circulates a text in one fascicle, three sheets, attributed to Vajrabodhi as translator, titled *Bodily Forms of the Sixteen Benevolent Spirit-Kings Who Guard the Prajñā*. Reading it, one can clearly see this is not from Vajrabodhi's brush or speech. The composition is quite crude. It is supposed that earlier masters recorded it based on Vajrabodhi's paintings, borrowing his name, without self-awareness of the presumption. We note that at the translation sessions, posts for oral translator, scribe, verifier of meaning, and literary polisher were established. The rendering of Sanskrit into Chinese was necessarily not the work of the translation master alone; there were both revised and unrevised versions. The only criterion was that the meaning be clear; how could literary polish be the standard? This year I obtained a copy from the abbot's quarters at Chishakuin, collated it against the copy held at Koikebō, and published it. The second year of Kyōwa, *mizunoe-inu* [1802], mid-summer month.

Inscribed by the śramaṇa Kaidō of Buzan Haseji-dera.

First collation completed by Jijun.

On the last day of mid-autumn, the third year of Bunsei, *kanoe-tatsu* [1820], collated once more against the copy that the ācārya Shūyō had Mitsuei proofread. Ryūkan.
