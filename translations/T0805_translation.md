# 佛說栴檀樹經
## Sūtra of the Sandalwood Tree, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0805

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha, together with his bhikṣus, was in the country of Vaiśālī. There was a householder who honored the Buddha's bright Dharma and invited the Buddha as an offering. When the Buddha had finished his blessing, the householder sat reverently before the Buddha, wishing to hear the meaning of the Dharma. The Buddha then smiled, and light of five colors came forth from his mouth, circled his body three times, and entered back through the crown of his head. Ānanda straightened his robes, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "The Buddha does not smile without reason. When the Buddha smiles, he will surely teach the Dharma and deliver beings. I pray you will explain."

The Deva Honored One said: "Excellent, Ānanda! With great compassion you wish to open the bridge of the Dharma for all. The deportment of a śramaṇa, you embody its form. I shall now set it forth. Listen carefully and receive it.

"In that country, five hundred men went to sea to gather treasure. They left their ship and returned on foot, traveling through deep mountains. At nightfall they stopped to rest. They prepared to set out early. Four hundred and ninety-nine men all departed together, but one man was sleeping soundly and missed his companions. Then it happened to rain and snow, and he lost the path. Stranded in the mountains, he wept and cried out to heaven. There was a great sandalwood tree, and the tree spirit said to the stranded man: 'You may stay here. I shall provide you with clothing and food. When spring comes, you may go.' The stranded man stayed. After three months, he addressed the tree spirit: 'I have received your grace and my life has been preserved. I have nothing to offer in return. But I think of my parents, who are now in my homeland. I truly wish to return. I beg you to send me on my way!' The tree spirit said: 'Very well!' The spirit then freely gave him a cake of gold: 'Not far from here you will find a town, and from there you can return to your homeland.'

"As the man was about to leave, he asked the spirit: 'This tree is fragrant and pure, rare in the world. Now that I must take my leave, I wish to know its name!' The spirit said: 'You need not ask!' The man said again: 'I have sheltered beneath this tree for three months. Now that I must depart, my heart is filled with reluctance. If I reach my homeland, I shall proclaim the tree's kindness!' The spirit then said: 'The tree is called sandalwood. Its roots, trunk, branches, and leaves cure a hundred illnesses among people. Its fragrance is heard from afar. It is a wonder of the world. People covet and seek it. You must not speak of it!'

"The man returned to his homeland, and his relatives near and far were joyful and glad to see one another. Not long after, the king fell ill with a headache. He prayed and made offerings to heaven and earth, to the mountains and rivers and all the spirits, but his illness did not abate. A famous physician examined him and said that only sandalwood could serve as a cure, and then the illness would be healed. The king immediately sought it among the people, but none had it. He then proclaimed throughout the kingdom that whoever could obtain sandalwood would be enfeoffed as a marquis and given the king's youngest daughter in marriage.

"The man, hearing of the great reward, went to the king and said: 'I know where sandalwood can be found!' The king ordered his close attendants to take the man to cut and bring back the fragrant tree. When they arrived at the tree, the emissaries saw it was towering and straight, its branches flourishing, its flowers and fruit resplendent, a sight rarely seen, and their hearts could not bear to cut it. Yet if they did not cut it, they would disobey the king's command and the illness would not be cured; if they cut it, their hearts ached within. They paced back and forth, not knowing what to do. The tree spirit then spoke from the sky: 'Go ahead and cut it, but leave the roots! After cutting it, smear human blood upon the stump and cover it with entrails, and the tree will grow back again and be restored as before.' When the emissaries heard the spirit's words, they ordered the tree cut down. The man was standing beside the tree, and as it fell to the ground, its branch struck and killed the man. The emissaries then deliberated with those around them: 'Just now the tree spirit said we would need human blood and entrails to anoint the tree. We were troubled in our hearts, not knowing whose to use. This man has now died; we shall use his.' They then cut him open and took his blood and organs. As the spirit had instructed, the tree immediately grew back. They loaded the cut timber onto carts and returned to the capital. The physician immediately prepared the medicine, and the king's illness was cured. The entire kingdom rejoiced. The king ordered that all the people of the kingdom who were ill should come to the palace gate. The king distributed the fragrant medicine, and all were cured. The king's body was at ease, the people were free from illness, and the entire kingdom was filled with joy, bringing about great peace."

Ānanda withdrew to his seat, bowed his head, and asked earnestly: "Why was this man so ungrateful, breaking his solemn vow to the tree spirit?"

The Buddha replied: "Long ago, in the time of Vipaśyin Buddha, there were a father and his two sons. The father upheld the five precepts, practiced the ten wholesome deeds, and observed the eight precepts of fasting without ever being slack. The elder son would always burn incense in the middle of the courtyard, in the open air, making offerings to all the Buddhas of the ten directions. The younger brother was foolish and did not know the Three Honored Ones. He would take his clothing and cover the incense. The elder brother scolded him: 'This is a grave offense! Why do you commit it?' The younger brother gave rise to an evil thought and vowed: 'I will cut off my brother's two feet.' The elder brother also gave rise to a thought: 'I will strike my brother dead.' The father said: 'Your quarreling, you two, gives me a headache.' The elder son replied: 'I wish that my body could be broken up as medicine to cure my father's illness.' What is spoken by the mouth must not be said in vain, and so life after life one receives the consequence.

"The younger brother gave rise to the evil intention of wanting to cut off his brother's feet, and so in a later life he led people to go and cut down the tree. The elder brother wished to strike his younger brother dead, and so he became the tree spirit, and the tree, as the embodiment of his own form, struck and killed his younger brother. The king who had the headache was the father. Because he had observed the fasting precepts with diligence, he was reborn as a person of honor. When he said 'you give me a headache,' in a later life he indeed had a headache. Each received their own consequences."

The Buddha said: "The retribution of transgression and merit follows one like a shadow follows a form."

The Buddha then broadly taught the precepts of body, speech, and mind. The householder thereupon entered the stream toward the fruit of the srotāpanna. He led his wife and children and offered his household wealth to the Buddha, and each made a vow for the path.

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the householder joyfully made obeisance.
