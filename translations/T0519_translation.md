# 佛說摩達國王經
## Sūtra of King Mada, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0519

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Juqu Jingsheng (沮渠京聲)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Rājagṛha, in the Bamboo Grove, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time, there was a king named Mada. The king was about to send out troops on a military campaign, and he selected several million people from throughout the country, all of whom were required to serve.

At that time, there was a bhikṣu who had already attained the fruit of arhatship. He had come to that country on his alms round and was taken into custody along with the others, brought to the palace gate. The king's horse-keeper assigned the bhikṣu to tend the royal horses, and he labored at this task for seven days. Afterward, the king personally came to inspect the military formations. The bhikṣu saw the king and, right there before him, rose lightly and flew into the sky, hovering in the air and displaying his supernatural powers. The king was terrified. He bowed his head to the ground and repented: "I was truly foolish and could not distinguish the true from the false. Investigate throughout the country and find who made this holy person do such work. That person shall be put to death."

The bhikṣu said to the king: "The fault is not the king's nor the people's. It was my own karma from a past life. Once, when I was practicing the path and constantly making offerings to my teacher, I prepared a meal for my teacher. My teacher said to me: 'First wash your hands, and then you may eat.' Foolishly, I thought to myself: 'My teacher does not tend royal horses; why should he not have washed his hands in advance?' My teacher then said to me: 'What you now think is a small matter, but afterward, what of the grave consequences?' When I heard these words, I was filled with anxiety. My teacher, knowing my thoughts, reflected: 'I shall eventually enter nirvāṇa; why should I cause another person distress?' That very night, at the third watch, he entered parinirvāṇa. From that time through the distant past, through repeated births and deaths, because of this, I received the retribution of my past sin and tended horses for seven days. Indeed, for every good and evil deed, there is corresponding fortune or calamity, like a shadow following the body."

When the king heard the bhikṣu explain the workings of sin and merit, his mind was opened and he was filled with joy. He requested to take refuge in the holy one. The bhikṣu said: "You should take refuge in the Buddha. The Buddha is the teacher of the three realms."

The king and all the people of his country followed the bhikṣu to the Buddha's dwelling. They bowed their heads and made obeisance to the Buddha, and received the five precepts, becoming upāsakas. The Buddha then manifested his excellent marks and supernatural powers for the king and the people, his radiance illuminating heaven and earth. He further taught them about impermanence, suffering, and emptiness. The king thereupon attained the fruit of stream-entry. All the people of the country received the five precepts and the ten virtues, took refuge in the Three Honored Ones, and observed the purificatory fast days each month, making this their constant practice.

Ānanda straightened his robes, made obeisance, and said to the Buddha: "Why were this king and the people of his country able to understand immediately upon seeing the Buddha and hearing the sūtra?"

The Buddha said: "Long ago, in the time of Kāśyapa Buddha, this bhikṣu was a śramaṇa, and the king was then an upāsaka. He made offerings with a pure mind for three days, wishing that wherever he might be born, he would always receive the merit of this. Now he has attained the path that transcends the world. Having come together at this assembly and heard the sūtra, he immediately attained the fruit of stream-entry."

The fourfold assembly of disciples, the heavenly nāgas and spirits, having heard the sūtra, were filled with joy. They came forward, made obeisance to the Buddha, and departed.
