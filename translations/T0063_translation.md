# 解夏經
## Sūtra on the Ending of the Rains Retreat

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0063

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmabhadra (Faxian)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in Rājagṛha, in the Kalandaka Bamboo Grove Monastery, together with an assembly of five hundred bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats. Their outflows were exhausted, their tasks accomplished, their heavy burdens removed, their own welfare attained, the bonds of existence severed, and their minds well liberated. Only one bhikṣu still occupied the stage of training, and the World-Honored One had already bestowed upon him a prediction that, having seen the Dharma and realized the Dharma, he would attain the full fruit.

At that time, the World-Honored One, the rains retreat being complete, at the time for ending the summer, on the fifteenth day, spread his seat among the assembly of bhikṣus and sat down. When the assembly was seated, the Buddha addressed the bhikṣus: "I have now attained the stillness of the holy life. This is my last body. With the supreme medicine I have severed all illness. My disciples, having thoroughly understood all dharmas, have fully penetrated them. Therefore I now expound the dharma of ending the rains retreat. Assembly of bhikṣus! Whatever I have done in body, speech, and mind during the summer, can you bear with it?"

At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra, having heard the Buddha's words, rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! As the Buddha has said, you have attained the stillness of the holy life, and so forth, up to asking whether we can bear with your actions of body, speech, and mind. We know that the Buddha's actions of body, speech, and mind are without any fault. We bhikṣus have nothing to bear with. Why is this? Our Buddha, the World-Honored One, can tame the difficult to tame, brings rest to those without rest, brings comfort to those without security, and causes those not yet at peace to attain peace. The Tathāgata well understands the right path, well explains the right path, and reveals the right path. And for us who delight in the awakening of śrāvakas, the Buddha has well expounded the teaching, enabling śrāvakas to practice in accordance with principle and realize the noble fruit. Therefore we have nothing to bear with regarding the Buddha's actions of body, speech, and mind."

Then the Venerable Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I now, in the Buddha's presence, regarding whatever unskillful actions of body, speech, and mind I have committed, seek the Buddha's forbearance."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Whatever your actions of body, speech, and mind, I shall bear with them. Why is this? You, Śāriputra, are endowed with morality and great learning, of few desires and content, have severed all afflictions, have generated great diligence, abide in right mindfulness, and possess the wisdom of meditative absorption, the wisdom of learning, swift wisdom, sharp wisdom, the wisdom of renunciation, the wisdom of thorough comprehension, vast and pure wisdom, profound wisdom, peerless wisdom, and the great jewel of wisdom. You cause the unseen to be seen, tame the untamed, teach the Dharma to those who have not heard it, and bring joy to those harboring anger. You can expound the Dharma tirelessly to the four assemblies. You are like the prince of a golden wheel-turning king who receives consecration and inherits the throne, ruling according to the Dharma. You, Śāriputra, are likewise. You are my son, receiving the consecration of the Dharma, inheriting the throne of the Dharma King, just as I turn the supreme Dharma wheel, and just as I have exhausted all outflows and realized liberation. Therefore, Śāriputra, regarding your three karmas, I now bear with them."

When Śāriputra heard the Buddha's forbearance, he sincerely prostrated in gratitude. He further addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! As the Buddha has borne with my three karmas, in this assembly of five hundred bhikṣus, regarding whatever unskillful actions of body, speech, and mind they have committed, may the World-Honored One likewise bear with them."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The three karmas of the five hundred bhikṣus I also bear with. Why is this? These five hundred bhikṣus are all arhats. Their outflows are exhausted, their tasks accomplished, their heavy burdens removed, their own welfare attained, the bonds of existence severed, and their minds well liberated. Only one bhikṣu still occupies the stage of training, and I have already bestowed upon this bhikṣu a prediction that, having seen the Dharma and realized the Dharma, he will attain the full fruit. Śāriputra! Therefore, regarding the three karmas of the five hundred bhikṣus, I bear with them all."

At that time, Śāriputra again addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The three karmas of myself and the assembly of five hundred bhikṣus, the Buddha has already borne with. I now have a question and wish to inquire further. May the Buddha, the World-Honored One, explain it for me. World-Honored One! Among these five hundred bhikṣus, how many bhikṣus have attained the dharma of the three knowledges? How many have attained liberation in both ways? And how many have attained liberation through wisdom?"

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Among these five hundred bhikṣus, ninety have attained the dharma of the three knowledges, ninety have attained liberation in both ways, and the remaining bhikṣus have attained liberation through wisdom. Śāriputra! Such bhikṣus have exhausted all afflictions and all abide in truth."

At that time, in the assembly there was a venerable named Vāgīśa, who thought: "I shall now, before the Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus, praise with a verse for the ending of the rains retreat." The Venerable Vāgīśa, having formed this thought, rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms reverently, and spoke this verse:

> On this fifteenth day, the rains retreat ends;
> Practicing the pure discipline,
> Five hundred bhikṣus
> Have all severed the bonds of affliction.
> All have exhausted the dharma of outflows
> And realized the noble fruit.
> Inwardly still, outwardly well tamed,
> Liberated and free from existence.
> Having exhausted the bounds of birth and death,
> Their tasks are all accomplished.
> The knots of ignorance and conceit
> Are severed without remainder.
> Our Buddha, the supreme honored one,
> Has severed the dharma of wrong thoughts
> And severed the dharmas with outflows,
> Well removing the illness of craving.
> Craving extinguished, it arises no more;
> Free from grasping, the great lion
> Has ended all the terrors of existence:
> Only our Buddha, the World-Honored One.
> Like a golden wheel-turning king,
> Always surrounded by a thousand sons,
> Governing well the four quarters,
> Taming all to the edges of the four seas.
> And like one victorious in battle,
> The supreme tamer and charioteer;
> The śrāvakas who attain the three knowledges
> Are likewise free from the dharma of death.
> The Buddha's children are all thus,
> Realizing cessation, never born again.
> I now pay homage to the Dharma King,
> The supreme lord, great as the sun.

When the bhikṣu Vāgīśa had spoken this verse, he returned to his original seat.

At that time, the Venerable Śāriputra and the bhikṣus, hearing the Buddha proclaim the dharma of ending the rains retreat, were gladdened in heart, leaping with joy, and faithfully received and practiced it.
