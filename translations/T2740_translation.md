# T2740 金剛般若義記

# Record of the Meaning of the Vajra Prajñā

*Note: This is an anonymous commentary (yìjì 義記) on the Diamond Sūtra (Jīngāng bōrě 金剛般若, T235). The surviving text is the opening portion (marked as "fascicle upper," juǎn shàng 卷上), providing an extensive introduction that discusses: the two categories of Buddhist teaching (Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna), the eight sections of the Prajñāpāramitā literature, the meanings of "vajra," "prajñā," "pāramitā," and "sūtra," and a twelve-part structural outline of the Diamond Sūtra. The bulk of the extant text is a meticulous analysis of the "evidence of trustworthiness" sequence (zhèngxìn xù 證信序) in the opening formula "Thus have I heard" (rúshì wǒ wén 如是我聞), examining each of the six elements (thus, I heard, one time, the Bhagavat, the place, the audience) with reference to the three holders of the tripiṭaka (Ānanda, Ānandabhadra, Ānandavajra/sāgara) and to Mañjuśrī's compilation of the Mahāyāna. The author and date are unknown, though the commentary draws on Bodhiruci (Liúzhī 流支) and other early Chinese exegetes.*

---

The true teaching is empty and quiescent, wonderfully transcending words and images. Since words and images are transcended, how could names and numbers find any place therein? Yet sentient beings dwell in the realm of seeing and hearing, inhabiting the domain of sight and sound. Unless one were to cast the shadow of images and spread the teaching of words and sounds, there would be no way to lead them to return to the source or draw them to enter the ultimate.

Therefore the Great Sage, the Tathāgata, about to display the true form in the pure land and manifest the responsive body in the sahā world, descended in spirit from Tuṣita, was born in the royal palace, vanquished Māra and realized the path, dwelt in the world and bestowed the teaching. The traces of form and speech arose from this point onward.

Yet the faculties of sentient beings are sharp or dull, different from one another; their minds are broad or narrow, of varying capacities. This is what causes the single-flavor holy canon to be divided into immeasurable sections. Though the teaching is divided into sections, broadly there are only two: first, the Mahāyāna full-letter Dharma-gate (dàshèng mǎnzì fǎmén 大乘滿字法門); second, the Hīnayāna half-letter Dharma-gate (xiǎoshèng bànzì fǎmén 小乘半字法門).

## The Two Teachings

The Mahāyāna full-letter teaching is what the Tathāgata taught from the beginning of his enlightenment to the time of his nirvāṇa, for the practitioners of the great path, the bodhisattvas and the rest. It comprises the *Avataṃsaka*, the *Daśabhūmika*, the *Mahāmegha*, the *Dharma-dundubhi*, the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā*, the *Mahāsannipāta*, the *Mahāparinirvāṇa*, and other immeasurable sūtras. These sūtras all elucidate the twofold emptiness of persons and dharmas, the four noble truths of non-creation, address those of the great capacity, advance the great practice, and transport beings to the utmost. Therefore they are called "Mahāyāna": words complete and meaning sufficient, hence "full-letter."

The Hīnayāna half-letter teaching is what the Tathāgata taught from the Deer Park at the beginning to the parinirvāṇa, for śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, the practitioners of the lesser path. It comprises the Vinaya, the Abhidharma, the Āgamas, the miscellaneous collections, and other immeasurable lesser-vehicle scriptures. These sūtras only elucidate the emptiness of persons and the four noble truths of creation, address those of the lesser capacity, advance the lesser practice, and transport beings not to the utmost. They are therefore called "Hīnayāna": words limited and meaning hidden, hence "half-letter." As the sūtra says: "Suffering and the rest, these I did not teach in those sūtras."

## The Eight Sections of Prajñāpāramitā

This present sūtra belongs to the Mahāyāna full-letter category. Within the Mahāyāna full-letter teaching, throughout the Tathāgata's lifetime, eight sections of prajñā were taught for the benefit of those present. What are the eight?

1. The first section: one hundred thousand verses.
2. The second section: twenty-five thousand verses.

These two sections remain in the western lands and are absent from this territory.

3. The third section: eighteen thousand verses. Here it is called the *Great Prajñāpāramitā* (Dàpǐn bōrě 大品般若).
4. The fourth section: eight thousand verses. Here it is called the *Lesser Prajñāpāramitā* (Xiǎopǐn bōrě 小品般若).
5. The fifth section: four thousand verses.
6. The sixth section: two thousand five hundred verses.

These two sections remain in the Indic original and have never been translated.

7. The seventh section: six hundred verses. Here it is called the *Mañjuśrī Prajñāpāramitā* (Wénshū bōrě 文殊般若).
8. The eighth section: three hundred verses. This is called the *Vajra Prajñāpāramitā* (Jīngāng bōrě 金剛般若).

Although the number is eight, there are only two names. The first seven sections all share the name *Mahāprajñāpāramitā*. The eighth section alone is named the *Vajra Prajñāpāramitā*. Though there are only two names, the reason for eight sections is that the occasions and assemblies were different, and the length varied between expansive and abbreviated; hence the count of eight sections.

The first seven, titled "Mahā" (Great), are named by contrasting the lesser to magnify the great, using the Dharma as the name; they are not without the meaning of "vajra." The eighth, titled "Vajra," employs a metaphor to illuminate the Dharma, displaying the function of cutting through delusion; it is not without the meaning of "Mahā." Each highlights one aspect by contrast.

Furthermore, the *Renwang* (Humane King), *Guāngzàn* (Praise of Light), *Dàkōng* (Great Emptiness), *Dàoxíng* (Practice of the Path), and others were said by Tripiṭaka Master Bodhiruci (Liúzhī sānzàng 流支三藏) to each be a single chapter from within the hundred-thousand-verse prajñā, not separate sections.

The great purport of all eight sections is to exhaust the essential meaning of the collected scriptures, plumb the very core of the myriad dharmas, reveal the eternal buddha-fruit, and reach the ultimate perfect reward. The buddha-nature is the direct cause; the ten bhūmis are the auxiliary cause. The two kinds of wisdom reveal the nature; when understanding is complete and the nature is revealed, one realizes the eternal fruit.

## The Meaning of "Vajra"

Now, the term "vajra" (jīngāng 金剛) is a name borrowed from a metaphor. The worldly vajra has many meanings; I shall briefly explain three:

1. The most refined of all jewels, rare and hard to obtain. If a person obtains it, poverty is eliminated and fortune gained.
2. Its substance is firm and solid; it can destroy all things and cannot be destroyed by anything.
3. Wherever the jewel resides, it can repel poisonous vapors.

The metaphor for prajñā is the same:

1. The wonderful wisdom of prajñā is the sovereign of the myriad practices. If a person obtains it, one departs from the poverty of birth and death and realizes the fortune and joy of nirvāṇa.
2. The wisdom of prajñā has dharmatā as its essence. It can exhaust the sources of delusion and cannot be destroyed by afflictions.
3. Wherever prajñā resides, one is not surrounded by deviant māras.

Because of this resemblance, it is borrowed as a comparison. Hence "vajra."

## The Meaning of "Prajñā"

"Prajñā" (bōrě 般若) is the pronunciation of the Indic term. In this land it means "wisdom" (zhìhuì 智慧): the ability to illuminate and exhaust the source of emptiness, to penetrate and survey the myriad existences. Hence "wisdom." The Indic pronunciation is retained as "prajñā."

## The Meaning of "Pāramitā"

"Pāramitā" (bōluómì 波羅蜜) is also a western term. In this land it means "crossing" (dù 度), also called "reaching the other shore" (dào bǐàn 到彼岸). The great nirvāṇa, purely transcending the defiled, is named "the other shore." If a person cultivates and studies prajñā, one can cross over birth and death and reach the great nirvāṇa. The Indic pronunciation is retained as "pāramitā."

## The Meaning of "Sūtra"

"Sūtra" (jīng 經): In the Indic language it is called *sūtra* (xiūduōluó 修多羅). In this land it means "root" (běn 本), for the words of the sage can generate good and eliminate evil in sentient beings, serving as the root of cultivating the path. Also, when principle and teaching are considered in relation to each other, they mutually serve as root. Principle does not reveal itself on its own; it is manifested through teaching. Without obtaining words, there is no way to attain realization. Hence the treatise says: "Though the fruit does not abide in the path, the path can serve as a cause." This shows that teaching is the root of principle. Yet teaching does not arise on its own; it is born from principle. Hence the treatise says: "If one does not realize, then one cannot speak." This shows that principle is the root of teaching. Possessing these two meanings, it is called "root."

The Chinese character "jīng" (經) is actually the name for the five classics of this land. It corresponds to *sūtra* but is not an exact translation. The reason it can be used is threefold: first, the speakers are comparable (sages in both cases); second, the meaning of "constancy" is the same (though ancient and modern sages come and go, the rules remain constant); third, it accords with what people esteem. Possessing these meanings, "jīng" is used in its place. "Jīng" means "constant" (cháng 常): though sages of earlier and later ages succeed one another, the decorum and rites of the noble remain constant throughout; hence "constant." The Buddha-Dharma is the same: though the buddhas of the three times variously pass away or remain, the teaching and its discipline remain constant; hence "sūtra."

## The Twelve-Part Structure

Concerning the treatise (*lùn* 論): after the Buddha's departure from the world, in the age of the semblance Dharma, in northern India (Běi Tiānzhú 北天竺) there was a Mahāyāna bodhisattva named Vasubandhu (Hé-xiūpántuó 和修槃陀), also called Vasubandhu, known in this land as "Celestial Kinsman Bodhisattva" (Tiānqīn púsà 天親菩薩). In the dharma-treasury transmission, he is the twentieth person. He was truly a practitioner of lofty conduct and supreme eloquence, who comprehensively understood the intent of the Tathāgata's greater and lesser teachings. Recognizing that this sūtra's text is concise and its meaning hidden, its principle and teaching profoundly subtle, and fearing that sentient beings of the final age, studying this abbreviated text, would have no way to attain understanding, he reverently relied on the holy canon and composed this comprehensive treatise, extensively explaining true reality and expounding the profound essentials. He wished to make the vajra teaching shine forth again and the inexhaustible Dharma-lamp illuminate the long night once more. The question-and-answer exposition is called a "treatise" (*lùn*).

The twelve divisions of the sūtra are:

1. The preface division
2. The protection and entrustment division
3. The abiding division
4. The division on practice in accord with reality
5. The division showing that the Tathāgata is not a conditioned characteristic
6. The division on those who have faith
7. The comparison of merit division
8. The division revealing the nature
9. The benefit division
10. The division severing doubts
11. The division on non-abiding in the path
12. The transmission division

By the three-part categorization: the first is the preface; the twelfth is the transmission; and the ten divisions in between constitute the main teaching.

## The Preface: Evidence of Trustworthiness

The preface has two kinds: first, the evidence of trustworthiness (zhèngxìn xù 證信序); second, the arousing sequence (fāqǐ xù 發起序).

The six phrases of the evidence of trustworthiness are:

1. "Thus" (rúshì 如是)
2. "I heard" (wǒ wén 我聞)
3. "One time" (yīshí 一時)
4. The Bhagavat (Póqiépó 婆伽婆)
5. The place of dwelling
6. Those who heard together

The overall purpose of the six phrases is to generate faith. "Thus" is a term of faith and compliance. The ocean of Buddha-Dharma is entered through faith and crossed through wisdom. One who has faith says: "This matter is thus." One who lacks faith says: "This matter is not thus." A person of faith, entering the Buddha-Dharma, obtains great benefit. Therefore, at the very beginning of the Buddha-Dharma, "thus" is proclaimed first. The detailed explanation is in the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra*.

"Thus" has two interpretations: one concerns the Buddha, and one concerns principle. Concerning the Buddha: the buddhas of the three times do not differ in what they teach; therefore it is called "thus." Because they teach the same thing, it can be called "is." Precisely because the buddhas all teach the same, it is worthy of faith. Concerning principle: the true characteristic of all dharmas is the same in past and present; therefore it is called "thus." Because it is spoken in accord with suchness, it can be called "is." Since these are words in accord with suchness, neither exaggerated nor diminished, they are definitively worthy of faith. Hence "thus is."

"I heard": who is the "I" here? Furthermore, the Buddha-Dharma teaches no-self; why say "I"? And it should properly be the ear that hears; why say "I heard"?

The explanation is: if speaking of particular transmission, this is Subhūti. If speaking generally, this is Ānanda saying "I heard." The Tathāgata's teaching has three vehicle dharma-treasuries, and the persons who transmit the Dharma are also of three kinds:

1. Ānanda (Ānántuó 阿難陀), meaning "Joy" (Huānxǐ 歡喜) in this land: holder of the Hīnayāna dharma-treasury.
2. Ānandabhadra (Ānántuó bátuó 阿難陀拔陀), meaning "Joy-Worthy" (Huānxǐ Xián 歡喜賢): holder of the middle-vehicle dharma-treasury.
3. Ānandavajra/sāgara (Ānántuó póqiéluó 阿難陀婆伽羅), meaning "Joy-Ocean" (Huānxǐ Hǎi 歡喜海): holder of the Mahāyāna dharma-treasury.

Though the three names differ, they are in fact one person. Just as a celestial woman said to Śāriputra: "Among the three vehicles, which would you wish to teach me?" The celestial woman replied: "If you would teach me with the lesser vehicle, I would be a śrāvaka" and so on. This is the same: because the Dharma differs, three names are created. This teaching comes from the *Sūtra of King Ajātaśatru's Repentance*.

Among these three, the first two include both directly heard and transmitted teachings. In the thousand-fold compilation, Ānanda ascended the seat and spoke the verse: "When the Buddha first taught the Dharma, at that time I did not see..." This shows that such teachings as the Buddha's traveling to Varanasi and teaching the four truths to the five monks were transmitted rather than directly heard. The third Ānanda can be said to have always heard directly. Hence the *Nirvāṇa Sūtra* says: "Ānanda, the one of great learning, naturally able to comprehend, understands what is permanent and what is impermanent." Some say he attained the "awareness-nature samādhi" and always heard everything. This confirms it is Ānanda who says "I."

However, each sūtra has a particular transmitter. The particular transmitter of this sūtra is Subhūti (Xūpútí 須菩提). How do we know? Because in the sūtra below, Subhūti, upon hearing the teaching, is moved to request and receive it.

Furthermore, in Mañjuśrī's compilation of the Mahāyāna, when the Tathāgata taught the Dharma in this world, and separately in other worlds the buddhas gathered to teach, this was called the "Sūtra of the Gathering of the Buddhas" or the "Buddhas' Assembly Sūtra." When Mañjuśrī later compiled the Mahāyāna, he summoned all bodhisattvas and great arhats, immeasurable and boundless in number. Each testified: "Such-and-such sūtra, I heard from the Buddha." Subhūti said: "The *Vajra Prajñā*, I heard from the Buddha." This textual evidence confirms it.

Yet the Buddha-Dharma teaches no-self; why say "I"? The explanation is: though in principle there is no self, the conventional designation is not destroyed. If one did not say "I," then there would also be no "hearing," and the path of teaching would be cut off. Therefore one is permitted to say "I." It is like exchanging gold for copper: in the rules of buying and selling, no one laughs. Within conventional truth, one provisionally says "I"; the wise do not find this strange.

"Hearing": when the ear-faculty is intact, sound is in the hearable domain, and the intention produces the aspect of hearing, and these conditions combine, one should say "the ear hears." Why then say "I heard"? The explanation is: though there is no sovereign self, there is still the provisional designation that coordinates the whole. Assigning the parts to the whole, one says "I heard."

"One time": this determines the time when the sūtra was spoken. As in the *Sūtra of the Humane King*, the Buddha taught the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā* in his twenty-ninth year. This includes the *Humane King Prajñā*, the *Praise of Light Prajñā*, the *Vajra Prajñā*, and others. Though we know the general time period, interpreters do not take this as definitive, as one cannot specify precisely when this particular sūtra was completed. Therefore it simply says "one time."

"Bhagavat": this is the speaker. Because one heard from the Buddha, it is worthy of faith. "Bhagavat" (Póqiépó 婆伽婆) is an Indic term. Among the immeasurable names of the buddhas, this name is considered supreme, comprehensively combining many meanings. Some say it means "understanding emptiness" and also "the ability to discriminate the general characteristics of all dharmas." Some say it means "one of great renown." Some say it means "the ability to destroy afflictions." Some say it means "great person." Since the meanings are many and cannot be definitively settled, the Indic term is retained.

"At Śrāvastī" (Shèpótí 舍婆提): this determines the place. The dharma-body has no form and does not require a land of residence. Some say the Tathāgata always abides in holy conduct. Why then say "at Śrāvastī"? The explanation is: the Buddha has two bodies. One is the body born of parents. Speaking of the body born of parents, one follows the place of responsive teaching; hence "at." "Śrāvastī" is an Indic term. In the past, two brothers, the younger named Śrava ("young") and the elder named Vasti ("indestructible"), studied the way of the sages at this place. Later, people built a city there and named it after them, abbreviating the names. The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* extensively explains the reasons for dwelling in this city. The Tathāgata most often resided in two cities: to repay the kindness of the body born of parents, he mostly stayed at Śrāvastī; to repay the kindness of the dharma-body, he mostly stayed at Rājagṛha. Also, these two cities had many sharp-witted and intelligent people and great debaters. The Tathāgata was like the lion king. There were also those with severe wrong views; the Tathāgata was like medicine. Also, these two cities were always prosperous. Also, to protect the Śākya clansmen among his disciples: for these and many other reasons, he was at Śrāvastī.

"Jetavana, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park" (Qíshù Jǐgūdú yuán 祇樹給孤獨薗): this specifies the particular location. The city itself is vast, with two monasteries: one the Hall of Mṛgāra's Mother, and one the Jetavana Monastery. Therefore the specific place must be indicated. "Jeta" (Qídá 祇陀) is Prince Jeta, son of King Prasenajit. "Anāthapiṇḍada" (Jǐgūdú 給孤獨) is the householder Sudatta, who was renowned for giving to orphans and the poor, and so was named "Giver to the Solitary and Destitute." Prince Jeta originally owned this park; Sudatta later paved it with gold to purchase it. Each contributed: Jeta's name was linked to the trees and Sudatta's to the park. Both are preserved: "Jetavana, Anāthapiṇḍada's Park."

"Together with a great assembly of monks" (yǔ dà bǐqiū zhòng 與大比丘眾): this lists the audience. Among the various assemblies, only the monks are mentioned. "Monk" (*bhikṣu*, bǐqiū 比丘) is an Indic term with no single equivalent in this land. It is a name for a renunciant. Some say it means "frightener of Māra": when the ordination is announced, Māra in the heavens hears and is greatly alarmed; hence "frightener of Māra." Others explain it through four modes of livelihood, or as "pure almsman." "Great" means these are all great persons reverenced by the unlearned. Or it refers to a large assembly. "Saṅgha" (*sēngqié* 僧伽) means "harmonious assembly": from four persons upward, up to hundreds of thousands, all share the single name of "harmonious assembly." "One thousand two hundred and fifty persons": this gives the round number. Among them were a thousand former disciples of certain teachers, and two hundred and fifty disciples of Śāriputra and Maudgalyāyana who were converted. Hence "one thousand two hundred and fifty persons together."

These persons always accompanied the Buddha. The explanation is: to repay the Buddha's kindness, they constantly accompanied him, adding to the solemnity of his assembly and enhancing the Tathāgata's prestige. Also, they listened to the Dharma. The question is posed: if they had nothing to seek, why listen to the Dharma? Even the Buddha listened to the Dharma, as the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā-śāstra* explains.

The above six phrases complete the evidence of trustworthiness. What follows is the arousing sequence. The arousing does not always involve gathering a great assembly of those with affinity. Sometimes the Tathāgata acts to provoke questioning; sometimes he enters samādhi. The circumstances for mounting the seat and entering are varied.
