# T2901 法句經

# Dharma Verses Sūtra

*Note: This sūtra (also titled "The Buddha Speaks the Dharma Verses Sūtra," Fó shuō fǎjù jīng 佛說法句經) is not to be confused with the famous Dhammapada translations at T210/T211. This is a distinct Mahāyāna sūtra set in the Sun-Moon Palace (Rìyuè gōng 日月宮中) with a celestial assembly of one hundred thousand bodhisattvas. The principal interlocutor is the Bodhisattva Bǎomíng (寶明, "Jewel Radiance"), who receives teachings on the emptiness of names, the contemplation of the three places (inner, outer, and middle), the nature of a good spiritual friend (shànzhīshì 善知識), and a climactic set of verses declaring that all dharmas, including the afflictions, are "originally undisturbed" (běnlái wú suǒ dòng 本來無所動). The sūtra also features Bodhisattva Pǔguāng Zhuāngyán (普光莊嚴, "Universal Light Adornment") arriving from the eastern buddha-land. The translator is unknown, and the text has fourteen chapters.*

---

## Chapter 1: The Sequence on All Bodhisattvas Merging Mind into Awakening

Thus have I heard. At one time the Bhagavat was in the Victorious Treasury Hall of the Sun-Moon Palace, together with one hundred thousand celestial bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. Their names were: Bodhisattva Śreṣṭha-rāśi ("Victorious Accumulation"), Bodhisattva Samantabhadra, Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, Bodhisattva Vajragarbha, Bodhisattva Vajraketu, Bodhisattva Vajramati, Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, Bodhisattva Viniścitamati ("Resolute Wisdom"), Bodhisattva Maitreya, Bodhisattva Tryadhva-pratibhāna ("Eloquence of the Three Times"), Bodhisattva Sadāpraṇidhāna ("Ever-Abiding Vow"), Bodhisattva Balavaśitā ("Power and Sovereignty"), Bodhisattva Anutpādakarman ("Non-Arising Activity"), Bodhisattva Sadāmaitrī ("Ever-Kindness"), and Bodhisattva Sadādarśanaikāloka ("Ever-Beholding One-Mark Radiance"): such were the one hundred thousand bodhisattva-mahāsattvas assembled. There was also a bodhisattva-mahāsattva named Bǎomíng (寶明, "Jewel Radiance"), together with immeasurable monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen, hundreds of thousands of myriads. There were also immeasurable devas, nāgas, yakṣas, Dharma-protecting good spirits, and countless bodhisattvas from other regions, all assembled together.

## Chapter 2: Bodhisattva "Indestructible in All Dharmas" Speaks of Past Causes

At that time, among the assembly there was a bodhisattva named "Indestructible in All Dharmas" Bǎomíng, who, by the Buddha's spiritual power, came forward and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! In the past, under the Buddha Dīpaṃkara (Rándēng fó 然燈佛), I left home to study the path. At that time my name was the monk Děngxián (等賢, 'Equal Worthiness'). At that time the World-Honored One bestowed upon me the prediction of my present bodhisattva status, giving me the name 'Indestructible in All Dharmas.'"

Then Bodhisattva Bǎomíng addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Why did you bestow upon me the name Bǎomíng? I humbly ask the World-Honored One to explain the reason for this name."

The Buddha said: "Listen well, listen well, and reflect carefully. I shall now, for the celestial assembly that seeks the celestial vehicle, explain this decisive, subtle, and essential Dharma of the great vehicle, as well as the vajra speech-phrases of your name."

The celestial assembly received the teaching and listened. The Buddha told Bodhisattva Bǎomíng: "Good man! For now, examine the names of these buddhas. Are they existent or nonexistent? Are they real or unreal? Good man! If names were existent, then telling someone about food should satisfy their hunger. If one could be satiated without eating, then all food and drink would be useless. Why? Because merely speaking of food would immediately satisfy, making eating unnecessary. But if names were nonexistent, then the Tathāgata Dīpaṃkara would not have bestowed the prediction upon me, nor given you your name. If there were no such bestowal, I should not have become a buddha. Good man! You should know: words and phrases, by their enduring suchness, by that very suchness, when one investigates all dharmas, the nature of names is empty. They are neither existent nor nonexistent. Good man! If names are empty, then regarding all abuse, slander, ridicule, censure, or praise, one should neither feel anger nor delight. Why? Because abuse and slander, and praise, are both equally empty."

## Chapter 3: Contemplating the Empty Nature of Sound and Realizing Ultimate Reality

"Good man! If one encounters such circumstances as described above, one should contemplate: 'This sound, is it large or small? Blue, yellow, red, or white? From what direction does it come? Now that it comes to abuse me, where does it attach? What is its shape and appearance?' Examining above and below with precision, one obtains nothing whatsoever. If there is nothing to obtain, one should not give rise to anger or delight. Having made this contemplation, hold firmly and do not abandon it. You should know that before long you will attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Why? By contemplating the empty nature of sound, one realizes suchness. It is like a person who at night dreams of flying through the air, and during sleep sees the body in flight. The bodhisattva-mahāsattva is just like this: contemplating the empty nature of sound, one will certainly attain the realization of emptiness. One comes to know that sound merely deceives the ear-faculty and has no real substance."

## Chapter 4: Contemplating the Three Places as Empty and Attaining Bodhi

"Good man! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva who wishes to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi should contemplate the three places: internal, external, and intermediate. These are the three places. Among the three places, there is no real dharma; this is liberation. Liberation is bodhi itself."

Bodhisattva Bǎomíng addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is meant by the contemplation of the three places?"

The Buddha told Bǎomíng: "Good man! To understand the three places, one should cultivate the contemplation of the body. The eye is internal; form is external; the mind is in between. These are the three places. Some sentient beings, from beginningless time, have not known whether these three things are empty or real. They falsely give rise to various afflictions and inverted delusions. Therefore I now teach true contemplation, to sever all delusions.

"Good man! The eye does not see itself. Form does not name itself. The mind has no shape or substance. These three are all nonexistent. Therefore the eye, not seeing itself, always dwells within. Form, not naming itself, always dwells without. The mind, having no form or appearance, has no fixed dwelling. Good man! The eye, not seeing itself, belongs to causes and conditions. Conditions have no seeing-characteristic; therefore the eye is empty. When form belongs to the eye, it is called form. But if the nature of the eye is empty, form also has no reality. Why? Because what is seen from the empty eye has no real substance.

"Good man! The bodhisattva-mahāsattva, knowing the eye to be empty, is internally undefiled. Knowing form to be empty, is externally unattached. Knowing the discriminating mind to be empty, extinguishes all formations. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mental faculty are likewise.

"Good man! Knowing the eye belongs to conditions and seeing has no self-nature, the eye sees throughout the day yet is as though it does not see. The nature of form belongs to the eye yet does not name itself; throughout the day it names, yet is as though it does not name. Good man! With this empty eye one constantly looks at empty form. Examining inwardly and outwardly, the mind cannot be found, and though one constantly seeks forms, they can never be fully grasped. The ear and sound, nose and scent, tongue and taste, body and touch, mental faculty and dharmas are likewise.

"Good man! One should contemplate: 'If the eye and form were not empty, the eye dwelling within should not discern the external, and form dwelling without should not follow the eye. The eye has a fixed dwelling, form also has a fixed dwelling, but the mind is unconditioned and should not dwell in the conditioned. Why? Because the conditioned and the unconditioned are opposed in nature.' Therefore, know that the eye, form, and mind are empty, penetrating, and unobstructed.

"Good man! The mental faculty is internal; dharmas are external; the mind is in between." The same analysis is then applied: the mental faculty does not know itself, dharmas do not name themselves, the mind has no form; all three are nonexistent. The faculty belongs to conditions, conditions have no knowing-characteristic, therefore the faculty is empty. If dharmas belong to the faculty, they are called dharmas; but if the nature of the faculty is empty, dharmas also have no reality. Knowing the faculty is empty, one is internally undefiled. Knowing dharmas are empty, one is externally unattached. Knowing the mind is empty, one extinguishes all formations.

"If the faculty and dharmas were not empty, the faculty dwelling within should not discern the external, and dharmas dwelling without should not follow the faculty. Therefore know that the faculty, dharmas, and mind are empty like space, unobstructed.

"It is like a mirage: seen from afar, it resembles water. An unwise person, driven by thirst, rushes toward it, but the closer one gets, the more it vanishes. Someone standing beside the mirage knows that this ground has never had water. Seeing the person running, they know it is a false appearance and laugh, saying: 'There is no water here; it is only the shimmering of heat, deceiving your eye-faculty.' When that person hears this, the heat and thirst of the mind are quenched.

"Form is just like this. Ordinary people, lacking wisdom, think it is real. Driven by the thirst of desire, they generate the appearance of craving and with blazing mind rush headlong toward it. But the characteristics of form belong to conditions: the more one examines, the more they vanish. One who has realized the true characteristic knows that within these aggregates there has never been form, and there has never been consciousness. Seeing ordinary people craving form and rushing about, one knows their delusion, laughs, and teaches the true Dharma, saying: 'Within the aggregates there are no aggregates. Within the mind there is no mind. Within thought there is no thought. These are merely the energy of conditions, deceiving your eye-faculty.' If a wise person hears this, the false thirst of mind is quenched. The realms, sense-fields, and entrances are likewise.

"Good man! It is like an ordinary person who at night dreams of the body emitting various kinds of radiance, eating a hundred flavors of food, traveling far to other lands, obtaining fine clothing and horses, attended by a hundred attendants. Suddenly anger arises, and one commands soldiers to kill thousands. But upon awakening, the bodily light immediately vanishes, and there was no distant journey at all. The radiance and forms are empty, without any existence. The traveler in the dream, the soldiers, and those killed all lack any form or appearance.

"Form is just like this. All sentient beings, from beginningless time, beneath the cliff of darkness, under the blanket of ignorance, sleep through the long night, dwelling in the house of dreams. They falsely perceive various forms and give rise to all dharmas: good men and good women, birth and death and nirvāṇa, killing and harming one another without end. All sentient beings consider this to be real. But one who has attained the path and awakened knows it is all false and empty, and comes to know that birth and death and nirvāṇa have the same characteristic, and that men, women, and all forms are empty in their original nature and substance.

"Good man! If form is like this, all dharmas are also the same: like an echo in an empty valley, like the solidity of a plantain tree, like the moon reflected in water, like a flower in the sky, like the child of a barren woman, like lightning enduring, like the hair of a turtle in water, like the horn of a running rabbit: ultimately without reality. All the wise should eliminate all views. Like a person born blind in this world, do not discriminate among forms. If one does not discriminate, know that before long one will attain the path of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

"Good men and good women! All sentient beings, because afflictions obstruct the cause of wisdom, from having no eyes generate the notion of eyes. By this false imagining they perceive external realms and call them 'form.' When craving and attachment arise, and because of craving and attachment, they transmigrate through the three realms of existence, like a whirling fire-wheel without rest."

## Chapter 5: Drawing Near to True Good Spiritual Friends

"Good man! All sentient beings who wish to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi should draw near to good spiritual friends, inquire about the essentials of Dharma, and they will certainly hear such profoundly essential phrases."

Bodhisattva Bǎomíng addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What constitutes a good spiritual friend?"

The Buddha said: "A good spiritual friend is one who skillfully understands the profound Dharma of emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness, non-arising, and non-ceasing; who thoroughly comprehends that all dharmas from the very beginning are ultimately equal, without karma, without retribution, without cause, without effect, with nature and characteristics as suchness; who abides in ultimate reality; and who, within ultimate emptiness, blazingly establishes. Such is called a good spiritual friend."

## Chapter 6: Twenty-One Similes for Good Spiritual Friends

"Good man! Good spiritual friends are your parents, for they nurture your bodhi-body. Good spiritual friends are the cause of your eyes, for they guide you on the bodhi-path. Good spiritual friends are your feet, for they carry you away from birth and death. Good spiritual friends are your staircase, for they support you in reaching the other shore. Good spiritual friends are your food and drink, for they enable the growth of your dharma-body. Good spiritual friends are your jeweled shelter, for they cover your merit-body. Good spiritual friends are your bridge, for they transport you across the ocean of existence. Good spiritual friends are your treasure, for they rescue you from poverty and suffering. Good spiritual friends are your sun and moon, for they illuminate you and free you from darkness. Good spiritual friends are your life, for they cherish and protect you without pause. Good spiritual friends are your armor and weapons, for they vanquish the māras and grant fearlessness. Good spiritual friends are your rope, for they pull you out of the hells. Good spiritual friends are your wonderful medicine, for they cure the illness of your afflictions. Good spiritual friends are your sharp knife, for they cut through the net of your attachments. Good spiritual friends are your timely rain, for they moisten and refresh your bodhi-body. Good spiritual friends are your bright lamp, for they dispel the darkness of the five hindrances. Good spiritual friends are your signpost, for they teach you to proceed on the correct path. Good spiritual friends are your firewood and flame, for they ripen the food of your nirvāṇa. Good spiritual friends are your bow and arrow, for they slay the thieves of your afflictions. Good spiritual friends are your warriors, for they defeat the army of your birth and death. Good spiritual friends are your tathāgata, for they destroy your afflictions and bring you to nirvāṇa.

"Good man! Good spiritual friends have such immeasurable merits. Therefore I now teach you to draw near to them."

## Chapter 7: Bǎomíng and the Audience Weep with Uncontrollable Sorrow

Then Bǎomíng, together with the celestial assembly, having heard the Buddha explain this wonderful Dharma and the profoundly essential teaching about good spiritual friends, cried out loudly and wept, tears falling like rain, grieving and lamenting, unable to contain themselves. They reflected: "From kalpas beyond reckoning, we have been guarded by good spiritual friends. It is for this reason that today we have encountered the Tathāgata and heard the profound Dharma. To have such an encounter is the power of good spiritual friends, not our own power." Reflecting that from the very beginning they had never repaid this kindness through drawing near, after speaking these words, they again cried out loudly in grief and lamentation, beating their chests and weeping to the heavens, like a person newly bereft of parents, wailing and sobbing, as if dying and being revived.

At that time the World-Honored One, seeing this, generated a compassionate mind and exhaled a long sigh. By the power of that sigh, the great trichiliocosm trembled, and all the buddha-lands likewise shook greatly.

## Chapter 8: Bodhisattva Universal-Light-Adornment and Others Bear Witness

Thereupon, in the east, beyond hundreds of thousands of myriads of koṭis of incalculable lands, there was a country called the Jewel Land, with a buddha named Ratnalakṣaṇa ("Jewel Characteristic"). There was a bodhisattva named Pǔguāng Zhuāngyán (普光莊嚴, "Universal Light Adornment"), who saw this earth tremble and addressed the Buddha Ratnalakṣaṇa: "World-Honored One! This earth-trembling, what auspicious sign is it?"

That buddha answered: "To the west of here, beyond hundreds of thousands of myriads of koṭis of incalculable lands, there is a country called Sahā. The Buddha Śākyamuni there is teaching the Buddha-Dharma for sentient beings, expounding the decisive Mahāyāna and the gratitude owed to good spiritual friends. Therefore this auspicious sign has appeared."

Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment addressed the Buddha Ratnalakṣaṇa: "World-Honored One! I wish, together with this celestial assembly, to go to that sahā world and, at the place of the Buddha Śākyamuni, inquire about the matter of serving good spiritual friends and about the profound Dharma."

The Buddha Ratnalakṣaṇa said: "Good man! Now is the right time. Go quickly."

Thereupon, Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment, together with immeasurable celestial bodhisattvas, each manifested their bodies seated on jeweled lotus lion thrones, emitting celestial radiance. Like celestial strongmen bending their arms, they arrived in an instant at the sahā world and remained suspended in space, serene and still.

Bodhisattva Bǎomíng, seeing this radiance, addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is this radiance? I have never seen it before."

The Buddha told the great assembly and Bodhisattva Bǎomíng: "These are celestial bodhisattvas from the land of the Tathāgata Ratnalakṣaṇa. Having heard that I am teaching you the Mahāyāna sūtra and the teaching of good spiritual friends, and having seen the sign of the earth trembling, they have come here."

Bǎomíng and the great assembly, hearing this, were all greatly delighted. Universal Light Adornment, from the sky, rained down a multitude of jeweled flowers. When the rain of flowers was done, he came to the place of the Buddha Śākyamuni, circumambulated countless times, joined palms in reverence, and stood to one side.

The Buddha told Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment: "Good man! When you set out, was your World-Honored One's energy and strength well? You have traveled far; are you not fatigued?"

Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment bowed at the Buddha's feet and replied: "My World-Honored One's vital force and strength are always fine, nothing else. He earnestly sends unlimited regards."

Then, among the assembly, Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī addressed Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment: "Good men! You have come from afar. What do you wish to inquire about?"

Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment addressed Mañjuśrī: "Venerable One! Having received word that the World-Honored One is teaching the profound Dharma and the merits of good spiritual friends, I have come wishing to inquire about the matter of serving good spiritual friends and their merits."

Mañjuśrī said: "If you have inquiries, please ask quickly. All these great assemblies desire to hear."

Thereupon, Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment, knowing the assembly's intent, bowed at the Buddha's feet and said: "World-Honored One! We have long had weak wisdom and have been unable to understand the profound and subtle meaning of the decisive Mahāyāna, nor have we known the abundant kindness and virtue of good spiritual friends. As the Buddha has previously taught, their kindness is weighty beyond reckoning. We humbly ask the World-Honored One to teach the great assembly the method of drawing near to good spiritual friends."

The Buddha said: "Excellent, excellent! Good man! You are able, for the sake of all beings, to ask about such a Dharma. Listen well, listen well, and reflect carefully. I shall teach you."

## Chapter 9: The Afflictions Are Bodhi

"Good man! Good spiritual friends have great merit. They can enable you, within greed, anger, delusion, wrong views, the five hindrances, the five desires, and all the dusts of labor, to establish the Buddha-Dharma. Without giving rise to a single thought, one obtains great merit.

"It is like a person who boards a sturdy ship to cross the great ocean: without moving body or mind, one arrives at the other shore. Good spiritual friends are just like this. With the great vow-ship, in the ocean of birth and death, they transport you. Without moving body or mind, you arrive at the shore of nirvāṇa.

"Good man! It is like a person who wishes to traverse a dangerous road and relies on one person who is skilled in martial arts, with armor and weapons all prepared, along with a fine jeweled chariot drawn by five horses. Body and mind are fearless, and one arrives without difficulty. Good spiritual friends are just like this: the dharma-body is robust, skilled in expedient means, with the martial arts of the six perfections, the armor of kindness and compassion, all fully prepared. Coming in the Mahāyāna drawn by the five supernatural powers, they transport you to fearlessness, freeing you from the three evil paths and all the difficulties of birth and death.

"Good man! It is like a person who relies on the laṅkā jewel and takes the sweet-dew elixir, eliminating all diseases so that life is not cut short. Good spiritual friends are just like this: relying on the great jewel of wisdom and taking the Dharma medicine, the illness of afflictions is eliminated, and the life of wisdom is inexhaustible.

"Good man! It is like a person who relies on the lion king and walks among all beasts without any fear. Good spiritual friends are just like this: if one relies on them, one roams amid the dust of toil without any fear.

"Good man! It is like a person who relies on Mount Sumeru; even if the eight winds blow, they cannot be shaken. Relying on good spiritual friends is just the same: the eight winds of difficulty cannot shake one.

"Good man! It is like a person who relies on the garuḍa king and surveys the great ocean without fear of any disaster or the danger of the makara fish. Relying on good spiritual friends is just the same: traversing the ocean of birth and death, one fears neither māras nor the difficulties of non-Buddhist paths.

"Good man! The immeasurable merits of good spiritual friends, if one wished to describe them in detail, could not be exhausted even through entire kalpas."

## Chapter 10: Seeking Good Spiritual Friends without Sparing Life or Possessions, and Removing Doubt

"If a wise person encounters a good spiritual friend, one should make offerings without sparing one's own life, to say nothing of material possessions, family, spouse, children, or country. How could one be miserly? Whatever actions the good spiritual friend takes, in whatever circumstances, one should not give rise to even a hair's breadth of doubt. Why? If one has a doubting mind, one cannot properly receive the profoundly essential Dharma phrases. All of you in the great assembly should sever doubts regarding good spiritual friends."

## Chapter 11: Universal Light Asks, and the Tathāgata Answers in Compassionate Verses

At that time, Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How does one properly receive the profoundly essential Dharma phrases? What is meant by 'profound'?"

The Buddha said: "Do not ask such a question. If I were to explain it now, śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and newly-aspiring bodhisattvas, upon hearing this Dharma, would have their minds constricted, and their wholesome roots would be severed."

Bodhisattva Universal Light Adornment said: "World-Honored One! For the sake of beings like us, please demonstrate through expedient means, to create future merit for all sentient beings."

Then the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> Children of the Buddha, listen well and with sincerity;
> I now speak as it truly is.
> The nature of all dharmas
> Has from the beginning never been disturbed.
> All buddhas rely on greed and anger
> As their seat upon the place of awakening.
> The dusts of labor are the seeds of buddhas;
> They have from the beginning never been disturbed.
> The five hindrances and five desires
> Are the tathāgata's family nature,
> Always adorned by these;
> They have from the beginning never been disturbed.
> Lust and wrong views,
> Together with the remaining fetters,
> Are the characteristic of ultimate liberation;
> They have from the beginning never been disturbed.
> All buddhas, from the very beginning,
> Have always dwelt within the three poisons,
> Nurturing the hundred dharmas,
> Thus becoming the World-Honored One.
> All dharmas, from the very beginning,
> Are neither this nor that;
> The nature of this and that is quiescent;
> It has from the beginning never been disturbed.
> All sentient beings
> Truly have no birth or death;
> Birth and death are nirvāṇa itself;
> This has from the beginning never been disturbed.
> The characteristics of all dharmas
> Arise from conditions, without any fabrication;
> The nature of fabrication is suchness;
> It has from the beginning never been disturbed.
> All words and letters
> Have no reality and no support;
> All are the same single quiescence;
> It has from the beginning never been disturbed.
> The merit of all giving:
> Therein, the three elements are empty,
> Ultimately unobtainable;
> The merit of giving is like a mirage.
> If one speaks of keeping precepts,
> There is no good and no deportment;
> The nature of precepts is like space;
> One who clings is lost in delusion.
> If one sees an angry person,
> And uses patience as a bridle,
> Know that anger is like a mirage;
> Patience too has nothing to be patient about.
> Those who speak of vigorous effort
> Speak for the sake of overweening pride.
> Where there is no overweening pride,
> There is no good and no vigorous effort.
> If one gives rise to the mind of effort,
> This is delusion, not true effort.
> If the mind can be free of delusion,
> Then effort is never in vain.
> If one studies the various samādhis,
> That is agitation, not seated meditation.
> When the mind follows the realm of objects,
> How can that be called concentration?
> The myriad phenomena, spread like a net,
> Are stamped by a single dharma.
> How is it that within the single dharma
> Various views are generated?
> "One" is also not truly one;
> It is meant to destroy all counting.
> Those of shallow wisdom hear this
> And take "one" to be truly one.
> If there are those who hear this Dharma,
> They should always practice the conduct of quiescence.
> Know that practice too is quiescent;
> This is the path of bodhi.
> If one wishes to realize this Dharma,
> Draw near to good spiritual friends,
> Learn well all expedient means,
> And deliver all sentient beings.
> I now speak this Dharma
> To gather in all sentient beings.
> If there were no view of self,
> Ultimately there would be nothing to teach.
> These are vajra phrases,
> Resolving all wrong views.
> All non-Buddhist schools,
> With all their might, cannot destroy them.
> If there are sentient beings
> Who hear such a sūtra as this,
> Though they be in the three evil paths,
> They will ultimately attain cool bliss.
> If one hears the name of this sūtra
> And understands even a single phrase,
> One will certainly be born in a buddha-land;
> How much more so one who recites it!
> Wherever this sūtra abides,
> I am always present there,
> Protecting such a person
> And enabling them to attain the unsurpassed path.

## Chapter 12: The World-Honored One, Having Spoken These Verses, Bestows the Prediction upon Bǎomíng, and Universal Light and the Assembly Attain the Forbearance of Non-Arising

When the World-Honored One had spoken these verses, Universal Light and the great assembly attained the forbearance of the non-arising of dharmas.

Bodhisattva Bǎomíng then received the prediction: "In a future life, after eighty myriads of kalpas have passed, you shall become a buddha named the Tathāgata Bǎomíng, Arhat, Samyak-Saṃbuddha, Vidyācaraṇa-Saṃpanna, Sugata, Lokavid, Anuttara, Puruṣadamya-Sārathi, Śāstā Devamanuṣyāṇāṃ, Buddha Bhagavat. At that time, the sentient beings shall be exclusively of the one great vehicle, with no śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas. The land shall be pure and adorned, and the lifespan shall surpass that of Amitāyus by a hundred thousand myriad times."

Having bestowed the prediction upon all the bodhisattvas, the Buddha was about to leave his seat.

## Chapter 13: Transmission

At that time, Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī came forward and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! In the future, into whose hands will this sūtra come? By what karmic connection will they hear this sūtra?"

The Buddha said: "Good man! This sūtra is profoundly deep and difficult to hear. It is like vajra: no ordinary being can see it, except for Indra. This sūtra is the same: śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas cannot perceive it, except for bodhisattvas. It is like the lion king: no beast dares approach, except the nāga king. This sūtra is the same: śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas have severed all hope of it; only bodhisattvas can receive it.

"Even if a person were to fill the four continents with pure gold and give it in charity, it would not equal hearing the name of this sūtra by ten thousand times. Even if a person were to fashion couches of the seven treasures, with cloths of crystal, and supply all beings throughout Jambudvīpa for an entire kalpa, it would not equal hearing the name of this sūtra by ten thousand times.

"If a good man or good woman is able to hear this sūtra, know that such a person has personally attended innumerable buddhas and planted the seeds of manifold virtue, and only thereby is able to hear it. Good man! In the future, this sūtra shall come into the hands of bodhisattvas of the eighth bhūmi."

## Chapter 14: Guard This Sūtra as One's Eyes, Preferring to Lose One's Life Rather Than Be Negligent

The Buddha told the great assembly and Bodhisattva Bǎomíng: "Guard this sūtra as you would guard your eyes. Rather than lose your life, do not be negligent regarding this sūtra. If any sentient being generates a mind of faith in this sūtra, know that such a person is a true disciple of the Buddha, without any doubt."

When the World-Honored One had finished speaking this sūtra, all the great assembly, devas, nāgas, and the eightfold assembly were all joyful, and they received and practiced it in accordance with the Dharma.
