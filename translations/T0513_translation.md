# 佛說琉璃王經
## Sūtra of King Virūḍhaka, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0513

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in Kapilavastu, at the Śākya monastery, beneath a nyagrodha tree, together with five hundred bhikṣus, his attendant Ānanda, and the vajrapāṇi warrior Rūpa.

In the city, five hundred elders of the noble Śākya clans together built a lecture hall for the World-Honored One. They made a solemn pact among themselves: "When the lecture hall is completed, we shall invite the Perfectly Awakened One to hold an offering there. No śramaṇa, brahmin, elder, householder, or commoner may ascend to this hall before the Buddha. Whoever violates this pact shall be punished without measure."

The king of Śrāvastī at that time had a crown prince named Virūḍhaka, who had been born together with a lapis lazuli jewel and was therefore given that name. Leading his guards on a visit to his maternal relatives, he came into the city, saw the lecture hall, lofty, spacious, and magnificently pure, elegant and extraordinary, rare in all the world. He went up into it and stopped there to rest and enjoy the cool.

The overseer of the lecture hall went and reported to the noble clans: "The crown prince of Śrāvastī has come and stopped in the lecture hall." When the noble clans heard this, they were furious and cursed him: "This is our family's property. What special virtue does he possess that he dares to ascend this hall? We built this hall for the Buddha, to prepare a fine meal and invite the World-Honored One and the holy assembly of arhats. Only after the offering is complete may we ourselves sit here. Yet this lesser person has presumed before his betters and placed himself here." They immediately sent a messenger to insult him to his face, urging him to leave at once without delay. The ground where he had walked was scraped clean of his footprints, and the jeweled stairs he had trod were replaced.

When Crown Prince Virūḍhaka heard their curses, his expression changed and his heart was filled with toxic rage. He commanded his grand historian: "Record this deeply. When I become king, I shall destroy this clan." The grand historian Asaddha, skilled in reading the heavens and interpreting omens, wrote down the account and placed it inside his sash. Harboring his malice and knowing it was wrong, Virūḍhaka departed and returned home, and never again came to pay respects to his maternal family.

The prince's father, King Prasenajit, together with Queen Mālikā, took their carriage and retinue to the Jetavana garden. He descended from the carriage, removed his canopy, took off his crown, and laid aside his sword. He removed his flywhisk, took off his shoes, and dismissed his four kinds of troops. Walking along a small path with Mālikā, he prostrated with all five limbs, bowed his head in reverence, and sat to one side.

Crown Prince Virūḍhaka had returned to the palace and found no one. He asked his attendants: "Where have the father-king and queen-mother gone?" They said: "They went to see the Buddha."

When the prince heard this, he eagerly led his forces, without disarming, and went straight to the monastery, saying: "Now is the time."

Thereupon the prince attacked and killed his escort, the king's close ministers, more than five hundred people, all annihilated at once. He stripped the king of his crown, canopy, sword, flywhisk, shoes, garments, and carriage ornaments. There was no one outside to report what had happened.

At that time, the World-Honored One was teaching the king and queen about the impermanence of the world, the coming together and parting of love and desire, and the verses of the Dharma. The king was established in non-retrogression, and the queen attained a glimpse of the path. When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the king bowed and withdrew. He did not see his attendants; only corpses strewn about. Only two men, the king's hat-bearer and crown-bearer, had escaped into the trees, and now they met the king. The king asked: "Where are all my officers?" The two men answered: "The prince led his troops by force and has returned to the palace."

The king said to Mālikā: "Our son has acted treasonously, plotting rebellion like this. Had I known of this beforehand, I would have stepped aside and given him the kingdom and gone to the monastery." Kinsmen on his left took pity on the king and queen, whose delicate bodies were unaccustomed to walking, and provided them a carriage, crude and humble though it was. They mounted and set out for the city gates.

Earlier, the prince had posted five hundred men at the gates and commanded the gate-keepers: "If the father-king comes, do not let him enter." The king said: "If I may not enter, where shall I go?" They said: "The prince's edict commands the great king to leave the territory."

King Prasenajit then, weeping and choking, lamented in verse:

> True indeed is the World-Honored One's teaching;
> What he proclaimed is certain and correct.
> Rise and fall, honor and lowliness:
> Nothing whatsoever has permanence.
> Better to keep the precepts and be mindful of the path
> Than to covet generous salary and position.
> To chance upon a Dharma assembly
> Is worth more than a hundred million lands.
> A king rules the country and indulges his will,
> Greedy and sullied, immersed in desires.
> To hear the Dharma and receive liberation,
> By this the dust and defilements are removed.

At that time, the numberless thousands of onlookers who heard the king's lament: eight hundred of them gave rise to the aspiration for the great path, and all were established in non-retrogression. With sorrowful countenances and no joy, Queen Mālikā said to the king: "Please do not be distressed. We may go together to the country of my father." They set out at once. After seven days and seven nights, they arrived at the settlement of Tuṣa in Kapilavastu. It happened to be dark and the gates were closed; they could not enter. Both were hungry and thirsty with nowhere to turn, no place to beg. They stopped at a waterside place where people washed vegetables and found some discarded radishes. They ate them, but the king's stomach swelled and the pain was so great that he died.

The queen, stricken with grief, raised a loud wail. The settlement's watchman asked: "Who is there?" She said: "I am the queen." He asked: "Where is the king?" The queen said: "How painful! The king has died by the water."

The settlement's gatekeeper immediately ran to inform the Śākya nobles. When they heard the terrible news, they rushed out in shock and alarm. They all came forth, brought funeral gifts, placed the body in a coffin, and cremated it according to proper rites. All mourned and lamented; none were unmoved.

At that time, the noble Śākyamaṇḍita, father of Gopā, together with the other powerful elders, lamented in verse:

> Having sons, having wealth, one worries and frets;
> Even the self is not one's own, how much less sons and wealth.
> The foolish rely on their power, but honor has its end;
> The prince used the kingdom, and is cast into hell.

Two hundred and fifty Śākya nobles, deeply contemplating impermanence, attained non-retrogression. Five hundred women, not yet ordained, attained the patience of non-arising dharmas.

Thereupon Crown Prince Virūḍhaka, hearing that the father-king had died, took the throne and proclaimed himself king. The heterodox grand historian took out the written record from his sash, verifying the original account of the remembered grudge. When Virūḍhaka heard it, he flew into a rage and his fury surged. He summoned the four kinds of troops and marched on Kapilavastu.

The Buddha, knowing his intention, came out from the monastery and stopped at a crucial point along the road, sitting beneath a withered tree. In a short while, the prince's army arrived.

King Virūḍhaka saw the World-Honored One from afar and immediately dismounted from his elephant-carriage. He bowed his head to the ground and knelt before the Buddha, asking: "God Among Gods! There are the bodhi tree, the Ficus indica, the Ficus religiosa, the nyagrodha, the udumbara, the sāla, the tāla, and the khaṇḍiśara: seven kinds of trees whose shade is lofty and great, whose virtue is luxuriant and flourishing. Why have you abandoned them to sit beneath this withered and thorny tree?"

The Buddha told King Virūḍhaka: "Though there are seven trees with shade luxuriant and flourishing, can such luxuriance be permanent? I sit beneath this thorny tree and find peace and security, because I grieve with compassion for my kinsmen."

The king thought to himself: "The ancient records and secret prophecies of the treasury say that when one deploys troops on a campaign and encounters a śramaṇa, the army should turn and go back. How much more so now that I have encountered the Buddha! How can I advance?" He bowed his head to the Buddha's feet and immediately turned back his forces.

He returned to Śrāvastī, and not long after, the attendant Ānanda, the warrior Rūpa, and the rest of the retinue accompanied the World-Honored One back to the nyagrodha garden. He had Ānanda spread a seat and announced to the fourfold assembly that all should gather. At that time, the Buddha's honored countenance was without radiance, his halo was without light, and his robes had changed color. Ānanda, after settling into his seat, straightened his robes, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms, and said: "In all my years attending the Honored One, I have never seen these three changes."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "In seven days from now, the Śākya nobles of Kapilavastu will all be slain and destroyed. I manifest these changes because I am wearing mourning for my kinsmen."

Mahāmaudgalyāyana came forward and said to the World-Honored One: "What is there to worry about? With my supernatural powers, which the Perfectly Awakened One has fully investigated, I could raise the entire Śākya country with my right palm, toss it into the sky where it would neither reach the heavens above nor touch the earth below. How then could King Virūḍhaka manage to kill them?"

The Buddha told Maudgalyāyana: "I know your majestic powers are more than sufficient for this. But the sins of past lives: who will take their place?"

Maudgalyāyana further said: "I could enclose this country in an iron mesh cage, cover it from above with a bowl, and hurl it to a land in another direction, with no trace left behind. Or I could divide the four sides of Mount Sumeru, place the people inside the mountain, and then close it up, so that each is safely situated. Or, since the great ocean is deep and vast, measuring three million three hundred and sixty thousand miles, I could float this country in its center, so that the people would have no thought of coming or going. Or I could set this country upon the summit of Mount Sumeru, or invert it, causing no destruction. Or I could sink it down to the vajra-foundation. Or I could hurl King Virūḍhaka's four kinds of troops beyond the great Iron-Ring Mountains, keeping the two enemies from fighting each other."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! The World-Honored One trusts you. These ten supernatural powers are sufficient for such a feat. But the calamitous sins of the Śākya nobles from past lives: who can fully repay them and take their place?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "Is there no stratagem that could protect this country and keep it safe?" The Buddha said: "If the Śākya people could be of one mind and have no dealings or connections with outside enemies, the country could be preserved."

The grand historian admonished the king three times: "The king should act now and march on the Śākyas." The king, hearing this, flew into a rage and raised his army. The World-Honored One, knowing this, went again and sat beneath the withered tree. This happened three times in all, and three times the king turned back.

On the fourth campaign, the Buddha did not intercede. Elite troops of the four divisions arrived at the border of the Śākya country. The Śākya nobles had also gathered in great numbers and came out to defend. The clansmen were valiant and fierce, with exceptional skill in archery. Some could shoot at forty miles, some at twenty miles, some at ten miles, some at seven miles, each according to their natural ability. They could shoot through flying birds and split an initial arrow, never missing. They could split a single hair into seven parts. Even at a distance of miles, they hit their targets. At the sound of the bowstring, they struck without fail; not a single shot was wasted. When battle was joined, they shot at King Virūḍhaka's army, piercing banners and snapping standards, tearing canopies and shattering flagpoles, severing chariot shafts and harness straps, cutting armor belts and traces, snapping bowstrings and crossbow cords, yet they did not harm the lives of elephants, horses, cattle, or other animals. They shot earrings, arm-rings, finger-rings, bracelets, and necklaces without touching flesh. They trimmed beards, left and right eyebrows, hair, and eyelashes without harming the body.

Virūḍhaka was terrified and asked his ministers: "How far away is the enemy? Yet their arrows reach this far and cause such destruction." They replied: "Some are forty miles away, some twenty miles, some ten miles, and the closest are seven miles."

The king, hearing this, was even more shaken and unable to compose himself. He was about to retreat and scatter. The grand historian admonished him: "Great King, do not fear! You must not break and flee. The Śākya people all observe the Buddha's precepts. They are lay devotees of pure faith and compassionate non-killing. They use arrows to frighten but have no intent to harm. They would rather lose their own lives than cut short the life of another. Regroup the formation, unite your will, and press the attack. Hold fast and be patient; victory will not be long."

The ministers protested: "We observe that the enemy's arrows strike from a great distance, invisible in their approach. It is not something force can resist. We fear being crushed, which would bring permanent ruin upon us all. The arrows cannot be withstood. Better to hold position and stay secure. The grand historian's counsel should not be followed exclusively. Each man's heart is stirring, his will set on fleeing." The king flew into a great rage and commanded them to advance into battle.

The Śākya forces outside the walls broke and fled into the city. They shut the gates and fortified their positions, arranging defenses in a ring. For seven days, the besiegers signaled terms: surrender or be annihilated. They called upon the enemies within to submit at once or face total destruction.

The Śākya nobles deliberated: should they hold the city and defend it, should they shut the gates and submit, or should they secretly flee? In their confusion and disarray, they drew lots to determine the will of the majority. Those who accepted the lots were many; those who did not were few. The minority yielded to the majority. The gates were opened, aiding the evil and bringing disaster. Inside and outside conspired, wanting the enemy to prevail. The few who counseled good were outnumbered. The gates were opened and the enemy entered, killing the five hundred gate-guards, slaughtering without measure. Thirty thousand nobles were captured alive and buried in the ground with only their heads exposed. Herds of elephants were driven foot to foot and trampled them to death. Then plows were driven over their heads. All who suffered this cruelty had attained stream-entry.

Śākyamaṇḍita, who was an old friend of King Prasenajit, addressed his countrymen: "Observe impermanence carefully. This bitter retribution is the repayment of past sins. Do not harbor resentment. The living are instantly followed by death; the existing suddenly perish. How many kinds of pain there are, the axe dismembering the body." He sighed in grief: "To share fortune at one time and to suffer disaster in one place."

More than seventy thousand of the noble clan, still living, were then captured. Iron chains were placed around their necks. A thousand noble women were strung on chains and stood in rows along the roadside. Noble young children and infants were placed on racks and shot dead.

King Virūḍhaka saw Śākyamaṇḍita suffering with the multitude and turned to ask his ministers: "Who is that person?" They answered: "Śākyamaṇḍita. He has come because he wishes to make a request." The king said: "Bring him forward."

Śākyamaṇḍita presented himself and said: "The king's father and great king treated me with generous kindness and heard my petitions. I beg to be heard in full." The king, knowing the circumstances, allowed him to speak freely. "I beg you to restrain your fury and temporarily halt the troops. Do not let them run wild, causing yet more destruction. I will enter the pool; in a moment I shall return. I have a secret plan to discuss with the king. After I emerge from the water, you may resume the military display." The king's heart and mouth agreed: "A person cannot stay long under the water." So he granted the request.

Thereupon Śākyamaṇḍita, for the sake of the people of his country facing this great calamity, bade farewell, entered the pool, untied his hair and bound it to a tree, and submerged himself in the water. He did not return for a long time. The king was greatly astonished and sent attendants to search for him. Beneath the tree's roots, they found his corpse and brought it out to the pool's edge. The king was deeply moved and filled with compassion: "For the sake of his clan, he drowned himself. Such is his righteousness. I am the ruler of the country, yet I cannot restrain a small anger. Should I press the battle harder, causing even more destruction?"

The first three hundred million had already perished. The next three hundred million were spared in turn, rescued and allowed to survive; they fled in panic and escaped with their lives. A further three hundred million had been at home making offerings and feasting, enjoying entertainment and music, unaware that outside there was a catastrophe of mass slaughter, unknowing of the panic and terror, peaceful and composed as usual, without any inkling.

King Virūḍhaka gave Śākyamaṇḍita an honorable burial and treated his descendants with generosity. Having subdued the Śākyas, the king installed new leaders, settled affairs, and returned to Śrāvastī.

The Buddha and his disciples went to Kapilavastu. He saw that many of the people were wounded and maimed. He also observed the many women who had been mutilated, stripped of hands, feet, ears, noses, and limbs, their bodies exposed, lying in ditches and trenches, with nothing to cover themselves. Such is the suffering of the world; the cruelty with which the merciless harm one another.

The Buddha said to the bhikṣus: "King Virūḍhaka has indulged his will in heinous evil. His sins are this great. In seven days from now, there will be a fire from hell that will burn him to death. In this present life he has committed sin, and in this present life he will receive retribution."

The grand historian's oracles foretold portents identical to the Buddha's words. The king was greatly terrified. He boarded a ship and went out to sea, hoping to save himself, and stopped in the middle of the ocean. When the seven days had passed, fire spontaneously arose from the water, burning the ship and the king. In an instant, all was reduced to ashes.

The World-Honored One, with compassion for those who lay exposed, used his supernatural power to shake the azure-indigo palace of Trāyastriṃśa Heaven. Śakra, the queen, Sujā, and innumerable devas each brought heavenly garments and came down to offer them, covering all those who lay exposed and suffering.

The Buddha spoke a verse for the women:

> All that your eyes, noble ones, now behold,
> The present has transformed to this.
> Exhaust the old; do not create the new.
> Hereafter you may gain lasting liberation.

When the Buddha had spoken the verse, he further taught the Dharma for them. All who had come to observe, the devas, nāgas, spirits, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, brahmins, householders, elders, and common people, innumerable thousands, having heard what the Buddha taught: five hundred bhikṣus exhausted the outflows and their minds were liberated. Five hundred brahmins and the remaining people present, seeing the country in ruins and the devastation and suffering, left the household life to follow the path and all became śramaṇas. Five hundred devas were established in the patience of non-arising dharmas. Two hundred asuras and a thousand nāga kings all gave rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed, true, and right awakening. The mutilated men and women in the ditches and trenches, when their lives ended, were all reborn in Trāyastriṃśa Heaven. Fifteen hundred people attained a glimpse of the path. One thousand people attained the fruit of non-returning.

When the Buddha had spoken thus, all who heard it bowed their heads and withdrew.
