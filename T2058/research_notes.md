# T2058 Research Notes: Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan (付法藏因緣傳)

## Phase 0: Pre-Translation Research

Date: 2026-04-21

## 1. Title and Standard References

**Chinese title:** 付法藏因緣傳 (Fù fǎzàng yīnyuán zhuàn)

**Alternative Chinese titles:**
- 付法藏因緣經 (variant in Song, Yuan, Ming, and Palace editions, where 傳 is replaced by 經)
- 付法藏傳
- 付法藏經
- 付法傳

**Japanese reading:** Fuhōzō innenden

**Common English renderings (no single standard):**
- "Accounts of the Causes and Conditions of the Transmission of the Dharma Treasury" (most literal)
- "History of the Transmission of the Dharma-Storehouse" (common in encyclopedias)
- "Tradition of the Causes and Conditions of the Dharma-Treasury Transmission" (Stuart Young's rendering)
- "Biographies of the Circumstances of Transmission of the Dharma Repository"
- "A History of the Buddha's Successors" (Nichiren Library's loose rendering)

**Recommended working title:** "Record of the Transmission of the Dharma Treasury" -- concise, accurate, and in line with the BDK convention of using "Record" for 傳 in biographical/historical texts.

**Taishō location:** Vol. 50, No. 2058, pp. 297a-322c

**Length:** 6 fascicles (卷)

## 2. Translators

### Jikiyaye (吉迦夜)

**Sanskrit reconstruction:** Kiṅkara (also sometimes given as Kiṃkārya or Kikkāya in Japanese). The Chinese 吉迦夜 is a phonetic transcription. The Nichiren Library and Tibetan Buddhist Encyclopedia both give "Kinkara" as the standard Sanskrit form, though the exact reconstruction is uncertain.

**Biography:** A monk from Central Asia (西域, the "Western Regions") who came to the Northern Wei capital at Pingcheng (modern Datong, Shanxi). Very little is known about him beyond his translation work. The Taishō header describes him as 西域三藏 ("Tripiṭaka Master from the Western Regions"), though variant editions call him simply 沙門 ("śramaṇa"). Some variant editions read 吉叉 rather than 吉迦夜.

**Active period:** Around 472 CE at the Northern Wei court.

**Other translations:** Together with Tanyao, he also translated the Za baozang jing (雜寶藏經, T203, "Storehouse of Various Treasures Sutra," Saṃyuktaratnapiṭaka Sūtra), completed in 472 CE.

**Note on identity:** This is NOT the same as the deity/servant figure Kiṅkara known from tantric iconography. The translator is a historical monk whose Sanskrit name happens to coincide with a common Sanskrit word meaning "servant."

### Tanyao (曇曜)

**Biography:** A major figure in Northern Wei Buddhism, active mid-fifth century CE. Originally an eminent monk in the Bei Liang kingdom (北涼, 397-439, in modern Gansu), he came to the Northern Wei capital of Pingcheng after the Wei conquest of Bei Liang.

**Persecution and revival:** When Emperor Taiwudi (太武帝) issued an anti-Buddhist edict in 446 ordering the execution of all monks, Tanyao initially resisted but eventually complied at the urging of Crown Prince Huang, secretly preserving religious implements. After Taiwudi's assassination in 452 and the accession of Emperor Wenchengdi (文成帝), Buddhism was restored, and Tanyao was appointed head of the shamen tong (沙門統, "Office of the Śramaṇa Superintendent"), a position he held for over twenty years.

**Yungang Caves:** Tanyao's greatest achievement was persuading Emperor Wenchengdi to commission the massive Buddha sculptures at the Yungang caves (雲岡石窟) west of Pingcheng. Caves 16-20, known as the "Tanyao Five Caves" (曇曜五窟), were carved between approximately 460-470 CE under his guidance and remain among the greatest achievements of Chinese Buddhist art. The themes from the Za baozang jing and the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan provided edifying narrative content for sculptors working in the caves.

**Administrative innovations:** Tanyao established the Saṃgha Household (僧祇戶) and Buddha Household (佛圖戶) systems to generate revenue for monasteries while providing famine relief.

### Date of Translation

The standard date given is **472 CE** (Northern Wei, Xiaowen Emperor's reign), placing it in the Pingcheng period (398-494). The CBETA header confirms the attribution to the Northern Wei (元魏), though variant editions read 後魏 ("Later Wei").

### Nature of the Work

Scholars regard the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan as more of a **compilation** than a straight translation of a single Sanskrit original. It draws on multiple Indian sources and may incorporate material from Sarvāstivāda transmission records. The text presents itself as a translation, but the degree of Chinese editorial shaping, particularly in the framing narrative and the selection/arrangement of material, suggests significant involvement by the Chinese collaborators.

## 3. Content and Structure

### Overview

The text narrates the transmission of the Buddha's dharma through a lineage of Indian patriarchs, from the Buddha's entrustment of the teaching to Mahākāśyapa down to the death of the twenty-third (or twenty-fourth, depending on how one counts) patriarch Āryasiṃha. Each patriarch's story includes: past-life narratives (avadāna/jātaka material), the circumstances of their enlightenment, their teaching career, and the moment of dharma transmission to their successor.

The text concludes with a lengthy exhortation on the value of the dharma and the importance of its preservation, including several illustrative stories.

### Fascicle-by-Fascicle Breakdown

**Fascicle 1:** Opens with a verse of homage, then narrates the Buddha's career from his defeat of Māra to his parinirvāṇa. The Buddha entrusts the dharma to **Mahākāśyapa** (摩訶迦葉). Extended biography of Mahākāśyapa: his past lives, golden-colored body, marriage to a woman of matching virtue, their mutual vow of celibacy, his meeting with the Buddha, attainment of arhatship, and his role after the Buddha's passing.

**Fascicle 2:** Mahākāśyapa transmits the dharma to **Ānanda** (阿難). Extended biography of Ānanda: past lives, role as the Buddha's attendant, his foremost status in learning (多聞第一), the First Council at Rājagṛha. Also covers **Śāṇakavāsa** (商那和修) and the beginning of his story.

**Fascicle 3:** Continues with Ānanda's transmission to Śāṇakavāsa and the story of **King Aśoka** (阿恕伽王/阿育王). Extended narrative of Aśoka's conversion to Buddhism and patronage of the dharma. Material overlapping significantly with the Aśokāvadāna tradition. **Upagupta** (憂波毱多) appears in connection with the Aśoka narratives.

**Fascicle 4:** Continues the Aśoka narratives, including the story of Aśoka's son **Kunāla** (拘那羅, also called Dharmavardhana/法增). Extended avadāna material about Aśoka's court and the later years of his reign.

**Fascicle 5:** Covers the rapid succession of patriarchs from Śāṇakavāsa's transmission to Upagupta, then through: **Dhṛtaka** (提多迦), **Micchaka** (彌遮迦), **Buddhanandi** (佛陀難提), **Buddhamitra** (佛陀蜜多, also 佛陀密多), **Pārśva** (脇尊者), **Puṇyayaśas** (富那夜奢), **Aśvaghoṣa** (馬鳴), and into **Kapimala** (比羅/毘羅) and **Nāgārjuna** (龍樹). The later patriarchs receive briefer treatment than the first few.

**Fascicle 6:** Begins with Nāgārjuna's transmission to **Āryadeva** (迦那提婆). Extended narrative of Āryadeva's career, including his encounter with the great god, his debates with non-Buddhist teachers, his conversion of a king, and his assassination by a vengeful non-Buddhist student. Then covers: **Rāhulata** (羅睺羅), **Saṅghanandi** (僧伽難提), **Saṅghayaśas** (僧伽耶舍), **Kumāralāta** (鳩摩羅馱), **Jayata** (闍夜多), **Vasubandhu** (婆修槃陀/婆修盤頭), **Manorhita** (摩奴羅/摩拏羅), **Haklena** (鶴勒那/夜奢), and finally **Āryasiṃha** (師子尊者). The text ends with Āryasiṃha's execution by King Mihirakula (彌羅掘) of Kashmir, from whose neck flows milk rather than blood. This terminates the lineage. A lengthy concluding section exhorts readers on the supreme value of the dharma, including the famous parable of the elephant near the slaughterhouse vs. the monastery, and the story of the skull-seller.

## 4. The Patriarchs

### The Standard List (as given in the text)

The text presents a lineage of transmissions but does not give a single numbered list. Scholars typically reconstruct the following sequence. The count varies: scholars speak of either 23 or 24 patriarchs. The discrepancy arises because **Madhyāntika** (末田地) appears in some reconstructions as a separate patriarch between Ānanda and Śāṇakavāsa but is not consistently treated as a full patriarch in the text itself. The most common scholarly count, following the Tiantai tradition's use of the text, is **23 patriarchs**.

**Note:** Madhyāntika (末田地) appears in the text as someone Ānanda sends to Kashmir to propagate the dharma, but the main transmission line runs from Ānanda directly to Śāṇakavāsa. Some later traditions (and some modern scholars) count him, yielding 24 patriarchs. The Tiantai school counted 23 based on this text, then added their own three patriarchs (Huiwen, Huisi, Zhiyi) to claim legitimate succession.

**Note on Vasubandhu:** This is NOT the famous Vasubandhu (世親) of the Yogācāra school (4th-5th century). The patriarch Vasubandhu/Vasubandhuka in this lineage is a much earlier, semi-legendary figure.

## 5. Scholarly Literature

### Key Works

**Stuart H. Young, *Conceiving the Indian Buddhist Patriarchs in China* (Honolulu: University of Hawaiʻi Press, 2015).** Kuroda Studies in East Asian Buddhism, no. 24. This is the single most important English-language monograph on the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan and its cultural context. Young examines how medieval Chinese Buddhists received and transformed the hagiographies of Aśvaghoṣa, Nāgārjuna, and Āryadeva as presented in this text and others. He analyzes how Chinese sangha leaders used these patriarchal narratives to demonstrate that Indian Buddhist enlightenment was accessible in China. The book treats the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan as a foundational text for understanding Chinese constructions of Indian Buddhist identity.

**Jean Przyluski, *La Légende de l'Empereur Açoka (Açoka-avadāna) dans les textes indiens et chinois* (Paris: Paul Geuthner, 1923).** Annales du Musée Guimet, Bibliothèque d'études, tome XXXII. 460 pp. The foundational comparative study of the Aśoka legend cycle across Indian and Chinese sources. Discusses the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan's Aśoka material (fascicles 3-4) in relation to the broader Aśokāvadāna tradition.

**John S. Strong, *The Legend of King Aśoka: A Study and Translation of the Aśokāvadāna* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1983; repr. Delhi: Motilal Banarsidass).** Princeton Library of Asian Translations. Strong's translation is of the Sanskrit Aśokāvadāna (corresponding to T2042/T2043), but his introductory study discusses the relationship between the various Chinese Aśoka texts, including T2058's treatment of the Aśoka material. Essential for understanding how fascicles 3-4 of the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan relate to the broader Aśoka legend tradition.

**Erik Zürcher, *The Buddhist Conquest of China: The Spread and Adaptation of Buddhism in Early Medieval China* (Leiden: Brill, 1959; 3rd ed. 2007).** While not primarily focused on T2058, Zürcher's foundational work provides essential context for understanding the Northern Wei Buddhist milieu in which the text was produced, including Tanyao's role and the Pingcheng-era translation projects.

**Oxford Reference entry:** The *Oxford Dictionary of Buddhism* (ed. Damien Keown, 2004) includes an entry for "Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan" characterizing it as a lineage history dated to 472 CE.

### No Known Complete English Translation

As of this writing, there is no complete published English translation of the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan. Partial translations and summaries exist in the scholarly works cited above, particularly in Young (2015), but the full text has not been rendered into English. This makes it a significant gap in the available English-language Buddhist canon.

Marcus Bingenheimer's *Bibliography of Translations from the Chinese Buddhist Canon into Western Languages* does not list a translation for T2058.

## 6. BDK Catalog Status

T2058 does **not** appear in the BDK English Tripiṭaka first-phase list of 139 works. A search of the BDK America website (bdkamerica.org) shows no published or planned translation of this text.

However, the BDK has published *Lives of Great Monks and Nuns* (BDK English Tripiṭaka 76-III through 76-VII), which includes translations of related biographical texts from the same section of the Taishō (vol. 50):
- *Biography of Aśvaghoṣa Bodhisattva* (T2046, 馬鳴菩薩傳)
- *Biography of Nāgārjuna Bodhisattva* (T2047, 龍樹菩薩傳)
- *Biography of Vasubandhu* (T2049, 婆藪槃豆法師傳)

These shorter individual biographies overlap in subject matter with T2058 but are distinct texts. The BDK's interest in this genre suggests T2058 could be a natural addition to the series.

## 7. Relationship to Other Texts

### T2042 (阿育王傳) and T2043 (阿育王經) -- The Aśokāvadāna

The Aśokāvadāna tradition is represented in Chinese by:
- T2042: *A-yu wang zhuan* (阿育王傳), translated by An Faqin (安法欽), c. 300 CE
- T2043: *A-yu wang jing* (阿育王經), translated by Saṅghapāla (僧伽婆羅), 512 CE

Fascicles 3 and 4 of T2058 contain extensive Aśoka material that overlaps significantly with the Aśokāvadāna tradition. The story of Upagupta's encounters with Aśoka, the tale of Aśoka's son Kunāla, and narratives of Aśoka's Buddhist patronage all have parallels in T2042/T2043. However, the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan frames this material within a patriarchal transmission narrative rather than presenting it as a royal biography. The Aśoka material serves the larger purpose of demonstrating the dharma's transmission through the generations.

### T2076: Jingde chuandeng lu (景德傳燈錄, "Record of the Transmission of the Lamp")

Compiled by Daoyuan (道原) and presented to the Song court in 1004 CE, completed 1009 CE. The Jingde chuandeng lu established the canonical Chan/Zen model of **28 Indian patriarchs** from Śākyamuni to Bodhidharma, expanding the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan's 23-patriarch lineage by adding five additional figures between Āryasiṃha and Bodhidharma. The Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan served as one of several source texts for the Indian portion of the Jingde chuandeng lu, but the Chan tradition significantly reworked the lineage to extend it to Bodhidharma, the purported first Chinese patriarch of Chan. The key innovation was bridging the gap between Āryasiṃha's death and Bodhidharma's arrival in China.

### T2585: Denkōroku (傳光錄, "Record of the Transmission of the Light")

Written by Keizan Jōkin (瑩山紹瑾, 1268-1325), the Denkōroku is one of the foundational texts of Sōtō Zen. It presents 53 dharma-transmission stories covering the lineage from Śākyamuni through the 28 Indian patriarchs to the Chinese and Japanese Zen lineage. The first 28 chapters (the Indian patriarchs) draw on the same lineage tradition that the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan helped establish, though the Denkōroku follows the expanded 28-patriarch Chan list rather than the original 23. The Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan is thus an ancestral source for the Denkōroku's Indian chapters, mediated through the Jingde chuandeng lu and other Chan transmission records.

**The 23-to-28 expansion:** The five patriarchs added by the Chan tradition between the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan's list and the 28-patriarch standard are:
- 24: Basiasita (婆舍斯多, Vaśasita/Vasiṣṭha)
- 25: Puṇyamitra (不如密多)
- 26: Prajñātāra (般若多羅)
- 27: Bodhidharma (菩提達磨) -- counted as 28th in some lists when the Buddha himself is counted as first
- (Or: the exact insertions vary by source; the key point is that the Chan tradition needed to bridge from Āryasiṃha to Bodhidharma.)

### Tiantai Use

The Tiantai school adopted the 23-patriarch lineage from the Fu fazang yinyuan zhuan as the basis for its own legitimation, then appended three Tiantai masters: Huiwen (慧文), Huisi (慧思), and Zhiyi (智顗, 538-597), claiming direct succession from the Indian patriarchal line. Some Tiantai sources count 24 patriarchs (including Vasumitra as an addition).

## 8. Translation Challenges and Notes

### Genre
The text sits at the intersection of several genres: Buddhist biography (傳), avadāna/jātaka narrative, lineage history, and doctrinal exhortation. The translation should preserve the narrative qualities of the text while being accurate about its doctrinal content.

### Aśoka Material
Fascicles 3-4 are substantially devoted to Aśoka narratives. Cross-referencing with Strong's translation of the Aśokāvadāna and with T2042/T2043 will be important for consistency of proper names and narrative details.

### Proper Names
The text uses multiple transcription variants for the same names. The translation should standardize to IAST Sanskrit forms while noting significant Chinese variants. The patriarch list above gives the standard scholarly reconstructions.

### The Concluding Exhortation
Fascicle 6 ends not with the death of Āryasiṃha but with an extended passage exhorting readers on the value of the dharma, including illustrative parables (the elephant, the skull-seller, etc.). This material may be a Chinese addition or expansion, and its tone shifts from narrative biography to homiletic discourse.

### Verse
Fascicle 1 opens with a four-line verse of homage. Other verses appear throughout the text, embedded in the prose narrative.

### BDK Terminology Conventions
Follow standard BDK conventions throughout:
- World-Honored One (世尊)
- Tathāgata (如来)
- Three Treasures (三宝)
- Roots of merit (善根)
- Skillful means (方便)
- Sentient beings (眾生)
- Wholesome/unwholesome
- Five aggregates
- Four Noble Truths
- Eightfold Path

## 9. Significance for Publication

This text is significant for several reasons:

1. **No existing English translation.** It fills a genuine gap in the available English-language Buddhist canon.

2. **Foundational for Chan/Zen lineage.** The text established the Indian patriarchal lineage that later became the basis for all Chan/Zen lineage claims. Anyone studying Zen history needs access to this source.

3. **Rich narrative content.** The avadāna and jātaka material, especially in the Aśoka sections, makes for compelling reading.

4. **Historical importance.** The text was produced at a pivotal moment in Chinese Buddhist history, during the post-persecution revival under the Northern Wei, and reflects Tanyao's ambitious program to establish Buddhism's legitimacy in China.

5. **Relevance to the Denkōroku.** For Sōtō Zen practitioners (a key audience for Dan's work), this text is the ultimate source for the Indian patriarchal stories that Keizan retells in the Denkōroku.
