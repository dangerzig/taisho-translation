# 佛開解梵志阿颰經
## Sūtra on the Buddha Enlightening the Brāhmaṇa Abat

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0020

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhī Qiān (支謙)

---

The Buddha said to Abhaya: "My monastics have abandoned all desires and uphold the precepts of the scriptures to sever birth and death. Thus in this present life they no longer bear the impulse to grieve, weep, or cling to attachments. I do not crave others, and others do not crave me. Yet through the Dharma I cherish all beings with compassion, wishing to liberate them.

"To practice the Way is to suffer for but one lifetime. To not practice the Way is to suffer without end. It is like a person bathing; one cleanses only the exterior, but the filth of the heart is not removed. One who attains arhatship removes all evil entirely. The minds of ordinary people and the minds of those who walk the Way are both like a stone lying on the ground: the sun cannot melt it, rain cannot dissolve it, wind cannot move it. Rising above the common and ordinary to attain the supreme Way, the mind becomes cool, free from the heat of passion. It is like the lotus flower arising from muddy water: its roots and leaves are forever cool, unstained by dust or water.

"The monastic reflects: 'Parents nurture their children; their kindness endures for one lifetime. The Buddha opens the world, enabling people to attain the Way.' Perceiving from beginning to end birth and death across the five paths, and knowing the span of human life, one's will becomes upright. Acting freely in accordance with this, one may ascend to the heavens and does so; one may enter the ocean and does so.

"It is like bathing a corpse in fragrant water: it cannot be made fragrant. Teaching an evil person to do good cannot ensure they become good. One whose heart is evil, their body and speech are evil as well. The teachings of the outer schools merely indulge the ears; they contain no true Way. A person of the Way, upon hearing such things, would never respond to them. Knowing that the minds of ordinary people are inverted in their perceptions, ignorant and unable to understand the Way, taking what is right to be wrong, unable to distinguish truth from falsehood; the sage, out of compassion, bestows loving care. The monastic holds to their intention like one wearing new garments, careful in sitting and rising, not wishing to stain them. Therefore, one who upholds the precepts is ever in contention with the mind, so that though a hundred evils may arise, none are admitted or received.

"Parents give birth to a child and train them in the Way from youth; if the child grows up and commits crimes and dies, one cannot blame the parents for it. It is like a chair that has wood but no rope bindings: one cannot sit on it. A child without a wise teacher likewise cannot attain the Way; and this applies to scholars as well. In my past lives I served many sages and worthies; what I received was extraordinary, and all were teachers of the unconditioned.

"One who attains arhatship can recount in their own words: 'At such a place I attained stream-entry; at such a place I attained once-returning and non-returning; reaching arhatship, I am completely liberated, no longer subject to birth and death.' One knows in full, broad and narrow alike, as when viewing a fine painting and distinguishing its five colors. Seeing all people in the world, one perceives that all possess the three poisons: arrogance, heedlessness, and the tendency toward greed and craving. Knowing oneself to be liberated, one no longer craves rebirth in the heavens, nor does one delight in the human realm. One thinks only of sentient beings, wishing to bring them to liberation.

"Those among ordinary people who have not yet heard this should carefully receive and study it; like threading colored silk through lapis lazuli beads, all five colors appear in full.

"The Dharma eye sees where a person's spirit comes from at birth and what path it tends toward at death; one knows: 'This person's spirit at death falls into hell; this one falls into the animal realm; this one into the hungry ghost realm; this one enters human form; this one ascends to the heavens at death.' Upon completing the Way, one knows within oneself: the five destinations are severed, one's aspirations are fulfilled. One regards one's body as mere earth: 'Take this body,' one says, 'you may even shatter it.' To discern truth from falsehood is like stepping into clear water where all things, sand, gravel, pearls, and gems, are fully visible.

"One foreknows the affairs of the world: one person becoming a hundred, a hundred persons becoming one. How so? One person gives birth to children; in turn, down to great-great-grandchildren, the line flourishes into a hundred. At other times a hundred people all die and only one remains. The monastic who attains the Way sees fully all that is good and ill, knowing which person at death will be born into a good path, and knowing which will fall into an evil path.

"One perceives the measure of the four elements within one's own body, and knows the length of a person's lifespan, their suffering and happiness. Originally, from ignorance, consciousness becomes volitional formations; formations receive name-and-form. All is but dependent arising and borrowed dwelling, one is born within a mother's womb, and each is anxious and concerned for the other. Parents say 'my child'; the child says 'my parents.' Yet the spirit revolves and transforms without self-knowledge. Those whose past karma is wholesome are reborn as humans with wealth, noble rank, and long life; those whose karma is unwholesome are born in suffering with short lives, each according to their original karma.

"All things between heaven and earth depend upon four elements: first earth, second water, third fire, fourth wind. Within the human body: what is solid is earth; what is moist and fluid is water; what is warm is fire; breath is wind. In life one borrows these; at death they return to their source. Tracing origin and end, each element returns to its own, yet ordinary people are unaware of this. Between heaven and earth those who are born are like a dream; the span of life and fortune is brief, and in confusion and turbulence one dies. It is like the wind stirring the ocean, waves pursuing one another without cease, birth and death are likewise, coming and going without rest.

"The monastic who attains the Way knows fully the arising and passing of heaven and earth, beginning and end; the affairs of one kalpa, and all the bodies through which one has passed. One also knows the affairs of countless kalpas long past. Thus one comes to know that among all the accomplished sages in the world who have attained the Way, none equals the Buddha. One knows within oneself: 'My mind and will, which once had ten thousand tendencies, have now come to rest in one. I am ever sorrowful for sentient beings, whose hearts are thrown into confusion by greed, desire, intoxication with lust, anger, and delusion, or by the entanglements of affection and love, without knowing this essential truth.'

"Attaining the Way, one's vision reaches everywhere; like seeing clearly in a mirror; flying without obstruction, passing through even stone walls. One can ascend Mount Sumeru and reach out to touch the sun and moon; one can cause water and fire to issue separately from the body; one can sink beneath the earth and emerge in another direction; one can walk through the sky, sitting and reclining at will; one can cause the Māra king, Brahmā, Śakra, and all the devas to bow in reverence without exception.

"It is like a potter who fires clay vessels that hold water without leaking. Ordinary people are like unfired clay; one who attains the Way is like fired pottery; it can be dried or saturated, submerged without breaking. Like a goldsmith who can fashion any vessel at will, one who attains the supernormal powers of locomotion is likewise able to transform in any manner. The potter and the smelter use blazing fire to shape their vessels; my monastics likewise concentrate the mind and bring the Way to perfection. When dry, cowhide crackles when rolled or spread; moistened with fat and oil, rolling or spreading it becomes soft. The mind of the Way is likewise: entirely pliant and gentle, no longer stiff or rigid.

"From a high tower one can see and hear people below; their singing and dancing, bells and drums, the sounds of the six domestic animals. The Dharma ear is likewise: one hears the music of heaven above, and also hears the sounds of hungry ghosts and beings in hell crying out in hunger, thirst, and pain.

"One perceives the minds of people in full: those with the manner of desire and those without; those with various inclinations toward joy, anger, hatred, and love; the foolish and the wise, the strong and the weak, the easily transformed and the difficult to transform, those fond of the Way and those not fond of it; all are discerned and known, as a person who loves bathing rubs the body thoroughly and, finding a spot missed, rubs it again with care. The Dharma eye perceives those who can be liberated; one then takes up the Buddha's teachings and opens, explains, and bestows them upon them.

"Those whose will is wholesome are reborn as humans; those whose practice is somewhat elevated ascend to the heavens at death; those who uphold the precepts in purity attain the four paths of the monastic.

"Those who attain the Way know the affairs of one lifetime, ten lifetimes, a hundred lifetimes, and countless lifetimes. They also know the times of beginning and end, the arising and passing of kalpas of heaven and earth; they know the bodies born across countless kalpas, born at different times, with parents of different names and family lines, with longer or shorter lifespans. They know: at such a time one rose from the human path to become a deva; from the deva path one descended to become human; or from the human realm one entered hell; from hell one became an animal or a hungry ghost; from the hungry ghost realm one became human; or from the human realm one again became a spirit; from spirits one entered hell and ascended to become a deva, all this is known distinctly through one's own contemplation. Like a traveler far from home who recalls every detail of their native land, one who contemplates the five paths knows all, and knows within oneself: 'I am already liberated.'

"Through the power of the Way one acts in freedom: wishing to live a hundred years, a thousand years, ten thousand years, or even countless kalpas, all is possible. Wishing not to eat for ten days, a hundred days, one year, a hundred years, this can extend without limit; wishing to eat, one eats. Like ascending a high tower and listening and watching those below, those sitting and standing to east, west, south, and north, the sounds of their voices, one hears and sees all.

"The person of the Way knows within themselves: 'My will has been purified; good and evil are both relinquished'; like a person who in former days made a mistake and broke the law, and the officers hung a dead dog around their neck and paraded them before the crowd, causing such shame that they wished to be quickly freed. One who attains arhatship regards the body with such shame as this.

"There are two types of arhats: one type is extinguished, the other is protective. The so-called extinguished type, concerned with their own attainment of the Way, immediately enters nirvāṇa. The protective type, concerned with others, liberates the world. It is like clear water in which sand, stones, fish, and turtles are naturally visible. When the mind of the Way is purified, one sees fully all that lies within the consciousness of every being in the world.

"Such is the monastic. Can your teacher's teachings and precepts equal this?"

Abhaya replied: "These are truly difficult to match."

The Buddha said: "I observe that in the world there are also ascetics who do not know the Buddha's Dharma; they dwell hidden away in marshes and thickets, eating fruits and berries, saying: 'Without need of a teacher, one may naturally attain.' Do they attain the Way?"

Abhaya replied: "They do not."

The Buddha said: "The Way is attained through the mind; there must be a teacher and the Dharma. This is the first of the foolish and deluded beliefs about the Way.

"There are also ascetics who gather the leaves, blossoms, and fruits of a hundred herbs and ingest medicinal formulas, believing themselves capable of becoming immortals. Do your teacher's disciples also believe this?"

Abhaya replied: "They do not."

"This is the second of the foolish and deluded beliefs about the Way. There are also ascetics who abandon their parents, clothe themselves in deer skin, sleep on beds of grass, go with disheveled hair and fast, and prostrate themselves to heaven seeking the Way; only tormenting themselves to no purpose, attaining nothing. Do you emulate this?"

Abhaya replied: "I do not."

"This is the third of the foolish and deluded beliefs about the Way. There are also ascetics who dwell deep in secluded places, inscribing above their gates 'I possess the Way,' who perform sacrificial rites to water, fire, the sun, the moon, and the five planets, slaughtering living beings as offerings to heaven, seeking merit by any means. Do you do this?"

Abhaya replied: "I do not."

"This is the fourth of the foolish and deluded beliefs about the Way."


The Buddha said to Abaṭṭha: "Since the opening of heaven and earth, there have been twenty-three great brahmin sages: Jītu, Liuhao, Jintuo, Jiāyī, Āróu, Jiāchén, Yí, Èchāo, Yánmáo, Bāmì, Jiānhuà, Āluén, Qútán, Qísǎng, Lèi, Kāśyapa, Bàofú, Āpān, [two names], Youchá, Bōlì, Jiǎojǐng, and Pīqū. All the cities and towns under heaven were built together by these twenty-three men. How does Vikkasā compare to such figures?"

He replied: "He does not measure up."

The Buddha said: "In what way is your teacher distinguished? Can he serve as teacher to kings and enable kings to attain the Way?"

He replied: "He cannot."

"Can you and your fellows serve as teachers to crown princes, ministers, and officials, enabling them to attain the Way?"

He replied: "We cannot."

"Can your teacher instruct farmers, artisans, merchants, the young and old, men and women, and enable you all to attain the Way?"

He replied: "He cannot."

"Have your teacher's ancestors ever attained the Way?"

He replied: "I have not heard such teaching from our teacher."

"What path do you and your fellows pursue?"

He said: "Our teacher says that those who uphold the Eight Precepts will ascend to Brahma-heaven after death."

"Have you ever seen any of your number uphold these Eight Precepts and ascend to Brahma-heaven?"

He said: "We have only heard our teacher say so."

The Buddha said to Abaṭṭha: "Those among my śramaṇas who have attained arhatship know the births and deaths within the kalpa; they distinguish each person, knowing who that person was at a given time, from whom they were born, and what they became. They know the affairs of all people on earth and in the heavens. They fly wherever they wish to go; they can appear and disappear; they can shake heaven and earth, move Mount Sumeru, pass in and out without obstruction, and transform themselves at will. When their fathers and mothers die, they know where they have fallen and, seeking them out, instruct and guide them, enabling their liberation. When a child attains the Way, both father and mother are saved. Moreover, my śramaṇas, holding a single pure intention, practice the two hundred and fifty precepts and proceed to the unconditioned Way."

The Buddha said to Abaṭṭha: "I renounced my kingdom and abandoned the throne, going forth as a śramaṇa. I devoted myself to severing the cycle of birth and death and have now attained the natural state. I am the Tathāgata, the Arahant, the Perfectly Enlightened One, the one endowed with wisdom and conduct, the Sugata, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Trainer of persons to be tamed, the Teacher of gods and humans; named Buddha, the Blessed One. I am teacher to all above heaven and below heaven. Those who uphold my sūtras and precepts without exception attain the Way. I ever hold compassion in my heart, transforming and teaching all under heaven, turning away from evil and moving toward the good. The good can be practiced always; evil cannot long endure. Suffering may be dwelt in for a long time; happiness cannot be relied upon. Happiness brings momentary gratification, but afterward one receives suffering. When retribution arrives and one repents, it is already too late."

At that moment, Abaṭṭha gazed intently at the Buddha's body and thought: "The Buddha possesses thirty-two marks, yet I cannot perceive a single one. Why is this?" The Buddha knew his thought, and immediately extended his tongue, first licking his left ear, then his right ear, then the line of his hair, covering his face with his tongue, and then slowly drawing it back beneath. Abaṭṭha exclaimed: "How rare it is to encounter one such as the Buddha! In ten thousand ages there appears one who possesses such a tongue-mark. How could I not have recognized this?"

The Buddha said: "You have all come a long way. Go and pay your respects to your teacher." All five hundred bowed at the Buddha's feet and departed.

Vikkasā was riding in his carriage when he came out and saw his disciples returning. He stopped and waited. When the disciples arrived, they descended from their carriages and performed obeisance. The teacher said: "The śramaṇa Gotama is famed throughout the world. Does he possess the marks? You were gone a long time; what was discussed?"

Abaṭṭha said: "All that was said this morning; not a word has been lost. Let us return home, eat, and I will explain it in full."

The teacher said: "Could not the Buddha at least have kept you for a meal?"

He replied: "The Buddha was seated beneath a tree with nothing whatsoever. Knowing when it was time for the meal, he sent us back. So we all returned together."

After the meal, Abaṭṭha related to his teacher in full everything the Buddha had said. The teacher said: "You speak the Buddha's words; have you omitted or added anything? Do you wish to make me follow him?"

Abaṭṭha said: "Hearing what the Buddha said, it surpasses us brahmins. I only fear that our lineage will not be able to serve him."

The teacher at once flew into a rage and stamped the ground, saying: "I have been a teacher for many lifetimes; how is it that I do not measure up?"

Abaṭṭha said: "Teacher, why not go yourself and observe his wisdom and ability?"

The teacher said: "Very well. I shall personally request to speak with the Buddha." That evening he prepared seats and cushions and provisions for five hundred people. At cockcrow the teacher himself set out, announced his name and title, and the Buddha received him. Having performed the ritual salutations and seated himself to one side, he folded his hands and said: "I have prepared a modest meal and respectfully request that the Buddha and the assembly of śramaṇas condescend to grace us with their presence." The Buddha silently consented. Vikkasā rejoiced, took his leave, and returned to make ready the food. Before midday, he again sent Abaṭṭha to go and welcome them. The Buddha and five hundred śramaṇas went together to the house. When all were seated and settled, food was served and water for washing passed around.

Vikkasā asked the Buddha: "Yesterday when Abaṭṭha returned, he conveyed your words, but I did not hear them clearly. I respectfully wish to hear them again." The Buddha said: "There is nothing added or omitted." And he again explained everything he had spoken the previous day. Hearing the Buddha's words, Vikkasā rejoiced, prostrated himself before the Buddha, and said: "Yesterday I was unreasonably angry at what Abaṭṭha reported." The Buddha said: "Though you were angered, he is a worthy disciple; like a fine horse that understands the intention of its rider." The Buddha then pronounced a blessing on Abaṭṭha: "May your years be long and your body free from illness."

Then the teacher praised the Buddha in verse:

> Fire illumines the darkness, the rivers and seas are lords of a hundred valleys,
> The sage broadly instructs and teaches, as a bright ruler in his kingdom.
> The maṇi jewel is foremost among gems, the moon is the brightest among stars,
> As the sun illumines all below heaven; in the three realms, the Buddha alone is honored.

The Buddha knew his mind was soft, upright, and free from error, and spoke a verse in reply:

> A person should practice benevolence and righteousness, give in charity, and cultivate merit;
> Awaken to what is not correct and guard the practice of the sūtras and precepts.
> The world is perilous, with little joy and much suffering;
> One should care for oneself and not be remiss.
> Be diligent in cutting off greed and desire, and the habit of fear;
> Birth, old age, sickness, and death; the pain of grief and lamentation;
> The parting from those one loves and cherishes; all of this is suffering.
> Therefore the sage seeks the unconditioned Way.

Vikkasā's mind opened in understanding. He rose, performed obeisance at the Buddha's feet, and said with tears streaming: "I think of my ancestors; none of them knew the Buddha. I pray the Buddha will have compassion for us. I have brothers, a wife and children, and all my household. I wish now to bring them here to receive the Dharma of the Buddha." The Buddha said: "You may." They all came at once and bowed at the Buddha's feet, took the Three Refuges, and together with Abaṭṭha and the others, upheld the Five Precepts.

Later, when Vikkasā came to the end of his life, his disciples asked the Buddha: "Where has this teacher gone after his death?" The Buddha said: "He has attained the third stage of non-returning. He has been reborn in the nineteenth heaven among the anāgāmins, and there he shall attain parinirvāṇa."

Abaṭṭha and the five hundred, wishing to become śramaṇas, were told by the Buddha: "Each of you return home, uphold the Five Precepts well, and when your resolve is firm, then you may leave the household life."

When the Buddha had finished expounding this sūtra, all rejoiced greatly, performed obeisance, and departed.
