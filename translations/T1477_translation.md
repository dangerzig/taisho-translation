# 佛說戒消災經
## Sūtra on the Precepts Dispelling Calamities Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1477

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the kingdom of Śrāvastī.

At that time, there was a certain district where everyone observed the Buddha's five precepts and ten wholesome deeds. In the entire district, there was no one who brewed alcohol. Among them was the son of a prominent family who wished to travel afar on a trading expedition. As he was about to depart, his parents said to their son: "Diligently uphold the five precepts and practice the ten wholesome deeds. Be careful never to drink alcohol and violate the Buddha's weighty precept." He accepted their instruction and set out.

He traveled to another country, where he encountered a former classmate and close friend. They were delighted to see each other. The friend took him home, brought out grape wine, and wished to drink it together. The son declined, saying: "In my homeland, everyone observes the Buddha's five precepts, and no one dares violate them. If one drinks alcohol, in a future life one is reborn as a foolish and ignorant person, unable to encounter the Buddha. Moreover, I left my parents, who admonished me. If I still violate their instruction and break the precept with steaming alcohol, no transgression could be greater. Dear friend, it has been long since we parted and now we meet again. Though my heart is joyful, you should not cause me to break the precepts and disobey my parents' instruction."

The host said: "You and I studied under the same teacher; in kindness we are brothers. My parents are like your parents, and your parents are esteemed by mine. How could one disobey them? If I were at your home, I would certainly comply with your parents." Unable to refuse, the son consented to drink. He lay drunk for three days. When he sobered up, his heart was filled with regret and fear.

When his business was concluded, he returned home and made a full confession to his parents. His parents replied: "You have disobeyed our instruction and furthermore violated the precepts. This is the beginning of disorder in the Dharma. You are not a filial son. Do not speak of this and set a precedent for the people of our land." They took his trading profits, expelled him from the country, and told him he must not remain.

Having broken the precept and been expelled by his parents, the son went to another country and lodged at an inn. The innkeeper served three demon-spirits that could take human form, appear face to face, eat and drink, and converse with people. The innkeeper had served them for many years, becoming exhausted and impoverished, his household wealth entirely spent. Illness, death, and mourning never ceased in his family. He grew weary and resentful of these ghosts, and privately discussed the matter with others.

The ghosts perceived the man's feelings of distress and discussed among themselves: "This man's property is completely exhausted, entirely because of us. We have never benefited him, and now he has grown resentful. We should seek precious jewels and give them to him, to make his heart glad." They went and stole fine jewels from the treasury of a king in another land, heaped them in the garden, and reported: "You have served us for many years with great toil and hardship. Now we wish to bless you and make you wealthy. Would this not be delightful?" The innkeeper said: "I am deeply grateful for the great spirit's kindness." The ghost said: "In your garden there is gold and silver. Go and take it, and great fortune shall come, fulfilling your wishes." The innkeeper went joyfully into the garden, saw the extraordinary treasures, carried them back to his house, and expressed his gratitude. "Tomorrow I wish to prepare a feast. Would you graciously attend?" The provisions and delicacies were all prepared. When the demon-spirits arrived at the gate, they saw the man from Śrāvastī staying at the innkeeper's house, and they turned and fled. The innkeeper chased after them, calling them back: "Today I have prepared a modest offering, and everything is ready. Since the great spirits have already deigned to come, why do you suddenly depart?" The spirit said: "You have a noble guest in your house. How could we approach?" And again they fled in alarm.

The innkeeper returned home and sat pondering: "In my house there is no extraordinary person, only this one man." He went out to speak with him, respectfully laid out what he had, and they enjoyed themselves greatly together. When the meal was finished, he asked: "What merit or virtue do you possess that the spirits I serve fear you and run away?" The guest explained in full the Buddha's merit, the five precepts, and the ten wholesome deeds. He confessed that he had actually violated the precept against alcohol and had been expelled by his parents. Yet because he still upheld the remaining four precepts, the heavenly spirits protected him, and the innkeeper's ghosts dared not face him. The innkeeper said: "Although I have served these spirits, I have long been weary of them. Now I wish to uphold the Buddha's five precepts." He thereupon received from the guest the three refuges, the five precepts, and the ten wholesome deeds, and cultivated them single-mindedly with diligence, not daring to be lax. He asked where the Buddha was and whether he could be seen. The guest replied: "The Buddha is in the Jetavana Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada in the kingdom of Śrāvastī. You can go there and see him."

The innkeeper set out single-mindedly toward Śrāvastī and passed through a roadside pavilion. In it was a beautiful woman who was the wife of a man-eating demon. The traveler was far along the road, and as evening was approaching, he asked the woman for a night's lodging. The woman replied: "Be careful not to stay here. You should hurry onward." The man asked: "Why? Is there something dangerous?" The woman answered: "I have already told you. Why do you ask further?" The man thought to himself: "The man from Śrāvastī, who still upheld only four of the Buddha's precepts, and yet my spirit was so terrified of him. I have now received the three refuges, the five precepts, and the ten wholesome deeds, and my mind is not lax. What have I to fear?" So he stayed the night. The man-eating demon, seeing the majestic spiritual power of the precepts' guardian hovering nearby, went forty li from the pavilion and did not return all night.

The next morning, the man set off again. He saw human bones and remains scattered about, devoured by the demon. His hair stood on end and his heart filled with fear. He regretted his decision and thought to himself: "When I was in my own country, my household had abundant food and clothing, and all was perfectly comfortable. I was led astray by that man who told me the Buddha was in Śrāvastī. I have yet to see anything marvelous; instead I see bones strewn about." Evil thoughts arose, and he said to himself: "Would it not be better to go back to that woman, take her back to my homeland, and live together as before? Would that not be pleasant?" He immediately turned back and returned to the pavilion, and asked the woman again for lodging. The woman said to the man: "Why have you returned?" He answered: "My travel plans did not work out, so I have come back. May I stay one more night?" The woman said: "You will die! My husband is a man-eating demon. He will return shortly. Go quickly!" But the man did not believe her and stayed, refusing to leave. His mind grew more confused, and lustful thoughts arose. He no longer believed in the virtue of the three refuges of the Buddha, the five precepts, or the ten wholesome deeds. The heavenly spirits immediately departed, and there was no one left to protect him. The demon was then able to return. The woman, fearing the demon would eat the man, took pity on him and hid him in a large jar. The demon smelled a human scent and said to his wife: "Have you obtained some meat? I want to eat it." The wife said: "I did not go out. Where would I have gotten meat?" The wife asked the demon: "Why did you not come home last night?" The demon said: "It was because of your doing, sheltering that noble guest, that I was driven away." Inside the jar, the man grew ever more terrified and could no longer recall the meaning of the three refuges. The wife said: "Why were you unable to get any meat?" The demon said: "Precisely because you sheltered a disciple of the Buddha. The heavenly spirits drove me out more than forty li, and I spent the night exposed and trembling. Even now I am unsettled, which is why I could not get meat." The wife, hearing this, was secretly pleased. She then asked her husband: "What are the Buddha's precepts, and how should they all be upheld?" The demon said: "I am extremely hungry. Quickly bring me the meat. You need not ask about this. These are the precepts of the unsurpassed, true, and correct path. I dare not speak of them." The wife said: "If you explain them to me, I will give you the meat."

Demons are by nature greedy and rapacious, their desire to eat without end. Pressed by his wife's questioning, the demon explained the three refuges and the five weighty precepts: first, compassionate benevolence and not killing; second, pure faith and not stealing; third, guarding chastity and not engaging in sexual misconduct; fourth, no false speech from the mouth; fifth, filial obedience and not becoming intoxicated. As the demon recited each precept, the wife accepted it. With all five precepts held in her mind and recited aloud, the man in the jar recognized the five precepts and also accepted them. The Lord Śakra, knowing that these two people had taken refuge in the Buddha in their hearts, immediately dispatched fifty benevolent spirits to protect them both. The demon then fled.

The next morning, the wife asked the man: "Were you frightened?" He answered: "Extremely frightened! Thanks to your benevolence, my mind has awakened and I now recognize the Buddha." The wife asked: "Why did you turn back yesterday?" He answered: "When I saw the bones of the long-dead and the recently dead strewn about, I was terrified, so I wrongly turned back." The wife said: "Those bones are what I have discarded. I was originally a daughter of a good family, kidnapped by the demon and made his wife. My grief and misery had no one to appeal to. Now, thanks to your kindness, I have heard the Buddha's precepts and been freed from this demon." The wife said: "Worthy sir, where do you intend to go?" The man replied: "I wish to go to the kingdom of Śrāvastī to see the Buddha." The wife said: "Excellent! I shall leave my homeland and my parents and follow you to see the Buddha." They set off together and met four hundred and ninety-eight people. They greeted each other and asked: "Worthy ones, where have you come from, and where are you going?" They answered: "We have come from the Buddha's presence." The man asked: "You have already seen the Buddha. Why are you leaving?" They replied: "The Buddha teaches the sūtras every day, but our minds are confused and we still do not understand. We are returning to our homelands."

The two worthy ones told them the whole story from beginning to end, explaining how the ghosts feared those of lofty practice who upheld the precepts. The minds of the four hundred and ninety-eight opened with understanding, and they all turned back together to see the Buddha.

The Buddha saw them coming from afar and smiled, and light of five colors issued from his mouth. Ānanda knelt and said: "The Buddha does not smile without reason. Surely there is something he wishes to say." The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Do you see those four hundred and ninety-eight people returning?" He replied: "I see them." The Buddha said: "These four hundred and ninety-eight people have now found their original teacher. Those who come to see the Buddha shall all attain the path." When the five hundred people arrived before the Buddha, they bowed in reverence, listened to the sūtra with single-minded attention, and their minds opened with understanding. They all became śramaṇas and attained the path of the arhat.

The Buddha said: "The one who violated the precept against alcohol was the innkeeper. He and this woman have been brother and sister through many lifetimes. Moreover, these two are the original teachers of those four hundred and ninety-eight people from their past lives. When people in the world seek the path, they must find their original teacher and their good spiritual friends; only then will they understand."

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, all the bhikṣus were greatly delighted, bowed before the Buddha, and departed.
