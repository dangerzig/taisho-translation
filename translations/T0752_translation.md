# 佛說五無返復經
## Sūtra of the Five Seemingly Ungrateful Ones, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0752

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Jūqú Jīngshēng (沮渠京聲)

---

At that time the Buddha was in the Jeta Grove Monastery, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. There was a brahmin in the country of Rājagṛha who heard that the people of Śrāvastī were kind and filial, devoted to the sūtras, cultivating the path, and making offerings to the Three Honored Ones. So he traveled to the country of Śrāvastī. There he saw a father and son plowing a field. A poisonous snake bit and killed the son, yet the father continued plowing without looking at his son or weeping.

The brahmin asked: "Whose child is this?"

The father replied: "He is my son."

The brahmin said: "If this is your son, why do you not weep but continue plowing as before?"

The man replied: "Those who are born must die. What is created must be destroyed. The good receive their reward, and the evil face their reckoning. What can grief and weeping overtake? If I were to stop eating, what benefit would that be to the dead? You are going into the city now. My home is at such-and-such a place. Please pass by and tell them: 'My son is dead. There is no need to bring food for two people.'"

The brahmin thought to himself: "This man has no gratitude or affection! His son lies dead on the ground and he feels no grief; instead he asks for food. This man has no love; there is none like him." The brahmin then proceeded into the city and went to the plowman's home. Seeing the dead boy's mother, he told her: "Your son is dead. His father says: 'Just bring food for one person.' Why do you not grieve for your child?"

The boy's mother replied to the brahmin with a parable: "A son is like a guest who comes to stay with someone. When he comes, one does not refuse him; when he goes, one does not detain him. This son of mine: I did not summon him to come; he came to me of his own accord and was born. His death, too, was his own departure. It was not within my power to make him stay or go. He followed his past karma and was born where his destiny directed."

Then the brahmin told the boy's elder sister: "Your brother is dead. Why do you not weep?"

The sister addressed the brahmin with a parable: "We brothers and sisters are like a carpenter who goes into the mountains and cuts timber to bind a raft, then puts it in the water. When a great wind suddenly comes and breaks the raft apart, the pieces scatter and drift away with the current, front and back separated, none looking back at the others. My brother too is like this. Because of the causes and conditions of past lives, we were temporarily brought together and born in one family. Each follows the length or brevity of their allotted life. Death is impermanent; what comes together must part. My brother's life was at its end. Each follows their own karma. Impermanence comes to meet us; following one's past karma, one cannot be rescued."

Then the brahmin told the dead man's wife: "Your husband is dead. Why do you not weep?"

The wife also spoke a parable to the brahmin: "We, husband and wife, were brought together by causes and conditions for but a moment. It is like birds that roost together in a tall tree at dusk, chirping and chattering. At dawn each flies away on its own, seeking food and drink. When there are conditions, they come together. When conditions are absent, they part. Husband and wife are like this. Impermanence comes to meet us; following one's own destiny, one cannot be rescued."

Then the brahmin told the dead man's servant: "Your master's son is dead. Why do you not weep?"

The servant also spoke a parable: "My master and I were brought together by causes and conditions. I am like a calf following the great cow. When someone slaughters the cow, the calf stands by but cannot save it. The command of impermanence cannot be thwarted. What good would grief and weeping do?"

When the brahmin heard all this, his mind was confused and his eyes dimmed; he could not tell east from west. "I heard that the people of this country are filial and devoted to the path, making offerings to the Three Honored Ones. So I came from afar, hoping to learn. Yet I have found no good response; instead I have met five ungrateful people. I have wearied my body and troubled my heart, traveling far to reach this place, all for nothing."

He then asked a passerby: "Where is the Buddha? I wish to go and ask him."

The passerby replied: "He is nearby, in the Jeta Grove Monastery."

The brahmin went immediately to the Buddha's dwelling, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and sat to one side. Head bowed in sorrow, he was silent and said nothing.

The Buddha knew his thoughts and said: "Why do you hang your head in sorrow and displeasure?"

The brahmin addressed the Buddha: "What I wished for did not come to pass. It was contrary to my hopes. This is why I am sorrowful."

The Buddha asked: "What have you lost?"

He addressed the Buddha: "I came from the country of Rājagṛha. I heard that the people of this country are filial and honor the Three Honored Ones, so I came from afar hoping to learn. But upon arriving, I encountered five ungrateful people. This is why I am sorrowful and displeased."

The Buddha said: "Who are these ungrateful ones?"

The brahmin addressed the Buddha: "I saw a father and son plowing a field. The son lay dead on the ground, yet the father felt no grief and instead asked for food. He spoke to me of impermanence. The mother, wife, sister, and servant: none showed any grief. These are great transgressions; they are ungrateful."

The Buddha said: "Not so. It is not as you say. These five people have the greatest gratitude of all. They know that life is impermanent and that grief and sorrow cannot overtake it. Therefore they are at peace within themselves, without grief or distress. Worldly people, who do not understand impermanence, are overcome with anguish and weeping and cannot master themselves. It is like a person who contracts a fever and becomes delirious, babbling nonsense. A good physician gives medicine, the fever is cured, and the babbling stops. Ordinary people are deluded: they grieve and weep and cannot resolve it themselves. But those who can understand impermanence no longer grieve, like the fever patient who is cured. These five people have all attained realization of the path."

When the brahmin heard the Buddha's words, he immediately reproached himself: "I was foolish and deluded, not understanding the meaning of the path. Now that I have heard the Buddha's words, it is like a blind person gaining sight, like seeing light in the darkness."
