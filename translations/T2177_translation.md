# 華嚴宗章疏并因明錄
## A Record of Commentaries and Treatises of the Huayan School Together with Hetuvidyā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2177

Compiled in Japan (Heian period) by Enshō (圓超)

---

I humbly reflect that the flourishing of the Buddha's teachings came about through causes such as these. In the realm of the Western Heavens, Śākyamuni, the Capable One, drove forth to the Deer Park and opened its source. At the court of the Eastern Han, Emperor Xiaomin dreamed of the Golden Man and set out to seek his traces. In our land, wondrous images of strangeness arrived upon the shores, and teachings of subtle words were heard throughout the realm. In the thirteenth year of the reign of Emperor Kinmei, who governed from the Golden Thorn Palace at Shikishima, the Buddha-Dharma was first transmitted.

Thereafter, up to the fourteenth year of the Engi era, three hundred and fifty-three years elapsed. During this interval, the Dharma treasuries were fully enumerated and the schools of teaching exhaustively classified. The transcription of sūtras and śāstras proceeded vigorously in both public and private institutions; yet the esoteric and exoteric commentaries and treatises were left entirely to individual discretion. Some were kept secret and not transmitted; others were scattered and not copied. The commentaries and treatises of the various schools gradually became blocked and obstructed, and there was no means by which to expound the teachings brought by the White Horse.

It is my humble reverence that the Meditative Emperor, before five hundred Buddhas in previous lifetimes, personally received their bestowal and commission, and throughout all the realm beneath heaven has manifested the form of a sovereign king. His reverence for the sacred teachings far surpasses the five regions of India; his promotion of the exoteric and esoteric traditions nearly doubles the endeavors of China itself. Accordingly, the Emperor issued an edict commanding the copying and transmission of commentaries and treatises, and directed the eminent scholars of the Six Schools each to submit a catalog of their respective school. I, Enshō, though I occupy a humble station among the lesser students of the Huayan school, have had the unworthy honor of presenting a catalog of my own school together with one on Hetuvidyā. I humbly rely upon worthy scholars of later times to correct and set forth these records aright.

Respectfully composed on the eighth day of the fourth month of the jiǎxū year.
