# 佛說罵意經
## Sūtra on Reproving the Mind

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0732

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高).

---

The Buddha spoke on reproving the mind: "All the insects and ants of the ten directions have devoured me, and I too have devoured all the insects and ants of the ten directions. How can one not feel shame? All the insects and ants of the ten directions have been my wives, and I too have taken all the insects and ants of the ten directions as wives. How can one not feel remorse? In the cycle of birth and death for incalculably long ages, one should cultivate and practice the Way, and not cultivate the deeds of animals. Those who commit sexual misconduct incur five punishments: first, loss of wealth; second, falling into terror; third, fear of officials; fourth, incurring enmity and calamity; fifth, at life's end, falling into the evil realms of hell."

People obtain bodies from food and drink combined with form. Having become human, there is nothing to fear except becoming an animal, or entering into hell or the hungry ghost realm. Renunciants shave their heads, beg for food, and endure hunger and thirst. They leave home and abandon possessions. Their eyes do not look wantonly; they guard the six sense faculties. They avoid evil conditions in order to escape suffering. Furthermore, a single thought can bring retribution lasting a hundred kalpas. It is like planting grain that yields a hundredfold or ten-thousandfold. Grain has no awareness, yet it always produces ten thousand times its yield. The human mind has awareness; the retribution, I fear, does not stop at that!

When a person sits practicing the Way and sees marine creatures or any beings, these were all aquatic creatures in past lives. If they are birds or beasts, they were commonly birds and beasts in past lives, and in this life they have obtained the requisite conditions. Offenses ought to be many, and everything one sees is tied to offenses. One should awaken to this thought: in past lives, one shared hell with these beings.

Past offenses bring decline; present offenses, not yet ripened, bring future calamity.

Evil has parents: delusion is the father of evil, and craving is the mother of evil. Goodness also has parents: the thirty-seven factors of awakening are the father of goodness, and the six perfections are the mother of goodness. There are yet other parents: the Buddha is the father, and the Dharma is the mother. Following the Buddha's words and practicing in accord with the Dharma: this is the conduct of following one's parents.

When a person falls into hell and the demon is about to strike with a club, if that person thinks: "This demon too will eventually perish and cannot last," then by thinking in this way, one is immediately liberated and reborn in heaven.

There are six things that cause decline: first, not serving the Buddha; second, not serving the Dharma; third, not serving the saṅgha of precept-keeping monks; fourth, not serving wise people; fifth, not encouraging others to practice the Way diligently; sixth, disputing with the virtuous. Reversing these six things leads to increase.

One should not commit offenses in the midst of merit; rather, one should create merit in the midst of hardship. When a person at a place of study does not practice the Way or recite sūtras, this is committing offenses in the midst of merit. When a person encounters illness, officials, fire, flood, or loss of wealth without becoming distressed, this is creating merit in the midst of hardship. One should not commit offenses in the midst of merit: burning incense, scattering flowers, and riding in carriages, yet finding fault with this and grasping at that, this is committing offenses in the midst of merit.

There are nine grades of killing, with offenses being lighter or heavier. It is better to kill a thousand ants than to kill a single fly. And so on upward to humans: killing greater beings incurs increasingly greater offenses. Building a hundred monasteries does not compare to saving a single life. Saving the lives of all people in all directions does not compare to guarding the mind for a single day. When a person attains a wholesome mind, the merit is immeasurable.

Giving material things to an evil person creates conditions for future association with evil people; this brings suffering. Giving material things to a good person brings merit that follows one. One should also not accept things from evil people, for later encounters with them will bring suffering upon suffering. It is better to receive from good people and give to good people than to receive from evil people and give to evil people. It is better to give to evil people than to accept from good people under false pretenses. It is better to receive from good people than to receive from evil people. When a person has an evil thought, it should be cut off; when one has a good thought, it too should be cut off. Evil thoughts lead to hell, the animal realm, and the hungry ghost realm. Good thoughts lead to heaven and the human realm. All of these should be cut off.

There are five māras that arise to disturb the mind and prevent a person from attaining the Way: first, the heavenly māra; second, the māra of defilements; third, the māra of the aggregates; fourth, the māra of afflictions; fifth, the māra of death. A practitioner walking the Way should be aware of these five māras.

There are five things that all buddhas of the ten directions cannot prevent: first, past lives must be forgotten; second, those whose lifespan should ascend will be reborn above; third, those whose lifespan should end will perish; fourth, those whose lifespan should age will grow old; fifth, those whose lifespan should die will die. These things cannot be prevented.

While living, a person encounters four situations: first, one with good intentions and good results following; second, one with evil intentions and evil results following; third, one with good intentions and evil results following; fourth, one with evil intentions and good results following.

All that a person possesses and thinks about will perish: the mind will perish, and the body will perish, and the conditions one encounters will perish. As for receiving retribution of fortune or misfortune in future lives, it is like planting fruit: this year's fruit ripens and falls to the ground, and next year there will be fruit again. Offenses are like the tree, mental conditions are like the fruit.

Whatever good or evil a person does, four spirits know of it: first, the earth spirit knows; second, the heavenly spirit knows; third, others nearby know; fourth, one's own mind knows.

There are three conditions that solidify the Way: first, action; second, reception; third, retribution. Doing evil deeds, this is action. Having been born, this is reception. Having received, this is retribution. There were five butchers' sons who, returning home in the evening, contemplated the Way. When their lives ended, in the evil realms, at evening they were entertained with the five pleasures, and at dawn they were again tormented with the five poisons.

There are two types of people who receive the Buddha's words: the wise and the foolish. These are the two types. Three things are invisible, just as when one upholds precepts, all evils become invisible.

After a renunciant has transmitted sūtras, there are four ways of receiving merit through practicing the Way: first, when practicing the Way single-mindedly, without eating or drinking, one may receive offerings from others; second, when one has always been poor and possesses nothing, one may receive offerings from others; third, when one already has sufficient means of one's own, one should not receive offerings from others; fourth, a person practicing the Way who eats only enough should not accept more than needed.

When people speak in accord with the sūtras and Dharma, one should accept it; when it does not accord, do not accept it. The same applies to hearing sūtras. When explaining sūtras for others, even if it disturbs their minds, there is no offense. Why? Because when originally explaining the sūtra, one did not intend to disturb minds. Though explaining sūtras may disturb people's minds, it is like someone who has eaten poison and is about to die: if taught to take an antidote, one does not die. The Buddha's sūtras are like the antidote.

A renunciant possesses four qualities that make one impervious to fire and invulnerable to weapons: first, one has not yet fulfilled the Buddha's commission; second, one has attained complete cessation; third, one has attained the fourth dhyāna; fourth, one's mind is in the Way, not in birth and death.

When the Buddha was in the world and visited a household, the host would close the door, not wishing others to see the Buddha. In a future life, both eyes become blind. Therefore the Buddha's sūtras should be made known openly. Offering lamps yields the divine eye, enables one to see afar, and brings radiance. Making wicks yields beautiful eye sockets. Offering sesame oil and ointment yields beautiful pupils. Offering fire yields discernment of truth. From these conditions, one who offers lamps to obtain good eyesight will, in future lives, obtain vessels of gold, silver, and precious jewels.

When someone comes speaking evil things to confuse and disturb the mind, this is Māra. One should be aware of what he does. Being called a good person born to evil parents, this is offense. When Māra's agents come cursing, it is merely wind; one should avoid it like an evil wind. If one does not avoid it, one is instead struck by the evil wind of wicked people. From these five one should sit firmly; these five kinds of conduct lead to the evil realms.

When entering an assembly, there are four situations where one should withdraw: first, when someone speaks non-Dharma to confuse minds, upon arriving one should withdraw; second, when one sees that what is said is wrong and does not restrain it, one should withdraw; third, when one sees that what is done is improper and does not correct it, one should withdraw; fourth, when what is asked is not in accord with the Dharma and one does not respond, one should withdraw.

In gatherings near renunciants, when fleas, lice, insects, or ants bite a person, there are four conditions: first, from past lives one must receive punishment from them; second, Māra comes to obstruct, not wanting one to attain the Way; third, one has not sought a clean place; fourth, one's mind is not equanimous.

There are eight types of people who cannot be trusted: first, the greedy; second, the envious; third, the wrathful; fourth, the frivolous; fifth, officials; sixth, those with duplicitous minds; seventh, enemies; eighth, women. Officials, water, fire, snakes, vipers, and sharp knives should not be approached; approach them and they kill.

There are five types with regard to coveting sūtras: first, wanting others to know one possesses sūtras, out of arrogance and desire for fame; second, wanting to use the sūtras to exhaust others while oneself not understanding; third, wanting to receive offerings by relying on sūtras; fourth, wanting everyone to receive sūtras from oneself so as to be their teacher; fifth, seeking sūtras in order to escape birth and death and attain the Way of liberation. The fifth type may be given the sūtras and the Way.

There are five types of sūtra students: first, those whose intent is to hear many sūtras; second, those who want to accumulate much merit; third, those who want to understand the sūtras; fourth, those who explain sūtras for others; fifth, those who want to cut off birth and death.

There are five kinds of envy: first, when serving a teacher together with others, wanting the teacher to love only oneself and not others, this is situational envy; second, thinking of oneself as merely human, born into a lowly family, this is birth envy; third, seeing others' wealth and wishing to be equal to them, this is wealth envy; fourth, when the Buddha has profound sūtras, wanting only oneself to obtain them and not wanting others to have them, this is sūtra envy; fifth, seeing others' beauty and thinking oneself alone is inferior, this is appearance envy. Committing these five envies prevents attaining the Way.

A renunciant should not fall into the five disputes: first, disputing about the Buddha; second, disputing about the Dharma; third, disputing about the precepts; fourth, disputing about the sūtras; fifth, disputing about the virtuous. A renunciant should not dispute about existence or nonexistence.

There are five false tenacities: first, false tenacity of the body; second, false tenacity of commitments; third, false tenacity of wrong views; fourth, false tenacity of craving; fifth, false tenacity of admonitions.

There are seven forms of lust: first, seeing attractive clothing; second, hearing the sound of jewelry; third, hearing a woman's voice; fourth, in the mind thinking and speaking of women; fifth, gazing with the eyes; sixth, thinking of conjugal rites; seventh, mental fantasy of transgression. These seven forms of lust prevent attaining the Way.

People who are forgetful have five conditions: first, bodily forgetfulness; second, forgetfulness from excessive thinking; third, attachment to craving; fourth, attachment to views; fifth, from past lives, meaning having previously troubled, cut off the speech of, or frightened others. Forgetfulness from fatigue means that the mind thinks to the point of exhaustion.

Drinking alcohol brings poison in four parts; drinking water brings heavenly vitality in four parts; sleeping brings death in four parts; speaking good words brings heaven in four parts; speaking evil things brings hell in four parts. A king had a wife whose brother was about to fall into hell. The wife said to the king: "Do not let my brother enter this prison!" The king said: "Then you stop him." As her brother was about to enter, the wife said: "This prison gate must not be entered!" The brother said: "There is much to be had inside." He ran into the prison. People are dragged by their offenses in just this way.

If someone takes another person's plow-yoke to use without informing the owner, intending to return it after plowing: the Buddha said, "This is not acceptable; it is already theft!" The person said: "I wish to make a golden yoke to repay it." The Buddha said: "The theft is not thereby absolved."

Upholding the precepts is true filial piety, repaying the kindness of one's parents. Why? Not killing any living being yields long life. Not stealing yields universal wealth. Not committing sexual misconduct, not being deceitful, yields universal trust. Not drinking alcohol yields universal purity. When parents are in such circumstances, they will be at peace. When staying overnight at a Buddhist monastery, one must not sleep on a renunciant's rope bed, bench, table, or under their bedding, for all of these are violations of the precepts.

When one has invited a renunciant, before the renunciant has eaten one should not ask about the sūtras; if the renunciant explains them, there is an offense. Only after the renunciant has eaten may one ask about sūtras and the Way.

Not upholding precepts and not practicing the Way while residing in a Buddhist monastery is worse than throwing oneself into a boiling cauldron. A boiling cauldron burns only one body. Not upholding precepts and not practicing the Way while in a Buddhist monastery burns countless bodies.

A renunciant should also explain sūtras. If someone asks about the sūtras, one should not refuse. But if someone brings gifts to a renunciant, one should not explain sūtras in response; one may explain only later. Receiving gifts and then explaining sūtras incurs an offense.

There are five conditions under which a renunciant should decline an invitation: first, if one is invited and another person is more suitable, one should not go; second, if one is invited but another person is more suitable, one should not go; third, if one is invited and the host says, "Actually, please invite someone else instead," then that other person should not go; fourth, if one has come from afar and was not invited immediately but only later, one should not go; fifth, if enemies who are ill-disposed live nearby or in the same area. While seated in the assembly, one should not go forth. Why? To stop the mind through sleep and rest.

Cessation has three levels: first, cessation of the activities of birth and death; second, the mind in practice has not yet arrived, because the conditions of birth and death have ceased in the mind.

Furthermore, regarding people who are fond of sleep: there are three conditions for excessive sleepiness: first, overeating; second, excessive drinking; third, worry. There are three further conditions: first, the body rests; second, the residual mind, exhausted from sleep, arises to receive a new mind; third, retention of what was received.

When a person sleeps, there is mind, consciousness, lifespan, life-force, respiration, and breath. During the day, there are altogether thirty-six thousand five hundred breaths, roughly equal. There are eight practices to eliminate sleepiness: first, eat little; second, sit; third, stand; fourth, walk; fifth, recite sūtras; sixth, gaze at the stars; seventh, wash the face; eighth, contemplate bones. If these do not work, one should think of all wholesome things. Once the mind has shifted, one should return to one's object. To obtain merit and the settled mind of the Way, when other thoughts arise, understanding will come of itself.

There is merit obtained through the body, merit obtained through speech and mind, and merit obtained through emptiness. Likewise, there are offenses of the body, offenses of speech and mind, and offenses of emptiness. Merit obtained through emptiness means dreaming of obtaining gold, silver, jewels, nobility, wealth, and happiness: this is merit obtained through emptiness. Offenses of emptiness means dreaming that someone comes to kill or violate you: these are offenses of emptiness. The mouth instructs others to do evil or urges others to kill; later, through these conditions, one is unexpectedly beaten by others: this is an offense of the mouth. The mind itself creates conditions whereby one is harmed or killed by others: this is an offense of the mind.

The reason people have good and evil dreams is this: when one desires food and drink but has nothing, there is only the mind, and so one dreams of fine food and drink. When the mind wishes to kill someone, one dreams that someone comes to kill. All of these have conditions, whether from past lives, future lives, or the present life. Dreams in the first part of the night are matters of the present, morning and evening.

Whatever good or evil is done in dreams is all created by the mind; its counterpart is also entirely of the mind. It is like someone who picks up an object, examines it, and then hides it away. Though it is no longer seen, when the mind thinks of it, it immediately appears, no different from seeing it. What was done in past lives creates its counterpart with the mind of the present life.

When a person dies and then sits up again, it is because in life they enjoyed frightening people as ghosts, or when ill they told those nearby: "Call to me and wake me." For these reasons, after death they sit up again.

There are four conditions for falling into the nāga realm: first, much generosity; second, much anger; third, scorning others; fourth, arrogance and conceit. These are the four things that make one a nāga. The first yields merit, but the latter three yield the body of a nāga.

The Vinaya sūtras say: among animals, those with horns did so because in past lives they enjoyed wearing horns and cross-shaped ornaments as adornments, and so they received the punishment of an animal body with horns. Some having unusual colors did so because they wore colorful garments with evil intentions, considering them beautiful, and so received this punishment.

Parrots with red beaks and red feet: in past lives they enjoyed colorful clothing and reddened lips. Women who enjoyed wearing long skirts in future lives fall among pheasants and long-tailed birds with long tails. All of these are things they enjoyed in past lives, and now through conditions they obtain them.

People and animals whose bodies are covered with sores: in past lives they pierced living fish and animals through the mouth with wood and bamboo, and so in this life they receive such punishment.

When people and animals see each other, some feel joy and some feel anger. Those who were on good terms in past lives feel joy upon meeting in this life. Those who were hostile in past lives feel displeasure upon meeting now.

Being reborn as an animal is evil merit. Obtaining food and feeling happy, this is evil happiness. It means that in past lives, when one did evil and then felt happy, one received this result. Animals that do not obtain fine food have three conditions: first, unfamiliarity; second, good merit is exhausted; third, offenses naturally take their course.

Animals also desire form, sound, scent, taste, and touch. They also communicate among themselves, but they cannot speak as humans do.

Women who have whiskers: in past lives they came from among sheep, chickens, or ducks, and therefore have whiskers.

Fish and turtles that have no voice: in past lives they cut off people's speech. Fish do not come alive immediately at birth but only after seven days. They can be on grass, wood, or dry ground for forty or fifty years and come alive when they get water. The reason they have many offspring is that there are many evil people, and those with the same offenses are born together. Those who are fond of anger, envy, delusion, and lust, and who practice these four, fall among monkeys. Those who act without integrity also fall among monkeys. Those who enjoy performing music and entertainment in future lives fall among birds, insects, or monkeys. Those who enjoy killing through sacrifice in future lives fall among sheep. Why? Because they cut off heads, flayed skin, and dismembered bodies. Or in past lives they enjoyed robbing people, stripping them of their clothing, causing them to suffer cold. Therefore they themselves first receive this retribution: silkworms spin thread and freeze themselves, then enter boiling water and fire to die. Those who are foolish and fond of killing in future lives become pigs. Those who frighten others in future lives become deer. Those excessively fond of fine flavors in future lives become flies. Those who enjoy beating others in future lives become donkeys. The reason donkeys have long ears is that they enjoyed pulling people's ears. Animals that enjoy striking people's ears were in past lives soldiers. Why? When one soldier shouts, all the others shout too; when one donkey brays, all the other donkeys bray too. Those who incur debts without repaying them become oxen. The reason oxen have split hooves is due to two conditions: first, unpaid debts; second, having enjoyed wearing wooden clogs as adornment. The reason horses have solid hooves is due to two conditions: first, unpaid debts; second, having enjoyed wearing wooden shoes as adornment.

There were six companions who all fell into hell together, in the same cauldron. Each wished to speak of their original offense. The first person said: "Sa!" The second said: "Na!" The third said: "Chi!" The fourth said: "She!" The fifth said: "Gu!" The sixth said: "Tuoluo!" The Buddha saw this and smiled. Maudgalyāyana asked the Buddha why he smiled. The Buddha said: "These six companions are together in a hell cauldron. Each wished to speak of their offenses, but the boiling churned them about. They could not utter more than one syllable each before sinking again. The first who said 'Sa' meant: 'In worldly terms it has been six thousand billion years; in hell this counts as one day. When will it ever end?' The second who said 'Na' meant: 'There is no set time; I do not know when I will be freed.' The third who said 'Chi' meant: 'Alas! Why did I spend my life pursuing profit? Unable to restrain my own mind. In past lives, however much I had, I never knew satisfaction.' The fourth who said 'She' meant: 'I pursued profit in earnest, yet my wealth now belongs to others, and I received only suffering.' The fifth who said 'Gu' meant: 'Who will guarantee me release from hell, so that I will never again transgress the Way's prohibitions, but ascend to heaven and receive celestial happiness?' The sixth who said 'Tuoluo' meant: 'This matter was never part of my original plan. It is like driving a cart and missing the main road, entering a crooked path and breaking the cart's spokes. Regret comes too late.'"

The Buddha spoke of four barriers. From the world up to the sixth heaven is the barrier of death. From the seventh heaven up to the eighteenth heaven is the barrier of emptiness. From the nineteenth heaven up to the twenty-third heaven is the barrier of impermanence. From the twenty-fifth heaven up to the twenty-eighth heaven is the barrier of liberation. Passing beyond these four barriers is the essential escape. The utmost merit does not extend beyond the twenty-eighth heaven; the utmost evil does not extend beyond Avīci hell. All other retributions of offense vary in degree. From a single thing arise the three poisons; from the three poisons arise the three evil paths. Without the three evil paths, all people would attain the Way. From Avīci hell up to the sixth heaven is one realm. From the seventh heaven up to the nineteenth heaven, all practice the four immeasurables, forming another realm. From the twenty-fifth heaven, practicing impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self, up to the twenty-eighth heaven is yet another realm. Without escaping the twenty-eighth heaven, the three poisons are not exhausted, and one falls back to become a human, going back and forth through the three realms. To seek the essential escape, one must extinguish conceptual thinking.

From Avīci, the great hell, up to the sixth heaven is the desire realm. From the seventh heaven up to the nineteenth heaven is the form realm. From the twenty-fifth heaven up to the twenty-eighth heaven is the formless realm, where there is no conceptual thinking and yet there is conceptual thinking. From Avīci hell upward to the twenty-eighth heaven is the realm of birth and death. Beyond the twenty-eighth heaven is the unconditioned realm. Cutting off lust, one reaches the form realm. Cutting off anger, one enters the realm of no-thought. Cutting off delusion, one reaches the essential liberation. The three realms have three aspects: first, from Avīci hell up to the sixth heaven is the domain of lust; second, from the seventh heaven up to the nineteenth heaven is called attachment to form; third, excluding the four heavens up to Anāgāmin, from the twenty-fifth heaven up to the twenty-eighth heaven, where there is no conceptual thinking and yet there is conceptual thinking, this is called the domain of formlessness. These are the three aspects, falling into birth and death through attachment to form. It is like the light of fire, which can be seen but not grasped. In the domain of lust and in the domain of attachment to form: these are the three aspects. From hell, the animal realm, and the hungry ghost realm, through lust and attachment to form one should emerge toward the three liberations within one's vows.

What is the essential Way? There are three roots: the evil root, the good root, and the Way root. Hell, the animal realm, and the hungry ghost realm: these are the evil root. From the human realm up to the sixth heaven: this is the good root. From the seventh heaven upward, emerging through the twelve gates: this is the Way root. Having reached the twenty-eighth heaven yet not attaining liberation, there are three conditions: first, craving; second, delusion; third, having a mind. Therefore one does not attain liberation. Having emerged through the twelve gates, one should aspire to be free of mind. The three poisons not being severed, one does not ascend to liberation because they are not yet exhausted. Why the twenty-eighth heaven? Because in previous bodily existence in the world, one was not attached to the body and dispersed the mind, thereby severing the outer seven things. Ascending to the sixth heaven, one receives merit and severs the inner three things. Above the eighteenth heaven, from the twenty-first heaven are four stations belonging to the non-returner.

Practicing the ten wholesome deeds, some are reborn in the first heaven and some in the sixth heaven. Because of differences in the amount of goodness, their places differ. Practicing the ten evil deeds, some enter hell and some enter the animal or hungry ghost realms. Because offenses are lighter or heavier, their places differ. Practicing goodness yet receiving evil, practicing evil yet receiving goodness: within goodness there is a small amount of evil, and within evil there is a small amount of goodness, subtle and invisible. If within goodness there were no small evil, one would never fall into evil. If within evil there were no small goodness, one would never escape. The utmost evil does not extend beyond Avīci hell; the utmost goodness does not extend beyond the twenty-eighth heaven. Not perceiving the subtle workings of the mind, one does not attain liberation.

All beings, from practicing the ten wholesome deeds, are reborn in heaven or among humans. Heavenly beings undergo transformation birth because when formerly in the world, they did not direct their minds toward form, perceiving the foulness and impurity of form. From this they obtained transformation birth. If one directs the mind, one must again pass through a woman's womb. Heavenly beings obtain transformation birth due to five conditions: first, not approaching women; second, the mind not arising; third, not wishing for children; fourth, enjoying sitting alone; fifth, not desiring worldly things. Not being attached to the body also yields transformation birth.

When a person's life is about to end, one should maintain mindfulness. Once the breath has ceased, the mind clings to exhalation and inhalation for a time. From the first heaven upward, this is mindfulness of the breath, contemplating the body as having thirty-two components. Consider: hair of the head, hair of the body, teeth, bones, skin, flesh, the five organs, making eleven things belonging to earth. Tears, mucus, saliva, pus, blood, fat, marrow, and urine, making seven things belonging to water. Warmth and heat that digest food, making two things belonging to fire. Wind has twelve things. These thirty-two components all arise from earth, water, fire, and wind. What constitutes earth? A person is born from the essence of grain: grain is earth, the mind is the seed, vital essence is water, and the two come together to produce the body. Seeking clothing and food is nourishing vitality and protecting the body's master. Since fundamentally there is nothing, all is impermanent and will be exhausted. Attaining the Way, one knows the body is not the self, contemplating that the body will not last long and must inevitably die and decay. The mind is the seed of being human, and so one should guard the mind single-pointedly. Foolish people cannot guard their spirit and consciousness; they only nourish the four limbs, deceived by form and flavor, thinking the body is the self. Not knowing evil, all things arise from the body. Craving flavors through eating and drinking, one falls into suffering, going back and forth in birth and death without liberation. Originally encountering adverse conditions, the spirit departs emptily, heading toward the paths of good and evil. The body dies and falls to the ground, decaying day and night, and fundamentally there is nothing. Only through mental activity does it transform and come into being. When the body dies, all returns to earth; all things are likewise. All passes away: this is impermanence. People do not examine themselves. The myriad thoughts are all not for one's own benefit: this is suffering. The body dies and is abandoned; the myriad thoughts are likewise and also perish: this is the self coming to an end. Being born again and suffering again, one creates good and evil actions, planting seeds without knowing their destination: this is non-self. A practitioner of the Way should seek to sever these. One who does not understand the four aspects of impermanence will never attain the Way. Examining one's own body, observing all the dead and decayed, knowing that all people and things are empty, empty and without substance, the mind then rests in stillness and one attains the practice of joy. Having attained practice, the mind becomes peaceful. Not departing from these five, the mind becomes unified: this is the Way.

Examining one's own body and knowing there is nothing, the body and mind come to rest. Feelings, perceptions, mind, and dharmas also come to rest in body and mind. Rest means three things. Feelings come to rest; feelings and mind come to rest: these are four things. Desire comes to rest; the mind rests on the inner three things. Perception comes to rest; volition comes to rest; consciousness ceases; outer dharmas come to rest. These are the four foundations of mindfulness. Resting, one then guards. Guarding and resting, one then contemplates. Therefore the sūtra says: "Calm and insight practiced together lead to attaining the four noble truths."

Therefore the Buddha said: "Sitting alone in contemplation of one's own mind means contemplating the cessation of form, feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness." The mind coming to rest of itself means the external mind has ceased. Always practicing thus, seeing the Way as it truly is, through sitting in practice one sees for oneself. Therefore the sūtra says: "When a monk can thus bring the inner mind to rest, then one guards that rest." This mind should first be contemplated, thinking of cessation and extinguishing thoughts. Thoughts wait upon one's own mind, and then one guards the mind. The mind does not leave the body. For a practitioner of the Way, waiting for the external refers to the myriad things. Mindfulness directed inward refers to thinking. Consciousness wishes to extinguish thoughts, waiting with constant mindfulness that all things are impermanent and decaying, that none of them are mine and that I am not the things. At the urgency of birth, thought upon thought, at the time of death: what does one take along? One takes goodness, takes the unified mind, takes recitation and the joy from it. The Buddha said: "These are your possessions; take them. Everything else is not yours." The mind should recognize and be mindful: all that we love must part, each decaying on its own. Thinking of these things only confuses the mind and follows one into offense. There must be a return to guarding the body in purity, progressing toward the gate of nirvāṇa.

The Buddha, from the single-pointed mind up to the nine contemplations, contemplated the four colors that must all dissolve: a person four or five days after death begins to smell and decay, the color turning truly blue-black. At five or six days, pus and blood flow from the mouth, nose, ears, and eyes, truly red. Then the flesh and muscles decay and fall apart, maggots grow in the intestines and stomach, eating the flesh in turn. The skin decays, the bones stand out truly white. After long, long ages, it turns black and becomes ash and dust. Earth, water, fire, wind, and space are all not mine! Mind, from countless ages past, you have been someone's wife, someone's slave, and have been animals, oxen, horses, and insects, enduring hardship and heavy burdens of debt. You have been slaughtered and flayed, minced and roasted by others. Now, being human, you again take others as wives, slaves, animals, oxen, horses, and insects, slaughtering, flaying, mincing, and roasting them at will. When the body dies, you must again receive all this in return. Practitioner of the Way! Do you not see that when a person dies and the breath ceases, they know nothing? The body lies stiff and straight, soon stinking, decaying, and revolting. Contemplate this carefully and fear arises, not wishing to see it. Why then are you not frightened? This is what enables one to ascend to heaven and attain the way of nirvāṇa.

The Buddha, knowing the nine contemplations are all empty and without substance, returned to the single-pointed mind. Practitioner of the Way, urgently extinguish thought! Attend with nothing else, and you will arrive. Things are deeply embedded in habitual action, and what is seen is not perceived because the mind is not present. Desires, craving, and thoughts of impermanence and decay: for lust, one should contemplate its counterpart; for anger, contemplate equanimity; for delusion, contemplate the root. Original practices are impermanent; the unconditioned is peaceful. One who does not know impermanence will never abandon craving, nor depart from the path of Vaiśālī. All that exists in the world is like a dream. In a dream one eats fine food and sees beautiful things; upon waking, nothing is there. All that exists in the world is just so: what is born dies, what is completed decays. All must return to emptiness. What is there to crave? When a person has a wife and children and possessions, it is the same. Why? When a person works hard and gains wealth and profit, when the household gathers in joy and happiness, it is like birds flocking together. All is impermanent. One day they part and are seen no more. Even if they were permanent, worry and fear would be endless, and the mind in birth and death would daily accumulate offenses. The wise person restrains oneself with few desires, seeking only one set of clothing and one meal. From settled mind one practices, not seeking a fixed place, always returning to guard the body in purity, severing desire and contemplating emptiness.

It was asked: "The practice of the Way and the guarding of the mind, from what did they originally arise?"

The teacher said: "Heaven and earth came into being by their nature. People descended from the fifteenth heaven, and their lifespans had no untimely death. Birth and death through the five destinies arose from the six sensory impairments." People are born with minds and intentions that are fundamentally good, without craving, attachment, feeling, perception, or the formations of birth and death, but they are deceived by the eyes, ears, nose, and mouth. The eyes first look at form, the ears listen to sound, the nose perceives fragrance, the mouth knows flavor, and the mind generates thoughts. These create the ten deeds that form the five aggregates. The mind becomes consciousness; together they make the six sensory impairments. From these, good and evil actions are planted and come to fruition. From this arise old age, sickness, death, and birth. Through the five destinies, seeking the Way and wishing to sever birth and death, one guards the mind and brings it to rest. The eyes rest from form, the ears rest from sound, the nose rests from fragrance, the mouth rests from flavor, and the body's attractions are severed. The six impairments of conduct are cut off. One contemplates and demolishes the mind's thoughts, and through sitting in meditation, the mind is extinguished. Consciousness that attains the Way: the five aggregates are completely extinguished. Knowing that fundamentally there is nothing, one contemplates emptiness, conceives of emptiness, and proceeds directly toward the gate of nirvāṇa. As for what one has guarded, the mind is the master of consciousness and conduct. Therefore the six sensory impairments must be regarded as the calamity, as the root of action and the planting of seeds in the five destinies. A practitioner of the Way, thinking carefully and guarding the four foundations of mindfulness, wishes to bring cessation without deviation. Mindfulness, consciousness, and perception: toward which path do they go? One who wishes to extinguish thought, consciousness, and perception should practice completely and not merely sever the body's ten deeds. The body, speech, and mind, these three being settled, the five aggregates and six sensory impairments are then corrected. The three being settled means: the mouth knowing nothing is the settling of speech; the body knowing nothing is the settling of the body; the mind thinking of nothing is the settling of the mind.

The mind has four illnesses. When delusion is excessive, the five aggregates become excessive. When the five aggregates are excessive, the mind runs wild and cannot practice. Unable to practice, anger arises of itself, and then lustful thoughts arise, uncontrollable, and one falls into delusion. Therefore, in practicing the Way, one must sever the five aggregates. Severing the five aggregates means that feelings and the desire for comfort are excessive. The teacher said: "The body does not wish to practice because of great exhaustion. The mind does not wish to practice because it does not wish to contemplate death, decay, suffering, and emptiness."

It was asked: "What is the fundamental birth?"

The teacher said: "Not having doubt is the fundamental root of birth."

"What is the direction? What is the counterpart? What is the practice?"

The teacher said: "Not turning the mind is the direction. Not turning thought is the counterpart. What accords with the mind is the practice."

It was asked: "What kind of field is this? Who is called the field?"

"Mind, you have a mind. Does the farmer not know it is without mind? The farmer does not perceive; do you who have a mind perceive? The farmer who is without mind does not perceive. Can it be said that mind perceives? The flavor of lust is also mind. Why? Because one does not perceive its approach. To leave home is to have no field; that is how it is."

It was asked: "Is there mind in the Buddha's homeland, or is it without mind?"

"That is how it is. Fundamentally there is neither body nor mind. One simply acts and receives accordingly. It is like the five kinds of seeds: fundamentally there is no seed, yet growth occurs. Human birth likewise: fundamentally there is no seed, yet there is birth. It is like lighting a fire: flame emerges as brightness. When the brightness departs the fuel, it ceases. When a person examines the body and knows it is non-self, all things likewise cease."

It was objected: "If fundamentally there is no mind, then what is guarded?"

The teacher said: "Precisely because fundamentally there is nothing, it can be guarded. To extinguish what fundamentally exists cannot be guarded."

The teacher said: "The Way has four essentials. First, the multitude holds the gate. Second, knowing the body is non-self, one demolishes the body and no longer cherishes it: this is escaping from the human gate up to the gate of the sixth heaven. Third, knowing impermanence and the mind no longer being directed toward things: this is escaping through the gate of the eighteenth heaven. Fourth, like emptiness extinguishing emptiness: this is escaping through the gate of the twenty-eighth heaven. Only when emptiness is extinguished does one enter the Way. Therefore the sūtra says: 'Practitioners of the Way who awaken attain escape, meaning awakening to suffering, emptiness, non-self, and impermanence.' Attaining escape means escaping through the four essential barriers. Attaining the first dhyāna and ascending to the seventh heaven: one has a body but only as a shadow. Why? Because through practice one has demolished the body. Contemplate the body, observing the head, the hair, the brain. Contemplate that hair fundamentally has no origin; it is produced by transformation and will all decay and fall. The brain is like congealed rice porridge and will all rot and stink. The eyes are merely cavities; fluid will all drain out, leaving emptiness. The ears are merely cavities, oozing foul water. The nose and mouth with saliva and mucus will all flow out, be scattered, and decay. The tongue, throat, lungs, liver, and heart, with foul blood in the heart. The gallbladder, diaphragm, and spleen are attached to the stomach. The kidneys are attached to the spine. The stomach contains flavors that digest food. The large intestine holds excrement. The small intestine has openings for urine and feces. The small intestines will all bloat, swell, rot, and decay. The intestines, stomach, excrement, and urine soak and splash each other: a foul and repulsive place. Below are the buttocks, flesh, blood, two shins, two thighs, and two feet. The flesh and muscles are consumed; the sinews and channels decay and fall apart. The skeletal joints separate and collapse. The shin bones stand white. The thigh bones are like cart wheels. The hip bones connect with the spine. The shoulder bones connect with the elbows, arms, and hands. The skin and hide also decay and rot; the joints separate and collapse. The neck bones connect with the skull. Blood and flesh are consumed, returning to ash and dust. All that creeps and crawls, once the out-breath does not return, has already passed from the world. The body lies stiff and straight, no longer moving. The fire departs and the body grows cold. The wind departs and the breath ceases. Fluid flows from the nine orifices, becoming the departure of water. No longer consuming food is the departure of earth. At three or four days, the color turns blue-black. Pus and blood flow from the mouth, nose, ears, eyes, and all nine orifices, truly red. Muscles, bones, and flesh decay. Intestines, stomach, five organs, and limbs all return to ash and dust. Observe all things as being thus; one's own body is likewise. All is extinguished, becoming emptiness. Knowing that the in-breath and out-breath are truly empty, one can then draw near to the Way."
