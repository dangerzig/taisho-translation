## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra of the Story of King Māndhātā, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling at Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, at Anāthapiṇḍika's park. At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, while in solitary retreat, gave rise to this thought: "All craving, with its attachments, is accumulated and stored away; craving knows no satiation." The Venerable Ānanda then rose from his seat, went to where the Bhagavān was, and upon arrival bowed with his head at the Bhagavān's feet and sat to one side. After a short while, the Venerable Ānanda drew back, knelt with joined palms, and addressed the Bhagavān: "Just now, having gone to my place of meditation, this thought arose in me: 'All craving with its attachments and accumulations is without satiation.'"

At that time, the Bhagavān said to Ānanda: "Just so, just so, Ānanda! Indeed, craving, attachment, and accumulation are truly without satisfaction. Why is this so? Ānanda, in times long past, there was a great king named Māndhātā — a true dharma-king who governed the people without violence or cruelty, endowed with the seven treasures. These seven treasures were: the wheel-treasure, the elephant-treasure, the dark-blue horse-treasure, the jewel-treasure, the woman-treasure, the householder-treasure, and the general-treasure — these are called the seven treasures. He also had a thousand sons, courageous, vigorous, and radiant in appearance, capable of repelling enemy armies. His realm extended as the rivers and great seas of this world, and he governed by the Dharma without recourse to weapons or punishment.

"At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā gave rise to this thought: 'I have heard that people of old had exceedingly long lifespans and were clever and wise.' He then said: 'Now I possess this Jambudvīpa with its power and supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace.' The king gave rise to this thought: 'I wish that the seven treasures would rain down upon my palace.' At that time, Ānanda, as soon as King Māndhātā had this thought, within seven days the seven treasures rained down.

"At another time, King Māndhātā again gave rise to this thought: 'I have heard that people of old had exceedingly long lifespans and were clever and wise. I have heard that Pūrvavideha is endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace. I wish to go there and govern.' At that time, Ānanda, as soon as King Māndhātā had this thought, he vanished from Jambudvīpa and appeared in Pūrvavideha, together with his fourfold army. At that time, the beings of Pūrvavideha, seeing King Māndhātā approaching from afar, gathered around him, each bearing silver bowls filled with gold dust or gold bowls filled with silver dust, and came to where King Māndhātā was. Having arrived, they addressed King Māndhātā: 'Welcome, Great King! This is the realm of Pūrvavideha, endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace. May the great king govern here; we are all the great king's subjects.'

"At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā governed there for countless hundreds of thousands of years. At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā at another time gave rise to this thought: 'I possess Jambudvīpa with its fulfillment of desires and supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace. I have already caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace for seven days. I now also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha with its supernatural faculties, abundant harvests, and teeming populace. I have heard that people of old were clever and wise and said: "There is the land of Godānīya, endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace."' The king gave rise to this thought: 'I wish to go to the realm of Godānīya and govern the people.'

"At that time, Ānanda, having arisen this thought, King Māndhātā vanished from Pūrvavideha and went to the realm of Godānīya, together with his fourfold army. At that time, the people of Godānīya, seeing King Māndhātā approaching from afar, each bearing silver bowls filled with gold dust or gold bowls filled with silver dust, brought these offerings and came to King Māndhātā's presence. Having arrived, they addressed King Māndhātā: 'Welcome, Great King! This is the realm of Godānīya belonging to the king, with abundant harvests and a teeming populace. May the great king govern the people here in Godānīya; we are all the great king's subjects.' At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā governed the people of Godānīya for countless hundreds of thousands of years, for countless myriads of years.

"At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā again at another time gave rise to this thought: 'I possess Jambudvīpa with its abundant nourishment and teeming populace, where I caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace for seven days; I also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha with its supernatural faculties, abundant harvests, and teeming populace; I also possess the realm of Godānīya with its supernatural faculties, abundant harvests, and teeming populace.' King Māndhātā then gave rise to this thought: 'I have heard that people of old were clever and wise and said: "There is the realm of Uttarakuru, endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a flourishing populace. In that land, all people are free and without masters, fulfilling their desires at will, with exceedingly long lifespans. When they die there, all are reborn in the heavens; they eat naturally-occurring rice and wear karpāsika garments."' At that time, King Māndhātā gave rise to this thought: 'I shall go there to govern.' Having arisen this thought, Ānanda, King Māndhātā vanished from Godānīya and went to the realm of Uttarakuru, together with his fourfold army.

"At that time, King Māndhātā, seeing from afar that the ground of that land was level and entirely of a dark blue-green color, addressed his ministers and people: 'Do you all see that the ground is level and of a dark blue-green color?' They replied: 'Indeed, Great King!' 'Know that these are the karpāsika trees. The people there all wear karpāsika garments; you too shall wear karpāsika garments.' At that time, King Māndhātā again saw that the ground was of a pure white color, and addressing his ministers: 'Do you all see that the ground is white?' They replied: 'Indeed.' 'This is naturally-occurring rice, without husks or stalks, requiring no threshing or winnowing. Its fragrance extends a hundred yojanas with the wind and fifty yojanas against the wind; it is supremely fragrant and delicious. The people of that land eat this rice; you, worthy ones, shall also eat this rice.' At that time, King Māndhātā again saw from afar that the ground was level and entirely of a dark blue-green color, and addressed his ministers and people: 'Do you all see this dark blue-green color?' They replied: 'Indeed, Great King!' 'This is soft grass four fingers in height, as supple as peacock feathers, each blade curling to the right, conforming perfectly to the body. The people of Uttarakuru all sit upon this grass; you too shall sit upon this grass.' At that time, King Māndhātā, seeing from afar city walls, towers, and battlements, addressed his ministers and people: 'Do you all see these towers and battlements?' They replied: 'Indeed, Great King!' 'These are the dwellings where the people live.'

"At that time, the people of Uttarakuru, seeing King Māndhātā approaching from afar, each bearing gold bowls filled with silver grain and silver bowls filled with gold grain, addressed King Māndhātā: 'Welcome, Great King! This is the realm of Uttarakuru belonging to the king, endowed with supernatural faculties, with abundant harvests and a flourishing populace. May the great king govern the people of Uttarakuru; we shall follow the king's instructions.' At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā governed the people of Uttarakuru for countless hundreds and thousands of years.

"At that time, King Māndhātā again at another time gave rise to this thought: 'The domains I govern include Jambudvīpa, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace, where I caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace for seven days; I also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; I also possess the realm of Godānīya, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; I also possess the realm of Uttarakuru, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace. I have heard that wise and clever people of old said: "There are the Trāyastriṃśa heavens, where the lifespan is exceedingly long and the countenances supremely beautiful, and there dwells a god named Śakra Devānām Indra." I shall now go to the Trāyastriṃśa heavens and receive the five heavenly endowments: heavenly lifespan, heavenly form, heavenly pleasure, heavenly supernatural powers, and heavenly supremacy. I now wish to go to the Trāyastriṃśa heavens.'

"At that time, Ānanda, having arisen this thought, King Māndhātā vanished from Uttarakuru and proceeded to dwell in the Trāyastriṃśa heavens, together with his fourfold army, and went to the Hall of the Good Dharma. At that time, Śakra Devānām Indra, seeing King Māndhātā approaching from afar, addressed him: 'Welcome, Great King! Please come to this seat.' At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā took the seat and sat together with Śakra Devānām Indra. The two kings sat side by side and were without distinction; their countenances, bearing, and features were exactly alike, differing only in the blinking of their eyes.

"At that time, King Māndhātā at another time gave rise to this thought: 'I possess Jambudvīpa, with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace, where I caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace for seven days; I also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha, with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace; I also possess the realm of Godānīya, with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace; I also possess the realm of Uttarakuru, with its supernatural faculties and teeming populace; and here too are these Trāyastriṃśa heavens with their long life, where I dwell at length.' At that time, the gods of Trāyastriṃśa assembled in the Hall of the Good Dharma and sat in their respective places. The gods of Trāyastriṃśa gave rise to this thought: 'This King Māndhātā is the king of Jambudvīpa, governing by the Dharma, possessed of the seven treasures, surrounded by a thousand sons, foremost and supreme among the four realms, governing the people by the Dharma without recourse to weapons or punishment.' At that time, Ānanda, Śakra Devānām Indra shared half his seat with King Māndhātā, and the two sat together. Their radiance and appearance were without distinction; their countenance and features were entirely the same, differing only in the blinking of their eyes.

"At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā enjoyed himself there amid the five objects of sensual desire without satiation, for countless hundreds of thousands of years, for countless myriads of years. At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā at another time gave rise to this thought: 'The domains I govern include Jambudvīpa, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace, where for seven days I caused the seven treasures to rain upon the palace; I also possess the realm of Pūrvavideha, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; I also possess the realm of Godānīya, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; I also possess the realm of Uttarakuru, with its supernatural faculties and flourishing populace; and here too are these Trāyastriṃśa heavens with their long life and resplendent beauty. There is this Hall of the Good Dharma, fully appointed with four gardens. What are the four? The Nandana garden, the Jeweled-Garland garden, the Rough-and-Firm garden, and the Variegated garden — these are the four gardens. There are the Pārijāta trees and the Kovidāra trees, all in full flourish, their fragrance extending a hundred yojanas with the wind and fifty yojanas against the wind. This is where the gods of Trāyastriṃśa take their pleasure, enjoying the five objects of sensual desire through four months. This Hall of the Good Dharma is entirely of blue lapis lazuli; this is the throne of Śakra Devānām Indra, surrounded by a hundred terraces all made of the seven treasures. Each terrace has seven hundred pavilions; each pavilion has forty-nine women; each woman has forty-nine attendants — all under the dominion of Śakra Devānām Indra.' At that time, Ānanda, King Māndhātā again gave rise to this thought: 'I shall now displace Śakra Devānām Indra and govern the gods of Trāyastriṃśa myself.'

"At that time, Ānanda, as soon as King Māndhātā had this thought, he fell from Śakra Devānām Indra's throne back to Jambudvīpa, together with his fourfold army. He lost his supernatural powers, and his whole body was wracked with pain, as a man on the verge of death. The wheel-treasure disappeared, the elephant-treasure perished, the horse-treasure also came to an end, the jewel-treasure vanished, the woman-treasure perished, and the householder-treasure and the general-treasure perished.

"At that time, Ānanda, the kinsmen of King Māndhātā of five kinds all gathered and came to his presence, addressing him: 'After the great king has passed away, there will surely be those who ask us: "What were Great King Māndhātā's final instructions at the time of his death?" What shall we answer them?' At that time, King Māndhātā replied: 'If after my death someone asks: "What were King Māndhātā's final instructions at the time of his death?" you should answer thus: "Know, worthy ones, that King Māndhātā ruled over the four continents, went to the Trāyastriṃśa heavens, and without ever finding satiation in the five objects of sensual desire, he died in that very state."'

"Ānanda, do you wish to know? That King Māndhātā was no other than myself. Do not think otherwise. Why? That king at that time, Ānanda, was indeed my own self. Through this teaching, Ānanda, you should know that the five objects of sensual desire are insatiable; attachment to desire, with its accumulation and storage, knows no satisfaction. What is meant by satisfaction? Only upon reaching the path of the noble ones does one truly find satisfaction."

At that time, the Bhagavān spoke these verses:

> Not through wealth and worldly deeds does one come to know the satiation of desire;
> Joy is little, suffering great — the wise do not pursue this.
> Should one find no love or delight within the five sensual pleasures,
> When craving is exhausted, joy is attained — such is a disciple of the Three Buddhas.
> Feeding on desire for koṭi upon koṭi years, in the end one enters hell;
> Where does the original longing for peace arrive? Life is cut short by suffering.
> All phenomena are entirely impermanent; all that is born must decay and perish;
> Birth after birth, all returns to cessation — that extinction is the supreme joy.

At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, having heard what the Buddha had taught, rejoiced and undertook to practice it.
