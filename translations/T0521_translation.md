# 佛說梵摩難國王經
## Sūtra of King Brahmadatta, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0521

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time, there was a king named Brahmadatta who constantly made offerings to the Buddha and the saṅgha. Whenever a purificatory day arrived, the king would prepare in advance by commanding his ministers to ready the carriages. The king would then lead a retinue of several thousand to the Buddha's dwelling, prostrating with all five limbs and touching his forehead to the Buddha's feet. Whenever the Buddha taught the sūtra Dharma for the devas and the people, the king would listen with joy and reverence. Within the king's palace, the Three Honored Ones were also worshipped, and the pure purificatory precepts were observed.

The king had a crown prince named Jūnlínrú, who was single-minded and diligent. He perceived that the world is impermanent: there is no birth without death. He did not covet worldly glory. He addressed the king: "A Buddha is rare to encounter in the world, and the sūtra Dharma is rare to hear. I now beg permission to follow the Buddha and become a śramaṇa." The king granted his request.

Jūnlínrú then took leave of the king and went to the Buddha's dwelling, requesting to become a bhikṣu. The Buddha then rubbed his head with his hand. The hair fell away and a kāṣāya robe appeared naturally upon his body. Jūnlínrú upheld the weighty precepts, practicing with diligence day and night without weariness. Within three months he attained the fruit of arhatship.

The king at that time did not know that his son had already attained the path. Seeing him laboring with austerities, eating rough and simple food, the king would visit to make offerings. Treating him differently from the other monks, with a partial mind, the king would say: "In my country there are precious treasures of the seven kinds, and fine, sweet foods of every kind, all without lack. Why do you take pleasure in being a śramaṇa?"

The Buddha, knowing that the king acted out of love and attachment, said to Jūnlínrú: "Rise and display your supernatural powers."

Jūnlínrú immediately received the Buddha's instruction. He touched his head to the ground, made obeisance to the Buddha, and then rose lightly and hovered in the sky, flying and transforming, dividing his body and reassembling it, appearing and vanishing without obstruction. When he had finished, he touched his forehead to the Buddha's feet. The king, seeing his son's virtue and attainment, was overcome with a mixture of grief and joy. He prostrated with all five limbs and made obeisance to Jūnlínrú.

The Buddha then had Jūnlínrú teach the king the essential points of the Four Noble Truths: suffering, emptiness, and impermanence. The king's mind was thereupon opened, and he immediately attained the fruit of stream-entry.

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "There are four circumstances under which a bhikṣu may accept offerings of food, fine flavors, and clothing from others, whether good or ill, and must not refuse: first, to bring merit to the household that gives; second, to avoid going against the intention of the giver; third, because one is elderly or the body is afflicted with illness; fourth, out of concern that others on the path will suffer hardship. One who wishes to eat fine food should keep the weighty precepts in mind. All sentient beings are my kin, but through the turning of the distant past, each has gone through birth and death and no longer recognizes the original connection. It is like a person whose body has sores or illness: they take medicine with the sole aim of recovery, not out of attachment. Those who wish to give should give with an impartial mind, without regard to great or small."

The Buddha then had Ānanda, at the time of the meal, announce the saṃgha-bhadra. The saṃgha-bhadra means that all the saṅgha's food should be shared equally.
