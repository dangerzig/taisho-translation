# 勝軍化世百喻伽他經
## Sūtra of Gāthās of One Hundred Parables for Transforming the World by Jayasena

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1692

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Devaśāntika (天息災).

---

> The sages of the past such as Sāṃkhya and others
> Spoke of all manner of canonical chapters and verses.
> Now I myself compose verses to delight foolish minds,
> Briefly chanting gāthās as one hundred parables.

> Practice kindness, practice righteousness, practice virtuous conduct;
> Be without self, without pride, without timidity.
> A teacher of true compassion worthy of reverence
> Can serve as a superior person and practice the path of liberation.

> Though poor and lowly, one maintains a resolute will;
> Though one's person is wealthy and noble, one is also gentle.
> When facing a powerful enemy, one has courage and strength:
> This is what is called the mark of a great person.

> A youth who practices goodness is rare among people.
> One who gives joyfully to those who come seeking,
> And who feels ashamed to hear others' praise:
> Such people are also hard to find.

> If one wishes for a fine reputation, first seek the Dharma;
> With mind devoted to the Dharma, virtue arises of itself.
> One who firmly and closely upholds all the precepts:
> Such a person in the world is exceedingly rare.

> One naturally good by nature, whose speech is also good,
> Who thoroughly knows both good and evil people,
> And who conceals and covers the faults of others:
> Such a wise person is hard to find in the world.

> Fire's nature is warmth, inherently hot;
> The moon's nature is coolness, and this too is so.
> The kṣatriya clan has the highest renown:
> How can those of lower clans find this strange?

> When kin face danger or hardship, one must rescue them;
> When others face difficulty, one does likewise.
> Exerting all one's strength for others without partiality:
> To live thus is called right livelihood.

> Generosity, patience, and the power of wisdom,
> Taming the sense-faculties and speaking with goodness:
> These are the true adornments of a noble person.
> Adornment with gold and jewels is like carrying a heavy burden.

> In all the world there has never been a single thing
> Not broken and emptied by impermanence.
> Only the unconditioned virtue of tranquility
> Remains solidly throughout the kalpas in permanent abiding.

> How excellent, a body of fine form and proper bearing,
> Cultivating and revering the light of virtuous conduct!
> Like the bright moon in the middle of the sky,
> Its pure and clear radiance illumines the pavilions.

> The wealthy and noble who practice generosity toward all,
> Whose discerning minds accomplish boundless dharmas,
> Whose courage and strength protect the weak:
> How excellent! This virtue is truly good.

> The virtuous esteem virtue and pity the virtueless;
> The foolish despise virtue and cast it away.
> Wisdom is like the red sun sending forth blazing light;
> Foolishness is like starlight, its radiance eclipsed.

> A worthy person can guard against all the body's faults,
> Singularly cultivating and revering lofty virtuous conduct.
> Yet if one even slightly indulges the mind and commits a single fault,
> The accumulated cultivation of many virtues is entirely lost.

> Evil people distance themselves from the virtues of the precepts,
> Always wishing to draw close to unwholesome people,
> Like abandoning a cool and meritorious pond
> To enter turbid and impure water.

> One applies oil to the body to remove grime,
> Then removes the grime and must wash off the oil.
> Just as in doing a task one aims for success,
> Once success is achieved, one lets go of the task.

> The evil person is always evil, like a black snake;
> The evil person is wildly intoxicated, like a drunken elephant.
> Good people feel fear and their hearts ache with grief;
> Evil people, in their confusion, feel joy and delight.

> A great fire spanning the sky is hard to extinguish at once;
> A deep cliff without bottom cannot be fathomed.
> With careful wisdom, observe dangerous and evil matters;
> Practice faith in goodness deeply, without doubt or slander.

> Falling from a cliff or entering a fire is greatly perilous,
> Yet sometimes one survives and rises to walk again.
> But if a person falls into the evil destinies,
> From that deep spring of evil destinies there is no escape.

> Great flooding waves cannot wash it away;
> Great blazing fires cannot burn it;
> Violent, evil gangs of thieves cannot seize it:
> This is the most supreme wealth in the world.

> The lowest people rely on possessing wealth;
> Middling people have nothing to rely on.
> Middling people are slightly pleased upon seeing wealth;
> The lowest people, relying on wealth, think it supreme in the world.

> Of all kinds of clan, form, and virtue,
> Companions, kin, and friends:
> Each one, not knowing whence they came,
> Devotes themselves solely to greed and folly, fond of wealth and gain.

> The rich speak falsely and people take it as truth;
> The poor speak truly and are deemed to be wrong.
> Flattery, deception, and obsequiousness have no genuine conduct:
> Worthy and good people feel shame upon hearing this.

> To have wealth and high status but lack virtue
> Is likened to how the virtuous receive praise.
> To be poor and lowly yet possess complete virtuous conduct:
> The foolish and ignorant instead slander and defame them.

> Courage and virtuous conduct, having them as if not:
> This is the true discernment of a good person.
> To leave behind wealth and guard the Way amid poverty,
> While kin belittle the poor, is truly false.

> A butcher's wealth and honor are praised as real;
> A superior person without wealth is deemed lowly.
> Kin and friends follow worldly sentiments,
> Serving the butcher and tavern-keeper without regard for good or evil.

> All know the peril of sinking into the evil destinies,
> Receiving punishment amid a hundred kinds of suffering.
> Yet when beggars come and go hoping for relief,
> They entirely refuse to give, stubbornly going against feeling.

> When a beggar is refused, contrary to their feeling,
> Angry and bitter, they sigh with resentment.
> Such people are hard-hearted and stingy in speech:
> They would rather give up gain than give up their life.

> Such people are miserly, base, and heavily deluded;
> Of rescue and generosity they know nothing at all.
> Hoarded and stored wealth will eventually scatter and decay;
> If one practices giving, it endures forever.

> One person thus guards much wealth;
> Foolish delusion grows thicker without reflection.
> Enduring suffering, how can one know that stinginess is vain?
> Whether many or few guard possessions, the suffering is equal.

> Neither using, nor employing, nor giving to others,
> With no knowledge whatsoever of good and fine things:
> Gold and silver piled up, filling the house,
> How does it differ from a pit filled with filth?

> A poor person who practices giving has truly perfected generosity;
> Such a one is called the most supreme person.
> The wealthy who slightly give away a small portion of wealth
> Are like a trickle from a river: who does not understand this?

> If a person practices in accord with the Dharma and is never lacking,
> Delighting in giving as in delighting in a beautiful woman,
> And if one gives surplus wealth at intervals,
> The fruit one reaps, full or partial, is likewise so.

> A pure mind-field serves the Dharma-king;
> Youthful precepts and virtue are like the fragrance of flowers.
> A compassionate and gentle heart, like a maiden's,
> With pleasing adornment and the fragrance of great practice.

> Visiting sacred sites with reverence and practicing generosity,
> With diligence, much learning, and endurance of hardship:
> If one's conduct lacks precepts and discipline,
> All previously cultivated good deeds are thrown away.

> In this life, one's reputation is known and esteemed;
> In the next life, born in heaven, revered by all.
> Blessings and longevity extend far with constant happiness:
> All from upholding the precepts comes success.

> Always hearing of the extreme suffering of the three paths of hell,
> One constantly guards deportment with perfect precept-virtue.
> When life ends and this fleeting existence is relinquished,
> Yama's evil destinies hold no terror for me.

> In cities, villages, and forests,
> There are the deluded and the wise.
> Whether one knows the Dharma or not,
> If one seeks the Well-Gone One, one must uphold the precepts.

> Firmly upholding the prohibitions to make them pure,
> One must constantly draw near to good teachers.
> When one cultivates wholesome karma in accord with the Dharma to perfection,
> All meritorious qualities are gathered together.

> Upholding precepts and the Dharma brings peace and happiness;
> If the mind is deluded and foolish, there is damage and injury.
> Virtue and life are extinguished in an instant:
> Why would a wise person drink wine?

> By drinking wine one displays foolishness and inferiority;
> Ultimately it leads to wrong, with no good reputation.
> Suddenly falling to the ground, like impermanence;
> Stains and filth cover the body, becoming impure.

> Though kin share a joyful drink together,
> Once drunk, they quarrel and would take each other's lives.
> Such faults arise in a single instant:
> This poison of wine is declared worse than poison itself.

> Offenses are often caused by lustful desire;
> Even at the cost of life, it still drags at the heart.
> All feelings of desire are without any benefit:
> What use is foolishly pursuing women?

> If one delights in one's own wife seeking pleasure,
> Through constant loving attachment, that may be appropriate.
> But to vainly pursue the wives of others:
> One will reap isolation and a heart full of fear.

> Blood, flesh, sinew, marrow, and the covering of skin:
> Inside and out, it is entirely an impure body.
> Even one's own body and wife are not truly one's portion;
> How can one covet the women of others?

> If a person's resolve is pure and free of lust,
> Knowing this union to be like an illusion or dream,
> Then by staying far from women,
> One attains peace of mind, free from delusion and error.

> Women can indeed provide pleasure;
> Wealth and luxury are also so.
> Kin together give rise to love and attachment,
> But life will not last long before impermanence comes.

> The fool only increases craving and attachment;
> The wise reflect that all is empty.
> If one dwells delighting in the dust of desire,
> When will one escape and attain bodhi?

> In cultivation, do not shrink from diligent hardship;
> Afterward, one will obtain a body of peace and happiness.
> These are good words of true benefit;
> To practice them can be likened to wondrous medicine.

> With all matters and actions largely understood,
> Faults and dangers can all be known.
> If it is right to act, then one should act:
> How can good deeds have any concealment?

> If one plans for the road ahead,
> First remove the crooked and disordered, and think correctly.
> Then in the future there will certainly be no faults;
> Naturally one will be at peace, and suffering will not arise.

> If one cultivates wholesome karma and makes it grow,
> With a single-pointed, tranquil mind, free from frivolity,
> Then enemies, afflictions, and illness
> Will naturally be removed, and foolish delusion will end.

> Harsh speech, divisive talk, and a base mind:
> The fool indulges the will and acts on impulse.
> How can one know the peacock's beauty and dignity?
> Can it be likened to the clamor of wolves, dogs, crows, and magpies?

> Rebuke the foolish and deluded who lack right understanding;
> Praise diligence and the gates of precepts and generosity.
> I say that one who practices this practice
> Gathers merit and secures the body, which is most supreme.

> The sovereign sound of Dharma is like song and music;
> Without a mind of joy, what else is there to rely on?
> If you sentient beings turn your backs on this,
> Are you animals or are you human?

> Whether beneficial or not, all remains unawakened;
> Whether real or unreal, neither is known.
> Thus, blindly, without any discernment:
> Though possessing human form, one is the same as livestock.

> Not distinguishing the worthy and good from the foolish,
> How can one tell a jackal from a lion?
> Seeing no superior or inferior, treating all the same:
> The wise should not dwell with them even briefly.

> Not claiming to be a sage, not being foolish,
> Not engaging in divisive speech, not being arrogant,
> Knowing something on the difficult-to-know plane of principle:
> This is called the adornment of a brāhmaṇa.

> With single mind and careful attention, cultivate the true practice;
> Faults should at all times never be accepted or practiced.
> The arrogant evil person stirs up quarrels and disputes:
> Such qualities and characteristics are not mine.

> The deluded mind inwardly harbors confusion;
> Compassion and patience are entirely absent, and ferocity abounds.
> Taking such violent faults of arrogance
> And grasping them as one's own virtues surpassing others.

> The supreme path of renunciation is not given heartfelt esteem;
> Good friends are entirely without respectful closeness.
> Never having made offerings to the teacher's instruction,
> One only befriends quarrels and disputes: truly great foolishness!

> The round moon at the sky's edge will inevitably wane;
> The fragrant flowers at the mountain's foot will not long bloom.
> Human life is impermanent, no different from these:
> If one must quarrel over self and other, for what purpose?

> Women's basic nature is ultimately without substance,
> Obstructing the cultivation of wholesome karma's causes.
> The āmalaka fruit has its pit:
> These are the three kinds of faults in the world.

> Revering the Dharma-master and visiting sacred sites,
> With a mind of contentment, harboring compassion and wisdom:
> These five kinds of worldly matters,
> Though said to be hard, are also easy to do.

> If a person knows the Dharma and constantly practices good,
> And further seeks out good friends,
> They are like selecting true gold from sand:
> All sentient beings know to esteem them.

> Traveling with foolish and inferior companions, one is not free;
> Naturally, one has no virtue and no esteem.
> Even if one renounces the household life, one is surely violent and evil;
> Even though alive, one has no good reputation.

> Why not trust in friends?
> Why not understand the devas and humans?
> Why not practice skillful means?
> Why make difficult for oneself what is hard to learn?

> Where does a miser understand generosity?
> Where is there water in shifting sands?
> Where is there fragrance in filth?
> Where is there kindness and duty in an evil person?

> Where is there virtue in one who hates and loves partially?
> Where is there a good person among enemies?
> What happy person understands contentment?
> Whose lifespan endures for long?

> A wanton woman is flighty and without deep faith;
> A foolish person is dull and without discernment.
> Wealth and honor briefly flourish, but who keeps them long?
> The karmic cause is firmly determined, hard to destroy.

> A brāhmaṇa obtaining food is delighted;
> A peacock hearing thunder is delighted;
> A good person rescuing others is delighted;
> The deluded fool destroying things is delighted.

> The deluded fool loves to engage in quarrels and disputes,
> Like a poor person finding a treasure, the heart rejoices.
> A worthy person hearing good words
> Is like a bee catching the fragrance of flowers.

> For the virtuous person, virtue is their kin;
> For the faulty person, faults are their enemy.
> For the menial servant, lowliness is their suffering;
> For the contented person, contentment is their happiness.

> Why shrink from going door to door with alms-bowl in hand?
> Why refuse labor and toil in distant lands?
> In the end, do not cling to a view of self regarding the body;
> Always regulate the mind's conduct in gentle harmony.

> With a humble mind, without love and without self-and-other,
> Like a deer without a home, dwelling in the wild forest:
> Even if one were wealthy, noble, and honored,
> One should have no small matter to request of others.

> Abandoning deluded conditions and all manner of pleasures,
> Entirely without fetters, free and at ease,
> A livelihood like that of geese and ducks:
> Growing up in the pure water, moving within it.

> In royal cities and villages where people dwell,
> Of the eight virtues, hardly one or two remain:
> Compassion, modesty, purity, conscience, shame,
> Knowledge of Dharma, selflessness, and the power of happiness.

> Among connected mountains, gorges, crags, and caves,
> Eating fruit, wearing bark, subduing the five faculties:
> The tranquil wild forest is well suited for contentment.
> Why must one seek people in villages?

> Dwelling in the mountains, not seeing others' doorways,
> Free and unbound, happily practicing:
> The benefit gained by dwelling within that mind
> Is to subdue the faculties and consciousness and attain long life.

> I have now finished teaching and transforming you all.
> Join your palms, tame your minds, and listen with your hearts.
> The treasury of all dharmas is true peace and happiness;
> You should joyfully seek it with single-pointed mind.

> If you know the good retribution of a human body,
> And yet in an instant it cannot be obtained,
> If after obtaining it you are foolish and do not create merit,
> You will once again deceive yourself and sink into decline.

> A water drop on the ground does not long remain:
> This can be likened to human life's fragile fate.
> The three kinds of unobstructed practice, who can accomplish them?
> Only the wise are able to carry them out.

> If one, according to one's strength,
> With focused intent, knows even a small part of the three,
> It is like a wild pigeon looking at its own body, thinking:
> "The peacock's adornment does not surpass me."

> Impermanence, birth, and death: who among people loves them?
> Has wisdom ever contemplated the five faculties?
> This body, though it abides, will ultimately not last long;
> Such a one is said to have lived in vain in the world.

> Thus Yama is seen by all people;
> Among beings receiving suffering, how many escape?
> There is a place untouched by old age and death, peaceful and happy:
> Why can you not practice to go there?

> All impermanent things and feelings must surely be fixed;
> Yet devoted solely to clinging to life, people remain unknowing.
> The road ahead is uncertain, and the light and shadow are swift:
> Why sit dully, without any reflection?

> Father, mother, wife, children, friends, and the rest:
> Their union is illusory and fleeting, lasting but a moment.
> The true Dharma is the kin one can rely upon,
> Able to remove the suffering of impermanent birth-and-death.

> Much seeking truly produces afflictions;
> Guarding the body truly produces fears;
> Destruction truly produces grief and sorrow.
> What benefit does the wise person seek in these?

> If one does not cultivate the practice of true suchness,
> The cycle of birth-and-death, when will it cease?
> The wise constantly observe this world:
> All becomes illusion and transformation, the power of foolish delusion.

> Jesting words and musical performances are all unreal;
> Greedy desire and pursuit are like an itch or sore.
> This life-diminishing impermanence is like an illusion or dream:
> Why not devote body and mind to the Buddha-Dharma?

> Whatever wrongful deeds are committed as offenses
> All equally destroy one's body.
> Why is the mind of worldly people so darkened
> That they do not understand to reflect on how wrongdoing arises?

> The good and evil matters harbored in the mind:
> The world-protecting devas and humans all fully know.
> If the mind cannot reflect upon this,
> When will the ground of intention dissolve its offenses?

> Sitting or standing according to conditions, using provisions,
> The body can somewhat rely on them for a brief time.
> Use these supporting conditions to practice goodness and benefit;
> All other possessions are the afflictions of people.

> Though one gazes upon adorned palaces and the like,
> Just use coarse and plain bedding for sitting and reclining.
> One who knows contentment naturally has a joyful heart,
> Like gazing upon a plain woman who surpasses a heavenly maiden.

> Know that all conditioned wealth in this world
> Can be seized by water, fire, and thieves alike.
> If one thus wishes to seek blessings for the next world,
> Do not seek this impermanent wealth.

> In debate, craft, trade, farming, and the warrior's way,
> If one does not follow proper principles, do not pursue them.
> One should know these matters should be as they are:
> Apart from merit, nothing is naturally accomplished.

> If one can do good or do what is not good,
> One should know it is no other person:
> All is created by one's own karma.
> Through this, sentient beings obtain everything.

> Thus, for all karma that one creates,
> If one can ensure no further existence arises,
> Then birth, old age, illness, suffering, and impermanence:
> In their endless succession, where do they come from?
