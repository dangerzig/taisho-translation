# 佛說稻芉經
## Śālistamba Sūtra (Rice Seedling Sūtra), Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0709

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard. At one time the Buddha was dwelling in Rājagṛha on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, twelve hundred and fifty in number, as well as a great assembly of bodhisattva mahāsattvas.

At that time the Venerable Śāriputra went to the place where Maitreya was walking in meditation. Maitreya and Śāriputra sat together on a rock. Then the Venerable Śāriputra asked Maitreya: "Today the World-Honored One, having seen a rice seedling, made this statement: 'You bhikṣus, to see the twelve links of dependent arising is to see the Dharma; to see the Dharma is to see the Buddha.' After the World-Honored One made this statement, he remained silent. Maitreya, why did the World-Honored One teach this sūtra? And with what meaning did he say that seeing the twelve links of dependent arising is seeing the Dharma, and that seeing the Dharma is seeing the Buddha? What is the meaning of all this? What are the twelve links of dependent arising? How does seeing dependent arising constitute seeing the Dharma? How does seeing the Dharma constitute seeing the Buddha?"

At that time Maitreya said to Śāriputra: "The World-Honored One, the Buddha, has always taught: 'To see the twelve links of dependent arising is to see the Dharma; to see the Dharma is to see the Buddha.' The twelve links of dependent arising are: conditioned by ignorance, formations; conditioned by formations, consciousness; conditioned by consciousness, name-and-form; conditioned by name-and-form, the six sense bases; conditioned by the six sense bases, contact; conditioned by contact, feeling; conditioned by feeling, craving; conditioned by craving, grasping; conditioned by grasping, becoming; conditioned by becoming, birth; conditioned by birth, old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish. Thus what arises is merely the great mass of suffering. Therefore the Buddha has taught the twelve links of dependent arising. What is this dharma that enters the noble path and the fruit of nirvāṇa? The Tathāgata has briefly taught this as the Dharma. What is the Buddha? One who can awaken to all dharmas is called a Buddha. If with the wisdom eye one sees the true Dharma-body, one accomplishes the dharmas to be learned for bodhi. How does seeing the twelve links of dependent arising constitute seeing the Dharma, and seeing the Dharma constitute seeing the Buddha? The Buddha has taught this: the twelve links of dependent arising are in constant succession; they are unborn, seen as they truly are, without inversion, unborn, uncreated, unconditioned, without abiding, unconditioned, beyond the domain of mind, quiescent, without characteristics. Therefore seeing the twelve links of dependent arising is seeing the Dharma: in constant succession, unborn, seen as they truly are, without inversion, unborn, uncreated, unconditioned, without abiding, unconditioned, beyond the domain of mind, quiescent, without characteristics. Therefore seeing the twelve links of dependent arising is seeing the unsurpassed path, endowed with the Dharma-body."

The Venerable Śāriputra asked Maitreya: "What are the twelve links of dependent arising called?"

Maitreya answered: "Having causes and having conditions is called the dharma of dependent arising. This is the Buddha's brief teaching on the characteristics of dependent arising. Because of this cause, this result is produced. Whether the Tathāgata appears in the world or not, the dharma of arising from conditions is so. Whether the Tathāgata appears in the world or not, this dharma of arising from conditions is also so. Its nature and characteristics permanently abide, free from all afflictions, ultimately real and not unreal, being the true and real dharma, free from inverted dharmas. Furthermore, the dharma of the twelve links of dependent arising arises from two kinds. What are the two? First, from causes; second, from conditions. The dharma of arising from conditions again has two kinds: internal dependent arising and external dependent arising. From what does the dharma of external dependent arising arise? Just as a seed can produce a sprout, from the sprout comes a leaf, from the leaf a node, from the node a stem, from the stem an ear, from the ear a flower, and from the flower the fruit. Without a seed there is no sprout, and so on until without a flower there is no fruit. When there is a seed, a sprout arises, and so on until when there is a flower, fruit arises. Yet the seed does not think, 'I can produce a sprout,' and the sprout does not think, 'I arise from a seed.' So on until the flower does not think, 'I can produce fruit,' and the fruit does not think, 'I arise from the flower.' Yet in reality the seed is able to produce the sprout. This is called the dharma of external arising from causes. What is called the dharma of external arising from conditions? It is because of the conjunction of six elements. What are the six elements? Earth, water, fire, wind, space, and time. The earth element provides firm support, the water element provides moist nourishment, the fire element provides warmth, the wind element stirs growth, the space element does not create obstruction, and the time element brings about seasonal change. When these six conditions are complete, things grow. If the six conditions are not complete, things do not grow. When earth, water, fire, wind, space, and time are in harmonious balance without excess or deficiency, things are able to grow. Yet earth does not say, 'I am able to support,' water does not say, 'I am able to moisten,' fire does not say, 'I am able to ripen,' wind does not say, 'I am able to stir growth,' space does not say, 'I am able to be unobstructed,' and time does not say, 'I am able to bring about growth.' The seed does not say, 'I produce a sprout from the six conditions,' and the sprout does not say, 'I arise from the various conditions.' Although they do not so think, in reality from the conjunction of the various conditions the sprout arises. It does not arise from itself, nor from another, nor from both self and other together, nor from a creator god, nor from time and space, nor from a primal nature, nor without a cause. This is called the order of arising dharmas. Thus the dharma of external arising from conditions should be known through five aspects as neither annihilated nor permanent, neither passing from here to there, as producing many fruits from a small cause, as similar in continuity and not producing a different thing. How is it not annihilated? Because from the seed there is a sprout, from the sprout a root, from the root a stem, in continuous succession without interruption. How is it not permanent? Because the sprout, stem, flower, and fruit are each distinct and different. The seed does not perish and then the sprout arise, nor does the sprout arise without the seed perishing. Yet in the dharma of dependent arising, when the sprout appears the seed decays. Because of successive arising it is not permanent. Because the names and characteristics of seed and sprout are each different, they do not pass from here to there. Because a small seed produces abundant fruit, one should know it is not one. Because the seed does not produce a different kind of fruit, this is called similar continuity. Through these five kinds, the dharmas of external conditions arise.

"The dharma of internal dependent arising arises from two kinds. What are the two? From causes and from conditions. What is arising from causes? From ignorance there come formations, and so on until from birth there come old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish. Without ignorance, formations cannot be established, and so on until without old age, death, there is no grief, lamentation, suffering, or anguish. Yet ignorance does not think, 'I can produce formations,' and formations do not think, 'I arise from ignorance.' So on until birth does not think, 'I can produce old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish,' and old age and death do not think, 'I arise from birth.' This is called the characteristic of arising from causes.

"What is arising from conditions? It is because of the conjunction of the six elements. What are the six? Earth, water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness. What is called the earth element? That which is solid and firm in the body is called the earth element. What moistens is called the water element. What warms is called the fire element. The in-breath and out-breath is called the wind element. What is unobstructed in the body is called the space element. The four aggregates and the five sense-consciousnesses, also called name, also called consciousness, constitute the consciousness element. When these six elements in combination are complete without diminishment, the body is formed. If these conditions are diminished, the body is not formed. Earth does not think, 'I am able to be firm.' Water does not think, 'I am able to be moist.' Fire does not think, 'I am able to be warm.' Wind does not think, 'I am able to breathe in and out.' Space does not think, 'I am able to be unobstructed.' Consciousness does not think, 'I am able to grow.' The body does not think, 'I arise from these various conditions.' Yet without these conditions, the body is also not established. The earth element has no self, no person, no sentient being, no life-force; it is neither male nor female, and is neither this nor that. So too with water, fire, wind, space, and consciousness: they have no self, no sentient being, no life-force, and are neither male nor female, neither self nor other.

"What is called ignorance? Within the six elements, generating a perception of unity, an aggregate perception, a perception of permanence, a perception of solidity, a perception of durability, a perception of pleasure, a perception of self, a perception of person, a perception of sentient being, a perception of life-force, a perception of moving creatures: due to lack of wisdom, generating such various perceptions is called ignorance. Because of ignorance, greed, anger, and delusion arise. Conditioned by ignorance, formations arise; formations likewise attach to conventional names, generating various deluded thoughts, which are called consciousness. Consciousness gives rise to name-and-form. Name-and-form gives rise to the six sense bases. The six sense bases give rise to contact. Contact gives rise to feeling. Feeling gives rise to craving. Craving gives rise to grasping. Grasping gives rise to becoming. Becoming is able to produce a future aggregation, which is called birth. After birth, deterioration is called old age. The destruction of the aggregates is called death. Ignorance and delusion produce grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish. The five senses encountering harm is called bodily suffering. The mind being ill at ease is called mental suffering. Thus the mass of suffering gathers, constantly dwelling in darkness, and this is called ignorance. Creating and accumulating karma is called formations. Discriminating dharmas is called consciousness. Establishing characteristics is called name-and-form. The opening of the six sense gates is called the six sense bases. Encountering objects is called contact. Experiencing suffering and pleasure is called feeling. Like thirst seeking drink, this is called craving. Being able to seize is called grasping. Creating further karma is called becoming. The arising of future aggregates is called birth. The ripening of the aggregates is called old age. Their destruction is called death. Brooding over and lamenting the past is called grief. Painful crying is called lamentation. Suffering is called suffering. Weariness and disturbance is called anguish. Mistaking truth for falsehood, and wrong views for right views: because of this lack of wisdom, it is called ignorance. Formations are of three kinds: meritorious formations, demeritorious formations, and imperturbable formations. By performing meritorious formations one obtains the wisdom of meritorious formations; by performing demeritorious formations one obtains the wisdom of demeritorious formations; by performing imperturbable formations one obtains the wisdom of imperturbable formations. So on down to old age, death, grief, lamentation, suffering, and anguish. These twelve links of dependent arising each have their causes and results. They are not permanent, not annihilated, not conditioned yet not apart from the conditioned, not subject to exhaustion, not subject to the cessation of desire, not subject to destruction. They exist inherently, their arising never interrupted, like a river flowing swiftly with no break in its current."

At that time the Bodhisattva Maitreya further said to the Venerable Śāriputra: "The twelve links of dependent arising are further increased by four conditions, namely: ignorance, craving, karma, and consciousness. Consciousness as a seed has its own nature as the cause; karma serves as the field; ignorance and craving, the afflictions, serve as the covering. The consciousness seed then sprouts. Karma provides the field for consciousness; craving provides the moisture for consciousness; ignorance provides the covering. Thus consciousness is accomplished. Yet karma does not think, 'I serve as the field for the consciousness seed.' Craving does not think, 'I serve as the moisture for the consciousness seed.' Ignorance does not think, 'I serve as the covering for the consciousness seed.' Therefore the consciousness seed is accomplished. Consciousness does not think, 'I arise from these various conditions.' Furthermore, karma serves as the field for consciousness, craving as moisture, and ignorance as covering. Thus the seed grows and becomes the sprout of name-and-form within the mother's womb. That sprout of name-and-form is not self-produced, not produced by another, not produced by both self and other together, not created by a creator god, not transformed by time, not produced by conditions, not produced by a single thing, and not produced without a cause. This dharma truly arises from the conjunction of parents and the various conditions. Yet the sprout of name-and-form fundamentally has no owner, no grasping and no releasing; its own nature and conditions are like empty space and magical illusion.

"Furthermore, eye-consciousness arises from five kinds of conditions. What are the five? The eye, form, light, space, and attention. From these five conditions eye-consciousness arises. The eye serves as the basis, form as the object, light provides illumination, space provides non-obstruction, and attention initiates activity. From these conditions eye-consciousness arises. If the eye, form, light, space, and attention are not in conjunction, eye-consciousness does not arise. Yet the eye does not think, 'I serve as the basis for eye-consciousness.' Form does not think, 'I serve as the object for consciousness.' Light does not think, 'I enable consciousness to illuminate.' Space does not think, 'I enable consciousness to be unobstructed.' Attention does not think, 'I initiate eye-consciousness.' Consciousness does not think, 'I arise from the various conditions.' Yet eye-consciousness truly arises from the conjunction of the various conditions. So too the successive arising of consciousness from the other sense faculties should be explained in the same way.

"Furthermore, no dharma passes from this world to the next world. It is only through the adornment of karmic results, causes, conditions, and deluded thought that things arise. Again, Śāriputra, it is like a clear mirror reflecting a face: the face appears in the mirror, yet in reality no face enters the mirror; it is through deluded thought and conditions that it appears. Again, Śāriputra, it is like the full moon high in the sky, forty-two thousand yojanas from the earth, yet its reflection appears in all the waters below. The moon does not descend there and arise in this water; it also appears through deluded thought and conditions. Again, it is like obtaining fire: given fuel it burns, and when the fuel is exhausted it is extinguished."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, no sentient being passes from this world to the next world, nor from the next world back to this world. It is only through the conjunction of karmic bonds that the consciousness seed everywhere obtains birth. Lodging in the mother's womb, the sprout of name-and-form arises. This dharma of causes and conditions fundamentally has no owner, no self, no grasping, no releasing; it is like empty space, like magical illusion, without any real dharma. Yet the karmic retribution of good and evil does not perish.

"Furthermore, the twelve links of dependent arising are also explained through five aspects. What are the five? Impermanence, non-annihilation, non-destruction, small cause producing great result, and resemblance. How is it impermanent? Because when one aggregate ceases another aggregate arises; cessation is not arising, and arising is not cessation; because arising and cessation are different, it is called impermanent. How is it not annihilated? It is like the rising and falling of a scale: when this ceases, that arises, and so it is called non-annihilation. How is it not destroyed? Because the causal karma performed by sentient beings is never destroyed. How does a small cause produce great result? Because whatever cause is created is like tending a field; through single-minded diligent effort, the fruit obtained is abundant. How is it resemblance? Because the causal karma one creates does not yield a different result; therefore it is called resemblance.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, as the Buddha has said: 'If someone can contemplate the twelve links of dependent arising, this is called right contemplation and right wisdom.' What is right contemplation and right wisdom? It is contemplating the arising in the three times of past, future, and present without generating thoughts of existence or non-existence, without coming from anywhere or going to any place. If śramaṇas, brahmins, and worldly people can contemplate this dharma as unborn, unceasing, uncreated, unconditioned, without grasping, without releasing, not inverted, quiescent, and at rest, without inherent nature, and if they can see the Dharma in this way, tranquil and fully understanding, without illness, without wound, in the space of a blink of an eye, the view of self is immediately eliminated, like cutting the crown of a tāla tree so that it cannot grow again. This is to attain the dharma of non-arising and non-ceasing. Śāriputra! If there are sentient beings who can endure this dharma, then the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Samyaksaṃbuddha, endowed with perfect knowledge and conduct, the Sugata, the knower of the world, the unsurpassed one, the tamer of persons, the teacher of devas and humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One, will certainly bestow upon them the prediction of anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi."

When the Bodhisattva Maitreya had finished speaking this dharma, the Venerable Śāriputra, along with the devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, and the entire great assembly, joyfully received the teaching, bowed in reverence, and departed.
