# 盧至長者因緣經
## Sūtra on the Causes and Conditions of the Elder Lūzhì

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0539

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

"Those who cling to miserly greed are despised by humans and devas. Therefore the wise should practice generosity. Why is this? I once heard that there was a great elder named Lūzhì whose household was enormously wealthy, with immeasurable property and overflowing granaries, like Vaiśravaṇa. Because in past lives he had cultivated the cause of giving toward supreme fields of merit, he obtained this reward. Yet when he gave, he could not do so wholeheartedly. For this reason, although he was wealthy, his mind was always base. The clothes he wore were greasy and filthy. What he ate was mixed grains, barnyard millet, weed sprouts, pigweed, bean leaves, grasses, and vegetables to fill his hunger. He drank sour water and plain water to quench his thirst. He rode a decayed old cart with woven grass and leaves for its canopy. Regarding his own wealth, he felt nothing but miserly attachment, exhausting his spirit and taxing his thoughts, diligently guarding and protecting it, toiling in weariness like a servant, the laughingstock of all."

At that time, Rāhula spoke this verse:

> "The causes of giving differ;
> The fruits received are each distinct.
> When faith and generosity are deep and sincere,
> The reward fulfills the heart's desire.
> But if one gives without earnest devotion,
> The gift brings no pure reward.
> Though Lūzhì was enormously rich,
> He was despised and laughed at by all."

"Once during a city festival, buildings were decorated and adorned with painted colors. Silk pennants and canopies were hung, with lapis lazuli ornaments displayed everywhere. Flower garlands were suspended, fragrant water sprinkled on the ground, and beautiful flowers arranged. Windows and doorways were adorned with flowers. Each household had various musical entertainments, singing, dancing, and amusements, enjoying happiness like the palaces of the devas. At every gate, golden vases were filled with fragrant water. In every lane, silk pennants and canopies were hung, various flowers scattered, and fragrant water sprinkled on the ground.

"At that time, Lūzhì saw all the people gathering in various assemblies, dancing and rejoicing, and he thought: 'Servants, beggars, and lowly people all borrow fine clothes and eat delicious food. I myself have ample clothes, jewelry, and treasures. Why should I not enjoy myself?' He rushed home, took his own keys, opened the treasury door, took out five coins, locked the door again, and thought: 'If I eat at home, I cannot serve my mother, wife, and relatives all at once. If I go to another's house, perhaps the host or beggars will come asking me for something.' So he used two coins to buy parched grain, two coins for wine, and one coin for scallions. From his own house he wrapped some salt in the fold of his garment and carried it out of the city toward a tree. Arriving at the tree, he saw many crows; if he stopped there, the crows would come snatching at his food. So he went to the charnel ground, but seeing dogs, he fled again to an empty, quiet place. He put salt in the wine, mixed it with the parched grain, and ate the scallions. Having never drunk wine before, he immediately became very drunk. Being quite drunk, he declared: 'The whole nation is making great merry today. Why should I alone not be merry?' He then rose to dance and sang loudly. The words of his song were:

> "'Even if Śakra himself
> Were merrymaking today,
> He could not surpass me,
> Let alone Vaiśravaṇa!'

"He further said: 'Today at my festival celebration, drinking freely in great merriment, I surpass Vaiśravaṇa and even excel the Lord of the Devas!'

"Śakra, Lord of the Devas, was traveling with countless heavenly hosts to the Jeta Grove. Along the way, he saw Lūzhì drunk and dancing by the roadside, singing that he surpassed Śakra. Śakra thought silently: 'This miserly man drinks in secret and insults me.' He thought further: 'I shall not go to the Buddha's abode yet, but first torment that one.'

"Śakra then transformed his own body to look exactly like Lūzhì and went straight to Lūzhì's house. He gathered together the parents, servants, and relatives, sat before the mother, and said: 'Hear my loving words! All this time, a great miser-demon has been following me, which is why I refused to eat, refused to give money, treasures, or goods to my parents and relatives. It was all because of the miser-demon. Today when I went out, I met a practitioner who gave me a fine spell that has dispelled the miser-demon. If that miser-demon should come again, he can never again trouble me. However, the miser-demon looks just like me. If he should come, the gatekeepers must beat him soundly with sticks. He will surely claim to be Lūzhì, but no one in the household should believe his words.' He then opened wide the treasury, brought out all manner of wealth, prepared fine food, and fed the mother, wife, and relatives until all were satisfied. When the meal was finished, he told the gatekeepers: 'Quickly shut the gates! If the miser-demon should come, wait until I have distributed jewels and given everyone clothing and made music; only then open the gates.'

"He then opened wide the treasury. The finest jewels he first gave to the mother, next to the wife, and to all the men and women in the household. To outside guests as well he gave jewels, clothing, and food, and provided various music. All the household members anointed their bodies with fine fragrances and burned dark aloeswood incense. At that time Śakra, holding the mother with one hand and the wife with the other, danced joyfully, sporting and amusing himself in ways beyond description.

"All the people of Śrāvastī heard that the elder Lūzhì's miser-demon had been exorcised, and everyone gathered to come see. When Lūzhì sobered up and returned to the city, he went back to his own house and saw crowds of people jamming the gate. He also heard singing and dancing from inside, and was greatly alarmed. He thought: 'Could the king be angry with me and have come with ministers and a great army to execute me? Or have the people of Śrāvastī gathered for the festival and all come into my house? Or have the devas come to increase my fortune and arrived at my house to make this music? Or have my household members broken open my treasury and are feasting?' Having considered all this, he rushed to the gate and shouted loudly, calling for his household members. But amidst the clamor of music, no one heard him.

"Śakra heard the shouting and said to everyone: 'Who is banging on the gate? Stop the music for now; perhaps the miser-demon has returned.' When people heard there was a demon, they flung open the gate and everyone ran to hide. Lūzhì rushed inside and saw Śakra surrounded by his relatives, seated in the place of honor with the mother on his right and wife on his left, all wearing fine clothes and beautiful jewels, with drums and strings playing and songs sung, drinking and celebrating, their faces radiant and cheerful as they sat in rows. Lūzhì was stunned and demanded: 'Who are you? Coming into my house and behaving so indulgently!' Śakra smiled faintly and said: 'Today the household members know me well enough.'

"The household members asked Lūzhì: 'Who are you?' Lūzhì answered: 'I am Lūzhì!' The entire household pointed at Śakra and said in unison: 'This is Lūzhì, our master.' Lūzhì asked them again: 'Then who am I?' The household answered: 'Though you look like him, you are the Lūzhì-demon.' Lūzhì said: 'I am not a demon! I am Lūzhì. You should all look carefully.' He turned to his mother and said: 'Mother is my mother, brother is my brother, younger brother is my younger brother, wife is my honored wife, son is my beloved son, and all the servants are mine.' He pointed at Śakra and told the household: 'This is an outsider whose appearance resembles mine, who has taken my form by illusion. Since childhood I have built up this enterprise and amassed these treasures in my storehouses. Who has bewitched you to scatter my wealth?'

"But no one in the household believed him. Śakra asked the mother: 'Do the two of us look extremely alike?' The mother answered: 'That demon's appearance is very similar to yours.' The mother then said to Śakra: 'Seeing your filial devotion in serving me, I truly know you are my own son. That one is truly a demon. If both of you were equally filial to me, I could not tell you apart. But because you are filial and that one is disobedient, I am certain you are my son.' She turned to the wife and said: 'That is your husband. Why do you not seize hold of him?' The wife blushed with shame and said: 'How awful! Why does he not simply vanish? I would never be wife to such a thing.' She said to Śakra: 'My lord! I would rather die at your side than live with that demon.'

"Śakra said to the household: 'If you truly know me to be Lūzhì, why did you let that demon in before?' Hearing this, they immediately dragged Lūzhì by the feet, beating him with sticks and driving him out the gate. In the lane, he raised his voice and wept loudly: 'How strange! Has my face and form somehow changed, that my own family rejects me like this?' He asked the passersby: 'Is my body the same as before? Is my face the same as before? Are my speech and gait, height and appearance different or the same?' They said: 'You are the same as before, no different from what you were.'

"He said again: 'Who am I now? Have I been transformed into someone else? What is my name? Where am I?' He sighed deeply: 'How strange, how very strange! What can I possibly say?' At that time, Lūzhì was like a madman. Other relatives who were not members of his household all came to console him: 'Do not be afraid! You are Lūzhì. You are now in the marketplace of Śrāvastī. We are your kin and have come to look after you. Take heart, and think of a plan to prove the truth.'

"When Lūzhì heard these words, his mind was somewhat calmed. Wiping his tears, he asked the others again: 'Am I really Lūzhì?' They answered: 'You are truly Lūzhì.' Lūzhì said to the crowd: 'Can you all bear witness for me?' Everyone said: 'All of us will bear witness that you are truly Lūzhì.'

"Lūzhì replied: 'If so, then hear me explain the full circumstances:

> "'Who is this young man
> Who looks exactly like me,
> Sitting knee to knee on the same bed
> With my beloved wife?
> My own dear family
> Beat me and drove me out,
> They all love that one instead,
> Keeping him settled in my house.
> I endured hunger and cold
> To amass my wealth,
> And now he uses it freely
> While I have not a hair's share,
> Living like Vaiśravaṇa,
> Indulging in clothing and food.
> All the people in the city
> Are suspicious and amazed,
> Each saying: "What is to be done about this?"
> Among them, those of clear wisdom
> Speak thus:
> "This cunning impostor,
> Whose appearance resembles Lūzhì,
> Knows of his great miserliness
> And has come to torment him.
> Let us bear witness together and rescue him;
> We should not simply abandon him."'

"At that time the people, hearing these words, were all of one mind and said: 'Lūzhì! What will you do now? What do you wish?' Lūzhì said: 'Gather together on my behalf. Tomorrow we shall go before the king.' Everyone agreed: 'Tomorrow we will escort you before the king.'

"The next day, the people said: 'Excellent! Now is the time.' Lūzhì said: 'This is a great matter. I have no access to my own wealth. If you can lend me some money, I shall repay you when I recover my property.' Everyone said: 'Whatever you need, we shall provide.' They asked: 'What do you require?' The elder Lūzhì said: 'Give me two pieces of cotton cloth, worth four zhu of gold, to present to the king.' Everyone laughed, thinking: 'Lūzhì has never done anything like this before. To speak of four zhu is, for him, a great act of giving.'

"Lūzhì then tucked the two pieces of cloth under his arm and went to the king's gate. He told the gatekeeper: 'I have come to make an offering.' The gatekeeper was extremely astonished and laughed: 'In thirty years, I have never heard of this man coming to the gate to make an offering. How can he suddenly do this today?' The gatekeeper went in to report to the king and said with palms joined: 'Something unprecedented! Lūzhì is now at the gate and wishes to make an offering.'

"The king, being deliberate in judgment, neither hastily angered nor hastily pleased, thought to himself: 'Could it be that because of the festival, various people have come to the gate? Lūzhì is so miserly that he should not have come to my gate either, and the gatekeeper should not be playing a joke on me. What does this mean? I cannot believe it. A king is like the great ocean that does not reject small streams. How can I judge the quantity of his offering?' The king then admitted him. The king thought: 'This Lūzhì is miserly by nature. Could he be at death's door, to suddenly act like this?'

"Lūzhì then went with the crowd before the king. When he tried to take out the two pieces of cloth to present to the king, his hand pulled at the cloth, but his armpit clamped down tightly and he could not pull it free. He turned and twisted, pulling with all his might, and finally got it out. But the moment he got it out, Śakra transformed it into two bundles of grass. Looking down and seeing the grass, Lūzhì was overcome with shame and sat on the ground. The king, seeing this, felt compassion and said: 'Even if it were bundles of grass, it would be no matter. Whatever you wish to say, speak freely.' Lūzhì, choking with grief, sobbed: 'Seeing this grass, my shame is so great that I cannot sink into the ground to hide. I do not know whether I have a body or not. What can I possibly say?'

"The king, hearing his words, was especially moved to pity and asked the bystanders: 'He is so choked with grief that he cannot speak. If you know what he means, speak on his behalf.' The bystanders told the king: 'The reason Lūzhì has come to petition the king is that some unknown person of identical appearance went to his home, falsely claimed to be Lūzhì, caused the household to become attached to him, scattered and used up all the wealth, and made the household unable to tell them apart. They drove Lūzhì out and treated him like a stranger. This is why his heart is anguished and he cannot get the words out.'

"The king said: 'If this is so, his suffering is justified. Why? His own wealth is being used by another. Nevertheless, I shall adjudicate the matter and see that his household and property are restored.' The king said further: 'Although people in this world may look alike in form, their minds may not be the same. And though their minds may be similar, their bodies in private places may have secret marks known only to themselves, and there must be some small difference. Do not worry. I shall investigate thoroughly on your behalf.'

"At that time, a minister named Sùjiù rose, joined his palms, and said to the king: 'Excellent, great king! The king's wisdom and compassion in redressing wrongs are exactly as they should be.'

"The minister Sùjiù then spoke this verse:

> "'For those in sorrow, fear, and dread,
> The king provides rescue and protection.
> For the impoverished and afflicted,
> The king serves as a friend.
> For the upright who cultivate virtue,
> The king is their dharma-companion.
> For those of evil conduct,
> The king is the elephant-goad.'

"Lūzhì then prostrated himself fully and said to the king: 'The places where my household secretly stores its treasures, that one can never know. My body has secret marks he cannot possibly know. I beg the great king to investigate on my behalf!'

"The king then sent a messenger to summon the person resembling Lūzhì, commanding him to come at once. He was summoned and came, arriving before the king and standing to one side. The king observed the two men and could not distinguish between them. He examined them carefully and was filled with wonder: 'Their ages, appearances, builds, faces, speech, laughter, and complexions are all identical, as if made by magical transformation, equal without any difference. Now these two men stand before me and I cannot tell them apart. This fills me with astonishment and perplexity.'

"The king asked the one who had been summoned: 'Who are you?' He sighed mournfully and said: 'I have lived in vain. I might as well be dead. How is it that I grew up in the king's domain and the king does not recognize me? He asks me: "What is your name?"'

"The king was slightly embarrassed, thinking: 'This is truly Lūzhì.' He said to the first: 'What more do you wish to argue?' Lūzhì answered: 'I am Lūzhì. That one is not.'

"The king said: 'You two are like reflections in a mirror, identical in complexion and appearance. How can I tell you apart?' Lūzhì said: 'It is for precisely this reason that I first came to the king. When a person is ill, suffering, in danger, or afraid, everyone turns to the king.' The king said: 'Indeed, it is for this that I receive the people's taxes.'

"The king considered briefly and said to Śakra: 'I wish to ask you something. Lūzhì is by nature miserly, yet you are generous. Your natures differ. How can you claim to be Lūzhì?' Śakra answered: 'The king is right to question so precisely. What the king says is true. However, I personally received the Buddha's teaching that those who are miserly fall into the realm of hungry ghosts, where for hundreds of thousands of millions of years they suffer hunger and thirst, seeking pus, blood, filth, and excrement but unable to obtain even a hair's worth; cool rivers and springs turn to flowing fire before them. Hearing that miserliness has such dire consequences, I grew frightened and wished to abandon this evil. For this reason, I immediately gave up my miserliness and a mind of generosity arose.'

"The king said: 'That makes sense. Just as a soiled garment is cleaned when washed with lye, the defilements of the mind are removed when one hears the Dharma.' The king said to his ministers: 'How can we determine which of these two is Lūzhì and which is not?' Sùjiù answered: 'Question them about the secret matters within the household. If there are any differences, we can then know.' The king said: 'I have many duties and cannot question them in such detail. As you suggest, let it be done.' He separated the two men, placing each in a different room, and asked them: 'Now write down the names, ages, and numbers of all your inner and outer relatives; all the buildings, gates, and property in your household; everything in your storerooms; everything above ground and below ground, every kind of thing. Each of you record it clearly in a document and bring it promptly.' The two men each brought their documents. Everything, including all the secret matters and even the handwriting, was identical in every way.

"The king, seeing this, was filled with wonder: 'Even exhausting all my wits and considering every possibility, I cannot distinguish them. This is not the work of a human; it must be the work of a non-human being.' The king said: 'Bring both men back before me.'

"After studying them for a long time, the king told a servant: 'Summon the mother.' She was summoned. Arriving before the king, she bowed respectfully. The king joined his palms and said: 'I too respect the elderly.' The old mother said: 'May the king live ten thousand years, be free from all enmity and harm, and never tire of cultivating merit!' The king ordered a seat prepared and bade the old mother sit. The king said: 'Of these two men, which is your son and which is not?' Śakra whispered to the mother: 'Do not make me suffer again as before.' The mother said: 'My son, do not worry.'

"The old mother said respectfully to the king: 'This child is filial and devoted, serving me with every kind of provision. This is my son. That one is neither respectful nor filial, and has always shown me no affection. I know he is not my son. Although I can tell good from bad between them, their voices and speech are alike, and I truly cannot distinguish them otherwise.'

"The king asked further: 'I wish to ask one more thing. When you raised this child, from when he was small and when you bathed him, did you ever notice any birthmarks, scars, or moles in hidden places on his body that you remember?' The mother said: 'Yes.' Śakra thought: 'Whatever the old mother says, I shall make the same.' He then listened carefully to the mother's words.

"The mother told the king: 'My son has a scar the size of a small bean under his left ribcage.' Śakra thought: 'Even if there were a scar the size of Mount Sumeru, I could make one, let alone a small scar.' He immediately created one by transformation.

"The king thought: 'Now I shall settle this with certainty.' He said: 'Each of you bare your left side and raise your arm high.' When they raised their arms, the two scars were identical. The king and all his ministers burst into loud laughter and said: 'Such a thing has never been heard or seen! It makes people laugh, makes people frightened, makes people bewildered. This is truly strange and deeply alarming.'

"The king told his ministers: 'This matter is beyond my understanding. We should bring these two men to the Jeta Grove and go before the Buddha, where it will surely be resolved. Let us set aside our celebrations here.'

"The king then spoke this verse:

> "'The sun of the Buddha rose long ago,
> Able to rescue the world,
> Liberating all from faults and evils,
> Drying up the ocean of craving.
> His face is like the full moon,
> His divine powers the all-seeing eye.
> The three realms all revere and serve him;
> He is sovereign among all.
> The Greatly Compassionate One can surely
> Dispel the doubts of all of us.
> Let everyone join in praise;
> This is truly excellent!'

"Having spoken this verse, the king and his ministers each adorned themselves with heavenly crowns and fine garments, jeweled necklaces, and ornaments. Holding incense and flowers, they followed behind the king. The two Lūzhìs were placed atop two elephants, elaborately adorned.

"The king himself rode in a plumed canopied chariot, with music of a hundred thousand kinds following behind, and proceeded to the Jeta Grove. He set aside the five royal insignia: crown, jeweled canopy, sword, leather sandals, and maṇi pearl. Composing himself, he went before the Buddha.

"At that time, the World-Honored One was surrounded by devas, nāgas, the eight classes of beings, and the fourfold assembly. The king and the great assembly prostrated fully, paying homage to the Buddha. Rising with palms joined, he addressed the Buddha: 'I and all beings in the three realms are shrouded in ignorance and cannot distinguish truth from falsehood. Only the Buddha's mind is pure. All sentient beings are set ablaze by hundreds of thousands of afflictions; only the World-Honored One is tranquil and has extinguished them. All worldly beings are bound by birth and death; only the Buddha alone has attained liberation and serves as the true friend of all sentient beings. For all who are blind and in darkness, the Buddha is their eyes. We have tried by every means but cannot distinguish which of these two men is Lūzhì and which is not.' The two Lūzhìs were placed before the Buddha. All the people sat in silence.

"The transformed Lūzhì sat with a radiant, cheerful expression, elaborately adorned with jewels, in silent composure. The real Lūzhì was haggard, wearing greasy, filthy clothes and covered with dust, in extreme anguish. He said: 'The World-Honored One's great compassion rescues all. I beg you to rescue me!'

"At that time, Śakra, seeing Lūzhì's distress, smiled faintly. King Prasenajit rose from his seat, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: 'In this matter, the Buddha alone can bear witness to the truth. All sentient beings are darkened by afflictions; only the World-Honored One holds the torch of wisdom, guiding sentient beings upon the path of liberation, like a great physician-king, like a guide. He bestows fearlessness upon all sentient beings and the wealth of wholesome roots. He crushes the bonds of defilement and is therefore called the Great Sage. Excellent, World-Honored One! May the fire of your wisdom burn away the dense thicket of my afflictions and doubts. We beg the World-Honored One to resolve our doubt: of these two men, who is real and who is not?'

"At that time, the World-Honored One raised his arm adorned with the marks of excellence and his beautifully adorned hand, and said to Śakra: 'What are you doing?'

"Śakra immediately dissolved the form of Lūzhì and returned to his original appearance, with radiant light of various kinds, adorned with a wish-fulfilling jewel and necklaces. He joined his palms toward the Buddha and spoke this verse:

> "'Always enslaved by miserliness,
> Refusing to clothe or feed himself,
> With five coins' worth of wine and parched grain
> He added salt and drank.
> Once drunk he grew boisterous,
> Dancing, laughing, and singing,
> Insulting us devas.
> For this reason,
> I deliberately tormented him.'"

The Buddha said to Śakra: "All sentient beings have faults and offenses. You should release and forgive him."

At that time, Lūzhì said to Śakra: "I painfully accumulated all my wealth through hardship. Did you not spend my property?"

Śakra said: "I did not diminish your wealth by even a hair."

The Buddha said to Lūzhì: "Go home and look at your property."

Lūzhì said: "All my wealth has already been used up. What use is going home?"

Śakra said: "I truly did not diminish your wealth by even a hair's breadth."

Lūzhì said: "I do not trust you, but I trust the Buddha's word." Because he trusted the Buddha's word, he immediately attained the fruit of stream-entry.

At that time, the devas, nāgas, the eight classes of beings, and the fourfold assembly, having seen and heard all this, attained the four fruits of the path and planted the causes of the three kinds of wholesome karma. The devas and the fourfold assembly, hearing what the Buddha taught, departed with joy.
