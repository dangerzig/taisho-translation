# 續大唐內典錄
## Continuation of the Great Tang Catalog of Inner Scriptures

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2150

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, compiled by Daoxuan (道宣).

---

### Laudatory Preface to the Continuation of the Great Tang Catalog of Inner Scriptures

The Great Sage, whose substance is discerning yet long abides in hidden stillness, universally nourishes all moving and rooted beings alike, bringing them to maturity as by a common vital force. Hidden and obscure, not shining forth, he is able to transform without nurturing. One does not call it intimacy, yet it returns to being, to form. The Way may be concealed, yet it towers and abides upon cliff-side discourses, ascending to prajñā. Amid the twin śāla trees, the empty sky contained six hundred thousand billion hundred-thousands proclaiming the nirvāṇa assembly. The bodhisattvas who delighted sentient beings: at that time, the vast and great filled the entire city, present in this land. Written down in the Qin and Han dynasties, suddenly the assemblies were first attended. Worthy monks arrived, white elephants drew them in unbroken file, establishing domains within the imperial capitals. In the inner chapel, the mind was devoted to translating the holy traces. Stone reliquaries were peacefully placed, and successive narrations were made. Moreover, they broadly delivered the multitude of beings beyond calculation.

As for the flower-incense offerings and gathering of the great assembly's lineage: in twenty-seven successive translations, the miscellaneous and extended Āgamas and other sūtras amounted altogether to twenty-eight translations. Truly these were accomplished through former kings and later emperors who, through translation, brought forth the heritage of the common people, each transforming in succession. New translations were distinguished from earlier ones; untranslated texts similar but not identical to existing ones were many. Concerning what was right and wrong, intermingled and confused, without losing track of the divisions and fascicles, the deep and great rolls and the many and broadly essential turns: the slight and meager accomplishments, not ascending to lofty chanting, the labor and power not exhausted, with new yet incomplete reality: these practices and realities were like the drum, hand, belly, and heart. When the intention is pure, how can one align the body and make a vow, reverently ascending to a single sūtra and seeing the attainment of this teaching, brush to paper?

In the Pure Cloister, speech begins at the very first. With great song enough to sink into and chant, the assembled dwelt in the cloister. The eminent monk who fully proclaimed was Vinaya Master Xuanzhang. Grasping his staff, he sought out the school; in cultivating practice and traveling in remembrance, the Dharma Master's nature and inclinations, if things did not descend in luxuriance, his love of knowledge and understanding, his manifold learning and gathering of ancient and modern, his many abilities and many talents in composing phrases and discoursing upon sūtras: the complexity of what must be read was vast, with a billion words on a single page. With one glance, his countenance was like a flower. Not drinking at two extremes, his love of virtue in the three robes was without attachment to purity or defilement. Casting aside worldly customs like casting aside mucus, without cease in his thoughts. Treating fine silk and gauze as lightly as a guest would treat dust, unable to linger or cherish. How wondrous were his two arms that never stole! The sacred diagrams from his brush never ceased; his brush never stopped. The founding traces of the Vinaya were established at the cloister of Zongchi Temple, east and west, in cultivation chambers. This was truly where his intention abided: painting images, writing prefaces and praises, one substance not hidden. His gathering from afar was without remainder. Beautifully excellent, each person before and after succeeded the worthy, their qualities difficult to equal, surpassingly distinct and beyond the common. This is the vast scope of the Dharma Master's attainment.

---

If one is upright regarding the Dharma, the name-jewel honors sincerity. Some, through the worthy, access the true ocean of the worldly Dharma's ferry; the sage's heights are vast. Through the course of regions, as the honorable and humble exist in the sun and moon, the brightness preserves the beauty of the Way's flourishing. How could one not, through the reversed and overturned fish-trap, prove the precious rank of the impoverished life? From the time the Transcendent Garden announced its completion, the name-waters purely ferried forth, spreading their name-designations among all classes, teaching by hand the dust-covered multitude. Following the conditions of capacity, he spoke counsel. Through abiding nature and desire, he made the voice-teaching's net and snare a single comprehensive whole, encompassing the great thousand. Those who received his Way did so.

### Continuation of the Great Tang Catalog of Inner Scriptures: Record of the Successive Translations of All Sūtras through the Generations, Section One, Beginning

Since the teaching flowed to the Eastern Land, it has passed through successive imperial dynasties. It has necessarily relied upon the rulers of the time to propagate and transmit it, with the voice and outline spreading. Only then could the mysterious and pristine foundation be established through proper editing. Wise rulers valued its meaning and intention, with their feelings devoted to supervision and protection. Dull rulers simply followed its established conventions and went along.

Thus from the Later Han onward through the Tang, though the ages changed between austerity and refinement, the religious principle was ever one. All reverently received the supreme instruction as the primary standard for extinguishing the bonds of affliction. Understanding the mysterious diagram, they mirrored the fundamental basis of birth and death. Thus they could transmit the sacred net from generation to generation with ever-increasing brilliance. This is the Dharma-gift's rushing current, age after age without cease.

Now, the Western Regions of the Five Indias have from ancient times honored the celestial language. The Eastern Land of the Nine Provinces has always followed the bird-track script. Therefore, the celestial writing and celestial language were things the far corners of the sea-realm had never imagined. The eight scripts and six types of writing were things the Great Land from ancient times had rarely beheld. This is why formerly one heard of repeated translation before seeing it here.

The national histories and the ritual classics raise substance and refinement in alternation. The Buddha's words and the secular classics: their diction and principle are as different as heaven. How do we know? The Buddha's dissemination of the teaching placed the Way first. Opening the confused, releasing the naive, resolving doubt, and removing obstruction: it is not about literary adornment or the preservation of scrolls. The intention lies in awakening the dimness and brightness of the principle within feelings, and in reaching the quickness and keenness of the spirit's thought. This is its purpose.

"Listen carefully, listen carefully. Reflect well upon this. I shall now analyze and explain it for you." This is the sage's speech.

"Excellent, excellent! We delight in hearing it. We humbly pray the World-Honored One will analyze and explain it." This is the reception of the Dharma.

Words are repeated and meaning is grasped; there is no concern about prolixity. One only discusses the correct speech, without narrating literary ornamentation. This is the original sūtra. Translation follows regional custom, and what is transmitted according to custom often falls into floating error. Much is lost. Therefore Dao'an composed his essay on the "Five Losses Easy to Commit," and Yancong composed his essay on the "Eight Inversions Hard to Avoid." This is honest testimony.

Other worldly customs are not worth mentioning. This record now sets forth the wise translators of each imperial era, and also displays how the rulers of each age competed in faith or hostility. This forms the first record.

Now, the dynastic periods of the Han, Jin, Sui, and Tang were times of unified rule under heaven, with a single calendrical and seasonal system and a single unified Dharma-gate. But the dynasties of Wei, Song, Qi, and Liang were times of territorial division and fragmentation, with Chinese and foreign peoples sharing in governance and translations issued from both sides. As for the broad divisions transmitting to the common world, those that transcend what came after and surpass what came before, when one sees the promulgation's continuous radiance extending far: now we arrange them according to their era, editing them accordingly. We also append the compositions of monastics and laypeople at the end, so that future generations may see them as if face to face.

---

In total, through eighteen successive dynasties, the continued production of all sūtras amounts to two thousand one hundred eighty-one works.

Since the teaching reached China, nine eras have passed. The dynasties were divided between legitimate and illegitimate; the territories were mixed between Chinese and foreign. Thus the Five Liangs, Four Yans, Three Qins, Two Zhaos, Xia, and Shu occupied narrow regions, while the Jin and Song occupied the land south of the Yangtze. The translation of sūtra-divisions followed the regions where they were produced. The above lists both the legitimate and the associated states, taking up the origins of their translations. The remainder are not discussed, as they had no communication with the Dharma. May future readers understand the purpose.

### Continuation of the Great Tang Catalog of Inner Scriptures

The above, one sūtra. In the era of Emperor Ming of the Later Han, the brāhmaṇa-śramaṇa Kāśyapa Mātaṅga from Central India translated it. Some say Zhu Shemoteng. The various catalogs both preserve the name; it is unclear which is correct. He had not previously translated, so we record it in full. In the tenth year of Yongping (67 CE), he accompanied the Han envoy Cai Yin on his return east to Luoyang. At the White Horse Temple, he translated this sūtra. Arranged according to the catalog, this is the ancestral sūtra of the Chinese lands. The old catalogs say that this sūtra-text was originally a digest from the Indian sūtras, drawing out the essentials from a larger collection, resembling the eighteen chapters of the *Classic of Filial Piety*. It is absent from Dao'an's catalog but appears in the old catalog, in Zhu Shixing's Han catalog, and in Sengyou's *Collected Records of the Tripiṭaka*. When the great Dharma was first transmitted, few people had faith. Therefore Mātaṅga harbored his deep understanding and did not translate further. He later died in Luoyang. The details are in Zhu Shixing's catalog and the *Lives of Eminent Monks* and other miscellaneous catalogs. Baochang's catalog says it was produced by Zhu Falan; this may be based on their having come at the same time.

The above, five works totaling thirteen fascicles, were translated by the Central Indian śramaṇa Zhu Falan, who came together with Kāśyapa Mātaṅga, arriving later after a difficult journey. He translated during the reign of Emperor Ming. Initially, he collaborated with Mātaṅga to produce the *Forty-Two Sections Sūtra*. After Mātaṅga's death, Falan completed the translations on his own.

In former times, Emperor Wu of the Han dug the Kunming Pool and found ashes and charcoal at the bottom. He asked Dongfang Shuo about it. Shuo said: "This is beyond my knowledge. You may ask the foreigners of the Western Regions." When Falan arrived, he was retroactively asked about it. Falan said: "These are the remaining ashes of a kalpa-conflagration." Shuo's words were thus confirmed, and believers greatly increased.

Also, the envoy Qin Jing returned from the Yuezhi kingdom with a painting-model of the sandalwood image made by King Udayana, the fourth-generation copy. Arriving at Luoyang, the emperor ordered it copied. It was displayed for offerings at the Xiyang City Gate and the Xianjie Mausoleum. From that time, the art of painting and coloring has flourished to the present day.

Also, when the buddha-dharma first arrived and the heterodox paths competed against it, Falan narrated the events of the time and composed the *Inner Record of the Han Dharma Origin* in five fascicles. The author is uncertain. It survives today.
