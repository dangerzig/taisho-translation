# Continuation of the Zhenyuan Catalog of the Buddhist Canon

## *Xù Zhēnyuán Shìjiào Lù* (續貞元釋教錄)

Taishō 2158

Compiled by Héng'ān (*Héng'ān*, 恒安) of the Southern Tang.

*Note: This is a bibliographic supplement to the Zhenyuan Catalog of the Buddhist Canon, compiled in 946 CE (the Bǎodà reign era of the Southern Tang). It records the efforts of the monk Héng'ān, who traveled to northern China, visited Mount Wutai, and brought back newly translated sūtras and treatises that had been added to the canon since the original Zhenyuan catalog of 799 CE. The text provides a detailed accounting of the history of the Chinese Buddhist canon from the Kaiyuan catalog (730 CE, 5,048 fascicles) through the Zhenyuan catalog (799 CE, 5,390 fascicles) to Héng'ān's own supplement adding 140 works in 413 fascicles. It includes records of translations by Vajrabodhi, Amoghavajra, Prajñā, and other translators of the Tang dynasty, as well as a memorial to the Southern Tang emperor requesting imperial authorization to copy and distribute these texts.*

---

It is said that when Mātaṅga entered the Han dynasty, the voice of the teaching first reached China. Through the successive reigns of the sagely Tang dynasty, the Brahmic scriptures became fully and perfectly gathered in the ocean-treasury [of the canon].

Furthermore, our Emperor Xuánzōng ruled over the universe. In the eighteenth year of the Kaiyuan era, the year *gēngwǔ* [730 CE], the śramaṇa Zhìshēng (*Zhìshēng*, 智昇) of the Western Chóngfú Monastery in the Western Capital compiled the *Kaiyuan Catalog of the Buddhist Canon* (*Kāiyuán Shìjiào Lù*) in twenty fascicles, designated as the Kaiyuan Catalog Collection. It comprised Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna sūtras, vinayas, and śāstras, along with biographies and compilations of worthies and sages, totaling 5,048 fascicles in 481 cases. It circulated widely throughout the world.

Then four more reigns followed: Xuánzōng, Sùzōng, Dàizōng, and Dézōng. In the tenth year of the Zhēnyuán era of Emperor Dézōng, the year *jiǎxū* [794 CE], a further sixty-five years had elapsed. During that interval, seven Indian monks (*fànsēng*) jointly translated Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna sūtras, vinayas, and śāstras, as well as vinaya texts by Yìjìng that had been omitted from the Kaiyuan catalog, along with the *Great Buddha-Name Sūtra*, separate biographies, and other works, totaling 134 titles in 299 fascicles.

The śramaṇa Yuánzhào (*Yuánzhào*, 圓照) of the Western Xīmíng Monastery in the Western Capital compiled the *Continuation of the Kaiyuan Catalog* in three fascicles. Then in the fifteenth year of the Zhēnyuán era [799 CE], by imperial command, he compiled the *Zhēnyuán Catalog of the Buddhist Canon* in thirty fascicles, incorporating the texts translated by the aforementioned seven Indian monks, totaling 332 fascicles. Combined with the earlier Kaiyuan Catalog Collection, the grand total came to 5,380 fascicles in 510 cases, designated as the Zhēnyuán Catalog Collection, which was promulgated and circulated.

[The text then describes Héng'ān's journey:] Only since [the catalog] reached the Jiāngnán region have one or two additional titles appeared that were not yet incorporated into the canon. In the *dīngchǒu* year of the Tiānyòu era [907 CE], I arrived in the Jiāngnán region, visiting famous mountains and seeking out learned masters. Beyond my study and interviews, while reading through the canon, I discussed matters with dharma-friends concerning the Zhēnyuán Collection, and privately resolved to take a solemn vow to obtain these scriptures and bring them back to the imperial capital, hoping to support the imperial transformation and forever bless the nation.

Relying on the sagely court and drawing upon imperial power, in the second year of the Shēngyuán era [938 CE] of the Great Tang [i.e., the Southern Tang], I traveled far to pay reverence at Mount Wutai. Returning from the northwest, I copied and recorded the sūtras, vinayas, śāstras, and other texts of the Zhēnyuán Catalog Collection. In the third year of the Bǎodà era [945 CE], I returned to the imperial capital. From the *jiǎxū* year of the Zhēnyuán era to the present, 152 years have elapsed.

I then humbly received the gracious consent of His Imperial Majesty to copy and circulate these texts, with an edict issued to the relevant offices to permanently incorporate them into the various canon collections. The Cavalier Attendant, Executive Officer of the Ministry of War, Drafter of Edicts, Cloud-Rider, and Bearer of the Purple-Gold Fish-Pouch, Qiáo Kuāngshùn (*Qiáo Kuāngshùn*, 喬匡舜), was commanded to compose a preface to be placed at the head of the scriptures. This will not only benefit one generation but serve as a vessel for a thousand ages.

[The catalog then records:]

Additionally, at the Western Canon Hall of Shēngyuán Monastery in the capital, I found the *Thousand-Bowl Mañjuśrī Sūtra* in one section of ten fascicles. This was also translated by the Tripiṭaka master Vajrabodhi (*Jīngāngzhì*, 金剛智) and is within the Zhēnyuán Catalog, though it had been omitted.

Also, the *Record of the Hierarchical Sources of All Sūtras* in one section of thirty fascicles is outside the Zhēnyuán Collection; I now include its count below.

Also, Layman Lǐ Tōngxuán's (*Lǐ Tōngxuán*, 李通玄) *Commentary on the Avataṃsaka* in one section of forty fascicles was presented by the monk Miǎnchāng in the second year of the Shēngyuán era. The Emperor Guāngwén Sùwǔ Xiào Gāo ordered the Secretary of the Ministry of Rites, Sūn Jì, to compose a preface, and it was incorporated into the canon.

---

## Summary of the Catalog

Excluding the Kaiyuan Catalog's existing count, the newly tallied total comprises 140 titles in 413 fascicles, forming 43 cases. The newly recorded scriptural titles and catalog entries are listed as follows.

### The Kaiyuan Catalog Collection

A total of 1,076 titles, comprising 5,048 fascicles in 481 cases. Compiled by the śramaṇa [Zhìshēng] of the Western Chóngfú Monastery in the Western Capital, in the eighteenth year of the Kaiyuan era, the year *gēngwǔ* [730 CE], by imperial command.

### The Zhēnyuán Catalog Collection

A total of 1,213 titles, comprising 5,390 fascicles in 510 cases. Compiled by the śramaṇa [Yuánzhào] of the Western Xīmíng Monastery in the Western Capital, in the fifteenth year of the Zhēnyuán era, the year *wùyín* [799 CE], by imperial command. Within this: 137 newly added titles comprising 343 fascicles in 30 cases.

### Newly Incorporated into the Canon: Continuation of the Newly Translated Zhēnyuán Catalog Collection

Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna sūtras, vinayas, śāstras, catalog entries, the *Source-Classification Record*, the *Thousand-Bowl Sūtra*, and others. Currently tallied for copying: 139 titles in 373 fascicles, forming 39 cases.

---

## Detailed Records

[The text proceeds to list the translations organized by dynasty and translator:]

**Xuánzōng's reign**: Nine titles in twenty fascicles, translated by monks not yet included in previous catalogs, including three titles in five fascicles by the North Indian Tripiṭaka master Amoghavajra (*Ācìdáxiàn*) translated at Ānxī; one Heart Sūtra in one fascicle by the Tripiṭaka Dharmarakṣa of Eastern India (this being a fifth translation, with all three divisions of preamble, main text, and peroration complete, unlike the earlier versions by Kumārajīva and Xuánzàng); and four titles in four fascicles by the South Indian Tripiṭaka Vajrabodhi, including the *Thousand-Bowl Mañjuśrī Sūtra* in ten fascicles.

**Translations by Amoghavajra**: Bearing the posthumous titles of Grand Preceptor, Grand Guardian, and Tripiṭaka of Great Broad Wisdom, the śramaṇa of Dàxīngshàn Monastery, whose Sanskrit name was Amuōgha-vajra (originally named Zhìzàng, later known as Bùkōng, "Amoghavajra"), served as National Preceptor for the Consecration-Rite under three emperors: Xuánzōng, Sùzōng, and Dàizōng. Under these three reigns, by imperial decree he successively translated sūtras, vinayas, and śāstras. A total of 109 titles in 142 fascicles in 14 cases.

**Translations by Prajñā**: Six titles in seventy fascicles. The Tripiṭaka śramaṇa Prajñā from Kaśmīra translated by imperial decree beginning in the fourth year of the Zhēnyuán era [788 CE]. Among these was the forty-fascicle *Gaṇḍavyūha* chapter of the Avataṃsaka, presented in Sanskrit manuscript by the king of Uḍḍiyāna to Emperor Dézōng.

**Translations by other monks**: Including one sūtra by the Kuchean Tripiṭaka Mokṣagupta translated at Ānxī, and two sūtras in ten fascicles by the Khotanese Tripiṭaka Śīladharma translated at Běitíng.

**Mahāyāna vinaya**: [Listed.]

**Mahāyāna śāstra**: Three titles in three fascicles, translated by Amoghavajra.

**Hīnayāna vinaya**: Seven titles in fifty fascicles by the Tang Tripiṭaka Yìjìng, translated between the first year of the Zhèngshèng era of the Great Zhou [695 CE] and the second year of the Jǐngyún era of the Great Tang [711 CE], previously omitted from the Kaiyuan Catalog and now supplemented.

**Zhēnyuán-era catalog materials**: The *Continuation of the Kaiyuan Catalog* in three fascicles by Yuánzhào, compiled in the tenth year of the Zhēnyuán era [794 CE]; the *Zhēnyuán Catalog* in thirty fascicles by Yuánzhào, compiled in the fifteenth year [799 CE]; and the *Record of the Hierarchical Sources of All Sūtras* in thirty fascicles by the monk Cóngfàn of the Xuānzōng reign.

---

## Grand Total

Combining all: the previously delivered sūtras, the Zhēnyuán catalog, the *Source-Classification Record*, the *Great Buddha-Name Sūtra*, separate biographies, the *Thousand-Bowl Sūtra*, and the *Avataṃsaka Commentary*, the grand total is 140 titles in 413 fascicles in 43 cases. If arranged separately after the Kaiyuan Collection, the listing is as above. If arranged together with the Kaiyuan Collection by Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna divisions, including the Avataṃsaka Commentary, the total comes to 1,214 titles in 5,431 fascicles in 518 cases.

May the golden words never fall into oblivion and the jeweled stanzas forever bloom in fragrance. This brief supplementary record is compiled so that nothing may be lost.

---

## Memorial to the Throne

Your servant speaks. Your servant, having carried the scriptures from afar and returned to the divine capital, humbly received imperial grace to circulate and record them. This fills me with gratitude and emotion.

Your Imperial Majesty reigns over the four seas with benevolence extending to the myriad regions. May his reign endure as the sands of the river, his foundation as the drops of the ocean.

Your servant, not considering his own insignificance, has presumed to compile from the newly translated Zhēnyuán Catalog Collection of Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna sūtras, vinayas, and śāstras, together with the *Great Buddha-Name Sūtra*, *Thousand-Bowl Sūtra*, and others, a total of 138 titles in 372 fascicles, arranged by section and case, along with the previously incorporated *Avataṃsaka Commentary*, into this *Continuation of the Newly Translated Zhēnyuán Catalog of the Buddhist Canon for the Bǎodà Era of the Great Tang*, one fascicle of seventeen sheets.

I humbly request that Your Imperial Majesty graciously consent to have it incorporated into the cases of the Zhēnyuán Catalog Collection that have been brought here, to be tallied, copied, and circulated, for the benefit of the nation.

---

*[Colophon]: Bǎodà, fourth year [946 CE], eleventh month. Submitted by the Right Street Bào'ēn Chán Cloister's scripture-obtaining Chán master-monk. In accordance with the Saṃgha Registry and the Department of State Affairs, the submitted Continuation of the Newly Translated Zhēnyuán Catalog, one fascicle, received the imperial endorsement and was issued to the relevant bureau. The Right Street Saṃgha Registry was thereupon ordered to distribute it for incorporation into the newly translated Zhēnyuán Catalog Collection, to be tallied and copied for circulation. The matter has been memorialized. Bǎodà, fourth year, twelfth month, fifteenth day.*
