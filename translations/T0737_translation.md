# 所欲致患經
## Sūtra on the Afflictions Caused by Desire

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0737

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was traveling in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus, five hundred bhikṣus in all.

At that time the bhikṣus put on their robes early in the morning, took their bowls, and entered the city of Śrāvastī. The non-Buddhist teachers of other schools questioned the bhikṣus: "Śramaṇas! What does the śramaṇa Gautama regard as the cause of affliction? By what does he distinguish form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness as suffering? What difference is there in all these dharmas? What is the aspiration? What is the cause of accomplishment? What dharma does the śramaṇa Gautama set forth in the present? What does he use to instruct others? What does he speak and teach about?"

The bhikṣus, hearing what the non-Buddhist teachers said, had no way to answer. They arose from their seats, turned away, and departed. Each thought to himself: "What they have said, we should inquire of the World-Honored One, receive his explanation, and then practice accordingly."

When the bhikṣus had finished their alms-round and completed their meal, they adjusted their robes and went to the Buddha's dwelling. They bowed at his feet, sat to one side, and addressed the World-Honored One, recounting in order all the difficult questions posed by the non-Buddhist teachers.

At that time the World-Honored One told the bhikṣus: "If the non-Buddhists ask you what pleasure there is in desire, what afflictions it brings, where it arises from, and how it is extinguished, you should answer them. The non-Buddhists will fall silent and have nothing to say in reply."

The Buddha said: "I see no one who can resolve this meaning and distinguish its highest significance. Why? Because no one can match it; it is beyond their domain. The Buddha examines the heavens above and the earth below, all the māras, Brahmā-devas, brahmins, all spirits and human beings, and finds that what can elicit this question and bring delight to the mind, making one love what is pleasant and become stained by desire, is: the ear hearing pleasant sounds, the nose perceiving pleasant scents, the tongue perceiving delicious flavors, the body perceiving fine and smooth sensations, and the mind delighting in what is pleasing, setting the will upon what is enjoyed, becoming stained by it, the mind craving dharmas. These five objects of desire arise from causes and conditions, and the mind takes them as pleasure."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "What are the afflictions of desire? Take a clansman who, according to his skill and means, establishes a livelihood with much anxious thought. Whether through craftsmanship, or serving as an official, or working as a painter, or practicing arithmetic, or carving and engraving, or plastering and measuring, or dealing in colored goods; whether enduring cold and frost, or encountering heat, hunger, thirst, and starvation; whether struck by wind and rain, or beset by mosquitoes and gadflies, the sense-faculties become disordered. Pursuing all these affairs, wishing the body to be at ease, he seeks wealth and treasure. Sitting and rising, he lets his mind run wild, indulging in wealth and treasure, weeping and worrying, beating his chest in anguish. I say that such people are in the darkness of delusion, achieving no fruit. Though still diligent, they do not leave their occupations of constructing houses and accumulating goods. If they obtain wealth, they fear losing it to government officials, floods, fires, thieves, enemies, or creditors who seize, burn, destroy, rob, assault, and ravage their homes, causing them to lose their wealth. That clansman harbors this worry in his heart. Should he suddenly encounter flood, fire, thieves, or enemies who seize and plunder, he weeps and grieves inconsolably: 'All the wealth I formerly accumulated through my livelihood is suddenly gone, with nothing to rely upon.' This is the sorrow and affliction of desire. From desire comes attachment, and from letting the mind run wild come these torments and resentments."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Furthermore, because of desire and craving, everywhere letting the mind run wild: the father speaks ill of the son, the son speaks ill of the father, the mother speaks ill of the daughter, the daughter speaks ill of the mother, the elder brother speaks ill of the younger, the younger speaks ill of the elder, the elder sister speaks ill of the younger, the younger speaks ill of the elder. Household and clan members slander each other in turn. This is the affliction of greed and desire, the suffering caused by it. All arises from excessive seeking, letting the mind run wild, drowning in desire."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Furthermore, the affliction of desire has attachment and craving as its root. Letting the mind run wild, because of greed for gain, they take up weapons, draw bows and fit arrows, and enter into battle. They raise the four divisions of the army: elephants, horses, chariots, and infantry, and the many soldiers fight together. This is like a severe net. Because of desire one loses one's own relatives, and relatives turn against relatives. Causes and conditions arise; because of greed one commits crimes and rushes about restlessly in pursuit of wealth. Sometimes one obtains wealth, sometimes one cannot, or one soon loses it. One grieves in anguish, slapping one's thighs and beating one's chest: 'I was formerly wealthy, and now it is all exhausted.' This is the affliction of greed and desire, the torment of attachment and craving, letting the mind run wild, drowning in it."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Furthermore, the affliction of desire has attachment and craving as its root. Letting the mind run wild: father and son resent each other, mother and daughter despise each other, husband and wife abandon each other, sisters harbor hatred, brothers despise each other, relatives and family members slander each other. This is the affliction of greed and desire, the torment of attachment and craving, letting the mind run wild, drowning in it."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Furthermore, the affliction of desire has attachment and craving as its root. Letting the mind run wild, they take up sharp swords, or hold knives and clubs. In secluded places, in the sand, among the trees, or in ruined compounds, their hearts filled with resentment, they take each other's lives. From afar they hurl fire-wheels and pour boiling oil upon each other. From this, evil arises, sometimes causing severe injury, sometimes causing death. This is the affliction of greed and desire, the torment of attachment and craving, letting the mind run wild, drowning in it."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Furthermore, the affliction of desire has attachment and craving as its root. Letting the mind run wild, they break into others' doors, steal others' goods and provisions, bore through walls, commit burglary at night, hide in concealed places, or flee as fugitives, starting fights and setting fires. When the ruler discovers this, officials bind and shackle them, confine them in prison, cut off ears, noses, hands, or feet, interrogate them with flogging and beating, or behead them. At times they are made to stand upon crushed ankles, strung up in deer snares, placed in steamers or cauldrons of boiling water, or have boiling oil poured over their bodies. This is the affliction of greed and desire, the torment of attachment and craving, letting the mind run wild, drowning in it."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Furthermore, the affliction of desire has attachment and craving as its root. Letting the mind run wild, the body commits many evils, curses and abuse pour from the mouth, the mind harbors malice, without guarding body or speech, without regard for the next life. When life ends, the spirit departs in an instant and falls into evil destinies, places of great suffering, where it is punished day and night for countless years. This is the affliction of greed and desire, the torment of attachment and craving, letting the mind run wild, drowning in it."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Furthermore, how does one abandon desire and find happiness in severing evil? One should cut off all desires, eliminate all craving, and relinquish all emotional attachments. This is the abandoning of desire. If there are śramaṇas or brahmins who see the faults of desire, and the afflictions that arise from it, and who clearly know desire as it truly is, then they encourage others to cross beyond desire. If through encouragement one attains liberation, yet if one still has the will fixed upon desire and wishes to cross to the other shore, that has never been. If there are śramaṇas or brahmins who delight in desire, who do not see the faults and impurities of desire, yet who could truly recognize the emotions, be free from craving for all feelings, instruct others, and cross to the other shore, themselves crossing over and delivering others: that would be accomplishing this matter beyond doubt. Take, for instance, the sight before one's eyes: a woman of good family, a brahmin's wife, at fourteen, fifteen, sixteen, or twenty years of age, neither too tall nor too short, neither too coarse nor too slender, neither too pale nor too dark, of beautiful and wondrous appearance, like a tree in full bloom."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "When one first sees her, because of the causes and conditions that give rise to pleasant delight, this is the pleasure of desire. What are the afflictions that come from the conditions of form? Here one sees a woman, aged and extremely old, eighty or ninety, a hundred or a hundred and twenty, her hair white, teeth fallen out, face wrinkled, skin loose, body heavy and short of breath, walking bent over with a cane, feeble and gasping, walking with great difficulty, body trembling and unsteady. What do you think, bhikṣus? Has not the beauty that was extremely fine and lovely now manifested its affliction?"

The bhikṣus replied: "Indeed. This is the sorrow and affliction of form."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Furthermore, one sees a woman after death: one day, two days, up to five or six days. The body's color has turned dark blue, swollen, rotting, and stinking. Foul and impure fluids flow from the nine orifices, and worms are born within the body, eating the flesh. What do you think, bhikṣus? The beauty that was formerly fine and lovely has lost its good appearance. Has not the affliction manifested?"

They replied: "Indeed."

The Buddha said: "This is the evidence of the affliction of form."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "If one further sees a woman, stinking and rotting on the ground, eaten by crows and birds, pecked by vultures and eagles, gnawed by tigers, wolves, and foxes, with countless worms emerging from the body to eat the flesh. What do you think, bhikṣus? The beauty that was formerly fine and lovely: has it not perished? Has not the affliction manifested?"

They replied: "Indeed."

The Buddha said: "This is the evidence of the affliction of desire."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "If one further sees a woman, the skin and flesh separated from the body, with only white bones remaining: the beauty that was formerly fine and lovely has vanished and no longer appears. Has not the affliction manifested?"

They replied: "Indeed."

"This is the evidence of the affliction of desire."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "If one further sees a woman, the bones disjointed: hands and feet, knees and shins, nose and ears, ribs and back, arms and elbows, head and neck, each in a different place. What do you think, bhikṣus? The beauty that was formerly fine and lovely: has it not vanished? Has not the affliction manifested?"

They replied: "Indeed."

The Buddha said: "This is the evidence of the sorrow and affliction of desire."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "One sees that woman, abandoned in the burial ground for countless years. The bones and joints have crumbled away, pale blue like jade, shattered to powder like flour. What do you think, bhikṣus? The beauty that was formerly fine and lovely: has it not vanished? Has not the affliction manifested?"

They replied: "Indeed."

The Buddha said: "This is the evidence of the sorrow and affliction of greed and desire."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "Who can leave desire behind, who can sever desire for form, remove and eliminate the emotions of form, uproot craving and not follow it, and so not be attached to form? If there are śramaṇas or brahmins who delight in form as described, taking it as pleasure, yet who see the evidence of its affliction, who see the suffering that arises from it, and who truly know it as it is, then they are equal in regard to form, their minds not leaning upon it, and they can instruct others and help them cross to the other shore. But if there are people who, leaning upon form, relying upon form, wish to cross over, that has never been. If śramaṇas or brahmins who delight in form, experiencing various emotions of desire, see the evidence of form's affliction and wish to leave form behind, uprooting all emotional attachment, then they see it as it truly is. Seeing all forms equally, they instruct others and help them cross to the other shore. Knowing what form leans upon and abandoning all forms: this can be accomplished."

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "What is the pleasure that comes from feeling, and how does one abandon the habit of feeling? Here a bhikṣu is at peace regarding all desires, separated from all evil and unwholesome dharmas. With directed thought and sustained thought, dwelling alone in tranquility, one practices the first dhyāna. If a bhikṣu attains this first dhyāna, then one does not covet the self, is not attached to others, is without contention, and the mind does not harbor ill will. This is the bhikṣu's feeling of pleasure free from anger. I have no resentment, being at ease in pleasant feeling. This is the observation of the arising of pleasant feeling. Then one delights in the cessation of all thoughts, delights in inner mindfulness and tranquility, the mind becoming unified, without thought and without activity, the will tranquil and attaining peace. This is the second dhyāna. If a bhikṣu practices the second dhyāna without coveting the self, without being attached to others, with mind neither increasing nor decreasing, then the delight of desire is observed as a practice free from desire, always settled in concentration. The body being at ease, as the noble ones have taught, one constantly observes with a concentrated mind. This is the third dhyāna. If a bhikṣu practices the third dhyāna, this is the pleasure that comes from feeling. Then one eliminates suffering and removes comfort, and the formerly experienced pleasant and unpleasant feelings: there is neither suffering nor pleasure. One observes with a resolved and concentrated will, fully complete and pure. If a bhikṣu practices the fourth dhyāna, this is the pleasure that comes from feeling.

"Furthermore, bhikṣus, when pleasure and agreeable desire arise from feeling, this is the pleasure of feeling. What is the sorrow and affliction of feeling? When suffering, sorrow, and confusion arise from feeling, this is the sorrow and affliction of feeling. Furthermore, feeling is impermanent, suffering, and subject to the dharma of separation. All feeling, with its arising of impermanence, suffering, and the dharma of separation: this is the affliction of feeling. What is the escape from feeling? When one severs all craving and desire regarding feeling, this is the escape from desire. If there are śramaṇas or brahmins who clearly understand the various pleasures that arise from feeling yet who bring about sorrow and affliction, who do not abandon all desire, and who truly know it as it is, yet lean upon feeling, instructing others to cross to the other shore, themselves attaining accomplishment while freeing others from their attachments: that has never been. If there are śramaṇas or brahmins who see the pleasure that comes from feeling, who see the affliction that comes from pleasure, who leave behind desire, and who truly know it as it is, who see all feelings equally without leaning upon them, who instruct others and help them cross to the other shore, themselves attaining accomplishment and also transforming others: this matter can be accomplished. This is the abandoning of delight."

Thus the bhikṣus, hearing the sūtra, rejoiced.
