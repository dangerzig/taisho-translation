# 婆羅門子命終愛念不離經
## Sūtra on the Brāhmaṇa Whose Son Died and Who Could Not Let Go of Love

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0091

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, a certain brāhmaṇa had an only son who died, and the brāhmaṇa could not let go of his love. After the death he could not eat, could not drink, would not wear clothes, would not apply perfume. He only went to the cemetery and wept, weeping and remembering the child held in his arms. Then the brāhmaṇa wandered until he came to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he and the World-Honored One exchanged greetings. After the exchange, the brāhmaṇa sat to one side. The World-Honored One asked: "Why, brāhmaṇa, are your faculties so unsettled?"

"Gautama! How could my mental faculty be settled? I had one son who died, and I cannot let go of my love. Since his death, I cannot eat, cannot drink, do not wear clothes, do not apply perfume. I only stay home weeping, and when I weep I remember holding him in my arms."

"Just so, just so, brāhmaṇa! Brāhmaṇa! From love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness."

"How so, Gautama? Can it be so, that from love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness? Gautama! From love there should arise only joy and cherished delight." Three times the World-Honored One told the brāhmaṇa: "Just so, just so, brāhmaṇa! From love there arises sorrow, grief, weeping, and unhappiness." Three times the brāhmaṇa said to the World-Honored One: "How so, Gautama? Can it be so, that from love there arises sorrow and unhappiness? Gautama! From love there should arise only joy and cherished delight." Thereupon the brāhmaṇa, having heard the World-Honored One's words, was neither pleased nor did he deny them. Neither pleased nor assenting, he rose from his seat and departed.

At that time, outside the gate of the Jetavana, there were some gamblers playing together. The brāhmaṇa saw them from a distance and thought: "Among worldly clever people, these are the best. I should tell these gamblers about my discussion with the ascetic Gautama." So the brāhmaṇa went to the gamblers and told them everything he had discussed with the World-Honored One. Having heard, the gamblers replied to the brāhmaṇa: "Brāhmaṇa! How could it be so, that from love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness? Brāhmaṇa! From love there should arise only joy and cherished delight." Thereupon the brāhmaṇa thought: "These gamblers' words agree with mine." And nodding his head, he departed.

The discussion then became widely known, and gradually reached the royal palace. King Prasenajit heard that the ascetic Gautama had said: "From love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness." Having heard, King Prasenajit told Queen Mallikā: "Mallikā! I have heard that the ascetic Gautama says: 'From love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness.'"

"Just so, just so, Great King! From love there arises suffering, sorrow, grief, and unhappiness."

"Mallikā! I have heard your teacher's words, and the disciple says the same. You, Mallikā, have the ascetic Gautama as your teacher, and now you say: 'From love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness.'"

"Great King! If you do not believe what I have said, you may go yourself or send a messenger."

Then King Prasenajit told the brāhmaṇa Nāḷijaṅgha: "Nāḷijaṅgha! Go to where the ascetic Gautama is. Having arrived, convey my words to the ascetic Gautama with boundless greetings: 'Is he well, comfortable, light of body, and strong?' Speak thus: 'Gautama! King Prasenajit sends boundless greetings and asks whether you are well, comfortable, light of body, and strong. Did the ascetic Gautama truly say: "From love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness"?' Nāḷijaṅgha! Whatever the ascetic Gautama says, receive it well, memorize it, and study it. Why? Because he does not speak untruthfully."

The brāhmaṇa Nāḷijaṅgha quickly accepted King Prasenajit's instruction and went to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he and the World-Honored One exchanged greetings. After the exchange, he sat to one side and addressed the World-Honored One: "Gautama! King Prasenajit sends boundless greetings and asks whether you are well, comfortable, light of body, and strong. Did the ascetic Gautama truly say: 'From love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness'?"

"Nāḷijaṅgha! I shall now question you in return; answer as you are able. Nāḷijaṅgha, what do you think? There may be someone whose mother dies. After the mother's death, the person goes mad, wandering naked without clothes, saying: 'I cannot find my mother! I cannot find my mother!' Brāhmaṇa, you should know: from love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness. Likewise with the death of a father, brother, sister, or wife. After the wife's death, the person goes mad, wandering naked without clothes, saying: 'I cannot find my father! I cannot find my wife!' Brāhmaṇa! You should know: from love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness.

"Brāhmaṇa! Once there was a man whose wife returned to her family home. Her relatives wished to take her away and give her to another man. The woman heard this, that her relatives wished to give her to another man. Having heard, she quickly ran back to her own husband. Having arrived, she told her husband: 'My lord, you should know: my relatives wish to give me to another man. Whatever you should do, you should do it now.' Then the man took a very sharp knife, took his wife's hand, and they went together back into the house, saying: 'We shall go together, we shall go together.' He killed the woman and killed himself. Brāhmaṇa! You should know: from love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness."

Thereupon the brāhmaṇa Nāḷijaṅgha, having heard the World-Honored One's words, received, memorized, and studied them well. Rising from his seat, he circumambulated the World-Honored One and departed, returning to where King Prasenajit was. Having arrived, he said to King Prasenajit: "It is truly so, Great King! The ascetic Gautama does say: 'From love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness.'"

Then King Prasenajit told Queen Mallikā: "It is truly so, Mallikā! The ascetic Gautama does say: 'From love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness.'"

"Therefore, Great King! I shall now question the King in return; answer as you are able. Great King, what do you think? Do you love General Viḍūḍabha?"

"Mallikā! I do love General Viḍūḍabha."

"Great King! If General Viḍūḍabha were to be destroyed or changed, would sorrow, suffering, and unhappiness arise?"

"Mallikā! If General Viḍūḍabha were destroyed or changed, there would indeed be sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness."

"Great King! You should know by this that from love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness. Great King, what do you think? Do you love General Xianshou, the great elephant Puṇḍarīka, the princess Vājirinī, the minister Vassakāra, and the people of Kāśī?"

"Mallikā! I love the people of Kāśī-Kośala."

"Great King! If the people of Kāśī-Kośala were to be destroyed or changed, would sorrow, suffering, and unhappiness arise?"

"Mallikā! All the pleasures of the five desires that I enjoy depend on the people of Kāśī-Kośala. Mallikā! If the people of Kāśī were destroyed or changed, my very life would not be whole. How could there not be sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness?"

"Great King! You should know: from love there arises suffering, sorrow, grief, and unhappiness. Great King, what do you think? Do you care for me?"

"Mallikā! I do care for you."

"Great King! If I too were to be destroyed or changed, would there not be sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness?"

"You, Mallikā! If you were destroyed or changed, I too would have sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness."

"Great King! By this you should know: from love there arises sorrow, grief, suffering, and unhappiness."

"Mallikā! From this day on, the ascetic Gautama shall be my teacher in this matter, and I shall be his disciple. Mallikā! I now take refuge in the World-Honored One, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. Before the World-Honored One I accept the lay precepts. From this day forward I shall forsake killing, and now I take refuge in the Buddha."

Thus it was spoken. King Prasenajit, hearing from afar what the World-Honored One had taught, was delighted and joyful.
