# 佛說淨飯王般涅槃經
## Sūtra of King Śuddhodana's Parinirvāṇa, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0512

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Juqu Jingsheng (沮渠京聲)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the city of Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak Mountain, together with a great assembly of bhikṣus. At that time, the World-Honored One's radiance was brilliant and resplendent, like the rising sun illuminating the world.

In the land of the Śākyas, the king named Śuddhodana governed with right Dharma, propriety, virtue, benevolence, and righteousness, always acting with a compassionate mind. He was then struck by a grave illness. The four great elements within his body all acted at once to ravage his frame. His joints were about to come apart, his breathing was unsteady, like a swift-flowing river. The prime minister sent orders throughout the country for all the renowned physicians to gather and examine the king's illness. They administered medicines suited to the disease and tried every kind of treatment, but none could effect a cure. The portents had already arrived; death would not be long.

The king was then feverish and restless, tossing and turning without cease, like a fish in shallow water. The queen and the palace ladies, seeing him thus, were even more grief-stricken and distressed.

At that time, King Śuklodana, King Droṇodana, King Mahānāman, and the assembled ministers all raised their voices and said: "If the king should now pass away, we shall lose our protector forever, and the country will be weakened." The king's body trembled. His lips and mouth were parched. His voice broke off again and again. His eyes grew dim and tears fell. The assembled kings all expressed their reverence, kneeling with palms joined, and together said: "Great King, by nature you have never delighted in wrongdoing. Not even for the snap of a finger have you ceased accumulating virtue. You have nurtured and protected the people, bringing peace to all, and your fame is known in the ten directions. Great King, why today are you so grieved?"

Then King Śuddhodana, forcing out his voice, said to the assembled kings: "Though my life may depart, I do not regard it as suffering. I only regret that I cannot see my son Siddhārtha. I also regret that I cannot see my second son Nanda, who has eliminated lust and all worldly desires. I further regret that I cannot see Droṇodana's son Ānanda, who holds the Buddha's Dharma treasury without losing a single word. And I regret that I cannot see my grandson Rāhula, who though young in years possesses pure and complete supernatural powers, and whose precept-conduct is without flaw. If I could see all these sons, though my illness is grave and I have not yet escaped birth and death, I would not regard it as suffering." All those at the king's side, hearing these words, wept without exception, their tears falling like rain.

Then King Śuklodana answered King Śuddhodana: "I have heard that the World-Honored One is on Vulture Peak Mountain in the city of Rājagṛha, fifty yojanas from here, a great distance. The king grows weaker by the moment. Even if a messenger were dispatched, the road is long and distant, and I fear it would be too late to be of any help. I only wish that the great king would not be so deeply grieved and anxious for his sons."

When King Śuddhodana heard these words, he wept and replied to King Śuklodana: "Though my sons are far away, my longing for them does not cease. Why? Because my son has become a Buddha. With great compassion, he constantly uses his supernatural powers, his divine eye to see clearly, and his divine ear to hear all, to rescue all beings who may be saved. If there were a hundred thousand million beings drowning in water, with a compassionate heart he would make a boat and raft to ferry them across, never growing weary. It is like a person surrounded by bandits, or encountering enemies, terrified and without recourse, with no hope of saving themselves, seeking only rescue and protection, relying on one with power to be delivered from fear and danger. It is like a person who contracts a grave illness and desires a good physician to cure the disease. My longing to see the World-Honored One today is just like this. Why? Because the World-Honored One, day and night, at the three times constantly uses his divine eye to observe all beings who should be taught, with a compassionate heart, like a mother thinking of her child."

At that time, the World-Honored One on Vulture Peak Mountain heard with his divine ear the words of King Śuddhodana and the other kings in the great city of Kapilavastu, and how the father-king was anxious and distressed. With his divine eye he saw from afar the father-king, ill and lying in bed, wasted and haggard, his life nearing its end. He knew the father's yearning to see his sons.

At that time, the World-Honored One said to Nanda: "Our father, King Śuddhodana, the foremost king of the world, is our father. He is now gravely ill. We should go to see him. His remaining life is little. Prepare at once and let us depart. We should go while he still lives, so that we may see him and fulfill the king's wish."

Nanda received the instruction, knelt, and made obeisance: "Yes, World-Honored One! King Śuddhodana is our father. His deeds are extraordinary: he was able to beget a holy son who benefits the world. We should go now to repay the kindness of his nurturing."

Ānanda, with palms joined, came forward and said to the Buddha: "I wish to accompany the World-Honored One, for I too long to see him. King Śuddhodana is my uncle. He permitted me to leave home and become a disciple of the Buddha. I received the Buddha as my teacher. That is why I wish to go."

Rāhula then came forward and said to the Buddha: "Though the World-Honored One is my father, who renounced the kingdom to seek the path, it was my grandfather the king who nurtured me and enabled me to leave home. Therefore I wish to go and pay my respects to my grandfather the king."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! You should know this is the right time, so that the king's wish may be fulfilled."

Thereupon the World-Honored One, with his supernatural powers, like a king of geese soared into the sky, and suddenly appeared in Kapilavastu, emitting a great radiance. The people of the country, seeing the Buddha approaching from afar, all raised their voices, weeping: "If the great king passes away, the name of the Śākya land will surely perish." The people of the city cried out to the Buddha and said to the World-Honored One: "In those days, when the prince leapt over the palace walls and went to the Lumbi grove to sit in contemplation, the father-king saw him and bowed in reverence. The great king's life will not last much longer. May the Tathāgata go to him while there is still time to see each other."

The people of the country threw themselves upon the ground, choking with grief and tears. Some tore off their necklaces; some ripped their garments; some pulled out their hair; some took ashes and dirt and threw it upon themselves. Their pain pierced to the marrow; they were like people gone mad.

When the Buddha saw this, he admonished the people of the country: "Impermanence and separation have existed since ancient times. All of you should reflect on this. Birth and death bring suffering; only the path is true." The Buddha rained the Dharma upon the minds of all beings and, with many kinds of Dharma, opened their understanding.

Thereupon the World-Honored One, with the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, and the eighteen dharmas unique to all Buddhas, emitted a great radiance. Again, with the thirty-two marks and the eighty minor marks, he emitted a great radiance. With the merit accumulated through immeasurable asaṃkhyeya kalpas, he emitted a great radiance. Its light shone and illuminated within and without, pervading the entire territory. When the light touched the king's body, his pain and suffering were eased. The king then said in wonder: "What light is this? Is it the light of the sun and moon, or the light of the devas? When it touches my body, it is like heavenly sandalwood, and the suffering within my body is stilled. I suspect and wonder: could this be my son Siddhārtha, come and manifesting this radiance as an auspicious sign!"

Then King Mahānāman came from outside into the palace and said to the great king: "The World-Honored One has arrived! He has brought his disciples Ānanda, Nanda, and Rāhula, flying through the sky. The king should rejoice and set aside his anguished heart."

When the king heard that the Buddha had come, his reverence and joy were so great that, without realizing it, he sat up. In a moment, the Buddha entered the palace. When the king saw the Buddha arrive, he raised both hands from afar and grasped the Buddha's feet, saying: "May the Tathāgata touch my body with his hand and bring me ease. I am oppressed by illness. It is like being pressed in an oil press; the pain is unbearable. My life is departing. Can it ever return? That today, at the last, I can see the World-Honored One, my grief and regret are removed."

The Buddha saw that the father-king was gravely ill, emaciated, his complexion changed beyond recognition, his body haggard and hard to behold. The Buddha told Nanda: "Observe the king. In former days his body was majestic and imposing, his countenance proper and handsome, his reputation known afar. Now, gravely ill, he is unrecognizable. Where now are his handsome features and his fame for strength and vigor?"

At that time, King Śuddhodana, with single-pointed mind and palms joined, praised the World-Honored One in verse:

> Your vows have been fulfilled,
> And you have also fulfilled the wishes of all beings.
> I am now gravely ill;
> May the Buddha deliver me from this ordeal.
> Adorning the Gautama clan,
> You are supremely extraordinary.
> In the final age you proclaim the right Dharma,
> Serving as protector where there is none.
> The Dharma-King, with the flavor of Dharma,
> Nourishes all beings.
> For people of the future,
> My son is supremely kind and filial.
> The highest treasure among humankind,
> Your fame reaches the great three-thousand worlds.
> Above, even to the Pure Abode Heavens,
> You walk alone, without equal or peer.

The Buddha said: "May the father-king grieve no more! Why? Because your virtue and merit are pure and complete, without any deficiency." The Buddha extended his golden arm from within his robe, his palm like a lotus flower, and placed his hand upon the father-king's forehead: "The king is a person of purity and precept-practice. The defilements of the mind have departed. You should now rejoice and not be troubled. You should truly contemplate the meaning of the sūtras and Dharma. In what is not firm, gain a firm resolve. You have already planted wholesome roots. Therefore, great king, you should rejoice. Though your life is about to end, you may set your mind at ease."

Then King Mahānāman, with a respectful heart, said to King Śuddhodana: "The Buddha is the king's son. His supernatural powers are complete; none can equal him. His second son, Nanda, is also a prince. He has crossed the ocean of birth, death, and desire, and the four paths are unobstructed for him. Droṇodana's son Ānanda has partaken of the flavor of Dharma. The Dharma spoken by the Buddha is like a deep ocean, yet he does not forget a single phrase, retaining it all. The king's grandson Rāhula has pure and complete virtue and merit. He has attained all the dhyānas and realized the four fruits of the path. These four sons have already shattered the net of Māra."

When King Śuddhodana heard these words, his joy was so great that he could not contain himself. With his own hand he took the Buddha's hand and placed it upon his heart. Lying down, he faced upward with palms joined and said to the World-Honored One: "I gaze upon the Tathāgata without blinking; I look and am never sated. My wish is fulfilled. My heart leaps with joy. From this I take my leave. The Tathāgata, the Arhat, has brought immeasurable benefit. All who have been able to see him and hear his teaching: these are people of great marks and great merit. Today the World-Honored One is my son. He has treated me with exceeding care and has not abandoned me."

The king, lying down, joined his palms and reverenced the World-Honored One's feet with his heart. The Buddha's palm was still upon the king's heart. Impermanence arrived; his life was exhausted, his breath ceased, and he suddenly passed to the next world. Thereupon the Śākyas all wailed and wept, throwing themselves upon the ground, striking the earth with both hands, loosening their hair knots and letting their hair fall in disarray. Together they cried: "We have forever lost our protector." Some tore off their necklaces; some ripped their garments; some threw ashes and dirt upon themselves; some pulled out their hair. Some spoke of how the king had governed the country with right statecraft and never wronged the people. Others said: "The minor kingdoms have all lost their protector! The most honored among kings has now passed away, and the country has lost its majestic power."

Then the Śākya sons washed the king's body with many kinds of fragrant water, wrapped it in kārpāsa cloth and various silk fabrics, and placed it in a coffin. They made a lion-throne adorned with the seven treasures, hung nets of pearls around its sides, placed the coffin upon the lion-throne, and scattered flowers and burned incense.

The Buddha and Nanda stood solemnly at the head of the bier. Ānanda and Rāhula stood at the foot. Nanda knelt and said to the Buddha: "The father-king nurtured me. I beg to be allowed to carry the father-king's coffin." Ānanda, with palms joined, came forward and said to the Buddha: "I beg to be allowed to carry my uncle the king's coffin." Rāhula then came forward and said to the Buddha: "I beg to be allowed to carry my grandfather the king's coffin."

At that time, the World-Honored One reflected that in future ages, people would be violent and ungrateful for the kindness of their parents' nurture. For the sake of such unfilial ones, and for the sake of all future beings, to establish the rites and protocols, the Tathāgata himself wished to carry the father-king's coffin. At that moment, the three-thousand great-thousand worlds shook in six ways, and all the mountains heaved and sank, like boats upon water.

At that time, all the devas of the desire realm, with innumerable hundreds of thousands of retinue, all came to attend the funeral. The Northern Heavenly King Vaiśravaṇa, leading the yakṣas, spirits, and others, hundreds of millions strong, came to attend the funeral. The Eastern Heavenly King Dhṛtarāṣṭra, with the gandharvas and spirits, hundreds of millions strong, came to attend the funeral. The Southern Heavenly King Virūḍhaka, with the kumbhāṇḍas and spirits, hundreds of millions strong, came to attend the funeral. The Western Heavenly King Virūpākṣa, with the nāga spirits, hundreds of millions strong, came to attend the funeral. All together they expressed their grief, raising their voices and weeping.

Then the Four Heavenly Kings privately deliberated: "We observe that the World-Honored One, for the sake of all unfilial people in future ages, with great compassion, has personally wished to carry the father-king's coffin." The Four Heavenly Kings then all knelt together and simultaneously said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! We beg to be allowed to carry the father-king's coffin. Why? Because we too are disciples of the Buddha, and we too have heard the Dharma from the Buddha, understood it, gained purity of the Dharma-eye, and attained stream-entry. For this reason, it is fitting that we carry the father-king's coffin."

At that time, the World-Honored One permitted the Four Heavenly Kings to carry the father-king's coffin. The Four Heavenly Kings each transformed their bodies into human form, and with their hands they raised the coffin and bore it upon their shoulders. The entire populace, the whole great assembly, wept without exception.

At that time, the World-Honored One's majestic radiance shone even more brilliantly, like ten thousand suns together. The Tathāgata himself held an incense burner and walked before the bier. They proceeded to the burial site on Vulture Peak Mountain. A thousand arhats, by their supernatural powers, flew through the sky and arrived. They bowed their heads to the Buddha's feet and said: "May the World-Honored One command us in whatever is needed!" The Buddha then told the arhats: "Go quickly to the islands of the great ocean and gather ox-head sandalwood and various fragrant woods." They received the instruction, and in the snap of a finger, each arrived at the ocean and gathered the fragrant firewood. In the time it takes to flex and extend an arm, they had returned.

The Buddha and the great assembly together stacked the fragrant wood, placed the coffin upon it, and set it alight. When the entire assembly saw the fire blaze fiercely, they all threw themselves upon the ground before the Buddha, weeping even more bitterly. Those who had attained the path all felt grateful and fortunate. Those who had not yet attained the path trembled with fear and awe, their hair standing on end.

At that time, the World-Honored One told the assembly: "All in the world is impermanent, suffering, empty, and without self. Nothing is firm. All is like illusions and transformations, like a mirage, like the moon reflected in water. Life does not endure. All of you, do not think that seeing this fire means only that it is hot. The fire of desires is far more intense than this. Therefore, you should exhort yourselves and be diligent. When you are forever free from birth and death, then you will attain great peace." The fire then burned the great king's body.

At that time, the assembled kings each brought five hundred bottles of milk to extinguish the fire. After the fire was quenched, they vied to collect the bones and placed them in a golden casket. Upon that very spot they erected a stūpa, hanging silken banners, canopies, and various kinds of bells, and they made offerings at the stūpa shrine.

Then the entire assembly raised their voices together and said to the Buddha: "The great King Śuddhodana has now passed away. Where has his spirit been reborn? May the World-Honored One explain and distinguish this for us!"

At that time, the World-Honored One told the assembly: "Our father King Śuddhodana was a person of purity. He has been reborn in the Pure Abode Heavens." When the assembly heard these words, they set aside their grief.

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, the devas, nāga spirits, and the Four Heavenly Kings with their retinue, and all the people of the world and the entire great assembly, made obeisance to the Buddha, and each departed.
