# 大乘百福莊嚴相經
## Mahāyāna Sūtra on the Marks Adorned by a Hundredfold Merit

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0662

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Divākara (地婆訶羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavat was in the great city of Śrāvastī, in the Palace of Universal Wonder. In order to guide and transform immeasurable sentient beings, he sat upon a jewel-adorned lion-throne, together with twelve hundred and fifty bhikṣus and a countless assembly of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. All were reverently surrounding him on every side, gazing up at the World-Honored One, their bodies and minds unmoving. At that time, in the assembly there was a great bodhisattva named Mañjuśrī who, empowered by the Buddha's awesome spiritual force, rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, pressed his palms together toward the Buddha, and spoke these words: "World-Honored One! I have heard that the Tathāgata possesses a great accumulation of merit. What is the measure of this great accumulation of merit? May the World-Honored One please explain it for me, so as to benefit immeasurable hundreds of thousands of sentient beings and cause their desires to be fully satisfied."

At that time the World-Honored One addressed Mañjuśrī: "Good man! You have already surpassed all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. With the great compassionate mind of wisdom, you ask the Tathāgata about such a matter on behalf of sentient beings. Listen attentively, listen attentively, and ponder it well. I shall analyze and explain it for you.

"Mañjuśrī! The merit accumulated by the ten wholesome deeds of all sentient beings in one Jambudvīpa: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, a hundredfold of it would constitute the merit of one cakravartin king who rules the four continents with sovereign freedom, fully endowed with the seven treasures and one thousand sons. What are the seven treasures? First, the gold wheel treasure; second, the white elephant treasure; third, the dark-blue horse treasure; fourth, the divine pearl treasure; fifth, the jade maiden treasure; sixth, the treasury minister treasure; seventh, the general treasure. Those thousand sons are each mighty, handsome, and courageous, able to vanquish all enemies. Mañjuśrī! Such is the accumulation of merit possessed by a cakravartin king.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of such a cakravartin king, together with all sentient beings of the four continents: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, a hundredfold of it would constitute the merit of the god Śakra, lord of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of the lord Śakra of the Trāyastriṃśa heaven, together with all sentient beings of the four continents: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, a hundredfold of it would constitute the merit of the Māra king of the sixth desire-heaven who instructs, receives, guards, and upholds.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of the self-sovereign Māra of the sixth heaven, together with all sentient beings of the four continents: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, a hundred-thousandfold of it would constitute the merit of the Brahmā king of the first dhyāna, whose mind of loving-kindness surveys the small chiliocosm.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of the Brahmā king of the first dhyāna whose mind of loving-kindness surveys the small chiliocosm, together with all sentient beings of that world: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, a hundred-thousandfold of it would constitute the merit of the Brahmā king of the second dhyāna who rules the medium chiliocosm.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of the Brahmā king of the second dhyāna who rules the medium chiliocosm, together with all sentient beings of that world: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, a hundred-thousandfold of it would constitute the merit of Maheśvara within the fourth dhyāna, whose mind of loving-kindness surveys the great trichiliocosm.

"Mañjuśrī! Maheśvara, sovereign of the great trichiliocosm, is not one accomplished through meager roots of merit. Why? Because Maheśvara possesses great wisdom and great awesome spiritual power. When the fire of cosmic destruction has burned the vessel-world and it is about to be reconstituted, from the fourth dhyāna heaven a great rain descends, lasting five intermediate kalpas without cease. The water extends above the entire great trichiliocosm up to the brahmā-world, without any deficiency. The great lord Maheśvara of the trichiliocosm can know every one of these raindrops.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit possessed by Maheśvara in this way: such an accumulation of merit is called brahmā-merit.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of the lord Maheśvara of the great trichiliocosm, together with all sentient beings of that world: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, a hundred-thousandfold of immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times that would constitute the merit of one independently arisen great pratyekabuddha.

"Mañjuśrī! Setting aside such a great trichiliocosm: suppose that in all the buddha-worlds of the ten directions, the accumulation of merit of all sentient beings together with all pratyekabuddhas were calculated and measured, and multiplied by immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times: that would constitute the merit of one bodhisattva in their final birth-body.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of such a bodhisattva in their final birth-body, together with all sentient beings in all the worlds throughout the ten directions to the limits of space, whether born from eggs, born from wombs, born from moisture, or born by transformation, whether with form or without form, whether with perception or without perception, or neither with perception nor without perception: if the accumulation of merit of all those sentient beings were calculated and measured, and multiplied by immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times, that would constitute the merit of a single hair-pore of the Tathāgata's body. Mañjuśrī! The hair-pores of the Tathāgata's body number ninety-nine thousand, and each one of those pores is endowed with the immeasurable accumulation of merit described above.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of all the Tathāgata's hair-pores: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, and multiplied by immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times, that would constitute the merit of one among the Tathāgata's minor marks.

"Mañjuśrī! The minor marks of the Tathāgata's body, briefly stated, number eighty. What are the eighty? First, the crown of the head is full and perfect. Second, the hairline is well defined. Third, the hair is dark blue. Fourth, the hair is fragrant. Fifth, the hair is exceedingly soft. Sixth, the hair is never tangled. Seventh, the hair is neither sparse nor thick. Eighth, the hair constantly grows. Ninth, the hair is wavy at the roots. Tenth, the hair-tips curl in spirals. Eleventh, the hair forms wheel-like patterns. Twelfth, the hair resembles auspicious characters. Thirteenth, the face is even and regular. Fourteenth, the forehead tuft is full and complete. Fifteenth, the eyebrows are dark blue. Sixteenth, the eyebrows are never disordered. Seventeenth, both eyes are beautiful. Eighteenth, both eyes are long and wide. Nineteenth, both eyes are clear and pure. Twentieth, both eyes are bright and luminous. Twenty-first, the color of the eyes is deep blue, like an utpala lotus. Twenty-second, the ears are long and fine. Twenty-third, the ears have no deficiency. Twenty-fourth, the ears have no flaw. Twenty-fifth, the nose is high, straight, and refined. Twenty-sixth, both cheeks are full. Twenty-seventh, the cheeks have no deficiency. Twenty-eighth, the cheeks have no flaw. Twenty-ninth, the teeth are perfectly round and regular. Thirtieth, the teeth are even and equal. Thirty-first, the lip-color is beautifully red, like a bimba fruit. Thirty-second, the tongue is red and soft. Thirty-third, the voice is like thunder. Thirty-fourth, the tone is clear and resonant. Thirty-fifth, the body is fully complete. Thirty-sixth, the flesh of the body is rich and fine. Thirty-seventh, the flesh of the body is even and regular. Thirty-eighth, the flesh of the body is soft. Thirty-ninth, the body gradually tapers in symmetry. Fortieth, the proportions of the body are well matched. Forty-first, the body is perfectly round and fine. Forty-second, the body has no deficiency. Forty-third, the body is soft and supple. Forty-fourth, the body is clean and pure. Forty-fifth, the body is light and delicate. Forty-sixth, the body does not sway. Forty-seventh, the body is supremely dignified. Forty-eighth, the body is free from blemish. Forty-ninth, the body's radiance dispels darkness. Fiftieth, the belly is beautiful. Fifty-first, the belly is round and full. Fifty-second, the belly does not protrude. Fifty-third, the navel is deep and concealed. Fifty-fourth, the navel is not crooked. Fifty-fifth, the navel is in proper proportion. Fifty-sixth, the underarms are smooth and full. Fifty-seventh, the forearms are slender and long. Fifty-eighth, the fingers are round and complete. Fifty-ninth, the fingers are slender and beautiful. Sixtieth, the lines of the hands are deep and fine. Sixty-first, the lines of the hands run through clearly. Sixty-second, the lines of the hands are not disordered. Sixty-third, the lines of the hands are lustrous. Sixty-fourth, the lines are neither coarse nor fine. Sixty-fifth, the lines taper to a fine point. Sixty-sixth, the kneecaps are round and broad. Sixty-seventh, the heels are smooth and full. Sixty-eighth, the feet press firmly on the ground. Sixty-ninth, the gait turns to the right. Seventieth, the gait is like an elephant king's. Seventy-first, the gait is like a bull king's. Seventy-second, the gait is like a goose king's. Seventy-third, the stride is as mighty as a lion king's. Seventy-fourth, the tips of the nails on the hands and feet are slightly raised. Seventy-fifth, the nails of the hands and feet are the color of red copper. Seventy-sixth, the nails of the hands and feet are all lustrous. Seventy-seventh, the sinews and veins are not visible. Seventy-eighth, the joints are tightly compact. Seventy-ninth, the sense faculties are undefiled. Eightieth, all who see him are delighted. Mañjuśrī! As described above, these eighty are called the merit of the Tathāgata's minor marks.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of the Tathāgata's eighty minor marks: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, and multiplied by immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times, that would constitute the merit of one among the eighty subsidiary marks on the Tathāgata's hands, feet, and other areas. Mañjuśrī! These subsidiary marks on the Tathāgata's hands, feet, and other areas number eighty. What are the eighty? First, the image of Brahmā. Second, the image of Śakra. Third, the image of Dhṛtarāṣṭra. Fourth, the image of Virūḍhaka. Fifth, the image of Virūpākṣa. Sixth, the image of Vaiśravaṇa. Seventh, the image of Śrī Devī. Eighth, the image of the sun deva-prince. Ninth, the image of the moon deva-prince. Tenth, the image of the water deva. Eleventh, the image of the fire deva. Twelfth, the image of the wind deva. Thirteenth, the image of the cloud deva. Fourteenth, the image of the great ṛṣi. Fifteenth, the image of the king of mountains. Sixteenth, the image of a boy. Seventeenth, the image of a girl. Eighteenth, the image of a jeweled banner. Nineteenth, the image of a parasol. Twentieth, the image of a jeweled crown. Twenty-first, the image of a flower garland. Twenty-second, the image of a pearl necklace. Twenty-third, the image of an earring. Twenty-fourth, the image of an armlet. Twenty-fifth, the image of a jeweled bracelet. Twenty-sixth, the image of a finger ring. Twenty-seventh, the image of a jeweled mirror. Twenty-eighth, the image of a white whisk. Twenty-ninth, the image of an auspicious character. Thirtieth, the image of a flower vase. Thirty-first, the image of a maṇi jewel. Thirty-second, the image of a jeweled sword. Thirty-third, the image of a vajra pestle. Thirty-fourth, the image of a bow. Thirty-fifth, the image of an arrow. Thirty-sixth, the image of a halberd. Thirty-seventh, the image of a lance. Thirty-eighth, the image of a battle-axe. Thirty-ninth, the image of a lasso. Fortieth, the image of a long hook. Forty-first, the image of a wheel-blade. Forty-second, the image of a golden hammer. Forty-third, the image of a celestial mace. Forty-fourth, the image of a celestial drum. Forty-fifth, the image of a celestial conch. Forty-sixth, the image of a waist-drum. Forty-seventh, the image of a flower-wheel. Forty-eighth, the image of a palace. Forty-ninth, the image of a jeweled throne. Fiftieth, the image of a bathing pool. Fifty-first, the image of a lotus. Fifty-second, the image of milled rice. Fifty-third, the image of barley. Fifty-fourth, the image of medicinal herbs. Fifty-fifth, the image of sacred kuśa grass. Fifty-sixth, the image of a flowering tree. Fifty-seventh, the image of a fruit tree. Fifty-eighth, the image of a garuḍa. Fifty-ninth, the image of a kalaviṅka bird. Sixtieth, the image of a jīvajīvaka bird. Sixty-first, the image of a peacock. Sixty-second, the image of a dove. Sixty-third, the image of a goose king. Sixty-fourth, the image of a blue bird. Sixty-fifth, the image of a parrot. Sixty-sixth, the image of a kingfisher. Sixty-seventh, the image of a lion within a wheel. Sixty-eighth, the image of a snow-mountain white elephant. Sixty-ninth, the image of a nāga king. Seventieth, the image of an elephant king. Seventy-first, the image of a horse king. Seventy-second, the image of a deer king. Seventy-third, the image of a bull king. Seventy-fourth, the image of a wild ox. Seventy-fifth, the image of a cow. Seventy-sixth, the image of a ram. Seventy-seventh, the image of a great sea-turtle. Seventy-eighth, the image of a great tortoise. Seventy-ninth, the image of a fish king. Eightieth, the image of a conch king. Mañjuśrī! As described above, these eighty are called the merit of the Tathāgata's subsidiary marks.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of the Tathāgata's eighty subsidiary marks: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, and multiplied by immeasurable hundreds of thousands of times, that would constitute the merit of one among the Tathāgata's thirty-two marks of a great person. Mañjuśrī! These marks of a great person of the Tathāgata number thirty-two. What are the thirty-two marks of a great person? First, the crown of the head has a fleshy protuberance, round, fine, and lofty. Second, the hair is deep blue, and each strand curls to the right. Third, the forehead is broad, level, and well formed. Fourth, the ūrṇā between the eyebrows is whiter than snow-white shell. Fifth, the eyelashes are dark and fine, like those of a bull king. Sixth, the mouth has forty teeth, neither more nor less. Seventh, the teeth are closely set, with no gaps. Eighth, the teeth are white and pure, free from any discoloration. Ninth, the mouth has four canine teeth of brilliant color. Tenth, the jaw is round and full, like a lion king's. Eleventh, the tongue is soft, thin, broad, and red. Twelfth, one obtains the supreme taste among all flavors. Thirteenth, the voice resounds like thunder with the brahmā-tone. Fourteenth, the collarbones are not visible, that area being smooth and full. Fifteenth, both shoulders are round, upright, and without deficiency. Sixteenth, both arms hang down so that the hands reach the knees. Seventeenth, the upper body is like a lion king's. Eighteenth, every hair on the body points upward. Nineteenth, the body is full and round, like a nyagrodha tree. Twentieth, the body is tall and majestic, measuring seven cubits. Twenty-first, the skin of the entire body is golden in color. Twenty-second, each hair-pore produces one hair. Twenty-third, the male organ is concealed and hidden from view. Twenty-fourth, both thighs are round and full of flesh. Twenty-fifth, the calves are fine and shapely, like those of an eṇeya deer king. Twenty-sixth, both feet are rich and full, without any deficiency. Twenty-seventh, the soles of both feet are entirely flat. Twenty-eighth, the skin, bones, and flesh of the feet are all prominently raised. Twenty-ninth, both hands and both feet are all soft and supple. Thirtieth, both hands and both feet have webbing between the fingers and toes. Thirty-first, both hands and both feet are all slender and long. Thirty-second, both hands and both feet all bear wheel-marks. Mañjuśrī! As described above, these thirty-two marks of a great person are called the merit of the Tathāgata's major marks.

"Mañjuśrī! The accumulation of merit of the Tathāgata's thirty-two marks of a great person: if such an accumulation of merit were calculated and measured, and multiplied by immeasurable asaṃkhyeya times, incalculable times, inconceivable times, then and only then would it constitute the merit of the Tathāgata's great dharma-conch, the voice that teaches and guides all sentient beings in accordance with their kind.

"Mañjuśrī! This great dharma-conch of the Tathāgata, in accordance with the inclinations of all sentient beings in immeasurable, boundless, asaṃkhyeya, and immeasurable worlds, can fully satisfy them in accordance with their inclinations. In the voice appropriate to each kind, the Tathāgata teaches and guides, benefiting and bringing happiness to these sentient beings.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as the Tathāgata's voice possesses such immeasurable power, so too does the Tathāgata's awesome radiance possess equally immeasurable power.

"Mañjuśrī! Just as the Tathāgata's awesome radiance possesses such immeasurable power, so too does the Tathāgata's body possess equally immeasurable power.

"Mañjuśrī! As described above, such an accumulation of merit is inconceivable and incalculable. It is not something that śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, or others can attain. Why? Because such an accumulation of merit arises from great wisdom, great compassion, and the supreme vow-power that pervades all. Therefore, śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and others cannot attain it.

"Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata's marvelous body of form is entirely established through the purest generosity, precept-keeping, meditation, and two causes. What are these two? First, the Tathāgata's supreme vow-power. Second, the Tathāgata's skillful guidance. What is meant by the Tathāgata's skillful guidance? It means causing sentient beings who have not yet planted roots of merit to plant them, causing those who have already planted roots of merit to bring them to maturity, and causing those whose roots are already mature to attain liberation. Through the power of these two causes, the marvelous body of form is attained. Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata's marvelous body of form is accomplished through these two causes; the Tathāgata's voice is likewise accomplished through these two causes.

"Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata's voice is accomplished through these two causes; the Tathāgata's awesome radiance is likewise accomplished through these two causes.

"Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata's awesome radiance is accomplished through these two causes; the Tathāgata's dharma-teaching is likewise accomplished through these two causes.

"Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata's dharma-teaching is accomplished through these two causes; the Tathāgata's conduct is likewise accomplished through these two causes.

"Mañjuśrī! The Tathāgata appears in the world out of compassion for sentient beings, appears in the world to benefit sentient beings, and appears in the world to bring happiness to sentient beings. Because the natures, inclinations, and aspirations of sentient beings are many and varied, each differing from the next, the Tathāgata, in accordance with their inclinations, manifests various bodies adorned with marks and excellences, teaching and guiding them through the dharma, causing them to be disciplined and to enter the buddha-dharma, bringing them to accomplishment."

At that time the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Mañjuśrī, hearing the Buddha's teaching on the accumulation of merit as described above, for the benefit of all devas and humans, spoke these words: "World-Honored One! Today I have obtained great excellent benefit. Today I have obtained what is greatly supreme. Today I have obtained what is without equal. Today I have obtained great auspiciousness. I contemplate that the Tathāgata, in the midst of the world, serves as the great refuge for all sentient beings: pure and unmoving, like empty space, difficult to encounter, rare to meet, and yet today I have seen him."

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, all the bhikṣus, the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, and the assembly pressed their palms together with joy, faithfully received the teaching, and put it into practice.
