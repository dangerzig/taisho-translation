# 佛說如來成道經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Tathāgata's Attainment of the Way

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2890

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

> His feet tread the wheel of a thousand blessings; his moving body is adorned like a vessel.
> Upon his chest, the svastika-mark is clearly and distinctly visible.
> Behind him, the halo radiates brilliance, bright and full.
> His lion-king cheeks bear hairs that grow in spiraling rightward curls.
> He extends his broad long tongue, which covers all the dark realms everywhere,
> Illuminating without limit, surpassing the brightness of the sun.
> Those who behold the Buddha's light are forever freed from death and rebirth.

> The Tathāgata: in parinirvāṇa, yet does not die; in nirvāṇa, yet is not born.
> Stirred, yet not made turbid; settled, yet not clarified.
> Borne up, yet not heavy; marked upon, yet not diminished.
> Dark, yet not obscure; revealed, yet not obvious.
> As for fame, he does not cling to it; as for gain, he does not contend.
> Insulted, yet not indignant; honored, yet not elated.
> Dispersed, yet not destroyed; gathered, yet not merged into one.
> Exalted, yet not imperiled; lowly, yet level.
> Flanked, yet without companion; vast, yet distinctly constituted.
> Slain, yet does not die; alive, yet is not born.
> White-haired, yet not old; young, yet not an infant.
> Look upon him, yet you cannot see his form; listen to him, yet you cannot hear his voice.

> His great body fills the eight extremities, pervading the vast expanse of empty space;
> When small, a horse gallops within the eye of a needle,
> His form concealed within a mote of dust.
> The ocean transforms into ghee and curds;
> He points to the ground, and it becomes lapis lazuli and quartz crystal.
> He grasps a mountain and instantly and fully knows its weight;
> The entire ocean is turned into an empty pit.
> The number of motes of dust can be reckoned;
> His mind knows all sentient beings.
> Heavenly palaces and lofty towers; a point of his finger transforms them into conjured cities.
> Whatever is needed, all is obtained without effort or exertion.
> In movement, vast and unimpeded; in stillness, deep and unwavering, never tilting.
> With the flesh eye alone, he perceives the affairs of the three ages;
> Employing the five eyes, he constantly abides as if blind.
> There is no sphere of not-not-thought; there is no sentiment of "this is, this is."
> He transcends the sphere of the three vehicles;
> He surpasses the sentient beings who traverse the six pāramitās.
> His mighty roar shakes all with awesome force, yet causes no fear or fright.
> Grasped, yet not clung to; observed, yet having form.
> Without origin, appearing and disappearing; moving and revolving, naturally at peace.
> When the eyes are closed, vision extends ten thousand leagues;
> When the eyes are opened, it ceases.

> This is the non-mark that can be marked, the non-name that can be named.
> Burned, yet not reduced to ash; submerged, yet not mired in mud.
> On a platform, yet does not rise; pressed down, yet not crushed.
> Picking up an object, he feels no joy; losing an object, he sheds no tears of sorrow.
> In the midst of the multitude's rejoicing, he does not rejoice;
> Alone, he does not feel desolate.
> Cut with a blade, he harbors no resentment;
> Attended with medicine and treatment, he is not conventionally healed.
> Both this mind and that mind are of a single kind: equanimity, immovably and perfectly apportioned.
> His body manifests in a hundred billion places, present everywhere equally.
> One name is "the Guide of the Lost"; the second name is "Tathāgata."

> When one praises him, the earth shakes and thunder rolls through the heavens;
> All disciples and assemblies each return to their proper refuge.

> If one has faith in this, heaven will surely shelter them;
> If one lacks faith, they will suffer cold with no clothing.
> If one copies one scroll, the household will be wealthy with abundant riches;
> If one copies two scrolls, wisdom will open and intelligence be sharpened.
> Teaching others to read and recite it, unexpected illness will not come.

From the sky, a voice of praise declares: "Inconceivable!"

---

**Translator's notes:** This is a Chinese apocryphal text of uncertain provenance, composed in rhythmic four-character phrases throughout. The opening section enumerates several of the thirty-two physical marks (*lakṣaṇa*) of a great man, including the thousand-spoked wheel on the soles of the feet, the svastika on the chest, the halo, the lion-jaw physiognomy, and the broad long tongue. The central body is a sequence of antinomies describing the Tathāgata's transcendence of all paired opposites. The phrase 槃而不死，涅而不生 deliberately splits the compound 涅槃 (*nirvāṇa*) across two parallel clauses. The closing section is a merit-transfer colophon of the type common to Chinese devotional apocrypha, promising rewards for copying and recitation.
