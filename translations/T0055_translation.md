## 苦陰因事經

I see and know as things truly are that sensual pleasures give rise to evil and unwholesome states, and I abide not in unwholesome conduct born of sensual desire; I dwell in the refuge of the unsurpassed peace as my ease and happiness. "Great warrior, on one occasion I was staying at Rājagṛha, at the Saptaparṇa Cave on the slope of the Vediyaka Mountain. Great warrior, rising in the late afternoon, I went along the slope nearby and from a distance I beheld some Nigaṇṭhas who were always kneeling, never sitting, undergoing extreme austerities. I approached them and said: 'Why is it, Nigaṇṭhas, that you are always kneeling, never sitting, subjecting yourselves to such extreme austerities?'

"They answered: 'Gotama, our teacher Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta has declared: "You Nigaṇṭhas, in the past you committed evil deeds; now by practicing these austerities you shall burn away those past evil deeds. As for your present conduct of body, speech, and mind — wherever evil is present, you must not commit it."'

"I said to them: 'Tell me, Nigaṇṭhas — are you able to have confidence in your teacher Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta and abide by his teaching? Do you not doubt your teacher?'

"They said: 'That is so, Gotama. We have confidence in and abide by our teacher Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta; we do not doubt our teacher.'

"I replied to them: 'It is so. Yet consider this, Nigaṇṭhas: there is a Nigaṇṭha who in the past committed evil deeds and who now practices extreme austerities. When that Nigaṇṭha dies and is reborn among humans, he will once again find himself among you Nigaṇṭhas, practicing just as you do — always kneeling, never sitting, performing austerities. So will it be with all of you.'

"They said: 'Gotama, it is not through virtuous conduct that one obtains good fortune. King Bimbisāra dwells in happiness and ease; you, Śramaṇa Gotama, cannot equal that.'

"'Nigaṇṭhas, in saying this you have spoken wrongly and incorrectly. Why so? It is the foolish and ignorant — those who are unsteady, unskillful, without restraint and without contentment — who say: "King Bimbisāra constantly abides in happiness and ease; you, Śramaṇa Gotama, cannot equal that." Nigaṇṭhas, you should first make clear in what sense I am said to constantly abide in happiness, before making comparisons between King Bimbisāra and Śramaṇa Gotama. Let me say to you, Nigaṇṭhas: as for my abiding in happiness and ease — is it not the equal of King Bimbisāra of Magadha? Is it not rather you who should be saying: "King Bimbisāra of Magadha constantly abides in happiness and ease; you, Śramaṇa Gotama, cannot equal that"?'

"'Śramaṇa Gotama, we now ask you: who abides in superior happiness and ease — King Bimbisāra of Magadha, or Śramaṇa Gotama?'

"'What do you think, Nigaṇṭhas? Is King Bimbisāra of Magadha able to be fully at ease in mind and speech? Can he experience uninterrupted bodily happiness for seven days and seven nights?'

"'No indeed, Gotama.'

"'What of six days, five days, four days, three days, two days, or one day and one night — can he be at ease in mind and speech and experience uninterrupted bodily happiness?'

"'No indeed, Gotama.'

"'What do you think, Nigaṇṭhas? Am I able to be at ease in mind and speech? Do I experience genuine happiness and ease in body for one day and one night?'

"'Yes indeed, Gotama.'

"'What of two days, three days, on up to seven days and seven nights — am I able to be at ease in mind and speech? Is my bodily experience one of uninterrupted genuine happiness and ease?'

"'Yes indeed, Gotama.'

"'What do you think, Nigaṇṭhas? Between the two of us, who is it who constantly abides in superior happiness and ease — King Bimbisāra of Magadha, or I?'

"'From what you, Śramaṇa Gotama, have said, we understand that it is Śramaṇa Gotama who abides in superior happiness and ease, not King Bimbisāra of Magadha.'

"Great warrior, seeing sensual pleasures as yielding little, knowing them to contain much suffering and ruin — the noble disciple who is unable to see with wisdom, as things truly are, that sensual pleasures give rise to evil and unwholesome states, and who does not enter into the delight of what is called the unsurpassed peace — such, great warrior, is the noble disciple who remains bound up with sensual pleasures.

"Moreover, great warrior, as for me: I see sensual pleasures as yielding little, knowing them to be full of much suffering and ruin. I see with right wisdom, as things truly are, that sensual pleasures give rise to unwholesome states; I abide not in the unwholesome born of sensual desire but dwell in the protection of the unsurpassed peace as my own happiness. Thus it is, great warrior, that I am not bound up with sensual pleasures."

Thus spoke the Bhagavat. The great warrior and the bhikṣus, having heard what the Bhagavat had said, rejoiced and were glad.
