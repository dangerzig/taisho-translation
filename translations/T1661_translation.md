# Treatise on the Bodhicitta Free from Characteristics

## *Pútíxīn Líxiàng Lùn* (菩提心離相論)

Taishō 1661

Composed by Bodhisattva Nāgārjuna (*Lóngshù*, 龍樹). Translated by Shīhù (*Shīhù*, 施護) of the Song dynasty.

*Note: This is a substantial Mahāyāna philosophical treatise attributed to Nāgārjuna, in which the nature of bodhicitta (the mind of awakening) is analyzed as being free from all characteristics (signless), unborn, and empty. The text systematically examines the aggregates, sense-bases, and elements through a Yogācāra-influenced Madhyamaka lens, arguing that all phenomena are consciousness-only manifestations without external referents, and that bodhicitta is ultimately identical with emptiness, suchness, and the ultimate truth. The treatise also includes a significant section on compassion and bodhisattva practice, arguing that the realization of emptiness, far from leading to quietism, is the very basis for active engagement in liberating sentient beings. The final section discusses the identity of the prajñāpāramitā with the bodhicitta itself.*

---

Homage to all buddhas! I shall now briefly explain the meaning of the bodhicitta. With utmost sincerity I bow to that bodhicitta, which is like a valiant army wielding victorious weapons; its meaning is the same [as that image]. That great bodhicitta is what all buddhas, world-honored ones, and all bodhisattva-mahāsattvas relied upon in generating the bodhicitta; my generation of the bodhicitta is likewise accomplished through it, all the way to sitting at the place of awakening and attaining the fruit of perfect enlightenment, this mind remaining firm. Moreover, this bodhicitta is the dhāraṇī practice-gate of all bodhisattvas; it is contemplated and generated in this way.

I now praise and explain the bodhicitta in order to cause all sentient beings to cease the suffering of saṃsāra: those not yet ferried across are universally enabled to cross; those not yet liberated are enabled to attain liberation; those not yet at peace are enabled to attain peace; those not yet in nirvāṇa are enabled to attain nirvāṇa. In order to fulfill such a supreme aspiration, and because it establishes the proper cause of its own essential nature, and because it enters the contemplation of the true reality of the ultimate meaning: the bodhicitta has the self-characteristic of non-arising. This is why I now explain it.

### The Bodhicitta Is Free from All Natures

What is called the bodhicitta is free from all natures.

**Question:** In what way is it free from all natures?

**Answer:** The aggregates, sense-bases, and elements are free from grasping and rejecting. The equality of the selflessness of dharmas means that one's own mind is originally unborn, because its own nature is empty.

What is the "self" spoken of here? The aggregates and other [categories] have referential indications, yet the discriminating mind appears before us without substance. Therefore, if one constantly and clearly comprehends the bodhicitta, one can abide in the empty characteristic of all dharmas.

Furthermore, the constantly comprehended bodhicitta takes great compassion as its essence, with the compassionate mind contemplating through great compassion. For this reason, within the aggregates no characteristic of a self can be obtained.

There are non-Buddhists who engage in practices of non-corresponding activities, grasping characteristics and discriminating, claiming that the aggregates exist and are not impermanent dharmas. But in truth, no self-characteristic can be obtained from them. In the true and real nature that upholds all dharmas, one cannot grasp permanence, nor is there impermanence. Within my aggregates, even the name has no reality; how much less could there be an agent or any discrimination?

If one says there is one dharma, or even says there are many dharmas, such speech is the turning of the worldly mind, following worldly conduct. That is a non-corresponding, permanent characteristic. This meaning is not so. Therefore, know that all dharmas are without nature, whether internal or external, and cannot be discriminated. What cause does the grasping mind have, such that it cannot be separated from worldly characteristics? If cause and characteristic are not different, then this is neither permanent nor a grasping agent. Know that the mind's nature cannot be grasped as permanent; therefore, its nature of impermanence is itself the permanent. If one knows that nature is impermanent, what action is performed, and from where does the grasping of self-characteristics arise? If one is free from the worldly, then within the aggregates there is no obstruction; the sense-bases and elements are comprehended in the same way, and the two dharmas of grasping and rejecting cannot be obtained.

### The Five Aggregates

The term "aggregate" (*yùn*, 蘊) here means form, sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness. These are called the five aggregates, in which the śrāvaka practitioners train. Furthermore, know that: form is like a mass of foam; sensation is like a bubble; perception is like a mirage; formations are like a plantain tree; and consciousness is like a magical person. The meaning of these five aggregates was taught by the Buddha, the Honored One of Two-Footed Beings, to the bodhisattvas as appropriate.

The form aggregate, briefly indicating its characteristics, consists of the four great elements and what they produce, and is called the form aggregate. The non-form [aggregates] are the remaining three: sensation, perception, and formations. The consciousness aggregate should be known from the teachings. Formations and perception will be explained below.

"Sense-bases" (*chù*, 處) here means the internal sense-bases of the eye and so forth, and the external sense-bases of form and so forth: these are called the twelve sense-bases.

"Elements" (*jiè*, 界) here means the elements of the eye-faculty and so forth, the elements of eye-consciousness and so forth, and the objective elements of form and so forth: these are called the eighteen elements.

These aggregates, sense-bases, and elements are free from grasping and rejecting, without direction, without parts, and cannot be discriminated. The view of those who engage in discriminative seeing is not correct; as soon as discrimination arises, there is attachment.

How then can they become corresponding? If one sees external objects with any characteristic, know that this [seeing] is overturned by the destruction of wisdom.

How should one understand the statement about nurturing the form of the mental faculty? Know that it is neither one nor different.

There are non-Buddhists such as the Parimalejaka (*Bōlǐ mò luó rě jiā*, 波哩沒囉惹迦) and others who, following various heterodox views, give rise to three kinds of discrimination. This meaning is incorrect. It is like a person in a dream committing acts of killing: what is done has no real characteristic of action. And like a person in a dream dwelling in the highest position: this also has no characteristic of a superior action. Why? Because the luminosity of consciousness destroys the characteristics of grasping and rejecting. The dharma of consciousness being thus, what external referent exists? Therefore, all dharmas have no external referent.

Know that all the manifestations of form-characteristics are the illumination of one's own consciousness, and the form-characteristics shine forth [from it]. It is like a person seeing a magical creation, a mirage, or a gandharva city and taking it to be real. Those without wisdom, with their foolish and grasping minds, view form and other [sense-objects] as real in just the same way, and through this, the self-attachment is the turning of this mind.

As previously explained regarding the meaning of the aggregates, sense-bases, and elements, know that these are free from the distinctions of their various divisions. They are merely posited by the mind's divisions and aspects; all the various characteristics are solely manifested by the mind. This meaning is established, as explained in the *Vijñaptimātratā* (*Chéng Wéishí*, 成唯識).

**Question:** It was stated earlier that there are five aggregates. What is the self-characteristic of consciousness?

**Answer:** As the meaning of "mind" (*xīn*) is explained, so too is "consciousness" (*shí*). As the Buddha, the World-Honored One, constantly says: "Know that all things are solely manifested by the mind." This meaning is extremely profound; because the foolish cannot comprehend it, they do not see the truth.

Therefore, if one can empty the self-characteristic, then in that mind no discrimination arises. Those who engage in discrimination do so because of wrong teachings; what they establish does not hold. Those who see according to reality see the selflessness of dharmas. This is the meaning of the selflessness of dharmas in the Mahāyāna: because one's own mind is originally unborn, whatever arises is also equal. One's own mind, in its supreme aspect, enters the true meaning, because it is produced from the practice-gate of yoga.

Here one should know: the subsequent basis has no real substance; this is called the manifest activity of the pure mind. If it is a past dharma, the past has no reality. If it is a future dharma, the future has not yet arrived. If it is a present dharma, the present does not abide. In which of the three times does one abide? Like an army or a forest, being constituted by many dharmas, know that consciousness has the characteristic of non-self; that consciousness also is not a basis.

If one has seen dharmas in this way, it is like red clouds swiftly scattering and vanishing. Therefore know: if a dharma exists, it appears from thought; the ālayavijñāna (*àlàiyé shí*, 阿賴耶識) is likewise. Sentient beings, whether coming or going, are naturally like this. Just as the great ocean is where all streams converge, the basis of the ālayavijñāna is the same.

If one contemplates that consciousness in this way, there can be no discriminating mind arising. If each individually knows according to reality, how can those various names be spoken? If each individually knows the nature of things, then each individually cannot describe it. One who speaks thus speaks a definitive statement. Therefore all dharmas are also definitely produced, turning and accomplishing with respect to all affairs. The knower and the known are two distinctions. If the known does not exist, how is the knower established? Both are without reality; how can the dharma be obtained?

Therefore know: what is called "mind" is merely a name, and that name also has nothing separate to be obtained, being only a referential indication. The self-nature of that name also cannot be obtained. For this reason, the wise should contemplate the self-nature of the bodhicitta as being like an illusion: whether internal, external, or in between, it cannot be found. There is no dharma to grasp and no dharma to reject. It is not visible as shape or color, not expressible as appearance. It is not the characteristic of male or female or eunuch. It does not abide in any form-characteristic. There is no dharma to be seen; it is not the domain of the eye.

Only all buddhas contemplate equally. Whether the mind has self-nature or is without self-nature, in the equal dharma, how can it be seen? What is called "nature" is so named because of discrimination. If one is free from discrimination, the mind's nature is altogether empty.

If there were a mind that could be seen through discrimination, how could one call it "empty" here? Therefore know: there is no knower and nothing known. If one can contemplate the bodhicitta in this way, one sees the Tathāgata.

If there is a knower and something known, then the bodhicitta cannot be established. Therefore it is without characteristics and without arising, and cannot be praised by the path of words.

### The Bodhicitta as Space

Furthermore, the bodhicitta is like space. Mind and space both lack any dual characteristic. This is the teaching of the equality of mind-emptiness and emptiness-wisdom, the supernatural powers of all buddhas, which are no different from buddha to buddha. All the activities of the buddhas of the three times are entirely contained and stored within the realm of bodhi. Although all those dharmas are contained and stored, they are constantly quiescent.

One should also contemplate that these are impermanent dharmas, like magical illusions, not what is contained and stored. They tame the three realms of existence, because they abide in the dharma of emptiness. All non-arising is called emptiness; all non-self is also called emptiness. If one takes non-arising and non-self as the contemplation of emptiness, that contemplation does not succeed.

If one discriminates the two kinds, defiled and pure, one falls into the two views of eternalism and annihilationism. If one says one contemplates emptiness with wisdom, that emptiness also has no separate substance. Therefore the bodhicitta is free from all objects of apprehension and abides in the characteristic of space.

If one contemplates space as one's abiding place, then two names should apply: "space" and "nature." Therefore know: "emptiness" is like a lion's roar in the world, which frightens all beasts. The single word "emptiness" silences all speech. Therefore know: everywhere is constantly quiescent; all things are empty.

Furthermore, the dharma of consciousness is an impermanent dharma, born from impermanence. That nature of impermanence is itself the bodhicitta. This teaching of emptiness is also not contradictory.

If the nature of impermanence is itself the bodhicitta, then one who delights in bodhi has an equal mind, and yet one does not speak of delighting in that emptiness. The mind that grasps at emptiness: how can it be obtained? Know that the original, inherent, true reality accomplishes the entire meaning of the bodhicitta.

Furthermore, know that things are without self-nature; the nature of being without self-nature is what this teaching means. What is the mind of which this speaks? If one is free from the self of persons and the self of dharmas, the mind does not abide. This is neither one dharma nor many dharmas; each self-nature is itself free from self-nature. Just as sugar and honey have sweetness as their self-nature, and fire has heat as its self-nature, the self-nature of the emptiness of all dharmas is the same.

The nature of those dharmas is neither eternal nor annihilated, neither obtained nor separated. For this reason, with ignorance as the first and old age and death as the last, the dharmas established by the links of dependent arising are like dreams and illusions, their essence also without reality. Therefore they are called the twelve-linked dharma; they are also called the wheel of the twelve links, revolving through the gates of birth-and-death. Yet in reality there is no self and no separate sentient being, no differentiation of the three karmas and their fruits.

If within this one comprehends the dharma of dependent arising, one can then escape through the gates of all realms of experience. That is not a characteristic of activity, nor does it destroy the correct cause, because it is born from the aggregates. The final limit of saṃsāra is not a characteristic of activity; all is without holder, born from utter emptiness. Dharmas are equal in producing causes and receiving fruits: this is what the Buddha teaches. All dharmas arise from the assemblage of their class, like striking a drum produces sound, like planting wheat produces sprouts. The meaning of the assemblage of dharmas is the same: like illusions, like dreams, manifested by dependent arising.

Dharmas arise from causes and yet are also unborn. Since the causes are themselves empty, what is produced? Therefore know: all dharmas are unborn, and this non-arising is called emptiness. As stated regarding the five aggregates: the nature of the aggregates is equal, and all dharmas should be contemplated in the same way.

If one speaks of emptiness as one speaks of the truly real, the emptiness that is spoken of is not annihilation in essence. Within the non-annihilated essence, nothing real can be obtained either. To call the essence empty, and emptiness also without essence.

If one comprehends that there is no reality, then the agent is impermanent. The afflictions and karma are accumulated as its essence, and that karma is also born from the mind. If the mind has no abiding place, how can karma be obtained? The happy mind is of a quiescent nature; that quiescent mind cannot be grasped. Those with wisdom can truly contemplate; seeing the truth, they attain liberation.

### The Bodhicitta as Supreme Reality

Furthermore, the bodhicitta is the supreme reality. This meaning of reality is called emptiness; it is also called true suchness (*zhēnrú*, 真如); it is also called the limit of reality (*shíjì*, 實際). This is the signless ultimate truth (*dìyī yì dì*, 第一義諦).

If one does not comprehend the meaning of emptiness in this way, know that such a person does not belong to the portion of liberation, and in the cycle of saṃsāra is greatly deluded, revolving through the six destinies.

### Compassion and Bodhisattva Practice

If a wise person can contemplate the bodhicitta according to reality, in correspondence with emptiness, and having so contemplated can accomplish the wisdom that benefits others, unobstructed and unattached, then this person knows gratitude and repays the Buddha's kindness. With a compassionate mind, one universally contemplates all sentient beings, parents, and relatives, who have various characteristics and are constantly burned by the fierce fire of afflictions, causing sentient beings to revolve in birth-and-death. Whatever suffering they undergo, one thinks: "I shall bear it for them." Whatever harmonious happiness there is, one thinks: "I shall universally bestow it."

One further contemplates the world's pleasant and unpleasant fruits, the good destinies and the evil destinies, the beneficial and the non-beneficial, all turning according to sentient beings. Yet sentient beings originally have nothing to obtain; it is according to differences of wisdom that various characteristics arise. All the Brahmā kings, Indras, world-protector devas, whether gods or humans, none are apart from the characteristics of the world.

One further contemplates all the hells, hungry ghosts, and animals. In these destinies, all sentient beings of immeasurable and countless kinds and appearances are constantly turned by non-beneficial suffering: oppressed by hunger and thirst, they kill one another and devour one another. Because of this, the fruits of suffering are not destroyed. All buddhas and bodhisattvas can truly contemplate the karmic-retribution events and self-characteristics of all sentient beings in the good and evil destinies. Having so contemplated, they generate a mind of skillful means, carefully protecting sentient beings and causing them to be free from defilements.

Bodhisattvas, with great compassion as their root and sentient beings as their object, do not become attached to the flavor of any meditative absorption; they do not seek the fruits and rewards of self-benefit. Surpassing the ground of the śrāvaka, they do not abandon sentient beings but cultivate the practice of benefiting others. They generate the great bodhicitta, produce the great sprout of bodhi, and seek the fruit of the buddha-bodhi.

With the mind of great compassion they contemplate the suffering of sentient beings. The Avīci hell is vast and boundless; following the causes of various karmas, the retribution of suffering revolves. These various transgressions receive various sufferings. The bodhisattva's compassionate mind thinks of bearing them on behalf [of beings].

These various sufferings have various characteristics; they are said to be without reality and yet not without reality. If one comprehends emptiness, one knows that this dharma follows the sequence of karmic fruits. Therefore, bodhisattvas, wishing to rescue all sentient beings, arouse a valiant mind and enter the mire of birth-and-death. Though dwelling in birth-and-death, they are without any defilement, like a lotus flower, pure and unstained.

Great compassion as their essence, they do not abandon sentient beings. Through the contemplation of the wisdom of emptiness, they do not separate from the afflictions. Therefore, bodhisattvas through the power of skillful means are shown to be born in the royal palace, to escape the city walls and leave home, to practice austerities and cultivate the Way, to sit at the place of awakening and become perfectly enlightened, to display supernatural powers and defeat the armies of Māra, to turn the great dharma-wheel for the salvation of sentient beings, to manifest the jeweled stairways of the three paths descending from heaven and perform various transformative manifestations, to conform to the world and enter great nirvāṇa.

In between, they manifest various form-appearances, acting as Brahmā kings or as Indra, whether gods or humans, turning according to various characteristics. Manifesting in these various ways, they are therefore called the Teachers and Guides who rescue the world. All of this is the power of the great compassionate vows of all buddhas and bodhisattvas, taming the world and causing all to abide in the corresponding supreme practice. Therefore, in the cycle of saṃsāra, they do not produce weariness or retreat.

From the one vehicle they teach the dharma of the two vehicles. The one vehicle and the two vehicles are both of true and real meaning. Whether the bodhi of the śrāvaka or the bodhi of the Buddha, the wisdom-body has one characteristic and samādhi has one essence. Although there is teaching, what is taught is not [ultimately] teaching. Those who speak of various characteristics do so merely to guide sentient beings. If sentient beings are benefited, the merit and wisdom of the buddha-bodhi are equal, and truly there are no two characteristics in which to abide.

If there is abiding in characteristics, this becomes a seed; the characteristic of that seed is born from its class, and therefore the shoots and stems of birth-and-death grow. As the Buddha, the World-Honored One, constantly teaches, he destroys the various characteristics of activity in the world, but merely uses skillful means for sentient beings; in reality there is no destruction. If one is free from discrimination, this meaning is extremely profound. In the extremely profound meaning, there are no two characteristics; though destruction is spoken of, this also is not destruction.

In the dharma of emptiness, there are no two characteristics. All dharmas uphold the true reality of their own nature. The prajñāpāramitā (*zhì bōluómìduō*, 智波羅蜜多) is itself the bodhicitta; the bodhicitta removes all views.

Therefore know: body, speech, and mind are impermanent dharmas, but are for the sake of benefiting sentient beings. The emptiness spoken of here is emptiness that is not annihilation. The existence spoken of here is existence that is not permanent. Therefore there is no birth-and-death and also no nirvāṇa, and all abide in the nirvāṇa of non-abiding (*wú zhù nièpán*, 無住涅盤). All buddhas and world-honored ones teach in this way.

The immeasurable merit born from the compassionate mind is itself the supreme, true, and real principle of emptiness. It is produced by the awesome spiritual power of all buddhas, accomplishing both self-benefit and the benefit of others.

I now bow to that nature of all things. I constantly revere that bodhicitta. May the praise I offer ensure that the seed of the buddhas is not cut off, and that all buddhas and world-honored ones constantly abide in the world.

The bodhicitta is the most supreme in the Mahāyāna. In this mind I abide with right mindfulness.

Furthermore, the bodhicitta abides in the mind of meditative equipoise (*děng yǐn*, 等引) and is born from skillful means. If one comprehends this mind, birth-and-death are equal, and both self-benefit and benefit of others are accomplished.

Furthermore, the bodhicitta is free from the characteristics of all views; the wisdom of non-discrimination truly turns. Those with wisdom who generate the bodhicitta obtain immeasurable and boundless stores of merit.

Furthermore, if a person in a single kṣaṇa contemplates and visualizes the bodhicitta, the store of merit obtained is beyond measure, because the bodhicitta itself is beyond measure.

Furthermore, the jewel of the bodhicitta is pure and unstained, the greatest, the most supreme, the highest, the foremost. It cannot be destroyed, is not subject to destruction, is true and solid. It can shatter the afflictions and all māras, and fulfill the vows of the practice of Samantabhadra (*Pǔxián*, 普賢) of all bodhisattvas.

Furthermore, the bodhicitta is the destination to which all dharmas return. What is spoken is true and real, free from all idle disputation. This is the pure practice-gate of Samantabhadra, free from all characteristics. This is so explained.

> The bodhicitta that I praise
> Is what the Honored One of Two-Footed Beings has correctly taught.
> The bodhicitta is the most honored and supreme;
> The store of merit obtained is also immeasurable.
> With this merit I bestow upon all sentient beings:
> May all swiftly cross the ocean of the three realms of existence.
> According to principle and reality, this praise is offered;
> The wise should study it in just this way.
