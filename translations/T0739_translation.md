# 佛說慢法經
## Sūtra of Disrespecting the Dharma, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0739

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Faju (法炬)

---

The Buddha told Ānanda: "There are people who serve the Buddha and afterward become wealthy and honored. There are also people who serve the Buddha and afterward decline and suffer misfortune."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "How is it that both serve the Buddha, yet one declines while the other prospers? How can this be?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "There are people who serve the Buddha and seek out an enlightened teacher. Having found one who is clear and thorough, they receive from that teacher the precepts and Dharma. They eliminate all wayward thoughts and accord with the sūtras. They are diligent in their practice and do not transgress the teacher's instructions. The one who receives the precepts does not violate them by so much as a hair. Such a person does not transgress the prohibitions of the path. The devas and good spirits constantly attend and protect them, so that whatever they undertake succeeds. Their wealth and gain increase a hundredfold, and all people respect them. In the future they will attain Buddhahood. How much more so will they obtain wealth and prosperity! People such as these who serve the Buddha are true disciples of the Buddha.

"Then again, there are people who serve the Buddha but do not encounter an enlightened teacher. They also have no sūtras or images, and they do not bow or pay respect. Not knowing and not understanding, they forcibly teach others to receive the Dharma and precepts without sincere faith. After receiving the precepts, they continue to violate them all. Their minds are dim and wavering. They are unwilling to read the sūtras, practice the path, or create merit. They believe one moment and doubt the next. They do not remember to observe the fast days, or to burn incense, light lamps, and bow in worship. They continue to be angry, shouting and cursing. In all their comings and goings they mutter imprecations, their mouths never closing properly. Their hearts harbor jealousy and enmity, and they cause others to kill living creatures. When they see sūtras or images, they feel no respect. If they happen to have a sūtra, they casually hang it on the wall, or toss it upon the bed, or place it among old clothes, worn-out baskets, and broken containers. Or they let their wife, children, and small infants handle it with unclean hands. It is stained by smoke and damaged by roof leaks, and they no longer even look at it. They do not burn incense, light lamps, or bow before it. They treat it no differently from secular books. The good spirits depart from them, and evil ghosts find their opportunity, pursuing them without cease, bringing decline and illness upon them. When they then fall ill, they become fearful and anxious, thinking to themselves: 'I have been serving the Buddha. Why do I still fall ill?' Unable to have faith in themselves, they call for a physician. The physician performs divination, inquiries, exorcisms, and protective rituals, all without benefit. They then resort to praying and making offerings to deviant spirits, and their transgressions only increase. Demonic specters and evil ghosts stand guard at their gate, and decline, death, and ruin do not leave their household. Their wealth and property waste away, and the household is beset by illness, one person after another, never leaving the sickbed. When life ends, they bear the guilt of their sins and fall into the hells, where they are punished for ages beyond number. These people simply sat and failed to be single-minded. Their will wavered and they had nothing firm to rely upon. Because they did not believe in the Buddha-Dharma, they received these punishments of sin, and their decline was thus.

"Worldly people who do not know the Buddha-Dharma say that serving the Buddha causes calamity and decline. They do not understand that such a person's own conduct was improper, violating the Buddha's sūtras and precepts. The mind was devoted to doing evil, complete in every kind of wrong. The person brought this upon themselves; there was no one else who gave it to them."

When Ānanda heard this, he touched his forehead to the ground, bowed to the Buddha, and joyfully practiced accordingly.
