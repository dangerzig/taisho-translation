# 賴吒和羅經
## Sūtra of Raṭṭhapāla

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0068

Translated from the Chinese. Wu kingdom, translated by Zhi Qian

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha, together with five hundred śramaṇas, was traveling through the Kuru country and came to the kingdom of Thullakoṭṭhita. The people, brāhmaṇas, and practitioners of that kingdom all heard that the Buddha had come traveling to their land. They had heard of the Buddha's merit and wondrous attainments: that he was free from desire, anger, and delusion; that whatever his mind spoke was balanced and correct; that he had attained the path of the Buddha; that he knew of his own past births; that he foreknew events past, future, and present; that his eye could see through all things and know the paths of good and evil destinations taken by the people and all creeping and crawling creatures of the world; that he could fly through the air and enter the earth, passing out through the seamless and entering through the holeless, transforming himself freely at will; that he knew in advance the thoughts in the minds of worldly people and all creeping and crawling creatures; that the Buddha had mastered his eye, mastered his ear, mastered his nose, mastered his mouth, mastered his body, mastered his mind; that among the ninety-six paths of the world, none compared to the Buddha's path; that the Buddha instructed the devas and humans of the world above and below, as parents instruct their children, enabling them to abandon evil and pursue good; that the Buddha served as teacher for the devas and humans of the world, and all whom the Buddha instructed, both devas and humans, attained the arhat's path of nirvāṇa. The entire nation's people all said: "The Buddha is an auspicious person who teaches the sūtras and precepts well. Let us go together to observe his virtue." The people of the kingdom, some in groups of fifty, some of a hundred, some of five hundred, went together to where the Buddha was. Some knelt before the Buddha, some circumambulated the Buddha three times, some pressed their heads to the Buddha's feet, some joined their palms, and some simply announced their names. Then all the people sat down.

The Buddha expounded the sūtras and precepts for the people, and all the people joined their palms toward the Buddha. Among the assembly there was the son of an elder named Raṭṭhapāla, who sat in the assembly listening to the Buddha expound the sūtra, taking it deeply to heart. Raṭṭhapāla reflected: "As the Buddha's sūtras and precepts teach, it is not fitting to live as a householder. A householder cannot purify himself and study the Buddha's Way." He thought: "It would be better to shave my hair, don the kāṣāya robe, and go forth as a śramaṇa." The people of Thullakoṭṭhita, having heard the sūtras and precepts and rejoicing, circumambulated the Buddha three times and each returned home. Raṭṭhapāla turned back halfway and went to where the Buddha was. He made obeisance before the Buddha, joined his palms, knelt, and addressed the Buddha: "I have reflected that the Buddha's sūtras and precepts show it is not fitting to live as a householder. A householder cannot purify himself and study the Buddha's sūtra path. I wish to shave my hair and beard, don the kāṣāya robe, and become a śramaṇa. I beg the Buddha to have mercy and allow me to become a śramaṇa."

The Buddha said: "Have you informed your parents?"

Raṭṭhapāla said: "I have not yet informed my parents."

The Buddha said: "It is the rule of all buddhas that without the parents' consent, one cannot become a śramaṇa, nor can one receive the precepts."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "Very well! I shall return and inform my parents. If they consent to my becoming a śramaṇa, I shall come back."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Consider it carefully."

Raṭṭhapāla then made obeisance to the Buddha and returned home. He went before his parents and said: "The Buddha's sūtras and precepts show it is not fitting to live as a householder. A householder cannot purify himself and study the Buddha's sūtra path. I wish to shave my hair and beard, don the kāṣāya robe, and become a śramaṇa."

His parents, hearing their son's words, wept together and said: "We are a couple with few children. We prayed to the devas, the sun and moon, bowing our heads in all four directions, begging for children to continue our family line, always fearing that when we suddenly die our family line would be extinguished. From heaven we obtained only you, our one son. The whole family cherishes and loves you without ever feeling satisfied. If you were to die, we would sit together and watch over your corpse until old age. Now you would abandon us while still alive?"

Raṭṭhapāla told his parents: "If you do not consent to my going to the Buddha to become a śramaṇa, from this day forward I will neither drink nor eat nor bathe. If you consent to my becoming a śramaṇa, that is good. Otherwise, I shall die!" He then lay down on the bare ground and did not eat for one day, two days, three days, four days, and five days.

The relatives and kin of Raṭṭhapāla, near and far, heard that Raṭṭhapāla had asked his parents for permission to become a śramaṇa and that his parents would not consent, so he lay on the bare ground and had not eaten for five days. The relatives and kin all came to Raṭṭhapāla and urged him to rise, bathe, and eat. They told Raṭṭhapāla: "Before your parents had you, they prayed to the devas, the sun and moon, bowing their heads in all four directions, asking for children, and obtained only you, their one son. You should support your parents and continue the family line. If you were to die, your parents would sit and watch over your corpse until old age. How much more so do you now wish to abandon them while alive?" Raṭṭhapāla did not respond. The relatives and kin all went back to his parents, weeping and saying: "This child will never accept our counsel."

Raṭṭhapāla also had close friends and acquaintances who heard that he wished to go to the Buddha to become a śramaṇa, but his parents would not consent and he lay on the bare ground, neither eating nor drinking for five days. The close friends and acquaintances all came to Raṭṭhapāla and urged him to rise, bathe, and eat, saying: "Before your parents had you, they prayed to the devas, the sun and moon, bowing their heads in all four directions, asking for children, and obtained only you, their one son. You should support your parents and continue the family line. If you were to die, your parents would wish to watch over your corpse until old age. Yet you now wish to abandon them while alive?" Raṭṭhapāla did not respond. The close friends and acquaintances went back to his parents, weeping and wiping their tears, and said to the parents: "You should release this son and consent to his becoming a śramaṇa. Why? If he delights in the Way and becomes a śramaṇa, you may later see each other while alive. If he does not delight in the Way, he will naturally abandon it and return home. What else can be done? You would rather let him die here, his body rotting and becoming food for insects and ants. What use is a dead body?" The son was now barely breathing, about to die. His parents, household, wife and children, musicians, relatives, and acquaintances all raised their voices in loud weeping. His parents, wiping their tears, told Raṭṭhapāla: "Our relatives and friends have agreed and set conditions: if we release you to become a śramaṇa, will you later return and let us see each other?"

Raṭṭhapāla said: "Release me to go to the Buddha to become a śramaṇa. As long as I live and do not die, I shall certainly return and let my parents see me." His parents, hearing their son's words, wept loudly again, then consented to let him go to become a śramaṇa. Raṭṭhapāla was filled with great joy and thought: "I have not eaten for five days, and my body is very weak and thin. The Buddha has by now traveled from Thullakoṭṭhita to Śrāvastī, five hundred leagues away. I should first nourish myself and wait until I am strong before going."

Raṭṭhapāla nourished himself for several days. When he had regained strength, he went to his parents and said: "Take care. I shall go to the Buddha to become a śramaṇa." His parents again wept loudly. Wiping their tears, they said: "Go, and take care of yourself." Raṭṭhapāla then pressed his head to his parents' feet, arose, circumambulated them three times, and departed. He traveled to Śrāvastī, to Jetavana, and went before the Buddha. Making obeisance, he said: "My parents have consented. Will the Buddha accept me as a śramaṇa?" The Buddha then ordained him as a śramaṇa. He donned the kāṣāya robe and received the śramaṇa sūtras and precepts. The Buddha assigned the arhats to instruct him daily. He did not dare to violate the sūtras and precepts, and through his own contemplation of the sūtra path, he attained the four dhyānas. He attained the first fruit of stream-entry, the second fruit of once-returning, the third fruit of non-returning, and the fourth fruit of arhatship. He then attained the four supernatural powers of flight, the divine eye to see through all things, the divine ear to hear all, and knew the words and thoughts of all devas, humans, and creeping and crawling creatures, and knew his own past lives and births. He followed the Buddha for ten years, like a shadow following a person.

After ten years, he thought: "When I first left home, I bid farewell to my parents and promised to return and see them." Raṭṭhapāla addressed the Buddha: "When I first left home, I promised to return and see my parents. I wish to travel to where my parents are." The Buddha reflected that Raṭṭhapāla could no longer fall into the snare of sensual desire as when he was a householder, having already been liberated from desire. The Buddha said: "Excellent." Raṭṭhapāla then made obeisance to the Buddha and departed. He traveled to the kingdom of Thullakoṭṭhita. Rising early, he donned his kāṣāya robe, took his almsbowl, and entered the neighborhood of his parents, going toward the family gate to beg for food. No one in the household would acknowledge or look at him. Why? Because they blamed the śramaṇa path for causing the living loss of their eldest son. The entire household detested the sight of śramaṇas and would not look at him. When Raṭṭhapāla reached the family gate, there was neither one who gave nor one who looked, neither one who informed the household. He received only abuse. Yet he was neither worried nor distressed. Just as he was about to leave, a maidservant was coming out the gate to discard some spoiled bean soup dregs. Raṭṭhapāla turned and saw the maidservant, and asked: "What will you do with that spoiled bean soup?" The maidservant said: "It is foul and can no longer be eaten, so I am going to throw it away." Raṭṭhapāla said: "If you intend to discard it, please give it to me." The maidservant poured it into his almsbowl. The maidservant secretly recognized Raṭṭhapāla by his hands, feet, and voice, and thought: "This is our eldest son." She immediately ran inside and told his mother: "The eldest son has come and is outside." The mother, overjoyed, told the maidservant: "If what you say is truly so, I shall this very day set you free as a free citizen, and I shall give you all the clothing, pearl ornaments, and rings that I am wearing." The mother then ran to where her husband was. The husband happened to be in the middle courtyard combing his hair. She told her husband: "The maidservant has seen our son Raṭṭhapāla, who is at the gate. I told the maid, 'If you have truly seen Raṭṭhapāla, I shall remove all my garments, pearl ornaments, and rings and give them to you, and set you free as a free citizen.'" The mother told her husband: "Hurry! Go out and search for him!" The husband hastily tied up his hair and ran through the streets and lanes searching for him. He found Raṭṭhapāla in a secluded place, looking up at the sun, having just received his meal and about to eat the spoiled bean soup dregs. The father came forward and said to Raṭṭhapāla: "Should you not come home and sit comfortably and eat fine food? Why are you here eating spoiled bean soup dregs?" Raṭṭhapāla told his father: "I have abandoned the household to study the Way as a śramaṇa. I have no home. Where would I find a home?" The father invited him to come home with him, but he would not go. The father then made a request for the following day: "Come tomorrow to eat at our home, and see your mother." Raṭṭhapāla said: "Excellent!" The father returned and told his wife: "Raṭṭhapāla has indeed come. I have invited him; he will come to eat tomorrow. He has accepted the invitation. Prepare whatever should be prepared, and prepare it generously." The mother immediately called all the servants and maids of the household before her and announced: "When I first entered this household, all the gold, silver, pearls, and precious jewels that my parents gave me: bring them all out and place them in the middle courtyard, and cover them." The maids received the mother's instruction and brought out all the gold, silver, pearls, and precious jewels, heaping them in the courtyard and covering them, so high they rose above a person's head.

At mealtime, Raṭṭhapāla donned his kāṣāya robe, took his almsbowl, and went to his parents' home. His parents, seeing their son approach the gate, the mother pulled away the covering from the heaped gold and silver, and came forward with both hands scattering gold and silver. She told Raṭṭhapāla: "Look at this gold, silver, and precious jewels. These are what your mother brought when she entered this household. These are what your father possesses. The gold, silver, and precious jewels are countless. You may take them, give alms, eat and drink, and enjoy yourself to the fullest. What is the use of being a śramaṇa? It is better to be a layman and live freely at home."

Raṭṭhapāla told his parents: "If you would follow my words, I wish to advise you on one matter." His parents said: "Very good! We accept your instruction." Raṭṭhapāla said: "Take the covering from the treasure and use it to make sacks. Gather all the precious jewels and fill the sacks, load them onto a cart, and take them to the bank of the Ganges. Find a deep spot and cast them in. Why? Those who hoard wealth and treasure are caused much worry: there is fear of government officials and thieves, fear of flood and fire, fear of enemies."

The parents then formed a plan: "Raṭṭhapāla cannot be persuaded by wealth and treasure. Let us try to win him back with the beautiful women and courtesans of old." The mother then went to the courtesans and beautiful women and instructed them to all bathe and adorn themselves, wearing their pearl ornaments and finest garments, dressed in the way Raṭṭhapāla had once delighted in. The mother instructed the courtesans: "When you go out and see Raṭṭhapāla, simply say: 'Young master! What celestial maidens are superior to us, that you would abandon us to practice the Way and seek other maidens?'" The courtesans received the mother's instruction and went out adorned. The courtesans told Raṭṭhapāla: "Young master! What celestial maidens are superior to us, that you would abandon us to practice the Way and seek other maidens?"

Raṭṭhapāla said: "I did not leave you all to seek celestial maidens." The courtesans, hearing his words, were immediately ashamed. They knelt, bowed their heads, and covered their faces with their hands, saying: "Since he does not want us as wives, he instead calls us 'sisters.'" Raṭṭhapāla told his parents: "Why do you trouble me so? If you wish to serve the meal, good. If not, then let it be." The parents then brought out the food and set it before him, and he ate. The parents, wishing to look upon their son longer and fearing he would leave after the meal, ordered all the doors to be shut and locked. When the meal was finished, he expounded the sūtra for his parents: "Wild creatures and animals should not be confined. When animals cannot be free, they will break loose and flee. I have finished eating; I must go. A wild beast that escapes runs into the deep mountains. Combing hair, applying oils, painting eyebrows, powdering white and darkening brows: these may beguile the foolish, but one who has transcended the world cannot be swayed by such things. Looking at a body of bones wrapped in skin and flesh, adorned with gold, pearls, earrings, and jade: to me, such people are like entering boiling water or fire. Fire has no particular object of love. Scenting the body with fragrance may beguile the foolish, but it cannot sway one who has transcended the world. Not knowing what one should do, yet doing it; unable to distinguish parents, unable to distinguish siblings: when the human heart has attachments, it cannot sever them of itself. Women are like many waters, all flowing into the great sea. The fool, approaching women, flows into hell, into the animal realm, into the realm of hungry ghosts. Those who wish to be freed from the anguish of birth and death, who wish to attain the path of nirvāṇa, should keep far from women." Having finished expounding the sūtra for his parents, he flew out through the skylight, like a mighty lion that breaks free and runs.

At that time, the king of the country, named Koravya, had been Raṭṭhapāla's close friend since childhood. The king had a lodge outside the city. Raṭṭhapāla flew to it and entered, and there was a tree called myrobalan, beneath which he sat. King Koravya happened to wish to go out to the lodge for amusement and ordered the lodge caretaker to sweep and clean it in advance. The caretaker, having received the order, went to sweep and clean. He saw Raṭṭhapāla sitting beneath the myrobalan tree. The caretaker went and informed the king: "The sweeping and cleaning is done. The close friend of whom the king often speaks, Raṭṭhapāla, is now sitting beneath a tree in the lodge. If the king wishes to see him, he may proceed." The king, hearing this, was greatly delighted. He immediately had his carriage prepared and went out. Arriving outside the lodge, he descended from the carriage and walked in to where Raṭṭhapāla was. He made obeisance and sat to one side. Raṭṭhapāla said: "The king has come; this is most excellent!" The king said: "Though I have come myself, you have been my friend since childhood. I wish to present you with gifts and treasures as my heart desires." Raṭṭhapāla replied: "It is not fitting to present me with gifts and treasures. I have now cast off my heavy burden; the prison has been unlocked and I am free. Does the king wish to place the prison and heavy burden upon me again? It is not fitting to bring such things." The king said: "Then what shall I present you?" Raṭṭhapāla said: "The king need only say: 'Let my kingdom flourish, the five grains ripen in abundance, and the people multiply. Let alms be easy to obtain. You may stay in my kingdom, and I shall not allow officials or people to trouble you.'" The king said: "I accept your instruction and shall do as Raṭṭhapāla says."

The king said: "I wish to ask something. Please hear me." Raṭṭhapāla said: "Very good!" The king then said: "Ordinary people become śramaṇas because of four kinds of suffering. What are the four? First, old age. Second, illness. Third, loneliness. Fourth, poverty. Those who have these four sufferings then go forth as śramaṇas. Now as I look at you, you have none of these four. For what reason have you become a śramaṇa?" The king said: "Those who become śramaṇas because of old age reflect that their strength is diminished, rising and sitting are difficult, they cannot travel far or manage business to earn wealth, and even if they had property they could not maintain it. For this reason they shave their hair and become śramaṇas. I look at you and see none of this: your hair and beard are still black, your body is whole and sound, you are in the prime of life and should be enjoying yourself. Your parents weep, not wishing you to become a śramaṇa. Second, if a person is afflicted with grave illness, their body weak and emaciated, they reflect that they cannot manage business or earn wealth, and even if they had property they could not maintain it. For this reason they shave their hair and become śramaṇas. I look at you: you have no grave illness, your body is strong and healthy. Third, some are alone and solitary, unable to manage business or earn wealth, and even if they had property they could not maintain it. For this reason they become śramaṇas. I look at you: aside from the royal family and their relatives, in my entire kingdom there is no one more prominent. Fourth, some are poor and cold, with nothing to sustain themselves. Reflecting on their poverty and inability to earn a living, they shave their hair and become śramaṇas, able to beg for alms to survive. I look at you: among the wealthy in my kingdom, none surpasses you. People become śramaṇas because of these four sufferings." The king asked: "Is there any reason other than these four for becoming a śramaṇa?"

Raṭṭhapāla said: "The Buddha spoke of four things that he constantly taught, which I have come to know for myself. He uses them to admonish people, and in my heart I confirm the truth of the Buddha's words. It is for this reason that I shaved my hair and beard, donned the kāṣāya robe, and became a śramaṇa. What are the four? First, no one born can avoid old age; there is no one who can stop the body from aging. Second, no one can avoid illness; no one can die in another's place. Third, when a person dies, their body is empty; they cannot take their possessions with them. Fourth, until death, no one can ever be satisfied with desire and possessions; people all become slaves to possessions and desire. The Buddha taught me these four things. I believed in my heart, and therefore I became a śramaṇa."

The king said: "Your account of these four things is too brief. I do not fully understand. Please explain them to me in more detail."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "I shall ask the king myself, and the king should answer me truthfully. When the king was twenty, thirty, or forty years old, in strength, in archery and sport, in mounting elephants and riding horses, in walking and running: at that time, did the king see himself as having any equal?"

The king said: "It is truly as Raṭṭhapāla says. When I was twenty, thirty, or forty, I saw myself as without equal in archery and sport, mounting elephants and riding horses, walking and running. Now I am old, my strength has declined, rising and sitting are difficult, and if I wish to step over something, I cannot cross it."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "This is the first thing the Buddha taught. It is for this reason that I shaved my hair and became a śramaṇa."

The king said: "What the Buddha teaches is truly extraordinary and excellent; it enters my heart."

Raṭṭhapāla asked the king: "In your kingdom, are there ministers and officials who depend on the king for their livelihood?" The king said: "Yes, there are."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "Has the king ever been struck by grave illness and confined to his bed?" The king said: "Yes, that has happened."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "When confined to bed with illness, did the king call upon his ministers and officials who depend on the king for their livelihood and command them, 'I am gravely ill. You must all share my illness and take it away'? Though the king might give such a command, could his ministers share the king's illness and take it away?"

The king said: "No. I would have to bear it myself; my ministers could not take my place."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "This is what the Buddha taught. This is the second thing. It is for this reason that I became a śramaṇa."

The king said: "Truly extraordinary and excellent; it enters my heart."

Raṭṭhapāla asked the king: "When a person's lifespan is ending and death approaches, this is what no one's mind desires. Yet though one does not desire it, one cannot avoid death." Raṭṭhapāla said: "If people know they must die, why do they not send their precious jewels ahead to their next place of birth?"

The king said: "One cannot send precious jewels ahead to one's next place of birth. All must be left behind; one goes empty."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "This is what the Buddha taught. This is the third thing. It is for this reason that I became a śramaṇa."

The king said: "What the Buddha teaches is truly extraordinary and excellent; it enters my heart."

Raṭṭhapāla asked the king: "Is the king's kingdom at peace, the people flourishing, the five grains ripening in abundance?"

The king said: "It is so."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "Suppose a person came from the east and truthfully told the king (and the king would believe his words): 'I have come from the east and seen a great kingdom, prosperous and thriving with an abundant populace. I know the roads and paths, and can lead the king's army to conquer that kingdom.' Hearing these words, would the king wish to send someone to take that kingdom?"

The king said: "Yes, coveting its profit, I would still wish to take it."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "And if another person came from the south, the west, or the north, describing a kingdom like the eastern one, would the king wish to take those as well?"

The king said: "Yes, coveting their profit, I would still wish to take them."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "And if another person came from across the sea and truthfully told the king (and the king would believe his words): 'On the other side of the sea there is a great kingdom, with the five grains ripening and the people flourishing. I know the roads and can lead the king's army to conquer that kingdom.' Hearing these words, would the king wish to send someone to take that kingdom?"

The king said: "Yes, coveting its profit, I would still wish to take it."

Raṭṭhapāla said: "The Buddha sees this, and knows that people's bitter craving is insatiable. This is the fourth thing. It is for this reason that I became a śramaṇa. The Buddha sees these four things and uses them to admonish people."

The king said: "What the Buddha teaches is truly extraordinary and excellent; it enters my heart." The king said: "The Buddha foreknows events past, future, and present. His goodness is truly like this?"

Raṭṭhapāla said: "The king has his own kingdom and the four quarters, yet he is never satisfied, and still craves the kingdoms beyond the sea. The Buddha sees that those in the world who possess wealth and treasure all guard and hoard them tightly, unwilling to give or share with others, miserly in their hoarding, always seeking more. Kings and common people alike never know contentment. Until death they do not abandon desire, yet they must part with everything in death. All their possessions are left behind as they go empty. They go toward the path of good or evil they have created. Good and evil follow a person like a shadow follows a body. After death, family and relatives weep and mourn, place the body in a coffin, and bury it. A person is born alone, dies alone, and must bear alone the consequences of good and evil they have done. No one can take their place. Food and drink, gold, silver, and precious jewels cannot cause a person to attain the Way. Wealth cannot protect a person's life or prevent aging and death. People's thoughts run in many directions, and what people love and desire: the human will constantly turns and cannot be single-minded. The Buddha said: 'Because people indulge their desires, they bring upon themselves calamity, enmity, and fear, like the thoughts of a burglar. Others discover them from behind. Through the evil their bodies have done, they bring about their own death. As worldly people create evil, after death they enter the hells, the animal realm, and the realm of hungry ghosts. Like a tree that grows flowers, leaves, and fruit: some blossoms fall while still flowering, some fall when the fruit is forming, some fall when fully grown, some fall when ripe. People are the same: some die while still in the womb, some die upon touching the ground, some die at half a year, some die in old age. Human life is unpredictable.'" Raṭṭhapāla said: "It is for this reason that I became a śramaṇa. Ordinary people think I became a śramaṇa because I could not manage a household. Though there are many kinds of discourse, none compares to studying the Way."

When Raṭṭhapāla had finished expounding the sūtra, the king attained the first fruit of stream-entry. He then received the five precepts: first, not to kill; second, not to steal; third, not to commit sexual misconduct with another's wife; fourth, not to speak falsely or divisively; fifth, not to drink intoxicants. The king, having received the precepts, made obeisance and departed.
