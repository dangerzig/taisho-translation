# 解捲論
## Treatise on Unrolling the Meaning

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1620

Composed by Dignāga (陳那). Translated from the Chinese. Chen dynasty, translated by Paramārtha (真諦).

---

The three realms have only verbal names as their substance. Due to forceful conceptual discrimination, they are not actually existent dharmas; therefore, the true is not grasped. Through the gate of discriminative analysis of the inherent nature of all dharmas, in order to generate non-inverted wisdom, this treatise is established.

> When the cognition of a snake arises upon seeing a rope,
> Seeing the rope, one knows there is no such object.

In dim and murky conditions, at a not too distant place, upon the rope's color and form one sees a semblance of a snake. Deceived by the object, not yet seeing the distinction, one holds that it is a snake and generates a definitive understanding. If one sees the distinctive characteristics of the rope, then because it does not accord with the conceptualization and because the snake-cognition arose falsely, the former understanding is merely confusion. Knowing this, there is no such object.

> If, having observed the rope's parts,
> The cognition of the rope is like the cognition of the snake.

If one analyzes and examines this rope-object part by part, the substance of the rope cannot be found. If there is no substance, then the cognition of the rope is like the cognition of the snake: merely confused cognition. Within the parts of the rope, one applies the same analytical examination. Since the substance and characteristics cannot be found, the cognitions of both the rope and its parts are entirely confused cognition.

> All things that are conventional designations,
> When their inherent nature is analytically examined,
> The conventional name arises from something else,
> Up to the domain of conventional knowledge.

Analyzing part by part and examining, one does not see the inherent substance of the rope and such things. Therefore, like the cognition of a snake, the cognition of the rope and so forth is merely confused cognition, with truly no object. All conventional designations, such as the existent dharmas of pots, garments, and persons, if one examines the parts such as clay and so forth up to and including the domain of conventional knowledge and the final parts, herein the conventional names "pot" and so forth arise from something else. At the final level, the indivisible, that which is difficult to manifest, all are nonexistent. All things of the category of conventional designations, at their final parts, admit no further analysis; there is only a single paramāṇu. If one separates a single great element, the remaining great elements and the one element both cannot be manifested. Being without substance, they are like the horns of a rabbit and so forth.

How are they different? The paramāṇu cannot be established as a single entity. If something exists as an entity, it must have directional differentiation, just like a pot and so forth. Pots and such objects exist in the world and have differentiation in six directions. Therefore, having parts, they do not constitute a single entity. If the paramāṇu exists, it should have six directions. Then it has parts and does not constitute a single entity. If it does not constitute a single entity, then it is composed of multiple entities, is no different from a pot, and also has no real substance.

> The wise, regarding conventional objects,
> Should not give rise to the thought that they are real.

Therefore the three realms contain only confusion. If the wise seek liberation, they should not give rise to the imputation of reality. Question: There are confused cognitions. Answer: If you say, "I accept that the inherent nature of external objects such as pots cannot be found; there are only discriminative confused cognitions arising with no real object as condition. Why? Illusory persons, gandharva cities, and such things truly do not exist, yet confused cognitions resembling illusions and so forth arise and are not nonexistent." This reasoning is unsound, because it is not established. How is it not established? Because what is seen does not exist in that way. This confused cognition resembles what is nonexistent. Since the object has no substance, how can the cognition be existent? Just as the inherent nature of the perceived object, the inherent nature of the perceiving cognition is also thus. Since the perceived object is nonexistent, this confused cognition cannot arise on its own. By the power of something else, but since that other is not established, what basis is there for arising? For this reason, how can the assertion that confused cognition exists be established? In the world, there is no such dharma where a cause for production, such as a seed, can exist when the produced sprout and other effects do not exist. Therefore, the analogy of illusion and so forth also cannot be established.

> All conventionally designated things,
> If one contemplates them with a subtle mind,
> The wise can eliminate desire and other afflictions
> As easily as removing the fear of a snake.

Just so: having recognized that the three realms are merely conventional designations, one eliminates the coarse cognition of pots and such things and cultivates a subtle mind. As in the world, objects like pots and garments are established through conventional designation; from the perspective of the conventional mind, this is not contradicted. Later, to dispel this conventional mind, one gives rise to the analytical mind and sees only confused cognitions, without external objects. Since the cause of this confused cognition is not established and it resembles something nonexistent, its substance is not established. Since both inner and outer are nonexistent, one meets with the emptiness of dharmas. All the afflictions of desire and so forth produced by discrimination, the wise easily remove. Just as one falsely gives rise to the thought of a snake upon seeing a rope and generates fear, if one sees the distinction and definitively knows it is a rope, one can remove the fear of the snake. Through contemplation, one can quickly and easily extinguish the inherent nature of the objects of desire and other afflictions. The delusions of desire and other afflictions are also thus.

> The wise do not contradict the world;
> They follow along in speaking of worldly dharmas.
> If one wishes to extinguish the obstruction of afflictions,
> One should investigate by relying on the true.

As with worldly objects like pots and garments, one accepts their existence without contradiction and explains them to others. Thus the wise person first follows along with such matters. Later, if seeking liberation, one should cultivate true principles and analytically examine the inherent nature of worldly dharmas. If one analyzes in accordance with principle, present afflictions are extinguished and those not yet arisen do not arise. Such is the purpose of establishing this treatise.
