# 梵摩渝經
## The Sūtra of the Brahmin Brahmāyu

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0076

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhī Qiān (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the country of Videha, traveling together with five hundred śramaṇas. At that time, there was a brahmin named Brahmāyu, a native of the country of Mithilā. He was old and aged, one hundred and twenty years of age. He was widely learned in all the scriptures, in astronomy, and in arts of divination, able to foresee what had not yet come to pass, and was the preceptor of the entire country. Brahmāyu heard from afar that the Buddha, a son of royalty born of the Śākya clan, had renounced his kingdom's glory and become a śramaṇa, had attained the Way and was called the Buddha, most pure and supremely honored, and was now in the country of Videha with five hundred śramaṇas, transforming sentient beings. Brahmāyu reflected deeply and exclaimed: "The śramaṇa Gautama! Divinely holy and majestic! He is the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One, Perfect in Knowledge and Spiritual Powers, a great man, honored and mighty, Lord of the Dharma, sage of sages, Teacher of Gods and Humans. The stains of his mind have been removed and all evils extinguished. Through self-awakening he knows all things. For śramaṇas, brahmins, devas, Brahmā, nāgas, and spirits he teaches the Dharma. His words, whether beginning, middle, or end, are pure above all, profoundly wondrous and surpassingly lofty, such as the sages of old have never heard." Brahmāyu expounded this at length for his disciples, declaring the Buddha to be the King of Sages, the Unsurpassed Perfectly Enlightened One, before whom they all should prostrate and receive his teaching.

Among the brahmin's disciples there was one nearly equal to a sage, whose name was Māṇava. He too was widely versed in the scriptures, equal in learning to his teacher. He had studied the secret prophecies and knew that a Buddha was to appear, whose body would bear the extraordinary thirty-two marks, supremely honored and without compare; these things he understood in his heart. The teacher said to Māṇava: "I have heard that Gautama is divinely holy and unsurpassed, revered by all the gods, peerless among the sages. Go and observe him. See whether his august bearing and true and noble form are truly as the scholars have praised, or not. If it is so, I shall hasten there to prostrate at his feet in reverence." Māṇava asked: "By what shall I judge him?" His teacher said: "Do the scriptures not say? In a future age there will be a king named Śuddhodana, and a queen named Māyā, virtuous and pure. They will give birth to a holy son, a god among gods, whose unique and honored form will be sixteen feet in height with thirty-two marks. Were he to remain in the world, he would become a wheel-turning emperor; were he to renounce the world and become a śramaṇa, he would surely become a Buddha."

Māṇava received his instructions, bowed at his teacher's feet, and went to the country of Videha. He proceeded to the Buddha's presence, exchanged salutations, withdrew to his seat, and quieted his mind. He examined the Buddha's body and its marks of excellence carefully, but could not see two of the marks: the broad, long tongue and the hidden male organ. His mind harbored doubt. The Buddha knew that Māṇava's mind was doubtful. Through his supernatural power, the Buddha revealed his hidden male organ, then extended his broad, long tongue to cover his own face and licked both his left and right ears, then drew the tongue back into his mouth. A five-colored light issued forth, circled his body three times, and vanished above the crown of his head. Māṇava's heart was stirred, joy and awe mingling together. He exclaimed with delight: "The śramaṇa Gautama is truly a Buddha! His marks and radiance are complete without exception. He is a wonder the world has rarely seen, truly the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One. I shall follow and attend him, observing the Honored One's exemplary conduct, so as to transform the ignorant and deluded, and also to report to my teacher." He then followed the World-Honored One, observing his inner meditation, his travels and teachings, his saving of sentient beings. Whether staying the night or returning, the Buddha was always with the Saṅgha and never alone. For six months Māṇava followed like a shadow following the body, witnessing all the Buddha's wondrous transformations and majestic virtue. He then bowed at the Buddha's feet, took his leave, and returned to his own country. Arriving at his teacher's place, he bowed as before and took his seat. The teacher said: "I sent you to observe Gautama, the Honored One among the Gods, his marks and spiritual transformations. Is what the scholars have praised truly not in vain? If it is so, I shall hasten there to prostrate at his feet, with the reverence of one who touches the dust beneath his feet."

Māṇava replied: "His marks, spiritual virtue, and powers surpass the heavens. His majesty is beyond description. Neither Śakra nor Brahmā can fathom him. No sage can calculate it. What the many sages have praised amounts to not even a fraction of a hundred million. It is not for me, a mere firefly's candle, to fully describe. Let me speak briefly of the essentials. His peerless marks number thirty-two. The first mark: the soles of his feet are level and even. The second: his hands and feet bear wheels with a thousand spokes. The third: his fingers are linked with a chain-like pattern. The fourth: his fingers are long. The fifth: his heels are full. The sixth: his hands and feet are fine and soft, the palms supple within and without. The seventh: the skin between his fingers and toes is webbed. The eighth: his calves are like those of a deer. The ninth: his male organ is hidden. The tenth: his body is the color of purple-gold, radiantly gleaming. The eleventh: his body is like vajra, free from all impurities. The twelfth: his skin is fine and soft; dust and water do not adhere to his body. The thirteenth: each pore bears a single hair. The fourteenth: his body hair is indigo-blue, curling to the right. The fifteenth: his body is well-proportioned. The sixteenth: his upper body is like that of a lion. The seventeenth: his body is upright and unbent, like the body of Brahmā. The eighteenth: his shoulders are full of flesh, well-joined to his body. The nineteenth: when standing upright, his two hands reach to his knees. The twentieth: his jaw is like that of a lion. The twenty-first: he has forty teeth. The twenty-second: his teeth are even. The twenty-third: his teeth are without gaps. The twenty-fourth: his teeth are incomparably white. The twenty-fifth: his tongue is broad and long. The twenty-sixth: he discerns each and every taste in succession. The twenty-seventh: his voice is like the voice of Brahmā. The twenty-eighth: seven parts of his body are full and rounded. The twenty-ninth: the whites of his eyes are a deep blue. The thirtieth: his eyelashes move up and down like those of a king of bulls. The thirty-first: between his brows there stands a white tuft of hair. The thirty-second: atop his head is a fleshy protuberance, whose light outshines the sun and eclipses the moon. The śramaṇa Gautama possesses all of these in their entirety. Not one of the thirty-two marks is deficient. His wondrous virtues are boundless, marvelous and precious, rare since ancient times.

"I observed Gautama's every step and movement. When he walks, he always lifts the right foot first. The length and pace, the speed and deliberateness of his steps are all in accord with propriety. When walking, his ankles and knees do not touch one another. He moves with a level body, his shoulders not swaying. When he wishes to look back, he does not use force. He stands still for a moment, then suddenly faces the other direction without turning his body. He neither lowers nor raises his head; his head and body are level. He looks straight ahead as he walks and never glances sideways. Such is the manner of his gait. When Gautama walks upon the road, the gods spread a jeweled canopy overhead, and flowers fall like snow. Neither heavenly dragons nor flying birds dare pass over him, and no being in the three realms can see the top of his head. The gods play music, leading the escort in attendance upon the Honored One. Nāgas and earth spirits level the road, making the high and low smooth as a whetstone. His feet do not touch the ground, yet the wheel marks appear, their radiance gleaming brilliantly for seven days before fading. Trees bow and incline as though performing the courtesy of a person kneeling. When he is invited and enters a dwelling, the doorframe, whether high or low, accommodates his body as he enters upright; the lintel does not rise, nor does Gautama stoop. He sits in the center of the seat, not leaning forward or backward. He sits with hands folded and never points or gestures, nor does he prop his cheek. When he descends from the seat, he does not turn but is suddenly on the ground. When Māra comes bearing malice, the Buddha's mind knows no fear and his countenance is all the more at ease. With a compassionate heart he takes pity, and the poison is entirely dissolved. When he receives water in his bowl, the bowl does not tilt and the water is neither too much nor too little. When he washes the bowl, both water and bowl are perfectly still without the slightest sound. He never places the bowl down upon the ground. When he washes his hands therein, both hands and bowl are clean. When he pours out the water from the bowl, the height, distance, and direction are exactly right. When he receives food in his bowl, the food does not soil the bowl. When a morsel enters his mouth, he chews three times and stops. Every grain is completely broken; none remains between his teeth. He perceives each and every flavor, knowing them all, sufficient to sustain the body but not for the sake of pleasure. Gautama receives food for eight reasons: not for amusement, without any unwholesome intention, with no desire in mind, without cunning or pretense, far removed from the dust of the three realms, to set the mind upon the Way so that the robe-offering reaches its fulfillment, to sever former painful sensations, to close the twelve seas of craving, to extinguish past transgressions and attain the power of the Way, to abide in emptiness without conceptualizing emptiness. He washes the bowl as before. Toward his Dharma robes and his almsbowl his mind has neither attachment nor aversion. For the household that has given offerings, after completing his blessing and teaching, he returns to the monastery and does not speak to his disciples of whether the food was good or bad. His food is digested of itself, without any impurity of excrement. Upon entering his chamber, he sits in stillness and deeply contemplates the various meditative absorptions. He comes out after a brief time and never misses the right moment. Day and night he neither sleeps nor yawns. He expounds the Dharma extensively, exhorting and encouraging his disciples to enter the hall of the Way. He never displays to his disciples any conduct that defiles the Way through wealth or sensuality. The Honored One's teaching is lofty and profound, beyond what the writings of sages and immortals have ever contained. In rising and sitting he is always in a state of purity, oriented toward the Way. When doing walking meditation, he does not glance about. His bearing is graceful as he adjusts his robe and arranges his sash. The Dharma robe upon his body, whether higher or lower, tighter or looser, always looks elegant. When he enters a garden and washes his feet, he does not wipe them, yet his feet are clean of themselves. His body's color is radiant like heavenly gold. His mind has no attachment, and his will is like empty space.

"When he sits in meditation, he is luminous and without thought. The three poisons, the four afflictions, the five aggregates, the six sense-bases, the seven fetters, and the eight delusions: all these he destroys with his unsurpassed wisdom. Through the meditative attainments of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, he cuts off the nine abodes of consciousness. Through the ten wholesome deeds he eliminates the ten evils. He composed the twelve divisions of scripture and uprooted the twelve links of dependent origination. The sixty-two wrong views and all the sores of affliction and defilements of thought are stilled in his mind. Through the four immeasurables and the Great Vehicle he liberates his own honored body and also saves all sentient beings. Before he expounds his teaching, though his disciples have not yet asked, he smiles of his own accord, and from his mouth light issues forth, circles his body three times, and gradually disappears.

"Ānanda straightens his robe and bows to ask, and the Buddha then delivers a great teaching. His voice has eight qualities: the most beautiful voice, the easily understood voice, the soft and gentle voice, the harmonious and well-modulated voice, the honored and wise voice, the unerring voice, the deep and wondrous voice, and the voice that is neither masculine nor feminine. His words are without omission; none can find a shortcoming. Whenever he delivers a great teaching, the twenty-four gods, Brahmā, Śakra, the Four Heavenly Kings, the sun, moon, and stars, all the spirits among them, the emperors and their ministers, the earth spirits and ocean nāgas all come to prostrate and listen to the sūtra. The sound of the teaching enters each ear, and each mind understands it as though it were spoken in their own language. The Buddha's wisdom is like the Kunlun River: a thousand tributaries and ten thousand streams all look up to it, and though the streams overflow, the river does not diminish by a single drop. The Buddha's wisdom surpasses even this. Sentient beings receive his knowledge and each is fully satisfied, yet the Buddha's wisdom does not diminish by the breadth of a hair. When the teaching is concluded, all the bodhisattvas, all the heavenly emperors and kings, ministers, people, nāgas, and spirits are without exception delighted. They prostrate, withdraw, and reverently uphold and practice what they have received. When the Buddha retires to his chamber in stillness, he never, by virtue of his unsurpassed and divinely honored merit, treats his disciples or any sentient being with contempt.

"I followed Gautama for six months like a shadow following the body, fully observing his rising and resting, his walking meditation and entering his chamber, his washing and eating, his blessings and teaching of the sūtras, his exhorting of his disciples, and his times of meditation." Māṇava said: "Gautama's exemplary bearing and deportment are thus. What I have described is like adding a single drop to a great ocean. It is not something that the thoughts of sages can approximate, not something the gods can fully comprehend, not something heaven and earth can discuss. How majestic! There is nothing above him. How vast! There is no shore. It cannot be measured or fathomed, and is difficult to fully describe."

When Brahmāyu heard from his disciple of the World Teacher's virtue, he was startled and wept, saying: "My years draw to their close in the west. I have nearly lived and will die in vain, without seeing the supreme wisdom of the World Teacher." Then Brahmāyu said with joy: "I am blessed indeed! To see the Buddha and then to die, that glory is hard to express. Though a fool may have a lifespan as long as heaven and earth, how is he different from clay and stone?" He immediately put on his formal robes, prostrated his entire body upon the ground, bowed his head three times, and said: "I take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Holy Saṅgha. May I, with what remains of my life, be granted an audience, to prostrate and receive his teaching."

The Buddha, with the clear sight of the six supernatural powers, perceived that Brahmāyu had taken refuge. The Buddha accepted his refuge from afar. From the country of Videha, the Buddha traveled to the country of Mithilā and sat beneath a tree. The king and ministers of the country, the brahmins and householders, spread the word among one another, saying: "The śramaṇa Gautama, born of the Śākya clan, a son of royalty, who might have lived in splendor, is instead pure and simple, with a nature tranquil and detached, free from the stain of greed and desire, the poison of anger, and the darkness of ignorance. He stands above all the sages like the moon among the stars. His spiritual virtue extends to all; he is revered by all the gods. He is the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One. His defilements and darkness are exhausted; his wisdom alone endures. His spiritual holiness is fully complete, and all the infinite things within it, subtle and detailed, whether manifest in the present or hidden in the future, nothing is not clear to him. His teachings and instructions are all true and sincere." The king and all his ministers, the brahmins and eminent men, said in unison: "What good fortune that we are alive to see the World Teacher! He is to be honored and revered. We should prostrate and receive the purifying transformation of his teaching." They all gathered together. Those with chariots, those on horses, those on foot, not a household was left behind. Upon arriving, some prostrated at the Buddha's feet, some knelt, some exchanged salutations, some announced their names. All then sat in silence.

When Brahmāyu heard that the Buddha had arrived with the Holy Assembly, his joy was immeasurable. He led his disciples to the Buddha's presence. Just as he reached the edge of the grove, he reflected: "I should first send someone to convey my sincere intention. Would it not be disrespectful to go directly?" He called to his disciple and said: "Carry my name and prostrate at the Buddha's feet, saying: 'The brahmin Brahmāyu, one hundred and twenty years of age, thirsts for the sage's example and delights in the pure breeze of his virtue. He rejoices that Gautama's daily life is peaceful, tranquil, and free from desire. He requests an audience.'" The disciple bowed to his teacher and went to the Buddha's presence. He prostrated, conveyed his teacher's request in full, and praised his teacher before the Buddha, saying: "My teacher, the national preceptor Brahmāyu, is widely learned in all the scriptures and has mastered the secret prophecies. He dwells in quiet retreat, with foreknowledge of astronomy. His charts and divinations of fortune and misfortune leave nothing unforeseen. He foresaw that in this age a World Teacher would appear, of great stature sixteen feet tall, with a heavenly bearing of purple-gold, possessing the thirty-two marks and eighty minor marks, a god among gods, king among sages. He has therefore hastened here to take refuge in the Three Treasures. He is just beyond the trees and has not dared to approach on his own. He wishes an audience to reverently receive the teaching of the divine transformation." The World-Honored One said: "Very good. Let him come forward." The disciple returned with the message, conveyed the Buddha's gracious instruction fully, and his teacher's wish was made known. The teacher then bowed to the ground and joyfully came forward. The brahmins, elders, and householders of the country saw their teacher from afar and were awed and trembling, hands pressed together and heads bowed. Brahmāyu said to them: "Be at ease and keep your seats. I shall now seat myself beside Gautama, the World-Honored Lord of the Dharma." He then prostrated his entire body upon the ground, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and sat with reverence and solemnity. He quieted his mind and carefully examined the Buddha's marks. He saw thirty of the wondrous marks, but two were not visible. In his uncertainty, he prostrated upon the ground and asked in verse:

> "In the scriptures of our brahmin tradition,
> The secret prophecies record the essentials of the age:
> In this turbid world, the king is named Śuddhodana,
> His queen is named Māyā,
> The prince is named Siddhārtha,
> His complexion the radiance of purple gold.
> His body bears the marks of the Honored One among the Gods;
> With patient endurance he governs by the Dharma.
> Are the unsurpassed and true marks,
> The thirty-two, complete?
> The chaste and hidden male organ,
> Can it be verified as free from desire?
> Does he possess the broad, long tongue
> That covers his face and licks his ears?
> Does his teaching surpass all the sages,
> Rare for even Brahmā and Śakra to hear?
> As the brilliant guide, the Teacher of Gods and Humans,
> Can he dispel all doubts?
> Abiding in the Way, at ease in the world,
> Shall he attain immortality in the life to come?
> Through immortal liberation, dwelling in nirvāṇa,
> Shall he forever leave the three realms?
> Mind, thought, consciousness, and spirit:
> Can he extinguish all suffering?"

The brahmin had expressed the doubts in his mind. The Buddha fully knew that the brahmin was uncertain about two of the marks. Through his supernatural power the Buddha revealed the hidden male organ, extended his broad, long tongue to cover his own face, licked both his left and right ears, and from his mouth a light issued forth that illuminated the entire country of Mithilā, circled his body three times, and slowly returned into his mouth. The Buddha then replied: "What you have asked about, the thirty-two marks of the Great Being, I possess them all. Not one is lacking. From innumerable eons I have practiced the four immeasurable minds, generosity, moral discipline, patience, vigor, meditative absorption, and wisdom. I have saved sentient beings as though protecting my own body. I severed craving, contemplated emptiness, and abided in the attainment of signlessness. The stains of my mind have been completely removed, without the slightest trace remaining. Through practicing these ways, all misfortunes have been extinguished and ten thousand virtues have been accumulated, thus forming the body of a Buddha, with marks and radiance that move alone through the three realms, forever departed from the ignorance and darkness of the five destinies, having attained the unsurpassed and supremely honored wisdom. Therefore I am called the Buddha. If there were any trace remaining in the mind, even as fine as a strand of silk or a single hair, of the poison of greed, anger, or ignorance, or the darkness of the five aggregates or the six sense-declines, the Way of the Buddha would not be accomplished. If among all persons and beings, up to the present and extending into the thoughts of sentient beings, and into the boundless eons of the future, with all their subtle and profound details, there were anything that was not known, then one would not be a Buddha. The four fearlessnesses, the eight qualities of voice, the ten powers, the eighteen qualities unique to a Buddha, the thirty-two marks, and the eighty minor marks: if even one were lacking, one would not be a Buddha. I now possess them all, without one being insufficient. Therefore I am called the Buddha. A śramaṇa who has attained the path of an arhat can divide one body into ten, ten into a hundred, a hundred into a thousand, a thousand into ten thousand, ten thousand into a number beyond count, and can also recombine innumerable bodies back into one; by pressing a finger to the ground, the three-thousand great-thousand world system trembles. This is because through the practice of mind they have attained the concentration of desirelessness. How much more so the Buddha! The merit of even one of the Buddha's marks, such as the eyebrow, could be calculated by counting the sands of the Ganges, yet the merit between the eyebrows is beyond all reckoning. How much more so the virtue of the entire body! Furthermore, brahmin, one who has faith in the Buddha and the Three Treasures will be at peace in this present life and will be reborn in heaven hereafter. Whatever you wish will follow your thought. Whatever doubts you have, ask without hesitation."

The brahmin thought: "What Gautama has said is profoundly wondrous and far-reaching, fully answering my question." He thought again: "Shall I now ask about matters of this present life, or about matters of the life to come?" Then he reflected further: "The essentials of the three times are clear to the Buddha alone. How could mere immortals, sages, or scholars illuminate them?" The brahmin said: "What is a brahmin? What is penetrating knowledge? What is purity? What is stillness? What is a Buddha?"

The Buddha replied to the brahmin: "I shall speak true words to illuminate your understanding. Listen attentively and hold them in your heart. To attain the three supernatural powers is what is called a brahmin. To clearly discern the past, to distinguish the places of rebirth, to see with the eye of the Way so that mountains and rocks cannot obstruct, to dispel darkness and resolve doubt, to be fully clear about the three times: this is called penetrating knowledge. To attain the six supernatural powers and to have the stains of the mind completely removed: this is called stillness. When the three poisons are destroyed and the mind is like heavenly gold: this is called purity. When the root of ignorance concerning birth and death has been utterly burned away without remainder, when one walks the path of purity and is preeminent over the three realms, when all ignorance is exhausted and there is nothing ungrasped, when one attains omniscience, then the honored title is Buddha."

The brahmin, overjoyed, rose to his feet, prostrated his entire body upon the ground, touched his head to the Buddha's feet, kissed the Buddha's feet with his mouth, stroked the Buddha's feet with his hands, and declared his own name: "I am the brahmin Brahmāyu. I take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Saṅgha." Weeping, he said: "Sentient beings are lost in darkness, shrouded by the six obscurations. They see the Buddha and do not revere him; they see the sūtras and do not read them; they see the śramaṇas and feel no devoted love in their hearts. They do not receive the divine transformation. This is lasting decline!" His many disciples, seeing their teacher's complete devotion, looked at one another and said: "Our revered teacher, who has mastered the scriptures and seen every text, whose renown extends to the four countries and who is honored by all scholars, has now humbled himself, pressing his palms together and prostrating at Gautama's feet. How much more should we do likewise!"

The Buddha told the brahmin: "Be seated again. I perceive your mind. You have true faith and wisdom directed toward the World-Honored One." He received the instruction and took his seat. The Buddha then further spoke of the virtue of keeping the precepts, the merit of generosity, the defilement and impurity of household life, and praised the exalted practice of aspiring to the Way. The Buddha then perceived that the brahmin had the mind of a superior person, joyful and broad in understanding. The Buddha taught him the essentials of the supreme Way: "All suffering, in its ten thousand forms, arises from the body. The wise person, seeing deeply, knows that in pleasure there lies the seed of misfortune. Turning back against the stream to seek the origin, one reaches the point of fundamental non-existence. This is what the superior person calls the wisdom of the noble truth. Not knowing that the body is the chief source of suffering, and that all arises from habitual accumulation: the superior person awakens to this. This the wise call the noble truth. The three realms are like an illusion. What is combined must separate; what flourishing does not decline? When conditions combine, calamity arises; when conditions separate, suffering ceases. The superior person contemplates the origin and thereby knows its emptiness. This the wise call the noble truth. Having known fundamental non-existence, one reaches the three realms, empties the mind, purifies one's conduct, abandons all desire, and attains the concentration of signlessness. What the mind takes hold of, the Three Treasures may be attained."

The brahmin's mind was opened, like a piece of pure white cloth, spotless and without stain, which upon being dyed takes on color. The brahmin's mind was illuminated. In past lives he had many times served the Buddhas and upheld the pure precepts. Now, hearing the Honored One's teaching, he fully understood the Way of the Unsurpassed, Perfectly Enlightened One. The stains of his mind were completely stilled. He entered the three gates of liberation and was forever separated from all suffering. He then addressed the Buddha: "Before I saw the Buddha, I held to the conduct of great error, blinded by ignorance, trusting the words of the mad and foolish and taking them for truth. Now, having first seen the Buddha, my madness is cured. The blind see, the deaf hear, the mute speak, the hunched stand straight; the prisoner is released from jail. Common people in their foolishness live and die in vain, never tasting the true Way of the Honored One among the Gods, dwelling forever in flames of torment. How painful! What good fortune that I am alive! I have been blessed to see the Buddha's supreme divinity. For my sake he has spoken the most profound Way, restoring me to the original, the unconditioned, the eternal. From this day forward, I take refuge in the Buddha, I take refuge in the Dharma, I take refuge in the Bhikṣu Saṅgha. I wish to become a pure lay devotee: I shall uphold benevolence and not kill, know contentment and not steal, be chaste and not engage in sexual misconduct, hold to truthfulness and not deceive, practice filial piety and not drink intoxicants. May the Honored One among the Gods have compassion for me. Tomorrow at dawn, I wish that you and the Holy Assembly would condescend to accept a simple meal." The Buddha silently accepted.

The brahmin, his heart full of joy, bowed at the Buddha's feet and returned home, where he prepared food of a hundred flavors. At dawn, at his home, he bowed to the Buddha, knelt upright, and reverently said: "May the Buddha, at the proper time, condescend to grace us with his honored presence." The Buddha put on his Dharma robes and went together with the Holy Assembly to the brahmin's home. They all took their seats according to the Dharma. The brahmin served the water for washing hands with his own hand, and attended to them with a reverent heart. This continued for seven days. When the Buddha had finished his teaching and transforming, he returned to the country of Videha. Not long afterward, the brahmin's life came to an end. When the bhikṣus heard of this, they together addressed the Buddha: "Now that the brahmin has passed away, to what destiny has he gone?" The World-Honored One said: "That brahmin possessed a saintly mind, broad in understanding, and had penetrated to the stage of non-return. The five hindrances were exhausted, and he was pure as heavenly gold. In that state of purity he attained the path of the arhat and passed into the unconditioned."

When the Buddha had finished speaking, the bhikṣus rejoiced.
