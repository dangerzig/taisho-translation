# 新集浴像儀軌
## Newly Compiled Ritual Procedure for Bathing Images

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1322

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, composed by Huilin (慧琳).

---

In the monasteries of the Five Regions of India, the saṃgha maintains their establishments with considerable splendor. Whether residing in saṃghārāma temples, araṇya monastic gardens, or other such places, they bathe the images and burn incense every day, gathering various renowned flowers to scatter as offerings. They bow, praise the Buddha, and circumambulate with sincere reverence. Day after day they practice thus with diligence, making it their lifelong commitment without exception. Each morning, if they have not performed this offering, they vow not to eat. This is the same in countries near and far, and there is no place where it is not done. This is the constant practice of devoted reverence maintained by the saṃgha.

As for the images, those made of clay, wood, or painted images cannot be bathed. But images of gold, silver, precious jade, copper, tin, brass, or stone are all suitable for regular bathing. When preparing the fragrant water for the next morning's image-bathing, on the previous evening one should separately soak various incenses, finely pounding them, wrapping them in silk, and steeping them overnight in water. Any kind of incense is suitable for bathing images. Each single-fragrance water should be placed in a separate bottle, and multiple incenses should not be mixed together in one vessel. The bottles used should preferably be ones that do not receive impure contact, namely those of gold, silver, various jewels, iron, copper, lead, tin, or white clean porcelain without cracks or damage. All must be scrubbed and polished until clean and bright, with the name of the fragrant water written on top, to be used in rotation. If one is poor and lacks such vessels, one may use new, clean stone, earthenware, or wooden bottles that have never been used before. These must not be used for any other purpose thereafter, as that would render them impure and defiled. They may, however, be used to hold the fragrant water remaining after the image-bathing is complete. Those who practice this method must be extremely careful and attentive regarding purity and impurity in their conduct and deportment. All offering vessels and implements should be understood according to this same standard.

When the time for the bathing comes, sweep the hall clean. In a spacious, clean area, plaster a large altar with pure cow dung, about one zhang in diameter. Place a raised platform in the center of the altar, and on it set a large vessel. Within the vessel, set a smaller stand and place the honored image upon it. Beside it, arrange a bench covered with an oilcloth, and line up the fragrant water bottles in order upon it. The five kinds of Dharma offerings, hundred-strand long threads, new clean soft cloths, and other such items should all be arrayed upon it.

When the assembly has gathered, they should jointly invite an ācārya learned in the Dharma, or the most senior elder among the assembly, to lead the group in chanting the verses of aspiration and dedication from the Image-Bathing Sūtra. The assembly should repeat every line in unison. The verses are:

> I now bathe and anoint all the tathāgatas,
> Assemblies of pure wisdom, merit, and adornment.
> May the beings of the five turbidities
> Swiftly realize the tathāgata's pure Dharma-body.
>
> The fragrance of discipline, concentration, wisdom, liberation, and the knowledge-and-vision of liberation
> Pervades the buddha-fields of the ten directions with constant sweet scent.
> May this incense-smoke likewise
> Perform immeasurable, boundless buddha-work.
>
> May the suffering wheel of the three evil paths also cease,
> And may all universally be freed from heat and obtain cool refreshment.
> May all arouse the mind of unsurpassed awakening
> And cross the river of craving to reach the other shore.

Then have one person hold a censer, kneel reverently, and in a melodious voice chant the Three Refuges Praise. At each line, the assembly performs one prostration. The praise is:

*Namo buddhāya guruve.*

*Namo dharmāya tayine.*

*Namo saṃghāya mahate.*

*Tribhyo 'pi sadā namo.*

Then have two persons blow conch shells and two persons chant praises. Various kinds of elegant music, strings, winds, and singing should all be performed simultaneously. The ācārya personally takes up a water bottle and pours the bathing water over the buddha image. Taking the long thread, one end is tied to the bottle and let down. Each person in the assembly holds the thread with palms joined at the heart, concentrating with sincere devotion and calling upon all the buddhas. If anyone cannot reach the thread, they may grasp the long robe of the person before them. The intention is as if each person were personally bathing the buddha image themselves. When all the fragrant water bottles have been poured out in succession one round, they must be replaced. After bathing one image, place the next unbathed image, refill each bottle with its corresponding fragrant water, and pour as before. The conch-blowing, praise-chanting, and music continue without stopping until all the honored images have been bathed.

When the bathing is complete, wipe the images dry with clean cloth and arrange them on the bench in their proper positions. Then take scented paste, and each person dips the tip of their ring finger in it, raising the finger to the heart. The ācārya chants the mantra for offering scented paste, and the assembly repeats every line in unison. At the final line, all flick it toward the Buddha from afar as an offering, and perform one prostration. The mantra for offering scented paste is:

*Oṃ sarva-tathāgata-gandha-pūjā-megha-samudra-spharaṇa-samaye hūṃ.*

Then take a flower tray, and the attendant gives each person in the assembly one flower. As before, they chant the mantra for offering flowers in unison and scatter the flowers toward the Buddha from afar. The mantra for offering flowers is:

*Oṃ sarva-tathāgata-puṣpa-pūjā-megha-samudra-spharaṇa-samaye hūṃ.*

Next the ācārya takes up the censer, and those on his left and right and the assembly each hold the robes of the person before them. They chant the mantra for burning incense in unison and offer it with wholehearted devotion. The mantra is:

*Oṃ sarva-tathāgata-dhūpa-pūjā-megha-samudra-spharaṇa-samaye hūṃ.*

The ācārya then takes up a tray of sweet pastries and, as before, chants the mantra for offering food with sincere devotion. The mantra is:

*Oṃ sarva-tathāgata-bali-pūjā-megha-samudra-spharaṇa-samaye hūṃ.*

Next, take up the lamp stand and chant the mantra for offering lamps once. The mantra is:

*Oṃ sarva-tathāgata-dīpya-pūjā-megha-samudra-spharaṇa-samaye hūṃ.*

Then have the praise-chanters each hold a censer and chant the wondrous praise for bathing images in a loud voice. Five praises are chanted, with the assembly kneeling reverently and prostrating once after each verse.

When the bathing and offering ceremony is fully complete, the ācārya takes the fragrant water used for bathing the Buddha and pours a little on the crown of his own head and sprinkles some on the heads of everyone in the assembly. This is the auspicious water of blessings from washing the holy ones adorned with ten thousand virtues. When sprinkled on the body and a small amount is drunk, it can cause the afflictions within the body to diminish, gradually leading to the accomplishment of unsurpassed awakening.

The entire assembly then prostrates with sincere devotion. Afterward, conch shells are blown and praises such as the aspiration and auspiciousness verses are chanted. With music and instruments leading the way, the assembly follows, circumambulating clockwise in proper order of seniority, three or seven circuits as appropriate, each chanting wondrous verses praising the Buddha from the Mahāyāna sūtras. When the Dharma ceremony is complete, they bow and take leave of the Buddha, each person carrying one image back to its original place. This is the standard method of image-bathing practiced in the West. Afterward, the saṃgha goes to the dining hall and only then takes their meal.

Alas, how sorrowful! In this land there is no one who knows of this. Buddha images go their entire existence without being bathed even once. Smoke-blackened, dust-covered, soiled by crows and rats, this is so throughout the entire country, and no one understands otherwise. Even when someone does bathe an image, following inherited ignorant customs, they separately fashion a nude figure and stand it inside a water vessel, using a small ladle to wash it. They make offerings with haughty and careless minds, utterly lacking any intent of reverence, and instead incur the sin of disrespect. How sad that they do not know the greatness of the field of merit for reverence!

Those of lofty discernment, worthy gentlemen, wise and sagacious persons, those who fear sin and seek merit, should pay special attention to this method. Even if one cannot bathe the images every day, one may do so monthly. The supreme merit and benefit obtained is as described by the Tathāgata's golden words in the Image-Bathing Sūtra.
