# 光讚經
## Brilliance Praise Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0222

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護).

---

## Fascicle One

### Chapter 1: Mahāprajñāpāramitā, The Brilliance Praise

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of five thousand bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats whose outflows were exhausted, free of all defilements, having attained mastery. Their minds were liberated through wisdom. They had attained benevolence and harmony and served as great guides. All their tasks were accomplished and brought to completion. They had cast off heavy burdens and attained their own benefit, removing the affliction of transmigration. With impartial liberation they had crossed beyond all conceptions, reaching the shore of the boundless, save only the Venerable Ānanda, a learner at the stage of stream-entry. Also present were five hundred bhikṣus, as well as bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, all assembled together.

Also present were bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who had attained dhāraṇī and samādhi, who cultivated the practice of emptiness, who honored signlessness, who did not cling to aspirations, who had attained the patient endurance of equality, who governed the immeasurable, and who had all attained the five supernatural powers. Their speech was swift and without weariness. They had renounced the desire for household profit. Their teaching of the Dharma did not seek offerings. They had reached the profound and wondrous Dharma, crossing to the boundless. They had attained fearlessness and transcended the affairs of Māra, freed from all the hindrances of the aggregates and coverings. They expounded on causes and conditions, following the mind's inclinations. Through innumerable kalpas they had practiced their vows with diligent effort. Their intentions were well directed: they were joyful in greeting and always first to approach others, free from resentful bonds. Entering into countless assemblies, their dignity was majestic and without any fearful difficulty. They could recall the affairs of immeasurable koṭis of kalpas. When they expounded the sūtras and the Dharma, they thoroughly understood the various meanings, knowing them to be like illusions, magical transformations, mirages, the moon reflected in water, dreams, shadows, echoes, and images in a mirror. They were courageous and without peer. With subtle and wondrous wisdom they knew the arising and conduct of all beings' minds. They transcended all discrimination; their intention harbored no harm. They were earnest in patient endurance, complete in their conduct. They clearly understood and faithfully assessed those whom they should liberate. They had taken up the limitless vow of gathering buddha-fields. Constantly in samādhi and concentration, they beheld with their eyes the countless buddha-worlds. Proficient and skillful, they entreated innumerable Buddha World-Honored Ones. Advancing and retreating, they could resolve the various kinds of views and attachments. With settled minds they enjoyed a hundred thousand practices. All the bodhisattvas' virtues were thus.

Their names were: Bhadrapāla Bodhisattva, Ratnākara Bodhisattva, Mahāsubhūti Bodhisattva, Nārada Bodhisattva, Gaukṣita Bodhisattva, Varuṇadatta Bodhisattva, Indra Bodhisattva, Bhadrapāla Bodhisattva, Sūkṣmamati Bodhisattva, Dhṛtimati Bodhisattva, Vardhamānamati Bodhisattva, Amoghadarśin Bodhisattva, Sthitapraṇidhāna Bodhisattva, Parivarta Bodhisattva, Nityodyukta Bodhisattva, Anikṣiptadhura Bodhisattva, Sūryateja Bodhisattva, Nirātman Bodhisattva, Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Jalinīprabha Bodhisattva, Ratnamudrāhasta Bodhisattva, Nityotthitahasta Bodhisattva, Nityāvanatahasta Bodhisattva, and Maitreya Bodhisattva. The assembly of bodhisattvas was thus beyond all limit, an incalculable number of koṭis of hundreds of thousands, all possessed of wondrous virtues, pure and identical in truth.

At that time the World-Honored One, seated upon his natural lion-throne, crossed his legs in the full-lotus posture and sat upright, his mind directed and settled. There was a samādhi called "King of Concentration" (*samādhi-rāja*). At that time, through this samādhi, he naturally entered correct absorption, thereby universally entering all concentrations. He gathered and held them in equality, guiding and directing them. When the Buddha entered samādhi and his mind became peacefully quiescent, he surveyed this world with the eye of the path. His body became luminously still and he smiled. From the soles of his feet he emitted sixty billion hundred-thousand rays of light. From his ten toes he emitted ten billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two sides, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two knees, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two calves, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two shoulders, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two elbows, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his navel, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his head, a billion hundred-thousand rays. From his ten fingers, ten billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two arms, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two eyebrows, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his neck, a billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two eyes, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his two ears, two billion hundred-thousand rays. From his nose, a billion hundred-thousand rays. From the four sides of his face, four hundred billion hundred-thousand rays. From his forty teeth, forty billion hundred-thousand rays. From the mark between his eyebrows, a billion hundred-thousand rays. From the crown-knot mark, sixty billion hundred-thousand rays. These illuminated the entire trichiliocosm without exception, and universally shone upon as many buddha-fields to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. The south, west, north, the four intermediate directions, above, and below were all likewise. Those beings who encountered this radiance all became tranquil in mind and generated the aspiration for the supreme, correct, and true path.

Thereupon the World-Honored One smiled with delight. From every pore he emitted numerous rays of light, illuminating the entire trichiliocosm, universally pervading the ten directions without omission. In as many buddha-worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, all those beings touched by the light became entirely quiescent, abiding in the supreme, correct, and true path. At that time the World-Honored One displayed the Tathāgata's pure, true, and wondrous radiance of his own nature, illuminating the entire trichiliocosm and reaching in each of the ten directions as many buddha-fields as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. If any person attained this light, they all ultimately reached the supreme, correct, and true path.

Thereupon the World-Honored One extended his tongue-base to cover the entire buddha-field, and emitted innumerable billion hundred-thousand rays of light, illuminating the entire trichiliocosm and pervading in each of the ten directions as many buddha-fields as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. Within that radiance, golden jeweled lotus flowers spontaneously grew. Upon each lotus sat a buddha, cross-legged upon a jeweled lotus, teaching the sūtras and the Dharma, expounding the six pāramitās. In all the ten directions it was likewise. If any being heard this Dharma teaching, all ultimately became firmly established in anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

Thereupon the World-Honored One, seated on his lion-throne, entered a samādhi called "Lion's Sport" (*siṃha-vikrīḍita*). Through this concentration he naturally entered correct absorption, and through his form and appearance universally displayed his majestic radiance, manifesting his supernatural powers. The trichiliocosm trembled in six ways. The periphery also shook, from the center spreading to the edges, gently and softly, out of compassion for all beings, causing them to obtain peace, happiness, and freedom from distress. At that time, in the trichiliocosm, beings in the hells, the realms of hungry ghosts and animals, those in unfavorable conditions and those gripped by terror, were spontaneously freed from the three evil paths. Having been freed, they all came forth and were born among humans, in the Heaven of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, the Yāma Heaven, the Tuṣita Heaven, the Nirmāṇarati Heaven, and the Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven. When those gods were born in the human and heavenly realms, they immediately recognized their former lives and went joyfully to the Buddha. They bowed at his feet, placed their palms together, and took refuge. In all of the ten directions it was likewise, without any difference.

At that time, among the beings of this trichiliocosm, the blind gained eyes and beheld forms and appearances. The deaf gained hearing and heard all sounds. The confused in mind were restored to sanity. Those in delusion immediately attained composure. The naked spontaneously received clothing. The hungry were spontaneously filled. The thirsty had nothing more to seek. The sick were healed. Those with bodily defects had all their faculties made whole. The weary spontaneously found relief, and those long dependent upon others no longer needed support. All beings attained minds of equality. They gazed upon one another as upon a father, a mother, a brother, a sister. Each with a common heart, impartial and without bias, all practiced loving-kindness. All beings cultivated the ten wholesome actions, pure in their brahma-conduct and free of all defilements. All people obtained peace and security, a peace and security like that of a bhikṣu who has attained the third dhyāna. At that time, beings attained wisdom, fully equipped with excellent, joyful, and well-settled concentration, freed from the lowly and having attained refinement.

At that time, the World-Honored One on his lion-throne, present in the trichiliocosm, was supreme in his surpassing supernatural majesty, his radiance blazing and brilliant, without any fear. His holy brightness shone with splendor, his honored countenance complete. It pervaded everywhere, illuminating as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and in the eight intermediate directions, above, and below, as many World-Honored One's lands as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. Like Mount Sumeru towering above all mountains, his brightness reached everywhere.

Thereupon the World-Honored One, carrying out the Tathāgata's command, through his natural holy decree caused all beings in the trichiliocosm to behold him together. At that time, in this world, the Śuddhāvāsa pure-abiding gods, Brahmā, the Paranirmitavaśavartin gods, the Nirmāṇarati gods, the Tuṣita gods, the Yāma gods, the Trāyastriṃśa gods, the gods of the Four Kings, and all people dwelling in the trichiliocosm saw themselves as if personally approaching the Tathāgata. They all spontaneously obtained heavenly flowers and ornaments, heavenly incense, heavenly mixed incense, heavenly powdered incense, heavenly blue lotuses, lotuses, and fresh flowers, with all manner of wondrous heavenly flowers complete with stems and leaves. Each one set out carrying these to the Tathāgata, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and scattered them upon him. The earthly flowers of land and water were likewise each taken in hand and offered to the World-Honored One in worship. The flowers and offerings that the gods and humans scattered rose up into space. The trichiliocosm was transformed into palaces and natural pavilions, from which hung heavenly flowers, silk canopies, banners, and streamers, floating and fluttering, brilliantly displayed. All the flowers and incense adorned the entire trichiliocosm so that its buddha-field became naturally majestic, its forms and appearances like the color of refined purple-gold. In all eight directions, above and below, it was likewise fragrant and luminous. Thereupon the people of all the cities of Jambudvīpa, beholding the Tathāgata's bodily manifestation of incalculable supernatural transformation, each thought in their hearts: "Today the Tathāgata is seated before me." Throughout the entire buddha-field it was likewise: each thought, "Today the Tathāgata sits before me, teaching the sūtras and the Dharma."

At that time, the World-Honored One, on his lion-throne, again smiled with delight and shed his light once more throughout the trichiliocosm, his vast radiance blazing and brilliant. The people of this land all saw together the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, with the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones presently there, together with bodhisattvas and assemblies of śrāvakas. Furthermore, all the beings in as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges also saw from afar this buddha-field, with Śākyamuni Buddha and the assembly of bhikṣus and bodhisattvas seated and teaching the sūtras. In all eight directions, above and below, it was likewise: they all saw from afar, equally and without difference.

Thereupon, past as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, at the farthest edge, there was a land called Jeweled Steps, and its buddha was named Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, presently dwelling there and teaching the *Mahāprajñāpāramitā Sūtra* for the benefit of beings. In that buddha-world there was a bodhisattva named Universal Radiance. Seeing the great light and the trembling of the earth, he went to Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata, bowed, and asked: "World-Honored One, for what cause and condition does this great light illumine this buddha-field and the earth greatly tremble? This self-manifest appearance of the Tathāgata's body must have a reason."

That buddha said to Universal Radiance Bodhisattva: "Son of good family, you should know that far to the west there is a world called Endurance, whose buddha is named Śākyamuni Tathāgata. He is presently teaching the Prajñāpāramitā for the bodhisattvas. This is the radiance of his supernatural power."

Universal Radiance Bodhisattva addressed Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata: "World-Honored One, I wish to go there to see Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bow, and pay my respects, as well as the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas and the assembly of pure youths who have attained dhāraṇī and the perfection of samādhi, with concentration and mastery, having crossed to the boundless: Śākyamuni, the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One." Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, said to Universal Radiance Bodhisattva: "Go, good man. You know when the time is right." Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata bestowed upon Universal Radiance Bodhisattva a golden lotus flower with a thousand petals: "Take this, good man, this jeweled lotus, and scatter it as an offering to Śākyamuni Tathāgata. Good man, if you wish to go and practice the conduct of quiescence, know that bodhisattvas born in the land of Endurance encounter much difficulty and are hard to meet."

Universal Radiance Bodhisattva received the golden lotus flower and, together with an immeasurable number of koṭis of hundreds of thousands of bodhisattvas, male and female, great and small, householders and renunciants, first offered worship to the buddhas, gods among gods, in the east, paying reverence, offering flowers, incense, mixed incense, and powdered incense. Then they proceeded to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed at his feet, and stood to one side. Universal Radiance Bodhisattva addressed the World-Honored One: "Great Sage, Jeweled Deeds Tathāgata sends his boundless greetings. He inquires whether you are well, vigorous in practice, and at ease. He also sends this golden lotus flower." The Buddha received the lotus flower and at once scattered it toward the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges. The flowers immediately pervaded all the buddha-worlds to the east, and on each one a buddha sat upon a naturally occurring golden lotus flower, teaching the sūtras and the Dharma, also expounding the six pāramitās. All beings who heard this teaching immediately became firmly established in anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Males and females, great and small, all bowed at the Buddha's feet, and each, through their merit, offered worship to the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One.

To the south of this place, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, at the farthest edge, was a buddha-world called Free from All Sorrow, whose buddha was named Sorrowless Chief, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Freed from Grief took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower upon him, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offering worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras.

To the west, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, was a world called Quiescence, whose buddha was named Jeweled Dragon, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Mind-Practice took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offered worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras.

To the north, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, was a world called Supremely Victorious, whose buddha was named Victorious over All Faculties, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Supreme Gift took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offered worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras.

Below, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, was a world called Benevolent Sage, whose buddha was named Sagely Chief, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Lotus-Enthroned took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offered worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras.

Above, past as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, was a world called Joyful Delight, whose buddha was named Chief of Delight, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. There a bodhisattva named Gift of Joy took leave of his buddha. That buddha bestowed a lotus flower, and together with countless bodhisattvas, he traversed the lands, offered worship to the buddhas, and came to Śākyamuni Tathāgata, bowed in worship, and sat to one side to hear the sūtras. In the four intermediate directions, it was likewise, equally and without difference.

At that time, upon this trichiliocosm there rained jeweled flowers, incense, banners, and canopies, naturally adorning fragrant trees and flowering trees, just as in the land called Lotus Steps, the world of the buddha Universal Flower, Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One, where Samantabhadra Bodhisattva resides. Sthitamati and the other devaputras, along with the remaining great spirits and the assembly of supremely powerful and boundless bodhisattvas bestowed by the World-Honored One, as well as all the gods, people of the world, māras, Brahmā, the śrāvaka assembly, gandharvas, asuras, spirits, and humans, all came together in assembly. These bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, while still in their youth, received food, drink, and merit spontaneously.

At that time the World-Honored One said to the Venerable Śāriputra: "If here there is a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, that one should diligently study the prajñāpāramitā."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, fully endowed and comprehending all dharmas, study the prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, having abided in the prajñāpāramitā and cultivated it without attachment to any location, thereby fulfills the dāna-pāramitā. Without deficiency in giving, one gives without any reluctance. One should fulfill the śīla-pāramitā. Through this cause and condition, one has never dwelt in wrongdoing or non-wrongdoing. One should also fulfill the kṣānti-pāramitā, giving rise to freedom from anger. One should study the vīrya-pāramitā and thereby receive a prediction. Through body and mind, one gives rise to diligent effort without arousing any outflows. One should fulfill the dhyāna-pāramitā. Because of this, one is free of seeking and desire."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva abides in the prajñāpāramitā, that one fulfills the four foundations of mindfulness (*smṛtyupasthāna*), generating nothing to be generated. One should also fulfill the four correct exertions (*samyakprahāṇa*), the four bases of supernatural power (*ṛddhipāda*), the five faculties (*indriya*), the five powers (*bala*), the seven factors of awakening (*bodhyaṅga*), and the eightfold path (*āryāṣṭāṅgamārga*), completely fulfilling the samādhi of emptiness, the samādhi of signlessness, and the samādhi of wishlessness. One should certainly fulfill the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables, the four formless samāpattis, and the eight liberations (*vimokṣa*), gradually fulfilling them and entering correct absorption. Through this as liberation, without any conception, without any internal conception, without a conception of worship, without a conception of light, without a conception of deep red, without a conception of putrefaction, without a conception of blue, without a conception of the eaten, bitten, wounded, and festering, without a conception of disorder, without a conception of dried bones, without a conception of scattered [remains], without a conception of any location. Having left behind all conceptions, one constantly dwells on the Buddha, mindful of the sūtras, mindful of the Saṃgha, mindful of the precepts. The mind is directed to generosity, directed to the gods, to the contemplation of breath, to the contemplation of death, to the conception of impermanence, to the conception of suffering and pleasure, to the conception of non-self, to the conception of transmigration, to the conception that all worlds are without delight, to the conception of habitual tendencies, to the conception of cessation. The wisdom of the path, the wisdom of exhaustion, the wisdom free of fever, the wisdom of non-arising, the wisdom of Dharma. Regarding all sūtras and Dharma, also the wisdom of non-wisdom, also the wisdom of non-self. Without internal wisdom, with subtle wisdom of mind, comprehending all wisdoms, wisdom as it truly is. All these are to be contemplated. What is practiced in samādhi is no different from signlessness, non-conception, and non-active concentration. The faculties differ, and different faculties yield different practices. Furthermore, there are practices that are difficult to attain: the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, great compassion and great loving-kindness. If one wishes to understand all of these, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to fully accomplish wisdom of the path, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to fully accomplish comprehensive wisdom and equip oneself with all supernatural wisdoms, one should practice the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to clearly understand all things, to draw near and remove the defilements, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice the prajñāpāramitā. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva studies the prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha further said to Śāriputra: "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to enter into quiescence, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to cross beyond the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas and abide on the stage of non-retrogression (*avaivartika*), one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to abide in the six supernatural powers, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to know the minds and root-conduct of all beings, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to surpass the wisdom of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to attain the gate of dhāraṇī, and if a good man wishes to encourage and assist the giving of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas and surpass them, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to surpass the moral discipline of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, and to encourage and assist the mind, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to fully accomplish samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge and vision of liberation, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to prominently accomplish dhyāna, samādhi, and samāpatti, and to encourage, assist, and gather together the mindfulness that liberates the mind, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to encourage and assist giving, distinguishing its limitless, immeasurable, and accomplished merits, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to fully accomplish the limitless and immeasurable keeping of precepts, patient endurance, diligent effort, concentration, and wisdom, one should study the prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha further said to Śāriputra: "If a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to fully accomplish and establish the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, and prajñā-pāramitā practice, and in all future lives to see the buddhas and attain buddhahood, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to accomplish the thirty-two marks and eighty minor characteristics, to fully accomplish the bodhisattva-nature, and while yet a pure youth to establish this stage and never be apart from the Buddha World-Honored Ones, and if whatever one aspires to in roots of virtue and offerings to the Tathāgata are to be fulfilled without fail; and if one wishes to fully satisfy what all beings desire in their hearts, whether food, drink, clothing, chariots, incense, flowers, mixed incense, perfumed ointments, bedding, lamps, towels, shoes, or whatever they should obtain, filling them with all wealth, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to fully equip as many beings as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, establishing them in the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, and dhyāna-pāramitā, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through a single wholesome root, accords with the virtues of the Tathāgata, inexhaustible and without diminishment, until the accomplishment of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva studies the prajñāpāramitā, the buddhas, gods among gods, in all eight directions, above and below, all together praise that person's merits. In the moment of generating the aspiration, if one wishes to travel to the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and in all ten directions, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes, with a single sound, to proclaim to the buddha-fields in all ten directions, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to establish the buddha-fields so that they are never broken off, and wishes to abide in internal emptiness, external emptiness, internal-external emptiness, emptiness of emptiness, great emptiness, ultimate emptiness, emptiness of the existent, emptiness of the nonexistent, conditioned emptiness, unconditioned emptiness, true emptiness, emptiness without worship, emptiness without cause, emptiness of causes, emptiness of the natural characteristic, emptiness of all dharmas, emptiness of non-apprehension, emptiness of nonexistence, natural emptiness, emptiness of the formless and the natural, emptiness of the practices conditioned by causes and supernatural power, and wishes to reach these, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to draw near to all tathāgatas, wishes to comprehend and understand all dharmas, and wishes to understand that all dharmas abide at their original limit, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who wishes to accomplish the prajñāpāramitā should abide in this way.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to count and know the sands, stones, trees, flowers, and all the motes of dust in the trichiliocosm, resolving every doubt, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to know the number of drops in all the great oceans, rivers, streams, springs, and sources of the trichiliocosm, and to cross over the ocean's creatures without harming them, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. Furthermore, Śāriputra, suppose all the fire in the trichiliocosm were to blaze simultaneously as at a kalpa-conflagration, and one wished to extinguish it all at once with a single motion, so that nothing burned, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished, when all the winds of the trichiliocosm blow up and crumble all the Sumeru mountains, reducing them to dust and ashes like burning reeds and straw, to extinguish the fire of the three realms with a single finger, whether a hand-finger or a toe, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished to fill the entire space of the trichiliocosm with one's body, sitting cross-legged pervading all space, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. To transform freely, without near or far, without great or small, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished to take all the Sumeru mountains of the trichiliocosm and lift them with one hand, placing them in extraordinarily many other buddha-worlds, without any thought of going and returning, without increase or decrease, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished to gather at one time all the buddhas, gods among gods, śrāvakas, and pratyekabuddhas in as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and to make offerings to them, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished, with a single robe, incense, flowers, powdered incense, perfumed ointments, silk canopies, banners, and streamers to make offerings to the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones and their śrāvaka assemblies, paying reverence to them all gathered at one time, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wished to establish all the beings of the trichiliocosm in the śīla-pāramitā, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge and vision of liberation, in the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, up to the fruit of nirvāṇa without remainder, causing them to enter parinirvāṇa, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā should practice giving as a pāramitā, studying it in this way: 'Such giving obtains great fruit and reward. Such giving causes birth among kings, nobles, and brahmins of great families and clans. Such giving causes birth among the gods of the Four Kings, Trāyastriṃśa, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, and Paranirmitavaśavartin. Such giving, relying on this generosity, contemplates the first dhyāna, the second, the third, and up to the fourth dhyāna, the samāpatti of infinite space in correct absorption, the samāpatti of infinite consciousness, the samāpatti of nothingness, the samāpatti of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Such giving arouses the noble eightfold path and attains the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, the fruit of arhatship, and the realization of a pratyekabuddha.' If one comprehends this, one should study the prajñāpāramitā in this way.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā always gives through skillful wisdom, in order to fulfill the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, dhyāna-pāramitā, and prajñā-pāramitā."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva fulfill the six pāramitās?"

He answered: "The giver has no thought of attachment; the recipient also does not forget the kindness. This is the dāna-pāramitā. One commits no offense and does not adorn oneself through the precepts. This is the śīla-pāramitā. One constantly maintains patient endurance, without anger or resentment toward any being. This is the kṣānti-pāramitā. One is diligent and untiring in wishing to liberate all beings. This is the vīrya-pāramitā. The mind is quiescent and without agitation. This is the dhyāna-pāramitā. One's wisdom comprehends emptiness, without reckoning a self. This is the prajñā-pāramitā. In brief, and to explain further: regarding wrongdoing and non-wrongdoing, and the absence of non-wrongdoing, this is the śīla-pāramitā. Being without anger or resentment is the kṣānti-pāramitā. Being diligent in body and mind without fatigue is the vīrya-pāramitā. Giving rise to non-distraction without any conceptual thought is the dhyāna-pāramitā. Comprehending all dharmas without any attachment is the prajñā-pāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to accomplish and acquire the merit of the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, of the past, future, and present, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. If one wishes to transcend all conditioned and unconditioned dharma-practices, understanding past, present, and future dharmas up to their original limit, where all dharmas arise without departing from their original limit, and wishes to attain this, and to practice all the dharmas of śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and bodhisattvas, and to worship the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, and wishes to attain the full retinue of the buddhas and the immeasurable companions, and wishes to attain the associates and party of bodhisattvas, and wishes to bring to purity all the merits and blessings, and wishes to practice giving with a mind that does not receive, without giving rise to the thought of transgressing the precepts, without anger, without laziness, without wishing to generate a distracted mind, and without wishing to generate a foolish mind, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to establish beings in the merit of giving, morality, and wisdom, encouraging them to cultivate these, and wishes to produce whatever merit should be produced, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to produce the five eyes, one should study the prajñāpāramitā. What are the five eyes? The fleshly eye, the heavenly eye, the wisdom eye, the Dharma eye, and the buddha eye. One should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to see the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, in as many worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, in the eight directions, above and below, and to hear with the heavenly ear all the sūtras and Dharma they teach, and also wishes to know the thoughts of the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to hear the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, throughout all ten directions teaching the sūtras and Dharma, hearing it without interruption until anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to see the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones of the past and their buddha-fields, and the present buddhas of the ten directions and all that exists in their lands, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to understand and know the twelve divisions of sūtras taught by the Tathāgata: the connected discourses, analytical discourses, verse discourses, hymn discourses, prompted discourses, discourses spoken on an occasion, birth stories, received discourses, extensive discourses, discourses on unprecedented dharmas, parables, and annotated discourses, which are not heard by the śrāvakas, and wishes to study and recite them all, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to hear the sūtras and Dharma taught by the Tathāgata in the eight directions, above and below, and to remember them without forgetting, and having grasped and retained them, wishes to teach them to the assembly and to others, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to inquire into the meaning of what the buddhas of the past and future have taught, and having heard it, wishes to teach it to others, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to illuminate the buddha-worlds to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, which are dark and gloomy, without the light of the sun and moon, and wishes to illuminate these and the worlds of the ten directions, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to convert beings in the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and throughout the ten directions, who are foolish and shrouded in darkness, who have not heard the name of the Buddha, have not been able to hear the sūtras, and have not seen the Saṃgha, and wishes to convert those beings and establish them in right view, enabling them to see the Buddha, to hear the sūtras and the Dharma, and to encounter the holy assembly, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes that all beings in as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and throughout the ten directions, who are born blind might gain eyes and behold forms, who are deaf might gain hearing, who are deranged might be restored to sanity, who are naked might receive clothing, who are hungry might obtain food, and who are thirsty might obtain water, and wishes, through the power of their vow, to bestow these blessings upon all, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, regarding those beings in this trichiliocosm and throughout the eight directions, above and below, in as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, who are in the evil destinies of the hells, hungry ghosts, and animals, wishes to extend benevolence to these beings so that they may attain their proper place, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to establish all the beings of as many buddha-worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges in the precepts, samādhi, wisdom, liberation, and knowledge and vision of liberation, in the fruit of stream-entry, the fruit of once-returning, the fruit of non-returning, the fruit of arhatship, and the realization of pratyekabuddha, up to the accomplishment of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, and also wishes to cultivate the Tathāgata's deportment and etiquette, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā should contemplate thus: 'Suppose there is what my own body cannot see, yet I wish to perceive it. One should study the prajñāpāramitā.' Suppose my body, from four inches of ground, with a single toe-tip, could pervade everything from the Heaven of the Four Kings through the desire and form realms up to the Akaniṣṭha Heaven, accompanied by an immeasurable retinue of koṭis of hundreds of thousands, going to the bodhi-tree and dwelling at the place of the path. One should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, seated at the bodhi-tree, with the gods of the Four Kings, the higher gods up to the pure-abiding gods equally and without difference, all coming with complete offerings, should accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, and whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down, the ground beneath would become vajra of itself. If one wishes to attain this, one should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā should contemplate thus: 'On what day shall I leave, renouncing the kingdom and forsaking the household, and on that very day accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, attaining perfect awakening? Having attained perfect awakening, on that very day I shall turn the wheel of Dharma. Having turned the wheel of Dharma, I shall cause immeasurable, incalculable beings to become free from dust and stain, and their Dharma-eye for all dharmas will become pure. Boundless and limitless multitudes of beings will attain the liberation of mind through the exhaustion of outflows, without further arising. Boundless and limitless beings will attain the non-retrogression of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.' Such a bodhisattva-mahāsattva should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who thinks: 'When I accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and attain perfect awakening, I shall have an immeasurable holy assembly of śrāvaka disciples who are learners. Through a single exposition of the sūtras and the Dharma, in a single sitting, they shall attain arhatship. All the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas shall attain the non-retrogression of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. There shall be an immeasurable, incalculable, boundless assembly of bodhisattvas, whose lifespans are immeasurable, whose light illumines far beyond all limits.' One should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva wishes to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and accomplish perfect awakening, and wishes that in that buddha-field there shall be no sound of the words 'lust,' 'anger,' or 'delusion,' and that all beings there shall have such an appearance as if they had fully accomplished the prajñāpāramitā: 'Excellent, well-tamed and docile! Excellent, wise and well-cultivated in brahma-conduct! And traveling in accord without lingering among beings is excellent!' One should study the prajñāpāramitā.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who vows: 'May I attain the fully accomplished holy understanding, and through the correct Dharma, the concentration of richness without any sound.' One should study the prajñāpāramitā. Thinking in one's mind: 'With my pure sound I shall cause the beings of as many worlds as there are grains of sand in the Ganges to attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.' One should study the prajñāpāramitā."

### Chapter 2: Conforming to Emptiness

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā and arouses these merits, the Four Heavenly Kings immediately rejoice: 'We shall present four bowls before the Four Kings, offering them as we offered them in the past to the tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones, and shall likewise make offerings to one who studies the path of Dharma.' At that time the Trāyastriṃśa gods also rejoice, as do the Yāma, Tuṣita, Nirmāṇarati, and Paranirmitavaśavartin gods: 'We shall all serve and make offerings to this good man. The bodies of the asuras shall diminish, and the bodies of the gods shall increase.' Throughout the trichiliocosm, up to the Akaniṣṭha Heaven, none fail to rejoice: 'We shall entreat and encourage the turning of the Dharma wheel.' Śāriputra, when a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, it increases and benefits and completes the six pāramitās. Good men and good women rejoice with delight: 'We shall be as the loving-kindness of parents, wife, children, relatives, and friends; the loving-kindness of parents, brothers, wife, children, intimate friends and companions, and the affection, respect, and joy of seeing each other.' The gods of the Four Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa, the Yāma, the Tuṣita, the Nirmāṇarati, the Paranirmitavaśavartin, up to the Akaniṣṭha, will not allow the bodhisattva to encounter any defilement or desire. With minds directed, they come to serve and bow, thinking: 'We shall also cause this one to attain the pure conduct of Brahmā, the practice free from impurity, without the habit of sensual desire, so as to be born in the Brahmā-heaven.' There is no negligence that engenders negligence. Those who have form cannot progress to anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Therefore the bodhisattva, through pure brahma-conduct, forsakes household affairs and then attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. One does not attain the Buddha path through impurity."

The Venerable Śāriputra addressed the World-Honored One: "Must the bodhisattva's dharma necessarily involve having parents, wife, and children, relatives and friends?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "There are bodhisattvas who necessarily have parents but should not have wives and children. Some, from the time of their initial aspiration, purely cultivate brahma-conduct, becoming a pure youth, up to the accomplishment of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. There are also bodhisattvas who, through skillful means (*upāya-kauśalya*), partake of the five desires and afterward forsake the household, attaining anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. It is like a clever and skilled magician and his disciple, well-trained in the art of illusion, who conjure the five desires and use these five pleasures for their own enjoyment and amusement. Śāriputra, what do you think: does that magician truly partake of the five desires?"

Śāriputra said: "No, God among Gods."

The Buddha said: "Just so, Śāriputra. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through skillful means, partakes of the five desires in order to convert beings. That bodhisattva-mahāsattva is not stained by the five desires. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, through countless means, sometimes praises desire and sometimes reviles desire as a blazing fire, desire as a flaw, desire as an enemy, desire as an adversary. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, for the sake of liberating beings, distinguishes the affairs of the five desires."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "God among Gods, how does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practice the prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not see the bodhisattva, nor does one see the word 'bodhisattva.' One does not see the prajñāpāramitā, nor does one see the words 'practicing the prajñāpāramitā,' nor does one see 'non-practice.' Why? The word 'bodhisattva' is naturally empty. What is empty has no form, no feeling, no perception, no formations, no consciousness, and is not different from the emptiness of form, not different from the emptiness of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. As form is empty, so too feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are empty. What is called emptiness is the emptiness that is form, the emptiness that is feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, being naturally so. Why? What is called 'bodhisattva' is merely a conventional name. What is called 'the path' is also a conventional name. What is called 'emptiness' is also a conventional name. The nature of dharmas does not arise and does not cease, and is without defilement. It has no support and no contention. If there is a bodhisattva whose practice is thus, one does not see any arising, nor does one see any ceasing, nor does one see any support, nor does one see any contention. Why? Words are established through deception, relying on adventitious conceptions. It is because of conceptions that these dharmas come about. From what are words established? They are merely entrusted to empty speech. Understanding thus, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā: one entirely does not see any name or designation. Having nothing to see and not being without seeing, one is without any support. This is practicing the prajñāpāramitā."

### Chapter 3: Practicing Emptiness, Part 1

The Buddha further said to Śāriputra: "When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, one should contemplate thus: 'What is called "bodhisattva," what is called "buddha," is merely a conventional name. What are called "form, feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness" are merely conventional names. They all stem from the self. What is called "self" is in fact nothing whatsoever. There is no self, no person, no life-force, no lifespan, no blood-bearing or crawling creature, no mind, no intention. Whatever is done and created, whatever is naturally habituated, undergone, and seen, known, and perceived, all these kinds are unobtainable, empty, and without any attachment. They are all conventional names, merely empty words.' Thus a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā: one does not see beings. If one has nothing to see, one also does not have seeing, nor does one see the words that are entrusted. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva whose practice is thus follows the Tathāgata's teaching and practices the prajñāpāramitā. Having set aside the Tathāgata, this wisdom surpasses all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. What one arouses through the practice of emptiness does not bring confusion. Why? Because that person, in what they cultivate, does not see any word upon which to rely. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva whose practice is thus practices the prajñāpāramitā."

The Buddha said: "Suppose, Śāriputra, bhikṣus like Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana filled the whole of Jambudvīpa, dense as bamboo, reeds, sugar-cane, rice, or hemp in a thicket, all fully endowed with wisdom. They could never match the bodhisattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā, not a hundredfold, not a thousandfold, not ten-thousandfold, not a hundred-millionfold, not even to begin comparison. Why? The bodhisattva's wisdom seeks to liberate all beings, and it is for this reason.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā, in a single day's practice of wisdom, surpasses all the attainments of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Set aside that Jambudvīpa filled with bhikṣus like Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana. Even if the entire trichiliocosm were filled with bhikṣus like Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana, all their wisdom could not match the bodhisattva-mahāsattva. Set aside that trichiliocosm of Śāriputras and Mahāmaudgalyāyanas. Suppose as many buddha-fields to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, pervading all ten directions, were all filled with bhikṣus like Śāriputra and Mahāmaudgalyāyana. Even they could not match a single day of the bodhisattva who practices the prajñāpāramitā. Such wisdom surpasses the wisdom of all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas by a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a hundred-millionfold, beyond any comparison."

Thereupon the Venerable Śāriputra addressed the World-Honored One: "The wisdom of the śrāvakas, the wisdom of the stream-enterer, once-returner, non-returner, arhat, pratyekabuddha, bodhisattva, and the tathāgata, worthy one, perfectly awakened one: reckoning all these wisdoms, none can be destroyed, none can be contested, none arise, and they are naturally empty. God among Gods, if what cannot be destroyed, cannot be contested, does not arise, and is naturally empty, how can there be any differentiation to be attained? How can the bodhisattva's single day of practiced wisdom surpass all the wisdom of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "What do you think? Why does the bodhisattva practice the prajñāpāramitā? In a single day, the wisdom that is practiced, the vows that are established, the cultivation of skillful means (*upāya*), and the practice of compassion are all for the sake of all beings. One thoroughly comprehends all dharmas in order to convert beings and bring them to cessation. Do the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas establish such a cause and such wisdom?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods." The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, what do you think? Do the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas have this thought: 'We shall attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening, and convert beings to the realm of nirvāṇa, causing them to attain cessation'?"

He answered: "No."

The Buddha said: "Therefore one should further know that all the wisdom of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, even a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a hundred-thousandfold, a hundred-millionfold wisdom, can never match it. What do you think? Do the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas have this thought: 'We shall practice the six pāramitās, convert beings, purify buddha-fields, fully accomplish the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, and accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, liberating and bringing to cessation immeasurable, limitless, incalculable beings'?"

He answered: "No, God among Gods." The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva generates the thought: 'I shall practice the six pāramitās, fully accomplish all dharmas, and accomplish anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, liberating incalculable beings.'"

The Buddha said: "It is like the palace of the sun, which, exerting its radiance, in a single moment universally illuminates the whole of Jambudvīpa without omission. Just so, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the six pāramitās, fully accomplishes the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, and attains anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, converting and liberating immeasurable, limitless, incalculable beings."

The Venerable Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva surpass the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas and attain the stage of non-retrogression, purely cultivating the Buddha path?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "Herein, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva, from the time of the initial aspiration, practices the six pāramitās, crosses beyond the dharmas of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness, and thereby surpasses the stages of śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, abiding on the stage of non-retrogression."

The Venerable Śāriputra further addressed the Buddha: "How is a bodhisattva-mahāsattva the most blessed among all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva, from the time of the initial aspiration, practices the six pāramitās until sitting at the bodhi-tree, always the most blessed among all śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas. Why? When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva appears in the present, the true and wondrous Dharma naturally arises. With it come the ten wholesome actions, the five precepts, the eightfold noble path and the eight precepts of purification, the four dhyānas, the four immeasurables, the four formless samāpattis, the four foundations of mindfulness, the four correct exertions, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, the eightfold path. Also appearing in the world are the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas. All such virtues appear in the world. Through this, the distinctions of noble families and clans, brahmins and great elders, the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven up to the heaven of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, pratyekabuddhas, and tathāgatas, worthy ones, perfectly awakened ones are known. It is because of this that such distinctions exist."

Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "How does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva bring blessings to purity and completion?"

The World-Honored One said: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva does not bring blessings to purity and completion from within the blessings themselves. Why? Ultimating emptiness is precisely how a bodhisattva-mahāsattva completes blessings. Why? Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva is a giver of gifts. What does one give? One gives the gift of wholesome Dharma, converting beings. What is the wholesome Dharma? The ten wholesome actions, the five precepts, the six pāramitās, the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and the eighteen unique buddha-dharmas. One converts stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, and pratyekabuddhas. Such is the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One's giver of gifts."

Śāriputra further addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, what practice does a bodhisattva-mahāsattva follow in practicing the prajñāpāramitā?"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "Herein, if a bodhisattva practices the emptiness of form, that is practicing the prajñāpāramitā. If one practices the emptiness of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, that is the practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, if a bodhisattva-mahāsattva comprehends the emptiness of the eye, the emptiness of the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of form, sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of the eye-element, the form-element, the eye-consciousness-element, the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind elements, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of the ear-element, sound, ear-consciousness, nose, smell, nose-consciousness, tongue, taste, tongue-consciousness, body, touch, body-consciousness, mind-element, dharmas, mind-consciousness, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of suffering, the emptiness of origination, the emptiness of cessation, and the emptiness of the eightfold path, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of ignorance, the emptiness of formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, and aging-and-death, this is the practice. If one comprehends the emptiness of all dharmas, this is the practice. Whatever is naturally conditioned or unconditioned, if one can comprehend its emptiness, this is the practice. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā comprehends original purity as empty, and one's own nature is likewise. This is the practice. Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā should comprehend these seven emptinesses. This is the true practice. Practicing the prajñāpāramitā through these seven emptinesses, one sees no accordance or non-accordance, no practice or non-practice of form. Without making this contemplation, one does not see feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. One does not see any dharma of form that arises or ceases. One does not see any dharma of feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness that arises or ceases. One does not see any dharma of form with any attachment or any contention. One does not see any dharma of feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness with any attachment or any contention. One does not see oneself as dwelling together with form, nor dwelling together with feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness. One does not see oneself as dwelling together with birth-and-death, nor as not dwelling together with birth-and-death. Why? There is ultimately no dharma that dwells together with anything. All things arise by conditions, and their original nature is empty. Śāriputra, form is empty, and there is no form. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are empty, and there is no consciousness."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "What is empty has no arising and no ceasing. If form is empty, there is no form. If feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are empty, there is no consciousness. If form is empty, there is nothing to be seen. If feeling is empty, there is nothing to be afflicted by. If perception is empty, there is nothing to be conceived. If formations are empty, there is nothing to be created. If consciousness is empty, there is nothing to be discriminated. Why? Śāriputra, form being different is not the same as emptiness. Emptiness is not different. Form is not separate. Form is naturally empty. Form is precisely emptiness. Feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness are not separately different, and emptiness is not different from them. If emptiness is not different, consciousness is not different. Consciousness is naturally empty. Consciousness is precisely emptiness."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "What is empty does not arise and does not cease, has no attachment and no contention, no increase and no decrease, no past, no future, no present. There is no form, feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness. There is no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind. There is no form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharma. Ignorance does not cease; ignorance does not move. There is no consciousness, no name-and-form, no six sense-bases, no contact, no feeling, no craving, no grasping, no becoming, no birth, no aging, no sickness, no death, nor the cessation of birth, aging, sickness, and death. There is no suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path. There is no attainment, and there is no time. There is no fruit of stream-entry, no fruit of once-returning, no fruit of non-returning, no fruit of arhatship, no realization of pratyekabuddha. There is no attainment of the path, and there is no Buddha path. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva whose practice of the prajñāpāramitā is thus, this is how the bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā: one does not see the prajñāpāramitā as according or not according, as practicing or not practicing. One does not see giving, precepts, patience, effort, meditation, or wisdom. One does not see the six pāramitās. One does not see form, feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. One does not see the eye as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. Likewise for the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. One does not see form, sound, smell, taste, touch, or dharmas as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. One does not see the four foundations of mindfulness as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. Likewise for the four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and eightfold path. One does not see the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and eighteen unique buddha-dharmas as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. One does not see the Tathāgata's sarvajñā-wisdom as according or not according, practicing or not practicing. This is how, Śāriputra, the bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā. This is the accordance of practice."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā: emptiness does not contend with emptiness; emptiness does not practice with emptiness. Signlessness does not contend with signlessness; signlessness does not practice with signlessness. Wishlessness does not contend with wishlessness; wishlessness does not practice with wishlessness. Emptiness does not correspond to emptiness; signlessness does not correspond to signlessness; wishlessness does not correspond to wishlessness. Why? Emptiness has no practice and no non-practice. Signlessness also has no practice and no non-practice. Wishlessness also has no practice and no non-practice. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice."

The Buddha further said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā comprehends that the natural characteristics of all dharmas cross beyond emptiness. Having crossed beyond emptiness, one does not contend with form and has no practice. One does not contend with feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness and has no practice. One does not contend with past form, nor does one see past form. One does not contend with past form, nor does one see future form. One does not contend with future form, nor does one see present form. One does not contend with present form. Likewise, one does not contend with past feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness, nor with future or present feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness, nor does one see past, future, or present feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not contend with the past regarding the future, does not contend with the future regarding the past, does not contend with the present regarding the past and future, does not contend with the past and future regarding the present. One does not see the three times as practicing emptiness in the prajñāpāramitā. One who practices thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā, whose practice is thus, as it should be practiced: one does not contend with or practice past sarvajñā, nor does one see it. Is there any past sarvajñā, together with the practice and its contention? One does not contend with or practice future sarvajñā, nor is there any practice, nor does one see it. Is there any future sarvajñā, together with the practice and its contention? Nor does one contend with or practice present sarvajñā, nor does one see present sarvajñā. Is there any sarvajñā, together with the practice and its contention? One who practices the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice the sarvajñā of form, nor does one see the sarvajñā of form. One does not practice the sarvajñā of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness, nor does one see the sarvajñā of feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. One does not practice the sarvajñā of the eye, nor does one see the eye. One does not practice the sarvajñā of the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, nor does one see the ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind. One does not practice the sarvajñā of form, nor does one see form. One does not see form, one does not practice the sarvajñā of sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas, nor does anything appear."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā also does not follow the sarvajñā of the dāna-pāramitā, nor does one see the dāna-pāramitā, the śīla-pāramitā, the kṣānti-pāramitā, the vīrya-pāramitā, or the dhyāna-pāramitā. Likewise one does not practice the sarvajñā of the prajñāpāramitā, nor does one see the sarvajñā of the prajñāpāramitā. One also does not follow the sarvajñā of the four foundations of mindfulness, nor does one see the sarvajñā of the four foundations of mindfulness. Likewise for the four correct exertions, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and eightfold path. Nor does one follow the sarvajñā of the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and eighteen unique buddha-dharmas, nor does one see them, nor does one see the Tathāgata's sarvajñā of the powers and dharmas. One who practices the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice sarvajñā as the Buddha, nor does the Buddha practice sarvajñā. One does not practice sarvajñā as the path, nor does the path practice sarvajñā. Why? The Buddha is sarvajñā, and sarvajñā is the Buddha. The path is sarvajñā, and sarvajñā is the path. The ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, and eighteen unique buddha-dharmas are likewise."

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice form as existent, does not practice form as non-existent. One does not practice feeling, perception, formations, or consciousness as existent or non-existent. One does not reckon form as permanent or impermanent. One does not reckon form as suffering or pleasure. One does not reckon form as having a self or being without self. The five aggregates and six sense-gates are likewise. One does not reckon the five aggregates as empty or non-empty, as having characteristics or being without characteristics, as having aspirations or being without aspirations. One practices the prajñāpāramitā thus: 'What I now practice is also without reception. There is no practice, and there is nothing taken. There is practice and yet no practice. There is reception and yet no reception. There is taking and yet no taking.'"

The Buddha said to Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the true practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice for the sake of the dāna-pāramitā, śīla-pāramitā, kṣānti-pāramitā, vīrya-pāramitā, or dhyāna-pāramitā when practicing the prajñāpāramitā. One does not practice the prajñāpāramitā for the sake of the stage of non-retrogression, or for converting beings, or for purifying the buddha-field, or for the Tathāgata's ten powers, four fearlessnesses, four analytical knowledges, or eighteen unique buddha-dharmas. One does not practice the prajñāpāramitā for the sake of ultimate emptiness, internal emptiness, external emptiness, internal-external emptiness, emptiness of emptiness, great emptiness, true emptiness, conditioned emptiness, unconditioned emptiness, ultimate emptiness, emptiness without grade, original purity emptiness, natural characteristic emptiness, emptiness of all dharmas, emptiness of non-arising, emptiness of non-ceasing, emptiness of the formless, natural emptiness, emptiness of the formed and formless, or the original foundation, or the dharma-realm, or the original limit. Why? When a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, regarding all dharmas there is nothing destroyed and nothing seen."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not practice for the sake of supernatural powers, nor for the heavenly eye, nor for the heavenly ear, nor for knowing others' minds, nor for recollecting past events. Why? When practicing the prajñāpāramitā, one does not even see the prajñāpāramitā. How much less would one behold the bodhisattva's supernatural powers? One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, when a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practices the prajñāpāramitā, one does not think: 'I shall use supernatural powers to go to the buddha-fields to the east, as many as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, to see the tathāgatas and bow to them.' Nor does one think: 'I shall reach the eight directions, above and below.' It is likewise, equally and without difference. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'I shall use the heavenly ear to hear all that the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, have taught.' Nor: 'I shall perceive and see what all beings think in their minds.' Nor: 'I shall recollect the places I have dwelt in past lives.' Nor: 'With the heavenly eye I shall see all beings wherever they are.'"

The Buddha said: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice. Thus, Śāriputra, one who practices thus liberates an immeasurable, incalculable assembly of beings. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can do thus, Māra and his retinue cannot find any opening. Furthermore, even in other world-systems, people hearing of such virtue from afar all bow in respect. Furthermore, the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, in as many buddha-worlds to the east as there are grains of sand in the Ganges, and in all eight directions, above and below, all together protect this bodhisattva, who will never fall to the stages of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas. The gods of the Four Kings up to the Akaniṣṭha all together protect this bodhisattva-mahāsattva, so that no one seeking an opening can find one. Whatever one undertakes and whatever one should do, one obtains merit in the present. Why? Because one directs a compassionate mind toward all beings. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā, with only slight effort attains the gate of dhāraṇī and the gate of samādhi. One swiftly draws near the Tathāgata, Worthy One, Perfectly Awakened One. In all one's births, one constantly encounters and sees the Buddha, never apart from the buddhas, until attaining anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi."

The Buddha said: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'Are there any dharmas? Are all dharmas in accordance or not in accordance? Are they equal or not equal?' Why? At that time, the practitioner does not see any dharma as according or not according, as practicing or not practicing, as equal or unequal."

The Buddha said: "One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'I shall swiftly comprehend the dharma-realm and reach perfect awakening, yet there is no perfect awakening.' Why? One who attains the dharma-realm also has nothing to awaken to. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not see that all dharmas and the dharma-realm have any illness or any quiescence. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'The dharma-realm of all dharmas has various kinds; I do not reckon them as separately different.' One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think: 'Regarding all these dharmas and the dharma-realm, I contemplate or do not contemplate, I see or do not see.' Why? One does not see any dharma that exists and that can be grasped for separate contemplation. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think that the dharma-realm is anxious about the practice of emptiness, nor does the practice of emptiness cause anxiety about the dharma-realm. One who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus, this is the accordance of practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not think about whether the eye-element is empty. One does not cause the eye-element to be anxious. Form does not cause anxiety about emptiness; emptiness does not cause anxiety about form. The form-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the emptiness-element does not cause anxiety about form. The eye-consciousness-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the consciousness-element does not cause anxiety about the emptiness of eye-consciousness. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind, sound, smell, taste, touch, and dharmas are likewise. The mind-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the emptiness-element does not cause anxiety about mind. The dharma-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the emptiness-element does not cause anxiety about dharmas. The consciousness-element does not cause anxiety about emptiness; the emptiness-element does not cause anxiety about consciousness."

The Buddha said: "Śāriputra, this is the foremost practice, namely the practice of emptiness. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice emptiness will not fall to the stages of śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas, can purify buddha-fields and convert beings, and will swiftly attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. Reckoning all the practices of the prajñāpāramitā, the practice of the prajñāpāramitā is the most honored, the greatest, the highest, without bottom, without peer. Why? The practice of the prajñāpāramitā is the supreme practice: the practice of emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva should practice thus and should uphold it in this way, and one will swiftly draw near the stage of receiving a prediction. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices in this accordance liberates and benefits immeasurable, incalculable beings. If one does not think: 'I practice the prajñāpāramitā, and the buddhas, World-Honored Ones, shall give me a prediction.' Nor does one think: 'I shall draw near, receive a prediction, purify the buddha-field, and attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening, and shall turn the wheel of Dharma.' Why? That practitioner does not cling to the dharma-realm, nor is it empty and quiescent, nor does one see any different dharma through which to practice the prajñāpāramitā and receive a prediction from the buddhas, gods among gods, and attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and perfect awakening. Why? A bodhisattva-mahāsattva practicing the prajñāpāramitā does not give rise to the conception of a person, the conception of a self, the conception of a lifespan, the conception of a life-force, the conception of a multitude, or the conception of views and knowledge. Why? Reckoning a self, beings do not arise and do not cease. Reckoning a person, the original nature does not arise and does not cease. That which does not arise and does not cease: what practice of the prajñāpāramitā does it have? A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice thus, with nothing arising in persons, practices the prajñāpāramitā. Beings are empty, beings are unobtainable, beings are quiescent. This is practicing the prajñāpāramitā. Thus, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva follows and cultivates emptiness as the foremost practice.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra, a bodhisattva-mahāsattva who can practice the prajñāpāramitā thus surpasses all practices. Set aside what has been described as the practice to be followed. It is the practice of great loving-kindness and the practice of great compassion. A bodhisattva-mahāsattva who practices these will never give rise to a mind of covetous jealousy, nor a mind of broken precepts, nor a mind of anger, nor a mind of laziness, nor a mind of agitation, nor a mind of wrong views."
