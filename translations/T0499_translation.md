# 佛為阿支羅迦葉自化作苦經
## Sūtra of the Buddha's Teaching to Acela Kāśyapa on Whether Suffering is Self-Created

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0499

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the city of Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa.

At that time, the World-Honored One, in the early morning, put on his robe and took up his bowl, and left Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa to enter the city of Rājagṛha to beg for food.

At that time, Acela Kāśyapa, on some small errand, had left the city of Rājagṛha and was heading toward Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa. From a distance he saw the World-Honored One, and upon seeing him, went to the Buddha and addressed him: "Gautama! I wish to ask something. Do you have the leisure to give me an answer or not?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "Now is not the time for discussion. I am entering the city to beg for food. When I return, then will be the proper time, and I shall explain it for you."

A second time Kāśyapa spoke likewise. A third time he asked again: "Gautama! Why do you make this difficult for me? How is this any different, Gautama? I now wish to ask something; please explain it for me."

The Buddha told Acela Kāśyapa: "Ask whatever you wish."

Acela Kāśyapa addressed the Buddha: "How is it, Gautama? Is suffering self-created?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "To say that suffering is self-created: this is indeterminate."

Kāśyapa asked again: "How is it, Gautama? Is suffering created by another?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "To say that suffering is created by another: this is also indeterminate."

Kāśyapa asked again: "Is suffering created by both self and other?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "To say that suffering is created by both self and other: this is also indeterminate."

Kāśyapa asked again: "How is it, Gautama? Is suffering created neither by self nor by other, arising without cause?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "To say that suffering is neither self-created nor other-created, arising without cause: this is also indeterminate."

Kāśyapa asked again: "Gautama! When asked 'Is suffering self-created?' you answered 'indeterminate.' 'Is it other-created?' 'Is it created by both self and other?' 'Is it neither self-created nor other-created, arising without cause?' You answered 'indeterminate.' Then does this suffering not exist at all?"

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "It is not that this suffering does not exist. Indeed, this suffering exists."

Kāśyapa addressed the Buddha: "Excellent, Gautama! You say this suffering exists. Please teach me the Dharma so that I may know suffering and see suffering."

The Buddha told Kāśyapa: "If the one who acts is identical with the one who experiences, I should say suffering is self-created. If it is another who acts and another who experiences, then it would be other-created. If the one who acts experiences it himself, and the other who acts also gives suffering, then it would be created by both self and other, and I do not say this either. If it were not due to self or other, and suffering arose without cause, I also do not say this. Departing from all these extremes, I teach the middle path. The Tathāgata teaches the Dharma: because this exists, that exists; because this arises, that arises. That is: conditioned by ignorance, there are formations, and so on, up to the complete arising of the great mass of suffering. When ignorance ceases, formations cease, and so on, up to the cessation of the great mass of suffering."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, Acela Kāśyapa, far removed from dust and free from defilement, attained the purification of the Dharma-eye.

Then Acela Kāśyapa, having seen the Dharma, attained the Dharma, known the Dharma, and entered the Dharma, having crossed beyond all doubt, not relying on the knowledge of others and not depending on the guidance of others, in the correct Dharma and discipline his mind was fearless. He joined his palms and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! I have now crossed over. From this day forth, I take refuge in the Buddha, take refuge in the Dharma, and take refuge in the Saṅgha. For the rest of my life I shall be an upāsaka. Bear witness and acknowledge me."

Acela Kāśyapa, hearing the Buddha's teaching, rejoiced and was delighted, made obeisance, and departed.

Then, not long after Acela Kāśyapa had taken leave of the World-Honored One, he was gored and killed by a cow protecting her calf. At the moment of death, his faculties were clear and his complexion was bright and fair.

At that time, the World-Honored One entered the city to beg for food. A number of bhikṣus had also entered the city of Rājagṛha to beg for food, and heard it reported that Acela Kāśyapa, having heard the Dharma from the World-Honored One, had taken his leave and shortly after was gored and killed by a cow protecting her calf, and that at the moment of death his faculties were clear and his complexion bright and fair. The bhikṣus, having finished their alms round and departed the city, put away their robes and bowls, washed their feet, went to the World-Honored One, prostrated at his feet, and sat to one side. They addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! This morning many of us bhikṣus entered the city for alms and heard that Acela Kāśyapa, having heard the Dharma and discipline from the World-Honored One, took his leave and shortly after was gored and killed by a cow protecting her calf, and that at the moment of death his faculties were clear and his complexion bright and fair. World-Honored One! To what destiny was he reborn? Where did he take birth? What did he attain?"

The Buddha told the bhikṣus: "He had already seen the Dharma, known the Dharma, and followed the Dharma without being attached to it. He has already attained parinirvāṇa. You should go and make offerings to his remains."

At that time, the World-Honored One gave Acela Kāśyapa the foremost prediction.
