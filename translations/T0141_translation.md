# 佛說阿遬達經
## Sūtra of Asudatta Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0141

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇabhadra (求那跋陀羅)

---

Thus have I heard:

The Buddha was in the country of Śrāvastī and addressed the bhikṣus: "Listen to what I say, for it is difficult to achieve. A father and mother bear a child and raise it with nursing and nourishment. When the child has grown, they wish it to see the light of the sun and the moon. The parents show the child all the things in the world, wishing it to know good from evil.

"Bhikṣus! If such a child were to carry its father on one shoulder and its mother on the other, even until the end of life without stopping, and further adorn their bodies with the most precious treasures in the world, bright moon-pearls, jade discs, lapis lazuli, coral, and self-born white pearls from wild creatures, it would still not be enough to repay the kindness of the parents.

"If the parents delight in killing, the child should admonish them to stop, causing them to no longer kill. If the parents have evil thoughts, the child should constantly admonish them to stop, causing them to always think of good and have no evil thoughts. If the parents are foolish and of little wisdom, not knowing the sūtras and the path, the child should tell them of the Buddha's sūtras. If the parents are greedy and jealous, the child should gently admonish them. If the parents do not know good from evil, the child should gradually and gently explain it to them.

"Bhikṣus! A child should be like this. A person who, being a child, desires finer clothing than the parents, food more delicious than the parents, and speech more elevated than the parents: after death, such a one will enter hell. A person who, being a child, serves the parents with filial devotion and practices in this way: after death, such a one will be born in heaven."

All the bhikṣus bowed their heads and received this good teaching.

The Buddha said: "There was a great and worthy upāsaka named Asudatta who took a wife for his son. The wife was named Yujā. Yujā was the daughter of a great and wealthy family. She did not treat Asudatta with the courtesy due to a mother-in-law and father-in-law, nor did she respect her husband, and this went on for several years. Asudatta, being a worthy upāsaka, said nothing and did nothing about it. He went to where the Buddha was and reproached himself: 'I took a wife for my son from a great wealthy family of the country, and my heart was full of joy. But now, having served me for several years, she is arrogant and willful, and does not observe the duties of a wife. I beseech the Buddha to condescend to come to my house tomorrow.'

"The Buddha remained silent and did not reply. His remaining silent and not replying meant that the next morning he would go. The next day the Buddha arrived at Asudatta's house. His daughter-in-law would not come out. The Buddha used his supernatural power to transform her mind, and she became joyful and came out to see the Buddha. She bowed before the Buddha.

"The Buddha said: 'You are a wife. By what means do you serve your husband?'

"Yujā said: 'I should serve my husband with my person.'

"The Buddha said: 'A wife who serves her husband may have three evils and four goods. What are the three evils?

"'The first evil is like living with a lazy person: not wishing to do any work, cursing and reviling until evening, greedy for fine things and fond of quarreling.

"'The second evil is like living with an enemy: not directing one's heart toward the husband, not wishing for the husband's good, not wishing for the husband's success, but only wishing for the husband to die.

"'The third evil is like living with a thief: not caring for the husband's possessions, only thinking of deceiving the husband, always wishing for one's own pleasure, not being obedient to the children and grandchildren, but only thinking of licentiousness.

"'One who dies living thus will revolve through the evil paths without any time of escape. These are the three evils.

"'What are the four goods?

"'The first good is: when the wife sees her husband return from outside, she should be like a mother seeing her child. When the husband is in difficulty, she should always wish to take his place.

"'The second good is: a wife should serve her husband as a younger sibling serves an elder, with mutual respect and deference. She should not consider the husband's faults as evil, should not think of licentiousness, and should always follow the husband's words.

"'The third good is: a wife should serve her husband as a friend. When they meet, they constantly think of each other. When the husband returns from another place, she should be as joyful as seeing a father or an elder brother, greeting him with a joyful countenance. Such should be the wife's frame of mind.

"'The fourth good is: a wife should serve her husband as a maidservant. Though the husband may scold her severely, she should not consider it evil. Though he may beat her, she should not consider it excessive. Though he may make her run errands, she should not consider it hardship. Even if the husband has faults, she should always think of serving him well and always care for the children and grandchildren. One who dies living thus will always be born in heaven, where she will have many attendants, and fine garments and precious gems will always adorn her person.'

"The Buddha said: 'Three evils and four goods: which do you wish to follow in serving your husband?'

"Yujā said: 'A wife should not serve her husband with the three evils. The four goods are fit to live by. From this day forward, I vow to serve my husband and father-in-law as a maidservant.'

"Yujā then knelt and attended to Asudatta, serving her husband with the proper courtesy of a wife."
