# Sūtra of Kāśyapa's Council

## *Jiāyè Jié Jīng* (迦葉結經)

Taishō 2027

Translated by An Shigao (*Ān Shìgāo*, 安世高) of the Later Han dynasty.

*Note: This is one of the earliest Chinese translations depicting the First Buddhist Council (saṃgīti) held shortly after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa. It narrates how Mahākāśyapa convened five hundred arhats at Rājagṛha, Ānanda's exclusion and subsequent attainment of arhatship, and the formal recitation and compilation of the sūtra, vinaya, and abhidharma collections. The text is notable for preserving details about Ānanda's "nine offenses" as charged by Kāśyapa, and for the moving accounts of the elder Gavāṃpati's parinirvāṇa. An Shigao's translation style, from the mid-second century, is characteristically archaic.*

---

Thus have I heard. At one time, not long after the World-Honored One had passed away, all the arhats gathered together to deliberate, for the sūtra collection, vinaya, and various teachings had not yet been compiled. Each thought to himself: "We have accomplished what was to be done, crossed over the mountain of defilements, dried up the river of craving. The sun of omniscience, the Buddha, god among gods, has suddenly vanished from sight. We are weary and grieved at carrying on with these bodies. Now we wish to enter parinirvāṇa." Then they spoke this verse:

> Having crossed the abyss of the foolish, the ocean of attachment so hard to traverse,
> Destroying the decrepitude of the mundane world and the wheel of birth-and-death,
> Seeing all kinds of beings attached to the body as to a basket of serpents,
> We shall enter nirvāṇa, our minds pure like a lamp extinguished.

Thereupon, countless thousands of arhats, each according to their preference, in mountain caves, by flowing rivers, springs, and deep embankments, entered parinirvāṇa, exhausting all bonds of attachment, like a lamp suddenly going out. At that time, countless thousands of arhats all entered parinirvāṇa. A deva dwelling in space addressed the great Kāśyapa: "Now the true ones have all entered the peace of nirvāṇa." The deva then spoke this verse:

> The voice proclaiming the Honored One's words, long unobstructed in its reach,
> Now has departed; the Buddha-ones have extinguished defilements and found peace.
> Like the foremost guide of all assemblies, with concentration, liberation, and wisdom,
> Suddenly there is a vast darkness of delusion, and the light of dharma-virtue is not seen.

When the Venerable Kāśyapa heard these words, he thought to himself: "Excellent indeed is what this deva has said! Before long, this world will again be plunged into darkness. What shall we do?" He then reflected and came to a clear understanding: "We should now gather and compile the teachings, collecting the sūtras and vinaya, the essential points of the dharma-transformations, out of compassion for the peace and well-being of the world. Why? Because the World-Honored One, through countless kalpas of practice, accumulated merit through arduous effort beyond measure, wishing to bring peace to the world by gathering this dharma and vinaya to rescue and protect it. Therefore, while the dharma-transformations of the Buddha have not yet been extinguished, we should jointly gather and compile and protect the dharma-transformations."

Thereupon, the Elder Mahākāśyapa and others assembled the community of bhikṣus and addressed them: "Aniruddha and others! The Able One is impermanent. The diamond mountain has crumbled. The victorious sun of the Buddha has grown dark. The darkness of impermanence has concealed the light of the Able One. The sun of impermanence has emptied the ocean of the Buddha. The fire of impermanence has burned up omniscience. Now is the time. Always protecting the world and mindful of the Father's merit, we should establish the Father's work, proclaim and praise the Father's teaching, and bring all tasks to completion." He then spoke this verse:

> Before the supreme meaning has been compiled, we must not yet abandon all for nirvāṇa.
> Fully establishing the legacy of the Buddha's heirs, we must gather together all the sūtra collections.

---

When the assembly of bhikṣus had said this, the Venerable Kāśyapa and five hundred arhats, wishing to compile the correct dharma, vinaya, and their meanings, proceeded to Rājagṛha (*Luóyuè qí*, 羅閱祇) to convene the rains retreat. At that time, the Elder Ānanda (*Ānán*, 阿難) had also completed his rains retreat. The senior elders reflected: "This Ānanda is the younger brother of the World-Honored One, and he constantly attended upon him; he heard, retained, and understood all the dharma with great wisdom." The holy assembly then praised him in verse:

> This harmonious and compliant assembly upholds the Buddha's teaching as in the palm of a hand.
> Praised by the One of Ten Powers, his words are pure, his retention wise.

---

On the fifteenth day of the seventh month, when the new year had concluded, they convened to compile the sūtra collections, vinaya, and all the piṭakas. All five hundred arhats gathered together. The senior Mahākāśyapa addressed the Elder Aniruddha (*Ānàlǜ*, 阿那律): "Please observe the world. Among the disciples of the one who has departed from the ten powers, the Tathāgata, which arhats whose tasks are accomplished have not come to this assembly?" Thereupon Aniruddha, with the divine eye, surveyed the world and replied: "Venerable Mahākāśyapa, there is an elder named Gavāṃpati (*Qiáohuán bō*, 橋桓鉢), who is in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven (*Shīlì tiāngōng*, 尸利天宮) observing the new year; he has not come to this assembly. Great Kāśyapa should dispatch a monastic messenger to summon him."

At that time, among the assembly was a junior monk who had received full ordination only three years before, a bhikṣu named Pūrṇa (*Bùnà*, 不那). He had severed the three defilements, attained the three penetrating knowledges, mastered the three piṭakas, achieved the three insights, was unattached to the three realms, and had attained sovereign freedom. Thereupon, Mahākāśyapa addressed the assembly: "Young bhikṣu, can you serve as the assembly's messenger?" The Elder Pūrṇa rose, stood with palms joined, and said: "As the Venerable One teaches, I am able to carry out this mission." Mahākāśyapa said: "Excellent! Excellent, bhikṣu! It is most admirable that in the assembly of noble ones there is such a young bhikṣu." Having said this, he spoke this verse:

> In Trāyastriṃśa, the many kinds of flowers blaze with splendor.
> Go swiftly to that place, like a bee gathering soft fragrance.
> Gavāṃpati, with supernatural powers, dwells in that sublime abode.
> As the assembly instructs, convey our message thus:
> "Mahākāśyapa and the others, the saṃgha, have sent me to tell you:
> A matter of the saṃgha is at hand. Come quickly, by the appointed time."

---

The Elder Pūrṇa, having received this command from the assembled arhats, like a garuḍa leaping from the nāga palace, arrived in the blink of an eye at Gavāṃpati's abode. He bowed, touched his feet, greeted him, and spoke this verse:

> O one of serene and excellent nature, who delights in the quiescence of samādhi and tamed composure,
> Whose intention is pure: Kāśyapa the pure and the other sovereign elders
> Have convened a saṃgha matter of the greatest importance for the Buddha's immeasurable [dharma].
> Therefore they wish to summon you: come down to see the noble assembly.

Thereupon the Elder Gavāṃpati, hearing Pūrṇa's words, reflected for a moment and said to Pūrṇa: "Venerable Pūrṇa, has the bhikṣu saṃgha been free from quarrels, disputes, and contention? Has the dharma-wheel turned by the One of Ten Powers not been thrown into disorder by the various heterodox paths? Have the non-Buddhists, like startled deer, attempted to destroy the buddha-dharma? Have they not, with the feeble light of fireflies, tried to obstruct the radiance of the sun? Have those of unquiet minds who merely bear the appearance of śramaṇas, without the conduct of brahmacārins, claimed purity for themselves?"

"And, Venerable Pūrṇa, you should speak: the Buddha instructed the bhikṣu assembly in his teaching, yet you now address Kāśyapa and the bhikṣu assembly as though the great compassion of the World-Honored One, now that he has entered parinirvāṇa, is ended. Now that the eloquent wisdom and the precious jewel of the world are not seen, have the heterodox paths thrown the correct dharma-teaching into disorder? The One of Ten Powers, the Dharma-Wheel King, sovereign and free, has he suddenly vanished from sight, and is the great compassion ended? He who brought peace to all and rescued all beings, quiescent and not arising: has the sun of the Buddha not set? Has the Buddha, like moonlight, not been obstructed and obscured? Has the bodhi-tree, whose awakening-mind produced flowers of flourishing beauty, and whose fruits of the śramaṇa life, returned to impermanence? Has the great lamp of the world not been blown out by the wind of impermanence? Has the water of impermanence not extinguished the fire of the Buddha? Have deluded children not thrown the Buddha's teaching into disorder? Has the dharma-wheel of meaning turned backward and not returned? Has the moon-radiance of the Buddha, whose brightness has ended, been obscured by the asuras?"

At that time, the Elder Pūrṇa said: "O Gavāṃpati, the ship of the Buddha has broken apart; the mountain of wisdom has collapsed. Those who upheld the dharma also wish to enter nirvāṇa and cross over from this world."

Gavāṃpati said: "Will not evil persons join together in quarrels and disputes? How can one hear the correct dharma in this world when it has become so silent?" He said to Pūrṇa: "It is as though the essential has been removed and what remains is without essence. Separated from the Tathāgata's light, the world no longer has the splendor of awesome power. Where should one seek it?" He then spoke this verse:

> The world has become empty; without the Buddha there is nothing to delight in.
> What is there to seek in Jambudvīpa? I shall enter nirvāṇa right here.

---

Thereupon the Elder Gavāṃpati took his robe and bowl and gave them to Pūrṇa, saying: "Offer these to the holy assembly and convey my words, saying: 'All venerable ones, please be patient, in the spirit of the excellent meaning of the Honored One, and be heedful.'"

When the Elder Gavāṃpati had finished speaking these words, he entered parinirvāṇa. After his parinirvāṇa, fire arose from his body and cremated it, like a great pile of firewood set ablaze. After the cremation, four streams of water descended from the sky and bathed the remains. The water was cool and clear, its color like that of crystal and lapis lazuli. Each stream of water naturally produced a sound and spoke a verse:

The first stream said:

> While dwelling in birth-and-death, wisdom should not trust in floating clouds.
> Impermanence has shattered the diamond; the mountain-king of the Buddha has already collapsed.

The second stream said:

> All that exists is constantly in motion, on account of the harm of fear and suffering.
> Without sovereignty, one abandons oneself. The Buddha praised the peace of nirvāṇa.

The third stream said:

> Thus, without heedlessness, one completes the work of one's own person.
> Amidst countless afflictions and disturbances, like a burning lamp, swiftly extinguish it.

The fourth stream said:

> Most excellent among the assembly, one should bow in homage.
> The Venerable Gavāṃpati has gone to parinirvāṇa.
> Delighting in following the One of Ten Powers, he wished to enter nirvāṇa as well,
> Like a six-tusked elephant whose calf follows after its mother.
> To him I bow in homage, and to the entire assembly of noble ones.
> May the venerable saṃgha pardon whatever fault was mine.

---

After the Elder Gavāṃpati's parinirvāṇa, the Elder Pūrṇa took the robe and bowl and returned in the snap of a finger. He offered them to the assembly, bowed to the elders in order, and spoke this verse:

> The most honored among humans, of great compassion and serenity:
> Gavāṃpati, hearing the news, immediately entered parinirvāṇa.
> To him I bow in homage, and to the entire assembly of noble ones.
> May the venerable saṃgha pardon whatever fault was mine.

After this was spoken, [Pūrṇa] himself entered parinirvāṇa. At that time, the entire assembly of monks reflected on impermanence for a moment, contemplating the sūtras, vinaya, and the understanding of all dharmas. Then all the bhikṣus assembled.

---

### Ānanda's Nine Offenses

At that time, the Elder Mahākāśyapa addressed Aniruddha: "Venerable one, please examine this assembly. Is there anyone bound by the fetters of desire, anger, and delusion, still under the veil of attachments and obscurations, still needing to learn, an ordinary worldling who has come to this assembly?" Thereupon Aniruddha surveyed the seated assembly and reported to Mahākāśyapa: "There is a bhikṣu named Ānanda, attendant to the World-Honored One, who still has more to learn and accomplish; he has come to this assembly."

The Elder Mahākāśyapa then said to the Elder Ānanda: "You should leave for now. We shall not compile the sūtras together with you."

Ānanda replied: "May the Venerable Kāśyapa be pleased with me. I have not violated the precepts, hold no wrong views, have not ruined any karma, have not lost my conduct, and have not transgressed against the assembly."

Kāśyapa replied: "Ānanda, since you attended upon the Tathāgata and were close to the Supreme Honored One, it would be no surprise if you had no violations of precept. But you claim you have committed no offenses. Then come, take the evidence and present yourself: I shall reckon your past faults."

When Mahākāśyapa formed this thought, the three-thousandfold world shook in six ways, and hundreds of thousands of devas dwelling in space cried out in distress: "Alas! This Mahākāśyapa, why does he speak so harshly?"

Then the Elder Kāśyapa said to Ānanda: "Venerable one, you say you have committed no offenses. Then why did you request the Buddha to allow women to go forth as śramaṇīs?"

Ānanda replied: "Venerable Kāśyapa, when the World-Honored One's [foster] mother passed away, Mahāprajāpatī Gautamī had labored and raised him, personally attending the World-Honored One. When he was a bodhisattva, she nursed and nurtured him to maturity. Wishing to repay her kindness, I therefore requested that she be allowed to become a śramaṇī. Out of compassion for the relatives, wishing them to cross over to liberation, for this reason I requested the Buddha to let them become śramaṇīs. I also heard that all past buddhas, the perfectly awakened ones, had four assemblies. I thought that the World-Honored One's dharma-teaching and transforming influence should not be diminished. Therefore I requested that women be allowed to go forth as śramaṇīs."

The Venerable Kāśyapa said: "Ānanda, this was insufficient for the meritorious act of repaying kindness, compared with the merit of offerings to the Tathāgata's dharma-body. Because you had women go forth as śramaṇīs, it is like a field of ripe grain that is destroyed when a great hailstorm falls from the sky. The Buddha's correct dharma was originally meant to endure long, but because of women leaving home to become śramaṇīs, it has been reduced to one thousand years."

"Also, Ānanda, you say you did it out of compassion for relatives and requested they become śramaṇīs. This does not accord with the dharma of a śramaṇa, acting from attachment to kinship."

"Also, Ānanda, you say that past perfectly awakened ones all had four assemblies, and therefore you requested they become śramaṇīs. But in those times, people's desire, anger, and delusion were thin, without binding fetters, delighting in seclusion, minds free of blemish. How can that be compared to people of the present age? This is the first offense." He placed a tally on the ground.

"Also, Ānanda, you have another offense. The World-Honored One said: 'Whoever is energetic and attains the four bases of supernatural power can dwell at will for a kalpa or more than a kalpa.' Why did you not request the World-Honored One to remain, out of compassion for the world?"

Ānanda answered: "Venerable one, at that time Māra Pāpīyas was obstructing and confusing my mind, which is why I did not make that request to the Buddha."

Kāśyapa replied: "This is a great offense. You attended upon the desireless one, who should have overcome Māra's power, yet you followed Māra's direction instead. This is the second offense."

"Ānanda, do you still not recognize these offenses? You have yet another: when the World-Honored One reproved you, you spoke resentful words about another, implicating another in the charge. This is the third offense."

"Next, you have another offense: you stepped over the World-Honored One's gold-threaded woven robe with your foot. This is the fourth offense."

"Ānanda, you have another offense: when the World-Honored One was about to enter parinirvāṇa, on the way to the twin śāla trees, he asked you for water, and you did not give it. This is the fifth offense."

"You have another offense: when the Buddha, the World-Honored One, spoke of the various minor precepts of accommodation, you did not ask him to distinguish these for future generations. This is the sixth offense."

"You showed the Tathāgata's 'horse-sheathed' sign to the assembly. This is the seventh offense."

"Ānanda, you have another offense: why did you display the World-Honored One's body of golden-purple color to women, causing them to weep and their tears to soil his feet? This is the eighth offense."

"Ānanda, you have another offense: in this assembly, none possess desire, anger, or delusion, yet you alone still have the blemish of the three defilements. You still have more to learn in order to accomplish the path. The assembly has already accomplished what was to be done, but you have not yet reached that point. This is the ninth offense. Leave this assembly for now; we shall never compile the sūtras together with you."

The Elder Ānanda surveyed the four quarters of the seated assembly, wept bitterly, and cried out: "How cruel! What cause has led to this? Today I am separated from the Tathāgata, without refuge, without protection, my eyes not seeing the light, the world plunged into darkness." He continued: "Moreover, the Venerable Mahākāśyapa: when the Buddha, the World-Honored One, was about to enter parinirvāṇa, he told Ānanda: 'Do not weep and grieve; I entrust you to Mahākāśyapa.' Yet now, for a small mistake, Kāśyapa does not pardon me. Venerable Kāśyapa, please be pleased and understanding. I shall not err again."

The Venerable Kāśyapa then said to Ānanda: "Do not weep. Venerable one, your roots of merit are fully complete. I shall include you at our dharma-assembly as promised. Words of honest counsel cannot but be spoken. Ānanda, please leave for now; I shall not compile the sūtras together with you."

---

### Ānanda's Awakening

Thereupon the Elder Aniruddha addressed Mahākāśyapa: "How can we exclude Ānanda? He is the Buddha's attendant, of vast learning and comprehensive retention, a treasury of essential teachings, third only to the Buddha. Shall we compile the sūtras without him?"

The Venerable Mahākāśyapa said: "I shall not compile the sūtra-meanings and dharma-essentials together with Ānanda, one who is still learning. Ānanda, please leave and withdraw. I and the accomplished arhats shall compile the sūtras together."

Thereupon Ānanda rose from his seat in grief, looked back at the assembly of bhikṣus with a sorrowful expression, and departed. That very night, his earnest effort, hearing, and understanding severed all fetters, and he attained the path of arhatship, the three penetrating knowledges, the fruits of great supernatural powers.

On the following day, the assembly of arhats, numbering in the hundreds of thousands, gathered. Like the asura releasing the moon's light, whose brightness shone forth universally illuminating the world, Ānanda's mind was joyful, freed from all blemishes, his task accomplished.

The Venerable Kāśyapa said: "Excellent! Excellent, Ānanda! You have attained the equal [fruit of arhatship]. My heart leaps with joy. What the World-Honored One repeatedly taught, you have now been ferried across. Thus, in due order, you have attained the severance of all outflows. Furthermore, Ānanda: the Buddha, the World-Honored One, expounded the dharma-eye; thanks to the grace of your vast learning and dharma-retention, it shall now be established for all time." Then he spoke this verse:

> When the Buddha dwelt in the world, the Honored One was foremost in all he reached.
> All the people had their paths and arts; they did not travel the same road.
> As for this flavor of the deathless, it was conveyed to the most worthy among people.
> Now the Buddha has entered the quiescence of nirvāṇa; therefore the work of teaching and transformation is rare.

---

### The Compilation

Thereupon the senior elders addressed Ānanda: "You should now remain and help to compile the correct sūtras, vinaya, and the understanding of all dharmas." When the assembly of hundreds of thousands had addressed Ānanda, mindful of the dharma, reverently and thoroughly, they surveyed the bhikṣus, contemplating the Buddha's merit, which was without limit. Then they spoke this verse:

> This assembly of bhikṣus, supreme, having departed far from the Buddha's virtue,
> No longer possesses awesome radiance, like the sky separated from the sun's light.

Thereupon the Elder Ānanda looked upon the lion's seat. The assembly of bhikṣus surrounded him on all sides, like a lion king seated among lions. When Ānanda was seated, the Elder Mahākāśyapa spoke this verse to him:

> O greatly wise one, please speak. Son of the Peaceful One, please expound.
> Which sūtra collection did the World-Honored One speak first?

When Kāśyapa had spoken this verse, Ānanda's mind immediately attained the Buddha's awareness and recalled the path of the sūtras. Without fear, without wavering, without any doubt, he faced toward the place of the World-Honored One's parinirvāṇa, joined his palms single-mindedly, and recited aloud in verse:

> "Thus have I heard. At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at Vārāṇasī,
> In the Deer Park of the Ṛṣis, and he spoke the Sūtra of the Complete Dharma-Wheel."

The venerable elders, very many in number, all joined in encouragement. Then, ascending the lion's seat and proceeding like a lion, [Ānanda] first spoke the words "Thus have I heard, at one time," and according to the Honored One's abode and what sūtras were heard, he recited them all. When the entire assembly of arhats heard these words, they all rose from their seats and descended to the ground, thinking: "Alas, the power of impermanence! We ourselves beheld the Tathāgata expounding the dharma just recently, it seems. Yet today, how is it that we hear it in this manner?" At that time, the realized ones spoke this verse:

> Alas! The three realms are an illusion, like the moon reflected in water,
> Like a magical creation, like a plantain tree without solidity.
> In the three realms, there is none to compare; merit most pure,
> The Buddha, who always has an end, has suddenly departed like a gust of wind.

At that time, the Elder Mahākāśyapa, after a moment's meditation, sighed and said: "Alas, the power of finitude! Nothing at all escapes it." Then he spoke this verse:

> It does not spare the unwise, nor does it protect the wise.
> Whether liberated or not yet ferried across, there is none who does not return to exhaustion.
> One cannot be saved by words or spells, nor freed by rough speech.
> Death in the world is equal for all, the same as the saltiness of ocean water.

---

Thereupon Mahākāśyapa, having heard these words from Ānanda, diligently received the Sūtra of Turning the Dharma-Wheel and addressed Ājñāta-Kauṇḍinya (*Āruò Jūlín*, 阿若拘隣) and the five bhikṣus: "Is what you received like this?" They answered: "It is so, just like this."

In this manner they compiled the correct sūtra collection, compiled the vinaya collection, and compiled the various dharma collections. When the compilation of the sūtras was done, the devas came and dwelt in space and cried out in praise:

> The most excellent assembled for Ānanda, revealing all the teachings and precepts,
> Compiling the sūtras of the correct dharma, out of compassion for all the people.
> Through this gathering of refined practice, the Śākyamuni's excellent guidance,
> For the future and the present, shall attain the foremost concentration.

When the compilation of the correct sūtras, vinaya-precepts, and understanding of all dharmas was complete, the Venerable Mahākāśyapa spoke this verse:

> This is the compassionate one's edifice, the sūtra collections he established.
> The compilation of the Ten Powers' teaching is thus without measure.
> The wrong views of the world, and the darkness of confused thought, are extinguished.
> Its light shines far, a great lamp kindled in the darkness.
