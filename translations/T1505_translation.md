# 四阿鋡暮抄解
## Commentary on the Digest of the Four Āgamas

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1505

Attributed to Vasubhadra (婆素跋陀). Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Saṃghabhūti (僧伽跋提) and Zhu Fonian (竺佛念)

---

## Preface to the Digest of the Four Āgamas

*Āgama* (阿鋡暮) means in Chinese "approaching the unconditioned." After Ānanda compiled the Twelve Divisions of scripture, he further selected and distilled their essentials, the dharma leading directly to the path, into the *Four Āgamas*. Together with the Abhidharma and the Vinaya, these form the Three Baskets (*Tripiṭaka*). Solitary scholars regarded this supreme virtue as having not yet fallen to the ground.

There was an arhat named Vasubhadra who extracted the cream of these and made it into a single work of nine chapters and forty-six sections. He eliminated redundancy and removed repetition; the text is concise yet the meaning is rich. It may truly be called the garland of the sūtras. A hundred lines of beauty and subtlety, distinguishing right from wrong, are all fully contained within it. Profound, profound and rich: the capacity for practice is fully realized therein.

There was a foreign monk named Indrīya, who first brought it to the Kingdom of Qianbu, keeping it secret on his person and not showing it to others. The king, named Miti, sought and obtained it for recitation, and thus it was disseminated. In the eighth month of the *renxu* year, I went east to visit the temple of my late teacher at Ye Monastery, and had Kumārabuddhi hold the Sanskrit, with Buddhānusmṛti and Buddharakṣa as translators, and Saṃghadeva, Tanjiu, and Sengrui as scribes. It was completed by the eleventh month of winter. That summer, the Abhidharma was produced; that winter, this text. In one year, the Three Baskets were complete, and I was deeply grateful. I only regret that at the age of eighty or ninety I first encountered this sūtra, and fear that the bamboo binding-strings will not last before the work is finished. If a few more years are added, there should be no great error.

Recently I instructed the translators simply to convert the Sanskrit directly into Chinese, explaining the regional expressions, but not daring to alter the literary quality of the sūtra. Where there were suspense-numbers or suspense-terms, I consulted the appropriate person and annotated them below. When I occasionally interpreted the meaning, I made it into a chapter heading. Chapters annotated with *sūtra* indicate that the annotator cited the sūtra text. Where it simply says *sūtra*, this is a citation as evidence, not an annotation.

## Fascicle 1, Part 1: Chapters 1-2

Before the Āgama Digest, homage to the Well-Gone One, to the Dharma, and to the community that destroys existence. What is to be explained shall be stated briefly and swiftly; therefore the dharma of three shall be expounded.

**Question:** You say there are three dharmas. What is the meaning of these three dharmas?

**Answer:** The dharma is structured through three gates of concluding sentences. It is composed in this order, and therefore the three dharmas are to be understood conceptually.

**Question:** What is the meaning of the order of the three dharmas?

**Answer:** The great sūtra chapters are connected with innumerable characteristics: lust, anger, attachment to flavors, diligence and conditions, food that sustains beings, limited wisdom, seeking the noble truths. These conceptual categories should be understood quickly. Through the device of the three dharmas, one distinguishes this order. All the world's concepts, and concepts following one's wishes, are bound together; therefore the three dharmas are made for understanding.

**Question:** This device corresponds to the three dharmas. State them.

**Answer:** Virtues, evils, and the basis for awakening and liberation. Virtues, evils, and the basis for awakening and liberation: this sentence-meaning is the root sentence. Each of the three has three degrees and three divisions.

**Question:** There is a sentence with three divisions. First explain liberation through contemplation. What is the meaning of liberation? That liberation corresponds to virtues, evils, and the basis for awakening.

**Answer:** "Already entered, no need to explain": this is liberation. In the world, all beings down to ants and insects, even ants seek happiness. "All beings" means that upon seeing food they move toward liberation. Pleasure and sensation are one meaning. The world has little correspondence with happiness and causes of happiness, not knowing happiness, the happiness of the path, the happiness of nirvāṇa, the happiness of being free from illness, and so forth. Since there is ignorance regarding the happiness of the path, this is what is desired. If it is desired, there is no need to explain it. One who has already entered the path needs no explanation, just as when a child asks "Which is the eye?", it immediately knows the place of the eye. This is self-knowledge; this needs no explanation. Likewise, the desire of the world for happiness is the desire of the path; therefore the path is explained as producing freedom from evil.

**Question:** What virtue, awakening, happiness, and liberation is there? If so, one cannot see gold and consider mere seeing to be wealth. One who is ill cannot say "I have heard" [and be cured]; therefore one does not awaken to liberation by mere knowledge.

**Answer:** In between, it is like a bright lamp entering and destroying darkness; it is not that after the bright lamp there is darkness. The one who is liberated through the arising of wisdom: wisdom and awakening are one meaning.

**Question:** What dharma of virtue is it that beings are deeply deluded about? Is it form, flavor, and fragrance? Is it what is received, not received, or already received?

**Answer:** Or following conception externally, the sūtra says these are virtues: merit, faculties, and freedom from evil. Merit, faculties, and freedom from evil: these three are the pure dharma-fruit of virtue. The concept "my virtue" encompasses all three. These shall be explained briefly.

### Merit: Giving, Morality, and Meditation

**Question:** Merit and faculties have been explained. What dharma is "freedom from evil"?

**Answer:** Merit consists of giving (*dāna*), morality (*śīla*), and meditation (*bhāvanā*). Repeatedly doing good, repeatedly sifting out evil: therefore it is merit. These three practices are giving, morality, and meditation. As the former teachers said:

> "Constantly one should create merit,
> The practice of giving with virtue.
> The lives of beings, their lifespans race by swiftly.
> If one sifts away and eliminates evil,
> The number of practices thus performed:
> Therefore merit is explained by this simile."

**Question:** Merit has been explained as giving, morality, and meditation. What is meant by "giving"?

**Answer:** One gives from one's own body; another receives from one's own wealth. The thought of giving departs with both. These three forms of giving are: the gift of dharma, the gift of food, and the gift of fearlessness. The gift of dharma, the gift of food, and the gift of fearlessness: these three are called giving. The gift of dharma is what is taught in the worldly and supramundane chapters. The gift of fearlessness consists of the Eightfold Path, the three refuges, and the five precepts. The World-Honored One said: "Taking refuge in the Buddha, innumerable beings gain fearlessness, freedom from jealousy, freedom from anger, and freedom from killing. And so forth for the rest."

**Question:** If one takes refuge yet is seen to kill insects, what dharma of fearlessness protects?

**Answer:** I do not speak of all beings. Those who through wrong views and delusion kill insects, who commit evils regarding others' property, take these three refuges. There is no one in the world of right views who, having taken refuge, would have no right views. One without right views is not one who has taken refuge. Therefore, immeasurable is the auspiciousness. The gift of food means food as the primary offering. Various other gifts include those to stūpas, incense, and so forth. Whether received by oneself or by both, therefore the two merits together produce great fruition.

The purity of giving is such. My former teacher said: Making with the wealth of faculties, having either mindfulness or both, having either meaning or worldly auspiciousness: this is purity through means. A small gift can produce great fruit. There is purity through conditions: even a small offering together with merit can be obtained.

### Morality

**Question:** What dharma is morality?

**Answer:** Morality is of body and speech: received from others, not harming others, and increasing through non-harm. Morality has three corresponding aspects, corresponding to body and speech together. Received from others means knowing oneself to be hungry and weak, not troubling others in receiving. Knowing that beings do not trouble other beings; thus not being attached to others' wealth or others' wives, and so forth. Not received from others refers to false speech, divisive speech, harsh speech, and idle talk, which are not dependent on another's reception. If one does not trouble others in the seven branches of non-harm, and with various poor and destitute beings one assists them with both effort and shared intention, then merit increases continuously from the present onward. Like the thought "From today I shall not kill," the merit increases through the interval with many correspondences. Like investing wealth and gaining returns, wholesome consciousness is nurtured and received. Like planting grain: branches and leaves increase until fruit is obtained. Likewise, continuously even through sleep the merit increases. Therefore increase is morality.

### Meditation

**Question:** What dharma is meditation?

**Answer:** Meditation consists of the dhyānas, the immeasurables, and the formless attainments. Meditation is wholesome practice, and therefore it is called meditation. Like sesame oil mixed with flowers, one practices meditation. Like a king's search, like all seeking the king's fruit, attainment comes from this seeking practice. Corresponding to pure fruit, there is meditation. The dhyānas mean contemplation, consisting of four practices.

**Question:** What dharmas are these?

**Answer:** The dhyānas involve the cessation of lustful conduct, the cessation of pleasure in sensation, and the cessation of painful sensation. The four dhyānas: In the first, unwholesome conduct involving desire ceases; through wholesome conditions the mind is fixed and abides; this is described as separation from desire. In the second, one's own activity is described; one's own investigation (*vitarka*) decreases, like the sound of a bell about to cease; therefore without activity, this is cessation of one's own activity, as separation from desire was previously explained. The cessation of delight in the third dhyāna: the mind's delight springs up like a fountain; this is without lustful conduct, cessation of increase, cessation of painful and pleasurable sensation. Pleasurable sensation is pleasure of body and mind without oppressive suffering. Beyond this, free from lustful conduct and delight, with cessation of pleasurable and painful sensation: this is the fourth dhyāna.

### The Four Immeasurables

**Question:** What dharmas are the immeasurables?

**Answer:** The immeasurables are loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. These are the four immeasurable contemplations. Beings are immeasurable; the conditions for receiving them are immeasurable; they cannot be properly measured; therefore they are called "immeasurable." Loving-kindness (*maitrī*) means love for all beings: the mind goes to all beings with the wish for their benefit; this is loving-kindness. Compassion (*karuṇā*) means love for beings who suffer, and love for oneself. Sympathetic joy (*muditā*) means joy in the happiness of beings, shared in rejoicing. Equanimity (*upekṣā*) means non-action; enduring even when beings commit non-dharmic acts. When one sees beings acting in ways contrary to conditions, the endurance that surpasses this is equanimity.

### The Formless Attainments

**Question:** What dharmas are the formless attainments?

**Answer:** The formless attainments are: the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, and the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. "Sphere" means "basis." Beings dwell in these four: space aids in transcending form; seeing the faults of form is an aid; therefore the mind is undefiled. Through the condition of space, the mind becomes one with space; the conception bound together with it is the sphere of infinite space. "Why rely on space? Because there is consciousness." Therefore, through the condition of consciousness one practices the sphere of infinite consciousness; this too is a basis. Liberation without a basis means "nothing to be done": this is the conception of the sphere of nothingness. Seeing the cessation of evil characteristics and seeing fear, with a single mind in the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception: this is the formless realm. Contemplation of space has been explained; the formless meditation has been explained. All of this is the explanation of merit.

### Faculties

**Question:** What dharmas are the faculties?

**Answer:** The faculties are: non-greed, non-hatred, and non-delusion. These three are the characteristics of the faculties.

**Question:** Whose faculties are these?

**Answer:** They are not the previously mentioned virtues. Therefore, all dharmas of liberation are entered through them. The faculties are as stated in correspondence: one should make them correspond. First, in general. Second, in specific correspondence: as non-greed corresponds to giving, non-hatred to morality, and non-delusion to meditation. Further: non-greed corresponds to the gift of food, non-hatred to the gift of fearlessness, non-delusion to the gift of dharma. Further: non-greed corresponds to not troubling others, non-hatred to non-harm, non-delusion to increase: thus the three aspects of morality. Further: non-greed corresponds to the dhyāna faculty, non-hatred to the immeasurables, non-delusion to the formless attainments: thus the three aspects of meditation. Further: non-greed corresponds to sacred enterprise, non-hatred to endurance, non-delusion to learning: thus the faculties of freedom from evil. Thus these are the roots of all wholesome practice.

### Freedom from Evil: Endurance, Learning, and the Noble Portion

**Question:** What dharma is "freedom from evil"?

**Answer:** Freedom from evil consists of endurance, learning, and the noble portion. These three constitute freedom from evil. "Freedom from evil" in common parlance means following one's inclinations without fearing evil. The World-Honored One has said of the supreme practitioner of freedom from evil: "This is endurance of increased suffering, where evil's power naturally subsides through non-anger and endurance." Endurance of increased suffering means that even as evil power inflicts more suffering, the power of endurance increases and evil's power naturally subsides. Weariness of suffering is the meaning of endurance.

### Learning

**Question:** What is learning?

**Answer:** Learning is the Sūtra (*sūtra*), the Abhidharma, and the Vinaya. Learning means the complete exhaustion of lust, hatred, and delusion. This is true learning; all else is not learning. The Three Baskets: Sūtra, Abhidharma, and Vinaya. "Sūtra" refers to what is spoken by the Omniscient One and approved by the Buddha: it manifests the cutting off of attachment and the Four Noble Truths, illuminating liberation without interior or exterior. "Abhidharma" means the chapters that correspond to and elucidate the contents of the Sūtra. "Vinaya" means the teaching of purity through non-conduct and non-livelihood. These three practices increase through learning. Desire ceases through the Vinaya; hatred ceases through the Abhidharma (since the Abhidharma deals with what is done regarding awakening, and therefore anger diminishes; anger leads to violation of precepts and to hell). Delusion ceases through conditions. All of this is learning.

### The Noble Portion

**Question:** What is the noble portion?

**Answer:** The noble portion consists of good companions, right contemplation, and attainment. Good companions, right contemplation, and right attainment: these are the three aspects of the noble portion. "Noble portion" means no one dares speak of it as evil. "Good companions" serve three functions: assistance, goodness, and power. Teacher, student, and companion: these are the ones who assist, who are good, and who have power, that is, good companions. Those three: teacher, student, and companion.

### Contemplation

**Question:** What is contemplation?

**Answer:** Contemplation consists of calming, alert diligence, and guarded mindfulness of practice. Calming, alert diligence, and guarded mindfulness form the basis; this is effortless action. "Calming" means when the mind is disturbed, stabilize it to a semblance of the object. "Alert diligence" means when the mind is lazy, instruct it and keep it from distraction. "Guarded attainment" means correspondence with equanimity. Like driving a chariot horse: when slow, spur it on; when the ox bolts, rein it in. Equanimous practice is guarding. One should observe the mind in this way.

### Attainment and Its Requisites

**Question:** What is attainment?

**Answer:** Attainment is the fruit of complete requisites and means. Through means one enters concentration without external offerings; this corresponds to entering the fruit of complete requisites.

**Question:** What are the complete requisites?

**Answer:** As stated regarding liberation.

**Answer:** The complete requisites are: proper living, restraint of the faculties, and approach to meditation. Proper assembly is wholesome practice; therefore it is called "complete requisites," like seed-requisites. The seed-requisites mean the branch-meaning. The three practices of complete requisites are: proper living, restraint of the faculties, and approach to meditation.

### Proper Living (Dhūtaguṇa Practices)

Proper living refers to the five-fold patched-robe ascetic practice of the bhikṣu, which fulfills proper living. "Proper living" means "increase" in this sense. Two kinds of craftsmen: a painter who can paint, and a sculptor who carves and chisels stone and wood to fashion images. Book-making, sūtra-making, and painting: of those two crafted images, one made by a skilled craftsman endures wind and rain; the other, though beautiful, cannot endure. Likewise there are two kinds of persons: the renunciant and the layperson. The renunciant has abandoned relatives and occupations, like the skilled craftsman. The layperson has wife, children, and servants, like the painter's work. The layperson delights in continued attachment; when separated from what is loved, there is grief, joy, fear, conflict, and contention. Through non-dharma there is destruction: such images are not sturdy, unlike the renunciant's. The World-Honored One said: "Like the peacock, a bird with a beautiful blue neck, its flight does not compare to the wild goose's stride. Likewise the layperson does not compare to the bhikṣu." The muni sitting in meditation in the wilderness: this is proper living.

The five-fold patched-robe ascetic bhikṣu practice: from the root of three *dhūtaguṇa* virtues there are twelve, and the remaining nine are secondary. Each should be explained separately. The World-Honored One, leading Sundarī-Nanda, spoke of three virtues to Nanda: "When will you, Nanda, be seen dwelling contentedly, wearing the coarse patched five-fold robe, undefiled by the offerings of the faithful, far from lustful conduct and able to leave behind its dangers?" Therefore know that these three virtues and four additional benefits constitute the root. Clothing, alms-food, and lodging each have their benefits.

### The Five-Fold Patched Robe

The five-fold patched robe consists of three robes, taking up, and obtaining. The five-fold patched robe is constant, and therefore it is called the five-fold patched robe. The portion of the five-fold robe is the five-fold robe. The five-fold robe has three branches fully complete, with the three robes as the primary; this is the meaning of the sūtra.

### Forest-Dwelling Practice

Forest-dwelling (*āraṇyaka*) means sitting under a tree, sitting in the open, and always sitting and always abiding. "I shall always sit under a tree, I shall always sit in the open, I shall always sit": this fulfills the *āraṇyaka* practice. The four benefits of lodging assist the faithful devotee who builds a lecture hall and furnishes it with soft bedding, intending it as a pleasant thought. For this purpose, the explanation is given. The World-Honored One said: "This is not appropriate for me. Having abandoned the home, dwelling in others' dwellings; abandoning the lecture hall above, one takes up the forest-dwelling practice."

### Alms-Round Practice

Alms-food means one sitting followed by no more eating. One who lives between charnel grounds takes the two practices of the monastic life: dwelling-obtained food and alms-round food. Dwelling-obtained food means a faithful household provides food daily. Or, fearing the monk's trouble in coming, an external supporter establishes a monastery dining hall and provides food there. Alms-food means going from house to house receiving food in small or large amounts. If one violates these two, it is not dharmic practice.

The World-Honored One instructed one meal per sitting:

> "If a person with a constant nature
> Thinks with longing and desire,
> But upon obtaining gruel and rice knows moderation
> And is able to restrain and stay awake."

Having obtained the one-sitting meal, one further seeks good drinks. The Buddha permitted drinking. But receiving various drinks in abundance, one cannot contemplate. Therefore the World-Honored One said: "What use have I for this? Abandon such drinks. When thirsty, water too can satisfy. After food, cease."

Thus with one-sitting food, one abandons subsequent bathing, anointing the body, and adorning the body. The World-Honored One said: "This too is pride about food. Food lengthens the body as the highest offering; it shall be completely abandoned at the charnel ground." Therefore he taught the charnel ground. Seeing at the charnel ground the food-transformation, and the half-consumed insects, the bloating and decomposition, the oozing of fat, blood, and marrow, a thousand bones jumbled: seeing this, pride ceases. Thus the bhikṣu's practice is fully complete. This is proper living.

### Restraint of the Faculties

**Question:** What is restraint of the faculties?

**Answer:** Restraint of the faculties is: not being forcefully suppressed, self-control, and non-attachment. These are the three aspects of faculty-restraint. The faculties cannot be forcefully stopped, like starving a horse of water and grain. If one does not guard the faculties, they cannot be restrained. A horse given too much water and grass has a full belly and cannot walk. If one does not follow the right times, the faculties cannot be forcefully suppressed. Like a blind person who also should be separated from lust: therefore one cannot forcefully suppress. Knowing the domain of practice and what is not received, one can guard through right contemplation.

Or the former teachers spoke of the faculty-domain: concept, reception, and abandonment. Unable to reach the domain means reception through self-abandonment, not being attached to proximity, that is, to the semblance of a woman's image. The most beautiful is seen like seeing one's mother, seeing the root. These are the three aspects of faculty-restraint.

### Approach to Meditation

**Question:** What is approach to meditation?

**Answer:** Approach to meditation is: endurance, non-conception, and right approach to contemplation; therefore it is called approach to meditation. Liberation has been explained earlier.

## Fascicle 1, Part 2: Chapter 2 (The Path)

**Question:** What is the meaning of "means"?

**Answer:** Means consists of morality, calming (*samatha*), and wisdom (*prajñā*). Means is the meaning of the path, leading primarily to liberation. This means has three aggregates (*skandha*): morality, calming, and wisdom.

### Right Speech, Right Action, Right Livelihood

Morality consists of right speech, right action, and right livelihood. Right speech means abstaining from false speech, divisive speech, harsh speech, and idle talk, and all other speech that is right speech. Right action means abstaining from killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct, and all other deeds that are right action. Right livelihood for a bhikṣu means receiving food obtained through the alms-round, clothing, bedding, and medicine; all else is wrong livelihood. For a lay disciple, right livelihood means avoiding five things: weapons, poison, alcohol, meat, and trafficking in living beings.

### Calming: Effort, Mindfulness, Concentration

Calming consists of effort, mindfulness, and concentration. Complete cessation of calming means the cessation of desire, anger, and delusion; primarily abiding in calming. These three are: effort, mindfulness, and concentration.

#### Effort: Faith, Practice, Non-Abandonment

Effort has three aspects: faith, practice, and non-abandonment.

**Faith** has three aspects: reverence, joyful practice, and confident understanding. Reverence means abandoning turbidity. Turbidity refers to personal faults: anger, jealousy, shamelessness, and such faults. Like birds, water buffalo, and pigs muddying spring water: this is called turbidity. When these evils depart, purity is obtained. Reverence, joyful practice, and delight in liberation: like a sick person whose body recoils from good food, but once the illness clears, desires food. So one afflicted by anger dislikes hearing the dharma; upon meeting a good companion, one delights in practice. Then one thinks of the most excellent dharma. This is joyful practice. Confident understanding means the ability to hold, like a person bitten by a snake who, when another person with merit, discernment, and skill in medicine and incantation is present, listens attentively to the incantation: that person can be cured.

**Practice** has three aspects: vigorous arising, constancy, and mindfulness. Vigorous arising means doing the good. Like drilling into dry wood with dry powder and cow dung to start fire: vigorously, again and again. Seeking and seeking again: this is constancy. Mindfulness means undistracted mindfulness, corresponding to a single condition. Like when hair and cap are on fire, the hand's only thought is to extinguish it. These are the three aspects of vigorous practice.

**Non-abandonment** has three aspects: not separating, not becoming weary, and not turning back. Not separating means not becoming exhausted through repeated effort but relaxing one's diligence. Not becoming weary means practicing for a long time without obtaining results in the interim, yet not becoming weary in mind. Not turning back means that even if results are not yet obtained, one is certain they will be obtained, and does not wish to abandon through forced exertion. Like the destination of a path: these three lead to fruition.

### Mindfulness

Mindfulness has four objects: body, sensation, mind, and dharmas, observed internally, externally, and both, without forgetting. Internally observing one's own body, externally observing others', and both internally and externally. Or internal as the received aggregates, elements, and sense-bases; external as others' received and unreceived. Or the three fetters: internal master, external master, both masters. The internal master is desire; the external master is anger (anger toward others, not toward oneself). There is this awareness: "Desire also involves others." This is not the meaning. Internal attachment stimulates others; desire is internal attachment. As the World-Honored One said, a woman internally perceives the female faculty, and so forth. This is from the sūtra. Delusion corresponds to both. The three fetters assist the three applications of mindfulness. The body has three aspects as explained above. Likewise sensation, mind, and dharmas: thus the twelve practices of mindfulness.

### The Three Samādhis

The three samādhis are: emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness.

**Emptiness:** All that exists is empty.

**Question:** There are many kinds of emptiness: the emptiness of form, the emptiness of a house. Which emptiness is meant here?

**Answer:** Emptiness means that both "I-making" and "mine-making" cannot be perceived. This is emptiness.

**Wishlessness** means having no attachment to the past or future, no intentional fabrication. "Wishlessness" means "no standing," in this sense. The mind has three non-abidings: with regard to the present, the past, and the future. All of these are conditioned.

**Signlessness** means that actions, fabrications, and both are without signs. Briefly, all the conditioned is to be abandoned. All formations are actions; what acts is formation; what is formed is what acts.

### Wisdom: Seeing, Discrimination, and the Ground of No-More-Learning

Wisdom has three aspects: the ground of seeing (*darśana-mārga*), the ground of cultivation (*bhāvanā-mārga*), and the ground of no-more-learning (*aśaikṣa-bhūmi*).

**The ground of seeing** has three wisdoms: dharma-knowledge (*dharma-jñāna*), inferential knowledge (*anvaya-jñāna*), and knowledge of what was previously unknown. Like a physician who knows whether an abscess is ripe or raw and lances it with a knife, then probes with a finger to feel the direction of the veins so as not to sever them, and then makes a wider incision. Likewise, the practitioner sees lustful conduct as suffering and impermanence, enters the vision of impermanence equally, and abandons the fetters that bind to suffering. With these two wisdoms, are they severed or not yet severed? One thus cultivates contemplation. Just as the desire-realm is impermanent, so too are the form-realm and the formless realm. Having contemplated this, the wisdom that severs the fetters of form and formless conduct is established.

Thus seeing the suffering of lustful conduct: this is the dharma-knowledge of the benefit of the suffering-realm. Likewise inferential knowledge, and likewise the knowledge previously unknown regarding form and formless conduct. Thus the three regarding the origin of suffering: lustful conduct benefits cessation. Calming is dharma-knowledge; calming is inferential knowledge; likewise the knowledge previously unknown regarding form and formless conduct. Thus the three regarding cessation. Likewise the path. The dharma-knowledge of the path of cessation of suffering from lustful conduct is also inferential knowledge; likewise the knowledge previously unknown regarding form and formless conduct: this is the path. These are the twelve wisdoms; the ground of seeing is known in detail.

**The ground of cultivation** has three wisdoms: knowledge of characteristics (*lakṣaṇa-jñāna*), knowledge of conduct (*carya-jñāna*), and knowledge of kinds (*gotra-jñāna*). Characteristics are arising, abiding, and ceasing. Conduct is seeing impermanence, suffering, and non-self. Kinds are: flavor, calamity, and escape.

**The ground of no-more-learning** has three wisdoms: the three superknowledges (*vidyā*), the six supernormal powers (*abhijñā*), and the four analytical knowledges (*pratisaṃvid*).

The three superknowledges are: knowledge of former lives, knowledge of the divine eye (seeing beings' rebirths), and knowledge of the destruction of the defilements.

The six supernormal powers in three groups: flying, clairaudience, and knowing others' minds. Flying includes: traversal through space, transformation, and the sacred power of mastery. Traversal through space means being able to enter water, enter the earth, become invisible, pass through walls, mountains without obstruction, and touch the sun and moon with one's hand. Transformation means being able to manifest as birds, horses, chariots, people, mountains, trees, cities, gardens, rivers, and water. The sacred power of mastery means being able to extend one's lifespan, transform water into butter, and emit light from between one's brows.

The four analytical knowledges are: knowledge of dharma, knowledge of meaning, ready knowledge, and knowledge of expression. Knowledge of dharma means skill in combining phrases and understanding the method of meaning. Knowledge of meaning means following the nature of things; as fire is called "heat," heat is its meaning, and one does not forget it. Ready knowledge means responding appropriately without error. Knowledge of expression means speaking in accordance with the situation, responding without contradiction.

## Chapter 3: The Fruits

**Question:** What are the fruits?

**Answer:** The fruits are: buddha, pratyekabuddha, and śrāvaka. These are the three fruits.

A **buddha** means one liberated from all fetters, possessing the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, freely obtaining all buddha-dharmas, with no distinction in morality, concentration, and wisdom.

A **pratyekabuddha** means one who is self-dependent; at that time, there is no other meaning. One who does not learn from others' teaching is a pratyekabuddha; one who learns from others' teaching is a śrāvaka.

**Śrāvakas** have intermediate faculties. Through the condition of faith as the primary faculty, there are soft, intermediate, and superior increases.

Śrāvakas who have abandoned the fetters proceed through trust-liberated (*śraddhādhimukta*), view-attained (*dṛṣṭiprāpta*), and body-witness (*kāyasākṣin*) categories. Those who have not yet abandoned all fetters proceed through the eight persons (*aṣṭau puruṣa-pudgalāḥ*), stream-enterer (*srotāpanna*), and once-returner (*sakṛdāgāmin*) categories.

The **arhat** is worthy of offerings from all beings. There are three kinds: those of sharp faculties, soft faculties, and intermediate faculties. Those of sharp faculties can abide for a kalpa, can destroy existence, and are without doubt regarding any dharma. Those of soft faculties may decline, are mindful, and need guarding. Those of intermediate faculties are in between.

---

## Chapter 4: Evil (Akuśala)

Evil consists of wrongful conduct (*duścarita*), craving (*tṛṣṇā*), and ignorance (*avidyā*).

### Wrongful Conduct of Body, Speech, and Mind

Wrongful conduct of body: killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct.

**Killing** has three aspects: intention, commanding, and acting. Intention has three aspects: wishing to act, causing another to act, and rejoicing when another acts.

Wrongful conduct of speech: four kinds: false speech, divisive speech, harsh speech, and idle talk.

Wrongful conduct of mind: three kinds: covetousness, ill-will, and wrong views.

**Wrong views** have three aspects: views contradicting action, views contradicting results, and nihilistic views. Views contradicting action means perceiving the pure as impure, the impure as pure, or both as one. Views contradicting results means viewing the result of suffering as happiness, or happiness in heaven or nirvāṇa as suffering. Nihilistic views mean: there is no such thing as giving, no such thing as sacrifice, no wholesome or unwholesome action, no hell, no animal realm, no hungry ghost realm, no heaven, no mother, no father, no beings spontaneously born, no renunciants or brahmins in the world.

---

## Chapter 5 (Fascicle 2): Craving

Craving consists of desire (*kāma*), anger (*pratigha*), and jealous conceit (*māna*).

**Desire** has three forms: attachment to sensuality (*kāma-rāga*), attachment to existence (*bhava-rāga*), and attachment to the holy life (*brahmacarya-rāga*). Attachment to sensuality involves the five sense-domains. Attachment to existence involves the three realms of existence. Attachment to the holy life means hoping to gain through practice, distress at not gaining, and sorrow at losing one's position.

**Anger** arises regarding self, kin, and enemies. For self and kin, there are four modes each: desiring to obtain happiness not yet obtained, fearing loss of happiness already obtained, wishing to be rid of suffering obtained, not wanting to obtain suffering not yet obtained. For enemies, the reverse: wishing them to obtain suffering they have not obtained, wishing their suffering not to cease, not wanting them to obtain happiness, wishing to destroy their happiness.

**Jealous conceit** has three forms: inferior conceit, equal conceit, and superior conceit. Each has three subdivisions. Inferior conceit includes: boasting, hypocrisy, and laziness. Equal conceit includes: self-conceit, arrogance, and disrespect. Superior conceit includes: excessive conceit, conceit within conceit, and limitless conceit.

---

## Chapter 6: Ignorance

Ignorance consists of: non-knowledge (*ajñāna*), wrong knowledge (*mithyā-jñāna*), and doubtful knowledge (*vicikitsā*).

**Non-knowledge** means ignorance of the conditioned, the unconditioned, and the inexpressible. Regarding the conditioned: ignorance of internal reception (the aggregates, elements, and sense-bases of self and others) and external reception (grass, trees, walls, and so forth). Regarding the unconditioned: ignorance of nirvāṇa with remainder and nirvāṇa without remainder. Regarding the inexpressible: ignorance of the instruction on reception, the instruction on method, and the instruction on cessation.

**Wrong knowledge** consists of views of the self, internal reception-views, and clinging to views. The self-view means apprehending the five sense-bases as "I" with a corresponding sense of mastery. Internal reception-views are views of eternalism and annihilationism. Clinging to views includes clinging to precepts and clinging to views.

**Doubtful knowledge** means doubt regarding the Three Jewels, the truths, and right attainment.

---

## Chapter 7: The Basis (Āśraya)

The basis consists of the aggregates (*skandha*), elements (*dhātu*), and sense-bases (*āyatana*).

### The Five Aggregates

The aggregates are: form (*rūpa*), formations (*saṃskāra*), and knowledge (*vijñāna*). "Aggregate" and "heap" and "bundle" are one meaning.

**Form:** The four great elements are earth, water, fire, and wind. Their characteristics: solidity is earth, moisture is water, warmth is fire, motion is wind. The derivatives of the great elements include smoke, clouds, dust, mist, shadow, light, mirror-images, the five pure faculties, and so forth.

**Formations:** These depend on body, speech, and mind. All conditioned things are formations.

**Knowledge** includes: sensation (*vedanā*), perception (*saṃjñā*), and consciousness (*vijñāna*). Sensation is of three kinds: pleasant, painful, and neither-pleasant-nor-painful. Each arises from its respective condition: painful sensation arises from the condition of pleasant, pleasant from painful, and neither from neither.

Pleasant sensation has three sources: sensual pleasure, the pleasure of freedom from evil, and the pleasure of cessation. Painful sensation has three sources: birth, old age, and death. Birth is like a boil arising; old age is like a ripening boil; death is like a boil bursting and flowing into the joints. Neither-pleasant-nor-painful sensation corresponds to the three realms.

Sensation arises from three sources: conditions, actions, and elements. Conditions are meritorious, non-meritorious, and indeterminate. Actions are: by oneself, by another, and by both. Elements include: seasons, illnesses, and sudden encounters. Seasons are summer, winter, and spring. Illnesses are wind, bile, and phlegm. Sudden encounters are non-sentient causes such as walls collapsing, trees and mountains falling.

**Perception** means: having perception, absence of perception, and perception of what has or lacks characteristics.

**Consciousness** arises through desire, comes to completion, and operates through the door of incomplete development. Three causes of arising: the World-Honored One taught that consciousness arises from formations; that name-and-form arises from consciousness; and that eye-consciousness arises from the eye conditioned by form. At the union of the father's and mother's essences in the mother's womb, consciousness arises: this is birth. Through the process of gestation, the embryo gradually develops and solidifies.

---

## Chapter 8: The Three Realms

The three realms are: the desire-realm, the form-realm, and the formless realm.

### The Desire Realm

The desire-realm contains: humans and heavenly beings (good destinies), and the hells, animals, and hungry ghosts (evil destinies).

**Humans** have female, male, and life-faculty characteristics, dwelling in the four continents: Jambudvīpa, Pūrvavideha, Godānīya, and Uttarakuru.

**Heavenly beings** of the desire-realm engage in sexual conduct of three kinds: by embrace, by speech, and by sight. The gods of the heaven of Delight in Transformation (*Nirmāṇarati*) engage through speech; those of the heaven of Mastery over Others' Transformations (*Paranirmitavaśavartin*) through sight; the Yāma gods through embrace; the Tuṣita gods through handholding; and the Trāyastriṃśa and Four Great Kings through union as humans do.

**Evil destinies:**

The **hells** have three categories: cold, hot, and circumstantial.

The *cold hells* are ten: three in which one can speak (Arbuda, Nirarbuda, Aṭaṭa), three in which speech is unclear (Hahava, Huhuva, Utpala), and four in which one cannot speak (Sugandhika, Kumuda, Puṇḍarīka, Padma). In these, extreme cold causes the body to crack and split like lotus flowers.

The *hot hells* have three groups: those with torment (Reviving Hell, Marketplace Hell, Black-Thread Hell), those with less torment (Great Assembly Hell, Wailing Hell, Roasting Hell), and those without respite (Wailing Hell, Roasting Hell, Avīci).

In the **Avīci hell** (without respite), the city is one hundred *yojanas* in each direction, covered with molten copper, fire burning in all four gates, above and below, with no space free from fire. Those who kill father, mother, or an arhat; those who with evil intent harm a Tathāgata; those who cause schism in the Saṃgha; and those who commit the ten evil actions: through these conditions they are reborn there.

**Animals** are of three kinds: those that travel on land, in water, and through the air. Also: footless, two-footed, and many-footed.

**Hungry ghosts** are of three kinds: those without food (flame-mouth, needle-mouth, foul-mouth), those with little food (foul-hair, needle-hair, goiter-throat), and those with much food (who eat what is discarded, what remains, and great flying spirits such as yakṣas, rākṣasas, and pūtana ghosts).

### The Form Realm

The form realm has three divisions corresponding to the second through fourth dhyānas: those with both investigation and examination (*savitarka-savicāra*), those without investigation but with examination (*avitarka-savicāra-mātra*), and those without either (*avitarka-avicāra*).

The first dhyāna gods: the assembly of Brahmā's Retinue (*Brahmapariṣadya*), the Brahmā Ministers (*Brahmapurohita*), and Mahābrahmā.

The second dhyāna gods: Limited Light (*Parīttābha*), Immeasurable Light (*Apramāṇābha*), and Radiant Light (*Ābhāsvara*).

The third dhyāna gods: Limited Purity (*Parīttaśubha*), Immeasurable Purity (*Apramāṇaśubha*), and Universal Purity (*Śubhakṛtsna*).

The fourth dhyāna gods: Cloudless (*Anabhraka*), Merit-Born (*Puṇyaprasava*), and Great Fruit (*Bṛhatphala*). Also the Unconscious Beings (*Asaṃjñisattva*) and the Five Pure Abodes (*Śuddhāvāsa*): Not Declining (*Avṛha*), Unafflicted (*Atapa*), Beautiful (*Sudṛśa*), Clear-Seeing (*Sudarśana*), and Highest (*Akaniṣṭha*).

### The Formless Realm

The formless realm has been previously explained with the four formless attainments: infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception. One enters these through right concentration and is reborn there. As a seed produces fruit, so the path produces its result.

---

## Chapter 9: The Bases (Āyatana)

The bases are: the bases of contact (*sparśa*), the bases of heterodox views, and the bases of liberation.

**Contact** has three kinds: proximate contact (nose, tongue, and touch), non-proximate contact (eye, ear, and mind), and non-active contact.

**Heterodox views** have three categories: theories of a single cause, deceptive theories, and meaningless theories. Single-cause theories include those attributing creation to the self, to others, or to both together. Deceptive theories include creation by Brahmā, by Maheśvara (Śiva), or by Viṣṇu. Also theories that attribute all creation to time (*kāla*), to atoms (*paramāṇu*), or to primal nature (*prakṛti*). Meaningless theories include the theory of spontaneous arising, the theory of forceful domination, and the theory that nothing exists.

**Liberation** has three bases: contemplation, meditation, and recitation. Through contemplation, pratyekabuddhas attain liberation. Through meditation, śrāvakas attain liberation. Through recitation (expounding, listening, and chanting), liberation is attained.

The World-Honored One's disciples are born into Four Noble Truths: through giving they are born, through cessation they are born, through wisdom they are born. Listening attains liberation; giving and expounding attain liberation; cessation through seated meditation attains liberation; wisdom through recitation attains liberation.

---

Thus conclude the Three-Dharma Divisions of Vasubhadra, comprising all nine chapters and forty-six sections.
