# 佛說花聚陀羅尼呪經
## The Buddha's Teaching: Puṣpakūṭa Dhāraṇī Mantra Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1358

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown.

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the palace of the Nāga King Anavatapta, together with five hundred bhikṣus. There was also a full assembly of one thousand bodhisattva mahāsattvas, all next in succession to become buddhas, who had attained the dhāraṇī stage on the tenth bhūmi. Like princes who will soon succeed to the throne, these bodhisattvas likewise, endowed with great merits and adorning themselves thereby, would soon attain the consecrated position and become buddhas. These bodhisattvas had each come from buddha-worlds in other directions to assemble here, and their minds harbored no doubt regarding the buddhas.

Then from within the assembly, a bodhisattva named Siṃhavikrīḍita rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and addressed him: "World-Honored One! If good men and good women throughout their entire lives offer the Tathāgata every kind of pleasurable object, will they obtain much merit?"

The Buddha addressed Siṃhavikrīḍita Bodhisattva: "Do not say such a thing with a doubting mind. Making offerings to the Tathāgata yields exceedingly great merit. Why? Because the Tathāgata possesses immeasurable virtue of discipline, immeasurable dhyāna-concentration and samādhi, immeasurable wisdom, immeasurable liberation, and immeasurable knowledge-and-vision of liberation, together with all the śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and worthy sages. The merit is exceedingly great and beyond measure." Then Siṃhavikrīḍita Bodhisattva, having heard what the Buddha taught, gave rise to faith and understanding, and with redoubled reverence made offerings, honored and praised him, presenting robes, lodgings, bedding, medicine for the sick, and every kind of pleasurable object, offering them all so that nothing was lacking.

"If good men or good women, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, take relics of the Buddha, even as small as a mustard seed, and make offerings to them and bow in worship, the merit is exactly equal to that described above. If furthermore a person takes the seven kinds of jewels as high as Mount Sumeru and for one kalpa makes gifts to the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, would the merit be great?" "No, World-Honored One!" The Buddha said: "If any renunciant or householder can offer a single coin in giving to a person who has just aroused the mind of enlightenment, the merit far surpasses the former a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a hundred thousand myriad-fold, and beyond all calculation or analogy." "It is so, it is so, as the Buddha has said."

"Furthermore, if a person, after the Tathāgata's parinirvāṇa, is traveling through the wilderness and sees a stūpa-temple of the Tathāgata, and a good man or good woman can offer even a single flower, a single lamp or candle, or a single lump of clay to plaster before the image; if a good man or good woman offers a single copper coin to a buddha-image for its repair; if one takes a handful of water and sprinkles it on the stūpa grounds, removing impurities, dried flowers, and candle-stubs; if one burns incense as an offering and takes but one step toward the stūpa-temple; if one once says 'Homage to the Buddha,' intending thus to honor the one who throughout innumerable asaṃkhyeya kalpas cultivated ascetic practices, accumulated all merits, attained perfect enlightenment, and benefited and brought to maturity innumerable sentient beings: for such a person to fall into the three evil destinies, even in hundreds of thousands of myriads of kalpas, is utterly impossible."

The Buddha addressed Siṃhavikrīḍita Bodhisattva: "There is a dhāraṇī called Puṣpakūṭa that greatly benefits devas and the people of the world. If anyone can receive, uphold, read, recite, master, and practice it in accordance with the Dharma, the merit obtained will exceed all of the above many times over. Furthermore, even a good man who makes offerings to śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and bodhisattvas for hundreds of thousands of myriads of times cannot equal even a fraction of it, and it is beyond all calculation and analogy. Such a person, at the end of their life, will never be born in the eight difficult places or in a lowly family. In every place of birth their six sense-faculties will be complete. They will know their own past lives. They will constantly encounter the Three Treasures, see the Buddha and hear the Dharma, and never lose the mind of enlightenment. They will obtain profound and immeasurable eloquence. Riding the six supernatural powers, they will travel to the buddha-worlds of the ten directions, receive the wondrous Dharma, and teach and transform sentient beings." Then the World-Honored One spoke the mantra:

*Tadyathā: tu-rā-ṇi tha-rā-ni tha-rā-ni ma-ṇi pa-pu-bha saṇ-ṇi siddhi caṇ-dī ne-mu-ji ne-hā-rī ru-ga pa-ti buddha pa-ti ti-rī u-kā-rā khā-ga-rā-rā ju-pa-ga-ti ti-jā ha-ti vi-śā-rā bud-dhi taṃ-ma pa-sī a-kṣa-yā ka-pī ka-pa ha-ti a-mi-ta ka-pī kṣu-ta śa-ṇi-ti jā-va-ti ṇi-ku-bha-mā i-hī-ti ti-jā ga-hā ha-ti in-ti-ya bud-dhi ti-ṇā bud-dhi svāhā.*

The Buddha said: "If good men and good women wish to practice this dhāraṇī: during the second month, the third month, or the eighth month, from the eighth day of the bright fortnight to the fifteenth, they should bathe themselves clean and put on new, clean garments. In a quiet place, they should seat themselves before a buddha-image, hang silk banners and canopies, offer flowers and incense, bow in worship and confess their transgressions. Six times daily, day and night, they should recite this dhāraṇī, whether sitting or walking, never allowing the mind to become distracted. When seven days are complete, they will see the Buddha. If they do not see him, they should continue for two more weeks or three more weeks, single-mindedly reciting this dhāraṇī, and they will certainly see the Buddha seated on a lotus, teaching the Dharma for them. At that time they will gain knowledge of their own past lives, firm power of memory, the dhāraṇī, and unobstructed eloquence. Whether one seeks broad learning, dhyāna-concentration, wisdom, eloquence, medical arts, mantra-techniques, craft skills, or literary arts, whatever the wish in their heart, all will be obtained, up to the attainment of buddhahood, never to be forgotten, except for the Four Noble Truths."

Then Siṃhavikrīḍita Bodhisattva, the devas, humans, and asuras, and all the bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully received it and put it into practice.
