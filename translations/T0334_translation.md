# 佛說須摩提菩薩經
## Sūtra of Sumatī Bodhisattva, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0334

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Heard as follows:

At one time, the Buddha was at Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas.

At that time, in the great kingdom of Rājagṛha, there was a householder named Ugra. Ugra had a daughter named Sumatī, eight years old, who through previous lives had reverently served immeasurably many hundreds of thousands of Buddhas of the past, with accumulated merit beyond reckoning. At that time, Sumatī went forth from the great kingdom of Rājagṛha to Vulture Peak. She proceeded to the Buddha's place, bowed her head at the Buddha's feet, and after bowing, withdrew and stood to one side. Joining her palms, she addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask a question. I pray the Buddha, with skillful means, will explain and resolve my doubts."

At that time, the Buddha was silent, and immediately knew the girl's intention. The Buddha told Sumatī: "Ask whatever you wish. The Tathāgata will now explain it for you in full detail, distinguishing each matter, and bring you joy."

Sumatī asked the Buddha: "How can a bodhisattva be born in a place where all who see them are always joyful? How can one obtain great wealth with abundant treasures? How can one not be separated from others? How can one avoid being born from a mother's womb, and instead always be born by transformation, standing upon a thousand-petaled lotus before the Dharma-King? How can one obtain supernatural powers, traveling from incalculable koṭis of lands to distant places to bow before the Buddhas? How can one have no enemies and no one who encroaches or envies? How can one's words be such that all who hear them trust, rejoice, and practice accordingly? How can one have no transgressions, and the good deeds one performs be indestructible? How can māra be unable to gain any advantage? How, at the end of one's life, can the Buddha appear before one, teach the Dharma, and immediately prevent one from falling into a realm of suffering? These are my questions."

At that time, the Buddha told Sumatī: "Your questions about the Tathāgata's meaning are excellent! How wonderful that it is so. If you wish to hear, listen carefully, receive it attentively, reflect upon it diligently, and I shall explain." The girl immediately said: "Very good, World-Honored One! I joyfully wish to hear." Thereupon Sumatī received the teaching and listened.

The Buddha said: "A bodhisattva has four practices by which all who see them are joyful. What are the four? First, anger and hatred do not arise, and one regards one's enemies as good spiritual friends. Second, one always has a mind of kindness toward all. Third, one always seeks the unsurpassed, essential Dharma. Fourth, one makes images of the Buddha. These are the four. By these four practices, the bodhisattva is such that all who see them are always joyful." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Not giving rise to anger destroys the root.
> Always practicing kindness, one attains the essential Dharma.
> Making Buddha-images, one's body is beautiful and pure.
> The mind rejoices, and people are joyful to see one."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which one obtains great wealth. What are the four? First, giving at the right time. Second, after giving, one's joy is redoubled. Third, after giving, one does not later regret. Fourth, having given, one does not seek a reward. These are the four. By these four practices, the bodhisattva obtains great wealth with abundant treasures." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Giving at the right time, without a regretful mind,
> joyfully giving without expectation:
> the giving one performs has courageous wisdom.
> Wherever one is, one is always greatly wealthy."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which one is not separated from others. What are the four? First, one does not spread malicious tales to stir up conflict between others. Second, one guides the ignorant to enter the Buddha's path. Third, if the right Dharma is damaged or in decline, one protects it and prevents its extinction. Fourth, one encourages all people, teaching them to seek Buddhahood with firm resolve. These are the four. By these four practices, the bodhisattva is not separated from others." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Not spreading tales to stir conflict between others,
> guiding the ignorant, protecting the right Dharma,
> encouraging people to seek Buddhahood:
> there will never be anyone who can separate them."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which one is born by transformation, standing upon a thousand-petaled lotus before the Dharma-King. What are the four? First, finely grind red lotus, blue lotus, yellow lotus, and white lotus, combining these four kinds of powder as fine as dust, making a full measure of soft and exquisite flower-powder, and with this make offerings to the World-Honored One, or to a stūpa or relics. Second, one does not cause others to give rise to anger. Third, one makes images of the Buddha seated upon a lotus. Fourth, one abides in joy upon attaining the highest awakening. These are the four. By these four practices, the bodhisattva is always born by transformation, standing upon a thousand-petaled lotus before the Dharma-King." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Offering the four flowers, a full measure soft and exquisite,
> removing anger and resentment, receiving the Dharma's meaning,
> attaining the supreme awakening, standing before the Buddha,
> making images: one is born within the flower."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which one obtains supernatural powers to go from one Buddha-land to another. What are the four? First, seeing others performing meritorious deeds, one does not interrupt them. Second, seeing others teaching the Dharma, one does not cause it to stop. Third, one always lights lamps in stūpas and monasteries. Fourth, one seeks samādhi. These are the four. By these four practices, the bodhisattva obtains supernatural powers to go from one Buddha-land to another." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Performing merit, giving the Dharma as a gift,
> hearing the sūtra taught, one does not interrupt.
> Always lighting lamps in the Buddha's monasteries,
> entering samādhi, one traverses all the lands."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which one has no enemies and no one who encroaches or envies. What are the four? First, toward good spiritual friends, one has no flattering mind. Second, one is not miserly or jealous regarding others' possessions. Third, seeing others give, one rejoices in support. Fourth, seeing a bodhisattva's various activities, one does not slander. These are the four. By always practicing these, the bodhisattva has no enemies and no one who encroaches or envies." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Toward good friends, without flattery,
> not stingy with others' possessions,
> seeing others give, one supports with joy.
> Practicing as a bodhisattva, one has no enemies."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which one's words are such that all who hear them trust, rejoice, and practice accordingly. What are the four? First, what the mouth speaks, the mind likewise affirms. Second, toward good spiritual friends, one always has utmost sincerity. Third, hearing others teach the Dharma, the mind does not judge right or wrong. Fourth, seeing others invited to teach the Dharma, one does not seek their faults. These are the four. By these four practices, the bodhisattva's words are such that all who hear them trust, rejoice, and practice accordingly." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "As one thinks, so one speaks.
> Toward good friends, one has utmost sincerity.
> Hearing the Dharma taught, one does not seek faults.
> When sūtras are expounded, the mind leaps with joy."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which one has no transgressions and one's good deeds swiftly attain a pure abiding. What are the four? First, the intentions of the mind are always directed toward goodness. Second, one always upholds precepts, samādhi, and wisdom. Third, from the first generation of the bodhisattva's aspiration, one gives rise to all-knowledge and ferries many across to liberation. Fourth, one always has great compassion for all. These are the four. By these four practices, the bodhisattva has no transgressions and swiftly attains a pure abiding." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always aspiring to goodness and broadly ferrying across,
> precepts, samādhi, and inseparable wisdom:
> one should teach all-knowledge to others.
> Practicing with a compassionate mind, one attains a pure abiding."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which māra cannot gain any advantage. What are the four? First, one is always mindful of the Buddha. Second, one is always diligent. Third, one is always mindful of the sūtra-Dharma. Fourth, one always establishes merit. These are the four. By these four practices, māra cannot gain any advantage over the bodhisattva." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "Always with a pure mind, mindful of the Buddha,
> with aspiring diligence in the profound Dharma,
> encouraging oneself to establish merit:
> by this, māra cannot gain any advantage."

The Buddha told Sumatī: "A bodhisattva has four further practices by which, at the end of one's life, the Buddha appears before one, teaches the Dharma, and does not allow one to fall into a realm of suffering. What are the four? First, for the sake of all people, one fulfills all aspirations. Second, when someone gives to those who are lacking, one wishes to make up what is insufficient. Third, seeing someone making various offerings that fall short, one supplements and assists. Fourth, one is always mindful of making offerings to the Three Treasures. These are the four. By these four practices, at the end of one's life, the bodhisattva sees all the Buddhas appear before them, who teach the Dharma and do not allow that person to fall into a realm of suffering." The Buddha then spoke in verse:

> "For the sake of all, fulfilling aspirations,
> with boundless compassion, encouraging sufficient giving,
> through various offerings, one attains keen wisdom.
> Making offerings to the Three Treasures, one attains the Buddha."

At that time, Sumatī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! The forty practices you have taught, I shall carry them all out without deficiency, making them all complete, not violating a single one. If I were to lose even one meaning, I would be cutting off the Buddha's Dharma of the kalpa and diminishing the assembly of disciples."

At that time, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, seated in the great assembly, questioned Sumatī: "These forty practices are what great beings perform, what bodhisattvas do; they are exceedingly difficult. How can a little girl like you accomplish them?"

Sumatī then answered Maudgalyāyana: "If it is truly the case that I can carry out these forty practices, then the three-thousand great-thousand world-system shall quake in six ways on my behalf, heavenly flowers shall rain down, and all the celestial musical instruments shall sound without being struck." As Sumatī uttered these words, at that very moment the three-thousand great-thousand world-system quaked in six ways, heavenly flowers rained down, and the musical instruments all sounded. The girl told Maudgalyāyana: "This is the proof of my sincerity. If in the future there are those who generate the bodhisattva's aspiration, it shall likewise be so for them. Before long, I too shall become a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha. If you trust my words, which are without falsehood, all those in this assembly shall become of one appearance, entirely golden in color." Just as she spoke, they all turned golden in color.

Thereupon Maudgalyāyana rose from his seat, adjusted his robes, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms, bowed before the Buddha, and addressed him: "All those who from now on first generate the great aspiration for the bodhisattva's way, I shall take refuge in them and bow to them in reverence. Why? An eight-year-old girl produces such responses; how much more so a great being, a mahāsattva!"

At that time, seated in the assembly was a great bodhisattva named Mañjuśrī, who addressed Sumatī: "In what dharma do you abide, that such responses manifest?"

Sumatī answered: "All dharmas are beyond reckoning and have no abiding place. Yet you ask me in what dharma I abide? It would be better not to ask than to ask such a question."

Mañjuśrī asked Sumatī: "What gives rise to such words?"

Sumatī answered Mañjuśrī: "One does not abide in any dharma, nor has any doubt, nor declares right or wrong."

Mañjuśrī asked Sumatī: "Did the Tathāgata originally not perform any practice?"

Sumatī answered: "It is like the moon's reflection appearing in water, or a dream, a mirage, or an echo in the deep mountains. The Tathāgata's original practice is likewise so."

Mañjuśrī asked Sumatī: "As you have spoken, combining these matters, can one attain Buddhahood or not?"

Sumatī answered: "Does the venerable one say that delusion, wisdom, and practice are three different things? They are not different. All dharmas are combined. Why? Whether right Dharma or not-right Dharma, all have no abiding place, nothing to grasp, and nothing to release. They are empty and without form."

Mañjuśrī further asked Sumatī: "How many people understand this meaning?"

Sumatī answered: "When a magician creates transformations at will, is there a limit? Even the magician's transformations are limitless. Those who have faith and understanding in this Dharma are likewise so."

Mañjuśrī asked Sumatī: "Since I am without transformation and without illusion, what dharma-practice should I undertake to accord with the path?"

Sumatī answered: "As you have spoken, it is most excellent. In the place of all dharmas, there is neither existence nor non-existence. As for the Tathāgata, there is neither union nor separation."

Hearing what she said, Mañjuśrī was greatly pleased and praised her. Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "Excellent indeed is what Sumatī has spoken, subtle and wondrous! How remarkable! She has deeply entered and attained the patience of the Dharma. How long has it been since she first generated the aspiration?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "This Sumatī has been established in the aspiration for unsurpassed, equal, universal deliverance for an incalculable time. Thirty billion kalpas before you, she first generated the aspiration for unsurpassed, right, equal, universal deliverance. You, only recently, entered the patience of the non-arising of dharmas. She was originally your teacher when you first generated the aspiration."

Hearing what the Buddha said, Mañjuśrī came forward and bowed, addressing Sumatī: "I have been separated from you for so long, and only now do I attend upon you again. Meeting my teacher, I receive the Dharma-instruction."

Sumatī answered: "Do not think in this way. Why? In the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, there is also nothing to be mindful of and no teacher."

Mañjuśrī asked: "Why do you not transform your female body?"

Sumatī answered: "In this there is nothing to attain. Why? Dharma has neither male nor female. Now I shall dispel your doubt."

Mañjuśrī said: "Excellent! I joyfully wish to hear."

Sumatī told Mañjuśrī: "If it is true that in the future I shall attain the state of a Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, of Perfected Conduct, Well-Gone One, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Trainer in the Path of the Dharma, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, Deva among Devas, and this is truly so, then I shall now transform into a man." As these words were spoken, she immediately became a man. Her hair fell away, and a kāṣāya robe appeared upon her body. She became a novice monk.

Sumatī further told Mañjuśrī: "If it is truly the case that when I become a Buddha in the future, in my land there shall be no three things. What are the three? First, no māra-affairs. Second, no hells. Third, no female dispositions. If this is my sincere truth, my body shall appear as a śramaṇa of thirty years." As Sumatī spoke these words, her form and complexion became that of one who is thirty years of age.

Sumatī then told Mañjuśrī: "When I become a Buddha, the people of my land shall all be golden in color. The cities and the ground shall be entirely of the seven treasures. There shall be trees of seven treasures, paths for eight kinds of practice, pools of seven-treasured water, and on all four sides and in the center shall grow lotus flowers of many-colored jewels and various treasures, neither too much nor too little, all in perfect balance." Sumatī said: "Just as your land is, so too shall my Buddha-land be. If my words are sincere, all those now in this assembly shall turn golden." As these words were spoken, at that moment the entire assembly turned golden.

At that time, the earth deity emerged from the ground, transformed into a celestial body, raised her voice, and praised Sumatī, proclaiming three times: "When the mahā-bodhisattva-mahāsattva Sumatī attains Buddhahood, the seven-treasured pools, waters, trees, flowers, and fruits of her land shall all be exactly so."

Thereupon the Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "This bodhisattva-mahāsattva Sumatī will before long attain Buddhahood, named Ratnaguṇa-samaṅgī Tathāgata (Jewel-Virtue Harmonious Tathāgata), Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha."

When the Buddha taught this sūtra and gave Sumatī the prophecy of Buddhahood, thirty billion people who had generated the aspiration for unsurpassed, equal, universal deliverance were all established on the ground of non-retrogression. Sixty thousand devas all obtained the Dharma-eye regarding all dharmas.

In the assembly, there were five hundred bodhisattvas who heard what Mañjuśrī spoke, which was profoundly deep. Their understanding was insufficient, and they were about to fall into doubt. Seeing that Sumatī's words were sincere and that every response confirmed them, they removed their outer garments and offered them to the Buddha, without flattery and without expectation. Using this merit to strengthen themselves in the aspiration for unsurpassed, equal, universal deliverance, they immediately attained the ground of non-retrogression, transcending ninety kalpas, never again subject to birth and death. At that time, the Buddha gave the five hundred the prediction: "After ten kalpas, in a kalpa named Dustless, the Buddha shall be named Dṛḍhaparigraha Tathāgata (Firm Receiver Tathāgata), Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha. These five hundred shall be born in that land, named Blazing Energy. In that same kalpa, they shall all attain Buddhahood together, all sharing one name: Alaṃkṛta-paracittajña Tathāgata (Adorned Knower of Others' Minds Tathāgata), Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "This sūtra brings great benefit. Is this not so? If in the last age there are bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, śramaṇas, or good men and good women who seek the bodhisattva's path and practice the six pāramitās without yet understanding skillful means, it would be better for them to write out this sūtra, chant it, read it, and study it, and further teach others to be always mindful of its contents, broadly explaining it for all who wish to hear."

The Buddha further told Mañjuśrī: "Bodhisattvas who have not previously heard or practiced this should study and uphold it. Why? It is like a wheel-turning sage-king ruling the world: for the duration of his reign, the seven treasures are never diminished. When the king's life ends, the seven treasures scatter. So it is, Mañjuśrī! When the sūtras and path of the Buddha abide in the world, the Buddha's seven factors of awakening will never be extinguished. If the Buddha-Dharma perishes, the factors of awakening and all dharmas will all come to an end."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "One should seek immeasurable skillful means, diligently seeking all the sūtras, studying, writing, and upholding them, teaching and explaining them for others, broadly elucidating their meaning with constant diligence. This is the Dharma-teaching. If good men and good women wish to seek the path, they should not have regrets along the way."

When the Buddha had finished teaching the sūtra, Sumatī Bodhisattva, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, and all the devas and humans, as well as the asuras, gandharvas, and worldly beings present in the assembly, all rejoiced and delighted in what they heard.
