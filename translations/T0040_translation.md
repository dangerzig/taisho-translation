## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra of King Mandhātā, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the Jeta Grove, in Anāthapiṇḍada's park, in Śrāvastī. At that time, Ānanda, in a secluded place, reflected: "Among the people of the world, those who even slightly become weary of the five objects of desire are few; those who die without ever knowing contentment are many." After midday, Ānanda came before the Buddha, made obeisance to the Buddha, stepped back, and addressed the Buddha: "In a secluded place, I reflected: among the people of the world, those who even slightly become weary of the five objects of desire are few; those who die without ever knowing contentment are many."

The Buddha said: "It is indeed as Ānanda says. Among the people of the world, those who even slightly become weary of the five objects of desire are few; those who die without ever knowing contentment are many. Why is this so? In times past there was a king whose name and title was Mandhātā. He was born emerging from the crown of his mother's head, and was therefore named Mandhātā. He later became a cakravartin king, and the east, west, south, and north all came under his dominion. He possessed the seven treasures: first, the golden wheel; second, the white elephant; third, the dark blue horse; fourth, the bright moon jewel; fifth, the jewel-woman consort; sixth, the holy counselor minister; seventh, the minister who guides the way and commands the troops. Such were the seven treasures of this cakravartin king. The king was humane and virtuous, governed by right Dharma, and did not trouble or vex the myriad people. He had a thousand sons, all upright in appearance and of superior talent, courageous and mighty in strength, and the four continents all submitted and came under his dominion. He reigned for several thousand years, and within his mind he thought: 'I possess the four continents; the people are flourishing and numerous; grain is plentiful and cheap; the people are largely prosperous.' The king again reflected: 'I have a thousand sons, all upright in appearance, of incomparably superior talent, courageous and mighty in strength. If heaven were to rain down coins of gold and silver upon me for seven days and seven nights, how delightful that would be!' Heaven heard his words and, in accordance with his wish, immediately rained coins of gold and silver for seven days and seven nights. The king saw heaven rain coins of gold and silver for seven days and seven nights and was greatly delighted, and together with his retinue he took pleasure for several thousand years. The king reflected: 'The four continents all belong to me; I have a thousand sons and the seven treasures, all present before me. Whatever I wished to obtain, I have already obtained; heaven moreover has not denied my wishes, but rained coins of gold and silver upon me for seven days and seven nights.' King Mandhātā heard that in the south there was the land of Jambudvīpa, greatly pleasant, with flourishing and numerous people. The king wished to go there; the moment the intention arose, he took up the seven treasures and the four kinds of troops, and together they flew and arrived. Jambudvīpa extended two hundred and eighty thousand li; upon seeing the king, that land immediately submitted and came under his dominion. Because the king had done good in former lives, he thus obtained such blessings.

"He dwelt in the land of Aparagodānīya for several thousand years. The king again gave rise to a thought and reflected: 'I possess a great country in the west named Aparagodānīya, extending three hundred and twenty thousand li in length and breadth; I possess the seven treasures; heaven rained coins of gold and silver upon me for seven days and seven nights; I have a thousand sons, all of upright and incomparable appearance, of superior talent, courageous and mighty in strength; I possess the southern land of Jambudvīpa, two hundred and eighty thousand li.' The king heard that in the east there was the land of Pūrvavideha, with flourishing and numerous people, grain plentiful and cheap, that land greatly pleasant. The king wished to go there; the moment the intention arose, he took up the seven treasures and the four kinds of troops, and together they flew and arrived. That country's king and people immediately submitted and came under his dominion, and the king thereupon governed the country by right Dharma.

"In this manner, after several thousand years, the king again gave rise to a thought and reflected: 'I possess Jambudvīpa, two hundred and eighty thousand li; I possess Aparagodānīya, three hundred and twenty thousand li; I possess Pūrvavideha, three hundred and sixty thousand li.' The king heard that in the north was Uttarakuru, a great delight under heaven, with flourishing and numerous people. The king wished to go to that land. Within that land there was no poverty, no distinction between the powerful and the feeble, the strong and the weak, no servants or masters, no high and low; all were of a single equal rank. He thought: 'Let my people, retinue, and officials eat together of the spontaneously arising polished rice, and make use of the spontaneously arising garments, bedding, adornments, and various precious things.' The moment the king's intention arose, he took up the seven treasures and the four kinds of troops, and together they flew into the territory of Uttarakuru. Looking from afar, he saw the earth was a pure blue, like the color of kingfisher feathers. The king asked his border ministers: 'Do you not see that the earth is a pure blue, like the color of kingfisher feathers?' The border ministers replied: 'Indeed, we see it.' The king said: 'This is the land of Uttarakuru.' As the king advanced further, he again saw the earth was a pure white, like snow. The king again addressed his border ministers: 'Do you see that the earth is a pure white?' The border ministers replied: 'Indeed, we see it.' The king said: 'This is the earth of Uttarakuru, where rice grows of its own accord, already husked; you shall all eat of it together.' Advancing again, he looked from afar and saw various treasure trees: hundred-variety clothing trees, upon which hung gold, silver, jade discs, rings, and jeweled necklaces. The king asked his border ministers: 'Do you all see these treasure trees?' The border ministers said: 'Indeed, we see them.' The king said: 'These are the hundred-variety clothing trees, the trees of gold, silver, jade discs, rings, and jeweled necklaces. Go forth and take from them together and adorn yourselves.' The king then proceeded to the land of Uttarakuru, and all its people submitted and came under his dominion.

"The king governed Uttarakuru for several thousand years, and again a thought arose. He reflected: 'I possess Jambudvīpa, Aparagodānīya, Pūrvavideha, and Uttarakuru, four hundred thousand li.' The king wished to ascend Mount Sumeru, king of the four-jeweled mountains, to the place where Śakra, King of the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, makes his abode. The moment the king's intention arose, he took up the seven treasures and his hundred officials, and together they flew to the summit of Mount Sumeru and proceeded into the palace of Śakra, King of the Devas. Śakra, seeing King Mandhātā come from afar, rose to welcome him, saying: 'I have often heard of your meritorious qualities and have long wished to meet you. Most excellent that you have come, worthy one!' He then took him by the hand, and they sat together, Śakra offering half of his own seat to King Mandhātā. Once seated, the king looked left and right at the heavens above. Jewel-women attendants waited upon him, and all was fashioned from the seven treasures: gold, silver, lapis lazuli, crystal, coral, amber, and musāragalva, forming the palaces. Seeing all this, his mind gave rise to the thought: 'I possess Jambudvīpa, Aparagodānīya, Pūrvavideha, and Uttarakuru; in my abode, heaven rained coins of gold and silver for seven days and seven nights.' King Mandhātā then thought: 'If Śakra, King of the Devas, were to die and depart, I would wish to take his place and govern the heavens, just as I governed the earth below. How delightful that would be!' The moment the king entertained this thought, his divine power departed him, and he came back down to earth, immediately stricken with illness, weak and feeble, confined to his bed. All the ministers and officials who had accompanied the king stood at the king's bedside and asked: 'Have you any last words to leave?' The king said: 'There are. Should any dare ask you, "What last words did the king leave?" you should tell them: "While the king was alive, he governed the four continents; heaven rained coins of gold and silver upon him for seven days and seven nights; the king had a thousand sons and the seven treasures, and was able to fly through the air. The king ascended to the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven; Śakra, King of the Devas, rose to welcome and comfort him, inquiring after his well-being, and offered him half of his own seat to sit upon. And yet he still gave rise to the thought of wishing to possess Śakra's position. The moment that thought arose, he came back down to earth and was immediately stricken with illness and weakness. He said in remorse: 'People until their death have no contentment; those who know contentment are few indeed.'"'"

The sūtra declares: one cannot be satisfied even by heaven raining coins of gold and silver for seven days and seven nights; the benefit is meager, and the fault is great and grave. A person of wisdom should reflect upon this matter. Moreover, having obtained even half of Śakra's seat was still not sufficient. Only when one practices and seeks the path, attaining Srotāpanna, Sakṛdāgāmin, Anāgāmin, Arhat, and Pratyekabuddha, and finally attaining the Buddha-path, does one know contentment." The Buddha told Ānanda: "King Mandhātā of that time was none other than myself."

The Buddha having spoken thus, Ānanda rejoiced and made obeisance to the Buddha.
