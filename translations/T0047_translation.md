## Fascicle 1

### The Sūtra on Overcoming Drowsiness, Spoken by the Buddha

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling in the Deer Park of Suṃsumāragira in the land of Bhagga. At that time, the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana was staying in the village of Kalyāṇamitra in Magadha. The Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana, dwelling alone in a secluded place, had fallen asleep while doing walking meditation. The World-honored One, knowing that the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana was alone in a secluded place doing walking meditation and had fallen asleep, immediately entered the samādhi-samāpatti corresponding to that state. With the mind of samādhi, as swiftly as a strong man might bend or extend his arm, the World-honored One vanished from the Deer Park of Suṃsumāragira in the land of Bhagga and appeared before the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana in the village of Kalyāṇamitra in Magadha. The World-honored One then arose from samādhi and said to the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "Maudgalyāyana! Maudgalyāyana! Are you drowsy?"

"Yes, indeed, World-honored One!"

"On account of what perception do you wish to sleep? Do not follow perception, do not discriminate among perceptions, do not proliferate mental formations — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If drowsiness is not thereby abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, extensively recite and review the Dhamma as you have heard and learned it — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If it is not abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, expound at length to others the Dhamma as you have heard and studied it — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If it is not abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, mentally recollect and practice the Dhamma as you have studied and heard it — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If it is not abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, splash cold water on your eyes and wash your limbs — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If it is not abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, rub your ears with both hands — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If it is not abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, rise and leave the assembly hall, look in all four directions and observe the stars — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If it is not abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, go to an open space and walk about, guard the sense faculties, keep the mind attentive to generosity, and thereafter sustain full awareness — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If it is not abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, return from your walking, take up your niṣīdana and spread it on the bed, and sit in the full cross-legged posture — thus drowsiness will be abandoned. If it is not abandoned, then, Maudgalyāyana, return to the assembly hall, fold the uttarāsaṅga in four and spread it on the bed, place the saṃghāṭī before the head, lie down on the right side, place foot upon foot, cultivate the perception of light, keep the mind undistracted, and constantly maintain the intention to rise, abiding in contemplation.

"Maudgalyāyana, do not delight in the bed, do not delight in lying on the right side, do not delight in sleep, do not relish the world's reverence as a pleasure. Why is this? Maudgalyāyana, I do not say that one should draw near to all things, nor do I say that one should not draw near to all things. How, Maudgalyāyana, do I say one should not draw near? Maudgalyāyana, I say one should not associate closely with householders. Maudgalyāyana, if one dwells in close association with householders, there will be discourse without shared study; from that discourse flattery and arrogance arise; from arrogance jealousy arises; through jealousy restlessness does not subside. Maudgalyāyana, when there is no subsiding of restlessness, samādhi recedes far away. This, Maudgalyāyana, is what I call the way of non-association. And how, Maudgalyāyana, do I describe the way of proper association? Maudgalyāyana, go to a secluded place, make a grass mat as a seat, remain silent, abandon all that is evil, keep away from the crowd, and constantly sit in contemplation. This, Maudgalyāyana, is what I call the way of proper association.

"Maudgalyāyana, when entering a village for alms, do not seek material gain, do not seek reverence. Maudgalyāyana, having put aside the intention to seek gain and reverence, then enter the village for alms. Maudgalyāyana, when entering a village for alms, do not enter a household with anticipatory expectations. Why? Maudgalyāyana, in the homes of householders there are many worldly entanglements. If a bhikkhu enters a householder's home and the householder does not speak with him, that bhikkhu thinks: 'Who has spoken ill of me to this householder, causing the householder not to speak with me?' Resentment arises; with resentment, conceit arises; with conceit, restlessness arises; with restlessness, the mind finds no peace. Maudgalyāyana, with an unpeaceful mind, samādhi recedes far away. Maudgalyāyana, when teaching the Dhamma, do not seek victory in debate; cultivate the mind of non-contention. If one seeks to win, much argumentation follows; from much argumentation conceit arises; from conceit jealousy arises; from jealousy restlessness arises. Maudgalyāyana, from restlessness, I say, samādhi recedes far away. Maudgalyāyana, when teaching the Dhamma, make it beneficial, speak with resolve, do not speak disparagingly of others, and let your discourse be like the roar of a lion. Thus, Maudgalyāyana, should you train."

Thereupon the Venerable Mahāmaudgalyāyana rose from his seat, arranged his robe on one shoulder, joined his palms toward the World-honored One, and said to the World-honored One: "World-honored One, how does a bhikkhu attain ultimate completion, ultimate freedom from defilement, the ultimate practice of brahmacariya?"

"This, Maudgalyāyana: whatever feelings a bhikkhu has, whether painful, pleasant, or neither painful nor pleasant, he should contemplate those feelings as impermanent, contemplate them as subject to decay, contemplate them as free from attachment, contemplate them as subject to cessation, contemplate them as rightly discerned, contemplate them as the place of stilling — thus should he contemplate those feelings. Contemplating those feelings as impermanent, contemplating them as subject to decay, contemplating them as free from attachment, contemplating them as subject to cessation, contemplating them as stilled, contemplating them as at rest — he does not cling to this world; not clinging, he is without fear; without fear, he attains parinirvāṇa with remainder; birth is exhausted, the brahmacariya is fulfilled, what had to be done has been done, nāmarūpa has been known as it truly is. This, bhikkhu Maudgalyāyana, is how one attains ultimate completion, ultimate freedom from defilement, fulfillment of brahmacariya, the ultimate practice of brahmacariya."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The Venerable Maudgalyāyana, having heard what the World-honored One had spoken, rejoiced and was gladdened.
