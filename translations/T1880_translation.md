# Cloud-Interval Classified Commentary on the Essay on the Golden Lion (金師子章雲間類解)

## Taisho T1880

**Original text by:** Fazang (法藏, 643-712), Tang dynasty (Huayan patriarch)
**Commentary by:** Jingyuan (淨源), Song dynasty
**Fascicles:** 1

*Note: Fazang (法藏 Fǎzàng), the third patriarch of the Huayan (華嚴 Huáyán, "Flower Ornament") school, composed the original "Essay on the Golden Lion" (金師子章 Jīn shīzi zhāng) for Empress Wu Zetian to explain the profound doctrines of the Avataṃsaka Sutra through the analogy of a golden lion. In the Song dynasty, Jingyuan (淨源 Jìngyuán) composed this "Cloud-Interval Classified Commentary" (雲間類解 Yúnjiān lèijiě), systematically annotating Fazang's text with references to the Huayan sutras and treatises. The commentary is organized into ten sections corresponding to ten doctrinal themes: dependent origination, form and emptiness, the three natures, signlessness, non-arising, the five teachings, the ten mysterious gates, the six characteristics, attaining bodhi, and entering nirvana.*

---

## Preface by Jingyuan

The Dharma cannot be revealed without metaphor; metaphor cannot arise without the Dharma. Therefore the supreme one, seeing that the nature of the one truth is not differentiated, used the golden lion to illustrate it. Seeing that the capacities of sentient beings are not equal, he used the various Dharma chapters to guide them. How rich! Were it not for our ancestor, the Worthy First (賢首 *Xiánshǒu*, i.e. Fazang), who set forth the literature of the One Vehicle and opened up the profundities of the ten directions, how could the stream of compassion have instructed the world, dispensing teachings according to each being's capacity? And were it not for the Celestially Appointed Sage Empress (天冊聖帝 *Tiāncè shèngdì*, i.e. Empress Wu), who humbled her heart despite commanding the myriad chariots to honor the teachings of the Three Jewels, how could she have understood the Dharma through metaphor and, through the Dharma, penetrated true nature?

Now this text is revered and esteemed in both meditation halls and lecture seats. Commentaries on it currently circulating in the world number nearly four. The Chan Master Qingyuan Zhiguan (清源止觀禪師) annotated it earlier; the Great Worthy Zhaosin Fadeng (昭信法燈大士) explained it later. In recent times, one bearing the same title Huazang (華藏), the Dharma Master Zhaoyu of Siqu (四衢昭昱法師), and the Venerable Chengqian of Wutai (五臺承遷尊者) have all composed commentaries. Having reviewed their words, I find that some are verbose yet deficient in meaning, and some have lengthy passages that deviate from the teaching. This causes those in the two paths of meditation and lecturing, just as they aspire to transmit and study these teachings, to instead fall into the trap of attachment and rejection.

I, Jingyuan, unworthy as I am, have long pondered these refined principles and have always been troubled by this situation. Accordingly, I have investigated the two Profound texts of the Jin-era sutra, and thoroughly examined the two commentaries on the Tang-era sutra. Where the text was verbose, I have pruned it. Where the meaning was deficient, I have supplemented it. Where passages were lengthy, I have trimmed them. Where the teaching deviated, I have corrected it. Throughout, where Dharma-language and profound terms clearly accord with the patriarch's chapter-principles, I have classified and explained each according to its doctrinal category. I completed this work at the Good Dwelling Pavilion (善住閣 *Shànzhù gé*) in Yunjian (雲間). Hence the title: "Cloud-Interval Classified Commentary."

Yuanfeng, third year, *gēngshēn* cycle, eighth day of the fourth month [1080 CE].

**Title:** *Huayan Essay on the Golden Lion* (華嚴金師子章)

"Huayan" (華嚴) designates the sutra to which this belongs. "Essay on the Golden Lion" (金師子章) is its proper title. Using metaphor to reveal the Dharma is already fully explained in the preface.

**Author:** Composed by the Sramana Fazang of the Great Jianfu Temple in the Capital (京大薦福寺沙門法藏述)

"Capital" (京) refers to Chang'an, the capital established by Gaozu of the Han dynasty. The Great Jianfu Temple (大薦福寺 *Dà Jiànfú sì*) was built by Emperor Zhongzong of the Tang dynasty. "Sramana" (沙門 *shāmén*) is the designation for Buddhist monastics who cultivate the good and extinguish evil. The next two characters are his name. His accomplishments as a renunciant who transmitted the Way and propagated the translations are fully recorded in the *Lives of Eminent Monks Compiled in the Sagely Song Dynasty*, and in the stele inscription by the Junior Supervisor of the Tang, Yan [Liben]. As for his judgment of the five chapters of the doctrinal path, Longxi [Li Yong] praised it in his treatise. As for the compilation of the six-fold contemplation gates from the sastra, Hedong [Wang Wei] commended it in his stupa inscription. Moreover, following the principles of this chapter as a model, both the commentaries on the *Flower Ornament* and the *Perfect Enlightenment Sutra* record them in detail. "述" (composed) means, as the *Record of Music* says, "to illuminate." Zheng Xuan said: "To expound the meaning."

## Chapter One: Clarifying Dependent Origination (明緣起第一)

The supreme sage transmitted his teaching with causes and conditions as the guiding principle. Conditions have the distinctions of internal and external, mundane and supramundane. Therefore this is designated as the first chapter, clarifying the various forms of dependent origination.

**Gold has no self-nature; following the conditions of the skilled artisan,**

Gold symbolizes true suchness (真如 *zhēnrú*), which does not maintain its own nature. The artisan represents birth-and-death, which follows the conditions of delusion.

**the form of a lion consequently arises.**

This symbolizes the union of truth and delusion, forming the alaya-consciousness (阿賴耶識 *ālàiyē shí*). This consciousness has two aspects: first, the aspect of awakening, which serves as the basis for pure dependent origination; second, the aspect of non-awakening, which creates defiled dependent origination.

**The arising is entirely due to conditions; therefore it is called dependent origination.**

The sutra says: "All dharmas arise from conditions. Without conditions, they do not arise." This is the gate of the non-obstruction of principle and phenomena, being one and the same dependent origination. The first sentence indicates the conditions. The middle sentence distinguishes the arising. The last sentence provides the overall conclusion.

---

## Chapter Two: Distinguishing Form and Emptiness (辨色空第二)

The previous chapter clarified dependent origination, which does not go beyond form and emptiness. Illusory form is the conventional truth. True emptiness is the ultimate truth. The non-obstruction of the two truths is the single Middle Way. Therefore the next chapter distinguishes form and emptiness.

**The form of the lion is void; there is only true gold.**

Since the illusory appearance of form is void, the nature of true emptiness is the sole reality.

**The lion is not [truly] existent; the gold-substance is not [truly] nonexistent.**

The form and appearance arise from conditions and therefore are not [truly] existent; this eliminates the ordinary person's view of solid reality. The empty nature does not change and therefore is not [truly] nonexistent; this eliminates the outsider's view of nihilistic emptiness.

**Therefore it is called form and emptiness.**

Since the form-aggregate is thus, all dharmas are likewise. The *Great Perfection of Wisdom Sutra* says: "If dharmas were not empty, there would be no path and no fruit." The first sentence provides a paired heading of form and emptiness. The next sentence provides a paired explanation. The last sentence provides a paired conclusion.

**Furthermore, emptiness has no self-characteristics; it is made clear in reference to form.**

This is true emptiness. It does not obstruct form. Thus contemplating emptiness, myriad practices surge forth.

**It does not obstruct illusory existence and is called form-emptiness.**

This is illusory form. It does not obstruct emptiness. Thus engaging with existence, the one path remains pure. In summary: first, with regard to the unchanging and condition-following nature of characteristics, the nihilistic and the substantive are eliminated. Then, with regard to not abiding in either birth-and-death or nirvana, compassion and wisdom are clarified.

---

## Chapter Three: Applying the Three Natures (約三性第三)

**The lion has emotional existence; this is called the universally discriminated (遍計 *biànjì*).**

This means that deluded emotion discriminates regarding the self and all dharmas, comprehensively calculating and clinging to each as truly existent. Like a foolish child who sees a person's face reflected in a mirror and clings to it as having a real body with flesh and bones. Therefore it is called "emotionally existent."

**The lion has seeming existence; this is called the other-dependent (依他 *yītā*).**

These clung-to dharmas arise in dependence on a combination of other conditions. They entirely lack self-nature and are merely empty appearances. Like a reflection in a mirror. Therefore it is called "seeming existence."

**The nature of gold does not change; therefore it is called the perfectly accomplished (圓成 *yuánchéng*).**

The original awakening of the true mind is revealed by initial awakening. It is perfectly complete, accomplished, genuine, and permanently abiding. Like the brightness of the mirror. Therefore it is called "unchanging."

The preceding explanation used the emptiness school to expound the meaning, building on the earlier chapter. This chapter applies the nature school to explain the text, also using one metaphor to explain another. According to the teaching, the chapter clarifies that each of the three natures has two aspects. The universally discriminated nature has two aspects: first, emotionally existent; second, lacking in principle. The other-dependent nature has two aspects: first, seeming existence; second, lacking self-nature. The perfectly accomplished nature has two aspects: first, unchanging; second, following conditions. The present text reveals only the first aspect of each and conceals the second. Respectfully considering the patriarch's intent, the paired and unpaired principles operate together with doctrinal purpose.

---

## Chapter Four: Revealing Signlessness (顯無相第四)

**Using gold to subsume the lion completely:**

Since one takes the true gold and fashions the lion, consequently all the lion's characteristics are exhausted.

**apart from gold there is no lion-characteristic to be found.**

Gold represents principle. The lion represents phenomena. This is also as Zhongnan [Du Shun] said: "Apart from true principle, there is not a shred of phenomena to be found."

**Therefore it is called signlessness.**

The *Names Chapter* of the sutra says: "One who penetrates the signless dharma abides in the Buddha's abode." The *Sutra of Innumerable Meanings* says: "That one dharma is called signlessness." The *Names Chapter* speaks from the perspective of the fruit; the *Sutra of Innumerable Meanings* speaks from the perspective of principle. Though principle and fruit differ, signlessness is one.

---

## Chapter Five: Explaining Non-Arising (說無生第五)

**When one truly sees the lion arising, it is only gold arising.**

The upper sentence indicates the deluded dharma following conditions. The lower sentence indicates the true nature not changing. The verse says: "Like gold fashioned into a ring, through all transformations there is no difference."

**Apart from gold there is not a single thing.**

Apart from the unchanging nature there is no appearance of following conditions. The *Chapter on Questions of Light* says: "There has never been a single dharma that enters into the dharma-nature."

**Though the lion has arising and ceasing, the gold-substance is fundamentally without increase or decrease.**

When phenomena are completed, there is seeming arising, yet the gold-nature does not increase; thus what arises is only the dharma arising. When the substance is emptied, there is seeming ceasing, yet the gold-nature does not decrease; thus what ceases is only the dharma ceasing.

**Therefore it is called non-arising.**

The *Mahaparinirvana Sutra* says: "The nature of the aggregates cannot be destroyed; therefore it is said there is no arising." It also says: "Because it is empty, it cannot be destroyed; this is the meaning of non-arising." The commentary says: "Non-arising is the substance of the Buddha-dharma. All sutras and treatises expound the principle of non-arising. The *Lankavatara* teaches that all dharmas do not arise. The *Madhyamaka* treatise takes non-arising as the substance of its thesis."

---

## Chapter Six: Discussing the Five Teachings (論五教第六)

The wondrous nature of non-arising transcends all categories and exceeds all traces. Yet when beings' capacities respond, they are differentiated according to their root natures. Therefore the next chapter discusses the five teachings.

**First: The lion, though a dharma of causes and conditions, with moment-to-moment arising and ceasing,**

The lion pertains to conditioned arising. The *Treatise on the Origin of Humanity* explains the Hinayana teaching likewise: "From beginningless time, through the power of causes and conditions, there has been moment-to-moment arising and ceasing in continuous succession without end."

**in reality has no lion-characteristic that can be found.**

The treatise continues: "Ordinary fools are unaware and cling to these as real."

**This is called the Sravaka teaching of the foolish regarding dharmas.**

Because they awaken through hearing the teaching of the Four Noble Truths, they are called sravakas (聲聞 *shēngwén*). Having eliminated the clinging to self but not yet penetrating to the emptiness of dharmas, they are called "foolish regarding dharmas." This single teaching encompasses the human and celestial teachings below it, since the profound necessarily includes the shallow. It also encompasses the pratyekabuddha above it, since their principle and fruit are the same. Just as when one classifies by persons one speaks only of the Sravaka-pitaka.

**Second: These very dharmas of conditioned arising**

Building on the previous to introduce the next. The words "lion" from the first chapter also apply to this and the following three.

**each lack self-nature; they are thoroughly and completely empty.**

Starting from the form of the body and the thoughts of the mind, ending with the omniscience of buddhahood: all lack self-nature, penetrating through to the surface of existence as nothing but true emptiness. Because the nature of form is itself empty, not that form is destroyed and then empty.

**This is called the Mahayana Beginning Teaching.**

"Beginning" means initial. The *Great Perfection of Wisdom Sutra* says: "Emptiness is the initial gate of the Great Vehicle." This teaching has two aspects: first, the Beginning Teaching, also called the Partial Teaching. The text here mentions only "Beginning Teaching" because the second and third time-teachings of the *Saṃdhinirmocana Sutra* both agree that beings of fixed nature and those without Buddha-nature cannot attain buddhahood. Therefore these are combined and called simply "Beginning Teaching."

**Third: Although thoroughly and completely empty, this does not obstruct**

True emptiness does not obstruct illusory existence. This is to distinguish the waves by means of the water.

**the wondrous existence of conditioned arising. The two characteristics exist simultaneously.**

Illusory existence does not obstruct true emptiness. This is to clarify the water by means of the waves.

**This is called the Mahayana Final Teaching.**

Conditioned arising has no [fixed] nature; everything is suchness. This is the most ultimate expression of the Great Vehicle, so it is called "Final." This also has two aspects: first, the Final Teaching, named in contrast to the Beginning Teaching; second, the Real Teaching, named in contrast to the Partial Teaching. "Partial" means provisional. The beginning is provisional while the final is real, because there is a school that reveals reality. However, both the Final/Real and the Beginning/Partial teachings are all within the gradual gate of the Great Vehicle.

**Fourth: These two characteristics mutually subsume one another, and both vanish.**

When principle subsumes phenomena, phenomena vanish; then true principle is not phenomena. When phenomena subsume principle, principle vanishes; then phenomenal dharmas are not principle. This is also the same as the gate of "mutual subsuming without remainder" in the commentary on the *Sutra of the Vows of Samantabhadra*.

**Deluded and false [characteristics] have no place to exist.**

Turning from the commentary on the above sentence: when both principle and phenomena vanish, then the false characteristics of emotional discrimination have nowhere to abide.

**Both are without power; emptiness and existence are both extinguished.**

Because of the preceding mutual subsuming, both are powerless. When principle subsumes phenomena, then wondrous existence is extinguished. When phenomena subsume principle, then true emptiness is extinguished. The *Brief Commentary on the Heart Sutra* says: "When both emptiness and existence vanish, the one flavor is constantly manifest."

**The path of language is cut off; the mind has nowhere to rest.**

Comprehensively concluding that both mind and language are inaccessible. The *Treasure Store Treatise* says: "When principle is merged, the path of language is cut off. When the essence is realized, the place where the mind operates ceases."

**This is called the Mahayana Sudden Teaching.**

"Sudden" means that language is suddenly cut off and the principle-nature is suddenly revealed. In a single thought of non-arising, one is already a buddha, and so forth. Therefore the *Lankavatara* says: "The sudden is like an image in a mirror, appearing suddenly and not gradually." This also has two aspects: first, the sudden that follows beings' capacities, as indicated in this text; second, the sudden of the manner of instruction, which is included under the following Perfect Teaching.

**Fifth: These very dharmas whose emotional [overlay] is exhausted and whose substance is revealed**

Emotion exhausted and views removed. The *Great Commentary* also says: "When emotion is exhausted, principle is revealed, and all views spontaneously vanish."

**merge into a single block.**

Regarding the dharma, they merge into the one true nature. Regarding the metaphor, they are one block of true gold. Therefore Minister Pei's preface says: "Melting down vases, plates, hairpins, and bracelets into gold."

**Their great function flourishes abundantly; whatever arises is entirely true.**

Their function is like waves surging and cauldrons boiling, running entirely on the true substance.

**The myriad phenomena are profuse, yet they participate without confusion.**

The myriad dharmas arise necessarily at the same time and in the same instant; in principle there is no before or after. Commenting on the above two points, following the *Contemplation of Returning to the Source*.

**All is one; all share the nature of no-nature.**

Understanding the one within the many. The sutra says: "In every mote of dust throughout the Flower Treasury World, one sees the entire dharma-realm."

**One is all; cause and effect are distinctly evident.**

Understanding the many within the one. The *Preface to the Complete Collection on Chan* says: "The fruit thoroughly penetrates the source of the cause, and the fully endowed position is called 'bodhisattva.'"

**Power and function mutually subsume; rolling up and spreading out are free and unhindered.**

When one has the power to subsume the many as its function, then it rolls up all the others and takes them into the one; this is the above-mentioned "all is one; all share the nature of no-nature." When many have the power to subsume one as their substance, then each spreads out its own position into all the others; this is the above-mentioned "one is all; cause and effect are distinctly evident." Though the text presents them sequentially, in meaning they are simultaneous. Therefore it says "rolling up and spreading out are free and unhindered."

**This is called the One Vehicle Perfect Teaching.**

What it teaches is solely the dharma-realm dependent origination, which is non-obstructing. Mutual identity and mutual inclusion are layered without end. This also has two aspects: the Shared Teaching of the One Vehicle, which perfectly and completely includes all the doctrinal schools; and the Distinct Teaching of the One Vehicle, which perfectly and completely distinguishes itself from all the doctrinal schools.

## Chapter Seven: Enumerating the Ten Mysterious Gates (勒十玄第七)

**First: Gold and lion are established simultaneously; they are perfectly complete.**

The lion's six sense-faculties and the gold are established at the same time. This indicates that persons and dharmas, causes and effects, substance and function are all perfectly complete. The *Chapter on Wondrous Adornments* says: "All the inexhaustible oceans of Dharma-gates converge at one Dharma site."

**This is called the gate of simultaneous completeness and mutual correspondence.**

The *Great Commentary* says: "Like a single drop of ocean water containing the flavors of a hundred rivers."

**Second: If the lion's eye subsumes the entire lion, then everything is purely the eye. If the ear subsumes the entire lion, then everything is purely the ear.**

Eye and ear mutually subsume, each being purely that one thing.

**When all the sense-faculties simultaneously subsume one another, they are each completely fulfilled.**

Bringing together the commonality of all the sense-faculties, as illustrated by the eye and ear individually.

**Then each and every one is both mixed and pure, forming a perfectly complete treasury.**

When eye is identical with ear and so forth, that is "mixed." It is like a bodhisattva entering one samadhi yet simultaneously cultivating the six paramitas and immeasurable other practices and virtues, all accomplished at once; therefore it is called "mixed." When ear is not eye and so forth, that is "pure." Also like entering one samadhi and practicing only giving to an immeasurable extent, with no other practices; this is called "pure." As the *Chapter on the Teaching and Its Meaning* says: "Pure and mixed are free and unhindered, completely fulfilled; this is called a perfectly complete treasury."

**This is called the gate of the various treasuries being both pure and mixed with complete virtue.**

This name follows the formulation of Zhixiang (至相). The Worthy First (Fazang) newly established the gate of "breadth and narrowness freely unhindered." Therefore the *Great Commentary* says: "Like a mirror one foot in diameter reflecting a vista of a thousand miles."

**Third: Gold and lion are mutually compatible and established; one and many are unobstructed.**

Many containing one means the six faculties are established. Many containing many means the lion remains unchanged.

**Within this, principle and phenomena are each different.**

The nature of gold symbolizes principle. The lion symbolizes phenomena. Though the two mutually contain each other, their nature and characteristics remain distinct.

**Whether one or many, each abides in its own position.**

The sutra's verse says: "With one buddha-land filling the ten directions, the ten directions entering into one, with no remainder: the original characteristics of the world are also not destroyed. The one of incomparable virtue is able to do this."

**This is called the gate of one and many mutually containing without being the same.**

The *Great Commentary* says: "Like a thousand lamps in one room, light and light interpenetrating."

**Fourth: Each of the lion's sense-faculties, each and every hair-tip, because gold subsumes the lion completely,**

Every sense-faculty and every hair each incorporates the entire body.

**each thoroughly pervades the entire lion. The eye is the ear; the ear is the nose; the nose is the tongue; the tongue is the body.**

The sense-faculties are mutually identical. Substance is not outside of function.

**They are freely and spontaneously established, without obstruction.**

The sutra says: "One is precisely many; many is precisely one. The text follows the meaning, and the meaning follows the text."

**This is called the gate of all dharmas being mutually identical and free.**

The *Great Commentary* says: "Like gold and the color of gold, the two are inseparable."

**Fifth: When one looks at the lion, there is only the lion and no gold; the lion is manifest and the gold is hidden.**

Characteristics manifest, nature hidden.

**When one looks at the gold, there is only gold and no lion; the gold is manifest and the lion is hidden.**

Nature manifest, characteristics hidden.

**When one looks from both perspectives, both are hidden and both are manifest.**

Nature and characteristics simultaneously: hidden and manifest appear together.

**The hidden is secret; the manifest is evident.**

The *Worthy First Chapter* says: "Entering concentration in the east, arising from concentration in the west."

**This is called the gate of the secret and the manifest being simultaneously accomplished.**

The *Great Commentary* says: "Like the crescent moon in a clear sky, darkness and light appearing together."

**Sixth: Gold and lion, whether hidden or manifest, whether one or many:**

When one contemplates the gold, the lion seems hidden; only one gold is manifest. When one contemplates the lion, the gold-nature seems hidden; the various sense-faculties are all manifest.

**are determined as pure or mixed, powerful or powerless.**

The one substance of true gold is pure and powerful. The six sense-faculties, differentiated, are mixed and powerless.

**This is that; that is this. Principal and attendant shine upon each other.**

This as principal, that as attendant: their light interacts and participates mutually.

**Principle and phenomena appear equally; all mutually contain one another.**

The *Chapter on the Teaching and Its Meaning* says: "Like a bundle of arrows, all appearing together evenly."

**Without hindering their establishment, subtlety and refinement are accomplished.**

The sutra says: "Within a single mote of dust there are innumerable lands; the lands in turn becoming dust, that is even harder to fathom."

**This is called the gate of subtle mutual containment and peaceful establishment.**

The *Great Commentary* says: "Like a lapis lazuli vase filled with many mustard seeds."

**Seventh: Each of the lion's eye, ear, limb, joint, and every hair contains a golden lion. All the lions in every hair simultaneously and at once enter into a single hair.**

Taking everything to subsume everything, all entering into one: this is the gate of interpenetration without obstruction. The verse says: "In every mote of dust of every buddha-land, buddhas equal in number to those dust-motes sit within a single pore."

**Within each hair there are boundless lions. And furthermore, each hair, carrying these boundless lions, in turn enters into a single hair.**

Again taking everything to subsume everything, carrying it, and entering into one: this is the gate of mutual inclusion without obstruction. The verse says: "In immeasurable oceans of lands within a single hair, they all sit on lotus seats of enlightenment."

**In this way, layer upon layer without end, like the jeweled net of the god Indra.**

In Sanskrit, "Sakra Devanam Indra" (釋迦提桓因陀羅), here meaning "the Able and Benevolent Lord of the Devas." The jeweled net refers to the jeweled net that adorns the Hall of Good Dharma. The metaphor is of lights endlessly reflecting one another.

**This is called the gate of the Indra-net realm.**

The *Great Commentary* says: "Like two mirrors facing each other, their reflections transmitting light endlessly back and forth."

**Eighth: This lion is spoken to indicate ignorance. The gold-substance fully reveals the true nature.**

Deluded dharmas of arising and ceasing are ignorance. The tathagatagarbha (如來藏 *rúláizàng*), which does not arise or cease, is the true nature.

**When principle and phenomena are discussed together, they illustrate the alaya-consciousness, producing right understanding.**

Principle and phenomena are truth and delusion. The treatise says: "Truth and delusion combined, neither identical nor different, are called alaya-consciousness." This consciousness has two aspects: awakening and non-awakening. Awakening produces the right understanding of the true nature. Non-awakening produces the right understanding of ignorance. From the perspective of Sudhana's visits to various teachers, when one encounters the three poisons, the three virtues become perfect: this is all right understanding.

**This is called the gate of relying on phenomena to reveal the Dharma and produce understanding.**

The *Great Commentary* says: "Like erecting a statue or raising an arm: whatever meets the eye is the Way."

**Ninth: The lion is a conditioned dharma; moment to moment it arises and ceases.**

Following the artisan's conditions, it changes and passes moment by moment.

**In an instant, it is divided into three periods:**

Drawing from the past to identify what follows.

**namely past, present, and future. Each of these three periods in turn contains past, present, and future.**

The *Chapter on the Practice of Samantabhadra* says: "Within the past there is the future; within the future there is the past." This is also from the *Chapter on Leaving the World*, answering the questions of Universal Wisdom.

**In total there are three-times-three positions, which together form a single unit of dharma.**

Just as the lion's sense-faculties and hairs are fundamentally one pure gold.

**Although the nine periods are each separate and distinct, because they are interdependently established, they interpenetrate without obstruction and together form a single thought-moment.**

The *Comprehensive Treatise on the Profound* says: "The ten periods of past and present, from beginning to end, are never apart from this very thought-moment."

**This is called the gate of the ten periods being separately constituted yet differently accomplished.**

The *Great Commentary* says: "Like a single night's dream in which one roams for a hundred years."

**Tenth: Gold and lion, whether hidden or manifest, whether one or many, each lack self-nature; they turn according to the mind.**

This means that the entire mind constitutes one thing, and following the mind it pervades everything; thus one is hidden and many are manifest. The entire mind constitutes everything, and following the mind it enters one thing; thus many are hidden and one is manifest. This indicates that the lion and the gold all turn around without fixed characteristics.

**We speak of phenomena, we speak of principle; there is accomplishment and there is establishment.**

The sutra says: "One should contemplate the nature of the dharma-realm: everything is created by the mind alone."

**This is called the gate of the mind-only turning and accomplishing.**

The Worthy First also revised this one gate to be the gate of "principal and attendant perfectly luminous with complete virtue." Therefore the *Great Commentary* says: "Like the North Star in its place, with all the stars attending it."

## Chapter Eight: Summarizing the Six Characteristics (括六相第八)

**The lion is the characteristic of totality.**

One that possesses the many is the characteristic of totality.

**The five sense-faculties being differentiated is the characteristic of distinction.**

The many that are not one is the characteristic of distinction.

**Sharing a common arising from one condition is the characteristic of sameness.**

The many kinds share a common sameness to form the totality.

**Eye, ear, and so on not blending into one another is the characteristic of difference.**

Their names and substances are separately different, manifesting within the sameness.

**The combination of all the sense-faculties constituting a lion is the characteristic of completion.**

One and many arise dependently, and the principle is wondrously completed.

**Each sense-faculty abiding in its own position is the characteristic of destruction.**

"Destruction" means that each abides in its own dharma and constantly does not act [as something else]. In the *Chapter on the Teaching and Its Meaning*, there is a verse in entry-lines. The six lines above are quoted and annotated accordingly. The final two lines express admiration and encourage practice: "Only the realm of wisdom, not of discursive consciousness: by this skillful means one comprehends the One Vehicle." That chapter extensively borrows the metaphor of a single building to illustrate the six characteristics. Later students who wish to honor the patriarch's instructions should thoroughly investigate it.

## Chapter Nine: Attaining Bodhi (成菩提第九)

**Bodhi is rendered in Chinese as "the Way" and "awakening."**

Translating from Sanskrit to Chinese, with both old and new renderings.

**When one sees the lion, one sees that all conditioned dharmas, without needing to be destroyed, are fundamentally quiescent and extinct.**

The *Vimalakirti Sutra* says: "Sentient beings are themselves quiescent extinction. They need not be further extinguished."

**Abandoning all grasping and rejecting, one enters, right here on this path, into the ocean of sarvajña. Therefore it is called the Way.**

The phrase "abandoning all grasping and rejecting" belongs in meaning to the sentence above but connects textually to the sentence below. This means: not rejecting all conditioned dharmas to grasp quiescent extinction as the unconditioned; this belongs in meaning to the upper sentence. Once the emotions of grasping and rejecting are gone, one naturally flows into the ocean of omniscience; this connects textually to the lower sentence. The eighth stage of Non-Movement also clarifies this principle. *Sarvajña* means "omniscience." Here the fruit-virtue is clarified as the Way; therefore it is deep and vast like an ocean.

**One then comprehends that from beginningless time, all inversions have fundamentally never been real. This is called awakening.**

The *Awakening of Faith* says: "All sentient beings are not called 'awakened,' because from the beginning they have been in continuous thought after thought, never having been apart from thought. Therefore it is said there is beginningless ignorance." This corresponds to the present text's "from beginningless time, all inversions." The treatise also says: "If one attains no-thought, then one knows that the characteristics of the mind, arising, abiding, changing, and ceasing... are fundamentally equal in the one awakening." This corresponds to the text's "fundamentally never been real; this is called awakening."

**Ultimately possessing omniscience of all kinds (一切種智 *yīqiè zhǒngzhì*): this is called attaining bodhi.**

This is the ultimate fruit. It is also called ultimate awakening. "Omniscience of all kinds" is one of the three kinds of wisdom. Previously, Guifeng (圭峯), in his commentary on the *Perfect Enlightenment Sutra*, interpreted "perfect luminosity" as omniscience of all kinds. The Worthy First, in his *Contemplation of Returning to the Source*, described attaining bodhi through perfect luminosity. The present text says that possessing omniscience of all kinds constitutes attaining bodhi. Broadly considered, though the phrasing differs slightly, the essential purport is the same. According to the *Awakening of Faith*, "having the great wisdom-function of immeasurable skillful means... is called omniscience of all kinds," all of which belongs to the Shared Teaching. Further, according to the Zhaosin commentary, it describes how the practitioners of the five teachings each attain bodhi; only the Perfect school, through the two gates of cause and effect mutually subsuming, represents the Distinct Teaching.

## Chapter Ten: Entering Nirvana (入涅槃第十)

**Seeing that the lion and gold, both characteristics, are completely exhausted: afflictions do not arise.**

Both characteristics completely exhausted: the observed realm is emptied. Afflictions do not arise: the perceiving mind is stilled. Inner and outer both vanish: profound quiescence prevails.

**Whether beauty or ugliness appears before one, the mind is peaceful as the ocean.**

The *New Record* says: "Like gold fashioned into vessels, skillful and clumsy are vastly different; this is beauty and ugliness appearing before one." The record continues: "Threading them all with one principle, only the gold is ultimate; this is the mind being peaceful as the ocean." The upper sentence echoes the commentary on "both characteristics completely exhausted." The lower sentence echoes the commentary on "afflictions do not arise."

**Deluded thoughts are all exhausted; there is no oppression or affliction. One exits the wrappings and separates from the obstacles, forever abandoning the source of suffering. This is called entering nirvana.**

Delusions and karma are all exhausted: there are no deluded thoughts of the truth of origination. The three sufferings all vanish: there is no oppression of the truth of suffering. Undefiled wisdom arises, exiting the wrappings and separating from the obstacles: the truth of the path is cultivated. Liberation is free and unhindered, forever departing from the source of suffering: the truth of cessation is realized. "Entering" means comprehending and awakening. *Nirvana* is translated as "perfect quiescence." The sutra says: "Transmigration is birth and death; non-movement is nirvana."

Now this chapter on nirvana is truly a profound treasury of the *Flower Ornament*. Therefore in the Jin-era translation, the chapter on the Nature-Arising of the Precious King was investigated deeply by Souxuan and Tanxuan (搜玄探玄). In the Tang-era translation, the chapter on the Appearance of the Tathagata was illuminated by the *Old Commentary* and the *New Record*, continuing the fragrance like successive flames. Moreover, in the kingdom of Goryeo (高麗), these texts are still fully preserved and their transmission has never been broken. Since all these works originated in China, I hope that all later descendants will seek teachers, study diligently, and together repay the weighty virtue of Yunhua (雲華), Xianshou (賢首), Qingliang (清涼), and Guifeng (圭峯).
