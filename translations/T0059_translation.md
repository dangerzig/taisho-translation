# 諸法本經
## 諸法本經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0059

Translated from the Chinese. 吳 支謙譯

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada in the kingdom of Śrāvastī. The Buddha addressed the bhikkhus: "Listen as I expound the root of all dharmas." They replied: "So be it."

The World-Honored One said: "If there are outsiders and practitioners of other teachings who come to ask: 'What is the root of dharmas?' you should answer: 'Desire is the root of all dharmas. What is their accumulation? Contact is their accumulation. What is their common destination? Feeling is their common destination. What brings them into existence? Mindfulness brings them into existence. What illumines the path? Contemplation illumines the path. What is foremost? Samādhi is foremost. What is supreme? Wisdom is supreme. What is unshakeable? Liberation is unshakeable. What is ultimate? Nirvāṇa is ultimate.'

"Thus, bhikkhus: desire is the root of all dharmas, contact is the accumulation of all dharmas, feeling is the common destination of all dharmas, mindfulness brings all dharmas into existence, contemplation illumines the path of all dharmas, samādhi is foremost among all dharmas, wisdom is supreme among all dharmas, liberation is the unshakeable ground of all dharmas, nirvāṇa is the ultimate end of all dharmas.

"Bhikkhus, you should train in this way: always cultivate the perception of having gone forth from household life, the perception of impermanence, the perception of impermanence as suffering, the perception of suffering as not-self, the perception of loathsomeness in food, the perception of impurity, the perception of death, the perception of the absence of joy in all the world, the perception of knowing the crooked and straight in the world, the perception of discerning what exists and does not exist in the world. The pleasures that the world habitually grasps, their passing away, and their final destination — all of these you should know through right view as they truly are. Bhikkhus, through this mindfulness, craving is severed, desire is abandoned, right wisdom is entered, and the end of suffering is reached."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, all rejoiced and undertook to practice it.
