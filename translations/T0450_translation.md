# 藥師琉璃光如來本願功德經
## Sūtra on the Merits of the Original Vow of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0450

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was traveling and teaching in various lands. He arrived at the city of Vaiśālī and stayed beneath a Music Tree. Together with a great assembly of eight thousand great bhikṣus, thirty-six thousand bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, and kings, great ministers, brahmins, householders, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, humans, non-humans, and an immeasurable great multitude, who reverently surrounded him as he expounded the Dharma.

At that time, Mañjuśrī, Prince of the Dharma, receiving the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, rose from his seat, bared one shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, and bowing toward the Bhagavān with palms joined, addressed him saying: "World-Honored One! We wish that you would expound the names of such Buddhas and the supreme merits of their original great vows, so that those who hear may have their karmic obstructions eliminated. This is for the benefit and joy of sentient beings during the age of the semblance Dharma."

At that time, the World-Honored One praised the youth Mañjuśrī, saying: "Excellent! Excellent! Mañjuśrī! With great compassion you have urged me to explain the names and original vow-merits of the Buddhas, in order to rescue sentient beings entangled in karmic obstructions, and to benefit and bring peace to sentient beings during the age of the semblance Dharma. Listen attentively now and reflect upon this well. I shall explain it for you." Mañjuśrī said: "Yes, please explain. We delight in listening."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "To the east of here, beyond buddha-lands as numerous as ten Ganges River sands, there is a world called Pure Lapis Lazuli. The Buddha there is called Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed Worthy, Tamer of Men, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, Bhagavān. Mañjuśrī! That Buddha, the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, when formerly practicing the bodhisattva path, made twelve great vows, causing all sentient beings to obtain whatever they seek.

"The first great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, my own body's radiance shall blaze forth, illuminating immeasurable, countless, boundless worlds. My body shall be adorned with the thirty-two marks of a great man and the eighty subsidiary characteristics, causing all sentient beings to become like me, without any difference.'

"The second great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, my body shall be like lapis lazuli, clear and luminous within and without, pure and without blemish. My radiance shall be vast and great, my merits towering. My body shall dwell in peace, adorned with a net of flames surpassing the sun and moon. Beings in darkness shall all receive illumination, and they may go wherever they wish and carry out all their activities.'

"The third great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, through immeasurable and boundless wisdom and skillful means, I shall cause all sentient beings to obtain inexhaustible supplies for their use, so that no being shall lack anything.'

"The fourth great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, if any sentient beings walk the wrong path, I shall cause them all to rest securely on the path of bodhi. If any walk the path of the śrāvakas or pratyekabuddhas, I shall establish them all in the Great Vehicle.'

"The fifth great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, if there are immeasurable and boundless sentient beings who practice the pure conduct within my Dharma, I shall cause them all to obtain the precepts without deficiency and to possess the three collections of pure precepts. Should any have transgressed, upon hearing my name they shall regain purity and not fall into evil destinies.'

"The sixth great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, if any sentient beings have inferior bodies with deficient faculties, are ugly, stupid, blind, deaf, mute, crippled, hunchbacked, leprous, insane, or afflicted with various kinds of illness and suffering, upon hearing my name they shall all become handsome, intelligent, and wise, their faculties complete, free from all illness and suffering.'

"The seventh great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, if any sentient beings are pressed by various illnesses, with no one to save them, no refuge, no physician, no medicine, no relatives, no family, and are impoverished with much suffering, once my name passes through their ears, all their illnesses shall be healed, their bodies and minds shall be at peace, their families and possessions shall all become abundant, and they shall eventually attain unsurpassed bodhi.'

"The eighth great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, if any women are oppressed by the hundred afflictions of womanhood and develop extreme aversion and wish to abandon the female body, upon hearing my name, they shall all be able to transform from female to male, possessing the marks of a man, and shall eventually attain unsurpassed bodhi.'

"The ninth great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, I shall cause all sentient beings to escape from Māra's snare and to be freed from all bonds of heterodox paths. Should they fall into the dense forest of various evil views, I shall draw them all forth and place them in right views, gradually causing them to cultivate the practices of bodhisattvas and swiftly realize unsurpassed, perfect bodhi.'

"The tenth great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, if any sentient beings are convicted by the king's law, bound, whipped, imprisoned in jail, or about to be executed, or suffer countless other calamities, insults, grief, and anguish, tormented in body and mind, upon hearing my name, through the power of my merit and awesome spiritual force, they shall all be freed from every suffering and sorrow.'

"The eleventh great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, if any sentient beings are tormented by hunger and thirst and create various evil karma in search of food, should they hear my name and single-mindedly recite and uphold it, I shall first satisfy their bodies with the finest food and drink, and afterward establish them in ultimate peace and joy through the flavor of the Dharma.'

"The twelfth great vow: 'I vow that in a future life, when I attain bodhi, if any sentient beings are impoverished and without clothing, tormented day and night by mosquitoes, flies, cold, and heat, should they hear my name and single-mindedly recite and uphold it, they shall immediately obtain all manner of fine garments according to their wishes, as well as all kinds of precious adornments, flower garlands, perfumes, music, and entertainments, all fully satisfying their hearts' desires.'

"Mañjuśrī! These are the twelve subtle and supreme vows made by that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, when he was practicing the bodhisattva path.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! The great vows made by that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, when practicing the bodhisattva path, and the meritorious adornments of that buddha-land: even if I were to speak for an entire kalpa or more, I could not exhaust them. However, that buddha-land is entirely pure. There are no women there, nor any evil destinies, nor the sound of suffering. The ground is made of lapis lazuli, the paths are bordered with golden cords, and the cities, towers, palaces, pavilions, balconies, windows, and lattices are all made of the seven treasures. It is just like the Western Land of Ultimate Bliss, with meritorious adornments equal and without difference. In that land there are two bodhisattva-mahāsattvas: one named Sūryaprabha, the second named Candraprabha. They are the foremost among that immeasurable, countless assembly of bodhisattvas, and are fully able to uphold the Treasury of the True Dharma of that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata. Therefore, Mañjuśrī! All faithful good men and good women should vow to be reborn in that buddha-land."

At that time, the World-Honored One again told the youth Mañjuśrī: "Mañjuśrī! There are sentient beings who cannot distinguish good from evil. They harbor only greed and stinginess, knowing nothing of giving or the fruits of giving. Foolish and lacking wisdom, they are deficient in the root of faith. They amass great wealth and diligently guard it. When they see a beggar approach, their hearts feel no joy. When circumstances force them to give, it is like cutting their own flesh, and they feel deep pain and regret. Furthermore, there are immeasurable stingy sentient beings who accumulate possessions but will not use them even for themselves, let alone share with parents, wives, children, servants, or beggars. When such sentient beings die, they are reborn in the realm of hungry ghosts or among animals. Because in their former human lives they once briefly heard the name of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, now in the evil destinies they briefly recall that Tathāgata's name. At the very moment of recollection, they vanish from that place and are reborn among humans. They remember their past lives and, fearing the suffering of the evil destinies, take no delight in sensory pleasures. They gladly practice generosity and praise those who give. Everything they possess, they share without attachment. Gradually they become able to give even their heads, eyes, hands, feet, blood, flesh, and body parts to those who ask, not to mention other material goods.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If any sentient beings, though having received the precepts from the Tathāgata, break the moral code; or though not breaking the moral code, break the rules of conduct; or though maintaining the moral code and rules of conduct intact, they destroy right views; or though not destroying right views, they abandon extensive learning and cannot understand the deep meaning of the sūtras spoken by the Buddha; or though extensively learned, they develop arrogant conceit, and because of this arrogant conceit their minds become obscured, so they affirm themselves and disparage others, criticize and slander the true Dharma, and become companions of Māra. Such foolish people practice wrong views themselves and also cause immeasurable koṭīs of sentient beings to fall into the great pit of danger. These sentient beings should flow endlessly through the hells, animal realms, and hungry ghost realms. But if they hear the name of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, they will abandon their evil conduct, cultivate wholesome dharmas, and not fall into evil destinies. Should any be unable to abandon their evil conduct and cultivate wholesome dharmas and thus fall into evil destinies, through the power of that Tathāgata's original vows, they will hear his name in their present state. When their lives there end, they will be reborn in the human realm. They will gain right views and energetic effort, well-regulated intentions, and will be able to renounce the household life and enter the homeless life within the Tathāgata's Dharma. They will uphold the precepts without transgression, possess right views and extensive learning, comprehend the profound meaning, be free from arrogant conceit, not slander the true Dharma, not become companions of Māra, and gradually cultivate the practices of bodhisattvas until they swiftly attain perfection.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If any sentient beings are stingy, jealous, praise themselves, and disparage others, they shall fall into the three evil destinies for immeasurable thousands of years to endure intense suffering. After enduring this intense suffering and dying, they will be reborn in the human world as oxen, horses, camels, or donkeys, constantly beaten and whipped, tormented by hunger and thirst, and always carrying heavy loads along the roads. Or they may be reborn as humans of the lowest station, becoming slaves and servants of others, driven and commanded, never free. If in their former human life they once heard the name of the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, through this good cause, now recalling it again, they wholeheartedly take refuge. Through the Buddha's spiritual power, they are freed from all suffering. Their faculties become sharp, and they gain wisdom and extensive learning. They constantly seek the supreme Dharma, always encounter good friends, forever sever Māra's snares, break the shell of ignorance, drain the river of afflictions, and gain liberation from all birth, old age, illness, death, grief, and suffering.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If any sentient beings delight in discord, quarreling, and litigation, troubling both themselves and others, and through body, speech, and mind create and increase various evil karma, constantly engaging in acts that harm one another, mutually plotting injury; if they invoke the spirits of mountains, forests, trees, and graves, kill living beings, take their blood and flesh to offer to yakṣas and rākṣasas; if they write their enemies' names, fashion their images, and use evil spells and curses against them; if they employ sorcery, poisonous insects, or raise corpse-ghosts to end another's life or destroy their body: should such sentient beings hear the name of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, all these evil things will be unable to cause them harm. All will turn toward one another with minds of loving-kindness, bringing benefit and peace, without any thought of injury or resentment. Each will feel joyful, content with what they have, not encroaching on one another, but mutually benefiting each other.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If among the four assemblies of bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās, or among other faithful good men and good women, there are those who are able to observe the eight precept fast for one year, or for three months, upholding the precepts, and who with these good roots vow to be reborn in the Western Land of Ultimate Bliss with the Buddha Amitāyus to hear the true Dharma, but are not yet certain of this: if they hear the name of the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, then at the time of death, eight bodhisattvas will come riding on spiritual powers to show them the path, and they will be spontaneously born amid various multicolored jeweled flowers in that land. Or some, on account of this merit, will be reborn in the heavens. Though reborn in the heavens, their original good roots will not yet be exhausted, and they will not again be reborn in the evil destinies. When their heavenly lifespan is finished, they will return to the human world, perhaps as wheel-turning kings ruling over the four continents, with majestic power and freedom, establishing immeasurable hundreds of thousands of sentient beings on the path of the ten wholesome actions. Or they will be born into families of kṣatriyas, brahmins, or wealthy householders, with abundant riches, full storehouses, handsome appearance, complete families, keen intelligence, bravery, and the strength of great warriors. If a woman hears the name of the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Tathāgata, and wholeheartedly receives and upholds it, she will never again receive a female body."

At that time, the youth Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha, saying: "World-Honored One! I hereby vow that during the age of the semblance Dharma, through various skillful means, I shall cause all faithful good men and good women to hear the name of the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, even awakening their ears with the Buddha's name while they sleep. World-Honored One! If there are those who receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra, or explain and illuminate it for others; whether they copy it themselves or have others copy it; who revere and honor it, making offerings with various flowers, incense, paste-incense, powdered incense, burning incense, flower garlands, necklaces, banners, canopies, and music; who place it in a bag of five-colored silk, sweep and purify a clean space, and set up a high seat to install it: at that time, the Four Great Heavenly Kings with their retinues, and countless hundreds of thousands of other celestial beings, will all come to that place to make offerings and protect it. World-Honored One! Wherever this sūtra-treasure circulates, if there are those who can uphold it, then through the original vow-merits of that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, and through hearing his name, one should know that in that place there will be no more untimely deaths. Nor will evil ghosts and spirits seize people's vital energy. And if it has already been seized, it will be restored as before, and body and mind will be at peace."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "So it is! So it is! Just as you have said. Mañjuśrī! If there are faithful good men and good women who wish to make offerings to that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, they should first fashion an image of that Buddha, prepare a pure seat, and install it there. They should scatter various flowers, burn various incenses, and adorn the place with various banners and streamers. For seven days and seven nights, they should observe the eight precept fast, eat pure food, bathe in fragrant water, and wear new, clean garments. They should generate minds free from defilement and turbidity, free from anger and harm, and toward all sentient beings give rise to thoughts of benefit, peace, loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. With music and song they should praise and circumambulate the Buddha's image to the right. They should also recall that Tathāgata's original vow-merits, read and recite this sūtra, contemplate its meaning, and explain and illuminate it for others. Then whatever they wish shall all be fulfilled: those seeking long life shall obtain long life, those seeking wealth shall obtain wealth, those seeking official position shall obtain official position, those seeking sons or daughters shall obtain sons or daughters. If someone has a frightening nightmare, sees evil omens, or strange birds gather, or a hundred strange phenomena appear in their dwelling: if this person reverently makes offerings to that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, with various wonderful requisites, then the nightmares, evil omens, and all inauspiciousness will all vanish and be unable to cause harm. Or if there is danger from water, fire, weapons, poison, precipices, wild elephants, lions, tigers, wolves, bears, venomous snakes, scorpions, centipedes, millipedes, or mosquitoes and flies: if one can wholeheartedly recall that Buddha and reverently make offerings, one shall be freed from all terrors. If another country invades, or bandits rebel: those who recall and revere that Tathāgata shall also all be freed.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If there are faithful good men and good women who, for the rest of their lives, serve no other devas but single-mindedly take refuge in the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṅgha, and who uphold the prohibitory precepts, whether the five precepts, the ten precepts, the four hundred bodhisattva precepts, the two hundred and fifty bhikṣu precepts, or the five hundred bhikṣuṇī precepts, and who among these have transgressed some and fear falling into the evil destinies: if they can single-mindedly recite that Buddha's name and reverently make offerings, they shall certainly not be reborn in the three evil destinies. Or if a woman, at the time of giving birth, endures extreme suffering: if she can wholeheartedly invoke that Tathāgata's name, praise him, and reverently make offerings, all her suffering shall be removed. The child that is born will have a complete body, a handsome appearance that delights all who see it, sharp faculties, keen intelligence, peace, and few illnesses. No non-human being will seize the child's vital energy."

At that time, the World-Honored One told Ānanda: "The merits of that Buddha, the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, that I have praised: these belong to the extremely profound domain of the Buddhas, difficult to comprehend. Do you believe this or not?" Ānanda replied: "Great Virtuous World-Honored One! I harbor no doubt regarding the sūtras spoken by the Tathāgata. Why is this? Because all the karma of body, speech, and mind of all Tathāgatas is entirely pure. World-Honored One! The sun and moon might fall from the sky, and Mount Sumeru, king of mountains, might be toppled, but the words of all Buddhas are never otherwise. World-Honored One! There are sentient beings whose root of faith is incomplete. Hearing about the extremely profound domain of the Buddhas, they think: 'How can merely reciting the name of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, a single Buddha, bring such great merit and benefit?' Because of this disbelief, they give rise to slander. Throughout the long night of ignorance, they lose great benefit and joy, and fall into the evil destinies, flowing endlessly without return." The Buddha told Ānanda: "If such sentient beings hear the name of the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, and wholeheartedly receive and uphold it without generating doubt, it is impossible that they should fall into the evil destinies. Ānanda! This is the extremely profound domain of the Buddhas, difficult to believe and understand. That you are now able to accept it, you should know, is entirely due to the Tathāgata's awesome power. Ānanda! All śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and bodhisattvas who have not yet reached the bhūmis are all unable to truly believe and understand this. Only bodhisattvas bound to one more birth can do so. Ānanda! A human body is difficult to obtain. Faith in and reverence for the Three Treasures is also difficult to achieve. Hearing the name of the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, is even more difficult than this. Ānanda! That Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata's immeasurable bodhisattva practices, immeasurable skillful means, and immeasurable vast vows: even if I were to speak for a kalpa or more, the kalpa might quickly end, but that Buddha's practices, vows, and skillful means would have no end."

At that time, among the assembly there was a bodhisattva-mahāsattva named Trāṇamukta. He rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, and bowing with palms joined, addressed the Buddha: "Great Virtuous World-Honored One! During the age of the semblance Dharma, there will be sentient beings afflicted by various illnesses, long sick and emaciated, unable to eat or drink, their throats and lips parched. They see darkness on all sides as the signs of death appear before them. Parents, relatives, friends, and acquaintances weep and surround them. Yet the sick person himself lies in his place and sees the messengers of King Yama leading his consciousness to the court of King Yama, the Lord of the Law. Now, all sentient beings have an innate spirit that accompanies them, recording all their deeds, whether sinful or meritorious, completely writing them down and presenting them all to King Yama, the Lord of the Law. At that time, the king examines and questions the person, calculates what they have done, and passes judgment according to their sins and merits. At that time, if the sick person's relatives and acquaintances are able to take refuge on their behalf in the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, and invite the saṅgha to recite this sūtra, light seven-tiered lamps, and hang five-colored life-extending spirit banners, then it is possible that the consciousness may return. It is as if in a dream one sees clearly. Or after seven days, or twenty-one days, or thirty-five days, or forty-nine days, when the consciousness returns, it is as if awakening from a dream, and one remembers and understands the karmic fruits of wholesome and unwholesome deeds. Having personally witnessed the karmic results, even at the cost of one's life, one will never again create evil karma. Therefore, faithful good men and good women should all receive and uphold the name of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, and to the extent of their ability, reverently make offerings."

At that time, Ānanda asked the Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta: "Good man! How should one reverently make offerings to that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata? And how should the life-extending banners and lamps be made?" The Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta said: "Great Virtuous One! If there is a sick person who wishes to be freed from illness and suffering, on their behalf one should for seven days and seven nights observe the eight precept fast, and with food and other requisites, to the extent of one's ability, make offerings to the bhikṣu saṅgha. Six times day and night, one should worship and make offerings to that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata. One should read and recite this sūtra forty-nine times, light forty-nine lamps, and fashion seven images of that Tathāgata. Before each image, place seven lamps, each lamp as large as a cart wheel, and for up to forty-nine days the light must not be extinguished. Fashion five-colored silk banners, forty-nine hand-spans in length. One should release various kinds of living creatures, up to forty-nine in number. Then one can pass through the dangerous crisis and not be seized by the various evil spirits.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! When a kṣatriya anointed king and others face disasters, such as epidemics among the people, invasion by foreign countries, rebellion within their own realm, inauspicious changes in the constellations, eclipses of the sun or moon, unseasonable storms, or drought past the proper season, that kṣatriya anointed king and others should at that time give rise to a mind of compassion toward all sentient beings, pardon all prisoners, and following the method of offerings described above, make offerings to that World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata. Through these good roots and the power of that Tathāgata's original vows, the kingdom shall at once be made peaceful: winds and rains shall come in their proper season, crops shall ripen, all sentient beings shall be free from illness and joyful, and in that land there shall be no fierce yakṣas or other spirits troubling sentient beings. All evil omens shall vanish at once. The kṣatriya anointed king and others shall enjoy increased longevity, vigor, freedom from illness, and ease. Ānanda! If the empress, consorts, crown prince, princes, great ministers, counselors, palace women, officials, or common people are afflicted by illness or other calamities, they too should fashion five-colored spirit banners, light lamps to burn continuously, release living creatures, scatter various flowers, and burn renowned incenses. Their illnesses will be cured and their various difficulties resolved."

At that time, Ānanda asked the Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta: "Good man! How can a life already at its end be extended?" The Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta said: "Great Virtuous One! Have you not heard the Tathāgata speak of nine kinds of untimely death? Therefore I urge the fashioning of life-extending banners and lamps and the cultivation of meritorious deeds. Through cultivating such merit, one may live out one's full lifespan without experiencing suffering." Ānanda asked: "What are the nine kinds of untimely death?" The Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta said: "If sentient beings, though their illness is slight, have no physician or caretaker, or if they encounter a physician who gives them the wrong medicine, they die an untimely death when in truth they should not have died. Furthermore, those who trust in the practitioners of worldly heretical paths, sorcerers, and diviners, who falsely speak of fortune and misfortune, become frightened and agitated. Their minds lose their composure, and they resort to divination to discover the source of misfortune, killing various living beings to propitiate the spirits, calling upon all manner of ghosts and specters, praying and begging for blessings, hoping to extend their years. In the end they cannot obtain this. Foolish, confused, and deluded, trusting in perverse and inverted views, they die untimely deaths and enter hell with no time of release. This is called the first untimely death. Second, being executed by the king's law. Third, through hunting, gaming, indulgence in lust and alcohol, and dissolute excess without restraint, having one's vital energy seized by non-human beings. Fourth, being burned by fire. Fifth, being drowned in water. Sixth, being devoured by various wild beasts. Seventh, falling from mountain cliffs. Eighth, being harmed by poisons, sorcery, spells, or corpse-ghosts. Ninth, dying from hunger and thirst, unable to obtain food or drink. These are the nine kinds of untimely death as briefly explained by the Tathāgata. Beyond these, there are immeasurable other kinds of untimely death, difficult to describe in full.

"Furthermore, Ānanda! King Yama presides over the register of names in the world. If any sentient beings are unfilial, commit the five heinous acts, insult and damage the Three Treasures, violate the laws between ruler and minister, or destroy trust and the precepts, then King Yama, the Lord of the Law, examines and punishes them according to the gravity of their offenses. Therefore I now urge all sentient beings to light lamps, fashion banners, release living creatures, and cultivate merit, so that they may pass through suffering and calamity and not encounter any of these disasters."

At that time, among the assembly there were twelve yakṣa generals, all seated together. They were called:

General Kumbhīra, General Vajra,
General Mihira, General Andīra,
General Anila, General Śaṇḍila,
General Indra, General Pajra,
General Makura, General Kiṃdala,
General Catura, General Vikarāla.

These twelve yakṣa generals each had seven thousand yakṣas as their retinue. Together they raised their voices and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! We today, having received the Buddha's awesome power and heard the name of the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, no longer have any fear of the evil destinies. We are united, all of one mind, and for the rest of our lives take refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Saṅgha. We vow to bear responsibility for all sentient beings, to act for their benefit and bring them peace and joy. In whatever village, city, country, or district, or in any quiet forest, wherever this sūtra circulates, or wherever anyone receives and upholds the name of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata and reverently makes offerings, we and our retinues shall guard and protect that person, freeing them from all suffering and difficulty, and fulfilling all their wishes. If any are afflicted by illness and seek relief, they too should read and recite this sūtra, and with five-colored thread tie knots using our names. When their wish is fulfilled, they may untie the knots."

At that time, the World-Honored One praised the yakṣa generals, saying: "Excellent! Excellent! Great yakṣa generals! You who wish to repay the kindness of the World-Honored One, the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, should always in this way benefit and bring peace to all sentient beings."

At that time, Ānanda addressed the Buddha, saying: "World-Honored One! What should this Dharma-gate be called? How should we receive and uphold it?" The Buddha told Ānanda: "This Dharma-gate is called 'The Exposition of the Original Vow and Merits of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata.' It is also called 'The Exposition of the Twelve Spirit Generals' Vow for Benefiting Sentient Beings and the Sacred Dhāraṇī.' It is also called 'The Removal of All Karmic Obstructions.' It should be upheld in this way." When the Bhagavān had finished speaking these words, all the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, the great śrāvakas, the kings, great ministers, brahmins, householders, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans, non-humans, and the entire great assembly, hearing what the Buddha had said, all rejoiced greatly, believed, accepted, and put it into practice.
