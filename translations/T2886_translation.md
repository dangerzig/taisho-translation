# Dhūta Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha for Mind-King Bodhisattva, Upper Fascicle

## *Fó Wèi Xīnwáng Púsà Shuō Tóutuó Jīng Juàn Shàng* (佛為心王菩薩說投陀經卷上)

Taishō 2886

With commentary by Chan Master Huìbiàn (*Huìbiàn*, 惠辨) of Wǔyīn Mountain Shìshì Monastery (*Wǔyīn Shān Shìshì Sì*, 五陰山室寺). An apocryphal text.

*Note: This apocryphal sūtra presents the Buddha teaching "Mind-King Bodhisattva" about the dhūta (ascetic) practice, here creatively interpreted as "casting off" (tóutuó) afflictions. The text blends Tathāgatagarbha and Huayan thought with Chan meditation themes. The "Mind-King" is explicitly glossed as a personification of the mind as ruler of the body. The text was likely composed in China and reflects the syncretic doctrinal environment of popular Buddhism.*

### Preface

As for the ultimate Way: it is perfectly bright, empty, and quiescent at its extreme. Compassion universally embraces and saves beings from the depths of the river of birth and death, floating the great precious boat to broadly ferry the four modes of birth across their difficulties. The grace reaches to the great Way, which sweeps clean the prison of the three realms from afar. The mirror of wisdom hangs forever, illuminating the vast night of ignorance. The dharma-king, with compassion and pity, guides and leads in many ways. The illnesses of manifold suffering are without limit, and the methods of administering medicine are not just one. Therefore the great bodhisattva shed his voice into the five turbidities, out of compassion for all beings, and joyfully requested the Tathāgata to establish the teaching and serve as guide.

As for the title, "Dhūta Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha for Mind-King Bodhisattva": because the king-mind is the ruler of the body, with the eyes and ears dwelling externally, it maintains the six sense-faculties, does not destroy the seeds of good and evil, and allows them to come and go without limit or obstruction, freely like a king. "Mind-King Bodhisattva" is another name for liberation, an alternative designation for attaining the Way. Because awakening accords with the capacity, it is called "spoken by the Buddha." As for "dhūta" (*tóutuó*, 投陀): it is a word from the western lands, translated into Chinese as "pepper" (i.e., pungent and purifying). Afflictions do not arise; it is quiescent and without characteristics. Therefore it is called "dhūta." "Sūtra" (*jīng*, 經) means "constant." If a person can awaken to the Way, then seeking to overcome death, this is called "constant."

### The Sūtra

At that time the Buddha was on the Great Forest Mountain (*Dàlínsǒu Shān*, 大林藪山). The mountain's dimensions, length and breadth, were eighty-four thousand *yojanas*. On the four sides of the mountain were immeasurable yakṣas, rākṣasas, tigers, wolves, lions, venomous snakes, and evil ghosts, killing and harming sentient beings and consuming their vital essence. On the four corners of the mountain were immeasurable worthy and good great bodhisattvas, studying the Great Vehicle, with resolute faith in the buddha-dharma, upholding the prohibitive precepts like a pure bright jewel, guarding the citadel of the mind, and subduing the external-path practitioners.

The five peak immortals (*wǔfēng shénxiān*, 五峯神仙) dwelt at the summit of the mountain. The immortals were proud, indulging freely in the five desires, not awakening to impermanence.

The Buddha, together with Kāśyapa (*Jiāyè*, 迦葉) and all the great bodhisattvas, filling the sky, was surrounded before and behind by the fourfold assembly of disciples, all gathered in one assembly. At that time the World-Honored One, amid the great assembly, sat upon a jeweled lotus flower in the cross-legged posture, serenely smiling, his majestic radiance universally illuminating all.

The Buddha addressed Mind-King Bodhisattva: "You should, for the sake of the great assembly, expound the extremely profound and unsurpassed wondrous dharma, causing all sentient beings to recognize that the nature of afflictions is empty and without any real existence, causing them to practice the one-mind dharma of dhūta, where suffering and bliss have one single characteristic."

Mind-King Bodhisattva, receiving the Buddha's divine power, rose into the sky and manifested transformations freely. Amid the great assembly he released the light of great wisdom. The light was like a hundred thousand million billion suns and moons, cool and temperate, according to the wishes of all sentient beings granting all they sought, universally illuminating the ten directions and gathering those with karmic connections.

All the great assemblies of devas, nāgas, the eight classes of beings, dharma-protecting good spirits, and heavenly kings, from the summit of existence above to the limit of space below, sentient beings of the six destinies of birth and death, receiving the light, rejoiced. The great assemblies gathered like clouds.

The bodhisattva-mahāsattva, perfumed by great compassion, further released six hundred million of the most supreme rays of light. Within those rays of light he proclaimed that the original nature of all sentient beings is pure, without birth and without extinction, without defilement and without purity. The boundary of birth-and-death and the boundary of nirvāṇa are equal: both are equally empty of all dharmas. Therefore, dwelling in quiet seclusion is itself dhūta. Quiescently contemplating the Way, severing and cutting off all grasping, not giving rise to discrimination. Why? Because birth-and-death and nirvāṇa are neither one nor two. Both are provisionally designated names.

Speaking falsely to lead the foolish: beings of the highest capacity penetrate the characteristics of dharmas. They do not enter nirvāṇa; they do not leave birth-and-death. Beings of the middle capacity seek nirvāṇa but, lacking skillful wisdom, make the true limit their home. Beings of the lowest capacity dwell in ignorance and delusion, without enlightenment and without knowledge, sinking and submerging in the sea of suffering.

"All you bodhisattvas, you should clearly understand: from where does this light come? Seeking inwardly and outwardly, it has no place of arising. It arises from the transformation of the mind, clear and constantly one."

The single ray of light touches the bodies of sentient beings and enters through the pores. When the light touches foolish beings, they enter immeasurable samādhis. When the light touches the eyes, they enter the wondrous-form samādhi. When the light touches the ears, they enter the sound samādhi. When the light touches the nose, they enter the fragrant-forest samādhi. When the light touches the tongue, they enter the dharma-joy samādhi. When the light touches the body, they enter the wisdom-brightness samādhi. When the light touches the consciousness, they enter the dharma-brightness samādhi. When the light pervades the ten directions, the immortals receive the light and their pride is instantly removed. Their body and mind become pure and they awaken to the patience of the unborn. The evil ghosts, venomous snakes, yakṣas, and rākṣasas who consumed people's vital essence all give rise to the bodhicitta. The bull-headed beings whose two views were in conflict and contention had their minds of strife pacified. The Māra King Pāpīyas (*Bōxún Mówáng*, 波旬魔王) opened the gates of hell and dispensed cool, pure water. The hungry beings were naturally satiated. They gave rise to the pure mind, feasted on the food of dharma-joy, and their bodies and minds were liberated. They entered deeply into the dharma-nature, free and unconditioned.

Although they attained the unconditioned, they examined their minds so as not to abide there. Studying skillful means, the dharma became their wife, and through transformation a single son was born. Dwelling in the deep palace, they contemplated with right mindfulness, studying great skillful means: not abandoning the conditioned, not abiding in the unconditioned. Both wisdoms and skillful means were fully complete in each thought-moment.

Though the son was young, he always delighted in leaving home, studying the unsurpassed Way, taking no pleasure in worldly affairs. He always cultivated the practice of few desires and contentment, practicing the six harmonies. The eye harmonizes with form. Why? Because the mind does not grasp at form, and deluded consciousness does not arise. This is the meaning of harmony. The same applies to the rest.

In movement and stillness, one is always one: guarding the citadel of the mind, not allowing thieves to enter. Someone said to the prince: "In the world there is supreme bliss. Indulge yourself and nurture the royal body." Having received these pleasures, heavenly music and all manner of fine delicacies naturally arrived. Celestial women of the five desires skillfully turned the five minds, and whatever was sought was obtained.

The prince, hearing this, seemed both grieved and glad: "Since time without beginning, I have been caught in the net of craving. Craving has netted my body and mind; I cannot free myself. How could I save others? Now for what reason do you again speak such words to deceive and delude me?"
