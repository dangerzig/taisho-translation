# 分別功德論
## Treatise on the Discrimination of Merits

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1507

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown (失譯)

---

# Fascicle One

The opening verse speaks of Kāśyapa reflecting on the fundamental dharma. He considered that the words and teachings of the sūtra-dharma were exceedingly numerous. How do we know this? Kāśyapa used comparisons to gauge the magnitude. His method of comparison began with ten donkeys: the strength of ten donkeys does not equal that of one ordinary camel; ten ordinary camels do not equal one ordinary elephant; ten ordinary elephants do not equal one thin-footed elephant; ten thin-footed elephants do not equal one food-stealing elephant; ten food-stealing elephants do not equal one lotus elephant; ten lotus elephants do not equal one blue-lotus elephant; ten blue-lotus elephants do not equal one red-lotus elephant; ten red-lotus elephants do not equal one white-lotus elephant; ten white-lotus elephants do not equal one Himalayan elephant; ten Himalayan elephants do not equal one fragrant elephant. From donkey to fragrant elephant is one measure. Now, eighty-four thousand such fragrant elephants, if their hides were written on inside and out with sūtra text and they were loaded to full capacity, would roughly convey the total volume of what Ānanda had heard and known. One can roughly estimate the grand total, but to elaborate every word and character would take an entire lifetime without completing it.

Reflecting that the sūtra-dharma was vast and immense, Kāśyapa wondered how to ensure it would circulate throughout the world so that beings for a thousand generations could receive the moisture of the dharma. In deep contemplation of the ultimate principle, he asked who could compile the dharma. Only Ānanda could gather it all. Kāśyapa then sounded the *gandhāra* bell to assemble the community.

At that time, eighty-four thousand arhats answered the summons and assembled. All of these were undefiled beings who had attained both liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom. The reason these noble saints were summoned was that all of them could enter the *nirodha-samāpatti* (cessation-attainment). Those who enter cessation-attainment can cause beings in the present life to obtain blessings and be saved from suffering.

Those in the great thousand-world system who are without attachment are beyond counting; excluding those on the three paths, each category is doubled. But here only those of sharp faculties who have attained joint liberation, who can use cessation-attainment to liberate sentient beings, are recorded. Therefore they are called "fields of merit." How do we know this? Long ago, Indra's merit was exhausted and his life was about to end. The five signs of decay appeared, and his heart was filled with fear. He wished to seek rescue and protection. He was about to go to the Buddha to seek help, but he reflected that the Buddha's grace was broad and gentle, and he feared the Buddha could not resolve his urgent crisis. He considered Śāriputra and Maudgalyāyana, but feared they too could not save his life in time. Only Mahākāśyapa, through the power of cessation-attainment, could provide immediate rescue from danger. So he went to Kāśyapa. At that time, Kāśyapa was about to visit a poor household to bestow merit. Indra carefully considered: if he appeared in his divine form, Kāśyapa might refuse his offering. So along the road he manifested a grass hut and appeared inside as a frail, sick man. Kāśyapa came to beg food from the sick man, who extended his hand and offered food. When Kāśyapa received it in his bowl, it transformed into ambrosia. Indra then revealed his divine form in the sky. Kāśyapa asked: "Why did you deceive me with false words?" Indra replied: "I did not speak falsely. I offered with complete sincerity. I am Indra, Lord of the Gods. The five signs of decay have come, and my life is about to end. Therefore I came to make this request: please save my life." Kāśyapa silently consented. Indra went to the Buddha to hear the dharma. In a moment he fell asleep, and in that sleep he died and was reborn. The Buddha told Indra: "Just now you died. Now you have been reborn and will not die again." He returned to his original form. This was accomplished through the power of Kāśyapa's cessation-attainment.

The reason Kāśyapa excelled in cessation-attainment is that Kāśyapa was originally a *pratyekabuddha*. The dharma of a pratyekabuddha is not to teach through verbal instruction but solely through supernatural powers, samādhi, and miraculous transformation. Although Mahākāśyapa attained arhatship, his original disposition remained.

The verse says "four assemblies," but this is an abbreviation. In principle, there should be four assemblies, but in addition there are eight further groups. Among humans: kṣatriyas, brahmins, elders, and śramaṇas. Among devas: the Four Heavenly Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa gods, Māra, and Brahmā. These make eight groups. In total there are twelve assemblies. To say "four assemblies" is simply to state the essential number in broad terms.

Regarding "all dharmas are exceedingly profound," this refers to the twelve links of dependent origination. The Buddha told Ānanda that the twelve links were exceedingly profound and subtle. Ānanda asked: "What is so profound about this chain of causes?" The Buddha replied: "Do not say it is not profound. In a past life you also said it was not profound."

Long ago there was an Asura king whose body was eight thousand *yojanas* tall, with upper and lower lips a thousand *yojanas* apart. The king had a small child whom he always loved and held on his knee. The ocean was 3,360,000 *li* deep, and when the Asura stood in it, the water came precisely to his navel. The child, seeing his father standing in the ocean, thought the water was shallow and wanted to go in and play. The father said: "You must not. The ocean is deep and will swallow you." But the child insisted, so the father released him. He sank to the ocean floor, terrified and gasping. The father reached in his hand and pulled him back out of the water, saying: "I told you not to, but you would not believe me. How was it?" The Buddha said: "At that time, the king was I myself. The child was you, Ānanda. In the past you did not believe in the depth, and now you still do not. You cannot even comprehend how ignorance conditions formations; how much less can you comprehend the thirty-seven factors of awakening?"

**The Four Inconceivables:** The Tathāgata spoke of four things that are inconceivable: (1) sentient beings, (2) the world, (3) dragons, and (4) the Buddha.

*The world is inconceivable:* Long ago, Pūrṇa was debating with a brahmin. The brahmin said: "I once went to a pond to meditate and saw four kinds of armies march into a lotus pore. I was startled and could not understand whether my eyes were playing tricks or whether it was real. I told people about it, but no one believed me." He then went to the Buddha and described what he had seen. The Buddha said: "This was a real event, not a hallucination. The Asuras had raised four kinds of armies to battle the gods. When the Asuras were defeated, they retreated and hid inside the lotus pore." This is beyond the reach of ordinary thought, and therefore it is said that the world is inconceivable. Some say the world was created by Brahmā, others say it was created by the gods of the six heavens. Brahmins further ask: "Who created Brahmā?" Some say Brahmā had a father; some say he created himself. Those who say he had a father say the father was a lotus. They ask where the lotus came from, and say it came from the navel of Udāyana. Where did Udāyana come from? From King Sañcaya. Of what lineage was King Sañcaya? Of the kṣatriya lineage. Yet they claim Brahmā is of the brahmin lineage; now they say he originated from a kṣatriya. What kind of reasoning is this? Furthermore, at the time of the cosmic conflagration, one can roughly make distinctions. How so? When the conflagration occurs, from the surface of the earth upward to the fifteenth heaven, everything is scorched clean. This much seems knowable. But from the sixteenth heaven upward through the thirty-three there are still heavens remaining. Although this world-system is destroyed, other world-systems persist. By this reasoning, one ultimately cannot know. This is why the world is inconceivable.

*Sentient beings are inconceivable:* Some say that after the cosmic conflagration, water fills the place of fire, and the wind called *sūkṣma* blows to form palaces. Below, there is "earth-fat." The gods of the Ābhāsvara heaven descend to play and gradually taste the earth-fat. Their bodies become heavy and they cannot return. Those who eat much are transformed into women. They gradually decline from earth-fat to flat cakes to rice, losing their supernatural powers and radiance, reverting to human form. Through good conduct they are reborn in heaven; through evil conduct, in the three lower realms. They transmigrate through the five destinies with no fixed pattern. Even if one tried to exhaustively trace the origin and history of a single person, one could not fully know it, let alone all sentient beings. This is why sentient beings are inconceivable.

*Dragons are inconceivable:* All clouds and rain arise from dragons. Rain comes from the dragons' eyes, ears, nose, and mouth. Does it come from the body? Or from the mind? Dwelling near Mount Sumeru, there are five kinds of gods who can also bring rain. How does one distinguish dragon-rain from heavenly rain? Heavenly rain descends as a fine mist; coarser rain is dragon-rain. What are the five kinds of gods? The first is the Bent-Leg Gods, the second is the Summit Gods, the third is the Heedless Gods, the fourth is the Powerful Gods, and the fifth is the Four Heavenly Kings. When the Asuras raise armies and attack the heavens, they first battle the Bent-Leg Gods, and if victorious, proceed upward to the Summit Gods, then to the Heedless Gods, then to the Four Heavenly Kings, and finally to the Trāyastriṃśa Gods. The four lower heavens use rain to repel the enemy, having no other weapons. There are two kinds of rain: joyful rain and angry rain. Harmonious and moderate rainfall is joyful; thunder, hail, and lightning are angry. The Asuras also bring rain, the gods also bring rain, and the dragons also bring rain. Since each can cause rain, the matter cannot be definitively settled. Therefore it is said that dragon-rain is inconceivable.

*The Buddha is inconceivable:* Long ago, the Buddha was in his quiet chamber. Brahmā-gods numbering as the sands of the Ganges came to the Buddha's abode, wishing to know what samādhi the Buddha was in, yet none of them could determine what concentration he was in. Supernatural transformations and secret matters such as these are beyond what the two vehicles can conceive of; how much less so for ordinary beings.

Ānanda deferred to Kāśyapa, saying: "You are the senior elder, fit to expound the dharma for the assembly. The reason is that, as a senior of long standing, you are widely learned and well known to the World-Honored One. For the sake of sentient beings in the future, so that the true dharma may long endure in the world, the Tathāgata shared half his seat in summoning you. You, Venerable One, are the senior elder of the Sangha, and your wisdom is vast and comprehensive. We humbly beg your compassion in proclaiming the dharma-treasure." Teachers of foreign lands say that the reason Kāśyapa did not teach the dharma was that among the four kinds of analytical knowledge, he lacked eloquent speech. They further say that since he was originally a pratyekabuddha, he relied solely on supernatural displays and transformations and had never expounded the dharma verbally. Kāśyapa declined, saying he was old and feeble, his mind dim and forgetful. The response was: "Can the true dharma of the Four Noble Truths ever decay or perish? It is like vajra, which cannot be diminished. Only the four great elements of birth and death are subject to increase and decrease." The Sarvāstivāda school further says that among the nine kinds of arhats, some can regress. Regression occurs for four reasons: advancing old age, oppression by illness, fondness for distant travel, and adverse reactions to medicine. It is through these four circumstances that forgetfulness may arise. But can the sublime wisdom of the ultimate truth be forgotten?

Kāśyapa urged Ānanda: "You are now in the prime of life, and moreover you possess both learning-knowledge and equal-knowledge, as well as total retention and strong memory. The Buddha always entrusted you whenever he taught a sūtra. Therefore you should proclaim the scriptural dharma." How do we know that Ānanda possessed equal-knowledge? Long ago, in the city of Śrāvastī, to the east, there was a great *nyagrodha* tree whose shade could shelter five hundred carriages. In the city there was a brahmin, foremost in the art of calculation among the ninety-five traditions. Under this tree he encountered Ānanda and said: "People say that the disciples of Gautama are foremost in wisdom. Is this so?" Ānanda replied: "What I know is little." The brahmin said: "I merely wish to ask one thing. How many stems, nodes, branches, and leaves does this tree have?" Ānanda looked up at the tree and immediately answered: "This tree has such-and-such stems, nodes, branches, and leaves, each with a specific number." Then he departed. The brahmin, reflecting afterward, thought: "This śramaṇa surely did not know the numbers; he just guessed on the spot. I will test him." He went about the tree and secretly removed sixty leaves, burying them in the earth. When Ānanda returned from his alms-round, the brahmin asked again: "I forgot the count. Please tell me once more." Ānanda looked up at the tree twice and said: "Why are there fewer leaves on this tree now?" The brahmin asked: "How many fewer?" Ānanda replied: "Sixty fewer." The brahmin clasped his hands and exclaimed: "Unprecedented!" He then asked: "Are you an arhat?" "No." "An anāgāmin or sakṛdāgāmin?" "No." "A srotāpanna?" "Why do you ask?" "Do you have a teacher?" Ānanda answered: "Yes. The son of King Śuddhodana, who left home and became a Buddha, is my teacher." The brahmin thought: "This is his teacher's wisdom, not his own." He followed Ānanda to the Buddha, asked to become a renunciant, and immediately attained arhatship. By this we know that Ānanda possessed equal-knowledge.

The reason Ānanda deferred to Kāśyapa was that Kāśyapa was the senior elder and the one Ānanda revered. For five hundred previous lives, Kāśyapa had constantly been Ānanda's father; the accumulated feeling of respect and reliance from those former existences was deep. The reason Kāśyapa was so earnest toward Ānanda was that their shared karmic bond was deep, with its legacy of kindness extending to future generations. Moreover, Ānanda's equal-knowledge and strong memory placed him foremost among the assembly. For entrusting the compilation of the remaining scriptures, all eighty-four thousand could not surpass him. The two depended on each other, like a blind man and a lame man relying on one another. Each benefited the other. It was as if two people suddenly found a thousand-catty ingot of gold: if they tried to take it alone, neither had the strength; if they tried to cut it in half, it could not be divided. So they agreed to combine their strength and carry it home together, and thus put it to great use. This may be called mutual wisdom. Kāśyapa and Ānanda are analogous to this. With the two united in their purpose, the dharma-treasure endures forever.

Ānanda reflected on how to organize the immense teachings. He first thought of grouping by number (ones, twos, threes up to ten), each topic following its kind. Some said this was not possible since the Buddha's teachings could not be arranged sequentially. Ānanda further reflected: "The sūtra-dharma is vast; I should divide it into three collections." At that moment, a god of the Śuddhāvāsa heaven secretly told Ānanda: "You should indeed make three divisions." Following the god's counsel, he made three divisions:

1. **Sūtras** (*qìjīng*): The Buddha's spoken dharma, taught to gods, kings, or non-Buddhist seekers, analyzed according to circumstances so that each could attain understanding. *Qì* ("contract/thread") means connecting, stringing together reasoned principles into a method of practice.

2. **Vinaya** (*pínī*): The rules of discipline, taught for the two sanghas (monks and nuns), checking evil and restraining wrongdoing, whether 250 or 500 rules. Like a king's secret treasury that external officials may not handle.

3. **Abhidharma** (*āpítán*): The "great dharma." Why "great"? Because the great wisdom of the Four Noble Truths is the banner of all dharmas, cutting off all wrong views, ignorance, and the flood of delusion. Also called the "incomparable dharma," because the eight knowledges, ten wisdoms, and undefiled right view transcend the obstacles of the three realms, having no equal. Kātyāyanīputra compiled and selected the essential wisdom from the sūtras and presented it to the Buddha for his seal of approval.

Ānanda further reflected: "The meaning of these three collections corresponds to the three gates of liberation. The sūtras' sublime wisdom accords with emptiness in principle. The vinaya's restraint of evil corresponds to signlessness. The abhidharma's right view follows the path of wishlessness." Therefore the three collections and the three liberations are in hidden accord. Ānanda further reflected: "The great body of the sūtras should be divided by meaning into four sections. The literary meaning is mixed and jumbled; one should arrange them by principle, with like following like, and larger and smaller in sequence. The first is the *Ekottara* (Numerical): beginning with one and proceeding to ten, one, two, three, increasing by subject, hence called 'increasing by one.' The second is called *Madhyama* (Middle): neither great nor small, neither long nor short, the subject matter being moderate and fitting, hence called 'middle.' The third is called *Dīrgha* (Long): it tells of events from the distant past, spanning kalpas without end, tracing origins and outcomes through the seven buddhas and the seven treasures of the holy king, hence called 'long.' The fourth is called *Saṃyukta* (Connected): sūtras on severing the fetters, difficult to recite and difficult to remember, with much miscellaneous and fragmentary content that easily causes people to forget, hence called 'connected.'"

When Ānanda finished compiling the three collections, he recorded ten sūtras as one verse. The reason for this was to help future reciters who feared they might forget or err; seeing the title, they could recall the original, reflect, and awaken of their own accord. Therefore he made ten sūtras into one verse.

The so-called "Miscellaneous Collection" (*zá zàng*) was not spoken by one person alone: some was spoken by the Buddha, some by disciples, some praised by gods; some told of past lives across three *asaṃkhyeya* kalpas of the bodhisattva. Its literary content and meaning are not uniform and exceed the three collections, hence the name "miscellaneous."

There are two kinds of giving: giving from faith and giving from fear. When one has established roots and attained patience, that is called giving from faith. When one gives under the compulsion of power, not from one's own intention, that is called giving from fear. Giving from faith completes the perfection; giving from fear merely seeks merit. The distinction between the path and the worldly needs no words to be apparent. Some say that those from the seventh *bhūmi* upward give their heads and eyes; those of the sixth *bhūmi* and below give material possessions. Those who regress from this point do not fall back into birth and death but inevitably reach nirvāṇa. How do we know this? The *Mahāprajñāpāramitā* text on original emptiness says that sixty bodhisattvas attained arhatship. This is that very matter.

Precepts like vajra are the Mahayana precepts. Precepts like an unfired clay vessel are the Hinayana precepts. Why so? Vajra cannot be destroyed. Long ago, a bodhisattva-bhikṣu of incomparable beauty went out to beg for alms. On the road he encountered a beautiful woman. The woman, seeing the bodhisattva, gave rise to desire and wished to become his wife. She reflected: "He may be impossible to win. But if I can merely sit with him, I will set forth the aspiration for supreme awakening." The bodhisattva, knowing her mind, went forward and sat with her. After a moment, she approached him. The bhikṣu remained silent and did not respond. She drew closer again, and he remained just as still. The bhikṣu then taught her the dharma of emptiness: "Where does the eye originally come from? Where does it go? If you say it comes from one's parents, before their union there was no eye. When it is destroyed, where does it go? By this reasoning, the eye has no real existence. The five sense faculties are likewise." Her understanding opened suddenly and she realized emptiness, attaining the path of stream-entry. She should have been taught the doctrine of existence, but he instead taught her emptiness. It is the bodhisattva's way to enter existence and teach emptiness, and so the original purpose was not fully accomplished. Ānanda happened to see this bhikṣu sitting with a woman, a violation of monastic propriety. He returned and reported to the Buddha: "I just saw a bhikṣu sitting together with a woman." The Buddha, already knowing, remained silent. The bhikṣu, knowing that Ānanda had reported to the World-Honored One, thought: "If I do not go to explain, those who slander me will fall into transgression." He was about to display supernatural transformation, but the Buddha did not permit it. He simply flew to the Buddha's presence. The Buddha said to Ānanda: "The bhikṣu you saw violating the rules just now, this bhikṣu who has come flying, is that very one. Have you ever seen anyone who violates the precepts against desire and can fly? This bhikṣu knew the woman's mind and sat with her. He then taught her the dharma of emptiness, analyzing the emptiness of the eye, and the five sense faculties likewise. The woman immediately became afraid, and through that fear attained the lesser vehicle. If the bhikṣu had taught her the doctrine of existence instead, she would have fulfilled her original aspiration." By this we know the bodhisattva had not yet attained non-regression, and his ability to discern the minds of others was not yet perfected. These are the so-called vajra precepts.

The so-called perfection of patience means silently accepting when one is reviled or defamed, and not retaliating. The bodhisattva who practices patience constantly maintains loving-kindness, equally regarding self and other. Since self and other are equal, enmity and friendship are not two. Therefore the sūtra says: "The loving-kindness of the lesser vehicle is like skin-deep compassion; the loving-kindness of the great being penetrates to the bone and marrow." How do we know this? When someone cuts and dismembers the bodhisattva's hands and feet, and they transform into milk, that is the proof of loving-kindness. The bhikṣu Kṣāntivādin is precisely this case. It is like a mother who, upon giving birth, immediately produces milk. This is moved by compassionate thought, which naturally transforms of itself. The great being is like this: entering the samādhi of loving-kindness, he can produce this response. When loving-kindness is perfected, even though one holds a bow and arrows, sentient beings instead come and draw near. When loving-kindness is not complete, even without holding a weapon, all who see one flee. By this evidence, the distinction between the great and the small has always existed.

The perfection of vigor means diligently performing all manner of good works and meritorious deeds. "Evil conduct" refers to the boy Jvālakumāra, who once slandered Kāśyapa Buddha, saying: "What Way could this bald-headed śramaṇa possibly have? The Way is hard to attain; how could he attain it?" As a consequence, he later underwent six years of austerities before attaining the Way. In the age of the decline of the dharma, monks who constantly quarrel are still receiving the retribution for careless speech. The six years of austerities were retribution for performing what should not have been performed. These are the bodhisattva's unwholesome actions of body and speech.

Meditation and concentration mean entering stillness, calm and unmoving.

Wisdom means knowing the number of dust motes, the number of sands in rivers, and calculations beyond reckoning. What the light of wisdom illuminates cannot be exhausted.

These six perfections without limit are entirely within the bodhisattva collection and should not be combined with the three baskets. Ānanda wished to keep the great and small vehicles distinct in their causes and conditions. What is not known to the other is inherently empty and difficult to make clear. When the great being doubts emptiness, it is because he does not seize upon realization; therefore it is called "hesitation."


# Fascicle Two

Maitreya's praise was because the six perfections of the great dharma had been compiled into one division; this is the bodhisattva collection. "Severing the fetters" means severing all attachment to wrong views and latent tendencies, and accomplishing the fruit of the path. This is the bodhisattva's work. Ānanda said only "heard" and not "seen." Could he not have seen the Tathāgata teaching the dharma? The reason "seen" is inappropriate is for the sake of future assemblies of the four groups. If one said "seen," then later assemblies of the four groups, following Ānanda's words and saying "seen," would be speaking falsely. Therefore he said only "heard" and not "seen."

Maitreya's praise of the compilation of the six pāramitās into one division (the bodhisattva collection). Ānanda's formula "Thus have I heard" (not "thus have I seen") is explained: using "heard" is for the sake of future audiences, since "seen" would imply present witnessing that later reciters could not claim.

The first turning of the dharma wheel was for Ājñātakauṇḍinya and the other four in the Deer Park at Vārāṇasī. In the kingdom of Magadha, he converted the three Kāśyapas. "Śākya" refers to Kapilavastu. When the sūtra's location need not be specified, it simply says "at Śrāvastī," because the Buddha spent twenty-five years in Śrāvastī, the longest stay in any country. The reason for this long stay was that Śrāvastī was the finest country, with many marvelous wonders. The people were prosperous and most upright in conduct. At the Jetavana monastery there were extraordinary spiritual signs: whenever the monks were in assembly for lectures, thousands of monkeys would come and sit on either side, silently observing and listening without a sound, and all sorts of birds would gather as well. When the assembly dispersed and each returned to their quarters, at the very next sounding of the bell the animals would assemble again. This was because the people of that country were especially humane and compassionate, and so even creatures of different species were drawn to them. The Buddha might sometimes briefly travel to accept invitations, or might use supernatural power to go and teach and then immediately return. Therefore the sūtras simply say "at Śrāvastī," which suffices to convey the essentials.

The reason the names of both Jeta and Anāthapiṇḍada are given separately is that these two had already passed away and were now in heaven, where they also assembled the gods and taught the dharma. At that time, they thought: "We were originally patrons of the Sangha. Do they no longer speak our names?" To satisfy the wishes of those in the heavenly realm, the two names are mentioned separately. Prince Jeta was the legitimate son of King Prasenajit. He had a park of eighty acres of land, level and with flourishing trees, where many birds and beasts came to gather daily. Jeta's mind dwelt on the Buddha, and he had always wished to build a monastery for the Buddha. Before he could do so, the elder Sudatta came to ask to purchase the land. Jeta had long been friendly with the elder and was fond of joking. He made a jesting promise to sell. Sudatta, having obtained the agreement, was overjoyed and turned to his attendants: "Quickly harness the elephants and load them with gold to spread on the ground." Gold was immediately carried out and spread on the ground. In a short time, forty acres were covered. Jeta said: "Stop, stop. I was only joking when I agreed. You need not spread any more." Sudatta went with the prince to the king and reported the matter. The king said: "By law, a word once given cannot be retracted. The promise has been made; there is no room for regret." Jeta said: "I will keep the trees; you may take the land." The two agreed and together built the monastery: seventy-two lecture halls and 1,250 rooms. The grounds were level and fair, with abundant fruit trees, flowing springs, and bathing pools of temperate and cool water. The view was clear in all four directions, unchanged in winter and summer. When all was finished, they invited the World-Honored One. The World-Honored One then came with 1,250 bhikṣus to dwell there, and the patrons provided the four requisites without lack. Because Anāthapiṇḍada was a minister of this kingdom, he respectfully yielded precedence to the prince. Therefore the sūtras always name Jeta first. Because their merits are linked and cannot be separated, the two names are always combined.

**The Ten Recollections (*anusmṛti*):**

"One should cultivate one dharma" means recollecting the Buddha. Each of the ten recollections is explained as follows:

**1. Recollection of the Buddha:** The Buddha's body is of vajra without any defilement. When he walks, his feet rise four inches from the ground, the thousand-spoke wheel-marks appear on the earth, and insects beneath his footprints are safe for seven days; if they die, they are reborn in heaven.

**2. Recollection of the Dharma:** Dharma precedes even the Buddha, for "the dharma produces all buddhas; the dharma gives rise to the buddha-path." Yet the Buddha is recollected first because dharma, however sublime, cannot be known without the Buddha, just as buried treasure cannot be found without a person of supernatural knowledge.

**3. Recollection of the Sangha:** How does one recollect the Sangha? The Sangha means the four pairs and eight grades, the twelve kinds of noble persons, who have renounced the world's greed and strife. They are the forerunners who open the way to merit; the path to the heavenly and human realms is made accessible through them. They are truly the supreme field of merit for sentient beings. How do we know the Sangha is a supreme field of merit? Long ago, there was a bhikṣu of meager merit named Brahmadatta. Among the 1,250 monks, his presence prevented the assembly from receiving food. No one knew whose fault it was. The Buddha divided the assembly into two groups. When Brahmadatta was in one group, that group could not receive food. The Buddha further divided that group in half, and the half with Brahmadatta still could not receive food. Division after division continued in this way until only two remained, and still they could not eat. Finally, Brahmadatta was alone, and it became clear he had no merit. Wherever food was served, if it was placed in his bowl, it would spontaneously vanish. The Buddha, pitying his plight, personally placed food in Brahmadatta's bowl, and by the power of his supernatural might, the food did not disappear. Because the Buddha wished him to gain merit in this very life, he had two bhikṣus in cessation-attainment sit on either side, and Brahmadatta offered food to these two. Those in cessation-attainment immediately receive the fruit of their offering. Next he had bhikṣus in the samādhi of loving-kindness sit on either side, then compassion, then sympathetic joy, then equanimity, cycling through the four immeasurables in succession. At that time, King Prasenajit heard that this bhikṣu had meager merit and that the Buddha had compassionately provided him with food. "I too should establish merit on his behalf." He immediately sent a servant with milled rice. A crow flew down and seized one grain of rice. The servant scolded: "The king prepared this merit for Brahmadatta. Why do you take it?" The crow immediately returned the grain to its place. The reason was that this bhikṣu, having received the power of the Sangha's merit, could not be harmed even by birds and beasts. By this evidence, we know that the Sangha is the supreme field of merit. Having already crossed over themselves, they can further lead others to the three vehicles. The meaning of recollecting the Sangha is like this.

**4. Recollection of Morality:** Next, recollecting morality: its meaning is as follows. From the five precepts, the ten precepts, the 250 precepts up to the 500 precepts, all serve to restrain body and speech, to check all that is wrong and improper, to control the six senses and sever all desires. When inner and outer are purified, one accords with the nature of morality. Long ago, two bhikṣus were traveling together to see the Buddha. They crossed a vast, waterless plain. At that time there was a small pool of standing water, filled with insects. One bhikṣu deeply considered the prohibitions and took non-transgression as paramount: "If I drink this water, I will kill a great many creatures. I would rather preserve the precepts and lose my life; I will have no regret in death." Thereupon he died, and was immediately reborn in heaven. The other bhikṣu thought: "I should drink the water and preserve my life so that I can reach the Buddha. Who knows where one will be reborn after death?" He drank the insect-filled water, killing a great many creatures. Although he survived to see the Buddha, he was far from the teaching. Weeping, he told the Buddha: "My companion has died." The Buddha pointed to the heavens and said: "Do you recognize this god? This is your companion. Through the merit of keeping the precepts intact, he was immediately reborn in heaven and is now here. Though you see me, you are far from me. Though he lost his life, he is always at my side. What you have come to see is merely my physical form. How could you recognize the true, sublime morality?" By this reasoning, keeping the precepts without transgression, whatever one aspires to, one attains. Among the ten recollections, morality comes first; among the six perfections, generosity comes first. The reason for this difference is that morality in the ten recollections belongs to the śrāvaka tradition, and for disciples, restraining the body comes first, which is why it is placed first. In the great being's path, generous giving is given priority. Why? The great being, born among gods and humans, keeps his mind set on benefiting others. The essential means of benefiting others requires giving. Since sentient beings depend on clothing and food for their lives, one first rescues their bodies through material giving, and then guides their spirits through the gift of dharma. Therefore the great being puts giving first. Morality has two kinds: worldly morality and path morality. The five precepts and ten good deeds are worldly morality; the three samādhis are path morality. The 250 to 500 precepts are also worldly morality; the sublime wisdom of the Four Noble Truths is path morality. Merely observing moral rules without transcending the three realms, only when one governs morality with wisdom to produce the undefiled does it accord with path morality. The morality of the śrāvaka tradition is like a flower on one's knee: the slightest movement causes it to scatter. The morality of the great being is like a flower pinned in the hair: it does not move whether one walks or stands. The lesser vehicle restrains the outer form, so any movement violates propriety. The great being governs the mind and is not bound by external rules. Though inner and outer differ, both lead to nirvāṇa. This is the meaning of recollecting morality.

**5. Recollection of Generosity:** Giving reaches nirvāṇa because material giving leads to dharma-giving, completing the perfection of generosity.

**6. Recollection of Heaven:** There are three kinds of heaven. What is "elected heaven"? This refers to the cakravartin king, who is elevated by the people. The reason he is called "heaven" is that the holy king teaches the world with the ten good deeds, causing people to be reborn in heaven. Being above all people, he is therefore called "heaven." Some say the holy king surpasses the Buddha. Why? When the holy king governs the world, no one falls into the three evil destinies; yet when the Buddha appears in the world, the three evil destinies are not cut off. In this respect, the king is superior. Others say the Buddha surpasses the holy king, because the holy king teaches the world with the ten good deeds and can elevate people no higher than the human and heavenly realms; but when the Buddha appears and teaches the world, beings can reach nirvāṇa. In this respect, the Buddha is superior. What is "birth heaven"? From the Four Heavenly Kings up to the twenty-eight heavens, all who receive blessings there are in birth heaven. The reason it is called "birth heaven" is that they transmigrate without ceasing and never escape birth and death. What is "pure heaven"? This refers to the three kinds of persons: buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, and śrāvakas, all of whom have exhausted the fetters and transcended the three realms, pure and free of desire. The eight pure-dwelling heavens surpass birth heaven and elected heaven but do not reach pure heaven, being in between.

Recollecting heaven means aspiring to what those heavens represent. By recollecting both birth heaven and elected heaven, one can also reach nirvāṇa. How so? In the city of Śrāvastī, there was a lay couple, husband and wife, who had no children. Both were diligent and kept the Three Jewels in their hearts. The wife died first and was reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven as a goddess of unrivaled beauty. She thought: "Who is fit to be my husband?" With her divine eye she surveyed the world and saw her former husband, who had left home to study the Way. He was old and his faculties were dim; he relied solely on sincere faith and always swept and maintained the stūpas and temples as his practice. She saw his diligence and knew he was destined for rebirth in heaven, where he would certainly become her husband again. One night, as he sat meditating in his quiet room, a sudden light appeared. Startled by this unusual occurrence, he looked up and saw a goddess. He asked her why she had come and from where. The goddess answered: "I have come from the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. I was formerly your wife, and now I am a goddess. In heaven there is no one fit to be my husband. Observing your diligence, I see that you should become my husband again. That is why I have come to tell you." When she finished speaking, she suddenly vanished and returned to heaven. After this, the old bhikṣu redoubled his efforts and additionally repaired the old temples. He was tireless morning and evening. His accumulated merit grew and his blessings increased, until he was destined for rebirth in the fourth Tuṣita heaven. The goddess again surveyed with her divine eye and saw that he was now destined for the fourth heaven. She came again and said: "Your accumulated diligence has already surpassed my realm. I can no longer have you as my husband." Having spoken, she departed. The bhikṣu practiced with even greater vigor than before. By day he walked in meditation; by night he contemplated in meditation. His mind grew ever more luminous as he reflected on the Four Noble Truths. Before long, he attained arhatship. This is what is meant by reaching nirvāṇa through recollecting heaven.

**7. Recollection of Tranquility:** Recollecting tranquility means attaining concentration. There are two kinds of tranquility: worldly tranquility and path tranquility. Worldly tranquility is like resting from physical fatigue, briefly pausing to relax, hence called worldly tranquility. Path tranquility refers to one who has attained concentration. How do we know this? Long ago, there was a bhikṣu named Surāta, who came to the city of Śrāvastī, traveling and teaching. West of the city, at the place where Aṅgulimāla had once killed people, the ground was broad and level with many trees. A brahmin was seated under a tree practicing meditation. He ate no grains, only fruits and berries; when there were no fruits, he ate grass and vegetables to sustain himself. He wore clothing of tree bark, and his body was emaciated, barely able to support itself. Surāta, passing by, saw him and took him for a practitioner. He sat in meditation to observe the brahmin's mind, to see whether he was truly concentrated. He saw that the brahmin's fundamental thought was to become king of that country. He reflected: "This is actually a great bandit!" He was about to leave, but feared that later the brahmin would fall into transgression. If he tried to teach him directly, the brahmin would surely not listen. "I must devise a skillful means to save this person." He sat under a nearby tree in meditation, not far away, and remained motionless for seven days. After seven days, he rose and went before the brahmin, snapped his fingers to rouse him, and said: "Companion! How are you?" The brahmin slowly raised his head after a long while and answered: "I am destitute and have nothing to offer you." The bhikṣu said: "I have something to give you." He then conjured an illusory chicken. "Kill this chicken and eat it." The brahmin exclaimed: "I do not even kill lice; how could I kill a chicken?" The bhikṣu said: "Your fundamental mind intended to kill countless people. You wanted to become king, and a king governing the world kills people daily. How can you say you do not kill? This chicken is the consciousness in your mind. By killing the chicken of desire, you can attain the great path of non-action. What use is being a king?" The brahmin then reflected: "This bhikṣu knows what is in my mind. He must be a sage. I should follow his teaching." The bhik���u further taught him the dharma, and the brahmin immediately attained the path. This brahmin's body was still in form, but his mind knew no tranquility. Only after he killed the "chicken of consciousness" could he be called truly tranquil. Therefore the later explanation says: "Mind, thought, and imagination are stilled."

**8. Recollection of the Body:** Recollecting the body means contemplating the thirty-six substances of the body, its foulness and impurity, with focused attention and undistracted thought. This too can lead to nirvāṇa. How do we know? Long ago, a bhikṣu who practiced as a forest ascetic was always going out to beg for food. By the riverbank he ate, and when finished, he washed his bowl. At that time, upstream on the bank near a charnel ground, there was a newly dead woman. The wind blew her hair, which suddenly fell into his bowl. The bhikṣu held the hair and examined it carefully. It was exceedingly fine and beautiful. He said to himself: "If this is a horse's tail, it is too fine. If it is a man's hair, it is too soft and fine. If I tie a knot and it does not come undone, it must be a woman's hair." He tied it, and the knot did not come undone. He then gave rise to a fantasy: "If the hair is like this, the person must be beautiful, with a face like a peach blossom, eyes like bright pearls, a nose like a straight-cut tube, lips like cinnabar, and brows like centipedes." Having made these distinctions, desire arose in him. He followed the stream, seeking to find the person, imagining her face. Pursuing without cease, he came upon a woman whose body had already been half-eaten by foxes and wolves. The corpse was rotting and foul, yet the hair still remained. He compared it and found the length matched. The desire he had conceived instantly dissolved of its own accord. He examined the body again, reflecting and discriminating: "When this person was alive, her form was beautiful. Now she is decayed and ruined for all to see. The fantasy I conceived was nothing but desire for the body and craving for pleasure, which gave rise to this thought. Her body is like this; how am I any different? On careful examination, my body too is composed of the four great elements. When merit is exhausted and conditions separate, it naturally dissolves." Seeing the transformation, his mind was awakened, and he immediately attained the path. By this reasoning, recollecting the body yields the fruits of the śramaṇa life.

**9. Recollection of Death:** The practitioner reflects: "Life passes without stopping; the sense faculties scatter and decay like rotten wood; the life-root is severed." One should recollect impermanence to awaken oneself. Long ago, there was a bhikṣu named Vakkali, who sat in meditation and practiced the Way for years, yet his defilements were not exhausted. He regarded his own body as a great burden and constantly thought of ending his life. He reflected: "The reason people cannot attain the Way is precisely because of this body, entangled in continuous transmigration. When will it ever cease?" He took a knife in hand, about to cut his own throat, and further reflected: "The World-Honored One has a teaching that forbids disciples from harming themselves. Even so, I now seek nirvāṇa. In nirvāṇa there is no body. Therefore I will first remove the body and take the unconditioned." He then raised the knife and cut his own throat. When the cut had reached halfway through, he attained the exhaustion of defilements. By the time the head was severed, he had attained nirvāṇa. At that moment, the great earth trembled, which moved Māra. Māra thought: "What auspicious sign could cause such a tremor?" With his divine eye he surveyed and saw the bhikṣu who had killed himself. "Where has his spirit gone?" He searched all the heavens but could not find it; he searched the human realm and could not find it; he searched the three evil destinies and still could not find it. Meanwhile, the Buddha was leading the bhikṣus to cremate the body when fire arose from the corpse. This was Māra, who had set the fire while searching for the bhikṣu's spirit, unable to find it anywhere. The reason for the search was to discover where the spirit had gone, in order to obstruct it and prevent its fulfillment. The bhikṣus then cremated the remains. The Buddha praised this bhik���u for attaining liberation through faith.

**10. Recollection of the Buddha (expanded):** The first ten recollections were taught by the Buddha himself, without anyone asking, for the benefit of beings of sharp faculties. The later ten recollections, taught in response to the bhikṣus' questions, are the Buddha's further exposition, analyzing the meaning for beings of dull faculties. "Fame and renown" means one later becomes a cakravartin king. "Great fruition and reward" means one later becomes Indra, Lord of the Gods. "All good things come to fruition" means one later obtains the reward of Brahmā. "Tasting the nectar of immortality" means one later becomes a pratyekabuddha. "Arriving at the unconditioned" means one later obtains the fruit of arhatship. The first teaching of the ten recollections did not include these five phrases. The reason they are added now is to make clear that the meaning of recollecting the Buddha is deep and subtle.

The Buddha taught that when his disciples enter parinirvāṇa, each does so through the ripening of past karmic debts. Maudgalyāyana was beaten to death; Śāriputra suffered intestinal disease. Likewise, all five hundred disciples entered nirvāṇa according to their individual past causes. Only Ānanda's parinirvāṇa was the most excellent of all. When Ānanda was about to enter parinirvāṇa, he first manifested signs of radiance. A brahmin who had studied mathematics under Ānanda, seeing Ānanda's complexion shining with an extraordinary glow, reported to King Ajātaśatru: "Ānanda's appearance is unusually bright. Might he be about to enter parinirvāṇa?" The king immediately sent people to pursue Ānanda. Ānanda had already taken five hundred disciples to the road midway along the Ganges riverbank. He boarded a boat and was about to cross. When they reached the middle of the river, the king had already arrived at the bank. The people of Vaiśālī, hearing that Ānanda had come, also sent five hundred youths to welcome him. Wishing to accommodate both kingdoms, Ānanda used supernatural power to hold the boat still in the middle of the current. He then transmitted the dharma to his two disciples. To the first, Madhyāntika, he said: "Go to Kaśmīra and establish the Buddha's dharma. That land does not yet have the Buddha's dharma; see to it that it flourishes there." To the second, Śāṇakavāsin, he said: "Go to the island of Siṃhala and establish the Buddha's dharma." When his instructions were complete, he performed the eighteen supernatural transformations, emitted fire to burn his body, and divided his relics in two, so that each kingdom could receive and make offerings. This was through the power of recollecting the Buddha, and thus he attained self-mastery.


# Fascicle Three

The Buddha told the bhikṣus that in his great compassion he wished his disciples to fully understand the meaning of recollecting the Buddha. It is like a father earnestly instructing his sons and grandsons, wishing them to achieve accomplishment without end. Having already concentrated on recollecting the Buddha, one contemplates the Tathāgata's form with eyes that never depart from it, just as Ānanda contemplated the Buddha without wearying, his mind recollecting without cease.

**Recollection of the Buddha (continued):** At that time, Ānanda had a boil growing on his back. The Buddha commanded Jīvaka to treat Ānanda's ailment. Jīvaka said to the Buddha: "I dare not bring my hands near Ānanda's back." The Buddha told Jīvaka: "Just treat him without hesitation. I myself will speak the dharma to Ānanda so that he does not feel the pain." The Tathāgata had Ānanda gaze intently at the Buddha's marks and characteristics, and the Buddha explained: "The body of the Tathāgata is of the nature of vajra, indestructible, accomplished through the merit of 3,200 blessings." Ānanda gazed without wearying, listened without tiring, and kept his mind from scattering. At that time, Jīvaka lanced the boil on Ānanda's back and applied a salve. The Buddha asked Ānanda: "Did you feel any pain on your back?" He replied: "I did not feel it." The reason he felt no pain was due to his recollection of the Buddha.

The ten powers are what accomplished this, along with the four fearlessnesses. Long ago, a householder took his slave to pay homage at a stūpa. The master said: "Homage to the World-Honored One of Ten Powers." The slave, bowing behind him, said: "Homage to the Tathāgata of Eleven Powers." The master said: "The Tathāgata has exactly ten powers. How can there be eleven?" The slave replied: "What harm is there in eleven? Just do not say nine. Saying eleven adds one more power; what fault is there in that?" The master was silent and returned home. He asked various dharma teachers: "How many powers does the Tathāgata have?" They answered: "Some say three powers, some say ten powers, some say innumerable." By this reasoning, one need not be limited to ten. The master thereupon renounced the world to study the Way, and freed his slave to be master of the household. To say four is not to say there are too few; to say innumerable is not to say there are too many. Responding appropriately to the occasion, there is no fixed measure.

**Recollection of the Dharma:** Recollecting the dharma means going from desire to desirelessness, from desire to the path, from defilements to freedom from defilements, from the conditioned to the unconditioned. How do we know this? Long ago, the World-Honored One was teaching the dharma in the kingdom of King Udayana. At that time, he was teaching the dharma for his mother Māyā in the Heaven of the Thirty-Three Gods. After ninety days he returned, descending north of the city of Kāśī. At that time, the bhikṣuṇī Utpalavarṇā thought to herself that she wished to be the first to see the Buddha. The kings of various countries, not having seen the Buddha for ninety days, all had feelings of longing and gathered like clouds. She thought: "I am a bhikṣuṇī; it is not fitting for me to be in this crowd. I should devise a means to be first." She then transformed herself into a cakravartin king, with attendants befitting the appearance of a holy king. The lesser kings, seeing the cakravartin, all scattered and withdrew. The bhikṣuṇī then resumed her original form and saw the Buddha, bowing and paying respects. When the various kings came to see the Buddha and no longer saw the cakravartin, they realized it was the bhikṣuṇī's transformation and said: "What we saw just now, was that a deception?" At that time, Utpalavarṇā thought to herself that she had been first to see the Buddha. The Buddha told Utpalavarṇā: "You claim to have seen the Buddha first. But there is one who preceded you." "Who might that be?" The Buddha said: "It was Subhūti, in a mountain cave east of Rājagṛha. He was there mending his robe. A deva told him: 'The Buddha has come down.' Subhūti said: 'As a disciple, I should go pay my respects.' But then he reflected further: 'Where is the Buddha? Is the golden color the Buddha? But gold has no limits. The Buddha said: "All dharmas are empty and without substance. If one understands that the twelve links of dependent origination are empty, neither made nor created, neither person nor self, neither alive nor dead, then one sees the dharma. To see the dharma as without life or death is to see me."' He then clasped his hands, rose, and called out: 'Homage!' Then he sat down again and continued mending." By this reasoning, Subhūti saw the Buddha before you. The Buddha is the lord of all dharmas; to understand that dharma is empty is precisely to recollect the dharma.

Recollecting the Sangha means recollecting the community of noble and holy persons. There are many kinds of assemblies: the ninety-five kinds of non-Buddhist traditions each have their own assemblies, some harmonious, some discordant, each with their own precepts and discipline. Some practice meditation; some take the attainment of "no-thought" to be the ultimate. Each trusts in what they follow and considers it true. But they have not attained the true Noble Eightfold Path, and therefore cannot reach nirvāṇa. Even those with the five supernatural powers who extend their lifespan, or those in the realm of no-thought who prolong their existence for kalpas, none can escape birth and death. Only the noble assembly of the Tathāgata, consisting of four pairs and eight grades of persons, is no longer swept away by the four swift currents or bound by the nine places of abiding. Therefore the sūtra says: "Among the ninety-six kinds of sangha, the Buddha's sangha is the most true." The four assemblies of the Tathāgata all share the designation of the Śākya lineage, just as the waters of four rivers, each with five hundred tributaries, all merge into the great ocean and become one taste. The sangha is the same: whether of kṣatriya, brahmin, householder, or commoner origin, all who leave home to study the Way share the Śākya lineage as one family, with no separate names. The broader the inclusion, the deeper the meaning. The Sangha encompasses the three vehicles: the arhat-sangha is within it, the pratyekabuddha is within it, and the Mahayana sangha is within it. Therefore it is called the "supreme field of merit." Within the three realms, there is no better ground for benefiting sentient beings. Although the Tathāgata himself attained perfect awakening, he always returns to confess before the Sangha, because the ground of the Sangha is thick and weighty. The buddhas of the three times, pratyekabuddhas, and disciples all attain liberation through the Sangha. This is like the bhikṣu Brahmadatta, who was saved and sustained by the power of the noble assembly.

**Recollection of Morality (continued):** Recollecting morality means maintaining pure precepts and possessing all the rules of discipline. It is like a potter who shapes and prepares clay, waiting for various requests for vessels: large or small, square or round, each suited to what is desired. Morality is the same: if one wishes to be reborn in heaven, one receives blessings in the three realms; if one wishes to sever the fetters and seek the path, one's aspirations are fulfilled. It is like an auspicious vase that grants whatever a person desires. With morality as the foundation, one additionally practices the thirty-seven factors of awakening and the various samādhis, severs the seven latent tendencies and nine fetters, and advances to accomplish nirvāṇa. It is like clay formed into a vessel that can never be broken again.

**Recollection of Generosity (continued):** Recollecting generosity means that giving has two aspects: giving with a master and giving without a master. There are also two kinds: one called "offering" and one called "relinquishing." And there are two further kinds: material giving and dharma giving. "Offering" is giving with a master; "relinquishing" is giving without a master. Relinquishing means relinquishing the fetters; offering means the recipient receives material goods or dharma. The reason generosity leads to nirvāṇa is that when one gives material goods or dharma to others without hoping for reward, without calculating self and other, with these three things unobstructed, it is the same as the unconditioned. If one can relinquish the fetters, that too is nirvāṇa. That both relinquishing and offering reach nirvāṇa is like an elephant pursuing a brave soldier: whether he advances or retreats, he will obtain the meat. If he advances, he breaks through the army; if he retreats, he perishes himself. Eating the meat is certain.

**Recollection of Death (continued):** How does recollecting death lead to nirvāṇa? Long ago, King Aśoka was devoted to the dharma and diligently practiced, always providing the four requisites to five hundred monks within his palace without lack, while also externally supporting five hundred forest-dwelling ascetic monks, sending provisions to supply them. He further established provisions at the four city gates for the destitute. As the offerings continued over time, his treasury gradually diminished. His younger brother, named Susīma, did not believe in the Three Jewels. The great minister Yaśas and the queen Shanrong also shared his disbelief. These three were of one mind in resenting the king, and repeatedly admonished him: "Supplying these monks is emptying the state treasury to no purpose. What use is it?" The king replied: "Guard your mouths well. The reason a person loses his head is on account of evil words."

Susīma said to the king: "These monks are all young men. They eat fine food to satiety, and their desires burn fiercely. Yet they dwell deep within the palace among women. How can they be trusted?" The king replied: "The monks control themselves with disciplinary laws and guard themselves according to the dharma. They restrain their bodies and observe the prohibitions, and are not swayed by sensual desires."

Later, Susīma went out hunting and saw a herd of deer with a human among them. He surrounded and captured the man, and asked: "Who are you?" The man said: "When I was eight years old, I lost my parents and fled into the mountains, where a deer nursed me. It has been this way ever since." He asked further: "When the deer had no milk, what did you eat?" "I followed the deer and ate grass and leaves to sustain my life." He asked again: "Do you ever have desires?" "Yes." Susīma then brought the man back and reported to the king: "This grass-eating man, with his emaciated body, still has desires. How much more so must these monks, who eat fine food to satiety and whose bodies are plump and full, have desires?"

The king thought: "What stratagem can I devise to convert my brother?" He devised an elaborate scheme, pretending he was about to go on an excursion. He mustered a great assembly and marched out, then secretly returned and hid himself. The king had previously arranged with his ministers: "After I leave, enthrone him as king." The ministers urged Susīma to put on the royal robes, and he feigned reluctance. The ministers said: "Just do it; we will support you." He then donned the heavenly crown and royal garments, and all proclaimed: "Long live the king!" Attendants stood at his left and right as befitting a holy king. King Aśoka, seeing that it was settled, emerged from outside and said: "How do you find being king?" His brother, seeing the king, was ashamed and did not know what to do. Aśoka said: "I merely went on a brief excursion, and you have done this? Is my iron wheel no longer here? How dare you act so presumptuously? I shall execute you in a moment!" He immediately ordered his ministers to seize and shackle him, and secretly sent a message to the monks: "Keep him in your thoughts; you must come to plead for his life."

"I am about to execute you. But considering that your days as king were few and you did not get to enjoy it fully, I will now grant you seven days as king, with all the royal trappings: ministers in attendance, palace women, music, food and drink. Indulge for seven days, and then face the ultimate punishment." It was carried out as ordered. Though the seven days were fulfilled, Susīma had no heart for enjoyment. The monks came to petition, carrying their bowls and staffs, and arrived at the palace gate. The king asked: "What do you monks desire?" They said: "We wish to have the condemned man." The king said: "This criminal deserves death. He cannot be given to you." The monks pressed again: "Just grant him to us. We will have him study the Way." The king said: "Ask this man whether he can study the Way." The monks asked: "We are petitioning for you to become a novice. Can you do it?" He replied: "Even if you asked me to become a slave, I would not refuse, let alone becoming a novice." The king said: "Being a monastic is difficult. Are you truly able? The monastic life requires coarse clothing and poor food, barely enough to sustain the body. You are accustomed to luxury. How can you endure such austerity?" He answered: "I was about to die. How could I not endure austerity?" The king said: "If you can endure it, I permit you to beg for alms for seven days."

The king ordered those within the palace: "When Susīma comes to beg, give him only the worst food, the foul, rotten leftovers." He was made to wear ragged clothing and visit all the residences. Everywhere he begged, he received only terrible food. Yet because his relief at escaping death was so great, he ate the bad food willingly. After the seven days, the king saw he had no resentment, and allowed him to enter the Way: "You always said that monks live at ease and their desires cannot be trusted. The food you begged was still within my palace, and even that was relatively fine. The food monks beg outside is even worse than this. Eating such food, how could they have sensual desires?" He was then entrusted to the monk Shanmian to become a śramaṇa. The king sent him to the Stone Chamber City to practice the various meditative contemplations, sometimes among the charnel grounds, sometimes under trees.

While in the charnel grounds observing dead corpses, he saw at night a hungry ghost beating a dead body. He asked: "Why are you beating this dead body?" The ghost replied: "Because this body caused me to suffer like this. That is why I beat it." The monk said: "Why not beat your own mind instead? What good does beating this dead body do?" A moment later, a deva appeared, scattering heavenly *mandārava* flowers upon a dead body. The monk asked: "Why do you scatter flowers on this foul corpse?" The deva replied: "It was through this body that I was reborn in heaven. This body was my benefactor, so I have come to scatter flowers to repay the kindness of the past." The monk said: "Why not scatter flowers upon your own mind, rather than on this foul corpse? The root of both good and evil lies in the mind. You are both abandoning the root and grasping at the branch."

At that time, Susīma thought to himself: "I was delivered from death. Through this very cause and condition, I should attain liberation." Thereupon he contemplated the body and recollected death, reflecting and discriminating, understanding impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self. He immediately attained arhatship. By this reasoning, recollecting death also leads to nirvāṇa.

**Recollection of the Body (continued):** How does recollecting the body lead to nirvāṇa? Long ago, a hundred years after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, there was King Aśoka, who ruled over Jambudvīpa. His ministers, queens, and elephants and horses each numbered eighty-four thousand. Once, while touring his kingdom, the king saw that King Yama had eighteen hells, with officials and wardens interrogating and punishing the guilty. The king asked his attendants: "What kind of person is this?" They answered: "This is the king of the dead, who distinguishes good from evil." The king said: "Even the king of the dead can make hells to punish sinners. I am the king of the living. Can I not also make a hell?" He asked his ministers: "Who can build such a hell?" The ministers replied: "Only an extremely wicked person can build a hell."

The king ordered his ministers to search for a wicked person. The ministers went and found a man sitting on the ground weaving felt. Beside him were a bow and arrows and a fishhook; furthermore, he was feeding birds poisoned rice. He was simultaneously weaving felt, catching fish, shooting birds, and trapping sparrows. The ministers returned and reported to the king that the wicked man was as described. The king said: "This man is supremely wicked. He can surely manage the hell." The king sent for him, saying: "The king wishes to see you." The wicked man said: "I am a lowly person with no knowledge. What use has the king for me?" The messenger said: "The king wants you to manage the hell." The man went home. He had an elderly mother at home, and told her: "The king has summoned me." The mother asked: "Why has the king summoned you?" He said: "The king wants me to manage the hell." The mother said: "If you go, how shall I survive?" She then embraced her son's feet and would not let go. The son was determined to go. He drew his sword and struck his mother, killing her, and went to the king. The king asked: "Your mother would not release you. How did you get away?" He said: "I killed her and came." The king said: "A truly wicked man. He can surely manage the hell." He entrusted this man with building a hell-city, furnished with boiling cauldrons and sword-trees. He appointed this man as the hell-king, established officers and aides, each with their duties, modeled on King Yama's realm. The king gave strict orders: "Whoever enters this city, regardless of rank or station, shall be punished." The king also said: "Even if I myself enter, do not let me out."

At that time, there was an elderly bhikṣu named Sudarśana, who always went begging for food. He came to the gate of this city and, seeing beautiful flowers and incense, thought that people were inside, and entered the city. He saw only people being punished, and in terror tried to leave. The prison guards would not let him out and were about to drag him to the boiling cauldron. The monk pleaded: "Give me a brief reprieve until midday." Then, after a moment, a man and woman who had committed adultery were brought in to be punished. They were placed in a mortar and pounded; in an instant, they dissolved into foam. The monk, seeing this, remembered the Buddha's words: "The human body is like gathered foam." "Truly these words are so!" After another moment, the foam turned white. He further reflected that the human body is like a heap of white ash, changing and inconstant, like an illusion, like a magical transformation. On careful examination, nothing is real. At that very moment, his mind awakened, his defilements were exhausted, and his fetters dissolved.

The prison guards again pressed him to enter the boiling cauldron. The bhikṣu laughed. The guards, enraged, had four men seize him by both arms and throw him upside-down into the cauldron. Immediately the liquid turned cold, and the bhikṣu transformed the cauldron into a thousand-petaled lotus, upon which he sat cross-legged in meditation. The guards, astonished, reported to King Aśoka: "Something wondrous has happened in the hell. We humbly request the king to come and see." The king said: "I gave a prior command that even if I entered, I should not be let out. How can I enter now?" The officers said: "Just enter; there will be no trouble." The king followed them in and saw the monk seated on the lotus. He asked: "Who are you?" The monk said: "I am a monk." The monk said to the king: "You are a foolish man." The king asked: "Why do you call me a foolish man?" The monk replied: "When you were once a child, you offered a handful of earth to the Buddha. The Buddha received it and blessed you, saying: 'In the future, you will become an iron-wheel-turning king named Aśoka, and in a single day you will erect eighty-four thousand Buddha-stūpas.' Is this hell a Buddha-stūpa?" The king's mind was immediately awakened. He came forward and repented, took Sudarśana as his teacher, disbanded the hell, and began works of merit, erecting eighty-four thousand stūpas and temples. By this reasoning, recollecting the body leads to nirvāṇa; this is the meaning.


# Fascicle Four

The reason the Tathāgata broadly declared each of the four assemblies to be foremost in their respective qualities was for the sake of the future, in the final age of the declining dharma. Among the four castes, non-Buddhist brahmins, and the four assemblies of disciples, there would be those who dispute with each other, each claiming themselves superior and others inferior. Such persons would be beyond counting. Therefore the Buddha preemptively guarded against what had not yet arisen, opening the path of self-sufficiency for each.

**Ājñātakauṇḍinya:** First among the five original disciples, first to receive the "come, monk" ordination.

**Udāyin:** Foremost in exhortation, for when the Buddha returned to convert his homeland, Udāyin went ahead to display supernatural transformations and convert countless people.

**Mahānāma:** Foremost in swift faculties, attaining supernatural powers even before exhausting all defilements.

**Subāhu:** Foremost in walking through the air as easily as on the ground.

**Maudgalyāyana:** Foremost in supernatural powers, silently traveling to other buddha-fields.

**Gavāṃpati (Cow-Foot Bhikṣu):** Because of two characteristics (cow-like hooves and ruminating after eating), he could not live among people and was sent to the heaven of the thirty-three gods. After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, when Kāśyapa summoned the assembly, a messenger was sent to bring him down. He refused, saying: "The world is empty now. Why should I go?" He entrusted his robe and bowl and entered parinirvāṇa in heaven.

**Svāgata:** Foremost in contemplation of the impure, who upon seeing his sleeping wife's white teeth perceived only bone, then contemplated the thirty-six impure substances of the body.

**Uruvilvā-Kāśyapa:** Foremost in attending to the sangha and providing the four requisites.

**Nadī-Kāśyapa:** Foremost in concentrated listening; his inner realization was so thorough that oil seeped from his pores like sweat.

**Aśvajit (Horse-Teacher Bhikṣu):** Foremost in deportment, having studied with the Buddha for only seven days. Despite having been a monkey for five hundred previous lives, his dignified bearing so impressed Upatiṣya (Śāriputra) and Kolita (Maudgalyāyana) that they followed him to the Buddha.

**Śāriputra:** Foremost in wisdom. The Buddha said: "If Mount Sumeru were an inkstone, the waters of the four oceans were ink, the bamboo and trees of the four continents were brushes, and all the people therein were scribes, they could not finish writing out Śāriputra's wisdom."

**Maudgalyāyana:** Foremost in supernatural powers. The World-Honored One also gave testimony of this. Once, during a time when the three calamities were afflicting the world and the people were starving, Maudgalyāyana thought: "Below the earth there still remains the earth-fat of ancient times. The people are now in great famine. I wish to use the four supernatural powers to overturn this earth and take the earth-fat beneath to feed the people." Having resolved this, he said to the Buddha: "I now wish to use the four supernatural powers to overturn the earth and take the earth-fat below to sustain the people. Is this acceptable?" The Buddha said: "Stop, stop, Maudgalyāyana! Although your supernatural powers can overturn this earth without difficulty, how can you hold the insects in one hand while overturning the earth with the other?" The Buddha said: "It cannot be done. The reason is that monks of future generations will mostly lack supernatural powers. If in the future there is famine, kings and ministers will order the monks to overturn the earth. If they cannot do it, people will say they are not real monks." By this testimony of supernatural power, Maudgalyāyana is called foremost.

**Śroṇa-Koṭiviṃśa (Twenty-Billion-Ear Bhikṣu):** Foremost in ascetic practice. Born with natural hair growing from the soles of his feet and jewels valued at two billion in his ears. He practiced walking meditation so vigorously that his soft feet bled, staining the path. When he grew discouraged, the Buddha taught him the parable of the lute: "If all the strings are too tight, can you produce beautiful music? If all are too slack? Only when they are neither too tight nor too loose can beautiful music be produced."

**Aniruddha:** Foremost in the divine eye. Once, when the Buddha was teaching the dharma to a great assembly, Aniruddha fell asleep. The Buddha saw him sleeping and said: "The Tathāgata is teaching the dharma now; why do you sleep? To sleep is to have the mind closed and blocked. How is that different from death?" Aniruddha was deeply ashamed and made a firm vow: "From this day forward, I dare not sleep again." He went without sleep for so long that his eyes went blind. The reason was that there are six kinds of nourishment, and the eyes have two: seeing forms and sleeping. The five other sense faculties likewise each have two kinds of nourishment. When the faculties receive their nourishment, the six sense-roots remain whole. Because his eyes lost the nourishment of sleep, his physical eye-root was destroyed. The Buddha ordered Jīvaka to treat him. Jīvaka said: "Without sleep, it cannot be cured." Having lost his physical eyes and unable to see, his five hundred disciples all abandoned him and scattered. He had to ask others to thread his needle and groped to mend his robes by touch. When the thread ran out, he needed another needle threaded but could find no one to ask. He called out: "Who wishes to earn merit? Thread my needle for me." The World-Honored One suddenly appeared before him and took the needle: "I will thread it for you." Aniruddha asked: "Who is it?" "I am the Buddha." "Surely the Buddha's merit is already complete; why do you still seek merit?" The Buddha said: "Can one ever have enough of merit?" Aniruddha reflected: "If even the Buddha still seeks merit, how much more should ordinary people?" His heart was deeply moved. He turned his gaze toward the Buddha, and through the intensity of his sincerity, he suddenly gained the divine eye. Having gained the divine eye, he further reflected and attained arhatship. Ordinary arhats all have three eyes: the physical eye, the divine eye, and the wisdom eye. Aniruddha had only two: the wisdom eye and the divine eye. When three eyes look together, they interfere with each other; the physical and divine eyes compete, and coarse and fine observations become jumbled, which is called interference. Aniruddha used the divine eye exclusively to observe the great thousand-world system, seeing both coarse and fine things completely, distinguishing among all forms whether they possessed consciousness or not. Therefore he is called foremost in the divine eye.

**Revata:** Foremost in meditation. Long ago, King Prasenajit invited him to sit in meditation under a tree. The king then invited him into the palace for meals. For six years, he never went anywhere else, always remaining at the one tree. The tree-spirit would not let him leave. How do we know? When he was about to move to another tree, the tree-spirit scattered flowers as an offering. By this, it was known the spirit did not want him to go. How do we know his mind had no other thoughts? Mahākauṣṭhila came to Revata and said: "Why do you not sit under a fine tree, instead of sitting under this withered one?" Revata replied: "You, Venerable One, are called foremost in the four analytical knowledges, able to discriminate the meaning of dharma and appropriate speech. But I wonder: under which of the four analytical knowledges does distinguishing a withered tree fall? I have been sitting here for nearly six years and cannot tell whether the tree is alive or dead. You, Venerable One, have just arrived and already make distinctions." The king invited him into the palace and supplied offerings day after day, having various queens take turns attending to him. After six years of offerings, when it came time for the dedication of merit, Revata did not know the name of his host. The king said: "For six years you have received my invitation and do not know my name. How deep your concentration must be!" Revata replied: "I sat under a tree and still did not know whether it was alive or dead; how could I know a person's name?" The merit of his offering and meditation was supremely pure. Whatever the king aspired to could lead to nirvāṇa; such is the excellence of this field of merit. Therefore he is called foremost in delighting in meditation.

**Therāpaṭamālā:** Foremost in organizing the sangha's duties, assigning monks to their proper divisions (sūtra study, vinaya, abhidharma, meditation, preaching, alms-begging, teaching).

**Rāṣṭrapāla:** Foremost in noble renunciation. Born into royal wealth, he refused to eat for six days until his parents consented to his ordination. Later, returning home for alms, he rejected his family's gold, jewels, and beautiful wives, saying: "These things only harm people."

**Kātyāyana:** Foremost in analyzing meaning. He compiled the Abhidharma in seven days of underground retreat, presented it to the Buddha, who approved it. He also correctly explained the Buddha's terse remark about "non-attachment, non-staining" by analyzing how consciousness arises through the eye's contact with form.

**Kuṇḍadhāna:** Foremost in distributing lots (*śalākā*). The lot records the number of persons and verifies whether they are sincere. If one answers sincerely and receives the lot, one obtains its merit. If one receives it falsely, the transgression accumulates greatly. In Chinese it is called "lot" (*chóu*); in Indian it is called *śalākā*. *Śalākā* also means "destruction and exhaustion": merit exhausts transgression, and transgression exhausts merit. How do we know? Long ago, the daughter of Anāthapiṇḍada was married off to a country of *nirgrantha* ascetics. She asked the Buddha whether this was acceptable. The Buddha said: "It is well to know the timing. Going will certainly be beneficial." After the woman arrived, she sent a distant invitation to the World-Honored One. The Buddha, knowing her intention, silently accepted the invitation. He commanded Ānanda: "Tomorrow we accept the invitation of the Śākya layman. Sound the bell, assemble the community, and distribute the lots for supernatural display." At the time of the lot distribution, the senior elder Kuṇḍadhāna had not yet attained supernatural powers. Hearing the lots being distributed for the invitation, he felt ashamed that he lacked supernatural powers. He looked at himself, a solitary figure seated at the head of the assembly, like an old wild fox on a mountain of purple gold. Deliberating anxiously, if he accepted the lot, he was not among those with supernatural powers; if he refused it, he occupied the senior seat, and even eight-year-old novices had attained supernatural powers while he, despite years of effort, had achieved nothing. Calculating thus, he thought: "What is the use of going on like this?" Deeply moved, he accepted the lot, and in the very moment of receiving and returning it, his defilements were suddenly and completely exhausted. If one accepts a lot falsely, the human body has 900,000 pores, and by that measure, one would not receive a human body for that many lifetimes. But if one receives with sincerity, one can immediately attain the proof of the exhaustion of defilements. This senior elder is the clear proof. The reason he was also the senior elder was that he was skilled in teaching the dharma in ways suited to people, and was nominated by the assembly to be senior. By this cause, the senior elder Kuṇḍadhāna is called foremost in receiving lots.

**Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja:** Foremost in subduing non-Buddhist teachers. In the city of Vaiśālī, there was an elder named Citta, who was always vexed by the six non-Buddhist teachers' arrogance. They said: "The śramaṇa Gautama claims to be supreme. We will compete with him in skills. If he displays one feat, we will display two, and so on, doubling up to thirty-two." The elder then invited both the inner and outer communities for a great offering. Afterward, he erected a tall pole four *zhang* and nine *chi* high, and placed a sandalwood bowl on top, proclaiming: "Whoever can reach up and take this bowl is foremost." Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja thought: "I should now display supernatural powers to silence the six teachers and subdue them." He further reflected: "The World-Honored One always admonishes his disciples not to display supernatural powers. But if I do not display them now, I fear they will forever remain in transgression. Yet if I display them, I fear I will violate the teacher's command." After much deliberation, he displayed his supernatural powers, extended his hand, and took the sandalwood bowl. He rose into the sky and circled the city seven times, then returned to his seat. He said to the brahmins: "Now you display your second feat." The six teachers were silent. At that time, the great spirit-general named Pañcika said to the six teachers: "Quickly display your second feat." The six teachers and their followers did not know where to go. By this reasoning, Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja is known to be foremost in subduing non-Buddhist teachers.

**Cakṣu Bhikṣu:** Foremost in caring for the sick, using five methods: distinguishing good medicine, rising first and sleeping last, speaking kind words with little sleep, providing dharma rather than craving food, and being fit to teach the dharma to the sick.

**Vaṅgīsa:** Foremost in composing verses. In a former life, this bhikṣu was the son of a wealthy householder, a man of natural talent and brilliance who composed praises in response to everything he encountered. Once, while traveling, he came upon a woman of an artisan family whose beauty was rare in the world. Seeing her, his heart was delighted and he wished to take her as his wife. He returned and told his parents of what he had seen, asking them to arrange the betrothal. His parents were displeased: "You are from a noble family. Why would you change course and ruin the family's standing?" But his determination was fierce, and he pressed them again: "If you do not arrange this for me, I cannot go on living." Seeing the earnestness of his words, the parents could not bear to forbid him and said: "Do as you wish. This is not our concern." He sent a messenger to the woman's family. The family, being artisans, valued skill above all and replied: "We do not covet your wealth. Only one who excels in all skills will receive her."

Vaṅgīsa, hearing this, went to study with artisan masters, and within a short time mastered every skill. He sent word again: "My studies are complete. You may now give her to me." The master replied: "If your skills are complete, you must demonstrate them before the king." It happened to be a festival, and the king assembled all the artisans to test their abilities. The greatest would receive a thousand pieces of gold. The king had also heard of this woman's beauty and wished to take her into his palace. They tested the various skills, and pole-climbing was the supreme test. A pole was erected, four *zhang* and nine *chi* high, with sword blades placed below, edges pointing upward, with just enough space between for footing. Vaṅgīsa climbed the pole, spun seven times in the air, and descended to open ground. The king, fearing he would lose the woman, pretended not to watch. The crowd proclaimed it wonderful. The king said: "I did not see it. If it was truly marvelous, do it again." Vaṅgīsa thought: "If I do not obey the king, I will certainly lose the woman. I must risk everything for this one purpose." He braved death and climbed again. When he reached the top of the pole, he looked at the woman's face and thought: "For the sake of this person, I have come to such extremity." His heart trembled and his body shook with fear that he would not survive: "Women are false and deceptive. What use is this?" The Buddha, knowing this man could be saved, and that without rescue he would fall into the three evil destinies, told Maudgalyāyana: "Use your supernatural powers to rescue him from this danger." Maudgalyāyana obeyed and went, manifesting a transformation in the sky, sitting cross-legged in midair. Below the pole he manifested a stairway of the seven treasures, invisible to others but seen only by Vaṅgīsa. He descended slowly, step by step, seven circuits down, supported by supernatural power, inwardly safe though outwardly in peril. The king and the crowd were greatly astonished. The king himself took the woman by the hand and gave her to Vaṅgīsa. Vaṅgīsa said: "I have no use for this false and deceptive thing that misleads people, confuses the honest and upright, and destroys kingdoms and families." He then followed Maudgalyāyana to the World-Honored One. The World-Honored One said: "Welcome, bhikṣu." And he became a monk. The Buddha taught him the Four Noble Truths, and he immediately attained arhatship. Joy arose from within and was expressed in words, and he composed verses praising the World-Honored One:

"On the pure fifteenth day, five hundred bhikṣus gather,
All fetters severed, these sages accept no further becoming.
Like a wheel-turning king surrounded by his ministers,
Ruling the four seas and all the earth, with no limit to his domain.
He who conquers people thus, this guide without superior,
Protects all the śrāvakas; the three knowledges destroy the nature of the fetters.
All the World-Honored One's children are free of dust and defilement.
Having broken through the net of craving and desire, I now pay homage to the moon among the stars."

By this cause, Vaṅgīsa is called foremost in composing verses to praise the Tathāgata's virtues.

**Mahākauṣṭhila:** Foremost in the four analytical knowledges. He could expound the meaning of a single phrase for seven days without exhausting it.

**Nandika:** Foremost in alms-begging. Although other bhikṣus also begged for alms, some did not fully keep the precepts, some had covetous minds, some looked left and right without focused attention, and some avoided cold or heat. But this bhikṣu, when begging for alms, was free of all such faults. Because his begging was pure, the merit of those who gave to him was great. A parable illustrates the distinction between great and small: someone asked about the art of archery. "One archer shoots at a strand of hair from a hundred paces; another shoots at the ground until dust rises. Which is more difficult?" The answer was: "The strand of hair is more difficult. Though one shoots, one may not hit the ground." That is hardly worth mentioning. Whether one gives to individual alms-begging monks or to the assembled Sangha, which is greater? Giving to a true forest-dwelling ascetic is like the parable of the strand of hair: the gift may or may not find a worthy recipient. Giving to the assembled Sangha is like shooting the ground: though shooting at the hair is more skillful, there are many misses; though shooting the ground is easy, one never misses the ground. The field of merit of the Sangha is thick and firm, so there is no increase or decrease; but among individual ascetics, some are pure and some are coarse, so there are gains and losses. Nandika achieved the purity, and therefore is called foremost.

**Śīla:** Foremost in eating at one sitting in one place.

**Kimbila:** Foremost in begging from only seven houses.

**Pūrṇamaitrāyaṇīputra:** Foremost in teaching the dharma. From his awakening to his parinirvāṇa, he converted ninety-nine thousand people.

**Upāli:** Foremost in keeping the vinaya. When the Buddha returned to his homeland to accept his father the king's invitation, the bhikṣus in his retinue, though pure in mind, were lacking in outward appearance. The king wished to encourage the young men of the Śākya nobility to ordain as bhikṣus to attend the World-Honored One. He issued a decree: "Any family with two brothers must have one ordain. Those who disobey shall be severely punished." Among the Śākyas, there was one named Mukharāja, the eldest of the clan, who was first in line to be tonsured. The Buddha commanded Upāli to shave his head, and further instructed: "These Śākya princes are proud and pampered with delicate bodies. Shave them gently and slowly with a light hand." Upāli used a light hand. Then it was too light and did not cut. Upāli then turned the blade and used its back. The Buddha said: "That will not do." He then used the flat of the blade. "That will not do either." Then he shaved from the crown of the head downward, cleanly removing every hair. All five hundred Śākya sons were shaved likewise. The Buddha said to Upāli: "Welcome, bhikṣu." He immediately became a monk. The Buddha then conferred the precepts, and he attained arhatship. Next the five hundred Śākya sons received the precepts. Upāli was their senior. He placed his hand on the heads of the five hundred, conferring the precepts as master. When it came time for them to bow to Upāli, the Śākyas, accustomed to pride and arrogance, could not bring themselves to bow, especially since he had been a servant of their own households. Each said: "This is our family servant. Why should we bow to him?" The Buddha said: "That is not so. In the dharma there is no distinction of noble and base. The first to attain is the elder brother; the later is the younger." After much deliberation, they restrained their minds and bowed. At that moment, the heavens and earth shook greatly, and the gods above praised them: "Well done, well done! Today the Śākyas have conquered their pride. This attitude is the hardest to overcome, and therefore the earth trembles."

From the time Upāli received the precepts from the Buddha, he never transgressed even to the extent of a hair's breadth. By this cause, he is called foremost. But is there more? North of the Jetavana monastery, there was a bhikṣu who had been ill for six years without recovery. Upāli went to visit and asked: "What ails you? If there is something you need, speak." The bhikṣu said: "What I need cannot be spoken." Upāli asked again: "What is it you need? If it is not available here, I will seek it from the four directions. If it does not exist in the world, I will seek it in heaven." The bhikṣu said: "What I need is available in Śrāvastī, but it violates the Buddha's teaching, so I cannot speak of it." Upāli said: "Just say it; do not worry." The bhikṣu said: "I only crave wine. If I could have five measures of wine, my illness would be cured." Upāli said: "Wait. I will ask the Buddha for you." He returned and asked the Buddha: "A bhikṣu is ill and needs wine as medicine. Is he permitted to drink it?" The World-Honored One said: "The rules I have established make an exception for illness." Upāli immediately went back, obtained wine, and gave it to the sick monk, whose illness was immediately cured. He then further taught him the dharma, and the monk attained arhatship. The Buddha praised Upāli: "You asked the Tathāgata about this matter, enabling the sick bhikṣu to be cured and also to attain the Way. If this bhikṣu had not been saved, he would afterward have fallen into the three evil destinies and become a being of consciousness with no prospect of escape. You have spoken the vinaya for future bhikṣus, enabling them to know what is weighty and what is light, and to find deliverance from danger. You are truly able to uphold the vinaya." The Buddha entrusted the vinaya collection to Upāli, saying: "Do not let it be lost. This collection, among all the collections, is the most interior. It must not be shown to novices or laypeople." By this further cause, he is also called foremost.

**Vakkali:** Foremost in liberation through faith. This bhikṣu was bedridden with illness for six years. Everyone who had cared for him had grown weary and abandoned him. The bhikṣu thought: "My illness has lasted so long that those who care for me are exhausted and disgusted. Moreover, the Tathāgata has not come to show compassion. I should end my own life to escape this suffering." He then asked for a knife, and speaking to the blade, he said: "You should not only kill me; you should also sever the fetters." Having said this, he cut his own throat. When the cut had reached halfway through, he had already exhausted his defilements. By the time the head was severed, he had attained nirvāṇa. At that moment, the great earth trembled, which moved Māra. Māra thought: "This bhikṣu has destroyed himself. Where has his spirit gone?" He searched all the heavens with his divine eye but could not find the spirit. He searched the human realm and could not find it. He searched the three evil destinies and could not find it. The Buddha, leading the bhikṣus to the cremation, saw fire arising from the corpse. This was Māra, who had set the fire while searching for the bhikṣu's spirit, unable to find it anywhere. The reason for the search was to discover the spirit's direction and obstruct it. The bhikṣus then cremated the remains. The Buddha praised this bhikṣu for attaining liberation through faith. Some say: one who has true faith entrusts his life to what is natural; he would not even carry a staff to protect himself, let alone harm himself. The answer is: the reason he took up the knife was that the knife represents the sword of wisdom, intended to sever the fetters. The body is the root of the fetters; when the root is uprooted, the branches follow; when the body is severed, the fetters are eliminated. Therefore taking up the knife is no obstacle. He took up the knife of faith to cut down the tree of doubt. The next line says: "The mind was without hesitation." From liberation through faith to liberation without doubt, this is the transformation of the dull into the sharp. By this meaning, he is called foremost in liberation through faith.


# Fascicle Five

The biographical accounts of the foremost disciples continue:

**Nanda:** Foremost in handsome appearance. Among the bhikṣus, each had his distinguishing marks: Śāriputra had seven, Maudgalyāyana had five, Ānanda had twenty, and Nanda alone had thirty. Nanda was golden in color; Ānanda was silver in color. His garments shone with splendor, he wore golden-embroidered sandals, and he carried a lapis lazuli bowl when he entered the city to beg for alms. All who saw him were delighted. Apart from the Tathāgata, none among the other disciples could match him. Therefore he is called foremost in handsome appearance. He is also said to have had perfectly still sense faculties. The Buddha brought his disciples to the Āmrapālī monastery in Vaiśālī. At that time, Nanda was walking outside in meditation. Āmrapālī, hearing the Buddha had come, was joyful and wished to make an offering. Outside, she saw Nanda walking in meditation. Her love and delight were deep. She bowed at his feet and touched them with her hands. Though he beheld her beauty, his mind was perfectly still and without the slightest thought of desire. Yet the body's response to pleasant contact naturally released impure fluids. Sweet nourishment moistens the body, and when the body is full it overflows. The release of impurity was a bodily function, not caused by the mind. Āmrapālī did not understand and suspected he had desire. The Buddha, knowing her thought, told Āmrapālī: "Do not give rise to doubt. In seven days, Nanda will attain arhatship." By this reasoning, we know his mind did not waver.

**Bhadra:** Foremost in concise teaching, using a single practice as the governing principle from which all other practices follow, like the Buddha's voice that contains eight tones, each tone containing a hundred teachings.

**Sena:** Foremost in expansive teaching, spending thirty years as an ordinary person teaching dharma extensively before attaining the path.

**Deva-Subhūti:** Foremost in wearing fine clothing. Among the five hundred disciples, there were two named Subhūti: one of royal lineage and one of the householder class. Deva-Subhūti was of royal lineage. He is called "Deva" because for five hundred lifetimes he was constantly reborn either in the Nirmāṇarati heaven above or in a royal family below, enjoying natural abundance without ever experiencing want. When the Buddha returned to his homeland, King Śuddhodana urged five hundred Śākya youths to ordain and attend the World-Honored One. This bhikṣu was among them. The Buddha strictly admonished the bhikṣus: "Those who follow the Way should restrain themselves, keeping to coarse clothing, poor food, a grass mat for a bed, and using their own bodily waste as medicine." This bhikṣu, hearing the Buddha's stern instruction, thought to himself: "I was born into wealth and nobility. Clothing and food have always come naturally. My palace is carved and ornamented with gold and silver beds and utensils of the seven treasures. I wear garments of gold thread and fine silk, and golden-soled sandals. Even so, I am not fully satisfied. How could I possibly wear the five-fold patched robe?" He resolved to return to lay life. He was at the Śrāvastī monastery when he went to take leave of the Buddha to return home. Ānanda said: "Please stay one night." Subhūti said: "The beds and furnishings of a monastic dwelling are unbearable. I will stay one night at a lay house, and return home tomorrow." Ānanda said: "Just stay. I will prepare everything properly." Ānanda went to the king's household and obtained all manner of fine furnishings: banners, canopies, flowers, incense, and oil for four lamps. Everything was lavishly arranged. The bhikṣu spent the night there. Suited to his original temperament, his mind settled into concentration, and he contemplated the Four Noble Truths. By the last watch of the night, he attained arhatship and flew up into the sky. Ānanda thought: "This bhikṣu may have abandoned the room and left. The borrowed royal furnishings may be taken by others." He went to check. The bhikṣu was not in the room. He looked up and saw him flying above in the sky. Ānanda reported to the Buddha: "Deva-Subhūti has already attained arhatship and is now flying in the sky." The Buddha told Ānanda: "Clothing is of two kinds: what should be used and what should not. Wearing fine clothing that aids the mind on the path should be used. Wearing fine clothing that harms the mind on the path should not be used. Therefore, Ānanda, some attain the Way through fine clothes, and some through patched and ragged robes. What matters is what awakens the mind, not the outward form of the clothing." By this reasoning, Deva-Subhūti is foremost in wearing fine clothing.

**Nandaka:** Foremost in instructing advanced students, compared to a skilled archer who fells an enemy with a single arrow.

**Sumanas:** Foremost in instructing nuns. This bhikṣu always used stern and cutting words to admonish the bhikṣuṇī community: "Women are full of emotions and contrivances, using charm and adornment to bewitch people. The bodily form is foul and impure, with nine orifices that are unclean. Of the thirty-six substances, not one is worth desiring." The reason he was named Sumanas is that "sumanas" is the name of a flower. When he was born, a flower naturally grew from his ear, and he was named after it. Once, King Bimbisāra came to the Buddha and saw this bhikṣu with a flower on his ear. Surprised, he asked the Buddha: "Is a bhikṣu permitted to wear flowers?" The Buddha told the king: "Try pulling it off yourself." The king then plucked the flower with his hand, but it grew back immediately. He continued to pluck, yet it continued to grow, until a pile of flowers had accumulated. The king's astonishment grew. He asked the reason. The Buddha told the king: "Long ago, when Vipaśyin Buddha appeared in the world, this bhikṣu was the son of a householder. During a festival, he played music and performed. When finished, he went to the Buddha. Seeing the Buddha, this householder was overjoyed and took the flower from his own ear and placed it on the ear of the Buddha. The Buddha then used his supernatural power to transform the flower, and in the sky it became a four-pillared pavilion, while the flower on his ear remained as before. The householder, seeing the transformation, made a vow: 'May I, in future lives, always encounter a buddha; may I be reborn with a handsome appearance; and may a flower grow from my ear.' Through the merit of this past vow, he has now received this reward." The king, hearing this account, was enlightened. He bowed at the Buddha's feet and departed. The reason Sumanas is foremost in instructing the bhikṣuṇīs is that the nuns were by nature emotional. When they saw the handsome bhikṣu with a flower on his ear, their hearts were filled with attachment. Through this attachment, he admonished them with stern words, and through those stern words, the attachment was dissolved. Therefore he is called foremost in instructing the bhikṣuṇī sangha.

**Śīvali (尸婆羅):** Foremost in merit. When Śīvali was first born, he held a priceless *maṇi* jewel in his hand as he emerged. The moment he touched the ground, he spoke: "Is there gold, silver, and the seven treasures in this world that can be used for giving? I now wish to perform a great act of giving." Long ago, in the time of Vipaśyin Buddha, this bhikṣu was a merchant who entered the sea to gather treasures. Passing through five perils, he reached the place of jewels and obtained a single precious pearl. He brought it back and offered it to the Buddha, vowing: "May I, wherever I am reborn, receive my reward naturally." By this cause, from birth he possessed extraordinary qualities, with jewels worth two billion naturally adorning his ears. At twenty, he left home to study the Way and came to the World-Honored One. The Buddha said: "Welcome, bhikṣu." He immediately became a monk. He contemplated the Four Noble Truths and attained arhatship. Five hundred youths also ordained and always attended Śīvali. He provided clothing and food for these five hundred, and wherever he went, supplies were never lacking. Once he stayed in a deep mountain south of Rājagṛha, filled with poisonous insects, tigers, wolves, and rākṣasas. He thought: "I wish to seclude myself in this mountain for a time." Indra, knowing his thought, immediately built five hundred rooms and a monastery in the mountain, with all manner of provisions. After the summer retreat, he thought: "I have been away too long; I should return to pay my respects." The weather was extremely hot, and he wished for coolness. Indra, knowing this, immediately sent clouds and rain. When he wanted a cool drink, sweet nectar descended. Whatever he wished for came to him according to his thought. Therefore he is called foremost in merit. He had an uncle who followed non-Buddhist teachings and was naturally stingy, unwilling to give. A friend once urged him to create merit for the next life. The uncle invited several thousand brahmins and gave a hundred thousand pieces of gold. Śīvali thought: "My uncle is stingy in life and creates no merit. Even when he gives, the gift does not reach a good field of merit. If I do not save him, he will be forever cast aside." He went to his uncle's house with his bowl to beg for food. The uncle said: "Why have you come so late? Yesterday I gave a great offering. If you had come yesterday, you could have received a *saṃghāṭī* robe." Śīvali said: "I have my own *saṃghāṭī*. I do not need one." "Then why have you come?" "I wish to receive food." The uncle refused to give. Śīvali then manifested himself in the sky, performing the eighteen transformations, emitting water and fire from his body. The householder thought: "He must be angry. What if he burns my house?" He called Śīvali down and offered him a seat. Śīvali sat and said: "I wish to eat." The uncle gave him foul and rotten food. He accepted it, blessed it, and ate. As the food entered his bowl, through the power of his merit, it was transformed into ambrosia. A god above exclaimed: "Well done, householder! You have performed a great offering indeed, an offering of merit! Nothing surpasses this." The householder thought: "When I gave a hundred thousand pieces of gold to the brahmins, no one praised me. Now I give this small amount of bad food and am praised as virtuous. Could this be false?" The god said again: "Though the gift is small, the field of merit is excellent. Therefore it is called a great offering." The householder reflected: "The god must be speaking the truth, having come twice to tell me." He then offered the hundred thousand pieces of gold to Śīvali. Śīvali said: "As a bhikṣu, I am not permitted to accept gold." He went to ask the Buddha. The Buddha said: "You may accept it and redistribute it as you see fit." He accepted the gold and gave it to his fellow practitioners. Then he taught his uncle the dharma, and the uncle attained the path of stream-entry. His ability to transform foul food into ambrosia demonstrates why he is called foremost in merit. From birth to nirvāṇa, he was never in want. When he entered parinirvāṇa, all manner of sweet foods and provisions rained down from his body. The reason was that his own merit was sufficient, and he further wished to extend it to all beings. By this he is again called foremost.

**Upasena:** Foremost in complete conduct, possessing all aspects of both inner realization and outer deportment.

**Bhadrasena:** Foremost in gentle speech that never offends. This bhikṣu was always careful with his mouth and never committed any of the four verbal transgressions. In the world, the axe is in the mouth; the reason one loses one's head is on account of evil words. This bhikṣu had permanently eliminated all coarse and violent speech. He always chose his words carefully, speaking slowly and thoughtfully, expressing himself only after reflection, and making sure his words were received with joy. Whether among elders, those of middle years, or the young, he adapted to what each preferred and was pleasing to all. To illustrate why this bhikṣu is praised for his gentle speech: there was once a bhikṣu who had already attained arhatship. Though his defilements were exhausted, he still had a verbal fault. Once, while crossing a river and finding the water growing deeper, he blurted out the harsh words: "Wretched slave-woman of loose morals!" The river goddess, hearing these evil words, thought: "This bhikṣu speaks evil words like this." She was about to push him into the water, but then thought: "Because he is a bhikṣu, I should first ask the Buddha." She went and asked the Buddha: "A bhikṣu crossing the river, when the water grew a little deep, cursed me, saying 'wretched slave-woman of loose morals.' Is it the dharma for a bhikṣu to curse?" The Buddha immediately sent a bhikṣu to summon the offending monk. The bhikṣu came. The Buddha told him: "You are a śramaṇa. Why do you curse?" The bhikṣu replied: "Your disciple did not curse. I merely said 'slave-woman of loose morals.'" The river goddess said: "See, this bhikṣu has cursed again. May the World-Honored One explain the origin of this." The Buddha said: "An arhat no longer has anger. It is merely a habitual verbal pattern. This bhikṣu was formerly a brahmin, and brahmins habitually use abusive language, saying 'womb-slave.' This is not necessarily done in anger, but simply an ingrained verbal habit. Moreover, for five hundred previous lives, he was your husband and regularly called you 'slave-woman.' Because of this old habit, the words recur." The goddess said: "Though he is an arhat, he still has verbal faults. I want no part of arhatship. May I in the future seek the unsurpassed, true, and perfect Way, liberating all beings just as the Buddha does." The Buddha told the bhikṣu: "Apologize to this woman." The bhikṣu apologized, and the goddess also apologized to the bhikṣu. They bowed to each other and parted. Even though defilements are exhausted, coarse speech can remain. How much more should ordinary people guard their words! By this cause, this bhikṣu is known as foremost in guarding his speech.

**Mahā-Katyāyana:** Foremost in *ānāpāna* (breath meditation), along with Rāhula. Among the 1,200 disciples, only this bhikṣu and Rāhula were able to practice breath meditation as the foremost. How do we know? Long ago, Rāhula was walking behind the Buddha. The Buddha, using skillful means, deliberately made his foot stumble so that Rāhula would see. Rāhula, seeing this, thought to himself: "This old man, carrying such a body, renounced the position of a cakravartin king to walk the path of begging. How is he not ashamed? I cannot continue begging like this. I should go back. My grandfather King Śuddhodana is still alive. Why should I endure such hardship?" The Buddha, knowing Rāhula's thoughts, told him: "Do you know? Even heaven and earth are impermanent. How much less can you rely on a cakravartin king? You must understand impermanence. All that has form is suffering; the body is not one's own. All things wear away and cannot remain long." Rāhula contemplated the four aspects of impermanence, but his mind had not yet awakened. The Buddha then taught him ānāpāna, the practice of guarding the breath. Ānāpāna means the in-breath and out-breath. One knows when the breath is long and when it is short. A short breath returns from the heart; a long breath comes from the heels. One further knows cold and warm: the in-breath is cold, the out-breath is warm. The reason for knowing long and short, cold and warm, is to distinguish the depth and extent of the five aggregates, to trace the origins and endpoints of the breath, and to know the source of illness. If one does not know where the in-breath comes from, and does not know where the out-breath goes, one understands that there is no coming or going. Illness is likewise. Reflecting in this way, Rāhula attained arhatship.

Mahā-Katyāyana's practice of ānāpāna was not the same as Rāhula's. His breath was under complete control. If he wished, it could enter and exit through his eyes or ears, according to his will. He could close his eyes, ears, nose, and mouth, and breathe through his 900,000 pores.

**Uttarabhadra:** Foremost in contemplating impermanence and non-self. His body was enormous, standing shoulder-height with the Buddha. Despite his great stature, he was always humble.

**Kumāra-Kāśyapa:** Foremost in varied discourse. This bhikṣu always expounded the Four Noble Truths for people, interspersing praises, verses, analogies, and illustrations, one truth with one verse of praise and one analogy, and so on through all Four Noble Truths. Therefore he is called foremost in varied discourse. "Kumāra" means "child"; "Kāśyapa" is his family name. He was literally the child of a maiden. How do we know? Long ago, there was a householder named Sudatta, of immeasurable wealth. He had an unmarried daughter who, while warming herself by the fire at home, conceived from the warmth entering her body. The parents were alarmed and interrogated her. She answered truthfully: "I do not know why this has happened." They pressed her further with beatings, but her account did not change. The matter reached the king. The king also questioned her sternly, and her answer was the same. She was sentenced to death. The woman cried out: "In all the world, must there be a lawless king who unjustly kills the innocent? If I am unchaste, let my body be examined. To be unjustly treated like this!" The king ordered an examination as the woman described, and there was nothing unusual. The king then said to her parents: "I wish to take her." The parents replied: "Do as you wish. What use is this dead girl to us?" The king brought her into the palace and cared for her. In due time, she gave birth to a son, handsome and fine. The boy grew up, left home to study the Way, became intelligent and learned, and through diligent practice quickly attained arhatship. He then returned to save his parents.

At that time, there was a king named Pāyāsi who held wrong views and did not believe in this life or the next, did not believe that good deeds bring blessings or that evil brings suffering. He held that at death the spirit is extinguished and there is no further rebirth. He did not believe in the Buddha, did not recognize nirvāṇa, and bound iron plates over his belly for fear that wisdom might leak out. He boasted that he was without peer. Kumāra-Kāśyapa went to his gate. The king, seeing his unusual garments and dignified bearing, engaged him in debate. The king said: "You monks say that doing good brings blessings and doing evil brings suffering. Now, in my own family, there was a man of the purest virtue. When he was about to die, I went with others to his side and told him: 'If, as you believe, the virtuous are reborn in heaven, come back and tell me.' He died long ago and has never come to tell me. Therefore I know that good deeds bring no reward." Kumāra-Kāśyapa replied: "A wise person understands through analogy. Suppose a man fell into a cesspit a hundred measures deep. Someone pulled him out, washed him clean, perfumed him, dressed him in fine clothes, and seated him on a high bed. If someone then said: 'Go back into the cesspit,' would he go?" The king said: "Of course not." The monk said: "Those reborn in heaven view the human world the same way. In heaven, the happiness is boundless, the five pleasures are freely enjoyed, and ambrosia is their food, which is naturally digested without any waste. Their bodies are fragrant and their breath is sweet. Looking down at the human world, it is like pigs living in filth. Even if they wished to return, the stench would drive them back. By this reasoning, how could they come to tell you?" He continued with parables by the dozens. The king's mind opened and he came to believe in the Three Jewels. By this cause, Kumāra-Kāśyapa is foremost in varied discourse.

**Mukharāja (Face King):** Foremost in wearing poor clothing without shame. This bhikṣu was of the Śākya lineage. When he was first born, he possessed extraordinary spiritual qualities. When his mother was pregnant, she consulted brahmins to read the signs. The brahmins said: "This child has the mark of a heavenly crown on his head." The mother, hearing this, was outwardly displeased but inwardly delighted. She thought: "A heavenly crown is a king's mark. There cannot be two kings in one country." She feared the king would harm the child, and so feigned displeasure. But inwardly she was glad, thinking: "If he is truly destined to be a king, he will naturally have protectors. Why worry?"

When he was four years old, King Śuddhodana was sitting on his throne in the great hall with all his ministers. The king thought to himself: "If my son had not renounced the world, I should have become a holy king. My son was born with the marks of a holy king, yet he left home. What use is this crown to me?" He took off his heavenly crown and cast it on the ground. The ministers were all worried and unhappy. At that time, the child Mukharāja was playing nearby. He saw the crown on the ground, picked it up, and placed it on his own head. He sat on the ground, propped his left hand on his shoulder, and stroked his beard with his right hand, in the manner of a king. The king and his ministers were astonished. The king said: "This child acts by divine impulse. Perhaps he will become a holy king. All the marks of a holy king that my son possessed have now passed to this child."

At age ten, Mukharāja thought: "Even a cakravartin king is impermanent. Moreover, I am not among the Śākyas who have left home. A human body is hard to obtain and a buddha's age is rare to encounter. Having encountered a buddha's age, I should leave home." He told his mother: "I wish to leave home and study the Way." His mother said: "You are my only child. If you leave me, I will surely die." Mukharāja then petitioned King Śuddhodana: "I wish to leave home. Will the king permit it?" The king was overjoyed: "Excellent!" Mukharāja said: "My only regret is that I am my mother's only child, and there is no one to whom I can entrust her." The king said: "If you can leave home, I will treat your mother as my own sister and provide her with half the kingdom." Mukharāja was delighted. He returned home and told his mother, who then consented. When he left to be ordained, he wore only a single white cloth. He came to the World-Honored One to request ordination. The World-Honored One said: "Welcome, bhikṣu." He immediately became a monk. The Buddha's rule required three robes, but Mukharāja simply dyed his single white cloth into a *kāṣāya* robe and used no other garment. He said to the Buddha: "Your disciple wishes to wear only this one garment for the rest of my life. May the World-Honored One permit it." The Buddha silently consented. From then on, he always wore that one garment. Therefore the World-Honored One said: "Among my disciples, none wears poorer clothing than Mukharāja." Among the eight reflections of a great person, he is foremost in being content with little and knowing sufficiency.

**Rāhula:** Foremost in keeping precepts. Some say Rāhula was fond of lying. How then can he be called foremost in keeping precepts? Others say Rāhula did not actually lie; he was simply angry at the Buddha. Why was he angry? Because the Buddha did not become a cakravartin king. Had he become a holy king, there would have been eighty-four thousand great ministers, eighty-four thousand consorts, and elephants, horses, and carriages each numbering eighty-four thousand. To have abandoned such a position and become a śramaṇa, begging from east to west, was that not shameful? Calculating the advantages of kingship and resenting the Tathāgata, Rāhula told lies. When visitors asked where the Tathāgata was, although the Tathāgata was truly at the Jetavana monastery, Rāhula would say he was at the Andhavana grove. When the Tathāgata was truly at the Andhavana, he would say he was at the Jetavana. He deceived visitors back and forth with falsehoods. Ānanda reported to the Buddha: "Rāhula is telling lies." The Buddha summoned Rāhula: "Did you really tell lies?" "Yes, I did." "Rāhula! Why do you tell lies? The reason I abandoned the position of holy king is that royal status cannot be relied upon. All things are impermanent, and nothing endures forever. Even Indra and Brahmā cannot be depended on, let alone a holy king. Rāhula! The number of times I have previously abandoned such positions is beyond reckoning, and yet you still resent me."

The Buddha said to Rāhula: "Bring me water." Rāhula filled a bowl with water and gave it to the Tathāgata. The Tathāgata held the bowl and said: "Do you see this water?" "Yes." The Buddha said: "Water filling the bowl without any lack is an analogy for keeping the precepts completely without any loss." He then poured out half and said: "Do you see this water?" "Yes." The Buddha said: "This water, having lost half, is an analogy for keeping precepts incompletely." He then poured out all the remaining water and showed Rāhula: "Do you see this empty bowl?" "Yes." The Buddha said: "Transgressing every precept until they are entirely lost is like this empty bowl." He then overturned the bowl on the ground and showed him: "Do you see this?" "Yes." The Buddha said: "Having transgressed all the precepts, one falls into hell. The bowl facing the ground is its analogy." From the time Rāhula received this admonishment, he never again committed even the slightest transgression. Therefore he is called foremost in keeping the precepts.

Some say there is more. Śāriputra was leading Rāhula on an alms-round in Śrāvastī. A brahmin saw Rāhula walking behind and, with malicious intent, struck him on the head until the blood flowed and stained his face. Rāhula immediately gave rise to an evil thought: "I will find a way to take revenge on this enemy. As for all who are called brahmins, I will destroy every one of them." Śāriputra, knowing what was in Rāhula's mind, wiped the blood for him and said: "Remember when your father, in a former life as King Sudarśana, had someone come and demand his eyes. He plucked out his eyes and gave them without any regret. While sitting in meditation in a garden, a king came and cut off his hands and feet, and he felt no resentment. When he was an elephant, he gave his tusks to people without weariness. How can you now give rise to such an evil thought?" Rāhula, hearing what his teacher said, immediately reproached himself: "How can I harbor ill will toward that person?" His mind became patient like the earth, and not a hair's breadth of harmful intent arose. The man who struck Rāhula's head later fell into the Avīci hell. By this cause, we know Rāhula is foremost in keeping the precepts.

**Panthaka:** Foremost in becoming invisible. "Panthaka" means "path," because he and his brother were twins abandoned on the road. Someone found and raised them. They each left home to study the Way, but no one gave them names, so they were called "Path-born" (Panthaka in the Indian tongue). In the kingdom of Magadha, violent thunderstorms and hail destroyed the crops for several years, and the people starved. The king, named Bimbisāra's grandfather, offered a reward to any who could stop the storms. A brahmin skilled in spells came and said: "I can stop the rain." The king let him try, and the seasons became harmonious and the crops flourished. The brahmin then asked the king for his promised reward, but the king, though he had verbally agreed, never actually paid. Ministers and common people likewise promised but did not pay. The brahmin, enraged, vowed to become a poisonous nāga and destroy the crops with hail: "Whenever anyone plants grain and it grows well, I will send a great hailstorm to destroy it, leaving neither root nor stem, let alone leaves." After his death, he was reborn as a nāga called "No-Leaf."

For years, whenever the people planted and the crops began to grow, the nāga destroyed them with hail. The people starved and many died. The Buddha, pitying them, wished to convert this nāga. He took Vajrapāṇi, Ānanda, and Panthaka to the nāga's abode. The nāga, seeing the Buddha approach, became hostile and said: "I will now send hail to kill this śramaṇa." He rained down boulders. The Buddha glanced to his right at Vajrapāṇi, who understood the Buddha's intent and raised his vajra club, deflecting the great boulders and blocking the nāga's pool. The nāga was furious and fire shot from its eyes. The Buddha glanced to his right at Panthaka. Panthaka understood the Buddha's intent: the Buddha wished him to subdue the nāga. Panthaka used his supernatural power to become invisible, and with water extinguished the fire from the nāga's eyes. The nāga then emitted fire from its ears, nose, and mouth, and Panthaka extinguished each in turn. The bhikṣu further used his supernatural power to enter and exit through the nāga's eyes, ears, nose, and mouth, invisible within while showing only his hands outside. The nāga, witnessing this transformation, submitted in its heart. The Buddha then walked back and forth before the nāga with the three companions. On the stone there were four sets of footprints, yet only three people were visible. The nāga asked the Buddha: "Where is the fourth person?" The Buddha replied: "Those are the tracks of your teacher." "What is his name? Why does he not appear?" "His name is Panthaka." The Buddha wished him to appear at a distance. Panthaka, understanding the Buddha's intent, manifested a hundred paces away. The nāga saw him from afar and was overjoyed. It bowed in respect. The Buddha then conferred upon it the eight precepts of the upoṣadha. From that time on, wind and rain were harmonious, the crops flourished, and the people were at peace. By this cause, Panthaka is known to be foremost in becoming invisible.

**Cūḍa-Panthaka:** Foremost in transformation. "Cūḍa" means "extremely dull." This bhikṣu's spirit was slow and obtuse. The Buddha taught him to recite "broom-sweep" (*sǎozhǒu*). When he got "broom," he forgot "sweep"; when he got "sweep," he forgot "broom." For six years he concentrated solely on this, and his mind finally achieved understanding. He reflected: "A broom is a besom; sweep means to remove. The besom is an analogy for the Noble Eightfold Path; the dust is the three poisons. Using the besom of the Eightfold Path to sweep away the dust of the three poisons: this is the meaning of 'broom-sweep.'" Deeply pondering this principle, his mind opened in understanding and he attained arhatship. The so-called "transformation of form" means using the sublime wisdom of the Four Noble Truths to transform the five-aggregate body. Is there more to the story? There was a brahmin named Brahmadeva, also called Śāstrin ("World-Master"), because he had studied all the scriptures, charts, prophecies, astronomy, and geography without exception. Proud of his great learning, he went about seeking a challenger: "Who can debate with me?" He heard that among the Śākya bhikṣus, the lowliest was Cūḍa-Panthaka, and among the lay followers, the lowliest was Guptamitra. "I will debate these two." He went to challenge Panthaka. He said: "Can you debate with me?" Panthaka replied: "I could even debate with your ancestor Brahmā. How much more with you, a blind man without eyes!" The brahmin seized on these words and demanded: "What is the difference between 'blind' and 'without eyes'?" Panthaka was silent and could not reply. He thought: "I have no words to answer him. I will answer with supernatural powers instead." He rose into the sky by supernatural power, flying four *zhang* and nine *chi* above the ground, and sat cross-legged. The brahmin looked up in awe at the supernatural display. His reverence arose within, hoping for a clear response. At that time, Śāriputra was walking at the Jetavana and heard with his divine ear the brahmin debating with Panthaka. He knew Panthaka had run out of words and was responding with a display. "If I do not go, the bhikṣu will be humiliated and the brahmin will not be converted." He used supernatural power to assume Panthaka's form, making Panthaka's own form invisible. The transformed figure asked the brahmin: "Are you a god or a human?" "A human." "Is a human the same as a male?" "A male." "What is the difference between 'male' and 'human'?" "'Human' is the general term; 'male' refers to the bodily form. How can they not differ?" "Likewise, 'blind' refers to not seeing the karmic consequences of good and evil in this life and the next. 'Without eyes' means lacking the eye of wisdom to sever the fetters. When I said 'blind,' earlier, I also meant 'without eyes.'" The brahmin's mind was opened and he attained the pure dharma-eye. By this cause, Cūḍa-Panthaka is known to be foremost in transformation.

**Śākyarājan (Prince of the Śākyas):** Foremost in noble birth. There are four lineages: kṣatriya, brahmin, householder, and commoner. The reason he is called "noble" is that all who become śramaṇas share the one Śākya lineage. Just as the waters of four great rivers, each flowing from the mouths of an ox, lion, horse, and elephant, each with five hundred tributaries, all merge into the great ocean and become one taste. Therefore the ocean is called "great" and is esteemed above all the hundred streams. The Śākya lineage is the same. Therefore he is called foremost in noble lineage.

King Śuddhodana had three younger brothers. The youngest was named Miśraśuddha, who had a small child of four years. Once, King Śuddhodana was seated on his throne in the great hall with all his ministers assembled. The king thought: "If my son had not left home, I should have become a holy king. My son was born to be a holy king, yet he left home. What use is this crown?" He took off his crown and cast it on the ground, saying whoever was meant to wear it could do so. The ministers were all anxious and unhappy. At that time, the little prince was playing nearby. He saw the crown on the ground, picked it up and placed it on his head, sat on the ground with his left hand on his shoulder, and stroked his beard with his right hand. The king and his ministers were astonished. The king said: "This child acts by divine instinct. Perhaps he will be a holy king. All the marks of a holy king that my son had may now belong to this child." After eight years, the king heard that Siddhārtha had become a Buddha and converted the three Kāśyapas and their thousand monks, along with Upatiṣya and Kolita with their 250 disciples, making 1,250 bhikṣus in all. He was coming from Magadha to the Śākya country, having sent Udāyin ahead with the news. The king was overjoyed and resumed wearing his crown. He had the roads leveled, swept, and perfumed to receive the Tathāgata. When the Tathāgata arrived, the king saw that the bhikṣus, though pure in mind, lacked impressive bearing. He selected five hundred fine-looking Śākya youths to ordain. Śākyarājan was first among them. When the Buddha was in the monastery amid the great assembly, he discussed the meaning of noble lineage. King Śuddhodana came into the assembly and bowed to the bhikṣu Śākyarājan. Everyone was puzzled. The Buddha, knowing their thought and wishing to resolve their doubt, asked the king: "Why do you bow to this bhikṣu?" The king replied: "This bhikṣu surpasses me in two respects. There are three kinds of heaven: elected heaven, birth heaven, and pure heaven. I possess only elected heaven. This bhikṣu possesses both birth heaven and pure heaven. Birth heaven: at four years old, he naturally picked up my crown and placed it on his own head. No one prompted him; it arose of its own accord. Therefore it is called birth heaven. Pure heaven: he has now exhausted the defilements and dissolved the fetters, free of all dust and impurity. Therefore it is called pure heaven. Because of these two respects in which he surpasses me, I bowed to him." By this cause, the bhikṣu Śākyarājan is known to be foremost in noble lineage.

**Dharmagupta-Bhāṣita (Long-life Bhikṣu):** Foremost in loving travel. The story has its origin. When the Buddha was in the world, a householder named Dharmagupta-Bhāṣita came to the Buddha and paid his respects. The Buddha said: "Dharmagupta-Bhāṣita, it has been a very long time since we last met. How good that we can see each other now." Someone asked the Buddha: "Why do you say it has been a very long time?" The World-Honored One said: "Do you wish to know?" "Yes." The Buddha said: "Long ago, an *asaṃkhyeya* kalpa past, there was a buddha named Dīpaṃkara. I was then a brahmin named Chaoshu. When Dīpaṃkara Buddha was about to enter a city, I met him midway on the road. Seeing the Buddha's radiance shining forth, I exclaimed: 'The World-Honored One's radiance surpasses the sun and moon! The World-Honored One's virtue surpasses heaven and earth! The World-Honored One's mind surpasses a loving mother in compassion!' Looking at myself, I had nothing to offer. 'Now is the time; this excellent field of merit can be planted.' Seeing a patch of mud on the road, fearing it would soil the Buddha's feet, I spread my hair over the mud and let the Buddha walk upon it. The Buddha then prophesied: 'You are so courageous! After another *asaṃkhyeya* kalpa, you will become a buddha named Śākyamuni.'

At that time, another brahmin standing nearby gave rise to hatred and said: 'This man is no different from an animal, walking over someone's hair!' From that time through *asaṃkhyeya* kalpas, he was constantly reborn as an animal. He was then in the great ocean as a *makara* fish, seven thousand *yojanas* long. Five hundred merchants were sailing in a ship when they encountered this great fish, which opened its mouth and was about to swallow the ship whole. The five hundred men were terrified. Each called upon their own deity. The leader of the merchants said: 'In this world there is a buddha named Śākyamuni. He rescues people from danger as none other can. Let us all call his name, and perhaps we will be delivered.' They all called out in unison. The fish, hearing the Buddha's name, recovered its past-life memory and thought: 'Śākyamuni Buddha has already appeared in the world. Why am I still in a fish body?' It immediately submerged back into the water. The five hundred merchants returned safely. The fish then heaved half its body onto a sandbank and went without food or drink for fourteen days. When it died, it was reborn in a householder's family as a son named Dharmagupta-Bhāṣita. Now at last he has come and met me. That is why I said 'a very long time.'" Dharmagupta-Bhāṣita, hearing this full account of origins, went to the seashore to find the old remains. He saw a great fish on the shore, its bones with all flesh gone. He walked along the ribcage and reflected: "This is my former body." He scattered flowers upon the old remains. Dwelling on the past, his mind was suddenly and completely awakened, and he attained the Way. By this cause, he is called foremost in loving travel.

**Kāśyapa-ghoṣaka:** Foremost in gathering assemblies for dharma teaching, with a voice that could be heard far and wide.

**Bākula:** Foremost in longevity, living to 160 years. In past lives, he had served sixty thousand buddhas, always practicing loving-kindness. Toward all creatures, from flying insects to crawling things, any form of life, he constantly showed compassion. Not even the slightest thought of harming arose. Through this merit of loving-kindness, he now receives its reward. The Buddha told Ānanda: "Among those living today, none surpasses me in purity of skin and body, like a lotus untouched by muddy water, with a lifespan of exactly eighty years. Yet Bākula, living to one hundred and sixty, surpasses me. The Tathāgata adapts to the world, wishing to accord with sentient beings and not display anything extraordinary. Therefore his lifespan is eighty years. Bākula receives the reward of his past lives' merit of loving-kindness, and therefore receives a doubled lifespan." Some ask: is it merely loving-kindness that produces such longevity, or is there something more? There is. Long ago, when Vipaśyin Buddha appeared in the world, 168,000 bhikṣus traveled to teach the dharma. At that time, a householder of pure and modest character, of inherently good and humble disposition, did not drink alcohol. During a festival, friends encouraged him to drink a little. Under the influence, he went to the World-Honored One, bowed, and invited the Buddha and all his disciples: "May you accept my invitation for ninety days. Any bhikṣu who falls ill may come to my home for medicine and whatever is needed." After speaking, he returned home and fell asleep. When he woke, his wife told him: "You gave strict orders earlier to prepare all the provisions for the offering." The householder was startled: "What did I say?" His wife said: "Before you fell asleep, did you not say something?" "I do not remember saying anything." His wife said: "You said: 'I have invited the Buddha and his disciples for ninety days, to provide whatever is lacking.' Did you not say this?" The householder reflected: "The harm of wine goes this far." He was ashamed, but since the invitation was made, he carried through. The next morning, he burned incense at home and invited the World-Honored One from afar. A bhikṣu came asking for medicine. The householder asked: "What ails you?" "I have a headache." The householder said: "You must have fluid above the diaphragm pressing upward to the head. That is why you have a headache." He gave a single *harītakī* fruit: "Just take this medicine and it will cure you." The bhikṣu took the medicine and was immediately cured. Through this merit, for ninety-one kalpas he was never ill. He was born into a householder's family and lived to eighty years. He left home to study the Way and spent another eighty years as a monk, making 160 years in all. Once, while still a householder, he was involved in a wrestling match and briefly had a headache, which immediately passed. After that, he was never ill again. Therefore Bākula is called foremost in longevity. To live beyond the normal hundred-year span by an additional sixty years, in this age of the five impurities, is the most extraordinary. It is like a lotus flower growing in the midst of filth.

According to this treatise, the Cinnabar Collection has it in three fascicles. The *Kaiyuan Catalog* says four fascicles, with a note saying "some say three fascicles, some say five," which is merely a difference in how fascicles are divided; the text itself has no additions or omissions. The catalog has an annotation saying: "This single treatise comments on the *Ekottarāgama Sūtra*, from the opening prologue through slightly past the middle of the chapter on foremost disciples, ending at 'Śākyarājan bhikṣu.'" The *Fashang Catalog* says it was translated by Dharmarakṣa, but this is incorrect. The sūtra passages cited and the explanations of textual phrases both accord with the original sūtra and appear to be by the same translator as the *Ekottarāgama*. Other catalogs all say the translator is unknown, and we provisionally follow this determination. The *Sengyou Catalog* says it was compiled by Kāśyapa and Ānanda, but this too is incorrect. In the first fascicle of this treatise, "teachers of foreign lands" and "the Sarvāstivāda school" are both cited. Therefore we know it was not compiled by those two venerable ones.
