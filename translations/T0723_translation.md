# 分別業報略經
## A Brief Treatise on the Classification of Karmic Retribution

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0723

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Saṃghabhadra (僧伽跋摩). Composed by the Bodhisattva Mahāvīra (大勇菩薩).

---

> To the most victorious, unsurpassed Honored One,
> Whose knowledge and vision are fully complete,
> I bow my head in reverence,
> And to the Dharma and the Sangha of true practitioners.
> I now compile and set forth
> The teaching of the one whose knowledge and vision are complete,
> On the origins of the five destinies,
> Arising from pure and impure karma.
> For all beings throughout the world,
> I reveal the meaning of the sūtras,
> According to the reach of my wisdom,
> Classifying karmic actions and their fruits.
> The Buddha, having self-awakened to the Dharma,
> Was urged by all the devas to teach.
> He then went to Vārāṇasī
> And proclaimed the meaning of the true truths:
> Suffering and the cause of suffering,
> The complete cessation of the accumulation of suffering,
> The eightfold right path fully complete:
> The pure path that ends all suffering.
> The unsurpassed Honored One among humans
> Taught the fruits of suffering and karma.
> From these, in succession, are produced
> Afflictions and various karmas.
> The various characteristics of afflictions
> And innumerable karmic actions:
> I shall now classify them in sequence,
> Following the teaching of the Great Sage.
> As revealed in the sūtras,
> Not contrary to the characteristics of dharmas,
> With true and definite meaning:
> The wise should receive and uphold this.
> Not made by a sovereign god,
> Not without cause in their fruition,
> Not arising from an inherent nature,
> Nor produced from time.
> A sovereign god, absence of cause,
> Inherent nature, and time:
> Because fruits have gradations of excellence and inferiority,
> One should know these are not the causes.
> From ignorance arise afflictions;
> From afflictions arise karmic actions.
> Through karma, the various destinies open.
> I shall now explain the distinctions.

> Those who create unwholesome karma
> Follow their karma into the evil paths.
> To those guilty beings,
> King Yama speaks with compassion:
> "Birth, old age, illness, and death are suffering;
> The king's law has apprehended you.
> Did you not see those divine messengers?
> Why did you not give rise to a superior awakening?
> Generous giving, pure morality,
> Can tame the body, speech, and mind.
> What were you seeking
> That you did not make a higher aspiration?"
> "Unfortunately, I encountered evil companions
> And heard only of wrongful matters.
> They increased my greed, anger, and delusion.
> How could I give rise to pure karma?"
> "You never cultivated goodness
> But only performed evil deeds.
> Unaware that punishment was coming,
> Now you enter the hells."
> Then the prison guards
> Seize the guilty beings,
> Driving them toward the gates of hell;
> In terror, their hair stands on end.

> Reviving Hell, Black-Rope Hell,
> Crushing Hell, the two Screaming Hells,
> Avīci, the great hell without respite,
> Burning Hot and Great Burning Hot,
> Earth-Sea and Dung-Pit,
> Sharp Sword-Leaf Forest,
> Knife-Path, Sword-Branch Trees,
> Ash River, and Iron Cauldron Hell:
> Those who create evil karma
> Are born in these narakas.
> I shall now explain that karma
> And the distinctions among their painful retributions.

> In Reviving Hell, one dies and revives again,
> Passing through billions of thousands of kalpas,
> Because of bearing grudges and enmity,
> Mutually wounding and harming one another.

> Entrapping others through false ways,
> Sowing discord with divisive speech,
> Slander and false testimony:
> After death, one falls into Black-Rope Hell.

> Slaughtering, hunting, and other killing:
> After death, one enters Crushing Hell,
> Where mountains grind and press the body,
> Crushing it so blood and marrow flow.

> Governing without compassion,
> Enforcing harsh laws with many pretexts,
> Devising various expedients
> To inflict all manner of cruel torments:
> One also enters Crushing Hell,
> Receiving painful retribution according to karma.
> Turning wheels and crumbling mountains, awls
> And iron stones grind and pound.

> Listening to lawsuits, swayed by greed, anger, and delusion and fear,
> Judging cases in violation of what is right:
> One also enters Crushing Hell,
> Where iron wheels sever the body.

> Relying on one's strength and power,
> Fiercely oppressing the weak and helpless:
> One also enters Crushing Hell,
> Where black elephants come competing to trample.

> Oppressing multitudes of people,
> Making them wail and weep:
> After death, one falls into Screaming Hell,
> Where the entire body constantly blazes.

> Cheating people with scales and measures,
> Evil at heart but smooth of tongue,
> Words and deeds lacking honesty:
> One enters the Great Screaming Hell.

> Alas, the Great Screaming Hell!
> Seeing it makes one's hair stand on end.
> Within it one endures the most extreme suffering,
> For accepting deposits and not returning them.

> Calling what is not Dharma the Dharma,
> Calling the Dharma not the Dharma,
> Holding wrong views, denying cause and effect,
> Being insolent and slandering the noble sages:
> Such people as these,
> After death, enter Avīci Hell.

> Parents, virtuous people,
> Śramaṇas, and brahmins:
> Offending and causing them grief,
> After death, one enters Burning Hot Hell.

> Parents, virtuous people,
> Śramaṇas, and brahmins:
> With evil intent inflicting pain upon them,
> After death, one enters Great Burning Hot Hell.

> Those who go forth to cultivate the pure life
> But transgress the precepts of the vinaya,
> Mutually criticizing and disparaging one another:
> After death, they enter the Burning Earth Hell.

> Violating the precepts, abandoning right livelihood,
> Earning a corrupt living through deception:
> After death, one enters the Burning Dung-Pit,
> Where poisonous worms bore through bone and marrow.

> Hunting in the fields and burning forests and marshes,
> Burning and harming creatures on land:
> After death, one enters the Fire-Sword Hell,
> Where one is burned, flayed, and dismembered.

> Luring sentient beings,
> Feigning friendship to take their lives:
> Crows, kites, packs of hungry dogs
> Come competing to devour the flesh.

> Destroying the bridge of the true Dharma,
> Leading people into wrongful conduct:
> After death, one passes through the road of sharp knives,
> Which sever feet and cut through muscle and bone.

> Long-bodied centipedes,
> Shaped like beautiful women,
> Coil around the body and suck the marrow and brain:
> This is due to sexual misconduct.

> Touching others' wives and women
> With deep-rooted lustful attachment:
> One is driven up the Sword-Branch Tree,
> Impaled through the body going up and down.

> Devising various means
> To kill aquatic creatures:
> After death, one enters the Boiling Ash River,
> Where the entire body dissolves and rots.

> Swallowing blazing iron balls,
> Molten copper poured into one's mouth,
> Iron nails driven into one's body:
> All because of stealing others' property.

> Intensified tenfold unwholesome deeds:
> At death, one enters the hells.
> For the next degree of offenses, one falls among animals;
> The remainder enter the realm of hungry ghosts.

> Those who hate the unwholesome conduct of others
> Yet constantly delight in evil dharmas themselves,
> Who rejoice at seeing others' suffering:
> After death, they become servants of King Yama.

> Having explained the karmic actions
> By which the gravest offenders enter the hells,
> I shall now explain the karmic fruits
> Of the animal and hungry ghost realms.

> Bodily, verbal, and mental transgressions:
> Three of body, four of speech, and three of mind.
> If these deeds are not intensified,
> After death, one falls into the animal realm.

> From excessive desire are born geese, doves,
> Peacocks, mandarin ducks, and other birds.
> From deluded karma are born
> Maggots, ants, moths, and such.
> Without wisdom, being fond of beating and binding:
> One is reborn among elephants and horses,
> Or as cattle, sheep,
> Deer, and various wild beasts.
> From anger and hatred are born vipers and serpents,
> Bees, scorpions, and poisonous creatures.
> From arrogance and conceit,
> And from secretly harboring malice,
> One is reborn as a garjara bird,
> Or as a tiger or lion.
> From vanity, cold-heartedness, and hostility:
> Pigs, dogs, donkeys, foxes, and wolves.
> From miserliness and refusal to give,
> Jealousy and frequent hatred,
> Restlessness and an unsettled mind:
> After death, one falls among monkeys.
> From brazenness and shamelessness,
> And from excessive, unrestrained speech:
> According to one's karma, one receives the fruit
> Of being reborn as a crow or other bird.
> From crooked greed and insatiability,
> And from divisive speech that destroys friendships:
> One is reborn as a cat or civet,
> Or as a bear.

> Those who practice great generosity
> But are quick-tempered and wrathful,
> And do not cultivate right mindfulness:
> They are later reborn as great powerful nāgas.
> Those who practice great generosity
> But are haughty and scornful of others:
> Through such karma, they are born
> As great powerful garuḍas.

> Robbing and stealing from virtuous people
> Their food and delicacies:
> One falls into the pūtana class of ghosts,
> Eating excrement and corpses.
> Intimidating and frightening the foolish, weak,
> Sick, and destitute:
> One becomes a bhūta ghost,
> Always eating embryonic matter.
> Scowling, mean and wretched conduct,
> Miserly and excessively greedy:
> After death, one becomes a lowly hungry ghost,
> With a body extremely dark and emaciated.
> Miserly, not giving,
> Or giving and then regretting it:
> After death, one falls among ghosts that eat vomit,
> Feeding only on pus, mucus, and saliva.
> Not cultivating merit or wisdom oneself,
> While denigrating others' practice of generosity;
> Begrudging fine things to others while settling for coarse;
> Delighting in filthy and foul conduct;
> Dwelling in sewers and drains:
> One always eats what is impure.
> Always craving others' possessions,
> Having wealth but not consuming it,
> Preferring to discard rather than give:
> After death, one becomes an angry hungry ghost.
> Fond of exposing others' secrets
> And harming people to take their wealth:
> Due to residual offenses, one becomes a hungry ghost
> That always feeds on human vital essence.
> Using coarse words to irritate and trouble others,
> Fond of exposing others' secrets,
> Rigid and difficult to tame:
> One is born as a flame-mouthed hungry ghost.
> Inciting others to quarrel and fight,
> Hoarding wealth in constant fear of losing it,
> Merciless and rigid:
> One later becomes a worm-eating ghost,
> Always devouring moths and ants,
> The entire body ablaze with fire.
> Obstructing others' generosity,
> Having wealth but refusing to give:
> One is reborn as a giant-bodied ghost,
> With a huge belly and a throat like a needle.
> Not giving and not consuming oneself,
> Hoarding for children and grandchildren:
> Through this karmic condition,
> One is later born as a light hungry ghost.
> When the children and grandchildren perform merits on one's behalf,
> Through this, one obtains sustenance.

> If one is a village headman
> And extorts others' wealth to make offerings,
> After death, one becomes a kumbhāṇḍa ghost,
> With food and drink always at one's disposal.
> If one kills many beings
> And gives the flesh as charity,
> Due to residual offenses, one becomes a rākṣasa,
> Always enjoying abundant fine food.
> With slight anger and little sorrow,
> Constantly practicing generosity,
> Adorning oneself with incense and flowers
> And enjoying various performances:
> One later becomes a gandharva,
> A celestial musician serving the devas.
> Giving for the sake of profit,
> But much given to anger and divisive speech:
> One later becomes a piśāca,
> With a most ugly body,
> Wild hair and red eyes,
> Sharp claws and long fangs.
> Extorting others' wealth
> To practice generosity widely,
> By nature restless and flighty:
> One is reborn as a burden-bearing ghost.
> Much given to anger, hard to satisfy,
> Yet delighting in practicing generosity,
> Fond of wine, singing, and dancing:
> One is later born as an earth spirit.
> Offering carriages and mounts to parents,
> Providing for relatives and good people,
> But by nature very miserly:
> One is reborn as a sky-roaming spirit.
> Offering dwellings, carriages, food, and drink
> As acts of generosity:
> One is reborn as an ethereal spirit,
> Always accompanied by a celestial palace.

> I have now briefly classified
> The animal and hungry ghost realms.
> I shall next explain in order
> The fruits of the good paths among humans and devas.

> Those who cultivate various pure practices
> Are later born in the good destinies.
> According to their karma, they receive their fruits.
> I shall now explain this truthfully.

> Devas, humans, and asuras,
> Those who seek long life:
> Not harming living beings is the foundation.
> The wise should understand this.
> Compassionately not harming life,
> Delighting in cultivating all manner of merits,
> Firm and unshakable:
> Wherever one is born, one is free from calamities.
> Toward all classes of beings,
> Not beating, binding, or imprisoning:
> Through such non-harmful karma,
> Wherever one is born, one is always free from illness.
> Never practicing generosity,
> Yet also not stealing others' wealth:
> Wherever one is born, one is always in want,
> Seeking much but obtaining little.
> Able to practice generosity widely,
> Yet also plundering others' property:
> Wherever one is born, one always obtains wealth,
> But immediately loses what is gained.
> Never stealing others' property,
> And occasionally practicing some generosity,
> Skillfully acquiring material goods:
> Whatever one obtains is never lost.
> Never stealing others' property,
> And also practicing generosity widely:
> Wherever one is born, one is greatly wealthy,
> And what one obtains is never lost.
> Firmly maintaining the observance days and precepts,
> Wherever one is born, one encounters the true Dharma;
> All who see one feel love and delight,
> And one's good name spreads everywhere.
> Body and mind are always at ease and happy,
> Easily satisfied and knowing contentment,
> Calm and free from worry and affliction,
> Straightforward and practicing right conduct.
> Wishing to repay the kindness of those who gave one life,
> With a faithful mind, one cultivates meritorious deeds.
> Wherever one is born,
> One always receives one's father's remaining wealth.
> If one gives food,
> One obtains long life, good complexion, strength,
> Eloquence, abundant wealth and treasures,
> Freedom from illness, and a peaceful mind.
> Giving clothing, one obtains conscience and shame,
> A lofty and distinguished bearing,
> Complete with all fine human qualities;
> All who behold one are delighted.
> One's body is always safe,
> And one's mind is always content and joyful.
> Giving a dwelling, one obtains a residence,
> With palaces of extreme grandeur and beauty,
> Treasuries fully stocked,
> With all provisions according to one's wishes.
> If one gives wells and bathing pools,
> As well as pure water and drinks,
> Life after life one never suffers thirst;
> Whatever one desires always follows one's wish.
> Building bridges and boats for those not yet crossed,
> Giving shoes to the barefoot:
> One always obtains elephants, horses, and carriages;
> Such a one is a deva among humans.
> If one gives parks and groves,
> One always obtains surpassing and wondrous fruits.
> All who depend on one's shade
> Have their minds at peace and free from afflictive heat;
> They are loved and delighted in by all,
> And their retinue is fully complete.
> If one gives medicines,
> One is later born without illness,
> With long life, constant happiness,
> And complete with form, strength, and wealth.
> For immeasurable hundreds of thousands of lifetimes,
> One does not encounter an age of pestilence.
> In the end, one meets the King of Dharma-Physicians,
> Who permanently uproots the root of birth and death.
> Building latrines and removing filth:
> Later one has no trouble with excretion;
> Body and mind are always pure,
> And all who see one are delighted.
> Through this, one parts from all defilements
> And ultimately attains great peace.
> If one gives for the sake of rebirth in heaven,
> Or for fame and reputation,
> Or to repay kindness and in hope of reward,
> Or out of fear:
> The fruit obtained is not pure,
> And what one receives is mostly coarse and rough.
> When ancestors establish a tradition of giving
> That children and grandchildren continue without interruption:
> Wherever one is born, one receives inherited blessings
> Of immeasurable remaining wealth and treasures.
> One who always praises the merit of giving
> But, having wealth, does not give it:
> Wherever one is born, one is always poor and destitute,
> Wishing to give but having no resources.
> One who always praises the merit of giving,
> Compassionately and generously helping others:
> Wherever one is born, one obtains great wealth.
> One who does not delight in cultivating merit
> But always delights in cultivating wisdom,
> Without practicing generosity:
> Wherever one is born, one is always intelligent,
> But poor and without property.
> One who only delights in practicing generosity
> But does not cultivate wisdom:
> Wherever one is born, one obtains great wealth,
> But is foolish and without understanding.
> One who cultivates both giving and wisdom:
> Wherever one is born, one possesses both wealth and wisdom.
> One who cultivates neither:
> Throughout the long night, one dwells in poverty and darkness.
> Giving without right faith:
> Later one obtains abundant material goods,
> But what one receives is all coarse and rough,
> And one's mind is always attached.
> Giving with deep faith:
> One is born with surpassing treasures,
> What one receives is all fine and beautiful,
> And one's mind is always content.
> Knowing the good fields of merit well,
> Giving with respect and joy:
> Wherever one is born, one's retinue is harmonious,
> And all together receive the reward of happiness.
> One whose mind is always disparaging toward giving,
> Making offerings to the field of merit with a haughty attitude:
> Later one obtains much wealth,
> But though one has it, one cannot use it.
> One is of common stock, without distinction,
> And not admired or respected by anyone.
> One whose mind does not disparage giving,
> Who respectfully cultivates merit and wisdom:
> One is born with surpassing and exceptional wealth,
> And all one's relatives respect and honor one.
> Giving as is appropriate,
> With a mind that is always joyful:
> One is born with wish-fulfilling wealth
> And uses it in accordance with the Way.
> Acquiring wealth through right means,
> Giving wisely:
> Wealth and treasures come of their own accord,
> And whatever one obtains is never lost.
> Giving at the right time without hesitation,
> With clear understanding, cultivating meritorious generosity:
> Seeking little, one obtains much,
> And always receives timely provisions.
> If one cultivates pure conduct,
> Staying far from the beloved of others:
> One is born with a virtuous and fine spouse,
> Complete in beauty and moral qualities.
> The wise one always distances
> From improper conduct at improper times.
> With mind at peace and body free from fault,
> Fully endowed with the qualities of a person of integrity.
> Purely cultivating the holy life,
> Praised by the noble sages:
> The body one receives is always fresh and clean,
> And one's reputation flows far and wide.
> Revered by all,
> Honored and served by the devas.
> If in this world one stays far from alcohol,
> Free from confusion and delusion:
> One's will is strong, one's memory unfailing,
> And one's discernment of meaning encounters no difficulties.
> If one does not speak falsely,
> Being sincere and not deceptive:
> The body one receives is fully complete,
> Untouched by evil reputation.
> If one does not speak divisively,
> But skillfully reconciles disputes:
> One is born as a leader among people,
> And one's retinue is never broken.
> If one does not speak harshly,
> But uses pleasant words that delight all who hear:
> One always hears pure and clear sounds
> That proclaim the most surpassing and wondrous Dharma.
> If in this world one stays far
> From meaningless speech,
> Speaking sincerely, appropriately,
> Knowing the right measure, and for others' benefit:
> Later one's words are always correct,
> And those who hear them gladly believe and follow.
> If one does not covet others' possessions,
> Never giving rise to thoughts of acquisition:
> Wherever one is born, one's mind is at ease,
> And one always obtains the surpassing wealth of devas.
> If one does not give rise to anger,
> With a mind free from thoughts of beating, binding, and oppression:
> One always cultivates kindness and patience,
> And is later reborn in the Brahmā heaven.
> If in this world one has
> Deep faith and right views,
> Speaking of existence and non-existence truthfully,
> And drawing near to good spiritual friends:
> One is later born as a deva among devas,
> Whose wisdom-light surpasses the sun and moon.
> As I have explained above,
> The immeasurable pure karmas:
> According to one's practice, each receives rebirth
> Among the various rewards of the world.
> If one wishes to seek great benefit,
> Fame, and the happiness of heavenly birth,
> And to find the enduring within the impermanent:
> One should diligently cultivate the roots of virtue.
> Performing both pure and impure karma,
> One adorns oneself with various fruits.
> If born in the human realm,
> One receives a mixed reward of black and white.
> In childhood, prime, middle age,
> And in the time of decline and old age:
> Each according to its original conditions,
> One alternately receives painful and pleasant retributions.
> Karma performed and then intensified
> Is received in sequence.
> Karma performed but not intensified
> Eventually yields its fruit after a long time.
> If one gives inconsistently,
> Between times one encounters poverty.
> If one constantly practices generosity,
> Wealth and happiness are without end.
> If one is frequently wrathful,
> One is later always born ugly.
> With kindness, patience, and no anger,
> The body one receives is always beautiful.
> If one does not cultivate wisdom,
> Wherever one is born, one dwells in ignorant darkness.
> Fond of wisdom and studying broadly:
> One is discerning and encounters the noble sages.
> If one can subdue arrogance,
> One is born into a noble family.
> Foolish, deluded, and conceited:
> One is always born among the lowly.
> Flattery leads to a crooked body;
> Deceitful speech leads to a stunted and ugly form.
> Seeing a saint and feeling no joy:
> Wherever one is born, one is always foolish.
> Mute and unable to speak,
> Blind and unable to see.
> When elders, teachers, and good friends
> Compassionately offer counsel,
> If one disdains and refuses to listen:
> Wherever one is born, one will be deaf.
> Bathing those of virtue,
> Making offerings of fine incense and flowers:
> Such a person, wherever born,
> Has a completely beautiful appearance,
> With a body that is extremely soft,
> Pure as refined true gold.
> Defiling what should not be defiled,
> Engaging in wrongful conduct at improper places,
> Harming the bodies and destroying beings:
> Through this, one receives a eunuch's body.
> If in this world
> One's desire and lust blaze fiercely,
> And one's body, speech, and faculties
> All take on feminine ways:
> One is always born in a female body,
> With much desire and little wisdom.
> If one gives lamps and candles,
> Teaches the pure path,
> Shows the right road to those who are lost,
> And regards all beings with equal love:
> One later obtains pure eyes,
> Clear and unobstructed.
> Regarding all beings as one's own children,
> Compassionate toward the poor and sick:
> Wherever one is born, one has many children and grandchildren,
> Like the moon among the stars.
> A compassionate mother nursing her infant,
> Observing the fasts and cultivating pure conduct,
> The pregnancies of all she loves:
> She does not violate any of them.
> Through such pure karma,
> One is born with many attendant women
> Surrounding one in delight,
> Like Śakra, lord of the devas.
> Loving and respectfully serving parents,
> Honoring all one's elders:
> One is later always born noble,
> With an extremely gentle body.
> If in this world one
> Firmly upholds the precepts of the vinaya:
> One later obtains unshakable wealth,
> Like the snowy mountain king.
> If in this world one
> Never transgresses propriety,
> And whether asked or unasked,
> Gives equally, satisfying all:
> One is later born with wondrous marks,
> With cheeks and jaw like a lion,
> Endowed with inexhaustible wealth,
> Like an island of precious jewels in the sea.
> With body, speech, and mind pure,
> And also practicing generosity,
> Without jealousy toward others
> And without guarding one's own wealth:
> Through such karmic conduct,
> One is later born in Uttarakuru.

> If one aspires to fame
> And seeks the happiness of heavenly rebirth,
> One should rely on good teachers,
> Keeping body, speech, and mind pure.
> Guarding and protecting all one's possessions
> With love and care:
> Through such karmic conditions,
> One is later born among the Four Heavenly Kings.
> If in this world one's will is strong and one does not follow others,
> One's conduct involves much deception,
> Yet one also cultivates wholesome dharmas
> And delights in watching others contend,
> While also practicing fine generosity:
> Through such karmic conditions,
> One is later reborn as an asura.
> Filially honoring and purely serving
> Parents and all elders,
> Patient and seldom angry,
> Not delighting in watching quarrels:
> Through such karmic conditions,
> One is later born in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven.
> If one does not fight oneself
> And also does not watch others quarrel,
> Diligently cultivating wholesome dharmas:
> One becomes a Yāma deva.
> Observing the body well,
> Fond of learning and broadly studying,
> Concentrating on contemplating meaning,
> Delighting in cultivating pure merits:
> Through such karmic conditions,
> One is later born in Tuṣita heaven.
> Cultivating surpassing generosity,
> Delighting in reciting the sūtras,
> Skillfully practicing wholesome dharmas
> By one's own effort, not dependent on others,
> Through sincere diligence:
> One is later born in the Nirmāṇarati heaven.
> Cultivating surpassing generosity
> And skillfully guarding the sense-faculties,
> Diligently and without regression,
> Rejoicing in others' merits:
> Through such karmic conditions,
> One is later born in the Paranirmitavaśavartin heaven.
> Abandoning blazing desires
> And cultivating the four brahmā practices,
> With joy accompanying freedom from attachment:
> One is reborn in the Brahmā palace.
> Also leaving behind examination and investigation,
> And also crossing beyond the joy born of detachment,
> With joy and happiness born of concentration:
> One is born in the Ābhāsvara heaven.
> Leaving behind the joy and happiness of concentration,
> With only equanimity and happiness,
> In mindful equanimity and samādhi:
> One is born in the Śubhakṛtsna heaven.
> Having completely crossed beyond suffering and happiness,
> With neither suffering nor happiness and equanimity,
> With equanimity and pure mindfulness:
> One is born in the Bṛhatphala heaven.
> Perceiving the faults of perception,
> And wearied of the five kinds of existence,
> Deeply attached to the state without perception:
> One is born in the Asaṃjñisattva heaven.
> Cultivating mundane and supramundane
> Refined meditations,
> By the power of correctly abiding in refined meditation:
> One is born in the five Pure Abodes.
> Cultivating the soft and middling levels:
> The Avṛha and Atapa heavens.
> Cultivating the three upper levels:
> Next one is born in the three Pure heavens,
> And so on, progressively ascending
> All the way to the Akaniṣṭha heaven.
> Based on the perception of the impermanence of form,
> One transcends and cultivates boundless space.
> Next, contemplating boundless consciousness,
> Then abandoning it to reach nothingness,
> And further leaving nothingness
> To reach the state of neither-perception-nor-non-perception.

> I have now explained birth and death,
> The existence of existence and its fruits.
> Regarding those karmic fruits,
> The wise should contemplate them.
> One should cultivate pure karma,
> Leave suffering, and swiftly receive happiness.
> I have explained the various births and deaths
> And the differences among karmic actions.
> They are not produced by a sovereign god,
> Nor do they exist spontaneously.
> They are not from time, nor without cause;
> They arise solely from afflictions.
> Seeing that all existence is impermanent,
> The wise do not become attached.
> Liberating themselves from all bondage,
> They permanently reach the place of peace.
