# 佛說四輩經
## Sūtra of the Four Assemblies, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0769

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time the disciples of the four assemblies, Śakra, nāgas, ghosts, spirits, and yakṣas all came to the Jeta Grove, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and withdrew to sit in their usual places.

The Buddha addressed all the disciples: "The sūtras and Dharma-teachings that I now set forth, and all the admonitions I give, you should each guard your own minds and be mindful of them. In the final age, when poison blazes in the world, the disciples of the four assemblies, whether monastics or householders cultivating the path, will all be intoxicated and maddened by various forms, no longer following and employing the Buddha's sūtras and Dharma. Relying on their own foolishness and self-will, they will cause my path to grow thin and weak, making the people of the world slander my path. Know that this is brought about by the arrogance and heedlessness of such disciples!" Śāriputra straightened his robes, joined his palms, and with single-minded attention listened as the Buddha taught the Sūtra of the Four Assemblies.

The Buddha said: "If in the final age a man is able to leave home, shave his head, and enter the path, he should first of all abandon desire and attachment. His aspiration should be set on the Great Vehicle. He should always take kindness, compassion, joy, and protection as his guiding principles, abandon perceptual thinking, and extend his mindfulness universally to all creatures that crawl, fly, or creep, regarding them as no different from himself. He must not give rise to resentment or anger without cause. He should enter deeply into the prajñāpāramitā, and by means of its skillful expedients, always guide those in darkness to enter the boundless. Through the virtue of the precepts he should remove the afflictions and illnesses of the mind. He must not engage in the occupations of the world. One who can practice in this way may leave home and be called a practitioner of the path. One should not rely on my Dharma and, having left home, consider oneself trustworthy without single-mindedly contemplating the path. Perhaps there may be lowly persons who lean upon the path to sustain their own livelihood, benefiting no one, yet expressing opinions freely. As teachers and leaders they behave with frivolity and amusement, without examining themselves, causing the honored Dharma to grow thin and weak so that the people of the world lose faith. This is not the Dharma of my monastic disciples!"

The Buddha said: "If a woman leaves home, shaves her head, and enters the path, having abandoned desire and attachment, she should devote herself to quiet and secluded places. She must not dwell in the same temple as men who have left home. When receiving instruction from a teacher, she should have companions of the same kind and must not go alone to receive it. She should always go out late and return early, and must not recklessly spend the night outside the temple. She may instruct women only and must not instruct men. The robes she wears must not be of carved silk, colored cloth, or fragrant materials. She must not speak frivolously or playfully, must not covet wealth and treasures. With her practice of the precepts pure, she is called a monastic practitioner of the path. If she speaks frivolously and playfully, laughs before she has even spoken, if her mind and aspiration are not still and her thoughts and conduct are impure, if she speaks harshly and curses, speaks lightly and without restraint, cannot dwell in quiet places, and is arrogant and self-exalting without examining and correcting herself, then though she has left home, she is still a base person and is not a monastic disciple!"

The Buddha said: "If a man whose mind and aspiration are bound to the path is unable to leave home and dwells amid desire and attachment, he should receive and uphold the five precepts and observe the six monthly fasting days. First and foremost, he should be filial to his parents, manage his household and raise his children. Morning and evening he should burn incense and light lamps, bow to the Three Honored Ones, repent before the ten directions, and show reverence to the four assemblies. He must not be heedless, arrogant, or self-exalting, and should abandon stinginess and greed. With utmost sincerity he must not deceive or slight the people of the world. He must not sit or dwell together or travel in company with the wives and women of worldly people, sharing the same mat or the same room but different beds. He should put away the four evils and with the four immeasurable minds regard all beings universally: the old as his father or mother, the young as his younger siblings or children. He should always use the medicine of the prajñāpāramitā to wash away all manner of illness. He must not give rise to anger or harsh speech without cause. He should always use boundless skillful means to guide and lead the people of the world, causing them to enter the Great Vehicle. He must not teach profound sūtras and abstruse meanings to beginners; he should teach them only to those who have transcended perceptual thinking and are without obstruction. He must not adorn his clothing, must not exchange playful or frivolous words with worldly wives and women, or engage in correspondence and exchange with them. If one avoids such entanglements, one is a pure practitioner of the path. If one's conduct is impure, if one covets profit, wealth, and beauty, or adorns oneself in worldly clothing, competing to outdo one another, forming only unwholesome associations, gazing after the objects of desire, speaking frivolously and playfully, laughing before one has even spoken, claiming kinship with the Dharma while speaking fine words, appearing pure and empty on the outside but harboring greed and delusion within, with one's mind set on wealth and goods to provide for oneself and support wife and children, showing disrespect to the Buddha, no longer studying the honored sūtras, and instead practicing the arts and worldly medicine of non-Buddhist paths, such as divination, spirit-placation, talismans, spells, exorcism, treating illness, and the arts of spirit-mediation, or with respect to wealth and goods, scheming above and below with a thieving mind, using the arts of spirit-mediation and not upholding my Dharma: the deterioration that is to come in the future will all be due to such persons. Therefore they are not disciples who study my Dharma!"

The Buddha said: "If a woman is unable to leave home and, dwelling amid desire and attachment, delights in the path in her heart, she should uphold the five precepts and observe the six monthly fasting days. She should be filial to her parents, to her parents-in-law, to her sisters-in-law and husband. She must not beat or curse her children or maidservants. She must not go about carelessly or stay overnight at others' homes. She must not converse with worldly men, jest, or exchange laughter. She must not give rise to anger without cause, curse, or speak harshly. She must not testify to others' wrongs, and must not implicate others' children, wives, concubines, servants, or slaves with false accusations. She should always devote herself single-mindedly to contemplation of the path as her first priority. She must not discuss the features and appearance of Dharma with ordinary worldly people. She must not engage in disputes with other men, and must not instruct other men. She must not speak of worldly omens of fortune and misfortune, good and evil, or portents of calamity. She must not be arrogant and self-exalting. Day and night, in the three periods, she should burn incense, bow to the Three Honored Ones, repent before the ten directions, receive and practice the sūtras, and encourage herself by reciting their words. She must not hope for offerings or covet profit and wealth. She must not be jealous of her husband. She should humble herself as a woman and aspire to be reborn as a man to receive blessings, so that she may ascend to the heavenly palaces and behold the naturally-so. This is the Dharma of a faithful laywoman who studies the path. If she is unfilial, arrogant toward her parents-in-law, jealous of her husband, beats and curses servants and slaves, creates evil while justifying herself, harbors resentment, anger, and malicious intent toward others, then one who conducts herself in this way is not a proper female disciple who studies the Dharma!"

The Buddha addressed all the disciples: "Purify and guard your minds, protect your body and speech. Always use the four immeasurables to save sentient beings, and with the precious wisdom of the path, bestow grace upon all. Following the Buddha's admonitions, you will certainly attain deliverance from the world!"

All the disciples and those assembled, hearing the Buddha teach the sūtra, joyfully received and practiced it. They made obeisance and departed.
