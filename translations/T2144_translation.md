# 大秦景教流行中國碑頌
## Hymn of the Stele on the Propagation of the Luminous Teaching of Daqin in China

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2144

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, composed by Jingjing (景淨).

---

In the beginning, there was the Constant-and-True, Silent Stillness, prior to all priors and without origin. Profoundly deep was the Numinous Void, posterior to all posteriors and wondrously existent. Grasping the totality of the mysterious to bring about creation, surpassingly holy and supremely venerable: this is none other than our Triune Wondrous Body, the True Lord Without Origin, Aluohe.

He established the Cross to determine the four directions and set the primordial wind in motion to produce the two vital forces. The dark and the void were transformed, and heaven and earth were opened. Sun and moon began their courses, and day and night came into being. He skillfully fashioned the myriad things and then established the first human, separately bestowing an excellent harmony and commanding him to govern the sea of transformation.

The original nature of primal unity was empty yet not full. The pristine, unblemished heart was originally without craving or desire. But then Sadān spread falsehood, embellishing the pure essence with adornment. He drove a wedge of division into the great equality of "this is so," and opened a crack of darkness into the "that is not." Thus it was that the three hundred sixty-five kinds shouldered one another and cut their ruts, competing to weave nets of doctrine. Some pointed to material things to establish their creed. Some sank into the dualism of being and non-being. Some offered prayers and sacrifices to invite blessings. Some boasted of goodness to deceive people. Scheming intellects were busy, busy; emotions of favor labored on and on. Blindly they gained nothing. Tormented, they burned all the more. Darkness accumulated, the way was lost; long they wandered, unable to return.

Thereupon our Triune One, dividing his Person, the Luminous and Venerable Messiah, veiling his true majesty, appeared in the world as a man. The spirit-hosts of heaven proclaimed the glad tidings, and a virgin gave birth to the Holy One in Daqin. A luminous star announced the auspicious sign; the Persians beheld the splendor and came bearing tribute.

Fulfilling the old law of the twenty-four sages who had spoken of it, he governed families and nations with a grand plan. Establishing the new teaching of the Triune Pure Wind, beyond words, he refined the good through right faith. He determined the eight boundaries as a standard, refining dust into truth. He opened the three constant gates, revealing life and destroying death. He hung up the Luminous Sun to shatter the dark abode. The delusions of the demonic were thereupon all overthrown. He steered the bark of compassion to ascend the Palace of Light. All ensouled beings were thereupon ferried across.

His mighty work thus accomplished, at midday he ascended in truth. Twenty-seven books of scripture were left behind, spreading the primordial transformation to open the gates of the spirit. Baptism by water and wind washes away vain adornment and cleanses to pristine purity. The seal of the Cross unifies the four illuminations in unobstructed harmony. Striking the wood sounds the note of benevolence and grace. Worshipping toward the east, one proceeds on the road to life and glory.

Keeping the beard signifies outward practice. Shaving the crown signifies the absence of inner attachments. They do not keep slaves, making noble and humble equal among persons. They do not accumulate wealth, demonstrating the giving away of all that is one's own.

Fasting subdues discernment and brings accomplishment. The precepts are made firm through calm and careful vigilance. Seven times a day they offer praise, greatly sheltering the living and the dead. Once every seven days they make an offering, washing the heart and returning to the pristine.

The True and Constant Way is wondrous and hard to name. Its merits and functions shine forth clearly. Forcibly it is called "the Luminous Teaching." Now, the Way without a sage cannot flourish; a sage without the Way cannot be great. When Way and sage are in accord, all under heaven is illumined.

Emperor Taizong the Cultured, whose radiant glory inaugurated the dynasty, as a brilliant sage governed humanity. In the kingdom of Daqin, there was a man of supreme virtue named Aluoben. Divining by the azure clouds, he bore the true scriptures. Observing the harmonies of the winds, he braved hardships and dangers. In the ninth year of Zhenguan (635 CE), he reached Chang'an. The emperor dispatched the Chief Minister, Duke Fang Xuanling, to lead a guard of honor to the western outskirts to welcome him as a guest into the palace. The scriptures were translated in the library; the Way was discussed in the imperial chambers. Having deeply recognized it as true and correct, the emperor specially decreed that it be transmitted and taught.

In the autumn of the seventh month of the twelfth year of Zhenguan (638 CE), a decree was issued: "The Way has no constant name; sages have no constant form. They establish teachings according to the region, secretly saving the multitudes. Aluoben, a man of great virtue from the kingdom of Daqin, bearing scriptures and images from afar, has come to present them at the Supreme Capital. Examining the purport of his teaching, it is mysterious and based on non-action. Observing its essential doctrine, it gives life and establishes what is fundamental. Its words are without prolixity; its principles have already forgotten the fish-trap. It benefits creatures and aids humanity, and should properly be practiced throughout the world." The authorities were ordered to construct a Daqin temple in Yining Ward of the capital, with twenty-one monks ordained.

When the virtue of the Zhou lineage declined, the azure chariot departed westward. When the Way of the great Tang shone forth, the Luminous wind blew eastward. Orders were promptly given for the authorities to take the emperor's true portrait and make a copy on the temple wall. The celestial countenance in flowing colors, brilliant and bright, illumined the Luminous gate. The holy traces soared auspiciously, forever radiant in the Dharma-realm.

According to the *Maps and Records of the Western Regions* and the historical records of the Han and Wei, the kingdom of Daqin: to the south it borders the Coral Sea; to the north it reaches the Mountains of Myriad Treasures; to the west it looks upon the transcendent realm of the Flower Forest; to the east it meets the Long Wind and the Weak Waters. Its land produces fire-resistant cloth, soul-restoring incense, bright-moon pearls, and night-shining jade. Its people know no bandits or thieves, and all have happiness and health. No law is practiced that is not luminous; no ruler is established who is not virtuous. Its territory is vast; its culture is flourishing and bright.

Emperor Gaozong the Great reverently continued his ancestor's work, beautifying the true teaching. In every province, Luminous temples were established. Aluoben was further honored as the Grand Dharma-Lord for the Protection of the Nation. The teaching flowed through the ten circuits; the nation was enriched in primal grace. Temples filled a hundred cities, and families prospered in luminous blessings.

In the Shengli years (698-700 CE), the Buddhist monks grew aggressive, wagging their tongues in the Eastern Capital. At the end of the Xiantian era (712-713 CE), petty men greatly mocked and slandered in the Western Capital. But there was the monk-chief Luohan, the great worthy Jilie, and others, noble scions of the Golden Region and lofty monks beyond the world, who together restored the mysterious net and repaired the severed cord.

Emperor Xuanzong of the Supreme Way ordered the five princes, including the Prince of Ningguo, to personally visit the temple grounds and establish an altar. The Dharma-pillar, briefly askew, was raised yet higher. The Way-stone, briefly tilted, was righted again.

At the beginning of the Tianbao era (742 CE), the Grand General Gao Lishi was ordered to bring the true portraits of the five sages and install them within the temple. A hundred bolts of silk were bestowed. Reverently contemplating the imperial images: though the dragon's whiskers were far away, the bow and sword could still be approached. The sun-horn radiated light; the celestial countenance was but a cubit away.

In the third year, a monk named Jihe from the kingdom of Daqin, gazing at the stars toward transformation and facing the sun to honor the sovereign, arrived. By decree, the monks Luohan, Pulun, and a group of seventeen, together with the great worthy Jihe, performed meritorious services at the Xingqing Palace. Thereupon the emperor inscribed the temple's signboard. The plaque bore the dragon-script. Its jeweled mounting of emerald and jade blazed like crimson clouds. The imperial composition soared through the void, surging beyond the horizon. Imperial favors piled high as the steep peaks of the Southern Mountains; their beneficent grace was as deep as the Eastern Sea.

Where the Way is, nothing is impossible; what is possible can be named. Where the sage is, nothing is not accomplished; what is accomplished can be described.

Emperor Suzong, Cultured and Bright, reestablished the Luminous temples in five prefectures including Lingwu. Primal goodness provided resources and blessings were opened. Great celebrations arrived and the imperial enterprise was founded.

Emperor Daizong, Cultured and Martial, broadly extended the holy dynasty and governed through non-action. On every birthday, he bestowed celestial incense to announce accomplished merit and distributed imperial delicacies to honor the Luminous assembly. Heaven, through its beauty and benefit, can produce abundantly. The sage, embodying the primal, can nurture all things.

Our Jianzhong Sagely-Divine, Cultured-and-Martial Emperor spreads the eight policies to promote and demote the obscure and the bright, and expounds the nine categories to renew the luminous mandate. His transformation penetrates the mysterious principle; his prayers are without shame of heart. As for one who is vast and empty, focused and calm, broadly compassionate in saving all from suffering, and generous in bestowing upon all living beings: this is the grand plan of our cultivation and practice, the graduated approach of our guidance.

If the winds and rains are timely, all under heaven is at peace, people are well governed, creatures are pure, the living prosper, and the dead find joy; if aspirations arise and responses echo, if feelings stir and eyes are sincere: this is the merit and function of the Luminous power's working.

The great patron, the Gold-and-Purple Grand Master of Illustrious Emolument, the Vice Military Commissioner of Shuofang, the Acting Palace Superintendent, the imperially-bestowed purple-robed monk Yisi, was harmonious and fond of generosity, hearing the Way and diligently practicing. Coming from afar from the City of the Royal Court, he arrived in China. His arts surpassed three generations; his skills were comprehensive in all ten fields. He first devoted his service at the vermilion court, then inscribed his name in the royal tents.

The President of the Secretariat, Duke Guo Ziyi of Fenyang, when first commanding the armies in Shuofang, was assigned by Emperor Suzong to accompany him on the campaign. Though intimate enough to be admitted to the private quarters, he did not set himself apart from the ranks. As the duke's claws and teeth, as the army's ears and eyes, he was able to distribute stipends and bestowals without accumulating them at home. He presented lapis lazuli from Linse and spread golden carpets at places of rest. Sometimes he maintained old temples; sometimes he broadly enlarged Dharma-halls. Adorning the corridors and chambers, they soared like pheasant-wings in flight.

Further promoting the Luminous gate, he practiced benevolence and distributed benefit. Every year he assembled the monks of four temples for devout and refined offerings throughout all of the fifty days. The hungry came and were fed; the cold came and were clothed; the sick were treated and healed; the dead were buried and put to rest. Among the pure-living followers of the Teaching, such excellence has never been heard. Among the white-robed Luminous knights, now we see such a man.

Desiring to carve this great stele to proclaim his distinguished deeds, the hymn says:

> The True Lord is without origin, deeply still and forever constant.
> Wielding creative power, he raised the earth and established heaven.
> Dividing his Person, he appeared in the world, saving and delivering without limit.
> The sun rose, darkness vanished; all realized the true and the mysterious.

> Illustrious, illustrious, Emperor Wen! His Way crowned all former kings.
> Seizing the moment, he quelled disorder; heaven was cleared and earth expanded.
> Bright, bright, the Luminous Teaching came to our Tang.
> Scriptures were translated, temples were built; a vessel for the living and the dead.

> A hundred blessings arose together; ten thousand states were in health.
> Gaozong continued the ancestral work, further building fine temples.
> Harmonious palaces, spacious and bright, filled the central land.
> The True Way was proclaimed and made clear; the Dharma-Lord was enfeoffed.
> People had happiness and health; creatures were free from calamity and suffering.

> Xuanzong inaugurated the holy era, duly cultivating the true and correct.
> The imperial signboard radiated splendor; the heavenly writing was luxuriantly bright.
> The imperial image was dazzling and brilliant; all the land paid it high respect.
> All achievements were harmonious and flourishing; the people relied on his blessings.

> Suzong brought restoration. Heaven's might guided the imperial chariot.
> The holy sun spread its brilliance; auspicious winds swept away the night.
> Blessings returned to the imperial house; baleful vapors were forever dispelled.
> He stilled the turmoil and settled the dust, creating our Chinese domain.

> Daizong's filial righteousness: his virtue matched heaven and earth.
> Opening generosity to foster growth, all things were nourished by his beauty and benefit.
> Incense announced accomplished merit; benevolence was practiced as his offering.
> From the Valley of the Sun came his might; from the Cavern of the Moon, all gathered.

> Jianzhong holds the supreme rule, carefully cultivating bright virtue.
> His martial presence pacifies the four seas; his culture purifies the myriad lands.
> His torch illumines what people hide; his mirror perceives the nature of things.
> The six directions are refreshed and revived; all the tribes take him as their model.

> The Way is indeed vast! Its response is indeed subtle.
> Forcibly to give it a name: it proclaims the Triune Lord.
> The Lord can accomplish; the minister can describe.
> We raise this grand stele and hymn the primal auspiciousness.

---

Erected in the second year of Jianzhong of the great Tang (781 CE), the year being zuoe, in the taicu month, on the seventh day, the great Luminous day. The cleric Wenri established it. At the time, the Dharma-Lord was the monk Ningsu, who had charge of the Luminous assembly of the East.

One thousand seventy-nine years later, in the jiwei year of Xianfeng (1859 CE), Han Taihua of Wulin came to view it. Fortunately the characters were all intact. He had a stele-pavilion rebuilt to shelter it. He regretted that his old friend Wu Zifang, the Governor, could not make the visit with him, and was melancholy for a long time.
