# 箭喻經
## Sūtra of the Arrow Simile

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0094

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the city of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Venerable Māluṅkyaputta, alone in a quiet place, had this thought arise: "The World-Honored One has set aside wrong views and removed wrong views, and does not declare whether the world is eternal, whether the world is not eternal, whether the world is finite, whether the world is infinite, whether the life force and the body are the same, whether the life force and the body are different, whether the Tathāgata exists after death, whether the Tathāgata does not exist after death, whether the Tathāgata both exists and does not exist after death, or whether the Tathāgata neither exists nor does not exist after death. I cannot endure this; it is not what I want; it is not what delights me. If the World-Honored One would definitively declare that the world is eternal, I would practice the brahma conduct under him. If the World-Honored One will not definitively declare the world to be eternal, then after discussion I should leave. Likewise regarding the world being not eternal, the world being finite, the world being infinite, the life force and body being the same, the life force and body being different, the Tathāgata existing after death, not existing after death, both existing and not existing, or neither existing nor not existing. If the World-Honored One would definitively declare that my words are truth and others are fools, I would practice the brahma conduct. If the World-Honored One will not definitively declare my words to be truth and others to be fools, then after asking I should leave."

Then the Venerable Māluṅkyaputta, rising in the late afternoon, went to where the World-Honored One was. Having arrived, he prostrated at the World-Honored One's feet and sat to one side. Having sat to one side, the Venerable Māluṅkyaputta addressed the World-Honored One: "World-Honored One! While I was in a quiet place, this thought arose: 'The World-Honored One has set aside wrong views and removed wrong views, and does not declare whether the world is eternal, up to whether the Tathāgata neither exists nor does not exist after death. This is not what I desire; I cannot endure it or take delight in it. If the World-Honored One definitively knows the world to be eternal, the World-Honored One should declare it. If the World-Honored One definitively does not know the world to be eternal, simply say, "I do not know." Likewise regarding the world being not eternal, up to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing after death. If the World-Honored One definitively knows my words to be truth and others to be fools, the World-Honored One should declare it. If the World-Honored One does not know my words to be truth and others to be fools, simply say, "I do not know."'"

"Māluṅkyaputta! Did I previously ever tell you: 'If I declare the world to be eternal, you should practice the brahma conduct under me'?"

"No, World-Honored One!"

"Likewise regarding the world being not eternal, up to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing after death: if I declared my words to be truth and others to be fools, should you practice the brahma conduct under me?"

"No, World-Honored One!"

"And did you, Māluṅkyaputta, previously tell me: 'If the World-Honored One definitively declares the world to be eternal, I shall practice the brahma conduct under you'?"

"No, World-Honored One!"

"Likewise regarding the world being not eternal, up to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing: if the World-Honored One declared my words to be truth and others to be fools, I shall practice the brahma conduct?"

"No, World-Honored One!"

"Māluṅkyaputta! I did not previously make such a promise to you, nor did you make such a promise to me. You foolish person! On what basis do you revile me?" Thereupon the Venerable Māluṅkyaputta, rebuked to his face by the World-Honored One, stood silent with nothing to say, his face sweating, turning away, silent and without words.

Then the World-Honored One, having rebuked Māluṅkyaputta to his face, addressed the bhikṣus: "If any fool should think, 'I will not practice the brahma conduct under the World-Honored One until the World-Honored One definitively declares the world to be eternal,' that fool, without knowing it, will die in the meantime. Likewise regarding the world being not eternal, up to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing after death: 'I will not practice the brahma conduct under the World-Honored One until the World-Honored One declares my words to be truth and others to be fools.' That person, without knowing it, will die in the meantime.

"It is like a person struck by a poisoned arrow. Their relatives, out of compassion, wishing them to be safe and wishing to benefit them, seek an arrow-removing physician. But the wounded person thinks: 'I will not have the arrow removed until I know whether that person's family name is such-and-such, personal name is such-and-such, appearance is such-and-such; whether they are tall, short, or of medium height; whether they are dark or fair; whether they are of kṣatriya, brāhmaṇa, merchant, or artisan caste; whether they are from the east, south, west, or north. Who shot this arrow at me? I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know whether the bow was made of sāla wood, tāla wood, or kiṅkilā aṅgulī wood. I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know whether the sinew binding the bow was from a cow, a goat, or a yak. I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know whether the bow-grip was made of white bone, black lacquer, or red lacquer. I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know whether the bowstring was from cow sinew, goat sinew, or yak sinew. I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know whether the arrow shaft was made of śara wood, bamboo, or rāgalī wood. I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know whether the sinew binding the arrow was from a cow, a goat, or a yak. I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know whether the feathers were from a peacock, a crane, or a vulture, and from which wing they were taken. I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know whether the arrowhead was of vatsa, vāla, nāra, or kāravī iron. I will not have the poisoned arrow removed until I know the ironsmith's family name, personal name, and appearance; whether tall, short, or of medium height; dark or fair; from east, south, west, or north.' That person, without being able to learn all this, will die in the meantime.

"Just so, if any fool should think, 'I will not practice the brahma conduct under the World-Honored One until the World-Honored One declares the world to be eternal,' that fool, without knowing it, will die in the meantime. Likewise regarding the world being not eternal, the world being finite, up to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing after death. If any fool should think, 'I will not practice the brahma conduct under the World-Honored One until the World-Honored One declares my words to be truth and others to be fools,' that fool, without knowing it, will die in the meantime.

"Whether the world is eternal: this is a wrong view, and yet one should still practice the brahma conduct under me. Likewise, whether the world is not eternal, up to whether the Tathāgata neither exists nor does not exist after death: this is a wrong view, and yet one should still practice the brahma conduct under me. Whether the world is eternal: this is a wrong view, and one need not practice the brahma conduct under me on that basis. Likewise regarding the world being not eternal through to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing: this is a wrong view, and one need not practice the brahma conduct under me on that basis. Whether the world is eternal or not: without such wrong views, one should still practice the brahma conduct under me. Likewise regarding the world being not eternal through to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing: without such wrong views, one should still practice the brahma conduct under me. Whether the world is eternal or not: without such wrong views, one need not practice the brahma conduct under me on that basis. Likewise regarding the world being not eternal through to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing: without such wrong views, one need not practice the brahma conduct under me on that basis.

"Whether the world is eternal: there is birth, there is old age, there is illness, there is death, there is sorrow, grief, weeping, and unhappiness. Thus this great mass of suffering arises. Likewise, whether the world is not eternal through to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing: there is birth, there is old age, up to this great mass of suffering arises.

"Whether the world is eternal: this cannot be declared. Likewise, whether the world is not eternal through to the Tathāgata neither existing nor not existing: this cannot be declared. Why can it not be declared? Because it is not the goal, it is not the Dharma, it is not the brahma conduct, it does not lead to supernatural powers, does not reach the true path, and is not concordant with nirvāṇa. Therefore it cannot be declared.

"What do I definitively declare? 'This is suffering': I definitively declare. 'The origination of suffering, the cessation of suffering, and the path leading to the cessation of suffering': I definitively declare. Why do I definitively declare this? Because this is the goal, this is the Dharma, this leads to supernatural powers, this is the brahma conduct, this reaches the true path, and this is concordant with nirvāṇa. Therefore I definitively declare it. What I do not declare, you should set aside. What I do declare, you should hold to."

The Buddha spoke thus. The bhikṣus, having heard what the World-Honored One taught, were delighted and joyful.
