# 佛說佛大僧大經
## Sūtra of Buddhadatta and Saṃghadatta, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0541

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Juqu Jingsheng (沮渠京聲)

---

The Buddha was in the kingdom of Rājagṛha. In the kingdom there was a wealthy man named Lì, whose gold, silver, and various treasures, lands, houses, cattle, horses, and servants were beyond counting. Lì was old and had no heir. By the law of that country, if a person died without children, after death all his property was confiscated by the government. Lì prayed to the sun and moon, all the devas, ghosts and spirits, the Nine-Children Mother, mountain spirits and tree spirits, asking them all for a son, but could not obtain one. Lì reflected: "Whenever people face need, they go to pay homage to the spirits of mountains and trees, sparing nothing. My wealth is depleted, my estate unattended, illness follows upon illness, calamity without end, servants die, livestock do not breed; all of this is the work of evil portents. The spirit-teachers delude and mislead me, disturbing my household, claiming blessings will come, yet calamity piles ever higher. It is like a blind man swallowing poison thinking it a good medicine, hoping for cure, but the poison takes hold and destroys him. Now I kill living creatures to sacrifice to ghosts and spirits and yet hope for heavenly blessings. How deluded! In this world there is the Buddha-path, the way of the lofty sage. There are those who have attained the way of the immortals, called true persons, arhats. These true persons are pure as lapis lazuli gems. Only through diligent contemplation can one behold them. Those who follow this path guard tranquility and stillness, free of desire and free of craving, taking this as their joy. In this present life they attain peace, and at the end they are reborn in the heavens."

Lì then resolved: "I shall set aside my former practices and make offerings to the Buddha and the Three Treasures." After making offerings for one year, his wife gave birth to a boy. Lì said: "By making offerings to the Buddha, my wish has been granted." He named the boy Buddhadatta, after the Buddha's disciples, known as bhikṣu sangha. He continued his devotions, and before a year had passed, another fine son was born. He named him Saṃghadatta. Lì raised the two boys and guided them in the holy path. Saṃghadatta was by nature benevolent and caring toward all beings, filially devoted to a degree hard to surpass. He recited the Buddha's Dharma and precepts, kept close to the śramaṇas, and was pure and content. His parents, seeing his aspiration, loved him especially.

Their father fell ill and was confined to bed. He called his elder son and, with tears, admonished him: "There is birth and there is death; all things are impermanent. Those who keep the precepts are safe; those who violate the precepts are in peril. Hold fast to the Buddha's precepts, and from beginning to end you shall be free of calamity. Saṃghadatta is still young, benevolent, filially devoted, and pure." Having entrusted Saṃghadatta to his elder son, his words just finished, he suddenly passed away.

The younger brother, bereft of his father, alone and without support, repeatedly asked his elder brother: "I wish to become a śramaṇa."

By the custom of that country, when a young man wished to seek a wife, he would pretend to say: "I wish to become a śramaṇa." His parents, fearing this, would immediately arrange a marriage for him. Buddhadatta, thinking his brother was like those others, immediately searched for the most virtuous girl of the finest family in the country. Her name was Kuàijiàn, and she was dazzlingly beautiful, without equal. Her height, her build, neither too plump nor too thin, were perfectly proportioned. She was chaste, compassionate, and filial, like the moon among the stars. Among all the women of the country, none failed to admire her.

When the bride arrived and entered the hall, the elder brother gathered the guests. The entire clan was joyful and harmonious. Before the assembled guests, the elder brother teased his younger brother: "On this day, would you still become a śramaṇa?"

Saṃghadatta answered: "Elder brother! Release me and let me become a śramaṇa. It is truly my longstanding wish." The elder brother then realized the boy truly intended it. Joking, he said: "You may follow your wish."

The younger brother was overjoyed. He made obeisance to his elder brother and that very day entered the mountains. He saw a śramaṇa, young and handsome, dwelling alone beneath a tree. He approached with palms joined, bowed his head in greeting, stood aside, and asked: "Venerable one, what led you to become a śramaṇa?"

This person had already attained the path of arhatship and could see innumerable kalpas, past and future. He said to Saṃghadatta: "The Buddha's sūtras say: 'A person attached to lust is like one whose body is on fire. He is like one holding a torch and walking against the wind. If he does not put it down in time, the fire will burn his hand. He is like a crow clutching meat while hawks and falcons chase him. If the crow does not drop the meat, disaster will befall its body and life.' Lust is just like this; nothing about it is without peril. That is why I became a śramaṇa. Honey smeared on the edge of a blade: a child, greedy for the sweetness, licks it and risks cutting his tongue. A person attached to lust, seeking only to satisfy his foolish mind without thinking of the consequences, risks burning his own body. It is like a starving dog finding a dried bone by the road. It gnaws and chews, wounding its mouth and chipping its teeth, only injuring itself with no benefit. Lust is like this: through a hundred thousand billion kalpas it brings not even a hair's breadth of merit, but only the retribution of the three evil destinies. That is why I became a śramaṇa. It is like trees lush with flowers and fruit: travelers covet them and hurl sticks and stones. In an instant, the flowers are damaged, the fruit falls, the branches and leaves are torn. The tree, because of its flowers and fruit, invites its own destruction. The moth, coveting the beauty of the flame, flies into the lamp. Its body is scorched and consumed; what did it gain? Those deluded by lust cannot distinguish good from evil. They abandon the wise and follow the foolish. Day by day they sink into darkness. They bring ruin to kingdoms and destruction to multitudes. After death they enter the hells. Their evil clings to them, their sin is complete, and repentance comes too late. The Buddha perceived this truth and revealed the holy path. I look to the Buddha's kindness, having been able to behold the sūtra precepts. My mind keeps to purity, and alone I am free of calamity. When I look back at the ways of the world, I see only delusion. That is why I became a śramaṇa."

Saṃghadatta, hearing this, bowed to the ground at his teacher's feet, knelt, and said: "The Buddha is truly the supreme sage, honored of all the devas! The sūtra has extinguished my ignorance and entered my heart. I wish to leave behind the turbidity of the world, walk the path of purity, and receive the śramaṇa precepts as my glory and fortune." The teacher then instructed him in the weighty precepts of the śramaṇa. After attending his teacher for several months, Saṃghadatta said to his teacher: "I wish to enter the mountains to abide in still meditation and seek the path of arhatship. Only then will my afflictions be extinguished."

The teacher said: "To dwell alone in the mountains is extremely difficult. One who dwells in mountain wilds should learn the constellations and understand the seasons. One should always store up water, fire, roasted grain, and honey. Why? Because when thieves and bandits come seeking water, fire, roasted grain, and honey in the hours from midnight to dawn, one should know to give them what they want. If one goes against their wishes, the thieves will kill."

Saṃghadatta said: "Yes! I reverently receive your compassionate instruction. I shall learn everything you command." Thereafter he entered the mountains.

His elder brother thought: "My brother has become a śramaṇa and will never take a wife." The wife Kuàijiàn was without equal in beauty. The elder brother's heart desired her. He went to Kuàijiàn, carrying a zither, and playing it seductively, he sang a song of lust:

> "Splendid golden flowers
> Grow in the open fields.
> If not picked in season,
> They wither and are cast away.
> How lush and flourishing you are,
> Your beauty ever fresh and new.
> Share your joy with me
> And strengthen this bond between us.
> Once the years have passed,
> Who will still treasure you?"

Kuàijiàn immediately understood that the brother intended wickedness. She answered Buddhadatta in song:

> "Majestic is my teacher,
> Honored of devas and humans.
> His disciples are pure,
> Known as śramaṇas.
> The true is called holy;
> Lust makes one a beast.
> I have received the solemn precepts
> And shall not serve two lords.
> I would rather die in an instant
> Than live in unchastity."

Buddhadatta composed a mournful and beguiling song:

> "My heart was set on you,
> So I sought a worthy matchmaker,
> Asked your name, consulted your teacher,
> And chose an auspicious time.
> In anxious anticipation
> I feared you would not come.
> Now that I behold your radiant face,
> My heart is filled with delight.
> If we do not now unite,
> Was not all this in vain?
> This vow is settled;
> Why do you, fair lady, hesitate?"

Kuàijiàn answered in alarm:

> "The Buddha established propriety;
> Between high and low there is order.
> An uncle's wife is as a daughter;
> A husband's elder brother is as a father.
> I have received and keep the precepts,
> Rising higher day by day.
> The true stands equal with the holy;
> Lust is no better than vermin.
> Alas, elder brother!
> How can you speak such words?"

The elder brother was besotted, his desire for Kuàijiàn growing ever more intense and impossible to change. Kuàijiàn sang again:

> "When a person lives in the world,
> Two things must be kept far away:
> Unfilial conduct and lustful disorder.
> To act against the Buddha's precepts,
> The devas and the worthy
> Shall mark you as their own."

Buddhadatta sang:

> "Your beauty and your form
> Are blazing and resplendent.
> Among all the beautiful women under heaven,
> Who can compare with your face?
> My heart delights in you;
> Therefore I have crossed great mountains."

Kuàijiàn thought: "This man desires me. His deranged lust will bring great calamity. I should describe the foulness and impurity of the body; only then will he desist." Kuàijiàn continued:

"You covet my body, but what is good about it? The head has nine bones joined together to form the skull. Inside there is only the brain. The face has seven orifices, all discharging mucus and saliva. Skin wraps the bones. As for the head and neck that you covet, they are merely skin and flesh wrapping each other. The body has hair, nails, teeth, skin, muscles, blood, brains, and bones, flesh. In the abdomen are the heart, spleen, kidneys, intestines, stomach, fat, lungs, excrement, and urine, pus, blood, heat, and cold. The feet connect to the shins, the shins to the thighs, the thighs to the hips, the hips to the waist, the waist to the spine, the spine to the ribs, the ribs to the neck, the neck to the skull. The arms connect to the elbows, the elbows to the shoulders. I am like a painted vessel filled with excrement and urine. The impurities within the body are loathsome to this degree. What is there to covet? Whatever people ordinarily find desirable, if one speaks of its foulness, the mind will find it worthless."

Buddhadatta thought: "This woman thinks of her husband. How would she ever listen to me? If I kill my brother, then she will have no choice but to follow me." In his rage, Buddhadatta went and recruited known criminals. He found reckless men at a tavern, approached them, and said: "You know the household slave I keep? He ran away and became a śramaṇa. He is now in the mountains."

The thieves said: "We know him."

Buddhadatta then gave them gold and silver and commanded them to kill the slave: "Quickly bring me his head, and the clothing on his body, his Dharma staff, and the sandals on his feet. Bring them all back to me, and I will reward you again with gold and silver."

The thieves were delighted and said: "Leave it to us." They went into the mountains. When they reached the younger brother's dwelling, they called out: "Śramaṇa! Come out at once!"

The younger brother came out and said: "What do you seek, sirs? I have water, fire, roasted grain, and honey to eat."

It was already past midnight. The thieves said: "We do not seek water, fire, roasted grain, or honey. We have not come to ask the time. We want your head, to take it away."

The younger brother, hearing this, was greatly terrified. He wept and said: "I am not the son of an elder or a lord! I abandoned the world for the path and have no quarrel with anyone. I have studied the path only a short time and have not yet attained the fruits of stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, or arhatship with the six supernatural powers. What good will killing me do?"

The thieves said: "We have come for your head. Your empty words and pleas for mercy are useless."

The younger brother thought: "These thieves have only heard I come from a wealthy family and think I have brought treasures here." He said to the thieves: "If you want treasures, my elder brother is at home. His name is Buddhadatta. I shall write a letter asking him to give you treasures. Whatever you desire, he will satisfy your wishes."

The thieves said: "It is your brother who sent us to kill you."

The younger brother said: "Then I am about to die. This is because of that woman. My teacher warned me beforehand: a person dwelling with lust is like one holding a torch walking against the wind; if he does not put it down in time, the fire will burn his hand. Like honey on a blade. Like a hawk chasing a crow. Like a dog gnawing a dried bone. Like a tree's flowers and fruit. Beauty brings harm to the body. How profound was my teacher's warning."

Weeping, he begged the thieves: "Grant me one year of life, so I may attain the path. I shall always be here; killing me then will not be too late."

The thieves said: "We want your head now. What do you mean, one year? Mountain-dwelling practitioners often attain the path. I fear you will use your supernatural powers and fly away. Say no more. We will take your head."

The younger brother said again: "Please do not kill me at once. First cut off one of my thighs and place it before me." The thieves then stepped forward and cut off one thigh, placing it before him. Enduring this pain, the agony beyond words, a deva descended to the practitioner and said: "Do not be afraid. Hold firm to your mind. In your past lives you were among the animals, and people slaughtered you and sold your flesh, not just once. In the hells and among hungry ghosts you have experienced it all. Suffering and pain have been with you from the beginning, not only now."

Saṃghadatta said to the deva: "Please, I beg you, go to my teacher and let him know. Through my teacher's guidance I was shown the path. Where shall I go after death?"

The deva immediately went and told his teacher: "Your worthy disciple is about to be killed. He weeps and begs for mercy, wishing to see his teacher."

The teacher flew to his disciple's side and taught him the Dharma: "Heaven and earth and Mount Sumeru will all one day be destroyed. The oceans will one day dry up. The seven suns will one day bring ruin. In the world there is a wind called Vairambhaka; when it arises, mountains crash into one another. Yet even this wind will one day cease. How much less your small body? It is beyond all comparison. You need only think of the Buddha. The Buddha always taught impermanence: what flourishes must decline, what comes together must part, glory and position cannot be preserved, and the body is no different."

Saṃghadatta then attained the fruit of stream-entry. When the second thigh was cut, he again reflected on his teacher's precepts and attained the fruit of once-returning. When the thieves cut off his left hand, he again reflected on his teacher's precepts and attained the fruit of non-returning. When the thieves cut off his right hand, he again reflected on his teacher's precepts and attained the fruit of arhatship. He was no longer afraid of the three evil destinies. Over birth and death he was sovereign, with nothing more to fear.

Saṃghadatta said: "Bring tree bark." They peeled bark from a tree and gave it to him. Saṃghadatta took a twig as a brush and pricked his own body for blood. He wrote on the tree bark: "Elder brother! Are you well and at ease? When our parents were alive, they entrusted me to your care. You did not honor that trust and violated our parents' dying instruction. On account of a woman, you have done violence to your own flesh and blood. To violate a parent's compassionate instruction is to be unfilial. To cruelly take a person's life is to be inhumane. To kill even one animal incurs no small sin; how much more to kill an arhat? I am about to cease, but you have brought this upon yourself. Think on it: my body, having form, can be killed. But the good departure into stillness, can you harm that? This is our long farewell. Strive! May you follow the true path."

He then stretched out his neck, extending it two feet, and said to the thieves: "Cut off my head. It is like cutting a head made of mud. Have no fear. I only fear that you will fall into the hells."

The thieves stepped forward and cut off his head. They took the clothing, staff, sandals, and bowl from his body and brought them to his elder brother. They gave the elder brother the clothing, staff, sandals, and bowl, and the elder brother rewarded the thieves with gold and silver. The elder brother took his younger brother's head and fashioned a dummy body, placing the head upon it. He dressed it in the clothing, and placed the staff, bowl, and sandals alongside it. He said to Kuàijiàn: "Your husband has returned home. Go and greet him."

Kuàijiàn was overjoyed and rushed to the spot. She saw him sitting with closed eyes and thought he was in meditation. She dared not call out. She prepared fine food and waited, thinking: "When he finishes meditating, I shall serve him the meal." By midday he had not stirred. She came forward and said: "It is already noon. I fear the time for eating will pass." Wondering why he did not respond, she tugged at his robe to rouse him. The head fell to the ground, and the body scattered apart, each piece in a different place. She was immediately struck with terror and cried out: "My husband, has it come to this? Have I been destroyed by a vicious murderer?" Grief and fury called upon heaven; her liver and heart were torn apart. Blood burst from her mouth, and she suddenly died. Her precept practice had been pure and undefiled, as hard to stain as the sky. Her heart was planted in the holy standard, as hard to move as the earth. Her chaste and virtuous conduct was as lofty and as hard to fathom as the heavens. Before she died, the devas sighed in sorrow and prepared a place for her rebirth. They welcomed her spirit, and she was reborn in Trāyastriṃśa heaven. By enduring a brief moment's resistance to lust, she obtained the inexhaustible glory of heaven.

The elder brother went to the inner chamber to see what had become of the wife. He saw his brother's head and body scattered in disarray, and his brother's wife lying dead to one side, blood from her mouth. Seeing her corpse, he cried out: "How terrible! I have acted against heaven! What I have done is unspeakably cruel, and it has come to this!" The elder brother then went to the thieves and asked: "When my brother was dying, did he leave any last words?"

The thieves said: "He left a letter." They showed it to him. The letter was agonizingly poignant. When he had finished reading it, his insides were choked and blocked, tears streaming down: "I violated our parents' dying instruction of compassion. I have done violence to my own flesh and blood, and have killed an arhat. Overwhelmed with grief, I shall die and enter the hells."

The king and his ministers and people, hearing what had happened, wiped their tears, choked with sobs, and praised the younger brother's pure virtue. They gave him funeral rites. The fourfold assembly erected a stūpa. Devas, nāgas, and ghostly spirits thronged the sky, scattering flowers and burning incense. None were without sorrow. The wife Kuàijiàn was given funeral rites by the people of the country. The mourning shook the entire nation. The devas praised her: through diligent practice she attained the path; the five precepts were never compromised; through chastity she was reborn in heaven. But one who violates the Buddha's Dharma and teaching, who is unfilial and murders a sage, after death enters the hells to be burned and tortured, and the years of suffering are beyond counting.

The Buddha then told his disciples that from this time forward they should admonish and encourage one another to cherish desirelessness.

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, the disciples were filled with joy, made obeisance, and departed.
