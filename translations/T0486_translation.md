# 師子莊嚴王菩薩請問經
## Sūtra on the Questions of Siṃhavyūharāja Bodhisattva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0486

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Puṇyodaya (那提)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in the city of Rājagṛha, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty great bhikṣus, five hundred bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, and immeasurable eight divisions of devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans and non-humans, who surrounded him on all sides, listening to the Buddha teach the Dharma.

At that time, among the assembly there was a bodhisattva-mahāsattva named Siṃhavyūharāja, who rose from his seat and came before the Buddha. He prostrated at the Buddha's feet, circumambulated him innumerable times, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! In the past, I have attended the great assemblies of hundreds of thousands of koṭi-nayutas of Buddhas, yet I have never beheld what I see today. I wish to ask a question; may you kindly grant me permission."

The Buddha said: "Ask whatever you wish; I shall explain it for you."

Then Siṃhavyūharāja Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What superior practices did the Tathāgata cultivate in the past, that you have now attained this status as the most honored among devas and humans, surrounded and attended by great bodhisattvas, great śrāvaka assemblies, and the eight divisions of devas and nāgas, who make offerings, pay reverence, honor, and praise you? May you expound the causes and conditions of former times, so that sentient beings may obtain great and excellent benefit."

The Buddha told Siṃhavyūharāja Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva: "Excellent! Excellent! You are now able to ask this question for the peace and happiness of sentient beings. Listen well and carefully reflect. I shall explain it for you. Good man! I recall that in immeasurable past worlds, there was a Buddha who appeared, named Acintyaprabhāsa Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Vidyācaraṇasaṃpanna, Sugata, Lokavid, Anuttara, Puruṣadamyasārathi, Śāstā Devamanuṣyāṇāṃ, Buddha, Bhagavān. At that time there was an elder named Uttaradāna, who was self-reliant in his wealth and power, did not believe in the correct Dharma, and had no mind of reverence toward the Buddha. There was a bhikṣu named Vijayasambhava, who, seeing this elder, gave rise to a compassionate mind and conceived a great skillful means, determined to cause this person to give rise to aspiration, cultivate practices, and attain perfect enlightenment. Having conceived this thought, he went to the elder's home. At that time, Uttaradāna, beholding the bhikṣu's extraordinary countenance, majestic appearance, complete virtues, tranquil faculties, and resplendent radiance, rose respectfully to welcome him. After prostrating, he offered a seat and invited him to sit. Joining his palms, he said: 'I am of humble virtue, and am unexpectedly honored by your visit.'

"Then the bhikṣu told the elder: 'There is a great Dharma-gate called the Eight Maṇḍalas, of immeasurable merit. I shall now teach it to you, wishing to broadly benefit all devas and humans with wisdom. If sentient beings hear this Dharma-gate and are able to practice it, wherever they are born they will obtain four superior rewards: first, they will be born together with good spiritual friends and great bodhisattvas, have a great retinue, and their resources and wealth will be abundant; second, with a large retinue, they will be free and unhindered; third, their bodily form will be complete and they will be free from illness; fourth, all their necessities will appear naturally at the mere thought, and even if crushed by a mountain, their bodies will feel no pain. They will be able to know the thoughts in the minds of sentient beings, and with loving-kindness and compassion, will protect and aid them.'

"Then Uttaradāna, hearing this Dharma, was overjoyed and bowed again with deep reverence, praising: 'Excellent! May you broadly expound the most supreme Dharma-gate of the Eight Maṇḍalas. I shall cultivate and study it.'

"The bhikṣu said: 'If you wish to know this most supreme Dharma, first make this vow: \"I wish to make offerings to all the Buddhas of the three times, the great assembly of bodhisattvas, and the śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas.\" Having spoken thus, at the site of the practice, one should construct a square altar called a maṇḍala. Its dimensions may vary according to circumstances; at the very smallest, it should be four finger-widths across, or one hand-span, using various kinds of incense and other materials, or drawn upon the ground. Within the square enclosure, arrange eight circular platforms for the purpose of making offerings to eight bodhisattvas. Which eight? Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Maitreya Bodhisattva, Ākāśagarbha Bodhisattva, Samantabhadra Bodhisattva, Vajrapāṇi Bodhisattva, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Sarvanīvaraṇaviṣkambhin Bodhisattva, and Kṣitigarbha Bodhisattva. Thus, elder! This most supreme Dharma-gate of the Eight Maṇḍalas was taught by that Acintyaprabhāsa Tathāgata. I personally received and upheld it, and now teach it to you. You should cultivate and study it and widely propagate it. Through these roots of merit, dedicate them toward anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

"'Elder, you should know: if there are those who practice these eight dharmas, it is as though they make offerings to all the Buddhas of the three times, the great assembly of bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, and śrāvakas. Such persons will always be protected by the devas. If a king of a country can personally cultivate and study this, or commission others to perform it, all evils within the kingdom will be extinguished. Good men and good women who are able to cultivate and study the eight Dharma-gates will not, after death, fall into the evil destinies, border regions, wrong views, unwholesome conduct, or be born into a poor family. Therefore you should know: those who wish to obtain superior rewards in the present and future should study these eight Dharma-gates as described above. Those who wish to be born with a dignified body, intelligence, sharp faculties, and wisdom, and those who wish to be born in the heaven of the Four Great Kings, should also cultivate and study the Eight Maṇḍalas. Likewise, those who wish to be born in the Trāyastriṃśa Heaven, Yāma Heaven, Tuṣita Heaven, Nirmāṇarati Heaven, or Paranirmitavaśavartin Heaven, and even those who wish to become Śakra, a Brahmā king, a Māra king, or a wheel-turning holy king, should all cultivate and study these eight dharmas as described above. Those who wish to be born among the great families of devas and humans, with a retinue fully accomplished, wealth and treasures overflowing, body and mind at peace, and fame spread far and wide, whose edicts are all believed and accepted, and who are the most honored and most supreme among all assemblies: all should cultivate and study these eight dharmas. Those who wish to attain the fruit of a stream-enterer, a once-returner, a non-returner, or an arhat, or the path of a pratyekabuddha, or to enter the stages of a bodhisattva, and even anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi: all should cultivate and study the Dharma-gate of offerings to the Eight Maṇḍalas.'"

The Buddha told Siṃhavyūharāja Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva: "At that time, the bhikṣu Vijayasambhava, who might he have been? He is now the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī. At that time, the elder Uttaradāna: that was none other than I, Śākyamuni Buddha. From that time onward, for many kalpas, I cultivated and made offerings through this Dharma-gate of the eight practices, fully obtaining the meritorious benefits described above. Other sentient beings who are able to cultivate and study it will also obtain the same as what I have attained. Good man! In my practice of the bodhisattva path, throughout three asaṃkhyeya kalpas, I perfected the six pāramitās, benefited sentient beings, and attained perfect and true enlightenment, with radiance, majestic virtue, and power to defeat the armies of Māra. Whose power was this? It was entirely due to the merit of making offerings through the Dharma-site of the Eight Maṇḍalas. Therefore, when sentient beings hear the above eight Dharma-gates, they should without exception study them. Whether with flowers, incense, lamps, food and drink, banners and canopies, garments, or music, praising and prostrating and making vows and repenting, according to their ability, all will receive the blessings of merit. When practicing the six pāramitās, one should establish the practice-site, using various fragrant waters or fragrant paste to anoint the ground, or water or earth, to construct a square or circular altar. This is called the pāramitā of generosity. When making offerings, if one's karma of body, speech, and mind does not harm sentient beings, this is called the pāramitā of moral discipline. When making offerings, if insects and ants enter the practice-site and one drives them away but they return, and at that time one patiently endures, this is called the pāramitā of patience. When making offerings, if one's wholesome mind continues without interruption, this is called the pāramitā of diligence. If the mind is not scattered and one makes offerings single-mindedly, this is called the pāramitā of dhyāna. When performing the ritual, if the square altars and circular platforms are without any deviation, and one skillfully understands the proper occasions for establishing and dismantling, this is called the pāramitā of prajñā.

"Good man! Thus this single dharma, following the transformations of the mind, can fully accomplish the six pāramitās. It should be widely taught to transform sentient beings, for their great benefit, and even unto the attainment of Buddhahood."

When this sūtra had been spoken, Siṃhavyūharāja Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva, and all the devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans and non-humans, all greatly rejoiced, believed, accepted, and respectfully practiced it.
