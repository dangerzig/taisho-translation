# 佛說大乘流轉諸有經
## Mahāyāna Sūtra of Transmigrating through the Existences, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0577

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Yijing (義淨)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in Rājagṛha, at the Kalandaka Pool Bamboo Grove, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, and great bodhisattva mahāsattvas, with immeasurable hundreds of thousands of devas, humans, and other great beings, all surrounding him in singleminded reverence. At that time the World-Honored One was teaching the wondrous Dharma realized by himself, which is good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, with skillful language and meaning, pure and complete, clean and bright, bearing the marks of the holy life.

At that time the lord of Magadha, King Yingsheng, went to the Bamboo Grove, bowed at the World-Honored One's feet, circumambulated him three times on the right, and sat to one side. Then King Yingsheng said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! How is it that the karma created long ago by a sentient being, which has long since ceased and perished, all appears before the mind at the time of death? And how is it that, though the essence of all dharmas is entirely empty, the retribution for karma created is not lost? May the World-Honored One have compassion and explain this for me."

Then the World-Honored One said to King Yingsheng: "Great King, you should know. Suppose a man in a sleeping dream sees a beautiful woman of the human world and engages in intimate acts with her. When he wakes from sleep, he remembers the beautiful woman he saw in the dream. Great King, what do you think? Is the beautiful woman of the human world in that dream real or not?"

The king said: "She is not!"

The World-Honored One said: "Great King, what do you think? If this man, thinking of the beautiful woman seen in his dream, clings to the memory and cannot let go, can this man be called a person of great learning and wisdom?"

The king said: "No! This is a fool, not a wise person. Why? Because the beautiful woman of the human world in the dream is ultimately empty in essence and cannot be found. How could he have engaged in intimate acts with her, that this man should cherish love and give rise to longing?"

The Buddha said: "Great King! Just so. When foolish, ignorant ordinary people see forms with the eye, delight arises in the mind and grasping follows. Once grasping arises, attachment and longing follow. Once attachment and longing arise, the emotions are stained with love. Once stained love arises, greed, anger, and delusion drive the creation of karma through body, speech, and mind. Yet this karma, once performed, ceases and perishes. When it ceases and perishes, it does not abide in the east, nor in the south, west, or north, nor in the four intermediate directions, above, or below. At the time of death, when the mental consciousness is about to cease, all the karma one has created appears before the mind, just as a man upon waking from sleep sees the image of the beautiful woman from his dream all appearing before him.

"Great King! When the preceding consciousness ceases and the subsequent consciousness arises, one may be born among humans, or born in the heavens, or fall into the animal realm, the hungry ghost realm, or Naraka. Great King! When the subsequent consciousness arises, there immediately arises a mind of the same type, continuing in a stream, clearly experiencing the ripened fruit of retribution. Great King! There has never been a dharma that transmigrates from this world to a future world. Yet there is death and birth, and the fruits of karma can be found. Great King, you should know: when the preceding consciousness ceases, it is called death; when the subsequent consciousness arises, it is called birth. Great King! When the preceding consciousness ceases, there is no place it goes. When the subsequent consciousness arises, there is no place it comes from. Why? Because its original nature is empty. Great King! The preceding consciousness is empty of the nature of preceding consciousness; death is empty of the nature of death; karma is empty of the nature of karma; the subsequent consciousness is empty of the nature of subsequent consciousness; birth is empty of the nature of birth. Yet the fruits of karma are never lost. Great King! Thus you should know: all sentient beings, because of delusion, fail to understand that there is no real existence, and give rise to false attachment, revolving in birth and death."

Then the World-Honored One, wishing to restate this meaning, spoke these gāthās:

> "All dharmas are merely provisional names,
> Established only by reliance upon names.
> Apart from the words that designate,
> What is designated cannot be found.
> Each with its own particular name
> Designates those various dharmas,
> Yet within the names the dharmas are not present:
> This is the self-nature of all dharmas.
> Because of names, the nature of names is empty;
> Within names, names do not exist.
> The names of dharmas are originally nothing;
> Falsely, names are designated by names.
> All dharmas are empty and false,
> Arising only from discrimination.
> This discrimination too is empty;
> Within emptiness one falsely discriminates.
> I teach that the worldly
> See forms with the eye:
> All of this is from wrong conceptualization;
> It is called conventional truth.
> I teach that all dharmas
> Arise through the aid of conditions;
> This is called 'approaching ultimate meaning':
> The wise should investigate this.
> The eye does not see forms;
> The mind does not know dharmas.
> This is called the ultimate truth;
> The foolish cannot know it."

When the World-Honored One had taught this Dharma, the lord of Magadha, King Yingsheng, received it deeply in his heart. The bhikṣus, the great bodhisattvas, the assembly of humans, devas, and others were all filled with great joy, and faithfully received and practiced it.
