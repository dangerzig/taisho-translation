# 大方廣如來不思議境界經
## The Great Vaipulya Sūtra on the Inconceivable Realm of the Tathāgata

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0301

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Śikṣānanda (實叉難陀)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the land of Magadha, beneath the bodhi tree, having attained perfect, right awakening. That bodhi tree, named Aśvattha, had deep and firm coiling roots, a trunk that rose straight and tall, round and without knots, like a sandalwood pillar. Flying birds above it would turn back in their wings, none able to pass beyond it. Its bark was fine, smooth, and lustrous, with many-colored patterns like fine silk. Dense and verdant leaves spread out in a broad canopy. All around it, wondrous flowers bloomed forth, radiating beauty and fragrance, extremely delightful. Apart from the Kubera and Pārijāta trees, none could compare to it. Further, innumerable small trees surrounded it. This tree-king stood dense and splendid, surpassingly fine, like Mount Sumeru towering above the other peaks. From one yojana away, it could be seen by all. Its fragrance flowed in all directions, its glorious radiance shone, and seen from afar at night, one would take it for a great heap of fire. Beneath it was beautiful and stately, like the Nandana Garden. On all four sides the ground was level, fragrant grass grew lush and fine like the neck-feathers of a peacock king, soft and aromatic; no beholder ever tired of the sight. The Tathāgata sat there, majestic and composed, surrounded by the great assembly, like the moon amid the stars.

At that time, beings from other lands, as numerous as the atoms of ten buddha-fields, came to adorn Vairocana's bodhimaṇḍa assembly, appearing in the forms of bodhisattvas and sitting in the assembly. Their names were: the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara, the bodhisattva Mañjuśrī, the bodhisattva Kṣitigarbha, the bodhisattva Ākāśagarbha, the bodhisattva Vajragarbha, the bodhisattva Vimalakīrti, the bodhisattva Subha-vyūha, the bodhisattva Sarva-nīvaraṇa-viṣkambhin, the bodhisattva Ratnapāṇi, the bodhisattva Mahāmati, and the bodhisattva Samantabhadra. These bodhisattva-mahāsattvas served as leaders. Further, innumerable billions of bodhisattvas, manifesting in the form of śrāvakas, also came to sit in the assembly. Their names were: Śāriputra, Mahāmaudgalyāyana, Subhūti, Rāhula, Ajñāta-kauṇḍinya, Mahākāśyapa, Upāli, Aniruddha, Revata, Ānanda, Devadatta, and Upananda, who served as leaders. All of them had long cultivated the six pāramitās, were near to bodhi, and in order to transform sentient beings, manifested the form of śrāvakas in this defiled land. Further, there were innumerable thousands of bhikṣuṇīs, with Mahāprajāpatī as their leader. They had all accomplished the deeds of great men. In order to tame beings of inferior capacity, although they appeared in female form, they were accomplished in masculine deeds. Further, there were Śakra, Brahmā, the world-protectors, devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans and non-humans. Among them there were no ordinary beings; all were great bodhisattvas.

At that time, the World-Honored One, sitting beneath the bodhi tree, with subtle, wondrous, pure adornment, like a wish-fulfilling jewel placed beneath a cintāmaṇi tree, abode in right mindfulness, unmoving, like Mount Sumeru. In order to enable all bodhisattvas and all sentient beings to understand the profound mystic dhyāna and awesome spiritual power of all the Buddhas, he entered a samādhi called the Inconceivable Realm of the Tathāgata. Immediately, within each of the World-Honored One's thirty-two marks, there appeared all the buddha-lands of the ten directions and all their Buddhas, as in a bright mirror universally displaying all forms. Furthermore, within each of his minor marks, there appeared the Tathāgata's past bodhisattva practices, from the initial circumstance of King Radiance up to the final encounter with Dīpaṃkara Buddha, each difficult ascetic deed: giving away heads, eyes, bodily limbs, skin and flesh, hands and feet, wife, children, servants and attendants, relinquishing sovereign rule, palaces, and the like. By the great power of this samādhi, all the Buddhas, when eating, when walking, when teaching the Dharma, and when entering parinirvāṇa, are constantly in this samādhi. Why is this? Because all the Tathāgatas, relying on this samādhi, accomplish immeasurable great awesome spiritual power, and even realize entry into the emptiness of all dharmas, enabling them to manifest all manner of sovereign deeds in all the buddha-lands of the ten directions. It is like a person who in a dream sees all kinds of varying events, but upon waking, what was seen is no more. Ordinary beings are likewise: due to the dream of ignorance, they falsely give rise to perceptions of real substance regarding all dharmas. All the Buddhas, being awakened, are without any attachment, and therefore in all the worlds of the ten directions, in a single thought, they can manifest immeasurable deeds of the Buddha, sovereign and unobstructed, benefiting and perfecting all sentient beings, causing them all to awaken and enter immeasurable profound and wondrous gates of liberation.

At that time, the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha, whose bodhisattva practices were not yet fully accomplished, asked the bodhisattva-mahāsattva Samantabhadra: "What is the name of the samādhi the Tathāgata has now entered? And how is it that in all the worlds of the ten directions, he can sovereignly manifest all manner of deeds of the Buddha and deliver sentient beings?"

At that time, the bodhisattva Samantabhadra told the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha: "Listen carefully, listen carefully! I shall explain for you."

Then all the bodhisattvas, gazing with single-minded reverence, sighed together: "Excellent question! Most profound and subtle! The venerable Samantabhadra knows all and sees all, and shall now expound it."

Immediately the great earth quaked in six ways, and wondrous celestial flowers rained down. The afflictions and sufferings of all sentient beings were slightly eased. The bodhisattva Samantabhadra said: "Child of the Buddha! This samādhi is called the Inconceivable Realm of the Tathāgata. It is the bodhi of all the Buddhas, for all the Tathāgatas constantly abide and rely upon it. From the time the World-Honored One received his prediction from Dīpaṃkara Buddha, he entered this samādhi. He has always been without effort, naturally responding and manifesting immeasurable deeds of the Buddha. On a single hair-tip in empty space, there are buddha-worlds as numerous as the atoms of all buddha-fields. In them he may appear being born in Tuṣita Heaven, descending from there and entering the womb, appearing to be newly born, walking seven steps, and proclaiming: 'I am now the very boundary of birth and death.' He may appear dwelling in the palace, leaving home and practicing austerities, or conquering Māra and attaining perfect awakening, turning the wondrous Dharma-wheel. He may appear abiding in the world for immeasurable kalpas, delivering all sentient beings and causing them to leave suffering, and even showing the appearance of entering parinirvāṇa. He may take all kalpas into a single kṣaṇa, or a single kṣaṇa into all kalpas, with neither increase nor decrease in kalpas or kṣaṇas. And so on, until all sentient beings have been completely liberated, kṣaṇa by kṣaṇa, everywhere at once in all these worlds, constantly performing these various deeds of the Buddha, never resting and without effort. Just as in every hair-tip measure of empty space, in immeasurable lands, thought after thought, there is the universal manifestation of the various awesome deportments and rules of all the Buddhas, all without effort, and so on to the very ends of space, in every hair-tip measure, it is also like this. Further, in all the atoms of all those lands, in each and every atom, there are further lands surpassing in number all the atoms of all buddha-fields. And in a single kṣaṇa, in each and every one of those lands, there is the natural and universal manifestation of all the Buddhas' awesome deportment and conduct: being born in the celestial palace and so on through to parinirvāṇa, liberating immeasurable asaṃkhyeya sentient beings. Thus, thought after thought, to the end of the future, constantly performing the deeds of the Buddha, benefiting sentient beings, to the very ends of empty space and the realm of sentient beings, without ever resting. Yet the buddha-lands do not shrink, and the atoms do not expand. Why is this? Because all dharmas are like illusion, like a mirage, not solid. It is like this very assembly: bodhisattvas, as numerous as the atoms of ten buddha-worlds, all dwell together in the twelve-yojana area of Magadha, not obstructing one another. Each and every atom receives innumerable buddha-worlds, whether facing each other, facing away, side by side, or containing one another, without any obstruction, and it is also like this. Like a person in a dream who in a single place sees all manner of things: because they are not real, there is no obstruction. All these lands are nothing but the manifestation of one's own mind. In some, one sees the kalpa-fire burning; in some, the fire has already burned out. In some, one sees wind forming worlds. Some are pure, some defiled. Some appear to have no Buddha. Sentient beings each follow their own karma and see differently. It is like hungry ghosts, tormented by hunger and thirst, who go to the Ganges: some see water, some see ashes, pus and blood, or feces and filth. Sentient beings are also like this: each following their own karma, they see their buddha-land as either defiled or pure, see the Buddha present in the world or having entered nirvāṇa, see him on the bodhimaṇḍa teaching the Dharma to the assembly. Some hear the teaching of the ultimate truth, some hear the praise of the practice of giving. Some see him walking, standing, sitting, or eating. Some see a body twice the height of an ordinary person, up to seven times, or one yojana, or a hundred yojanas, or a thousand yojanas. Some see his awesome radiance like the rising sun, or like the full moon. Or due to karmic obstructions, they encounter a World-Honored One who long ago entered parinirvāṇa. Some do not even hear the name of any Buddha. Like those hungry ghosts who at the Ganges do not see any water but only see various filth. Or they see the various Buddhas, each coming from their own lands, displaying the awesome form of great bodhisattvas and entering this assembly. In a single land, sentient beings see only the kalpa-fire burning. In a single land, sentient beings fill it and all see the Buddha. Or they see the Tathāgata gathering all lands into a single buddha-land, or placing a single buddha-land into all lands. Like many persons with cataracts who, in the same place, each see differently and do not obstruct one another, all due to cataracts preventing them from seeing true colors. Sentient beings are also like this: the nature of form is unobstructed, but because the mind's conditions differ, right seeing is obscured and the true reality is not understood.

"Child of the Buddha! Now I shall further briefly explain for you the dharma of abiding in this samādhi. Just as the Buddha, World-Honored One, abides in this samādhi, in a single thought-moment, pervading the ends of empty space, in every hair-tip measure, in immeasurable buddha-lands, and in each and every atom of those lands, there are lands as numerous as the atoms of the dharma-realm. For the benefit of sentient beings, kṣaṇa by kṣaṇa, pervading each and every land, there is the manifestation of deportments as numerous as the atoms of ten buddha-fields, the deportments of all the Buddhas' skillful means. And so on, until all sentient beings have fully realized anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, this is never interrupted. As with this one Buddha, the second, the third, and so on through all the Buddhas of the ten directions, the awesome virtue and great power manifested by each is also just like this."

When the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha heard this teaching, right there on his seat he attained this samādhi. He immediately saw those immeasurable Buddhas and understood the awesome virtue and skillful means of all the Buddhas. Through the power of samādhi, he was likewise able to tame sentient beings. Bodhisattvas as numerous as the sands of a hundred Ganges each variously realized various samādhis, patient endurances, and various grounds. Avalokiteśvara and the other great bodhisattvas dwelling on the tenth ground, whose meritorious practices of virtue were all fully accomplished, had long since attained this samādhi. In a single kṣaṇa they contain immeasurable kalpas; in a single atom they contain immeasurable lands. In a single thought they pervade all the lands, delivering immeasurable sentient beings, always without effort, naturally manifesting the deeds of the Buddha. Though they heard this Dharma, they made no further progress, like a full vase of water placed in the rain: it cannot hold another drop. These bodhisattvas were also like this.

At that time, the World-Honored One, abiding in samādhi, emitted from between his brows a light called Great Manifestation. All the bodhisattvas with active effort who had not yet realized the ten grounds, upon encountering this light, all saw in empty space, in every hair-tip measure and in every atom, immeasurable buddha-lands, like a lapis lazuli vase filled with white mustard seeds, with every seed visible to the observer. Those bodhisattvas, seeing within atoms all the buddha-lands, also saw it just like this. And they saw all the Buddhas in those lands. In the body of each and every Buddha, they saw the bodies of all the Buddhas. Each and every Buddha had immeasurable names. All were for the benefit of each and every sentient being: thought after thought, in each and every buddha-land, there was the natural and responsive manifestation of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. Like a wish-fulfilling jewel placed on a high banner, it naturally rains down all manner of precious jewels, fulfilling the wishes of sentient beings. The Tathāgata is also just like this: manifesting perfect awakening, he naturally delivers immeasurable sentient beings. The sentient beings in those lands are each different, yet they do not obstruct one another, like one with spiritual powers who travels through empty space, through mountains, rivers, and stone walls without any obstruction. Why is this? Because all destinies are like illusion, like a mirage, not solid. All the bodhisattvas, having witnessed this, each saw their own body pervading all the lands. In a single thought-moment, before each and every Tathāgata, for one kalpa, or two kalpas, or three kalpas, or a hundred or a thousand kalpas, or a single thought-moment, or a single muhūrta, they reverently made offerings. From the Tathāgatas they heard the teaching of the pāramitās, or the dhāraṇī gates, or the teachings on the various grounds. They saw the Tathāgatas' divine transformations. Taking all kalpas into a single thought, the thought arose of how rare and hard to encounter this is, thinking: "How does the World-Honored One possess such sovereign awesome virtue, that in a single kṣaṇa he causes me to fully accomplish the good roots and merit of immeasurable kalpas, and swiftly realize the Tathāgata's Inconceivable Realm Samādhi and its great awesome spiritual power?"

At that time, the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha, wishing to benefit sentient beings, further asked the bodhisattva Samantabhadra: "For one who wishes to realize this samādhi, what merit, generosity, precepts, and wisdom should they cultivate?"

Then the bodhisattva Samantabhadra, who throughout the ten directions, in all the pure lands, manifests perfect awakening and transforms sentient beings, told the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha: "Child of the Buddha! If one wishes to realize this samādhi, one should first cultivate merit, gathering good roots: always making offerings to the Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha, and to one's parents. For all beings who are poor, suffering, helpless, with no refuge, and worthy of compassion, one should receive and not abandon them, even to the point of giving one's own body and flesh without any stinginess. Why is this? Because making offerings to the Buddha produces great merit, leading to the swift attainment of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, bringing all sentient beings peace and happiness. Making offerings to the Dharma increases wisdom, and one realizes mastery over the Dharma, able to correctly know the true nature of all dharmas. Making offerings to the Sangha increases the immeasurable provision of merit and wisdom, achieving the Way of Buddhahood. Making offerings and attending to one's parents, preceptors, elders, and all those from any lifetime in birth and death from whom one has received kindness, one should think of repaying with increased offerings of gratitude. Why is this? Because among those who know and repay kindness, even amid birth and death, their good roots are never destroyed. Those who do not know kindness: their good roots are cut off, and they create all manner of evil. Therefore, all the Tathāgatas always praise those who are grateful and censure the ungrateful. Furthermore, one should always have compassion for suffering sentient beings. Through such broad and great good roots, the bodhisattva never regresses. If a person can diligently cultivate merit, always remember to repay kindness, and be compassionate toward sentient beings, then bodhi is already in their hand. One should know that the Buddha has said: one who can make offerings to these three kinds of fields, in each accomplishes immeasurable good roots.

"Guṇagarbha, you should know: the bodhisattva should next plant vast seeds, for from them the sprout of this samādhi arises and becomes the fruit of bodhi. How does one plant the seeds? One should take various exquisite garlands, perfumes, unguents, powdered incense, and various forms of music, and reverently offer them to the Buddhas presently in the world or to Buddha images, thinking: 'As described above, pervading empty space, in every hair-tip measure and within every atom, in immeasurable lands, one by one I see the awesome power of all the Buddhas and all the bodhisattva assemblies. Before them all, with a single mind in right recollection, I universally make offerings. The dharma-nature of one Tathāgata that I offer to is the dharma-nature of all Tathāgatas. If I make offerings to one Tathāgata, I make offerings to all the Tathāgatas. By the divine power of each and every Buddha, however many kalpas can be taken into a single thought, for that many kalpas I make offerings to the Tathāgata.' If sentient beings have faith in and understand this Dharma, planting the great seed, they will attain the Tathāgata's Inconceivable Realm, the vast and great samādhi. Therefore, good men, you should use this Dharma to make offerings daily. By this means, even a single respectful bow before all the Buddhas can cause the seed to grow and the sprout of samādhi to arise. One should also constantly irrigate it with generosity, precept-keeping, great vows, and wisdom. Furthermore, when the bodhisattva irrigates the samādhi by practicing generosity, they should not discriminate between meritorious fields, enemies or friends, good or evil, those who keep the precepts or break them, the rich or the poor. Further, one should think: 'Even giving to the wealthy, who have no need, I should still cultivate the practice of giving.' The bodhisattva should also keep the precepts purely. Seeing those who break the precepts, one should give rise to great compassion and should not feel aversion or anger toward them. Furthermore, one should deeply generate the great aspiration for bodhi: 'I shall certainly, thought after thought, universally in every hair-tip measure pervading empty space, and in all the atoms of all buddha-lands, in the immeasurable worlds therein, attain perfect awakening, turn the wondrous Dharma-wheel, and deliver sentient beings. Just as the World-Honored One Vairocana does now, exactly the same, without arising any effort, containing immeasurable kalpas in a single thought.' Then, in each and every one of these lands, one manifests the deportments of all the Buddhas, as numerous as the atoms of buddha-fields. In each deportment, one delivers sentient beings as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, all causing them to leave suffering, to the very ends of empty space and the realm of sentient beings, without ever resting.

"Child of the Buddha! As for cultivating wisdom, listen with one-pointed mind. I shall now explain for you. If good men or good women wish to seek unsurpassed bodhi and aspire to realize this samādhi, they must first cultivate wisdom, because this samādhi is attained through wisdom. Those who cultivate wisdom should distance themselves from false speech, frivolous speech, and all scattered and useless matters. Although they give rise to great compassion for all sentient beings, they should always collect their minds, unstained and unscattered. They should go to a monastery and behold a Buddha image, gold-adorned and solemn, or made of pure gold, fully endowed with all marks, with immeasurable transformation-Buddhas in samādhi seated in rows within the circular halo. Before the image, one should prostrate with one's head at the feet and think: 'I have heard that the Buddhas of the ten directions, presently in the world, are immeasurable, namely: Sarvārthasiddha Buddha, Amitābha Buddha, Ratnaketu Buddha, Akṣobhya Buddha, Vairocana Buddha, Ratnacandra Buddha, Ratna-āloka Buddha, and others.' Toward whichever of those Buddhas the mind delights in and reveres, one should give rise to great pure faith. Imagine the Buddha image as the Tathāgata's true body, reverently and respectfully, as if seeing him present before one. Observe carefully from above and below, with single-minded concentration. Go to a secluded place, sit upright, and contemplate: as though the Buddha were present before one, at a distance of one hand-span, always holding the mind's recollection without letting it be lost. If it should momentarily be lost, go back and observe again. While observing thus, give rise to the most profound reverence and respect, as if the Buddha's true body were present before one, seeing it clearly, no longer understanding it as a mere image. Having seen this, one should then offer exquisite garlands, powdered incense, and perfumes at the Buddha's place, circumambulating to the right, making various offerings. One should thus concentrate the mind's recollection, always as though the World-Honored One were present before one. For the Buddha, World-Honored One, is one who sees all, hears all, and knows all, and fully knows my mind. Having examined and verified this in such a way, once the visualization is accomplished, return to a secluded place, keeping the recollection before one without letting it be lost. With single-minded diligent cultivation, when twenty-one days are complete, if one is a person of merit, the Tathāgata will appear before one. If past-life karma creates obstructions preventing one from seeing, one should persevere with single-minded diligence, never retrogressing, with no other thought, and one will still swiftly see the Buddha. Why is this? Because if one, for the sake of unsurpassed bodhi, concentrates the mind on a single thing and cultivates it, there is nothing that will not be accomplished. If one repeatedly retreats in discouragement from what one is cultivating, one cannot even achieve one's own liberation; how much less could one deliver all sentient beings from their suffering? If one encounters the straight path to bodhi that leads to swift attainment and yet cannot diligently cultivate it, one should know that such a person is merely a heavy burden upon the earth. It is like a person who in the great ocean drinks a single handful of water: they have already drunk all the water of the rivers of Jambudvīpa. Just so, if the bodhisattva cultivates this ocean of bodhi, all samādhis, all forms of patient endurance, all grounds, and all dhāraṇīs have already been cultivated. Therefore, one should always be diligent and unflagging, far from heedlessness. Concentrating the mind with single focus, one shall certainly bring about the appearance of the Buddhas before one.

"When, through such cultivation, one first sees the Buddha, one should think: 'Is this the true Buddha? Or is it the image?' If one knows that what is seen is the true Buddha, then before the Buddha, place both knees on the ground, join the palms respectfully, and recollect: 'All the Buddhas, of the awesome virtue and great compassion described above, in the hair-tip measures of empty space and in atoms, immeasurable in number, have come to appear before me due to their great compassion.' One should then make a request: 'May the World-Honored One please teach me the great samādhi Dharma of the Tathāgata's Inconceivable Realm.' If the Tathāgata teaches everything, one should receive it with determined faith, without giving rise to doubt. If one hears it, right in that place one fully accomplishes it. Or, if past karmic obstructions prevent one from asking, then one should think and observe: 'All dharmas are like illusion, like a mirage, like cataracts, like a shadow, like a reflection, like a dream. Carefully observing the nature of dharmas as empty and quiescent, yet knowing that the Tathāgata understands all dharmas as being like illusion, like a dream. The Tathāgata's own nature is neither illusory nor dreamlike; it is like empty space. Yet through wisdom and compassion, the Buddha, World-Honored One, has appeared before me. May he now emit the great compassion-light of blue flame for my sake, and may all my afflictions be extinguished.' Then the Buddha, for one's sake, emits from between his brows a light named Blue Flame. When that light merely shines upon one, all afflictions are extinguished. Right there one realizes a dharma called the Dharma-Light Patient Endurance, and becomes able to understand all manner of samādhis. On the seventh night, the Tathāgata appears in a dream and bestows the prediction of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. If one knows that what was seen is the image, then one should think: 'All Tathāgatas and all sentient beings are also like images, merely appearing according to thought, without real substance.' Knowing thus that all Tathāgatas are like illusion, like transformation, like a dream, like a mirage, and that their nature is naturally so: seeing the Tathāgata face to face is also like seeing in a dream, with no reality to grasp. Not born yet born, not extinguished yet extinguished, not going yet going, not known by consciousness yet known, not conditioned yet manifesting conditioned activities, not spoken yet expounding Dharma, without self, without life-span, without sentient beings, without a nurturer, without a being born into a destiny, without perception, without action, without a knower, without an eater, neither aggregates nor not-aggregates yet displaying the appearance of aggregates, and likewise for the eighteen elements and twelve sense-fields: they are all ultimately nonexistent, yet also not other than these. Therefore, all Buddhas and all dharmas are truly equal, all of one character, like a mirage. All sentient beings, all Buddhas, and all lands are nothing but one's own mind, manifested through the consciousness's conceptual perception. The forms produced from consciousness and conceptual perception as conditions are ultimately without reality. The Tathāgata has already left behind all consciousness and conceptual perception; therefore one should not see the Tathāgata through form-images. Knowing that the images seen arise from thought, one should know that even all the true Buddhas in every hair-tip measure of empty space are also like this: like empty space, equal and without difference. If I discriminate, I see a Buddha; if I depart from discrimination, there is nothing to see. One's own mind creates the Buddha; apart from the mind, there is no Buddha. And so, all dharmas of the three realms, past, present, and future, are also like this: they are all without any reality, relying only upon one's own mind. If a bodhisattva can understand that all Buddhas and all dharmas of the dharma-nature are merely the measure of the mind, they attain the patient endurance of accordance, or enter the first ground. Upon leaving the body, they are swiftly born in the world of Abhirati, or born in the pure buddha-land of Sukhāvatī, where they always see the Tathāgata and personally attend and make offerings."

At that time, the bodhisattva Guṇagarbha again addressed the bodhisattva Samantabhadra: "If sentient beings hear this dharma-gate and receive, uphold, read, recite, explain, and copy it, how much merit do they gain?"

The bodhisattva Samantabhadra said: "Child of the Buddha, listen carefully! If a person could gather all the sentient beings of the three realms and cause them all to be freed from birth and death and attain the fruit of arhatship, and if, for each and every arhat, for a hundred kalpas, one offered the supreme celestial clothing, bedding, food, drink, and medicine, with various offerings, and after each entered parinirvāṇa, one raised a seven-jeweled stūpa for each and reverently made offerings; and if further a person for a hundred kalpas purely upheld the prohibitory precepts, or cultivated patient endurance, or cultivated diligence, or cultivated dhyāna: though such a person would gain immeasurable merit, it would not compare with a person who hears this dharma-gate, reveres it with faith, and does not disparage it. Their merit surpasses all of that, and they will swiftly attain perfect awakening."

At that time, in all the lands of the ten directions, the immeasurable Buddhas all manifested their bodies and praised the bodhisattva Samantabhadra, saying: "Excellent, excellent! Child of the Buddha! It is so, it is so! As you have spoken."

Then the Tathāgata Śākyamuni emitted from his face immeasurable colored light, illuminating the three realms. Various flowers rained down, and wondrous musical instruments sounded of their own accord without being struck. The great earth trembled gently. Within the light, verses were spoken:

> "If one hears this Dharma with a purified mind,
> One attains the various grounds, samādhis, and dhāraṇīs,
> Together with the precepts, patient endurance, mastery, and spiritual powers,
> And swiftly realizes the unsurpassed bodhi of the Buddha.
> One turns the never-before-heard wondrous Dharma-wheel,
> Just as the great ṛṣis of the past did.
> In a single thought, containing many kalpas,
> In a single atom, universally manifesting immeasurable lands.
> Immeasurable sentient beings submerged in the three realms,
> Constantly oppressed by all manner of suffering,
> Bound by wrong views, they lose the right path;
> Thought after thought, they are all delivered."

When the bodhisattva Samantabhadra taught this dharma-gate, having long since realized it, billions of thousands of devas and humans, freed from all suffering, all attained non-retrogression from anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi. The bodhisattva Guṇagarbha, together with all the rest of the bodhisattva assembly, the devas, nāgas, asuras, and all the world, all greatly rejoiced, believed, received, and practiced accordingly.
