# 大毘盧遮那經阿闍梨真實智品中阿闍梨住阿字觀門
## The Gate of the Ācārya's Abiding in the Contemplation of the Syllable A, from the Chapter on the Ācārya's True Wisdom in the Mahāvairocana Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0863

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, composed by Wéijǐn (惟謹)

---

## Ritual Procedures for Entering Realization, One Fascicle

Now, the term "ācārya" means one who understands the most secret within the secret: the great mind of mantra-wisdom. The syllable *a* is called the seed; therefore all things are thus. In accord with the Dharma, one universally receives them all. If within this teaching one understands this vast and great wisdom, the great merit of perfect enlightenment, one is called an ācārya; this is none other than the Tathāgata, and is also called the Buddha. In Sanskrit, *hṛdaya*: *hṛdaya* means the true and real heart, which is the syllable *a*.

Now, the syllable *a* is the mother of all the Buddhas, the birthplace of all mantras, the most supreme and wondrous syllable of the Buddha's heart.

If one speaks of the internal sound of the syllable *a*, it is the sound of the throat. One should know that this syllable *a* pervades all the limbs and organs: this is the meaning of "primordially unborn." In Sanskrit there are two kinds of sounds. One is called *akṣara*, the root syllable, also called "the meaning of non-movement"; "non-movement" means the meaning of the bodhicitta. Like the first two sounds, *a* and *ā*, these are root syllables. Next, from *i* and *ī* through *u* and *ū*, a total of twelve syllables, these are all syllables of augmented generation, and all are feminine voice. The root syllables are masculine voice: masculine voice is the meaning of wisdom; feminine voice is the meaning of concentration. Another explanation distinguishes five voices: the syllable *a* is the body-voice; the syllable *ā* is the practice-voice; the twelve syllables from *i* and *ī* through *u* and *ū* are all voices of samādhi; the syllable *aṃ* is the voice of realizing sambodhi; the syllable *aḥ* is the voice of entering mahā-parinirvāṇa. If one sees *ṅa*, *ña*, *ṇa*, *na*, *ma* with the upper dot, these too are the voice of realizing sambodhi. If one sees *ya*, *ra*, and the other eight syllables with the side dot, these too are the voice of mahā-parinirvāṇa. The root syllables pervade everywhere; the augmented syllables also pervade everywhere. Root and augmented are not different from each other. All are based on the root syllable's body, with strokes added to the sides and corners. Therefore root and augmented are neither one nor different. It is like water held in a vessel: the vessel supports the water, and the water does not leave the vessel. Thus they mutually support each other and can pervade both inner and outer. Not only does the syllable *a* pervade everywhere; next, from *ka*, *kha*, *ga*, *gha*, *ṅa* through the syllable *kṣa*, these too pervade everywhere. Why? Because all are root sounds, and the root sound is the syllable *a*.

Furthermore, take the syllable *ka*: each has its own augmented syllables generated from it, such as the syllables *ke* and *kai* within the syllable *ka*. These are all feminine voice, produced by adding strokes to the body of *ka*, thus becoming augmented syllables. The body is the meaning of wisdom, and the augmentation is concentration and wisdom mutually supporting each other, merging into one without losing the body. Śamatha and vipaśyanā practiced together also pervade everywhere. Therefore the augmented pervades the root, and the root pervades the augmented; the generated pervades the seed, and the seed pervades the generated. In Sanskrit this is called *upavāha*, also meaning "arising," just as a seed produces fruit, and the fruit in turn becomes a seed. Now this syllable *a* is like a seed, able to produce many fruits, each of which again produces a hundred, a thousand, ten thousand times more, extending and transmitting without limit, beyond description. Seeing the seed, one recognizes the fruit; since the cause is thus, one should know the fruit must also be thus. This syllable *a* is also like this. From this root, teacher-less, spontaneously arisen wisdom, all wisdom-activities are generated from it and distributed throughout the limbs and organs. "Distributing to the limbs and organs" means one's own mind. Through this own-mind, all bodily parts are encompassed. Apart from the mind there is no body; apart from the body there is no mind: this too is the same as the syllable *a*. If one pervades and distributes this syllable, one becomes the same as all the Buddhas. The fruit that comes from the syllable is the Buddha, for one who realizes right knowledge is called "Perfectly Awakened." One who knows the nature and principle of this syllable thereby obtains the name "Tathāgata." The nature and principle of this syllable is that the syllable *a* is the meaning of "primordially unborn."

Furthermore, just as a person has a mind that pervades the limbs, all experiencing pain and pleasure, so the syllable *a* also pervades all limbs and organs: this is the meaning of the mind being primordially unborn. The syllable *a* pervades all syllables. If any syllable lacks the syllable *a*, that syllable cannot be formed; the syllable *a* is required. If a syllable has no head, it cannot become a syllable; therefore *a* is the head. From the single sound of the syllable *a*, within all sounds whatsoever there is the sound of the syllable *a*. Without obtaining the sound of this syllable *a*, sound arises from the outer form of the syllable; since sound arises, and since sound pervades the limbs and organs, it can express all worldly and world-transcending dharmas. If one merely sees the syllable's sound, it can convey and express the principle. Moreover, it requires phonemes, speech, teeth, and palate in order to express: blue, yellow, red, white; east, west, south, north; square, round, large, small; upper, lower, noble, humble; all categories of things can then be comprehended. Therefore the sūtra says: "O Vajradhara! This is the Dharma-gate that pervades all places."

Furthermore, the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra* says: "Such syllable-gates can, with respect to all worldly and world-transcending skillful dharmas, by wisdom penetrate and reach the other shore. Diverse lands and strange arts are thoroughly mastered without remainder. Writing, characters, calculation, and numbers contain their deep understanding. Medical methods and incantations skillfully heal all diseases. When beings are possessed by ghosts and demons, cursed by enemies with malicious spells, afflicted by baleful stars and strange prodigies, pursued by corpses, struck with madness or wasting illness: all such ailments can be cured and healed. One also skillfully distinguishes the places where gold, jade, pearls, shells, coral, lapis lazuli, maṇi-gems, sphaṭika crystals, kīsara-stones, and all treasures are produced, their different kinds, and their relative values. Villages, townships, districts, cities, great and small capitals, palaces, gardens, cliffs, springs, marshes, and thickets: wherever people dwell, the bodhisattva can follow and protect according to each region. One also skillfully observes astronomy, geography, human physiognomy, auspicious and inauspicious signs, the sounds of birds and beasts, the appearances of clouds and mists, the abundance or scarcity of yearly harvests, and the safety or danger of nations. All worldly arts and skills are thoroughly mastered to their very source." Furthermore, the sūtra says: "O Vajradhara! Therefore with the syllable *a* as the head, the syllable *a* is the heart of mantras, the syllable of the Buddha's heart." What has been explained above, the ācārya's abiding in the Buddha-ground, is not yet fully clarified. This is precisely the gate of abiding in the contemplation of the syllable *a* herein. This syllable *a* is also called śamatha, also called the wisdom of vipaśyanā, also called the true and precious wisdom, also called sarvajña-jñāna, the wisdom of all wisdoms. Therefore the sūtra says: "Sarvajña, root wisdom, subsequent wisdom: from subsequent wisdom arises great compassion; from great compassion arise the three kinds of transformation bodies."

Now, if the ācārya contemplates this syllable and attains correspondence, then one's own body becomes identical with the body of Mahāvairocana Buddha. It is also called the vajra-body of indestructibility, also called the body of universal manifestation, also called the three kinds of mind-born body.

The ācārya contemplates this syllable *a* wheel like the luminous wheel of a peacock's tail-feathers, its radiance encircling the practitioner who abides within it. This is abiding in the Buddha's station. Therefore, from the pure Dharma-realm's circle of mirror-light, the syllable *a* emerges. From the syllable *a*, revolving and turning, all the syllables are generated, pervading all mantra-names, circling back and forth a hundred-thousand-million-fold, entering the gates of all revolving dhāraṇī-samādhis without obstruction. Therefore, from the syllable *a*, revolving and turning, the three bodies and four wisdoms are generated, turning the Dharma-wheel.

If the practitioner serves as an ācārya for others, one must first establish the Great Compassion Womb-Treasury Maṇḍala-King, abide in the Buddha's station, and with these assembled syllables form the body to abide in the Buddha's station. When distributing the syllables, they are divided into four layers, and at each layer there are three kinds of taking refuge. The head is the first division; the throat is the second division; the heart is the third division; the navel is the fourth division.

The method for distributing the syllables is as follows: beginning from the white ūrṇā-mark between the practitioner's eyebrows, contemplate the syllable *ka*. Rotate to the right following the sun's course, making a connected ring. The four layers are likewise; each one assembled into a connected body.

Therefore the sūtra says: "*Ka*, *ca*, *ṭa*, *ta*, *pa*: the initial, middle, and final characteristics, augmented by the qualities of equipoise and classified entry, naturally attain the practice of the bodhicitta, accomplish perfect enlightenment, and reach parinirvāṇa." This refers to the meaning of the four layers of the initial *a*, *ā*, *aṃ*, *aḥ*. Furthermore, the twenty syllables *ka*, *ca*, *ṭa*, *ta*, *pa*, and so on are the middle meaning. The twelve syllables from *i*, *ī* through *u*, *ū* are the meaning of augmented equipoise and classified entry. The twenty syllables *ṅa*, *ña*, *ṇa*, *na*, *ma* and so on are the final meaning.

Therefore, speaking comprehensively, the mutual entry is spontaneous. Another explanation divides into three sections: the head is the initial; the throat and heart are the middle; the navel is the final. If using the syllable *ka* as the wheel, then do not use the syllable *kaṃ*; if using *kaṃ* as the wheel, then do not use *ka*. This is called: generating the bodhicitta; practice and fruition as the middle; great quiescence as the final. This is called the three divisions. Once the ācārya has thus formed the body through the three layers, the maṇḍala and the disciples are also distributed in the same way. When all three are accomplished, this is called the Secret Maṇḍala. If one does not penetrate the intent herein, and merely follows the outward procedural methods in performing it, it cannot be called well-done. The effort will be wasted with nothing accomplished.

Furthermore, this method of distributing syllables is the three-layered Secret Maṇḍala of the Great Compassion Womb-Treasury. Unless one has gathered the practice of true mantras and is worthy of transmission, it should be transmitted only through mental intention, and cannot be committed to writing. Therefore the master transmits it orally. The twelve syllables from *i*, *ī* through *u*, *ū* are all scattered outside the syllable-wheel as

rays of light. Furthermore, *ṅa*, *ña*, *ṇa*, *na*, *ma*, and the five other syllables follow the three wheels in their turning, entering spontaneously. This wheel here is the meaning of the three turnings of the Dharma-wheel.

When the practitioner attains correspondence in this way, distributing the syllables and maintaining recitation, this is the body of a vidyādhara, like the empowerment of the spiritual power of Mahāvairocana Tathāgata, equal and without difference. This wheel is also called the wheel of dependent origination. When the master, the disciples, and the maṇḍala all have the syllables distributed in this way, it is also called the Secret Wheel. These syllables are also the syllables of the heart of all the Buddhas, also called the Mantra Wheel.

"Wheel" in Sanskrit is called *akṣara-cakra*. *Akṣara-cakra* means: from a single syllable *a*, the wheel generates many syllables, and is therefore called a wheel. The syllable *a* is the essential nature of bodhi. Just as Mahāvairocana abides in the essential nature of the bodhicitta, manifesting in various ways, opening universal gates of benefit, transforming and appearing in limitless and boundless forms: though such traces are left inexhaustibly, in truth he constantly abides without moving, with neither arising nor ceasing. It is like a chariot wheel: though it turns without end, the center never moves. Because it does not move, it can govern all beings. Moving yet non-moving: this is the meaning of the syllable *a* as "primordially unborn." Being without birth and without cessation, neither moving nor regressing, it generates all syllables. Wheel after wheel, endlessly: this is called the non-moving wheel.

If the ācārya can thus penetrate the non-moving wheel and distribute the syllables, then one's body naturally, in all its expressions, is nothing other than secret seals. Whatever the mouth speaks is entirely mantra. The mind constantly abiding is nothing other than the gate of contemplation. All who see, hear, touch, or know will certainly be established in unsurpassed bodhi, and the merit accomplished is true and not false. If one can be thus, one is the same as Mahāvairocana Tathāgata, performing the Buddha's work and constantly illuminating the world.

When the ācārya engages in recitation, one may also contemplate the phrase-wheel. The phrase-wheel means: contemplate within the heart of the principal deity a circle of radiance, with the syllables of the mantra distributed and revolving, connected and clearly manifest before one. Contemplate this syllable-wheel as being like white milk, flowing in sequence and pouring into the practitioner's mouth, or pouring down upon the crown, continuously without interruption, filling the entire body. Within the circle of radiance is the seed-syllable, the syllable always clear and distinct, like flowing water without end. Having thus recited until exhaustion, simply abide in the quiescent mind of the syllable *a*.

Furthermore, "three lakṣa" means a number: that is, three hundred thousand recitations. When the practitioner further sees the principal deity's seed-syllable and seal, from within the principal deity's seed-syllable and seal one sees the principal deity. Having thus accomplished this, one can then universally distribute the syllables to form one's own body. One then truly knows one's own mind and accomplishes the body of Universal Illumination. This is the meaning of *lakṣa*. If one does not attain correspondence in this way, then even reciting a thousand-million *lakṣa* over a hundred years, one still will not succeed, let alone three *lakṣa*.

If the practitioner attains equality of the three aspects: first, the true nature of the body, this is one *lakṣa*, removing all bodily defilements; second, the true nature of speech, this is two *lakṣa*, removing all verbal defilements; third, the true nature of mind, removing all mental defilements. When the three defilements are removed, the three merits arise, and one partially realizes the Tathāgata's merit. When karmic activity thus corresponds, and one abides in the bodhicitta-precepts, all the stages and pāramitās are completely fulfilled. In whatever Dharma-teaching one dwells, one relies on the mantra-vows. Whatever wish produces fruition, one should constantly, toward self and others, with compassion rescue and protect.

The sūtra says: "Vajradhara said to the Buddha: 'How does the Tathāgata, the Arhat, the Samyak-saṃbuddha obtain sarvajña-jñāna? He broadly proclaims and distributes various paths, expounding sarvajña-jñāna. Each speaks in the same language of its own voice, dwelling in various modes of conduct, yet the path of sarvajña-jñāna is of one taste. World-Honored One! It is like earth, water, fire, wind, and space: their self-nature pervades all places, and all do not go beyond the gate of the syllable *a*.'" Therefore Vajrapāṇi asked Mahāvairocana: "Such wisdom: what is its cause? What is its root? What is its ultimate consummation?" The Buddha told Vajrapāṇi: "The bodhicitta is the cause; compassion is the root; skillful means is the ultimate consummation. O Vajradhara! What is bodhi? It means truly knowing one's own mind. This is the single characteristic of the syllable *a*, which is also neither one nor different."

Furthermore, the sūtra says: "Listen well, O Vajrapāṇi! I now teach the practice of the maṇḍala and the Dharma-gate that fulfills sarvajña-jñāna." Therefore, when the myriad practices have reached their ultimate fullness with nothing more to add, and the expedients for responding to beings have exhausted their function, this is the supreme fruit of ghee, the source of the three mysteries. The wisdom of that most true reality obtains the name "ācārya." Therefore one should be skilled in skillful means and understand what the Buddha has taught. The sūtra says:

> "Furthermore, O Vajradhara! What all the Buddhas have taught:
> The distribution of all the syllable-gates. Buddha-sons, listen with one mind!
> The syllable *ka* is at the base of the throat; the syllable *kha* is at the upper palate.
> The syllable *ga* is the neck; the syllable *gha* is in the throat.
> The syllable *ca* is the root of the tongue; the syllable *cha* is the middle of the tongue.
> The syllable *ja* is the tip of the tongue; the syllable *jha* is where the tongue arises.
> The syllable *ṭa* is the shins; the syllable *ṭha* should be known as the thighs.
> The syllable *ṇa* is said to be the waist; the syllable *ḍha* is where one sits.
> The syllable *ta* is the lowest part; the syllable *tha* should be known as the belly.
> The syllable *da* is the two hands; the syllable *dha* is called the flanks.
> The syllable *pa* is the back; the syllable *pha* should be known as the chest.
> The syllable *ma* is one elbow; the syllable *ba* is below the arms.
> The syllable *ma* (nasal) abides in the heart; the syllable *ya* is the form of the private parts.
> The syllable *ra* is called the eyes; the syllable *la* is the broad forehead.
> *Ī* and *i* are at the two corners of the eyes; *ū* and *u* are the two lips.
> *Ai* and *e* are the two ears; *o* and *au* are the two cheeks.
> The syllable *aṃ* is the phrase of bodhi; the syllable *aḥ* is parinirvāṇa.
> Knowing all these dharmas, the practitioner accomplishes perfect enlightenment.
> All the wealth of sarvajña constantly abides within one's heart.
> The world honors one as Sarvajña: this is Sarvajña."
