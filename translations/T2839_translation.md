# 讚禪門詩
## Poem in Praise of the Gate of Chan

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2839

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown.

---

The Master asks: "Adding three dots to form a word, what thing is it?" The student says: "It is 'Buddha.'" The Master says: "Buddha is a Sanskrit word of the western lands. In this land it means 'awakening.' As for the meaning of awakening, it means the mind-substance is free from thought. The characteristic of being free from thought is equal to the realm of space, pervading everywhere without omission. The dharma-realm has one characteristic, which is the Tathāgata's equal Dharma-body. Based on this Dharma-body, it is called 'original awakening.'"

The Master asks: "What is awakening?" Answer: "Awakening is separation. Separation means being free from thought. Each and every one is free from both form and mind."

The Master asks: "What is form?" Answer: "It is the body. Body and mind are both free; at a single moment, pervadingly free; in all things, constantly free."

The Master asks: "Have you attained this freedom?" Answer: "Attained freedom from freedom. Freedom is Buddha. What is Buddha? Buddha is awakening. Awakening has three kinds: first, self-awakening; second, awakening others; third, complete enlightenment. Freedom from mind is called self-awakening; freedom from form is called awakening others; freedom from both mind and form is called complete enlightenment. 'Complete' means that in a single thought-moment of freedom, all is free; there is no freedom and no non-freedom. This is the perfectly pure Tathāgata. In Sanskrit, Vairocana; in this land, 'radiance pervading everywhere,' the equal dharma-nature. Based on this dharma-nature, it is called 'original freedom.' This is its abbreviated name."

Explaining the meaning of the three awakenings as pervading the characteristic of freedom from thought:

The Master asks: "Is it with characteristics or without characteristics?" The student says: "Without characteristics." Explanation: Characteristics without anything characterized: this is the true characteristic. This is called "freedom from characteristics," pervading the realm of space equally. Space is without mind; freedom from thought is without mind. Uniting with space, this is called "equal to the realm of space." In space there is no center and no periphery; the bodies of all buddhas are likewise. The mind, being the same as space, pervades everywhere. This pervades "without anywhere it does not reach." Space is without mind and pervades everything; freedom from thought is without mind and pervades everything. This is called "pervading everywhere without omission."

This pervades "the dharma-realm of one characteristic":

The Master asks: "What is 'dharma'?" The student says: "What the intellect knows is called dharma. Where the dharma known by the intellect and the five sense-faculties reaches is called 'realm.' When the eye sees form, the intellect uses knowledge: this is the defiled dharma-realm. When the mind does not arise and does not follow the same knowing: this is the pure dharma-realm." This pervades the six sense-faculties' dhāraṇī, from the *Buddhabhiṣeka-sūtra*, fascicle six.

The Master says: "What is Buddha?" The student says: "It is awakening. Awakening is freedom from the eye. Freedom from all form is called the dhāraṇī of sovereign mastery of the eye. The rest follow the above pattern." When the eye sees form, the intellect uses knowledge: this is the defiled dharma-realm. When the mind does not arise and does not follow the same knowing: this is the pure dharma-realm. It gathers all form-dust. This is called the gate of comprehensive retention. This is called sovereign mastery. The rest follow the above pattern: the eye-realm, the form-realm, and the eye-consciousness-realm. The remaining sense-faculties follow the above pattern.

This pervades "realm," which has the meaning of "storehouse." Within it, boundlessly vast and great merit is contained. As the *Vijñaptimātratā* says: "This is the undefiled realm. One should properly contain boundless merit."

This pervades "the dharma-realm of one characteristic." "One characteristic" means no characteristic. What is "no characteristic"? "No characteristic" is "without characteristics." "Without characteristics" is the true characteristic. This is called "the dharma-realm of one characteristic."

The *Ṣaḍāyatana-vimokṣa-dvāra* says: The eye is empty, without self and without what belongs to self; its nature is itself empty. The rest of the sense-faculties follow the above pattern. The ear-faculty is without self and without what belongs to self; its nature is itself empty. The rest follow the above pattern.

This pervades "the Tathāgata's equal Dharma-body": Freedom from mind, and mind is thus; freedom from form, and form is thus; mind and form are both thus. This is the Tathāgata's equal Dharma-body. There are five kinds of Dharma-body: first, the Dharma-body of true characteristics, because characteristics have nothing characterized; second, the Dharma-body of merit, because freedom from thought is the merit and manifesting principle is the virtue; third, the Dharma-body of dharma-nature, because the nature of all dharmas is freedom; fourth, the Dharma-body of response and transformation, because the six sense-faculties are undefiled; fifth, the Dharma-body of space, because it is equal to the realm of space in substance.

This pervades: relying on the meaning of "gathering, substance, and assemblage." "Substance" refers to freedom from mind; "relying" refers to freedom from form; "assemblage" refers to freedom from both.

This pervades original enlightenment and initial awakening: freedom from thought is called original awakening; freedom from form is called initial awakening. When both form and mind are free, nature and characteristics are perfectly fused; principle and phenomena both pervade without obstruction. This is called the concluding summary of the meaning of the three awakenings.

If one is free from the mind, craving does not arise. If one is free from form, anger is not produced. If both form and mind are free, ignorance does not appear. Furthermore, freedom from mind is escape from the desire realm; freedom from form is escape from the form realm; freedom from both is escape from the formless realm.

This pervades craving, anger, and ignorance: "As for the meaning of awakening, it means the mind-substance is free from thought. The characteristic of being free from thought is equal to the realm of space, pervading everywhere without omission." This is called self-awakening. "Freedom from the characteristic of anger is equal to the realm of space, pervading everywhere without omission." This is called awakening others. "Freedom from the characteristic of ignorance is equal to the realm of space, pervading everywhere without omission. The dharma-realm has one characteristic, which is the Tathāgata's equal Dharma-body." This is called complete enlightenment.

This pervades the meaning of the three realms: "As for the meaning of awakening, it means the mind-substance is free from desire. Freedom from desire is equal to the realm of space, pervading everywhere without omission." This is self-awakening. Freedom from the characteristic of the form realm and freedom from the characteristic of the formless realm: this is called the three poisons escaping the three realms.

This pervades the three gates of liberation: Freedom from mind enters the gate of emptiness-liberation; freedom from form enters the gate of signlessness-liberation; freedom from both enters the gate of wishlessness-liberation.

This pervades the meaning of the five dharmas: Freedom from mind is called "non-arising"; freedom from the form-characteristic is called "non-production"; freedom from both is called "freedom from false conception." Freedom from mind is right wisdom; freedom from form is suchness; freedom from both is right wisdom and suchness together.

This pervades the three self-natures: Freedom from mind: the self-nature of false imagination does not arise. Freedom from form: the self-nature of dependent arising does not arise. Freedom from both: this is the self-nature of ultimate accomplishment.

This pervades the two kinds of selflessness: Freedom from mind attains the selflessness of persons; freedom from form attains the selflessness of dharmas; freedom from both attains the wisdom of the two kinds of selflessness.

This pervades the three kinds of patience: the patience of the original nature of non-arising, the patience of natural non-arising, and the patience of enduring the suffering of non-arising. Freedom from mind realizes the patience of original nature; freedom from form attains the patience of natural non-arising; freedom from both realizes the patience of enduring suffering.

This pervades the meaning of the three virtues: *mahā-prajñā*, liberation, and the Dharma-body. Freedom from mind manifests the principle: the great wisdom called *mahā-prajñā*. Freedom from form manifests liberation. Freedom from both manifests the Dharma-body.

This pervades the three bodies: Freedom from mind attains the transformation-body; freedom from form attains the reward-body; freedom from both attains the Dharma-body.

This pervades the six pāramitās: Giving has three kinds. Freedom from mind is the giving of material goods; freedom from form is the giving of fearlessness; freedom from both is the giving of Dharma. Morality has three kinds. Freedom from mind gathers deluded sentient beings; freedom from form gathers scattered goodness; freedom from both attains all the precepts of right conduct. Patience has three kinds. Freedom from mind attains the highest; freedom from form attains the middle; freedom from both attains the lowest. Vigor has three kinds. Freedom from mind attains the armor of vigor; freedom from form attains the gathering of goodness; freedom from both attains the benefit and joy of beings. Dhyāna has three kinds. Freedom from mind attains peaceful abiding; freedom from form attains the power of attraction; freedom from both attains accomplishment of tasks. Wisdom has three kinds. Freedom from mind attains the non-discriminating wisdom of the emptiness of persons; freedom from form attains the next level; freedom from both attains the lowest level. One should always cultivate in accordance with these three.

Furthermore, laterally this pervades the three awakenings, severing the three poisons, escaping the three realms, entering the three gates of liberation, being free from the three natures, attaining the two kinds of selflessness, realizing the three kinds of patience, manifesting the three virtues, accomplishing the three bodies. All are pervaded through freedom from mind and freedom from form. Therefore it is called "comprehensively displaying the five sense-faculties as all being gates of wisdom."

Explaining the mind-faculty: non-movement is the gate of wisdom. Non-movement of the mind-consciousness is the gate of opening wisdom.

Concluding summary: If one does not attain skillful means, the state of non-movement is actually a state of movement, and one falls into heterodox concentration, becoming attached to the flavor of dhyāna and falling into the nirvāṇa of the two vehicles. Non-movement through attaining skillful means is right concentration, which is perfect quiescence, the great nirvāṇa.

This pervades the great nirvāṇa: it is perfection. Perfection is the great nirvāṇa. Non-movement of form is perfection. Non-movement of mind is the function of wisdom, which is the knowing of non-movement of form. This is the function of discernment, which is seeing. Non-movement of both is the opening of the Buddha's knowledge and vision, attaining the great nirvāṇa. Nirvāṇa is the unmoving knowledge and vision. This is the third level of right speech. All are pervaded through the mind not thinking and the mouth not discussing, pervading all dharmas.

From the various liberations to entering the gate of non-duality: entering through the eye into form, manifesting the dharma of inconceivable display. Gods and humans of the world cannot know this. Entering through the dharma of form into the eye, contemplating the eye as unborn and without self-nature, speaking of it as empty, quiescent, and without any existence. Furthermore, entering right absorption through the eye-faculty, arising from samādhi within the dharma of form. Furthermore, entering through the eye into samādhi within the dharma of form, arising from it. Furthermore, entering right absorption of equal awakening through the eye-faculty, arising from samādhi within the dharma of form. Furthermore, entering right absorption through the eye-faculty, arising from samādhi within the dharma of form.

## Poem in Praise of the Gate of Chan

> Whose traces are the sixteen-foot body? Revered through the three ages.
> The principle of bodhi transcends past, present, and future.
> To ascend to the other shore, there is no path of learning.
> All depends on the mind that evaluates like grass.

*Recorded by the novice Minghui on the twenty-third day of the second month, dīngyǒu year.*
