# Treatise in One Hundred Characters

## *Bǎizì Lùn* (百字論)

Taishō 1572

Composed by the Bodhisattva Āryadeva (*Típó*, 提婆). Translated by Bodhiruci (*Pútí Liúzhī*, 菩提流支) of the Later Wei dynasty.

*Note: This is a compact Madhyamaka treatise attributed to Āryadeva (c. 3rd century CE), the chief disciple of Nāgārjuna. Through a rapid succession of dialectical exchanges with various non-Buddhist (Sāṃkhya, Vaiśeṣika, and other) opponents, it systematically refutes the doctrines of oneness, difference, existence, non-existence, causation, selfhood, atomism, permanence, and the conditioned and unconditioned, arriving at the Madhyamaka conclusion that all dharmas are like a dream: neither truly existent nor truly non-existent, neither uncaused. The treatise concludes with a verse summary affirming the middle way beyond existence and non-existence as the path to nirvāṇa. The Chinese title "Hundred Characters" refers to the hundred-character verse (*śatākṣara*) that summarizes the work at its conclusion.*

---

> I now take refuge in the wise teacher,
> Whose name is Deva, of great wisdom,
> Who could expound the true Dharma in a hundred characters,
> Removing all wrong views and pointing toward true reality.

### Purpose of the Treatise

It is asked: Why compose this treatise? To refute the view of self and the [view that] all dharmas each have their own intrinsic characteristic.

---

### Refutation of Oneness

The Sāṃkhya says: "All dharmas are of one characteristic. This is my solemn thesis. For what reason do I establish that all dharmas are of one characteristic? Because all things share in common the quality of oneness. Just as pots, clothing, and other objects each possess the quality of being one thing. Therefore, know that all dharmas are of one characteristic; the meaning of 'oneness' is thus established."

The Buddhist replies: "Not one. Why? Your solemn thesis establishes the meaning of 'one characteristic.' But is that [thesis itself] one, or two? If it is one, then there is only the thesis and there should be no 'oneness' [distinct from it]. For this reason, the 'oneness' you establish is already refuted."

### Refutation of Difference

The Vaiśeṣika says: "Since you have refuted oneness, I now establish difference, abandoning the fault of oneness."

The Buddhist replies: "If you establish difference, I return to establishing oneness. Why? If you establish difference apart from a cause, I can equally establish oneness apart from a cause."

The Vaiśeṣika says: "I firmly establish difference. Why? Because all dharmas are distinguished, each having a different characteristic. Just as elephants, camels, deer, and horses are all different in their characteristics. Therefore all dharmas have different characteristics; the meaning of 'difference' is established."

The Buddhist replies: "You say the meaning of difference is established because 'this' and 'that' have different characteristics. But precisely because their characteristics differ, each thing is individually one. Your thesis of 'difference' is thereby undermined. Since the thesis is undermined, the characteristic of difference is not established."

### Refutation of Existence

The opponent says: "Since the characteristics of oneness and difference both fail, I now establish the characteristic of existence. Because dharmas each have their own characteristic, the meaning of 'existence' is established. Because 'existence' is established, oneness and difference are also established."

The Buddhist replies: "If you now establish existence, you must have a reason. If you establish existence without a reason, I can equally establish non-existence without a reason."

The opponent says: "I firmly establish that all dharmas exist. Why? Because one presently sees that dharmas each have their own characteristics. Just as flowers in the sky have no substantial characteristic and therefore cannot be apprehended, yet pots, clothing, and other objects have present utility: know that all dharmas are of the characteristic of existence."

The Buddhist replies: "You who establish existence: is it because of an existing characteristic that they exist, or because of a non-existing characteristic? Both have faults. If because of a present characteristic the meaning of existence is established, then the present characteristic is 'existent' and 'existence' is also 'existent'; two 'existents' logically cannot establish each other. If you say it is because of non-existence, then your thesis is destroyed. Since both 'existent' and 'non-existent' are not valid causes, the meaning of 'existence' is refuted."

### Refutation of Non-Existence

The opponent says: "If you refute my 'existence,' then you establish non-existence. If the meaning of 'non-existence' is established, then existence is also re-established."

The Buddhist replies: "You who establish non-existence: by what cause is it established? If you establish non-existence without a cause, I can equally establish existence without a cause."

The opponent says: "Because things have no substantial characteristic. Just as in hot weather, a mirage has no substantial characteristic, and certainly no water can be obtained. For this reason, not even a speck of dust among all dharmas can be apprehended as having a characteristic; therefore the meaning of 'non-existence' is established."

The Buddhist replies: "Your 'non-existence': does it have a cause or not? If you say it has no cause, then your thesis is merely empty words. If you say it has a cause, then your thesis is destroyed. Likewise, your 'nothing at all' is also not established."

### Refutation of Causation

The opponent says: "All dharmas have causes. Your refutation of existence and non-existence is unreasonable. Why? Because, seeing that there is clay, thread, reeds, and so on, we know all dharmas have causes."

The Buddhist replies: "No causes. You say that because causes exist, things exist. But if there are causes, things are [already] non-existent [i.e., do not need to be produced]. If the pot already exists in the clay, the clay, reeds, and thread are all not causes. Why? Because [the effect] already exists in the cause. If the effect does not previously exist in the cause, that too is not a valid causal example. Just as oil does not exist in sand; sand is not the cause of oil. If you say [the effect] both exists and does not exist [in the cause], the meaning also fails, because of two faults."

"Furthermore: what exists is not produced, and what does not exist is not produced. If produced from no cause, what use would a cause be? If produced from an existing cause, your thesis is destroyed, for you first said all dharmas arise from causes. This is not so."

[The treatise continues through a series of rapid dialectical exchanges, each beginning with an opponent's position and ending with the Buddhist refutation:]

- **Against self (ātman)**: If the self is identified with cognition, cognition is impermanent (pot-cognition ceases, cloth-cognition arises). If the self has no cognition, it has no suffering or pleasure and thus no characteristic. If the self merely "conjoins" with cognition, then cognition conjoined with self is also not-cognition.

- **Against "oneness" of a thing with its existence**: If the pot and its "oneness" (*ekatva*) are not different, then every "oneness" should be a pot, resulting in many pots. If "oneness" is not the pot, then there is no pot.

- **Against perception establishing existence**: Do the eyes see, or does consciousness see? If the eyes, corpses with eyes should see. If consciousness, the blind with consciousness should see. If neither alone sees, their conjunction also does not see, like one blind person not seeing and many blind persons also not seeing.

- **Against the pre-existence of effects in causes**: If the effect already exists in the cause, no artisan is needed. If the cause already contains the pot, the potter is unnecessary; if the thread already contains the cloth, the weaver is unnecessary. Therefore the effect does not pre-exist in the cause. Furthermore, if effects pre-existed, there would be no future dharmas, no arising and ceasing, no good and evil, no karmic retribution; everything would be nothing.

- **Against the arising of effects from non-existence**: What does not exist (like tortoise-hair) cannot be woven into something useful. Therefore: what exists is not produced, what does not exist is not produced.

- **Against arising from self, from other, or from both**: All have faults. If self-arising, why would further arising be needed? If not from self, how from other? If from both, both faults apply. Therefore all dharmas are without arising.

- **Against the three conditioned marks (arising, abiding, ceasing)**: Whether sequential or simultaneous, both have faults. If sequential: at the time of arising there is no abiding or ceasing; at the time of ceasing there is no arising or abiding. If simultaneous: arising and ceasing are contradictory and cannot coexist. Furthermore, if the marks are conditioned, each mark needs its own three marks, leading to infinite regress. If the marks are unconditioned, how can conditioned marks characterize the unconditioned?

- **Against the unconditioned (space, etc.)**: If space has location, it has parts, limits, and is therefore impermanent. If a permanent cause produces a permanent effect, and impermanent causes produce impermanent effects, then effects produced from causes (like pots from clay) are impermanent because of having location.

---

### All Dharmas Are Like a Dream

The Buddhist says: "Just as things seen in a dream are not truly existent yet not non-existent, and not without cause. Conventional dharmas are the same: neither with the mark of existence, nor with the mark of non-existence, nor without cause. Like a house: if it had inherent substance, one should see it before it is built. If it were nothing, one should never see it. Yet dependent on beams, rafters, foundation, and walls, it has functional utility; it is not without cause. Therefore all dharmas are neither existent, nor non-existent, nor without cause. They are therefore like a dream."

The opponent says: "If all dharmas are like a dream, why, when people young and old reach for a pot, do they not grasp cloth? If when grasping cloth they also do not grasp a pot? Since we see that grasping a pot does not grasp other things, and names are definite, all dharmas are not like a dream."

The Buddhist replies: "Names are not essences. If a name were the essence, then merely having the name 'pot' should entail having the function of holding milk and curd. If merely by having the name one already had the function, there should be no need for a potter to make it or a buyer to purchase it. Furthermore, 'body' has three names: male, female, and neither-male-nor-female. If one takes the essence from the name, it would encompass all three. If one seeks names from names, the three do not subsume each other. Therefore name and essence are different."

"Furthermore, a pot has a sound that can be heard, a form that can be seen, an odor and texture that can also be apprehended. If so, there should be many pots. Also, a pot has mouth, neck, base, and belly; these names are not one, so again there should be many pots. By such examination, names and words are conventionally established; know that they have no reality."

As the Buddha said in verse:

> "In the world there are conventional names,
> Their characteristics like a mirage in the heat.
> Sounds are like an echo.
> The characteristics of the world are like a dream."

The opponent says: "Though you have refuted in various ways that dharmas are existent, if you say they are existent, that destroys your thesis. If you say they are non-existent, what is there to refute?"

The Buddhist replies: "If your dharmas have substance and characteristics, then I have something to refute. If originally they have no substance, then I have nothing to refute."

---

### Concluding Verse

> The great person has the mark of equanimity;
> The mind has neither attachment
> Nor non-attachment;
> There is no abiding whatsoever.

> All things that have substance and characteristics,
> Whether with desire or with desire severed,
> Accomplish indestructible faith
> And abandon all wrong views.

> Clearing away the net of wrong views,
> All defilements completely extinguished,
> Able to discard the thorns of the three poisons,
> Diligently practicing the right path.

> Skillfully investigating such dharmas,
> Deeply giving rise to the mind of faith and reverence,
> With a faithful mind seeking the true Dharma,
> Not heading toward the three realms of existence.

> Not grasping at non-existence either,
> One realizes and verifies the path of quiescent cessation.
> All dharmas are not one;
> Such dharmas are also not different.

> How could there be a mark of existence?
> Dependent dharmas have no inherent substance.
> They are not established through mutual dependence.
> "It is just so by nature" does not hold.

> Your dharmas then are not established.
> Without employing causes in this way,
> You should explain the substantial characteristics.
> Oneness already has faults.

> If so, then there is no substance.
> The five sense faculties do not apprehend objects.
> Form-dharmas have names and words,
> But what is seen also has no substance.

> What exists need not be produced;
> Those dharmas have no arising.
> Conditioned dharmas have no substance;
> Likewise, there are spatial parts.

> Equally, like a dream, without difference;
> Characteristics also without difference.
> This is the Treatise in One Hundred Characters,
> Spoken by Deva.
