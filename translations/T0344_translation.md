# 佛說太子和休經
## Sūtra of Prince Hexi Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0344

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

The Buddha was in the city of Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak Mountain, together with ten thousand bodhisattvas, one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, upāsakas and upāsikās, the deva-kings Brahmā and Śakra, and an immeasurable number of humans, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas, all seated together.

The son of King Ajātaśatru, a prince named Hexi, together with five hundred sons of elders, each bearing a golden flower-parasol, departed the city of Rājagṛha and accompanied the prince to the Buddha. Having offered the parasols to the Buddha, each joined their palms, bowed their heads to the ground in homage to the Buddha, and stepped back to stand before him. The prince joined his palms and addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva obtain a beautiful countenance? By what causes is a bodhisattva not born from a woman's womb but born within a lotus flower? By what causes can one know the events of one's past lives? May the Buddha grant his great kindness and explain these things in detail."

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva who practices patience without anger will be reborn with a beautiful countenance in a future life. The bodhisattva who does not engage in licentiousness or have sexual relations with women will not be born from a woman's womb but will be born by transformation within a lotus flower in a future life. The bodhisattva who delights in upholding the sūtras and precepts and teaching others will, in a future life, naturally know the events of immeasurable past ages."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva have the thirty-two marks? By what causes the eighty excellent characteristics? By what causes do those who see the Buddha's bodily form never tire of gazing upon it?"

The Buddha told the prince: "When one was formerly a bodhisattva, one delighted in giving to people. Whatever anyone came seeking, whether robes, food and drink, gold, silver, precious treasures, carriages, horses, servants, wife and children, flesh, head, or eyes, one never refused anyone and was without any covetousness or miserliness. By this cause one obtains the thirty-two marks. The bodhisattva has a compassionate mind, with pity for the humans, insects, and creatures of the world, regarding them as one's own infant child, wishing to liberate them. By this cause one obtains the eighty excellent characteristics. The bodhisattva regards enemies as parents, with a mind of evenness without difference. By this cause, those who see the Buddha's bodily form never tire of gazing upon it."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva know the profound sūtras and wisdom? By what causes does one know the peace and comfort of samādhi? By what causes does all that the Buddha says cause those who hear it to be joyful?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva delights in copying sūtras, reciting, studying, and teaching them. By this cause one knows the profound sūtras and wisdom. The bodhisattva always loves concentrating the mind in tranquility. By this cause one attains the peace and comfort of samādhi. The bodhisattva speaks always with sincerity, never deceiving or being negligent toward others. By this cause one's words are sincere and trusted, and those who hear them are all joyful."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes are the deeds of the body, words of the mouth, and thoughts of the mind of a bodhisattva all pure? By what causes can Māra not find an opening? By what causes do the people not dare to slander the Buddha's sūtras and path, or the sangha of bhikṣus?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva delights in attending upon the Buddha, in the sūtras and the path, and in the sangha of bhikṣus. By this cause one attains purity. The bodhisattva, day and night, practices with diligence. By this cause Māra cannot find an opening. The bodhisattva speaks always with sincerity and without deception. By this cause the people do not dare to slander the Buddha's sūtras and path, or the sangha of bhikṣus."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva obtain a long lifespan? By what causes is one free from illness? By what causes does one's household live in harmony and mutual respect, without others causing separation?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva has a compassionate mind and does not take life. By this cause one obtains a long lifespan in a future life. The bodhisattva does not frighten others with weapons. By this cause one is born free from illness in a future life. The bodhisattva who, seeing people in conflict, delights in rescuing and reconciling them to bring about harmony: by this cause one is born in a future life where others do not cause separation."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva easily obtain wealth and riches without difficulty? By what causes does one not lose wealth, nor have it stolen or robbed? By what causes does one attain a position of honor and nobility?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva who is wise and not foolish: by this cause obtains great wealth without difficulty. The bodhisattva who delights in giving without miserliness does not lose wealth. Those who see others prosper and gain money without feeling envy: by this cause they attain honored status. The bodhisattva who does not kill and is not arrogant: by this cause attains noble rank."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva obtain the divine eye of penetrating vision? By what causes the divine ear of comprehensive hearing? By what causes does one know the good and evil destinations of birth and death in the world?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva who delights in lighting lamps at the Buddha's temple: by this cause obtains the divine eye of penetrating vision. The bodhisattva who delights in offering music at the Buddha's temple: by this cause obtains the divine ear of comprehensive hearing. The bodhisattva who enters samādhi and attains dhyāna: by this cause knows the good and evil destinations of birth and death in the world."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva obtain the supernatural flight of the four bases of power? By what causes can a bodhisattva remember the events of immeasurable past kalpas? By what causes, when a bodhisattva attains Buddhahood, does one enter parinirvāṇa?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva who gives generously, providing carriages, horses, elephants, mules, donkeys, camels, and sandals to others: by this cause obtains the supernatural flight of the four bases of power. The bodhisattva who delights in mindfulness of all the Buddhas' samādhis and supernatural abilities, studying from them and delighting in teaching others: by this cause can remember the events of immeasurable past kalpas. When the bodhisattva attains the Buddha's intention free from attachment: by this cause one enters parinirvāṇa."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva preside over a buddha-land? By what causes does one know that in a future life one will have a sangha of bhikṣus? By what causes does one's radiance illuminate the ten directions?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva who constantly makes many vows: by this cause presides over a buddha-land. The bodhisattva who gives generously to the people and delights in teaching them to practice the six pāramitā sūtras: by this cause obtains a sangha of bhikṣus in the future. The bodhisattva who makes parasols of the seven treasures as offerings to the Buddha and the Buddha's temples: by this cause one's radiance illuminates the ten directions."

When the Buddha had explained these matters in detail for the prince, the prince was exceedingly joyful, and all five hundred sons of elders were also greatly joyful.

The prince addressed the Buddha: "In my future lives, will I receive and practice all that the Buddha has taught, and will all be fully accomplished?"

The Buddha gave a great laugh, and five-colored light came forth from his mouth, illuminating the ten directions. The Bodhisattva Maitreya rose, came forward, knelt, joined his palms, and addressed the Buddha: "The Buddha does not laugh without reason. For what cause has five-colored light come forth to illuminate the ten directions?"

The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Maitreya: "Listen as I explain. Prince Hexi and the five hundred sons of elders, in past lives, made offerings to ten billion Buddhas and all practiced the bodhisattva path. Long ago, in the time of the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, these five hundred were all my disciples whom I taught. In future lives they shall all meet together and make offerings to six hundred million Buddhas. In a future kalpa named Mahāpāramitā, during that single kalpa, these five hundred shall successively become Buddhas, all under the same name: Jñānaketudhāna. When they become Buddhas in the future, their lands shall be like the land of the Buddha Amitābha, and the bodhisattvas who come and go, fly about, and perform transformations shall all be like those of the land of the Buddha Amitābha. Those who hear this sūtra shall all be reborn as bodhisattvas in the land of the Buddha Amitābha, like the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī and the Bodhisattva Samādhipātra; when they become Buddhas in future lives, they shall be like the Buddha Amitābha."

When the sūtra had been spoken, Prince Hexi and the five hundred sons of elders, all the bodhisattvas, bhikṣus and bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas and upāsikās, all the devas, Brahmā, humans, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas were all greatly joyful, bowed to the Buddha in homage, and departed.
