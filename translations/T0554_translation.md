# 佛說奈女耆婆經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Nai-Fruit Maiden and Jīvaka

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0554

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by An Shigao (安世高)

---

When the Buddha was in the world, in the royal garden of the kingdom of Vaiśālī, a nai-fruit tree spontaneously grew. Its branches and leaves were luxuriant, its fruit was especially large, and it possessed both luster and color, with a fragrance and flavor quite extraordinary. The king truly cherished this nai-fruit tree; none but the noble beauties of the inner palace were allowed to eat its fruit.

In that kingdom there was a brahmin householder whose wealth was immeasurable, without equal in the whole country. Moreover, he was brilliant and broadly learned, with talent and wisdom surpassing all. The king valued and esteemed him, and appointed him as a great minister.

The king invited the brahmin to a feast, and when the meal was finished, rewarded him with one nai-fruit. The brahmin, seeing the fruit's extraordinary fragrance and beauty, asked the king: "Might there be a small sapling beneath this nai-fruit tree that I could beg from you?" The king said: "There are many small saplings. I fear they hinder the great tree, so I always have them removed. If you want one, I shall give you one now." He then gave him one nai-fruit sapling.

The brahmin took it home and planted it, watering it morning and evening. Day by day it grew larger; its branches and foliage were fine and full. In three years it bore fruit, with luster, size, and color just like the king's nai-fruit. The brahmin was overjoyed and thought: "My household has immeasurable wealth, no less than the king's. Only this nai-fruit I lacked, which made me feel inferior. Now I have it, and I am no less than the king." He picked one and ate it, but it was intensely bitter and astringent, utterly inedible.

The brahmin grew greatly troubled and distressed. He withdrew to consider: "It must be that the soil lacks richness and moisture." So he took the milk of a hundred cows and fed it to one cow. Then he took that one cow's milk and boiled it into ghee, and poured it on the nai-fruit tree's roots. Day after day he poured it, and by the following year the fruit was sweet and delicious, just like the king's. But suddenly a knobby growth appeared on the side of the trunk, as large as a fist, growing larger day by day. The brahmin thought: "This sudden growth may harm the fruit. I would like to chop it off, but I fear I would injure the tree." For several days he deliberated, hesitating and undecided. Then from the growth a branch suddenly sprouted, pointing straight upward, tall and smooth and well-formed. It rose above the treetop, seven zhang from the ground. At its crown it divided into many branches, spreading outward on all sides like a flat canopy. Its flowers and leaves were even more luxuriant and beautiful than the original tree.

The brahmin was amazed, wondering what might be atop the branch. He built a scaffolding and climbed up to look. There, within the flat canopy atop the branch, he found a pool of water, both clear and fragrant, as well as various flowers of brilliant and vivid colors. Parting the flowers, he saw beneath them a baby girl lying in the pool. The brahmin took her in his arms, brought her home, and raised her, naming her the Nai-Fruit Maiden. By the age of fifteen, her beauty and bearing were without equal in all the world, and her renown spread to distant kingdoms.

Seven kings came at the same time to the brahmin's home, seeking to betroth the Nai-Fruit Maiden as their queen. The brahmin was terrified, not knowing to whom he should give her. So in his garden he built a tall tower, placed the Nai-Fruit Maiden atop it, came out, and said to the kings: "This girl was not born from me. She appeared atop the nai-fruit tree. I do not know whether she is the daughter of a deva, a nāga, a spirit, or some phantom creature. Now seven kings have all come seeking her hand. If I gave her to one king, the other six would be angry. I dare not hold her back. The girl is now atop the tower in the garden. Your Majesties may discuss among yourselves: whoever is fit to have her may take her. It is not for me to decide."

The seven kings then began to dispute among themselves, arguing ceaselessly without resolution. That evening, King Bimbisāra entered through a hidden passage, climbed the tower, and spent the night with her. The next morning, as he was about to leave, the Nai-Fruit Maiden said: "Your Majesty has graciously condescended to come near me. Now you would abandon me and leave. If I should bear a child, it would be of the royal line. To whom shall I entrust it?" The king said: "If it is a boy, return him to me. If it is a girl, you may keep her." The king then removed his gold signet ring from his hand and gave it to the Nai-Fruit Maiden as a token of trust. He went out and told his ministers: "I have already had the Nai-Fruit Maiden. Having spent one night with her, she is nothing extraordinary, no different from an ordinary woman, so I shall not take her." The troops of Bimbisāra's army all cheered "Long live the king!" and said: "Our king has already had the Nai-Fruit Maiden." The six other kings, hearing this, each departed.

The Nai-Fruit Maiden subsequently gave birth to a boy. At the moment of birth, the child emerged holding a bag of needles and medicines in his hands. The brahmin said: "This is the son of a king, yet he holds medical instruments. He must be a king of physicians." He was named Jīvaka.

By the age of eight, Jīvaka was brilliant and gifted. In his studies of writing and texts, he far surpassed his peers. When playing with the neighboring children, he always looked down on them for not being his equal. The children together taunted him: "Fatherless child, born of a loose woman! How dare you look down on us?"

Jīvaka was startled and fell silent, making no reply. He went home and asked his mother: "I see that I surpass all the other children, yet they taunt me saying, 'Fatherless child.' Where is my father now?" His mother said: "Your father is none other than King Bimbisāra." Jīvaka said: "King Bimbisāra is in the kingdom of Rājagṛha, five hundred li from here. How did he come to father me? If what you say is true, what proof is there?" His mother then brought out the signet ring and showed it to him, saying: "This is your father's ring."

Jīvaka examined it and saw the inscription of King Bimbisāra's seal. He then took the ring and journeyed to the kingdom of Rājagṛha. He went straight through the palace gate, and no one at the gate stopped him. He came before the king, made obeisance, knelt, and said: "I am the king's son, born of the Nai-Fruit Maiden. I am now eight years old. Only now have I learned that I am of the king's line, so I have brought this signet ring as proof and have come from afar to return home."

The king saw the inscription, recalled his earlier pledge, and recognized Jīvaka as his son. He was moved with tender pity and installed him as crown prince. Two years later, Prince Ajātaśatru was born. Jīvaka then addressed the king: "When I was born, I held a bag of needles and medicines in my hands. This means I am destined to be a physician. Although the king has made me crown prince, this is not what I desire. The king now has a legitimate son born to him, who should inherit the royal succession. I wish to be allowed to go and study the art of medicine." The king consented.

The king said: "Since you will not be crown prince, you must not eat the king's provisions for nothing. You should study the way of medicine." The king then commanded all the foremost physicians in the kingdom to teach Jīvaka their complete arts. But Jīvaka only played and amused himself, never once receiving instruction. His teachers reprimanded him: "The art of medicine is indeed humble and lowly, truly not something befitting a prince of the highest rank. Yet the great king's command cannot be disobeyed. Since receiving this order, much time has passed, yet the prince has not received even half a word of medical knowledge. If the king should question us, how shall we answer?"

Jīvaka said: "I was born with the proof of medicine in my hands. That is why I petitioned the great king to give up glory and rank in order to study the art of medicine. How could I be lazy and need a teacher's prodding? It is simply that the masters' knowledge has nothing worth learning." He then took up the Materia Medica, the Formulary, and the various classics on acupuncture and pulse-reading, and posed difficult questions to the teachers. The teachers were stumped, unable to answer. They all prostrated before Jīvaka and knelt with palms joined, saying: "Only today do we truly know that the prince is divinely gifted. Truly, this is beyond our reach. All the questions you have posed are difficulties that our teachers through the generations were unable to resolve. We beg the prince to explain them fully, unraveling the puzzles of our lifetime." Jīvaka then explained the principles for them. The physicians were overjoyed. They all rose and prostrated again, receiving his teachings.

Thereupon Jīvaka went about treating the sick. Whoever he treated was cured, and he became famous throughout the kingdom. Later, wishing to enter the palace, at the palace gate he encountered a young boy carrying firewood. Looking at the boy, Jīvaka could see clearly all his five viscera, intestines, and stomach, each distinctly visible. Jīvaka thought: "The Materia Medica speaks of a Medicine King herb that, when viewed from the outside, reveals the internal organs. Could there be a Medicine King among this boy's firewood?" He went up to the boy and asked: "How much for the firewood?" The boy said: "Ten coins." He paid the boy ten coins. The boy set the firewood down on the ground, and immediately everything became dark again; Jīvaka could no longer see inside the boy. Jīvaka reflected further: "Which piece among the bundle is the Medicine King?" He untied the two bundles and tested each piece one by one, placing it against the boy's belly. When a piece did not reveal anything, he tried another. He went through both bundles until, at the very end, there remained one small twig barely a foot long. He tried holding it up, and at once he could again see clearly inside the belly. Jīvaka was overjoyed, knowing this small twig was certainly the Medicine King herb. He returned all the firewood to the boy. The boy, having already received his money, and his firewood being restored as before, went off happily.

At that time in the kingdom there was a householder's daughter, fifteen years old, who on the very day she was to be married suddenly suffered a headache and died. Jīvaka heard of it and went to her home. He asked the girl's father: "What illness did this girl normally have, that she came to die of it?" The father said: "My daughter had suffered from headaches since she was small, growing worse month by month. This morning it struck with exceptional severity, and she died of it."

Jīvaka then approached. Using the Medicine King herb to see inside her head, he saw that thorn-worms, large and small, had been breeding, numbering several hundred. They had bored into and fed upon her brain; when the brain was consumed, she died. With a golden scalpel he cut open her skull, removed all the worms, and sealed them in a jar. He then applied three kinds of divine ointment: the first to repair the damage between the bones where the worms had fed; the second to regenerate the brain; the third to heal the external incision. He told the girl's father: "Keep her quiet and still, and take care not to startle her. In seven days she will recover and be as she was before. On that day, I shall come again."

After Jīvaka departed, the girl's mother began to weep: "My child will die a second time! How can anyone cut open the head, treat the brain, and expect the patient to live? How could her father bear to let a stranger do such a thing to our child?" The father stopped her: "Jīvaka was born grasping needles and medicines. He renounced his princely rank to become a physician, solely for the sake of all people's lives. He is a divinely appointed king of physicians. How could he act carelessly? He instructed us: 'Take care not to startle her.' Yet now you weep and cry, disturbing her. You will prevent our child from coming back to life!" The mother, hearing the father's words, stopped weeping. They tended and cared for the girl in silence for seven days.

At dawn on the seventh day, the girl drew a breath and awoke, as if waking from sleep. She said: "My headache is completely gone now. My whole body is at ease. Who cared for me and brought me to this state?" The father said: "You had already died. The physician-king Jīvaka came to care for you, opening your skull and removing the worms, and thus you were born again." He opened the jar and showed her the worms. Seeing them, the girl was greatly frightened. Deeply grateful, she exclaimed: "Jīvaka's powers are truly extraordinary! How can I repay his kindness?" The father said: "Jīvaka made an appointment with me, saying: 'I shall come today.'" Before long, Jīvaka arrived. The girl was overjoyed, went out the door to greet him, prostrated at his feet, knelt with palms joined, and said: "I wish to serve as Jīvaka's maidservant, attending him for the rest of my life, to repay the grace of being given life anew."

Jīvaka said: "I am a physician. I travel about treating the sick and have no fixed dwelling. What use have I for a maidservant? If you truly wish to repay me, give me five hundred liang of gold. I have no use for this gold myself. The reason I ask is that whoever studies the Way should properly express gratitude to their teachers. Although my teachers had nothing they could teach me, I did formally serve as their student. With the gold I receive from you, I shall give it to them." The girl then presented five hundred liang of gold to Jīvaka. Jīvaka received it and gave it to his teachers. He then petitioned the king: "I wish to return briefly to visit my mother in the kingdom of Vaiśālī."

In that kingdom there was also a householder's son who was fond of martial pursuits. He built a wooden horse, over seven feet tall, and practiced on it every day, learning to mount it. When he first learned to get on, after much practice he suddenly slipped past the seat, fell to the ground, and died.

Jīvaka heard of it and went to examine him with the Medicine King herb. He saw that the boy's liver had been twisted backward and his vital energy was blocked, which was why he had died. He again used his golden scalpel to open the belly, reached in with his hand to realign the liver and put it back in place. He then applied three kinds of divine ointment: the first to repair the tissue where his hand had grasped and manipulated; the second to restore the flow of vital energy; the third to close the incision. He told the boy's father: "Take care not to startle him. In three days he will recover."

The father followed these instructions, tending to him in silence. On the third day, the boy exhaled and awoke, as if waking from sleep, and immediately sat up. Before long, Jīvaka came. The boy joyfully went out the door to greet him, prostrated, knelt, and said: "I wish to serve as Jīvaka's servant, attending him for the rest of my life, to repay the grace of being brought back to life."

Jīvaka said: "I am a physician. I travel about treating the sick. Wherever I go, the households of the sick vie to serve me. What use have I for a servant? My mother raised me with much toil and hardship, and I have not yet repaid her kindness. If you wish to thank me, give me five hundred liang of gold, so that I may repay my mother's kindness." He then took the gold and presented it to the Nai-Fruit Maiden, and returned to the kingdom of Rājagṛha. Through restoring these two people to life, Jīvaka's fame spread throughout the world, and all came to know of him.

To the south there was a great kingdom, eight thousand li from Rājagṛha. Both Bimbisāra and the other smaller kingdoms were all vassals of this king. This king had fallen ill, and for many years he had not recovered. He was constantly afflicted by fits of rage. He would kill people for the slightest glance: if someone looked at him, he killed them; if they averted their eyes, he killed them too. If a servant moved too slowly, he killed them; if they hurried, he killed them as well. His attendants did not know where to put their hands and feet. When physicians prepared medicine, he would accuse them of poisoning him and kill them too. The palace women, ministers, and physicians he had killed in this way were beyond counting. His illness grew worse by the day. A fiery poison attacked his heart, leaving him restless, gasping for breath, as if his body were on fire. Hearing of Jīvaka's fame, he sent a letter commanding King Bimbisāra to summon Jīvaka.

Jīvaka, hearing that this king had killed many physicians, was greatly frightened. Bimbisāra too had compassion for his young son and feared he would be killed. He would have liked not to send him, but feared retribution. Father and son clung to each other, anxious day and night, not knowing what to do. At that time, King Bimbisāra took Jīvaka and they went together to consult the Buddha.

The Buddha told Jīvaka: "In your past life, you and I made a vow together: we would both rescue and heal the illnesses of all people under heaven. I would treat the inner illness, and you would treat the outer illness. Now I have attained buddhahood, and in accordance with that original vow, you have been born before me. This king's illness is grave, and he has sent from afar to summon you. How can you not go? Go quickly to rescue and heal him. Use skillful means, and his illness will certainly be cured. The king will not kill you."

Empowered by the Buddha's spiritual authority, Jīvaka then went to the king's court. He examined the pulse and used the Medicine King herb to see inside. He saw that in the king's five viscera and all his hundred vessels, the blood and vital energy were in turmoil; throughout his entire body there was nothing but the venom of snakes and scorpions.

Jīvaka said to the king: "Your Majesty's illness can be treated, and treatment will certainly bring a cure. However, it would be proper for me to see the queen mother and consult with her about compounding the medicine. If I do not see the queen mother, the medicine can never be completed."

The king, hearing these words, did not understand the reason and was inclined to be angry. Yet he was suffering from his illness and, having long heard of Jīvaka's fame, had summoned him from afar in hope of benefit. Moreover, this was only a child who surely had no treacherous intent. Restraining himself, the king consented, and dispatched eunuchs and attendants to escort Jīvaka in to see the queen mother.

Jīvaka said to the queen mother: "The king's illness can be treated. I must now compound the medicine, but the formula must be kept secret and not disclosed. I request that all attendants be dismissed." The queen mother dismissed the eunuchs and attendants. Jīvaka then said: "When I examined the king just now, I saw that all the blood and vital energy in his body consist of the venom of snakes and scorpions. He does not seem to be of human origin. Whose son is the king really? If the queen mother tells me the truth, I can effect a cure. If you do not tell me, I cannot treat him, and the illness will not be cured."

The queen mother said: "Once, long ago, I was lying down for an afternoon rest in the Golden Pillar Hall when suddenly something came and pressed upon my body. I was half-dreaming, half-awake, as if in a nightmare, and it had intercourse with me. I woke with a start and saw a great scorpion, over three feet long, leaving from above me. I then discovered I was with child. The king is truly the offspring of that scorpion. I have been ashamed of this and have never spoken of it. Yet this young man has now perceived it. How divinely perceptive! If the illness can be treated, I entrust the king's life to this young man. What medicine should be used?"

Jīvaka said: "Only ghee."

The queen mother exclaimed: "Be careful, young man! Do not speak of ghee! The king has an extreme aversion to the smell of ghee and an extreme aversion to hearing the very word. Hundreds and thousands of people have been put to death in the past merely for uttering the word 'ghee.' If you say this, he will surely kill you. And if you try to make him drink it, he will never swallow it. Please use some other medicine."

Jīvaka said: "Ghee counteracts venom. That the venomous illness makes him detest ghee confirms it. If the king's illness were mild, or if it were some other kind of poison, there would be other medicines that could cure it. But the scorpion's venom is severe and has pervaded his entire body; nothing but ghee can dissolve it. I shall boil and refine it until it becomes like water, with no smell and no taste. The king will not realize what it is, and will drink it of his own accord. Once the medicine takes effect, he will certainly be cured. There is nothing to worry about." He then went out to the king and said: "I have consulted with the queen mother about the formula and shall now compound it. It will be ready in fifteen days. I have five requests. If the king grants them, his illness can be cured. If not, it cannot."

The king asked: "What are these five requests?"

Jīvaka said: "First, I request new garments from the king's first storehouse, never yet worn, for me to put on. Second, I request free passage in and out of the palace gates without being stopped. Third, I request daily private visits with the queen mother and the queen, without restriction. Fourth, when the king drinks the medicine, he must drink it all at once without pausing. Fifth, I request the king's white elephant that can travel eight thousand li in a day, for me to ride."

The king, hearing this, was furious: "You little rat! How dare you make these five demands! Explain yourself at once, or I will beat you to death! Why do you want my new garments? Do you intend to kill me and wear my clothes, impersonating me?"

Jīvaka said: "In compounding medicine, one must be ritually pure and observe the precepts. I have been traveling a long time, and my clothes are all soiled with dust and grime. I simply wish to wear the king's garments while compounding the medicine."

The king's anger subsided: "Very well. But why do you want free passage in and out of the palace? Do you plan to bring soldiers in to attack and kill me?"

Jīvaka said: "All the physicians the king has previously employed have been suspected and mistrusted, and then executed. They dared not administer their medicines. All the ministers, great and small, say the king will kill me too. The king's illness is severe, and I fear outsiders may plot rebellion. If I am allowed to come and go freely without being stopped, all people great and small will know the king trusts me and will certainly take my medicine, and the illness will be cured. Then they will not dare harbor rebellious thoughts."

The king said: "Very well. But why do you want to visit my mother and wife privately every day? Do you intend some impropriety?"

Jīvaka said: "The king has killed so many people that all his ministers and subjects, great and small, live in terror. None wish for the king's well-being, and none can be trusted. If someone else helps compound the medicine, they could slip in poison when I am not looking, and I would not notice. That would be no small matter. Upon reflection, the only trustworthy people in all the world, whose loyalty is unwavering, are one's mother and one's wife. I wish to visit the queen mother and the queen to compound the medicine with them. The medicine must be boiled for fifteen days before it is ready, so I need daily access to tend the fire and adjust the dosage."

The king said: "Very well. But why must I drink the medicine all at once without pausing? Do you want to slip in poison and fear I will notice?"

Jīvaka said: "Medicine has precise dosages, and the potencies must be properly complementary. If you pause in the middle, the potencies will not connect properly."

The king said: "Very well. But why do you want my white elephant? This elephant is the treasure of my nation. It can travel eight thousand li in a single day. It is the reason I can keep all the other kingdoms in submission. Do you want to ride it away, stealing it to help your father attack my kingdom?"

Jīvaka said: "In the mountains to the south there is a marvelous medicinal herb, four thousand li from here. After the king takes the medicine, he should immediately obtain this herb and take it again as a supplementary dose. I wish to ride the white elephant to go and gather it, departing in the morning and returning by evening, so that the medicine's efficacy will be continuous." The king was fully satisfied and consented to all five requests.

Thereupon Jīvaka boiled and refined the ghee for fifteen days until it was ready, transformed into something like clear water, yielding five sheng in all. Together with the queen mother and the queen, he brought the medicine out and said to the king: "You may now take the medicine. I request that the white elephant be saddled and placed ready before the hall." The king consented.

The king looked at the medicine: it was like clear water, with no smell or taste at all. He did not know it was ghee. Moreover, the queen mother and the queen had personally overseen its preparation, so he trusted it was not poison. In accordance with the original agreement, he drank it all in one draught.

Jīvaka then mounted the elephant and rode straight home to his own kingdom. After traveling three thousand li, being still young and not yet strong enough for such speed, he became dizzy and exhausted, and stopped in the mountains to lie down and rest.

Around midday, the king belched and smelled ghee. He flew into a rage: "That little rat tricked me with ghee! No wonder the rat asked for my white elephant: it was precisely so he could flee!" The king had a warrior-minister named Crow who alone was swift enough on foot to catch the elephant. The king summoned Crow and said: "Go quickly and bring that rat back alive. I will beat him to death before my own eyes. You are by nature unable to exercise restraint, and are greedy for food and drink, which is why you are called Crow. These physicians are fond of using poisons. If the rat prepares food for you, do not eat it under any circumstances!"

Crow accepted the command and set off. He caught up with Jīvaka in the mountains. Crow said: "Why did you trick the king with ghee, calling it medicine? The king has ordered me to bring you back. Come with me at once and make a full confession; perhaps you may hope to survive. If you try to run, I will kill you right now. You cannot escape."

Jīvaka thought: "Although I devised this plan to obtain the white elephant, I still cannot escape. I must devise another plan; I cannot simply go back." He said to Crow: "I have not eaten since this morning. If I go back, I will surely die. Could you spare me just a moment, so I may eat some fruit and drink some water in the mountains, and die on a full stomach?"

Crow, seeing that Jīvaka was a frightened child afraid of death, and hearing his pitiful words, took pity and consented: "Eat quickly and then let us go. Do not linger."

Jīvaka took a pear, bit off half and ate it, then placed a purgative drug under his fingernail. He set the remaining half on the ground. He then took a cup of water, drank half, secretly slipped the drug from under his nail into the remaining water, and set it down on the ground. Then he sighed and said: "This water and this pear are both celestial medicines. They are clear, fragrant, and delicious. Whoever drinks and eats of them finds the body at ease, all illnesses cured, and strength doubled. What a pity they are not found near cities where the people could share in them, but only deep in these mountains where no one knows of them." Then he went further into the mountains, as if searching for other fruit.

Crow, being by nature greedy and unable to endure hunger and thirst, and having heard Jīvaka praise them as divine medicines, and having seen Jīvaka himself eat and drink from them (so he assumed they were not poisoned), took the remaining pear and ate it, and drank the remaining water. Immediately he developed severe diarrhea, purging like a stream of water. He collapsed on the ground, and every time he tried to rise, he became dizzy and fell again, unable to move.

Jīvaka came to him and said: "The king has taken my medicine, and his illness will certainly be cured. However, the medicine has not yet fully taken effect, and the residual poison has not yet been completely expelled. If I go back now, the king will certainly kill me. You know nothing of medicine, yet you want to capture me to save your own skin, so I have deliberately made you ill. Your illness is not serious. Be careful not to move about. In three days you will recover. But if you try to get up and chase me, you will die without question." He then mounted the elephant and departed.

Jīvaka passed through a town and told the village headman: "This is an envoy of the great king who has suddenly fallen ill. You must go quickly and carry him back to shelter. Take good care of him, give him a proper bed and mats, and feed him rice porridge. Take care that he does not die. If he dies, the king will destroy your village." Having said this, he departed and returned to his own kingdom.

The headman followed these instructions, carrying Crow to shelter and nursing him. After three days, the drug had run its course and the purging ceased. Crow then returned and went before the king. He kowtowed and confessed: "I was truly foolish. I disobeyed the king's instructions and, trusting Jīvaka's words, ate and drank his leftover fruit and water. I was struck by the purgative and suffered diarrhea for three days, recovering only this morning. I deserve to die."

During the three days before Crow's return, the king's illness had already been cured. The king reflected with regret that he had sent Crow in pursuit. When he saw Crow return, he was both sad and glad: "It is fortunate that you did not bring the boy back at once. In my rage at that time I would certainly have beaten him to death. I received his grace, and my life was saved. To have killed him in return would have been a grave and perverse wrong." He then regretted all the people he had wrongly killed before, and had them all given proper burials. He restored their families and bestowed money and wealth upon them. Wishing to see Jīvaka and repay his kindness, he dispatched envoys to respectfully escort him. Jīvaka, although he knew the king's illness had been cured, still harbored residual fear and was reluctant to go again. Jīvaka went to the Buddha's abode, bowed at his feet, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, that country has sent envoys to summon me. Should I go?"

The Buddha told Jīvaka: "In your past life you already made a great vow. You should bring this merit to fruition. How can you stop midway? You should go again. You have treated his outer illness; I too shall treat his inner illness."

Jīvaka then accompanied the envoys back. The king, seeing Jīvaka, was overjoyed. He drew Jīvaka to sit beside him, took his arm, and said: "Thanks to the kindness of this benevolent one, I have been given a new life. How can I repay you? I shall divide my kingdom and give you half. The palace women, treasuries, and precious goods I shall all divide in half. I earnestly hope the benevolent one will accept."

Jīvaka said: "I was originally a crown prince. Though it is a small kingdom, it too has people and abundant treasures. I had no desire to rule a kingdom, which is why I sought to become a physician. My work is to go about treating the sick. What use have I for territory, palace women, or treasures? I need none of them. The king already granted my five requests, and the outer illness was cured. If the king will grant me one more request, the inner illness too can be removed."

The king said: "I shall follow the benevolent one's instruction. Please let me hear this one further request."

Jīvaka said: "I request that the king invite the Buddha and receive the illuminating Dharma from him." He then described the Buddha's merit, majestic and supremely honored.

The king heard this with great delight and said: "I wish to send Minister Crow and the white elephant to escort the Buddha. Can this be done?" Jīvaka said: "The white elephant is not necessary. The Buddha understands all things and knows from afar the thoughts of people's minds. Simply fast, keep the precepts, purify yourself, prepare offerings, burn incense, and from afar pay homage to the Buddha and kneel to invite him. The Buddha will certainly come of his own accord." The king did as instructed.

The next day, the Buddha came with 1,250 bhikṣus. When the meal was finished, the Buddha taught the king a sūtra. The king's mind was opened and he understood. He aroused the aspiration for supreme, perfect enlightenment. The entire kingdom, great and small, all received the five precepts. Each paid homage with respect and departed.

Now the Nai-Fruit Maiden, having been born in a miraculous manner, grew up to be exceptionally intelligent. Learning from her father, she became broadly versed in the sūtras, the Way, astrology, and various arts, surpassing even her father. In addition, she mastered vocal music, with a voice like the Brahma-heaven. The daughters of householders and brahmins, five hundred in all, came to study under her, regarding her as their great teacher.

The Nai-Fruit Maiden would regularly teach and transmit the sūtras and arts to her five hundred students. Sometimes they would go together to gardens and ponds to play and make music. The people of the kingdom, not understanding the situation, began to slander her, calling her a loose woman. Her five hundred students were all called her wanton entourage.

At the same time as the Nai-Fruit Maiden's birth, two other girls had also been born in the kingdom: the Sumanā Maiden and the Padma Maiden.

As for the Sumanā Maiden: she was born from a sumanā flower. A certain householder's family regularly pressed sumanā flowers to make fragrant ointment. Beside the pressing-stone, a small knobby growth appeared, the size of a pellet. Day by day it grew until it was as large as a fist. Then the stone burst open. Within the growth there was a glow like a firefly, which shot out and fell to the ground. After three days, a sumanā plant grew, and after three more days it bloomed. In the center of the flower was a baby girl. The householder took her and raised her, naming her the Sumanā Maiden. She grew up beautiful, talented, bright, and wise, second only to the Nai-Fruit Maiden.

At that same time, in a brahmin's family, a blue lotus flower spontaneously grew in the bathing pool. The flower was especially large, and day by day it grew bigger, until it was as large as a five-dou jar. When the flower opened, inside was a baby girl. The brahmin took her and raised her, naming her the Padma Maiden. She grew up to be beautiful, talented, bright, and wise, just like the Sumanā Maiden.

When the kings of various countries heard that these two girls had beauty unmatched in the world, they came one after another to seek their hands. The two girls said: "We were not born from a womb, but appeared from within grasses and flowers. We are different from ordinary people. How could we be expected to follow worldly custom and marry?" Hearing that the Nai-Fruit Maiden was brilliant beyond any equal in the world, and that she had been born in the same miraculous way as themselves, they both took leave of their parents, went to attend the Nai-Fruit Maiden, and asked to become her students. Their brilliance and broad learning surpassed the other five hundred.

When the Buddha was traveling in the kingdom of Vaiśālī, the Nai-Fruit Maiden led her five hundred students out of the city to welcome the Buddha. She prostrated, knelt with palms joined, and said: "I beg the Buddha to come to my garden tomorrow for a meal." The Buddha silently consented. The Nai-Fruit Maiden returned home to prepare the offerings.

As the Buddha proceeded into the city, the king also came out of the palace to welcome the Buddha. After paying homage, the king knelt and invited the Buddha, saying: "I beg the Buddha to come to the palace tomorrow." The Buddha said: "The Nai-Fruit Maiden has already invited me first. Your Majesty is second."

The king said: "I am the king! I invite the Buddha with utmost sincerity and earnestly hope you will accept! The Nai-Fruit Maiden is merely a loose woman who goes about every day with her five hundred wanton students, acting improperly. Why would the Buddha refuse me and accept her invitation?"

The Buddha said: "This woman is not a loose woman. In her past lives she accumulated great merit and has already made offerings to three hundred million buddhas. Moreover, in the past the Nai-Fruit Maiden, together with Sumanā and Padma, were always sisters. The Nai-Fruit Maiden was the eldest, Sumanā the middle, and Padma the youngest. They were born into a great and wealthy family. Together the three sisters supported five hundred bhikṣuṇīs, providing food and drink daily, as well as making clothing and supplying whatever was lacking, throughout their entire lives. The three women always made this vow: 'May we in our next lives encounter a buddha, and may we be born by spontaneous transformation, not through the womb, far removed from all defilement.' Now, in accordance with that original vow, they have been born in the time of my appearing.

"However, though in the past they made offerings to the bhikṣuṇīs, being children of a wealthy and powerful family, their speech was sometimes haughty and playful. From time to time they would tease the bhikṣuṇīs, saying: 'You nuns have been in the village so long; surely you must want to marry! It is only because we support and look after you that you cannot give free rein to your desires.' Because of this, they now receive this residual karmic consequence: although day after day they study the sūtras and practice the Way, they are falsely slandered in this life. Their five hundred students at that time also contributed their efforts to the support of the nuns, sharing in the same joyful mind. Therefore they have gathered together again now and still follow one another.

"As for Jīvaka, at that time he was the son of a poor family. Seeing the Nai-Fruit Maiden's generosity, he greatly admired and rejoiced in it, but had no resources of his own. So he would constantly sweep and clean for the bhikṣuṇīs. After finishing, he would always make this aspiration: 'May I be able to sweep away the illness and defilement from the bodies of all people under heaven. How wonderful that would be!' The Nai-Fruit Maiden took pity on his poverty and appreciated his diligence, and always called him 'my child.' When the bhikṣuṇīs fell ill, she would always send Jīvaka to fetch a physician and prepare medicines, saying: 'May you in a future life share with me in obtaining this blessing.' Through fetching physicians and effecting cures, Jīvaka then made this vow: 'May I in a future life become a great king of physicians, always treating the illnesses of the four elements in all people's bodies, and may every treatment succeed.' Because of these past causes and conditions, he has now been born as the Nai-Fruit Maiden's son, in accordance with their original vows." The king, hearing the Buddha's words, knelt at length in repentance and deferred his invitation to a later day.

The next day, the Buddha and the bhikṣus went to the Nai-Fruit Maiden's garden, where he fully expounded the merit of their original vows. When the three women heard the sūtra, their minds were opened and they understood. Together with their five hundred students, they all rejoiced simultaneously and attained the path of the arhat.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "You should receive and uphold this teaching, and expound it for the fourfold assembly. Do not let it be cut off. All sentient beings should guard their body, speech, and mind, and not give rise to arrogance and heedlessness. Because the Nai-Fruit Maiden in a past life playfully teased the bhikṣuṇīs, she was falsely slandered as a loose woman in this life. You should cultivate the karma of body, speech, and mind, always making wholesome vows. Those who hear this should rejoice, have faith, take pleasure in it, and uphold it. Do not slander others, lest you fall into hell and suffer residual consequences as animals for hundreds of thousands of kalpas. When finally reborn as a human, you will be poor and lowly, will not hear the correct Dharma, will be born into a family of wrong views, and will always encounter evil rulers, with a body that is not whole. You should cultivate your practice, receive, uphold, recite, and study this, and throughout all future ages never let it be cut off."
