# 佛說金光王童子經
## Sūtra of the Boy Suvarṇaprabhārāja, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0548

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Faxian (法賢)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in the city of Kapilavastu, at the Nyagrodha grove vihāra. At that time, among the Śākya clan there was a boy named Suvarṇaprabhārāja. His form was dignified and his limbs were well-proportioned. His radiance was dazzling, like the sun and moon. He was remarkably intelligent and exceptionally keen, surpassing others. He was naturally self-possessed and earnestly diligent. His teachers, friends, and elders guided his mind through discipline. He possessed all manner of rare treasures and fine playthings. Though he constantly enjoyed them, he was not attached to them. Together with his retinue, consorts, and one thousand five hundred female musicians, he constantly dwelt in his palace enjoying happiness.

One day, this boy happened to hear someone say: "The World-Honored One, having seen the suffering of the cycle of the three realms in the past, renounced the position of a wheel-turning king, left the household to practice austerities, and fulfilled the buddha-path. Sitting beneath a tree, he subdued the four kinds of Māra and realized unsurpassed enlightenment." When the boy heard this, his heart was filled with great joy. He immediately brought his retinue and attendants, surrounding him on all sides, and went to the Buddha's dwelling.

As he approached the gate of the vihāra, he set aside his pride. Together with his retinue, he proceeded on foot. When he reached the Buddha's presence, he joined his palms, bowed in reverence, and praised the Buddha: "The Tathāgata's body is like a golden mountain, and like a jeweled banner adorned with precious gems. His face is like the autumn moon, full and pure. The thirty-two marks and eighty refinements radiate light that shines afar, like a thousand suns. He is noble and auspicious, of great wisdom and diligence, endowed with great merit, complete in both blessings and wisdom. He knows all dharmas, resolves all doubts, possesses omniscience, and is the teacher of devas and humans. He is the father of the three realms who saves all sentient beings, appearing in the world to the joy of all who see him."

After the boy had offered this praise, he circumambulated the Buddha three times, bowed at the Buddha's feet, sat to one side, and wished to hear the wondrous Dharma. He also thought: "I was born in a royal palace with such good fortune and great power. What causes and conditions brought this about?"

At that time, the World-Honored One, knowing the thoughts in the boy Suvarṇaprabhārāja's mind, and that the assembly wished to hear the Dharma, broadly taught the twelve links of dependent origination and the Four Noble Truths. The teaching was good in the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end. The words were skillful and wonderful, the meaning profound, pure and unmixed, complete in the marks of the holy life of purity.

Having expounded this Dharma, the Buddha then told the story of the boy Suvarṇaprabhārāja's past lives and the workings of cause and effect. He said to the boy: "Long ago, in the past, ninety-one kalpas ago, the Tathāgata Vipaśyin, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One, appeared in the world, teaching and transforming sentient beings for their benefit and happiness. When the Buddha's work was done, he entered parinirvāṇa.

"At that time there was a man whose occupation was farming. Though he labored diligently, he always lacked. Yet he delighted in cultivating all manner of good deeds. From his own food and livelihood he set aside and accumulated wealth, and he made an image of Vipaśyin Buddha and used it for offerings. The fruit of this I shall now describe.

"Bhikṣus! The merit obtained by that farmer: for a hundred thousand lifetimes he became a cakravartin king ruling the four continents, flying through the sky, endowed with the seven treasures, attaining great sovereignty, his body radiating light. In lifetime after lifetime he experienced the supreme happiness. After exhausting this fortune, he was born in the six desire heavens as the lord of those heavens, experiencing great happiness for a hundred thousand lifetimes. For another hundred thousand lifetimes he became a king of Brahmā's heaven. For a hundred thousand lifetimes he became the lord of the Ābhāsvara heaven. For a hundred thousand lifetimes he became the lord of the four formless realms. During all this time he experienced heavenly bliss. Afterward, descending to the human realm, he was born among the kṣatriya, brahmin, and vaiśya clans, always holding a royal position and attaining great sovereignty.

"Bhikṣus! This boy has now come from the heavenly realm and been born again in a royal palace. The merit he created in the past cannot be diminished or damaged. He shall not fall into lower destinies. Hereafter he shall attain the undefiled and realize cessation.

"Bhikṣus! The one who made the image is this very boy Suvarṇaprabhārāja. I have now explained his causes and effects. Furthermore, if a person uses crystal, maṇi gems, gold, silver, brass, copper, or even wood, stone, or ivory, casting, painting, carving, or sculpting a Buddha image, whether it contains relics or not, whether short or tall, large or small, even pressing a Buddha image from fragrant clay, such is the fruit of making images.

"Bhikṣus! If a good man or good woman takes any one of these images and, with a sincere and pure mind, bathes it in accordance with the Dharma using fine fragrant water, the merit obtained I shall now describe. Such a good man or good woman, from life to life, shall always be born among the highest families of the kṣatriya, brahmin, vaiśya, and śūdra clans. Their merit shall be complete and their features fully endowed. They shall obtain knowledge of past lives and good retinues. Their servants, carriages, horses, precious treasures, and material possessions shall all be fully provided. All sentient beings shall rely on and look up to them. All who see them shall be delighted, and their words shall always be followed. The Buddhas, bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, and arhats shall always remember them. Whatever they wish for, they shall obtain.

"Bhikṣus! Even the merit of bathing a Buddha image cannot be fully conveyed by simile. How much more so the merit of making an image? As for jealousy, hatred, love, greed, anger, ignorance, and all afflictions, one shall swiftly attain liberation from them. Gradually progressing in practice, one shall realize unsurpassed enlightenment."

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke again in verse:

> "After my passing into cessation,
> If men or women
> Use various treasures to make images
> And bathe them with fine fragrant water,
> And with pleasing flowers
> Offer them with a sincere mind,
> In lifetime after lifetime
> They shall receive the reward of a dignified form.
> All sentient beings
> Shall gaze upon them without tiring.
> They shall receive every kind of reward,
> Both honor and happiness.
> Through this remaining merit,
> They shall leave behind all afflictions.
> The clouds of ignorance and darkness removed,
> They shall be like the moon abiding in empty space.
> Later, borne by superior power,
> They shall be reborn in the heavenly realms
> And become lords of the heavens,
> Enjoying happiness for a hundred kalpas.
> When their heavenly lifespan ends,
> They shall descend and become wheel-turning kings.
> Their form shall be like a heavenly body,
> Their merit supreme.
> The seven treasures shall always accompany them;
> They shall have supernatural powers and sovereignty.
> Touring the four continents,
> All things shall follow their wishes.
> Later they shall enter the holy path,
> And at last realize nirvāṇa.
> Furthermore, there are sentient beings
> Who encounter a Buddha's stūpa
> Containing relics
> As fine as particles of dust.
> If such a person rejoices
> And makes various offerings,
> The great accumulation of merit
> Shall be equal to what was described before.
> The Tathāgata's transformation bodies
> Pervade the three realms.
> The merit of one who makes offerings
> And the resulting rewards,
> Including renown and fame,
> Shall also pervade the ten directions.
> With a body colored like true gold,
> Radiance always dazzling,
> In the heavens and among humans
> They shall enjoy immeasurable happiness."

At that time, the boy Suvarṇaprabhārāja, having heard the Buddha's extensive and wondrous teaching and the story of his past causes and conditions, felt no attachment to his retinue. He grew weary of wealth and honor, abandoned his pleasures, and within the true Dharma sought to leave the household life.

The Buddha immediately ordained him as a śramaṇa, and he soon realized the fruit of arhatship. His retinue also, while seated in the assembly, all realized the fruits of the path.

When the Buddha had finished speaking this sūtra, the boy Suvarṇaprabhārāja, his retinue, and all in the assembly were filled with joy and faith. They bowed to the Buddha and withdrew.
