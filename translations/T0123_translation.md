# 佛說放牛經
## Sūtra on Herding Cattle Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0123

Translated from the Chinese. Later Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in the country of Śrāvastī, in the Jetavana, the garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the Buddha told the bhikṣus: "There are eleven practices. If a cowherd does not know the proper methods of herding cattle and does not understand how to tend them, what are the eleven? First, the cowherd does not know the cattle's color. Second, he does not know their markings. Third, he does not know how to brush them. Fourth, he does not know how to protect their wounds. Fifth, he does not know how to make smoke. Sixth, he does not know how to choose the right road. Seventh, he does not know how to care for the cattle with affection. Eighth, he does not know by which road to cross the water. Ninth, he does not know how to seek good water and grass. Tenth, when milking the cattle he does not leave a remainder. Eleventh, he does not know how to distinguish and tend those that are fit for use from those that are not. If a cowherd does not understand these eleven things and does not know how to tend and protect his cattle, the cattle will never multiply and will diminish day by day.

"Bhikṣus! If one does not know how to practice these eleven things, one is like such a cowherd and will never succeed as a śramaṇa. In this Dharma one will never plant the root-stock of the Dharma and Vinaya; there will be no leaves or branches to give sheltering shade. If one who does not practice these eleven things forces himself to become a śramaṇa, after death he will fall into the three evil destinies. What are the bhikṣu's eleven practices? A bhikṣu does not know color, does not know markings, does not know when to brush, does not know how to protect wounds, does not know how to make smoke, does not know how to choose the right road, does not know how to show care, does not know by which road to cross the water, does not know the feeding ground, and does not know how to respect the elders.

"Bhikṣus! How does one not know color? A bhikṣu does not know the four great elements and does not know the form derived from the four great elements. A bhikṣu who is thus does not know color. Bhikṣus! How does one not know markings? A bhikṣu does not know the marks of causes and conditions of delusion, and does not know the marks of causes and conditions of wisdom. How does one not know the marks of causes and conditions of delusion? A bhikṣu does not know dark conditions, does not know bright conditions, and does not know conditions that are both dark and bright. How does one not know the marks of wisdom? One does not know dark conditions, does not know bright conditions, and does not know conditions that are both bright and dark. A bhikṣu who is thus does not know markings.

"Bhikṣus! How does one fail to brush when one should brush? A bhikṣu, when thoughts of desire arise, delights in and clings to them, and does not relinquish, forget, or sever them. When thoughts of foolish attachment, greed, and miserliness arise, along with other evil thoughts, one embraces them all without spitting them out or letting go. A bhikṣu who is thus should brush but does not brush.

"Bhikṣus! How does one fail to protect wounds when one should protect them? A bhikṣu, seeing form, gives rise to perception; hearing sound, one becomes attached; thinking of physical forms, one does not know this to be harmful. One does not guard the eye faculty, nor the ear, nose, tongue, body, or mind, allowing them all to chase after external objects without being able to guard them. A bhikṣu who is thus should protect wounds but does not protect them.

"How, bhikṣus, does one fail to make smoke when one should make smoke? A bhikṣu does not know how to explain to others what one has learned. A bhikṣu who is thus should make smoke but does not make smoke.

"How, bhikṣus, does one not know how to choose the right road? A bhikṣu does not enter the straight path but walks on the wrong road. How does one walk the wrong road? A bhikṣu enters the quarter of prostitutes, places where alcohol is served, and places of gambling. A bhikṣu who is thus does not know the road.

"How does a bhikṣu not know care? A bhikṣu, when the jewel of the Dharma is being expounded, does not listen with wholehearted devotion. A bhikṣu who is thus does not know care.

"How, bhikṣus, does one not know how to cross the water? A bhikṣu does not know the four truths. What are the four truths? A bhikṣu does not know the truth of suffering, the truth of the arising of suffering, the truth of the cessation of suffering, and the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. A bhikṣu who is thus does not know how to cross the water.

"How, bhikṣus, does one not know the feeding ground? A bhikṣu does not know the four foundations of mindfulness. What are the four foundations of mindfulness? A bhikṣu does not know the internal contemplation of the body, the external contemplation of the body, or the internal and external contemplation of the body. One does not know the internal contemplation of feelings, the external contemplation of feelings, or the internal and external contemplation of feelings. One does not know the internal contemplation of the mind, the external contemplation of the mind, or the internal and external contemplation of the mind. One does not know the internal contemplation of dharmas, the external contemplation of dharmas, or the internal and external contemplation of dharmas. A bhikṣu who is thus does not know the feeding ground.

"How, bhikṣus, does one not leave a remainder when milking? A bhikṣu, when invited to a meal by a king, elder, or faithful layman or laywoman, and served various delicacies offered with sincere attention, does not know moderation. After eating, if there is a remainder, one still wishes to take it away. A bhikṣu who is thus does not know how to leave a remainder.

"How, bhikṣus, does one not know how to respect the elders? A bhikṣu, in the matter of respectful offerings, how does one fail to know? When there is a senior bhikṣu who has long practiced the virtues of the path and is broadly learned, a junior bhikṣu does not wholeheartedly pay respects. Seeing him, one does not rise or yield one's seat. One is casual, playful, and does not treat him with a good mind. A bhikṣu who is thus does not know how to respect elders.

"If a bhikṣu does not know how to practice these eleven things, in my Dharma he should not be a śramaṇa. He does not plant the root-stock of the Dharma and Vinaya; there are no branches or leaves to give sheltering shade, and all withers and decays by itself. It would be better to return to lay life. If one forces oneself to be a śramaṇa, one will surely enter the three evil destinies.

"Bhikṣus! If a cowherd knows the eleven practices and tends and protects his cattle, he can cause them to multiply and increase. What are the eleven? This cowherd knows color, knows markings, brushes them, protects their wounds, makes smoke, chooses the road, crosses the water, cares for the cattle with affection, seeks good water and grass, knows how much to leave when milking, and distinguishes the good cattle from the poor and tends those that are fit for use."

The Buddha then spoke these verses:

> "When a cowherd is careful and attentive, the owner of the cattle gains merit.
> Six head of cattle in six years become sixty without loss.
> A cowherd who is intelligent and knows how to distinguish the various markings:
> Such a cowherd was praised by the Buddhas of the past."

"Likewise, if a bhikṣu practices these eleven things, he will be able to plant in this Dharma the root-stock of the Dharma and Vinaya, and the branches and leaves will flourish and spread, giving great sheltering shade, never withering or decaying. What are the eleven? A bhikṣu knows color, knows markings, knows how to brush, knows how to cover wounds, knows when to make smoke, knows the road, knows care, knows how to cross the water, knows the feeding ground, knows how to leave a remainder, and knows how to respect the elder bhikṣus of long learning and advanced age with respectful offerings.

"How does a bhikṣu know color? A bhikṣu knows that the four great elements give rise to form. A bhikṣu who is thus knows color.

"How does a bhikṣu know markings? A bhikṣu distinguishes delusion from wisdom. What is delusion? To think what should not be thought, to do what should not be done, and to say what should not be said: this is delusion. What is wisdom? To think what is proper, to do what is proper, and to say what is proper: this is wisdom. Being able to distinguish delusion from wisdom: this is knowing markings.

"How does a bhikṣu brush when one should brush? A bhikṣu, when thoughts of desire arise, is able to control them, put them far away, and avoid them, spitting them out like a loathsome sight. When anger, miserliness, greed, and other evils arise, one is able to control them, put them far away, and avoid them, spitting them out like a loathsome sight. A bhikṣu who is thus brushes when one should brush.

"How does a bhikṣu protect wounds when one should protect them? A bhikṣu, when the eye sees form, does not distinguish good from bad, guards the eye faculty, does not cling to external forms, and puts far away all evils, protecting the eye faculty. When the ear hears sound, the nose smells fragrance, the tongue tastes flavor, the body craves fine textures, and the mind has many thoughts, one controls them so they do not become attached, protecting all these faculties and not being stained by external objects, spitting them out like a loathsome sight. A bhikṣu who is thus knows how to protect wounds.

"How does a bhikṣu make smoke at the proper time? A bhikṣu teaches others broadly according to what one has learned, heard, and understood. A bhikṣu who is thus knows how to make smoke.

"How does a bhikṣu know the road? A bhikṣu walks with careful attention on the eightfold path, and knows the places one should not go: the quarter of prostitutes, drinking establishments, and gambling halls. One never enters such places recklessly. A bhikṣu who is thus knows the road.

"How does a bhikṣu know care? A bhikṣu, seeing the jewel of the Dharma being expounded, listens with wholehearted devotion and leaps with joyful delight. A bhikṣu who is thus is called one who knows care.

"How does a bhikṣu know how to cross the water? A bhikṣu knows the four truths. What are the four truths? The truth of suffering, the truth of the arising of suffering, the truth of the cessation of suffering, and the truth of the path leading to the cessation of suffering. A bhikṣu who is thus knows how to cross the water.

"How does a bhikṣu know the feeding ground? A bhikṣu knows the four foundations of mindfulness. What are the four foundations of mindfulness? A bhikṣu contemplates the body internally, contemplates the body externally, and contemplates the body both internally and externally. One contemplates feelings internally, contemplates feelings externally, and contemplates feelings both internally and externally. One contemplates the mind internally, contemplates the mind externally, and contemplates the mind both internally and externally. One contemplates dharmas internally, contemplates dharmas externally, and contemplates dharmas both internally and externally. A bhikṣu who is thus knows the feeding ground.

"How does a bhikṣu know how to leave a remainder? A bhikṣu, when a king, elder, or faithful layman or laywoman invites with a mind of joyful faith and provides food and drink and various delicacies, offering them with added respect and encouragement, knows moderation. Sustaining the body, one then stops, reflecting on the Buddha's words: 'Though the donor gives abundantly, one should know one's own limit and not receive to the point of exhaustion.' A bhikṣu who is thus knows how to leave a remainder.

"How does a bhikṣu know how to respect the elder bhikṣus of long learning and advanced age with respectful offerings? A bhikṣu should draw near to such persons, paying respect and making offerings, going out to greet them and welcoming them upon their return, yielding one's seat when one sees them coming, offering what one can according to one's ability, and never being heedless or lazy. A bhikṣu who is thus knows how to respect elders.

"A bhikṣu who can practice these eleven things, in this Dharma plants the root-stock of the Dharma and Vinaya, and the branches and leaves flourish and spread, providing great sheltering shade, pure and undefiled."

At that time, the World-Honored One spoke these verses:

> "With faith, diligence, and learning, receiving food and knowing moderation,
> Respectful toward the elders: such conduct the Buddha has praised.
> These eleven Dharma practices the bhikṣu learns thus:
> Day and night with concentrated mind, in six years one attains arhatship."

The bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha taught, joyfully received and practiced it.
