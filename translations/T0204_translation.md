# 雜譬喻經
## Sūtra of Miscellaneous Parables

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0204

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by Lokakṣema (支婁迦讖)

---

## (1)

Once there was a bhikṣu of keen intelligence and wisdom who fell gravely ill. His disciple asked: "Have you attained arhatship?" He answered: "Not yet." "The state of non-returner?" "Not yet." The disciple asked: "Master, your attainment in the path is lofty and your reputation far-reaching. How is it you have not reached it?" The master told him: "I have already attained the fruit of once-returner, but have not yet passed beyond the second fruit." The disciple asked: "Having already attained the fruit of once-returner, what obstacle prevents you from reaching arhatship?" He answered: "I wish to see Maitreya Buddha. At that time, in three assemblies, two hundred and eighty billion people will attain arhatship, along with bodhisattvas beyond reckoning. Maitreya Tathāgata will have a great body, most honored, one hundred and sixty zhang tall. The people of his land will all have the complexion of peach blossoms. All will live eighty-four thousand years. The land will be level, and clothing and food will appear of their own accord. Jambudvīpa will be three hundred thousand li in both length and breadth. I wish to see this, and therefore I do not take arhatship. At the time of Maitreya Buddha, there will be two foremost disciples: the first named Miscellaneous-Giving, the second named Counted-and-Counted. I also wish to see them, to know how they compare to me." The disciple then asked: "Where did you hear this?" The master answered: "From the Buddha's sūtras." The disciple said: "Birth and death are full of toil and suffering. Even if Maitreya should have some different Dharma, would it be worth waiting for?" He answered: "There is no difference." "Are the six pāramitās, the four immeasurables, the four kinds of grace, and the Four Noble Truths any different?" He answered: "No." "If everything is the same and there is no difference between here and there, why wait any longer? You have now received the Buddha's grace; to turn instead to Maitreya is unjustified. You can attain liberation now, without needing to wait for him." The master said: "Enough! Please go out for now. I must contemplate." The disciple went out, and before he had reached the door, the master had already attained arhatship. The disciple returned and asked: "How is it now?" The master said: "I have attained arhatship." The disciple bowed and said: "In the time it takes to give a sharp rebuke, you have already realized the fruit."

## (2)

Once there was a bhikṣu who, when he attained the state of meditative absorption, was not burned by a wildfire. People who saw him thought he was a ghost and struck at him with a sword, but the blade broke and could not penetrate. Because of his one-pointed concentration, the blade could not enter; because of his body's suppleness, the fire could not burn him. There was another person who had attained absorption, and his disciple called him to come eat, but he did not notice. The disciple then pulled his arm, and the arm stretched to over a zhang in length. The disciple was alarmed and tried to tie it back in place, fearing that once tied it could not be undone. When the master emerged from meditation, his arm was in pain. He asked his disciple, who explained what had happened. The master said: "You should have woken me from absorption rather than pulling my arm and nearly breaking it. When a person attains meditative absorption, the body becomes as supple as cotton. It is the same when one is in the mother's womb."

## (3)

Once in the country of Kaśmīra there was a bodhisattva. At the moment of his birth, the earth quaked greatly, and his parents were alarmed. At that time, an arhat came and bowed his head to the ground, made offerings, and scattered flowers upon him. Later the child grew up, left home, and became extraordinarily wise and eloquent. Yet he was dissolute and without proper restraint. Nevertheless, all who heard him teach attained the path. At that time, two men who were both bhikṣus had been keeping the precepts in a monastery with purity and diligence for many years, but their minds remained unopened. A heavenly spirit told them: "In that country there is a bhikṣu who has converted many." The two men then made the long journey to request his teaching. At the time, this bhikṣu in that country was consorting with a courtesan. The two men sought an audience. The first entered, made obeisance, and sat to the side. The courtesan was still lying there, of surpassing beauty. With single-minded attention he listened to the teaching, harboring no other thought, and attained the path of stream-entry. He bowed his head and went out. Then the second man entered, made obeisance, sat down, and listened. But seeing the courtesan lying there, he thought: "This person is impure and depraved. We made this long and difficult journey for nothing." He rose and left. The bhikṣu said: "Why are you so troubled? Do you have wrong views?" The man said: "We mistakenly made this arduous journey, and the master is corrupt and engages in such dissolute behavior." The bhikṣu said: "You are greatly in error. A student of the Dharma should simply set his mind right and receive the teaching with wisdom. Why do you criticize right and wrong, generating evil thoughts yourself, and end up gaining nothing?" The man corrected his mind, and they entered together to hear the teaching. He too attained the path of stream-entry. The first attained arhatship. The master then hosted them and they returned to their home country. Afterward, the master was put in charge of a temple and spent great quantities of sangha property and consorted with women for entertainment, going to excess. The assembled monks deliberated on expelling him. An arhat said: "Do not expel him yet! Although he uses sangha property, he converts many." So they did not expel him. His associates went to him and said: "Your former disciples should be asked to repay what is owed to the community." They went to that country and obtained abundant treasures, returning to repay the monks severalfold.

## (4)

Once there was a virtuous man who upheld the Dharma with diligence. He fell ill and suddenly died. His wife and children wailed inconsolably. They cremated the body, gathered the bones, and buried them. Afterward, they neglected the sūtras and the path, ceasing to set out incense and lamps. Though the family was wealthy, on the first and last days of each month they would slaughter animals and prepare elaborate feasts, go to the grave mound to gather together, and weep and mourn in heartrending grief. The deceased, through the merit of his precepts and virtue, had been reborn in heaven. With his divine eye he looked down from afar, pitied them, and laughed at the utter foolishness of it. He then transformed himself into a small child near a pasture, tending an ox. The ox suddenly died. The child then began wailing, cutting grass and placing it before the ox, coaxing it to eat, and then beating it and calling it to rise. He wept and beat himself all day long. The people thought it strange and laughable, and they all went over to scold him: "Whose child are you? The ox is dead; you should go home and tell your family. What good does wailing do? Does a dead ox know anything?" The child said: "I am not foolish. The ox has only just died; there is still some hope. But your father died long ago. You prepare a hundred kinds of food and go to his grave to wail together. What do burned bones know?" When the people heard this, they were instantly enlightened. The child said: "I was originally your father. Through the Buddha's grace, I was reborn in heaven and have come to release you from your delusion." He then resumed his heavenly form. "If you wish to be like me, increase your diligent practice and offerings." Then he suddenly vanished. The wife, children, and all the relatives then returned home and devoted themselves to the precepts, virtue, generosity, and helping others. All ceased their sorrow, and all attained the path of stream-entry and were reborn together in heaven.

## (5)

In the ocean there was a country named Siṃhadvīpa, which produced many precious treasures but had no rock sugar. A merchant loaded more than five hundred carts of rock sugar and went there, intending to present it to the king, thinking the reward would surely surpass what he could get by selling it in the market. He placed the rock sugar at the palace gate and submitted a memorial explaining the matter. After a month, no one had paid any attention. He grew angry and said: "That king is also a person, and I am also a person. Our eyes, ears, noses, mouths, and four great elements are all the same. Yet I cannot even get a single audience with him. Why? Because the king's merit surpasses that of ordinary people. I too shall cultivate merit, and I shall make the king come to me without even realizing it." He then went and became a śramaṇa. He used the sugar to make offerings to the Three Jewels, sought a quiet place, and contemplated the emptiness of suffering and the non-existence of self. Before he was even halfway through, his understanding was opened, he was freed from bonds, and he attained the six supernatural powers. Whoever attains arhatship in one spot without moving causes the earth to shake, and Śakra and the devas come to offer congratulations. Thereupon Śakra and the devas all descended, made obeisance, and rejoiced with him. The bhikṣu asked Śakra: "What do you all do in heaven?" He answered: "In heaven there are four pleasure parks. Three of them are places of the five desires, and one is a place of the path and virtue. There, we sometimes discuss the Buddha's noble scriptures, and sometimes we discuss the diligent practitioners among the four assemblies of the world who uphold the Dharma." The bhikṣu asked: "In discussing practitioners, do you regard them as equal, or are there differences in depth?" Śakra said: "We discuss all good people in general. But since the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, there are three people whom the devas have discussed without ever ceasing." The bhikṣu asked: "Who are these people? Please tell me one by one." Śakra said: "In the country of Vārāṇasī, there was a man who became a śramaṇa and made this vow: 'I shall walk in meditation without rest, and until I attain arhatship, I shall never lie down.' So he walked in meditation day and night. His feet broke open and bled, and hundreds of birds followed pecking at the blood. After three years, he attained the path. The devas praised him, and all paid him reverence. There was another man in the country of Rājagṛha who also became a śramaṇa. He spread grass for a cushion and sat upon it, making this vow: 'Until I attain the path, I shall never rise.' But shade and shelter made him drowsy, so he had someone make an awl eight cun long, and whenever drowsiness came, he jabbed both thighs. Through the pain of his wounds he stayed awake, and within one year he attained arhatship. The devas also praised this as unprecedented. There was yet another man in the country of Kauśāmbī who also became a śramaṇa. He was in a steep and precipitous stone cave in the mountains, where no one could come or go. Māra Pāpīyas, seeing his diligence, transformed into a water buffalo and appeared before the bhikṣu, snorting and rolling its eyes, about to charge him. The bhikṣu was greatly frightened but then thought: 'This is a place no buffalo could reach. How can there be one here? Could this be the work of Māra?' He then shouted: 'Are you the wicked Māra doing this?' Māra, realizing he had been recognized, resumed his true form. The bhikṣu said to Māra: 'What do you want from frightening me?' Māra said: 'I saw the practitioner's diligence and feared he would escape my realm, so I came to frighten you.' The bhikṣu then said: 'The reason I became a śramaṇa is to seek liberation from the world. I also wish to see the Buddha's marks of excellence, but the Buddha has already departed this world and no one can see him. I have heard that Māra can transform into the Buddha's form. If you manifest this for me, I shall cease my diligent practice.' Māra agreed, and immediately transformed into the Buddha, standing before him. The bhikṣu contemplated this, and instantly attained arhatship. The devas in the sky praised this boundlessly. Māra was seized with regret and anguish, and instantly vanished."

Śakra said to the bhikṣu: "These three people are praised by all the devas without ceasing to this day." The bhikṣu said to Śakra: "These three people clearly understood that suffering is empty, and therefore they did not spare their decaying bodies. I originally had no particular aspiration, and was slighted by others. Yet by pursuing the path, I have transcended the three realms, which is also marvelous, and I too have attained arhatship." The devas replied: "Now, when we return to heaven, we shall place the practitioner at the very top." Thereupon the devas made obeisance and departed. When the king of that country heard that the sugar merchant had diligently practiced and attained the path, he immediately went to him, bowed his head to the ground, and asked forgiveness. The bhikṣu then became the national teacher, and he caused the Three Jewels to flourish and the country to be at peace. The blessings and liberations that resulted were beyond reckoning.

## (6)

Once there was a sick man whom no physician could cure. He went directly to the king, whose name was Sarvadāna, and with his whole person took refuge in the great king: "In your compassion, I beg you to cure my illness." The king then entrusted him to his physicians and ordered them to treat the illness. The physicians reported to the king: "The medicine for this illness cannot be obtained." The king asked the physicians: "What is the medicine called?" "In the world there is no person free of the five poisons. The flesh of such a person, made into a broth, will cure the illness. What are the five poisons? First, freedom from the mind of lust and desire. Second, freedom from the mind of anger and resentment. Third, freedom from the mind of ignorance and delusion. Fourth, freedom from the mind of jealousy and envy. Fifth, freedom from the mind of cruelty and oppression. If such a person exists, the illness will be cured." The king told the physicians: "This person has come to me for refuge. I alone am free of these poisons." He then cut flesh from his own body and gave it to them to make a broth. The sick man drank it and was cured, and then generated the aspiration for the great vehicle.

## (7)

Once there was a householder who always wished to see Mañjuśrī. The householder made a great offering of charity and set up a high seat. When all was ready, an old man appeared, extremely ugly, with rheum flowing from his eyes, mucus running from his nose, and drool coming from his mouth. The householder saw him on the high seat and thought: "Today I have set up this seat of charity, and an eminent śramaṇa should occupy it. What sort of person are you?" He then pulled the old man down to the ground. When the offering was complete, the householder lit lamps and burned incense in the Buddha's temple and said: "By this merit, may I see Mañjuśrī in this very life." He then returned home, and being exhausted, he lay down and fell asleep. In a dream, someone spoke to him: "You wished to see Mañjuśrī, but when you saw him you did not recognize him. The old man who came to the high seat was none other than Mañjuśrī, and you pulled him down to the ground. This has happened to you seven times in succession; each time you saw him and did not recognize him. How will you ever see Mañjuśrī?" If a person seeks the bodhisattva path, they should regard all people with an equal mind. For one who seeks the bodhisattva path, Mañjuśrī will come to test them. One should be aware of this.

## (8)

A parable is told for the household of impermanence. There was a great tree whose fruit was as large as a two-sheng jar. When its fruit was about to ripen, a crow flew down and perched on a branch. Just as it alighted, a fruit fell and struck the crow on the head, killing it. The tree spirit, seeing this, spoke this verse:

> "The crow did not come seeking death;
> The fruit did not fall for the crow.
> The fruit ripened; the crow was fated to die.
> Causes and conditions brought it thus."

In this world, the karmic consequences of good and evil deeds come together sooner or later, without advancing or retreating. A wise person, upon incurring guilt, does not complain, and upon receiving blessings, does not rejoice. Only then does one truly trust the Buddha's words and uphold them without departing. Within the three realms, there are ninety-six kinds of paths. The people of the world each serve what they follow, hoping their spirits will bring benefit. But these minor paths have not yet understood the making of merit; how can they hold fast to virtue? The reason is that they do not recognize the supreme radiance of the Three Jewels, do not hold fast to the pure truth of the five precepts, and do not possess the profound insight of the Eightfold Right Path. How then can they save and benefit people? Therefore they are called barren fields.

## (9)

Those who can revere the Buddha and the Three Jewels perceive clearly through the three ages of time, understand the blessings of the heavenly realms, and are informed of the punishments of the mountain hells. With genuine faith in the Three Jewels, they block the three evil paths. With the power of strong wisdom, they dispel the darkness of ignorance in the three realms. By cultivating the six kinds of pure spiritual water, they wash away the filth of the six calamities. Therefore they can lightly part with wealth, diminish for their own bodies and mouths, and practice equal giving, thereby planting the roots for future lives. The reward of giving even one thing is ten thousand fold, as swift as an echo answering a sound. Therefore it is said: "The great path is the good field of the three realms." How is this demonstrated? Long ago, King Aśoka, when he was still a small child, met the Buddha on the road. Overcome with joy, he offered a handful of sand with utmost sincerity to the Buddha. Through the merit of this offering, he was able to become a holy king, ruling over four hundred thousand li and sixteen great countries. From this it is clear that the Buddha is the supreme good field. Long ago, the Buddha's brother Nanda, in the time of Vipaśyin Buddha in a past age, once washed the assembly of monks as an act of merit. Through the merit of this deed, he was naturally drawn to be reborn among the Śākya clan. His body bore the five or six marks, and his divine countenance gleamed with golden radiance. Riding on the merit of his former life, he was born in the same age as the Buddha, and through concentrated practice at the place of enlightenment, he attained the six supernatural powers. If the ancients, by giving even one thing, received such vast rewards, how much more so for those donors today who can give abundantly! Through the practice of universal equality, one shall surely attain the honored title. With increasing joy, one broadly liberates all beings.

## (10)

The Dharma says: "The spirit, once stained, does not decay through a hundred million kalpas. It is refined through the fires of birth and death, and only through attaining the path does it cease." Long ago, five hundred and ten years after the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, there was a king who was diligent and courageous, rare in the world. He made offerings to sixty thousand śramaṇas for three months at a time, with the most sweet-smelling and exquisite delicacies of the finest worldly flavors. The senior monk, who sat in the highest seat, was broadly learned in all the scriptures, exploring the ancient and reaching the present. He had attained arhatship. Four hundred and eighty li to the east of this country, there was another king who made offerings to five hundred brāhmaṇas, also with the finest worldly delicacies. He made a hundred kinds of banners and pennants adorned with colored silks, cotton, gold, treasures, and various objects. Each banner was worth five hundred taels of gold. He entertained them with music and performances. Whoever could perform such arts and skills would receive a banner. Poor people from all the neighboring countries, hearing that this king possessed such treasures, gathered from the four directions like clouds, five hundred people in all. Their route passed by the monastery, and each practiced his skills, hoping to win the treasures. But their provisions ran out and they could not reach their destination. So they went before the senior monk and asked to become śramaṇas. The senior monk then examined them with his insight: in the time of Vipaśyin Buddha, these people were servants and guests in the household of a virtuous man. They had once prepared food and drink for practitioners of the path, and they had also heard the Dharma proclaimed. From that time onward, they had enjoyed heavenly and human blessings of their own accord. Their blessings were now exhausted, but the Dharma's words still remained. These men could be saved. He then shaved their hair and beards, conferred upon them the precepts, and brought them into the palace to eat. Returning, all were greatly overjoyed. The master, knowing their minds, told them that this food was not to be eaten carelessly. Anyone who eats this food without utmost sincerity will serve the king as an ox, horse, servant, or slave for countless kalpas. When the five hundred newly ordained bhikṣus heard this, they were terrified. They steeled their resolve and practiced with diligence, and within ninety days all attained arhatship. Having attained the path, the bhikṣus wished to tell their own story. They ran about wildly, shouting as they entered the king's gate: "Come, let us wrestle! The three poisons, the twelve links of dependent origination, the five aggregates, and the six sources of decay: I have wrestled them all down! Who can face me?" The entire assembly was astonished: "What kind of talk is this?" The bhikṣus said: "We originally practiced skills and crafts, hoping to win those treasures and provisions. Instead we became śramaṇas and attained arhatship on our own. The evil forces of the three realms have all been wrestled down by us. Through the great master's grace, our joy and bliss are boundless."

## (11)

Once there were two brothers living together, of great wealth and power, with immeasurable possessions. Their parents had passed away, leaving them without anyone to rely on. Though they were brothers, their aspirations were different. The elder brother loved the path and righteousness; the younger brother loved family business, official rank, and salary, and was greedy for worldly glory and pleasure. They lived near the Cock-Crow Monastery of Pāṭaliputra, not far away. The elder brother devoted himself to studying, consulting, and receiving the sūtras and the path, and did not concern himself with household affairs. His younger brother, seeing that the elder did not tend to family matters, constantly resented him: "We are brothers. Our parents died early, and I toil and worry about our livelihood, yet you abandon the family business and follow śramaṇas to listen to Buddhist sūtras. Can śramaṇas give you clothing and treasures? Our family grows poorer and poorer, our wealth dwindles by the day, and people mock us, calling us lazy and saying our household line will die out. A proper son should establish achievements and carry on his parents' legacy without fail; only then is he a filial son." The elder brother replied: "The five precepts, the ten wholesome deeds, offerings to the Three Jewels, practicing the six pāramitās, sitting in meditation and fixed contemplation, and using the path to transform one's kin: this is true filial piety. The path and the mundane world are naturally opposed. What the path delights in, the world despises; what the world prizes, the path considers base. The wise and the foolish do not plan together, just as light and darkness cannot coexist. Therefore, the wise person leaves darkness and moves toward light to attain the truth of the path. What you now delight in causes me nothing but suffering. All things are empty and false, not real. You are deluded and reckon them as real, but do you know the bitterness of it?" His younger brother, filled with anger, lowered his head and refused to believe. The elder brother, seeing this, said to him: "You are greedy for family affairs and value wealth above all. I love the sūtras and the path and value wisdom above all. I now wish to leave home and entrust my life to the field of merit. Reckoning how this life is as fleeting as flying dust, when impermanence suddenly arrives we are bound by our offenses. Therefore I leave the world, avoiding danger and seeking safety." The younger brother, seeing that his elder brother's will was set on the path and righteousness, was silent and made no reply. The elder brother then left home and became a śramaṇa. Morning and night he practiced with diligence, reciting sūtras and contemplating the path, sitting in meditation, analyzing and reflecting, never resting or growing lax. He then perfected the faculties, the powers, and the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment, and his practice accorded with the sūtras. He attained the fruit of the path. He then went to his younger brother and urged him to practice the Dharma: "The five precepts and ten wholesome deeds are the basis for rebirth in heaven. Generosity and study are the foundation of wisdom in the path." The younger brother, hearing these words, grew even angrier and replied: "You yourself chose to be idle and did not tend to the family business, ruining our household. You can do this on your own; do not try to teach me. Leave at once and do not meddle in my affairs." The elder brother then departed. The younger brother, greedy for family business and ceaselessly striving, never once steadied his mind with the Dharma. When his lifespan ended, he fell into rebirth as an ox, plump and large. A merchant bought him and loaded him with salt to sell, going back and forth many times, until the ox became so emaciated and exhausted that he could go no further. Struggling up a hill, he collapsed and could not rise. The merchant beat and prodded him, and he could barely twitch his head. At that time, the elder brother was traveling through the sky and saw this from afar. He immediately contemplated: "Where did this being come from?" He examined its past and saw that it was his own younger brother. He said to the ox: "Brother! Your house, your land, and all those things you strived for so eagerly: where are they now? You have thrown yourself into rebirth as an ox." He then used his spiritual power to illuminate the ox's former life, and the ox recognized himself. Tears fell from his eyes, and he reproached himself: "In my former life, I did not practice good deeds. I was stingy, greedy, and jealous. I did not believe in the Buddha's Dharma and treated the noble sangha with contempt. I indulged my desires and did not believe my brother's words. I went against the holy teaching and acted with obstinate self-will. Therefore I have fallen among the oxen, exhausted and miserable. What good is regret now?" The elder brother knew the ox's thoughts and was filled with grief and sorrow. He then told the ox's owner the whole story: "This was my younger brother. He did not believe in the Three Jewels, turned from truth to falsehood, was stingy and jealous, self-indulgent and greedy, refusing to give. He has fallen among the oxen, emaciated and miserable, most pitiable. He is now old, exhausted, and no longer of any use. I beg you to give him to me, so that I may save the remainder of his life." The merchant, hearing this, gave the ox to him. He then took the ox to the temple, had it contemplate the Three Jewels, and fed it regularly. When its life ended, it was reborn in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. At that time, all the merchants reflected: "We toil ceaselessly in business and trade, unable to give generously, and we do not practice the Dharma or understand the path and righteousness. When we die, we too may not escape such a fate." They then all left home, abandoned their wives and children, and gave up their cherished possessions. They became śramaṇas, practiced with diligence and without sloth, and all attained the path as well. From this we can see that worldly wealth and treasure bring no benefit to people. To revere the Three Jewels, cultivate oneself and study wisdom, hear widely, and practice the path brings peace in every life.

## (12)

Once in the country of Śrāvastī there was a poor family. In their courtyard was a grapevine with several clusters of grapes. The woman of the house wished to offer them to a practitioner. But at that time, the king had already invited the monks for a full month's offering, and the poor family lacked the influence to compete. The king's invitation having taken precedence for the full month, she finally found one practitioner and made her offering. She said to the practitioner: "I have wished to make this offering for a full month, and only today is my wish fulfilled." The practitioner said to the upāsikā: "You have been making offerings for a full month." The upāsikā said: "I only offered one cluster of grapes. How can that count as a full month's offering?" The practitioner said: "For a full month you held the intention to give. That itself counts as a full month."

There are eighteen things that are extremely difficult for people in this world. First, it is difficult to be alive during a Buddha's time. Second, even if one is alive during a Buddha's time, it is difficult to be born as a human being. Third, even if one is born as a human being, it is difficult to be born in a central land. Fourth, even if one is born in a central land, it is difficult to be born in a family of good lineage. Fifth, even if one is born in a family of good lineage, it is difficult to have all four limbs and six faculties complete. Sixth, even if one has all four limbs and six faculties complete, it is difficult to have material resources. Seventh, even if one has material resources, it is difficult to encounter a good teacher. Eighth, even if one encounters a good teacher, it is difficult to attain wisdom. Ninth, even if one attains wisdom, it is difficult to develop a good mind. Tenth, even if one develops a good mind, it is difficult to practice generosity. Eleventh, even if one can practice generosity, it is difficult to find a virtuous and worthy person to give to. Twelfth, even if one finds a virtuous and worthy person, it is difficult to go to that person's dwelling. Thirteenth, even if one goes to that person's dwelling, it is difficult to find the right moment. Fourteenth, even if one finds the right moment, it is difficult to receive, hear, inquire, and speak of what is right. Fifteenth, even if one receives what is right, it is difficult to understand wisdom. Sixteenth, even if one understands wisdom, it is difficult to receive and accept the profound sūtras of various kinds. Seventeenth, even if one can receive the profound sūtras, it is difficult to practice accordingly and attain the path. These are the eighteen things.
