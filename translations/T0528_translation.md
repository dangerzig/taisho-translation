# 佛說菩薩逝經
## Sūtra of the Bodhisattva Shi, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0528

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Bai Fazhu (白法祖)

---

The Buddha was on Vulture Peak, together with five hundred bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats. At dawn they all donned their robes, took their bowls, and entered the city, each going about to beg for food.

At that time, in the city there was a great and wealthy elder, foremost in the country. His dwelling was on high and dry ground, with fine buildings and towers, encircled by walls with seven gates. His name was Dānnikānai. Dānnikānai had a son named Shi, who was sixteen years old. Dānnikānai had passed away, so Shi lived alone with his mother.

When the Buddha came to Dānnikānai's house to beg for food, Shi was standing inside the third gate. From afar he saw the Buddha coming and was filled with joy. Shi thought: "How supremely handsome and beautiful this person is! Beautiful as a bright moon-pearl, radiant as the sun and moon, his complexion like gold, like the full moon on the fifteenth day."

When the Buddha arrived and stood outside the gate, Shi ran in and said to his mother: "I have seen a person coming who is supremely handsome, without equal in all the world. Since I was born I have never seen anyone like this. He is now standing outside the gate, wishing to beg for food." Shi said to his mother: "I should give to him."

His mother was very stingy and refused to give. Shi said to his mother: "Stinginess and greed bring no benefit to oneself. This is no ordinary person; we should give to him."

His mother again did not respond. Shi said again: "This person surpasses all others; he is the teacher of the world. To give to this person is like a sick person finding a good physician."

Shi said to his mother: "Please, for my sake, give to him. This person's name is renowned throughout the world. He is still standing outside the gate now."

His mother still refused. Shi repeatedly implored his mother. His mother grew greatly angry and said to Shi: "You pester me without stop and make me flustered! This person has not come for food; he only wants to mock you. You are a child; what do you know? If you don't stop asking, you'll get a beating from me before you stop!"

The Buddha, knowing in advance that the mother was stingy, released his divine radiance, which shone through all seven gates. Shi, seeing the Buddha's radiance, was immediately opened in mind. He went again to his mother and said: "Foolish people do not understand generosity. It is like a blind person falling into fire, suffering only because they are blind and have no eyes. People of the world, on account of stinginess and greed, with minds thinking evil, mouths speaking evil, and bodies doing evil, who foolishly do not believe in the Buddha and do not believe in the sūtras, after death will all fall into the hells, among animals and ghostly spirits. Those who are not stingy or greedy, whose bodies do good, whose mouths speak good, and whose minds think good, will in turn receive their own blessings. The wise should make offerings of food to the Buddha. One should always keep company with the wise, for it makes one wise."

Shi said to his mother: "If you will not give, then bring me my food portion for today. Today I shall go without food and give my portion to him. Please, quickly bring my portion. I fear this person will leave me, and he is hard to encounter."

His mother again refused. Shi then went himself to take his food portion and also the fine undergarment he wore. He took them out to the Buddha, bowed with his head at the Buddha's feet, then stood aside with palms joined, and said to the Buddha: "I offer all the clothing and food I have. May it please the Buddha to accept them." The Buddha was silent and did not respond. Shi again addressed the Buddha: "The Buddha is the father and mother of all people in the world. Please deliver us. Please have compassion and accept this for my sake, so that I may obtain merit." He asked three times in this way, and the Buddha then accepted Shi's clothing and food. Shi was filled with great joy.

The Buddha said to Shi: "You have overcome all the stingy. By offering the Buddha clothing and food today, may you obtain your heart's desire."

Shi was filled with great joy, and again addressed the Buddha: "In my heart I do not wish to become the second-heaven Lord Śakra of Trāyastriṃśa, nor do I wish for Brahmā's heaven, nor do I wish for worldly wealth and power. I only wish that my radiance and wisdom may be like the Buddha's."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! May all your heart's desires be fulfilled."

Lord Śakra, hearing Shi's words, stood behind the Buddha with palms joined. Śakra then spoke before the Buddha, saying to Shi: "To seek to become a Buddha is very hard. After ten kalpas, a hundred kalpas, a thousand kalpas, ten thousand kalpas, a hundred million kalpas, you still will not be able to become a Buddha. Now you have offered only a small amount of food and clothing to the Buddha, and you wish to become a Buddha? This cannot be attained."

Śakra further said to Shi: "Even if you offered the four treasures in heaps as great as Mount Sumeru, and with a compassionate mind thought of all people of the world for a hundred thousand kalpas, a million kalpas, ten million kalpas, you still could not become a Buddha."

Shi replied to Śakra: "It is like a craftsman who takes an axe and enters the forest to cut timber. He will select the best and straightest wood, and only take what pleases him." Shi said to Śakra: "I do not expect to become a Buddha merely from one offering of food and clothing to the Buddha. With a constant wholesome mind, with diligence and contemplation and without slacking, in the end I shall become a Buddha."

Śakra replied to Shi: "To seek buddhahood is extremely arduous. Would it not be better to seek to become Brahmā or Lord Śakra?"

Shi replied to Śakra: "If you could see within Jambudvīpa, and it were entirely filled with fire reaching up to heaven, I would cast my body into it, and still I would seek buddhahood without ceasing."

Śakra said: "If you fill Jambudvīpa with fire, there are people, animals, and crawling creatures within it. You must not kill them. If your mind thinks evil, you will fall into the hells. How then could you become a Buddha?"

Shi replied: "The people and crawling creatures of the world I entrust with a compassionate heart to Maitreya Buddha. Maitreya Buddha will deliver them. My quest for the buddha-path will never cease."

Shi further said to Śakra: "The heavenly King Brahmā, though honored, will in the end return to impermanence. None can escape death."

Śakra then joined his palms and said to Shi: "Your resolve is so firm, your determination to seek buddhahood without ceasing; in the end you too shall become a Buddha."

Shi replied to Śakra: "Do not rely on your heavenly position and be conceited. There are those who surpass you."

Shi said: "With my compassionate mind, with diligence and without slacking in seeking buddhahood, in the end I shall become a Buddha." Śakra then fell silent, with nothing more to say.

The Buddha then said to Shi: "In your past lives you have made offerings and served sixty thousand Buddhas. Your mind has always wished to become a Buddha. You have always maintained a wholesome mind in making offerings to the Buddhas."

The Buddha said to Shi: "After two million billion kalpas, you will no longer fall into the hells, among animals, or among ghostly spirits."

The Buddha further said to Shi: "You have always maintained a compassionate mind and made offerings to the Buddhas. Hereafter you shall become a gold-wheel-turning king, and ten billion minor kingdoms shall all be subject to you. You shall rule the four continents and travel always by flight. Your conduct shall always be righteous. When your life ends, you shall be reborn in Brahmā's heaven and in Tuṣita heaven. When your heavenly lifespan ends, you shall again be reborn as a gold-wheel-turning king. When that life ends, you shall again ascend to Brahmā's heaven. Thus ascending and descending for two million billion kalpas, when this is complete, you shall become a Buddha named Sumerikālā, and you shall deliver the people of heaven and earth. When you descend to the earth, your radiance shall illuminate three thousand Sumeru worlds, reaching up to the twenty-eighth heaven. Heaven and earth shall quake greatly. Day and night shall both be bright. The people's lifespan shall be seven thousand billion years. Your body shall be two hundred zhang tall. There shall be twelve thousand cities, each four hundred and eighty li in extent. The city gates shall all be adorned with carvings of the seven treasures.

"When you become a Buddha, you shall hold two great assemblies of all the śramaṇas to teach the sūtras. At the first assembly, six thousand billion śramaṇas shall all attain the path of arhatship. At the second assembly, four thousand billion śramaṇas shall all attain the path of arhatship. At that time, among the people there shall be no thieves. Men and women shall all be of one mind. Among the people none shall do evil. All the evil destinies and places of suffering shall be sealed off. There shall be no mountains, forests, or valleys; the earth shall be level and smooth. The people shall have no illness and no suffering. The people shall all be happy, each keeping to their dwelling. The heavens shall rain three times a day, just enough to settle the dust. When the people wish to discuss anything, they shall all gather together. In summer it shall not be very hot, and in winter it shall not be very cold, but always pleasantly moderate.

"Your teaching hall shall be named Nanditā. If any person practices generosity and makes offerings to Buddhas and arhats, and seeks to become a Buddha, a pratyekabuddha, or an arhat, they shall all attain it. The buddha-path must not go unsought. The Buddha's wisdom is beyond measure. In the ten directions there is no end or limit. The Buddha's wisdom is the same: without end or limit. The people of the world do not know where birth comes from, nor where death leads."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the sūtra, Shi, the bhikṣus, and Lord Śakra all made obeisance to the Buddha.
