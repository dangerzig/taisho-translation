# 諸教決定名義論
## Treatise on the Definitive Meaning of Names in All Teachings

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1658

Composed by Maitreya (慈氏). Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護).

---

> Homage to all the buddha world-honored ones, homage to the proclaimed Dharma of the three vehicles,
> Homage to all the harmonious saṃgha, homage to the principle of Samantabhadra's dharma-realm.

I shall now briefly explain the root syllables of all teachings. What is their true meaning? It is as follows:

The syllable *oṃ* is supreme among all. I now bow to this syllable, which is pure, abiding in the characteristic of non-duality. If a person can turn this syllable upon the tongue, that person attains the root of all true wisdom. This syllable is profoundly secret in all teachings.

Furthermore, there are three syllables. The vajra syllable *hūṃ* is the primary cause; from this syllable all the ritual procedures of the true Dharma are proclaimed. The mind of the three realms is thus in accordance with this Dharma. The syllable *āḥ* expresses characteristics; the syllable *aṃ* is emptiness-nature. That syllable *āḥ* is also the mother of wisdom. Whether the path or the non-path, all are proclaimed from it. It is also the root of the syllables of the dharma-realm, pervading and entering emptiness-nature, corresponding from beginning to end. Through this right wisdom, accomplishment is attained. Whatever Dharma is proclaimed is all accomplished; what is not proclaimed is likewise. Therefore all activities are entirely equal, turning in accordance within the cycle of transmigration.

What are the syllables of the dharma-realm? I shall now explain. They are:

*ka kha ga gha, ca cha ja jha, ṭa ṭha ṇa ḍha, ta tha na dha, pa pha ma bha, ya ra la, vaṃ saṃ haṃ.*

These syllables, as previously stated, are subsumed under the syllable *aṃ*. Their characteristic is now explained: this is emptiness-nature, from which all the profound teachings arise. This is also called "the emptiness of emptiness." All expressive characteristics emerge from it, namely:

*ka kha ga gha, ca cha ja jha, ṭa ṭha ṇa ḍha, ta tha na dha, pa pha ma bha, ya ra la va, sa ha.*

These syllables, as previously stated, are subsumed under the syllable *āḥ*. Their characteristic is now explained: each and every syllable, from its initial corresponding practice born of omniscient wisdom, is such that self and dharmas are both entirely equal, ultimately abiding through vajra empowerment.

Furthermore, all activities arise from the vajra-samādhi. Namely:

*ka kha ga gha, ca cha ja jha, ṭa ṭha ṇa ḍha, ta tha na dha, pa pha ma bha, ya ra la va, sa ha.*

These syllables are all subsumed under the syllable *a*. Their characteristic is now explained: all vajra activities. Furthermore, now explained:

*u ū i e ṛ ṝ lṛ lṝ ī ai oṃ au.*

Herein the characteristic of the syllable *oṃ* is now explained: all syllables emerge from it.

Herein the characteristic of the syllable *hūṃ* is as previously stated: all activities arise from the vajra-samādhi.

Furthermore, as previously stated, these syllables are the three bodies, abiding in accordance with reality in both nature and characteristics. Namely:

The syllable *hūṃ* is the dharmakāya; the syllable *āḥ* is the saṃbhogakāya; the syllable *oṃ* is the nirmāṇakāya. These three syllables subsume these three bodies. The paths of liberation of the three vehicles are thus distinctly explained, and this is the right cause for their proclamation. All the śrāvakas, pratyekabuddhas, and omniscient wisdom emerge from this and proclaim all dharmas. These three syllables are also the three vajra activities, abiding in accordance with reality. Namely:

*oṃ āḥ hūṃ.*

Herein, the syllable *oṃ* is called the vajra activity of body; the syllable *āḥ* is the vajra activity of speech; the syllable *hūṃ* is the vajra activity of mind.

Furthermore, the two syllables *a* and *āḥ* abide in emptiness-nature. Herein the syllable *āḥ* is also right wisdom; herein the syllable *a* is the supreme secret of perfect awakening.

Furthermore, the syllable *hūṃ* is the wisdom of mind, which realizes all dharmas. As stated above, all syllables should be known to arise from the three syllables *aṃ*, *a*, and *hūṃ*. From these, dharmas give rise to various characteristics, which shall now be distinguished. All dharmas are subsumed by the two syllables *aṃ* and *a* from beginning to end. Herein the syllable *hūṃ* produces all things: within the three realms, it manifests all forms. All devas, humans, nāgas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and gandharvas; accomplished vidyādharas; Śrī Devī, Sarasvatī, Umā Devī; Indra, Brahmā, Nārāyaṇa, Maheśvara; such devas and their consorts; all men and women within the realm of sentient beings; and even all buddhas and bodhisattvas: all emerge from this syllable *hūṃ* through transformation. Each and every mind abides in the characteristic of this syllable.

When the mind contemplates this syllable, one should abide in space, producing without obstruction. The mind of the three realms is identical with this one mind. Having entered this mind, one attains what is called the direct realization of bodhi. One should know that this mind is without equal, without grasping, without attachment, without abiding, without expression, without characteristics. It is the omniscient wisdom equal to space. It corresponds with nothing to be obtained; it is the right practice corresponding with neither self nor other. In the world, even the lowest classes such as the caṇḍālas and their practices, and even the activities of animals and their kind, with all their various differences: although these practices differ, none depart from the right practice corresponding with omniscient wisdom.

Furthermore, the nature of all syllables is beyond expression. Yet omniscient wisdom proclaims all syllables through skillful means.

What is the meaning of syllables herein? The meaning of space. What is the meaning of space? The meaning of emptiness-nature. What is the meaning of emptiness-nature? The meaning of *śūra*. What is the meaning of *śūra*? The meaning of inexpressibility. What is the meaning of inexpressibility? The meaning of signlessness. What is the meaning of signlessness? The meaning of omniscience. What is the meaning of omniscience? The meaning of the wish-fulfilling jewel. What is the meaning of the wish-fulfilling jewel? The meaning of wisdom. What is the meaning of wisdom? The meaning of mind. What is the meaning of mind? The meaning of the great sovereign of the three realms. What is the meaning of the great sovereign of the three realms? The meaning of universal illumination. What is the meaning of universal illumination? The meaning of Brahmā. What is the meaning of Brahmā? The meaning of the Deva of Great Power. What is the meaning of the Deva of Great Power? The meaning of the Lord (Īśvara). What is the meaning of the Lord? The meaning of Buddha. What is the meaning of Buddha? The meaning of Vajrasattva. What is the meaning of Vajrasattva? The meaning of Avalokiteśvara. What is the meaning of Avalokiteśvara? The meaning of the world. What is the meaning of the world? The meaning of transmigration. What is the meaning of transmigration? The meaning of nirvāṇa. What is the meaning of nirvāṇa? The meaning of the innumerable. What is the meaning of the innumerable? The meaning of the unknowable. What is the meaning of the unknowable? The meaning of the unborn. What is the meaning of the unborn? The meaning of the unceasing. What is the meaning of the unceasing? The meaning of the formless. What is the meaning of the formless? The meaning of the soundless. What is the meaning of the soundless? The meaning of the rootless. What is the meaning of the rootless? The meaning of the unnourished. What is the meaning of the unnourished? The meaning of non-abiding. What is this non-abiding? Non-abiding is precisely "nothing whatsoever," apart from the wisdom of all existences and their mental discriminations, surpassing all buddhas and the bodhi of buddhas, abiding in the root of the vajra syllable *hūṃ*. This syllable *hūṃ* further becomes a lotus-fire-maṇḍala, abiding in the emptiness of emptiness, the two roots of the dharma-nature free from defilement, the supreme purified corresponding practice.

Having thus understood, one should with courageous diligence within the cycle of transmigration cause all to attain the great bliss of nirvāṇa. If one abides in this mind, one is a wise person who extinguishes the Dharma of suffering. Both roots are entirely equal. This is the supreme diligent practice, accomplishing the great bliss-Dharma of the Sage, abiding in right mindfulness. Like the light of the sun illuminating the entire world, the wisdom of mind abides in equanimity and correspondence. All the profoundly secret matters as previously stated, both the *śūra* and the space-division, are all explained thus.

Having thus accomplished the supreme bliss-Dharma, one attains the correspondence with the self-nature of all buddhas. One understands that all sentient beings in the world, their birth-dharmas, and the two kinds of joy and love in harmonious correspondence, are to be known as without nature. Those impermanent dharmas are ultimately empty and quiescent.
