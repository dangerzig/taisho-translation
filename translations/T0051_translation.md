# 梵志計水淨經
## Sūtra on the Brahmin Who Reckoned Purity by Water

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0051

Translator unknown

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Bhagavān was on the bank of the Uruvelā River, alone beneath a tree, having just attained complete and perfect enlightenment. At that time, there was a brahmin who reckoned purity by water. After his midday meal, he wandered about and came to where the World-Honored One was. The World-Honored One saw from afar the water-reckoning brahmin approaching from a distance. Having seen the water-reckoning brahmin, on account of him, the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus:

"If one has twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will fall into an evil rebirth and be born in niraya. What are the twenty-one? The fetter of wrong view clinging to the mind; unlawful desire; the wrong dharma of deceiving the world; greed; anger; sloth; drowsiness; restlessness; shamelessness; doubt; hatred and resentment; avarice, jealousy, and non-relinquishment; flattery and deceit; excessive shamelessness and lack of conscience; envy; heightened envy; and the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind. If one has these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will fall into an evil rebirth and be born in niraya, just as a soiled child's garment whose owner gives it to a dyer or a dyer's apprentice, who soaks it in alkali earth, cow dung, or clay and washes it until it is thoroughly clean. Even though the dyer and the dyer's apprentice wash that soiled child's garment with alkali earth, ash, cow dung, and clay until it is thoroughly clean, the child's garment still retains its dark grime. In just this way, if one has these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one falls into an evil rebirth and is born in niraya. What are the twenty-one? From the fetter of wrong view clinging to the mind up to the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind.

"If one is free of these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will be born in a good destination, in the heavens. What are the twenty-one? From the fetter of wrong view clinging to the mind up to the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind. If one is free of these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will be born in a good destination, in the heavens, just as a cloth woven in Vārāṇasī whose owner gives it to a dyer or a dyer's apprentice, who soaks it in alkali earth, ash, and cow dung and washes it until it is thoroughly clean. Even though that cloth woven in Vārāṇasī is washed by the dyer and the dyer's apprentice with alkali, ash, and cow dung until it is thoroughly clean, the cloth woven in Vārāṇasī becomes supremely pure and white. In just this way, if one is free of these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will be born in a good destination, in the heavens. What are the twenty-one? From the fetter of wrong view up to the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind: one will be born in a good destination, in the heavens.

"The fetter of wrong view clinging to the mind, having seen it, one should abandon it; up to the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind, having seen it, one should abandon it. Then, with the mind suffused with loving-kindness, having fully pervaded one direction, one dwells in meditative attainment. Likewise the second, third, and fourth directions, above and below and all quarters everywhere: with the mind suffused with loving-kindness, without duality, without hatred, exceedingly vast and great, boundless and immeasurable, having fully pervaded all directions, one dwells in meditative attainment. In the same way, with the mind suffused with compassion, with sympathetic joy, and with equanimity, having fully pervaded all directions everywhere, one dwells in meditative attainment. This is the brahmin's inner and outer washing and purification, not merely external purification."

> "Śramaṇa Gautama! Go bathe in the water!"

> "O brahmin, if Gautama were to wash in water,
> all the world should attain precepts, merit, and deliverance."

> "O Gautama, all the world washes in water to remove all evil;
> soaking and washing in pure water, ordinary fools always delight in this."

> "This cannot remove dark deeds of darkness.
> What use is that water purification? What does one see from being in water?
> When people commit evil deeds, what can this water purify?
> True and firm purity lies in upholding the precepts and practicing pure conduct;
> one who constantly practices pure conduct properly attains the observance of precepts.
> Not killing, not stealing, not speaking falsely: thus one attains equal deliverance.
> O brahmin, dwell in this and perform all wholesome deeds, and you will attain peace and security.
> O brahmin, why return home? What need have you for water and a home?
> O brahmin, far better to seek the pure and wholesome Dharma;
> what need is there for foul and base water
> that merely removes dust and filth?"

"Perhaps Gautama thinks thus: 'Far better to seek the pure and wholesome Dharma; there is no need for foul and base water, which is used only to remove dust and filth.' Excellent, Gautama! Excellent, Gautama! I now take refuge in the World-Honored One, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha of bhikṣus. World-Honored One, I undertake the precepts of an upāsaka. From this day forward, for the remainder of my life, I renounce killing and take refuge in the Buddha."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The bhikṣus, having heard what the World-Honored One said, rejoiced and departed.
