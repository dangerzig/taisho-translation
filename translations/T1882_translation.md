# 三聖圓融觀門
## Gate of Contemplation on the Perfect Interfusion of the Three Sages

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1882

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, expounded by Chengguan (澄觀).

---

The supreme sages observed people and established their teachings; their words were never stated in vain. Following their pointed indications and bringing their import to light, everything they touched becomes comprehensible. Because Mañjuśrī, of wondrous virtue, prompted the demonstration through the two sages representing the Dharma with their twofold significance, I have accordingly composed this "Contemplation on the Perfect Interfusion of the Three Sages." Even the intelligence of a single hair-tip would find it difficult to fully accomplish this contemplation. Roughly relying on teaching and principle, I briefly indicate the essential framework, humbly hoping that those of far-reaching insight will empty themselves and seek it therein.

The Three Sages are our original teacher Vairocana Tathāgata and the two great bodhisattvas Samantabhadra and Mañjuśrī. When the Great Awakened One appeared in the world, his supporting attendants were numerous as dust-motes. Yet in the *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*, only these two sages are singled out as the chief figures, because they are employed as symbolic representations of the Dharma, and not without purpose.

I now briefly present two approaches: first, contrasting pairs to reveal their symbolic significance; second, mutual interfusion to reveal perfect roundness.

**I. Contrasting Pairs**

Among the Three Sages, the two sages represent the cause and the Tathāgata represents the fruit. Setting aside the fruit for now, I shall discuss only the two causes. If one awakens to the profound subtlety of the two causes, one will then know the deep wonder of the ocean of fruition.

The Dharma-gates of the two sages can be briefly arranged in three pairs:

**1. The pair of the capacity that believes and that which is believed in:** Samantabhadra represents the dharma-realm that is believed in, namely the tathāgatagarbha while still enmeshed in defilements. Therefore the *Prajñāpāramitā-naya-sūtra* says: "All sentient beings are the tathāgatagarbha." This is because the self-substance of Bodhisattva Samantabhadra pervades everywhere. That at the first assembly he immediately enters the samādhi of the tathāgatagarbha-body has its meaning here. Mañjuśrī represents the mind that is capable of believing. The *Buddhābhiṣeka-sūtra* says: "All buddhas generated the aspiration for awakening because of Mañjuśrī," representing dependence on faith for the initial arising. That Sudhana first sees him and generates the great aspiration corresponds to the stage of faith. The sūtra says: "Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī gives birth to all bodhisattvas without rest." However, faith merely has believing but cannot yet see. Since the object believed in and the object ultimately realized are one and the same principle, without the initial Samantabhadra, how could faith first arise? Since the principle can only be fully seen at the ultimate stage, Mañjuśrī is placed at the beginning and Samantabhadra at the end.

**2. The pair of understanding and practice:** Samantabhadra represents the myriad practices that are carried out. Throughout the sūtras, above and below, all speak of "the practices of Samantabhadra." Mañjuśrī represents the understanding that gives rise to practice, for he thoroughly comprehends both principle and phenomena and has exhausted all skillful means. Maitreya says: "That you first succeeded in seeing the good teachers, hearing the bodhisattva practices, and entering the gates of liberation was all due to the spiritual power of Mañjuśrī." Also: "Mañjuśrī is always the teacher of all bodhisattvas." Also: "Through the power of Mañjuśrī's mental resolve."

**3. The pair of principle and wisdom:** Samantabhadra represents the dharma-realm that is realized, namely the tathāgatagarbha freed from defilements. "The boy Sudhana enters his body." Also: "He attains the body of ultimate equality across the three times." That a single hair-tip is vast and without limit is because it accords with the dharma-nature. The body-characteristics of Samantabhadra are like space. Also, seeing Samantabhadra one immediately attains the pāramitā of wisdom, which shows that wisdom arises in dependence upon principle. Mañjuśrī represents the great wisdom that realizes. The Buddha he originally served is named "Immovable Wisdom." Maitreya says: "Mañjuśrī is always the mother of immeasurable hundreds of thousands of koṭis of nayutas of buddhas." In the various sūtras, the Dharma-gates that Mañjuśrī expounds mostly reveal the import of prajñā. Also: "Born from the great ocean of Mañjuśrī's wisdom." One sees the later Mañjuśrī only after seeing Samantabhadra, showing that one must have wisdom to realize principle. Therefore the ancient masters inscribed the later Mañjuśrī as "wisdom's illumination of the non-dual characteristic." Not looking at the bodily form represents the extreme depth of ultimate wisdom: mind and object both exhausted, faith and understanding both transcended. Furthermore, principle opens into substance and function, while wisdom divides into expedient and ultimate. Thus the two wisdoms of Mañjuśrī realize the substance and function of Samantabhadra. Regarding this one approach, the ancient masters personally asked the Tripiṭaka Master, who said: "There is a sūtra that explains this, but it has not yet been transmitted to this land." Also, this approach additionally represents concentration and wisdom: principle is intrinsically quiescent, and wisdom is precisely discernment. It also represents substance and function: Samantabhadra's quiescent principle serves as the mind's substance, and Mañjuśrī's illuminating wisdom serves as its great function.

**II. Mutual Interfusion Revealing Perfection**

This also has two parts:

**First, the Dharma-gates of the two sages are each self-interfusing:** Mañjuśrī requires faith in order to bring understanding to completion. With understanding but without faith, wrong views increase; with faith but without understanding, ignorance grows. When faith and understanding are true and correct, one comprehends the original source, completing one's ultimate wisdom. When ultimate wisdom turns its illumination back, it is no different from the initial mind. Therefore, at the very first generation of the aspiration, one already achieves perfect awakening. Furthermore, the wisdom of the preliminary expedient means does not depart from the substance of wisdom. The later Mañjuśrī is named "wisdom's illumination of the non-dual characteristic." The illumination of faith is not separate from wisdom. Therefore, from the formless body he extends his right hand. Thus the three aspects of Mañjuśrī interpenetrate in hidden fashion.

**Next, the three aspects of Samantabhadra are self-interfusing:** If principle lacks practice, principle ultimately cannot be revealed. When practice is initiated based on substance, practice must accord with substance. Through practice one realizes principle, so there is no principle outside practice; through principle one reveals practice, so there is no practice outside principle. With each realization of principle, nothing fails to be complete: one realization is the realization of all. Therefore the Dharma-gates obtained from a single hair of Samantabhadra surpass the previous ones beyond description. This is also the function that is identical with substance. The Dharma-gates of hair-tips and pores involve conditioned arising without limit. Thus the three aspects of Samantabhadra permeate and overlap in layer upon layer.

**Second, the Dharma-gates of the two sages mutually interfuse:** One must rely on faith to know the dharma-realm. If one's faith does not trust the principle, that faith becomes deviant. Subject and object are non-dual. Not to trust that one's own mind possesses the tathāgatagarbha disqualifies one as a bodhisattva. Next, one must rely on understanding to initiate practice. When practice is initiated in accord with understanding, practice is not different from understanding; thus understanding and practice are non-dual. Next, since wisdom is the function of principle, one embodies principle and achieves wisdom, and that wisdom in turn illuminates principle. Only when wisdom and principle merge can it be called true wisdom; thus principle and wisdom are non-dual. Therefore the sūtra says: "There is no wisdom outside suchness that could realize suchness, nor is there suchness outside wisdom that wisdom could enter."

Furthermore, the quiescent illumination of the dharma-realm is called calming (śamatha), and quiescence that always illuminates is called contemplation (vipaśyanā). When contemplation exhausts enumeration and reaches the ultimate, it wondrously accords with quiescence: this is the non-duality of concentration and wisdom. The function that is identical with substance is called wisdom; the substance that is identical with function is called principle: this is the non-duality of substance and function. Thus the hidden interpenetration of Mañjuśrī's three aspects is precisely the layered permeation of Samantabhadra's three aspects. These two being non-different is called "Samantabhadra's Indra-net practice." Therefore the chapter on Samantabhadra's practices, contrary to the rest of the sūtra above and below, extensively displays the perfect interfusion of principle and phenomena as Samantabhadra's practice. It is not merely phenomenal practice that is called Samantabhadra's practice. Since the two sages' Dharma-gates interfuse yet are not called "Mañjuśrī's practice," this is because wisdom is subsumed under principle: there is only the one dharma-realm of mind. To raise one is to gather all completely.

Since the Dharma-gates of the two sages mutually interfuse, when Samantabhadra's cause is fulfilled, characteristics are transcended and language exhausted. Submerging into the ocean of cause and fruit, this is called Vairocana, "Universal Illumination." Only realization corresponds to it. In the "Dharmadhātu" chapter, that after Samantabhadra there immediately follow verses praising the Buddha's virtues reveals the characteristics of the fruit. At the chapter's beginning, the Tathāgata himself enters samādhi and manifests characteristics without words, representing the inexpressibility of what is realized. That Samantabhadra then opens and reveals, emitting light to cause awakening, represents the inexpressibility of that which realizes. That Mañjuśrī opens and reveals has precisely this meaning.

If one combines the Dharma-gates of the Three Sages to form the sūtra's title: Samantabhadra is "Great" (*mahā*), because the principle-substance he realizes includes everything. Mañjuśrī is "Expansive" (*vaipulya*), because wisdom above principle serves as the operative function. Furthermore, both together are Samantabhadra: principle embraces both substance and function, and both together are what is realized. Mañjuśrī and Samantabhadra both adorn with the myriad practices of the Flower Garland. Spreading forth and opening out, faith, wisdom, understanding, and practice are all causal "flowers" that "adorn" the intrinsically quiescent substance. Vairocana is "Buddha." Perfectly completing all causes and realizing the above substance and function, the exposition becomes the "Sūtra," because it is revealed through words. Since the title is all-inclusive without remainder, it exhausts the meaning of the entire *Avataṃsaka Sūtra*. Furthermore, the teachings of the Buddha's entire career do not depart from this principle, wisdom, and so forth.

However, the above principle, wisdom, and so forth all do not depart from the mind. Mind, Buddha, and sentient beings are without difference. If one can comprehend the mind, then thought after thought the cause is perfectly complete, and thought after thought the fruit is perfectly fulfilled. The "Manifestation" chapter says: "The bodhisattva should know that in his own mind, thought after thought, there is always a buddha achieving perfect awakening." Yet the difference within unity does not obstruct outward contemplation; do not cling to verbal formulations. If one's contemplation accords with this, then wherever one's eyes meet objects, one constantly sees the Three Sages and the bodhisattvas of the ten directions, because one is all, and because mind and objects are non-dual. If one cultivates in accordance with this, even if in a single lifetime one does not fully accomplish it, the Three Sages will certainly be perfected.
