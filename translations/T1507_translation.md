# Treatise on the Discrimination of Merits
## 分別功德論

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1507, 5 fascicles

Translated from the Chinese. Anonymous translator.

---

# Fascicle One

The opening verse speaks of Kāśyapa reflecting on the fundamental dharma. He considered that the words and teachings of the sūtra-dharma were exceedingly numerous. How do we know this? Kāśyapa used comparisons to gauge the magnitude. His method of comparison began with ten donkeys: the strength of ten donkeys does not equal that of one ordinary camel; ten ordinary camels do not equal one ordinary elephant; ten ordinary elephants do not equal one thin-footed elephant; ten thin-footed elephants do not equal one food-stealing elephant; ten food-stealing elephants do not equal one lotus elephant; ten lotus elephants do not equal one blue-lotus elephant; ten blue-lotus elephants do not equal one red-lotus elephant; ten red-lotus elephants do not equal one white-lotus elephant; ten white-lotus elephants do not equal one Himalayan elephant; ten Himalayan elephants do not equal one fragrant elephant. From donkey to fragrant elephant is one measure. Now, eighty-four thousand such fragrant elephants, if their hides were written on inside and out with sūtra text and they were loaded to full capacity, would roughly convey the total volume of what Ānanda had heard and known. One can roughly estimate the grand total, but to elaborate every word and character would take an entire lifetime without completing it.

Reflecting that the sūtra-dharma was vast and immense, Kāśyapa wondered how to ensure it would circulate throughout the world so that beings for a thousand generations could receive the moisture of the dharma. In deep contemplation of the ultimate principle, he asked who could compile the dharma. Only Ānanda could gather it all. Kāśyapa then sounded the *gandhāra* bell to assemble the community.

At that time, eighty-four thousand arhats answered the summons and assembled. All of these were undefiled beings who had attained both liberation of mind and liberation by wisdom. The reason these noble saints were summoned was that all of them could enter the *nirodha-samāpatti* (cessation-attainment). Those who enter cessation-attainment can cause beings in the present life to obtain blessings and be saved from suffering.

[The text explains why cessation-attainment is important by telling the story of Indra, whose merit was exhausted and who was about to die. The five signs of decay appeared, and he was terrified. He wanted to go to the Buddha for help but feared the Buddha would be too slow. He considered Śāriputra and Maudgalyāyana but feared they too could not act quickly enough. Only Mahākāśyapa, through the power of cessation-attainment, could provide immediate rescue. Indra went to Kāśyapa, who was about to visit a poor household for alms. Indra, knowing Kāśyapa might refuse his offering if he appeared in his divine form, disguised himself as a sick man in a grass hut along the road. Kāśyapa begged food from the sick man, who extended his hand to offer it. When Kāśyapa received it in his bowl, it transformed into ambrosia. Indra then revealed his divine form in the sky. Kāśyapa asked: "Why did you deceive me with false words?" Indra replied: "I did not speak falsely. I offered with complete sincerity. I am Indra, Lord of the Gods. The five signs of decay have come, and my life is about to end. Therefore I came to make this request: please save my life." Kāśyapa silently consented. Indra went to the Buddha to hear the dharma. He soon fell asleep, and in that sleep he died and was reborn. The Buddha told him: "Just now you died. Now you have been reborn and will not die again." He returned to his original form. This was accomplished through the power of Kāśyapa's cessation-attainment.]

The reason Kāśyapa excelled in cessation-attainment is that Kāśyapa was originally a *pratyekabuddha*. The dharma of a pratyekabuddha is not to teach through verbal instruction but solely through supernatural powers, samādhi, and miraculous transformation. Although Mahākāśyapa attained arhatship, his original disposition remained.

[The text then discusses the composition of the assembly, noting that the verse mentions "four assemblies" but in reality there were more: human assemblies (kṣatriyas, brahmins, elders, and śramaṇas) and divine assemblies (Four Heavenly Kings, the Trāyastriṃśa gods, Māra, and Brahmā), making twelve in total.]

Regarding "all dharmas are exceedingly profound," this refers to the twelve links of dependent origination. The Buddha told Ānanda that the twelve links were exceedingly profound and subtle. Ānanda asked: "What is so profound about this chain of causes?" The Buddha replied: "Do not say it is not profound. In a past life you also said it was not profound."

[The Buddha then tells the story of the Asura king whose body was eight thousand *yojanas* tall, with lips a thousand *yojanas* apart. The king had a small child whom he always held on his knee. The ocean was 3,360,000 *li* deep, and when the Asura stood in it, the water came to his navel. The child, seeing his father standing in the ocean, thought it was shallow and wanted to play in the water. The father said: "You must not. The ocean is deep and will swallow you." But the child insisted, so the father released him, and he sank to the ocean floor, terrified and gasping. The father reached in and pulled him out. "I told you not to, but you wouldn't believe me. How was it?" The Buddha said: "At that time, the king was I myself. The child was you. In the past you did not believe in the depth, and now you still do not. If you cannot even comprehend how ignorance conditions formations, how much less can you comprehend the thirty-seven factors of awakening?"]

**The Four Inconceivables:** The Tathāgata spoke of four things that are inconceivable: (1) sentient beings, (2) the world, (3) dragons, and (4) the Buddha.

[Extended narratives follow for each:

*The world is inconceivable:* The story of Pūrṇa debating a brahmin who had once seen four kinds of armies enter a lotus pore at a pond, and could not understand whether it was real or a hallucination. The Buddha confirmed it was real: the Asuras, defeated in battle with the gods, had hidden inside the lotus pore. This is beyond the reach of ordinary thought.

*Sentient beings are inconceivable:* After the cosmic conflagration, water fills the place of fire. Wind forms palaces. Below, there is "earth-fat." The light-sound gods descend, taste the earth-fat, become heavy and cannot return. Those who eat much transform into women. They gradually decline from earth-fat to flat cakes to rice, losing their supernatural powers and light, reverting to human form. Through good conduct they are reborn in heaven; through evil conduct, in the three lower realms.

*Dragons are inconceivable:* Rain comes from dragons' eyes, ears, nose, and mouth, or from their body, or from their mind. Five types of gods dwelling near Mount Sumeru can also cause rain. Two kinds of rain exist: joyful rain (harmonious) and angry rain (with thunder and hail).

*The Buddha is inconceivable:* Once the Buddha was in his quiet chamber when brahmā-gods numbering as the sands of the Ganges came to his abode, wanting to know what samādhi the Buddha was in, but none could determine it.]

[The text then discusses Ānanda's deference to Kāśyapa as elder and president of the council. Foreign teachers say Kāśyapa did not teach the dharma because he lacked eloquent speech among the four kinds of analytical knowledge, and because he was originally a pratyekabuddha who relied on supernatural display rather than verbal instruction.]

[Kāśyapa urged Ānanda to compile the dharma. To demonstrate Ānanda's "equal wisdom" (*samajñāna*), the text tells the story of Ānanda meeting a brahmin expert in mathematics under a great *nyagrodha* tree in Śrāvastī. The brahmin asked: "How many stems, nodes, branches, and leaves does this tree have?" Ānanda looked up once and gave the exact count for each. He then left. The brahmin, suspecting Ānanda had guessed, secretly removed sixty leaves and buried them. When Ānanda returned, the brahmin asked for the count again. Ānanda looked up twice and said: "Why are there fewer leaves on this tree now?" "How many fewer?" "Sixty fewer." The brahmin clasped his hands and exclaimed: "Unprecedented!" He asked if Ānanda was an arhat, an *anāgāmin*, a *sakṛdāgāmin*, or a *srotāpanna*. Ānanda replied he was none of these. "Do you have a teacher?" "Yes. The son of King Śuddhodana who left home and became a Buddha is my teacher." The brahmin thought: "This is his teacher's wisdom, not his own," and followed Ānanda to the Buddha, asked to become a renunciant, and immediately attained arhatship.]

Ānanda reflected on how to organize the immense teachings. He first thought of grouping by number (ones, twos, threes up to ten), each topic following its kind. Some said this was not possible since the Buddha's teachings could not be arranged sequentially. Ānanda further reflected: "The sūtra-dharma is vast; I should divide it into three collections." At that moment, a god of the Śuddhāvāsa heaven secretly told Ānanda: "You should indeed make three divisions." Following the god's counsel, he made three divisions:

1. **Sūtras** (*qìjīng*): The Buddha's spoken dharma, taught to gods, kings, or non-Buddhist seekers, analyzed according to circumstances so that each could attain understanding. *Qì* ("contract/thread") means connecting, stringing together reasoned principles into a method of practice.

2. **Vinaya** (*pínī*): The rules of discipline, taught for the two saṅghas (monks and nuns), checking evil and restraining wrongdoing, whether 250 or 500 rules. Like a king's secret treasury that external officials may not handle.

3. **Abhidharma** (*āpítán*): The "great dharma." Why "great"? Because the great wisdom of the four noble truths is the banner of all dharmas, cutting off all wrong views, ignorance, and the flood of delusion. Also called the "incomparable dharma," because the eight knowledges, ten wisdoms, and undefiled right view transcend the obstacles of the three realms, having no equal. Kātyāyanīputra compiled and selected the essential wisdom from the sūtras and presented it to the Buddha for his seal of approval.

[Ānanda further reflected that these three collections correspond to the three gates of liberation: sūtras correspond to emptiness, vinaya to signlessness, and abhidharma to wishlessness. He then divided the sūtra collection into four sections: (1) *Ekottara* (Numerical), (2) *Madhyama* (Middle), (3) *Dīrgha* (Long), and (4) *Saṃyukta* (Connected). He organized the sūtras into verses of ten, so that future reciters could remember them by seeing the title and recalling the content.]

The so-called "Miscellaneous Collection" (*zá zàng*) was not spoken by one person alone: some was spoken by the Buddha, some by disciples, some praised by gods; some told of past lives across three *asaṃkhyeya* kalpas of the bodhisattva. Its literary content and meaning are not uniform and exceed the three collections, hence the name "miscellaneous."

[The text discusses the six pāramitās as belonging to the bodhisattva collection. Two kinds of giving are distinguished: giving from faith and giving from fear. Those from the seventh *bhūmi* upward give their head and eyes; those of the sixth *bhūmi* and below give material possessions.]

[A story illustrates the "diamond precepts" of the Great Vehicle: A handsome bodhisattva-bhikṣu was begging alms when he encountered a beautiful woman who desired him. Knowing her mind, the bodhisattva sat down with her. When she approached him, he taught her the emptiness of the eye and the five sense faculties, and she attained the path of stream-entry. Ānanda, having seen the bhikṣu sitting with a woman, reported a violation to the Buddha. The bhikṣu flew through the air to the Buddha. The Buddha told Ānanda: "Have you ever seen someone who violates the precepts against desire and can fly? This bhikṣu knew the woman's mind and sat with her to teach her the dharma of emptiness."]

[Further sections cover: the perfection of patience (the story of Kṣāntivādin bhikṣu); the perfection of vigor (the story of the boy Jvālakumāra who slandered Kāśyapa Buddha); the perfection of meditation; and the perfection of wisdom.]


# Fascicle Two

[Fascicle Two continues commentary on the *Ekottarāgama*. It discusses:]

Maitreya's praise of the compilation of the six pāramitās into one division (the bodhisattva collection). Ānanda's formula "Thus have I heard" (not "thus have I seen") is explained: using "heard" is for the sake of future audiences, since "seen" would imply present witnessing that later reciters could not claim.

[The text provides context for the sūtra settings: the first teaching at the Deer Park in Vārāṇasī, the conversion of the three Kāśyapas in Magadha, and the significance of Śrāvastī, where the Buddha spent twenty-five years (the longest stay in any city). The reason was that Śrāvastī was the finest country with many wonders: at the Jetavana monastery, thousands of monkeys would come to sit silently and listen to the dharma, and birds would gather as well.]

[The story of the Jetavana's founding: Prince Jeta owned eighty acres of parkland. The elder Sudatta (Anāthapiṇḍada) wanted to buy it. Jeta jokingly said: "Cover it with gold." Sudatta immediately had elephants carry gold to spread across the ground, covering forty acres before Jeta stopped him. The king ruled that a word once given cannot be retracted. Jeta kept the trees; Sudatta took the land. Together they built the monastery: seventy-two lecture halls and 1,250 rooms.]

**The Ten Recollections (*anusmṛti*):**

[Each of the ten recollections is explained with narrative illustrations:]

**1. Recollection of the Buddha:** The Buddha's body is of vajra without any defilement. When he walks, his feet rise four inches from the ground, the thousand-spoke wheel-marks appear on the earth, and insects beneath his footprints are safe for seven days; if they die, they are reborn in heaven.

**2. Recollection of the Dharma:** Dharma precedes even the Buddha, for "the dharma produces all buddhas; the dharma gives rise to the buddha-path." Yet the Buddha is recollected first because dharma, however sublime, cannot be known without the Buddha, just as buried treasure cannot be found without a person of supernatural knowledge.

**3. Recollection of the Saṅgha:** The saṅgha consists of four pairs and eight grades of noble persons. [The story of Brahmadatta, a monk of meager merit, illustrates the power of the saṅgha: wherever he went, the assembly could not receive food. The Buddha divided the assembly repeatedly until Brahmadatta was alone, revealing him as the cause. The Buddha personally gave him food and arranged for him to offer to monks in cessation-attainment and the four *brahmavihāras*, gradually building his merit.]

**4. Recollection of Morality:** [The story of two monks crossing a waterless plain: one chose to die rather than drink insect-filled water and was reborn in heaven; the other drank and survived to see the Buddha, but the Buddha said: "Though you see me, you are far from me. He who died is always at my side."]

**5. Recollection of Generosity:** Giving reaches nirvāṇa because material giving leads to dharma-giving, completing the perfection of generosity.

**6. Recollection of Heaven:** Three kinds of heaven: "elected heaven" (cakravartin kings, elected by the people), "birth heaven" (from the Four Kings up through twenty-eight heavens, where beings enjoy merit but eventually fall), and "pure heaven" (buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, and śrāvakas who have exhausted all defilements). [An old monk sweeping temples was visited by his former wife, reborn as a goddess in the Trāyastriṃśa heaven. She came to tell him he would be reborn as her husband if he continued his merit. He redoubled his efforts, surpassed her heaven, and she visited again saying he had exceeded her realm. He practiced even more vigorously, contemplated the four truths, and attained arhatship.]

**7. Recollection of Tranquility:** [The story of the brahmin who sat under a tree practicing austerities but whose mind sought to become king. A bhikṣu named Surāta sat nearby for seven days without moving. He then conjured an illusory chicken and told the brahmin: "Kill this chicken and eat it." The brahmin refused. The bhikṣu said: "Your original mind intended to kill countless people. You wanted to become king, and a king kills people daily in governance. This chicken is the consciousness in your mind. By killing the chicken of desire, you can attain the great path of non-action." The brahmin, realizing the bhikṣu knew his innermost thoughts, accepted the teaching and attained the path.]

**8. Recollection of the Body:** [The story of a bhikṣu who found a strand of hair floating in his alms bowl from a woman's corpse upstream. He was captivated by the hair and fantasized about the woman's beauty. Following the stream, he found the half-eaten corpse. Seeing the reality of the body, his desire dissolved, and he attained the path.]

**9. Recollection of Death:** [The story of the bhikṣu Vakkali, who suffered chronic illness for six years. In despair, he took a knife to his throat, and at the moment between life and death, he attained arhatship. The Buddha confirmed his awakening.]

**10. Recollection of the Buddha (expanded):** [The long story of Ānanda's parinirvāṇa: before his death, Ānanda sent his two disciples, Madhyāntika to Kaśmīra and Śāṇakavāsin to Siṃhala (Sri Lanka), to spread the dharma. At the Ganges river, he divided his relics between the two kingdoms that had come to claim him.]


# Fascicle Three

[Continues commentary on the ten recollections with further elaboration:]

**Recollection of the Buddha (continued):** [The story of Ānanda's back boil: the Buddha commanded Jīvaka to treat it, but told Ānanda to gaze intently at the Buddha's marks while Jīvaka lanced the boil. Ānanda, absorbed in contemplating the Buddha's form, felt no pain at all.]

[The story of the master and slave at the stūpa: the master said "Homage to the Tathāgata of Ten Powers." The slave said "Homage to the Tathāgata of Eleven Powers." The master objected that the Buddha had exactly ten powers. The slave replied: "What harm is there in eleven? Just don't say nine." This led the master to consult dharma teachers, who said the Buddha has three powers, or ten powers, or innumerable powers. The master renounced the world.]

**Recollection of the Dharma:** [The story of Subhūti, who was praised by the Buddha as the first to see him, even before the nun Utpalavarṇā who transformed into a cakravartin king to push through the crowd. While she thought she was first, Subhūti was in a mountain cave mending his robe. When told the Buddha had descended from heaven, he reflected: "Where is the Buddha? Is the golden color the Buddha? But gold has no limits. The Buddha said: 'All dharmas are empty and without substance. If one understands that the twelve links are empty, neither made nor created, neither person nor self, neither alive nor dead, then one sees the dharma. To see the dharma without life or death is to see me.'" He clasped his hands and called out: "Homage!" then sat down and continued mending.]

**Recollection of the Saṅgha (continued):** The four pairs and eight grades of noble persons are discussed. The saṅgha contains the three vehicles: arhat-saṅgha, pratyekabuddha, and Mahāyāna. Even the Buddha regularly confesses before the saṅgha, because "the ground of the saṅgha is thick and weighty."

**Recollection of Morality (continued):** Two kinds of precepts: worldly (five precepts, ten good deeds) and path precepts (three samādhis, the four truths' sublime wisdom).

**Recollection of Heaven (continued):** [The elaborate story of King Aśoka's brother Susīma (修伽妬路), who did not believe in the Three Jewels. Aśoka devised an elaborate scheme: he pretended to leave on a journey, his ministers crowned Susīma as king, then Aśoka returned and sentenced him to death for usurping the throne. He gave Susīma seven days as king before execution. A monk interceded to save him. Susīma was sent to practice in charnel grounds, where he saw a hungry ghost beating a corpse ("This body caused my suffering") and a deity scattering flowers on a corpse ("This body was my benefactor"). Contemplating these visions, he attained arhatship.]

**Recollection of the Body (continued):** [The elaborate story of King Aśoka and the monk Sudarśana, who stumbled into Aśoka's hell-prison and witnessed bodies being crushed in a mortar, dissolving into foam. Remembering the Buddha's words that "the human body is like gathered foam," he attained arhatship in the boiling cauldron, which turned cold as he sat on a thousand-petaled lotus. This led Aśoka to repentance and the construction of eighty-four thousand stūpas.]


# Fascicle Four

[This fascicle presents the biographies and distinctive merits of the foremost disciples:]

**Ājñātakauṇḍinya:** First among the five original disciples, first to receive the "come, monk" ordination.

**Udāyin:** Foremost in exhortation, for when the Buddha returned to convert his homeland, Udāyin went ahead to display supernatural transformations and convert countless people.

**Mahānāma:** Foremost in swift faculties, attaining supernatural powers even before exhausting all defilements.

**Subāhu:** Foremost in walking through the air as easily as on the ground.

**Maudgalyāyana:** Foremost in supernatural powers, silently traveling to other buddha-fields.

**Gavāṃpati (Cow-Foot Bhikṣu):** Because of two characteristics (cow-like hooves and ruminating after eating), he could not live among people and was sent to the heaven of the thirty-three gods. After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, when Kāśyapa summoned the assembly, a messenger was sent to bring him down. He refused, saying: "The world is empty now. Why should I go?" He entrusted his robe and bowl and entered parinirvāṇa in heaven.

**Svāgata:** Foremost in contemplation of the impure, who upon seeing his sleeping wife's white teeth perceived only bone, then contemplated the thirty-six impure substances of the body.

**Uruvilvā-Kāśyapa:** Foremost in attending to the saṅgha and providing the four requisites.

**Nadī-Kāśyapa:** Foremost in concentrated listening; his inner realization was so thorough that oil seeped from his pores like sweat.

**Aśvajit (Horse-Teacher Bhikṣu):** Foremost in deportment, having studied with the Buddha for only seven days. Despite having been a monkey for five hundred previous lives, his dignified bearing so impressed Upatiṣya (Śāriputra) and Kolita (Maudgalyāyana) that they followed him to the Buddha.

**Śāriputra:** Foremost in wisdom. The Buddha said: "If Mount Sumeru were an inkstone, the waters of the four oceans were ink, the bamboo and trees of the four continents were brushes, and all the people therein were scribes, they could not finish writing out Śāriputra's wisdom."

**Maudgalyāyana:** Foremost in supernatural powers. [He once proposed turning the entire earth over to access the "earth-fat" below to feed starving people. The Buddha refused, saying future monks would lack such powers and kings would demand they perform such feats.]

**Śroṇa-Koṭiviṃśa (Twenty-Billion-Ear Bhikṣu):** Foremost in ascetic practice. Born with natural hair growing from the soles of his feet and jewels valued at two billion in his ears. He practiced walking meditation so vigorously that his soft feet bled, staining the path. When he grew discouraged, the Buddha taught him the parable of the lute: "If all the strings are too tight, can you produce beautiful music? If all are too slack? Only when they are neither too tight nor too loose can beautiful music be produced."

**Aniruddha:** Foremost in the divine eye. [He fell asleep during the Buddha's discourse and was rebuked. He vowed never to sleep again, lost his physical eyesight as a result, but gained the divine eye. The Buddha threaded his needle for him, saying: "Can one ever have enough merit?"]

**Revata:** Foremost in meditation. [He sat under one tree for six years without distinguishing whether it was alive or dead. When King Prasenajit hosted him for six years, Revata could not remember the king's name: "I sat under a tree for six years and did not know whether it was alive or dead. How could I know a person's name?"]

**Therāpaṭamālā:** Foremost in organizing the saṅgha's duties, assigning monks to their proper divisions (sūtra study, vinaya, abhidharma, meditation, preaching, alms-begging, teaching).

**Rāṣṭrapāla:** Foremost in noble renunciation. Born into royal wealth, he refused to eat for six days until his parents consented to his ordination. Later, returning home for alms, he rejected his family's gold, jewels, and beautiful wives, saying: "These things only harm people."

**Kātyāyana:** Foremost in analyzing meaning. He compiled the Abhidharma in seven days of underground retreat, presented it to the Buddha, who approved it. He also correctly explained the Buddha's terse remark about "non-attachment, non-staining" by analyzing how consciousness arises through the eye's contact with form.

**Kuṇḍadhāna:** Foremost in distributing lots (*śalākā*). [He was the elder monk who, despite not having attained supernatural powers, received the lot with sincerity and, in the moment of receiving and returning it, instantly attained arhatship.]

**Piṇḍola-Bhāradvāja:** Foremost in subduing non-Buddhist teachers. [He used supernatural powers to retrieve a sandalwood bowl placed atop a tall pole, then challenged the six heretical teachers to match him.]

**Cakṣu Bhikṣu:** Foremost in caring for the sick, using five methods: distinguishing good medicine, rising first and sleeping last, speaking kind words with little sleep, providing dharma rather than craving food, and being fit to teach the dharma to the sick.

**Vaṅgīsa:** Foremost in composing verses. [A dramatic story: as a young man, he climbed a pole studded with swords to win a woman in a contest. Maudgalyāyana appeared in the air and saved him. He renounced the world and composed spontaneous verses praising the Buddha.]

**Mahākauṣṭhila:** Foremost in the four analytical knowledges. He could expound the meaning of a single phrase for seven days without exhausting it.

**Nandika:** Foremost in alms-begging. [Compared to an archer: one who shoots at a strand of hair at a hundred paces is more skilled than one who shoots the ground, but the ground-shooter never misses.]

**Śīla:** Foremost in eating at one sitting in one place.

**Kimbila:** Foremost in begging from only seven houses.

**Pūrṇamaitrāyaṇīputra:** Foremost in teaching the dharma. From his awakening to his parinirvāṇa, he converted ninety-nine thousand people.

**Upāli:** Foremost in keeping the vinaya. [He shaved the heads of five hundred Śākya princes. The Buddha appointed him the senior monk, forcing the proud Śākyas to bow to their former servant. The earth shook when they finally bowed. Also: Upāli once sought the Buddha's permission to give wine as medicine to a sick monk, saving both his life and enabling his arhatship.]

**Vakkali:** Foremost in liberation through faith. [Sick for six years, he cut his own throat with a knife. Between life and death, he attained arhatship. Māra searched for his consciousness in all realms but could not find it.]


# Fascicle Five

[The biographical accounts continue:]

**Nanda:** Foremost in handsome appearance. He had thirty of the thirty-two marks (more than any other disciple except the Buddha). Despite his extraordinary beauty, his mind remained completely still. [The courtesan Āmrapālī touched his feet, and though his body responded involuntarily, his mind remained unmoved. The Buddha confirmed that Nanda would attain arhatship within seven days.]

**Bhadra:** Foremost in concise teaching, using a single practice as the governing principle from which all other practices follow, like the Buddha's voice that contains eight tones, each tone containing a hundred teachings.

**Sena:** Foremost in expansive teaching, spending thirty years as an ordinary person teaching dharma extensively before attaining the path.

**Deva-Subhūti:** Foremost in wearing fine clothing. [He was a prince accustomed to luxury who could not bear coarse robes. When about to return to lay life, Ānanda prepared a magnificent room with the king's furnishings. That night, his mind settled into meditation, he contemplated the four truths, and attained arhatship, then flew into the sky. The Buddha explained: "Clothing that helps the mind on the path is to be used; clothing that hinders it should be avoided. Some attain the path through fine clothes, others through ragged ones."]

**Nandaka:** Foremost in instructing advanced students, compared to a skilled archer who fells an enemy with a single arrow.

**Sumanas:** Foremost in instructing nuns. [Born with a flower naturally growing from his ear, a reward from a past life when he placed a flower on the ear of Vipaśyin Buddha.]

**Śīvali (尸婆羅):** Foremost in merit. [Born holding a priceless *maṇi* jewel. Wherever he went, his merit caused spontaneous abundance: clouds rained provisions, Indra built monasteries for him, and even foul food placed in his bowl transformed into ambrosia through the power of his accumulated merit.]

**Upasena:** Foremost in complete conduct, possessing all aspects of both inner realization and outer deportment.

**Bhadrasena:** Foremost in gentle speech that never offends. [Contrasted with an arhat who cursed a river goddess with the habitual phrase "wretched slave woman" even after attaining arhatship, showing that even enlightened beings may retain verbal habits from past lives.]

**Mahā-Katyāyana:** Foremost in *ānāpāna* (breath meditation), along with Rāhula. [The story of Rāhula: as a child, he resented the Buddha for abandoning the throne. The Buddha taught him impermanence and then breath meditation, through which he attained arhatship. Mahā-Katyāyana's breath could enter and exit through his eyes, ears, or any of his nine hundred thousand pores.]

**Uttarabhadra:** Foremost in contemplating impermanence and non-self. His body was enormous, standing shoulder-height with the Buddha. Despite his great stature, he was always humble.

**Kumāra-Kāśyapa:** Foremost in varied discourse. [He converted King Pāyāsi, who denied the afterlife, using a series of brilliant parables. The most famous: "If a man fell into a cesspit a hundred measures deep and was pulled out, washed, perfumed, and seated on a fine bed, and someone said 'Go back into the cesspit,' would he go?" "Of course not." "Those reborn in heaven regard the human world the same way."]

**Mukharāja (Face King):** Foremost in wearing poor clothing without shame. [As a four-year-old, he picked up the crown that King Śuddhodana had cast aside in despair over Siddhārtha's renunciation, placed it on his own head, and sat with the posture of a king. At age ten, he asked to renounce the world. His single white cloth, dyed into a robe at ordination, was the only garment he ever wore for his entire monastic life.]

**Rāhula:** Foremost in keeping precepts. [As a child, he told lies to visitors about the Buddha's whereabouts out of resentment. The Buddha used a bowl of water to teach him: full water represents complete precepts; half-empty represents broken precepts; empty represents precepts entirely lost; overturned represents falling into hell. After this lesson, Rāhula never again committed even the slightest transgression. Later, when a brahmin struck his head until it bled, Śāriputra reminded him of the Buddha's past lives of patience, and Rāhula's anger vanished like dew.]

**Panthaka:** Foremost in becoming invisible. [He used his power of invisibility to subdue a wrathful nāga who was destroying crops with hail, entering and exiting the nāga's body through its eyes, ears, nose, and mouth without being seen.]

**Cūḍa-Panthaka:** Foremost in transformation. [Extremely dull-witted, he spent six years trying to memorize "broom-sweep." Finally he understood: the broom represents the Eightfold Path; the dust represents the three poisons. When a brahmin challenged him to debate, he flew into the air. Śāriputra then assumed his form and defeated the brahmin in argument.]

**Śākyarājan (Prince of the Śākyas):** Foremost in noble birth. [The story of King Śuddhodana's four-year-old great-nephew who picked up the discarded crown. When the Buddha returned to Kapilavastu, King Śuddhodana bowed to this monk, explaining that he possessed two of the three kinds of heavenly status (birth-heaven and pure-heaven) while the king possessed only one (elected-heaven).]

**Dharmagupta-Bhāṣita (Long-life Bhikṣu):** Foremost in loving travel. [An extensive past-life narrative: in an *asaṃkhyeya* kalpa past, a brahmin disparaged Dīpaṃkara Buddha and was reborn as a whale seven thousand *yojanas* long. Five hundred merchants were nearly swallowed, but calling the Buddha's name awakened the whale's past-life memory. It released them, beached itself, and starved for fourteen days before dying and being reborn as a human who came to the Buddha.]

**Kāśyapa-ghoṣaka:** Foremost in gathering assemblies for dharma teaching, with a voice that could be heard far and wide.

**Bākula:** Foremost in longevity, living to 160 years. [In a past life under Vipaśyin Buddha, he gave a single *harītakī* fruit as medicine to a sick monk. The resulting merit kept him free from illness for ninety-one kalpas.]

[The fascicle concludes with an editorial note explaining that this treatise comments on the *Ekottarāgama* from the prologue through slightly past the middle of the chapter on foremost disciples, ending at "Śākyarājan bhikṣu."]
