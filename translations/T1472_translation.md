# Deportment for Novices

## *Shāmí Wēiyí* (沙彌威儀)

Taishō 1472

Translated by Guṇavarman (*Qiúnà Bámó*, 求那跋摩) of the [Liu] Song dynasty.

*Note: This is a vinaya manual detailing the proper deportment (wēiyí) and daily conduct for Buddhist novice monks (śrāmaṇera). Attributed to the Indian monk Guṇavarman (367-431 CE), who traveled to China and translated several vinaya texts, the work provides exhaustive rules for every aspect of monastic life: how to serve one's preceptor (upādhyāya) and instructor (ācārya), how to prepare tooth-sticks and washing water, how to handle the robe (kāṣāya) and bowl (pātra), how to carry the staff (khakkhara) and sandals, how to behave when eating together or separately, when begging for alms in a city, when stopping by a river or under a tree, how to serve the community on duty days, how to prepare vegetables, draw water, wash cauldrons, tend the fire, sweep the floor, wash the bowl, pour water for hand-washing, enter the bathhouse, use the latrine, and deliver messages. The text concludes with the "ten numbers" (the ten fundamental dharma-categories a novice must know before receiving full ordination), the five meanings of being a novice, and a formal address to the saṃgha for the occasion of the posadha (fortnightly observance). The work is a practical companion to the ten precepts and reflects the actual rhythms of early Chinese monastic life.*

---

Having received the ten precepts of a novice, one becomes a "worthy person" (*xiánzhě*) on the path. Next, one should be taught what is to be employed, gradually accumulating from small beginnings. One should learn the rules of deportment and conduct.

One should know how many years one's preceptor has been ordained, and the names of one's three teachers. One should be taught to know the year, month, and day when one first received the precepts. One should know the duties of serving the preceptor; the duties of attending the instructor; the duties of providing tooth-sticks and washing water; the duties of presenting the kāṣāya robe and caring for the bowl; the duties of holding the staff and carrying the sandals.

[One should know how to behave] when accompanying the preceptor or instructor to an invitation; when visiting the household of a king; a kṣatriya; a brahmin; when eating together in a row or eating separately; when going together into the city to beg for alms or returning together to one's quarters; when stopping by water to eat in the late afternoon or eating under a tree; when going ahead to wait; when sharing bowls or exchanging bowls; when eating facing each other or eating in sequence; when washing and rinsing after eating or departing after washing the bowl.

One should thoroughly know the duties of serving the community on one's duty day. When one reaches twenty years of age and wishes to receive full ordination, one should know all of this. If, being a worthy person, one cannot fully answer the questions of the bhikṣus, one should not be given full ordination. Why? Having been a novice, one does not know what a novice should practice. The affairs of a śramaṇa are great, difficult, and extremely subtle.

### Five Things the Teacher Should Teach the Novice

(1) One should be respectful toward senior monastics. (2) One must not address senior monastics by their personal names. (3) When senior monastics recite the precepts, one must not eavesdrop. (4) One must not seek out the faults of senior bhikṣus. (5) When a senior bhikṣu makes an error, one must not spread word of it. These are rules of deportment.

### Five Things to Practice

(1) One must not secretly curse a senior bhikṣu. (2) One must not treat a senior bhikṣu lightly by joking in front of them, imitating their speech, appearance, or manner of walking. (3) When one sees a senior bhikṣu passing, one should stand up; if one is reading sūtras, one should stop. If it is mealtime or one is performing community tasks, one need not stand. (4) When walking and meeting a senior bhikṣu, one should stop, step aside off the path, and yield. (5) If one has been playing around and a senior bhikṣu appears, one should immediately stop and apologize.

### Ten Things in Serving the Preceptor

(1) One should rise early. (2) Before entering the room, one should first snap the fingers three times [to announce oneself]. (3) One should prepare the tooth-stick and washing water. (4) One should present the kāṣāya robe and then hand over the sandals. (5) One should sweep the floor and replenish the washing water. (6) One should open up, sweep, and wipe the bed and mat. (7) When the teacher goes out and has not yet returned, one must not leave the room unattended; when the teacher returns, one should receive the kāṣāya and fold it. (8) If one has committed a fault and the preceptor or instructor admonishes one, one must not talk back. (9) One should lower one's head, accept the teacher's words, depart, and reflect on them in practice. (10) When leaving the room, one should pull the door closed behind.

### Five Things in Serving the Instructor

(1) One should regard the instructor in all matters just as one regards oneself. (2) One must not play around. (3) If the instructor scolds you, you must not answer back. (4) If sent to remove an unclean vessel, one must not spit in displeasure or become angry. (5) In the evening, one should massage the instructor's body.

### Providing Tooth-Sticks and Washing Water (6 rules)

(1) Break the tooth-stick to the proper length. (2) Split the end. (3) Wash it clean. (4) Replace the old overnight water. (5) Clean the water pitcher. (6) Fill it with water and bring it in without making noise.

### Presenting the Kāṣāya Robe (4 rules)

(1) Gently present it with one hand supporting the bottom. (2) Check the top and bottom are in order. (3) Stand upright while holding the teacher's robe. (4) Place it upon the teacher's shoulder.

### Eating Together (4 rules)

(1) When seated, remain six feet from the teacher. (2) Wait until the teacher's donation-acknowledgment is finished before presenting the bowl. (3) Do not eat before the teacher. (4) After eating, rise and take the bowl back yourself.

### Entering the Community Assembly (5 + 5 + 5 rules)

First five: (1) One should study diligently. (2) One should practice various tasks. (3) One should serve the community. (4) One should present offerings to senior monastics. (5) When one wishes to receive full ordination, the three teachers will be easy to obtain.

Second five: (1) One should bow to the Buddha. (2) One should bow to the bhikṣu saṃgha. (3) One should greet the senior seat. (4) One should yield the senior's place. (5) One must not dispute over seating.

Third five: (1) One must not call out, talk, or laugh across the seating area. (2) One must not repeatedly get up and leave. (3) When the saṃgha calls "Novice so-and-so," one should immediately rise and respond. (4) One should follow the saṃgha's instructions. (5) If the karmadāna (provost) assigns a task, one should report to the teacher before going.

### Entering Another's Room

A novice, while still occupied with community duties, must not carelessly enter a senior monastic's room. There are three reasons one may enter: (1) when sent briefly by the preceptor or instructor; (2) when asked to fetch something; (3) when going to ask about the sūtras.

Seven rules for entering a room: (1) Snap the fingers three times before entering. (2) Do not stand in the doorway. (3) Do not idly discuss others' affairs. (4) Stand with palms joined and speak according to the Dharma. (5) If told to sit, do not cross one's legs. (6) Do not play around. (7) Do not block others; when leaving, pull the door closed behind.

### When Sent on a Journey Alone

Three things to know when questioned: (1) If asked "What is your preceptor's name?", reply with the name. (2) If asked "How many years has your preceptor been ordained?", reply with the number. (3) If asked "Where is your preceptor from?", reply with the prefecture and county. Likewise for questions about the instructor. And if asked about oneself: give one's home prefecture, name, and the number of years, months, and days since becoming a novice.

### Entering the Bathhouse (5 + 5 rules)

First five: (1) Lower your head upon entering. (2) Avoid the senior's place. (3) If a senior is reciting sūtras, do not speak wildly. (4) Do not splash water on each other. (5) Do not pour water on the fire to extinguish it.

Second five: (1) Do not play around. (2) Do not break the jars or vessels. (3) Do not waste water. (4) Do not muddy the bath-beans or sesame oil. (5) After leaving, do not stay to do laundry.

### The Ten Numbers

The ten numerical categories every novice must know: (1) All sentient beings subsist through food and drink. (2) The two truths. (3) The three feelings (pleasant, painful, neutral). (4) The four noble truths. (5) The five aggregates. (6) The six sense-bases. (7) The seven factors of awakening. (8) The eightfold noble path. (9) The nine abodes of sentient beings. (10) The ten totalities of perception (*kṛtsnāyatana*). These are called the ten numbers.

### The Five Meanings of Being a Novice

(1) Having aroused the aspiration and departed the household, one bears the Way in one's heart. (2) Having abandoned one's former appearance, one wears the Dharma-robes. (3) Having permanently severed family attachments, one is without personal bonds. (4) Having entrusted one's body and life, one follows and honors the Way. (5) Aspiring to the Mahāyāna, one acts for the sake of liberating others.

### Formal Address to the Saṃgha

"Respectfully addressing the great assembly of virtuous saṃgha in the four directions! The novice so-and-so, together with all [fellow novices], bows with head to the ground in homage. We have heard that when the great sun of the Way spreads its halo, all living things receive its brightness; when the True Honored One expounds the teaching, all who have understanding attain awakening. ... The novices, hearing of the poṣadha (fortnightly observance) and recitation of the precepts, leap with joy and wish to practice generosity, but having no material wealth, we offer five hundred tooth-sticks and one thousand counting-sticks, humbly presented to the pure assembly as an expression of our sincere hearts, to remove impurities. We humbly trouble the virtuous saṃgha to compassionately accept them and recite blessings."
