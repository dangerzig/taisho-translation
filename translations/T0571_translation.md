# 佛說婦人遇辜經
## Sūtra of the Woman Who Met with Calamity, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0571

Translated from the Chinese. Western Qin dynasty, translated by Sheng Jian (聖堅)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with a great assembly of one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus.

At that time there was a man without a wife who went to Śrāvastī and married a woman of the country. They already had two sons: the elder was seven years old, and the younger was still an infant in arms. The mother was again pregnant and about to give birth. According to the customs of India, when a woman's time draws near, she returns to the land of her parents.

The husband and wife took a carriage with the two children and set out for Śrāvastī. Midway they stopped to eat and rest, and also to tend to an ox. A poisonous snake coiled around the ox's legs, and the ox broke free from its pen. The husband went to retrieve the ox, intending to yoke it and depart, but found the ox had been killed by the snake. The snake then left the ox and coiled around the husband and killed him.

The wife, seeing this from afar, was terrified and trembling, weeping and calling to heaven. There was no one to rescue her. The sun was about to set. Not far from the road there was a flowing river, and by the watermill there were houses. The wife, pressed by the approaching darkness and fearing bandits, abandoned the carriage and took the two children to the water's edge. She left the elder by the riverbank and carried the infant across the water. When she had reached midstream, a wolf seized the elder son. The boy cried out for his mother. The mother turned to look and saw her son being devoured by the wolf. Panicked and terrified, she lost hold of the infant in her arms, who fell into the water and was carried away by the current. The mother's anguish deepened. Dazed and disoriented, she stumbled in the water and lost the child she was carrying in her womb.

Having managed to cross the river, she asked a traveler on the road: "Are my parents safe and well?"

The traveler replied: "Yesterday their house caught fire, and both your parents burned to death. Nothing remains."

She also asked the traveler: "Are my husband's parents, my father-in-law and mother-in-law, safe?"

The traveler replied: "Yesterday a band of fierce bandits attacked their household. Your father-in-law and mother-in-law are both dead. Not one survived."

The mother, hearing this, was overwhelmed with sorrow and terror. Her mind was confused and bewildered; she could not tell east from west. She tore off her clothes and ran naked in a daze. Travelers on the road saw her and were greatly alarmed. Some said she had a demonic illness, afflicted by ghosts and spirits. Some said she was delirious with grief and had lost her mind. Some spat on her in contempt and fled to avoid her. Others felt pity and compassion.

At that time the Buddha was in Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada. The woman, running wildly, came in that direction and passed by the Jetavana. At that time the World-Honored One was teaching the Dharma to a great assembly. The fourfold assembly of disciples, devas, nāgas, spirits, and beings from all the ten directions were all listening to the sūtra.

It is the nature of all Buddhas that when the blind see a Buddha, they gain eyesight; the deaf gain hearing; the mute can speak; diseases are cured; the weak become strong; poison ceases to act; and the confused mind finds stillness.

When the woman saw the Buddha, her mind immediately became settled, and she was no longer sorrowful. She looked down at her naked body and, overcome with shame, fell to the ground. The Buddha called to Ānanda: "Bring garments for this woman." Ānanda immediately obeyed and brought her garments. When she had dressed, she bowed at the Buddha's feet and sat to one side.

The Buddha then taught the sūtra, revealing merit and wrongdoing: "Human life is impermanent. Those who meet must part. Those who are born must die. None who are born do not come to an end. All things are originally empty. They arise and cease through one's own actions. One revolves through the five destinies like a turning wheel. Having understood that there was originally nothing, one no longer gives rise to distinctions."

The woman, hearing the Buddha's words, her mind was opened and she understood. She immediately generated the mind of unsurpassed true awakening and at that very moment attained the stage of non-retrogression. Her sorrow was removed, like the sun with no clouds.

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the fourfold assembly was filled with joy. The devas, nāgas, and spirits bowed their heads and withdrew.
