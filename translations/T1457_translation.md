# Summary Verses of the Mūlasarvāstivāda Vinaya Miscellaneous Matters

## *Gēnběn Shuō Yīqiè Yǒubù Lüè Pínàiyé Záshì Shè Sòng* (根本說一切有部略毘奈耶雜事攝頌)

Taishō 1457

Translated by Yìjìng (*Yìjìng*, 義淨) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This text is a systematic verse index to the massive "Miscellaneous Matters" (Záshì, 雜事) section of the Mūlasarvāstivāda Vinaya, one of the most detailed vinaya traditions preserved in Chinese. It organizes the vast material of forty fascicles into eight major divisions, each with ten sub-sections summarized in mnemonic verses (shè sòng, 攝頌). The verses function as a table of contents, allowing monastics to recall and locate specific rules and narratives within the larger work. The colophon records that the translation was completed in 710 CE (the fourth year of the Jǐnglóng era) with the assistance of numerous Indian and Central Asian scholars.*

---

In this "Miscellaneous Matters" there are altogether eight divisions. A single verse for the major divisions encompasses the entire framework. Within each major division there are separate sub-divisions, summarized in a total of eight verses. Within each sub-division there are ten verses, making ninety verses altogether, plus the internal summary verses, amounting to roughly a thousand lines. If one can recite and retain them in memory, one can thereby comprehend the overall meaning.

### The Major Division Summary Verse

> Bricks and stones, and ox-hair; the three robes and the senior;
> Relics and the sinews of fierce beasts; Gupta, nuns, and removal of stūpas.

---

### First Division Summary Verse

> Brick-rubbing, nail-trimming, bowl; mirror, birth, support, treading, robes;
> Water-strainer, sprouting beans, special; foot-washing, skirt: these should be tied.

**Sub-section verses (ten lines):**

> Brick-rubbing, stone, white earth, ox-bezoar, eye-beneficial incense;
> Driving stakes and other occasions; jeweled ornaments: know these accordingly.
> Trimming nails and hair, polishing to a shine; in spring, eating small fruits;
> For thirst, the five kinds of medicine are permitted; broadly explaining the great occasion of birth.
> Mending bowls, keeping possessions, knives and needle-cases;
> Together with three kinds of stretched cloth: these the Great Sage has permitted.
> Looking in mirrors and reflecting in water; one should not use combs or brushes;
> Leaving long hair on the crown; the bathing room and the *kuliṇapī* [bathing rules].
> One should cover the private parts facing away; not engage in singing, dancing, or music;
> Reciting in a melodious voice is permitted; there are four kinds of bowl-use.
> Treading cloth and various bags; mattresses and sitting-mats;
> On occasion, separating from the three robes; the six kinds of mental-recollection method.
> Water-strainers of five kinds; shared vessel, single robe, and food;
> Exposed body when eating and drinking; bathing matters: know these accordingly.
> Beans sprouting on impure ground; spitting out food, finger-pointing, and begging;
> Copper vessels should not be made; storing salt and the like is permitted.
> One should make a foot-washing place and also a foot-washing basin;
> In hot weather, fanning is needed; for mosquitoes, the five kinds of whisks are permitted.
> Tying the lower skirt not too high; not carrying heavy burdens;
> If sick, staff and sling are permitted; eating garlic and the like is allowed.

### Second Division Summary Verse

> Ox-hair, parasols and canopies; wrapping in felt, the Śrīmālā occasion;
> Going forth, medicine, hot-water vessels; door-panels, hammers, and hatchets.

**Sub-section verses (ten lines):**

> Ox-hair and private places; not sharing a bed, not wearing a single cover;
> If one obtains white-colored cloth, it should be dyed and covered before use.
> Parasols and canopies are of no [merit for] future lives; singing voices, not setting fires;
> When traveling, seeking a preceptor; felt blankets, not worn inside-out.
> Wearing felt: what is permitted and not; harsh sounds, not placing the bowl [carelessly];
> Robes allow three kinds of buttons; one should know the strips are also three.
> The Śrīmālā and Durgata occasions; next, the regulation of jeweled ornaments;
> Gold strips and colored objects: none of these should be kept.
> Going forth has five benefits; not handling money, the study-ordination;
> Speaking verses before the great assembly; smoke-tubes and mouth-rinsing are permitted.
> Medicinal bathing is appropriate; nasal irrigation, copper cups are permitted;
> Riding in palanquins is allowed for the elderly and ill; one should know about latrine matters.
> Water-bottles: know pure and impure; [the prayer] "May the World-Honored One live long";
> Because of this, a nun passed into nirvāṇa; five kinds of food for chewing and eating are both permitted.
> Installing door-panels, hinge-holes, leather flaps in the middle window;
> Internal width, lattice screens, pivots; opening a shop requires a sheep-hoof [latch].
> Iron hammers and iron prongs; iron spades and wooden shovels;
> Cooking-pot stands, stove-platforms (five hundred); axes and chisels: the assembly permits all.
> Axes (permitted), hatchets, three ladders; bamboo, wood, and rope as needed;
> Irrigating from below, the method of building a monastery; explaining the occasion of difficult births and the like.

### Third Division Summary Verse

> Three robes, and clothing racks; river-side, building monasteries, salt;
> Face-wiping and body-wiping cloths; monastery seats, and knives should be kept.

**Sub-section verses (ten lines):**

> Three robes: strip-and-leaf measurements; bed-legs, dusters for traveling dust;
> Walking-places: wearing felt mats; keeping stone-pounders when timely.
> Clothing racks and lamp-cages; do not let insects be harmed;
> In heat, three-sided shelters are permitted; one may note Nanda's body [as example].
> By the riverside, the regulation on tooth-sticks; Rāhula sent out the door;
> What is proper and improper to combine; the two practices [of medicine] should be administered.
> Building monasteries, installing eave-nets; broadly explaining the occasion of sweeping grounds;
> Seeking the dharma: the story of the two children; in hot times, building shelters is permitted.
> Stone salt placed inside horn-containers; medicine vessels using felt mats;
> Installing platforms; when reciting sūtras, placing an object under one's feet.
> Face-wiping cloths: thin and sparse; spittoons and body-lining cloths;
> Iron troughs, paving foundations; using a sun-gem [crystal] to wash clothes.
> Body-wiping cloths, stepping on a snake's neck; stone vessels giving rise to doubt;
> Dyeing cloth: many kinds; as one wishes, throughout the monastery.
> Building monasteries and necessary supplies; perforated beds, ritual courtesy;
> Keeping a separate hair-cutting robe; flower garlands hung at sleeping-places.
> Fine seats, beds as gifts; incense-paste and bowl-cages;
> Oil vessels, dharma-speaking processions; cloth bags holding three cords.
> Razors should be kept; and nail-clippers and such items;
> Bed-supports and reclining pillows; fragrant earth: use as one wishes.

### Fourth Division Summary Verse

> Seniority, and walls and fences; occasions of damage, and caring for the sick;
> Caṇḍāla, pigs, sugarcane, monastery; bowl, dependency, and tree-planting method.

**Sub-section verses (ten lines):**

> Seniority reversed in sequence of speaking; or they may [continue] together to the end;
> Filtering to make unseasonable drinks; location is not the limit of standard.
> Walls, fences, nuns' shaving implements; not wearing polished garments;
> If receiving little, still divide equally; cleansing rites: one should know them.
> The occasion of damage: mending is necessary; [the bhikṣu] Bright Moon, hearing, recited;
> Dependence: know the distinctions; three persons sitting together is permitted.
> Caring for the sick, exempting fundamental offenses; approaching completion, not climbing trees;
> Kings and ministers do not receive ordination; cutting off hands should not be done.
> Caṇḍāla, Sudāyin; great robes: temporary use;
> Śirimokha, Vasudatta; taking the bowl with the thought of [belonging to] the deceased.
> Aśvajit, Guarding-the-Moon; taking with the thought of theft from a layperson;
> This agrees with the great vinaya; therefore it is not repeated redundantly.
> Pigs, sugarcane, palm-fruit; felt blankets; Black Joy returns;
> Placing knives and needles; not using lapis-lazuli vessels.
> Painting throughout the monastery; making fire and bathing;
> Bowl-water, not treading on leaves; connected shoes: not appropriate for eating.
> Without a bowl, ordaining a great thief; the rains-retreat without a preceptor;
> After five years, sharing equally in provisions; carrying heavy loads should not be done.
> The four reliances, seeking the six requisites; thieves stealing bhikṣu robes;
> Entrusted storage of five special kinds; one should know the method of dyeing robes.
> Know the method of planting trees; thief's felt made into a miracle;
> If one obtains a superior outer robe, one should not cut off its border.

### Fifth Division Summary Verse

> Cremation, interrogation, three turnings; forfeit-and-confession, my body's death;
> Boundaries; a bhikṣu should not; incompatible, and five kinds of leather.

[Sub-section verses follow in ten lines covering cremation rites, Maudgalyāyana's death, the Buddha's three turnings of the dharma-wheel, the first teaching of the five monks, forfeit-and-confession rules, affairs after death, boundary regulations, and the five kinds of leather that should not be used, with the exception that bear-skin shoes may be worn for hemorrhoid ailments.]

---

### Sixth Division Summary Verse

> Sinews of fierce beasts: should not; lamplight and the hero;
> Pack-rope and ordination of nuns; permitting Mahāprajāpatī.
> Nuns not walking ahead of elders; they may receive other bedding;
> Not permitted to splash water: this summary verse should be known.

[Sub-section verses follow in ten lines covering the prohibition on using sinews and skins of fierce beasts, the narrative of the Buddha's birth and its portents (the four heavenly kings, lotus steps, and the sage Asita's visit), the story of Dīpaṃkara (*Dēngguāng*, 燈光) becoming king, the narrative of the hero Vīra (*Yǒngjiàn*, 勇健), pack-rope regulations, the ordination of nuns with the eight rules of respect (*bā jìngfǎ*, 八敬法), and the narrative of permitting Mahāprajāpatī Gautamī to go forth, including the five benefits of going forth and various regulations for nuns.]

---

### Seventh Division Summary Verse

> Gupta, nuns not dwelling; monk's-foot [shoes], hermaphrodites;
> Path-small, karmic acts, wine-selling; sex-change, monastery-outside, stone.

[Sub-section verses follow in ten lines covering the story of Gupta sleeping with a child, nuns not dwelling in wilderness hermitages or outside-city monasteries, the monk's-foot shoes permitted, regulations regarding hermaphrodites and intersex persons, minor path regulations, karmic procedures, wine-selling women's houses, the rule for monastics who undergo sex-change (expelled after three times), and regulations for not performing confession outside the monastery.]

---

### Eighth Division Summary Verse

> Removing stūpas, confession, gate-front; being appointed should not be kept;
> Not sharing with women; because of wife, purging medicine, three robes, snake.

[Sub-section verses follow in ten lines covering stūpa repair, Upāli's (*Bōlí*, 波離) regulations, monastic governance rules, confession for nuns, the poṣadha (*zhǎngjìng*, 長淨, "purification") ceremony, appointment regulations, prohibitions on keeping copper vessels or altering wine, the prohibition on bathing with women, the staff regulation prompted by a lay-woman, purging medicine and tooth-poison, licking-knives and washing, the three robes and their proper wearing, wilderness regulations, and the snake-prompted inspection of bedding.]

The final verses conclude with the narrative of the bhikṣu Varṣākāra (*Xíngyǔ*, 行雨), the Buddha's declaration of old age, the journey toward parinirvāṇa, the four black and four white [dharmas], the conversion of Subhadra, the distribution of relics among the kingdoms, and the First and Second Councils of five hundred and seven hundred.

---

Fascicle 37 records the four black and four white [dharmas], then explains the parinirvāṇa events.

Fascicle 38 explains the occasion of the non-Buddhist Subhadra attaining the fruit, then the dispute of the kingdoms over the relics.

Fascicle 39 explains the brahmin distributing relics to the kingdoms, and Gavāṃpati's council and other matters.

Fascicle 40 explains the councils of five hundred and seven hundred.

*[Colophon]: In the fourth year of the Jǐnglóng era of the Great Tang, the year gēngxū, the fourth month, the fifteenth day: the Tripiṭaka Dharma Master, the Great Worthy Yìjìng, expounded and interpreted the Sanskrit text and composed and corrected the Chinese text.*

*Assisted by: the Tokharian Great Worthy Dharmaśarman, verifying the Sanskrit meaning; the Central Indian Great Worthy Banu, verifying the Sanskrit meaning; the Kashmiri Great Worthy Dharmananda, verifying the Sanskrit text; and numerous other Chinese scholar-monks and Indian brahmins verifying the translation, with over thirty scholars of the Academy of Literary Refinement jointly supervising.*
