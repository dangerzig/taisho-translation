# 觀自在菩薩如意輪呪課法
## Ritual Manual for the Cintāmaṇicakra Dhāraṇī of Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1952

Author unknown.

---

### Preface

Zhiyi, the patriarch of the Sui dynasty, in expounding the *Mahā-śamatha-vipaśyanā*, set forth the practice-methods of various sūtras according to the four kinds of samādhi. Though many read it, few practice it. Why is this so? Because people's faculties are shallow and their appreciation of the Dharma-flavor is diluted. In studying under teachers, they labor at terminological analysis; in serving the Buddha, they are lax in the nourishment of practice. Therefore, the ten contemplations that constitute the vehicle and the five repentances that aid the path barely survive as empty words. During the leisure time between lectures and study, I have earnestly sought the benefit of regular dhāraṇī-recitation. Searching through the sūtra repository, I found four versions of the Cintāmaṇicakra dhāraṇī. Upon careful examination, the texts are in fact the same text in different translations. The sentences are concise and the ritual procedures are simple. Through it, one can block evil and exhaust the three obstructions. Through it, one can uphold goodness and perfect the twofold adornment. The previous Indian Dharma-master, whose taboo name was Zunshi, constantly studied this sūtra and recognized it as essential for benefiting beings. He particularly favored Yijing's translation because the dhāraṇī words are easy to recite, and so had printing blocks carved for distribution to the fourfold assembly. However, Yijing's translation is rather abbreviated. The ritual method described merely says "concentrate the mind and recite orally." As for the ceremonial procedures and contemplative visualization, they are never indicated. When receiving and upholding it, one may feel something is lacking. I have therefore drawn from various texts to supplement it. From beginning to end, the work is organized into seven sections: (1) Ritual Method; (2) Contemplative Visualization; (3) Reverence and Praise; (4) Recitation; (5) Repentance and Vows; (6) Verification of Signs; (7) Resolution of Doubts. All of these follow the original sūtra, ensuring that the source and the stream are not separated. They model themselves on the earlier formulation, valuing the continuation of the inherited tradition. I acknowledge the fault as mine, and await future sages to judge.

### Section One: Ritual Method

The sūtra says: "If there are good men, good women, bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās who arouse the aspiration and seek results in this present life, they should single-mindedly receive and uphold this dhāraṇī. When about to receive and uphold it, one need not inquire about auspicious or inauspicious days, months, or stars, nor separately cultivate fasting or precepts. One need not bathe or wear pure garments. One need only concentrate the mind and recite orally without slackening. A hundred thousand kinds of wishes will all be fulfilled. There is no other mantra that can equal the power of this Wish-Fulfilling Dhāraṇī King." One should recite one hundred and eight times daily, and one will see Avalokiteśvara and the rest. Śikṣānanda's translation also gives the same number, adding that one should recite continuously for seven days and seven nights. Divākara's translation says one should recite one thousand and eight times during the latter part of the night or at dawn, and up to three thousand times daily during the latter part of the night. Bodhiruci's translation says one should always recite one thousand and eighty times at the fifth watch, and also six times daily, one thousand and eighty times each session, continuously without interruption. Each individual syllable should be recited until it reaches three lakṣas. It further says: if a bhikṣu or bhikṣuṇī recites one hundred and eight times; if a layman, one hundred and six times; if a laywoman, one hundred and three times; if a boy, one hundred times; if a girl, ninety times: this is called the regular method. All surpassing accomplishments will be achieved.

Now let us discuss this. The language of western India may differ as transmitted through the five regions of India; the Chinese translations therefore also differ because the Tripiṭaka masters' source texts are not the same. This is a matter of sacred judgment, difficult to fathom through ordinary reasoning. Since we are using Yijing's translation, we should take one hundred and eight times as the standard. This is because it represents breaking through the one hundred and eight afflictions and accomplishing the one hundred and eight samādhis. Furthermore, Yijing says: "If one completes even a single recitation, all the above matters will be fulfilled as wished." Ratnacinta's translation also says: "When receiving and upholding, one need not exert painful effort; merely reciting, all will be accomplished." Given this, even if one does not reach one hundred and eight times, reducing the number accordingly should also be without fault.

The times of recitation prescribed in the various versions differ, because people's faculties and inclinations vary. Now, if one follows the method of reciting for seven days and seven nights, one must recite six times daily, one hundred and eight times each session, like the *Great Compassion Sūtra*'s limit of three sets of seven days, or the *Sūtra of Invoking Avalokiteśvara*'s limit of seven sets of seven days, and so forth. If one follows the method of reciting daily at the fifth watch or dawn, this constitutes the regular daily course. The number is not fixed. If it were otherwise, how could one complete three lakṣas? Surely not within seven days could one reach such a great number. Furthermore, if one cannot manage the fifth watch or dawn, one may recite at any time that suits. The latter part of the night was specified because at that hour people's minds are clear and sharp. Yijing's translation is so phrased to allow temporal flexibility.

According to Bodhiruci's translation: "Now, day and night, dwelling in a quiet room, face east, sit in full lotus, and visualize Holy Avalokiteśvara as present before your eyes. Recite without distraction. Burn aloeswood incense, mentally make offerings, reverently prostrate. Whatever flowers and incense one can provide, offer them without interruption." One should know that before reciting the dhāraṇī, one must concentrate the mind in a quiet room and contemplate. Then enter the ritual space and make prostrations and offerings. The image should face west. One need not construct a maṇḍala, but should arrange a seat in three tiers. On the upper tier, place the Dharma-jewel, namely the dhāraṇī sūtra that is upheld. On the left, place an image of Śākyamuni; on the right, an image of Amitābha. On the middle tier, place only the image of Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, with flowers or lamps on either side. On the lower tier, arrange only the offering implements. If one cannot arrange things as above, simply use the ordinary ritual-space format, or keep only the bodhisattva's image with offerings as one is able. Although it is said that bathing and pure garments are not necessary, if one is doing the special seven-day practice, using them is better. Therefore, Jñānānanda's translation says: "If one wishes Holy Avalokiteśvara to appear and grant one's wish, one should bathe in pure water, anoint the body with fragrant paste, and wear clean garments," and so forth. That sūtra broadly explains the offerings, invocations, welcoming, and sending-off, each with its own mudrā and mantra. The other translations all lack these, and we do not employ them here.

### Section Two: Contemplative Visualization

From beginningless time, sentient beings have been obstructed in their clarity and stillness solely by dullness and distraction. Therefore, the dusts of affliction enslave them outwardly, and the eye of wisdom is blinded inwardly. The events of the eight sufferings press and afflict without ceasing. The characteristics of the twofold adornment are exhausted and bereft, with nothing to grasp. Thus one drifts through accumulated kalpas, exposed and alone, without self-reflection. All is summoned by the mind. The sage, having compassion, uses hidden and secret words to express the subtle and wondrous Dharma, teaching one to receive and uphold it. First, one receives the worldly joys; next, one recovers the Tathāgata-nature. Earnestly, it is like the mud-dauber wasp chanting over the caterpillar. If one not only recites with the mouth but also adds contemplation with the mind, how much more swiftly it will be!

Bodhiruci's translation says: "If one truly accomplishes this supreme Dharma of the dhāraṇī, in all places, whether eating or not eating, whether pure or impure, one should single-mindedly visualize Holy Avalokiteśvara, with marks and excellences perfectly complete, like the sun first rising, with radiance shining brilliantly. Recite this dhāraṇī without false thoughts. Constantly uphold it without interruption, entirely without transgression. Then the bodhisattva will manifest a golden body, remove all obstructive defilements, bestow spiritual power and blessing, and whatever one's mind seeks and wishes will all be fulfilled." Divākara's translation says: "When reciting, one should be mindful of Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva and seek him as one's refuge." Thus, when mind and mouth correspond and mindfulness and recitation function together, the receiving and upholding is at its utmost. However, the beginner's mind races and scatters, and it is difficult to gather. Therefore, in a quiet room, sitting in full lotus, one first contemplates the holy form.

Since the sūtra text is brief, we now supplement and make it explicit. The bodhisattva's original realization is the wondrous awakening, called "King of the True Dharma." His manifest trace is at the stage of succession, called "Avalokiteśvara." Though the origin and trace differ, both follow the essence of true suchness to give rise to the function of responsive transformation. The essence is like a mirror; the function is like a reflection. Therefore, the true does not respond of itself; the response depends on the stimulus. Just as a mirror does not reflect of itself; the reflection depends on the form. Furthermore, stimuli are superior or inferior, and the responses are great or small. The great response is a body eight hundred thousand koṭi of nayutas of yojanas tall. The small response is a body identical to sentient beings in all places. The great response appears in the Pure Land, while the small response roams through the impure lands. The sūtra says the bodhisattva's dwelling at Mount Potalaka indicates his place of sojourn in this world. Now, when first beginning to cultivate contemplation, the mind's visualization is weak, and one should contemplate the small response. What is its appearance? According to Bodhiruci's translation, one paints the image of Avalokiteśvara. Now, paint a thirty-two-petaled open lotus flower. Upon the flower's platform, paint the Cintāmaṇicakra Holy Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, facing west, sitting in full lotus. His countenance is joyful and serene; his body is golden in color. He wears a jeweled crown with a transformation-buddha and bodhisattva. His left hand holds an open lotus flower, upon whose platform is a cintāmaṇi jewel. His right hand is in the Dharma-teaching mudrā. He wears heavenly garments, pearl earrings, bracelets, and seven-jeweled necklaces of various adornments, and his body emits a multitude of radiances. The practitioner should focus the mind on this holy form and contemplate it. One should know that this form arises from mental visualization. Like flowers in empty space, it is fundamentally without substance. This form and this mind are originally Avalokiteśvara's wondrous, pure, luminous essence. The flowers in space are the provisional; "fundamentally without substance" is emptiness. The wondrous essence is the middle. The three truths are perfectly interfused: neither one nor different, inconceivable. Not only is the holy form thus, but the teaching of the Dharma is also the same. Because sentient beings are equipped in their nature with all dharmas, they can respond to conditions and give rise. Because the bodhisattva has cultivated and realized all dharmas, he can universally manifest through every gate. Though dharmas differ, in emptiness and the middle they are the same. If there were only difference without sameness, the principle of sympathetic response would be lost. If there were only sameness without difference, the phenomenal workings of sympathetic response would be lost. Losing the principle is like ice and charcoal that cannot be combined. Losing the phenomenal workings is like a valley echo that cannot be summoned. When both principle and phenomena are complete, sympathetic response is accomplished.

One should further know that the Dharma of the three truths is precisely the meaning expressed by the mantra. Therefore, Yijing's translation calls it "the Unobstructed Avalokiteśvara Lotus Cintāmaṇicakra King Dhāraṇī." "Unobstructed" means the bodhisattva possesses the wisdom of the three truths without the obstruction of the three confusions. "Avalokiteśvara" means using this wisdom to contemplate sentient beings and thereby attain sovereign mastery. This is naming the dhāraṇī after the person who speaks it. "Lotus" means: born yet bearing fruit, symbolizing the merit of the Dharma-body being originally complete. "Wish-fulfilling" refers to a jewel. The sūtra says: "It rains wondrous precious treasures, like a wish-fulfilling tree and wish-fulfilling jewel." This symbolizes the merit of liberation that can produce all dharmas. "Jewel-wheel" has the power of crushing and subduing, symbolizing the merit of prajñā that can destroy all dharmas. Furthermore, the lotus emerges from water, free from defilement, pure: this symbolizes prajñā. Its calyx is fragrant: this symbolizes liberation. The jewel-body is originally round: this symbolizes the Dharma-body. Its color is lustrous and pure: this represents prajñā. The wheel-body is a jewel: this is like the Dharma-body. Its function of turning: this is like liberation. These three each have threefold meaning, symbolizing the three virtues. Raising one encompasses three; speaking of three is precisely one. Because it expresses this meaning, encompassing and governing all dharmas, surpassing all, it is called "King." Blocking the evil of the nine realms and upholding the good of the buddha-realm, it is called "dhāraṇī." Śikṣānanda's translation calls it "the Secret Treasury Divine Dhāraṇī." If it were not the Dharma of the three virtues and three truths, how could it bear this name? The bodhisattva's own practice resides solely in emptiness and the middle. Transforming others proceeds from the provisional to meet beings. Sentient beings receive the transformation within the provisional. When the transformation is complete, it also resides solely in emptiness and the middle. Therefore, the *Awakening of Faith* treatise says: "If one departs from the activity-consciousness, there are no perceived characteristics. Because the buddhas' Dharma-body has neither this nor that form-characteristic, they do not mutually perceive one another." A brief explanation is given thus. If one wishes to know the detailed practice-characteristics, one should consult the *Śamatha-vipaśyanā* of Yuquan.

### Section Three: Reverence and Praise

With all reverence, single-mindedly I bow to the eternal Three Jewels of the ten directions. I now, in accordance with the Dharma, solemnly present incense and flowers in offering to the buddhas, world-honored ones of the boundless dharma-realm in the ten directions; to the twelve divisions of scripture; and to the holy assembly of the three vehicles. Having made offerings, with all reverence:

> Within the Tathāgata-garbha is the true Dharma-jewel,
> Responding to the nature of sentient beings,
> Stimulated and thereby penetrating.
> The great compassionate great being, Holy Avalokiteśvara,
> Has spoken the mantra called the Cintāmaṇicakra,
> Which causes the extinction of
> Immeasurable sufferings and perils,
> And can also accomplish all that is sought.
> Therefore Śākyamuni earnestly praises it:
> Among all dhāraṇīs,
> This one is without equal.

Single-mindedly I bow to our original teacher, Śākyamuni, the World-Honored One.

Single-mindedly I bow to Amitābha, the World-Honored One of the Land of Ultimate Bliss.

Single-mindedly I bow to the buddhas, world-honored ones of the dharma-realm in the ten directions.

Single-mindedly I bow to the Unobstructed Avalokiteśvara Lotus Cintāmaṇicakra King Dhāraṇī.

Single-mindedly I bow to the twelve divisions of scripture of the dharma-realm in the ten directions.

Single-mindedly I bow to Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva Mahāsattva.

Single-mindedly I bow to Mahāsthāmaprāpta Bodhisattva Mahāsattva.

Single-mindedly I bow to the Wish-Fulfilling Vidyārāja and all the great bodhisattva mahāsattvas.

Single-mindedly I bow to the holy assembly of the three vehicles of the dharma-realm in the ten directions.

### Section Four: Recitation

The sūtra says: Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One, I now have a great dhāraṇī-vidyā called the Unobstructed Avalokiteśvara Lotus Cintāmaṇicakra King, the foremost and most rare, able to bestow benefit in accordance with the mind in all that is sought, and all will be accomplished. World-Honored One, in your great compassion, permit me to speak it. I shall, relying on the Buddha's awesome power, bestow it upon sentient beings." The World-Honored One praised the bodhisattva, saying: "So it is, so it is! You are able to compassionately pity all sentient beings. I shall protect and support you." The bodhisattva, having received the Buddha's permission, with compassionate vows filling his heart, then before the Buddha spoke the dhāraṇī:

*[Dhāraṇī]*

At that time, after Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva had spoken this dhāraṇī, the great earth quaked in six ways. The palaces of all the devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, and others all whirled around in confusion. All evil māras and those who create obstruction saw their own palaces burst into flames, and there was none who was not terrified. Even the sentient beings suffering in the hells were all freed from their suffering and reborn in the heavens.

### Section Five: Repentance and Vows

Universally for the four kindnesses, the three realms of existence, and all sentient beings of the dharma-realm, we pray that the three obstructions may be severed. Taking refuge, we repent. With utmost sincerity we repent. I, bhikṣu so-and-so, take refuge in the eternal Three Jewels of the ten directions, in Śākyamuni, in Amitābha Buddha, and in Holy Avalokiteśvara, the one endowed with great compassion. May they arise in compassionate pity and serve as clear witnesses. I, for the sake of all sentient beings of the dharma-realm: from beginningless time, the mind-nature has been like a maṇi jewel, its own essence pure, its spiritual function originally so, serving as the same secret treasury shared by all tathāgatas. Yet through the movement of false thoughts, the illusion of saṃsāra arises. Within birth-and-death, one receives all manner of affliction. That is to say, in the past and present one has created and accumulated the karma of the four grave offenses, the five heinous acts, and the ten unwholesome deeds, and should fall into the suffering of the Avīci Hell. Because of evil karma, one's present body is beset by all manner of illness and calamity. As the sūtras broadly describe the various evil causes and conditions, now relying on the teaching of the greatly compassionate Holy Avalokiteśvara, I recite and uphold the Cintāmaṇicakra, that such karmic obstructions as these may be extinguished, and a hundred thousand kinds of wishes may all be fulfilled. I humbly pray that the bodhisattva will accept my repentance. According to what I seek, may you bestow the maṇi jewel, raining wondrous precious treasures, both worldly and supramundane provisions of merit and wisdom. May all follow the mind and be fully sufficient. Until the end of this life, may I not enter the womb, but be born by transformation from a lotus flower in the Land of Ultimate Bliss. May I see the true bodies of Amitābha Buddha and Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva. May I hear the wondrous Dharma-sound and realize the nature of perfect penetration. Thereafter, may I universally manifest through every gate, benefiting sentient beings, utterly delivering all from the dusts of affliction, and together accomplishing the knowledge of all seeds.

Homage to the buddhas of the ten directions. Homage to the Dharma of the ten directions. Homage to the Sangha of the ten directions. Homage to our original teacher Śākyamuni Buddha. Homage to Amitābha Buddha. Homage to the Cintāmaṇicakra Dhāraṇī. Homage to Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva. Homage to Mahāsthāmaprāpta Bodhisattva. Homage to the Wish-Fulfilling bodhisattva mahāsattvas.
