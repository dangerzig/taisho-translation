# 王梵志詩集
## Poetry Collection of Wang Fanzhi

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 2863

Translated from the Chinese. Author unknown (attributed to Wang Fanzhi).

---

**Preface**

As for the Buddhist teaching: the path and the Dharma are without self, suffering, and emptiness. Knowing the meager karmic connections of former lives and the causes and effects of what follows, this compilation exhorts to goodness and admonishes against transgression. Though the table of contents has but a few entries, the poems composed number over three hundred. They speak of the affairs of the times and are not vain or empty. We recite the noble writings of Wang Fanzhi and practice the essential meaning of Ding and Guo. They do not draw upon the sūtras but set forth everything in common speech. Not only do the wise change their minds with ease; even the foolish are moved to change their countenance. Near and far, the word has spread, urging and summoning people to goodness. Greedy and rapacious officials who plunder: self-serving functionaries who draw their salary should naturally become upright and circumspect. Though each may be ignorant and dull, their feelings are stirred to the utmost. After one cursory reading, upon three reflections one does not forget. Even if a great elder were to lecture and preach, it would not match reading these good writings. A rebellious son who neglects his duties is transformed into the most filial. A lazy wife rises morning and evening to serve her parents-in-law. A rough and slovenly husband is born to shame. Wanderers in various provinces remember their homeland. The lazy husband rises at night; the lazy wife works until dawn. Before the bolt of silk they all come together. Knowing the consequences of sin and merit, they diligently plow and toil to fill their mouths with grain. With a single will and the five senses, they do not change or waver. In eastern and western provinces, all sing its praise. If only one reads these verses thoroughly, the stubborn, the foolish, the dim, and the dull will all become worthy and good.

---

Looking out at the people of this world,
In villages, wards, and community altars:
When one family has a death,
The whole village gathers to weep.
Opening their mouths to wail over another's corpse,
They do not know their own departure is pressing.
We are but ghosts of the long sleep,
Come briefly to stand upon the earth.
Like the felt used for swaddling a child,
Turning from the dry spot to the wet:
Those who died before are buried deep,
And those who die after follow right in.

---

When I was rich and had money,
My wife and children looked upon me kindly.
If I took off my clothes,
They would fold my robe and jacket for me.
When I went out to seek my livelihood,
They would see me off to the road.
When I brought things home,
Seeing me, their faces were full of smiles.
They circled me like white doves,
Lovely as parrots.
By chance, for a brief time, I became poor;
They looked at me with scorn.
People have seven periods of poverty
And seven of wealth, four retributions in turn.
Those who, when rich, ignore others:
Just watch what comes down the road.

---

When every mouth in the family has died,
And I die with no relatives left,
Hurry to sell the property
And have memorial vegetarian feasts performed for me.
May I be reborn in a good place.
When my body dies, hire someone to bury me.
The money and goods the neighbors can put forward;
Let them sort out among themselves how to divide it.

---

The body is like a sheep in a pen;
When the fated hour comes, how pitiable and fitting.
The sheep runs about covered in wool;
People wear fine garments.
Strip the clothes off and stand there naked:
One is in truth no better than a sheep.
Sheep die every day;
People likewise perish every day.
Seized by the head and dragged away,
They are just the same as a plump, fine sheep.
The sheep dies in bitter toil;
The person departs without a wound or scar.
When life is cut off, one is driven after the other;
The soul wanders through foreign lands.
If you have money, do many deeds of merit.
Eat well and wear good clothes.
The foolish create vast transgressions;
The wise should think it over carefully.

---

How laughable the people of this world!
The foolish are many, the clever few.
They do not worry that the road of death is long,
But cling greedily to bitter afflictions.
At night they sleep and wander the realm of ghosts;
At dawn they return to the way of humans.
Suddenly they rise and drag each other about,
Bickering and demanding their grandfathers' heirlooms.
In poverty and suffering, with nowhere to get them,
They seize each other for flogging and beating.
With suffering and pain while alive,
It would be better to die early.

---

Others laugh at my poverty, my poverty.
My poverty is the greatest joy!
Without an ox and without a horse,
I do not worry about being raided or plundered.
You are rich, and your household levies are high;
Interest and taxes eat it all away.
I have nowhere to be summoned;
When full, I eat and stretch out my legs.

---

You, rich man, wear a brocade robe,
Yet are constantly bound and entangled.
In poverty and hardship there are no worries;
Grass clothing fits the body as it will.
There are greatly foolish lords
Who are alone without sons or daughters,
Yet greedily and widely seek to amass wealth,
Supporting slaves and many maidservants.
When the death-summoner calls at the gate,
There is no time to bid farewell to the neighbors.
In death one gets four planks of wood
And a single yellow quilt.
Money, property, slaves, and maidservants go on being used;
Let them manage their own affairs.
Having money but not knowing how to use it,
One enters the capital empty-handed.
A foolish, ignorant ghost, broadly speaking,
Burdened and confused, the body suddenly dies.
Immediately surrounded by the death-summoners,
Hands bound behind the back, beaten with clubs,
Crossing the River of No Return.
Thrown to the ground before the court,
Cangue and cudgel rise all over the body.
Dead for a week,
Punished by the ghost-officers, bull-headed,
With iron pitchforks the soldiers
Pound and grind the body.
Dying again and reviving again,
Beaten in confusion, losing one's senses,
With no way to see one's home.
The wife has been taken by another man; the son serves a new stepfather.
The slave serves a new master;
The maidservant follows a new mistress.
The team of four horses with golden saddle,
Engraved stirrups and silver crupper and bridle,
The horn bow has no master to draw it;
The jeweled sword lies upon the ground.
After a hundred days of mourning,
The whole household has forgotten you.
Your money and goods are used by others:
Since ancient times, this has been the ordinary way.
Those before hoarded and accumulated;
Those after have no sense of shame.
This is a miser,
Who was not willing to die in poverty.

---

Husband and wife sit facing each other;
A thousand years would not be enough.
When one of them falls ill in bed,
The other watches from afar, hands not touching.
The heart's reckoning comes home at last;
In vain the money was spent.
Treasures piled high as a mountain,
But in death one gets a single coffin.
Empty hands grasping two fists,
The mouth vainly filled with jade.
Forever parted from the mirror on the dressing table,
No heart to open the wardrobe. The mirror is covered in dust;
The scissors grow rusty among the clothes.
The seat where one used to sit singing and dancing:
There is no way to practice the tunes again.
The pípá's laughing voice is silent;
The strings of the zither break and do not continue.
The flowers grieve the eyes of those who come after;
The one who went before had thin fortune.
Living, one sat in a hall of the seven jewels;
Dead, one enters the earth and strikes against the corners.
The hearse is dragged along;
The ghosts still weep for one another.
Each day, how many thousands of such affairs;
Light and shadow are swift and rapid.
The rich prepare coffins of wood;
The poor are wrapped in mats at the corners.
Together they sing "alas, alas,"
And send the departed to an empty tomb.
A thousand farewells are ten thousand farewells;
One is forever parted from the joys of one's life.
The wise enter the Western Paradise;
The foolish fall into the hells.
The head plunges into the sea of suffering,
Dark and dim, with no awareness.
When you walk about in your hometown with head held high,
You might as well show off your boots and jacket.
If you have money, just use it;
Do not plan for a thousand years.
Among those who reach a hundred, there is scarcely one;
Those reaching seventy are rare.
With eyes wide open, we watch others die,
Yet cannot awaken to our own fate.
Foolish skin wrapping pus and blood,
Stubborn bones doggedly keeping up.
Two legs walking: a rack for clothes,
Step by step heading toward Avīci.
Both eyes blind, they do not recognize the ghosts.
The death-summoners rush to pursue.
A red cord strung through the neck,
Hands tied behind, clubs beating the back and skin.
Bareheaded and barefoot they run,
With nothing to wear upon the body.
Alone the heart races within,
While on all four sides soldiers surround them.
Up ahead, ten checkpoints for taxes;
Behind, iron hammers pound.
The death-summoners draw their bows to shoot;
The agony is worse than knife or awl.
Messengers call at the gate;
In panic and fright, there is no delay.
Naked, they are driven after the others;
The whole household wails in grief.
The house has no one to protect it;
Wife and children are bullied by others.
Money and property no longer concern one;
Estates and lands are parted from forever.
The three souls have nowhere to rest;
The seven spirits scatter and fly from the head.
Sinking and drifting through the three evil destinies,
No one at home even knows.
Having clothes but unable to wear them,
Having horses but unable to ride,
Having slaves but unable to command them,
Having maidservants who do not follow,
Having food but unable to eat,
Ever facing hunger ahead.
Dark, dark, the sufferings of hell,
With no time in sight to emerge.
One after another they go;
Who among them returns to life?

---

I earnestly exhort all you worthies:
Build bridges and roads to ferry your body across. Gain merit and be reborn in the Western Paradise. Know contentment; this body is limited. The body's appointed term is too harsh. But if one indulges, like a candle-person the brain grows dull and foolish. Buying brocade for the wife, bundled tight, the mind goes astray. One gains only a brief period of toil, and the neglected body enters bitter poison.

---

Watch from the side as people build their houses, planning as though for ten thousand years. Yet among all people, scarcely one reaches a hundred. Even if granted a long span, cut it in half and it is like a shooting star's light for a brief instant. Midway through, children are few, whether sons or daughters. Do not be startled or flustered; examine the three realms. In the dark month, it waxes by the month. Fattening what harms the body, day by day creating transgressions without knowing satisfaction.

---

*Deng Qingchang, Scholar-Attendant of Dayun Temple.*
