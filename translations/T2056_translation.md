# Account of the Conduct of the Late Great Worthy of the Great Tang, the Posthumously Titled Minister of Works, the Great Dialectician of Correct and Broad Wisdom, the Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra

## *Dàtáng Gù Dàdé Zèng Sīkōng Dà Biànzhèng Guǎngzhì Bùkōng Sānzàng Xíngzhuàng* (大唐故大德贈司空大辨正廣智不空三藏行狀)

Taishō 2056

Compiled by Zhào Qiān (*Zhào Qiān*, 趙遷) of the Tang dynasty.

*Note: This is the official biography (xíngzhuàng) of Amoghavajra (Bùkōng, 705-774 CE), one of the most influential Buddhist masters in Chinese history. A patriarch of the esoteric (Vajrayāna) tradition, Amoghavajra served as imperial preceptor to three Tang emperors (Xuánzōng, Sùzōng, and Dàizōng) over nearly thirty years. The biography, written by his disciple Zhào Qiān, recounts his birth (attended by miraculous signs), his training under Vajrabodhi (Jīngāngzhì), his voyage to Sri Lanka to receive the complete transmission of the Vajraśekhara and Vairocanābhisaṃbodhi tantras from Ācārya Samantabhadra, his many feats of rain-making and exorcism for the Tang court, his massive translation output (over 120 fascicles of esoteric scriptures), and his death in 774 CE attended by numerous portents. The text is a primary source for the history of esoteric Buddhism in China.*

---

The Imperial Consecration Great Master, whose dharma-name was Amoghavajra (*Bùkōng*, 不空), transmitted the vajra-seal of the great bodhicitta through the vows and practices of Samantabhadra. Following the Buddha's command, he rescued all beings. Holding the great Dharma-treasure, he came in accordance with the times, assisting three imperial reigns for nearly thirty years.

### Birth and Early Life

The great master was originally from the Western Liang Prefecture, of a brahmin family of North India. His father died early, and he was raised by his maternal uncle, taking his mother's surname. Before his mother, Lady Kāng, conceived, a physiognomist told her: "In the future, you will surely give birth to a bodhisattva." Having spoken, he vanished. Greatly marveling at this, she bathed, changed her garments, abstained from speech, and maintained mindfulness. Within three days, while sitting and lightly dozing, she dreamed that the Buddha smiled subtly, and light flowed from his eyes into the crown of her head. Startled awake, perspiration covered her whole body. She then knew she was with child. After this, every night her chamber was bright as daylight. She was pregnant for twelve months before giving birth.

He could speak from birth. His spiritual bearing transcended the ordinary; his vital essence was exceptional. The six pāramitās and four immeasurable minds seemed to be innate knowledge rather than learned from a teacher. Only a buddha with another buddha could fully fathom him.

### The Lineage of Transmission

In ancient times, the World-Honored One Vairocana entrusted the esoteric teaching of the Vajraśekhara Yoga, the king of true words and dharma-seals, to Vajrapāṇi Bodhisattva. After nearly a thousand years, it was transmitted to Nāgārjuna Bodhisattva. After several hundred years, Nāgārjuna transmitted it to Nāgabodhi Ācārya. After several more hundred years, Nāgabodhi transmitted it to Vajrabodhi Ācārya. Vajrabodhi transmitted it to the present great master. Although the source is one, the streams diverge; in all, only some tens of persons have received it. In the legitimate family succession, our master is the sixth.

### Training under Vajrabodhi

Initially, the great master followed his maternal family on a tour of the great country, arriving at Wǔwēi and Tàiyuán at the age of ten. At thirteen, he began studying under the Great Propagation Master, the patriarch [Vajrabodhi], mastering Sanskrit dialogue and Brahmanical treatises. He could recite texts from memory and achieve thorough comprehension within a set number of days, to the patriarch's great amazement.

Subsequently, he was conferred the bodhicitta precepts and led into the great maṇḍala of the Vajradhātu. At the test of casting flowers [onto the maṇḍala to determine his affinity], it was known he would have a great future. At fifteen he first took tonsure; at twenty he received full ordination. He was skilled in the vinaya of the Sarvāstivāda, understood many languages, and could read foreign scripts.

He studied the *Śabdavidyā* (Science of Grammar) for twelve years of effort, completing it in six months. He recited the *Mañjuśrī Vow*, which should take a year, completing it in two nights.

Later, he earnestly implored the patriarch to transmit the five divisions and three mysteries of yoga. After three years of seeking without fulfilling his wish, he resolved to return to India for the Dharma's sake. That day he lodged at the Xīnfēng inn. That night, the patriarch happened to dream that all the images of buddhas and bodhisattvas in the monasteries of the capital were moving eastward. Startled awake, he urgently summoned the master to return. Upon hearing he had come back, the patriarch was greatly delighted, saying: "All of my dharma-treasury I shall transmit to you." The next morning, he transmitted the dharma of the five divisions, the consecration and homa ācārya teachings, the Mahāvairocana *siddhi* ritual, and all the mudrās and practices of the buddha-crown division, each and every one transmitted to the utmost.

### Voyage to Sri Lanka

Several years later, the patriarch received an imperial decree to return to his homeland. The great master accompanied him. When they reached Henan Prefecture, the patriarch fell ill and passed away. This was the autumn of the twenty-ninth year of the Kaiyuan era [741 CE].

Following his late master's final testament, the great master was instructed to go to Siṃhala (Sri Lanka). In the early Tianbao years, he arrived at the Southern Sea Commandery. When the trading ships had not yet arrived, the Investigating Commissioner Liú Jùlín thrice invited the great master and earnestly requested consecration. The master consented, and provisionally at the Fǎxìng Monastery established a ritual arena. Because of Commissioner Liú, the four assemblies all benefited, and tens of thousands were brought across.

Before the master departed, he entered the maṇḍala and, facing the image of the principal deity, applied the three mysteries of vajra empowerment in recitation and walking meditation. Within ten days, Mañjuśrī manifested in person. Knowing his great vow would not be in vain and his longtime wish was fulfilled, he then led his disciples Hánguāng, Huìbiàn, and thirty-seven monastics and laypeople, took up his staff, and boarded the ship.

[The biography recounts the perilous sea voyage: encountering a great black storm that terrified the merchants; the master wielded the five-wisdom bodhicitta vajra in his right hand and the Prajñāpāramitā scripture in his left, reciting the *Mahāpratisārā* dhāraṇī, and after just one recitation the winds calmed and seas stilled. A second storm brought great whales spouting waves like mountains, but again the master's recitations saved the ship.]

Upon reaching Sri Lanka, the king sent envoys to welcome him. The king was greatly pleased and invited the master to stay in the palace for seven days, each day bathing him with fragrant water from a golden vessel. The prince, queens, ministers, and counselors paid reverence to the master as the king did.

The master then sought out Ācārya Samantabhadra and others, presented gold, jewels, and brocade, and requested the opening of the eighteen assemblies of the Vajraśekhara Yoga and the Vairocana Great Compassion Garbhakośa, the establishment of ritual arenas, and permission for his disciples Hánguāng and Huìbiàn to jointly receive the five-division consecration.

From that point, the master sought out every authentic teaching of the true words, along with more than five hundred sūtras and śāstras, the samaya seals and secret mudrās of all the honored ones, their forms, colors, and images, ritual arenas, banners, and emblems, both textual meaning and the nature of characteristics: he exhausted every source.

[The biography then recounts the episode of the trained elephants: when the king staged an elephant spectacle, the master secretly recited the Buddha-Eye mantra and formed the great seal. Standing in the street in a state of compassionate samādhi, more than ten maddened elephants, within a few paces, suddenly collapsed and fled. The entire nation marveled.]

### Return to China and Service to Three Emperors

Returning to the capital in the fifth year of the Tianbao era [746 CE], the master presented the Sri Lankan king's letter and gifts to Emperor Xuánzōng. By imperial decree he established a maṇḍala within the palace and conferred the five-division consecration on the emperor.

That year a drought persisted through the summer. The emperor requested the master to pray for rain, commanding that the date could not be postponed and the rain could not be violent. The master performed the Mahāmayūrī Vidyārājñī altar ritual. Before three days had passed, nourishing rain fell everywhere. The emperor was greatly delighted and personally bestowed a precious box, a purple kaṣāya robe (which the emperor himself draped upon the master), and two hundred bolts of silk.

[The biography continues with accounts of the master's service through three reigns, including:]

- Stopping a storm by empowering a silver vase (and the comical episode when a palace goose knocked the vase over, causing the wind to resume)
- Secret correspondence with Emperor Sùzōng during the An Lushan rebellion, providing strategic counsel that proved accurate
- Building the Mañjuśrī Pavilion at Dàxīngshàn Monastery, with the emperor himself as patron
- Rain-making rituals that succeeded within the time limits set by the emperor
- Translation of the *Renwang Jing* (Humane King Sūtra) and *Miyan Jing* (Secret Adornment Sūtra), for which the emperor personally composed prefaces; on the day of promulgation, auspicious clouds appeared
- Receiving the title Grand Preceptor (*Tèjìn*), the honorary rank of Director of the Court of State Ceremonial, and enfeoffment as Duke of the Sù Commandery

### Final Days and Death

In the ninth year of the Dàlì era [774 CE], sensing the approach of death, the master expressed displeasure at the conferral of worldly titles, saying: "The holy assembly is solemn and serene, extending hands to comfort us. The white moon is full. The time of my departure has come. Why must I steal name and position at the end of my life?"

In the middle of the night during the twelfth month, he commanded his disciple Zhào Qiān to bring brush and ink, saying: "I wish to compose a brief *Ritual for Nirvāṇa and Cremation*, so that future generations may follow this procedure for my funeral."

From spring through summer, he ceased eating and sleeping, proclaiming the wondrous Dharma and admonishing his disciples. He constantly spoke of the *Samantabhadra-praṇidhāna* and the *Sūtra of the Arising of Boundless Gates*, urging them to recite and uphold it, sighing deeply again and again.

To those who had previously received the dharma from him, he especially instructed them to direct their minds to the contemplation of the bodhicitta, the principal deity's great seal, and the true meaning of the syllable A: "Comprehend that dharmas are unborn; realize the body of great awakening." Pointing to his palm, he comforted and instructed them earnestly, with grief and joy mingled: "You should value the Dharma above your very lives. What you have heard is not easy to come by. I think back to former days, braving dangers and perils, entrusting my body for the Dharma's sake, traveling and bowing through many countries, going back and forth over a hundred thousand *lǐ*. You should reflect on this meaning and swiftly cultivate your practice. Do not pursue profit and disgrace your persons; do not seek fame and lose the Way."

On the fifteenth day of the sixth month of the ninth year of the Dàlì era [July 7, 774 CE], at the noon hour, he bathed in fragrant water, changed into new robes, sat upright with composed countenance, ordered the drafting of his farewell memorial to the emperor, gazed toward the north, then lay on his right side facing east, entered the great-seal body-samādhi, and departed. Though his spirit had gone, his countenance remained as before. As his breath was ending, his complexion became even more radiant. This was the power of the Dharma at work; how could the marks of death mar it?

He had lived seventy years, with fifty years since ordination. His dharma-disciple Huìlǎng succeeded to the consecration position; the others who knew the dharma numbered some tens of persons.

The emperor mourned deeply and suspended court for three days. He bestowed three hundred bolts of silk, two hundred bolts of cloth, four hundred *shí* of rice and flour, seven *shí* of oil, fifteen cartloads of firewood, three cartloads of charcoal, four hundred thousand cash, and an additional two million cash for the construction of the stūpa. Imperial messengers came daily to offer condolences.

### Portents at Death

Before the master's passing, many portents appeared: various monks dreamed that a thousand-fathom jeweled banner suddenly toppled; the new Mañjuśrī Pavilion suddenly seemed to collapse with a great crash; the vajra-wisdom pestle flew up into the sky; the pool behind Dàxīngshàn Monastery completely dried up; the bamboo grove bore fruit; and the courtyard flowers changed color. There were dozens of such extraordinary signs.

On the seventh day of the seventh month, the cremation rites were performed. Those who came in sympathy numbered hundreds of millions. By imperial decree, the eunuch envoy Liú Xiānhè made a sacrifice and announced the posthumous title: Minister of Works (*Sīkōng*), with the honorific epithet "Great Dialectician of Correct and Broad Wisdom, the Tripiṭaka Master Amoghavajra," honoring his virtue.

When the cremation fire was extinguished, several hundred relics (*śarīra*) were recovered from the ashes. Eighty were submitted to the palace. On the crown of the skull, one relic was half-hidden and half-visible. By subsequent imperial decree, a relic-stūpa was built at the master's former residence at Dàxīngshàn Monastery, with over ten thousand strings of cash provided for its construction.

*[Colophon by Zhào Qiān]: I, the humble Qiān, attended the master with towel and staff for nine years, and held his brush and inkstone for eight. I was privileged to participate in his translation work and secretly received his esoteric transmission. All the circumstances of the master's transformative activities I personally received from him. During his lifetime he commanded me to compose this account. While serving him, I had no leisure to compile it. Moreover, by his deathbed testament, I could not refuse despite my reluctance. With his breath departing and tears hard to restrain, I have written down these matters. Of the original events, not one in ten remains. Respectfully submitted.*
