# 六度集經
## Collection of the Six Perfections

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0152

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Kang Senghui (康僧會)

---

# Fascicle One

## Chapter One: The Perfection of Giving

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa in Rājagṛha, together with five hundred arhats and a thousand bodhisattvas. Among them was a bodhisattva named Anīkṣipta. When the Buddha preached the dharma, he always calmed his mind and listened with compassion, silent and free of thought, his mind settled on the sūtra. The World-Honored One, knowing this, spoke for him: "The bodhisattva's six perfections are supreme and difficult practices that swiftly lead to buddhahood. What are the six? First is giving, second is morality, third is endurance, fourth is diligence, fifth is meditation, and sixth is wisdom, the supreme and lofty practices.

"What is the perfection of giving? It means nurturing beings with kindness, pitying the erring with compassion, rejoicing in the virtuous, protecting and saving all beings, spanning heaven and earth, vast as rivers and seas. Giving to beings means feeding the hungry, giving drink to the thirsty, clothing the cold, cooling the hot, healing the sick with medicine, and offering carriages, horses, boats, jewels, and precious things, even wife, children, and kingdom, giving freely to any who ask. Like Prince Sudāna, who gave to the poor and destitute, loving them as his own children, and when his father the king banished him, he felt pity but no resentment."

### (1)

"Long ago, when the bodhisattva's mind had penetrated truth, he saw the impermanence of the world and the fragility of life, and he exhausted his wealth in giving. Śakra, lord of the gods, saw the bodhisattva nurturing beings with kindness and saving them through giving, his merit towering and his virtue weighty in the ten directions. Fearing the loss of his own position, Śakra conjured a hell and placed it before the bodhisattva, saying: 'Those who give to save others, when they die their souls enter the great mountain hell, where they are burned, boiled, and subjected to ten thousand torments. Giving brings suffering; will you still give?'

"The bodhisattva replied: 'How could one who practices the virtue of giving enter the great mountain hell?'

"Śakra said: 'If you do not believe, ask those suffering there.'

"The bodhisattva asked a sinner: 'What brought you to hell?'

"The sinner said: 'In my past life, I emptied my household to help the poor and rescue others from hardship. Now I bear heavy punishment in the great mountain hell.'

"The bodhisattva asked: 'If kindness brings calamity, what happens to those who receive the gifts?'

"Śakra said: 'Those who receive kindness ascend to heaven when they die.'

"The bodhisattva replied: 'My purpose in rescuing others is solely for their sake. If what you say is true, that is exactly my wish. To suffer punishment for acts of kindness, I would willingly do so. To endanger oneself for the sake of others is the highest aspiration of a bodhisattva.'

"Śakra asked: 'What do you aspire to, that you pursue such lofty practice?'

"He answered: 'I seek buddhahood, to rescue all beings and bring them to nirvāṇa, beyond birth and death.'

"Hearing his sacred purpose, Śakra prostrated himself and said: 'In truth, there is no such thing as entering the great mountain hell through kindness and charity. Your virtue shakes heaven and earth. I feared you would take my position, so I conjured this hell to confound your resolve. I foolishly deceived a sage; I beg forgiveness for this grave error.'

"Having repented, Śakra bowed and withdrew. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving in the perfection of kindness."

### (2)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a great king named Sarvadatta, who gave to all beings whatever they asked and constantly pitied those in distress. Śakra, fearing the king's virtue would earn him the throne of heaven, went to test him. He commanded a subordinate king: 'Transform into a dove and fly to the king, feigning terror and begging his protection. The king, being compassionate, will surely shelter you. I will follow as a hawk and demand the dove. The king will never surrender it, and will cut flesh from his own body to equal the dove's weight.'

"Śakra transformed into a hawk, and the subordinate king became a dove. The dove flew to the king's feet, trembling: 'Great King, save me! My life is finished!'

"The king said: 'Fear not! I will protect you.'

"The hawk arrived and said: 'That dove is my food. Please return it.'

"The king said: 'The dove came seeking my protection, and having given my word, I will not break it. I will give you my own flesh, a hundred times the dove's weight.'

"The hawk refused all other meat and demanded the dove. The king said: 'I have given my word; what can I offer so you will release the dove?' The hawk agreed to accept the king's flesh equal to the dove's weight. The king cut flesh from his own thigh and weighed it, but the dove kept growing heavier. He cut all the flesh from his body, yet it did not equal the dove's weight. The pain was immeasurable. Still with a compassionate heart, the king wished the dove to live, and told his ministers: 'Kill me quickly and weigh my marrow to match the dove.'

"The hawk, seeing the king's unwavering resolve, resumed his true form along with the subordinate king. Śakra prostrated himself: 'Great King! What do you aspire to, that you endure such suffering?'

"The king said: 'I do not seek the throne of Śakra or the position of a universal monarch. I see beings sunk in darkness, unable to see the Three Jewels or hear the Buddha's teaching, indulging in calamitous deeds and casting themselves into the hell of no escape. Seeing this foolishness, I am moved to pity. I vow to seek buddhahood and rescue beings from their afflictions, bringing them to nirvāṇa.'

"Śakra then had heavenly physicians apply divine medicine, and the king's wounds healed, his strength surpassing what it had been before. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (3)

"Long ago, when the bodhisattva was destitute, he traveled with merchants to another land. They all had faith in the Buddha and gave to the poor. His companions asked: 'What will you give?' He answered: 'Since this body is merely borrowed and all things are impermanent, I see the fish of the sea, great devouring small, and my heart is grieved. I will offer my body to save the small ones.' He threw himself into the sea. The great fish were sated, and the small ones survived. His soul was reborn as king of the sturgeon, his body measuring leagues in length.

"By the shore was a nation suffering drought, where the people were starving and devouring one another. The fish wept: 'How the beings suffer! My body has leagues of flesh that could sustain these people for a month.' He cast himself onto the shore, and the entire nation ate of him, carting away flesh for months while the fish still lived.

"A heavenly spirit descended: 'How can you endure such suffering? Why not release your life and escape this pain?'

"The fish said: 'If I die, my spirit will depart and my body will rot. The people will starve again and devour one another. I cannot bear to see it.'

"Eventually someone cut off his head with an axe, and the fish died. His soul was instantly reborn as a prince. Born with supreme wisdom, he extended fourfold compassion, his kindness equal to heaven and earth. Pitying the people's poverty, he wept when he spoke of it. The drought continued, and he fasted, prostrated himself, and repented: 'The people's misfortune is my own fault. May my life be taken if it will bring rain.' He grieved daily like a filial son mourning a parent. His sincerity reached far, and five hundred pratyekabuddhas came to his land.

"The king heard this and rejoiced. He welcomed them with prostrations and invited them to the palace. The queen and prince were all reverent. Finest food and robes were offered. The king prostrated and wept: 'My heart is impure and my conduct unclean. I have failed the teaching of the Three Jewels and brought suffering upon the people. May calamity be taken from the people and visited upon me.'

"The pratyekabuddhas said: 'You are a benevolent ruler, and the buddhas know your virtue. We now bestow blessings upon you. Command the people to plant their crops at once.'

"The king obeyed. All the people set to work, and the grain transformed into melons, each containing several bushels, fragrant and delicious. The entire nation rejoiced and praised the king's virtue. Neighboring kingdoms all submitted, and the people gathered like clouds. The entire land kept the precepts and took refuge in the Three Jewels. When the king and his people died, they were all reborn in heaven."

The Buddha said: "The poor man at that time was I myself. Through countless kalpas of kindness and saving beings, the merit did not decay, and now I have attained buddhahood, honored as the God of Gods, the hero of the three realms. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (4)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a renunciant dwelling in the mountains, diligent in contemplation. Finding a tigress who had just given birth, exhausted and starving, about to devour her own cubs, the bodhisattva was deeply grieved. He reflected: 'I have vowed to seek the path for the sake of all beings. This body will one day be discarded in old age and death. Better to give it in kindness and perfect my merit.' He threw himself headfirst into the tiger's mouth, wishing for a swift death so he would not feel the pain. The mother and cubs were both saved. The buddhas praised his virtue, and all gods, nāgas, and spirits of the path were moved. Some attained the fruits of stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, and arhatship; some awakened to pratyekabuddha; some aroused the aspiration for supreme, perfect awakening. By this fierce resolve, the bodhisattva leapt nine kalpas ahead of other bodhisattvas. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (5)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a great king named Pianyu in the land of Qianyi. His people followed his example of benevolence, and the prisons were empty. A renunciant from another land, envious of the king's generosity, came and asked for the king's head. The king offered precious things and jewel-crafted heads instead, but the renunciant wanted only the real head. The king tied his hair to a tree and offered his head. A tree spirit struck the renunciant down to protect the king. The four heavenly kings guarded the land, and the kingdom flourished."

The Buddha told the monks: "The king of Qianyi was I myself. The renunciant was Devadatta. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (6)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a great king who toured his realm monthly to help the poor and destitute. Śakra, jealous of his virtue, tested him repeatedly: first obtaining a loan, then demanding the kingdom itself, then demanding the king's chariot and horses, then demanding repayment of the loan. The king's wife and child were imprisoned unjustly through Śakra's machinations. Yet the king meditated and discerned Śakra's schemes. Śakra's heavy evil ripened and he fell alive into the great mountain hell. The king was reunited with his family and ruled both kingdoms."

The Buddha said: "The king was I myself. The wife was Yaśodharā. The son was Rāhula. Śakra was Devadatta. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (7)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a great king who never toured his realm. His minister persuaded him to go out. Seeing the wealthy households, the king coveted their goods and seized them for the treasury. One householder explained that true wealth lies in Buddhist merit, not material possessions, which belong to five claimants: water, fire, thieves, the government, and death. The king was awakened, studied the Buddhist scriptures, and practiced great generosity. He established Buddhist temples, kept the six monthly fasts, and his kingdom prospered for three years. After death, he was reborn in the second heaven."

The Buddha told the monks: "The king was I myself. The householder was Śāriputra. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (8)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a great householder named Xiantan, wealthy beyond measure. Seeing the impermanence of worldly life, he gave away all his possessions and then practiced medicine to heal the sick. When his resources were spent, he went pearl-diving at sea. His merchant companions, coveting his precious white pearl, pushed him into a well. Protected by the gods, he survived and found his way home. When the king demanded answers from the merchants, they confessed. Xiantan pleaded for their lives, and the king pardoned them. The merchants returned his goods, and the entire kingdom embraced the Buddha's precepts."

The Buddha said: "Xiantan was I myself. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (9)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was born with the name Pushi (Universal Giver). At birth he declared his vow to save all beings. At age ten, he left home as a renunciant and traveled across the sea. He encountered three jeweled cities guarded by great serpents, and through his meditation on loving-kindness, he pacified each serpent and entered. In each city, heavenly beings offered him a miraculous pearl. When the sea dragon stole the three pearls, Pushi vowed to drain the ocean. With the help of a heavenly being, he drained eight-tenths of the water, and the sea spirits returned the pearls. Pushi then traveled through all the nations, giving away his treasures until no land had any poor, and all kingdoms adopted the five precepts and ten virtues."

The Buddha told the monks: "Pushi was I myself. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (10)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a great king named Long Life, with a prince named Long Living. A cruel neighboring king invaded. King Long Life refused to wage war, saying: 'To kill my people for my own sake is not benevolent.' He and his prince fled to the mountains.

"The cruel king posted a reward for their capture. King Long Life encountered a destitute brahmin who had come seeking the king's charity. The king offered the brahmin his own head so the brahmin could claim the reward. The brahmin reluctantly agreed, and King Long Life was executed at the crossroads. The nation mourned. Prince Long Living, disguised among the crowd, heard his father's final teaching: 'Do not pursue vengeance. Follow the buddhas' way of endurance.'

"The prince eventually entered the cruel king's service in disguise and rose to become his trusted companion. Three times while hunting, the prince held a sword over the sleeping king but remembered his father's dying words and stayed his hand. Finally he revealed himself and forgave the king. The king repented, returned the kingdom, and the two nations lived in peace.

"King Long Life was I myself. The prince was Ānanda. The cruel king was Devadatta. Through lifetime after lifetime, Devadatta pursued me with malice, and I always forgave him. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

# Fascicle Two

### (11) The Sūtra of King Pāya

"Long ago, King Pāya of Vārāṇasī ruled with kindness, feeding all the people within his city of four hundred leagues. When a neighboring king attacked, Pāya refused to fight, saying: 'To sacrifice my people's lives for my own is not benevolent.' He fled to the mountains. There he met an old brahmin seeking his charity. The king offered his own head, drew his sword, and cut his own throat to save the brahmin from poverty. The neighboring king, learning the truth, preserved the king's body for thirty-two years and then enthroned his son."

The Buddha told the monks: "King Pāya was I myself. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (12) The Sūtra of the Prince of Vārāṇasī

"Long ago, two princes of Vārāṇasī yielded the throne to each other. The elder and his wife became renunciants by a river. He rescued a mutilated criminal cast adrift, nursing him back to health. But his wife committed adultery with the criminal and tried to murder her husband by pushing him off a cliff. A tree broke his fall, and he was carried to safety on a turtle's back. He returned to his kingdom and ruled with great benevolence. When his wife arrived disguised with the criminal, the truth was revealed. The ministers debated punishments. The king, true to his compassion, would not take revenge."

The Buddha said: "The king was I myself. The criminal was Devadatta. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (13) The Sūtra of King Sarvadāna

"Long ago, King Sarvadāna (meaning 'One Who Gives All') never refused any request. Mañjuśrī, wishing to test him, appeared as a young brahmin and asked for the king and queen as slaves. The king joyfully agreed, and both he and his queen followed the brahmin barefoot as slaves. The queen, pregnant and in great pain, was beaten and forced to march. They were sold to separate masters. The queen's baby was killed by her mistress. Yet through all this, neither the king nor queen bore any resentment.

"In an instant, everything vanished like a dream, and the king and queen found themselves back in their palace with all as before. Mañjuśrī appeared in the sky on a lotus of seven jewels and praised their sincerity. The three-thousandfold world shook, and the entire nation aroused the aspiration for the supreme path. The king and queen attained the acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The king was I myself. The queen was Yaśodharā. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

### (14) The Sūtra of Sudāna

This is the longest story in the giving chapter, telling the famous tale of Prince Sudāna (Sudinna/Vessantara). The prince gave away everything, including the royal white war-elephant, and was banished to the mountains. There, an old brahmin took his two children as servants, tying them with ropes. Though the children wept and called for their mother, the prince kept his vow never to refuse any request. Śakra then tested the prince by asking for his wife, but revealed himself and returned her. The old brahmin sold the children to their grandfather the king. Eventually the prince was recalled from exile and ruled with even greater generosity than before.

The Buddha told the monks: "The prince was I myself, born from Tuṣita heaven through King Śuddhodana. The father was Ānanda. The wife was Yaśodharā. The son was Rāhula. Śakra was Maitreya. The old brahmin was Devadatta. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of giving."

# Fascicle Three

### (15)

King Hemo ruled his great kingdom for eighty thousand years with perfect benevolence, teaching all his people the ten virtues and giving generously to all in need. When a thief was caught and confessed his crime was driven by poverty, the king blamed himself: "When my people hunger, it is I who starve them." He emptied the treasury to relieve all poverty. The kingdom prospered, crime ceased, and all the people kept the ten virtues. After death, the king and all his people were reborn in heaven.

The Buddha said: "King Hemo was I myself."

### (16) The Sūtra of the Four Clans

The Buddha taught a household of the four castes, desperately poor but devout, about the four mental attitudes of giving: kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. He explained that a gift given with joy brings rich reward, while a gift given grudgingly brings meager return.

### (17)

The Buddha compared the merit of various offerings: from the vast material gifts of the brahmin Vīlāma to feeding a single person of pure precepts, then stream-enterers, once-returners, non-returners, arhats, pratyekabuddhas, and buddhas. But establishing a shrine, keeping the three refuges, the five precepts, and the monthly fasts surpasses all. And even the precepts are surpassed by impartial loving-kindness toward all beings, whose merit is beyond measure.

### (18)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a deer king of magnificent five-colored fur. When the king's hunt slaughtered many deer, the deer king went to the king and offered to send one deer daily for the royal kitchen, sparing the others from panicked slaughter. The king agreed. When a pregnant doe's turn came, the deer king went himself to the chopping block. The king was so moved by this selfless act that he declared: 'A beast possesses the compassion of heaven and earth, while I, a human ruler, kill living things daily!' He forbade all hunting in his land."

The Buddha said: "The deer king was I myself. The human king was Śāriputra."

### (19)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a mother goose who tore flesh from beneath her own wings to feed her three starving chicks. When the chicks realized the meat smelled like their mother, they refused to eat, saying: 'Better that we die than harm our mother.' A heavenly spirit praised both the mother's kindness and the children's filial piety."

The Buddha said: "The mother goose was I myself. The three chicks were Śāriputra, Maudgalyāyana, and Ānanda."

### (20)

"Long ago the bodhisattva was a peacock king who abandoned his five hundred wives to pursue a peahen, serving her like a servant to gather sweet fruits. Captured by a hunter for the queen's medicine, the peacock king offered to heal the queen with his compassionate incantations instead. He healed the entire nation's diseases through blessed water. Then he proclaimed 'three follies': his own folly in abandoning five hundred wives for one peahen; the hunter's folly in refusing a mountain of gold for the king's promise; and the king's folly in releasing such a healer."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The peacock king was I myself. The human king was Śāriputra. The hunter was Devadatta."

### (21)

"Long ago an aged brahmin lived in the mountains. Four animals, a fox, an otter, a monkey, and a rabbit, wished to provide food to keep him from leaving. The monkey gathered fruit, the fox found a bag of flour, the otter caught a fish. The rabbit, having nothing to give, gathered firewood, lit a fire, and threw itself into the flames, saying: 'My body, though small, can provide one day's meal.' But the fire would not burn the rabbit. The buddhas praised the rabbit's virtue, and the brahmin stayed to teach them."

The Buddha said: "The brahmin was Dīpaṃkara Buddha. The rabbit was I myself. The monkey was Śāriputra. The fox was Ānanda. The otter was Maudgalyāyana."

### (22)

A wealthy householder taught a prodigal friend five times, giving him a thousand gold each time, but the friend squandered it all. Once, the householder pointed to a dead rat on a dunghill and said: "A clever person could build a fortune from that dead rat." A beggar boy overheard, took the rat, cooked it with begged seasonings, and sold it for two coins. Through diligent trading, he became wealthy. In gratitude, he fashioned a golden rat on a silver tray and presented it to the householder, who gave him his daughter in marriage.

The Buddha said: "The householder was I myself. The prodigal was Devadatta. The one who prospered from the rat was the monk Panthaka."

### (23)

A lone mother, a hired field-watcher, gave her only meal to a passing monk and placed a lotus flower on top. The monk displayed supernatural powers. The mother wished for a hundred sons like him. After death, she was reborn as a brahmin's daughter (conceived when a deer licked the brahmin's urine). Lotus flowers bloomed wherever the girl stepped. She married a king and bore a hundred eggs. Jealous consorts cast the eggs into the river, but Śakra sealed them. Another king found and raised the hundred sons, who grew into mighty warriors. When they attacked their mother's kingdom, she called from the city wall, and her milk shot into each of their hundred mouths. All recognized her as their mother. Ninety-nine became pratyekabuddhas; one became king and ruled with the ten virtues.

The Buddha said: "The king who remained was I myself."

### (24)

A brahmin and his five hundred disciples hosted a buddha named Tathāgata Kṣipta for seven days. The youngest disciple, assigned to tend the lamp, wrapped his head in white cloth, dipped it in oil, and lit it himself to illuminate the hall. His head burned all night without harm. The Buddha prophesied: "After countless kalpas, you shall become a buddha named Dīpaṃkara, with light radiating from your head and shoulders." The brahmin then asked for a prophecy for himself, and the Buddha said: "When that disciple becomes a buddha, he will give you your prophecy."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The disciple was Dīpaṃkara Buddha. The brahmin was I myself."

### (25)

The bodhisattva, a wealthy householder, bought a turtle at market to save it from being cooked. He released it into the water, vowing: "May all beings in hell and among hungry ghosts gain freedom, just as this turtle has today." The grateful turtle later warned him of a coming flood. The bodhisattva saved a snake, a fox, and a drowning man. The fox found buried gold and gave it to the bodhisattva. But the man the bodhisattva had saved betrayed him, claiming the gold was stolen. The bodhisattva was imprisoned. The snake healed the prince from a snakebite, and the bodhisattva was freed and made minister of state. He then taught the king the Four Noble Truths: impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self. The kingdom was transformed.

The Buddha said: "The householder was I myself. The turtle was Ānanda. The fox was Śāriputra. The snake was Maudgalyāyana. The drowning man was Devadatta."

### (26)

A monk found a louse while meditating and, rather than kill it, placed it on a bone where it could feed for seven days. In this life, a householder who had provided seven days of offerings to the Buddha suddenly lost interest after seven days. The Buddha explained: the merit of saving the louse for seven days resulted in exactly seven days of generosity now. How much greater, then, is the merit of giving to a monk of pure conduct!

The Buddha said these things, and the monks rejoiced.

# Fascicle Four

## Chapter Two: The Perfection of Morality

"What is the perfection of morality? The foolish and violent love to harm living things, steal, commit sexual misconduct, use divisive speech, harsh speech, false speech, and idle chatter, and harbor jealousy, anger, and delusion. The bodhisattva would rather be flayed and dismembered than commit any of these evils. He trusts in the Buddha, the Three Jewels, and extends the fourfold compassion universally."

### (27)

A king who first encouraged Buddhism then tested his subjects by outlawing it. All the hypocrites abandoned the faith. Only one elderly upāsaka refused: "I would rather die than abandon the Three Jewels." The king had him arrested, but secretly sent people to listen. Hearing the upāsaka's steadfast deathbed teaching to his son, the king was so moved that he appointed the upāsaka as chief minister.

The Buddha said: "The king was Maitreya. The upāsaka was I myself."

### (28)

The bodhisattva was an elephant king with six tusks. His junior wife, jealous of a lotus given to the senior wife, died of rage and was reborn as a queen. She feigned illness, demanding the six-tusked elephant's tusks. A hunter, disguised in monk's robes, shot the elephant. Though in terrible pain, the elephant said: "Take the tusks quickly, before anger arises in my mind. To harbor resentment leads to the hells; to practice endurance and kindness leads to heaven." The queen, holding the severed tusks, was struck by lightning and fell into hell.

The Buddha said: "The elephant king was I myself."

### (29)

The bodhisattva was a parrot king who observed that all the king's captive parrots were being fattened and cooked. He taught his flock: "Reduce your eating! Greed is a prison." He himself ate little, grew thin enough to slip through the cage bars, and called to the others: "Abandon greed and you can escape as I have." Such is the bodhisattva's keeping of morality.

### (30)

Prince Dharmadāna was falsely accused of impropriety by a palace concubine he had rejected. Though exiled, he governed his border province so virtuously that the population grew. The concubine and her co-conspirator forged a royal edict to gouge out his eyes. The prince, remembering his father's teaching about keeping faith, had his eyes removed rather than disobey. Blinded, he wandered as a musician until his wife found him. Returning home, the truth emerged. The father-king lamented: "Women's deceit is like poison mixed with rice!" The concubine and her accomplice were punished.

The Buddha explained that in a past life, the concubine had vowed: "I will gouge out your eyes," and the prince had replied: "I will bury you in thorns with hot glue in your wounds." Every deed brings its consequence.

### (31)

During a famine, two elder brothers killed their wives for food. The youngest brother refused, saying: "To kill another to save oneself is not the Buddha's way." He took his wife into the mountains. She later betrayed him with a crippled man, pushing him off a cliff. But a spirit saved him. He was found by merchants and made king of a faraway land, where he ruled with the five precepts and ten virtues.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The king was I myself. The crippled man was Devadatta."

### (32)

A poor porter who understood bird language heard crows plotting to have his employer killed for a precious pearl. Though desperately poor, the bodhisattva refused to kill: "I would rather die poor and virtuous than live wealthy and wicked."

### (33)

The bodhisattva, a poor laborer on a merchant ship, was cast overboard when a sea spirit demanded a sacrifice. But his devotion to the Three Refuges and Five Precepts kept him safe; a fish swallowed the ship instead, and he floated to shore.

### (34)

A poor ascetic who guarded tombs was given valuables by mourners but always returned them to the landowner. The landowner, impressed by his honesty, gave him a wife and household.

### (35)

The bodhisattva traveled with his uncle selling goods. The uncle tried to cheat a widow out of a golden bowl, claiming it was worthless. The bodhisattva, coming after, recognized its true value and traded all his merchandise for it. The uncle, learning of the bowl's worth, died of rage at the riverbank.

The Buddha said: "The bodhisattva kept faith and gained treasure. Devadatta was greedy and deceitful and lost his life."

### (36)

Two brothers went trading. A king offered his daughter first to the younger, then to the more impressive elder. The elder brother refused, saying: "A brother's wife is like a daughter." The princess, enraged, vowed revenge across lifetimes. In a later birth, she became a turtle who lured a monkey (the elder brother reborn) to ride on her back, planning to feed him to her sick husband. The monkey escaped by claiming he had left his liver hanging in a tree.

The Buddha said: "The elder brother was I myself. The male turtle was Devadatta; the female was his wife."

### (37)

Merchant sailors were seduced by beautiful spirit-women on an island. After five years, the bodhisattva discovered they were foxes who devoured men. A divine horse rescued those who did not look back, but those who turned with longing were consumed. The fox-women later followed the men and bewitched a king, who was devoured. In a later life, the same fox-spirit was reborn as a brahmin's beautiful daughter and offered to the Buddha. The World-Honored One said: "The Sixth Heaven's Māra offered me three maidens, and they turned into hags. This bag of filth you offer now is no different."

### (38) The Sūtra of Prince Mupō

Prince Mupō was born with supreme wisdom but kept silent for thirteen years. When soothsayers advised the king to bury the "mute" prince alive, Mupō finally spoke, revealing his past life as a king who suffered in hell for sixty thousand years merely for the disturbance his royal procession caused to the people. "The pleasures of royal life, wife, children, and subjects, cannot follow us into hell. I wish to become a monk." The king was deeply shaken and thereafter ruled with justice.

### (39) The Sūtra of Mīlān

Mīlān the merchant, shipwrecked on a mysterious shore, passed through four jeweled cities, each more beautiful than the last, with four, eight, sixteen, and then thirty-two celestial maidens. Never satisfied, he kept leaving. Beyond the fourth city, he found an iron city where a burning wheel was placed on his head for six hundred million years, the karmic retribution for having once stepped on his mother's head. The Buddha concluded: "I was Mīlān. Insatiable desire leads only to more suffering. Only attaining the path brings true satisfaction."

### (40) The Sūtra of King Mūrdhaja

King Mūrdhaja was a universal monarch who possessed the seven treasures and ruled the four continents. Yet even after celestial rain of jewels, he was not satisfied. He ascended to Śakra's heaven and coveted Śakra's throne. At that evil thought, his supernatural powers vanished and he fell ill. On his deathbed he taught his successors: "Greed is the blade that kills, the foundation that destroys kingdoms."

The World-Honored One said: "Only those who attain stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, arhatship, pratyekabuddha, or supreme perfect awakening can truly abandon the five desires. King Mūrdhaja was I myself."

### (41) The Sūtra of King Puming

King Puming was captured by the tyrant Aqun, who had imprisoned ninety-nine kings. Allowed one final visit to a brahmin teacher, Puming received four verses on impermanence:

> When the kalpa ends, heaven and earth burn,
> Sumeru and the great seas turn to ash.
> Gods and dragons, their merit spent, perish within;
> Even heaven and earth must end; what kingdom lasts?

Puming joyfully shared these verses with Aqun, who was so moved that he released all hundred kings. Later, Aqun was reborn as the murderer Aṅgulimāla, who killed ninety-nine people and wore their fingers as a garland. The Buddha himself converted Aṅgulimāla, who became an arhat and even assisted at a difficult childbirth through the power of truth.

The Buddha told the monks: "King Puming was I myself. Through the four verses on impermanence, I saved a hundred kings in the past and brought Aṅgulimāla to the path in the present."

# Fascicle Five

## Chapter Three: The Perfection of Endurance

"What is the perfection of endurance? The bodhisattva observes that beings, blinded by ignorance, are arrogant and aggressive, always wanting to surpass others, coveting rank and sensory pleasures. Jealousy dwells within, anger without. They cycle through the five destinies, suffering immeasurably. The bodhisattva resolves: 'I would rather endure boiling oil and dismemberment than allow anger to arise toward any being. To endure the unendurable is the source of ten thousand blessings.'"

### (42)

The bodhisattva lived among graves, eating cow dung and drinking urine to sustain himself while practicing endurance. People spat on him, cursed him, and threw stones, but he felt not the slightest anger, only compassion: "How pitiful that these people have never seen the Buddha's teaching!"

### (43) The Story of Śyāma

Śyāma, the supremely filial son, cared for his blind parents in the mountains. A hunting king accidentally shot him with a poisoned arrow. Śyāma's dying concern was only for his parents: "Who will care for my blind mother and father?" The parents, brought to his body, prayed with total sincerity. Śakra descended and revived Śyāma with divine medicine. The king decreed that henceforth all his people should follow the ten virtues and practice Śyāma's filial piety.

The Buddha said: "Śyāma was I myself. Through lifetime after lifetime of supreme filial conduct, I attained the position of God of Gods."

### (44) The Story of Kṣāntivādin

The bodhisattva was a forest-dwelling ascetic named Kṣāntivādin ("Endurance-Speaker"). King Kali, hunting in the forest, asked if he had seen a deer. The ascetic, unwilling either to betray the animal or to lie, simply bowed his head in silence. Enraged, the king drew his sword: "Who are you?" "I am the endurance practitioner." The king cut off his right arm. Again he asked. Again: "I am the endurance practitioner." The king cut off his left hand, then his feet, then his ears, then his nose, blood flowing like a spring.

The Four Heavenly Kings arrived in fury, ready to destroy the king and his nation. But the ascetic said: "This suffering is the result of my own past karma. If I allowed vengeance, the retribution would compound through countless kalpas." The people came and wept: "Your merit protects our land; how could the king do this?" The ascetic said: "Bring my severed arm." Mother's milk flowed from it. "I have a mother's compassion; here is the proof."

His brother, also an ascetic, came and reattached the severed limbs through spiritual power, and the body was restored. The king's wickedness brought drought, famine, and the displeasure of heaven upon his kingdom.

The Buddha said: "Kṣāntivādin was I myself. The brother was Maitreya. The king was the arhat Kauṇḍinya."

### (45)

The bodhisattva was abandoned at birth, adopted, then abandoned again and again by his foster father. Cast into the water, rescued by a sheep whose milk sustained him; thrown among moving carts, picked up by a merchant; left in bamboo in the mountains, found by a woodcutter. Each time the foster father reclaimed him, then plotted to kill him again. When the father sent a letter ordering a blacksmith to throw the boy into the fire, the boy's younger brother (the father's natural son) intercepted the letter and was burned instead. Still the father schemed. He sent the boy to a distant estate with a sealed letter ordering his drowning. On the way, a brahmin's daughter secretly read and rewrote the letter, changing it to a marriage order. The boy married the daughter, and when the father learned, he died of rage. The bodhisattva mourned with perfect filial piety, and the whole nation praised him.

The Buddha said: "The boy was I myself. The wife was Yaśodharā. The foster father was Devadatta."

### (46)

The bodhisattva was a just king whose uncle usurped his throne. Rather than fight, the king fled with his queen. A sea dragon stole the queen. With the help of a monkey king and his army, the bodhisattva built a causeway of stones across the sea, defeated the dragon, and rescued his queen. When the uncle-king died, the bodhisattva was restored to his throne. The queen proved her chastity: "Though I dwelt in the monster's den, I was like a lotus in mud. If my words are true, let the earth crack open." The ground split open, confirming her purity.

The Buddha said: "The king was I myself. The queen was Yaśodharā. The uncle was Devadatta. Śakra was Maitreya."

### (47)

The bodhisattva, as a monkey, rescued a man trapped in a ravine by carrying him out on his back. Exhausted, the monkey rested. The ungrateful man thought: "I could kill and eat this monkey." He smashed a stone on its head. Bleeding, the monkey leapt into a tree and said without anger: "I cannot save you in this life; may you meet the buddhas in future lives and learn the path through their teaching. May no being in the world ever harbor such evil as this man."

The Buddha said: "The monkey was I myself. The man in the ravine was Devadatta."

### (48)

Two bodhisattvas were reborn as dragons. They went ashore as small snakes to play, and a venomous viper attacked them. One dragon wanted to destroy the viper with his supernatural power, but the other counseled endurance in verse:

> The lustful madman has no heart of kindness;
> The jealous one would harm the sage; only silent endurance brings peace.
> The lawless one has no inner compassion;
> The miser harms the generous; only silent endurance brings peace.

The two dragons returned to the sea, manifesting their full power with thunder and rain. The viper was so terrified it could not eat for seven days.

The Buddha said: "The dragon who counseled endurance was I myself. The other dragon was Ānanda. The viper was Devadatta."

### (49)

A king who had become a monk found a hunter, a bird, and a snake trapped in a deep pit. He rescued all three. The hunter repaid his kindness with betrayal: when the monk came for alms, the hunter's wife deliberately delayed until past noon, when a monk cannot eat. The bird stole a queen's pearl and brought it to the monk. The hunter then betrayed the monk to the authorities. The monk, interrogated, refused to reveal that a bird had brought the pearl, knowing it would cause all birds in the kingdom to be killed. He silently endured torture. The snake then bit the prince; the monk healed the prince with the snake's antidote and was freed. The ungrateful hunter and his family were executed.

The Buddha said: "The monk was I myself. The bird was Śāriputra. The snake was Ānanda. The hunter was Devadatta."

### (50)

The bodhisattva was a nāga king named Bandaka, who left the sea to practice austerities under a tree. A snake-charmer found him, pulled out all his teeth, beat him, and carried him in a small box, making him perform in markets across many kingdoms. Though suffering immensely, Bandaka harbored no resentment: "This is my past karma ripening." When the charmer brought him to the kingdom of his own grandfather, his mother and siblings, in the form of birds, found him. The king wished to kill the charmer, but Bandaka said: "This is my past karma. Do not add to the cycle of vengeance." The charmer was paid off and released, but was later killed by bandits. The nāga family returned to the sea.

The Buddha said: "Bandaka was I myself. The charmer was Devadatta."

### (51) The Sūtra of the Sparrow King

The bodhisattva was a sparrow king who saw a tiger choking on a bone. For days the sparrow entered the tiger's mouth to peck out the bone, until its own beak was raw. When the bone was finally freed, the sparrow perched on a tree and preached: "Killing is the greatest evil. Practice compassion." The tiger snarled: "You barely escaped my mouth, and you dare lecture me?" The sparrow, seeing the tiger could not be reformed, flew away in sorrow.

### (52) The Sūtra of Visiting the Naked Land

Two cousins traveled to a land where people wore no clothes. The younger cousin adapted to local customs, prospered in trade, and was loved by the people. The elder cousin insisted on his own ways, offended everyone, and had his goods confiscated. The elder cousin swore eternal vengeance on the younger. The younger cousin (the bodhisattva) vowed: "Through every lifetime, I will encounter the Buddha and serve my cousin with kindness."

The Buddha said: "The younger cousin was I myself. The elder was Devadatta. Lifetime after lifetime, Devadatta persecuted me, and I always forgave him."

### (53) The Sūtra of Six Years of Hunger

A brahmin once drank water from someone's lotus pond without permission and regarded it as theft. He went to the king demanding punishment. The king, overwhelmed by affairs of state, forgot the brahmin in the garden for six days. Because of this negligence, when the bodhisattva finally attained buddhahood, he underwent six years of austerities before his path was complete. The queen who laughed at the starving brahmin was reborn as Yaśodharā, who suffered six years of difficult pregnancy with Rāhula. The prince who locked the brahmin deep in the garden was reborn as Rāhula, who spent six years in the womb.

"Every deed, no matter how small, brings its consequence."

### (54) The Sūtra of the Śākya Clan's Karmic Debt

This extensive narrative tells how King Virūḍhaka attacked and slaughtered the Śākya clan. The Buddha traced the origin to a past life when the Śākyas were fishermen who caught fish from a lake. The neighboring nation that bought the fish with delight were the people of one city now destroyed. Even the Buddha himself suffered a headache during the massacre, because in that past life he had seen a fish head being crushed and said "Good."

Maudgalyāyana offered to save the Śākyas with his supernatural powers, but the Buddha replied: "You can counter physical forces, but you cannot counter karma." He placed one Śākya under his alms-bowl for protection, but even that person died. Seven days after the massacre, fire from heaven consumed Virūḍhaka and his entire army.

# Fascicle Six

## Chapter Four: The Perfection of Diligence

"What is the perfection of diligence? To concentrate on the depths of the path and advance without slackening, whether lying, sitting, walking, or breathing. One's eyes always behold the buddhas' spiritual manifestations before one; one's ears always hear the true teaching. Even if the path ahead holds boiling oil and poisoned blades, the bodhisattva plunges forward joyfully to save beings from their difficulties."

### (55)

In a time when no Buddha existed and no scriptures remained, the bodhisattva desperately sought the dharma. A cruel man said: "I know one verse of the Buddha's teaching, but you must let me drive a needle into every pore of your body." The bodhisattva agreed without hesitation. Needles were driven into his entire body, but he entered a state of painlessness through joy at hearing the dharma. The verse was: "Guard the mouth, control the mind, let the body commit no evil. Remove these three conducts, and one gains the path of the sages."

The Buddha said: "The one who taught the verse was Devadatta. Though Devadatta knew the Buddha's words, he was like a blind man holding a torch: he lit the way for others but could not see himself."

### (56)

The monkey king led his five hundred followers into a royal garden for food. Trapped by the king's soldiers, the monkey king tied vines to a great tree and leapt across the gap to another tree, forming a bridge. But the vine was too short, so his body hung suspended. He ordered his troop to climb over his body. All five hundred escaped, but both his shoulders were torn apart. The human king was so moved that he proclaimed: "A beast's leader kills himself to save his flock. Can I, a human ruler, do less?" He freed the monkey and forbade all harm to monkeys.

### (57)

A deer king named Xiufan, with nine-colored fur, saw his herd surrounded by hunters. He lay down at the escape gap, telling his herd: "Climb on my back and leap to freedom!" All the deer escaped, but the king's body was torn to shreds. The human king wept: "This beast embodies the great compassion of heaven and earth." The deer herd gathered around their wounded king, licking his wounds and bringing herbs. The king decreed an end to all hunting.

### (58)

The bodhisattva was a nine-colored deer king named Xiufan who saved a drowning man. The man promised never to reveal the deer's location. But when the queen dreamed of the nine-colored deer and demanded its hide, the man betrayed the deer for a reward. Instantly, his face was covered with sores. The deer confronted the king: "I saved this man from drowning, and he repaid me with treachery. Better to pull driftwood from the water than to save a faithless man." The king was so moved that he protected all deer. The treacherous queen died of rage. When Śakra tested the king's resolve by filling the land with deer that ate all the crops, the king said: "I would rather lose my crops than break my word." Śakra then removed the deer, and the harvest was tenfold.

### (59)

The bodhisattva was a horse king named Kūya who rescued merchants from an island of seductive female demons. The demons appeared as beautiful women but were actually man-eating ghouls. The horse flew across the sea to warn the merchants: "Those who do not look back will be saved; those who turn with longing will be devoured." Some were saved; the lustful ones perished.

### (60)–(63)

Brief tales of the bodhisattva as a fish king who held up a net with his tail so all the fish could escape; a turtle king who foresaw danger from elephants and evacuated his followers; a parrot king who feigned death to escape his flock's constant noise and achieve solitary meditation; and a pigeon king who starved himself thin enough to escape a cage, calling to the other pigeons: "Abandon greed and you can escape as I have."

### (64) The Sūtra of the Bee King

Two monks, Jingjibian (Diligent Eloquence) and Delezhen (Virtue Joy Right), listened to a buddha's teaching. Jingjibian attained the stage of non-retrogression; Delezhen fell asleep. Jingjibian, using skillful means, transformed into a bee king. He buzzed at Delezhen's eyes to startle him awake. Then the bee landed on a lotus, feeding on nectar, and fell asleep itself, falling into mud. Delezhen spoke verses mocking the bee, and the bee replied with verses comparing the Buddha to sweet nectar and the five destinies to mud:

> The Buddha is like sweet nectar;
> One should hear without satiation.
> Do not be lazy;
> There is no benefit in that for anyone.

Delezhen, thus admonished, attained the acceptance of the non-arising of dharmas.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "Jingjibian was I myself. Delezhen was Maitreya."

### (65) The Buddha's Three Laughs

Walking through the market, the Buddha laughed at an old man weeping over his dead son while selling fish (lacking all compassion for the fish he killed daily), and then laughed at a pig wallowing in urine. The Buddha explained: the old fisherman had once been a universal monarch whose pride led to rebirth as a fish-measurer. The pig had been a neighbor who worshipped demons and indulged in debauchery, while the Buddha's past self had been a devout upāsaka who kept the monthly fasts. The neighbor was now reborn as a dung-beetle.

### (66) The Child Who Understood the Dharma

A seven-year-old cowherder, hearing a monk recite the Prajñāpāramitā, understood its meaning instantly and debated the monk, surpassing him. Killed by a tiger, the child was reborn in a wealthy family. While still in the womb, he caused his mother to recite the Prajñāpāramitā continuously. The family thought she was possessed, but a visiting monk recognized the teaching. Born, the child immediately chanted scripture. At age seven, he corrected errors in the monks' texts and converted eighty-four thousand people to the Great Vehicle.

The Buddha said: "The child was I myself. The monk was Kāśyapa Buddha."

### (67)

The bodhisattva, among five hundred merchants at sea, saw a storm that would destroy them all. He reflected: "The sea spirit hates dead bodies." He cut his own throat. The sea spirit, repelled by the corpse, cast the ship to shore. Śakra revived the bodhisattva with divine medicine and filled the ship with treasures a thousandfold.

### (68)

A boy saved his nation from a greedy king who was hoarding all the people's gold to bribe the king of hell. The boy retrieved a gold coin from his dead father's mouth and, before the king, explained: "The Buddha teaches that merit and demerit follow us like a shadow. Gold placed in a corpse's mouth does not bribe the lord of death. Your Majesty's good deeds from past lives are what earned you this throne." The king released all prisoners and returned the confiscated gold.

### (69)

The bodhisattva was a heavenly king who traveled through the world teaching the Buddha's dharma. Devadatta was a demon king who taught people to follow their desires without consequence. When they met, Devadatta threatened: "Evil is iron; good is gold. Iron cuts gold. Step aside, or I will cut you down." But Devadatta's evil ripened and he fell into the great mountain hell.

### (70)

Two bodhisattva brothers encountered a kingdom terrorized by a venomous dragon. The elder reflected: "The precepts forbid killing, but this dragon devours an entire nation. If we do not act, the suffering will continue for kalpas." One transformed into an elephant, the other into a lion. They fought the dragon, and all three died. The kingdom was saved, and the entire land embraced the Buddha's teaching. Both bodhisattvas were reborn in the fourth heaven.

### (71) Maitreya as a Woman

The bodhisattva, as Śakra, recognized an old spiritual friend reborn as a wealthy merchant's wife. He appeared as a merchant and laughed at five things: a child beating another child (actually the wife's own reborn father); a boy playing a drum made from the skin of his own former body (as an ox); a man killing an ox to cure his father's illness (the father would become an ox, the ox would become human); and a baby scratching its mother's face (the baby was the mother's former rival wife, getting revenge). The bodhisattva explained the hidden karmic connections in each scene, teaching that the world shifts unpredictably between love and hate across lifetimes.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The woman was Maitreya. Śakra was I myself."

### (72) The Woman's Three Wishes

The bodhisattva was born as a woman whose jealous husband forbade her from visiting the Buddha. With the help of a devout neighbor, she went to hear the Buddha teach and made three wishes: to always have a virtuous spouse, to have a pure body, speech, and mind in every life, and to attain omniscient wisdom and save all beings. In her next life, she was reborn in a religious household, married a scholar, and discovered a stone stūpa with a golden Buddha image in her courtyard. She devoted herself to worship, and the entire kingdom followed her example for eighty-four thousand years.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The woman was I myself. The husband was Maitreya."

### (73) The Sūtra of Dīpaṃkara's Prophecy

The bodhisattva was born as a widowed woman, poor but devout, who earned her living selling lamp oil. She supplied oil for an elderly nun's daily lamp offering to the Buddha. When the Buddha prophesied that the old nun would become a buddha in a future age, the woman rushed to the Buddha and asked for a prophecy too. The Buddha said: "A woman's body cannot become a buddha. You must abandon your female form." She climbed to a rooftop and leapt, vowing: "I offer this impure body to hungry beings. May I gain a male body and receive a buddha's prophecy." The Buddha softened the ground beneath her; she landed unharmed and was instantly transformed into a man. The Buddha said: "Your courage is rare in this world. When the nun attains buddhahood, she will give you your prophecy."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The old nun was Dīpaṃkara Buddha. The widow was I myself."

# Fascicle Seven

## Chapter Five: The Perfection of Meditation

### (74)

This section provides a comprehensive exposition of the four dhyānas:

The first dhyāna involves abandoning the five sensory desires and the five hindrances (craving, anger, drowsiness, restlessness, and doubt). Like a person who has escaped ten enemies and rests safely in the mountains.

The second dhyāna: joy arises from within, not through external objects. Evil no longer enters through the senses. Like a mountain spring that produces pure water from within.

The third dhyāna: even the joy of the second dhyāna is released. The mind is calm like an unopened lotus covered by water.

The fourth dhyāna: both good and evil are set aside. The mind is clear like a lapis lazuli gem. Like a potter who has refined clay free of sand and can shape any vessel, or a goldsmith who has smelted pure gold and can craft anything at will. The practitioner gains the power to fly, walk on water, divide his body, pass through walls, see and hear everything, know all minds, and recall all past lives. From the four dhyānas, one can attain any level of awakening, from stream-entry to supreme perfect enlightenment.

### (75)

Detailed instructions on the practice of the four dhyānas: the three aids of effort, mindfulness, and contemplation; the progressive elimination of the ten evils and cultivation of the five goods (reflection, mindfulness, love, joy, one-pointedness).

### (76)

Twenty or more contemplations that lead to one-pointed meditation: seeing the old, the sick, the dead, decaying corpses, the terrors of hell, remembering the Buddha's magnificent form, reflecting on the dharma's meaning, and contemplating the body's four elements, impurity, and the sixteen stages of breath awareness.

Also, the cultivation of the four immeasurables (kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity), compared to a mother's care for her child.

### (77)

The prince (the future Buddha) encountered four sights during his outings from the palace: an old man, a sick man, a corpse, and suffering worms turned up by a plow. Each time, he returned to the palace in deep contemplation. His father, the king, tried to distract him with more pleasures and entertainers.

### (78)

The prince's departure from the palace: At seventeen he had mastered all learning. At nineteen, surrounded by fifteen hundred courtesans, the gods put them all to sleep. The prince gazed upon the sleeping women and saw their bodies as hollow wooden mannequins, their beautiful finery cast about, saliva running from their mouths, their hair disheveled. He saw through their outward beauty to bones, organs, excrement, and impurity. He called his groom Chandaka: "Saddle Kaṇṭhaka!" A hundred billion Śakras, four hundred billion heavenly kings, and gods, nāgas, and spirits escorted him in silence, opening the palace gates without a sound.

### (79)

Under the bodhi tree, the prince resolved: "Even if my muscles and bones wither, I will not rise from this seat until I attain buddhahood." In the first watch, he gained knowledge of all his past lives. In the second, he gained knowledge of the births and deaths of all beings. In the third, the three poisons were extinguished. At dawn, the Buddha-path was complete. He went to the pool of Nāga King Mucalinda, who covered him with seven heads for seven days of rain. When the rain stopped, the nāga appeared as a young brahmin and asked: "Are you free from cold, heat, hunger, and thirst?" The Buddha replied with a teaching on supreme happiness: freedom from desire, cessation of the three poisons, and the bliss of nirvāṇa. The nāga was the first animal to take refuge in the Buddha.

### (80)

Five hundred ox-carts passed the Buddha while he was in deep meditation, but he neither heard nor saw them. A man named Paukkeṣa was astonished and took refuge. The Buddha related how he had similarly been in meditation when lightning struck and killed four oxen and two plowmen nearby, yet he perceived nothing. Paukkeṣa offered a thousand gold-embroidered garments and invited the Buddha and the sangha to visit his land.

### (81)

The bodhisattva Sadāprarudita ("Ever-Weeping") wandered crying because he had been born in a time without buddhas and without scriptures. A heavenly spirit instructed him: "Go east, abandoning all concepts of form, sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness. A bodhisattva named Dharmodgata dwells in the city of Gandhāvatī. He teaches the Prajñāpāramitā." Sadāprarudita set out eastward. Buddhas appeared from above and praised him. He entered a meditation in which all buddhas of the ten directions spoke to him: "All buddhas of past, present, and future sought the path just as you do. You will certainly become a buddha."

### (82)

Two ascetics had an argument. One inadvertently stepped on the other's head while reciting scriptures at night. The offended one cursed: "May your head split into seven pieces at sunrise!" The other, to prevent this, stopped the sun from rising for five days, plunging the kingdom into darkness. The king and his people traveled to the mountain and mediated peace. The curser's head was smeared with mud (to fulfill the curse harmlessly), the sun was released, and the two ascetics taught the king the five precepts and ten virtues.

The Buddha said: "The one who stopped the sun was I myself. The one who cursed was Maitreya."

# Fascicle Eight

## Chapter Six: The Perfection of Wisdom

### (83)

A lengthy sūtra in which the Buddha tells Śāriputra the story of his groom Chandaka's past merit. The bodhisattva, as Prince Surā, saved a heavenly maiden from being sacrificed by his grandfather's brahmin priests. He debated the brahmins, refuting their claim that killing animals and using divine blood could build a stairway to heaven. He taught the six perfections as the true path to heaven. The prince married the heavenly maiden. Later, accompanied by two ascetics and a monkey (who had been transformed by Śakra), the prince journeyed through seven mountains to the heavenly maiden's celestial home, encountering five hundred pratyekabuddhas along the way. He lived with his bride for seven years in heaven, then returned to rule his grandfather's kingdom with the five precepts, six perfections, and ten virtues.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "The prince was I myself. The monkey was Chandaka, who in this life wished to be reborn as my horse. One of the ascetics was Maudgalyāyana. The other was Chandaka."

### (84) The Sūtra of King Zhēluó

The bodhisattva was a prince born extremely ugly, but with extraordinary wisdom and the voice of a lion. His mother-in-law, protecting him, forbade his wife Princess Moonlight from ever seeing him by daylight. When the princess finally saw him and fled in horror, the prince said: "Her departure brings peace to the world." He pursued her to her father's kingdom, disguising himself as a potter, a dyer, a groom, and a cook, producing works of extraordinary beauty at each trade. When seven rival kings all received (forged) letters offering Moonlight as a bride, war loomed. The prince climbed a high platform and addressed all seven armies with a voice like a lion's roar, teaching the Buddha's dharma. All seven armies were struck unconscious by his power. He then arranged for the king's seven other daughters to marry the seven kings, bringing lasting peace to nine nations.

The Buddha explained the karmic origin: in a past life, when the bodhisattva had been a farmer and saw his wife offering food to a pratyekabuddha, he first felt anger (resulting in his ugly birth) but then repented and shared in the merit (resulting in his later transformation to beauty).

### (85) The Bodhisattva Escapes the Ghost Wife

At age sixteen, the bodhisattva realized that the Buddha's teaching was the only true path. Fleeing an arranged marriage, he ran away. But at every stop, a beautiful woman appeared, seduced him, and kept him for years. After five, ten, and then more years, he would awaken and flee again, only to be trapped once more. Finally he realized: "These are all the same ghost-wife from lifetime after lifetime!" He aroused the four contemplations of impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self. The ghost-wife vanished instantly. Buddhas appeared before him, and he attained liberation.

### (86) The Sūtra of the Youth's Prophecy

The bodhisattva was born as a young brahmin named Megha ("儒童," Youth). His teacher sent him out to teach. At a contest of learning, Megha surpassed five hundred brahmins. The prize was a woman and five hundred silver coins, but Megha refused: "I seek the path of desirelessness." The woman, admiring his virtue, followed him on foot across many countries. Meanwhile, Megha heard that Dīpaṃkara Buddha was approaching. He bought five lotuses from the flower-girl (the same woman) and scattered them over the Buddha. The flowers hung suspended in the air. He spread his deerskin cloak and then his own hair over muddy ground for the Buddha to walk upon. Dīpaṃkara prophesied: "After ninety-one kalpas, you shall become a buddha named Śākyamuni, the Able Sage."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Megha was I myself. The flower-selling woman was Yaśodharā."

### (87) The Sūtra of King Moṭiao

In the distant past, the holy king Moṭiao was a universal monarch who ruled the four continents. Whenever he saw a white hair on his head (a sign of impermanence), he abdicated in favor of his son and became a monk. His descendants continued this practice for 1,084 generations. When the dharma began to decline, Moṭiao was reborn again as the universal monarch named Nán (South), who restored the true teaching. He commanded all people, from queen to commoner, to keep the eight precepts and six monthly fasts. Under his rule, doors were never locked, no one lied, and all venom and disease vanished. Śakra himself came to invite King Nán to visit the heavens. The king asked first to see hell and the realm of hungry ghosts, then ascended. In the heaven of the thirty-three gods, King Nán was honored equally with Śakra. But the king wished to return to earth: "The heavenly seat is borrowed; I must return to teach my descendants the dharma."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "King Nán was I myself. His descendants continued to abdicate and become monks for 1,084 generations."

### (88) The Sūtra of Aranemi

The monks discussed the brevity of life. The Buddha taught through the story of Aranemi, a wealthy man in a time when people lived 84,000 years. Even then, Aranemi saw that life was impermanent and became a monk. He taught his disciples with vivid similes: human life is like dew on grass at dawn, like bubbles on rain-struck water, like a flash of lightning, like oil sizzling on a hot pan, like thread on a loom growing shorter, like a cow being led to slaughter (one step closer to death with each step), like water flowing downhill without a moment's pause.

The Buddha said: "Aranemi was I myself. Even in a lifetime of a hundred years, subtract fifty for sleeping, ten for infancy, ten for illness, and twenty for worries and tasks: one has perhaps ten years of true happiness."

### (89) The Sūtra of King Mirror-Face

Monks visited a lecture hall of non-Buddhist brahmins, who were arguing fiercely about whose teaching was correct. The Buddha told the parable of King Mirror-Face, who brought many blind men to touch an elephant. Each touched a different part: the one who held the foot said the elephant was like a lacquer tube; the one who held the tail said it was like a broom; the one who held the belly said it was like a drum; the one who held the ear said it was like a winnowing basket. Each insisted he was right, and they quarreled bitterly.

King Mirror-Face laughed and said in verse:

> Now for these sightless ones
> Who argue and call themselves right,
> Seeing one part, they deny the rest;
> Seated before one elephant, they quarrel.

"Those who study only fragmentary teachings without seeing the vast ocean of the Buddha's dharma are just like these blind men." Thereupon all the people of the kingdom began to recite the Buddha's scriptures.

The Buddha said: "King Mirror-Face was I myself. The blind men were the arguing brahmins."

### (90) The Sūtra of King Chāwei (Discerning the Subtle)

King Chāwei, understanding that consciousness arises from the four elements (earth, water, fire, wind), wished to prove to his skeptical ministers that the soul transmigrates. He devised an experiment: he plied a cobbler with wine until the man passed out, then placed him in the royal bed dressed in royal robes. When the cobbler awoke, he was told he was the king and was made to govern. After days of miserable incompetence, the king drugged him again and returned him to his shop.

When the cobbler woke in his hovel, he told the king: "I dreamed I was king, and it was more painful than anything! Being king is no pleasure at all." The king told his ministers: "This man, in one body, cannot even distinguish what happened to him yesterday. How much less can we perceive the transformations of the soul across lifetimes?"

The Buddha said: "The king was I myself."

### (91) The Sūtra of Brahmā the Emperor

The Buddha told the monks: "In a past life, I practiced the four immeasurable minds for seven years. After death, I was reborn as Brahmā, lord of heaven. Through seven cycles of cosmic creation and destruction, I ascended to the fifteenth Pure Heaven. I then served as Śakra thirty-six times, then as a universal monarch ruling the four continents with the seven treasures and a thousand sons. I governed with the five teachings: kindness (not killing), generosity (not stealing), purity (not committing sexual misconduct), honesty (not lying), and filial sobriety (not drinking). In that era, prisons were never built, punishments never administered, wind and rain came in season, crops were abundant, and all people kept to the path. After death, all were reborn in heaven; none fell into the three evil destinies."

The Buddha said: "Through countless lifetimes of practicing the four immeasurables, I rose to be Brahmā above and universal monarch below, and ultimately attained buddhahood, the solitary and supreme one of the three realms."

The monks heard the sūtra and rejoiced, bowed to the Buddha, and departed. Such is the bodhisattva's practice of wisdom in the perfection that knows no limit.
