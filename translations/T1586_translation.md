# 唯識三十論頌
## Thirty Verses on Consciousness-Only

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1586

Composed by Vasubandhu (世親). Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Xuanzang (玄奘).

---

Bodhisattvas such as Dharmapāla composed the *Establishment of Consciousness-Only* based on these thirty verses. Here their significance is briefly indicated. In these thirty verses, the first twenty-four verses clarify the characteristics of consciousness-only; the next one verse clarifies the nature of consciousness-only; the last five verses clarify the practice and stages of consciousness-only. Within the twenty-four verses, the first one-and-a-half verses briefly explain the characteristics of consciousness-only; the next twenty-two-and-a-half verses broadly explain the characteristics of consciousness-only. An outsider asks: "If there is only consciousness, why do the world and the sacred teachings speak of self and dharmas?" The verse is raised in reply:

> [1] By the provisional designation of self and dharmas,
> Various appearances manifest.
> Those depend on what consciousness transforms.
> These transforming consciousnesses are only three:
>
> [2] Namely, the maturation, the deliberation,
> And the consciousness that cognizes objects.

The next twenty-two-and-a-half verses broadly explain the characteristics of consciousness-only. Since the preceding verse text briefly indicates three transforming consciousnesses, now their characteristics are explained in detail. The characteristics of the first transforming consciousness: what are they? The verse says:

> The first is the ālaya-vijñāna,
> The maturation of all seeds.
>
> [3] Unknowable are its appropriation,
> Its location, and its cognition. It is always associated with contact,
> Attention, feeling, perception, and volition.
> It is associated only with the feeling of equanimity.
>
> [4] It is unobscured and indeterminate;
> Contact and the rest are likewise.
> It constantly turns like a torrent;
> It is abandoned at the stage of the arhat.

The first transforming consciousness has been explained. The characteristics of the second transforming consciousness: what are they? The verse says:

> [5] Next, the second transformation
> Is the consciousness called manas.
> It turns dependent on that and takes that as its object;
> Its nature and characteristic is deliberation.
>
> [6] It is always accompanied by four afflictions:
> Self-delusion, self-view,
> Self-pride, and self-love,
> Together with contact and the rest.
>
> [7] It belongs to the obscured and indeterminate.
> It follows rebirth and bondage.
> In the state of the arhat, the cessation attainment,
> And the supramundane path, it does not exist.

The second transforming consciousness has been thus explained. The characteristics of the third transforming consciousness: what are they? The verse says:

> [8] Next, the third transformation
> Is differentiated into six kinds.
> Cognizing objects is its nature and characteristic;
> It is wholesome, unwholesome, or neither.
>
> [9] Its mental factors are the universal,
> The particular, the wholesome, the afflictions,
> The secondary afflictions, and the indeterminate;
> All are associated with three feelings.
>
> [10] First, the universals are contact and the rest.
> Next, the particulars are desire,
> Conviction, mindfulness, concentration, and wisdom;
> Their objects differ.
>
> [11] The wholesome are faith, shame, embarrassment,
> The three roots of non-craving and the rest,
> Diligence, tranquility, non-heedlessness,
> Equanimity in action, and non-harmfulness.
>
> [12] The afflictions are craving, aversion,
> Delusion, pride, doubt, and wrong view.
> The secondary afflictions are anger, resentment,
> Concealment, vexation, jealousy, miserliness,
>
> [13] Deceit, flattery, harmfulness, arrogance,
> Shamelessness, lack of embarrassment,
> Restlessness, torpor,
> Non-faith, laziness,
>
> [14] Heedlessness, forgetfulness,
> Distraction, and incorrect knowing.
> The indeterminate are regret and sleep,
> Investigation and analysis, each pair being twofold.

The mental factors associated with the six consciousnesses have been explained. How should one understand the conditions for their manifestation? The verse says:

> [15] Depending on the root consciousness,
> The five consciousnesses manifest according to conditions,
> Either together or not together,
> Like waves depending on water.
>
> [16] The mental consciousness always manifests,
> Except in the heaven of non-perception,
> The two attainments without mind,
> Deep sleep, and fainting.

The characteristics of the three transforming consciousnesses have been broadly distinguished as the basis for the two aspects transformed by each. How should one understand that the self and dharmas provisionally designated based on what consciousness transforms are not separately real, so that all is consciousness-only? The verse says:

> [17] All these transformations of consciousness
> Are the discriminating and the discriminated.
> Since thereby both are non-existent,
> Therefore all is consciousness-only.

If there is only consciousness and there are no external conditions at all, from what do the various discriminations arise? The verse says:

> [18] Because the all-seeds consciousness
> Transforms in such and such ways,
> Through the power of mutual influence,
> Such and such discriminations arise.

Although there is internal consciousness yet no external conditions, by what means does the cycle of birth and death of sentient beings continue? The verse says:

> [19] Because of the latent impressions of karma,
> Together with the latent impressions of the twofold grasping,
> When the prior maturation is exhausted,
> A further maturation arises.

If there is only consciousness, why does the World-Honored One speak of three natures throughout the sūtras? One should know that the three natures also do not depart from consciousness. Why? The verse says:

> [20] Through such and such imagining,
> Various things are imagined.
> This imagined nature
> Is entirely without existence.
>
> [21] The dependent nature
> Is produced by discriminating conditions.
> The perfected nature, in relation to that,
> Is always free from the former nature.
>
> [22] Therefore the perfected and the dependent
> Are neither different nor non-different,
> Like the characteristic of impermanence and the rest:
> If one does not see one, one does not see the other.

If there are three natures, how can the World-Honored One say that all dharmas are without self-nature? The verse says:

> [23] Based precisely on these three natures,
> The three naturelessnesses are established.
> Therefore the Buddha declared with hidden intent
> That all dharmas are without nature.
>
> [24] The first is the naturelessness of characteristics.
> The next is the naturelessness of self-origination.
> The last, by being free from the former
> Imagined nature of self and dharmas,
>
> [25] Is the supreme meaning of all dharmas,
> Which is also suchness.
> Because it is always such in its nature,
> It is the true nature of consciousness-only.

The last five verses clarify the practice and stages of consciousness-only. The treatise states: "Regarding these established nature and characteristics of consciousness-only, who, through how many stages, and how does one enter into realization?" One who possesses the two kinds of Mahāyāna lineage. First, the innate lineage, meaning the uncontaminated dharma-cause naturally obtained, adhering to the root consciousness since beginningless time. Second, the lineage developed through practice, meaning the habitual development acquired through hearing, after hearing the Dharma of the equal flow of the dharma-realm. Only by possessing both lineages can one enter into realization. What are the five stages? First, the stage of accumulation, meaning the cultivation of Mahāyāna factors conducive to liberation, deeply believing and understanding based on the nature and characteristics of consciousness. Its characteristics: what are they? The verse says:

> [26] As long as one has not yet aroused the consciousness
> That seeks to abide in the nature of consciousness-only,
> The latent tendencies of the twofold grasping
> Have not yet been subdued and eliminated.

Second, the stage of intensified effort, meaning the cultivation of Mahāyāna factors conducive to penetration. In the stage of intensified effort, one can gradually subdue and eliminate the apprehended and the apprehender. Its characteristics: what are they?

> [27] To posit even a little something before oneself
> As the nature of consciousness-only:
> Because there is still something apprehended,
> One does not truly abide in consciousness-only.

Third, the stage of penetration, meaning the path of seeing where bodhisattvas dwell. In the stage of penetration, one penetrates in accord with reality. Its characteristics: what are they?

> [28] When, regarding the object,
> Wisdom has nothing at all to apprehend,
> Then one abides in consciousness-only,
> Because one is free from the marks of the twofold grasping.

Fourth, the stage of cultivation, meaning the path of cultivation where bodhisattvas dwell. In the stage of cultivation, one sees principle in accord with reality and cultivates again and again. Its characteristics: what are they?

> [29] Without apprehension, inconceivable:
> This is supramundane wisdom.
> By abandoning the twofold coarseness,
> One directly realizes the transformation of the basis.

Fifth, the ultimate stage, meaning abiding in unsurpassed, perfect bodhi, emerging from obstructions in complete radiance, able to transform sentient beings to the end of the future. Its characteristics: what are they?

> [30] This is the realm without outflows,
> Inconceivable, wholesome, and permanent.
> The body of bliss and liberation:
> The Dharma[-body] of the great Sage.
