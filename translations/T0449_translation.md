# 佛說藥師如來本願經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Original Vow of the Medicine Master Tathāgata

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0449

Translated from the Chinese. Sui dynasty, translated by Dharmagupta (達摩笈多)

---

*Preface by the monk Huìjǔ:*

*The Sūtra of the Medicine Master Tathāgata's Original Vow is the essential method for invoking blessings and eliminating disasters. Mañjuśrī, through the power of loving-kindness and compassion, requested the teaching of this honored name. The Tathāgata, with a mind devoted to benefiting beings, extensively set forth his merits and deeds. The twelve great vows reveal the vast reach of his causal practices, and the adornments of the seven treasures display the pure perfection of his fruition-virtue. Recollecting and reciting his name releases all suffering; prayer and offerings fulfill all wishes. For the sick who seek rescue, those at death's door may be restored to life; for kings who ward off disaster, calamity is transformed into fortune. Truly this is the divine talisman that dispels a hundred misfortunes, and the marvelous art that eliminates the nine untimely deaths.*

*In the era of Emperor Xiaowu of the Song dynasty, the śramaṇa Huìjiǎn of Lùyě Temple had previously translated this sūtra, and it circulated in the world; however, the Sanskrit and Chinese did not harmonize well, and the phrasing was mixed and rough, causing those who recited it much confusion. Huìjǔ had studied Sanskrit writing from an early age and regularly consulted the palm-leaf texts, hoping to encounter this sūtra and verify its errors. In the seventeenth year of Kāihuáng he first obtained one copy, but fearing omissions and mistakes, did not dare translate it immediately. By the eleventh year of Dàyè he obtained two more copies; comparing them against each other, he then settled the text. Together with Tripiṭaka Dharma Master Dharmagupta and the Sui dynasty translation śramaṇas Fǎxíng, Míngzé, Chángshùn, Hǎiyù, and others, at the Translation Hall of the Shànglín Garden south of the Luò River in the Eastern Capital, they retranslated this text. Deeply aware of the former errors and guarding against future mistakes, every word that left their mouths was verified three times before being written. The transmission of the profound meaning was accomplished with no major error. On the eighth day of the twelfth month of that year, the translation and collation were completed, forming one fascicle.*

*It is our wish that the profound meaning of this sūtra be understood by all, that the name of that buddha be heard everywhere, that the twelve yakṣa generals, mindful of the Buddha's grace, protect the nation, and that the seven thousand retainers, sustained by the power of the sūtra, benefit the people, so that the imperial reign may endure long and all beings may be at peace. Thus this preface is offered for posterity.*

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Bhagavat was traveling among the people. He came to the land of Vaiśālī and stayed beneath the Music Tree, together with a great assembly of eight thousand monks, thirty-six thousand bodhisattvas, kings, ministers, brahmins, householders, devas, nāgas, asuras, gandharvas, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, and others. A great assembly surrounded him, and he taught the Dharma before them.

At that time the Dharma-prince Mañjuśrī, through the Buddha's awesome spiritual power, rose from his seat, bared one shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms with a bow toward the Bhagavat, and said: "World-Honored One! May you please teach the names of the buddhas and the supreme great vows they made in the past. When sentient beings hear this, their karmic obstructions will be eliminated. This is for the sake of gathering in sentient beings of the future when the true Dharma is declining."

At that time the Bhagavat praised the youth Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! Mañjuśrī, you of great compassion, giving rise to immeasurable compassion, urge me to teach. This is to benefit beings bound by various karmic obstructions, and to bring benefit and peace to all devas and humans. Mañjuśrī! You should remember well and listen to what I teach."

The youth Mañjuśrī, delighting to hear the Buddha's teaching, said: "Just so, World-Honored One!"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "To the east, beyond buddha-lands as numerous as ten Ganges River sands, there is a world called Pure Lapis Lazuli. In that land there is a buddha named Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened, Accomplished in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Tamer of Humans, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One.

"Mañjuśrī! When the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata was formerly practicing the bodhisattva-path, he made twelve great vows. What are the twelve?

"The first great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining right awakening as a buddha, have my own body blaze with radiance, illuminating immeasurable, countless, boundless worlds. Adorned with the thirty-two marks of a great man and the eighty minor excellences, may my body be such, and may I cause all sentient beings to be like me, without any difference.'

"The second great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, have a body like lapis lazuli, pure within and without, without blemish or stain, with radiance vast, awesome virtue blazing, the body well-established, adorned with a net of flames surpassing the sun and moon. If there are sentient beings born in the darkness between worlds, or humans in darkness and night who cannot tell directions, by my light may they go wherever they wish and carry out their tasks.'

"The third great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, through boundless and limitless wise skillful means, cause immeasurable sentient beings to enjoy inexhaustible provisions. May not a single person lack for anything.'

"The fourth great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, if there are sentient beings who walk wrong paths, establish them all on the path of awakening. If any walk the śrāvaka-path or the pratyekabuddha-path, may I establish them all in the Great Vehicle.'

"The fifth great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, if there are sentient beings who cultivate the holy life within my Dharma, all these sentient beings, immeasurable and boundless, may they all obtain unbreached precepts, complete in the three collections of precepts, with not one who violates the precepts and falls into evil destinies.'

"The sixth great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, if there are sentient beings whose bodies are inferior, whose faculties are incomplete, who are ugly, dull-witted, deaf, blind, lame, hunchbacked, leprous, or insane, or who have any other kinds of bodily illness, upon hearing my name, may they all gain complete faculties and full-formed bodies.'

"The seventh great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, if there are sentient beings afflicted by many illnesses, without protection, without refuge, without dwelling, separated from all the necessities of life and medicine, without relatives, poor and pitiable: if such a person hears my name, may all their afflictions be eliminated, free from all pain and distress, until they reach supreme awakening.'

"The eighth great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, if there are women who are oppressed by the hundred evils of womanhood and in their aversion wish to renounce the female form: upon hearing my name, may they be transformed from a female body to the characteristics of a man, until they reach supreme awakening.'

"The ninth great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, cause all sentient beings to be liberated from Māra's net. If any have fallen into the dense forest of various wrong views, may I establish them all in right views and gradually guide them with the bodhisattva's path.'

"The tenth great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, if there are sentient beings bound, whipped, and beaten by various royal laws, imprisoned and sentenced to death, afflicted by immeasurable disasters, burned by grief, with body and mind suffering: may all these sentient beings, through the power of my merit, be liberated from all suffering and distress.'

"The eleventh great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, if there are sentient beings whose bodies are burned by the fire of hunger, and who for the sake of food commit various evil deeds: may I first satisfy their bodies with the most exquisite food of wonderful color, fragrance, and flavor, and afterward establish them in ultimate peace and joy through the flavor of the Dharma.'

"The twelfth great vow: 'May I in a future life, at the time of attaining awakening, if there are sentient beings who are poor and without clothing, afflicted day and night by cold, heat, mosquitoes, and gadflies: may I bestow upon them clothing for their use, of various colors according to their preferences, and also all jeweled adornments, flower garlands, perfumes, drums, music, and entertainments. Whatever all sentient beings need, may they all be fully satisfied.'

"These twelve great vows were made by that World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Awakened, when he was formerly practicing the bodhisattva-path.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! All the vows of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata and the merit-adornments of that buddha-land could not be fully described even if one were to speak for an entire kalpa. That buddha-land is entirely pure, with no female form, free from all evil desires, and without the sound of suffering in any evil destiny. The ground is of lapis lazuli. The city gates, walls, doors, windows, halls, chambers, pillars, beams, brackets, and surrounding nets are all made of the seven treasures. Like the Land of Ultimate Bliss, the Pure Lapis Lazuli realm is adorned in this way.

"In that land there are two bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, the first named Sūryaprabha (Sunlight) and the second named Candraprabha (Moonlight). Among the immeasurable and countless bodhisattvas, these two are the foremost leaders, upholding the treasury of the true Dharma of that World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata.

"Therefore, Mañjuśrī, faithful good men and good women should aspire to be reborn in that buddha-land."

The World-Honored One further told the youth Mañjuśrī: "Mañjuśrī! There are sentient beings who do not recognize good from evil, who are extremely greedy and insatiable, who do not know generosity or the fruits of giving, who are foolish and without wisdom, deficient in the root of faith, who hoard wealth and grudge sharing it. When they see a beggar approaching, their minds are displeased, as if their own flesh were being cut. Moreover, there are immeasurable miserly beings who will not use their wealth even for themselves, let alone share it with parents, wife, children, servants, workers, or other beggars. Such beings, at the end of their human lives, are reborn in the realm of hungry ghosts or as animals. Yet because in their former human life they once heard the name of the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, whether in the ghost realm or the animal realm, if that Tathāgata's name briefly appears before them, at that very moment of mindfulness, their life in that realm ends and they are reborn in the human world. They gain the knowledge of past lives, fear the evil destinies, take no pleasure in sensual enjoyment, delight in giving with wisdom, and praise those who give. Whatever they possess, they can completely relinquish. Gradually they can even give their head, eyes, hands, feet, blood, flesh, and body to those who seek them, let alone other material goods.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! There are sentient beings who, though they follow the Tathāgata and receive the precepts and training rules, nevertheless violate the precepts, violate right conduct, or violate right views. Some receive the training rules and guard the precepts, yet do not seek broad learning and cannot understand the profound meaning of the sūtras taught by the Tathāgata. Others, though learned, become arrogant with false pride, affirming themselves and denying others, slandering the true Dharma and becoming allies of Māra. These foolish people and immeasurable other hundreds of thousands of millions of beings who walk wrong paths should fall into the hells. Such beings should cycle endlessly through the hells, but because they have heard the name of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, through that buddha's awesome power, the Tathāgata's name briefly appears before them in the hells. They immediately relinquish that life and are reborn in the human world. With right views and diligence, a pure and wholesome mind, they are able to leave the household life and, within the Tathāgata's teaching, go forth to study the path, gradually cultivating the bodhisattva practices.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! There are sentient beings who, out of jealousy, only praise themselves and do not praise others. Because of self-aggrandizement and belittling others, they spend immeasurable thousands of years in the three evil destinies suffering various torments. After immeasurable thousands of years, when their lives there end, they are reborn in the animal realm as cattle, horses, camels, or donkeys, whipped and beaten, afflicted by hunger and thirst, carrying heavy burdens on the road. If reborn in the human world, they always occupy the lowest stations, serving as slaves, driven and worked by others. Yet if in their former human life they heard the name of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, through this wholesome root, they are liberated from all suffering. Their faculties become sharp, they become intelligent and broadly learned, always seeking the wholesome root. They find good companions who always accompany them. They can sever Māra's snare, break the shell of ignorance, drain the river of afflictions, and be liberated from all the suffering of birth, old age, sickness, death, grief, and distress.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! There are sentient beings who delight in discord and mutual litigation. These beings generate evil minds against each other, and with body, speech, and mind constantly commit various evils, seeking to harm one another by every means. They invoke forest spirits, tree spirits, mountain spirits, tomb spirits, and various other spirits, slaughter animals, take their blood and flesh, and make offerings to yakṣas, rākṣasas, and other blood-eating beings. They write their enemies' names, make effigies of them, employ various poisons, sorcery, voodoo, corpse-raising spells, and other methods, seeking to take their lives or destroy their bodies. Yet because they have heard the name of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, none of these evil deeds can cause harm. They all come to generate minds of loving-kindness, beneficial minds, and minds free from resentment, and each finds joy and mutual acceptance.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If among the fourfold assembly of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen, or other faithful good men and good women, any receive and observe the eight precepts for one year, or for three months, and with this wholesome root aspire to be reborn before the Tathāgata Amitābha in the Western Land of Ultimate Bliss: because they have heard the name of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, at the end of their lives, eight bodhisattvas will come through the air, show them the path, and they will be spontaneously born within variously colored padma-lotuses in that realm. Or if such a person wishes to be reborn in the heavens, they will immediately be reborn there, and the wholesome roots of their past will never be exhausted. They will never again be reborn in the evil destinies. When their heavenly life ends, they will be reborn in the human world as a wheel-turning king, sovereign over the four continents, establishing immeasurable hundreds of thousands of millions of beings in the ten wholesome paths of action. Or they may be reborn in a great kṣatriya clan, a great brahmin clan, or a great householder family, with gold, silver, grain, and silk filling their storehouses, with perfect form, mastery, and a complete retinue, brave and strong as a great hero. If a woman hears this Tathāgata's name and receives and upholds it with a devoted mind, that woman will forever leave behind the female body."

At that time the youth Mañjuśrī addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! In the future, I will by various skillful means cause faithful good men and good women to hear the name of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, even awakening their ears with the Buddha's name during sleep. If they receive, uphold, read, and recite this sūtra, or expound and explain it for others, whether they copy it themselves or have others copy it, whether they take the sūtra-scroll and wrap it in five-colored pure silk, sweep a clean place to set it down, and make offerings with various flowers, various incenses, perfumes, garlands, jeweled banners, pennants, and canopies: at that time the Four Great Heavenly Kings with their retinues, and hundreds of thousands of millions of other devas, will all come to that place. Wherever this sūtra-scroll circulates, if anyone recites and upholds this sūtra, because they have heard the name of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata and his supreme great vows of the past, know that in that place there will be no untimely death, and they will not have their spirit seized by any ghost. If it has already been seized, it will be restored as before."

The Buddha said: "Just so, just so! Mañjuśrī, it is as you have said. Mañjuśrī! If faithful good men or good women wish to make offerings to that Tathāgata, they should create an image of the Tathāgata, observe the eight precepts for seven days and seven nights, eat pure food, scatter various flowers in a clean place, burn various incenses, adorn the place with various silks, banners, and pennants, bathe and purify themselves, and put on new clean garments. They should generate minds free from filth and turbidity, free from anger and malice, and generate minds of benefit toward all sentient beings, minds of loving-kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity. They should play drums and music, sing praises, and circumambulate the Buddha's image to the right. They should be mindful of that Tathāgata's great vows of the past and explain this sūtra. Whatever they contemplate and wish for, all their desires will be fully satisfied: those who seek long life will obtain long life; those who seek merit will obtain merit; those who seek mastery will obtain mastery; those who seek sons or daughters will obtain sons or daughters.

"Or if someone suddenly has an evil dream, or sees various evil signs, or strange birds gather, or at their dwelling a hundred uncanny things appear: if this person makes offerings and pays reverence to that Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata with various fine provisions, all evil dreams, evil signs, and inauspicious events will vanish. Whether there be fear of flood, fire, weapons, poison, precipices, evil elephants, lions, tigers, wolves, bears, venomous snakes, scorpions, centipedes, or millipedes: if one is mindful of and makes offerings to that Tathāgata, all fears will be resolved. If another country invades or bandits rebel: one should also be mindful of that Tathāgata and pay reverence with respect.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī! If there are faithful good men or good women who, throughout their lives, take the three refuges and do not serve other devas, or who uphold the five precepts, the ten precepts, the bodhisattva's one hundred and four precepts, or the monk's two hundred and fifty precepts, or the nun's five hundred precepts, and among the precepts they have received, they violate some and fear falling into evil destinies: if they can make offerings to the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, they will certainly not receive retribution in the three evil destinies. If a woman is about to give birth and is enduring extreme pain, if she can recite the name and make offerings to the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, she will swiftly be relieved. The child she bears will have a complete body, handsome form, and all who see it will rejoice. The child will have sharp faculties, intelligence, and peace, with little illness, and no non-human being will seize its spirit."

At that time the World-Honored One told Ānanda the Elder of Wisdom: "Ānanda! Do you believe the merits I have praised of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata? Do you have much doubt regarding such profoundly deep realms of all the buddha-tathāgatas?"

Ānanda the Elder of Wisdom addressed the Buddha: "Great Virtue, World-Honored One! I have no further doubt about the Dharma taught by the Tathāgata. Why? Because all the actions of body, speech, and mind of the tathāgatas are without exception pure. World-Honored One! This sun and moon, though possessing such great supernatural powers and awesome might, could be made to fall. Mount Sumeru, the king of mountains, could be moved. But the words of the buddhas are without any discrepancy. Great Virtue, World-Honored One! Yet there are sentient beings whose root of faith is insufficient, who upon hearing of the realm of the buddha-tathāgatas, think: 'How can merely reciting that Tathāgata's name produce such great merit?' They do not believe or accept it and even generate slander. Such people, through the long night, will be without benefit and will fall into the evil destinies."

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! If that Tathāgata's name enters a person's ear, it is impossible that this person will fall into the evil destinies. Ānanda! The realm of the buddhas is truly hard to believe. That you now believe and accept it, know that this is entirely through the Tathāgata's awesome power; it is not within the capacity of any śrāvaka or pratyekabuddha to believe and accept, with the sole exception of the one-birth-supplement bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. Ānanda! A human body is hard to obtain, and faith, reverence, and respect for the Three Treasures is also hard to obtain. To hear that Tathāgata's name is even more difficult than these. Ānanda! The World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata's immeasurable bodhisattva-practices, immeasurable skillful means, and immeasurable vast vows: even if I were to spend one kalpa or more describing that Tathāgata's bodhisattva-practices and vows, even to the end of all kalpas, the former practices and supreme great vows of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata would not be exhausted."

At that time in the assembly there was a bodhisattva-mahāsattva named Trāṇamukta (Rescue and Liberation). He rose from his seat, bared one shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, and with palms joined and bowing, addressed the Bhagavat: "Great Virtue, World-Honored One! In the future, there will be sentient beings afflicted with grave illness, long sick and emaciated, unable to eat, parched with thirst, their lips and throat dry, the signs of death appearing before them, their eyes unable to see, surrounded by weeping parents, relatives, friends, and acquaintances. The body of this person lies in its place while Yama's messengers lead the person's consciousness and bring it before King Yama, the Lord of the Dharma. Behind this person there is a spirit born at the same time who records all of their deeds, whether sinful or meritorious, and presents it all to King Yama, the Lord of the Dharma. King Yama then examines and questions the person, calculates their deeds, and assigns them in accordance with their good and evil.

"If someone can take refuge on behalf of this sick person in the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata and make offerings according to the Dharma, then the person's consciousness can return. When this person's consciousness has returned, it is as if awakening from a dream; they remember and know everything. Whether after seven days, twenty-one days, thirty-five days, or forty-nine days, when the consciousness has returned, they fully recall all the retribution of their good and evil karma. Because they have witnessed it themselves, they will not commit evil deeds even at the cost of their lives. Therefore, faithful good men and good women should make offerings to the Medicine Master Tathāgata."

At that time Ānanda the Elder of Wisdom asked the Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta: "Good man! How should one make offerings to the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata?"

The Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta said: "Great Virtue Ānanda! If there is a sick person who wishes to be freed from grave illness, on behalf of this person, for seven days and seven nights, one should receive and observe the eight precepts, provide food, drink, and various provisions as one is able, and make offerings to the sangha of monks. Six times day and night one should bow and make offerings to the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, read and recite this sūtra forty-nine times, light forty-nine lamps, create seven images of that Tathāgata, and before each image place seven lamps, each lamp as large as a cart-wheel. The light should continue without interruption for forty-nine days. One should also make five-colored banners forty-nine feet in length.

"Furthermore, Great Virtue Ānanda! If a consecrated kṣatriya king or the like faces the arising of disasters, such as: an epidemic among the people, invasion by another country, rebellion within one's own domain, strange transformations of the stars, an eclipse of the sun or moon, untimely wind and rain, or drought when rain is overdue: at that time the consecrated kṣatriya king should generate a mind of loving compassion toward all sentient beings, pardon those who are imprisoned, and according to the offering method described above, make offerings to the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata. Through this wholesome root, and through the power of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata's original great vows, the king's domain will immediately become peaceful and secure. Wind and rain will come in season, crops will ripen, the land will be prosperous, and all the sentient beings in that realm will be free from illness and at peace, filled with joy. Within that realm there will be no yakṣas, rākṣasas, piśācas, or other evil ghosts and spirits disturbing the beings. All evil signs will immediately disappear. That consecrated kṣatriya king's lifespan, physical strength, freedom from illness, and mastery will all be increased."

At that time Ānanda the Elder of Wisdom asked the Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta: "Good man! How can a life that has already reached its end be further extended?"

The Bodhisattva Trāṇamukta said: "Ānanda! Have you not heard the Tathāgata teach about the nine kinds of untimely death? For this reason he teaches mantras, medicines, and skillful means.

"There are sentient beings whose illness is not grave, yet who have no doctor or medicine, and no one to care for them. Or if they do encounter a doctor, the treatment is wrong, and they die when they should not. This is the first untimely death.

"The second untimely death is being killed by the king's law.

"The third untimely death is going hunting, being reckless, indulging in drink and lust without moderation, and having one's spirit seized by non-human beings.

"The fourth untimely death is being burned by fire.

"The fifth untimely death is being drowned in water.

"The sixth untimely death is entering among lions, tigers, leopards, and other fierce beasts.

"The seventh untimely death is being weakened by hunger and thirst, unable to obtain food and drink, and dying from this.

"The eighth untimely death is being harmed by sorcery, poisonous drugs, corpse-raising ghosts, and the like.

"The ninth untimely death is throwing oneself off a cliff. These are what the Tathāgata calls, in summary, the nine great untimely deaths. Beyond these there are immeasurably many other untimely deaths."

At that time in the assembly there were twelve yakṣa generals, all present at the gathering, namely:

Kumbhīra, Vajra, Mihira, Andīra, Anila, Saṇḍila, Indra, Pajra, Mahura, Cindāla, Catura, and Vikarāla.

These twelve yakṣa generals each have seven thousand yakṣas as their retinue. They all spoke in unison, addressing the World-Honored One: "We today, through the Buddha's awesome power, have heard the name of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata. We no longer have any fear of the evil destinies. We now, all together and of one mind, from this day until the end of our lives, take refuge in the Buddha, take refuge in the Dharma, and take refuge in the Sangha. We shall bear the burden of all sentient beings, acting for their benefit and bringing them peace and happiness. In whatever village, city, or settlement, or in secluded places, wherever this sūtra circulates, and wherever the name of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata is upheld and approached with offerings, we and our retinues shall protect such people and cause them to be liberated from all suffering and difficulty. Whatever they wish for, we shall cause to be fully satisfied."

At that time the World-Honored One praised the yakṣa generals: "Excellent, excellent! Great yakṣa generals! If you are mindful of the grace of the World-Honored Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata, you should always be mindful of benefiting all sentient beings."

At that time Ānanda the Elder of Wisdom addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is this sūtra called? How should we uphold it?"

The Buddha said: "Ānanda! This Dharma-gate is called 'The Supreme Great Vows Made in the Past by the Medicine Master Lapis Lazuli Light Tathāgata.' Uphold it thus. It is also called 'The Self-Vow of the Twelve Yakṣa Generals.' Uphold it thus. It is also called 'The Purification of All Karmic Obstructions.' Uphold it thus."

When the Bhagavat had spoken these words, all the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas, the great śrāvakas, the kings, ministers, brahmins, householders, and the entire great assembly of asuras, gandharvas, and others, hearing what the Buddha had taught, all rejoiced and put it into practice.
