# 佛說金剛場莊嚴般若波羅蜜多教中一分

## A Portion of the Teaching on the Vajra-Ground Adorned Prajñāpāramitā

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0886

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護).

---

At that time, all the tathāgatas of the ten directions again gathered together and entreated: "We request the World-Honored One, the great secret lord of vajra-yoga, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, to teach the Dharma-gate of the fundamental natureless prajñāpāramitā."

At that time, the World-Honored One, the great secret lord of vajra-yoga, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, having heard the tathāgatas' entreaty, entered the samādhi of all-buddha-realm great wisdom vajra. Having emerged from this samādhi, in an instant he abided in the true wisdom nature of all tathāgatas and of the Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī. Within this nature, he further entered the samādhi of all tathāgatas' heart-light, wondrous, firm-natured wisdom. Within this samādhi, from the hearts of all tathāgatas, there appeared the light of all buddha-realms, illuminating the entire dharma-realm universally, reaching all sentient beings without exception, causing the wisdom of all sentient beings to become purified. From the bodhicitta of all sentient beings, there arose the Dharma of attraction, summoning, and abhiṣeka, just as is well performed and properly established within the samādhi of Vajrasattva, bringing all to fulfillment. Furthermore, from the Secret Great Being of Jewel-Light, Banner, and Smile, there arose the nature of supreme sovereign mastery, abiding within the awakening-nature of Vajrasattva's samādhi. Furthermore, from the Secret Great Being of Dharma-Benefit, Cause, and Speech, there arose the nature of firm-hearted purity of action, abiding in the treasury of the supreme dharma-nature of the intrinsically pure samādhi of Padmasattva. From this, various supreme works were established, and through the Dharma of guarding mindfulness, sentient beings were brought to maturity. From the body, speech, and mind of all tathāgatas, extending to the entirety of the sentient-being realm, all were universally brought to maturity and properly established. Then all returned to the heart of the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, and entered into the wisdom-nature of all tathāgatas to abide therein. At that time, the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, spoke this verse:

> "Great indeed is the undefiled cause: right wisdom,
> Born from all tathāgatas;
> Right wisdom, stainless and inconceivable,
> Is the origin of the nature of all beings.
> Great indeed are the inconceivable dharmas;
> All buddha-activities are entirely pure.
> When one discriminates between nature and naturelessness,
> This mind turns in both domains."

At that time, the Bodhisattva Mahāsattva Mañjuśrī, within the assembly, manifested supernatural power and from the Tathāgata's heart projected sixteen great bodhisattva forms, surrounding the World-Honored One Tathāgata Mahāvairocana. On the left and right of all the tathāgatas, he manifested the radiance of a great sun maṇḍala, and within that radiance he further manifested a wondrous moon maṇḍala. The Bodhisattva Mañjuśrī sat in the center and spoke thus: "We request the World-Honored One, the great secret lord of vajra-yoga, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, to proclaim the true Dharma-phrases of the teaching on the vajra-ground adorned prajñāpāramitā."

At that time, the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, praised the Bodhisattva Mahāsattva Mañjuśrī, saying: "Excellent, excellent! Mañjuśrī! You are able to ask the Buddha about such true meaning. Listen carefully, attend well, and I shall now proclaim it."

Then the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, abiding in the sovereign nature of all tathāgatas, proclaimed the great vidyā-phrases of the dharmas of the vajra-ground adorned prajñāpāramitā, namely the four foundations of mindfulness and so forth.

"The four foundations of mindfulness: internally and externally within the body, one practices contemplation with right mindfulness and clear knowing; internally and externally within feelings, one practices contemplation with right mindfulness and clear knowing; internally and externally within the mind, one practices contemplation with right mindfulness and clear knowing; internally and externally within dharmas, one practices contemplation with right mindfulness and clear knowing. When these four foundations of mindfulness, the contemplation of body, feelings, mind, and dharmas, are practiced with right mindfulness and clear knowing, all becomes purified; one abides in the practice of non-attachment, and in the world there is nothing whatsoever, while body, speech, and mind constantly abide in the Dharma-gate of the equality of prajñāpāramitā. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality of the four foundations of mindfulness. The great vidyā is:

"*Aṃ gaṃ oṃ catvāri smṛty-upasthānāni.*

"Furthermore, the four right efforts: regarding unwholesome dharmas not yet arisen, one arouses vigor to prevent their arising, with collected mind and right mindfulness; regarding unwholesome dharmas already arisen, one arouses vigor to sever them entirely, with collected mind and right mindfulness; regarding wholesome dharmas not yet arisen, one arouses vigor to cause them to arise and grow, with collected mind and right mindfulness; regarding wholesome dharmas already arisen, one arouses vigor to cause them to increase and grow, to be firm, vast, and cultivated to perfection, with collected mind and right mindfulness. These four right efforts mean that in all dharmas there is no severance yet there is severance; this is the awakened understanding of the truth of ultimate meaning. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the vast gate of right effort. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ samyak-prahāṇa sarva-tathāgata-samādhiṣṭhāya a ra hūṃ.*

"Furthermore, the four bases of supernatural power: the base of supernatural power endowed with the samādhi of desire and the practice of severance; the base of supernatural power endowed with the samādhi of vigor and the practice of severance; the base of supernatural power endowed with the samādhi of mind and the practice of severance; the base of supernatural power endowed with the samādhi of wisdom and the practice of severance. These four bases of supernatural power are born from the illusion-like nature of all dharmas. This is the body-gate of the prajñāpāramitā of ultimate meaning that turns the wheel of all dharmas. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equal wisdom of the perfection of supernatural power. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ ṛddhi-pāda-pāramitā-rāvaya ra hūṃ hūṃ.*

"Furthermore, the five faculties: the faculty of faith, the faculty of vigor, the faculty of mindfulness, the faculty of concentration, and the faculty of wisdom. These five faculties are entirely free from conceptual elaboration, turning in the equality-nature of all dharmas. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality of faculties. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ indriya-vaṃ dha-prājñe-jñāna-mate-pagata-ya a oṃ.*

"Furthermore, the five powers: the power of faith, the power of vigor, the power of mindfulness, the power of concentration, and the power of wisdom. These five powers are the equal practice of all dharmas. In all powers, they are entirely free from conceptual elaboration, originally pure. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the increase of powers. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ sarva-tathāgata-bala a hūṃ hūṃ va.*

"Furthermore, the seven factors of awakening: the factor of mindfulness, the factor of investigation of dharmas, the factor of vigor, the factor of joy, the factor of tranquility, the factor of concentration, and the factor of equanimity. These seven dharmas abide in the nature of equality. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the arising of the characteristic of equality. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ sapta-bodhy-aṅga-samata-supratiṣṭhite dhruṃ vaṃ.*

"Furthermore, the eightfold noble path: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, and right knowledge. These eight dharmas are born from the great bodhicitta; the right path is inexhaustible, and one rightly awakens to the wisdom of omniscience. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality of the eightfold noble path. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ ārya-aṣṭāṅga-mārga-maṇḍa.*

"Furthermore, the three samādhis: emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness. What is the samādhi of emptiness? If the characteristic of emptiness, the characteristic of nothingness, the characteristic of no intrinsic nature, and all dharmas share a single characteristic, and that characteristic is unborn, this is called the samādhi of emptiness. What is the samādhi of signlessness? If in all dharmas there is no characteristic of action, no expectation, no differentiation, one abides in the characteristic of quiescence, free from characteristics, and the mind rests on a single object, this is called the samādhi of signlessness. What is the samādhi of wishlessness? If in the characteristics of suffering, impermanence, and so forth, in all dharmas there is no nature of awakened understanding, no arising of action, and whether one knows in this way or knows differently from this way, there is no seeking, no practice, and no expectation, this is called the samādhi of wishlessness. These three samādhis, and indeed all natures and all samādhi-gates, enter into the gate of prajñāpāramitā. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ kha-samā sarva-dharma khaṃ.*

"Furthermore, the eight liberations: liberation through contemplating forms while possessing form; liberation through contemplating external forms while having no perception of internal form; the liberation of purity; then, going beyond all perceptions of form, not giving rise to perceptions of resistance and not making the perception of self, one contemplates boundless space, which is the liberation of the base of boundless space; then, going beyond the base of boundless space, one contemplates boundless consciousness, which is the liberation of the base of boundless consciousness; then, going beyond the base of boundless consciousness, one contemplates the base of nothingness, which is the liberation of the base of nothingness; then, going beyond the base of nothingness, one contemplates the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, which is the liberation of the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception; then, going beyond the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, one rightly contemplates in the attainment of the cessation of perception and feeling, which is the liberation of the cessation of perception and feeling. These eight dharmas are the characteristic of all dharmas free from conceptual elaboration, neither defiled nor pure, neither concentrated nor scattered, neither attainment nor non-attainment. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of freedom from appropriate and inappropriate domains. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ samanta-bhadri sarva-bhāva-viśuddhe dhaṃ thaṃ khaṃ.*

"Furthermore, the nine successive absorptions: having abandoned desires and unwholesome dharmas, with initial and sustained application, with the joy and bliss born of seclusion, one enters the first dhyāna; with the subsiding of initial and sustained application, with inner clarity and one-pointed mind, without initial or sustained application, with joy and bliss born of concentration, one enters the second dhyāna; having abandoned attachment to joy, abiding in equanimity and mindful practice, the body experiences wondrous bliss, with bliss free from joy, one enters the third dhyāna; with both suffering and pleasure severed, as previously arisen pleasant and unpleasant feelings both subside, with neither suffering nor pleasure, with equanimity and purity of mindfulness, one enters the fourth dhyāna; going beyond all perceptions of form, not giving rise to perceptions of resistance, not attending to various perceptions, one contemplates boundless space, which is the absorption of the base of boundless space; going beyond the base of boundless space, one contemplates boundless consciousness, which is the absorption of the base of boundless consciousness; going beyond the base of boundless consciousness, one contemplates the base of nothingness, which is the absorption of the base of nothingness; going beyond the base of nothingness, one contemplates the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, which is the absorption of the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception; and the attainment of the cessation of perception and feeling. These nine dharmas, whether with nature or without nature, are entirely free from discrimination; this is the equal nature of the non-dual awakened understanding of all dharmas. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality of all liberations. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ ananta-vimokṣa-mukha śūṃ.*

"Furthermore, the six recollections: recollection of the Buddha, recollection of the Dharma, recollection of the Sangha, recollection of generosity, recollection of the precepts, and recollection of the heavens. These six dharmas and all dharmas share a single recollection, which is the emptiness of ultimate meaning; all dharmas share a single characteristic, which is signlessness, and even this characteristic is left behind. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the samādhi-gate of the equality-gate. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ sarvānusmṛti-vālaṃ dhaṃ saṃpanna-dhi śi hūṃ hūṃ hūṃ.*

"Furthermore, the ten stages of the bodhisattva: the stage of arousing the aspiration, the stage of the spiritual youth, the stage of first undertaking practice, the stage of cultivation, the stage of noble birth, the stage of complete accordance, the stage of right mind, the stage of non-retrogression, the stage of receiving abhiṣeka, and the stage of one more birth. These stages of the bodhisattva are not stages; leaving behind all stages, one enters the ultimate meaning and abides in the nature of a single stage. A single stage is no stage. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of no-stages. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ sarva-avasthāna-vigate hūṃ.*

"Furthermore, the ten sovereign masteries of the bodhisattva: mastery over lifespan, mastery over karma, mastery over provisions, mastery over resolve, mastery over vows, mastery over supernatural powers, mastery over birth, mastery over powers, mastery over dharmas, and mastery over mind. These ten sovereign masteries are not sovereign mastery, because their intrinsic nature is absent; one thereby attains the supreme quiescent nature of the most excellent purity of well-abiding among all sovereign masteries. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of all sovereign masteries. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ sarva-vaśite traṃ dhruṃ hūṃ.*

"Furthermore, the ten perfections: the perfection of generosity, the perfection of morality, the perfection of patience, the perfection of vigor, the perfection of meditation, the perfection of wisdom, the perfection of skillful means, the perfection of vows, the perfection of powers, and the perfection of knowledge. These ten perfections encompass all perfections. They are the peerless perfection, the perfection free from conceptual elaboration, the perfection of true meaning, the perfection of the true meaning of all dharmas, the vajra perfection, the jewel perfection, the dharma perfection, the karma perfection, and the perfection of the equality of all dharmas. All such dharmas are firmly established, in accordance with the corresponding Dharma-gate. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality of all perfections. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ sarva-pāramitārtha-paripūraṇi oṃ prāṃ hūṃ dhaṃ.*

"Furthermore, the ten grounds: the ground of joy, the ground of freedom from defilement, the ground of radiance, the ground of blazing wisdom, the ground of invincibility, the ground of manifest presence, the ground of far-reaching, the ground of immovability, the ground of excellent wisdom, and the ground of Dharma-cloud. These ten grounds are one with all grounds in a single meaning, which is the meaning of wisdom. This meaning of wisdom is also nonexistent, without characteristics, without words, without sound, without name, indescribable, originally pure, manifesting stainless light, abiding in true meaning. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of ground-purity. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ bhūmi-viśuddhani sarvārtha-paripūraṇi vaṃ pūṃ.*

"Furthermore, the four brahma-practices: loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity. These four dharmas constitute abiding in brahma-practice. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality-nature of all dharmas. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ brahma-vihāra-adhiṣṭhāna gaṃ kṣaṃ ndruṃ kṣa hūṃ ṇa druṃ.*

"Furthermore, the ten powers: the power of wisdom regarding what is and is not the case, the power of wisdom regarding karmic retribution, the power of wisdom regarding various realms, the power of wisdom that discerns the faculties of self and others, the power of wisdom regarding various resolves, the power of wisdom regarding the cessation of suffering and the path, the power of wisdom regarding defilement and purity, the power of wisdom regarding the recollection of former abodes, the power of wisdom of the five supernatural knowledges, and the power of wisdom regarding the exhaustion of outflows. These ten powers are the nature of equal and excellent arising from all tathāgatas. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality of the ten powers. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ daśa-bala-mati svāḥ.*

"Furthermore, the four fearlessnesses: the Tathāgata, Worthy of Offerings, Perfectly Enlightened, clearly knows these dharmas and attains perfect enlightenment, possessing omniscience. In the great assembly of all worlds, devas, humans, Māra, Brahmā, śramaṇas, brāhmaṇas, and others, he is fearless. With impartial contemplation, he attains the practice of great peace, the practice of courage, turns the wheel of the true Dharma, and does not turn the wheel of any worldly dharma. Furthermore, the Tathāgata clearly knows these dharmas and has completely exhausted all outflows; he is fearless. Furthermore, the Tathāgata teaches that defiled dharmas obstruct the noble path; in this impartial teaching he is fearless. Furthermore, the Tathāgata teaches that cultivating right practices can exhaust all suffering; in this impartial teaching he is fearless. These four dharmas are all taught as truth. The true meaning within the true is fearlessness, equally abiding in the illusion-like nature of all dharmas, free from the nature of self and what belongs to self. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality of fearlessness. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ vaiśāradya-samata-prāpta a ā āṃ aḥ.*

"Furthermore, the four analytical knowledges: analytical knowledge of words, analytical knowledge of dharmas, analytical knowledge of meaning, and analytical knowledge of eloquence. These four dharmas abide in the nature of equality. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the illusion-like nature of all dharmas and unimpeded analytical knowledge. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ pratisaṃvid na svā hūṃ.*

"Furthermore, the eighteen unique qualities: the Tathāgata's body is without error, his speech is without error, his mind is without error, he has no unsettled mind, no differentiated perception, no unexamined equanimity, no diminution of aspiration, no diminution of vigor, no diminution of mindfulness, no diminution of wisdom, no diminution of liberation, no diminution of the knowledge and vision of liberation, the turning of unobstructed and unattached volitional knowledge and vision regarding the past, the turning of unobstructed and unattached volitional knowledge and vision regarding the future, the turning of unobstructed and unattached volitional knowledge and vision regarding the present, bodily actions that follow wisdom, verbal actions that follow wisdom, and mental actions that follow wisdom. These eighteen dharmas: the Tathāgata, at all times and in all places, abides equally and well in all of them, entering into the great wisdom-purity of the emptiness-nature of all dharmas. This is called the prajñāpāramitā teaching of the equality of the unique qualities. The great vidyā is:

"*Oṃ āveṇika-buddha-dharma-samādhi-avira hūṃ khaṃ.*"

At that time, all the tathāgatas who had come from the ten directions, having heard the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, proclaim the secret dharma-phrases, together praised him in verse:

> "Excellent! The unsurpassed bodhicitta,
> The supreme sovereign mastery of all tathāgatas;
> Excellent! The supreme dharma of all buddhas,
> Which is the renowned samādhi;
> Excellent! The vast wisdom of the vajra,
> Well-abiding in the firm vajra-mind;
> Excellent! The immeasurable gates of all dharmas,
> Well-abiding in the purity of ultimate meaning.
> Well spoken are these secret phrases,
> From which all tathāgatas are born."

At that time, the World-Honored One, the Tathāgata Mahāvairocana, with joy paid homage to all tathāgatas, and also praised them in verse:

> "Homage and prostration to all tathāgatas,
> The great dharma-body of self and other's purity;
> I first pay homage with secret words,
> Great-powered vajra firm wisdom.
> All dharmas are ultimately quiescent in nature,
> Abiding in the gate of the vajra dharma-nature;
> Having fully attained the great Mahāyāna phrases,
> Homage to the great compassion, great sovereign mastery,
> Originally pure, great fearlessness,
> Supreme, most excellent, and foremost.
> With faith I pay respect to all the sugatas,
> The words of the supreme true Dharma;
> With the Dharma-gates I have proclaimed,
> I universally make offerings to all buddhas."
