# 思惟略要法
## Essential Methods of Contemplation

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0617

Translated from the Chinese. Later Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什)

---

Illnesses of the body are of three kinds: wind, cold, and heat. Their afflictions are slight. Illnesses of the mind are of three kinds, and their calamities are deep and grave: they bring suffering through kalpas beyond counting. Only the Buddha, the supreme physician, can formulate the remedy. The practitioner has been long afflicted by these illnesses through countless worlds. Now, first undertaking this practice, one should make the mind resolute and focused, sparing neither body nor life. It is like a man entering a den of bandits: if his mind is not resolute, he cannot defeat the bandits. Defeating the army of disordered thoughts is just the same. As the Buddha has said: "Even if flesh and blood are exhausted, so long as skin and sinew remain, do not abandon diligence." It is like a man whose body and clothes are on fire: he wants only to put out the fire and has no other thought. Escaping the suffering of afflictions is just the same. One should endure the pains of illness and hardship, hunger and thirst, cold and heat, and anger, and should avoid noisy crowds, taking pleasure in dwelling in solitude and stillness. Why? Because the din of many voices disorders concentration, like entering a thicket of thorns.

In seeking the first dhyāna, one should first practice the various contemplations: either the four immeasurables, or the contemplation of impurity, or the contemplation of dependent origination, or the buddha-contemplation samādhi, or ānāpāna. After that, entering the first dhyāna becomes easy. If a person of sharp faculties directly seeks dhyāna, they should contemplate the various dangers and faults of the five desires, seeing them as a pit of fire or a privy. One should regard the ground of the first dhyāna as a cool, refreshing pool or a lofty observation terrace. When the five hindrances are removed, one attains the first dhyāna. Just as when the ṛṣi Pāri was first learning dhyāna and saw a dead woman along the road, swollen, rotting, and stinking, he focused his mind on the image and contemplated his own body as no different from hers, and through concentrated meditation in a quiet place attained the first dhyāna. When the Buddha was sitting in meditation by the Ganges River, a bhikṣu of limited learning asked the Buddha: "How does one attain the Way?" The Buddha said: "Do not grasp what belongs to another." The bhikṣu understood the emptiness of dharmas and immediately attained the first fruit of the path. A bhikṣu of much learning, wondering why he himself had attained nothing, asked the Buddha. The Buddha said: "Take a small stone from the Ganges and wash it with water from a small stream." The bhikṣu did as instructed. The Buddha asked: "Which has more water, the Ganges or the stream?" He answered: "They cannot be compared." The Buddha said: "If you do not wash with the finger, though the water be vast, it is of no use." The practitioner should be diligent, using the finger of wisdom and concentration to wash away the mind's defilements. Without doing so, one cannot be freed from the Dharma.

## Contemplation of the Four Immeasurables

Those who seek the Buddha's Way should first practice the four immeasurable minds. When the mind is immeasurable, the merit too is immeasurable. Among all sentient beings there are three categories: first, parents, relatives, and good friends; second, enemies, adversaries, and those who always wish to cause harm; third, neutral persons, neither intimate nor hostile. The practitioner should regard all three kinds of people with a mind of loving-kindness, as if they were one's own kin: the elderly as parents, those of middle age as siblings, the young as one's own children. One should constantly cultivate such a mind of loving-kindness. People become enemies through unwholesome conditions; when those unwholesome conditions are exhausted, they may become kin again. Enmity and intimacy have no fixed nature. Why? Because in this life they are enemies, but in the next they may become kin. The mind of anger and hatred brings the loss of great benefit, destroys the merit of patience, undermines the practice of loving-kindness, and obstructs the causes for the Buddha's Way. Therefore one should not harbor anger or hatred. One should regard enemies as one regards one's own kin. Why? Because enemies enable one to gain the causes for the Buddha's Way. If enemies did no evil to me, I would have nothing to endure, and they would be my good spiritual friends, enabling me to perfect the pāramitā of patience. Having gained this loving-kindness even toward enemies, one should extend loving-kindness and affection to sentient beings in all ten directions, pervading the entire world. Seeing all sentient beings subject to impermanence and change, afflicted by old age, illness, death, and manifold sufferings, with not a single creature at peace, from wriggling insects to creeping worms, one gives rise to compassion. When one sees sentient beings gaining happiness in this life and the next, the happiness of heavenly birth, and the happiness of the noble path, one gives rise to sympathetic joy. When one sees sentient beings with neither suffering nor happiness, without worry or gladness, one governs oneself with wisdom. Simply taking sentient beings as one's object, one gives rise to equanimity. This is called the four immeasurable minds. Because one's loving-kindness pervades sentient beings in the ten directions, they are called "immeasurable." The practitioner should constantly cultivate this mind.

If at any time a mind of anger arises, like a snake or fire upon one's body, one should immediately cast it off. If the mind races and scatters into the five desires, or is covered by the five hindrances, one should use the power of diligence and wisdom to forcibly rein it in and bring it back. Cultivating the mind of loving-kindness, always wishing that all beings may attain the happiness of buddhahood, and practicing it without cease, one comes to leave behind the five desires, remove the five hindrances, and enter the first dhyāna. The sign of attaining the first dhyāna is joy and happiness pervading the body, and the arising of joyful delight amid wholesome dharmas, seeing various subtle and wondrous forms. This is called the first gate of the Buddha's Way: the meritorious conditions of dhyāna concentration. Having attained these four immeasurable minds, being patient toward all sentient beings without anger, this is called patience toward sentient beings. Having attained patience toward sentient beings, one easily attains patience toward dharmas. Patience toward dharmas means that all dharmas are unborn, unceasing, and ultimately empty in nature. Being able to accept and trust this Dharma of patience is called patience of non-arising. One receives the prediction of supreme, perfect enlightenment (anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi) and shall certainly become a buddha. The practitioner should cultivate in this way.

## Contemplation of Impurity

Desire, anger, and delusion are the great diseases of sentient beings. Attachment to the body and clinging to desires give rise to anger. Being deluded by inverted perceptions is delusion. Covered by delusion, one becomes attached to superficial appearances both internally and externally. Through long habit, the stained mind is difficult to dispel. To remove desire, one should contemplate impurity. Since anger arises from external conditions, once desire is checked, anger too can be controlled. It is like splitting bamboo: the first node is the hardest. Having subdued desire, the other two afflictions submit of themselves. The contemplation of impurity means knowing that this body arises from impurity, dwells in the womb, and emerges from impurity. Beneath the thin skin, there is nothing but impurity. Externally, the four great elements transform food and drink to fill the interior. Contemplating with a focused mind, from the feet to the hair, from the hair to the feet, within this skin-bag there is nothing pure: brain membrane, mucus, saliva, pus, blood, feces, urine, and so on. Briefly stated, there are thirty-six impure substances; explained in full, they are beyond counting. It is like a farmer opening a granary, distinguishing each kind of hemp, rice, beans, and wheat. The practitioner, with the mind's eye, opens this bodily granary and sees various foul substances: liver, lungs, intestines, stomach, worms stirring and feeding, and the nine openings constantly discharging impurity without cease. The eyes weep tears, the ears exude wax, the nose drips mucus, the mouth ejects saliva and vomit, and the two lower orifices constantly discharge feces and urine. Though one covers the body with clothing and food, in truth it is a walking privy. The body being like this, how could it be pure? One should further contemplate this body, which is merely called "a person" by convention. The four great elements have been combined, like a house: the spine is the ridgepole, the ribs are the rafters, the bones are the pillars, the skin is the four walls, and the flesh is the plaster. It is an empty and false assemblage. Where is the "person" in it? Fragile and unreal, an illusion that lasts but a moment. The foot bones are topped by the shin bones, the shin bones by the thigh bones, the thigh bones by the spine, and the spine by the skull: bones stacked upon bones, precarious as piled eggs. Contemplating this body closely, there is nothing worth grasping. In this way, the mind gives rise to revulsion. Constantly mindful of the thirty-six impure substances, distinguishing them as they truly are, the inner body is like this and the outer body is no different. If the mind does not settle, restrain it and bring it back to the focused contemplation of impurity. The sign that the mind has settled is a softening of the body, gradually attaining ease and happiness. If the mind still does not settle, one should reprove it: "Through countless kalpas I have always followed you, passing through the three evil destinies, suffering ten thousand kinds of bitter torment. From this day forward, I shall subdue you; you shall follow me." Then yoke the mind again and bring it to accomplishment. If one becomes extremely revolted by the body, one may advance to the white-bone contemplation, which also allows entry into the first dhyāna. If the practitioner aspires to the Great Vehicle, at life's end they will be reborn before any buddha they wish. Failing that, they will certainly reach Tuṣita Heaven and see Maitreya.

## Contemplation of White Bones

The white-bone contemplation means removing all skin, blood, sinew, and flesh until nothing remains, the bones stacked upon each other, white as shell or snow, and shining with the same luster. If one does not see this, consider the parable of the leper. A physician told his household: "If you make him drink blood that looks the same as milk, his disease can be cured." Everything in the house was made white. Blood was placed in a silver cup, and the patient was told: "Drink this milk, and the disease will certainly be cured." The leper said: "This is blood." They replied: "White things cure you. Do you not see that everything in the house is white? Because of your karmic burden you see blood. Simply concentrate on perceiving milk, and do not call it blood." After seven days of this, it actually changed into milk. How much less should one fail to see that which truly is white? Having seen the bone-person, one should further contemplate: within the bone-person, the mind arises and ceases in continuous succession, like a thread stringing beads. Whatever the mind's eye sees, including the contemplation of external bodies, is all the same. If the mind wishes to settle, be diligent and do not relent, like drilling for fire when smoke appears, or digging a well and finding moisture: success will come before long. If the mind is calm and settled, then with eyes open or closed, the luminous bones are clearly visible. It is like still water that reflects one's face; when turbid, nothing is clear; when dried up, nothing is seen.

## Contemplation of the Buddha in Samādhi

The Buddha is the king of the Dharma, able to cause people to attain all manner of wholesome dharmas. Therefore those who practice dhyāna should first contemplate the Buddha. Contemplating the Buddha causes the grave offenses of countless kalpas to become slight, and enables one to attain dhyāna concentration. When one contemplates the Buddha wholeheartedly, the Buddha also holds one in mind. It is like a person held in mind by the king: enemies and creditors dare not approach. For one who is mindful of the Buddha, other unwholesome dharmas do not come to disturb. If one is mindful of the Buddha, the Buddha is always present. How should one recollect? There is nothing people trust more than their own eyes. One should contemplate a fine image as if it were the true Buddha. First from the uṣṇīṣa, the white ūrṇā between the brows, down to the feet, and from the feet back up to the uṣṇīṣa: thus, mark by mark, carefully take each in. Then in a quiet place, with eyes closed, meditate and focus the mind on the image, not allowing other thoughts. If other thoughts arise, bring the mind back. With the mind's eye, observe and contemplate as one wishes to see. This is called attaining the contemplation-of-the-image samādhi. One should reflect: "I have not gone there, nor has the image come here. That I can see it is because the mind is settled and the thought is steady." After that, one advances to contemplate the Buddha's living body, and one will see it as if face to face, no differently. The human mind races and scatters, dwelling on many unwholesome dharmas. One should be like a nursemaid watching over her child, not letting it fall into pits, wells, or dangerous paths. The Buddha-contemplation is like the child, and the practitioner is like the nursemaid. If the mind does not settle, one should reprove it: "Consider that old age, illness, and death are very close at hand. If born in heaven, one is attached to exquisite desires, and there are no wholesome dharmas to tame the mind. If one falls into the three evil destinies, the suffering and terror are such that no wholesome mind can arise. Now I have received the wondrous Dharma. How can I not wholeheartedly concentrate?" One should further reflect: "Born in the age of the declining Dharma, the Dharma is nearly extinguished. It is like the amnesty drum being struck and the prison gate being opened to release the prisoners, but the drum is fading and the gate is almost shut. How can one afford to be complacent and not seek release from prison? Since beginningless time, through countless past worlds, the death and rebirth, suffering and affliction I have endured are beyond measure. Now the Dharma I have received has not yet been brought to fruition. The thief of impermanence may strike at any moment, impossible to guard against, and I would again endure inconceivable kalpas of the suffering of birth and death." In these various ways, whip the mind and make it settle. The sign that the mind has settled is that whether sitting, lying, walking, or standing, one constantly sees the Buddha. After that, one advances further to contemplate the living body and the dharma-body. Having attained the initial contemplation, subsequent ones become progressively easier.

## Contemplation of the Living Body

The contemplation of the living body means: having already contemplated the image and brought the mental visualization to accomplishment, one collects the mind and enters concentration, and immediately sees the Buddha. Building upon the image, one should now contemplate the living body. Visualize the Buddha seated beneath the Bodhi Tree, with radiant light shining forth and marks of excellence extraordinary. Or as in the Deer Park, seated and teaching the four truths to the five bhikṣus. Or on Vulture Peak, emitting great light and teaching Prajñā to the great assembly. Choose any one of these settings, fix the mind on it, and do not let it scatter outward. When the mind's visualization settles, one immediately sees the Buddha, and the entire body is pervaded with ease and happiness, a bliss reaching to the very marrow. It is like finding a cool pool in the heat, or a warm room in the cold. No worldly happiness can serve as a simile for it.

## Contemplation of the Dharma-Body

The contemplation of the dharma-body means: having already seen the Buddha's living body in the sky, one should, building upon the living body, contemplate the inner dharma-body: the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, great loving-kindness and great compassion, and immeasurable wholesome deeds. It is like first visualizing a golden vase and then contemplating the maṇi pearl within the vase. The reason the dharma-body is honored and wondrous, with divine wisdom beyond compare, is that nothing is distant or near, nothing difficult or easy, and limitless worlds are all as if before one's eyes, with not a single person outside its scope, and all dharmas thoroughly comprehended. One should constantly focus without letting the mind scatter. If the mind turns to other objects, rein it in and bring it back. Furthermore, all beings, whether foolish or wise, at the time of death lose their sense faculties and plunge as if into a black pit. If they could raise their voice, it would reach the Brahma-heaven. The great force, great suffering, great terror, and great fear of the thief of death are unsurpassed. Only the Buddha alone has the power to rescue and deliver, and to bestow upon all people the bliss of the heavenly and human realms and nirvāṇa. Furthermore, all the buddhas, life after life, for the sake of all sentient beings, have never spared their own bodies or lives. Just as Śākyamuni Buddha in a past life, when he was a prince, went out and saw a leper along the road, and ordered a physician to treat him. The physician said: "One must make the patient drink the blood of a person free from anger, and apply marrow to his body. Only then can the disease be cured." The prince reflected: "Such a person is hard to find, and even if found, it would be wrong to harm them." He then immediately offered his own body to effect the cure. For the sake of all sentient beings, it is the same. The Buddha's grace is deeper than that of parents. Even if all sentient beings were one's parents, and the Buddha were the other portion, between the two, one should always be mindful of the Buddha and not of other things. Contemplating the Buddha's various kinds of merit, whichever merit one calls to mind: if this samādhi is perfected, it will sever the bonds and fetters, and one may even attain patience of non-arising. If during the process various hindrances arise, one should apply the appropriate remedy to each one. If one does not attain samādhi, one will be the foremost and most honored of the six desire-heavens, flying freely with palaces following wherever one goes. Or one will be born before a buddha. It is never in vain. It is like mixing medicine with copper: if it does not turn to gold, it at least becomes silver.

## Contemplation of the Buddhas of the Ten Directions

To contemplate the buddhas of the ten directions: sitting in meditation, visualize the eastern direction as bright, clear, and pure, with no mountains, rivers, or stone walls, seeing only a single buddha seated in the lotus posture, hand raised, teaching the Dharma. With the mind's eye, observe and contemplate: the light, marks of excellence, and characteristics are all vividly clear. Fix the mind on the Buddha without turning to other objects. If the mind turns elsewhere, bring it back. Having seen in this way, increase to ten buddhas. After seeing them, increase further to hundreds, thousands, and beyond, until they are limitless. Nearby they seem close together; farther away they spread wider, seeing nothing but buddhas, their lights connecting to one another. With the mind's eye observe and contemplate. Having accomplished this, turn the body to the southeast and repeat the same contemplation. Having accomplished that, continue with the south, southwest, west, northwest, north, northeast, above, and below, all in the same way. Having seen buddhas in every direction, as was done for the east, one should then sit upright and contemplate all the buddhas of the ten directions together, seeing them all in a single moment of mindfulness. When the settled mind has been accomplished, all the buddhas of the ten directions will teach the Dharma within the samādhi. The clouds of doubt dissolve, and one attains the patience of non-arising. If, due to past karmic obstructions, one does not see the buddhas, one should repent, rejoice, and exhort the buddhas six times throughout the day and night. Gradually one will come to see them. Even if the buddhas do not teach the Dharma, at that time the mind attains happiness and the body feels at ease. This is called the contemplation of the buddhas of the ten directions.

## Contemplation of the Buddha Amitāyus

There are two kinds of people who contemplate the Buddha Amitāyus. For those of dull faculties, one should first teach them to observe with the mind's eye one inch above the forehead. Remove the skin and flesh and see only the red bone. Fix the mind on this without allowing other thoughts. If the mind turns elsewhere, bring it back. Having attained this vision, one should next teach them to transform this red bone into a white square one inch across, white as shell. Having attained this vision, one should next teach them to transform their entire body into white bones, without skin or flesh, white as shell or snow. Having further attained this vision, one should teach them to transform this bone-body into lapis lazuli, whose pure luminous color allows one to see through from surface to core. Having attained this vision, one should next teach them to emit white light from this lapis lazuli body, starting nearby and extending far, pervading the entire Jambudvīpa, seeing only light and no objects. Then draw the light back into the body. After drawing it in, emit it again as before. In all these contemplations, proceed from easy to difficult, and the whiteness should start small and increase gradually. When one can do this, one should emit this white light from the body, and within that light, contemplate the Buddha Amitāyus. The Buddha Amitāyus has a body beautiful and great, with wondrous light. Facing west, sitting solemnly, one carefully takes each mark. Then contemplate the entire body: seated in the lotus posture, with a countenance majestic like a mountain of purple gold. Fix the mind on the Buddha without turning to other objects. If the mind turns elsewhere, bring it back. Constantly maintaining this, as if sitting face to face with the Buddha, no differently: before long, one will be able to see him. For those of sharp faculties, one should simply first create a visualization of brightness, brilliant and empty and pure. Within that brightness, contemplate the Buddha, and one will be able to see him. If the practitioner wishes to be born in the land of the Buddha Amitāyus, one should contemplate the Buddha Amitāyus in this way.

## Contemplation of the True Nature of All Dharmas

The contemplation of the true nature of all dharmas means knowing that all dharmas arise from causes and conditions. Because they arise from causes and conditions, they have no independent existence. Having no independent existence, they are ultimately empty in nature, existing merely as conventional designations with nothing real in them. If a dharma truly existed, one should not say it does not exist. To say that it existed before but now does not exist would be the extreme of annihilation. Neither permanent nor annihilated, neither existent nor non-existent: where the mind's discriminations cease and words too come to an end, this is called the most profound and pure contemplation. Furthermore, one should contemplate that desire, anger, and delusion are themselves the true nature of reality. Why? Because these dharmas are neither inside nor outside. If they were inside, they should not depend on external conditions to arise. If they were outside, they would have no basis. Having no basis, there is also no arising and no ceasing: empty, without any existence, pure and unconditioned. This is called the contemplation of the true nature of desire, anger, and delusion. Furthermore, all dharmas are ultimately pure. This purity is not something that the buddhas and noble ones have caused to be so. It is merely that ordinary beings, not yet having attained the contemplation of wisdom, see various false and deluded dharmas with their various appearances. One who has realized the true nature contemplates them as images in a mirror: they merely deceive the eye, but in reality there is no arising and no ceasing. Contemplating dharmas in this way is exceedingly profound and subtle. If the practitioner can concentrate the mind in meditation, deeply and calmly contemplating the true nature without giving rise to wrong views, one can immediately attain the patience of non-arising. This Dharma is difficult to focus on, and the mind frequently races and scatters. If it does not scatter, it may sink into torpor. One should always purify the mind and observe clearly. If the mind is hard to control, reprove it: "Through countless kalpas you have always engaged in mixed deeds without satiation, chasing worldly pleasures without realizing the suffering. All worldly craving for pleasure brings calamity. Following karmic conditions, one is born into the five destinies: all this is the mind's doing. Who makes you act thus? You are like a mad elephant, trampling and destroying everything, with nothing to restrain you. Who will tame you? If you can be well tamed, you will be free from worldly calamity. Know that dwelling in the womb is impure, a cramped and oppressive agony pressing upon the body, just like hell. Once born into the world, old age, illness, death, sorrow, and grief bring ten thousand afflictions, with no freedom. If born in heaven, one will again fall. The three realms hold no safety. Why are you attached to them?" Having thus reproved the mind in various ways, return to the original contemplation. The sign that the mind has settled is a softening of the mind, and the appearance of various colored lights emanating from the body. This is called the contemplation of the true nature of all dharmas.

Those who wish to be born in the land of the Buddha Amitāyus should contemplate the Buddha Amitāyus as described above. One should also contemplate the true nature of all dharmas, and further contemplate that the entire world is like a dream, like an illusion, with nothing real in it. It is only through inverted and delusory dharmas that afflictions arise senselessly, bringing the retribution of suffering. It is like watching small children quarreling over tiles, stones, and sticks of wood, and coming to blows. Observing the world is the same. One should give rise to great compassion and vow to liberate all beings. One should constantly tame the mind and cultivate the two kinds of patience: patience toward sentient beings and patience toward dharmas. Patience toward sentient beings means that even if sentient beings as numerous as the sands of the Ganges cause all manner of harm, the mind does not become angry; and if they offer all manner of respect and worship, the mind does not become elated. Furthermore, one contemplates that sentient beings have no beginning and no end. If they had a beginning, there would be no causes and conditions. If there are causes and conditions, there is no beginning. If there is no beginning, there is also no middle and no end. Contemplating in this way, one does not fall into the two extremes of permanence and annihilation. Using the path of peace and security to observe sentient beings, one does not give rise to wrong views. This is called patience toward sentient beings. Patience toward dharmas means contemplating all dharmas as exceedingly profound, pure, and ultimately empty in nature. The mind is without any obstruction and can endure this truth. This is called patience toward dharmas. Even those who have newly aroused the aspiration, though they have not yet attained this patience toward dharmas, should cultivate their minds in this way. Furthermore, contemplating that all dharmas are ultimately empty in nature, while constantly giving rise to great compassion for sentient beings, one dedicates all wholesome roots and makes the vow to be born in the land of the Buddha Amitāyus. One will then be born there.

## Lotus Samādhi Contemplation

For three times seven days, practicing with single-minded diligence as taught and rightly recollecting the Lotus Sūtra, one should visualize Śākyamuni Buddha on Vulture Peak, seated together with the Buddha Many Treasures in the seven-jeweled stūpa. The emanation-buddhas of the ten directions fill all the lands to which the sentient beings have been removed. Each buddha has a one-life-supplement bodhisattva as attendant, just as Śākyamuni Buddha has Maitreya as his attendant. All the buddhas manifest their spiritual powers, with light illuminating limitless lands. To reveal the truth of the Dharma, they extend their tongues, and their voices fill the worlds of the ten directions. What the Lotus Sūtra teaches is this: all sentient beings of the three times and ten directions, whether great or small, even those who merely once say "Homage to the Buddha," shall all become buddhas. There is only the One Great Vehicle; there is no second or third. All dharmas have one characteristic and one gate: the unborn, the unceasing, the ultimately empty. There is only this Great Vehicle, and there is no second. Practicing such contemplation, the five desires are naturally cut off, the five hindrances naturally removed, and the five faculties increase and grow. One immediately attains dhyāna concentration. Abiding in this concentration, with deep love for the Buddha, one should further enter this exceedingly profound and subtle teaching of one characteristic, one gate, purity. One should venerate the great bodhisattvas such as Samantabhadra, Bhaiṣajyarāja, Mahāpratibhāna, Avalokiteśvara, Mahāsthāmaprāpta, Mañjuśrī, Maitreya, and others. This is called single-minded, diligent practice, as taught, of the right recollection of the Lotus Sūtra. This means joining it with dhyāna concentration to make the mind firm. If one practices in this way for three times seven days, then the bodhisattva Samantabhadra, riding a six-tusked white elephant, will come to that place, as described in the sūtra.
