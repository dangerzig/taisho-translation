# 度一切諸佛境界智嚴經
## Sūtra on the Wisdom-Adornment that Crosses Over into the Realm of All Buddhas

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0358

Translated from the Chinese. Liang dynasty, translated by Saṃghapāla (僧伽婆羅) and others

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was dwelling on the peak of Gṛdhrakūṭa Mountain in the city of Rājagṛha, in the Dharma-Realm Palace on the summit, together with a great assembly of twenty-five thousand bhikṣus, all of whom were arhats whose outflows were exhausted and who were free from afflictions, whose minds were well liberated and whose wisdom was well liberated, who had tamed their faculties, great nāgas who had accomplished what was to be accomplished, who had set down the heavy burden and attained their own purpose, whose bonds of existence were exhausted and whose minds had attained mastery. Their names were Ājñātakauṇḍinya and the others, along with the eight great śrāvakas. There were also seventy-two hundred million nayutas of bodhisattva-mahāsattvas. Their names were: Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Auspicious Practice Bodhisattva, Buddha-Auspicious Bodhisattva, Medicine King Bodhisattva, Ever-Arising Bodhisattva Mahāsattva, and others. They were able to turn the wheel of non-retrogression, skilled in asking about the Incomparable Jewel-Crest Sūtra, abiding on the stage of the Dharma-Cloud. Their wisdom was like Mount Sumeru. They constantly cultivated emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness; the radiance of the profound Dharma of non-arising and non-substantiality. Their merits were complete and their conduct fully accomplished. Sent by the tathāgatas of countless nayutas of worlds, they possessed great spiritual powers and abided in the characteristic of no-nature.

At that time the World-Honored One had this thought: "These bodhisattva-mahāsattvas have come from worlds as numerous as the sands of the Gaṅgā. I should teach them the Dharma so they may attain great power. I should manifest spiritual transformations and emit great radiance, since the bodhisattvas are about to question me."

At that time the World-Honored One emitted great radiance that universally illuminated the immeasurable, inconceivable worlds of three-thousand great-thousand systems of atoms in the ten directions. Then from each of the ten directions, throughout ten buddha-worlds, there came indescribable thousands of millions of hundreds of millions of nayutas of atoms' worth of bodhisattvas, each departing from their own realms riding upon inconceivable, immeasurable spiritual powers and gathering here. They also made offerings to the Tathāgata with inconceivable offerings, and creating lotus seats as they wished, they sat before the Buddha, gazing upon the World-Honored One without averting their eyes for even a moment. At that time, upon the Dharma-Realm Palace, there arose a great jeweled lotus lion-treasury throne, immeasurable hundreds of millions of yojanas in length and breadth, made of immeasurable radiant maṇi jewels. Lightning-lamp maṇi jewels formed its lattice-nets, maṇi jewels of inconceivable power formed its poles, and incomparable maṇi jewels formed its retinue. It was adorned with surpassingly analogous maṇi jewels, covered with a canopy of sovereign-king maṇi jewels, inlaid with various maṇi treasures, and hung with banners of various colors. Those great maṇi jewels encircled it and emitted ten kinds of immeasurable hundreds of millions of nayutas of radiance, illuminating the worlds of the ten directions.

At that time, indescribable hundreds of thousands of millions of hundreds of millions of nayutas of atoms' worth of devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas, as well as Śakras, Brahmās, and the four heavenly kings, came gathering here from the ten buddha-worlds in the ten directions. There were also devas riding jewel-crowned palaces, with countless, inconceivable celestial women playing hundreds of thousands of millions of hundreds of millions of nayutas of musical instruments, who also came to gather here. There were also devas riding jewel-flower palaces, nāga jewel-sandalwood divine-pearl palaces, pearl palaces, jewel-garment palaces, golden-radiance maṇi-jewel palaces, jambu-river gold palaces, immeasurable-radiance maṇi-jewel palaces, sovereign-king maṇi-jewel palaces, wish-fulfilling maṇi-jewel palaces, Śakra-covering maṇi-jewel palaces, and ocean-treasury pure-jewel-adorned universal-radiance great maṇi-jewel wish-crowned palaces, together with countless, inconceivable thousands of millions of hundreds of millions of nayutas of celestial women playing various instruments, who all came to gather here. They each made offerings to the Buddha with countless, inconceivable offerings, and having made offerings, each sat down as they wished, gazing upon the World-Honored One without averting their eyes for even a moment.

At that time, the entire great trichiliocosm became the color of jambu-river gold, adorned with various kinds of maṇi-jewel trees, celestial flower trees, jewel-garment trees, and nāga jewel-sandalwood trees. Maṇi jewels of sun, moon, and lightning-lamp formed lattice-nets covering the entire world, hung with various banners. Countless thousands of millions of hundreds of millions of nayutas of celestial women held various necklaces and various jeweled flowers.

At that time, from the great jeweled lotus lion-throne, this gāthā issued forth:

> "You may now sit at ease; I shall speak the truth.
> The throne of the king of humans, the lion-seat, is made by the merits of the Tathāgata.
> Today my vow is fulfilled: to make offerings to the Honored One of Two Feet.
> May the World-Honored One now sit upon this seven-jeweled lotus throne,
> And emit great radiance illuminating me and all.
> May he teach the supreme, wondrous Dharma for the benefit of devas and humans.
> Sentient beings who hear the Dharma shall sit upon the lion-throne.
> Such great radiance issues from the Tathāgata's body,
> Illuminating immeasurable worlds and causing all to rejoice.
> O Guide, Deva among Devas, may you now gather me in.
> In the past, at this very place, I have already encountered eight hundred million buddhas.
> I only pray that the present World-Honored One will surely have compassion and gather me in."

At that time the World-Honored One rose from his throne of radiance and sat upon the jeweled lotus-treasury lion-throne in the cross-legged posture. Seeing that all the bodhisattva assembly had gathered, he was about to teach the Dharma of emptiness for the sake of inspiring the bodhisattvas. At that time the bodhisattvas had this thought: "The youth Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva is about to ask the Tathāgata about the unborn and the unperishing. We have not heard this Dharma for a very long time."

At that time Mañjuśrī, already knowing the Tathāgata's intention to teach the Dharma-characteristic and the thoughts in the minds of the bodhisattvas, said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the Dharma of no-arising and no-perishing: what is its characteristic?" Mañjuśrī spoke this gīti:

> "No-arising and no-perishing: how can they be known?
> O Great Muni, you should teach by analogy.
> This great assembly has all gathered here,
> Delighting to hear this meaning; we pray the Buddha will explain.
> The bodhisattvas now present, sent by all buddhas,
> Also delight to hear the subtle and wondrous Dharma-characteristic."

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Excellent, excellent! Your present question can greatly benefit all the world and enable the bodhisattvas to accomplish the Buddha's work. Mañjuśrī, listen carefully and do not give rise to alarm or doubt. Mañjuśrī, the unborn and unperishing is precisely the Tathāgata. Mañjuśrī, suppose the great earth were made of lapis lazuli, and the reflections of Śakra's Vaijayanta Palace and its furnishings appeared within it. The people of Jambudvīpa, seeing the reflections of palaces in the lapis lazuli ground, would press their palms together and make offerings, burning incense and scattering flowers: 'May we be born in such palaces! May we sport like Śakra and the rest!' Those beings would not know that this ground merely reflects the palaces' images. They would practice generosity, uphold precepts, and cultivate various merits for the sake of obtaining such palace rewards. Mañjuśrī, these palaces in reality have no arising or perishing. Because the ground is pure, the reflections appear within it. Those palace reflections both exist and do not exist; they are unborn and unperishing.

"Mañjuśrī, sentient beings seeing the Buddha is just like this. Because their minds are pure, they see the Buddha's body. The Buddha's body is unconditioned: unborn, unarisen, inexhaustible, unperishing; neither form nor not-form; neither visible nor invisible; neither worldly nor not-worldly; neither mind nor not-mind. Because sentient beings' minds are pure, they see the Tathāgata's body and scatter flowers, burn incense, and make various offerings: 'May we attain such a form-body!' They practice generosity, uphold precepts, and perform various merits for the sake of attaining the Tathāgata's subtle body. In this way, Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata by his spiritual power appears in the world, enabling all sentient beings to gain great benefit. Like a reflection, like an image, he appears according to what sentient beings see."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this gīti:

> "The Tathāgata abides eternally, unborn and unperishing,
> Neither mind nor form, neither existent nor non-existent.
> Like a lapis lazuli ground reflecting palace images:
> These reflections neither exist nor do they not exist.
> When beings' minds are pure, they see the Tathāgata's body;
> Neither existent nor non-existent, it too is just like this.

"Mañjuśrī, just as when the sun first rises it illuminates the high mountains first, then the middle mountains, and lastly the low ground, the Tathāgata is likewise. He is without mind, thought, or consciousness; without characteristics and apart from characteristics. He has severed all characteristics. He does not cling to that, does not cling to this; he does not abide on this shore, does not abide on the far shore, does not abide in midstream. He is inconceivable, beyond the reach of thought. Neither high nor low, without bondage and without liberation, neither with knowledge nor without knowledge, neither affliction nor non-affliction, neither real nor unreal, neither wisdom nor non-wisdom, neither conceivable nor inconceivable, neither active nor inactive, neither mindful nor not mindful, neither mind nor not-mind, neither intention nor not-intention, neither name nor not-name, neither form nor formless, neither grasping nor not-grasping, neither spoken nor unspoken, neither expressible nor inexpressible, neither visible nor invisible, neither guide nor non-guide, neither attaining fruition nor not attaining fruition. In this way, Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata's sun of wisdom illuminates the three realms with its radiance: first illuminating the bodhisattvas, like illuminating the high mountains; next illuminating those who delight in the pratyekabuddha and śrāvaka paths; and lastly illuminating those who delight in good roots, and even those of wrong fixation, in order to increase wholesome dharmas and generate future causes and conditions.

"Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata is equal, without high, middle, or low, and constantly practices equanimity of mind. Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata does not think: 'To these beings I will teach the superior Dharma; to those beings I will teach the inferior Dharma.' Nor does he think: 'These beings have great aspiration; those have middling aspiration; those have small aspiration. These delight in wholesome dharmas; those delight in unwholesome dharmas. These are of right fixation; those are of wrong fixation.' The Tathāgata's wisdom-radiance makes no such discriminations, because he has already severed all discriminating thoughts. Because sentient beings have various good roots, the Tathāgata's wisdom manifests in various ways.

"Mañjuśrī, in the great ocean there is a maṇi jewel called 'Fulfilling the Wishes of All Sentient Beings.' When placed atop a standard, it provides whatever sentient beings need, yet that maṇi jewel has no mind, thought, or consciousness. The Tathāgata, being without mind, thought, or consciousness, is likewise: immeasurable, unreachable, ungraspable, inexpressible, free from faults, free from ignorance, neither real nor unreal, neither permanent nor impermanent, neither radiant nor non-radiant, neither worldly nor non-worldly, without examination and without analysis, unborn and unperishing, inconceivable, without mind and without substance, unmoving and not-acting, immeasurable and boundless, inexpressible and without language, neither joyful nor non-joyful, numberless and beyond number, without going and without coming, without a place of action, with all destinies severed, invisible, ungraspable, without calculation, neither empty nor non-empty, neither compounded nor not-compounded, inconceivable and unknowable, neither defiled nor pure, neither name nor form, neither karma nor fruition, neither past nor future nor present, without any existence, without sound and without characteristic, apart from all characteristics, neither inside nor outside nor in between. In this way, Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata, pure and abiding upon the great banner of loving-kindness and compassion, manifests various bodies and teaches various Dharma in accordance with what sentient beings delight in.

"Mañjuśrī, just as an echo arises from sound, being neither inside nor outside nor in between, unborn and unperishing, neither cut off nor eternal, the Tathāgata is likewise: neither inside nor outside nor in between, unborn and unperishing, without name and without characteristic, manifesting in various ways in accordance with sentient beings.

"Mañjuśrī, just as all grasses and trees grow in dependence on the earth, while the earth itself is equal and free from discrimination, so too the good roots of all sentient beings grow in dependence on the Tathāgata: the śrāvaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, and the bodhisattva vehicle, and even the good roots of the naked Nirgrantha ascetics and all non-Buddhist practitioners also grow in dependence on the Tathāgata. The Tathāgata is equal and without discrimination, just like this.

"Mañjuśrī, just as empty space is equal, without low, middle, or high, the Tathāgata's equality is likewise. Sentient beings themselves perceive low, middle, and high. Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata does not think: 'This being of small aspiration: I should manifest a lesser body. This being of middling or great aspiration: I should manifest a middling or great body. This being of small aspiration: I should teach the lesser vehicle. This being of middling aspiration: I should teach the pratyekabuddha or śrāvaka vehicle. This being of great aspiration: I should teach the Mahāyāna.' Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata has no such thoughts: 'This being delights in generosity: I should teach generosity. Likewise with discipline, patience, vigor, meditation, and wisdom.' Why? Because the Tathāgata's dharma-body is equal, apart from mind, thought, and consciousness, and without discrimination.

"Mañjuśrī, all dharmas are entirely equal. Because they are equal, they are without abiding. Because they are without abiding, they are without movement. Because they are without movement, they are without dependence. Because they are without dependence, they are without a place. Because they are without a place, they are unborn. Because they are unborn, they are unperishing. If one can see in this way, the mind is not inverted. Because it is not inverted, it accords with reality. Because it accords with reality, there is no activity. Because there is no activity, there is no coming. Because there is no coming, there is no going. Because there is no going, there is suchness. Because there is suchness, one follows the dharma-nature. Because one follows the dharma-nature, one is unmoving. If one follows the dharma-nature and is unmoving, one attains the dharma-nature. If one attains the dharma-nature, there is no longing. Why? Because one has attained the path. If one has attained the path, one does not abide in any dharma. Because one does not abide in any dharma, there is no arising and no perishing, and no name and no characteristic. Mañjuśrī, if sentient beings cling to any dharma, then afflictions arise. Because afflictions arise, they do not attain bodhi."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, how does one attain bodhi?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "Without root and without place: this is how the Tathāgata attains bodhi."

Mañjuśrī said to the Buddha: "What is the root? What is the place?"

The Buddha told Mañjuśrī: "The view of a self is the root. Untrue thinking is the place. Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata's wisdom is equal to bodhi, and equal to all dharmas. Therefore, without root and without place, this is attaining bodhi. Mañjuśrī, bodhi is quiescence. What is quiescence? Internal quiescence and external quiescence. Why? The eye is empty; it is not self, not belonging to self. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind are empty; they are not self, not belonging to self. Knowing the eye is empty, one does not engage with form: this is called quiescence. Likewise, knowing the ear is empty, one does not engage with sound: this is called quiescence. Knowing the nose through the mind are empty is likewise. Mañjuśrī, bodhi is unmoving and inactive. 'Unmoving' means not grasping any dharma. 'Inactive' means not discarding any dharma.

"Mañjuśrī, because the Tathāgata is unmoving, he accords with suchness and reality. Whoever accords with suchness and reality does not see this shore or that shore. Because one does not see this or that, one sees all dharmas. Because one sees all dharmas, one is called Tathāgata.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is without characteristic and without condition. What is without characteristic? What is without condition? Not apprehending eye-consciousness is 'without characteristic'; not seeing form is 'without condition.' Not apprehending ear-consciousness is 'without characteristic'; not hearing sound is 'without condition.' The same applies through the mind and dharmas.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is neither past, nor future, nor present; it equally severs the flow of the three times. Mañjuśrī, how does one sever the flow? By not giving rise to consciousness of the past in the mind, not activating future cognition, and not moving or abiding in present intention; by not thinking, not perceiving, and not discriminating. Mañjuśrī, bodhi is without form and unconditioned. Why is it without form? Because it cannot be cognized by the six consciousnesses. Why is it unconditioned? Because it is without arising, abiding, or perishing. This is called severing the flow of the three times.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is an indestructible statement. What is indestructible? What is a statement? Signlessness is 'indestructible,' and reality is the 'statement.' Having no abiding place is 'indestructible,' and the dharma-realm is the 'statement.' Being unmoving is 'indestructible,' and the nature of emptiness is the 'statement.' Being ungraspable is 'indestructible,' and signlessness is the 'statement.' Being imperceptible is 'indestructible,' and non-fabrication is the 'statement.' Being without longing is 'indestructible,' and having no self-nature is the 'statement.' Sentient beings having no self-nature is 'indestructible,' and empty space is the 'statement.' Being ungraspable is 'indestructible,' and non-arising is the 'statement.' Being unperishing is 'indestructible,' and the unconditioned is the 'statement.' Being inactive is 'indestructible,' and bodhi is the 'statement.' Quiescence is 'indestructible,' and nirvāṇa is the 'statement.' Not being reborn is 'indestructible,' and non-arising is the 'statement.'

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi cannot be perceived by the body, nor can it be perceived by the mind. Why? The body is without awareness, like grass and wood. The mind is deceptive and unreal. Mañjuśrī, if one says bodhi is perceived by body and mind, this relies on conventional designation and is not the true meaning. Why? Because bodhi is neither body nor mind, neither false nor real. Mañjuśrī, bodhi cannot be expressed in words. Why? Like empty space, it has no location, is unborn and unperishing, and has no name. Mañjuśrī, the truth of all dharmas is inexpressible. Why? Because all dharmas are not truly real, are without language, and are unborn and unperishing.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is ungraspable and without a place. How is it ungraspable and without a place? Eye-consciousness is ungraspable; not apprehending form is 'without a place.' Ear-consciousness is ungraspable; not apprehending sound is 'without a place.' The same applies through the mind and dharmas. Bodhi is imperceptible: because the eye does not grasp, form is not apprehended, and because form is not apprehended, consciousness has no place of abiding. Because the ear does not grasp, sound is not apprehended, and because sound is not apprehended, consciousness has no place of abiding. The same applies through the mind and dharmas.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is spoken of as emptiness, because it empties all dharmas. Emptiness is what the Tathāgata knows; emptiness is what the Tathāgata perceives. Mañjuśrī, emptiness does not arise from some other emptiness to be perceived by the Tathāgata. Why? Because it is without characteristics.

"Furthermore, Mañjuśrī, the wisdom that is the cause of bodhi is also of the nature of emptiness. Why? Because it is without characteristics. Mañjuśrī, emptiness and bodhi are both without any substantial existence: nondual, numberless, without name and without characteristic, apart from mind, thought, and consciousness, unborn and unperishing, without activity and without a place, without sound and without expression. Mañjuśrī, they are merely spoken of with names, but truly they cannot be expressed.

"Mañjuśrī, the Tathāgata fully knows that all dharmas from the very beginning are unborn and unarisen, inexhaustible and unperishing, without name and without characteristic, apart from mind, thought, and consciousness. Knowing thus, he is thus liberated: neither bound nor freed.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is equal to empty space. Empty space is neither equal nor unequal. Bodhi likewise is neither equal nor unequal. Such is the Dharma-characteristic that the Tathāgata has perceived. Mañjuśrī, just as a single atom is neither equal nor unequal, all dharmas are likewise. By true wisdom, one can know thus. Mañjuśrī, how does true wisdom know dharmas? That which has not yet arisen arises, and having arisen, immediately perishes. All those dharmas are in reality unborn, because nothing is grasped.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is a statement of reality. The statement of reality means: the characteristic of bodhi is inseparable from reality. Form, feeling, perception, volition, and consciousness are inseparable from reality. The characteristic of bodhi is inseparable from reality for the earth element, and likewise the water, fire, and wind elements are inseparable from reality. The characteristic of bodhi is inseparable from reality for the eye-realm, the form-realm, and the eye-consciousness realm, and likewise through the mind-realm, the dharma-realm, and the mind-consciousness realm, which are also inseparable from reality. This is called the statement of reality.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is entering non-activity through activity. Mañjuśrī, what is activity? What is non-activity? Activity is engaging with all wholesome dharmas. Non-activity is not apprehending any wholesome dharma. Activity is the mind not abiding. Non-activity is the signless liberation. Activity is measurable. Non-activity is immeasurable. Why is it immeasurable? Because there is nothing to be cognized. Mañjuśrī, bodhi is without outflows and without grasping. 'Without outflows' means extinguishing the four floods. What are the four? The flood of desire, the flood of existence, the flood of views, and the flood of ignorance. Not being attached to these four floods: this is called extinguishing the four floods. 'Without grasping' means extinguishing the four kinds of grasping. What are the four? Grasping at desire, grasping at views, grasping at rules and rituals, and grasping at a doctrine of self. These are the four kinds of grasping. All of these graspings are darkened by ignorance and deceived by craving-thirst, because they mutually give rise to one another.

"Mañjuśrī, by true wisdom one severs the root of grasping at a doctrine of self. When the root of grasping is severed, the body becomes purified. The purified body is without arising or perishing. Mañjuśrī, what is without arising or perishing does not give rise to mind, thought, or consciousness, and does not think or discriminate. If there is discrimination, it constitutes ignorance. If this ignorance does not arise, then there are no twelve links of dependent origination. Without the twelve links of dependent origination, there is non-arising. Non-arising is the path. The path is definitive meaning. Definitive meaning is the ultimate meaning. The ultimate meaning is the meaning of no-self. The meaning of no-self is the inexpressible meaning. The inexpressible meaning is the meaning of the twelve links of dependent origination. The meaning of the twelve links of dependent origination is the meaning of the Dharma. The meaning of the Dharma is the meaning of the Tathāgata. Therefore I say: to see the twelve links of dependent origination is to see the Dharma; to see the Dharma is to see the Buddha. To see in this way is to see without seeing.

"Mañjuśrī, bodhi is purity, stainlessness, and freedom from affliction. Mañjuśrī, emptiness is purity. Signlessness is stainlessness. Wishlessness is freedom from affliction. Furthermore, non-arising is purity. The unconditioned is stainlessness. Non-perishing is freedom from affliction. Furthermore, self-nature is purity. Purity is stainlessness. Stainlessness is freedom from affliction. Furthermore, non-discrimination is purity. Not-discriminating is stainlessness. Extinguishing discrimination is freedom from affliction. Reality is purity. The dharma-realm is stainlessness. True contemplation is freedom from affliction. Empty space is purity. Empty space is stainlessness. Empty space is freedom from affliction. Inner-body wisdom is purity. Inner practice is stainlessness. Not apprehending inner or outer is freedom from affliction. Knowing the aggregates is purity. The self-nature of the elements is stainlessness. Not discarding the sense-bases is freedom from affliction. The wisdom of exhaustion regarding the past is purity. The wisdom of non-arising regarding the future is stainlessness. The wisdom of the dharma-realm regarding the present is freedom from affliction. Mañjuśrī, this is called purity, stainlessness, and freedom from affliction. This is quiescence. Quiescence is inner and outer quiescence. Inner and outer quiescence is great quiescence. Because of great quiescence, one is called Muni. Mañjuśrī, as is empty space, so is bodhi. As is bodhi, so are all dharmas. As are all dharmas, so are all sentient beings. As are all sentient beings, so is the realm of experience. As is the realm of experience, so is nirvāṇa.

"Mañjuśrī, all dharmas are equal to nirvāṇa. Because they are supremely boundless, there is no antidote for them. Because there is no antidote, they are originally pure, originally stainless, and originally free from affliction. Mañjuśrī, having thus perceived all dharmas, the Tathāgata contemplates all sentient beings and gives rise to great loving-kindness and compassion, enabling them to sport in the abode of purity, stainlessness, and freedom from affliction.

"Mañjuśrī, how do the bodhisattvas practice the bodhisattva practice? Mañjuśrī, the bodhisattva does not think, does not act for the sake of perishing, and does not act for the sake of arising: this is practicing the bodhisattva practice. Furthermore, Mañjuśrī, the bodhisattva's past mind has already perished and does not act. The future mind has not yet arrived and does not act. Although the present mind exists, it too does not act. Why? Because one does not cling to past, future, or present. Mañjuśrī, this is called the bodhisattva practicing the bodhisattva practice.

"Mañjuśrī, generosity and the Tathāgata are without duality: this is the bodhisattva's practice. Likewise, discipline, patience, vigor, meditation, and wisdom are nondual with the Tathāgata: this is the bodhisattva's practice.

"Mañjuśrī, if a bodhisattva does not practice form as empty, this is the bodhisattva's practice. And not practicing form as non-empty is also the bodhisattva's practice. Why? Because the self-nature of form is empty. Likewise, the bodhisattva does not practice feeling, perception, volition, and consciousness as empty or non-empty: this is the bodhisattva's practice. Why? Because mind, thought, and consciousness are ungraspable. Mañjuśrī, all dharmas that are without anything whatsoever should be cultivated and realized. When realized, there are no afflictions arising and no afflictions perishing. Mañjuśrī, arising and perishing are mere conventional designations. In true reality there is no arising and no perishing.

"Mañjuśrī, suppose all beings of the six destinies and four modes of birth, whether with form or formless, with perception or without perception, two-footed, four-footed, many-footed, or no-footed, all obtained human bodies. Having obtained human bodies, they generated the aspiration for bodhi. Having generated the aspiration for bodhi, each bodhisattva made offerings of food, drink, clothing, bedding, and medicine, together with all objects of delight, to buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Gaṅgā, as well as to all the bodhisattvas, pratyekabuddhas, and śrāvakas, throughout kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Gaṅgā, and even after those buddhas' entry into nirvāṇa, erected stūpas of the seven treasures a hundred yojanas in height, covering them with jeweled canopies and hanging maṇi-jewel ornaments, and suspending various banners and canopies, with sovereign-king maṇi jewels forming lattice-nets. Yet if there were a bodhisattva who, with a pure mind, heard this Sūtra on the Wisdom-Adornment that Crosses Over into the Realm of All Buddhas, and having heard it, joyfully received, upheld, and understood it, or even expounded a single verse or a single sentence of it for others, this merit would surpass the former by a hundredfold, a thousandfold, a ten-thousandfold, a hundred-million-fold, and even beyond all calculation and analogy. Why? Because this sūtra extensively teaches the inconceivable, pure, signless, and subtle dharma-body. Mañjuśrī, if Gaṅgā-sands' worth of innumerable bodhisattvas, in Gaṅgā-sands' worth of innumerable buddha-worlds all made of jambu-river gold, with even the trees, flowers, and fruits all of jambu-river gold, adorned with celestial garments upon the trees, covered over with nets of all-radiance maṇi jewels, with sovereign-king maṇi jewels as palaces, and lightning-radiance maṇi jewels as terraces and steps, hung with many jeweled banners, were to make daily offerings of all this to Gaṅgā-sands' worth of innumerable buddhas for countless kalpas: if a bodhisattva merely mindfully recalled this sūtra, or expounded a single sentence of it, the merit of all those bodhisattvas' generosity compared to this merit would not equal one hundredth, one hundred-thousandth, one hundred-millionth, and would be beyond all calculation and analogy. Thus with all other merits compared to this sūtra's merit, none can match it."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke this gīti:

> "If there are those who receive and uphold this
> Subtle dharma-body sūtra,
> The merit and benefit they gain
> Cannot be calculated or measured.
> Suppose all sentient beings
> Were all born on the human path,
> All generated the aspiration for bodhi
> In pursuit of omniscience,
> And all these bodhisattvas
> Became great patrons of giving,
> With all manner of offerings
> Making offerings to countless buddhas,
> Together with all bodhisattvas,
> Pratyekabuddhas, and śrāvakas,
> And even after they entered nirvāṇa
> Erected seven-jeweled stūpas
> A hundred yojanas in height,
> Adorned with various treasures:
> If a person upholds this sūtra
> Or speaks a single verse,
> This merit surpasses the former
> Immeasurably and without limit,
> Because what this sūtra teaches
> Is the signless dharma-body.
> Therefore the wise
> Should mindfully receive and uphold it,
> Reading, reciting, and copying it,
> Making offerings with flowers and incense.
> The meritorious fruition thus gained
> Is inconceivable.
> Before long, one will reach the place of enlightenment,
> Vanquish Māra, and attain perfect awakening.
> This sūtra
> Is praised by all buddhas.
> It is the wondrous dharma-body,
> Without characteristics, without words.
> Therefore the merit of one who upholds it
> Is immeasurable."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, Mañjuśrī and all the bodhisattvas, the immeasurable pratyekabuddhas and śrāvakas, the devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans and non-humans, and the entire great assembly, hearing what the Buddha had taught, joyfully put it into practice.
