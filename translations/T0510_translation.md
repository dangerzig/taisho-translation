# 採花違王上佛授決號妙花經
## Sūtra of the Flower-Gatherers Who Defied the King, Offered to the Buddha, and Received the Prediction of the Name Wonderful Flower

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0510

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhu Tanwulan (竺曇無蘭)

---

Long ago, the World-Honored One was traveling in Rājagṛha, teaching the sūtras and spreading wisdom. His words were good at the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end. Their meaning was subtle and wondrous, purely cultivating the holy life, and his teaching was vast and universal.

At that time, the king assigned several dozen servants to constantly gather fine flowers for the royal household. One day, the palace ladies, attendant women, great and small, all went outside the city to gather flowers. When they wished to return and enter the city, they happened to encounter the Buddha on the road. From afar they saw the World-Honored One, his excellent marks and majestic radiance, immeasurably magnificent, like the moon among the stars, like the sun newly risen illuminating the world, surrounded by the noble assembly of disciples and bodhisattvas before and behind.

They went to the Buddha and prostrated in reverence. In their minds they thought: "Human life is hard to preserve, a Buddha's world is rare to encounter, and the sūtras are rare to come upon. Now I have met the Great Sage. It is like a sick person finding a good physician. I am lowly and poor, and moreover bound in servitude to the court. The suffering of being yoked and enslaved means I can never do as I wish. The king is strict and harsh; my duty is to gather flowers and present them early. If I miss the appointed time, I may be executed. Yet the sun does not return to midday, and the noble assembly is rare to encounter, appearing perhaps once in a hundred million ages. Better to cast away my body and life, offer these flowers to the Buddha and scatter them upon the noble assembly, and thereby receive the sūtra precepts and hear the profound Dharma of inexhaustible wisdom. In innumerable kalpas, I have been killed by others beyond counting, yet I have never sacrificed my body and life for the Dharma. Now I make offerings to the World-Honored One in the work of the Three Treasures. Even if I am killed for it, I shall not fall into suffering but will certainly be reborn in a place of peace." They then scattered the flowers upon the Buddha and the noble assembly, took refuge with their lives, and with single-minded devotion, prostrated again.

The Buddha, knowing their aspiration to give rise to the mind of the great path, was deeply compassionate toward them. He fully expounded the Dharma of the Great Vehicle for them: the six pāramitās, the four immeasurables, the four kindnesses, and the three liberations of the bodhisattva. At that time, all the flower-gatherers gave rise to the aspiration for unsurpassed, true enlightenment. Their minds understood the Buddha's wisdom and reached the stage of non-retrogression and the patience of non-arising. The Buddha then gave them the prediction: "In the future you shall become Buddhas, named Wonderful Flower Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Vidyācaraṇasaṃpanna, Sugata, Lokavid, Anuttara, Puruṣadamyasārathi, Śāstā Devamanuṣyāṇāṃ, Buddha, Bhagavān." Those nearby who heard this were all filled with joy, and received the great Dharma and made offerings to the Three Treasures.

When the flower-gatherers had made their offerings and received the prediction, they prostrated at the Buddha's feet and returned home. They bade farewell to their parents and wives and children: "My life will now end, for the king will have me killed."

The parents were startled and asked: "What is your crime?"

The sons answered: "We were sent by the king to gather flowers. On the way, we saw the Buddha from afar and offered the flowers to him. The king is strict and harsh. We have missed the appointed time and have no flowers left. We will certainly be put to death. That is why we bid you farewell."

When the parents heard this, their grief deepened. What could be done? They opened the flower baskets to look, and found them full of fine flowers, mixed with sumanā fragrance. The fragrance was rich and dense, wafting far in all directions. The parents said: "You may present these to the king."

The sons each said: "Others have seen us, and the report will certainly reach the king. Moreover, we are late. I fear there will be no safety."

The king was greatly angered that they had not come on time and had scattered the flowers elsewhere. He dispatched a high minister with a large force of soldiers to arrest and bring them in. They received the king's order, and the flower-gatherers were bound with their arms behind their backs and brought into the palace, sentenced to be executed in the marketplace. The men showed no fear, and their faces did not change color. The king was astonished and asked them: "Your crime puts your lives in doubt. You have been bound and brought here to be killed. Why are you not afraid? Why do your faces not change?"

They addressed the king: "A person who is born must die; what is created must come to ruin. For innumerable kalpas, I have, in wrongful ways, not spared my body and life. This morning, while gathering flowers, I encountered the World-Honored One. I offered the flowers to him, prostrated, and took refuge. At that time I already knew that defying the command meant death. Better to die with virtue than to survive without it. When I looked back at my flower baskets, they were once again full as before. All of this is covered by the Tathāgata's benevolent grace."

The king was greatly amazed. His mind did not trust this, so he went in person to the Buddha. He prostrated at the Buddha's feet, sat to one side, joined his palms, and asked the Buddha: "Is this true?"

The Buddha said: "Indeed, Your Majesty! These persons, with utter sincerity, wished to ferry across all beings of the ten directions, and not sparing their bodies and lives, they took the flowers and scattered them upon the Buddha. Their intention was without thought of reward, and so they received the prediction. In the future they shall become Buddhas named Wonderful Flower, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha. Because they gave rise to the great aspiration and received the prediction, the fragrance of their compassionate mind filled the flower baskets, known to all."

The king was overjoyed. He quickly loosened all their bonds and repented, reproaching himself: "My foolish mind did not comprehend. I bound and shackled bodhisattvas. I beg forgiveness for my crime."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! One who can reform oneself is the same as one without fault."

When the Buddha had spoken thus, the king, ministers, and people were all filled with joy, made obeisance, and departed.
