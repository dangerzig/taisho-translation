# 鹹水喻經
## 鹹水喻經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0029

Translated from the Chinese. 失譯

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was dwelling at Śrāvastī, in the Jeta Grove, the Garden of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the World-Honored One addressed the bhikṣus: "I shall speak to you of seven matters through the simile of water. Listen carefully and reflect carefully upon them; I shall now speak." They replied: "So be it, World-Honored One!" At that time, the bhikṣus received instruction from the Buddha.

The World-Honored One said: "What, bhikṣus, are the seven matters of the simile of water? There is a person who sinks beneath the water; one who rises to the surface only to sink again; one who rises to the surface and looks all around in the four directions; one who rises to the surface and does not sink again; one who makes their way out of the water; one who makes for the far shore; and one who has already reached the far shore and stands there with a purified mind.

"What manner of person sinks and drowns in the water? Suppose there is a person whose whole body is entirely wrapped and bound by unwholesome dhammas, fully ripened in evil, who falls into hell and endures suffering for an eon with no possibility of remedy or cure. This is called the person who is always sinking and drowning in the water. This is called the first type, who enters the water and drowns.

"What manner of person rises to the surface only to sink again? Suppose there is a person who, though sinking thus, has faith in the wholesome dhamma, harbors a sense of shame and remorse, seeks skillful means, and in regard to all wholesome dhammas harbors shame and remorse. Yet this person rises to the surface only to sink and drown in the water again. This is called the second type of person who drowns in the water.

"What manner of person rises to the surface and looks all around in the four directions? Suppose there is a person who, rising from the water, has faith in the wholesome dhamma, has a mind of shame and remorse, has a spirit of courageous energy, and in regard to all unwholesome dhammas has shame and remorse. This person rises above the water and does not sink and drown again. O noble ones, this is called the third type, represented by one who rises from the water.

"What manner of person rises from the water and stands? Suppose there is a person who, thus rising from the water, has faith in the wholesome dhamma, has shame and remorse, has diligence, and in regard to all wholesome dhammas harbors shame and remorse. This person, having destroyed the three fetters, attains stream-entry (srotāpatti) and does not fall back, and will certainly attain what is to be gained. This is called the fourth type, represented by one who rises from the water and stands.

"What manner of person rises from the water and makes for the far shore? Suppose there is a person who, thus rising from the water, has faith in the wholesome dhamma, has shame and remorse, has a spirit of courageous energy, and in regard to all wholesome dhammas harbors shame and remorse. This person, having destroyed the three fetters and with greed, hatred, and delusion attenuated, attains once-returning (sakṛdāgāmitva), comes to this world one more time, and exhausts the root of suffering. This is called the fifth type, represented by one who, having risen from the water, makes for the far shore.

"What manner of person has already reached the far shore? Suppose there is a person who, rising from the water, has faith in the wholesome dhamma, has shame and remorse, has a spirit of courageous energy, and in regard to all wholesome dhammas harbors shame and remorse. This person, having destroyed the five lower fetters, attains non-returning (anāgāmitva), attains parinirvāṇa in that realm, and does not return to this world again. This is called the sixth type, represented by one who, having risen from the water, has already reached the far shore.

"What manner of person has already reached the far shore and, with purified mind, stands upon the far shore? Suppose there is a person who, rising from the water, has faith in the wholesome dhamma, has shame and remorse, has a spirit of courageous energy, and in regard to all wholesome dhammas harbors shame and remorse—or suppose there is a person who has exhausted all āsavas and become free of āsavas, with liberation through mindfulness and liberation through wisdom, swiftly realizing direct knowledge in the present life, dwelling in joy, and exhausting the very source of birth and death. The holy life has been fulfilled; what was to be done has been accomplished; there is no further receiving of a mother's womb. This is called the seventh type, represented by one who, having risen from the water, stands upon the far shore.

"Thus, bhikṣus, these seven types of persons—I have now spoken to you of the seven persons represented by the simile of water. All that World-Honored Ones together with their disciples should speak, out of great loving-kindness, wishing to grant peace and security to all beings and to enable all to attain liberation—this is it: in secluded dwellings, beneath trees, in open empty places sitting in the open air, you all should practice meditation without slackening. If you do not diligently strive now, you will later be filled with regret. This is my command and instruction."

At that time, the bhikṣus, having heard what the Buddha had spoken, rejoiced and undertook to practice it.
