# 梵志計水淨經
## 梵志計水淨經

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0051

Translated from the Chinese. 失譯

---

## Fascicle 1

Thus have I heard.

At one time, the Bhagavān was on the bank of the Uruvelā River, sitting alone beneath a tree, having just attained complete and perfect awakening. At that time, there was a brahmin who reckoned purity by water. After his midday meal, he wandered about and came to where the Blessed One was. The Blessed One saw from afar the water-reckoning brahmin approaching from a distance. Having seen him, on account of him, the Blessed One addressed the monks:

"If one has twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will fall into an evil rebirth and be born in the Niraya hell. What are the twenty-one? The fetter of wrong view clinging to the mind; unlawful desire; the wrong dharma of deceiving the world; greed; anger; sloth; drowsiness; frivolity; shamelessness; doubt; hatred and resentment; avarice, jealousy, and non-relinquishment; flattery and deceit; excessive shamelessness and disgrace; envy; heightened envy; and the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind. If one has these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will fall into an evil rebirth and be born in the Niraya hell — just as a soiled child's garment whose owner gives it to a dyer or a dyer's apprentice, who soaks it in alkali earth, cow dung, and clay and washes it until it is thoroughly clean: even though the dyer and the dyer's apprentice wash that soiled child's garment with alkali earth, ash, cow dung, and clay until it is thoroughly clean, the child's garment still retains its dark grime. In just this way, if one has these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one falls into an evil rebirth and is born in the Niraya hell. What are the twenty-one? From the fetter of wrong view clinging to the mind up to the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind.

"If one is free of these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will be born in a good destination, in the heavens. What are the twenty-one? From the fetter of wrong view clinging to the mind up to the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind. If one is free of these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will be born in a good destination, in the heavens — just as a fine cloth woven in Vārāṇasī whose owner gives it to a dyer or a dyer's apprentice, who soaks it in alkali earth, ash, and cow dung and washes it until it is thoroughly clean: even though that fine cloth woven in Vārāṇasī is washed by the dyer and the dyer's apprentice with alkali, ash, and cow dung until it is thoroughly clean, the fine cloth woven in Vārāṇasī becomes supremely pure and white. In just this way, if one is free of these twenty-one fetters clinging to the mind, one will be born in a good destination, in the heavens. What are the twenty-one? From the fetter of wrong view up to the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind — one will be born in a good destination, in the heavens.

"The fetter of wrong view clinging to the mind — having seen it, one should abandon it; up to the fetter of heedlessness clinging to the mind — having seen it, one should abandon it. Then, with the mind suffused with loving-kindness, having fully pervaded one direction, one dwells in meditative attainment. Likewise the second, third, and fourth directions, above and below and all quarters everywhere: with the mind suffused with loving-kindness, without duality, without hatred, immeasurably vast and great, boundless and undivided, having fully pervaded all directions, one dwells in meditative attainment. In the same way, with the mind suffused with compassion, with sympathetic joy, and with equanimity, having fully pervaded all directions everywhere, one dwells in meditative attainment. This is the brahmin's washing and purification, inward and outward — not purification by external means."

> "Ascetic Gautama, come bathe in the water!"

> "O brahmin, Gautama's bathing in water
> is for the sake of precepts, of merit, and of liberation throughout all the world."

> "But Gautama, for all the world, bathing in water removes all evil;
> soaking and washing in the pure water — ordinary fools always take delight in this."

> "Dark deeds of darkness cannot be removed by that water.
> What use is water? What does one see from being in water?
> For those who commit evil deeds, what can water purify?
> True and firm purity lies in upholding the precepts and practicing pure conduct;
> one who constantly practices pure conduct properly attains the observance of precepts.
> Not killing, not stealing, not speaking falsely — thus one attains perfect liberation.
> O brahmin, dwell in this and perform all wholesome deeds, and you will attain peace and security.
> O brahmin, why return home? What need have you for water and a home?
> O brahmin, far better to seek the pure and wholesome Dharma —
> what need is there for foul and base water,
> which merely removes dust and external filth?"

"Gautama has thus declared: 'Far better to seek the pure and wholesome Dharma; there is no need for foul and base water, which only removes dust and external filth.' Well said, Gautama! Well said, Gautama! I now take refuge in the Blessed One, the Dharma, and the Saṃgha of monks. Blessed One, I undertake the precepts of an upāsaka. From this day forward, for the remainder of my life, having renounced killing, I take refuge in the Buddha."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The monks, having heard what the Blessed One said, rejoiced and departed.
