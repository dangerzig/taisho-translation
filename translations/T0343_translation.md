# 佛說太子刷護經
## Sūtra of Prince Śuddharakṣa Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0343

Translated from the Chinese. Western Jin dynasty, translated by Dharmarakṣa (竺法護)

---

The Buddha was in the city of Rājagṛha, on Vulture Peak Mountain, together with one thousand two hundred bhikṣus and twelve thousand bodhisattvas, and upāsakas and upāsikās, the deva-kings Brahmā and Śakra, and an immeasurable number of humans, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas, all gathered together.

The son of King Ajātaśatru, a prince named Śuddharakṣa, came from the capital with five hundred sons of ministers and elders, each bearing a golden flower-parasol. They departed the city of Rājagṛha and came together to the Buddha. Having offered the golden flower-parasols to the Buddha, they stepped back and stood with palms joined, bowed their heads to the ground in homage, and then all stood before the Buddha with palms joined.

The prince Śuddharakṣa, son of King Ajātaśatru, addressed the Buddha: "I wish to ask about certain matters. If the Buddha consents to speak, I shall ask. If not, I dare not ask."

The Buddha said: "Ask whatever you wish."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva obtain a beautiful countenance? By what causes is a bodhisattva not born from a woman's womb but born by transformation within a lotus flower? By what causes can a bodhisattva know the events of their own past lives? May the Buddha, in his great kindness, explain this for us."

The Buddha told the prince: "Those who can practice patience without anger will be reborn as beautiful persons. Those who do not engage in licentiousness or have sexual relations with women, when their life draws to an end, whether they have lived for a year, a month, or seven days, in their next life they will be born already knowing the events of their past lives through immeasurable ages."

The prince addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva possess the thirty-two marks? By what causes the eighty excellent characteristics? By what causes do those who see the Buddha's body never tire of gazing upon it?"

The Buddha told the prince: "When one was formerly a bodhisattva, one delighted in giving all manner of things to the Buddhas, bodhisattvas, teachers, parents, and the people, giving to all who came to ask. By this cause one obtains the thirty-two marks. The bodhisattva has a compassionate mind, with pity for all the beings of the ten directions, even the smallest insects and creatures, regarding them as one's own infant child, wishing to liberate them all. By this cause one obtains the eighty excellent characteristics. The bodhisattva regards enemies and parents equally, with a mind of evenness without difference. By this cause, those who see the Buddha never tire of gazing upon him."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva know the profound sūtras, wisdom, and the practice of dhāraṇī? By what causes does one know the peace and comfort of samādhi and mental concentration? By what causes is all that the Buddha teaches excellent and pleasing, so that all who hear it are joyful and accept it with faith?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva delights in writing, accepting, reciting, and studying. By this cause one knows the profound sūtras, wisdom, and attains the practice of dhāraṇī. The bodhisattva always delights in concentrating the mind with right intention. By this cause one attains the peace and comfort of samādhi. The bodhisattva speaks always with sincerity and without deception. By this cause, all that one says is believed by the people, and all who hear it are joyful."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva study the sūtras, hear the Buddha's words, and have all the people believe them? By what causes does one know the sūtras, vinaya, and proper conduct? By what causes is one filially obedient, following the Buddha's teaching without transgression?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva, life after life, is without flattery or deceit. By this cause, when studying the sūtras and hearing the Buddha's words, one knows them all without forgetting. The bodhisattva enters the profound sūtras without fear or terror. By this cause one gains the sūtras and vinaya and knows proper conduct. The bodhisattva, life after life, reveres the Buddha, reveres the sūtras, and reveres the teacher and parents. By this cause one gains wisdom."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes is a bodhisattva, life after life, born near a Buddha? By what causes, when asking the Buddha and praising the sūtras, does one comprehend the wisdom within?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva, life after life, when seeing someone teaching a sūtra, does not interrupt or create disorder and does not criticize. By this cause one is born near a Buddha. The bodhisattva frequently praises the profound sūtras. By this cause one understands the wisdom within."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes is one not born in evil circumstances? By what causes is one born in the heavens? By what causes does one not crave sensual desire?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva, life after life, has faith in the Buddha, faith in the sūtras, and faith in the saṅgha of bhikṣus. By this cause one is not born in the eight difficult conditions. The bodhisattva upholds the precepts without fault. By this cause one is born in the heavens. The bodhisattva knows that the fundamental nature of all dharmas of the sūtras is emptiness. By this cause one does not crave sensual desire."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes are the deeds of the body, the words of the mouth, and the thoughts of the mind of a bodhisattva all pure? By what causes can Māra not find an opening? By what causes do people not dare slander the Buddha, the sūtras, or the saṅgha of bhikṣus?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva attends upon the Buddha, delights in studying the sūtras, and loves the saṅgha of bhikṣus. By this cause one attains purity. The bodhisattva, day and night, practices the path with diligence and without slackening. By this cause Māra cannot find an opening. The bodhisattva speaks always with sincerity. By this cause the people do not dare slander the Buddha, the path of the sūtras, or the saṅgha of bhikṣus."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva have a beautiful and resonant voice like the voice of Brahmā? By what causes does one have the eight kinds of voice? By what causes can one know the thoughts of all people and reply to them all?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva, life after life, speaks with sincerity and without deception. By this cause one has a beautiful and resonant voice like the voice of Brahmā. The bodhisattva, life after life, does not use harsh speech. By this cause one obtains the eight kinds of voice. The bodhisattva, life after life, does not speak divisively or falsely. By this cause one can know and respond to the thoughts of all people."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does one obtain a long lifespan? By what causes is one's body free from illness? By what causes does one's household live in harmony and love, without others causing separation?"

The Buddha told the prince: "Those who do not kill: by this cause they are later reborn as human teachers with long lives. Those who do not strike others with weapons: by this cause they are later reborn free from illness. Those who see people in conflict and delight in mediating and reconciling them: by this cause they are later reborn without others being able to separate them."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva gain abundant wealth and precious treasures that never leave them? By what causes does one not lose one's wealth, nor have it stolen or robbed? By what causes does one attain honor and high position?"

The Buddha told the prince: "Those who do not covet others' wealth: by this cause they are later reborn wealthy and happy. Those who delight in giving without miserliness: by this cause they do not lose their wealth, which only increases. Those who, seeing others prosper, feel no envy, and do not become arrogant: by this cause they are later reborn in high and honored positions."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does one obtain the divine eye of penetrating vision? By what causes does one obtain the divine ear of comprehensive hearing? By what causes can one know the life and death of the beings of the world?"

The Buddha told the prince: "By delighting in lighting lamps before the Buddha: by this cause one is later reborn with the divine eye of penetrating vision. By delighting in offering music at the Buddha's temple: by this cause one is later reborn with the divine ear of comprehensive hearing. The bodhisattva delights in concentrating the mind, entering samādhi and attaining dhyāna: by this cause one knows the changes of life and death in the world."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva obtain the supernatural flight of the four bases of power? By what causes can a bodhisattva remember the events of immeasurable past kalpas? By what causes does a bodhisattva attain the three liberations and the Buddha's parinirvāṇa?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva delights in giving, always providing carriages, horses, donkeys, mules, elephants, camels, sandals, and boats to the Buddhas, the saṅgha of bhikṣus, and to the people. By this cause one obtains the supernatural flight of the four bases of power. The bodhisattva constantly focuses the mind on all the Buddhas' samādhis, studying from them and delighting in teaching others. By this cause one can remember the events of immeasurable past kalpas. The bodhisattva attains the stage of non-retrogression, with a mind free from attachment. By this cause one can sever the root of birth and death, attain the Buddha's path, and enter parinirvāṇa."

The prince again addressed the Buddha: "By what causes does a bodhisattva preside over a buddha-land? By what causes does one preside over a saṅgha of bhikṣus? By what causes does one's radiance illuminate the ten directions?"

The Buddha told the prince: "The bodhisattva originally makes great vows. By this cause one presides over a buddha-land. The bodhisattva delights in giving to those who beg, and delights in teaching the people to practice the six pāramitās. By this cause one later obtains a saṅgha of bhikṣus. The bodhisattva delights in making parasols of the seven treasures as offerings to the Buddha. By this cause one's radiance illuminates the ten directions."

When the Buddha had explained these matters in detail for the prince, the prince was joyful, and all five hundred sons of elders were also greatly joyful. Prince Śuddharakṣa again addressed the Buddha: "May it be that in my future lives, I shall receive all that the Buddha has taught, be able to practice it, and have all my wishes fulfilled."

The Buddha then gave a great laugh, and five-colored light came forth from his mouth. The Buddha told the Bodhisattva Maitreya: "Listen as I explain. Prince Śuddharakṣa the bodhisattva and the sons of elders, in past lives, all made offerings to one hundred and eight hundred million Buddhas and all practiced the bodhisattva path. Long ago, in the time of the Buddha Dīpaṃkara, these five hundred were all disciples of the Buddha; they are those I taught. In future lives, they shall all meet together and, after making offerings to six hundred million Buddhas, in a future kalpa named Mahāpāramitā, during that single kalpa, these five hundred shall successively become Buddhas, all under the same name: Jñānaketudhvaja. When they become Buddhas in the future, their lands shall be like the land of the Buddha Amitābha, and the bodhisattvas who come and go and fly about shall all be like those of the land of the Buddha Amitābha. If there are people who hear this sūtra and rejoice in it with faith, they shall all be reborn in the land of the Buddha Amitābha."

When the Buddha had finished speaking, Prince Śuddharakṣa and the five hundred sons of elders, all the bhikṣus and bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas and upāsikās, devas, humans, ghosts, spirits, and nāgas were all greatly joyful, bowed to the Buddha in homage, and departed.
