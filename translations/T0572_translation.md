# 佛說長者法志妻經
## Sūtra of the Wife of the Elder Dharmacintā, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0572

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was in Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana Monastery of Anāthapiṇḍada, together with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus and ten thousand bodhisattvas.

At dawn the Buddha put on his robe, took his bowl, and entered the city to beg for food. The bhikṣus and bodhisattvas all attended him. The devas, nāgas, spirits, gandharvas, kinnara spirits, garuḍa spirits, mountain spirits, and the spirit-king of music all scattered flowers and burned incense, played celestial music, and praised the Buddha's merit. They spoke this verse:

> "Through countless billions of kalpas,
> His accumulated practice is beyond measure.
> With compassion for all beings,
> He causes them to undertake the great path.
> The three realms are like an illusion;
> All things are utterly empty.
> The one who comprehends this wisdom
> Liberates all in the ten directions.
> The thirty-two marks shine bright,
> The eighty refinements are beautiful.
> From his mouth come myriad billions of sounds;
> His merit adorns his form.
> Though dwelling in the three realms,
> He reveals the three places of practice.
> The three defilements are now extinguished;
> He removes the calamities of the three times.
> His mind is like a bright moon-pearl,
> Dwelling in desire yet free of attachment.
> His conduct is equal, beyond love and hate;
> Toward all things he is without preference."

Then the people, hearing this praise of the Buddha's merit, gathered from across the country to watch the Buddha approach. His every movement was measured and composed. The majesty of his countenance was like the moon among the stars, like the rising sun illuminating all under heaven without obstruction, like Lord Brahmā amid the devas, like Śakra in the Trāyastriṃśa palace as the most honored among the gods, like Mount Sumeru standing in the great ocean, immovable amid the four continents. They leaped with joy, joined their palms, and took refuge.

The Buddha arrived outside the gate of the elder Dharmacintā and proceeded into the inner hall, emitting a great radiance that illuminated the ten directions.

At that time the elder's wife had adorned her bed and seat. She had ornamented her body with many jewels and necklaces, had applied sandalwood fragrance, and her face was painted and beautiful, resplendent in five colors, thinking that beauty could be kept forever. When servants committed the slightest error, she beat them cruelly without distinguishing right from wrong.

From afar she saw the Buddha's radiance, surpassing that of the sun. She thought to herself: "This brilliance is unlike the sun or moon, or the light of Śakra, Brahmā, or the devas. Its brightness is cool, bringing boundless peace. Bathed in it, I am free from all affliction, neither hungry nor thirsty, naturally satisfied. Why should I continue to wield the rod against my servants?" She hurried toward the inner hall and beheld the World-Honored One, whose excellent marks and majestic radiance were beyond all comparison. His faculties were still and settled, without any decline. He was like a mountain of the seven treasures, gleaming and towering. Feeling shame, fear, sorrow, and joy all at once, she bowed at the Buddha's feet: "I repent of my offenses and wrongdoings. What I have done is inexcusable. Being a woman, I was unable to restrain myself. Anger and pleasure were governed only by my whims. I now confess my offenses and dare not conceal them."

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! You have gained a great benefit and been freed from all decline. Having seen the faults of your present body, you shall reform the past and cultivate the future. A human body is hard to obtain, and the Buddha's sūtras are hard to encounter, appearing only once in a billion ages. Why do you fall into a woman's body? Because lust, vanity, and coquetry reside within. Unable to cultivate the body, one gives free rein to the mind, with jealousy and excessive speech, attachment to appearance and reliance on pride. The world is impermanent. Wealth, power, and authority last but a moment. You should regard those below you as you would your own children. Wealth and poverty wax and wane like the moon. They are like the rising and setting of the sun, like the arising of water, fire, and wind: none endure for long. All paths, worldly and spiritual, arise from the mind. The heavens, the human realm, Naraka, the realm of hungry ghosts, and the animal realm: all come from oneself alone. Buddhas, Gods of Gods, pratyekabuddhas, and śrāvakas are likewise. This body of mine, with its thirty-two marks and eighty refinements, penetrating and seeing the ten directions: all of this comes from understanding and realization."

The woman, hearing the Buddha's words, was immeasurably joyful. Again she took refuge and reproached her own blindness, accepting the teaching and receiving what she had never before heard, the unsurpassed instruction.

The Buddha said: "Practice the meaning of the ten good deeds: the body does not kill, steal, or engage in sexual misconduct. The mouth does not speak falsely, with a forked tongue, frivolously, or harshly. The mind is free from jealousy, anger, and ignorance. You should follow the six perfections: generosity, keeping precepts, patience, diligence, one-pointed concentration, and wisdom. Follow the four immeasurable minds: kindness, compassion, joy, and equanimity, broadly extending great compassion. You will of yourself attain the thirty-two marks and the eighty refinements. As for your servants, you should teach them about hardship, birth and death, and the consequences of merit and wrongdoing. Show them the perils of the three evil paths. Instruct them in the Dharma, precepts, and principles of the Way: this is superior to beating them with rods.

"There are four things that constitute true adornment. What are these four? First, firm faith. Second, the precepts. Third, samādhi. Fourth, wisdom. These are the four. A bodhisattva adorns herself through these, with mind set on the Great Vehicle. There is neither male nor female, just as in illusion. What a painter creates follows the intention and is accomplished accordingly. Understanding the wisdom of emptiness, all things are originally pure. Attaining the bodiless body and the four fearlessnesses, with the four kinds of unguarded action, one walks alone through the three realms and liberates all."

The woman, hearing the Buddha's teaching, her mind opened and she leaped with joy. She immediately generated the mind of unsurpassed true enlightenment and attained the stage of non-retrogression.

Then Śakra came and stood behind the Buddha. He said to the woman: "The buddha-path is difficult to attain. It would be better to seek to transform from female to male, or to become the sun, the moon, a heavenly lord, or a wheel-turning holy king."

At this the woman spoke in verse:

> "Heavenly lord, sun, moon, and king,
> Wheel-turning sovereign of the four continents:
> Their power and authority last but a brief while,
> Not to be relied upon for long.
> Greatness is like the morning dew;
> In a dream one sees things,
> But upon waking all has vanished,
> And one knows not where it has gone.
> The five aggregates are like illusion;
> The three realms are made by oneself.
> The three times are all equal;
> The mind of the Way has no peer.
> Truly understanding this,
> Who is male? What is female?"
> Śakra, hearing these words,
> Fell silent and had nothing to say.

The Buddha said: "Excellent! Excellent! It is truly as you say. The three places are like illusion, like a shadow, like an echo, like a mirage, like the moon in water, like a plantain tree. Ordinary people do not understand and posit a self, and so they rely on the three realms, unable to save themselves."

The woman's mind was immediately liberated, and she transformed into a man. He leaped into the sky and descended to bow at the Buddha's feet.

The Buddha told her: "In a future age, after kalpas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, you shall become a Buddha named Vimalārāja Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, Well-Gone, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Tamer of Persons, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, God of Gods, in an age called Light-Purity."

At that time the assembly, together with countless thousands of devas and humans, seeing these transformations, all generated the mind of unsurpassed true enlightenment.

Then all the servants of the elder's wife came forward and said to the Buddha: "Is there any difference between the noble and the lowly?"

The Buddha said: "Originally all is nothing; it follows where the mind abides. Though one is a servant, if one generates the mind for the Way, one can become a Buddha. Though one is noble and exalted, if one gives rein to arrogance, one will not escape the evil destinies of Naraka, the realm of hungry ghosts, and the animal realm. Like the waxing and waning of the moon, like the flourishing and withering of a tree, all things are impermanent and none can be relied upon. Only the deep wisdom of the Way can be kept forever, like empty space, with neither advance nor retreat."

Then all the servants leaped with joy and generated the great resolve for the Way. They transformed into men and attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas.

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The five aggregates have no abode. The six sense-faculties have no root. The twelve links of dependent origination have no beginning or end. The four elements are merely borrowed conditions. What is there that constitutes a person?"

When the Buddha had spoken thus, all were filled with joy.
