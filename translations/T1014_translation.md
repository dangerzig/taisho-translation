# 無量門破魔陀羅尼經
## Infinite Gateways Māra-Crushing Dhāraṇī Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1014

Translated from the Chinese. Liu Song dynasty, translated by Guṇavṛddhi (功德直) and Xuànchàng (玄暢).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was staying at the Great Forest Storied Pavilion in Vaiśālī, together with a great assembly of twelve hundred fifty monks. At that time the World-Honored One silently reflected: "My work of transformation is nearly complete. I should relinquish my life-force. After three months I shall enter parinirvāṇa." Then the Tathāgata, right there in that place, commanded the Elder Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "Go and announce throughout the great-thousandfold world system to all the monk assemblies, causing them to gather at once." Maudgalyāyana addressed the Buddha: "I respectfully receive this instruction." In one instant he reached the summit of Mount Sumeru and proclaimed in a great voice that resounded throughout the great-thousandfold world system. Then forty hundred-thousand monks suddenly came and assembled at the Great Forest Storied Pavilion. When those monks had seen the World-Honored One, they prostrated at the Buddha's feet and withdrew to stand to one side.

Then Śāriputra gave rise to this thought: "I too should, by the power of supernatural penetration, go throughout the three-thousandfold great-thousandfold world system to wherever monks reside, and announce to all those practicing the śrāvaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, and those who study the Mahāyāna, that they should all assemble at the Great Forest Storied Pavilion." Then Śāriputra, by his supernatural power, went throughout the three-thousandfold great-thousandfold world system to wherever monks resided and announced to all those practicing the śrāvaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, and those seeking the Mahāyāna: "You should now all assemble at the Great Forest Storied Pavilion." Then the great assembly, as instructed, came and bowed, and withdrew to stand to one side.

Then the World-Honored One told the bodhisattvas: "You should all come and assemble at once." Then Amoghadarśin Bodhisattva, Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva, Durgati-pariśodhana Bodhisattva, Sarva-daurmanasya-tamomardana Bodhisattva, Sarvadāna Bodhisattva, Sarva-nīvaraṇa-viṣkambhin Bodhisattva, Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, Gandhahastin Bodhisattva, Pratibhānottama Bodhisattva, and Maitreya Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva, all came together to the Tathāgata's presence.

Then the World-Honored One told them: "Good sons! You should now go to the buddha-fields of the ten directions, as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, and tell all the bodhisattva-mahāsattvas who have attained the patience of non-arising dharmas and those who abide in non-retrogression, and all other bodhisattvas up to the great bodhisattvas of the one-lifetime successor stage, to come and assemble."

Then those bodhisattvas received the holy command and went throughout the ten directions to summon the bodhisattvas. When those great beings heard the announcement, they all said "Excellent!" and respectfully assented. Then ninety billion hundred-thousand nayutas of bodhisattvas, all of whom had attained the patience of non-arising dharmas; three billion hundred-thousand nayutas of bodhisattvas who abided in non-retrogression; and further billion hundred-thousand great bodhisattvas, each of whom was a one-lifetime successor: all these bodhisattvas, by the power of supernatural penetration, in one instant all came and assembled at the Great Forest Storied Pavilion. When the great assembly had seen the World-Honored One, they prostrated at the Buddha's feet and withdrew to stand to one side.

Then Śāriputra, seeing the bodhisattvas of the ten directions all assembled, gave rise to this thought: "I shall now request the Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly Enlightened One, and ask about these characteristics. By my questioning, may all bodhisattvas' doubts be severed and may they attain profound eloquent wisdom. May they hear the wondrous dharma from buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges and never for one instant forget it, until they attain supreme enlightenment.

"All bodhisattvas should cultivate these four pure and wondrous practices. What are the four? First, purity of sentient beings. Second, purity of dharma. Third, purity of eloquence. Fourth, purity of buddha-fields.

"Furthermore, may bodhisattvas generate four kinds of right mindfulness. What are the four? Right mindfulness of the body, right mindfulness of speech, right mindfulness of mind, and right mindfulness of birth.

"Furthermore, there are four things by which one gradually enters deeply into the dhāraṇī gates. What are the four? First, attaining inexhaustible eloquence and skillfully entering the dhāraṇī gate. Second, knowing beings' faculties through skillful means and thereby entering the dhāraṇī gate. Third, knowing conditioned karmic fruition through skillful means and thereby entering the dhāraṇī gate. Fourth, attaining profound patience of non-arising dharmas and thereby swiftly and skillfully entering the dhāraṇī gate."

Then Śāriputra, in accordance with his reflections, addressed the Buddha: "May these bodhisattvas cultivate the pure practices. May the World-Honored One compassionately teach them so they may practice."

Then the Buddha told Śāriputra: "These practitioners have generated the vast mind. If they wish to cultivate the bodhisattva dharma, and further wish to be unattached to dharma, neither grasping nor rejecting, these practitioners should uphold and recite these wondrous and divine dhāraṇī phrases:

*Aṇi akī makī samantamukī yukteni lukteni lukti-sabhī hilī kapi kapasi salī sarva-bhāvatī hilā hilī hilī hilīlī hilā hilī jātī javane jarā-jarāne aṭṭati arāṇe nivṛtti nibhattāne nirddhātini hā vimalī śudhānī parākṛti-tipānī bhāvibhāvane asaṅghidāme vipulā-sabhī saṃkarīṣaṇī tilī tilī mahā-tītilī yākibhāvatī jālī ajālī madhālī samājālī jiddhā-sandhi aṣṭakī asaṅga-vihālī asaṅga-nihālī vihāla-vimālī nihāla-śudhāni jikhiṃ-sumī dāmī dā-mabhāvatī mahā-sabhī samanta-sabhī vipula-sabhī vipula-lāmī samantamukī sarvatra-okatī anajñethi dhāraṇī dhāraṇī nidāna gudī svāhā*

Then the World-Honored One told Śāriputra: "The dhāraṇī I have just spoken, these bodhisattvas and practitioners should all uphold, recite, and master it. They should not grasp the conditioned or unconditioned. In all dharmas they should be unstained and unattached, neither slandering them nor generating aversion, neither embracing them nor diligently seeking to cultivate them. If they grasp conditioned or unconditioned dharmas, they should swiftly distance themselves and not cling.

"One does not see dharmas coming together or scattering, does not see dharmas arising or ceasing, does not see dharmas as having past, future, or present, does not see dharmas increasing or decreasing. One does not see the twelve links of dependent origination arising or ceasing. One neither expounds dharma nor does not expound it.

"Bodhisattvas should always maintain right mindfulness: the buddhas are neither form nor non-form, neither mark nor non-mark, neither meaning nor non-meaning, neither precept nor non-precept, neither concentration nor non-concentration, neither wisdom nor non-wisdom, neither liberation nor non-liberation, neither knowledge-and-vision of liberation nor non-knowledge-and-vision of liberation, neither of noble birth nor not of noble birth, neither with retinue nor without retinue, neither practice nor non-practice, neither arrival nor non-arrival, neither timely nor untimely, neither aggregates, sense-bases, and elements nor non-aggregates, sense-bases, and elements, neither knowledge nor non-knowledge, neither teaching dharma nor non-teaching dharma, neither self-purity nor other-purity, neither purity of beings nor impurity, neither self-benefit nor other-benefit, neither dharma nor discipline, neither purity of body and speech nor purity of mind, nor purity of past, future, and present conduct, neither for oneself nor for others."

He told Śāriputra: "One who speaks thus, this is named the bodhisattva's mindfulness of the Buddha, which encompasses all dharmas. It is named 'arriving forever at the subtle and wondrous dhāraṇī of equal accumulation of all dharmas.' It is also named 'the dhāraṇī gate of the foremost eloquence of ultimate truth, the most supreme and unobstructed.' It is also named 'the dhāraṇī gate of fulfilling all aspirations.' It is also named 'the dhāraṇī gate of assuredly attaining one portion of enlightenment through various profound samādhis.' It is also named 'the dhāraṇī gate of eloquently gathering all other good roots.' It is also named 'the dhāraṇī gate of true practice in the wondrous meaning of the nature and characteristics of the dharma treasury.' It is also named 'the dhāraṇī gate of skillful means in the conditioned that transcends and subdues all māras.'

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! This dhāraṇī is named 'the teaching of the immeasurable dharma gate.' These practitioners will all attain its profound meaning and benefit and swiftly attain the unsurpassed path of enlightenment. Why? This dhāraṇī is named the treasury of profound dharma of all buddhas' definitive Mahāyāna and all merit. This place is named the unstained dharma-precept dhāraṇī gate that crushes the birth-and-death practices of all sentient beings."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva practitioner who is endowed with four things attains the dhāraṇī. What are the four? Not being attached to impure desire; not generating jealousy toward any sentient being; giving material possessions without regret or grudging; and day and night always delighting in cultivating wholesome dharmas. Śāriputra! A bodhisattva endowed with these four things attains the dhāraṇī."

Then the World-Honored One spoke these verses:

> Abandon that impure desire,
> And cast off the base conduct of Māra.
> Such desire and its causes
> Lead straight to the hells.
>
> Toward others' kin, fame, and gain,
> Do not generate jealous thoughts.
> View beings with eyes of compassion,
> And attain a great, majestic, wondrous form.
>
> When beings quarrel and contend,
> It all arises from this body.
> Therefore one should abandon attachment;
> One will surely attain the dhāraṇī.
>
> Day and night, always seek the dharma,
> With one mind delighting in enlightenment.
> The practitioner will then attain
> This dhāraṇī.
> One should always practice
> These four things in one's presence.

Then the Tathāgata told Śāriputra: "These four things should always be studied and cultivated. Furthermore, Śāriputra! There are four more things; the practitioner who is endowed with these attains the dhāraṇī. What are the four? First, one should always diligently cultivate the forest-dwelling practice. Second, one should be capable of upholding profound dharma patience. Third, one should not be attached to gain, fame, or reputation. Fourth, one should abandon all things, able to give up all places of attachment and desire, even body and life, let alone other possessions. Śāriputra! A bodhisattva endowed with these four things attains the dhāraṇī."

Then the World-Honored One spoke these verses:

> Always cultivate the forest-dwelling life;
> Abide in diligence without negligence.
> Diligently practice the mind of profound patience,
> As urgently as rescuing one's burning head.
> This practice is the most supreme,
> Praised by all the buddhas.
>
> Do not, toward others' retinue,
> Reputation, gain,
> Or material possessions,
> Generate a mind of craving and desire.
> Be of few desires and content,
> Like a bird that stores nothing.
>
> Having obtained a human body,
> One should always cultivate all virtues.
> How wonderful to attain the buddha-dharma!
> Renounce the household, the root of suffering.
>
> Pride and all afflictions
> Have already been purified.
> One should diligently honor
> And respect the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha.
>
> Craving for gain destroys mindful wisdom,
> And also ruins faith, precepts, and dharma.
> Enlightenment becomes very distant;
> Therefore one should abandon craving.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! The practitioner should accomplish four more things to attain the dhāraṇī. What are the four? There are eight syllables with meaning. What are the eight?

"The syllable *a* is the foremost meaning: all dharmas enter the meaning of non-self.

"The syllable *la* refers to the marks of excellence: these marks enter the meaning of the tathāgata's dharmakāya.

"The syllable *bha* refers to the infant: the dharma of infancy definitively enters the meaning of intelligence and wisdom.

"The syllable *ja* refers to birth, aging, sickness, and death: these afflictions enter non-arising and non-ceasing.

"The syllable *ka* refers to the creation of karma: it enters non-karmic-fruition.

"The syllable *dha* refers to dhāraṇī, the dhāraṇī dharma: emptiness, signlessness, and wishlessness enter the dharmadhātu.

"The syllable *śa* refers to *śamatha*: *vipaśyanā* and *śamatha* enter all dharmas.

"The syllable *kṣa*: all dharmas are non-abiding, inexhaustible, indestructible, and without before or after. All such dharmas enter nirvāṇa.

"These eight syllables all enter this meaning. Bodhisattvas should follow and enter it; this is named the first practice.

"One should skillfully write out and diligently uphold this dhāraṇī scripture; this is named the practitioner's second practice. Every half-month one should read and recite this dhāraṇī dharma; this is named the practitioner's third practice. The practitioner should always single-mindedly contemplate this dhāraṇī dharma, gladdening all sentient beings, guiding their minds and making them earnest; this is named the practitioner's fourth practice.

"Śāriputra! The practitioner endowed with these four things attains this dhāraṇī."

Then the World-Honored One spoke these verses:

> Write and uphold the dhāraṇī;
> Contemplate the eight syllables.
> Every half-month,
> Single-mindedly and constantly recite them.
> Sentient beings, in this place,
> Cultivating and continually remembering,
> Draw near the vast
> And profoundly wise enlightenment,
> And will definitively see in person
> All the buddhas of the ten directions.
> Therefore in this dharma
> One should cultivate a diligent, studious mind.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "A bodhisattva-mahāsattva has four dharma-benefits that they should always cultivate single-mindedly. What are the four? First, being rightly mindful of all the buddhas of the ten directions. Second, being utterly free from all māra-activity. Third, such a person swiftly separates from karmic obstructions. Fourth, they swiftly attain the unbroken, subtle, and profoundly eloquent speech. Śāriputra! These four dharma-benefits the practitioner should single-mindedly and diligently cultivate through this dhāraṇī, and will surely obtain."

Then the World-Honored One spoke these verses:

> Be rightly mindful of all the buddhas;
> The māra host will not detect you.
> All karmic obstructions
> Will be swiftly left far behind.
> You will also attain immeasurable billions
> Of eloquent dhāraṇī.

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "Long ago in past lives, through immeasurable, boundless, incalculable kalpas, at that time there was a buddha named Ratnajid-agniskandha-prabhā Tathāgata, Arhat, Perfectly Enlightened One, complete with the ten epithets, who appeared in the world.

"Śāriputra! When Ratnajid-agniskandha-prabhā Tathāgata was about to enter parinirvāṇa, there was a cakravartin king named Nakṣatradhāra, endowed with the seven treasures, who ruled the four continents. That king had a son named Acintyaguṇottama. When that prince reached the age of sixteen hundred, he first heard this dhāraṇī dharma from Ratnajid-agniskandha-prabhā Buddha. He immediately understood, and devoted himself to diligent cultivation. For seventy thousand years, day and night without rest, he never wearied, and his eyes never closed.

"For seventy thousand years the prince relinquished body, life, wealth, and the throne. For another seventy thousand years, alone in a quiet place, he diligently practiced walking meditation, his sides never touching the ground. From nine billion hundred-thousand nayutas of tathāgatas he heard the dharma, and upheld, recited, and mastered it all.

"The prince then went forth and cultivated the path. For ninety thousand years he upheld and accomplished this immeasurable dhāraṇī gate. When that monk had accomplished this, he broadly proclaimed its meaning to all beings. In that very life, the monk-prince converted eighty billion nayutas of hundred-thousand beings, establishing them all on the path of supreme perfect enlightenment and in the stage of non-retrogression.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! At that time in the assembly there was an elder's son named Candradhvaja. That elder's son heard this immeasurable dhāraṇī gate from the monk dharma-teacher. Having heard it, he immediately rejoiced and received it with reverence. By the good-root merit of Candradhvaja's rejoicing, he encountered ninety billion buddhas and world-honored ones, made reverent offerings, and attained this most supreme dhāraṇī dharma. In all discourse he was the most excellent, and he attained the foremost unbroken eloquence.

"The monk-prince, having made offerings to those buddhas through three kalpas, attained the unsurpassed path.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! Do not have doubts. That monk-prince dharma-teacher of that time is today Amitāyus Tathāgata. The Candradhvaja of that time is Dīpaṃkara Buddha."

The Buddha told Śāriputra: "I, together with the bodhisattvas of this Bhadrakalpa, all heard this dhāraṇī dharma and generated rejoicing. By the good-root merit of this, throughout forty hundred-thousand kalpas we always diligently turned away from the dharma of birth and death. From ninety billion tathāgatas we attained the foremost dhāraṇī, with discourse of surpassing excellence and supremely outstanding eloquence.

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! Therefore, if practitioners wish to swiftly attain the unsurpassed path, they should uphold this dhāraṇī dharma. If they are unable to uphold, write, or recite it, they should simply generate rejoicing. Why? By this good root, the practitioner will surely attain the stage of non-retrogression and reach the unsurpassed path. How much more so for those who uphold, write, recite, and broadly expound it to others! The merit they obtain, except by the tathāgata, cannot be fathomed by any sentient being. How much more so for those who contemplate it and broadly explain it to others!"

"Furthermore," he told Śāriputra, "if a practitioner single-mindedly and continually recites this dhāraṇī, there are eight yakṣas dwelling in the Snow Mountains who will guard and protect that practitioner day and night, removing all misfortune and increasing their power. What are the eight? First, Śūra. Second, Sthira. Third, Prabhūta. Fourth, Nārāyaṇa-bala. Fifth, Nalendra. Sixth, Durdarśa. Seventh, Karāla. Eighth, Subāhu."

He told Śāriputra: "The practitioner should, on fine white silk, paint images of these eight spirit-deities in vivid colors, making them extremely pure. No glue derived from sentient beings should be used.

"When the practitioner wishes to recite this sūtra, they should first bathe and put on clean garments. With single-minded intent, invoke these eight spirit-deities and set out for them various fragrant and pure food and drink, various fine incenses, scattered flowers, flower garlands, and pure oil lamps as offerings. The practitioner should further paint a circular seat on the ground, sit upon it, place the right knee on the ground, hold an incense burner, and with one mind compassionately think of immeasurable sentient beings. Softly recite the dhāraṇī seven times, and the eight spirit-deities will manifest their bodies.

"In the desire heavens there are bodhisattvas. These eight great beings, when they see the practitioner reverently reciting this dhāraṇī, also constantly protect and remember them and increase their power. What are the eight? Samantāloka Bodhisattva, Jñānaprabhā Bodhisattva, Sūryaprabhā Bodhisattva, Praśna Bodhisattva, Sarvapraṇidhāna-paripūrṇa Bodhisattva, Mahābala Bodhisattva, Nakṣatrarāja Bodhisattva, and Sumati Bodhisattva.

"These eight bodhisattvas and eight spirit-deities cause the practitioner to cultivate single-mindedly and attain the dharma-benefit and power of the dhāraṇī. They cause all sentient beings to love and remember this practitioner, and all needs are fulfilled as wished, without want. Within a hundred yojanas, there will be no misfortune.

"Such a practitioner should cultivate the dhāraṇī, generating true vows, remembering it with diligent effort, delighting in all wholesome dharmas, distributing charity, precepts, and equanimity broadly and generously, regardless of amount, without any grudging.

"The practitioner who upholds this dhāraṇī should also honor the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha, constantly generating reverence and awe toward the Three Jewels, and single-mindedly cultivate profound dharma patience."

When the World-Honored One taught this dhāraṇī, three thousand times the sands of the Ganges of hundred-thousand billion nayutas of bodhisattvas all attained this dhāraṇī gate named "Teaching the Immeasurable," and also realized the stage of non-retrogression, destined to attain supreme perfect enlightenment. Sixty *vimbara* devas and humans who had not yet generated the aspiration for supreme enlightenment all now generated the aspiration for the unsurpassed path.

Then Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is this sūtra called? How should it be practiced?"

He told Śāriputra: "This sūtra is named the 'Immeasurable Gateway Dhāraṇī'; uphold it thus. It is also named the 'Crushing All Māras Dhāraṇī'; uphold it thus. It is also named the 'Bodhisattva's One-Portion Transcendent-Mind Dhāraṇī'; uphold it thus. It is also named the 'One-Portion Attaining Omniscience Dhāraṇī'; uphold it thus."

When the World-Honored One had finished teaching this sūtra, Śāriputra and the great śrāvakas, the bodhisattvas, humans, devas, asuras, garuḍas, gandharvas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, and all the world, having heard the Buddha's teaching, rejoiced and leapt with joy, received it reverently, paid homage, and departed.
