# 四十二章經
## Sūtra in Forty-Two Sections

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0784

Translated from the Chinese. Later Han dynasty, translated by Kāśyapa Mātaṅga (迦葉摩騰) and Dharmaratna (法蘭)

---

In the past, Emperor Xiaoming of the Han dreamed at night of a divine being whose body was of golden color, with the light of the sun around its neck, flying before the palace hall. His heart was filled with delight and he was greatly pleased. The next day he asked his assembled ministers: "What god was this?"

The learned Fù Yì said: "Your servant has heard that in India there is one who has attained the path, called the Buddha, who can rise lightly into the air and fly. This was probably his spirit."

Thereupon the emperor understood and immediately dispatched the envoys Zhang Qian, the imperial guard officer Qin Jing, the academician's disciple Wang Zun, and others, twelve persons in all, to the country of the Great Yuezhi, where they copied the Buddha's sūtra in forty-two sections. It was kept in the fourteenth stone casket. Thereupon stūpas and monasteries were erected, and from then on the Dharma and its path spread and circulated, with monasteries established everywhere. The distant peoples who submitted and wished to become subjects were beyond counting. The land was peaceful and tranquil, and all sentient beings have been recipients of this grace, which continues to this day without end.

The Buddha said: "Leaving family and kin to go forth from home for the sake of the path, one is called a śramaṇa. One constantly practices the two hundred and fifty precepts, and for the four truths one practices and advances. One's aspiration is for purity, and one accomplishes arhatship. The arhat can fly and transform, can extend the lifespan, and can shake heaven and earth. Next is the anāgāmin; the anāgāmin, at the end of life, ascends in spirit to the nineteenth heaven, and there attains arhatship. Next is the sakṛdāgāmin; the sakṛdāgāmin ascends once and returns once, and then attains arhatship. Next is the srotāpanna; the srotāpanna dies seven times and is born seven times, and then attains arhatship. When desire and craving are cut off, it is like having one's four limbs severed: one no longer uses them."

The Buddha said: "Shave the beard and hair to become a śramaṇa. Receive the Dharma-path. Abandon worldly possessions and beg only for what is sufficient. Eat one meal a day and rest beneath a single tree for a single night. Be careful not to do so a second time! What makes people foolish and depraved is desire and craving."

The Buddha said: "Sentient beings do good through ten deeds and do evil through ten deeds. Three of the body, four of the mouth, three of the mind. The three of the body are: killing, stealing, and sexual misconduct. The four of the mouth are: divisive speech, harsh words, false speech, and frivolous talk. The three of the mind are: jealousy, anger, and delusion, with disbelief in the Three Honored Ones and taking what is false to be true. An upāsaka who practices five things without laxity or backsliding, and progresses to the ten things, will certainly attain the path."

The Buddha said: "If a person has many faults yet does not repent, and suddenly halts the mind, the offenses come to settle upon the body, like water flowing to the sea, which naturally becomes deep and vast. But if one commits evil, knows it is wrong, corrects the fault and attains the good, the offenses daily diminish, and eventually one attains the path."

The Buddha said: "If someone is foolish and considers me to be not good, I use the four immeasurables to compassionately protect and aid that person. If the person comes again with evil, I again respond with good. The atmosphere of merit and virtue always remains here; the atmosphere of harm and grave misfortune rebounds upon that person."

Someone heard that the Buddha guarded the path with great benevolence and compassion, responding to evil with good, and so came to revile him. The Buddha was silent and did not answer, feeling pity for the person's ignorance and the madness and foolishness that drove him. When the reviling ceased, the Buddha asked: "If you bring a gift to someone and the person does not accept it, does the gift return to you?" He said: "It returns to me." "Now you have reviled me, but I too do not accept it. You yourself carry it back; the calamity rests upon your own body! It is like an echo responding to a sound, like a shadow following a form. In the end there is no escaping it. Be careful not to commit evil."

The Buddha said: "An evil person who harms a worthy one is like looking up at the sky and spitting: the spit does not soil the sky but returns to soil one's own body. It is like throwing dust against the wind: the dust does not soil the other person but blows back upon oneself. A worthy person cannot be destroyed; the calamity will certainly destroy oneself."

The Buddha said: "One who practices the path should strive for universal love. The greatest generosity is to broadly bestow compassion and virtue. If one guards one's aspiration and serves the path, one's blessings are very great. When one sees another person making an offering for the path and helps with joy, one also receives a blessing."

Someone asked: "Would that person's blessing not be diminished?"

The Buddha said: "It is like a torch. Thousands and hundreds of people each come with a torch to take its fire, to cook food and dispel darkness. That fire remains as before. Blessings are just like this."

The Buddha said: "Feeding a hundred ordinary people is not equal to feeding one good person. Feeding a thousand good people is not equal to feeding one person who upholds the five precepts. Feeding ten thousand who uphold the five precepts is not equal to feeding one srotāpanna. Feeding a million srotāpannas is not equal to feeding one sakṛdāgāmin. Feeding ten million sakṛdāgāmins is not equal to feeding one anāgāmin. Feeding a hundred million anāgāmins is not equal to feeding one arhat. Feeding a billion arhats is not equal to feeding one pratyekabuddha. Feeding ten billion pratyekabuddhas is not equal to using the teaching of the Three Honored Ones to deliver one's parents in a single lifetime. Teaching one's parents through a hundred billion lifetimes is not equal to feeding one who studies to become a Buddha, aspiring to save all sentient beings. Feeding good people brings the deepest and most weighty blessings. Among ordinary people who make offerings to heaven and earth, ghosts and spirits, none can compare with being filial to one's parents. The two parents are the most sacred."

The Buddha said: "There are five difficult things in the world: for the poor to give generously is difficult; for the powerful and wealthy to study the path is difficult; to control fate and not die is difficult; to obtain and behold the Buddha's sūtras is difficult; and to be born in the age of a Buddha is difficult."

A śramaṇa asked the Buddha: "By what conditions does one attain the path? How does one come to know one's past lives?"

The Buddha said: "The path has no form. Knowing it intellectually is of no benefit. What is essential is to guard one's aspiration and practice. It is like polishing a mirror: when the grime is removed, brightness remains, and one naturally sees one's own form. Sever desire and guard emptiness, and one sees the truth of the path and knows one's past lives."

The Buddha said: "What is good? Only practicing the path is good. What is greatest? When the aspiration accords with the path, that is greatest. What has the most power? Patience is the strongest. The patient one is without enmity and is surely honored by all. What is brightest? When the grime of the mind is removed and evil conduct is extinguished, when inside one is pure and without blemish; before heaven and earth existed, and reaching to the present day, all things in the ten directions, even those sprouts not yet seen, there is nothing one does not know, nothing one does not see, nothing one does not hear. Attaining omniscience: can this not be called brightness?"

The Buddha said: "People who harbor desire and craving cannot see the path. It is like muddy water into which five colors of dye are cast: though one stirs it vigorously and all the people come and look down into the water, none can perceive their own reflection. Desire and craving are tangled within; the mind is muddied, and so one cannot see the path. When the water is clear and the filth removed, pure and without defilement, one naturally sees one's own form. It is like a fierce fire beneath a cauldron in which the water boils and leaps, covered above with a cloth: though all creatures come and look down, none can perceive their reflection. Within the mind the three poisons surge and boil; the five hindrances cover the outside. In the end one cannot see the path. Only when the grime of the mind is completely removed does one know whence the spirit comes, where birth-and-death tends, and where the Buddha-lands and the virtue of the path abide."

The Buddha said: "One who practices the path is like carrying a torch into a dark room. The darkness is immediately dispelled while the light remains. When one studies the path and sees the truth, foolishness and delusion are completely destroyed, and there is nothing one does not see."

The Buddha said: "What do I think of? I think of the path. What do I practice? I practice the path. What do I speak of? I speak of the path. I contemplate the true path and am not heedless for even an instant."

The Buddha said: "Behold heaven and earth and contemplate their impermanence. Behold the mountains and rivers and contemplate their impermanence. Behold the myriad things, their forms abundant and flourishing, and contemplate their impermanence. If one holds the mind thus, one attains the path swiftly."

The Buddha said: "Even a single day of practice, constantly mindful of the path and practicing the path, and one thereupon attains the root of faith. Such blessings are immeasurable."

The Buddha said: "Carefully contemplate the four great elements within the body. Each has a name, yet together they are nothing. This 'I' of ours is merely a temporary lodging; life, too, does not last long. All such things are like illusion."

The Buddha said: "People follow their passions and desires in pursuit of reputation and fame. It is like burning incense: people smell its fragrance, yet the incense itself is consumed in its own burning. The fool craves worldly fame and reputation and does not guard the truth of the path. Vain fame endangers the self, and regret comes afterward."

The Buddha said: "Wealth and sensual beauty are to people like honey on the blade of a knife to a small child. The sweetness is not even enough for a single taste, yet there is the danger of cutting the tongue."

The Buddha said: "A person bound to wife, children, and a fine house suffers worse than imprisonment in shackles and chains. In prison there may be a pardon, but with wife and children and the desire of passion, though there be the peril of a tiger's mouth, one still willingly rushes in. Such offenses have no pardon."

The Buddha said: "Among desires, none is greater than sexual desire. The desire for sensual beauty is the greatest, and there is nothing beyond it. Fortunately there is only this one. If there were a second, not a single person in the whole world could practice the path."

The Buddha said: "Desire and craving in relation to people is like carrying a torch and walking against the wind. The fool who does not let go of the torch will surely burn his hand. The poisons of lust, anger, and delusion dwell in the body. If one does not quickly use the path to remove this calamity, there will surely be peril. It is like the fool who stubbornly grasps the torch and burns his own hand."

A deva offered a jade maiden to the Buddha, wishing to test the Buddha's mind and observe his path. The Buddha said: "A leather bag full of filth! What have you come for? Such things may deceive the vulgar, but they cannot move one who has the six supernatural powers. Away! I have no use for you." The deva, with even greater reverence for the Buddha, then asked about the meaning of the path. The Buddha explained it, and the deva immediately attained the fruit of srotāpanna.

The Buddha said: "One who practices the path is like a piece of wood in the water, following the current as it goes. It does not touch the left bank, nor does it touch the right bank. It is not taken by people, not obstructed by ghosts and spirits, not held back by a whirlpool, nor does it rot. I guarantee that it will enter the sea. If a person practices the path and is not deluded by passions and desires, not deceived by the various evils, and is vigorous and without doubt, I guarantee that person will attain the path."

The Buddha told the śramaṇas: "Be careful: do not trust your own mind. Your mind cannot ultimately be trusted. Be careful not to engage with sensual form. Engaging with form brings calamity. Only when you have attained the path of the arhat may you trust your own mind."

The Buddha told the śramaṇas: "Be careful not to look at women. If you see one, do not look. Be careful not to speak with them. If you speak with them, command the mind to walk uprightly and say: 'I am a śramaṇa dwelling in a turbid world. I should be like a lotus flower, unsullied by the mud. The old I regard as my mother, the senior as my elder sister, the young as my younger sister, the very young as my child. I treat them with propriety.' Yet the mind must still carefully contemplate, from the head to the feet, examining within: what does that body contain? Only a vessel of foul secretions and various impurities. By this one releases the mind."

The Buddha said: "One who practices the path and departs from passions and desires should be like grass that sees fire: the fire comes, and one has already retreated. The practitioner who sees desire and craving must surely distance himself from it."

The Buddha said: "A person was afflicted by lust, and his passions would not cease. He sat upon an axe-blade and cut off his own organ. The Buddha said to him: 'Rather than cutting off the organ, it would be better to cut off the mind. The mind is the chief minister. If one stops the chief minister, all subordinates come to rest. If the deviant mind does not stop, what good does it do to cut off the organ? One will die shortly after.'" The Buddha said: "The worldly hold inverted views, like this foolish person."

There was a lustful young woman who had made a promise with a man. When the appointed time came and he did not come, she reproached herself and said: "Desire! I know your origin. You are born from thought and imagination. If I do not think of you or imagine you, then you do not arise." The Buddha, walking on the path, heard this and told the śramaṇas: "Remember this! This is a verse of Kāśyapa Buddha that has been circulating among the common people."

The Buddha said: "From desire and craving, people generate sorrow. From sorrow, they generate fear. Without craving, there is no sorrow; without sorrow, there is no fear."

The Buddha said: "Practicing the path is like a single person going to war against ten thousand. Donning armor and bearing weapons, one goes out the gate intent on battle. If one's resolve is timid and cowardly, one retreats of one's own accord. Or one turns back halfway. Or one fights and is killed. Or one wins a great victory, returns home, and is greatly honored. If a person can firmly hold the mind, advancing with keen vigor, not deluded by the words of the worldly, the mad, and the foolish, with evil utterly exhausted, one will certainly attain the path."

A śramaṇa was reciting the sūtras at night with great sorrow, his mind harboring regret and doubt, wishing to give up and return home. The Buddha called the śramaṇa and asked him: "When you were at home, what did you practice?"

He replied: "I was always playing the lute." The Buddha said: "What if the strings were slack?" He said: "It would not sound." "What if the strings were too tight?" He said: "The sound would break." "What if the tension was exactly right?" "Then all the notes would be full and resonant." The Buddha told the śramaṇa: "Studying the path is just like this. Hold the mind with proper balance, and the path can be attained."

The Buddha said: "One who practices the path is like the smelting of iron, gradually going deeper, discarding the slag. The finished implement is sure to be fine. Studying the path is a gradual deepening, removing the grime of the mind. With diligence one approaches the path. If one is violent, the body becomes weary; if the body is weary, the mind becomes vexed; if the mind is vexed, one's practice declines; if practice declines, one accumulates offenses."

The Buddha said: "Practicing the path is bitter, and not practicing the path is also bitter. From birth to old age, from old age to sickness, from sickness to death: the suffering is immeasurable. The mind is vexed and offenses accumulate. Through birth-and-death without cease, the suffering is beyond words."

The Buddha said: "For a person to leave the three evil paths and be born as a human is difficult. Already born human, to be born male rather than female is difficult. Already born male, to have the six senses complete is difficult. With the six senses complete, to be born in the central lands is difficult. Already in the central lands, to encounter and serve the Buddha-path is difficult. Already serving the Buddha-path, to encounter a ruler who honors the path is difficult. To be born into a bodhisattva family is difficult. Already born into a bodhisattva family, to have faith in the Three Honored Ones and to be born in the age of a Buddha is difficult."

The Buddha asked the śramaṇas: "How long is a human life?" One answered: "A few days." The Buddha said: "You have not yet understood the path."

He asked another śramaṇa: "How long is a human life?" He answered: "The space of a meal." The Buddha said: "You have not yet understood the path."

He asked another śramaṇa: "How long is a human life?" He answered: "The space of a single breath." The Buddha said: "Excellent! You may be called one who understands the path."

The Buddha said: "Though a disciple departs and is thousands of leagues from me, if he is mindful of my precepts, he will certainly attain the path. Though one stands at my left side, if the mind is given to what is wrong, in the end one will not attain the path. What matters is practice. What good is it to be near yet not practice, even by a ten-thousandth part?"

The Buddha said: "Practicing the path is like eating honey: the center and the edges are all sweet. My sūtras are just like this: their meaning is entirely joyful. The practitioner attains the path."

The Buddha said: "One who practices the path and can pull out the root of desire and craving is like plucking beads from a string, one by one. Eventually they are all gone. When evil is exhausted, one attains the path."

The Buddha said: "All you śramaṇas who practice the path should be like an ox bearing a heavy load, walking through deep mud, utterly exhausted, not daring to look left or right, intent only on getting free of the mud so as to rest at last. The śramaṇa should regard passions and desires as even worse than that mud. With a straightforward mind, be mindful of the path, and one can be freed from all suffering."

The Buddha said: "I regard the position of a sovereign as a passing traveler. I regard treasures of gold and jade as gravel. I regard the beauty of fine silk as tattered cloth."
