# 佛說須摩提長者經
## Sūtra Spoken by the Buddha on the Elder Sumati

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0530

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Thus have I heard.

At one time the Buddha was in the land of Śrāvastī, at the Jetavana monastery, together with a great assembly of five hundred bhikṣus. At that time, when the proper hour arrived, the World-Honored One put on his robe and took his bowl, and together with Ānanda entered the city to beg for food.

At that time, in the city there was a son of a great elder named Sumati. This man had died. His father and mother, the clan, relatives, and all their friends at once broke into wailing and lamentation, beating their breasts and throwing themselves on the ground, crying out in anguish and collapsing senseless upon the earth. Some called out for father and mother or brothers; some cried for their husbands or lords. With all manner of wailing and weeping, some threw dirt upon themselves, and some took knives to cut their hair. It was as if poison arrows had pierced their hearts, and their anguish was immeasurable. Some covered themselves with their garments and wept, like great trees in a forest shaken and struck against one another by a mighty wind; or like fish out of water writhing upon the ground; or like a great tree that has been felled, crashing down in disorder. Such was the bitter torment inflicted upon their bodies.

At that time the World-Honored One, already knowing but purposely asking, said: "Ānanda, why is that crowd wailing and weeping so?"

Ānanda said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, in this city there was a son of a great elder named Sumati, whose life has ended and who has died. This man's father and mother, brothers, wife and children, clan, relatives, and friends, bound by the ties of love, are thus confused and distraught. May the World-Honored One, for the sake of delivering all beings, go to that place. The buddhas, the world-honored ones, do not teach without being invited. I now, on behalf of those people, invite the Buddha, the World-Honored One, with great loving-kindness and compassion, to go there."

At that time the Tathāgata accepted Ānanda's invitation and went to the family's home. When those people saw the World-Honored One from afar, each one wiped their face with their hand and came forward to greet the Buddha. Arriving before the Buddha, they prostrated at his feet. Choking with grief, they could not speak. They wished to sigh deeply but, out of reverence for the Buddha, did not dare to exhale, and stood there holding their breath.

Then the Buddha addressed the father and mother of the elder, the brothers, clan, relatives, and friends: "Why do you weep and grieve, clinging to these illusory dharmas?"

All these people spoke with one voice to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, in this city this one person alone was intelligent and wise, handsome and extraordinary, in the prime of his youth, without equal among all people. We think of him without ceasing; our hearts cannot let go. All looked up to him and gazed at him without satiation. His speech was gentle and harmonious; he was filial to his parents and respectful to his brothers. Moreover, he possessed abundant wealth and treasures: gold, silver, lapis lazuli, giant clam shells, carnelian, coral, and amber. His storehouses were overflowing and his precious treasures complete. He had carriages and horses, food and drink, medicines, clothing, bedding, servants, and attendants. All these were fully provided. Yet in a single morning his life ended. Therefore we weep and mourn, unable to control ourselves. Excellent, World-Honored One! We beg you to teach us the Dharma through skillful means, so that we may be free from all afflictions, and from now on never again suffer such pain. May we sever the roots of greed, aversion, delusion, and all bonds. May we cross beyond the shore of birth, old age, sickness, and death, and forever leave the ocean of sorrow, grief, suffering, and torment. Wherever we are born, may we encounter buddhas, assemblies of spiritual friends, and never encounter evil conditions."

Then the World-Honored One addressed the father and mother of the elder, the clan, relatives, friends, and the great assembly: "Have you ever seen anyone who was born but did not grow old, fall ill, or die?"

All these people said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One, we have never seen such a one."

The Buddha further addressed the great assembly: "If you wish to be free from birth, old age, sickness, death, sorrow, grief, suffering, and torment, then no longer dwell on the bonds of attachment and love. Set your hearts upon right view and take refuge in the Three Treasures. Why? In all the world there is none who surpasses the Buddha, who can guide the blind and deluded masses. Among all caravan leaders and king physicians, none can compare with the Buddha's marks of excellence. Why? The body of the Tathāgata is itself the King of Medicine, and the Dharma taught by the Buddha is itself the finest medicine."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Throughout the worlds of the ten directions,
> none who are born fail to die.
> On the road of birth and death's coming and going,
> only the Dharma can eliminate this.
>
> "In the lands of the ten directions,
> there is none at life's end who can save.
> Only the Buddha can sever and remove this.
> Therefore take refuge in the Buddha."

The Buddha further addressed the great assembly: "How do you understand what is meant by 'death'?"

The people answered: "We do not know, World-Honored One."

The Buddha told the great assembly: "Killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, false speech, divisive speech, harsh speech, frivolous speech, jealousy, anger, and delusion: if a person practices these ten evils, this is called 'death.'"

The Buddha further told the great assembly: "If a person is contrary and does not honor their parents, does not practice the true Dharma, and does not respect śramaṇas, brahmins, and elders, such a person is also called 'dead.'"

He further told the assembly: "If a person does not revere the Three Treasures and the śramaṇas who keep the precepts and possess virtue, such a person is also called 'dead.'"

He further told the assembly: "If a person is stingy and greedy, jealous and arrogant, does not keep the precepts, and the members of their household also do not keep them; if their speech is coarse and evil, delighting in injuring others; if they are deranged and lazy, with an unsettled mind; if their six faculties are deficient and they lack wisdom; if they cannot be upright but readily believe the words of others; if they constantly harbor jealousy and resentment while praising themselves; if they suppress the goodness of others and proclaim the faults of others; if they are self-aggrandizing and unable to draw near to śramaṇas and brahmins, and do not hear the true Dharma, such persons are also called 'dead.'"

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Whoever performs unwholesome deeds,
> delighting in the ten evils,
> whose mind is ever arrogant,
> who does not revere the Three Treasures,
> who cannot keep the pure precepts
> and is lazy without diligence:
> all such persons
> are called 'dead.'
>
> "Those who delight in evil
> are born in evil destinies at death.
> If a person practices goodness,
> then they will be born in heaven.
>
> "If a person does not trust the Buddha
> and also does not practice the Dharma
> but practices what is not Dharma,
> that person is called 'dead.'"

He further told the assembly: "If a person obtains wealth and honor yet has no arrogant mind, if their thoughts are always at ease, neither exalting themselves nor debasing themselves, regarding all equally as they regard themselves; if, though obtaining wealth and honor, their mind remains unchanged, always contemplating impermanence, not considering anything as their own, seeing excess as toxic as venom, understanding and knowing that all dharmas will eventually be parted from; if, knowing this, they diligently cultivate; if they know that all dharmas have nothing to rely upon, do not regard any name or profit as 'mine,' and also do not cling to any worldly thing; if they constantly cultivate their minds, draw near to the wise, stay far from evil friends, and always seek distance from them; if they never transgress the Dharma taught by the Buddha."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Few are the beings in this world
> who obtain great wealth and honor
> yet are not arrogant.
> Those who practice arrogance cannot escape suffering.
> If one is not arrogant, one swiftly attains liberation.
> Those without arrogance
> will certainly attain liberation.
> Those with arrogance will surely fall into evil destinies.
> Those who sever arrogance are not called 'dead.'
> Those with arrogance
> are truly called 'dead.'"

He further told the assembly: "Do you know that there are birth, old age, sickness, and death, and that in this life and the next the spirit revolves, taking on new forms?"

The people answered: "We do not know, World-Honored One."

The Buddha said: "You should know that sentient beings, bound by these four things, have their spirits revolving through the five destinies, not knowing where birth comes from or where death leads."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Taking the impermanent as permanent,
> the impure as pure,
> what is truly suffering as pleasure,
> and what is without self as having self:
>
> "Sentient beings in the round of birth and death
> are deeply attached to inverted views.
> For thousands of millions of kalpas
> they do not know the root of birth and death.
>
> "If there is a person who can understand
> the great Dharma of true reality,
> they can know that this is impermanent,
> the very root of the greatest suffering.
>
> "If a person sees defilement and turbidity
> and severs the root of the three poisons,
> they will surely accomplish
> the great Dharma of the unsurpassed."

He further told the assembly: "Because of afflictions, various causes and conditions arise. Because of causes and conditions, one receives all manner of suffering. Because of this, one revolves through birth and death. Form does not go to the next world; sensation, perception, formations, and consciousness do not go to the next world. Why? The five aggregates cannot be found, are not solid, and do not pause for even a moment."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Because of the causes and conditions of afflictions,
> various evil causes and conditions arise.
> Because of these causes and conditions,
> one receives immeasurable suffering.
>
> "Because one receives these sufferings,
> immeasurable afflictions arise again.
> The root of all birth and death
> revolves in just this way.
>
> "All beautiful forms in the world
> are like foam on water.
> All manner of painful sensations
> are like bubbles in the rain.
>
> "All the myriad perceptions
> are no different from a mirage.
> All the formations
> are by nature like a plantain tree.
>
> "All the consciousnesses
> are like an illusion, without substance.
> Such wondrous Dharma as this
> was spoken from the Tathāgata's own mouth.
>
> "The wondrous Dharma of all buddhas
> has already been taught for you.
> Out of compassion for sentient beings,
> I teach this Dharma of ambrosia."

He further told the assembly: "Earth does not go to the next world. Water, fire, and wind also do not go to the next world. Why? Earth has no feeling and no knowledge. The four great elements have no consciousness. Earth is a false composite of the four elements. For this reason they do not go to the next world."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Among all dharmas,
> there is no form, no color,
> no sensation at all:
> they are empty and false, without reality.
>
> "The four elements are falsely combined,
> soft and without any firmness.
> To wish them to reach the next world:
> such a thing could never be."

He further told the assembly: "The eye does not go to the next world. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind also do not go to the next world. Why? The eye is empty, without self, impermanent, and does not abide for even a moment. If one wished to make it stay, it could not be done. When there are conditions it arises; when conditions scatter it ceases. In its arising there is no place from which it comes, and in its going there is no place to which it goes. The ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind are also like this. All of you should know: these six faculties exist when conditions combine and do not exist when conditions scatter. They are like a guest who cannot stay for long, or like a debtor who counts the days until the debt is repaid. When the days are complete, the debtor departs; there is never a time of permanence. When gone, all is empty. In the end nothing can be found; there is no coming or going. These six faculties are also like this."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "The faculties are without solidity.
> This dharma is like empty space:
> unstable, without longevity,
> not something that can be called 'mine.'
>
> "By causes and conditions they have function,
> but in the end there is nothing fixed.
> Dharmas composed through combination
> cannot be carried to the next world."

When the Buddha taught this sūtra, three hundred bhikṣus exhausted all outflows, resolved all bonds, and accomplished the path of arhatship. Five hundred devas became free from dust, removed from defilement, and attained the pure Dharma-eye. Furthermore, eight thousand devas and humans all aroused the mind of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi.

The Buddha further addressed the great assembly: "You should all contemplate impermanence and never depart from this mindfulness. Great assembly, I know that the buddhas of the past acted as great bridges for all sentient beings, possessing great loving-kindness and compassion that reached everywhere. The buddhas of the past were named Kāśyapa Buddha, Krakucchanda Buddha, Kanakamuni Buddha, Suye Buddha, Śikhin Buddha, and Dīpaṃkara Buddha. Buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, all tathāgatas, severed every unwholesome dharma and cultivated immeasurably profound wholesome dharmas. In all dharmas they were without obstruction, and yet they were all impermanent. In the past there were also immeasurable pratyekabuddhas who delighted in tranquility and solitude, who well cultivated their minds, and who were also all impermanent. The disciples of the past buddhas were immeasurable and boundless. All had exhausted outflows and attained liberation of mind, the three knowledges, the six supernatural powers, and the eight liberations. They had forever departed from birth and death and reached the other shore, yet they too were transformed by impermanence. In the past there were also sages of the five supernatural powers who purely maintained their precepts, with lifespans of immeasurable kalpas, and all returned to impermanence. In former times there were also immeasurable wheel-turning sacred kings and their lesser kings, fully endowed with the seven treasures and lacking nothing, and they too were impermanent. Throughout immeasurable past lives I served as various kings, giving my head, eyes, marrow, brain, hands, feet, kingdom, city, wife, children, elephants, horses, seven kinds of treasure, palaces, towers, and all the instruments of the five pleasures, giving everything. At that time I also cultivated the pure precepts without any deficiency. Whenever anyone came seeking such things, I gave them joyfully, without anger or jealousy. With vigorous diligence, my body and mind were untiring. I deeply cultivated dhyāna, liberation, and samādhi. With profound and keen wisdom, vast wisdom, unobstructed and unequaled, supremely deep wisdom, I was endowed with such immeasurable merits. When I practiced the bodhisattva path, by these merits I sat beneath the bodhi tree and with a vajra-resolve made this vow: 'Without rising from this seat, I will vanquish the four māras and attain omniscience, the ten powers, the four fearlessnesses, and the eighteen distinctive qualities of a buddha.' As soon as I formed this thought, the Heavenly Māra Pāpīyān led his armies, armed and adorned, surrounding the bodhi tree for thirty-two yojanas on every side, with this evil intention: 'With my armies I will surely destroy this person and prevent him from attaining the path.' At that time I merely extended my hand and touched the earth, and Māra's hosts and retinue were at once routed and scattered. That which I know, have attained, and have awakened to should be confirmed by direct realization. I should attain the path. At that time I gathered immeasurable merit and wisdom and, with a single moment of corresponding wisdom, attained anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi and turned the Dharma wheel, accomplishing myself and also accomplishing all sentient beings. At that time there were three yakṣas: one named Āḷavaka, a second named Viśanaga, and a third named Suciloma. Such and immeasurable ghosts and spirits I transformed and caused to keep the precepts. Among the ninety-five kinds of heterodox paths, I am the most honored, the most supreme, without equal. Having severed the roots of all three poisons, without the afflictions of birth, old age, sickness, or death, I have accomplished the unsurpassed Dharma of the path. Yet I too will be turned by impermanence. Three months from now I will enter parinirvāṇa."

He further told the assembly: "All of you, contemplate this impermanence, which never spares anyone. The Tathāgata has attained omniscience, and his physical form and excellent marks are fully accomplished, yet he cannot be exempt. The buddhas of the past, future, and present also all return to impermanence. Therefore, all of you should deeply contemplate the dharma of impermanence. If you can do this, you will no longer have a mind that clings to attachment and love, and you will be without the thought of greed, aversion, or delusion. You will forever sever the suffering of birth, old age, sickness, and death, be free from all unwholesome dharmas, and increase immeasurable pure practices. You will deeply comprehend the twelve links of dependent arising in all dharmas. By this cause and condition, you will always encounter the buddhas. Why? If a person comprehends the twelve links of dependent arising, that is seeing the Dharma. If one sees the Dharma, that is called seeing the Buddha. If one wishes to see the Buddha, one should keep the pure precepts with flawless deportment."

At that time the World-Honored One spoke in verse:

> "Kings of the past, raised in deep palaces,
> powerful and handsome, adorned and decorated,
> with elephants, horses, chariots,
> and abundant wealth and treasure:
> such kings also returned to impermanence.
>
> "Sages of the past, wearing deerskin garments,
> Devāyana and other great sages,
> thoroughly versed in the scriptures of the heterodox paths:
> such sages also returned to impermanence.
>
> "Arhats of the past who severed the three poisons,
> possessed of the three knowledges and six supernatural powers,
> unattached to the three realms,
> free from all delusion and desire,
> the finest fields of merit:
> such sacred ones also returned to impermanence.
>
> "Those who, without hearing a single sentence,
> well severed all bonds,
> diligently striving for their own sake,
> fields of great merit,
> like a rhinoceros with its single horn,
> dwelling alone in mountain forests,
> the renowned pratyekabuddhas:
> they too returned to impermanence.
>
> "The hosts of the Heavenly Māra,
> all routed at once;
> having also severed all bonds,
> he attained the path of buddhahood.
> Having attained the path,
> he turned the Dharma wheel.
> Yet even such a buddha
> must return to impermanence.
>
> "The buddhas of the past who knew the affairs of the three realms,
> the buddhas of the future who will strengthen sentient beings,
> the buddhas of the present in lands numerous as the Ganges sands:
> such buddhas also return to impermanence.
>
> "The power of impermanence does not spare the desire realm,
> the form realm, or the formless realm,
> nor sages, kings, the noble or the lowly, the high or the low.
> It also does not spare
> the buddhas, pratyekabuddhas, or those in training or beyond training.
> Impermanence does not fear,
> does not distinguish wealth or beauty,
> does not inquire about the strong or weak
> or the greatly wise.
> It seizes people firmly.
>
> "By this one should know: impermanence is the greatest suffering.
> One should seek the true Dharma.
> In the past, as a king, I gave away palaces,
> gardens, pleasure-pools, flowers, and abundant fruit,
> kingdom, city, wife, children,
> and head and eyes in giving.
> By this merit I sought the path of the Buddha.
>
> "In former times
> I gave hands and feet in offering.
> Through such deeds
> I cultivated and practiced patience.
> When a hawk pursued a dove,
> I cut my own flesh to ransom it,
> for the sake of the unsurpassed path
> enduring all manner of suffering.
>
> "I practiced austerities
> and long cultivated what is difficult to practice.
> I vanquished the Māra King
> beneath the tree at the site of enlightenment
> and attained the path of buddhahood,
> free from all defilement.
> I turned the Dharma wheel
> at the Deer Park.
>
> "I subdued
> the wrathful yakṣa
> among the seven mountains,
> dwelling at the Himalayas.
> I subdued him
> and his retinue,
> yet I cannot subdue the power of impermanence.
>
> "I was able to subdue,
> as if taming a mountain cliff,
> such fierce elephants,
> transforming them into disciples.
> I subdued them and their retinue,
> yet I cannot subdue
> the power of impermanence.
>
> "Among all the dialecticians
> and the various heterodox paths,
> when I debated with the true Dharma,
> none were my equal.
> Beings of different destinies
> I transformed into disciples,
> yet I cannot subdue
> the great power of impermanence.
>
> "The bonds of views and desire,
> aversion and delusion,
> such blind and darkened dharmas:
> I have already completely eliminated them.
> I have lit the great lamp of wisdom,
> illuminating the three-thousandfold world,
> yet I cannot subdue
> the great power of impermanence.
>
> "I subdued the Heavenly Māra King
> and all his hosts,
> shattered the great blindness,
> and illuminated it with the light of true Dharma.
> I also subdued the dialecticians
> and the various soothsayers,
> yet I cannot subdue
> the great power of impermanence."

At that time the Venerable Ānanda came forward and said to the Buddha: "Excellent, World-Honored One! You have well distinguished and explained this Dharma. What should this sūtra be called? How should it be upheld?"

The Buddha said: "This sūtra is called 'Removing All Sorrow.' You should receive and uphold it. It is also called 'Assembly Before All Buddhas,' and also called 'Manifestation to Sentient Beings as Spoken by the Tathāgata.' It should be received and upheld."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "In future ages, if a good man or good woman receives and upholds this Sūtra of Manifestation to Sentient Beings as Spoken by the Tathāgata, for seven lifetimes they will recall their own past lives. Poison will not harm them, fire will not burn them, water will not sweep them away, and they will not fall into the hells, the realm of hungry ghosts, the animal realm, or the eight difficult conditions. When they relinquish this body, they will be born before Maitreya and will be present at Maitreya's first assembly."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, Ānanda and the entire great assembly of devas, nāgas, yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans and non-humans, having heard what the Buddha taught, rejoiced and put it into practice.
