# 須達經
## The Sudatta Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0073

Translated from the Chinese. Xiao Qi dynasty, translated by Guṇavṛddhi (求那毘地)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Bhagavān was in Śrāvastī, in Jeta's Grove, in the park of Anāthapiṇḍada. At that time, the householder Sudatta came to the World-Honored One. Having arrived, he bowed at the World-Honored One's feet and sat to one side. After the householder Sudatta had sat to one side, the World-Honored One asked: "Householder, do you give gifts while dwelling at home?"

"Yes, World-Honored One. At home there are gifts given, but what is given cannot be fine food. There is only a gruel mixed with barley and hemp seeds, with a piece of ginger, given as an offering."

"Householder, this is not an excellent gift. When there is excellent giving, both the giver and the recipient receive a reward. As for what is not excellent giving, householder: when one gives without faith, does not give at the proper time, does not give with one's own hand, does not go in person to give, and does not know or have confidence that there is a reward for giving, then know that the reward will be as follows: one's mind will not dwell in fine dwellings, one's mind will not dwell amid fine possessions, nor in fine clothing, nor in fine food, nor in the fine five pleasures. Why is this? Because, householder, this is the reward of non-excellent giving.

"Householder, one who does not give excellently should instead give with faith, give at the proper time, give with one's own hand, go in person to give, and give with knowledge and confidence, knowing there is a reward as the result of giving. Know that such a person will receive this reward: their mind will dwell in fine family livelihood, they will have supremely fine possessions, supremely fine clothing, supremely fine food, and their mind will enjoy the fine five pleasures. Why is this? Because, householder, one should know that this comes from giving.

"Householder, one who gives excellently but gives without faith, does not give at the proper time, does not give with one's own hand, does not go to give, and neither knows nor has confidence, neither knowing there is a reward as the result of one's actions, and yet still gives: know that such a person will receive this reward: their mind will not dwell in fine family livelihood, their mind will not dwell in fine clothing, their mind will not dwell in fine food, their mind will not dwell in the fine five pleasures. Why is this? Because, householder, this is not true giving.

"Householder, one who gives excellent and fine gifts, who gives with joyful faith, gives at the proper time, gives with one's own hand, goes in person to give, and gives with knowledge and confidence, knowing there is a reward as the result of one's actions: know that such a person will receive this reward: their mind will dwell in fine family livelihood, and in the fine five pleasures, and their mind will dwell in fine food. Why is this? Because, householder, this is the reward of giving at the proper time.

"Why is this? Householder, in the distant past there was a great brahmin named Velāma, who was greatly wealthy, extremely wealthy, with abundant money and abundant possessions of every kind. He gave great gifts in the following manner: eighty-four thousand golden bowls filled with crushed silver. He gave great gifts in this manner: eighty-four thousand silver bowls filled with crushed gold. He gave great gifts in this manner: eighty-four thousand elephants adorned with all their trappings, white as snow. He gave great gifts in this manner: eighty-four thousand horses adorned with all their trappings and necklaces of gold. He gave great gifts in this manner: eighty-four thousand cows, each tied with a cloth, yielding vessels always full of milk. He gave great gifts in this manner: eighty-four thousand maidens of upright and surpassing beauty, all adorned with the finest ornaments. He gave great gifts in this manner, along with food and delicacies of every flavor beyond count.

"Householder, the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave such great gifts to the ordinary people of Jambudvīpa. But to give to even a single sage would have been of greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave such great gifts to the sages of Jambudvīpa, it would not compare to giving to a single stream-enterer; this produces greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave to the ordinary people, sages, and a hundred stream-enterers of Jambudvīpa, it would not compare to giving to a single once-returner; this produces greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave to the ordinary people, sages, a hundred stream-enterers, and a hundred once-returners of Jambudvīpa, it would not compare to giving to a single non-returner; this produces greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave to the ordinary people of Jambudvīpa up to a hundred non-returners, it would not compare to giving to a single arhat; this produces greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave to the ordinary people of Jambudvīpa up to a hundred arhats, it would not compare to giving to a single pratyekabuddha; this produces greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave to the ordinary people of Jambudvīpa up to a hundred pratyekabuddhas, it would not compare to giving to the Tathāgata, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One; this produces greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave to the ordinary people of Jambudvīpa up to a hundred pratyekabuddhas, to build a dwelling and offer it to the Saṅgha of the four directions would produce greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave to the ordinary people of Jambudvīpa up to building a dwelling and offering it to the Saṅgha, it would not compare to taking the threefold refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Bhikṣu Saṅgha with a pure mind and receiving the precepts; this produces greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave such great gifts, from the ordinary people of Jambudvīpa up to taking the threefold refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Bhikṣu Saṅgha with pure conduct and receiving the precepts, it would not compare to cultivating loving-kindness toward all beings even for the time it takes to milk a cow; this produces greater merit. Though the great and wealthy brahmin Velāma gave to the ordinary people of Jambudvīpa up to cultivating loving-kindness toward all beings for the time it takes to milk a cow: to contemplate that all conditioned things are impermanent, suffering, empty, and without self, even for the time of a finger-snap, produces greater merit.

"You, householder, may think: 'That wealthy brahmin Velāma was someone else.' Do not think so. I myself was that great and wealthy brahmin named Velāma! Thus, householder, one who abides in benefiting oneself, benefiting others, and benefiting many people, who has compassion for the world, and who brings meaning, happiness, and peace to gods and humans, and who teaches the Dharma in this way, but has not yet reached the final end, not yet reached final purity, not yet reached the complete holy life, not yet fully practiced the holy life, such a one has not yet been freed from birth, old age, sickness, death, grief, sorrow, and displeasure. I say that such a one has not yet been freed from suffering.

"But now, householder, the Tathāgata has appeared in the world, the Worthy One, the Perfectly Enlightened One, Perfect in Knowledge and Conduct, the Well-Gone One, the Knower of the World, the Unsurpassed One, the Guide of the Way, the Teacher of Gods and Humans, the Buddha, the World-Honored One. Now, for his own sake, for the sake of others, for the sake of many people, out of compassion for the world, he brings meaning, happiness, and peace to gods and humans. The Dharma that I now teach reaches the final end, reaches final purity, reaches the complete holy life, fully practices the holy life. Now one can be freed from birth, old age, sickness, death, grief, sorrow, displeasure, and suffering. I say that one is freed from suffering."

Thus spoke the Buddha. The householder Sudatta, having heard what the World-Honored One said, rejoiced and was delighted.
