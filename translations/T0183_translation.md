# 一切智光明仙人慈心因緣不食肉經
## Sūtra of the Causes and Conditions of the Seer Sarvajñaprabha's Compassionate Mind and Not Eating Meat

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0183

Translated from the Chinese. Translator unknown

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was dwelling in the country of Magadha, at the seat of enlightenment, in Mikā village, in the temple of the Īśvara deity.

At that time, there was a brāhmaṇa's son from the Kapila clan named Maitreya. His body was golden in color, bearing the thirty-two marks and eighty minor marks. He radiated silver light, adorned with golden ornaments, like a white silver mountain. His majestic radiance was immeasurable. He came to the Buddha's presence.

At that time, the World-Honored One was with one thousand two hundred and fifty bhikṣus, walking through the grove. There were also five hundred matted-hair ascetics. From afar they saw Maitreya's dignified bearing and his pure marks. They prostrated with all five limbs on the ground, like a silver mountain collapsing, forming a gathering of golden flowers interspersed with many treasures. Upon golden flowers and a golden platform, with fruits of the seven treasures, from within the platform pavilion came a wondrous sound that spoke this verse:

> "I see the Honored Sage,
> Whose countenance is always pure.
> His hundred blessings and marks are extraordinary;
> In the world he has no equal.
> The stains of defilement are forever exhausted,
> And his wisdom is fully complete.
> I single-mindedly take refuge forever;
> Body and mind are without weariness.
> Therefore with my five limbs
> I seek to attain supreme ease,
> To be freed from suffering, without fear.
> I bow in respect to Śākyamuni."

At that time, the ascetics, having seen and heard these things, said to the Buddha: "World-Honored One! This young man has such dignified bearing and immeasurable radiance, no different from the Buddha himself. Under which Buddha did he first generate the aspiration for the path? Which sūtra does he uphold and practice? We beg the Honored One of Heaven to explain this for us!"

The Buddha told the ascetic Śikhin: "Listen carefully now! Reflect well upon this! I shall explain it in full and bring you joy!

"Long ago in the past, through immeasurable and boundless asaṃkhyeya kalpas, there was a world named Splendid Flower. The Buddha there was named Maitreya, and he always taught all beings with the compassionate mind and the four immeasurable states. That Buddha taught a sūtra called the 'Compassionate Samādhi Light Great Compassion Ocean Cloud.' If anyone heard it, they would immediately transcend the offenses of a hundred billion ten-thousands of kalpas of birth and death, and would surely attain Buddhahood, without any doubt.

"At that time, in that country there was a great brāhmaṇa named Sarvajñaprabha, who was intelligent, wise, and broadly learned in many sūtras. He was proficient in all the sixty-four worldly arts and skills. Hearing that a Buddha had appeared in the world and taught the 'Compassionate Samādhi Light Great Compassion Ocean Cloud' sūtra, he used all the worldly doctrines and arguments to challenge that Buddha, exhausting all his rhetoric, but could not refute him. He then believed and submitted, becoming a disciple of the Buddha. He immediately generated the aspiration for the supreme, perfect enlightenment and said: 'I now, within the Buddha's Dharma, shall recite and uphold the Great Compassionate Samādhi Light Great Compassion Ocean Cloud sūtra. By this merit, may I in a future age, after kalpas beyond reckoning, surely become a Buddha, and may my name be Maitreya.' He then left the household life and entered the deep mountains, with long, matted hair as his distinguishing mark. He practiced the brahma-conduct. For eight thousand years, he lived with few desires and nothing to attend to, sustaining himself by begging for alms. He recited and upheld this sūtra, with single-minded focus, eliminating all disturbance.

"At that time in the world, a rain of meteors appeared. The king was given to excess. Comets streaked across the sky. Rain fell without ceasing. Floodwaters rose. The seer sat upright but could not go out to beg for alms. Seven days passed.

"At that time, in that forest there were five hundred white rabbits. Among them was a rabbit king, a mother with her young one. Seeing that the seer had not eaten for seven days, she said: 'This seer, for the sake of the Buddha's path, has not eaten for many days. His life will not last much longer. The banner of the Dharma is about to collapse, and the ocean of the Dharma is about to run dry. I should now, for the sake of the supreme great Dharma, cause it to endure long. I will not begrudge my body and life.'

"She then told the other rabbits: 'All conditioned things are impermanent. Sentient beings love their bodies, yet are born in vain and die in vain, never having served the Dharma. I now wish to be a great bridge for all sentient beings, to cause the Dharma to endure long, and to make offerings to this Dharma teacher.'

"At that time, the rabbit king spoke this verse to the assembled rabbits:

> "'If there are beings among the animals
> Who hear the names of all the Buddhas,
> They shall forever leave the three evil paths
> And not be born in the eight places of difficulty.
> If they hear the Dharma and practice accordingly,
> In every birth they shall always encounter a Buddha.
> With faith in the Dharma and no doubt,
> They take refuge in the noble Saṅgha.
> Following all the practices of the precepts,
> They shall swiftly attain Buddhahood,
> And surely reach the great nirvāṇa,
> Forever receiving the supreme bliss.'

"When the rabbit king had spoken this verse, she told the other rabbits: 'I now wish to offer my body to the Dharma. You should all rejoice. Why? Through many kalpas, I have lost my body countless times. Driven by the three poisons, I have taken the form of birds and beasts, born in vain and dying in vain, never having served the Dharma. I now wish, for the sake of the supreme Dharma, to give up my body and life and make offerings to this Dharma teacher.'

"At that time, the mountain tree spirit piled up fragrant firewood and lit it ablaze. The rabbit king, mother and child, circumambulated the seer seven full times and said: 'Great teacher! I now make an offering of the Dharma to the honored one.' The seer said: 'You are an animal. Though you have a compassionate heart, how can you accomplish this?' The rabbit said to the seer: 'I offer my own body to the virtuous one, so that the Dharma may endure long and all sentient beings may receive its benefit.' Having spoken thus, she said to her young one: 'You may go freely and seek water and grass. Focus your mind in contemplation and hold the Three Treasures in right mindfulness.' At that time, the rabbit's young one, hearing its mother's words, knelt and said to its mother: 'As you have spoken of the supreme great Dharma and wish to make an offering, I too desire to do the same.' Having spoken, it threw itself into the fire. The mother followed after. At the moment when the bodhisattva gave up her body, heaven and earth shook greatly, reaching up to the form realm and all the heavens. They rained celestial flowers as offerings.

"When the flesh was cooked, the mountain tree spirit said to the seer: 'The rabbit king, mother and child, threw themselves into the fire to make an offering. The flesh is now cooked. You may eat it.'

"When the seer heard the tree spirit's words, he was so grief-stricken that he could not speak. He placed the sūtra he had been reciting upon the leaves of a tree, and then spoke this verse:

> "'I would rather set my body ablaze and blind my eyes
> Than bear to kill and eat sentient beings.
> The sūtras of compassion taught by all the Buddhas
> Speak of practicing compassion thus:
> I would rather shatter my bones and spill my brains
> Than bear to chew the flesh of sentient beings.
> As the Buddha has taught, those who eat meat
> Cannot fulfill the practice of compassion.
> They shall always receive a body of short life and many illnesses,
> Lost in birth and death, never attaining Buddhahood.'

"When the seer had spoken this verse, he made this vow: 'May I, life after life, never give rise to the thought of killing. May I never eat meat. May I enter the white-light compassionate samādhi, and when I finally attain Buddhahood, may I establish the precept of abstaining from meat.' Having spoken thus, he threw himself into the fire pit and died together with the rabbits. At that moment, heaven and earth shook in six ways. By the power of the heavenly spirits, the trees radiated light, golden and gleaming, illuminating a thousand lands.

"At that time, the people of that country saw golden light emanating from the mountain trees. Following the light, they arrived and saw the seer and the two rabbits dead in the fire. They found the verses he had spoken and also obtained the Buddha's sūtra. They brought them back and presented them to the king. The king, hearing this Dharma, had it proclaimed and spread throughout the land. All who heard it generated the aspiration for the supreme, perfect, true path."

The Buddha told Śikhin: "You should know: the white rabbit king of that time is my present self, Śākyamuni Buddha. The rabbit's young one of that time is now Rāhula. The seer who recited the sūtra of that time is now this brāhmaṇa's son in the assembly, the Bodhisattva Mahāsattva Maitreya. After my parinirvāṇa, in five billion six hundred million years, in the land of the wheel-turning sage king Saṅkha, in the Flower Grove Garden, at the diamond seat beneath the Nāgapuṣpa Bodhi tree, he shall attain the Buddha path and turn the wondrous Dharma wheel. The five hundred rabbits of that time are now Mahākāśyapa and the five hundred bhikṣus. The two hundred and fifty mountain tree spirits of that time are now Śāriputra, Maudgalyāyana, and the two hundred and fifty bhikṣus. The kings of a thousand countries of that time are now Bhadrapāla and the one thousand bodhisattvas. The people of that king's country who heard the sūtra are those who, from the time of my appearance in the world until Rucika, have received the Dharma as disciples, attained the path, and been liberated."

The Buddha told Śikhin: "When a bodhisattva seeks the Dharma, he endures hardship through many kalpas, not sparing his body or life. Though he may receive an animal's body as karmic retribution, he is always able to serve the Dharma, not begrudging his body or life. He throws himself into the fire pit and offers his body. By this he transcends the offenses of nine hundred trillion kalpas of birth and death. Thus he was able, among countless Buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges, to precede Maitreya and attain the Buddha path first. How can you not strive earnestly for the Dharma?"

When the Buddha spoke these words, Śikhin and the five hundred ascetics sought to go forth under the Buddha. The Buddha said: "Welcome!" Their hair fell away of itself, and they immediately became śramaṇas. The Buddha taught them the Dharma, and their minds were suddenly opened. They attained the path of the arhat. Eighty thousand devas also generated the aspiration for the supreme, perfect enlightenment.

The great assembly, hearing what the Buddha taught, each praised the bodhisattva's practice.

Śāriputra said to the Buddha: "After the seer of that time threw himself into the fire pit, where was he reborn?" The Buddha told Śāriputra: "After the seer threw himself into the fire pit, he was reborn in the Brahma world, where he universally taught the great Brahma Dharma for all. When he finally attains Buddhahood, he shall turn the great Brahma wheel. The sūtras he teaches shall also be named the 'Compassionate Samādhi Light Great Compassion Ocean Cloud.' In the prātimokṣa he establishes, those who do not practice compassion shall be called violators of the prohibitions. Those who eat meat shall have violated a grave prohibition. In their future lives, they shall always drink boiling copper. When that seer attains Buddhahood, it shall be as described in the Sūtra of the Descent of the Bodhisattva Maitreya."

The venerable Ānanda, hearing what the Buddha taught, rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, placed his right knee on the ground, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and said: "World-Honored One! When Maitreya attains Buddhahood, in the precepts he establishes, he will use the compassionate mind to prohibit the eating of meat, making it a grave violation. How extraordinary! How remarkable!"

The great assembly then spoke with one voice, praising the beings of that future land for their precept of not eating meat: "We wish to be born in that land!" The World-Honored One predicted that they would all be reborn there.

The venerable Ānanda again said to the Buddha: "What should this sūtra be called? How should it be upheld?"

The Buddha told Ānanda: "The essence of this Dharma is called 'The Bodhisattva White Rabbit King Who Did Not Spare His Body or Life for the Supreme Path.' It is also called the 'Sūtra of the Causes and Conditions of the Seer Sarvajñaprabha's Compassionate Mind and Not Eating Meat.' Uphold it thus." The venerable Ānanda and all the bhikṣus, hearing what the Buddha taught, joyfully accepted and practiced it.
