# Miscellaneous Notes on the Vinaya

## *Lǜ Záchāo* (律雜抄)

Taishō 2790

Author unknown. A Dunhuang manuscript.

*Note: This is a Dunhuang manuscript preserving a catechism-style study guide on Buddhist vinaya (monastic discipline). Written in question-and-answer format, it covers essential topics including the meaning of "bhikṣu," types of ordination, grounds for losing precepts, types of karmic procedures (karman), medicines permitted for monks, the distinction between vinaya terms (śīla, vinaya, prātimokṣa), types of restraint, confession procedures, the summer retreat (varṣā), the invitation ceremony (pravāraṇā), the structure of the Dharmaguptaka Vinaya in Four Parts (Sìfēn Lǜ), the twenty Hīnayāna schools, and when the Buddha began instituting precepts.*

Question: What is the meaning of "bhikṣu" (*bǐqiū*, 比丘)?

Answer: There are three kinds of meaning: frightening Māra, and being able to destroy afflictions; therefore one is called a bhikṣu.

Answer: [There are eight kinds of bhikṣu.] The bhikṣu in name. The resembling bhikṣu. The self-proclaimed bhikṣu. The alms-begging bhikṣu. The patched-robe bhikṣu. The fetters-destroying bhikṣu. The "Welcome!" bhikṣu. The karma-procedure bhikṣu.

Question: How many kinds [of ordination] are there?

Answer: According to the *Vinaya in Four Parts*, there are four kinds: (1) "Welcome!" ordination; (2) triple-refuge ordination; (3) eight-respect ordination; (4) karma-procedure ordination.

Question: How many kinds of grounds for losing [the precepts] are there?

Answer: According to the *Abhidharmakośa*, there are five kinds: (1) losing precepts by death; (2) losing by cutting off wholesome roots; (3) losing by developing both sexual organs; (4) losing by formal renunciation; (5) losing by transgression.

Question: How many kinds of karma-procedures (*jiémó*, 羯磨) are there, and what is the meaning?

Answer: There are three kinds of karma-procedures: (1) simple announcement (*dānbái*, 單白); (2) announcement plus one motion (*bái'èr*, 白二); (3) announcement plus three motions (*báisì*, 白四). The meaning is "conducting business." Because they conduct the preceding dharma-business, they are called "conducting business."

Question: How many kinds of matters do karma-procedures address?

Answer: Gathered together, there are three kinds: (1) matters involving persons, such as ordination; (2) matters not involving persons, such as marking boundaries; (3) matters involving both persons and non-persons, such as requesting a staff or separation from one's robes.

Question: How many kinds of medicines may a bhikṣu consume in total?

Answer: There are four kinds: (1) timely medicine (food before noon); (2) untimely medicine (juices taken after noon); (3) seven-day medicine (ghee, oil, etc.); (4) lifetime medicine (medicines taken as needed throughout life).

Question: What is the difference between "precept" (*jiè*, 戒), "vinaya" (*lǜ*, 律), "vinaya" (*pínǐ*, 毘尼), and "prātimokṣa" (*bōluótímùchā*, 波羅提木叉)?

Answer: "Precept" (*jiè*) means preventing wrong and stopping evil. If one does not commit evil, that is called "precept." "Vinaya" (*lǜ*) means "the dharma." Without the dharma, one cannot regulate one's conduct and deportment. This is a combined meaning-and-form interpretation; therefore it is called "vinaya." "Vinaya" (*pínǐ*) means "taming and subduing"; it can tame the three karmas of body, and so on. "Prātimokṣa" (*mùchā*) is translated here as "liberation." Being liberated from all bonds is called "liberation."

Question: What are the five vinaya texts?

Answer: (1) The preface to the precepts. (2) The bhikṣu precepts as instituted. (3) The bhikṣuṇī precepts as re-instituted. (4) The sūtra section, being the various rules of conduct. (5) The supplementary vinaya in accord with the sūtra section, and so on.

Question: How many kinds of restraint (*lǜyí*, 律儀) are there?

Answer: There are four kinds: (1) meditation restraint, which is the śīla of the form realm; (2) undefiled restraint, which is the śīla arising in the person of one who has attained the undefiled; (3) severance restraint, which means severing afflictions and is therefore called severance restraint; (4) individual-liberation restraint, which is the śīla of the desire realm, individually preventing wrong and therefore called liberation restraint.

Question: Briefly speaking, how many kinds of confession of precepts (*shuōjiè*, 說戒) are there, and how do they vary in formality?

Answer: There are three kinds: (1) karma-procedure confession; (2) face-to-face confession; (3) mental-recollection confession. The abbreviated confessions have three-five-fifteen kinds and seven kinds. From informal toward formal, there are seven abbreviations. From formal toward informal, there are fifteen kinds of abbreviation.

Question: How many kinds of summer retreat (*ānjū*, 安居) are there?

Answer: There are four kinds: (1) forgotten-completion summer retreat; (2) boundary-reaching summer retreat; (3) face-to-face summer retreat; (4) mental-recollection summer retreat relying on conditions. "Tranquil" (*ān*) means purifying by contemplation; "dwelling" (*jū*) means staying in the place.

Question: What is the meaning of the summer retreat?

Answer: During the ninety days, one marks the mind for cultivating the Way and dwells here; therefore it is called "summer retreat." There are also three reasons: (1) Because going about without purpose obstructs the cultivation of the Way; (2) Because it would damage and injure living creatures, violating the way of compassion; (3) Because it would invite worldly criticism and suspicion.

Question: How many kinds of invitation ceremony (*zìzì*, 自恣) are there?

Answer: There are three kinds: (1) karma-procedure invitation; (2) face-to-face invitation; (3) mental-recollection invitation. There are also three kinds: (1) timely invitation; (2) augmented invitation; (3) reduced-kalpa invitation.

Question: What is the meaning of the invitation?

Answer: Because one is confused about one's own faults, one gives others free rein to point out offenses of seeing, hearing, and suspicion. Therefore it is called "invitation" (literally, "giving free rein to oneself").

Question: What does "Vinaya in Four Parts" (*Sìfēn Lǜ*, 四分律) mean, and what is the name of the vinaya master?

Answer: "Four" is the number. The Sanskrit original was in four bundles. It is also said that it requires four recitations to complete; therefore it is called "Four Parts." The *Vinaya in Five Parts* and *Vinaya in Ten Recitations* have the same kind of meaning. The bhikṣu precept text constitutes the first part. From the bhikṣuṇī precepts onward is the second part. From the summer-retreat section (*jiàndù*, 揵度) onward is the third part. From the dwelling section onward is the fourth part. Therefore it is called the *Vinaya in Four Parts*. The vinaya master's name is Dharmagupta (*Tánwúdé*, 曇無德), translated as "Dharma-Protector."

Question: Among the twenty Hīnayāna schools, which school does this vinaya belong to?

Answer: It belongs to the Dharmaguptaka school. [The twenty schools are:] Mahāsāṃghika, Ekavyāvahārika, Lokottaravāda, Kukkuṭika, Bahuśrutīya, Prajñaptivāda, Caityaka, Aparaśaila, Uttaraśaila, Sthaviravāda, Sarvāstivāda, Haimavata, Vatsiputrīya, Dharmottarīya, Bhadrayānīya, Saṃmatīya, Ṣaṇṇagarika, Mahīśāsaka, Dharmaguptaka, Kāśyapīya, Sautrāntika.

Question: How many years after the Tathāgata attained the Way did he begin instituting precepts?

Answer: During the first twelve years after the Tathāgata attained the Way, because the bhikṣus' transgressions were few, he only briefly instituted prohibitive precepts. This was for those of sharp faculties. From the twelfth year onward until his parinirvāṇa, because the bhikṣus' transgressions became numerous, he extensively instituted prohibitive precepts. This was for those of dull faculties.

Question: How many conditions are required for receiving full ordination?

Answer: Six conditions are required: (1) The ordinand's body is free of the thirteen obstacles. (2) The age is a full twenty years. (3) Robes and bowl are complete. (4) The three preceptors are of the proper number, in accord with the dharma, and pure. (5) One must be within the marked boundary, not in a separate assembly or within range of a separate group. (6) The one announcement and three motions, together with the preliminary and concluding verbal procedures, are all complete. Furthermore, according to the *Māyā Sūtra*, there are ten kinds of ordination. For bhikṣus there are five: (1) supreme-dharma ordination; (2) "Welcome!" ordination; (3) dharma-speech ordination; (4) command-hearing ordination; (5) karma-procedure ordination. For bhikṣuṇīs there are also five: (1) supreme-dharma ordination; (2) "Welcome!" ordination; (3) preceptor-dharma ordination; (4) envoy ordination; (5) karma-procedure ordination. Together these make ten kinds.

Question: What is the essential doctrine (*zōng*, 宗) of the vinaya, and what is its substance (*tǐ*, 體)?

Answer: Its essential doctrine is the precepts. Yet there are differing explanations. According to the Eastern Pagoda Commentary (*Dōngtǎ Shū*, 東塔疏), the precepts are the essential doctrine. As for the substance of the vinaya: it is properly verbal and bodily karma as substance. One may choose as one sees fit. Answer: Among the twelve sense-fields, its substance is the dharma-field. According to the Sautrāntika school, its substance is the non-associated formations. According to the Sarvāstivāda school, its substance is form. Both are subsumed under the dharma-field.

Question: Among the three karmas that the vinaya prevents, why does it not prevent mental karma?

Answer: Because the precepts it prevents are individual-liberation precepts, which are form-dharmas, they only prevent bodily and verbal karma. As for preventing the mind, that pertains to the precepts of meditation and the path. Therefore they can prevent the mind. Furthermore, because the power of the śrāvaka is limited, they do not prevent mental karma; they only address bodily and verbal karma.

Question: Among the thirteen [saṃghāvaśeṣa offenses] of the same category, why do nine result in an offense on first commission, while four require admonition before the offense is complete?

Answer: Because the conditions for committing offenses differ. Those that rely on a support require the saṃgha to establish an admonition. Therefore the four precepts, being similar in their reliance on support, require the saṃgha to establish an admonition. The nine precepts have no such reliance; committing the act immediately constitutes the offense. Therefore no admonition is required.

Question: What offense does "corrupting families" incur?

Answer: Because it violates the saṃgha's admonition, one incurs a saṃghāvaśeṣa offense. If one simply commits the act without having been admonished, the offense classification differs. Picking flowers by oneself incurs a pācittika offense. Corrupting families and engaging in bad conduct incurs a duṣkṛta offense.

Question: What matter does the precept against corrupting families admonish?

Answer: The words of the admonition constitute a peripheral matter. If the saṃgha says there is partiality or there is anger, that constitutes a saṃghāvaśeṣa.
