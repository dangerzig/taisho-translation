# 大方廣佛花嚴經修慈分
## Chapter on Cultivating Loving-Kindness from the Avataṃsaka Sūtra

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0306

Translated from the Chinese. Tang dynasty, translated by Devaprajña (提雲般若) and others

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time, the Buddha was on Vulture Peak Mountain in the city of Rājagṛha, together with an immeasurable assembly of great bodhisattvas, with Maitreya Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva as their leader.

At that time, from the east, ten billion Brahmā devas, all abiding in loving-kindness, came to the Buddha's place, prostrated at the Buddha's feet, and with many wondrous offerings made offerings to the Buddha. When the offerings were complete, each sat upon a lotus seat born of abundant merit, with reverence and respect gazing upon the Tathāgata. From the south, west, north, the four intermediate directions, above, and below, the Brahmā devas who came all did likewise.

At that time, the Brahmā hosts in the Buddha's presence each gazed upon one another with eyes of loving-kindness, and together at the same time, with open countenances, gazed attentively at Maitreya Bodhisattva.

Then Maitreya Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, knelt with palms joined, and addressed the Buddha: "Greatly virtuous World-Honored One! You who are omniscient, who correctly understand and perceive the nature of all dharmas, who fully know the good and evil karma of sentient beings: it is because of this that ordinary, deluded beings transmigrate through birth and death. You skillfully open and awaken the path of the three vehicles, and the return of the three vehicles to the single vehicle. You perceive without error that within the veils and coverings of afflictions of all sentient beings, there is the tathāgatagarbha. You also know that all dharmas are empty, like dreams, illusions, and mirages, without solidity. Yet your great compassion is inexhaustible. Through skillful means, you cause ordinary beings to see the wondrous marks of the Buddha's form-body. The Buddha's body is what the prajñāpāramitā accomplishes: naturally true, eternally abiding and unchanging, like empty space. If there are sentient beings who diligently cultivate merit and wisdom, who do not follow the mind-consciousness in chasing after sense objects, who are not like thirsty deer in the wilderness pursuing mirages as though they were water: such persons will be able to see the Buddha, constantly hear the teaching of the Dharma, and also be able to follow the teaching and practice in accord with principle. World-Honored One! I now wish to ask the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyaksaṃbuddha a small question. May you compassionately explain it for me. World-Honored One! How can a bodhisattva, with little exertion toward anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, at ease and without weariness, swiftly realize the vast Buddha-Dharma? How can a bodhisattva, while in birth and death, not suffer the oppression of immeasurable sufferings and swiftly bring the Buddha-Dharma to perfection?"

At that time, the World-Honored One told Maitreya Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva: "Excellent, Maitreya! Your questions to me have always been good. What you now ask is most in accord with my mind. Out of compassion for all devas, humans, and all sentient beings in the world, for the benefit and happiness of immeasurable beings, you are able to ask me such a question. I shall now explain it for you, analyzing it in detail, so that all bodhisattvas, without undergoing hardship, may swiftly realize the Buddha's bodhi.

"Child of the Buddha! If there are sentient beings who, seeking bodhi and cultivating various practices, wish to be constantly at ease, they should cultivate the mind of loving-kindness in order to tame themselves. By cultivating in this way, in every moment of thought they constantly and fully practice the six pāramitās. They will swiftly attain the stages of the various patiences, swiftly perfect unsurpassed perfect enlightenment, become complete in the ten powers, four fearlessnesses, eighteen exclusive dharmas, thirty-two marks, and eighty minor characteristics, adorning their bodies with the most supreme merit. To the end of the future they will abide in eternal ease and bliss, and will also be able to eliminate the heavy karmic obstructions accumulated since beginningless time by all sentient beings.

"Child of the Buddha! If bodhisattvas cultivate the mind of loving-kindness, they should be in an empty, quiet, and secluded place. With pure faith, gathering all mental dharmas, they should contemplate their bodies, examining the upper and lower limbs: all are aggregates of atoms, composed of the combination of earth, water, fire, and wind. They should further reflect that within each and every one of those atoms, there is empty space, and all that empty space has the characteristic of containing and receiving. They should also visualize those atoms as pure, clear, and luminous: on the outside like lapis lazuli, on the inside like purple gold, wondrously beautiful, soft, and fragrant. They should further contemplate that all the sentient beings in all the worlds, each and every one of their limbs, and each and every atom of their limbs, are all likewise. When the bodhisattva has made this definitive understanding regarding the atoms of oneself, others, and all sentient beings, they should further visualize that within each and every atom of their own body there are buddha-lands. The palaces within are made of lapis lazuli, with silver gates and golden pillars, vast and lofty, their light shining through. Jeweled halls are arrayed, jeweled walls surround them, and jeweled pavilions and towers are distributed throughout. In each there are heavenly jeweled beds with layered cushions and embroidered coverings placed upon them. There are also immeasurable excellent gardens surrounding and adorning them. In those gardens there are bathing pools, all with banks of the seven treasures, golden railings encircling them on all four sides, clear springs flowing and filling them, fragrant sandalwood paste for mud, golden sand strewn between, water of eight meritorious qualities filling them to clarity and purity. Padma flowers, utpala flowers, kumuda flowers, and puṇḍarīka flowers, lotuses in full bloom, are spread all over them. On all four sides of the pools are many jeweled trees, with true pearls for flowers, their radiance flourishing. Their fruits are ripe, complete in fragrance and flavor. Beneath all the trees are placed heavenly jeweled seats. Before each seat, jeweled vessels are arrayed in rows, filled to overflowing with sweet-dew delicacies. They should further visualize that all these buddha-lands have blue-green lapis lazuli for their ground, wondrously adorned with the seven treasures in variegated patterns. The atoms of all the soil in these lands are pure, fine, and wonderful, like heavenly jewels, their light brilliant as the blazing sun, their color beautiful as Jambu-river gold, their fragrance pervasive as uraga-candana, their texture soft as kācilindika cloth, and when they touch the body they give rise to delight.

"Having made this contemplation, one should then visualize that from the east, all the sentient beings of all worlds all come and enter the palaces of these buddha-lands within oneself. From the south, west, north, the four intermediate directions, above, and below, all the sentient beings of those worlds all do likewise. One should further visualize that all these sentient beings of the six destinies are all of the same deportment, similar in appearance, with soft bodies always bearing fragrance, with the marks of a man, fully adorned, free from all suffering and affliction, receiving heavenly happiness. When these sentient beings need clothing and adornments, they immediately go to the kalpa-trees, and whatever they need appears at the mere thought, and they are beautifully adorned with various accoutrements, like the Paranirmitavaśavartin devas. Moreover, fragrant breezes come from the eight directions, and when these breezes touch the body, they cause the mind to feel delight. There are musical instruments that, without anyone playing them, move with the breeze and produce wondrous sounds. These sentient beings may be in the palaces, or wandering in the gardens, or eating heavenly delicacies, or holding jeweled vessels and drinking sweet dew. Some sit upon lotus terraces, wearing garlands that drape down on both sides, with abundant wealth and complete provisions. According to their preferences, they enjoy various pleasures, with joyful faces, body and mind at ease, always free from illness, in the prime of youth and beauty, neither aging nor dying. Their merit, virtue, and power are all of one kind. None are servants to others. They are all able to destroy lust, anger, and delusion, and will realize bodhi and ultimate ease and bliss.

"Child of the Buddha! The practitioner of loving-kindness, while among these sentient beings, if they see a single sentient being who is antagonistic toward them, and their mind dwells on this without generating love and affection, should then deeply examine themselves with wisdom: 'In past lives, I certainly created heavy karmic obstructions toward this person. Because of this, today again it obstructs my bodhi. If I do not generate joy toward this person, then toward all other sentient beings as well, I should not perform any benefit.' Why? Because since beginningless time in birth and death, there is no sentient being who has not in the past harmed me. If toward this sentient being I do not generate compassion, then toward all the rest it will be the same. I now universally benefit all sentient beings, and therefore toward this person I will certainly practice loving-kindness. They should further reflect: 'The causes and conditions of anger cause sentient beings to fall into the hells. If one harbors resentment, in future lives one will be born among venomous snakes. If in my future lives I receive such retribution, it will greatly please that person. Therefore I should abandon all anger and resentful thoughts. If I am much given to anger and resentment, the Buddhas of the ten directions presently existing will all see me and think: "How is it that this person, who seeks bodhi, gives rise to anger and resentment? This deluded person, because of anger and hatred, cannot even free himself from his own sufferings; how then can he rescue all sentient beings?" Those with much anger, in every life where they are born, will have bodies filled with evil poison.' Therefore one should cultivate the mind of compassion and loving-kindness, forever abandoning anger and resentment, equally and impartially benefiting and bringing ease and bliss to all sentient beings.

"Having reflected in this way and separated from anger and resentment, one should next visualize that the Buddhas of the ten directions, together with the bodhisattvas and the great śrāvaka assemblies, all come and enter the palaces of one's buddha-lands. These Tathāgatas are in bodily stature one and a half times the size of a human, complete in all marks, upright, fragrant, and pure, adorned with heavenly garments. Each sits upon a lion seat of a thousand-petaled lotus, each surrounded by an immeasurable assembly, covered with jeweled canopies, hung with many jeweled banners, and draped with various garlands all around. Heavenly instruments play without being struck, their music harmonious and elegant, delighting all who hear. Fragrant breezes gently stir, blowing the jeweled trees, banners, canopies, nets, garlands, and other objects, which produce wondrous sounds praising the various merits of the Tathāgata. Golden vessels adorned with the seven treasures, their radiance like the sun and moon, their fragrance like firm dark candana, filled with sweet dew: these are offered to all the Buddhas, bodhisattvas, and śrāvaka assemblies. All the bodhisattvas and arhats sport in joyful happiness within the most supreme Dharma of the Tathāgata. One should further visualize that all sentient beings sit before the Buddhas, and the Buddhas teach them the practice of cultivating loving-kindness, just as I now cultivate it. The sound of their words is beautiful and delights the heart, causing all sentient beings to obtain the most supreme happiness. It is like a person who obtains sweet-dew water and bathes, relieving fatigue and suffering, the body and spirit refreshed. So too is this: when the Dharma moistens the mind, afflictions are extinguished, body and mind become tranquil, and one forever attains ease and bliss.

"One should further visualize that each and every atom of all these jeweled banners, canopies, garments, and other objects has a radiance brighter than the sun, is soft, fine, and smooth like the touch of a heavenly body, emits a fragrance like ox-head candana, has a color pure like vaiḍūrya, in which the images of all things appear. One should also visualize that each and every atom of the bodies of those Tathāgatas is soft and radiant, surpassing the former atoms a hundredfold, a thousandfold. One should further contemplate that all the sentient beings one has visualized are empty in nature and without self, like dreams, illusions, mirages, and eye-floaters. All the Buddhas are also thus: their self-nature is empty and originally without self. Ordinary, deluded beings, due to their ignorance, falsely cling to a self-nature in them, and therefore cannot be liberated from birth and death. One should further contemplate that the forms and characteristics of all dharmas are subtle and all are empty and quiescent. Ordinary people, through their own discrimination, give rise to various sense-realms. Within their own discrimination, they bind themselves. As long as they have not yet understood the self-nature of mind, for that entire time they are as if in a dream, falsely attached to various objects. One should further contemplate: all three realms are empty; space does not obstruct space; my mind of loving-kindness is still narrow and limited. One should also reflect: just as all sentient beings and all Buddhas are empty in nature and without self, one should know that one's own body is also thus, and all these lands are merely visualized.

"Having understood this, one should further contemplate that within each and every atom of all that exists, there are buddha-lands of the three times, and these buddha-lands are the most ultimately pure, surpassing all the buddha-lands previously visualized. The Buddhas of the three times, the sentient beings of the three times, and the adornments of the three times are all fully present. The kalpas of the three times enter into a single moment of thought. In each and every moment of thought, the Buddhas of the three times sit everywhere and universally appear before all sentient beings. Some enter dhyāna concentration, some teach the wondrous Dharma, some partake of fine food, some drink sweet dew. Before each and every Buddha, the bodhisattvas and arhats of the three times sit in a circle. The joys of the three times fill their bodies. One also sees one's own body in the presence of all the Buddhas, receiving such bliss of the three times. One should further visualize that in each and every moment of thought, one's body of the three times each holds immeasurable supreme offerings and makes offerings to all the Buddhas, bodhisattvas, and śrāvakas, and also gives to all classes of sentient beings. In each and every moment, from one's body arise various fragrant clouds. Within the clouds are immeasurable jeweled canopies, adorned and embellished, covering all the Buddhas, Tathāgatas, bodhisattvas, and śrāvakas, and the sentient beings of the six destinies above. The clouds further rain heavenly sweet dew, and fine dark ox-head candana powder, māndārava flowers, mahāmāndārava flowers, padma flowers, kumuda flowers, puṇḍarīka flowers, wondrous fragrant flowers, and wondrous-mind flowers, all showering down from the sky. Lightning flashes blaze like the spreading rays of the sun. Thunder rumbles, and all who hear it are delighted. All the Buddhas, bodhisattvas, śrāvakas, and sentient beings, whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down, in all four postures their bodies constantly receive the most supreme ease and bliss.

"Child of the Buddha! Just as a bhikṣu who enters the meditation on totality regards all things as earth, or as water, fire, or wind, and with such understanding gathers the mind: so too the practitioner of loving-kindness, with the superior understanding of loving-kindness, gathers and upholds the mind. One should further reflect: 'The ease and bliss I now bestow upon sentient beings is merely a visualization, like an illusion and a conjuration. Just as a magician creates magical effects, so too do I bestow upon sentient beings various kinds of ease and bliss. Furthermore, just as magical objects have no self-nature, all sentient beings are also thus, originally without the nature of self or mine. Furthermore, just as a thirsty deer, in the midst of a mirage, falsely generates the thought of water and wearily pursues it, my mind practicing loving-kindness should be known to be likewise. Furthermore, just as in a mirage, water cannot be found, all dharmas are also thus, without any self-nature. Furthermore, just as in a dream one sees various things and the dreaming mind discriminates them as real, yet upon waking they are nowhere to be found: one should know that all dharmas are also thus. Just as a person with diseased eyes sees various things in the pure sky and considers them real, but later obtains agada medicine and cures the eye disease, and all the things seen vanish: so too sentient beings, because of the view of self and the view of extremes, have the thought of self. If they obtain the medicine of wisdom and eliminate these views, all thoughts of self will also cease. Therefore, I should cultivate loving-kindness in this way, as if awakening from a dream, free from self and mine.'

"Child of the Buddha! You should know that this practitioner of loving-kindness, as long as they are not yet able to separate from discrimination and not yet able to cease giving rise to the view of self and mine, will constantly obtain the merits of the six kinds of Brahmā-heavens. If they abandon discrimination and are free from self and mine, this is called the great, vast loving-kindness. All karmic obstructions from past lives will be entirely eliminated, and before long they will realize unsurpassed bodhi.

"Child of the Buddha! All bodhisattvas should cultivate the mind of loving-kindness in this way. Because you cultivate loving-kindness, you are called 'the one of loving-kindness.'

"Child of the Buddha! If good men and good women hear this Sūtra on Cultivating Loving-Kindness, they will be able to eliminate the evil karmic obstructions accumulated since beginningless time, be free from all sickness and calamity, be loved and respected by all people, and in the interim or at the time of death, they will certainly come to see all the Buddhas of the ten directions. They will receive a prediction of anuttarā-samyak-saṃbodhi, or attain samādhi, or attain Dharma-patience, or gain entrance to the gate of dhāraṇī. Their minds will be at peace, free from the fear of death. They will forever be free from the suffering of all evil destinies and will certainly be born in the supremely pure land of the Buddha of Ultimate Bliss.

"Child of the Buddha! Just as if a person were to fill the three realms with the seven treasures and offer them to the Tathāgata three times daily for a full kalpa, the merit of such a person should be understood as equal to merely hearing this sūtra; how much more so if one can cultivate and practice it! Even if immeasurable Buddhas and Tathāgatas were to speak of such merit for a full kalpa, they could not exhaust it."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, Maitreya Bodhisattva-Mahāsattva and the Brahmā hosts who had come from the lands of the ten directions all greatly rejoiced, believed, accepted, and respectfully practiced it.
