# Treatise on the Essential Meaning of the Nine Verses of the Holy Mother of Buddhas Prajñāpāramitā

## *Shèng Fómǔ Bōrě Bōluómìduō Jiǔsòng Jīngyì Lùn* (聖佛母般若波羅蜜多九頌精義論)

Taishō 1516

Composed by the Bodhisattva Śreṣṭhadeva (*Shèngdé Chìyī*, 勝德赤衣, 'Red-Robed Bodhisattva of Superior Virtue'). Translated by Dharmapāla (*Fǎhù*, 法護) and others of the Song dynasty.

*Note: This is a tantric commentary on nine verses that condense the Prajñāpāramitā into contemplative formulas. Each verse uses a simile to demonstrate the emptiness of one aspect of experience: the six sense-bases are like reflections, visible form is like a magical illusion, sound is like an echo, smell-taste-touch are like a dream, actions are like a mechanical puppet, momentary dharmas are like a mirage, subject-and-object are like mirror-images, realization is like the moon reflected in water, and ultimate wisdom is like space. The second fascicle includes an elaborate tantric visualization (sādhana) involving seed-syllables (hṛīḥ, hūṃ, ka, ha), fire-wheels, lotus thrones, moon-disks, and the visualization of the bodhisattva Māmakī (or a related prajñā-deity) with eight arms, three faces, and various implements. The text bridges the Prajñāpāramitā philosophical tradition and the Vajrayāna contemplative tradition.*

---

## Fascicle One

> The wisdom of Prajñāpāramitā,
> Its essence an accumulation of wholesome treasures and assemblage of merit:
> All the pāramitās
> Are by nature eternally abiding.
> Free from all conceptual elaboration, without obstruction,
> Free from all discrimination, attaining peace.
> Most supreme, subtle, and without self-nature,
> Free from all names, characteristics, and the like.
> Through skillful means, the dharma of the three vehicles is taught;
> Yet the attainments of those three vehicles
> Are all causes of the wisdom of all wisdoms.
> Homage to the Prajñāpāramitā,
> The supreme wisdom that reaches the other shore.
> If a person delights in correct contemplation,
> One should, regarding the meaning of these nine verses,
> Comprehensively and in accord with principle, reflect and discern.

### The Nine Verses

> From karma's dominant force arises
> What is called the characteristic of the six sense-bases.
> This itself is said to further produce [phenomena]:
> Its causes appear like reflections.

> Like an illusory conjured city,
> The observer is also an illusion.
> Like the colors one sees there,
> Karma-created, the world is likewise.

> Whatever sounds of teaching the dharma exist,
> These are the domain of hearing.
> All are like echoes responding to sounds,
> Through conditions, hearing and what is heard arise.

> Smelling fragrance, discerning taste,
> Touch and other objects of attachment:
> All of these are like dreams.
> Though obtained, they are nothing at all.

> Like a mechanical wheel fashioned into a person,
> All formations act without substance.
> As those actions are performed,
> The body-wheel is likewise without self.

> Whatever various things are obtained,
> They arise in the most extreme instant (*kṣaṇa*).
> These are equal to a mirage:
> Seen, they immediately perish without characteristic.

> What is grasped is like a reflection.
> From beginninglessness it arises from the mind.
> Both that characteristic and consciousness
> Are mutually like reflections.

> Contemplating within one's own pure seed,
> When the moon of wisdom appears,
> It is like the moon in water:
> Manifest before one, yet nothing at all.

> The wisdom of one who is in correspondence (*yoga*):
> That is itself the characteristic of space.
> Therefore what wisdom knows
> Is entirely like the characteristic of space.

---

### Commentary

As the preceding verse states:

> "The supreme wisdom that reaches the other shore:
> If a person delights in correct contemplation,
> One should, regarding the meaning of these nine verses,
> Comprehensively and in accord with principle, reflect and discern."

"Supreme wisdom" (*shènghuì*, 勝慧) means the characteristics of hearing, reflecting, and meditating. "Other shore" (*bǐàn*, 彼岸) means the limit, the furthest edge. "Reaching" means arriving at the ultimate edge, the place free from all discrimination. This meaning holds through to the end. "Correct contemplation" (*zhèngguān*, 正觀) means the characteristic of non-inversion. "Delights in" (*lèyù*, 樂欲) means attention and aspiration by nature. "That meaning" (*bǐyì*, 彼義) means the meaning contained in those nine verses when they are recited; "meaning" is the gate of meaning. "Reflect and discern" (*sīzé*, 思擇) means to contemplate and determine.

What is contemplated? The verse says "comprehensively" (*zǒnglüè*, 總略). "Comprehensively" means to encompass and summarize.

Here one should ask: Why teach comprehensively? Answer: In order to enable those of dull faculties to understand the meaning.

Having established the nine verses above, the commentary now explains them in sequence.

---

#### First Verse

> "From karma's dominant force arises
> What is called the characteristic of the six sense-bases.
> This itself is said to further produce [phenomena]:
> Its causes appear like reflections."

"Karma" (*yè*, 業) means wholesome and unwholesome karma. "Dominant force" (*zēngshàng*, 增上) means the dominant force of karma. Through the dominant force of those karmas, there is arising. What arises? The verse says "the characteristic of the six sense-bases." "Bases" (*chù*, 處) means the places upon which consciousness depends and from which it arises; therefore they are called "bases." What are they? The six internal bases of the eye and so forth. "Characteristic" (*xiàng*, 相) means a sign or indication.

If these six sense-base characteristics give rise to something, then from them further dharmas are produced. This is stated thus: the meaning is conclusively established.

Question: In the ultimate truth, what is the self-nature? The verse itself explains: "Its causes appear like reflections." Because reflections are taken as the simile, within reflections the agent, the action, and the thing done are all empty and without nature. This meaning is concluded.

---

#### Second Verse

> "Like an illusory conjured city,
> The observer is also an illusion.
> Like the colors one sees there,
> Karma-created, the world is likewise."

Just as a magical illusion conjures a city, the one who later observes it is also an illusion. Neither of the two truly exists. Why? Because they are not truly produced by nature. Yet both the seer and the seen, those two forms of color, are externally resistant and are all karma-created illusions. What is seen in the three realms of the world is like that; the meaning is the same. This illusion and that illusion have no difference in nature. Therefore the next verse says "like sound and echo." To establish this meaning:

---

#### Third Verse

> "Whatever sounds of teaching the dharma exist,
> These are the domain of hearing.
> All are like echoes responding to sounds,
> Through conditions, hearing and what is heard arise."

"Teaching the dharma" means what is produced by the dominant force of the speaker. The corresponding sound is the domain of hearing. If the domain of hearing is thus, then all remaining dharmas likewise arise in this way. Therefore the simile of sound and echo is taken. Sound and echo are the same as all other dharmas. The term "all" here indicates no difference. "Through conditions" means that hearing and the rest arise through conditions. If all that exists is produced, then hearer, heard, and all that is obtained are conditioned. Therefore all sounds are like echoes. As stated, this meaning is concluded. Therefore the next verse says "all like a dream." To establish this meaning:

---

#### Fourth Verse

> "Smelling fragrance, discerning taste,
> Touch and other objects of attachment:
> All of these are like dreams.
> Though obtained, they are nothing at all."

"Smelling fragrance" means the domain of the nose-consciousness: the fragrances and such that are produced. "Discerning taste" means the domain of the tongue-consciousness: discerning the various tastes. "Touch" means the domain of the body-consciousness: feeling the various tangibles. In all such domains, what is sought and delighted in gives rise to attachment; in each domain, there is particular binding. According to each binding, fragrance, taste, touch, and the rest are each separately experienced. If in those domains one grasps at an "obtained" characteristic, it cannot actually be obtained. Therefore the verse says "all like a dream." "All" here indicates no difference.

---

#### Fifth Verse

> "Like a mechanical wheel fashioned into a person,
> All formations act without substance.
> As those actions are performed,
> The body-wheel is likewise without self."

Like a mechanical device (*huànlún*, 幻輪) that fashions the appearance of a human body: the person produced by the illusion fully possesses various actions. It is also like a person who nominally has an agent and an action, and who also has activities of going and coming. "All" (*zhū*, 諸) means various categories of what is done. What is done? The body produced by illusion. If such a body is produced by illusion, then that illusory body truly has no self. "No" (*wú*, 無) means "free from." "Self" (*wǒ*, 我) means "master" or "sovereign." "No self" means being free from a self. Therefore in this there is no agent. In the ultimate truth, there is nothing at all. Therefore the verse says "all formations act without substance." "Without substance" means without power or capacity. Now, since there is no power or capacity, there is no sovereign agent. If the person produced by illusion has no sovereign, then despite what is displayed, there is nothing real. All dharmas are likewise, ultimately without substance. Here one should understand the intention of "no difference." Therefore the next verse says "equal to a mirage." To establish this meaning:

---

#### Sixth Verse

> "Whatever various things are obtained,
> They arise in the most extreme instant.
> These are equal to a mirage:
> Seen, they immediately perish without characteristic."

"Various" (*zhǒngzhǒng*, 種種) means many categories. "Obtained" (*suǒdé*, 所得) means the object-characteristics grasped by discrimination. Those grasped objects arise in the most extreme instant (*jí chànà*, 極剎那); "most extreme instant" means instant after instant. "Arise" (*shēng*, 生) means coming into being. If there is arising in the most extreme instant, then all dharmas produced from those extreme instants are impermanent. This meaning is concluded.

---

## Fascicle Two

Here one should ask: In the ultimate truth, what is the self-nature? Answer: The verse itself uses the simile "equal to a mirage."

"Mirage" (*yángyàn*, 陽焰) means the provisional combination of three things: ground-dust, sunlight, and [heated air]. Like a mirage-cluster, it is seen and then immediately perishes. Therefore the verse says "seen, they immediately perish without characteristic."

All the various obtained domains are likewise: though each is manifested, all are without self-nature. Why? Because the self-natures of those things do not harmonize before and after; their natures are not equal. Fools grasp at them, turning within a single nature. Therefore these all belong to the worldly, conditioned realm of sentient beings.

---

#### Seventh Verse

> "What is grasped is like a reflection.
> From beginninglessness it arises from the mind.
> Both that characteristic and consciousness
> Are mutually like reflections."

"What is grasped is like a reflection" means this resembles that. What is it similar to? Like seeing the image of a face in a mirror. How so? It arises from the mind: since it is produced by mind alone, the mind is bound to what it grasps, the external object-characteristics, without abandoning them by nature. This meaning is concluded.

Furthermore, just as the externally grasped face-image in the mirror is actually dharmas whose habitual seeds are received into the mind, within the non-dual one grasps at the resistant characteristic of duality. From beginninglessness, arising from the mind like those reflections, whether simultaneously or at different times, what is observed and examined is all without nature. Not only are the mental dharmas [without nature], but the object-characteristics can also generate the cognizing consciousness. Therefore there are characteristics.

"Characteristics" means "gathering together." Not only one dharma: because what is grasped has such characteristics, what grasps is also the same. The intention here is: what is grasped has no nature; what grasps is also unreal. The verse says "mutually." "Mutually" means combined: mutually combined, all are like reflections. Being like reflections, the grasper and the grasped, the two, are mutually inseparable by nature. Those two dharmas, within the non-obtained, mutually emerge from the mind-dharma and manifest the appearance of being obtained.

The overall intention: the self-nature of the mind is originally bright, without the two characteristics of grasper and grasped, originally free from the defilements of desire, ignorance, and the like: pure, white, and free from all grasping. Therefore the next verse says "like the moon in water." To explain this meaning:

---

#### Eighth Verse

> "Contemplating within one's own pure seed,
> When the moon of wisdom appears,
> It is like the moon in water:
> Manifest before one, yet nothing at all."

"Contemplating" (*guān*, 觀) means concentration (*samādhi*); concentration means the characteristic of one-pointedness of mind. Within that concentration there is contemplation, because the mind has mastery. "One's own" (*zì*, 自) means one's own seed. "Pure" means clean. "Clean" means free from turbidity. From the seeds of one's own body, speech, and mind, there arises the image of the immediately preceding condition (*samanantarapratyaya*).

The syllable *hrīḥ* transforms into a universally blazing great radiance. Within that light, from the mind-seed there emerges ambrosia-light, vast and illuminating. That light further becomes an extremely great fire-wheel. Riding that fire-wheel, wisdom and skillful means emerge. From these is further born the syllable-gate *ha*. That syllable vibrates and emits a great sound. Within it appears an eight-petaled lotus. The syllable *ha* is at the center; inside and outside, one visualizes the sixteen divisions arranged in their positions. One further visualizes the syllable *ka*, which becomes stars arrayed in a circle all around. Furthermore, in the corresponding directions, one visualizes buddha-lotuses; those sixteen divisions all become moon-disks. Having contemplated thus, one further visualizes, upon the surface of one's own pure seed, the syllable *hūṃ* appearing with blazing radiance. Within that light there emerges a great fire of blazing flames.

One should contemplate one's own body: from within the wisdom-moon, the bodhicitta arises. From this further arises the vajra wisdom-moon. One should know: the "moon" is the vajra-wisdom pervading the world, whose wisdom-light illuminates all forms and characteristics. Therefore the verse says "when the moon of wisdom appears."

That vajra of wisdom accomplishes and produces wisdom and skillful means, the characteristic of nirvāṇa beyond comparison. From wisdom further arises, within the vajradhātu, the form of the Bodhisattva Māmakī (*Mómózhǐ*, 摩摩枳). Contemplating the extremely profound, most supreme, and secret samāpatti, clouds of mystery spread everywhere, universally manifesting radiance.

That bodhisattva has a blue-colored body, eight arms, and three faces: the central face is blue, the right face is yellow, and the left face is white. In the first right hand, a sword; in the second, an arrow; in the third, a hook; in the fourth, a vajra. In the first left hand, a wheel; in the second, a bow; in the third, a lasso; in the fourth, a bell. That bodhisattva unites principle and wisdom; all actions follow skillful means. Adorned with all characteristics, wearing the crown of Akṣobhya Buddha, manifesting a joyful and pleasing appearance, seated in cross-legged posture. Like the pure flower of *atasī* (*āduōxī*, 阿多西) with a supremely radiant solar disk, and further like the form of the great-bliss self-nature Vajrasattva, sprinkling ambrosia everywhere.

This bodhisattva's body is the Tathāgata's body, produced from wisdom and skillful means. Therefore the verse says "like the moon in water."

The word "like" (*ruò*, 若) here means "as if": as if the moon in water. This is emptiness. From this empty dharma, all dharmas are produced. What is produced is by nature originally unborn. Therefore the simile says "like the moon in water."

Here, if within the self-nature of the dharmadhātu one grasps at an existent nature, it is in truth without nature. Why? Because the verse says "manifest before one, yet nothing at all."

Since within the suchness (*tathatā*) of the non-obtaining of all dharmas, any action or realization is in truth impossible, whatever action or realization exists is all the expedient establishment of dharmas, equal to space. To establish this meaning:

---

#### Ninth Verse

> "The wisdom of one who is in correspondence:
> That is itself the characteristic of space.
> Therefore what wisdom knows
> Is entirely like the characteristic of space."

"Correspondence" (*xiāngyìng*, 相應) should be understood as the correspondence of wisdom and concentration. That correspondence is the vajra; having the dharma of correspondence, one is called "one who is in correspondence" (*xiāngyìng zhě*, 相應者).

Through the wisdom of that one in correspondence, within the signless and non-dual [sphere] of all dharmas, through wisdom and skillful means the Tathāgata's body is produced. This is not the conceptual-elaboration self-nature of the aggregates, sense-bases, elements, and so forth as previously described. This meaning is concluded.

The Tathāgata's body is constituted by the unmoving self-nature of the dharmadhātu and is originally unborn. Why? Because the self-nature of tathāgatas and the like is free [from determinations]. This is "without nature" (*wúxìng*, 無性), which cannot be spoken of.

"Without nature" means originally unborn; therefore it is called "without nature." Therefore the verse says "like the characteristic of space."

How so? Being free from the nature of all conceptual elaboration, it is like space. Since space has such a characteristic, one should examine the characteristic of space in accord with principle. "Characteristic" means indication or sign.

Furthermore, one should know: within the unobstructed self-nature of all dharmas, whatever function there is, it is wisdom entering the mind and mental factors of the three realms. "Like the characteristic of space" indicates that what is known (the obscuration of ignorance that follows upon sentient beings and their environments, the two worlds, the conceptual-elaboration self-nature of the aggregates, sense-bases, and elements) is the domain of wisdom. Having a produced nature, this known domain, according to its binding, is the awakening and comprehension of the discriminated domains of what is known. Therefore this is called "what is known" (*suǒzhī*, 所知). Thus omniscience (*yīqièzhì*, 一切智) and the wisdom of all wisdoms (*yīqièzhì zhì*, 一切智智): both are like space. One should contemplate them thus.

To summarize this meaning, the verse says "entirely" (*jiē*, 皆). "Entirely" means without remainder; without even a small remainder.

Here, excluding those among the śrāvaka practitioners who delight in realizing nirvāṇa with remainder (*sopadhiśeṣanirvāṇa*) (who, though they have realized the principle of the selflessness of persons, still grasp at the aggregates as existent), the remainder who realize nirvāṇa without remainder (*nirupadhiśeṣanirvāṇa*) and the characteristic of liberation are included in this teaching.

As the Buddha has said: "All seeds, all things, all existences are empty." Here we also exclude the emptiness taught by certain non-Buddhist schools. For this reason, one should know that in the world there is not even the slightest dharma that truly exists. All is like the characteristic of space. "Like the characteristic of space" should be understood as the true reality of the self-nature of space itself.

---

### Concluding Verse

Having explained these nine verses as described, in order to further reveal the fruit of the various wisdoms for the bodhisattvas and others, a summary verse is spoken:

> Reflecting in accord with principle on this true nature,
> All natures are without any support.
> All those who possess the supreme vow-mind of bodhi
> Shall obtain the great adornment of wisdom.
