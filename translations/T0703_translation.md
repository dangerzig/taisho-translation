# 燈指因緣經
## Sūtra on the Causes and Conditions of Lamp-Finger

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0703

Translated from the Chinese. Later Qin dynasty, translated by Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什).

---

If one plants even a small amount of good in the field of supreme merit, one will enjoy the pleasures of gods and humans and eventually attain nirvāṇa. Therefore the wise should diligently cultivate wholesome deeds. What is meant by "field of merit"? It is the Buddha himself. The Buddha's body radiates light like a mass of molten gold, adorned with virtuous qualities and wisdom. He possesses the perfectly complete eye and is well able to observe the faculties of all beings. He serves as a lamp for the dark world, and acts as a kindly guide for the deluded. He is replete with all virtues, and his fame is heard everywhere. The Sage, the World-Honored One, is the refuge of all. Therefore, when gods and humans wholeheartedly cultivate merit, none fails to receive its reward.

Long ago, in the city of Rājagṛha, surrounded by five mountains, among the five Magadha districts it occupied the innermost position. Within this royal city, the streets were well-aligned, the parks were broad and spacious, the terraces and lookouts were grand and beautiful, the halls and chambers were fine and exquisite, with lofty eaves open and bright, surrounded by balustrades. There were lovely groves and pools, most delightful. Their waters were clear and pure, warm and cool in due measure, with channels flowing in circuits, filling one into another. The forest trees were deep and verdant, branches and foliage dense and luxuriant, flowers and fruits abundant and flourishing, screening out the sun and moon. When the wind blew through the flowering groves, it produced a subtle and wondrous fragrance. That fragrance was rich and sweet, permeating all four directions, pervading the entire city of Rājagṛha. The victorious wise ones who cultivated the holy life, seeing that this place was exceptionally splendid, felt joy in their hearts and came from afar to gather there.

At that time, the ruler of this city was King Ajātaśatru, whose virtuous influence spread far and near, governing the kingdom with the true Dharma. Many people cultivated goodness, and the country was prosperous, the people numerous, peaceful, and happy. In the city there was a wealthy householder whose family fortune was immense, his storehouses overflowing, like Vaiśravaṇa, yet he had no heir. He prayed and made offerings to the spirits, begging for a son. Before long, his wife discovered she was with child, and after the full ten months, she gave birth to a boy. This child had planted the seeds of good fortune in previous lives. On the day he was born, one of his fingers emitted a great light that illuminated ten leagues in all directions. His parents were overjoyed and immediately gathered their relatives and physiognomists, held a great feast, and gave the child a name. Because of his finger's radiance, they named him Lamp-Finger (Dīpaṅguli). All who were gathered at the assembly, seeing his extraordinary sign, exclaimed in wonder. Among those assembled was a brahmin named Vaśu, versed in the four Vedas and widely learned, who knew all manner of things. Seeing the child's remarkable and extraordinary features, he smiled and said: "This child must be Nārāyaṇa, Śakra Devānām Indra, the Sun Deva, or some other great celestial being who has come to take birth here." When the child's parents heard these words, their joy redoubled, and they held a great charitable assembly, giving alms and making merit for seven days and seven nights. Thus word spread throughout the entire kingdom, and everyone said, "The householder has produced a blessed child!" The sound of his praise reached even the king.

When the king heard this, he immediately ordered the child to be brought. The householder received the command and at once carried the child to the palace gate. It happened that the king was holding a banquet with music and entertainments, and no one was available to announce the visitor, so the householder could not enter. But the child's finger-light shone through and illuminated the palace gardens, blazing brightly upon the king's person and throughout the palace buildings, so that all objects took on the color of gold. The light pervaded the entire inner palace, like a great body of water filling it to the brim. The king, astonished, asked: "Where does this light come from, suddenly illuminating my palace? Could it be that the World-Honored One, wishing to convert sentient beings, has come to my gate? Or could it be that the great celestial beings, Śakra Devānām Indra, the Sun Deva, or others, have descended?" The king immediately sent someone to look outside the gate. The messenger looked, returned, and reported to the king: "The child whom Your Majesty summoned earlier is now outside the gate. The child is on his nursemaid's shoulder, and his finger emits a radiant light that shines through to here. That is where the light comes from." The king ordered: "Bring the child in at once." Having seen the child, the king was deeply amazed, and took the child's hand himself to examine his features. After gazing at him carefully, he said: "The six non-Buddhist teachers claim there is no cause and effect. They are truly deceivers and frauds. If there were no cause and effect, how could this child, from the moment of his birth, have such extraordinary beauty and such conspicuous finger-light? Seen in this way, these non-Buddhists are causing sentient beings to fall headlong into the evil destinies. I am now certain that this child was not created by Maheśvara, nor by spirits, nor did he arise spontaneously. He must have obtained this fortunate reward through merit accumulated in past lives. Now I know that the Buddha's words are true and without falsehood. The Buddha taught that various kinds of karma adorn the world, yet all sentient beings, even seeing retribution with their own eyes, fail to cultivate merit. How strange!" The king further said: "I am still not certain whether this finger's radiance might be produced by the sun. To verify this, we must wait until midnight." When evening came, he placed the child upon an elephant and had the elephant lead the procession, while the king and his ministers together entered the park. The light from the child's fingers illuminated the darkness brilliantly, and looking at the birds and beasts, flowers and fruits in the park, everything was as clear as in daytime. Having observed this, the king heaved a sigh and said: "How true and wondrous is what the Buddha taught! Today I have conceived great and firm faith in cause and effect, and I deeply scorn the extreme delusion of the six teachers. Therefore my reverence for the Buddha has increased all the more."

At that time, Jīvaka addressed the king, saying: "In the sūtras, the Buddha said: 'It is because one does not see the workings of karma that greed and miserliness arise; because one sees the workings of karma, greed and miserliness are forever extinguished.' Having now seen Lamp-Finger's karmic reward, even if one were destitute, one ought to exhaust everything in cultivating wholesome deeds. How much more so for those who are wealthy and refuse to make merit?" While these words were being spoken, dawn broke, and they returned with Lamp-Finger to the palace. The king was greatly pleased and bestowed lavish treasures upon the child before sending him home.

Lamp-Finger gradually grew up. His father the householder sought a match for him, selecting from families of high standing equal to his own, and took a wife for him. The householder was wealthy, refined in manners and propriety. The household was harmonious, and the family fortune continued to grow. But prosperity inevitably gives way to decline, and those who come together must part. The householder and his wife both died at the same time. Just as the sun, reaching the place of setting, hides its radiance; as the sun once risen makes the moon's light invisible; as fire reduced to ash sees its blaze forever extinguished; as vigor and good complexion are ruined by illness; as the years of youth are encroached upon by old age; and as beloved life is seized by death, so his parents having died, the family livelihood gradually diminished. Lamp-Finger had been raised in wealth and ease since childhood and knew nothing of managing household affairs. He consorted with bad companions, indulged himself recklessly in wine and sensual pleasures, and spent money without limit. With no one to manage his stores and warehouses, they waxed and waned like the moon, diminishing day by day. According to the custom of that kingdom, once a year there was a great festival, and the people gathered at Mount Pañcaka. At that time, Lamp-Finger dressed extravagantly, with attendants and musicians all grandly arrayed, rivaling the king himself, and went to the festival grounds. The great assembly there, seeing him thus, all admired him respectfully. Then the crowd together drank and made merry, drums and bells competing, strings and songs sounding everywhere, dancing in the open fields, playing on the plains, their sounds of revelry shaking the mountains and filling the valleys. Afterward, a band of thieves, knowing Lamp-Finger had gone to the festival and had not yet returned, seized the opportunity to go to his house and steal all his wealth, taking everything. When Lamp-Finger returned in the evening and saw that his home had been plundered by thieves, with nothing left but wood, stone, and tiles, he fainted and fell to the ground. Others splashed water on him and he came to, weeping in sorrow, and thought: "My father took great pains to establish and maintain the family fortune, laboring to accumulate the treasures in our storehouses. He did all this, giving birth to me, hoping to have someone to entrust it to. How is it that I have not continued my father's work, but have idled and drifted, letting others take advantage of me? My father's remaining wealth has been lost in a single day, the storehouses are empty, the livestock scattered. I look around my house and find only myself, alone. The ornaments I wear and my carriages I must trade for food to meet my immediate needs. Once those are spent, what shall I do?"

At that time, his finger-light also went out. His wife, despising his poverty, abandoned him and left. His servants fled, his relatives severed ties. Even those who had once been his most intimate friends now treated him like enemies, fearing that he might beg from them and showing anger in advance. Even his wife had abandoned him; how much more so everyone else? One should understand that poverty is comparable to the hells. To live in destitution is no different from death. Having been accustomed to wealth and comfort, to suddenly fall into dire poverty, losing all support, with nowhere to find shelter: the fire of anxiety blazes in the heart, the poison of grief scorches and burns. His once-handsome complexion faded and a haggard appearance took its place. His body grew gaunt and emaciated, worn by hunger and thirst. His eyes sank deep into their sockets, the bones of his joints protruded, his skin stretched thin, sinews and veins showing through, hair tangled and wild, hands and feet shriveled and thin, his complexion ashen white, his whole body cracked and fissured. Without any clothes, he went to rubbish heaps to pick up coarse and tattered rags, piecing them together just enough to cover his private parts, the rest of his body exposed. He lay in refuse heaps with no mat or bedding. His former relatives and acquaintances saw him but did not recognize him. He begged for food along the streets like a starving crow. When he went to friends' houses hoping to beg for food, the gatekeepers turned him away. If he managed to slip in, he was shoved out and humiliated. When the master of the house came out intending to beat him with a whip, he bowed low, prostrated himself, and begged forgiveness, but the master looked upon him with contempt and did not even glance back. Even when allowed inside a house, because he was held in such low regard, no one spoke to him or offered him a seat. They gave him a small amount of food and drink, tossing it into his bowl, not allowing him to eat his fill.

According to the customs of that country, festivals were held for weddings, births, and head-shavings. He went to these gatherings hoping to beg for leftover food, but being despised, he was not invited to sit. They drove him about on errands. When he asked for more food, he received only a few scraps of leftovers, eating from the same bowl as the servants. He thought to himself: "How strange! How did I come to be so impoverished and wretched?" He said to himself: "My spirit is so confused and my mind so lost that I do not know whether this is still my original body or whether I have taken on a new one. My bitter suffering is unmatched in this world. Like a flowering tree without blossoms that the bees abandon; like frost-struck grass whose leaves curl and wither; like a dried-up pool where wild geese no longer swim; like a burned forest that deer do not approach; like a field whose crops have been harvested clean, with no one to glean; so is my poverty today. If I speak of my former wealth, people think it mere idle talk and will not believe me. Though people are many in this world, none knows me. Because of my poverty, every direction is closed to me. Like a wilderness burned by fire, where no one wishes to go; like a withered tree without shade, where no one seeks shelter; like seedlings struck by hail and frost, discarded and not gathered; like a den of poisonous snakes, which all avoid; like food mixed with poison, which none will taste; like an empty burial ground, which no one approaches; like a foul privy, where filth accumulates; like a butcher, whom people despise; like a habitual thief, whom people suspect. I too am like this: wherever I go, my every action invites reproach; whatever I say, my every word provokes censure. Though I speak good words, others consider them wrong. If I do a good deed, others consider it base. If I act deftly, they complain of frivolity; if I act slowly, they call me dull. If I praise others, they say it is flattery; if I do not praise, they criticize me, saying, 'This poor man never has a good word.' If I teach something, they say I am a deceitful old man who pretends to knowledge. If I speak at length, they say I am garrulous; if I keep silent, they say I am hiding my feelings. If I speak frankly, they call me coarse; if I try to accommodate, they call me sycophantic. If I frequently visit, they say I am scheming; if I do not visit, they say I am arrogant. If I agree with what others say, they say I am falsely currying favor; if I do not agree, they say I am self-willed. If I humble myself and defer, they curse me as mean and lowly; if I do not humble myself, they say, 'This poor man still thinks he is somebody.' If I relax a little, they call me a fool without propriety; if I restrain myself, they say I am putting on a show of respectability under my roughness. If I show good cheer, they say I am reckless and look like a madman; if I appear sorrowful, they say I harbor poison and never have a cheerful thought. If, hearing someone's words that are incomplete, I explain for them, they say I am a wretch who replaces wisdom with foolishness and has no shame. If I remain silent, they call me a blockhead who knows no reason. If I joke a little, they say I do not believe in karmic consequences. If I ask for something, they say I am shameless and greedy; if I ask for nothing, they say, 'He asks for nothing now, but later he will expect something big.' If I quote from scriptures, they say I am pretending to be learned; if my words are plain, they fault me for crudeness. If I discuss matters publicly on their merits, they say I am forcing my views; if I speak privately with good intentions, they say I am a slanderer. If I wear new clothes, they say I have borrowed finery; if I wear old clothes, they say I am wretched and shabby. If I eat and drink much, they say I am a starving glutton; if I eat and drink little, they say my belly is really hungry but I am pretending to be frugal. If I expound the scriptures, they say I am showing off my knowledge and exposing their ignorance; if I do not expound, they say I am a fool without learning, fit only to tend cattle. If I recount my former accomplishments, they say I am boasting of my deeds; if I say nothing of myself, they say my family background is shallow. For those in poverty, every step and word, every bow and glance, is nothing but fault. But for the rich and noble, even when they do wrong, no one sees any fault; whatever they do is considered perfectly fitting.

"The poor are like risen corpses: everyone is terrified. Like encountering a fatal disease, impossible to cure. Like a desolate wilderness with neither water nor grass. Like falling into the great ocean, drowning in the flood. Like someone pressing down on one's throat so one cannot breathe. Like a film over the eyes, not knowing where to go. Like thick grime, difficult to wash away. Like an enemy who, though sharing food and clothing, never abandons malice. Like a well in summer that one enters and cannot breathe. Like entering deep mud, stuck and unable to get out. Like a flash flood in the mountains, swift currents sweeping away and breaking trees. Poverty is like all these, full of hardship. And poverty can also destroy youthful vigor, good complexion, strength, reputation, lineage, wisdom, moral conduct, generosity, conscience, benevolence, faithfulness, bravery, and resolve. It can also give rise to hunger and cold, enmity, recklessness, narrow-mindedness, sorrow, bitterness, blame, and debt. All these sufferings arise from poverty. Just as a buried treasure contains many different things, so the buried treasure of poverty contains many kinds of bodily and mental suffering. But the wealthy possess fine dignified bearing, handsome appearance, generous temperament, broad-minded character, and flourishing propriety. They give rise to wisdom and courage, increase the family fortune, promote harmony among kin, and their good name is heard far and wide."

Lamp-Finger reflected: "My present misery has few parallels in the world. I wish to end my life, but I cannot bring myself to do it. What means can I find to sustain myself?" He further thought: "The most despised work in the world is carrying corpses. Though this work is loathsome, it at least brings no karmic retribution of suffering in future lives. If I were to do anything else, I might be involved in taking life or committing other unwholesome deeds. Considering this, let me take up this work." At that time, someone heard what he said and hired him to carry a corpse. Lamp-Finger took the payment and followed him, carrying the dead body to the charnel ground, intending to throw it down. But just then the corpse tightly embraced Lamp-Finger, like a small child clinging to its parents, gripping so firmly it would not let go. He used all his strength to pull it off but could not. The dead body stuck to his back like glue, impossible to remove. He pushed and shoved, but it would not separate, and he was filled with great terror. He thought: "Today, carrying this dead body, where can I go to survive?" He went to a caṇḍāla village and called out: "Who can remove this dead body from my back? I will pay you generously." The caṇḍālas all worked together with all their strength to pull it off, but it would not budge. Others who saw him cursed Lamp-Finger, saying: "What is this madman doing carrying a corpse into our village?" They competed to beat him and throw stones at him. His body was battered and torn, pain and fear overwhelming him at once. Someone took pity on him and led him toward the city. When he arrived at the city gate, the gatekeepers drove him back and beat him, refusing to let him near: "What kind of fool tries to enter the city carrying a corpse?" Seeing his body covered in wounds from the beatings, he was filled with bitter anguish and cried aloud, saying: "It was only for food that I took up this base work. Today I have suddenly encountered this great suffering. Because of my poverty, I did not choose my work and took up this lowly trade, hoping to earn a wage to keep myself alive. How is it that in a single day I should meet with such torment? I would rather die in some other way than go on living carrying a corpse!" He wept and spoke these words. At that time, the gatekeeper took deep pity on him and let him return home.

When he arrived at his empty dwelling, the beggars and destitute people who had been living there, seeing the corpse on his back from afar, all fled. Once he was inside, the corpse fell to the ground by itself. Lamp-Finger's terror only increased, and he fainted and fell to the ground; after a long time he revived. He then noticed that the dead body's fingers were entirely of pure gold. Though still afraid, seeing the fine gold, he went closer to look, and tested it with a knife: it was indeed real gold. Having obtained the gold, his heart filled with joy, and he cut off the head, neck, hands, and feet. After he had cut them off, they immediately grew back. In a short while, the gold heads, hands, and feet had accumulated into a pile taller than a person. Like a king who has lost his kingdom and regains his throne; like a blind person who recovers sight and can see clearly; like one who has long yearned for a beloved and finally meets her; like a practitioner of meditation who suddenly attains realization of the Way: so was Lamp-Finger's joy. His storehouses and treasures surpassed what he had before, his dignity and renown exceeded his former days, and his relatives, friends, wife, children, and servants all returned. Lamp-Finger sighed and said: "How strange it is! Wealth has great power, and can cause people to come flocking with utmost speed. How strange it is! Poverty has great power, and can cause even those closest to us to abandon us with utmost speed. When I was poor before, all my former intimates and associates cut off contact; not a single person would speak to me. Today they all eagerly attend upon me, joining their palms in respect. Even if one's birth were as exalted as Śakra's, one's courage as great as Rāma's, one's knowledge as vast as a divine teacher's, without wealth none of it would be worth anything. Whether the rich are foolish or wise, all call them good. Even those who truly know nothing are considered wise, and they gain reputations for courage and every virtue. Though one be ugly, old, and decrepit, young and beautiful women are happy to come to his side."

King Ajātaśatru heard that Lamp-Finger had become wealthy again and immediately sent people to take some of his treasure. But whatever they took turned into dead flesh, which they threw back into the house, and it became real gold again. Lamp-Finger, knowing the king desired this treasure, presented the gold head, hands, and feet to the king. The king received them and brought them back to the palace. Afterward, Lamp-Finger reflected and spoke this verse:

> "The five desires are most volatile, like lightning, poison-snakes, and insects.
> Glory and pleasure do not long endure." And so a mind of weariness arose in him.

He then distributed his precious treasures to the people, went forth from home in the Buddha's teaching to seek the Way, and through diligent cultivation attained arhatship. Though he had attained the fruit of the Way, the corpse-treasure continued to follow him always.

The monks asked the Buddha: "By what causes and conditions did the monk Lamp-Finger have such finger-light from birth? By what causes and conditions did he experience such poverty? And by what causes and conditions does this corpse-treasure constantly follow him?"

The Buddha told the monks: "Listen with sincere attention! I shall explain his past causes for you. The monk Lamp-Finger, in an ancient past life, was born into the family of a great householder in the country of Vārāṇasī. When he was a small child, he went out in a carriage to play. Returning late, the gates had already been shut. He called loudly for someone to open the door, but no one came. After a long time, his mother came and opened the door for him. He angrily cursed his mother, saying: 'Has the whole household gone to carry dead bodies? Have thieves come to rob us? Why is there no one to open the door for me?' Because of this karma, after death he fell into the hells. After the residual retribution of the hells, he was reborn among humans and experienced this poverty and suffering. As for the causes of the finger-light and the corpse-treasure, I will explain further. Ninety-one kalpas ago there was a buddha named Vipaśyin. After that buddha entered nirvāṇa, his teaching remained in the world. At that time, Lamp-Finger was a great householder of immense wealth. He went to a stūpa temple, prostrated with reverence, and saw that one finger of a clay image was broken and falling off. He repaired the finger, covering it with gold leaf. When the repair was complete, he made this vow: 'By the merit of this offering of incense, flowers, music, and of repairing this image, may I be reborn in heavenly or human realms and always enjoy honor, nobility, and wealth. Should I ever lose my fortune, may I quickly recover it. And may I go forth in the Buddha's teaching and attain the Way.' Because he repaired the Buddha-image's finger, he obtained the finger-light and the corpse-treasure. Because of his harsh speech, after leaving the hells, he received the retribution of poverty."

When the Buddha had finished speaking the Sūtra on the Causes and Conditions of Lamp-Finger, all the devas and humans scattered celestial flowers and played celestial music. Having finished their offerings, they returned to their heavenly palaces.

Because of these causes and conditions, even a small amount of merit cultivated toward an image yields such karmic reward, all the way to nirvāṇa. If this is so for a mere image, how much more so for the Dharma-body of the Tathāgata himself? For those who practice as the Buddha taught, such merit is beyond measure. If one wishes to be reborn among gods and humans and enjoy all manner of happiness, one should listen to the Dharma with a sincere mind. Because of the causes of harsh speech, one receives great suffering as retribution; therefore one should fear the multitude of sufferings and distance oneself from harsh speech and all unwholesome deeds. Considering this, the wealth and glory of all worldly people is not worth clinging to, nor should one take pleasure in the honor and nobility of gods and humans. One should know that poverty is a great mass of suffering; to cut off poverty, one should not be stingy or greedy. Therefore the sūtra says: "Poverty is indeed the greatest suffering."
