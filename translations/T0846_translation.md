# 外道問聖大乘法無我義經
## Sūtra on the Non-Buddhist's Question about the Meaning of Non-Self in the Holy Mahāyāna Dharma

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0846

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dharmadeva (法天)

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the great assembly. At that time there was a non-Buddhist who had doubts he wished to resolve. Confused about the Mahāyāna practice, he came to the place of the Buddha, bowed his head with reverence, joined his ten fingers, and asked about the meaning of non-self: "Great man! You are the omniscient one, and you constantly teach that this body is without self. If the body is without self and the fundamental nature is also without existence, how do you explain the existence of such things as weeping and laughing, play and jest, hatred and love, and divisive speech? From what do they arise? This is my doubt. I pray you will kindly remove it. As the Tathāgata has said, regarding the body and fundamental nature, how is it with regard to existence and non-existence?"

The Buddha said: "Non-Buddhist! Listen carefully and receive this well. I shall explain it for you."

The Buddha said: "Since the body and fundamental nature are originally empty in essence, to speak of either existence or non-existence makes two dharmas. To say it exists is all the more false."

The Buddha said: "Contemplate the entire body: hair, nails, skin, body-hair, both hands, both feet, down to fat, sinews, spleen, intestines, bone, and marrow. Search throughout, inside and out: you will not find any fundamental nature."

The non-Buddhist said: "Great man! If this fundamental nature cannot be found there, how could I, with my fleshly eyes, be able to see it? Or can it be seen with the heavenly eye?"

The Buddha said: "The heavenly eye sees that it is without color, without mark, and without abiding. Such seeing is non-seeing."

The non-Buddhist said: "If you speak thus, then the Great Sage speaks falsely. If that is not the case, how do we visibly see such things as weeping and laughing, play and jest, anger and rage, hatred and love, and divisive speech? Since it is thus, how can you say it does not exist? And when you say it either exists or does not exist, does that not make two meanings?" He further said: "Great man! If you cannot speak of it as either existing or not existing, how can you say that some are attached and some are unattached?" He further said: "Emptiness: what is it like?"

The Buddha said: "Just so, just so. Emptiness is not 'like' anything, because its essence cannot be grasped."

The non-Buddhist said: "If that is so, then weeping and laughing, play and jest, anger and rage, hatred and love, and divisive speech: where are they to be seen?"

The Buddha said: "They are like a dream, like a magical illusion, like a transformation, like the mark of a reflected image."

The non-Buddhist said: "What is the mark of a dream? What is the mark of magical illusion and transformation? What is the mark of a reflected image?"

The Buddha said: "Magical illusion and transformation are without mark. Emptiness is without anything to grasp. A dream is originally empty in essence, like a mirage. A reflected image is without color, empty and unreal. All that is seen, and indeed all things whatsoever, are like illusion, like transformation, like a dream, like a reflection. You should see them thus.

"Furthermore, there are two kinds of seeing that adorn suchness. That which adorns: this is called 'self,' this is called 'other.' This is named 'person,' named 'pudgala,' named 'human being,' named 'worldly conceptualization.' As for possessions, men and women, brothers, wives and concubines, and the like, these are called 'the adornment of what the mind conceptualizes.' In this dharma as it truly is, there is no self and no other, no person and no life, no pudgala and no sentient being, no world and no seer, no possessions and no men or women, no friends and no wives or concubines. In none of these things can an intrinsic nature be found. How then, in the world-transcending realm, could there be adornment, karmic retribution, good and evil, arising and ceasing? In the adornment of suchness and its karmic retribution, there is no good and no evil, no arising and no ceasing, no affliction and no happiness. All dharmas are each just like this!

"Furthermore, the two kinds of adornment, worldly and world-transcending, cause sentient beings, because of their adornment, to generate afflictions and dwell in cyclic existence, revolving for a long, long time without knowing suchness. Those who understand the Dharma contemplate these adornments and suspect that these are the causes of suffering. They regard suffering as evil and seek to be far from it, seeking liberation, yet they do not see the path. Foolish and deluded sentient beings, because of their confused clinging, revolve through birth-and-death and fall into the evil destinies. They practice worldly dharmas and do not see suchness, going round and round through cyclic existence, like a shuttle weaving a net, the thread going back and forth, again and again.

"Furthermore, it is like the sun and moon, which travel in two ways, appearing and disappearing by day and night in the world. All conditioned things are impermanent and will before long be destroyed. The revolution through birth-and-death, the coming and going, is just like this. Yet the essence of suchness is apart from all adorning words.

"Furthermore, devas, gandharvas, and their consorts who dwell in the heavens, because of the karmic retribution of their adornments, fall into all existences. There are also vidyādharas, accomplished yakṣas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas who, because of the karmic retribution of all their adornments, again fall into the hells. The evil-effort devas, though they accomplish merit through their supernatural powers, because of the karmic retribution of all their adornments, may also fall into those heavens.

"Furthermore, even Śakra and wheel-turning kings, who possess the most excellent virtues and the most excellent phrases, because of the karmic retribution of all their adornments, are reborn among animals. The wise, at all times, should distance themselves from the supreme great pleasures of the heavens, and constantly contemplate the mind of bodhi, which is luminous and vast, without intrinsic nature, without obstruction, and without any abiding place. All is empty, and one should further distance oneself from all idle speculation.

"Non-Buddhist! The mark of the mind of bodhi is neither hard nor soft, neither hot nor cold, without touch and without grasping. Furthermore, the mark of the mind of bodhi is neither long nor short, neither round nor square, neither fat nor thin. Furthermore, the mark of the mind of bodhi is not white, not black, not red, not yellow, without color and without mark. That mind of bodhi does not create marks and is not luminous display. It is without nature and without bondage. Like empty space, because it is without color, the mark of the mind of bodhi is apart from observation. Non-Buddhist! You do not know the mark of the mind of bodhi because it corresponds with prajñā-pāramitā. Furthermore, the mark of the mind of bodhi is intrinsically pure in nature, without anything and without comparison, invisible to sight. It is the most excellent phrase. Furthermore, the mark of the mind of bodhi is not like any object; nothing resembles it. It is like water forming foam: though one sees it, it has no real existence. It is like a magical illusion, like a mirage. It is like a lump of clay made into various pots and vessels: though they have many names, all are mere idle speculation. Greed, anger, delusion, and the rest are also illusory existences, of one taste with emptiness. Like lightning, they abide for but an instant and then are no longer seen. Contemplating that prajñā-pāramitā and performing various good deeds are also just like this! As for conversation, laughter, play and jest, song and dance, joy and pleasure, food, drink, desire, and love: all are like a dream. The practices of sentient beings are ultimately empty in essence. The mind is like empty space. Where then should doubt take its stand? Practice the practice of prajñā! Constantly observe in this way and understand the nature of all things. Naturally one attains liberation and reaches the most excellent phrase. The unsurpassed bodhi taught by all the Buddhas arises from this. You should contemplate in this way. One who contemplates thus attains the most excellent nirvāṇa. Even the transgressions committed in past lives are all completely eliminated, and immeasurable virtue is generated. In this very life one is no longer stained by transgressions. With focused and dedicated contemplative practice, one will certainly accomplish the goal. If one is not yet in accord with suchness, one should recite the non-suchness mantra and the vajra-bell suchness non-arising seal, and undertake the practice of accordance with suchness. One will certainly perfect the merits described above."

At that time the non-Buddhist, having heard these words, examined and observed carefully. All the nets of his doubt were entirely severed. Having contemplated in this way, he attained entry into the Great Vehicle. Gazing up with joy, he made obeisance and withdrew.
