# 佛說無量門微密持經
## Sūtra of the Subtle and Secret Dhāraṇī of Immeasurable Gates, Spoken by the Buddha

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1011

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

Heard as follows:

At one time, the Buddha was traveling in the kingdom of Vaiśālī, at the Great Tree Monastery. The Buddha told the worthy Mahāmaudgalyāyana: "Go and invite all the bhikṣus traversing the three-thousand great-thousand world system who practice either the disciple's path or the bodhisattva's path to assemble." Maudgalyāyana received the instruction, ascended to the summit of Mount Sumeru, and with the spiritual power of the path, his voice resounded throughout the Buddha-realm, making it universally known. Immediately, four hundred thousand bhikṣus assembled at the monastery. He further manifested his supernatural powers, causing all the bhikṣus of this world who practiced meditation to assemble at the monastery. Having bowed their heads, they stood to one side. The Buddha further told Huìjiàn Bodhisattva, Jìngshǒu Bodhisattva, Chúyōu Bodhisattva, Yújiè Bodhisattva, Qùgài Bodhisattva, Kuīyīn Bodhisattva, Dàiqì Bodhisattva, Zhòngshǒu Bodhisattva, Biànyīn Bodhisattva, and Maitreya Bodhisattva: "All of you, go and invite the bodhisattvas of the ten directions, throughout immeasurable Buddha-lands, who are one-birth-supplement, who have attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, who are non-retrogressive, and who have attained resolute faith, to all assemble here." They all received the instruction and responded accordingly. At that time, eight hundred koṭis of one-birth-supplement bodhisattvas, three hundred koṭis of bodhisattvas who had attained the patience of the non-arising of dharmas, ten billion non-retrogressive bodhisattvas, and six hundred koṭis of resolute-faith bodhisattvas, all bearing the Buddha's holy command, came by supernatural powers and assembled.

The worthy Śāriputra, seeing the great assembly gathered, thought: "I should ask the Tathāgata about the wondrous practice, to reveal its essentials, so that all the bodhisattvas may fulfill all their aspirations and attain unobstructed eloquence, hearing from afar the Dharma taught by the Buddhas in immeasurable lands of the ten directions without forgetting it, up to the unsurpassed, right, and true path of perfect awakening, and swiftly enable the bodhisattvas to attain the four purities. What are the four? First, purity of person; second, purity of dharma; third, purity of wisdom; fourth, purity of the adorned Buddha-land. May it also swiftly enable the bodhisattvas to attain the four joyful aspirations. What are the four? First, joyful harmony of body; second, joyful harmony of speech; third, joyful harmony of mind; fourth, joyful harmony of cessation. Having attained the four joyful aspirations, one enters the four gates of dhāraṇī. What are the four? First, entering the gate of dhāraṇī through practice according to the text; second, entering the gate of dhāraṇī through deep inner patience; third, entering the gate of dhāraṇī through understanding the faculties and virtues of beings; fourth, entering the gate of dhāraṇī through knowing the good retribution of practice." Śāriputra, contemplating these immeasurable grounds of the bodhisattva's purity and wisdom, wished the Buddha to teach them.

Thereupon the Buddha addressed the worthy Śāriputra, praising him: "Excellent! Your intention is most apt, wishing to enable all the bodhisattvas to swiftly accomplish the practice of dhāraṇī. Śāriputra! If a bodhisattva practices the essential phrases of this dhāraṇī, one swiftly enters the immeasurable gates and attains the subtle and secret dhāraṇī. It is called the unconditioned and without direction; as right understanding and interpretation, it is responsive and cessative; inwardly illumined and in accord with the path, it treads upon the supreme trace; like the subtle and wondrous practice, unmoving and quiescent, immeasurable and unsurpassed; subtle, secret, and stainless, pure and spontaneous, with nothing and nothing to attach to; bright and radiant, joyful and delighted, courageous in result and of excellent renown; moving the unmoving with right movement, approaching the path, able through skillful giving to roam without obstruction and enter all the Dharma-gates, strong and powerful, with radiance shining far, understanding the equal, the mind entering all without interrupting the real dhāraṇī. Thus, Śāriputra! The bodhisattva who practices the dhāraṇī is without number, for one who practices immeasurable dharmas does not do so through knowing. All dharmas are severed and awakened to, yet without doing and without seeing, they are conjoined with dharma and free. Not seeing arising and not seeing ceasing, without the wisdom of past, future, or present, nor knowing dharmas already accomplished or not yet accomplished, one follows the Buddha in mindful practice without being mindful of marks; without preference, without lineage, without following, without region, without exhaustion of effort, without knowing and without not-knowing, without purity of person, without teaching of dharma, without self-benefit and without benefit of others, without discipline, without practice and without removal, without mindfulness of body and without mindfulness of person. In all dharmas, to practice without a receiver is called mindfulness of the Buddha, and it is the right refuge for all dharmas. This is the fearless treasury of the meaning of dhāraṇī; the wondrous vow is already fulfilled, the supreme path is already complete. Taming and stilling all samādhis, all the roots of virtue are not attained from another but arise from the Dharma itself. Lineage and excellent marks: this practice is spontaneous, without deviant practice and without severed practice. This is the bodhisattva's ability to study the subtle and secret dhāraṇī of the immeasurable gates, non-retrogressive on the unsurpassed, right, and true path. Why? It is through the practice of sentient beings that one attains the immeasurable dhāraṇī of accomplished practice." Thereupon the Buddha spoke in verse:

> "Dharma is empty; no seeking, no attaining.
> On the path, do not indulge yourself.
> Uphold the sūtra without confusion,
> and you will swiftly attain the dhāraṇī.
> The meaning of this sūtra is wondrous;
> uphold the boundless with constant mindfulness.
> One who studies and hears this wisdom
> may achieve the practice of the path.
> A bodhisattva who attains this dhāraṇī,
> always practicing with vigor,
> will hear the Dharma taught by all the Buddhas
> in the ten directions,
> and can receive and uphold it all,
> grasping the meaning rightly, never forgetting.
> The understanding shines like the sun,
> freely practicing the subtle and wondrous path.
> Up to the unsurpassed Dharma,
> the vast great gate of dhāraṇī,
> through all of this one approaches,
> able to guard and protect the sūtra.
> Even if all people
> together through an entire kalpa found it hard to hear,
> one can resolve all fetters;
> such wisdom is inexhaustible.
> One who sits upon the supreme path
> becomes a child of the Dharma-King,
> always richly protecting and upholding the Dharma,
> with an inner nature that delights in this sūtra.
> What bodhisattvas hold precious,
> the Buddhas of the ten directions also cherish.
> Renown covers the world;
> this practice is immeasurable.
> Eighty koṭis of Buddhas
> one will all see at the end of life.
> Every one extends a hand
> to welcome the practitioner of this dhāraṇī.
> If for a thousand past kalpas
> one has committed unwholesome deeds,
> in one month they can be removed;
> one need only uphold this sūtra.
> The merit a bodhisattva has generated
> and accumulated over koṭis of kalpas,
> one who well recites this dhāraṇī
> can in one day equal it.
> With a mind as strong as a powerful mind,
> contemplate this supreme dhāraṇī.
> Directing one's practice toward all roots of virtue,
> one will surely attain the path of awakening.
> Suppose all beings in the three realms
> were entirely to become māras:
> they could not succeed in doing harm
> to one who practices this sūtra.
> What is taught here is like truth within illusion,
> the most essential of all Dharma.
> All the Buddhas have come forth from this;
> the bodhi-mind arises from this origin.
> Long ago, when I was studying the path,
> I received my prophecy from Dīpaṃkara.
> Buddhas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges:
> I beheld all of them therein.
> If you wish to hear all the Buddhas
> of the ten directions, awakened to all they teach,
> you should devote yourself to this sūtra;
> everything can then be swiftly attained.
> If you wish to adorn the Buddha-land,
> to complete the assembly of disciples,
> with radiant marks and excellent features,
> you should obtain them through this sūtra.
> Able to dispel negligent thoughts,
> devoting seven days exclusively to this,
> eighty koṭis of Buddhas will arrive
> and together confer this dhāraṇī.
> Not thinking with the mind about the mind,
> nor thinking about the absence of mind,
> the mind without thinking:
> only then can one attain the dhāraṇī.
> One should thoroughly understand this sūtra
> and never forget the path.
> Attaining this dhāraṇī is like the ocean;
> one no longer toils for wealth.
> Wishing to bring peace to devas and humans,
> with all aspirations free of difficulty:
> this is the seat of the path.
> One need only practice with a right mind."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! A bodhisattva has four practices by which one swiftly attains this dhāraṇī. What are the four? Having become disgusted with desire; having done no harm to any sentient being; using all possessions for generosity; and with tireless enthusiasm, delighting in the Dharma day and night. There are further four practices by which one swiftly attains this dhāraṇī. What are the four? Being able to dwell in mountain wilds; inwardly practicing deep patience; not craving others' offerings; and practicing immeasurable giving without sparing one's body or life. There are further four practices by which one swiftly attains this dhāraṇī. What are the four? First, entering the meaning of the eight characters. The eight characters are: trace, discernment, contemplation, relinquishment, compassion, taming, cessation, and patience; one should constantly write and uphold these. Second, reciting and teaching always with a tamed mind. Third, inwardly the nature accords with this Dharma-essential. Fourth, encouraging others to practice the great path. A bodhisattva who does thus will swiftly attain the dhāraṇī." Thereupon the Buddha spoke in verse:

> "One should abandon desire and practice austerity,
> swiftly leave the ground of ignorance.
> Do not indulge in doing evil;
> evil causes one to fall into evil paths.
> This one does not approach that,
> not knowing matters of profit and fame.
> Gazing upon all people with eyes of love,
> one will surely be honored as one who has attained beauty.
> All people's contentions
> arise from craving as their root.
> Therefore, ceasing all anxious striving,
> by abandoning this one attains the dhāraṇī.
> One who desires the Dharma, day and night,
> casts aside all else and seeks only the path.
> Attaining the dhāraṇī with the mind directed,
> one therefore always encounters the sūtra.
> Dwelling in seclusion, studying the Buddha's teaching,
> abiding here without knowing that,
> deeply patient, casting aside negligence,
> always as urgent as putting out a fire on one's head.
> The Dharma does not rely on knowing profit,
> nor does one seek anything for the body.
> Seeing loss, one should abandon profit;
> people take profit as their practice.
> How joyful to obtain the Buddha's teaching,
> leaving home and departing from manifold sufferings.
> One of faith accomplishes the study of the path;
> revering the Buddha-Dharma is wisdom.
> The wise do not crave profit;
> how much less one who practices the Dharma.
> The Buddha has accomplished this from afar;
> therefore one should abandon profit and seek.
> One should contemplate the meaning of the eight characters,
> and skillfully write and uphold this sūtra.
> Always reciting to tame oneself,
> one also transforms others.
> Attaining a seat at the great wisdom station,
> seeing all in the ten directions,
> immeasurable Buddhas appear before one,
> encouraging the accomplishment of one's aspiration for the path."

"Furthermore, Śāriputra! A bodhisattva has four virtues for this dhāraṇī. What are the four? First, constantly being mindful of the Buddhas; second, being without deviant practice; third, swiftly removing the hindrances to practice; fourth, attaining entry into the subtle and secret dhāraṇī of the immeasurable gates. Once, Śāriputra, immeasurable and incalculable kalpas ago, there was a Buddha named Ratnāgratejas, the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, of Perfected Conduct, Well-Gone One, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Trainer in the Path of the Dharma, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One, who ferried immeasurable beings across. When he was about to enter final cessation, in the world there was a wheel-turning king named Guāngbǐng, whose crown prince was named Wúniàn Déshǒu, eighteen hundred years old, who heard this dhāraṇī from the Buddha and immediately practiced it. For a further seven thousand years he never slept, for another seven thousand years he never gave rise to bodily desire, for another seven thousand years he never thought of wealth or profit, and for another seven thousand years he concentrated his mind in practice without ever leaning to one side. Thereupon he beheld ninety koṭis of Buddhas, heard all of them teach the Dharma, received and upheld it from them all, and then became a śramaṇa for ninety thousand years. With this subtle and secret dhāraṇī of the immeasurable gates, he expounded and taught to the multitudes. In one single lifetime, he brought eighty koṭis of people to accomplishment, causing them to practice the unsurpassed, right, and true path, guiding and encouraging them to leave the household life and establishing them on the ground of non-retrogression. At that time, in the assembly there was a son of a distinguished family named Yuèxíng ('Moon-Practice'), who heard this essential Dharma and devoted his mind wholeheartedly with joyful aspiration. By this root of virtue, he encountered seventy koṭis of Buddhas, from each of whom he received the supreme dhāraṇī, and attained the immeasurable eloquence of the bodhisattvas. After three kalpas he beheld all the Buddhas. At the end of those three kalpas, Moon-Practice attained Buddhahood and was named Dīpaṃkara, the Tathāgata, Arhat, Samyak-saṃbuddha, of Perfected Conduct, Well-Gone One, Knower of the World, Unsurpassed, Trainer in the Path of the Dharma, Teacher of Devas and Humans, Buddha, World-Honored One. Furthermore, that crown prince Wúniàn Déshǒu, who taught and expounded the Dharma, is the Amitāyus Buddha of the Western direction today. All the bodhisattvas in this Bhadrakalpa who hear this Dharma and aspire with joy will have four hundred thousand kalpas of the practice of birth and death removed. Furthermore, one who studies this and wishes to swiftly become a Buddha: if one obtains this sūtra and has an aspiring, joyful mind intent on practicing the path, one will thereby be established on the ground of non-retrogression and will assuredly attain the unsurpassed, right, and true path. How much more so for one who writes, upholds, recites, and practices it: all the people in the world could not calculate or describe such merit." Thereupon the Buddha spoke in verse:

> "One who constantly cultivates mindfulness of the Buddha:
> no evil can find an opening.
> The hindrances to practice are swiftly removed,
> and one attains the wondrous, immeasurable dhāraṇī.
> Hearing the sūtra with joyful aspiration,
> writing, upholding, and reverently reciting it:
> devas and humans cannot calculate
> such vast and boundless merit.
> Wherever born, one always sees the Buddha,
> with faith and aspiration never wavering.
> Deeply understanding the essential of this profound sūtra,
> one swiftly awakens to this supreme path.
> Attaining the dhāraṇī, the evil paths are cut off.
> One never loses the great treasure of samādhi.
> Beautiful in form, always seeing the Buddha,
> one arrives at the supreme path of awakening.
> The distinguished son, hearing the dhāraṇī,
> immediately recalled his past lives.
> Seeing ninety koṭis of Buddhas,
> he attained the path according to his aspiration.
> If one wishes to swiftly become a Buddha,
> with the power of the path subduing all the māras,
> wishing to fulfill the hundred marks of merit,
> practicing this is not difficult.
> If one filled Ganges-sands of lands
> with heaped-up treasures for giving,
> compared to the merit of writing and upholding this,
> such giving could never compare."

"Thus, Śāriputra! For the bodhisattva who practices this dhāraṇī, there are eight great spirits dwelling in the Snowy Mountains who together guard and protect them. Their names are: Courageous-Decision Spirit, Stalwart-Fruit Spirit, Abundant-Plenty Spirit, Heroic-Valor Spirit, Embodied-Practice Spirit, Pure-and-Clean Spirit, Hard-to-Conquer Spirit, and Great-Peace Spirit. These spirits will surely come. One should always bathe, keep one's garments clean, maintain proper deportment in walking meditation, hold compassionate thoughts for sentient beings, and contemplate this essential Dharma. The spirits, their faces never far away, will surely bring calm and steady recitation. Furthermore, there are eight bodhisattvas now in the Desire-Realm Heaven whom one should constantly call to mind. Their names are: Without-Love Heaven, Joyful-Approval Heaven, Wisdom-Light Heaven, Treasury-of-Gold Heaven, Accumulated-Practice Heaven, Wish-Fulfilled Heaven, Star-King Heaven, and Careful-Practice Heaven. They all diligently practice this dhāraṇī. One should contemplate the dhāraṇī with constant reverence. Having already practiced as one should the subtle and wondrous patience of the Dharma, one must not treat it lightly or carelessly."

When this Dharma was taught, bodhisattvas as numerous as the sands of the Ganges all attained this dhāraṇī and became non-retrogressive. Sixty nayutas of devas and humans who had not yet generated the aspiration for the path now all generated the mind of the unsurpassed, right, and true path.

The worthy Śāriputra addressed the Buddha: "What should this sūtra be called?" The Buddha said: "This essential Dharma is called the Subtle and Secret Dhāraṇī of the Immeasurable Gates. It is also called Accomplishing the Path, Subduing Māras, and Attaining All-Knowledge. One should receive and uphold it." When the Buddha had finished teaching, all rejoiced and accepted it.
