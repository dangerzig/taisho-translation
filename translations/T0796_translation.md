# 佛說見正經
## Sūtra on Seeing Rightly

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0796

Translated from the Chinese. Eastern Jin dynasty, translated by Zhú Tánwúlán (竺曇無蘭).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in Rājagṛha, in the Jeta Grove monastery. At the proper time for the meal, together with five hundred monks, bodhisattvas, and a thousand upāsakas, all carrying offerings, they went outside the city of Rājagṛha. There was a great tree called the Sweet Fragrance tree, with deep roots and a massive trunk, luxuriant branches and leaves, and red flowers and fruits of sweet flavor. Beneath the tree was a broad, level area with stones arranged into seats. The Buddha wished to stop there, and the upāsakas immediately spread out mats and cushions. The Buddha then sat down, and the disciples and bodhisattvas also took their seats.

At that time there was a monk named Jiànzhèng, who had newly entered the Dharma robes. His mind harbored doubt, and he thought to himself: "The Buddha teaches that there are future lives. Yet when people die, none has ever returned to report about it. How can this be known? I should ask the Buddha about this." Before he could speak, the Buddha already knew.

The Buddha first spoke: "Disciples! This tree originally grew from a single seed. Nourished by the four great elements, it attained this immense growth, sheltering all these people. When it was a seed, roots, trunk, leaves, and fruit had not yet come into being and were not yet seen. When the conditions of the four great elements converge, sprouts, leaves, stems, nodes, flowers, and fruit are produced, each growing beyond its original form, until it becomes a tree, extending far and wide. At first it is called a seed. The seed produces a sprout; the sprout produces a stem; the stem produces leaves; the leaves produce flowers; the flowers produce fruit. In continuous transformation, it is not the same as before yet is not separate from what came before: this is called impermanence. It finally becomes a great tree. The tree again produces fruit; the fruit again becomes a tree. Year after year the growth increases, and so on without limit."

The Buddha told the disciples: "If you were to compress and gather the flowers, fruits, stems, nodes, roots, and trunk, could you make them return to being a seed?"

The disciples all said: "That is impossible. They have already been transformed and cannot be made to return. Day by day they move toward decay, while seeds continue to produce anew. Thus it goes without end: each generation produces the next in continuous transformation, and all eventually return to decay. They cannot be made to return to the original seed."

The Buddha told the disciples: "Birth and death are just the same. The spirit-consciousness initiates dharma-activity. Dharma-activity constitutes delusion. Delusion leads to clinging and craving. Delusion is like the tree's seed: the seed is small yet grows into a great tree. A single delusion brings about many conditions, and these many conditions all originate from delusion. From delusion arise formations; from formations arises consciousness; from consciousness arise name-and-form; from name-and-form arise the six sense-entries; from the six sense-entries arise contact; from contact arises feeling; from feeling arises craving; from craving arises grasping; from grasping arises becoming; from becoming arises birth; from birth arise old age and death. Together these twelve links of dependent arising constitute a body. Having a body, one inevitably proceeds to old age and death. The spirit-consciousness transforms and shifts, following one's actions to the next place. One acquires different parents, receives a different bodily form, has different sense faculties, different habits, different joys and sorrows, different customs. All is entirely different from before, and one cannot return. No longer recognizing the past, one regards what is newly perceived as real, as permanent, clings to what one depends upon and calls it truth, and asserts that there are no past or future lives. The spirit-consciousness migrates, and existence follows from one's actions. When the spirit-consciousness has migrated, one has different parents, receives a new body, has different sense faculties, different habits, different joys and sorrows, different customs, and no longer recognizes the past. Nor can one return to the former body, former habits, or former circumstances. It is like the tree that cannot return to being a seed."

At this, the monk Jiànzhèng, hearing the Buddha's words, rose from his seat, knelt with folded hands, and said to the Buddha: "My doubts have not yet been removed, and the essential point is not yet clear. I now wish to ask a question born of ignorance. I pray the Buddha will have compassion on us and resolve it. From the time of my birth until now, I have seen not a few people die. Whether father and son, brothers, husband and wife, relatives near and distant, or friends who loved one another, or enemies who hated each other, after death their spirit-consciousness has never returned to show its face and report on good and evil. Why is this? What obstructs and prevents the spirit-consciousness from returning to report to people? Please explain this in detail, so that our fetters may be removed and we may quickly see the truth."

The Buddha said to the monk: "The spirit-consciousness has no form. When it migrates, it follows one's actions into existence. If the body has created merit, the merit-consciousness is reborn accordingly, and it too cannot return to report to people. Why? It is like a smelter who melts ore into iron. Once it has become iron, it is cast into a vessel. Once it has become a vessel, can it be made to return to being ore?"

Jiànzhèng said: "Truly it cannot. Ore has already become iron and can never again return to being ore."

The Buddha said: "The migration of consciousness, dwelling in the intermediate state, is like ore that has already been smelted into iron. From the intermediate state, the consciousness passes into another body, like iron that has been cast into a vessel. The form dissolves and the body changes; one cannot return to the former consciousness. Why? The good and evil of one's actions, consciousness goes to receive them. Transforming and changing, it is like ore becoming iron. Cultivating the five wholesome deeds and receiving a human body, one then has different parents. Having parents, one has six bonds of confinement: first, one abides in the intermediate state and cannot return; second, one enters the womb of the body one is to receive; third, the pain of initial birth causes one to forget the former consciousness; fourth, upon falling to the ground, the former consciousness perishes and new perceptions arise; fifth, once born, attachment to food arises, and craving causes the former consciousness to be severed; sixth, from the day of birth, growing day by day and habituated to new perceptions, the consciousness is extinguished and there is no longer any memory of past lives.

"Disciples! It is like a merchant who travels to the four quarters of the world, experiencing suffering and happiness everywhere. If his mind thinks of what one particular region possesses, once this thought arises, the thoughts of the other three regions are extinguished. Birth and death are just the same. From the actions performed in this life, one goes to the next life to receive their consequences. Having received them, new thoughts and perceptions arise, and the former consciousness is extinguished. It is like the merchant who thinks of one region and the thoughts of the three other regions are extinguished. Due to these six bonds, one is obstructed and confined, and one no longer returns to the former consciousness. It is like the seed that has become a tree, the ore that has become iron: the original is transformed and given a new name, and one no longer returns to show one's face and report."

The Buddha said: "It is further like a potter who kneads clay into a vessel and fires it, transforming it into tile. Can the tile be made to return to being clay?"

The disciples all said: "Truly it cannot. Clay has already been fired and refined, changed in form and become tile. It cannot be made to return to being clay."

The Buddha said: "Disciples! The migration of the spirit-consciousness, following one's actions to receive a body, is like clay becoming tile. Without the practice of the Way, one no longer recognizes the past and cannot return to report. Monks! It is further like a great tree, tens of arm-spans in girth. A skilled artisan shapes, carves, sculpts, and engraves it into a hundred marvelous works. If someone wished to gather all the chips and shavings and carvings and make them return to being a tree, could it be done?"

The disciples said: "Truly it cannot. The tree has already been cut and broken, carved piece by piece until nothing remains. Branches and leaves have dried and decayed. They can no longer be assembled to make a tree."

The Buddha told the disciples: "The spirit-consciousness in this world performs good and evil actions. At death, the consciousness migrates, following its actions to receive a body. What it sees and habituates itself to is not the former body. It cannot return, and no longer recognizes the past or shows its face to report. It is like the tree already cut that cannot be reassembled to grow again."

The Buddha said: "It is further like a craftsman who burns sand to make a red pigment, which further transforms into a white form that melts like water. Disciples, could you make the red pigment return to being sand?"

The disciples said: "Truly it cannot. Sand, once transformed, cannot be restored."

The Buddha said: "Birth and death are just the same. If a person has not yet developed the aspiration for the Way and does not possess the pure eye, when the body dies and the consciousness departs, it follows one's actions in transformation. Receiving another body, passing through different lives, entering a different womb, with different habits and perceptions, one no longer recognizes the past. It is like sand becoming pigment: it cannot be restored."

The Buddha said: "Disciples! It is further like water placed in a round vessel: its form follows the roundness. Transferred to a square container, its form becomes square. Large, small, curved, or straight, it follows whatever shape it falls into. Disciples! Birth and death are just the same. The spirit-consciousness fundamentally has no fixed form. Following good and evil actions, it goes to receive a body. White, black, tall, short, suffering, happiness, good, evil: it receives and transforms according to actions, like water conforming to its vessel. Perhaps from the human realm, having committed what is contrary to the Dharma, at death one falls into the animal realm, receiving a wretched body, no longer recognizing the past or able to show one's face to report. Disciples! It is like a grub that lives in the earth, voiceless and wingless. When the season comes, it transforms into a cicada, flying to a tree and singing without cease."

The Buddha asked the disciples: "Could you return the cicada to the earth and make it a grub again?"

The disciples said: "Truly it cannot. The grub has already transformed. Leaving the dark for the light, its bodily form has changed. It is destined to die, or be eaten by various birds. It cannot return to being a grub."

The Buddha said: "Disciples! Birth and death are just the same. When life ends and the body dies, the spirit-consciousness migrates to receive a new body. Covered by the five aggregates, with different perceptions and habits, there too it must undergo old age and death. It cannot return, no longer recognizing the past or showing its face to report. It is like the cicada in the tree that cannot return to being a grub."

The Buddha told the disciples: "It is further like a piece of raw meat that, left uneaten past its time, becomes putrid, breeding maggots. Could you make it return to being fresh meat?"

The disciples said: "Truly it cannot. The meat has already rotted and decayed; it cannot be restored to freshness."

The Buddha said: "Birth and death are just the same. If a person in the world thinks evil, speaks evil, and acts evil, at death the spirit-consciousness migrates to receive a hell-body, an animal-body, or the body of a fish or insect. In each place, one's perceptions are different and not the same as before. Enmeshed in the net of offenses, one no longer recognizes the past and cannot return to show one's face and report. It is like putrid meat that cannot be restored to freshness."

The Buddha told the disciples: "It is further like a dark, moonless, overcast night. If you placed five-colored objects in the darkness and had a thousand or ten thousand people look at them in the night, could any one of them distinguish blue, yellow, red, and white?"

The disciples all said: "Even if there were billions upon billions of people, countless in number, looking in the dark, none could see them, much less distinguish the five colors."

The Buddha said: "If someone held a torch and let people look, could they see?"

The disciples said: "With people relying on the torchlight to look, they could all distinguish the five colors."

The Buddha said: "If a foolish person turned their back to the torch and walked into the deep darkness, advancing ever farther, and wished to see the five colors, could they see them?"

The disciples said: "A foolish person with their back to the light and facing the dark, the farther they advance the darker it becomes. They would never see the colors."

The Buddha told the disciples: "People in birth and death are just the same. All people, all beings that crawl, fly, or creep, having received bodily form, are blinded by the darkness of delusion. Without the practice of the Way, without studying the affairs of body and mind, without having attained the wisdom eye, yet wishing to know the destinations of birth and death, the comings and goings of the spirit-consciousness, and to have the dead return to show their faces and report: this is like wishing to see the five colors on a dark, moonless night. One can never see them. If one cultivates the precepts and the thirty-seven factors of awakening, guards and restrains the mind, and pursues the practice of purity, it is like following someone carrying a torch and distinguishing the five colors. One who follows the Buddha's Dharma teaching can then clearly discern birth and death, fully seeing the five destinies and where the spirit-consciousness goes to receive good or evil, just as torchlight illuminates colors and all is clearly seen. If a person from the start has not studied the affairs of body and mind, has turned their back on the sūtras and precepts, and follows the three worldly currents, indulging at will, severing the true Dharma, not believing, not delighting in it, not willing to practice it: this is like turning one's back to the torch and entering the darkness. Doubt and confusion deepen daily, and one will never have a moment of seeing, knowing, or understanding."

The Buddha told the disciples: "Do not follow your deluded, ignorant, and fettered minds and refuse to believe in the pure and true Way, thereby casting yourselves into hell and receiving suffering in the body. I have therefore used these analogies to make it clear. You should always strive to practice the sūtras and precepts, keeping them in your hearts."

The Buddha told the disciples: "People born into this world, having received bodily form, see with the physical eye only the affairs of the present life. Parents and relatives are seen with perfect clarity. Yet one cannot see or know the place from which one came in a previous life. As one proceeds through old age and death, going to be reborn in a future life and receiving a different body, one also cannot recognize or know the affairs of this present life. Why? With each birth and death, the spirit-consciousness transforms and shifts. The twelve links of dependent arising have delusion as their master. In the darkness of confusion, one progressively loses recognition of the past.

"Disciples! It is like boiling and bleaching white silk, then dyeing it into different colors: blue, yellow, red, or black. The original is transformed and changed; it cannot be turned back again. The transformations of birth and death are like silk receiving dye. Consciousness has no permanent form; it is colored by the actions it follows. Without the pure eye, one does not recognize the past. The mind constitutes dharma-activity: whatever is conceived comes into being. In a single lifetime, a person has ten thousand thoughts. The retribution of good and evil is received: the new is received and the old is extinguished. This is the law of birth and death; it is the nature of the darkness of delusion. Those who wish to see and know the comings and goings of birth and death should broadly study and practice the affairs of body and mind, enter deeply into purity, contemplate beginning and end, and only then will awakening come, like waking from sleep."

The Buddha told the disciples: "The spirit-consciousness, following the dharma of the darkness of delusion, performs good and evil actions during life. At death it is transformed and goes to receive them, following good and evil actions into various forms and signs. It is like fire that appears when fuel is present, and when the fuel is exhausted, it is extinguished. If the mind-consciousness does not perform good or evil actions, it too is extinguished, with nothing to be seen. Those who have not attained the Way are submerged in birth and death, progressively losing recognition of the past. It is like a grimy mirror, clouded with dirt and grime: hold it to the face and nothing can be seen. The mind-consciousness, clouded and obscured, migrating through birth and death, oppressed by dread and filled with darkness, dragged about by fortune and misfortune, no longer recognizes the past: it is like the grimy mirror.

"It is further like deep, turbid water: though fish and insects are within it, nothing can be seen. Birth and death are disordered and confused; worry and anxiety obscure and block. Being reborn, one forgets the past. This too is like turbid water. It is like walking on a dark night with closed eyes: nothing whatsoever can be seen. Birth and death are dark and obscure. One drifts with fortune and misfortune, now rejoicing, now in anguish, tethered and constrained by what one receives, no longer recognizing the past. It is like walking in the dark with closed eyes."

The Buddha told the disciples: "I, now being a Buddha, possess the pure wisdom eye. All of birth and death, all comings and goings through the three realms: the Buddha fully knows and sees. It is like crystal, lapis lazuli, or a precious pearl threaded with colored silk: the blue and yellow are all visible. The Buddha sees birth and death like observing a threaded pearl. It is like pure water, clear to the very bottom, in which fish and insects are all plainly visible. The Buddha sees birth and death like fish in clear water.

"It is like a great bridge over which all travelers come and go without ceasing. The Buddha sees birth and death, the comings and goings through the five destinies, like watching people on a bridge. It is like standing on a high mountain with a distant view of everything. The Buddha's mind is lofty and far-reaching, fully comprehending birth and death without any exception."

The Buddha told the disciples: "If you follow my teaching, you can fully know the events of birth and death across billions of kalpas. You should practice the thirty-seven essential factors: the four foundations of mindfulness, the four right exertions, the four bases of supernatural power, the five faculties, the five powers, the seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold noble path, in order to remove the mind's impurities, extinguish the three poisons, dissolve doubt and fetters, and attain purity. Gaining the Buddha's wisdom-mind, you will then know the affairs of past and future, seeing them as in a clear mirror, where all is visible."

The Buddha told the disciples: "The good and evil that worldly people do receives its corresponding retribution in the life after death. But because people have not yet attained the three pure eyes, they do not see and do not know, no longer recognizing the past. Confined by the six bonds, acting through the physical eye, they cannot see the basis of corresponding retribution and conclude that it does not exist. Those who have not attained the Way all perform turbid and defiled actions, sunk in ignorance. Through the transformations of birth and death, receiving different bodily forms, the physical eye is deluded and confused, separated from the old and bound to the new. The four kinds of suffering disorder and confuse, and one can never know that consciousness follows actions in corresponding retribution.

"Now among present-day people, some receive blessings and some receive misfortune, some feel affection and some feel aversion: these are the evidence of retribution for past actions. Because one does not possess the three pure eyes, one does not see and does not know, and remains bound in doubt. All people have come into this world originally accompanied by delusion, without the aspiration for the Way or the practice of purity. Yet they wish to know the affairs of past lives and the evidence of the returning retribution of consciousness. It is like someone without hands wishing to write, or someone without eyes wishing to see: it can never be done. Therefore the Buddha has appeared in the world, unfolding the Way of the sūtras, to free the minds of people. Those who wish to see and know the spirit-consciousness's comings and goings and what is received in birth and death should follow the Buddha's teaching, practice the thirty-seven factors of enlightenment and the boundless perfection of wisdom, examine the mind, discipline the mind, regulate the mind, and correct the mind, and enter the wondrous depths of dhyāna and samādhi. Only then can one fully know where the spirit-consciousness goes, and the events of its comings and goings.

"You, my disciples! You should diligently study and know the affairs of body and mind, fully understand all counterparts, and when they arise, extinguish them, so as not to be led into error. Be steadfast in the true Dharma. Do not cease in this, and your doubts will be resolved."

The Buddha told the disciples: "The spirit-consciousness has a name but no form. Following good and evil actions, it depends on the four great elements as its body. At the time of initial birth, the body is small, the faculties not yet complete, and consciousness is limited, with knowledge not yet developed. As it grows, the six sense faculties become complete, and consciousness also grows with the body. Craving, desire, and various habits daily increase and reach fullness. Upon reaching old age, the four great elements deteriorate, and consciousness likewise dims, the six sense faculties diminishing. Within a single lifetime, all is in constant transformation, never the same as before. What one learned and perceived in youth is forgotten in old age. How much more so in a different life, covered by the darkness of the womb? Without having attained the aspiration for the Way, performing the deluded and defiled actions of ignorance, yet wishing to see the comings and goings of the mind-consciousness and to receive a face-to-face report: this is impossible.

"If a person has not practiced the Way yet wishes to see and know the affairs of past lives, it is like trying to thread a needle in the dark, or seeking fire in water: one will never succeed. You, my disciples! You should diligently practice the sūtras and precepts, deeply contemplate birth and death: from where does it originally come? To where does it ultimately go? What is the cause of its comings and goings? What conditions does it depend upon? Contemplate truly and thoroughly the Dharma of emptiness and nothingness. When purity is attained and fetters are removed, your doubts will resolve of themselves."

When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra, Jiànzhèng and the five hundred others, together with all the upāsakas, all attained the fruit of stream-entry. All the bodhisattvas attained the samādhi of non-retrogression. Each rose and circumambulated the Buddha three times, prostrated with their heads touching the ground, completed their obeisances, and all accompanied the Buddha back to the monastery.
