# 廣大蓮華莊嚴曼拏羅滅一切罪陀羅尼經
## Dhāraṇī Sūtra of the Vast Lotus-Adorned Maṇḍala for Eliminating All Sins

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 1116

Translated from the Chinese. Song dynasty, translated by Dānapāla (施護).

---

Thus have I heard:

At one time the Buddha was in the country of Vārāṇasī, in the Deer Park, beneath a campaka aśoka tree, together with a great assembly of twelve thousand and fifty bhikṣus, as well as Maitreya Bodhisattva and other bodhisattva mahāsattvas, and devas, nāgas, yakṣas, rākṣasas, asuras, gandharvas, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, mahoragas, humans and non-humans, who reverently surrounded him as he taught the Dharma. Good at the beginning, good in the middle, and good at the end, its meaning was profound and far-reaching, the brahma-conduct pure, and everything fully complete.

At that time there was a great king in the country of Vārāṇasī named Brahmāyus, whose body and mind were purely good and compassionate toward beings. He nurtured all sentient beings on earth as if they were his own children. The king left the city to visit a Buddhist monastery. Arriving at the gate of the vihāra, he paused for a moment and looked around. The gatekeeper announced to the holy assembly: "The great king Brahmāyus is now outside the gate and wishes to enter the monastery." The holy assembly at once instructed the saṃgha steward to arrange flower garlands. When the steward heard this, he opened the hall doors and searched for flower garlands but found none. He saw flower garlands upon the Buddha's head, took them, and proceeded with the senior monks and the rest of the holy assembly to welcome the king, and presented the flower garlands to him. The king accepted them and placed them upon his head. After a short while, he suddenly developed a headache. The king thought to himself: "Why is this so? It must be that I have fallen ill from going out in the summer heat." The king immediately took his leave of the holy assembly, returned to the palace, and told his attendants: "I have a headache and intend to bathe. Quickly prepare fragrant water." The king then removed all his ornamental garments and bathed. A palace attendant who was skilled at bathing washed the king's body. Although they bathed for a long time, the headache did not abate. The king then issued a decree summoning the royal physicians. When the physicians arrived, the king told them: "Because of the summer heat, I went out of the city for a stroll, and on the road I developed a headache. I then bathed, but the illness has not been cured. What do you think?" The physicians replied: "Your Majesty's ailment is due to internal heat. We should apply gośīrṣa sandalwood to your body." The king followed their advice and applied the sandalwood, but still did not improve. Through the day and night he suffered greatly. All the physicians then came together to the king's side, looked at one another, and said: "The cause of this illness is unfathomable, and we are deeply worried. Since the king is thus afflicted, what means can we devise to cure his illness?" While they were deliberating, the king had a younger sister named Supriyā, who had aroused the mind of bodhi and was filled with faith, gravity, and compassion. Seeing the king's suffering, she became fearful. Touching his head with her hand, she said to the king: "Why are you so afraid, like a timid person?" The king answered: "Supriyā! I do not know how I might be free from this pain and fear." His sister said: "If that is so, Your Majesty, please go to the Buddha. The Buddha possesses great compassion and will certainly provide relief." The king said: "Your suggestion is excellent! How could I have forgotten! I must go at once." He then commanded his ministers to quickly arrange chariots and horses. The charioteer harnessed five hundred carriages, and the king, with his retinue and ministers, departed from the city and traveled to the campaka aśoka tree, to the place of the Buddha. Along the way, there was a woman who gathered firewood for a living. With a bundle of firewood on her shoulder, she was giving birth by the roadside. When the king's sister saw this, she could not bear to look. She covered her face with her hand and collapsed on the ground in a faint. The king, seeing this, asked his sister: "What is your distress? Tell me." The queen, named Surasundarī, thought for a moment and told the king: "Supriyā fainted and collapsed because she saw the firewood-gathering woman by the roadside giving birth; the suffering was so severe. Your sister's heart is compassionate, and she could not bear to watch. That is why this happened." When the king heard this, he told the queen: "The retribution of sinful karma cannot be escaped." The king then ordered his palace attendants to give the poor woman provisions and send her home quickly. His sister, who had fainted, was revived by fanning with a flywhisk and said: "Namo buddhāya. Great King! The suffering of that poor woman was too great; I could not bear to see it." They continued onward and glimpsed the park and grove in the distance. As they drew near the vihāra, the king descended from his chariot and, taking Supriyā by the hand, entered the park. He saw the World-Honored One like a mountain of wondrous gold, radiating great light like a hundred thousand suns. Upon seeing him, the king released his sister's hand, bared his right shoulder, removed his crown, went before the Buddha with palms joined in reverence, circumambulated the World-Honored One, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and lay prostrate for a long time. The World-Honored One then extended his golden arm and touched the top of the king's head, saying: "Rise, rise." When the king heard the Buddha's command, he immediately rose, and in an instant his headache was cured. His body and mind were at ease and the king was greatly delighted. Supriyā and the retinue sat together in one place. The king saw that his sister's expression was one of sorrow and asked: "Why are you troubled?" He then had her address the Buddha. When his sister heard this, she rose from her seat, bared her right shoulder, placed her right knee on the ground, joined her palms in reverence, gazed upon the honored countenance, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! Last night the great king suffered greatly from a headache, and all the king's family and ministers were filled with worry and distress. Because of this, we all came to the Buddha's presence. Along the road, we saw a firewood-gathering woman in tattered garments with disheveled hair, crying out and wailing as she gave birth. Seeing this, I was greatly frightened and deeply abhorred the female body. World-Honored One! How can we be freed from the female body? Furthermore, the great king's headache could not be cured by famous physicians. Yet now, by the World-Honored One's touching of his head, in an instant he was at peace and happy. What is the cause? We pray that the Buddha will compassionately explain."

At that time, when the World-Honored One heard these words, from his face he emitted great light of various colors, illuminating immeasurable and boundless worlds. This light then re-entered his mouth. At that time, the Venerable Ānanda, seeing the Buddha's light universally illuminating the worlds, was moved by the Buddha's majestic virtue and felt wonder in his heart. He rose from his seat, joined his palms in reverence, bowed at the Buddha's feet, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! What is the cause today for emitting great light that universally illuminates the buddha-fields? May the World-Honored One briefly explain." The Buddha addressed Ānanda: "In a past life, in this city there was a great king named Dhṛtaprabhā. That king's queen was named Aśokā, and his beloved second consort was named Anupamā. On the border of that kingdom was a small state that raised an army and invaded the great kingdom. King Dhṛtaprabhā then marshaled his four divisions of elephants, horses, chariots, and infantry, along with all his ministers, and with music and attendants departed the city to repel the invaders. While the king was on the road and the army paused briefly, the consort Anupamā realized she was with child and about to give birth. She told her palace attendants, who then had an official inform King Dhṛtaprabhā. When the official had reported this, the king issued an order that the consort should return to the palace. After the consort returned, the birth proved difficult. The minister Cārikā was sent to report the matter urgently to the king. When the king heard this, he mounted his chariot and returned to the palace. He went to the consort's quarters and inquired after her well-being. Seeing the consort's suffering in childbirth, the king burned incense and poured water before the Three Treasures, offering prayers and making vows. The remaining water he gave to the consort to drink. At that time, Queen Aśokā harbored jealousy in her heart. She took the king's hand and said: 'The consort Anupamā is by nature mad and shameless, naked with hair hanging down, no different from a ghost or spirit.' When the king heard this, he was ashamed and turned away. After a short while, Anupamā gave birth to a prince, whose body was of true golden color, with beautiful features, dignified appearance, and fully endowed with merit. She presented the prince to the great king, and the king was greatly delighted. After some time, the palace women gathered together, and one of them repeated Queen Aśokā's jealous words. The consort Anupamā happened to overhear these words and questioned the palace woman, demanding the full account. The palace woman, not daring to conceal anything, reported everything. When Anupamā heard this, her heart was cut as if by a knife, and she cried out: 'How am I mad? How am I shameless?' She beat her breast in anguish, and fainted and collapsed. The palace women sprinkled water on her face and fanned her, but she did not revive for a long time, and because of this she died. The palace women then cried out loudly. The king suddenly heard the cries and, finding it alarmingly strange, ordered an official to inquire as to the reason. The official, following the command, asked the palace gatekeeper. The gatekeeper in turn asked the palace women: 'Why are you weeping? The king urgently wants to know.' The palace women, choking with tears, said: 'The consort Anupamā has suddenly passed away; that is why we are weeping. Please report to the king at once.' When the ministers heard this, they were filled with grief and sorrow on their faces and rushed to the king. The king, seeing them from afar and knowing some calamity had occurred, asked the messenger: 'Is it the prince who is ill? Is it the consort who is ill?' The messenger said: 'The consort Anupamā has suddenly died.' When the king heard this, he was overcome with grief, and like a tree with its roots cut off, he fainted and collapsed. His ministers sprinkled water on his face, and after a long time he revived. The ministers said: 'We pray that the king may rest his mind and not grieve. The palace has hundreds and thousands of beautiful consorts to bring joy and lasting happiness to the king.' When the king heard this consolation, he was restored to composure." The Buddha told Ānanda: "The jealous Queen Aśokā of that time is the poor firewood-gathering woman who was giving birth by the roadside today. The consort Anupamā, whose nature was compassionate, is today Supriyā. Ānanda! What do you think? If a person acts out of excessive greed and jealousy, in future lives they will receive greatly unpleasant and evil karmic retribution."

The Buddha told Ānanda: "As for King Brahmāyus's headache, when the king entered the grove, the senior monk ordered the steward to take flower garlands to welcome the king. The steward, named Śuddhasenya, was a young novice whose body and mind were scattered and whose nature was coarse and impetuous. Entering the hall and finding no other garlands available, he simply took the garland from atop the Buddha's head and used it to offer to the king. After the king accepted it and placed it on his head, in an instant he developed a headache, causing all his ministers and family to be filled with worry and distress." When the king heard the Buddha's explanation of cause and effect without the slightest error, he affirmed: "It is so, it is so."

At that time there was a bodhisattva named Mahāmati in the great assembly, who rose from his seat, bared his right shoulder, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and addressed the Buddha: "World-Honored One! If a sentient being takes the permanent property of the saṃgha for personal use, what retribution will they receive?" The Buddha said: "Such a person, upon death, will fall into the Avīci hell." Mahāmati Bodhisattva further addressed the Buddha: "How can such a person be rescued? How can they be comforted? What is the remedy?" The World-Honored One then extended his right hand and addressed Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva: "Speak! Speak! You have the mantra ritual of the great compassionate mind, able to rescue and deliver all sentient beings. If such a being has entered the Avīci hell, this mantra can well rescue, comfort, and be their support." When Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva heard these words, he rose from his seat, joined his palms, and said: "World-Honored One! Listen well. I have the great compassionate mind dhāraṇī, which can bring vast benefit to all sentient beings." The World-Honored One praised him: "Excellent, excellent! I now listen attentively and seal it with the great vajra seal."

Then Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva Mahāsattva further addressed the World-Honored One: "This great vidyā dhāraṇī can definitely eliminate all sins, all evil destinies, and all suffering, and further enable sentient beings to abide securely on the path of bodhi. This mantra is subtle, wondrous, and most supreme, like a great jeweled tree, able to fulfill all wishes." The World-Honored One further said: "Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva! You should speak the heart of the inconceivable vast lotus-adorned maṇḍala dhāraṇī, and let this invincible and powerful mantra fulfill all the wishes of sentient beings." Then Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva, having been requested by the Buddha World-Honored One a second and third time, bowed at his feet, stood before the Buddha, and spoke this great compassionate mind dhāraṇī:

*Namo ratna-trayāya. Nama ārya-avalokiteśvarāya bodhisattvāya mahāsattvāya mahā-kāruṇikāya. Tadyathā: padme padme padma-pratiṣṭhite padma-udbhava mahā-maṇḍala-bhiyu-hi sara-sara kara-kara kiri-kiri kuru-kuru mahā-bhaya-samāti duna-duna viduna-viduna hrīḥ-yuṃ mahā-vinīye śudhāya śudhāya sarva-janma-karma-pāraṃ-pāraṇi me buddhāya buddhāya mahā-jñāna-pradīpe svāhā.*

When Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva had spoken this great compassionate great vidyā dhāraṇī, the entire great earth quaked in six ways. The palaces of all the devas, the palaces of the nāgas, and the palaces of all the yakṣas, gandharvas, asuras, garuḍas, kiṃnaras, and mahoragas all shook greatly. All the māra-kings were greatly terrified and their hearts were filled with distress. All evil nāgas and all ghost-spirits fainted, collapsed, and vanished from sight. All sentient beings in the hells, receiving the light of the mantra which shattered the darkness of their sinful suffering, were immediately liberated and reborn in heaven. With heavenly utpala flowers, kumuda flowers, white lotus flowers, mandārava flowers, and mahā-mandārava flowers, they made offerings before the Buddha. Then the World-Honored One, emitting a vast and subtle Brahmā-voice like the sound of a kalaviṅka, praised Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva: "The dhāraṇī you have spoken is profound and inconceivable. For the sake of all sentient beings, further explain the ritual procedures of the vastly beneficial maṇḍala for painting images and recitation." Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva then received the command and spoke the ritual procedure: "One must obtain pure maidens to spin the silk thread, and have a person who keeps the precepts and is ritually pure weave a bolt of silk, four cubits, three cubits, two cubits, or down to one cubit in length. Soak it in fragrant water, stretch it upon an upper rod, and have a person of pure precepts paint the banner-image upon it. In the center of the banner, first paint Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva seated upon a lotus seat, wearing celestial garments with scarves across the chest, with a crown upon the head, and within the crown Amitābha Buddha, adorned with all ornaments. The left hand holds a lotus; the right hand forms the wish-granting mudrā. On the left of Avalokiteśvara, paint Śrī Bodhisattva holding a white flywhisk. On the right, paint Padmaśrī Bodhisattva holding a lotus. Each of these two bodhisattvas sits upon a lotus seat. At the top of the banner, paint two heavenly beings facing each other, holding flower garlands. At the bottom of the banner, paint the Earth Deva holding a lotus canopy. Paint the two great nāga kings Nanda and Upananda, holding up Avalokiteśvara's lotus seat with their hands. To the right beneath the seat, paint the practitioner kneeling on the right knee and holding a lotus. In the empty spaces around the four edges of the banner, paint lotuses throughout, in various colors. At the bottom of the banner, paint the great ocean with aquatic creatures, and utpala flowers, kumuda flowers, and white lotus flowers, each in full bloom. Then, on a bolt of silk, paint a hundred-petaled lotus in four colors and place it before the banner. Upon that lotus, present the five offerings. Then, with rice flour, white flour, or fragrant clay, make a wheel the size of one's thumb. Each day, offer eight hundred lotuses dipped in white sandalwood water. Make a maṇḍala with white sandalwood water, and using the lotus-seal while reciting the lotus mantra, offer flowers up to one lakṣa. Do this on the eighth or fifteenth day of the waxing moon. Observing pure fasting and precepts, make eight hundred gold or silver lotuses as eight kinds of vast adornments, or make lotus canopies and lotus banners as offerings. Within the banner, paint small images of the holy assembly. Make a great offering with five kinds of food and drink. Offer one excellent lotus to the holy assembly, and then perform the ceremony of installing the image with praise and celebration. All bhikṣus, bhikṣuṇīs, upāsakas, and upāsikās who have faith should make offerings of food. By cultivating in this way, all past sins of misusing the permanent property of other buddha images, stūpas, and monasteries will be entirely eliminated. Karmic obstructions will be purified, and one will not fall into evil destinies but will be reborn in a buddha-field. At the time of death, while seated, one will see the holy Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva within the pavilion of the vast lotus-adorned maṇḍala, who will speak words of comfort: 'Do not fear, do not fear. You shall be reborn in a superior place and will not receive a female body.'"

When Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva had spoken these words, in the great assembly there was a bodhisattva named Siṃhamati, who rose from his seat, joined his palms toward the Buddha, and addressed the World-Honored One: "If King Brahmāyus, not knowing it was a flower garland from the Buddha's head and carelessly wearing it, developed a headache in just an instant and suffered greatly, then what retribution will a person receive who knowingly takes the permanent property of a buddha image, stūpa, or monastery for personal use?" The Buddha said: "Siṃhamati Bodhisattva! Excellent, excellent! You do well to ask this. King Brahmāyus, whose mind was pure and who had deep faith in the Three Treasures, received a present-life retribution of headache from mistakenly wearing the garland. This is like a spot of ink on a clean white garment: when people see it, the ink appears very slight. But if sentient beings in future ages misuse the permanent property of the Three Treasures, it is like a dark garment thrown into a vat of ink. The grave sins thus incurred cannot be remedied by any other dharma.

"Furthermore, Siṃhamati Bodhisattva! If a faithful patron donates wealth to build monasteries, stūpas, buddha images, or makes offerings to the Three Treasures, and then a king or minister seizes the saṃgha's property for personal use, causing the bhikṣus to suffer poverty, diminishing their authority, weakening their vigor, and cutting off their recitation practice with such failings, those kings and ministers will receive great sinful suffering no different from what was described before. If there is a bhikṣu who, although possessing faith, understanding, eloquence, and wisdom, delights in associating with kings and high ministers, extensively seeking wealth, arrogant and haughty, and breaking the precepts, I shall now use an analogy to explain this matter. Siṃhamati Bodhisattva! It is like a person who, being hungry, eats food that contains poison, whether one ounce, one fraction, or even as little as a mustard seed. Having eaten it, that person will certainly die. A renunciant is also like this: relying on the power of kings and deceitfully seeking wealth, the offerings they receive are not rightful sustenance. Such a person will certainly receive evil retribution." Siṃhamati Bodhisattva then addressed the World-Honored One: "If a renunciant wearing the Dharma-robe wrongly seeks wealth, is arrogant and haughty, and kings and ministers revere and make offerings to them, then there should be no merit." The Buddha said: "Siṃhamati Bodhisattva! Do not speak thus. It is like a person who has fainted and collapsed: relying on others to support and lift them, they can stand again. It is also like a great elephant stuck in mud: people cannot raise the elephant's body, but another elephant can support and extricate it. Again, it is like a person who has been anointed as king but later loses the throne: ordinary people cannot protect and restore them, but only a powerful minister of great authority and valor can restore the kingship. Siṃhamati Bodhisattva! My teaching is also like this. Whether one follows the Dharma or does not, all are children of the Buddha and all can bring benefit. If one gives rise to contempt and disdain, where will merit be found?" When the entire great assembly heard these words, they spoke with one voice: "World-Honored One! We have now been delivered by the Buddha and are greatly joyful. May all sentient beings have their wishes fully fulfilled."

The Buddha said: "If any person copies this dhāraṇī of the true Dharma, whether writing it themselves or having another write it, and receives, upholds, reads, recites, reverently worships, and makes offerings, the merit obtained will be supreme and most excellent. If a person merely hears the name of this second turning of the Dharma-wheel, the true Dharma of the great and powerful dhāraṇī transmitted by Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva Mahāsattva, that person has already erected the Dharma-banner, sounded the Dharma-conch, and deeply planted roots of merit." Then King Brahmāyus and the entire great assembly gave rise to pure faith, rose from their seats, joined their palms toward the Buddha, and made this vow: "We vow that henceforth we shall never again take any of the permanent property of the Buddha's monasteries, stūpas, or holy assembly, not even a single flower or a single fruit. Furthermore, at the four great city gates, we shall build four great monasteries, all adorned with the seven treasures. May the Buddha bear witness." The Buddha said: "It is so, it is so. You should act victoriously, abandon jealousy, and attain true quiescence." When King Brahmāyus had made this vow, he circumambulated the Buddha three times, and with his retinue returned to his own land. He then taught this true Dharma to hundreds of thousands of beings, widely practiced generosity, and built a great Buddhist monastery. The king's sister, the upāsikā Supriyā, in the inner palace taught the wondrous Dharma to five hundred thousand palace women and made sincere vows. Having made these vows, all the palace women transformed their female bodies and became men. All who saw this were greatly astonished. Having faith in the dhāraṇī Dharma, they received and practiced it, and proclaimed aloud: "Only the Buddha, only the Dharma, only the Saṃgha are the supreme field of merit and the true refuge. If anyone makes offerings and upholds this dhāraṇī, that person plants the roots of merit of the Buddha and obtains the most supreme merit."

When the Buddha had spoken this sūtra, the bodhisattva mahāsattvas and the devas, humans, asuras, gandharvas, humans and non-humans, all greatly rejoiced, faithfully received it, and departed.
