# Commentary on the Sūtra of Amitābha Spoken by the Buddha

## *Fó Shuō Āmítuó Jīng Shū* (佛說阿彌陀經疏)

Taishō 1759

Composed by Wónhyo (*Yuánxiǎo*, 元曉) of Silla.

*Note: This is a commentary on the shorter Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra (the Amitābha Sūtra, T366, translated by Kumārajīva) by the great Korean Buddhist scholar-monk Wónhyo (617-686 CE) of Silla. Wónhyo was one of the most influential Buddhist thinkers in Korean history, renowned for his synthetic approach to Buddhist philosophy. His commentary interprets the sūtra through the lens of the Jìngtǔ Lùn (Treatise on the Pure Land, attributed to Vasubandhu), systematically correlating passages of the sūtra with the fifteen kinds of meritorious adornment enumerated in that treatise. Notably, Wónhyo identifies the calling of Amitābha's name (recitation) as an "auxiliary cause" (zhùyīn) rather than the "direct cause" (zhèngyīn) of rebirth, arguing that the direct cause is the arousing of the bodhicitta (aspiration for supreme enlightenment). This interpretation distinguishes his reading from other commentators and was highly praised by later Chinese scholars. A colophon by the Song-dynasty monk Zōngxiāng (1198 CE) praises Wónhyo's commentary as surpassing all others.*

---

In explaining this sūtra, there are three gates of analysis: first, stating the general meaning; second, explaining the doctrinal purport of the sūtra; third, entering the text for interpretation.

### Part One: The General Meaning

The mind of sentient beings as mind is apart from characteristics and apart from nature, like the ocean, like space. Being like space, there is no characteristic it does not encompass: how could there be places of "east" and "west"? Being like the ocean, there is no nature it does not maintain: how could there not be times of movement and stillness?

Thus, either through defiled karma one follows the five turbidities (*wǔzhuó*) in long-flowing [saṃsāra], or, relying on pure conditions, one transcends the four floods and reaches permanent quiescence. Both such movement and stillness are a great dream; viewed from awakening, there is neither flowing nor quiescence. The impure land and the pure realm are originally one mind; birth-and-death and nirvāṇa ultimately have no two boundaries.

Yet the awakening of non-duality is difficult to attain, and the dream of delusion about the one [mind] is not easy to leave. Therefore the great sages descended in traces, both far and near, and the teachings they set forth either praised or censured. Thus Śākyamuni, the Well-Gone One, manifested in this impure land, cautioning against the five turbidities and encouraging departure [for the Pure Land]; while Amitābha Tathāgata presides over that pure realm, guiding the three grades of beings to birth there.

This sūtra, then, expresses the great intention of both Honored Ones in appearing in the world, and the essential gate for the four classes of practitioners entering the path. It reveals the Pure Land as worthy of aspiration and praises the wondrous virtues as worthy of refuge.

### Part Two: The Doctrinal Purport

This sūtra takes as its principal theme the two kinds of purity that transcend the three realms, and its intent is to cause all sentient beings to attain non-retrogression from the unsurpassed Way. What are the two kinds of purity? As the treatise says: "This purity is of two kinds: (1) purity of the vessel-world (*qìshìjiān*); (2) purity of the sentient-being world." There are four gates of entry: (1) the gate of full perfection, entered only by buddhas; (2) the gate of one-directional [progress], entered by bodhisattvas of the eighth bhūmi and above; (3) the gate of pure [practice], entered by bodhisattvas of the third bhūmi and above; (4) the gate of the rightly-determined assembly, entered only by those who are non-retrogressing. This sūtra's principal theme of two kinds of purity specifically reveals the fourth gate: even indeterminate śrāvakas and ordinary beings can be born there.

[The commentary addresses several points of doctrinal difficulty: whether there are women in the Pure Land (no; the "mother" mentioned in the *Śabdarāja Sūtra* refers to the manifestation at the time of descent, not to actual female rebirth); whether the small size of the city (10,000 yojanas) contradicts the immense size of Amitābha's body described in the *Contemplation Sūtra* (the buddha has many cities of various sizes, manifesting appropriately in each).]

### Part Three: Entering the Text

The text has three divisions: prologue, main teaching, and transmission.

**Prologue**: The six phrases of the prologue include two heading-phrases and four attesting-phrases. "Thus" (*rúshì*) gathers all the dharma that was heard, expressing the mind of faith and accord. "I heard" (*wǒ wén*) specifies the person who heard, expressing the intention of non-contention.

**The names of the śrāvaka assembly**: Śāriputra means "Son of the Body" (*shēnzǐ*). Maudgalyāyana means "Praise and Recitation." Kāśyapa means "Drinking Light." Kātyāyana means "Fan-Rope." Mahākauṣṭhila means "Great Knee." Revata means "Provisional Harmony." Cūḍapanthaka means "Snake-Slave" or "Little Path." Nanda means "Rejoicing." Ānanda means "Delight." Rāhula means "Obstruction" or "Palace-Born." Gavāṃpati means "Cow-Cud." Piṇḍola means "Elder" and Bhāradvāja means "Sharp Faculty." Kālodāyin means "Black-Upper" (he was Siddhārtha's teacher before the renunciation). Kapphiṇa means "Room-Star." Bākula means "Good Appearance." Anuruddha means "Without Poverty" or "As Wished."

**Purity of the environment** (vessel-world): There are two gates of meaning and six textual sections, with a total of fifteen meritorious qualities. [The commentary correlates each passage with the corresponding verse from the *Jìngtǔ Lùn*:]

(1) "No suffering, only the reception of every joy": this is the meritorious quality of freedom from all difficulties. (2) Seven-layered railings, nets, and rows of trees: adornment of the ground. (3) Three qualities within this passage: pools with golden sand (adornment of water); staircases and towers of gold, silver, etc. (various things); lotuses like wheels with blue radiance (adornment of wondrous color). (4) Five qualities: celestial music, golden ground, raining flowers, sovereign freedom, and provisions for use. (5) Two qualities: transformed manifestation (birds teaching the Dharma) and great meaning (absence of evil destinies). (6) Two qualities: adornment of space and adornment of the nature.

These fourteen qualities all transcend the three realms and six destinies; therefore they are collectively called "the pure world."

**Purity of the beings** (sentient-being world): Four meritorious qualities are displayed: (1) the host's merit (Amitābha's immeasurable light and immeasurable life); (2) the companions' merit (all śrāvaka disciples are arhats, the "pure lotus assembly"); (3) the great assembly's merit (all beings born there abide in the rightly-determined assembly and never retrogress); (4) the chief merit (many are at the stage of one more birth before buddhahood, i.e., ekajātipratibaddha).

**On the two causes of rebirth**: The sūtra says "one cannot be born in that land through few roots of merit, merit, and conditions." This reveals that the mind of great bodhi, which gathers many roots of merit as its conditions, is the direct cause of rebirth. One who arouses the bodhicitta can gather all the supreme roots of merit and sever evil karma: therefore it says "not through few roots of merit." ... Holding the name and achieving one-pointed concentration is the auxiliary cause. One to seven days denotes the variation: superior persons accomplish it quickly, inferior persons more slowly. The *Śabdarāja Sūtra* speaks of ten days of recitation for those of lesser capacity.

**The corroborating evidence**: The buddhas of the six directions all praise and confirm the sūtra.

[Colophon by Zōngxiāng, bhikṣu of the Song dynasty, dated 1198 CE]: "Among commentaries on the shorter Amitābha Sūtra, the Silla Master Wónhyo not only places the passage 'When the Buddha had finished teaching this sūtra' and below as the transmission section, but also identifies 'holding the name' as the auxiliary cause. This particularly stands out, surpassing all other commentaries."
