# 猘狗經
## Sūtra of the Mad Dog

Taishō Tripiṭaka No. 0214

Translated from the Chinese. Wu dynasty, translated by Zhi Qian (支謙)

---

The Buddha was in Rājagṛha, on Mount Gṛdhrakūṭa. On the fifteenth day of the month, during the recitation of the precepts, Ānanda knelt and said to the Buddha: "Now the Buddha is the savior of all, enlightening and guiding beings of the five destinies, those blind and benighted, enabling them to escape the evil paths. After the Buddha's parinirvāṇa, he will leave behind relics and the twelve divisions of the scriptures in the world. His disciples shall carry on the Buddha's majestic spiritual power, transmit the Buddha's sūtras and precepts, enlighten and deliver the people, confer upon them the precept-dharma, and enable people to make offerings. This is no different from the Buddha himself."

The Buddha said: "If there are people who receive the precepts from my disciples and then turn to envy and resent their teachers, such people have come from the evil paths."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "What do you mean by 'evil paths'?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "In the time of a past Buddha, there was a mad dog that turned and bit its own master. That former Buddha, out of compassion, recited a blessing for the mad dog. Seeing the Buddha's majestic spiritual power, the mad dog became joyful. That dog is now in the hells, its punishment not yet complete. After that Buddha's parinirvāṇa, when its punishment is finished, by virtue of its former moment of joy, it will be reborn into the human realm and receive the precepts from my disciples. It will happen that it receives the precepts from the very one who was its master when it was a mad dog. Because the dog retains vestiges of its former consciousness, it will turn and bite its former master." The Buddha said: "If there are people who, having received the precepts from a teacher, then slander and speak ill of that teacher, saying 'This is not my practice,' they are like a mad dog that turns and bites its own master. Those who slander and speak ill of their Dharma teacher were, in their past lives, dogs."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "When the dog's punishment is complete and it enters the human realm, why does it still turn and bite its former master?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "When that dog attains the human realm and upholds the Buddha's precepts and Dharma, receiving instruction, it becomes greedy for offerings and provisions, and in its stupidity fails to understand. It then slanders and speaks ill of its teacher, and therefore falls into the five grave evil destinies." The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Listen carefully! The Buddha shall explain this for you in full."

Ānanda said: "Yes! I accept the teaching."

The Buddha said: "There are people who uphold the Buddha's Dharma, precepts, and practice, and who teach others to serve the Buddha, yet who cause people to fall into the hells."

Ānanda rose in alarm, knelt, and asked the Buddha: "How is it that one who teaches people and should lead them to attain the Buddha's path instead causes them to fall into the hells?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Do you believe the Buddha's words or not?"

Ānanda said: "I believe the Buddha's words."

The Buddha said: "If you believe the Buddha's words, why are you alarmed to hear that one who receives the precepts may fall into the hells? If people did not fall into the hells, would that not make the Buddha's words false?"

Ānanda rose again, made obeisance, touched his head to the ground, circumambulated the Buddha three times, then clasped the Buddha's feet, knelt, and asked the Buddha: "Ānanda does not understand and does not yet know the roots of people's faculties. May the Buddha explain the meaning of causing people to enter the hells."

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "In the final age to come, there will be disciples who act as teachers but are lazy and idle, unable to study diligently, lacking wisdom. They will be greedy and corrupt, desiring people's offerings of money, grain, and silk, using these to support themselves. They will not be diligent in the Buddha's Dharma but will flatter and follow others. Seeing people who are greedy to kill, they will not confer the precept against killing. Seeing people who are fond of alcohol, they will not prohibit drinking, allowing some amount. When conferring the precepts upon people, they will say that one must pay a certain amount of money and goods to create merit, desiring only to obtain people's possessions. This is selling the precepts. It causes people to develop a casual and imprecise attitude toward the precepts, and they then violate the major precepts such as killing. Teachers who act thus lead people into the hells. Because they betray the Buddha's clear teaching, the guardian spirits of the Buddha's path find fault with them. They then fall ill from malevolent spirits, and if their offenses are grave, they may even die. After paying for their offenses, they enter the hells."

Ānanda asked the Buddha: "Those who are newly inspired and happen to encounter a bad teacher may not understand and may think the Dharma is truly like that, so they trust and accept such words out of ignorance." Ānanda asked the Buddha: "If they later meet a wise teacher, may they receive the precepts again?"

The Buddha said to Ānanda: "Within my Dharma, which is vast and great, it is certainly possible to repent and cleanse oneself anew. When one first generates aspiration, the mind is always darkened and benighted. Having been misled by a bad teacher, one truly did not know. To go and receive the precepts again is to truly enter the Dharma for the first time. When one did not know and did not understand, one was not a disciple of the Buddha but merely a minor good person in the world, without great merit."

When Ānanda heard what the Buddha taught, he rejoiced and made obeisance.
